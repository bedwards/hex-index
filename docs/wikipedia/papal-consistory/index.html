Papal consistory

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Papal consistory

On June 27, 2026, inside the Paul VI Audience Hall in Vatican City, two hundred and some cardinals from every corner of the globe stood together. They were not there to vote for a new pope; that sacred, secretive act belongs to the conclave. They were there to stand as one body, a collective voice of counsel and witness before Pope Leo XIV. This gathering was an extraordinary consistory, summoned just months after the closing of the 2025 Jubilee's Holy Door. It was a moment of profound gravity, born from a specific desire: to offer support and counsel to the Holy Father in the exercise of his lofty and weighty responsibility in the governance of the universal Church.

To understand why this meeting mattered so deeply in the summer of 2026, one must first strip away the modern gloss of ceremony and look at the ancient roots of the word itself. The term consistory derives from the Latin con-sistere, meaning "to stand together." It is not a metaphor for consultation or a loose advisory group; it is an architectural description of a physical reality. In the early centuries of the Church, before the complex machinery of the Vatican bureaucracy existed, the pope did not rule alone. He conferred with his Roman presbytery—a council of priests and deacons who oversaw different districts of Rome. As the Church grew and these local overseers were replaced by the cardinalate, this tradition of standing together persisted. The cardinals, from whose midst the popes themselves were historically chosen, continued to meet at the direct request of their pontiff.

For centuries, these gatherings were not merely ceremonial. They were the engine rooms of decision-making. Pope Leo IV, in a time when the papacy faced existential threats and internal strife, ordered that consistories be held twice weekly. The logic was one of relentless vigilance. Under Pope John VIII, this frequency was relaxed slightly to twice-monthly, yet the mandate remained clear: the pope needed the direct support and advice of Roman bishops and those traveling from other regions who happened to be in Rome. In these halls, matters of state were decided, and justice was dispensed directly by the pontiff with the weight of the entire college behind him.

Then came the Gregorian Reform, a seismic shift that sought to limit outside influences on the papacy and purify the selection of popes. With this reform, the power of the cardinals increased dramatically. The consistory evolved into the primary mechanism for revealing the list of those elevated to the rank of cardinal, a tradition that by the 17th century settled into a rhythm of roughly twice-yearly gatherings. However, as the Church's administrative needs grew more complex, the heavy lifting of justice and governance began to shift. Responsibility for judicial matters was transferred to the Roman Rota, and the broader functions of the Church were decentralized to the Roman Curia.

This bureaucratic expansion had an unintended consequence: it reduced the need for regular consistories as functional governing bodies. Slowly, the role of the consistory transformed from a council of war and justice into a primarily ceremonial function. The cardinals still stood together, but often to witness rather than to decide. For decades, this shift created a quiet disquiet among the clergy. Without these regular meetings, the College of Cardinals lost one of its few official occasions for future members of a papal conclave to meet and get to know each other. The isolation of individual cardinals in their dioceses grew, creating a disconnect between the global body and the center.

The ceremony of creating new cardinals became the focal point where this ancient tradition of "standing together" was most visibly preserved. At a consistory for creation, the pope creates new cardinals in the presence of a number of, if not all, extant cardinals. The process is steeped in symbolic weight. Though the names of the new cardinals are typically announced in advance, they do not formally become members of the College until the moment the pope publishes the decree of elevation at the consistory itself. This holds true even if a new cardinal cannot be present; their status is conferred by the collective presence of the body. For those who attend, the ritual is visceral. The new cardinals are presented with their rings, zucchetto (small skullcaps), and biretta (four-cornered silk hats) by the pope. These items are not mere accessories; they are scarlet, the distinctive color of cardinals' vesture, a symbol of their willingness to die for the faith. Furthermore, at the consistory, new cardinals—barring certain exceptions—are assigned titular churches in the Diocese of Rome. This ties them permanently to the local church of the bishop of Rome, grounding their global authority in a specific, physical parish in the Eternal City.

History has shown that consistories can serve as platforms for moments of unprecedented clarity and change. On February 11, 2013, Pope Benedict XVI used an ordinary consistory to announce his decision to retire as pope effective February 28, 2013. He did so in Latin, the language of the Church's deepest traditions, before the entire College of Cardinals. It was a shocking moment that shattered the expectation of a papacy for life and demonstrated the consistory's power as a stage for historic declarations. Benedict himself created cardinals at five consistories during his pontificate, with the last held in November 2012, just months before his resignation.

Pope Francis, upon taking office, sought to reinvigorate the collegial spirit that had been lost. He followed the custom of holding day-long meetings with the entire College and various advisers on the day preceding the consistory of creation. In 2007 and 2010, Benedict had done this, but Francis made it a consistent practice to foster dialogue. His 2014 consistory for creating new cardinals was preceded by an extraordinary consistory where Cardinal Walter Kasper gave a landmark address designed to launch the discussions of the Synod on the Family held later that year. In 2015, a similar extraordinary consistory—held immediately prior to a consistory to create cardinals—discussed the urgent reform of the Roman Curia.

Francis created cardinals at ten consistories at roughly annual intervals, maintaining a steady drumbeat of renewal. His last such gathering occurred in December 2024, just a few months before his death. The regularity of these events was a deliberate attempt to keep the College connected. Yet, even under Francis, the trend of fewer all-cardinal consistories compared to some predecessors sparked debate. The fear was that without these gatherings, the Church lost its ability to discern collectively.

The election of Leo XIV in 2025 marked a new chapter in this history. His approach signaled a return to the consistory as a vital instrument of governance and discernment, not just ceremony. On June 13, 2025, he held his first ordinary consistory of his papacy. The agenda was specific: he announced the canonization dates of nine Blesseds, reaffirming the Church's role in recognizing holiness. But it was the extraordinary consistory that followed which captured the world's attention.

Summoning all cardinals worldwide to the Vatican on January 7-8, 2026, Leo XIV sought to do more than just update a list of names. This gathering took place immediately after the closing of the Holy Door at St. Peter's Basilica on January 6, marking the conclusion of the 2025 Jubilee. The timing was not accidental. The Jubilee had been a time of pilgrimage and reflection; this consistory was intended to channel that energy into governance. It was designed to foster discernment and offer "support and counsel to the Holy Father." The language used in the Vatican statements emphasized the weighty responsibility of the papacy, suggesting that Leo viewed his office as one that required the constant, active input of his brothers in the episcopate.

The outcome of this gathering was immediate and forward-looking. At the end of the January consistory, Pope Leo announced a bold new schedule: he intended to hold another extraordinary consistory in June 26-29, 2026, and one each year after. This decision came in direct response to a request from pre-2025 conclave general congregations. The cardinals themselves had asked for this regularity, recognizing that the isolation of their diocesan lives was eroding the collegial nature of their office.

The second extraordinary consistory of Leo's pontificate took place as promised, from June 26-27, 2026, at the Paul VI Audience Hall. The atmosphere was one of renewed purpose. Unlike the sporadic meetings of previous decades, this event was part of a structured rhythm of governance. It signaled that the College of Cardinals was no longer merely an advisory body to be consulted in emergencies or for ceremonial appearances. They were becoming a true college again, standing together in the face of modern challenges.

The history of the consistory is also marked by the mysterious practice known as in pectore. This Latin phrase means "in the breast" or "in the heart." It is a way of creating a cardinal without public announcement. The pope reserves the appointment, keeping the name hidden to protect the new cardinal from political persecution or danger in their home country. These cardinals exist within the College but are known only to the Pope until he chooses to reveal them. This practice underscores the consistory's dual nature: it is both a public stage for glory and a private sanctuary for protection.

As we look at the evolution of the papal consistory from the weekly mandates of Leo IV to the strategic gatherings of Leo XIV, a clear narrative emerges. The institution has oscillated between being a functional council of state and a ceremonial pageant. In times of crisis or reform, the balance tips back toward function. When the Church feels fragmented, the need for cardinals to "stand together" becomes urgent.

The events of June 2026 were not just about scheduling future meetings; they were a reaffirmation of a fundamental truth about Catholic governance. The Pope is the successor of Peter, but he does not rule in a vacuum. He rules with the counsel of the College, a body that represents the local churches scattered across the globe. When the cardinals gather in Rome, they are bringing the realities of their flocks into the heart of the Church's decision-making process.

The scarlet biretta and zucchetto worn by new cardinals are not just symbols of rank; they are reminders of a commitment to unity. The assignment of titular churches in Rome is a reminder that every bishop, no matter how far from the center, has a specific place within the Roman diocese. These traditions, preserved through centuries of change, serve as anchors for a Church that often feels adrift.

Pope Leo XIV's decision to institutionalize annual extraordinary consistories acknowledges the complexity of the modern world. The issues facing the Church—geopolitical instability, social fragmentation, and internal reform—are too vast for any single mind to navigate alone. By summoning the entire College annually, Leo is ensuring that the "standing together" of con-sistere remains a living reality, not just an etymological curiosity.

The contrast between the sporadic consistories of the late 20th century and the rigorous schedule established by Leo XIV highlights a shift in ecclesiastical priorities. The disquiet over the lack of consistories that plagued the pontificate of Francis's predecessors was a symptom of a deeper malaise: the isolation of the episcopate. By restoring the rhythm of regular, high-level gatherings, the Vatican is attempting to heal that fracture.

It is worth noting the specific details of these recent meetings. The extraordinary consistory in January 2026 took place just one day after the Jubilee's Holy Door closed. This proximity was symbolic. The Jubilee had called the faithful to conversion and renewal; the consistory called the leaders of the Church to apply that renewal to their governance. The discussions were not abstract theological exercises but practical counsel on how to guide a universal Church through turbulent times.

The June 2026 gathering, held at the Paul VI Audience Hall, continued this momentum. With cardinals from every continent present, the visual of two hundred men in red robes standing together was a powerful image of unity. It was a rejection of the idea that the Church is merely a collection of isolated national entities. Instead, it presented a vision of a global communion, united under the Pope and bound by their shared responsibility.

The legacy of Benedict XVI's 2013 resignation announcement serves as a reminder of the consistory's power to shape history. It was not a press conference; it was a formal, liturgical act witnessed by the entire College. Leo XIV is building on this legacy, using the consistory not just for announcements but for sustained dialogue and discernment.

The practice of holding day-long meetings prior to the creation of cardinals, a custom revived by Benedict and continued by Francis, has found its place in Leo's pontificate as well. These pre-consistory sessions allow for deep, unstructured conversation before the formal ceremonies begin. It is here that the real work often happens—where strategies are discussed, concerns are raised, and consensus is built.

The role of the Roman Curia in all this cannot be overstated. While the Consistory is the body of cardinals, the Curia is the administrative machinery. The transfer of judicial functions to the Roman Rota centuries ago changed the dynamic, but it did not eliminate the need for papal oversight. Leo's consistories suggest a renewed desire to bridge the gap between the administrative state and the spiritual counsel of the College.

In the end, the story of the Papal Consistory is a story about authority and community. It is about how a single man, the Pope, exercises his supreme authority not by fiat alone, but through the collective wisdom of those who stand with him. From the weekly meetings of Leo IV to the annual summits of Leo XIV, the impulse remains the same: to stand together.

The events of 2026 have set a new precedent. The cardinals are no longer just attendees at the Pope's whim; they are active participants in a structured rhythm of governance. They meet not only to receive hats and rings but to offer counsel, to discern, and to bear witness to their shared mission. As the Church moves forward from the Jubilee year, the consistory stands as a testament to the enduring power of community in the face of isolation.

The scarlet hats are bright, the halls are grand, and the rituals are ancient. But beneath the ceremony lies a profound truth: the Pope needs his cardinals just as much as they need him. In standing together, they fulfill the oldest command of their order. They con-sistere. And in doing so, they ensure that the Church remains a living, breathing body, guided by many voices speaking with one heart.
