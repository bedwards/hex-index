Theonomy

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Theonomy

In the quiet theological archives of the 20th century, a radical proposition took root that sought to collapse the distance between the pulpit and the courthouse. It began with a simple, etymological fusion: theos, meaning God, and nomos, meaning law. From these Greek roots emerged the concept of Theonomy, a hypothetical Christian form of government where divine law, specifically the judicial codes of the Old Testament, serves as the supreme and binding constitution for civil society. This is not a vague spiritual idealism but a rigorous legal framework. Theonomists argue that the laws given to ancient Israel were not temporary cultural artifacts but the eternal, unchanging will of the Creator, designed to govern all human authority—individual, family, church, and civil. For the movement's chief architects, Gary North, Greg Bahnsen, and R.J. Rushdoony, the question was never whether biblical law should influence society, but how any society could claim legitimacy if it ignored the specific penalties and statutes found in Scripture.

The central tension of Theonomy lies in its rejection of the traditional Christian distinction between the "moral," "ceremonial," and "civil" laws of the Bible. While mainstream Christian theology has long held that the ceremonial and judicial laws of the Old Covenant were fulfilled or abrogated by the coming of Christ, Theonomy posits a different reality. It presumes that the judicial laws of biblical Israel have never been revoked. Therefore, the argument goes, all civil governments are morally and legally obligated to enforce them, including the specific capital penalties prescribed for crimes like adultery, witchcraft, and blasphemy. This is not a suggestion for voluntary compliance; it is a mandate for the state. The movement operates on the "regulative principle of the state," a corollary to the famous regulative principle of worship, which dictates that civil governments must refrain from coercion unless Scripture explicitly prescribes their intervention. If the Bible is silent on an issue, the state has no right to legislate; if the Bible commands a penalty, the state has no right to ignore it.

The Architects of a New Covenant Order

The intellectual architecture of modern Theonomy was constructed in the mid-to-late 20th century, rising from the ashes of the cultural upheavals of the 1960s. R.J. Rushdoony, a pastor and theologian, is often cited as the father of the movement. His 1973 work, The Institutes of Biblical Law, laid the groundwork by arguing that the Bible is the abiding standard for all human authority. Rushdoony believed that the fragmentation of Western society was a direct result of the abandonment of God's law, and that the only path to restoration was a "Christian reconstruction" of society based on biblical principles. He did not view this as a political strategy in the secular sense but as a theological necessity. For Rushdoony, there is no such thing as a neutral public square; every law, whether secular or religious, ultimately rests on a foundation of either God's law or man's law.

Joining Rushdoony was Greg Bahnsen, a philosopher and apologist who provided the rigorous logical framework that allowed Theonomy to move from the fringe into serious theological discourse. Bahnsen's 1977 magnum opus, Theonomy in Christian Ethics, remains the definitive text of the movement. In it, he systematically dismantled the idea that the Old Testament judicial laws were merely civil accommodations for a specific time and place. Bahnsen argued that the "moral" law (the Ten Commandments) and the "judicial" law (the civil statutes) were inextricably linked. To discard the penalties while keeping the principles was, in his view, a logical inconsistency. He famously asserted that the laws of God are the standard which Christian voters and officials ought to pursue, and that the civil law given to Israel is continuously binding, save for the cultural connotations specific to the nation itself.

Gary North, an economist and historian, brought a pragmatic, almost revolutionary zeal to the project. North viewed Theonomy not just as a theological stance but as a blueprint for a new economic and political order. He argued for the "universal development of Biblical theocratic republics," a phrase that would become a lightning rod for critics. North's influence was profound in the 1980s and 1990s, where he and his colleagues established institutions like the Institute for Christian Economics and the Chalcedon Foundation to promote these ideas. Together, this triad of thinkers created a cohesive vision where the Bible was not merely a book of personal salvation but a constitution for the state. They asserted that for an adequate and genuinely Christian ethic, humanity must have God's word, only God's word, and all of God's word.

The Regulative Principle and the Limits of State Power

One of the most misunderstood aspects of Theonomy is its relationship to state power. To the casual observer, the demand for the enforcement of Old Testament penalties might suggest a totalitarian impulse. However, theonomists argue the exact opposite. They contend that Theonomy is actually a doctrine of limited government. The "regulative principle of the state" is the key to this understanding. Just as the Reformed tradition argues that the church may only do what Scripture explicitly commands in worship, Theonomy argues that the state may only do what Scripture explicitly commands in civil governance.

"The civil government has no right to legislate on matters where God has remained silent."

This principle creates a radical constraint on political authority. In a Theonomic framework, the state cannot tax beyond what is necessary for its biblical functions, cannot legislate morality in areas not explicitly covered by Scripture, and cannot intervene in the family or church unless biblical law dictates such intervention. The state is not the source of law; it is the servant of a higher law. This stands in stark contrast to the modern liberal state, which claims the authority to define right and wrong based on shifting cultural consensus or utilitarian calculations. For theonomists, the modern state is a usurper, arrogating to itself powers that belong only to God.

This framework also leads to the controversial conclusion that certain crimes, defined in the Old Testament, must be punishable by death. Under a strict Theonomic system, homosexual acts, adultery, witchcraft, idolatry, and blasphemy are not merely sins to be confessed or vices to be discouraged; they are capital offenses against the sovereign authority of God. The rationale is that these crimes undermine the very fabric of the covenant community and the glory of God, which is the ultimate end of civil government. Theonomists argue that failing to execute these penalties is a dereliction of the state's duty to uphold justice.

The Aquinas Distinction and the Shadow of Natural Law

The historical roots of Theonomy are often traced back to Thomas Aquinas, the 13th-century Dominican theologian. Aquinas famously wrote, "if a sovereign were to order these judicial precepts to be observed in his kingdom, he would not sin." This statement is frequently cited by Theonomists as a patristic endorsement of their view. However, a closer reading of Aquinas reveals a nuanced distinction that theonomists themselves acknowledge but critics often miss.

Aquinas did not believe that the specific judicial precepts of the Old Testament were binding in their literal form for all times and places. Instead, he taught that these precepts contained varying degrees of universal principles of justice that reflected natural law. He argued that while the specifics of the Mosaic law were particular to Israel, the general equity or underlying principles of justice were universally applicable. This led to a category often called "General Equity Theonomy," though it is distinct from the strict Theonomy of Rushdoony and Bahnsen. Aquinas believed that a king could adopt the principles of biblical justice without adopting the penalties of biblical law.

The modern Theonomic movement, however, rejects this distinction. They argue that the "general equity" approach is a slippery slope that allows human reason to override divine command. If the specific penalties are not binding, then the specific laws themselves become optional, and the authority of Scripture is diminished. For Bahnsen and North, the move to "general equity" was a concession to humanism, a way of keeping the idea of God's law while discarding its power. They insisted on the necessity, sufficiency, and unity of Scripture. If the Bible says a crime deserves death, then the state must impose death, regardless of whether it seems "cruel" or "outdated" by modern standards.

The Fracture: From Capital Punishment to Covenantal Theology

The history of Theonomy is not a monolith; it is a story of internal debate and evolution, particularly regarding the application of Old Testament penalties. While the movement's founding fathers were adamant about the binding nature of the death penalty, cracks began to appear in the 21st century. The most significant shift came from Joel McDurmon, the former President of American Vision, a major organization dedicated to promoting Theonomic principles.

In a move that shocked the movement's traditionalists, McDurmon and other newer writers began to argue that the death penalties of the Mosaic law were no longer binding in the New Covenant era. They proposed a version of "Covenantal Theonomy" that maintained the moral and judicial principles of the Bible but rejected the specific application of capital punishment for crimes like adultery or blasphemy. They argued that the New Covenant brought a change in the administration of justice, shifting from the physical execution of sinners to spiritual restoration and church discipline.

This shift ignited a fierce debate within the Reformed community. J.D. Hall, a former pastor and prominent critic of Theonomy, seized upon this development to argue that modern theonomists had abandoned the very core of the movement. In a 2015 debate with McDurmon, Hall argued that if the death penalties are no longer binding, then the claim that the judicial laws are "continuously binding" becomes meaningless. He contended that one cannot hold to Theonomy in any meaningful way while selectively discarding the most visible and controversial aspects of the Mosaic law.

"Abandoning Mosaic penologies such as the death penalty means that McDurmon and others who hold similar positions cannot be said to hold to theonomy in any meaningful way."

This internal fracture highlights the tension at the heart of the movement. Is Theonomy a static adherence to the text of the Old Testament, or is it a dynamic application of biblical principles to changing cultural contexts? For the purists, the answer is clear: the text is the text, and the penalties are the penalties. For the moderates, the spirit of the law must be distinguished from the letter of the law to ensure its relevance in a post-Christian world.

The Critique: Dominion Theology and the Fear of Theocracy

To its critics, Theonomy is not a nuanced theological position but a dangerous form of dominion theology, often equated with theocracy. The term "dominion theology" has become a catch-all for any Christian movement that seeks to exert political control over society, and Theonomy is frequently cited as its most extreme manifestation. Critics point to the goals articulated by early theonomic authors, such as the exclusion of non-Christians from voting and citizenship, as evidence of an anti-democratic agenda. They argue that a system which mandates the death penalty for blasphemy or idolatry is inherently intolerant and incompatible with the pluralistic values of modern civilization.

"Critics see theonomy as a significant form of dominion theology, which they define as a theocracy."

The critique goes deeper than just the penalties. Critics argue that Theonomy fails to account for the redemptive-historical movement of the Bible. They contend that the Old Testament laws were given to a theocratic nation-state that no longer exists, and that the New Testament church is a spiritual community, not a political entity. To impose the laws of ancient Israel on a modern, multi-ethnic nation, they argue, is to ignore the fulfillment of the law in Christ.

Furthermore, the movement has faced criticism from within the broader Reformed tradition. Some in modern Reformed churches criticize any relationship between the historic Reformed faith and Theonomy, arguing that it distorts the teachings of the Reformers like Calvin and Knox. While Calvinists have historically affirmed the moral law, they have generally rejected the idea that the civil statutes of Israel are binding on modern states. The debate has led to a proliferation of critical literature, including Theonomy: A Reformed Critique (1990), edited by William Barker and W. Robert Godfrey, which gathered leading Reformed theologians to dismantle the Theonomic argument.

Despite these criticisms, there are still Calvinists who affirm that Theonomy is consistent with the historic Reformed confessions. They argue that the confessions themselves uphold the abiding authority of the moral law and that the distinction between moral, ceremonial, and judicial law is not as sharp as the critics claim. This internal debate within the Reformed community underscores the complexity of the issue. It is not simply a matter of "Christian" versus "secular," but a deep theological disagreement about the nature of law, covenant, and the role of the state in God's redemptive plan.

The Legacy of a Radical Vision

Today, as we look back on the trajectory of Theonomy from its inception in the 1970s to the present day, its legacy is a complex tapestry of influence and controversy. The movement has undeniably shaped the landscape of American conservative Christianity, pushing the boundaries of how Christians think about law, politics, and culture. Even those who reject Theonomy have been forced to engage with its arguments, refining their own views on the relationship between the Old and New Testaments.

The impact of Gary North, Greg Bahnsen, and R.J. Rushdoony extends beyond their writings. They created a network of institutions, schools, and publishing houses that continue to promote a vision of a society ordered by God's law. While the movement may have softened its edges in recent years, with figures like Joel McDurmon moving away from the death penalty, the core commitment remains: the Bible is the supreme standard for all human authority.

The debate over Theonomy is not merely an academic exercise; it is a struggle over the soul of the Western legal tradition. It asks the fundamental question: Where does law come from? Is it the product of human reason and social contract, or is it the revealed will of a sovereign God? For the theonomist, the answer is clear. The law of God is the only foundation upon which a just society can be built. For the critic, the attempt to impose this law is a dangerous overreach that threatens the freedoms of conscience and the diversity of modern life.

As the 21st century progresses, the shadow of Theonomy continues to loom over the intersection of faith and politics. Whether one views it as a radical call to righteousness or a perilous path to tyranny, it remains one of the most provocative and thought-provoking movements in modern Christian thought. The questions it raises about the nature of authority, the role of Scripture, and the purpose of the state are as relevant today as they were when Rushdoony first penned his Institutes. Theonomists may not have achieved the "universal development of Biblical theocratic republics" they once envisioned, but they have succeeded in forcing the church to confront the uncomfortable reality of its own relationship to the law of God.

In the end, Theonomy stands as a testament to the enduring power of the Old Testament and the relentless human desire to see the kingdom of God fully manifested on earth. It is a vision that is both inspiring and terrifying, a blueprint for a society that is either the ultimate realization of divine justice or the ultimate nightmare of religious coercion. The choice of how to interpret this vision, and whether to embrace or reject it, remains one of the defining challenges for the Christian church in the modern world. The debate is far from over, and as long as the question of God's law remains open, the shadow of theonomists will continue to stretch across the political landscape.
