Paregoric

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Paregoric

In the summer of 1865, a woman in Mississippi watched her family succumb to "cramp colic" and "cholera morbus" with a grim resignation that defined an era. The village drinking water was contaminated, a silent killer, yet no one connected the two. Instead, her father reached for the medicine chest, dispensing a golden, anise-scented liquid with the casual frequency of a cook adding salt to a stew. He called it paregoric. To the modern eye, this scene is a portrait of medical desperation, but in the mid-19th century, it was a portrait of survival. Paregoric, or camphorated tincture of opium, was not merely a drug; it was the bedrock of household medicine, a panacea that soothed teething infants, halted violent diarrhea, and quieted the frantic coughs of the dying. It was a substance so ubiquitous that for generations, it was the difference between life and death in a world without antibiotics, sanitation, or a deep understanding of bacteriology.

The story of this elixir begins not in the dusty apothecaries of the American South, but in the lecture halls of Leiden University in the early 18th century. Jakob Le Mort, a professor of chemistry who lived from 1650 to 1718, was obsessed with the humoral theories that dominated medicine at the time. He sought to balance the body's fluids through a carefully constructed mixture. Le Mort prepared an elixir for asthma that he christened "paregoric." The name itself is a linguistic journey from the Ancient Greek parēgorikós, meaning "soothing" or "consoling." In its earliest usage, the word was applied to oratory, describing the act of talking over or calming a dispute. Le Mort's creation, however, took that concept of soothing and made it chemical. His original formula was a complex alchemy of honey, licorice, flowers of Benjamin (benzoin), opium, camphor, oil of aniseed, salt of tartar, and spirit of wine.

By 1721, Le Mort's creation had crossed the Atlantic of knowledge to appear in the London Pharmacopoeia as the "Elixir Asthmaticum." The ingredients were not random; they were assembled according to the prevailing medical logic of the day, designed to counteract the specific imbalances believed to cause respiratory distress and gastrointestinal turmoil. Yet, the core of the remedy remained the opium, a potent extract of the poppy that acted as a powerful sedative and analgesic. Over the next two centuries, the formula would be tweaked, standardized, and democratized, but the essential nature of the drug remained constant: it was a liquid calm in a chaotic world.

The Panacea of the Common Folk

By the 18th and 19th centuries, paregoric had transcended the realm of the academic physician to become a staple of the common household. It was the go-to remedy for a staggering array of ailments, reflecting a time when the line between a cough, a fever, and a digestive crisis was often blurred. Parents gave it to children to calm fretful nights and to rub on gums to counteract the agony of teething. It was an expectorant for the chesty, an antitussive for the coughing, and, perhaps most critically, an antidiarrheal for a world plagued by waterborne diseases.

The dosage was a matter of tradition and intuition. A formula published in Dr. Chase's Recipes in 1865 offers a glimpse into the precise, almost ritualistic preparation of the medicine. The recipe called for "Best opium 1/2 dr." (a dram being roughly 1.8 grams), dissolved in about two tablespoons of boiling water. To this base, the apothecary added benzoic acid, oil of anise, clarified honey, camphor gum, and a significant volume of 76 percent alcohol. The mixture was to be macerated, or kept warm, for two weeks. The instructions for consumption were stark in their simplicity: "For children, 5 to 20 drops, adults, 1 to 2 tea-spoons."

Another recipe, found in The Medical Companion, Or Family Physician from 1827, was even more straightforward. It demanded purified opium, flowers of Benzoin, camphor, and essential oil of anise-seed, each two drachms, steeped in two pints of brandy. The mixture was to be digested for eight or ten days, with the bottle shaken frequently, before being strained. These were not the sterile, standardized pills of the 20th century; they were home-brewed elixirs, potent and dangerous, yet trusted implicitly by generations.

The reliance on paregoric was born of necessity. In the aftermath of the American Civil War, as the Mississippi woman recalled, the drinking water of entire villages was contaminated. Families suffered "violent attacks of stomach and intestinal trouble," terms that covered a multitude of biological horrors. Without the ability to treat the underlying infection, the medical focus shifted to symptom management. Paregoric stopped the diarrhea, slowed the gut, and sedated the pain. It did not cure the cholera or the dysentery, but it bought time. It allowed the body to rest, and in many cases, that rest was enough for the patient to survive the acute phase of the illness.

The cultural penetration of the drug was so profound that the word "paregoric" extended beyond the liquid tincture. It came to describe boiled sweets, particularly the Army & Navy brand, which contained the substance. These hard candies were a way to deliver the soothing properties of opium to children in a form that felt like a treat rather than medicine. The term itself, evolving from the Greek concept of soothing speech, had become synonymous with relief in its most physical form.

The Shadow of Addiction and the Rise of Regulation

For hundreds of years, paregoric flowed freely through society, its addictive potential masked by the immediate, desperate need for relief. However, the 20th century brought a seismic shift in the understanding of narcotics. As the addictive properties of opium became more widely understood, the golden age of the patent medicine began to crumble. The mysterious compositions of these household remedies, once a mark of their specialized nature, became a source of public suspicion and regulatory scrutiny.

The turning point arrived with the Pure Food and Drug Act of 1906 in the United States. This legislation was a direct response to the "mysterious compositions" of patent medicines that had flooded the market. The Act required that certain specified drugs, including alcohol, cocaine, heroin, morphine, and cannabis, be accurately labeled with their contents and dosage. No longer could a bottle of paregoric hide its opium content behind a trade name or a decorative label. The law mandated transparency.

The impact was immediate. It is estimated that the sale of patent medicines containing opiates decreased by 33 percent after labeling was mandated. The public, once blind to the ingredients they were ingesting, now had to confront the reality that their cough syrup and diarrhea cure contained the same drug found in the most dangerous narcotics. The same year, in 1906, Britain instituted laws requiring the disclosure of ingredients and the limitation of narcotic content, followed by Canada in 1908.

The regulatory net tightened further with the Harrison Narcotics Tax Act of 1914 in the United States. This act restricted the manufacture and distribution of opiates, including laudanum and coca derivatives. It was a comprehensive effort to control the flow of narcotics, following similar moves in France with the Loi des stupefiants in 1916 and Britain's Dangerous Drugs Act in 1920. The Harrison Act regulated "opium or coca leaves, or any compound, manufacture, salt, derivative or preparation thereof," but it contained a crucial loophole for low-concentration medical products.

Paregoric, with its relatively low concentration of opium compared to laudanum or raw opium extracts, was classified as an "Exempt Narcotic." This classification allowed it to remain available for legitimate medical use while still being subject to oversight. The logic was that the benefits of the drug in treating specific conditions outweighed the risks, provided it was used within reasonable limits.

The Era of the Exempt Narcotic

Until 1970, paregoric occupied a unique legal space in the United States. It could be purchased at a pharmacy without a medical prescription, in accordance with federal law. However, this freedom was not without its strings. Federal law dictated strict limits on the quantity that could be dispensed. No more than two ounces of paregoric could be given to the same purchaser within a 48-hour period. This restriction was designed to prevent hoarding and abuse while still allowing families to treat acute episodes of illness.

The transaction itself was a ritual of accountability. Purchasers were required to sign a register or logbook, creating a paper trail of every bottle sold. Pharmacies were technically required to request identification from any purchaser not personally known to the pharmacist. This system placed the pharmacist in the role of gatekeeper, a position of trust and responsibility. Even where the law permitted over-the-counter sales, the discretion of the individual pharmacist often determined whether a customer could walk away with a bottle.

The landscape varied wildly from state to state. Michigan, for example, allowed over-the-counter sales of paregoric until April 1964. Yet, curiously, it still allowed OTC sales of certain exempt cough medication preparations that contained 60 mg of codeine per fluid ounce. This inconsistency highlighted the fragmented nature of drug regulation in the early 20th century. The rules were a patchwork of local laws and federal mandates, creating a confusing environment for both consumers and sellers.

As the decades passed, the understanding of addiction and the dangers of opioid use deepened. The "stubborn adherence to the clinical deduction" that paregoric was useful in certain types of cough, noted by clinicians in 1944, began to face new challenges. The medical community started to weigh the risks of long-term exposure against the short-term benefits. The era of the "soothing" elixir was coming to an end, replaced by an era of strict control and classification.

The Modern Classification and the Schedule Shift

The final blow to the era of the freely available paregoric came with the Controlled Substances Act of 1970. This landmark legislation reorganized the legal status of drugs in the United States, categorizing them into five schedules based on their potential for abuse and medical utility. Paregoric was classified as a Schedule III drug. This classification meant that while it still had accepted medical uses, it had a potential for abuse that was lower than Schedule I or II drugs, but higher than Schedule IV or V.

The classification as a Schedule III drug required a prescription for purchase in most contexts, effectively ending the era of the over-the-counter bottle. However, the law was nuanced. Drugs that contained a mixture of kaolin, pectin, and paregoric, such as Donnagel-PG and Parepectolin, were classified as Schedule V drugs. These products were considered to have a lower potential for abuse due to the dilution of the opium and the presence of other ingredients.

For a time, these Schedule V formulations remained available over-the-counter in many states. They were the last vestiges of the old world, where a mother could walk into a pharmacy and buy a remedy for her child's diarrhea without a doctor's note. This availability persisted until the early 1990s, when the tide turned once again. The FDA began to ban the sale of anti-diarrheal drugs containing kaolin and pectin, citing a lack of evidence for their efficacy and concerns about safety.

Furthermore, Donnagel-PG contained tincture of belladonna, a substance that became prescription-only on January 1, 1993. This combination of regulatory actions effectively removed the most common forms of paregoric from the over-the-counter market. The drug that had once been a household staple was now a strictly controlled prescription medication, a relic of a different medical age.

The Current State of a Forgotten Remedy

Today, paregoric exists in a liminal space. It is currently listed in the United States Pharmacopeia, a testament to its enduring status as a recognized medical preparation. However, its availability is precarious. The manufacture of the drug was discontinued for several months beginning in late 2011. This hiatus caused anxiety among those who relied on the drug, particularly for the treatment of severe diarrhea or as part of a weaning protocol for infants born to opiate-addicted women.

Production and distribution resumed in 2012, but the lapse in manufacturing raised questions about the drug's future. It remains unclear whether the interruption actually resulted in a shortage, as prescription drugs are often still available for many months after manufacture has ceased due to existing stockpiles. In France, the narrative has taken a slightly different turn. Paregoric was available without a prescription until 1986, but nowadays, its primary use is to wean infants born to opiate-addicted women. This specific application highlights the drug's continued utility in managing opioid withdrawal, a function that aligns with its original soothing properties.

The principal active ingredient in modern paregoric remains powdered opium. In the United States, the formula for Paregoric U.S.P. is a precise mixture: tincture of opium 40 mL, anise oil 4 mL, benzoic acid 4 g, camphor 4 g, glycerin 40 mL, alcohol 450 mL, and purified water 450 mL, diluted with alcohol to 1000 mL. The final product contains the equivalent of 0.4 mg of anhydrous morphine per milliliter. This concentration is low, but it is significant enough to produce a powerful effect, especially in children or those with a low tolerance.

The survival of paregoric through the centuries is a testament to the stubbornness of clinical observation. Despite the rise of modern medicine, the proliferation of synthetic drugs, and the tightening of regulations, paregoric has persisted. In 1944, clinicians evaluating its expectorant action noted that its survival was likely due to "keen clinical observation" that recognized its utility in certain types of cough. That observation holds true today. While it is no longer the first line of defense for a child's cough or a case of diarrhea, it remains a vital tool in specific medical scenarios.

A Legacy of Soothing

The story of paregoric is more than a history of a drug; it is a history of how humanity has sought to soothe its pain. From the humoral theories of Jakob Le Mort to the contaminated wells of post-Civil War Mississippi, from the open shelves of the 19th-century apothecary to the locked cabinets of the modern pharmacy, paregoric has been a constant companion in the human struggle against suffering.

It was a drug that blurred the lines between medicine and poison, between relief and addiction. It was used to treat the symptoms of a world that did not understand the causes of disease. It was a remedy that allowed families to survive the "violent attacks" of cholera and dysentery when no other options existed. And yet, its legacy is complicated. The same soothing properties that calmed a fretful child also led to addiction and dependency in a generation that did not fully understand the risks.

Today, paregoric is a shadow of its former self. It is a prescription drug, tightly regulated, and rarely the first choice of a physician. But for those who still need it, it remains a powerful and effective treatment. The anise oil still provides a familiar scent, the camphor still offers a cooling sensation, and the opium still provides the deep, chemical calm that has soothed humanity for three centuries.

In the end, paregoric stands as a reminder of a time when medicine was a blend of art, science, and necessity. It was a product of its time, born of the best knowledge available, and used with the best intentions. Its journey from a household staple to a controlled substance mirrors the broader evolution of medicine itself—a journey from the mysterious and the magical to the regulated and the precise. But the core truth remains: it is a substance of soothing, a chemical embrace in a world that often feels too harsh to endure. The word parēgorikós means to console, to talk over, to calm. And in that sense, paregoric has succeeded, even if the world has changed around it.

The survival of this elixir is a testament to the enduring human need for relief. Whether it was the mother in Mississippi dispensing drops to a child with "cramp colic" or the modern physician administering it to an infant withdrawing from opioids, the function of the drug remains the same. It is the soothing agent, the quiet voice in the storm of pain. And as long as there is pain to soothe, paregoric, in whatever form it takes, will likely find a place in the medicine cabinet of the world.

The history of paregoric is a mirror held up to the history of medicine itself. It reflects our progress, our failures, our misunderstandings, and our relentless pursuit of relief. From the elixirs of Leiden to the Schedule III classifications of the 21st century, the story of paregoric is the story of our relationship with pain and the substances we use to manage it. It is a story of survival, of regulation, and of the enduring power of a simple, soothing word.

"The survival of paregoric through the centuries, and particularly through recent critical decades, is probably due to keen clinical observation and stubborn adherence to the clinical deduction that paregoric is useful in certain types of cough."

This quote, penned by clinicians in 1944, captures the essence of the drug's journey. It was not the grand theories of the day that kept paregoric alive, but the stubborn, practical observation that it worked. In an age of high-tech medicine and molecular biology, that simple observation remains relevant. Paregoric works. And in the end, that is the only thing that matters to the patient seeking relief.
