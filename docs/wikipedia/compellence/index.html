Compellence

16 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Compellence

In the winter of 1962, the United States did not fire a single shot at the Soviet Union, yet it successfully forced the removal of nuclear missiles from Cuba. This was not a victory of brute military might, but a triumph of a specific, high-stakes diplomatic art form known as compellence. Unlike the passive stance of waiting for an enemy to make a mistake, compellence is the active, often agonizing process of forcing an adversary to change their behavior. It is the strategic application of pressure designed to make an opponent retreat, reverse an action, or fundamentally alter their course of conduct. When policymakers speak of "coercion" in the abstract, they are often conflating two distinct concepts: deterrence, which aims to preserve the status quo by threatening punishment for future actions, and compellence, which seeks to disrupt the status quo by demanding immediate change. The difference is subtle but profound. Deterrence is drawing a line in the sand and waiting to see if the other side crosses it; compellence is stepping over the line yourself, or pushing the other side back, until they comply.

This distinction is not merely academic semantics; it is the difference between a successful crisis management and a catastrophic war. As the geopolitical landscape shifts in 2026, with tensions flaring from the Middle East to Eastern Europe, understanding the mechanics of compellence is more critical than ever. It is the engine behind the "carrot and stick" philosophy that drives modern statecraft, a delicate balance where the threat of violence must be potent enough to be believed, yet controlled enough to avoid spiraling into total war. To master compellence is to master the psychology of fear and the calculus of risk, transforming the raw potential of military power into a precise instrument of political will.

The Anatomy of Coercive Pressure

At its core, compellence is a political-diplomatic strategy aimed at influencing an adversary's will or incentive structure. It is not simply the use of force; it is the strategic combination of threats and the limited, selective use of force in discrete and controlled increments. The goal is to induce an adversary to comply with demands or to negotiate the most favorable compromise possible, all while managing the crisis to prevent unwanted military escalation. This requires a sophisticated manipulation of the adversary's decision-making calculus. By altering the costs and benefits of specific actions, the coercing state attempts to make compliance the only rational choice for the opponent.

Daniel Byman and Matthew Waxman, leading scholars in the field of international security, define compellence with surgical precision as "getting the adversary to act a certain way via anything short of brute force." This definition highlights a crucial paradox: the coercing state must possess the capacity for organized violence, but the ultimate success of the strategy lies in the adversary's choice not to exercise it. The violence remains a latent threat, a sword of Damocles hanging over the negotiations, rather than a tool of destruction. If the sword falls, the strategy has arguably failed.

The success of compellence hinges entirely on the credibility of the threat. Joseph Nye, a preeminent voice in American foreign policy, emphasizes that compellence depends upon the credibility and the cost of the threat. A threat that is not credible is merely noise; it fails to produce acceptance and can actively damage the reputation of the coercing state. Nye warns that threats are costly when they fail, not only in encouraging resistance in the target but also in negatively influencing third parties observing the outcome. If a state issues a threat it cannot or will not execute, it invites a broader loss of resolve that can undermine general deterrence and future compellence efforts. The reputation for resolve is a finite resource, and compellence is the test that depletes it fastest.

The Schelling Revolution: From Waiting to Acting

To understand compellence, one must look to the intellectual architecture laid down by Thomas Schelling in his influential 1966 work, Arms and Influence. Before Schelling, the prevailing theory of conflict was dominated by deterrence—the idea that stability was maintained by the mutual threat of destruction. Schelling argued that this was a passive and incomplete picture of coercion. Deterrence, he posited, is merely a threat aimed at keeping an adversary from acting. In a deterrence scenario, "initiative is placed on the opponent to take the first action triggering a response from the coercer." The coercer waits, hoping the adversary is too afraid to move.

Schelling believed this passive approach did not present a "comprehensive picture of coercion." He introduced the concept of compellence to fill the gap. In contrast to deterrence, compellence shifts the initiative for the first action to the coercer. While deterrence means waiting passively in the hope of not seeing a response, compellence is active, thereby "inducing his withdrawal, or his acquiescence, or his collaboration by an action that threatens to hurt."

The practical difference is stark. Deterrence can be described as "drawing a line in the sand" and acting only if the adversary crosses it. Compellence, however, "requires that the punishment be administered until the other acts rather than if he acts." It is a continuous pressure, a sustained application of force or the threat of force, rather than a one-time conditional warning. As Schelling noted, "Coercion composed of both compellence and deterrence is about action and inaction." One is a shield; the other is a spear.

This shift in initiative makes compellence inherently more difficult to implement than deterrence. In a deterrence scenario, the coercer can maintain the status quo with minimal effort, simply by maintaining a posture of strength. In compellence, the coercer must actively disrupt the status quo, forcing the adversary to make a concession that they do not want to make. This requires not just military strength, but diplomatic finesse, precise timing, and a deep understanding of the adversary's political constraints. The coercer must be willing to escalate, to take risks, and to potentially lose face if the strategy fails.

George's Framework: The Three Objectives

Building on Schelling's foundational work, Alexander L. George, a pioneer in the field of political psychology and former professor at Stanford University, refined the theory of compellence into a practical diplomatic strategy. Unlike Schelling, who sometimes blurred the lines between "coercive warfare" and compellence, George viewed compellence as a specific subset of coercion. He argued that compellence should encompass "defensive" actions only: to force a target to stop or reverse an action already taken, rather than an offensive goal of forcing them to do something new.

George identified three distinct objectives that compellence seeks to achieve. First, it attempts to persuade an adversary to turn away from a goal they are currently pursuing. Second, it seeks to convince an adversary to reverse an action they have already taken. Third, and most ambitiously, it may persuade an adversary to make "fundamental changes in its government." These objectives represent a ladder of escalation in political ambition, from tactical reversals to regime change.

To achieve these objectives, policymakers must navigate a series of "empty boxes" that must be filled with specific strategic choices. George argued that constructing a compellence strategy requires deciding: what to demand of the opponent; whether and how to create a sense of urgency for compliance with the demand; whether and what kind of punishment to threaten for noncompliance; and whether to rely solely on the threat of punishment or also to offer conditional inducements of a positive character to secure acceptance of the demand.

This framework transforms compellence from a vague concept of "pressure" into a structured methodology. The "carrot" must be balanced against the "stick." Motivation is used to induce a target to submit to your wishes, while appearing threatening at the same time. The challenge lies in the calibration: too much threat, and the adversary dig in; too little, and the threat is ignored. Too many carrots, and the adversary sees weakness; too few, and they see no path to a face-saving exit.

The Variants of Pressure: From Ultimatum to the Screw

Alexander George developed a framework in which a number of "variants" or methods of using compellence could be deployed. These variants represent different ways of filling the "empty boxes" of demand, urgency, and punishment. The classic "ultimatum" is the most direct and dangerous form. An ultimatum has three distinct components: a demand on the opponent, a time limit or sense of urgency for compliance, and a threat of punishment for noncompliance that is both credible and potent. The ultimatum is a high-stakes gamble. It leaves the adversary with no room for negotiation and forces a binary choice: comply or suffer.

The second variant is the "tacit ultimatum." This is similar to the classic ultimatum in that it contains a demand and a threat, but it does not set forth an explicit time limit. This ambiguity can be a double-edged sword. It allows the adversary to save face by complying before a specific deadline is reached, but it also risks the adversary miscalculating the coercer's patience. The tacit ultimatum relies on the adversary reading the implicit signals of urgency.

The third variant, the "try-and-see," addresses strictly the demand component. There is no time limit set, and no sense of urgency is conveyed. Instead, the coercer makes a single threat or takes a single action "to persuade the opponent before threatening or taking another step." This is a test of resolve, a probe to see how the adversary reacts before committing to a larger strategy. It is a cautious approach, often used when the stakes are high and the risk of escalation is extreme.

The fourth variant is the "gradual turning of the screw." This approach is similar to the "try-and-see" method but involves a sustained escalation. The coercer makes a threat and then "relies on the threat of a gradual, incremental increase of coercive pressure rather than threatening large escalation to strong, decisive military action if the opponent does not comply." This method is designed to wear down the adversary's resolve over time, increasing the cost of resistance incrementally until compliance becomes the only viable option. It avoids the shock of an ultimatum but maintains a relentless pressure that can be psychologically exhausting for the target.

The Mechanics of Punishment and Denial

Thomas Schelling and Robert Pape further distinguished between the specific mechanisms of coercive strategies. They identified three primary methods by which compellence operates: punishment, risk, and denial.

Punishment seeks to raise the costs for the adversary. This is the most visceral form of compellence, involving the infliction of pain or loss to force a change in behavior. The logic is simple: the cost of continuing the current course of action must exceed the cost of complying with the demand.

Risk aims to raise the probability of future costs for the adversary. Unlike punishment, which inflicts actual damage, risk creates a looming threat of catastrophe. It manipulates the adversary's fear of the unknown, making them believe that if they do not comply, the consequences will be severe and potentially uncontrollable.

Denial seeks to prevent the adversary from obtaining their objectives. This strategy renders the adversary's actions futile. By making it impossible for the opponent to achieve their goals through military or political means, the coercer forces them to abandon the endeavor. Denial is often considered the most effective form of compellence because it does not rely on the adversary's fear of pain, but on the rational realization of futility.

Robert Pape added a fourth strategy: decapitation. This entails targeting the leadership of the adversary, aiming to remove the decision-makers who are driving the conflict. By eliminating the brain of the operation, the coercer hopes to induce chaos or a change in direction.

Alexander Downes and Kathryn McNabb Cochran further distinguished between two types of punishment strategies: coercive victimization and eliminationist victimization. Coercive victimization raises the costs of war for a government by targeting its civilians, aiming to create internal pressure on the leadership to surrender. Eliminationist victimization, a more extreme and morally fraught strategy, involves removing civilians from territory, often through forced displacement or genocide, to achieve strategic objectives. While the latter is a gross violation of international norms, it represents the darkest extreme of the compellence spectrum, where the destruction of a population is used as a bargaining chip.

Intrawar Compellence: Bargaining in the Fire

Perhaps the most complex and dangerous application of compellence is "intrawar compellence." This refers to the use of compellence within the context of a war that has already broken out. The actors are still seeking to coerce the adversary to take a certain action, even as bullets are flying and cities are burning.

Thomas Schelling famously observed that "War is always a bargain." Even in the heat of battle, states are engaged in a continuous, often unspoken negotiation. Every military action is a signal, a move in a larger game of chess where the objective is not just to destroy the enemy, but to force them to accept a specific political outcome. Intrawar compellence is the art of using limited military force to send a message that is loud enough to be heard but controlled enough to leave room for a settlement.

This form of compellence is fraught with peril. The fog of war makes it difficult to signal intent clearly. A limited strike intended to send a message of resolve can be misinterpreted as the beginning of a total war, triggering an escalation spiral that neither side wants. The challenge for the coercer is to maintain the initiative, to keep the adversary off-balance, and to ensure that the threat remains credible without crossing the line into mutual destruction.

Successful instances of compellence in one case may have a deterrent effect on other states. If a state successfully compels an adversary to retreat, it signals to the international community that it is willing to use force to achieve its goals. This reputation for resolve can discourage other potential adversaries from challenging that state's interests. Conversely, a reputation for a lack of resolve can undermine general deterrence and future compellence. If a state issues threats and fails to follow through, or if it backs down when challenged, it invites further aggression. The credibility of the threat is the currency of compellence, and once spent, it is difficult to replenish.

The Stakes of 2026

As we navigate the geopolitical turbulence of 2026, the principles of compellence are being tested in real-time. The conflicts in Ukraine, the tensions in the Middle East, and the strategic competition with China are not just battles of firepower, but battles of will. In each of these theatres, states are attempting to manipulate the decision calculus of their adversaries. They are weighing the use of limited force against the risk of escalation, offering inducements while threatening punishment, and trying to find the precise moment to apply the "screw" of pressure.

The history of compellence teaches us that it is harder to successfully implement than deterrence. It requires more than just military strength; it requires a deep understanding of the adversary's psychology, a clear definition of objectives, and the discipline to stop at the right moment. It is a strategy that walks a razor's edge, where the difference between success and catastrophe is often measured in minutes, misinterpreted signals, or a single miscalculation.

Yet, in a world where nuclear weapons make total war unthinkable, compellence remains the primary tool for resolving disputes. It is the mechanism by which the status quo is challenged and changed without triggering the apocalypse. From the Cuban Missile Crisis to the modern-day standoffs, the story of international relations is, in many ways, the story of compellence. It is the story of states trying to force each other to move, to yield, to bend, without breaking the fragile peace that holds the world together.

The "carrot and stick" philosophy is not a simple binary. It is a complex dance of threat and promise, of fear and hope. As policymakers in 2026 grapple with the challenges of a multipolar world, the lessons of Schelling, George, and their successors remain as relevant as ever. The art of compellence is the art of making the adversary see that the cost of resistance is higher than the cost of surrender. It is the art of winning without fighting, or at least, winning without the war that would destroy everyone.

In the end, compellence is a testament to the power of the human mind in the face of violence. It is the realization that the most powerful weapon in the arsenal is not the missile, but the threat of the missile, and the belief that the adversary will choose to back down. It is a strategy that demands courage, precision, and an unwavering commitment to the goal. And in a world on the brink, it may be the only thing standing between peace and chaos.

The variants of compellence—ultimatum, tacit ultimatum, try-and-see, and the gradual turning of the screw—are not just theoretical constructs. They are the tools of the trade for the world's most powerful leaders. Each one carries its own risks and rewards, its own potential for success or failure. The choice of which variant to use, and when, is one of the most critical decisions a leader can make. It is a decision that can shape the course of history, determine the fate of nations, and define the legacy of a generation.

As the world watches the unfolding dramas in Ukraine, Iran, and the Indo-Pacific, the eyes of history are on the strategists who are trying to master this elusive art. They are the architects of the new world order, the ones who must navigate the treacherous waters of compellence to steer their nations toward a safer future. The stakes have never been higher, and the margin for error has never been smaller. But if history is any guide, the art of compellence, with all its complexities and dangers, will continue to be the defining feature of international conflict in the years to come.

The future of global stability depends on the ability of states to wield compellence with wisdom and restraint. It depends on the ability to distinguish between the threat of force and the use of force, between the demand for compliance and the acceptance of defeat. It depends on the recognition that in the game of compellence, the ultimate victory is not the destruction of the enemy, but the preservation of the self. And that is a lesson that every leader, in 2026 and beyond, must learn.
