Common Core

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Common Core

In 2010, America attempted something it had never managed before: getting almost every state to agree on what children should actually learn in school. The Common Core State Standards Initiative was, depending on who you ask, either the most ambitious educational reform in a generation or a federal overreach dressed up in state clothing. Either way, it changed how millions of American children learned to read, write, and do math—and then it sparked a backlash so fierce that many states scrambled to distance themselves from standards they had eagerly adopted just years earlier.

The Problem Common Core Tried to Solve

Imagine moving from California to Texas in the middle of fifth grade. In California, you might have spent weeks learning long division. In Texas, your new classmates might have covered that last year—or might not cover it until next year. For a nation that prides itself on equal opportunity, American education has always been strangely fragmented. Each of the fifty states sets its own standards for what students should know, and those standards can vary wildly.

This wasn't always seen as a problem. Local control of education is an American tradition stretching back centuries. But by the 1990s, a growing chorus of voices—governors, business leaders, educators—began asking uncomfortable questions. How could American students compete globally when a high school diploma in Mississippi might mean something entirely different than one in Massachusetts? How could employers trust that job applicants had basic skills when "basic" varied so dramatically from state to state?

The movement for national standards had been building for years. But standards without coordination led to a patchwork. What was needed, reformers argued, was a common set of expectations—not a federal curriculum dictating which books to read or which teaching methods to use, but a shared understanding of what students should know at each grade level.

How Common Core Came to Be

The initiative emerged from two organizations that most Americans have never heard of: the National Governors Association, or NGA, and the Council of Chief State School Officers, known as CCSSO. These are not federal agencies. They're membership organizations representing governors and state education leaders. This distinction would later prove crucial in the political debates over Common Core.

In late 2008, the NGA convened a working group to draft the standards. The team included academics like William McCallum from the University of Arizona and Phil Daro, alongside educators and researchers. David Coleman, who would later become president of the College Board (the organization that runs the SAT), played a central role in shaping the English language arts standards. Jason Zimba and Susan Pimentel, founders of an organization called Student Achievement Partners, helped write both the math and English standards.

On June 1, 2009, the initiative was officially announced. Its stated purpose was modest and sensible: to provide "a consistent, clear understanding of what students are expected to learn, so teachers and parents know what they need to do to help them." The standards would be "robust and relevant to the real world," preparing students for college and careers and positioning them to compete in an increasingly global economy.

The actual drafting process involved more than just the core team. Work groups consulted with educators, administrators, researchers, civil rights organizations, business leaders, and parent groups. Draft after draft was revised based on feedback. The final standards were released on June 2, 2010—one for mathematics and one for English language arts.

The Incentive That Changed Everything

States weren't required to adopt Common Core. But the federal government created a powerful incentive to do so.

On July 24, 2009—before the standards were even finalized—President Barack Obama and his Secretary of Education, Arne Duncan, announced something called Race to the Top. This was a competition for federal education grants, with billions of dollars available to states that embraced certain reforms. To be eligible, states needed to adopt "internationally benchmarked standards and assessments that prepare students for success in college and the workplace."

Technically, states could adopt other college-ready standards and still compete. But there was a catch: adopting Common Core by August 2, 2010, earned extra points in a state's application. Forty-one states took the hint. Within months of the standards' release, forty-six states and the District of Columbia had signed on.

Four states stayed out from the beginning: Texas, Virginia, Alaska, and Nebraska. Texas, with its fierce tradition of independence and its own large-scale standardized testing system, saw no need to join a multi-state initiative. Virginia had recently updated its own standards and felt confident in them. Alaska and Nebraska had their own reasons for staying outside.

Minnesota took a middle path, adopting the English language arts standards but rejecting the mathematics standards.

What the English Standards Actually Required

The English language arts standards covered five areas: reading, writing, speaking and listening, language, and media and technology. The goal was to ensure students graduated high school ready for college-level work—specifically, able to handle complex texts without remediation.

For reading, the standards emphasized increasing complexity. Each grade level expected students to handle more challenging material and develop deeper comprehension skills. The standards didn't mandate specific books—that was left to states, districts, and teachers—but they did include suggested "exemplar texts." These ranged from ancient works by Ovid and the plays of Shakespeare to modern authors like Amy Tan and Atul Gawande. Classic myths, foundational American documents, and seminal works of American literature were considered essential content, but beyond those categories, local authorities retained control.

For younger students, from kindergarten through fifth grade, the standards included foundational reading skills: print concepts, phonological awareness (understanding the sounds in language), phonics (connecting sounds to letters), and fluency. The standards explicitly referenced research on how children learn to read, and included detailed guidance on phonics instruction—what the standards called "phoneme-grapheme correspondences," the technical term for the relationship between sounds and the letters that represent them.

The writing standards emphasized argument and evidence. Students were expected to construct logical arguments based on claims, solid reasoning, and relevant evidence—even in elementary school, where this took the form of opinion writing. Research skills featured prominently: both short, focused projects and longer in-depth investigations. The standards included sample student writing at various performance levels, giving teachers concrete examples of what proficient work looked like.

Speaking and listening standards recognized that literacy isn't just about reading and writing. Students needed to gain, evaluate, and present complex information orally. The standards emphasized academic discussion—one-on-one, small group, and whole class—both formal presentations and informal collaboration.

Language standards covered vocabulary and conventions. Rather than treating vocabulary as a separate subject, the standards embedded it across all areas: students would learn words through conversation, direct instruction, and reading. The standards acknowledged that while formal English matters in academic and professional settings, students also need to make "wise, skilled decisions" about language use in different contexts.

Finally, the standards addressed media and technology, reflecting the reality of twenty-first-century life. Students were expected to analyze and produce various forms of media. Keyboarding was required. Cursive handwriting was not.

The Math Standards: Deeper, Not Wider

American math education had long been criticized as "a mile wide and an inch deep." Students touched on dozens of topics each year but rarely mastered any of them. Then they repeated much of the same material the next year, because teachers couldn't assume prior learning had stuck.

Common Core math tried to fix this by demanding greater focus and coherence. The standards identified specific content for each grade from kindergarten through eighth grade, then organized high school math into six conceptual categories: number and quantity, algebra, functions, modeling, geometry, and statistics and probability.

But the content standards were only half the story. The other half consisted of eight "Standards for Mathematical Practice"—habits of mind that students were supposed to develop across all grade levels:

  1. Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them.
  2. Reason abstractly and quantitatively.
  3. Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others.
  4. Model with mathematics.
  5. Use appropriate tools strategically.
  6. Attend to precision.
  7. Look for and make use of structure.
  8. Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning.

These practices were adapted from recommendations by the National Council of Teachers of Mathematics and a National Research Council report called "Adding It Up." The idea was that mathematical proficiency involved more than just getting right answers—it required understanding why procedures work, recognizing patterns, building logical arguments, and applying math to real situations.

The standards deliberately avoided prescribing pedagogy. They said what students should know, not how teachers should teach it. They didn't dictate the order in which topics should be covered within a grade level, and at the high school level, they didn't even specify which standards belonged to which course. Districts could organize high school math traditionally (Algebra I, Geometry, Algebra II) or use an integrated approach common in other countries. Either way, functions and mathematical modeling were supposed to weave throughout the curriculum rather than being isolated in separate courses.

The Question of English Language Learners

When Common Core was first released, it didn't include standards for students learning English as a second language. The plan was for individual states to handle English language proficiency development on their own. This quickly proved inadequate.

English language learners—students whose primary language isn't English—face a double challenge. They need to learn academic content while simultaneously acquiring the language in which that content is taught. Without coordinated standards for language development, states struggled to align their English learner programs with Common Core expectations.

Several initiatives emerged to fill this gap. WIDA, a consortium of states, developed standardized tests and language development standards aligned with Common Core. The Council of Chief State School Officers created a framework to help states revise their standards. The federal Department of Education funded grants to develop new assessments specifically for English language learners.

These new assessments had ambitious requirements. They needed to measure proficiency across all four language domains—reading, writing, speaking, and listening—for every grade from kindergarten through twelfth. They had to indicate whether students had reached the level of English proficiency necessary to fully participate in academic instruction. And they needed to work with technology, using it "to the maximum extent appropriate" for development, administration, and scoring.

The Backlash

Within a few years of adoption, Common Core faced a revolt.

The opposition came from multiple directions. Some conservatives saw it as federal overreach, a nationalization of curriculum despite the initiative's state-level origins. The fact that the Obama administration had incentivized adoption through Race to the Top fed this perception. Some argued that the standards weren't actually voluntary if federal money depended on them.

Teachers had their own complaints. Many felt the standards had been imposed without adequate training or resources. The math standards, in particular, required teaching methods unfamiliar to many educators. Parents encountered homework assignments that used unfamiliar approaches to arithmetic, leading to viral social media posts mocking "Common Core math."

Some critics from the left worried that the standards narrowed curriculum, emphasizing tested subjects at the expense of arts, social studies, and physical education. Others questioned whether the standards were developmentally appropriate, particularly for younger children.

At least twelve states introduced legislation to prohibit implementation. Several states that had initially adopted the standards—Indiana, Arizona, Oklahoma, South Carolina, and Florida among them—decided to repeal or replace them. New York eventually replaced its Common Core standards with something called the Next Generation Learning Standards, though critics noted the new standards bore a strong resemblance to what they replaced.

In December 2015, Congress passed the Every Student Succeeds Act, replacing the controversial No Child Left Behind. The new law explicitly prohibited the Department of Education from "influencing, incentivizing, or coercing" states to adopt Common Core or any other common standards. The era of federal pressure was officially over.

The Legacy

Common Core didn't disappear, but it faded from headlines. Many states that kept the standards stopped calling them Common Core, rebranding them with state-specific names. The substance often remained even as the label vanished.

The initiative's impact on American education remains debated. Supporters point to greater consistency, clearer expectations, and a focus on deeper understanding over rote memorization. Critics argue the standards were too rigid, poorly implemented, or inappropriately pushed onto states by federal incentives.

What's clear is that Common Core represented a particular moment in American education reform—a moment when standardization seemed like the answer to educational inequity, when governors and business leaders could agree that consistency had value, and when the federal government was willing to use its financial leverage to push states toward common goals. Whether that approach helped or harmed American students depends heavily on whom you ask, and probably on which state and which classroom you're talking about.

The standards themselves remain copyrighted by the NGA and CCSSO. States can use them freely if they adopt them in full, but any other use requires attribution and a statement that the use is "in support of" the Common Core initiative. It's a small detail, but a telling one: even the legal framework around Common Core reflects the tension between state autonomy and national coordination that defined the entire project.

Other subjects eventually got their own multi-state standards. The Next Generation Science Standards, released in 2012, took a similar approach to science education and were adopted by many states. They're not technically part of Common Core, but they share its DNA—the same belief that American education could benefit from more consistency, the same faith that standards could drive improvement, and the same vulnerability to political backlash when parents and politicians decided that "common" sounded too much like "federal."

For millions of American students, Common Core shaped how they learned to read, write, and do mathematics during a formative period of educational history. Whether that shaping was for better or worse, the initiative stands as a reminder that in American education, the question is never just "what should children learn?" but always also "who gets to decide?"
