Amplify (company)

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Amplify (company)

In 2010, News Corporation, the global media conglomerate that owned The Wall Street Journal and Fox News, wrote a check for $360 million to buy a small software company out of Brooklyn. The target was Wireless Generation, a firm founded just a decade prior by Larry Berger and Greg Gunn. The purchase was not a standard media acquisition; it was a bet on the future of American schooling, a gamble that the chaotic, analog world of K-12 education could be tamed, digitized, and sold back to districts for a profit. By the time the ink dried on that deal, Wireless Generation was already the digital backbone for three million students and 200,000 educators, managing the complex data streams of the New York City Department of Education. But the story of what happened next—the rebranding to Amplify, the rise and fall of a proprietary tablet, the pivot to curriculum, and the eventual return to independent leadership—reveals far more than a simple corporate timeline. It exposes the friction between the logic of Silicon Valley and the messy, human reality of the classroom.

To understand Amplify, one must first understand the vacuum it filled. In the early 2000s, American education was drowning in data but starving for insight. Districts collected terabytes of student information, but it sat in silos, useless to the teacher standing in front of a classroom of thirty children. Wireless Generation, founded in 2000, built the first real infrastructure to bridge this gap. They didn't just build software; they built the nervous system for New York City's schools. They developed and maintained ARIS, the online warehouse that held the academic DNA of the city's students. They wrote the algorithm for the School of One, a revolutionary math help system that attempted to personalize learning for thousands of students simultaneously. Larry Berger, the company's founder and CEO, saw a future where technology didn't just record history but actively shaped instruction.

The 2010 acquisition by News Corp was a watershed moment. Rupert Murdoch's empire, known for its political influence and print dominance, had suddenly become the largest private owner of educational technology in the United States. The price tag of $360 million for a 90% stake signaled a massive shift in the industry's center of gravity. News Corp didn't just buy a company; they bought a vision. They immediately injected another $540 million into the venture, aiming to expand digital and tablet-based curriculum aligned with the emerging Common Core State Standards. To lead this charge, they appointed Joel Klein, the former chancellor of the New York City Department of Education, as the new CEO. Klein was the perfect bridge: a former regulator who understood the bureaucracy of public schools, now a private executive tasked with injecting market efficiency into that same system.

Klein's mandate was clear: integrate computer technology into the common educational environment. Under his leadership, the company shed its old name. In 2012, Wireless Generation became Amplify. The new moniker was aspirational, suggesting a signal booster for learning, but it also carried the weight of corporate ambition. The company moved quickly to solidify its position in the assessment market, partnering with the Smarter Balanced Assessment Consortium in 2012 to develop reporting tools. By 2013, they had created a digital library of formative test items, attempting to turn the annual ritual of standardized testing into a continuous stream of diagnostic data.

But Amplify's most audacious move came in 2013 with the release of the Amplify Tablet. It was a customized Asus Android device, hardened for the classroom and loaded with a suite of subscription-based software. This was not a passive consumption device; it was an interactive engine for K-12 teaching. The following year, they released a new version designed by Intel, doubling down on the hardware strategy. The logic was seductive: if you control the device, you control the curriculum. You could track every click, every hesitation, every correct answer in real time. It was the ultimate tool for the data-driven classroom Klein championed.

However, the hardware strategy collided with the harsh realities of the education market. Schools are notoriously slow to adopt new technology, bound by procurement cycles, budget constraints, and deep skepticism of vendor lock-in. The tablet, once a symbol of the future, became a logistical nightmare. By 2015, just two years after its initial launch, Amplify ceased marketing the tablet to new customers. It was a quiet retreat, a tacit admission that the vision of a proprietary hardware ecosystem was too fragile to sustain against the tide of commoditized devices like the iPad and Chromebook. The tablet did not fail because the technology was bad; it failed because the market was not ready to buy a walled garden.

The retreat from hardware coincided with a major shift in ownership. In October 2015, News Corp sold Amplify to a management team backed by private investors, including Emerson Collective, the philanthropy and investment vehicle of Laurene Powell Jobs. The financial terms were undisclosed, but the message was clear: the media giant was exiting the business of running a school district's software. Larry Berger, the original founder, returned as CEO, bringing the company full circle. The new leadership was less about the spectacle of a new tablet and more about the substance of the curriculum itself.

Under Berger's renewed leadership, Amplify began to pivot toward the core of what teachers actually needed: content. In 2018, Fast Company reported that the company had generated $125 million in revenue, serving nearly four million students. The focus shifted to developing digital supplemental programs like Amplify Close Reading and Amplify Fractions. These were not gimmicks; they were targeted interventions designed to address specific gaps in student learning. The company was learning that the "data into instructional support" promise could only be fulfilled if the instruction itself was high-quality. Data without a curriculum is just noise.

The company's growth accelerated in the early 2020s. In October 2021, Amplify raised $215 million in growth funding, a clear signal that investors saw value in their data-driven, content-rich model. The strategy was paying off. By May 2022, they had reached a significant milestone: over 10 million students in all 50 states. This ubiquity was the result of a strategic acquisition that would define the company's future trajectory. In May 2022, Amplify acquired Desmos, a beloved platform known for its free, accessible online graphing calculator and its vibrant community of math educators. The acquisition of Desmos and the website teacher.desmos.com was a masterstroke. Desmos was the "cool" tool that students and teachers loved; Amplify was the enterprise infrastructure. By merging them, Amplify gained instant credibility in the math community.

Yet, the integration of Desmos was not without its complexities. Desmos Studio was spun off as a separate public benefit corporation, maintaining its mission of building free-to-use math tools. This decision acknowledged a critical truth in the education sector: trust is fragile. Teachers were wary of a for-profit company owning the tools they relied on for free. By keeping Desmos' free tools independent, Amplify managed to expand its commercial reach without alienating the grassroots educators who drove the brand's reputation. In May 2023, Amplify secured additional funding from Cox Enterprises and others to further its K-12 product offerings, cementing its status as a dominant player in the ed-tech landscape.

The evolution of the company's curriculum portfolio reveals a deepening commitment to the "science of reading" and evidence-based practices. In 2025, Desmos Classroom was renamed Amplify Classroom, expanding its scope beyond math to offer new lessons in science and literacy. This rebranding marked the final step in the company's transformation from a hardware vendor to a comprehensive curriculum provider. Amplify partnered with the Core Knowledge Foundation to publish and distribute materials for the Core Knowledge Language Arts (CKLA) program and its Spanish-language counterpart, Caminos. CKLA is a rigorous, knowledge-based curriculum that teaches reading, writing, listening, and speaking to students from preschool through Grade 5. It is distinct from the "balanced literacy" approach that dominated schools for decades; instead, it is grounded in the science of reading, emphasizing vocabulary, background knowledge, and structured phonics. The curriculum includes material related to literature, American history, and the natural sciences, aiming to build a broad base of knowledge that supports literacy.

The development of Amplify Science further illustrates this commitment to rigor. Created in collaboration with the Lawrence Hall of Science at the University of California, Berkeley, the curriculum is aligned to the Next Generation Science Standards. This partnership brought academic credibility to the product, ensuring that the digital tools were not just engaging but pedagogically sound. Similarly, the development of mCLASS assessments in partnership with the University of Oregon highlighted the company's focus on early intervention. The mCLASS platform helps teachers administer DIBELS (Dynamic Indicators of Basic Early Literacy Skills), providing real-time data and targeted instructional resources to catch struggling readers before they fall behind. These partnerships are not merely marketing stunts; they represent a strategic alignment with the institutions that define educational best practices.

The journey of Amplify from a Brooklyn startup to a national powerhouse is a microcosm of the broader transformation of American education. It reflects the tension between the efficiency of the market and the complexity of human learning. When Joel Klein first took the helm, the goal was to integrate technology into the classroom. When Larry Berger returned, the goal shifted to ensuring that the technology served a curriculum that actually worked. The failure of the tablet was a necessary lesson; it proved that hardware is a commodity, but curriculum is king. The acquisition of Desmos showed that even in a for-profit model, the spirit of open access must be preserved to maintain trust.

Today, Amplify operates in all 50 states, a testament to its resilience and adaptability. It has moved beyond the hype of the "digital revolution" to the grind of daily instruction. It is no longer just about collecting data; it is about turning that data into action. When a teacher in a rural district in Mississippi or a bustling classroom in Chicago uses Amplify, they are interacting with a system that has been refined over two decades. They are using a platform that has survived the rise and fall of hardware trends, the scrutiny of media ownership, and the shifting winds of educational policy.

The story of Amplify is also a story about the role of private capital in public education. The involvement of News Corp, Emerson Collective, and Cox Enterprises has provided the fuel for rapid growth, but it has also raised questions about the commercialization of schooling. The fact that a media conglomerate once owned the data of three million students is a detail that cannot be ignored. It forces a conversation about who owns the educational record of a child and what incentives drive the software they use. Yet, the company's evolution suggests a maturation of the industry. The pivot away from proprietary hardware and toward partnerships with public institutions like the University of Oregon and the Lawrence Hall of Science indicates a recognition that the best solutions are not built in a vacuum but in collaboration with the experts who understand the craft of teaching.

As we look at the landscape of 2026, Amplify stands as a major pillar of the K-12 infrastructure. It has survived the skepticism of the early 2010s and the hardware misstep of the mid-2010s to become a leader in curriculum and assessment. Its success is not measured in stock prices or device shipments, but in the millions of students it serves and the teachers who rely on its tools to make sense of their classrooms. The company has learned that the path to the future of education is not paved with tablets, but with knowledge, data, and the relentless pursuit of better instruction. The story of Amplify is far from over, but its foundation is now built on something far more durable than silicon: the enduring need to help every child learn to read, write, and think.

"The goal was to encourage the integration of computer technology into the common educational environment."

Joel Klein's words from 2010 sound almost quaint today, a statement of optimism that has since been tempered by the realities of implementation. But the core truth remains. Technology is not the answer; it is the medium. The answer lies in the curriculum, the assessment, and the teacher. Amplify's journey from Wireless Generation to its current form is a reminder that in education, the most powerful technology is the one that disappears into the background, allowing the work of learning to take center stage. The company has moved from selling a vision of a digital classroom to building the actual tools that make that classroom work. It is a story of failure, adaptation, and ultimately, a return to the fundamental mission of education: to amplify the potential of every student.
