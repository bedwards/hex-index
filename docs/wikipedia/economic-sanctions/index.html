Economic sanctions

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Economic sanctions

In 1935, diplomats gathered in Geneva with a specific hope: that the League of Nations could stop war without firing a single shot. When Italy invaded Abyssinia (modern-day Ethiopia), the League imposed sanctions on Mussolini's regime. The logic was simple and seductive—starve a nation of trade to force it to retreat. But the execution was fatally flawed. Oil supplies, the very lifeblood of the Italian war machine, were not cut off. The Suez Canal remained open. The result was not peace, but a grim validation of the aggressor's resolve; Italy completed its conquest, the sanctions were lifted in 1936, and Italy left the League entirely by 1937.

This failure set a precedent that haunts international relations to this day: economic coercion is an instrument of immense power, yet it remains a blunt, often indiscriminate hammer. Today, as we navigate a fractured global order in June 2026, sanctions have become the default weapon of foreign policy. They are the "hammer" that governments reach for when they fear war but crave results. But who exactly does this hammer strike? Is it the tyrant in his palace, or the mother buying bread in a market that has run dry?

Economic sanctions, or embargoes, are commercial and financial penalties applied by states or institutions against other states, groups, or individuals. At their core, they are a form of coercion designed to disrupt economic exchange to force a change in behavior. The theoretical framework is elegant: compel an actor to do something they would not otherwise do, or deter them from actions they were planning. These tools can be wielded broadly against entire nations or narrowly against specific individuals—so-called "smart sanctions" like asset freezes and travel bans intended to bypass the suffering of ordinary citizens.

The history of this strategy is as old as modern commerce itself, though its most comprehensive early attempt occurred during the Napoleonic Wars. Between 1803 and 1815, Emperor Napoleon I sought to cripple the United Kingdom economically. In 1806, he promulgated the Continental System, a sweeping decree that forbade European nations from trading with Britain. The ambition was grand: strangle the British economy until it capitulated. In practice, however, the French Empire could not enforce the embargo completely. Smuggling flourished, and the blockade proved as harmful, if not more so, to the continental nations involved as it did to the British. It was a lesson in economic interdependence that would take nearly two centuries to fully digest.

By the time of the Hague Conventions in 1899 and 1907, the idea of coordinated economic pressure had entered the legal lexicon. Diplomats and scholars began discussing sanctions as a mechanism to enforce international law, a notion championed by reform-minded lawyers from Latin America and China leading up to World War I. When that war erupted, blockades became a central feature of the conflict. The devastation was total, but it also sowed the seeds for a new international architecture.

After the First World War, the League of Nations emerged with sanctions codified in its Covenant. Leaders viewed them as a viable alternative to the carnage they had just witnessed. Article 10, Article 11, and subsequent clauses permitted economic pressure in cases of violated treaties, threats of war, or failure to submit disputes to arbitration. The Abyssinia crisis tested this system and found it wanting.

The pattern repeated itself as the world moved toward the Second World War. In the lead-up to the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor in 1941, the United States imposed severe trade restrictions on Japan, freezing assets and cutting off oil exports to discourage further conquests in East Asia. These measures were intended to be a brake on aggression. Instead, they acted as a catalyst for war, pushing a resource-starved Japan toward a desperate gamble.

The aftermath of 1945 saw the League replaced by the United Nations, a more expansive and ostensibly more powerful body. Throughout the Cold War, the use of sanctions increased gradually, often stifled by superpower vetoes. But with the end of the Cold War came an explosion in their deployment. According to the Global Sanctions Data Base, there have been 1,325 sanctions imposed between 1950 and 2022. The sheer volume suggests a shift in global strategy: war is no longer just fought on battlefields; it is fought through ledgers, bank transfers, and port closures.

Governments now routinely use these measures as an alternative weapon to achieve policy goals without the bloodshed of direct conflict. The objectives are varied: changing policy, destabilizing regimes, resolving territorial conflicts, fighting terrorism, preventing war, ending war, restoring human rights, and promoting democracy. Yet, a stark reality underpins this proliferation. Sanctions are usually imposed by larger countries upon smaller ones. The rationale typically cites security threats or the unfair treatment of citizens by the target regime. But the power dynamic is inherently asymmetrical.

The distinction between international sanctions and unilateral coercive measures is critical. The United Nations defines unilateral coercive measures as economic actions taken by one state to compel policy changes in another, including trade embargoes and financial interruptions. These measures have faced increasing criticism from the UN itself. On December 4, 2025, the world marked the first International Day Against Unilateral Coercive Measures—a symbolic but necessary nod to their growing controversy.

Unilateral sanctions often trigger a phenomenon known as "over-compliance." This is where global banks and corporations, terrified of secondary penalties or reputational damage, refuse to trade with sanctioned countries even in ways that are not explicitly prohibited by the laws. The result is an economic paralysis far worse than the sanction itself. These measures are frequently imposed under the pretext of criminal responsibility but without due process. They often violate fundamental aspects of international law, including the immunity of state property, diplomatic immunities, and the sovereign equality of states.

Morgan, Syropoulos, and Yotov have analyzed how sanctions have evolved from broad trade embargoes to targeted financial restrictions on individuals. Their findings are sobering: while sanctions are increasingly frequent, they are inconsistently effective. The economic costs fall heavily on civilians. Trade patterns shift toward neutral countries, often strengthening authoritarian allies rather than isolating the target. Enforcement complexity rises as targets find loopholes.

The consensus among these scholars is that sanctions remain politically appealing but economically blunt instruments. Their success depends entirely on coordination, credible goals, and minimizing humanitarian fallout—three conditions rarely met simultaneously. Attia argues that sanctions serve more as symbolic or political devices than as costly coercive instruments. They allow governments to appear decisive without taking the risks of war.

But what is the actual price? A study by Neuenkirch and Neumeier provides grim statistics. UN economic sanctions had a statistically significant impact on targeted states, reducing GDP growth by an average of 2.3–3.5 percent per year. In the case of comprehensive UN embargoes, that figure soared to more than 5 percent annually. These negative effects typically persist for ten years. By contrast, unilateral US sanctions had a considerably smaller impact on GDP growth, restricting it by only 0.5–0.9 percent per year, with an average duration of seven years.

The human dimension of these numbers is where the true story lies. Oryoie, A. R., demonstrated that economic sanctions result in welfare losses across all income groups in Iran. Counterintuitively, wealthier groups often suffered greater percentage losses compared to poorer groups due to asset freezes and the collapse of high-end markets. Yet for the poor, the consequences are existential. When a nation is cut off from global finance, medicine becomes scarce. Food prices skyrocket. Infrastructure crumbles because spare parts cannot be imported.

Sanctions also recoil on the imposing country. If import restrictions are promulgated, consumers lose choices of goods. If export restrictions prevent companies from trading with the target, the imposing country loses markets and investment opportunities to competing nations. The economic pain is rarely one-sided, but it is often more concentrated in the target.

The question of efficacy remains the most contentious debate in international relations. Hufbauer, Schott, and Elliot (2008) argued that regime change is the most frequent foreign-policy objective of sanctions, accounting for just over 39 percent of cases. They found a success rate of 34 percent. However, when Robert A. Pape examined their study, he stripped away the ambiguity. He found that only 5 of their reported 40 successes were actually effective, reducing the success rate to a dismal 4 percent.

The difficulty lies in measuring success. If a country changes its behavior, was it because of the sanctions? Or was it due to internal political shifts, diplomatic pressure, or the natural exhaustion of conflict? It is difficult to determine why a regime or country changes. The "success" stories are often retrospective rationalizations by governments desperate to justify the cost and the moral compromise.

We must also consider the externalities. Scholars have long considered the policy externalities of sanctions, particularly regarding humanitarian consequences. Country-wide sanctions in earlier decades were controversial for this very reason. As a consequence, since the mid-1990s, United Nations Security Council (UNSC) sanctions have tended to target individuals and entities rather than whole populations. The logic was sound: punish the leaders, spare the people. But in practice, the "smart" nature of these sanctions is often an illusion. When a central bank is frozen, every citizen loses access to international currency. When an airline is grounded, families cannot visit dying relatives abroad.

The MoldATSA scandal you recently read about adds another layer of complexity to this history. It reminds us that the mechanisms of finance are fragile and easily weaponized. When the rules of global commerce are bent for political ends, the ripple effects are unpredictable. The sanctions regime is not a sterile tool of policy; it is a living system of incentives and punishments that interacts with human behavior in chaotic ways.

The debate over whether to use sanctions is no longer about whether they work in isolation, but whether they are worth the collateral damage. If 34 percent (or perhaps only 4 percent) of regimes change, do we accept the suffering of millions of civilians as a necessary cost? The answer depends on who you ask. For the policymakers in Washington, London, or Brussels, sanctions are a "viable alternative to war." They offer a way to project power without risking soldiers' lives.

But for the civilian in the target country, there is no distinction between a sanction and a siege. A blockade that stops food shipments has the same result as a military assault that cuts supply lines: hunger. The language of "smart sanctions" tries to sanitize this reality, but it cannot erase it.

The evolution from the Continental System to the modern global financial network shows a consistent pattern. States attempt to use economic leverage to impose their will on others. Sometimes they succeed in altering behavior; often, they entrench resistance. The Napoleonic era taught us that you cannot starve an island economy without starving your own continent. The 20th century taught us that sanctions can be a prelude to war rather than a substitute for it.

Today, as we stand in mid-2026, the world is more interconnected than ever before. This makes sanctions both easier to impose and harder to enforce. A single country can freeze assets with a keystroke, but a global shadow economy rises instantly to replace lost trade routes. The "over-compliance" phenomenon creates a self-fulfilling prophecy of isolation that may have little to do with the actual terms of the sanction.

The future of sanctions lies in their ability to adapt. Will they become more targeted? Or will they evolve into something even more pervasive, affecting digital currencies and data flows? The answer is uncertain. What is certain is that the human cost remains the constant variable. As Neuenkirch and Neumeier's data shows, the GDP contraction is real. The decade-long persistence of these effects means that generations are raised in economies scarred by policies they had no say in.

We must question the official framing. When a government claims "precision sanctions" are hitting only the bad actors, we should ask: what happens to the supply chain? What happens when a hospital cannot import insulin because the bank fears a violation of a technicality? The narrative of moral clarity often dissolves upon contact with the messy reality of global economics.

The MoldATSA scandal is not an anomaly; it is a symptom. It reveals that the financial architecture we rely on for stability is also the weapon of choice for coercion. The lines between commerce and conflict have blurred beyond recognition. In this new landscape, the question is no longer just whether sanctions work. The question is what kind of world we are willing to build if the answer requires us to ignore the suffering of those caught in the crossfire.

Ultimately, the history of economic sanctions is a history of hope and hubris. It began with the hope that war could be abolished through trade barriers. It has led to a reality where trade barriers often become the prelude to deeper conflict or the cementing of authoritarian grip. The data suggests they are blunt instruments. The moral argument suggests they are a necessary evil. But for the millions living under their weight, there is no abstract debate. There is only the struggle to survive.

As we look forward from 2026, the challenge for international law and diplomacy is to find a way to hold leaders accountable without condemning populations to poverty. If sanctions are to remain a tool of foreign policy, they must be reimagined. They need mechanisms that protect humanitarian corridors with absolute certainty. They require rigorous oversight to prevent over-compliance from becoming a weapon in itself.

Until then, the legacy of 1935 hangs over us. The League failed because it could not stop oil. Today's institutions fail because they cannot separate the leader from the led. The economic penalty is applied to the state, but the pain is felt by the individual. And that is the enduring paradox of sanctions: a tool designed to protect human rights often ends up violating them in the most fundamental way possible.

The story of sanctions is not finished. It is being written every day in boardrooms and backchannels, in the freezing of accounts and the closing of borders. But the next chapter will be defined by how well we learn from the past. Will we continue to rely on these blunt instruments? Or will we find a way to make diplomacy work without breaking the backs of those it claims to save?

The answer lies not in the data, but in our willingness to confront the human cost. Because in the end, no GDP statistic can measure the loss of a child's future, and no success rate can justify a generation raised in scarcity. The economy is a means to an end, not an end in itself. When we forget that, sanctions cease to be policy and become punishment for its own sake.
