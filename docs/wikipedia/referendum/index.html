Referendum

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Referendum

In 1986, Switzerland held a referendum on whether to join the United Nations. The Swiss voted no. It would take another sixteen years—until 2002—before they finally agreed to join what was by then a sixty-year-old international institution that included virtually every other country on Earth.

This is the referendum in its purest form: a nation of eight million people, offered a simple yes-or-no question, collectively shrugging and saying "not yet."

What Exactly Is a Referendum?

A referendum is democracy without the middlemen. Instead of electing representatives who then make decisions on your behalf, you get to vote directly on the question itself. Should we change the constitution? Should we leave a political union? Should we allow casinos? The ballot comes to you, and your vote counts toward the actual outcome.

This sounds straightforward, but the details matter enormously.

Some referendums are binding, meaning the government must implement whatever the voters decide. Others are merely advisory—essentially a very expensive opinion poll that politicians can ignore if they find the results inconvenient. The 2016 Brexit referendum in the United Kingdom was technically advisory, though ignoring a 52-48 result would have been politically suicidal.

The word itself comes from Latin, and here's where things get unexpectedly contentious. "Referendum" is a gerundive form of the verb "referre," meaning "to carry back"—as in, a proposal that must be carried back to the people for their approval. In proper Latin grammar, it cannot stand alone as a noun. English, being English, took the word anyway and pressed it into service.

This creates an amusing grammatical dispute: what's the plural? English grammar says "referendums." Latin purists insist on "referenda." The Oxford English Dictionary has weighed in, noting that since "referendum" as a Latin gerund has no plural, and the gerundive "referenda" would mean "things to be referred" (implying multiple issues, not multiple votes), the English plural "referendums" is actually the more defensible choice.

Nobody has ever changed their vote based on this debate, but academics have written papers about it.

The Swiss Invention

The modern referendum traces its origins to the Swiss canton of Graubünden in the sixteenth century. Switzerland remains the undisputed world champion of direct democracy, having held nearly six hundred national referendums since becoming a modern state in 1848.

To put that in perspective: Switzerland votes on national referendums roughly four times per year. Swiss citizens have directly decided questions ranging from whether to ban minarets on mosques (they voted yes to the ban in 2009) to whether to give every citizen an unconditional basic income (they voted no in 2016, by a margin of 77 to 23 percent).

Italy comes in a distant second with seventy-eight national referendums, more than half of which were proposed by the Radical Party. Most other democracies use referendums sparingly, reserving them for constitutional changes or questions of national identity.

Mandatory Versus Optional

Some referendums are legally required. If Australia wants to amend its constitution, it must hold a referendum—there's no other path. The same is true in Ireland, Switzerland, Denmark, and forty-nine of the fifty American states. Delaware, characteristically contrarian, allows constitutional amendments without a public vote.

Financial referendums are common at the local level in the United States. California's state government cannot borrow more than three hundred thousand dollars without asking voters to approve a bond proposition. This constraint was designed to prevent politicians from loading up on debt that future generations would have to repay—though California has found creative workarounds.

Then there are optional referendums, which happen because someone decided to ask. A legislature might refer a question to voters when the issue is too controversial for elected officials to touch directly. Citizens themselves might gather enough signatures to force a vote. In some systems, the executive branch can call a referendum to break legislative gridlock or to claim a popular mandate for a contested policy.

The philosopher Immanuel Kant once proposed a fascinating type of mandatory referendum that has never been implemented: a war referendum. Before any country could declare war, its citizens would have to vote to approve it. Kant reasoned that people who would bear the costs of war—dying in battle, paying higher taxes, rebuilding destroyed cities—would be far more reluctant to start one than politicians sitting safely in their capitals. The idea was seriously debated in the United States during the 1930s as the Ludlow Amendment but never passed. One can only imagine how twentieth-century history might have differed.

More Than Yes or No

Most referendums offer a binary choice: accept or reject. But this simplicity can be a straitjacket. What if voters want something different from both the proposal and the status quo?

Some countries have experimented with multiple-choice referendums. Sweden held one in 1957 and another in 1980, each offering three options. Australia's 1977 referendum on choosing a new national anthem gave voters four choices. New Zealand went further in 1992, presenting five different electoral systems for voters to consider.

Guam holds the record: a 1982 referendum offered six options, plus a blank line for voters who wanted to write in their own seventh alternative.

Multiple choices create a new problem: how do you determine the winner? If no option gets a majority, you need some method to resolve the split. Different jurisdictions have tried different approaches. Sweden used simple plurality—whoever gets the most votes wins, even without a majority. Australia used instant-runoff voting, where voters rank their preferences and the least popular options are eliminated one by one until someone crosses fifty percent.

The Irish Citizens' Assembly studied this question in 2018. Seventy-six of its members favored allowing more than two options in future referendums, and fifty-two percent supported preferential voting for such cases. The debate continues.

The Quorum Trap

Many referendums include a participation quorum—a minimum number of people must vote for the result to count. This sounds reasonable. You wouldn't want a handful of activists deciding a major question while everyone else stayed home.

But quorum requirements create a perverse incentive. If you oppose a referendum, you might be better off not voting at all. Stay home, encourage your allies to stay home, and the referendum fails not because most voters rejected it, but because not enough people showed up.

Italy experienced this in 2005 with a referendum on fertility laws. Opponents of the proposed changes didn't argue against them—they campaigned for abstention. The strategy worked. A majority of those who voted supported the changes, but turnout was too low to meet the quorum. The "no" side won by not playing.

This phenomenon, sometimes called the "quorum paradox," has been demonstrated both mathematically and empirically. The mere existence of a participation requirement tends to depress turnout below the threshold. It encourages manipulation of election timing, voter suppression, and inflated turnout figures.

The Venice Commission—the European body that advises on constitutional matters—now recommends against any quorum requirements in referendums.

When Courts Get Involved

Important referendums frequently end up in court, either before or after the vote.

In 2017, Spain's Constitutional Court suspended Catalonia's independence referendum before it could take place. The Catalan government held it anyway, declaring independence after ninety percent of voters (in a boycotted election with low turnout) voted to leave Spain. The Spanish government responded by taking direct control of Catalonia and prosecuting the referendum's organizers.

After Britain's Brexit vote, legal challenges argued the referendum was tainted by campaign finance violations and misleading claims. British courts dismissed these challenges, but the litigation revealed how few legal guardrails existed for referendum campaigns compared to regular elections.

International tribunals traditionally stayed out of referendum disputes, treating them as purely domestic matters. This changed in 2021 when the European Court of Human Rights, in a case called Toplak and Mrak v. Slovenia, ruled on a complaint from two disabled voters who couldn't access their polling places. The court's willingness to hear the case extended its jurisdiction to referendum conduct for the first time.

The Dark Side of Direct Democracy

Not everyone celebrates referendums. Critics argue that complex policy questions can't be reduced to yes-or-no choices, that voters lack the expertise to decide technical issues, and that well-funded campaigns can manipulate public opinion through advertising and propaganda.

The historical record offers uncomfortable examples.

Adolf Hitler used plebiscites to consolidate power, holding referendums in 1934 and 1936 that produced impossibly high approval ratings for policies that had already been implemented. Benito Mussolini did the same in Italy. António de Oliveira Salazar in Portugal, Francisco Franco in Spain, Park Chung Hee in South Korea, Ferdinand Marcos in the Philippines—dictators have repeatedly used referendums to drape authoritarian rule in democratic legitimacy.

Germany's postwar constitution contains no provision for federal referendums. Hitler's abuse of the plebiscite is widely cited as the reason why.

Even in functioning democracies, referendums can be weaponized. Governments sometimes call votes not to genuinely consult the public but to outflank political opponents, bypass parliamentary scrutiny, or energize their electoral base. The question put to voters may be designed to produce a predetermined answer.

John Bruton, who served as Ireland's prime minister in the 1990s, offered a cynical but arguably accurate observation about the dynamics at play. All governments are unpopular, he noted. Given the chance, people will vote against them in any referendum. Therefore, avoid referendums. And therefore, don't raise questions that require them.

The Referendum Renaissance

Despite these concerns, referendums are more popular than ever. After a decline in the mid-twentieth century, their use has been increasing since the 1970s.

Political scientists attribute this to dealignment—the weakening of traditional ties between voters and political parties. When people identified strongly as Democrats or Republicans, Labour or Conservative, they trusted their party to handle policy questions on their behalf. As those identities weakened, voters became more interested in deciding specific issues themselves rather than delegating to party platforms they only partially endorsed.

Technology has also played a role. Modern campaigns can reach voters directly through social media and targeted advertising, bypassing the party structures that once served as gatekeepers. It's easier than ever to organize a petition, gather signatures, and force an issue onto the ballot.

The result is a world where direct democracy coexists uneasily with representative democracy. Most countries still rely primarily on elected legislatures to make laws, but referendums provide an escape valve for issues where politicians fear to tread—or where citizens have lost faith in their representatives' willingness to act.

Plebiscite Versus Referendum

Before we finish, a note on terminology. "Referendum" and "plebiscite" are often used interchangeably, but some countries draw distinctions.

The word "plebiscite" comes from Roman history. The plebiscita were decrees of the Plebeian Council, the popular assembly that represented Rome's common citizens as opposed to its aristocratic patricians. The term carries connotations of the masses asserting themselves against elite authority.

In Australia, some people claim that "referendum" refers only to votes on constitutional changes, while "plebiscite" covers everything else. This distinction is not consistently applied—non-constitutional state votes are routinely called referendums, and historically Australians have used the terms interchangeably.

Ireland makes a cleaner distinction. The 1937 vote to adopt the Irish constitution was called a plebiscite. Every subsequent vote to amend that constitution has been called a referendum.

For most practical purposes, the words mean the same thing: the people get to decide.

The Promise and the Peril

The referendum embodies democracy's highest aspiration—that ordinary citizens, not just political elites, should decide how they are governed. It also embodies democracy's greatest risk—that majorities can be manipulated, misinformed, or simply wrong.

Switzerland votes constantly and seems none the worse for it. Britain held one referendum on EU membership and spent years consumed by the aftermath. The difference may lie not in the mechanism but in the political culture surrounding it: how questions are framed, how campaigns are conducted, whether voters trust the process, and whether everyone agrees to accept the result.

The Athenians, who invented democracy, made most of their decisions through direct citizen assemblies. They also voted to execute Socrates and launch the disastrous Sicilian Expedition. Rome's plebiscites eventually gave way to emperors. The lesson may be that direct democracy, like any powerful tool, depends entirely on how it's used.

Or perhaps the Swiss have simply had more practice.
