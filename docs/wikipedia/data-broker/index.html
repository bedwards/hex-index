Data broker

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Data broker

In 2012, the data giant Acxiom made a bold claim that would have been the stuff of science fiction just two decades prior: it possessed files on 2.5 billion different people. That number is staggering not merely for its scale, but for its implication. The entire global population hovers around 8 billion, yet a single corporation asserted it could construct a digital dossier for nearly a third of humanity, and likely far more if one counts the multiple profiles it might hold for a single individual across different datasets. This is not a dystopian fantasy; it is the operational reality of the data broker industry, a shadowy ecosystem that has quietly evolved from a niche service for librarians and researchers into the world's most pervasive surveillance economy.

At its core, the business of the data broker is the commodification of human existence. A data broker is an individual or company that specializes in the collection of personal data—ranging from income levels and ethnicity to political beliefs and precise geolocation—and sells or licenses this information to third parties. The sources of this data are as varied as they are invasive. While the industry has deep roots in public records, the modern era has seen an explosion of Internet-based harvesting. Since the 1990s, brokers have scoured census and electoral roll records, social networking sites, court reports, and purchase histories. They aggregate this fragmented information into comprehensive profiles that often contain thousands of data points about a single person. A typical profile might include a person's age, race, gender, height, weight, marital status, religious affiliation, political affiliation, occupation, household income, net worth, home ownership status, investment habits, product preferences, and even health-related interests.

To understand how we arrived here, one must look back to the mid-20th century. Credit scores were first used in the 1950s, marking the beginning of the systematic quantification of individual risk. During that same decade, information brokering emerged as a distinct career path for individuals. However, the business did not become widely known or specifically regulated until the 1990s. In the 1970s, the profession was dominated by individuals with library science degrees, trained in the art of retrieval and synthesis. The term "information broker" was often synonymous with the highly skilled, fee-based researcher. Kelly Warnken, a pivotal figure in this early era, published the first fee-based information directory in 1977. This was followed by the launch of the Journal of Fee-Based Information Services in 1979 and the publication of The Information Brokers: How to Start and Operate Your Own Fee-based Service in 1981. These works codified a profession that was then viewed as a specialized service for the discerning.

The landscape shifted dramatically as the 20th century drew to a close. Technological developments, specifically the rise of the Internet, the exponential increase in computer processing power, and the precipitous decline in data storage costs, dismantled the barriers to mass data collection. Suddenly, it was not just feasible but profitable to collect, analyze, store, and transfer massive amounts of data about individuals. The line between the "information professional" and the "information broker" blurred. People with degrees in science, law, business, and medicine entered the fray, transforming the industry from a service of retrieval into an industry of aggregation and prediction. By the turn of the millennium, there was no required academic qualification for the job. One might find a data broker with a bachelor's degree in business or marketing, while another might be a former librarian or a veteran of a database provider company. The profession had become the "business of buying and selling information as a commodity."

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) in the United States has defined these entities with cold precision: "companies that collect information, including personal information about consumers, from a wide variety of sources for the purpose of reselling such information to their customers for various purposes, including verifying an individual's identity, differentiating records, marketing products, and preventing financial fraud." Gartner, a leading research and advisory firm, offers a slightly more technical definition, describing an information broker as a business that "aggregates information from a variety of sources; processes it to enrich, cleanse or analyze it; and licenses it to other organizations." Note the distinction in Gartner's language: data is often "licensed for particular or limited uses" rather than sold outright. This semantic nuance is crucial for the industry's legal maneuvering, even as the practical effect remains the transfer of personal data for profit.

The scope of data harvested is virtually limitless. Brokers pull from census records, change-of-address filings, motor vehicle records, user-contributed content, social media, media reports, court documents, voter registration lists, consumer purchase histories, most-wanted lists, terrorist watch lists, bank card transaction records, health care authorities, and Web browsing histories. They may also purchase information from other companies, such as credit card processors, creating a feedback loop of commercial surveillance. The resulting datasets are then used to create the aforementioned profiles, which are sold to a dizzying array of clients. Advertising companies use these profiles to target specific groups with surgical precision. Financial institutions use them to assess risk and verify identity. Political parties utilize them to micro-target voters. Government agencies and historians rely on them for research. Even non-profit organizations use broker data to identify potential grant recipients, and real estate agents employ them to conduct land title searches.

Yet, the implications of this system are not merely commercial; they are deeply personal and often dangerous. The aggregation of data can have severe consequences for safety and privacy. "People-search" whitepage sites, which often pull from these broker databases, make information like property records and tax filings available for a small fee or even for free. While this sounds benign, it has profound implications for stalking, harassment, and domestic violence. A predator can trace a victim's location, financial status, and family connections with a few clicks. The data brokers themselves often argue that they are secondary researchers, saving their clients time and money by performing the complex retrieval and analysis work. They claim to be essential for fraud detection and risk mitigation. The FTC has categorized the primary uses of data brokers into three broad areas: advertising, fraud detection, and risk mitigation. However, the system is not foolproof. Data brokers must screen their clients carefully to prevent criminals from obtaining data for nefarious purposes. History has shown that this screening is often insufficient. US broking companies LexisNexis and ChoicePoint have both been duped by phoney clients, leading in one case to identity theft on a massive scale. These breaches exposed the fragility of a system built on the premise that data, once collected, could be securely managed and ethically distributed.

The regulatory landscape surrounding data brokers is a patchwork of confusion and inaction, particularly in the United States. As of 2026, there is no federal regulation protecting consumers from data brokers in the US. The absence of a federal framework has allowed the industry to grow unchecked, with companies like Acxiom, Experian, Epsilon, CoreLogic, Datalogix, Intelius, PeekYou, Exactis, and Recorded Future operating with minimal oversight. Some states have begun enacting laws individually to address the issue, but these efforts are fragmented and often limited in scope. The lack of a unified federal standard means that a consumer's privacy rights depend entirely on their zip code. In contrast, the European Union has taken a more aggressive stance. The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) serves to regulate data brokers' operations, imposing strict requirements on consent, data minimization, and the right to be forgotten. This transatlantic divide highlights a fundamental philosophical difference: the EU views personal data as an extension of individual rights, while the US largely treats it as a commercial asset.

The evolution of the industry also reflects a shift in the nature of the data itself. While early information brokers focused on public records and specialized research, modern data brokers have moved into the realm of behavioral prediction. They do not just report on what a person has done; they attempt to predict what they will do next. Geolocation data, included in marketing resources from giants like Acxiom, Experian, and Oracle, allows for location-based marketing services that track individuals' movements in real-time. This level of granularity transforms the data broker from a passive recorder of history into an active participant in shaping behavior. Advertising companies, for instance, are often less interested in specific personal details about an individual than they are in the profiles and categories into which that individual has been sorted. A person is no longer a unique human being but a collection of attributes: "high-income male, 35-44, interested in luxury vehicles, politically conservative, living in a suburban household."

The sheer volume of data collected is almost incomprehensible. Some datasets include thousands of pieces of information about a single person. These profiles are sold and resold, often without the knowledge or consent of the individuals involved. The term "information broker" is sometimes abbreviated to IB, but the industry is also known by a multitude of other names: data brokers, independent information specialists, information or data agents, data providers, data suppliers, information resellers, data vendors, syndicated data brokers, or information product companies. Information consultants, freelance librarians, and information specialists are also sometimes termed information brokers, further blurring the lines between the ethical retrieval of public information and the predatory harvesting of private data. The diversity of terminology reflects the industry's desire to distance itself from the negative connotations of "surveillance" while continuing to profit from the trade of personal information.

The clients of these brokers come from a wide range of industries and professions. Manufacturing firms use data to optimize supply chains and understand consumer trends. Financial institutions rely on it for credit scoring and fraud prevention. Political parties use it for voter suppression or mobilization. Government agencies use it for security and law enforcement. Historians use it for demographic research. The ubiquity of these services means that almost every interaction one has with the modern world—buying a car, applying for a job, voting in an election, or browsing the web—is filtered through the lens of the data broker. The information is used to determine creditworthiness, to screen for employment, to approve housing applications, and to tailor the advertising one sees on every digital platform. The system is designed to be invisible, operating in the background to facilitate the smooth functioning of the global economy.

Despite the lack of federal regulation, the pressure on the industry is mounting. The sheer scale of data collection has raised public awareness and concern. The term "data broker" has entered the popular lexicon, often carrying a negative connotation of intrusion and exploitation. The industry's defense—that it provides a valuable service by aggregating public information and protecting against fraud—is increasingly scrutinized. The question of where the line should be drawn between legitimate business intelligence and invasive surveillance remains a central debate in the digital age. The FTC's definition of the industry focuses on the purpose of the data collection: verification, differentiation, marketing, and fraud prevention. But these purposes are often achieved through methods that compromise individual privacy and autonomy.

As we move further into the 21st century, the role of the data broker is likely to expand, not contract. The proliferation of the Internet of Things (IoT), the increasing sophistication of artificial intelligence, and the growing availability of biometric data will only deepen the pool of information available for collection. The industry has already demonstrated its ability to adapt to new technologies and new sources of data. From the library science roots of the 1970s to the algorithmic behemoths of the 2020s, the data broker has proven to be a resilient and adaptable entity. The challenge for society is to determine how to harness the utility of this information while protecting the fundamental rights of individuals. The current regulatory vacuum in the United States stands in stark contrast to the global trend toward greater data protection. Without federal intervention, the industry will continue to operate in the shadows, collecting and selling the digital lives of billions.

The story of the data broker is the story of the modern economy. It is a story of efficiency, innovation, and profit, but also of privacy, power, and control. The 2.5 billion profiles claimed by Acxiom are not just numbers; they are the digital ghosts of the world's population, packaged and sold to the highest bidder. As the industry continues to evolve, the question is no longer whether we can stop the collection of data, but how we can ensure that the collection of data serves the public good rather than undermining it. The absence of federal regulation in the US leaves consumers vulnerable, relying on a patchwork of state laws and the goodwill of companies that have no incentive to be transparent. The future of the data broker industry will depend on the balance between the economic benefits of data aggregation and the ethical imperatives of privacy and consent. Until that balance is struck, the industry will remain a powerful, often hidden, force shaping the lives of individuals around the world.

The legacy of the early information brokers, with their library degrees and fee-based directories, is a distant memory. The modern data broker is a creature of the algorithm, operating on a scale that would have been unimaginable to the pioneers of the field. The tools have changed, but the fundamental transaction remains the same: the exchange of personal information for value. Whether that value is measured in dollars, security, or political influence, the cost is often borne by the individual whose data is being traded. As we navigate this complex landscape, it is essential to understand the mechanisms of the data broker industry, the sources of its power, and the implications of its existence. The data broker is not just a business; it is a mirror reflecting the values and priorities of the society that created it. And in that reflection, we see a world where privacy is increasingly a luxury, and personal data is the most valuable currency of all.
