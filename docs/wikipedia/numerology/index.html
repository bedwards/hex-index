Numerology

7 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Numerology

In 590 BCE, a philosopher and mathematician named Pythagoras walked the streets of Miletus—a coastal city in modern-day Turkey that would become the cradle of Western numerical mysticism. He wasn't alone; across the sea on the island of Samos, his followers gathered to learn isopsephy: the art of assigning divine meaning to numbers, and numbers to words. Twenty-six centuries later, we still haven't quite finished asking whether the universe speaks in mathematical code.What if numbers aren't neutral descriptors of reality—but windows into something metaphysical? This question has haunted human thought for millennia, dividing the boundary between ancient wisdom and contemporary superstition, between mathematics proper and mystical speculation.

Numerology—known before the 20th century as arithmancy, from the Greek arithmos (number) and manteia (divination)—centers on this very belief: that numerical relationships reveal occult, divine, or mystical connections between numbers and phenomena. It encompasses the practice of assigning numerical values to letters in words and names—called gematria—and applying those values to personal interpretation. When used to analyze a person's name, it becomes a form of onomancy, part of a broader tradition linking numerology to astrology and other divinatory arts.

The word itself wasn't recorded in English before roughly 1907, but the practice is ancient. The earliest evidence appears in an Assyrian inscription from the 8th century BCE, commissioned by King Sargon II: "the king built the wall of Khorsabad 16,283 cubits long to correspond with the numerical value of his name." Here was a ruler explicitly constructing a temple based not on architectural whimsy but on alphanumeric symbolism—the very definition of numerological thinking in the ancient world.

Rabbinic literature took this further, using gematria to interpret passages in the Hebrew Bible. Jewish mystics discovered hidden connections between words whose numerical values matched, building an entire system of textual interpretation that persists today in orthodox practice. The traditional mystical approach—assigning mystical meaning to words based on their numerical values and tracing connections between words of equal value—is still called gematria.

The systems enabling this numerical mapping originated in the Greek city of Miletus, thus known as the Milesian system. Using alphabetic letters to represent numbers developed there in the 6th century BCE—early examples include vase graffiti dating back to that period. Aristotle recorded that Pythagoras himself practiced isopsephy, the predecessor of Hebrew gematria—a tradition he founded after his arrival in Samos.

Pythagoras was a contemporary of philosophers Anaximander and Anaximenes, and historian Hecataeus—all of whom lived in Miletus across the sea from Samos. The Milesian system became standard by the reign of Alexander the Great (336–323 BCE) and was adopted throughout the Hellenistic period.

By Ptolemy II Philadelphus (284–246 BCE), Egypt officially adopted the system, and it remained in use well into late antiquity.

By the late 4th century AD, following the Edict of Thessalonica in 380 AD and the subsequent enforcement of Nicene Christianity under Theodosius I, departures from beliefs endorsed by the state church were classified as civil violations within the Roman Empire. Numerology—then referred to as isopsephy—remained in use in conservative Greek Orthodox circles, particularly in mystical and theological contexts.

Some of the most elaborate numerological frameworks came not from religious traditions but from early alchemical theory. Arab alchemist Jabir ibn Hayyan (died circa 806–816) framed his experiments in an elaborate numerology based on the names of substances in the Arabic language—a stunning intersection of science, mysticism, and linguistic symbolism.

In 1658, Sir Thomas Browne published The Garden of Cyrus, a literary discourse where he attempted to demonstrate that the number five and the related quincunx pattern could be found throughout the arts, in design, and particularly in botany. This work represented one of the earliest attempts at systematic numerological analysis in English literature—treating natural forms as numerically significant.

The practice extends beyond Western traditions. Some approaches to understanding meanings of the Qur'an—the sacred text of Muslims—include interpreting numerical symbols combined with purely textual approaches. The Mandaean number alphasyllabary (Mandaic: gmaṭ aria) is used for numerology, and the Book of the Zodiac remains an important Mandaean text on this topic.

Various systems assign numerical values to alphabet letters:

  • Abjad numerals in Arabic and Hebrew
  • Armenian numerals
  • Greek numerals
  • The traditional Jewish practice of mystical word-meaning based on numerical values

In the Pythagorean method—using place-value for number-letter attributions, as did ancient Hebrew and Greek systems—the letters of the modern Latin alphabet are assigned numerical values 1 through 9. Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa applied arithmancy to the classical Latin alphabet in the 16th century in Three Books of Occult Philosophy, mapping letters according to their place-value at that time (note: U, J, and W weren't commonly considered part of the Latin alphabet then).

So-called angel numbers—defined by Doreen Virtue and Lynnette Brown in 2004 as numbers consisting of repeating digits such as 111 or 444—became a popular numerological phenomenon. By 2023, numerous publications had printed articles suggesting these numbers carried numerological significance.

But in 2024, Virtue renounced the concept entirely, telling The Cut: "It's garbage. I regret it, and I'm sorry that I made them." A remarkable reversal from someone who helped popularize one of modern numerology's most widespread trends.

English-language systems proliferated:

  • The first English gematria was used by poet John Skelton in 1523 in his poem "The Garland of Laurel"
  • Willis F. Whitehead made the next reference in 1899, describing "English Cabala" in The Mystic Thesaurus
  • In 1952, John P.L. Hughes published The Hidden Numerical Significance of the English Language, or, Suggestive Gematria, based on a lecture delivered at Holden Research Circle
  • William Eisen described a system related to the Spiritualist Agasha Temple of Wisdom in The English Cabalah (1980–82)
  • William G. Gray proposed another system in his 1984 book Concepts of Qabalah, republished as Qabalistic Concepts—including correspondence attributions of letters to positions on the Tree of Life
  • Michael Bertiaux described Angelic Gematria in The Voudon Gnostic Workbook (1989)
  • David Rankine described a system of English gematria using prime numbers, called Prime Qabalah, in Becoming Magick (2004)
  • A system known as Trigrammaton Qabalah (TQ), first published by R. Leo Gillis in 1996, included a gematria based on one of the Holy Books of Thelema written by Aleister Crowley in 1907 (Liber Trigrammaton). Its primary feature: a new understanding of the Cube of Space and its 26 components (edges, faces, vertices)—which coincidentally equal the number of letters in the English alphabet.

In modern culture, numerology continues to shape personal decisions. Australian MP Sussan Ley credibly altered the spelling of her own name according to numerological advice, believing it would bring excitement—a story widely reported in 2025.

Celebrities have marked entry into what they call a "9-year" numerological cycle signifying transformation and closure, with symbolic gestures associated with personal renewal. Gwyneth Paltrow posted rain imagery as part of such symbolism.

Sometimes, scientific theories themselves get labeled "numerology" if their primary inspiration appears to be patterns rather than empirical observation—colloquial usage that's quite common within the scientific community, used mostly to dismiss a theory as questionable science. The best-known example involves coincidental resemblances between large numbers that intrigued mathematical physicist Paul Dirac, mathematician Hermann Weyl, and astronomer Arthur Stanley Eddington.

The question remains: are these numerical coincidences evidence of hidden mathematical architecture in the universe—or simply human pattern-seeking in an indifferent cosmos?

Perhaps the answer lies not in denying either possibility but in recognizing what Pythagoras discovered in Miletus twenty-six centuries ago: that numbers may be the most universal language humans have ever attempted to speak—and one we still don't fully understand.
