Magnesium

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Magnesium

The Element That Burns Brighter Than the Sun

Strike a match to a strip of magnesium and you'll witness something extraordinary: a light so blindingly white that it can permanently damage your eyes, a flame so hot it cannot be extinguished by water. In fact, pour water on burning magnesium and you'll make things dramatically worse—the metal will rip the oxygen right out of the water molecules, releasing hydrogen gas and intensifying the inferno.

This is magnesium. Element twelve on the periodic table. A metal so reactive it seems almost alive, yet so fundamental to life that every cell in your body depends on it.

Forged in Dying Stars

The magnesium in your bones, in your morning supplement, in the lightweight frame of a racing bicycle—all of it was manufactured in the cores of ancient stars billions of years ago. The process is elegant in its violence: take a carbon nucleus, add a helium nucleus, wait. Add another helium. Then another. What you get is magnesium.

These stellar forges operate for millions of years, fusing lighter elements into heavier ones in their desperate bid to generate enough energy to hold themselves together against the crushing force of their own gravity. Eventually, they lose this battle. When massive stars exhaust their fuel, they collapse and then explode as supernovas, scattering their freshly minted elements across the cosmos like seeds.

Some of that magnesium drifted for eons through the interstellar void before condensing into the cloud of gas and dust that would become our solar system. Today, magnesium comprises about thirteen percent of Earth's total mass. It's the eighth most abundant element in the planet's crust and the fourth most common element overall, trailing only iron, oxygen, and silicon.

When scientists examine certain ancient meteorites—objects formed in the earliest days of our solar system—they find unusual concentrations of a particular magnesium isotope called magnesium-26. This isotope is the decay product of aluminum-26, a radioactive form of aluminum with a half-life of about 717,000 years. The presence of excess magnesium-26 tells researchers that these meteorites solidified before the primordial aluminum-26 had finished decaying. These rocks are among the oldest objects in our solar system, time capsules from an era before the Earth existed.

A Metal of Contradictions

Pure magnesium is a study in contradictions. It's remarkably light—only two-thirds as dense as aluminum, which is itself famously lightweight. It has the lowest melting point of any alkaline earth metal, becoming liquid at just 650 degrees Celsius. It's also the most chemically reactive member of its family, eager to combine with almost anything it touches.

And yet magnesium survives just fine in ordinary air. How?

The moment fresh magnesium is exposed to atmosphere, it begins reacting with oxygen to form magnesium oxide. But rather than consuming the metal entirely, this oxide forms an incredibly thin protective shell—a passivation layer, in chemistry terms—that shields the underlying metal from further attack. It's like how a scab protects a wound, except this scab is only a few molecules thick and forms almost instantaneously.

This self-protective behavior is both blessing and curse. The oxide layer allows magnesium to be stored and handled without special precautions. But it also means that getting magnesium to react when you actually want it to requires overcoming that initial barrier. In bulk form, magnesium is surprisingly difficult to ignite. Powder it finely, though, and you've dramatically increased its surface area relative to volume. Fine magnesium powder will ignite readily and burn with explosive intensity.

The Brilliant Flash of Old Photography

Before electronic flash units, photographers relied on magnesium's spectacular combustion to illuminate their subjects. Early flash powder was typically a mixture of magnesium with an oxidizer like potassium chlorate. Igniting this mixture produced the brilliant white light needed to expose photographic plates in an era before sensitive film.

The results were effective but hazardous. Flash powder explosions caused numerous injuries and fires. The timing was also imprecise—you had to coordinate your shutter click with a literal explosion. Modern camera flashes achieve the same brilliance through electrical discharge in xenon gas tubes, which is considerably less likely to burn down your portrait studio.

Magnesium's brilliant flame found another application in warfare. Incendiary bombs containing magnesium were devastatingly effective because the fire they produced couldn't be extinguished by conventional means. Water, as mentioned, only makes magnesium fires worse. Even sand isn't particularly effective, since burning magnesium reaches temperatures hot enough to reduce silicon dioxide. Specialized dry powder extinguishers or simply letting the fire burn itself out were often the only options.

The Grignard Revolution

In 1900, a French chemist named Victor Grignard discovered something that would transform organic chemistry forever. By reacting magnesium metal with organic compounds containing halogens—chlorine, bromine, or iodine—in the presence of ether, he created a new class of highly reactive reagents that now bear his name.

Grignard reagents are essentially magnesium atoms bonded to carbon atoms in a way that makes the carbon highly nucleophilic—meaning it seeks out and attacks positively charged sites on other molecules. This might sound technical, but the practical implications were revolutionary. Suddenly chemists had a reliable, controllable way to form carbon-carbon bonds, the fundamental linkages that make up all organic molecules.

Before Grignard reagents, building complex organic molecules was often a matter of luck and persistence. After their discovery, it became almost routine. The pharmaceutical industry, the plastics industry, the entire field of synthetic organic chemistry—all trace significant portions of their methodology back to Grignard's discovery. He received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1912, just twelve years after his initial publication.

Common Grignard reagents include phenylmagnesium bromide and ethylmagnesium bromide. They're still used daily in research laboratories and industrial facilities worldwide, more than a century after their invention.

Inside Your Cells

While chemists were learning to exploit magnesium's reactivity in the laboratory, biologists were discovering just how essential this element is to life itself. Magnesium is the eleventh most abundant element by mass in the human body, and every single one of your trillions of cells requires it.

The numbers are striking. Over three hundred different enzymes in your body depend on magnesium ions to function. These aren't obscure metabolic backwaters—magnesium is essential to adenosine triphosphate, better known as ATP, the molecule that serves as the universal energy currency of all living things. Without magnesium, ATP doesn't work properly. Without functional ATP, nothing in your cells works at all.

Magnesium is also intimately involved with your genetic material. Both DNA and RNA are polyphosphate compounds, and magnesium ions interact with these phosphate groups in ways that stabilize the molecules and enable their functions. The enzymes that copy DNA, the machinery that reads RNA to build proteins—all of it requires magnesium.

This essential role explains why magnesium deficiency can manifest in so many seemingly unrelated ways: muscle cramps, fatigue, irregular heartbeat, anxiety, headaches. Your body is desperately trying to function without a critical component.

The Medicine Cabinet Staple

Long before anyone understood the biochemistry, people recognized that certain magnesium compounds had medicinal value. Milk of magnesia—a suspension of magnesium hydroxide in water—has been used as an antacid and laxative since the nineteenth century. The name comes from its white, opaque appearance, not from any actual dairy content.

As an antacid, magnesium hydroxide works by neutralizing stomach acid. The reaction produces magnesium chloride and water, effectively reducing the acidity causing heartburn or indigestion. As a laxative, the same compound draws water into the intestines through osmosis, softening stool and stimulating bowel movements.

Epsom salts—magnesium sulfate heptahydrate, to give its chemical name—have their own medical history. Named after the English town of Epsom where the compound was first isolated from mineral springs in the early 1600s, these salts are still used in baths for muscle relaxation and, taken internally, as a powerful laxative. Some research suggests that magnesium can be absorbed through the skin during Epsom salt baths, though the extent of this absorption remains debated.

In more serious medical contexts, intravenous magnesium sulfate is used to treat eclampsia, a dangerous complication of pregnancy characterized by seizures and dangerously high blood pressure. The magnesium helps stabilize nerve cell membranes and reduce the abnormal electrical activity that causes seizures. It's considered essential medicine by the World Health Organization.

The Lightweight Champion

The same properties that make magnesium biologically essential—its light weight and high reactivity—make it industrially valuable. Magnesium alloys, blends of magnesium with small amounts of other metals, represent a compelling compromise between strength and weight.

Pure magnesium is actually too soft and reactive for most applications. It's brittle, prone to cracking along internal planes called shear bands. Add just one percent aluminum, however, and the metal becomes dramatically more workable. Other common alloying elements include zinc, manganese, and rare earth metals, each contributing different properties.

The aerospace industry was an early adopter of magnesium alloys. When every gram matters—and in aircraft, every gram absolutely matters—magnesium's density advantage over aluminum translates directly to fuel savings. Racing cars, bicycles, laptops, camera bodies—anywhere weight is a critical factor, magnesium alloys often appear.

But magnesium's reactivity remains a challenge. Traditional magnesium alloys are prone to corrosion, especially in the presence of trace amounts of iron, nickel, copper, or cobalt. These elements form tiny electrochemical cells within the alloy, galvanic couples that accelerate oxidation. Manufacturers must maintain extremely tight control over alloy composition to minimize this effect.

Heat presents another problem. Magnesium alloys tend to creep—gradually deform under sustained stress—at elevated temperatures. This limits their use in engine components and other high-temperature applications. Alloying with zinc and rare earth elements helps, but doesn't eliminate the issue entirely.

And then there's the fire risk. Under normal conditions, bulk magnesium alloys don't spontaneously ignite. But heat them enough—say, in a machining operation that generates friction, or in a crash that deforms metal rapidly—and combustion becomes possible. Adding small amounts of calcium significantly reduces flammability. Research into rare earth element additions suggests it may eventually be possible to create magnesium alloys that resist ignition even at temperatures approaching the metal's boiling point.

From Seawater to Metal

Given magnesium's abundance in Earth's crust, you might expect it to be easy to obtain. But remember that thin protective oxide layer? Magnesium in nature is almost always combined with other elements, locked into stable compounds that require significant energy to break apart.

The most important magnesium minerals include dolomite (a calcium-magnesium carbonate), magnesite (magnesium carbonate), and brucite (magnesium hydroxide). But the largest accessible reservoir of magnesium is the ocean itself. Magnesium ions are the second most abundant positively charged particles in seawater, after sodium. Every cubic kilometer of seawater contains about 1.3 billion kilograms of magnesium.

There are two primary industrial routes to metallic magnesium: thermal reduction and electrolysis.

The Pidgeon process, named after Canadian researcher Lloyd Montgomery Pidgeon who developed it in the 1940s, dominates global production. It begins with dolomite ore, which is heated to drive off carbon dioxide and produce a mixture of magnesium and calcium oxides. This mixture is then combined with ferrosilicon—an alloy of iron and silicon—and heated further in a vacuum. The silicon reduces the magnesium oxide, liberating magnesium as a gas while forming stable calcium silicate. The gaseous magnesium condenses on cooler surfaces where it's collected as a solid.

The Pidgeon process has several advantages. It's technically straightforward, produces very pure magnesium, and doesn't require expensive equipment. Its main disadvantage is energy intensity—all that heating requires substantial fuel. China, with its abundant coal and lower environmental regulations, came to dominate Pidgeon process production. As of 2017, China produced approximately 930,000 tonnes of magnesium annually, roughly 85% of world production.

This concentration has geopolitical implications. In September 2021, Chinese government efforts to reduce energy consumption in manufacturing led to sharp cuts in magnesium production. Prices spiked globally, and industries dependent on magnesium alloys scrambled for alternatives.

The electrolytic route works differently. Seawater is treated with calcium hydroxide, which precipitates magnesium hydroxide. This hydroxide is filtered out and reacted with hydrochloric acid to produce magnesium chloride. The magnesium chloride is then melted and electrolyzed—an electric current passed through the molten salt breaks it apart, depositing metallic magnesium at the cathode while chlorine gas bubbles off at the anode.

The Dow process, developed by the Dow Chemical Company, once made the United States a major magnesium producer. A facility on the shores of Corpus Christi Bay in Texas extracted magnesium directly from seawater using this electrolytic method. At its peak in the mid-twentieth century, the United States supplied nearly half of global magnesium. Today, US market share has dwindled to roughly seven percent, with only a single domestic producer remaining: US Magnesium, located at the Great Salt Lake in Utah.

Detecting the Invisible

How do chemists know when magnesium is present? Several elegant tests exist.

The classical method involves adding ammonium chloride, ammonium hydroxide, and monosodium phosphate to a solution suspected of containing magnesium. If magnesium ions are present, they combine with the phosphate and ammonium to form magnesium ammonium phosphate, a white crystalline precipitate. The appearance of this white solid confirms magnesium's presence.

A more visually striking test uses azo violet, an organic dye. In alkaline solutions containing magnesium, this dye turns deep blue. The color change occurs because azo violet molecules adsorb onto the surface of magnesium hydroxide particles that form in basic solution. No magnesium, no particles, no blue color.

The Hydrogen Storage Dream

One of the most intriguing potential applications for magnesium lies in energy storage. Magnesium hydride—magnesium combined with hydrogen—can theoretically store hydrogen at higher densities than compressed gas cylinders or even liquid hydrogen. By weight, magnesium hydride contains about 7.6% hydrogen, one of the highest percentages among simple metal hydrides.

The appeal for clean energy applications is obvious. Hydrogen fuel cells produce only water as waste, but storing enough hydrogen for practical applications remains challenging. Magnesium hydride could potentially solve this problem, absorbing hydrogen for storage and releasing it when heated.

As of 2020, significant research was ongoing, but practical magnesium hydride storage systems remained elusive. The temperatures required to release hydrogen from the compound are too high for many applications, and the kinetics—how fast the hydrogen can be absorbed and released—need improvement. Still, the fundamental chemistry is promising enough to sustain continued investigation.

A Brighter Future

New methods of magnesium production continue to emerge. Solid oxide membrane technology represents a particularly promising approach, using yttria-stabilized zirconia as an electrolyte to reduce magnesium oxide directly to metal. When silver serves as the anode, the only byproduct is pure oxygen gas—no chlorine, no carbon dioxide. Reports from 2011 suggested this method could reduce production costs by forty percent compared to traditional electrolysis.

The carbothermic process offers another alternative, using carbon to reduce magnesium oxide at high temperatures. The chemistry is simple: carbon plus magnesium oxide yields carbon monoxide plus magnesium. The challenge lies in preventing the reaction from reversing as the products cool. Various strategies—rapid quenching, dissolution in metal solvents—address this problem with varying degrees of success.

Meanwhile, metallurgists continue refining magnesium alloys to address their traditional weaknesses. Better corrosion resistance, higher temperature stability, reduced flammability—each improvement opens new applications. The total market for magnesium alloys remains small compared to aluminum (roughly one million tonnes versus fifty million tonnes annually), but the gap has been narrowing.

From the hearts of dying stars to the pills in your medicine cabinet, from the brilliance of a photographer's flash to the lightweight frame of a racing bicycle, magnesium's story spans cosmic time and intimate biology. It's an element of extremes: violently reactive yet essential to life, common as dirt yet difficult to obtain in pure form, dangerous when mishandled yet indispensable to modern technology.

Every breath you take depends on enzymes powered by magnesium. Every muscle contraction, every thought, every heartbeat—all enabled by this light, reactive metal forged in stellar furnaces billions of years before Earth existed. The next time you take a supplement or admire a sleek magnesium alloy gadget, remember: you're holding stardust made practical.
