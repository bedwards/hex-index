Exorbitant privilege

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Exorbitant privilege

In February 1965, President Charles de Gaulle made a move that would shake the foundations of the post-war global economy, sending a squadron of French destroyers across the Atlantic Ocean with a singular, audacious mission. They were not there to blockade a port or threaten an invasion; they were there to pick up gold. De Gaulle had announced his intention to convert France's massive holdings of U.S. dollars into gold at the official exchange rate of $35 per ounce, a demand he argued was backed by the very treaties that created the modern financial system. The French Navy's arrival in New York to collect the bullion was a physical manifestation of a growing global frustration: the United States was printing money while the rest of the world was paying the price in goods and labor. This moment of high-stakes confrontation did not break the system immediately, but it cracked the dam. Just six years later, in August 1971, President Richard Nixon would be forced to end the convertibility of the dollar to gold entirely, an event known as the "Nixon Shock." Yet, paradoxically, the system did not collapse. Instead, the dollar emerged from the chaos with an even more dominant status, retaining a unique, almost magical power that French Finance Minister Valéry Giscard d'Estaing had famously termed "exorbitant privilege."

To understand why a currency can possess such privilege, one must first grasp the mechanics of the global financial bloodstream. In the world of international finance, the United States occupies a position that is mathematically impossible for any other nation to replicate. The U.S. dollar is the world's primary reserve currency, the standard against which all other currencies are measured and the asset held by central banks to ensure their own stability. As of 2025, dollars constituted 58% of all central bank reserves held outside the United States. The dominance is even more stark in the realm of trade: the overwhelming majority of international commerce is conducted in greenbacks. In Asia alone, 74% of trade is denominated in dollars, while in the Americas, that figure skyrockets to 96%. Furthermore, approximately 90% of all foreign exchange transactions globally involve the U.S. dollar.

This monopoly creates a self-reinforcing cycle of demand. When a country like Japan or Brazil wants to buy oil, machinery, or technology, they often need dollars to complete the transaction. To get these dollars, they must sell their own goods to the United States. This dynamic allows the U.S. to run massive trade deficits, importing far more than it exports, without facing the balance-of-payments crises that would cripple a lesser nation. If a country like Turkey or Argentina runs a deficit, investors panic, their currency crashes, and they cannot afford to import essentials. The U.S. simply prints the currency needed to pay for its imports. As American economist Barry Eichengreen summarized with biting precision: "It costs only a few cents for the Bureau of Engraving and Printing to produce a $100 bill, but other countries had to pony up $100 of actual goods in order to obtain one." This is the essence of seigniorage revenue—the profit made by the issuer of money—and for the United States, it is an endless stream of free capital.

The benefits of this position extend far beyond cheap imports. The most immediate and tangible advantage is the cost of borrowing. Because the dollar is perceived as the ultimate "safe asset," there is a perpetual, insatiable global demand for U.S. government debt. When the U.S. Treasury issues bonds, the world buys them, not necessarily because they believe in the fiscal health of Washington, but because they have nowhere else to go. This demand drives up the price of the bonds, which in turn drives down the interest rates the U.S. government must pay. The United States can borrow beyond the fiscal capacity of any other state, funding wars, social programs, and infrastructure at interest rates that would be the envy of any other debtor nation. This privilege allows for a level of fiscal flexibility that would be considered reckless for any other country. If the U.S. were to issue the same amount of debt as a typical emerging market, its borrowing costs would skyrocket, potentially triggering a recession. Instead, the world absorbs the debt, subsidizing American living standards and multinational corporations.

This phenomenon is not unique to the United States; it is the recurring destiny of hegemonic powers. History provides a clear lineage of this privilege. In the 17th and early 18th centuries, the Dutch Republic enjoyed a similar status, with the Dutch guilder serving as the world's reserve currency. Later, during the 19th and early 20th centuries, the British pound sterling held this crown. Around 1815, British national debt accounted for more than half of the world's traded securities. The United Kingdom was the center of global trade, the supplier of safe assets, and the beneficiary of the same low borrowing costs and trade advantages the U.S. enjoys today. The transition from the pound to the dollar was not seamless, but the underlying economic logic remained constant: the hegemon provides the world with a store of value, and in return, the world provides the hegemon with cheap credit and real resources.

However, the concept of exorbitant privilege is often misunderstood as merely a financial trick. It is, in reality, a geopolitical weapon. The dominance of the dollar gives the United States unparalleled leverage over global economic policies and financial markets. Because so many countries rely on the dollar for international transactions, the U.S. can impose costly sanctions that effectively cut off nations from the global economy. To sanction a country in the dollar system is to freeze its assets, block its trade, and isolate it from the primary engine of global commerce. This power is not derived from military might alone, but from the structural reality of the financial system itself. The dollar's status provides domestic stability and liquidity in global markets, but it also allows the Federal Reserve to set monetary policy with a degree of autonomy that other central banks can only dream of. The Fed can raise or lower interest rates to fight inflation or stimulate growth in the U.S. without worrying as much about the immediate, catastrophic effects on exchange rates or capital flows that would plague other nations. The world adjusts to the Fed's decisions, not the other way around.

Yet, this system is not without its deep contradictions and inherent risks. Economists have long debated two specific empirical puzzles that arise from the exorbitant privilege. The first is the "position puzzle." The United States has a deeply negative net international investment position (NIIP), meaning it owes the world far more than the world owes it. In a normal economy, such a massive debt load would signal impending doom. The U.S. has accumulated current account deficits for decades, yet its NIIP is much smaller than the sum of those deficits would suggest. How is this possible? The answer lies in the nature of the assets. The U.S. tends to borrow in its own currency (cheap, safe bonds) while investing abroad in higher-yielding, riskier assets like foreign equities. This mismatch allows the U.S. to sustain deficits that would otherwise be unsustainable.

The second is the "income puzzle." Despite having a negative NIIP, the U.S. consistently earns a positive income balance. In other words, the return the U.S. gets on its foreign investments is higher than the interest it pays on its domestic debt. This is the ultimate manifestation of the privilege: the U.S. pays low rates to the world to borrow money, while the world pays high rates to the U.S. for the privilege of investing in American assets. This creates a flow of income from the rest of the world to the United States, effectively taxing the global economy to subsidize the American balance sheet.

The stability of this system, however, is increasingly fragile. The exorbitant privilege relies on trust. It relies on the perception that the United States is a stable, reliable, and predictable partner. Investors flock to U.S. debt because they believe the rule of law is strong, the Federal Reserve is independent, and the political system will honor its debts. This belief is not guaranteed. Recent years have seen growing concerns about the durability of the dollar's dominance. Scholars and economists have pointed to the positive feedback effects that make it hard for other currencies to challenge the dollar. The network effects are immense; once the world settles on a currency, it is incredibly difficult to dislodge it. As long as the dollar remains the safe haven during times of international financial stress, demand for it will increase, reinforcing its status.

However, the foundations are showing signs of stress. U.S. administrations have historically maintained international alliances and safeguarded domestic institutions, such as the independence of the Federal Reserve, which make the dollar desirable. But the political landscape has shifted. Recent U.S. political instability and specific policy shifts have raised alarms. The second Donald Trump administration, for instance, has made attempts to weaken Federal Reserve independence, attack official statistical agencies, and question long-standing international commitments. These actions strike at the very core of the trust required to maintain the dollar's reserve status. If investors begin to believe that the U.S. political system is too volatile or that the institutions underpinning the currency are compromised, the demand for U.S. assets could evaporate. The moment that trust is lost, the exorbitant privilege turns into an exorbitant burden. Investors in government bonds would suffer substantial losses, and the affected government would be forced to resort to "financial repression," a strategy where interest rates are kept artificially low or capital controls are imposed to manage debt, a tactic that usually signals a loss of sovereignty.

The French challenge of the 1960s serves as a historical warning of what happens when the privilege is questioned. De Gaulle's move to exchange dollars for gold was a direct attack on the "asymmetric financial system" that France viewed as unfair. In the Bretton Woods system established in 1944, U.S. dollars were convertible to gold, creating a rigid link between the currency and a tangible asset. France argued that this system resulted in "America's exorbitant privilege" where foreigners were "seeing themselves supporting American living standards and subsidizing American multinationals." The French Navy's mission to collect the gold was a physical assertion that the U.S. could not print money forever without consequence. The resulting drain on U.S. gold reserves was a primary factor leading to the Nixon Shock. While the gold standard was abandoned, the dollar did not lose its status; it simply became a fiat currency, backed not by gold, but by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government and the sheer inertia of global usage.

Today, the debate over the future of the dollar is more intense than ever. Some scholars argue that democracies tend to retain the advantages of exorbitant privilege because investors perceive them as less risky for investment. The predictability of democratic institutions, the transparency of markets, and the rule of law create a premium on democratic currencies. However, this perception is fragile. If the U.S. is seen as retreating from its role as the guarantor of global stability, or if its domestic political turmoil threatens the integrity of its financial institutions, the premium could vanish. The Euro, the Chinese yuan, and other currencies are constantly vying for a larger share of the reserve market, but none have yet come close to displacing the dollar. The network effects are too strong, and the U.S. capital markets are still the deepest and most liquid in the world.

The consequences of a potential shift are staggering. A significant decline in the dollar's value would not just hurt the U.S.; it would trigger a global financial earthquake. Since many countries hold their reserves in U.S. dollars, any significant shift in the value of the dollar can affect global financial stability. A weaker dollar would make U.S. exports cheaper and more competitive, but it would also make imports more expensive, fueling inflation. For the rest of the world, a collapse of the dollar would mean a loss of value in their reserves and a chaotic scramble for a new reserve currency. Until then, the United States continues to enjoy its exorbitant privilege, borrowing the world's savings to fund its own ambitions, while the rest of the world waits to see if the bill will eventually come due.

The story of exorbitant privilege is the story of the modern global economy. It is a system built on a delicate balance of trust, power, and economic reality. From the Dutch Republic to the British Empire, and now to the United States, the pattern repeats: the hegemon provides the safe asset, and the world pays for it in goods, services, and cheap credit. The French Navy's journey across the Atlantic in 1965 was a moment of confrontation, a reminder that the privilege is not a right, but a loan that can be called in. As the 21st century progresses, the question is no longer whether the U.S. has this privilege, but how long it can keep it. The answer lies not in the printing presses of the Bureau of Engraving and Printing, but in the stability of American democracy, the independence of its central bank, and the ability of its leaders to maintain the trust of a global electorate that is increasingly skeptical. The privilege is exorbitant, yes, but it is not eternal. The history of finance is a graveyard of empires that thought their currency would last forever, and the United States is no exception to the laws of economic gravity.
