OpenCola (drink)

10 min read

Based on Wikipedia: OpenCola (drink)

On January 27, 2001, a digital revolution took a tangible, fizzy form in Toronto. Three men—Grad Conn, Cory Doctorow, and John Henson—did not release a new operating system, a security protocol, or a peer-to-peer file-sharing network. Instead, they released a recipe for cola. This was OpenCola version 1.0, a beverage whose ingredients and preparation instructions were placed under the GNU General Public License, the same legal framework that governed the Linux kernel and the Apache web server. In an era where the soft drink industry was defined by trade secrets guarded with the ferocity of state intelligence, the team at the now-defunct P2P company Opencola offered up the formula for one of the world's most recognizable flavors to the public domain, inviting anyone to mix, modify, and improve it.

The concept was a radical provocation. For decades, the dominant narrative of the beverage industry had been one of proprietary obscurity. The "secret formula" was not merely a list of chemicals; it was a brand asset, a mystique that justified premium pricing and corporate invincibility. Coca-Cola, the industry titan, treated its recipe as a closely guarded treasure, reportedly locked in a vault and known only to a select few executives. To suggest that this alchemy could be replicated in a home kitchen, or a local garage, by anyone with access to gum arabic and cassia oil was to challenge the very foundations of modern branding. Yet, the team at Opencola did not view this as a gimmick. They saw it as a pedagogical tool, a liquid demonstration of the open-source philosophy that was then reshaping the software world. They wanted to show that the principles of free software—transparency, collaboration, and the rejection of artificial scarcity—could apply to the physical world, to the very things we consume.

The recipe itself is a study in specificity, a stark contrast to the vague "natural and artificial flavors" that populate the labels of mass-market sodas. The flavoring formula, the heart of the drink, requires a precise balance of oils and extracts. It calls for 10.0 grams of food-grade gum arabic to provide the necessary viscosity, mixed with 3.5 mL of orange oil and 3.0 mL of lime oil. The complexity deepens with the addition of 2.75 mL of cassia oil, a spice closely related to cinnamon, followed by 1.25 mL of lemon oil, 1.0 mL of nutmeg oil, and a delicate touch of 0.25 mL each of coriander, neroli, and lavender oils. This is not a simple syrup of sugar and water; it is a botanical composition. To this aromatic base, the recipe adds 2.36 kg of plain granulated white table sugar and 2.28 liters of water, along with 30.0 mL of caramel color to achieve the signature dark hue. The acidity is calibrated with 3.50 teaspoons (18 mL) of 75% phosphoric acid or citric acid, and the caffeine content is listed as optional, measured at 0.50 teaspoons (3 mL). The final step involves mixing this concentrate and diluting it five parts water to one part syrup, ideally with sodium-free soda water, to yield approximately 24 liters of the finished product.

But the ambition of the project did not stop at the flavoring. The creators of OpenCola understood that true open-source methodology requires the entire supply chain to be transparent. If the syrup were open, but the carbonation came from a commercial canister of carbon dioxide produced by a multinational chemical giant, the project would remain partially proprietary. Therefore, the full documentation included instructions for making soda water from scratch using basic ingredients like yeast and sugar. This ensured that the entire process, from the raw materials to the final fizz, could be replicated without reliance on any closed-source commercial product. It was a totalizing vision of independence, a declaration that the consumer need not be a passive recipient of corporate production but an active participant in the creation of their own sustenance.

The launch of OpenCola was timed to coincide with the burgeoning optimism of the dot-com era, a period when the internet was widely seen as a great equalizer. The company, Opencola, was a peer-to-peer software firm based in Toronto, founded with the aim of creating decentralized communication tools. The drink was never intended to be a competitor to Coca-Cola or Pepsi in the traditional sense of market share. It was a marketing vehicle, a tangible artifact designed to explain the abstract concepts of free and open-source software to a lay audience. As Laird Brown, the company's senior strategist, later noted, the project's unexpected success was fueled by a "widespread mistrust of big corporations and the proprietary nature of almost everything." In the early 2000s, the public was beginning to question the dominance of giants like Microsoft and the pharmaceutical industry. OpenCola tapped into this sentiment, offering a beverage that felt like an act of resistance.

The response was immediate and overwhelming. Within months, the recipe had been downloaded thousands of times. People began brewing the drink in their kitchens, in community centers, and in small-scale production facilities. The open nature of the license meant that the recipe was not static; it evolved. Users submitted modifications, tweaks to the acidity, adjustments to the spice profile, and variations in the sweetener. The drink took on a life of its own, transcending its original purpose as a software promotional tool. By the time the company ceased operations in 2003, OpenCola had sold 150,000 cans. This was a staggering figure for a beverage that had no distribution network, no marketing budget, and no physical product until the consumer or a third party created it. The irony was palpable: the company was better known for the drink it made to sell its software than for the software itself.

The trajectory of OpenCola serves as a unique case study in the intersection of culture, technology, and commerce. It demonstrated that the "open-source" model was not limited to code. The philosophy could be applied to food, to culture, and to the very mechanics of production. The success of the drink highlighted a deep-seated desire for transparency. In a world increasingly dominated by complex, opaque supply chains, the idea of knowing exactly what was in your drink, and having the legal right to change it, was profoundly appealing. It was a rejection of the "black box" consumer experience. When you bought a can of Coke, you were buying a product of a process you could not see, controlled by a corporation you could not influence. When you made OpenCola, you were engaging in a collaborative act with a global community of makers.

However, the story of OpenCola is also one of the limitations of the open-source model when applied to the physical world. While the software could be copied and distributed at near-zero marginal cost, the production of a beverage required capital, equipment, and logistical coordination. The "OpenCola" that reached the 150,000 consumers was likely produced in small batches by enthusiasts or local micro-brewers, rather than in a massive industrial facility. The scale of production was inherently limited by the nature of the model. Unlike software, where a single line of code can be replicated infinitely without degradation, physical goods require resources. The project succeeded in creating a cult following and a cultural artifact, but it did not displace the giants of the industry. The trade secrets of the major soda companies remained intact, protected by patents, trademarks, and the sheer inertia of the market.

The legacy of OpenCola is visible in the subsequent emergence of other open-source projects. The success of the cola inspired the creation of "Free Beer," a Danish project that applied the same principles to brewing, and "Cube Cola," a UK-based initiative. These projects formed a loose network of "open-source beverages," all united by the belief that the recipes for our daily consumables should be public knowledge. They challenged the notion that flavor is the exclusive property of a corporation. The movement also found a digital home on platforms like GitHub, where the original recipe and its variations were preserved and continued to be refined. The defunct OpenSoda.org served as a continuation of this work, archiving the methodologies and recipes that had emerged from the initial experiment.

The story of OpenCola is inextricably linked to the careers of its founders. Cory Doctorow, in particular, went on to become a prominent author and activist, known for his work on digital rights and the future of technology. His involvement in OpenCola was a precursor to his later advocacy for a more open and equitable digital world. The project was a practical application of the theories he would later articulate in his novels and non-fiction works. It was a demonstration that the principles of freedom and sharing were not just abstract ideals but could be woven into the fabric of everyday life. The drink was a metaphor for the internet itself: a network of nodes, each contributing to the whole, sharing resources, and evolving through collaboration.

In retrospect, the timing of the launch was crucial. 2001 was a year of transition. The dot-com bubble had burst, but the spirit of the early internet remained. The events of September 11 later that year would shift the global focus toward security and surveillance, making the themes of openness and decentralization even more poignant. OpenCola arrived just before the world turned inward, offering a moment of radical generosity and trust. It was a brief period when the idea of a "gift economy" in the physical world seemed plausible. The 150,000 cans sold were not just a measure of commercial success; they were a measure of the number of people who believed in the possibility of a different kind of world.

The recipe remains a testament to the complexity and beauty of the open-source ethos. It is a document that invites engagement, not just consumption. It asks the reader to participate, to question, to modify. It is a reminder that the things we consume are not fixed entities but are subject to our influence. The ingredients are simple, but the implications are vast. By making the recipe for cola open, the creators of OpenCola were making a statement about the nature of ownership, the role of the consumer, and the potential of human collaboration. They showed that even in the most commercialized sectors of the economy, there is room for the spirit of the commons.

The closure of the Opencola company in 2003 did not mark the end of the drink. The recipe lived on, scattered across the internet, printed on home pages, and shared in community forums. The drink became a symbol of a specific moment in history, a moment when the internet promised to democratize not just information, but also the physical world. It was a time when the boundaries between the digital and the analog seemed fluid, and when the idea of a "free" cola was not just a marketing slogan but a reality. The story of OpenCola is a reminder that the most powerful ideas are often the simplest: share what you know, trust your community, and build something together. In a world that often feels divided and controlled, the legacy of OpenCola offers a glimpse of a different possibility, one where the recipe for our future is open for all to read, and all to change.
