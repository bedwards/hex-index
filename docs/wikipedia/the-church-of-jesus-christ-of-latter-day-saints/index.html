The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints

Based on Wikipedia: The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints

On June 27, 1844, the mob that stormed the jail in Carthage, Illinois, did not just kill two men; they extinguished the life of Joseph Smith, the founder of a movement that would come to encompass 17.5 million souls, and his brother Hyrum, the designated successor. Their deaths were not a distant historical footnote but a visceral rupture that tore a young religious community apart, forcing a choice between martyrdom and migration that would define the trajectory of a faith for nearly two centuries. This is the story of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, a denomination that emerged from the fiery fervor of the Second Great Awakening in western New York to become a global powerhouse, navigating a history as turbulent as it is transformative.

To understand the scale of the organization today, one must look beyond the modern skyline of Salt Lake City, Utah, its current headquarters. As of 2024, the church claims more than 17.5 million members worldwide, with a significant concentration of over 6.8 million living in the United States. The physical footprint of this faith is staggering: more than 200 dedicated temples stand as sacred anchors in communities across the globe, served by a volunteer force of over 109,000 missionaries who knock on doors from the streets of São Paulo to the suburbs of London. Yet, the church's identity remains a complex tapestry of restorationist theology and nontrinitarian doctrine, a faith that insists on the literal restoration of ancient Christianity while standing distinctly apart from the Catholic, Orthodox, and mainline Protestant traditions that often view it as an outlier.

The theological engine of the church runs on an open canon, a concept that fundamentally alters the relationship between the believer and the divine. Unlike denominations that close their scriptural books centuries ago, the Latter-day Saint tradition recognizes four standard works: the Holy Bible, the Book of Mormon, the Doctrine and Covenants, and the Pearl of Great Price. While the Bible is revered, the majority of the church's distinct canon consists of texts believed to be modern revelations given to Joseph Smith. These include the Book of Mormon, described by Smith as a chronicle of ancient prophets in the Americas translated from golden plates, and the Doctrine and Covenants, which contains direct revelations on church governance and doctrine. For the faithful, these are not merely historical documents but living words, with Smith's writings often described as lost parts of the Bible or new dispensations of truth. This belief in continuing revelation allows the church to adapt and evolve, yet it also anchors its members in a strict code of conduct. Adherents adhere to laws of sexual purity, health (including the abstention from coffee, tea, alcohol, and tobacco), fasting, and Sabbath observance. The financial backbone of the organization is the tithe, a requirement for members to contribute ten percent of their income, a practice that funds the church's massive humanitarian and missionary operations.

The path from a small gathering in upstate New York to a global institution was paved with violence, displacement, and the relentless drive of a charismatic leader. Joseph Smith formally organized the Church of Christ on April 6, 1830, in western New York. The initial converts were drawn by the promise of the Book of Mormon and the vision of Smith, who claimed that at age 14, two heavenly personages had appeared to him, a vision now regarded by the church as the most significant event in human history since the resurrection of Jesus. Smith's ambition was grand: to establish the New Jerusalem, or Zion, in North America. In 1831, the church moved its headquarters to Kirtland, Ohio, while simultaneously establishing an outpost in Jackson County, Missouri, where Smith intended to build the city of Zion.

But the reception of these newcomers was not one of welcome. In 1833, tensions in Missouri boiled over, and settlers violently expelled the Latter-day Saints from Jackson County. The church attempted to recover the land through a paramilitary expedition, a desperate and costly failure that only deepened the rift. Despite this, the church flourished briefly in Kirtland, where Smith published new revelations and the community constructed the Kirtland Temple. The dedication of the temple was a moment of profound spiritual intensity, described by participants as a day of Pentecost. However, the era ended in disaster. A financial scandal in 1838, involving a failed bank and widespread defections, shattered the Kirtland community. Smith regrouped with the remaining faithful in Far West, Missouri, but the cycle of persecution began anew.

The situation in Missouri escalated into a state-sanctioned campaign of terror. Believing the Latter-day Saints to be an insurrection, the Missouri governor issued an extermination order, commanding that they be "exterminated or driven from the State." The human cost of this decree was immense. Families were driven from their homes in the dead of winter, their property seized, and their lives threatened. Hundreds were imprisoned, tortured, and killed. This was not a war of armies but a war of attrition against civilians, a systematic effort to erase a community from the map. In 1839, the survivors found refuge in the swamplands along the Mississippi River in Illinois, where they founded Nauvoo.

Nauvoo became a miracle of engineering and community spirit. The swamp was drained, and a city rose from the mud, growing rapidly as missionaries sent to Europe and elsewhere returned with waves of new converts. It was here, in the relative safety of Illinois, that Smith introduced the church to its most controversial doctrines. He began practicing and teaching polygamy to his closest associates, a radical departure from Christian norms that would haunt the church for decades. He also introduced the endowment ceremony, a ritual he claimed was revealed by God to allow righteous people to become gods in the afterlife, and a secular institution to govern the Millennial kingdom. Smith's full account of his First Vision was also formalized during this time, cementing the theological foundation of the faith.

The peace of Nauvoo was short-lived. Smith's growing political power, his control over the city's militia, and the introduction of polygamy made him a target. On June 27, 1844, Smith and his brother Hyrum were arrested on charges of treason and taken to the jail in Carthage. A mob, composed of men from the surrounding counties, stormed the jail. The violence was swift and brutal. Hyrum was shot first, falling dead instantly. Joseph was shot multiple times, his body riddled with bullets as he fell from the window, a martyr to the very cause he had built.

The deaths of the Smith brothers created an immediate succession crisis. With no clear heir designated to lead the entire movement, the church fractured. The majority of the members, however, rallied behind Brigham Young, the president of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles. Young, a man of iron will and organizational genius, argued that the priesthood authority had been passed to the Twelve, and he assumed leadership. Other splinter groups followed different leaders, such as James Strang or Sidney Rigdon, and these groups, collectively known as the Latter Day Saint movement, continue to exist today. The largest of these, the Community of Christ, is based in Independence, Missouri, and retains the Book of Mormon and some of Smith's revelations but diverges significantly on doctrine and the concept of a living prophet.

For Young and his followers, the path forward was westward. After two years of escalating conflict with Illinois residents, who now sought to drive them out of the state as well, Young made a fateful decision. He would lead his people to a place where they could practice their religion without interference. In 1846, the Mormon pioneers began their exodus, a journey that would become the stuff of legend and tragedy. Thousands of families, many of them recent immigrants from Britain and Scandinavia, left their homes with little more than a handcart or a covered wagon. The journey was grueling. Children died of dysentery, the elderly perished from exposure, and the weak were often left behind.

In 1847, Young and the vanguard company arrived in the Salt Lake Valley, a desolate basin that was then part of the lands of the Ute, Goshute, and Shoshone nations and claimed by Mexico until 1848. Young declared, "This is the place," and the work of colonization began. Between 1847 and 1869, around 80,000 settlers arrived in the region, creating a vast network of settlements known as the Mormon Corridor. They built irrigation canals to tame the desert, established farms, and constructed a society that was both theocratic and communal. Young governed both the church and the state, wielding absolute authority in a land that was not yet a U.S. territory.

It was during this period of settlement that the church publicly announced the practice of plural marriage in 1852, a decision that would isolate them from the rest of the nation and lead to decades of federal persecution. The U.S. government viewed polygamy as a threat to the republican ideal of monogamy and the family unit, leading to the passage of the Morrill Anti-Bigamy Act in 1862 and the Edmunds-Tucker Act in 1887, which disincorporated the church and confiscated its assets. The practice of polygamy, which had been a secret doctrine for years, became a public flashpoint. It was not until 1890, under the leadership of Wilford Woodruff, that the church issued the Manifesto, officially curtailing the practice. The ban was solidified in 1904, ending a chapter of history that had defined the church's early struggles.

Despite the end of polygamy, the church continued to grow, expanding beyond the Intermountain West to become a national and international organization. The missionary program, which had begun in earnest in the 1840s, expanded to include the Sandwich Islands (Hawaii), India, Chile, Australia, China, and South Africa. The church's structure evolved into a hierarchical system descending from areas to stakes and wards, with a volunteer clergy at the local level. Men are ordained to the priesthood if they live by the church's standards, while women, though not ordained to the priesthood, occupy significant leadership roles in auxiliary organizations.

Today, the church faces a new set of challenges. Modern criticism focuses on the church's historical claims, particularly regarding the origins of the Book of Mormon and the practice of polygamy. There are ongoing disputes over the treatment of minorities, including the church's historical exclusion of Black men from the priesthood until 1978 and its stance on LGBTQ+ issues. The church's finances, a subject of intense scrutiny, remain opaque to the public, with critics questioning the allocation of the billions of dollars collected in tithes.

Yet, for the 17.5 million members who call themselves Latter-day Saints, the church remains a beacon of faith and community. They believe that the president of the church is a modern-day "prophet, seer, and revelator," a role that allows the church to adapt to changing times while maintaining its core doctrines. The belief in Jesus Christ and his substitutionary atonement remains central, even as the church's theology diverges from mainstream Christianity. The ordinances of baptism, endowment, and celestial marriage are seen as essential steps toward exaltation, a state of becoming like God.

The story of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints is one of resilience in the face of unimaginable hardship. From the violent expulsion from Missouri to the perilous journey across the plains, from the secrecy of polygamy to the global reach of the modern church, the faith has survived by the sheer determination of its members. It is a story of a people who believed they were building Zion, a city of the righteous, and who paid a heavy price for that belief. The human cost of that journey is etched into the history of the American West, in the names of the pioneers who died on the trail and the families who lost their homes. It is a history that cannot be ignored, a testament to the power of faith and the enduring cost of persecution.

As the church moves forward into the 21st century, it stands at a crossroads. The questions of its past—its claims of divine revelation, its treatment of minorities, its financial practices—continue to challenge both its members and its critics. But for the millions who gather in wards and stakes around the world, the answer remains the same: the church is the restored kingdom of God on earth, led by a prophet who speaks for Christ. Whether one views this as a miraculous truth or a compelling fiction, the impact of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints on the world is undeniable. It is a faith built on the ashes of a mob's violence, nurtured in the swamps of Illinois, and transplanted to the desert of Utah, where it has blossomed into a global institution that continues to shape the spiritual landscape of the modern world.

The legacy of Joseph Smith is not just in the books he wrote or the revelations he claimed, but in the lives of the millions who followed him. It is in the missionaries who serve in foreign lands, the families who gather for Sunday worship, and the volunteers who provide humanitarian aid. It is a legacy of faith that has weathered the storms of history and emerged, battered but unbroken.
