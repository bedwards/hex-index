Group of 77

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Group of 77

On June 15, 1964, in the bustling halls of the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development in Geneva, a historic line was drawn in the sand of international diplomacy. Seventy-seven nations, representing the vast majority of the world's population but a fraction of its economic power, signed the "Joint Declaration of the Seventy-Seven Countries." This was not merely a diplomatic formality; it was the birth of a counterweight to the established order, a coalition designed to dismantle the monopoly of the wealthy North over global economic rules. The Group of 77, or G77, emerged from the ashes of colonialism with a singular, defiant mission: to promote the collective economic interests of the developing world and forge a unified voice in a United Nations system that had long been dominated by the superpowers of the Cold War.

Decades later, the number "77" has become a misnomer, a historical artifact that no longer reflects the scale of the movement. The coalition has swelled from its founding roster to include 134 member countries, encompassing nearly the entirety of the Global South. Yet, the spirit of that original declaration in Geneva remains the engine of the organization. It is a testament to the enduring power of South-South cooperation, an ideology that posits that developing nations share more in common with each other than with the industrialized giants that often dictate their fate. Today, with Iraq holding the chairmanship for 2025, succeeding Uganda, the G77 stands as the largest intergovernmental organization of developing countries at the United Nations, a behemoth that continues to shape the agenda of global trade, climate, and development.

The Architecture of Resistance

To understand the G77, one must understand the world it was born into. The early 1960s were a period of profound geopolitical flux. As empires crumbled and new nations gained independence, they found themselves entering a global economy rigged against them. The rules of trade, finance, and development were written in Washington, London, and Paris, often ignoring the realities of nations struggling with poverty and the legacy of exploitation. The G77 was the answer to this asymmetry. It was conceived not as a political bloc in the traditional sense, but as a strategic alliance to enhance negotiating capacity.

The group's institutional roots were cemented at its first major meeting in Algiers in 1967. It was here that the Charter of Algiers was adopted, transforming the loose coalition of signatories into a permanent body with a structured mandate. The intellectual architect behind this transition was Raúl Prebisch, a towering figure in development economics who had previously led the United Nations Economic Commission for Latin America (ECLA). Prebisch understood that developing nations could not succeed by simply playing by the rules set by the rich; they needed to rewrite the rules. Under his leadership, the G77 began to build the permanent institutional structures that would allow it to operate year-round, rather than just during sporadic UN conferences.

The organization quickly realized that to be effective, it needed a presence wherever the levers of global power were pulled. Thus, chapters of the G77 were established in the key diplomatic hubs of the world. There are now chapters in Geneva at the UN headquarters, in Rome at the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), in Vienna at the UN Industrial Development Organization (UNIDO), in Paris at UNESCO, and in Nairobi at the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP). Furthermore, recognizing that the heart of the global financial system beats in Washington, D.C., the group established the Group of 24 (G-24). This specialized chapter, founded in 1971, coordinates the positions of developing countries on international monetary and development finance issues, ensuring their interests are represented in negotiations at the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank. Every member of the G-24 is also a member of the G77, creating a seamless bridge between political advocacy at the UN and technical negotiation in the financial capitals.

The Ideology of the Global South

The G77 is often described as a coalition of convenience, but its longevity suggests a deeper, ideological cohesion. At its core lies a shared South-South ideology, a belief that the development challenges facing the Global South are systemic and require collective solutions. This ideology has driven the group to champion several major global causes throughout its history. The G77 was instrumental in forming a common stance against the apartheid regime in South Africa, using its collective weight to isolate the regime diplomatically and economically. It has also been a vocal proponent of global disarmament, arguing that the resources wasted on arms races should be redirected toward human development.

Perhaps the most significant ideological contribution of the G77 was its role in advocating for the New International Economic Order (NIEO). In the 1970s, the group pushed for a radical restructuring of the global economy, demanding fairer trade terms, technology transfer, and greater control over natural resources for developing nations. While the NIEO as a comprehensive framework never fully materialized in its original form, the pressure exerted by the G77 forced the international community to acknowledge the disparities in the global system and led to significant reforms in trade and development policies.

However, the group is not without its complexities. The G77 lists China as one of its members, a relationship that is unique and somewhat ambiguous. While the Chinese government provides consistent political support to the G77 and has made financial contributions to the group since 1994, it does not consider itself a formal member. Beijing views itself as a developing nation and a champion of the Global South, but it maintains a distinct diplomatic identity. As a result, official statements from the group are often delivered in the name of "The Group of 77 and China" or "G77+China." This nuance reflects the delicate balance China strikes between leading the developing world and maintaining its own sovereign agenda, a dynamic that has become increasingly central to the group's influence on the world stage.

The Climate Crucible

No issue has tested the G77's unity and relevance in recent decades quite like climate change. The group's position on environmental matters is rooted in a clear historical narrative: developed countries bear the historical responsibility for greenhouse gas emissions. The G77 points to the stark disparity in per capita emissions between the industrialized North and the developing South, arguing that the nations that fueled their growth on fossil fuels should not expect developing nations to sacrifice their own economic potential to solve a crisis they did not create.

Consequently, the G77 has often resisted binding commitments to reduce emissions that could hinder its members' development. From the group's perspective, environmental initiatives are secondary to the more urgent imperatives of economic development and poverty eradication. This stance has drawn sharp criticism from the wealthier nations and environmental advocates, who accuse the G77 of blocking progress on pro-environmental initiatives. Critics argue that the group's refusal to accept binding targets undermines global efforts to limit warming to 1.5 or 2 degrees Celsius.

The G77, however, retorts with a counter-critique that cuts to the heart of global inequality. At the 1992 United Nations Conference on Environment and Development in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, the group highlighted the insufficient attention paid by wealthy nations to poverty eradication. They argued that it is hypocritical to demand that nations struggling to feed their populations cut emissions when the developed world has failed to provide the promised financial and technological support for sustainable development. The group maintains that climate justice is inextricably linked to economic justice; without addressing the poverty gap, environmental goals are unattainable. This friction between the urgent need for decarbonization and the undeniable right to development remains the defining tension of the G77's environmental diplomacy.

The Fluidity of Membership

The history of the G77 is also a history of the shifting tides of global development. The list of its members is not static; it is a living document that reflects the rise and fall of nations, the changing definitions of "developed" and "developing," and the geopolitical realignments of the 21st century. As of 2023, the group comprises all UN member states, plus the observer State of Palestine, with a specific and revealing set of exclusions.

The criteria for exclusion are telling. The group excludes members of the Council of Europe (with the exception of Azerbaijan), members of the Commonwealth of Independent States Free Trade Area (except for Tajikistan), and members of the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), with the exception of its four Latin American members. It also excludes two microstates in Oceania: Palau and Tuvalu. These exclusions highlight the group's self-definition as a coalition of the non-OECD, non-European, and non-wealthy. Once a country ascends to the ranks of the developed world, it often graduates from the G77.

Mexico serves as a prime example of this dynamic. A founding member of the G77, Mexico left the group in 1994 after joining the OECD, signaling its transition to a developed economy in the eyes of the international community. Similarly, South Korea, another founding member, departed in 1996 upon its accession to the OECD. New Zealand, which had signed the original "Joint Declaration of the Developing Countries" in October 1963, pulled out before the G77 was even formally established in 1964, eventually joining the OECD in 1973. These departures are not merely bureaucratic; they represent a profound shift in national identity and economic status.

The history of the group also includes the complexities of decolonization and state succession. Yugoslavia was a founding member and even presided over the group from 1985 to 1986. However, as the country fractured in the late 1990s, its status became untenable. By the late 1990s, it was noted that Yugoslavia "cannot participate in the activities of G77," and it was finally removed from the list in late 2003. Of its successor states, Bosnia and Herzegovina was the only one listed as a member on the official website in 2007, but it too was removed in February 2020. Cyprus, a founding member, left the list after acceding to the European Union in 2004, as did Malta, which had joined in 1976. The case of Vietnam is particularly intricate: South Vietnam was a founding member, while North Vietnam never joined separately; later, South Yemen joined separately from North Yemen between 1967 and 1975. These historical footnotes reveal the G77's evolution from a Cold War alliance to a modern coalition that must navigate the messy realities of statehood and sovereignty.

There are also curious anomalies in the group's geographical logic. Romania was classed as a Latin American country for the purposes of the G77, having joined in 1976. This classification was a bureaucratic necessity; the G77 was divided into geographical regions, and since there was technically no "European" area for developing nations (as Europe was considered the domain of the developed world), Romania was placed under the umbrella of Latin America. Romania left the group following its accession to the European Union, just as its European neighbors did. Palau, which joined the Group in 2002, withdrew in 2004, deciding that it could best pursue its environmental interests through the Alliance of Small Island States. These shifts demonstrate the pragmatism of the G77, a willingness to adapt its internal structures to the realities of its members' changing circumstances.

The Future of the Coalition

As the G77 enters its second half-century, it faces a world that is both more interconnected and more fractured than ever before. The rise of China as a global superpower has altered the dynamics within the group, providing a powerful ally but also raising questions about the future of the "developing world" label. The group's ability to maintain a unified front on issues like climate change, trade, and debt relief will be tested by the diverging interests of its 134 members. The gap between the least developed countries and the emerging giants within the group is widening, creating potential fissures in the coalition's solidarity.

Yet, the G77 remains the primary vehicle for the Global South to assert its voice. In an era where the North-South divide is as relevant as ever, the group's mission to promote collective economic interests and enhance joint negotiating capacity is more critical than it was in 1964. The group's success is measured not just in the treaties it signs or the resolutions it passes, but in its ability to force the international community to confront the inequalities that plague the global system. From the Charter of Algiers to the climate summits of the 2020s, the G77 has been the relentless advocate for a more just world order.

The story of the G77 is the story of the developing world's struggle for agency. It is a story of 77 nations coming together to say "enough" to a system that marginalized them, and 134 nations today continuing that legacy. Whether through the nuanced diplomacy of the G77+China formula or the steadfast resistance to binding emissions targets, the group remains a potent force. It reminds the world that development is not a one-way street, that history matters, and that the future of the planet cannot be written without the consent of the majority of its people. As Iraq takes the helm in 2025, the G77 stands ready to face the challenges of a new era, carrying the torch of the New International Economic Order into the unknown future.
