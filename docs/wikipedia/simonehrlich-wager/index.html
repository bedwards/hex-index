Simon–Ehrlich wager

16 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Simon–Ehrlich wager

On September 29, 1990, a plain white envelope arrived at the home of economist Julian Simon in the United States. Inside was a check for $576.07. It was not a birthday gift, nor a dividend from a stock portfolio. It was the settlement of a wager that had captivated the intellectual world for a decade. The check came from Paul Ehrlich, the celebrated biologist and author of The Population Bomb, and it represented a fundamental admission that his dire predictions about resource scarcity had been wrong. The money, adjusted for inflation, was worth roughly $1,420 in the currency of 2025, but the symbolic value of the transaction was incalculable. It marked the end of one of the most famous intellectual duels of the late 20th century, a contest that pitted the grim Malthusian fear of overpopulation against a radical optimism about human ingenuity.

The origins of this clash can be traced back to a specific moment of rhetorical escalation in 1980. Julian Simon, a professor at the University of Maryland, was reading the pages of Social Science Quarterly when he encountered a statement from Ehrlich that he found not only scientifically dubious but intellectually reckless. Ehrlich had written, "If I were a gambler, I would take even money that England will not exist in the year 2000." To Simon, a man who believed that human beings were the "ultimate resource" capable of solving their own problems, this was the height of pessimism. He did not simply write a rebutting op-ed. He issued a challenge.

Simon proposed a wager that was as simple as it was profound. He offered to stake $10,000 on his belief that the cost of non-government-controlled raw materials would not rise in the long run. He told Ehrlich to choose any raw material he wanted and any date more than a year away. If the inflation-adjusted price of that material increased over that period, Simon would pay Ehrlich. If the price decreased, Ehrlich would pay Simon. It was a direct invitation to put money where one's mouth was, moving the debate from the realm of abstract academic theory to the cold, hard reality of the marketplace.

Ehrlich, confident in his thesis that the earth's resources were finite and rapidly depleting, accepted the challenge. He did not choose a single commodity. Instead, he selected a basket of five metals that he believed were critical to modern civilization and, crucially, prone to scarcity. He chose copper, chromium, nickel, tin, and tungsten. These were not random picks; they were the backbone of industrialization, essential for everything from electrical wiring to high-strength alloys. Ehrlich and his colleagues calculated that these metals would undergo massive price increases as the population swelled and the resources were stripped from the earth.

The terms were formalized on September 29, 1980. The payoff date was set exactly ten years later, on September 29, 1990. The mechanics were precise: on paper, they bought $200 worth of each of the five metals at the market prices of that day, creating a total portfolio value of $1,000. The bet hinged on the inflation-adjusted price of this basket. If the total value rose above $1,000 by 1990, Simon would pay the difference to Ehrlich. If the value fell below $1,000, Ehrlich would pay Simon.

The decade that followed was not a quiet one. The world changed dramatically. Between 1980 and 1990, the global population grew by more than 800 million people. This was the largest increase in population in a single decade in the history of humanity. From the perspective of the Malthusian argument, this was the perfect storm. More people meant more mouths to feed, more hands to work, and more demand for the very metals Ehrlich had selected. Logic dictated that with demand skyrocketing and supply finite, prices should have soared. The stage seemed set for Ehrlich to collect a fortune.

Yet, the market, that great and often unforgiving arbiter of truth, told a different story. By September 1990, the price of every single metal in Ehrlich's basket had fallen. The inflation-adjusted cost of these resources had plummeted. Chromium, which had sold for $3.90 a pound in 1980 (a sum equivalent to $15.24 in 2025 dollars), was trading at $3.70 a pound in 1990 (equivalent to $9.12). Tin, a metal that had commanded $8.72 a pound in 1980 (equivalent to $34.07 in 2025), had crashed to $3.88 (equivalent to $9.56). The declines were not marginal; they were structural. Tin and tungsten, in particular, had lost more than half their real value over the decade.

When the dust settled, the calculation was clear. The basket of metals, which had been worth $1,000 in 1980, was worth significantly less in real terms in 1990. Julian Simon had won. The mechanism of human innovation had outpaced the mechanism of consumption. New methods of extraction were discovered, recycling technologies improved, and substitution became viable. The market found ways to stretch the finite. On October 1990, Paul Ehrlich mailed the check to Simon, acknowledging the loss. It was a moment of high drama in the history of economics and environmentalism, a rare instance where a philosophical debate was settled by a ledger.

The Anatomy of the Debate

To understand why this wager was so significant, one must understand the intellectual landscape of the time. In 1968, Paul Ehrlich had published The Population Bomb, a book that became a cultural phenomenon. It argued that mankind was facing a demographic catastrophe. Ehrlich painted a picture of a world where population growth was quickly outstripping the supply of food and resources, leading to mass starvation and societal collapse. He was a voice of warning, urging immediate and drastic action to curb population growth. His views were supported by a growing consensus that the Earth was a closed system with hard limits.

Julian Simon was the antithesis of this worldview. A highly skeptical economist, he viewed human beings not as a burden on the planet, but as the solution to it. His core thesis was that the more people there are, the more minds there are to invent new ways to produce food, find energy, and use resources efficiently. He argued that resources were not finite in a practical sense because human knowledge was the only true finite limit, and that knowledge was expanding. To Simon, the rising price of a resource was a signal that triggered innovation, leading to cheaper alternatives or more efficient use, ultimately driving the price down.

The wager was the ultimate test of these competing theories. Ehrlich believed in the physical constraints of the planet. Simon believed in the boundless potential of the human mind. By betting on the price of metals, they were betting on which force would dominate: the scarcity of matter or the creativity of man.

The Aftermath and the Counter-Argument

The immediate aftermath of the bet was a flurry of analysis and commentary. Ehrlich did not immediately concede that his entire worldview was wrong. In his book Betrayal of Science and Reason, he wrote that Simon had asserted "that humanity would never run out of anything." Ehrlich clarified that he and his fellow scientists viewed renewable resources as more important indicators of the state of the planet than metals. He maintained that he had agreed to the bet only to engage with Simon, and that the choice of metals was a tactical decision rather than a holistic view of environmental health.

Simon, for his part, was not content to rest on his victory. He offered to raise the wager to $20,000 and to use any resources at any time that Ehrlich preferred. He was confident that the trend would hold. But Ehrlich, perhaps sensing the difficulty of winning a bet on a specific commodity basket, pivoted. He countered with a challenge to bet on a different kind of trend: the future of the climate. He suggested they bet that temperatures would increase in the future. The two men were unable to reach an agreement on the terms of this second wager before Simon died in 1998, leaving the climate debate to be fought in other arenas.

The debate did not end with the check. The scientific community began to scrutinize the bet more closely, looking for nuances that the headline "Simon Wins" might have obscured. Some observers argued that Ehrlich could have won if the bet had been for a different period. Ehrlich himself pointed out that the five metals in question had increased in price between the years 1950 and 1975. The 1980 to 1990 period, he suggested, was an anomaly.

Asset manager Jeremy Grantham took this analysis further. He calculated that if the Simon-Ehrlich wager had been extended to a longer period, specifically from 1980 to 2011, the outcome would have been different. In that extended timeframe, Simon would have lost on four of the five metals. This suggested that the victory might have been more about timing than a fundamental shift in the laws of economics.

However, other economists pushed back against the idea that Simon was merely lucky. Economist Mark J. Perry examined an even longer period, from 1934 to 2013. He analyzed the inflation-adjusted price of the Dow Jones-AIG Commodities Index and found an "overall significant downward trend." Perry concluded that Simon was "more right than lucky." The long-term data suggested that the human drive to innovate consistently outpaces the depletion of resources over centuries, even if there are short-term fluctuations.

A more rigorous statistical analysis was conducted by Kiel et al. in 2010. They claimed that when considering all possible time periods from 1900 to 2008, Simon would have won the bet in only 38.4% of all cases. Conversely, Ehrlich would not only have won more often but also by a wide margin. They viewed the specific period from 1980 to 1990 as one of the 15 worst periods over the entire study period for resource prices. Their conclusion was provocative: the bet did not prove that there is no such thing as a shortage of raw materials. Rather, it proved that in betting, it is better to have luck on your side than expertise. They posited that the public conclusion—that resources are infinite—was a misinterpretation of a statistical fluke.

The Second Challenge: A Different Kind of Bet

Despite the disagreement over the interpretation of the first bet, the intellectual rivalry continued. Simon, eager to prove his point again, was approached by Ehrlich and climatologist Stephen Schneider. They proposed a new wager, but this time the terms were vastly different. Instead of betting on a single basket of metals, they challenged Simon to bet on 15 current trends. They would bet $1,000 that each of these trends would get worse over a ten-year future period, specifically from 1994 to 2004.

The list of 15 bets was comprehensive, covering climate, agriculture, biodiversity, and human health. The three years 2002–2004 would, on average, be warmer than 1992–1994. There would be more carbon dioxide in the atmosphere in 2004 than in 1994. There would be more nitrous oxide, more ozone in the lower atmosphere, and significantly greater emissions of sulfur dioxide in Asia. The list continued: less fertile cropland per person, less agricultural soil per person, less rice and wheat grown per person, and less firewood available per person in developing nations. It included the remaining area of virgin tropical moist forests, the oceanic fishery harvest per person, the number of plant and animal species still extant, and the number of people dying of AIDS. It even included a biological trend: the decline in sperm cell counts of human males and the increase in reproductive disorders. Finally, they bet that the gap in wealth between the richest 10% and the poorest 10% of humanity would be greater in 2004 than in 1994.

The scope of this second challenge was staggering. It moved beyond simple resource scarcity to encompass the complex web of environmental and social degradation that Ehrlich and Schneider feared. It was a bet on the future of the entire planet.

Simon declined the offer. He did not do so out of fear of losing, but because he believed the terms were fundamentally flawed. He explained his refusal with a striking analogy. He characterized their offer as follows: "Let me predict, and this is for real, that the average performances in the next Olympics will be better than those in the last Olympics. On average, the performances have gotten better, Olympics to Olympics, for a variety of reasons."

Simon argued that Ehrlich and Schneider were not betting on the performance itself, but on the conditions surrounding it. "What Ehrlich and others say is that they don't want to bet on athletic performances, they want to bet on the conditions of the track, or the weather, or the officials, or any other such indirect measure." In Simon's view, the quality of human life was the performance, and the environment was just the track. He believed that even if the track conditions deteriorated (more pollution, less forest), the athletes (humans) would find ways to perform better (higher living standards, better health) because of their ingenuity.

The Ultimate Resource

The core of Simon's philosophy was laid out in his 1981 book, The Ultimate Resource. In it, he noted that not all decreases in resources or increases in unwanted effects correspond to overall decreases in human well-being. He introduced the concept of an "optimal level of pollution." This was a controversial idea that accepted that some increases in certain kinds of pollution might be necessary to increase overall well-being. He acknowledged that any increase in pollution was a cost that must be considered, but he argued that it was not an absolute evil if it facilitated economic growth and improved living standards.

Simon's theory of resource development actually predicted some of the trends Ehrlich and Schneider listed as "losses." He pointed out that due to increased efficiency, the amount of cropland required and actually used to grow food for each person has decreased. The land that was once needed to feed a person could be returned to nature. The decline in the price of metals meant that we could build more with less. The increase in pollution in some areas was often a byproduct of industrialization that eventually led to cleaner technologies.

To Simon, the decline in the number of species or the loss of forest area was not an inevitable tragedy if it meant that humans could feed themselves and thrive. He believed that the market would eventually price in these environmental costs, leading to conservation. The bet on the metals was just the first chapter in a much larger story about how humanity interacts with the planet.

The Legacy of the Wager

The Simon-Ehrlich wager remains a touchstone in the debate over sustainability. It is a story of two giants of the 20th century, one a biologist who saw a world on the brink, and the other an economist who saw a world of endless possibility. The outcome of the bet was clear: Simon won. The prices of the metals fell. The check was written. But the interpretation of the result remains fiercely contested.

For the optimists, the wager is proof that human ingenuity can solve any problem. It is evidence that we are not running out of resources, but rather that we are learning to use them better. It validates the idea that population growth is not a curse, but a blessing that brings more minds to the table.

For the skeptics, the wager is a cautionary tale about the dangers of short-term thinking. It is a reminder that winning a bet on a specific commodity over a specific decade does not mean that the planet is healthy. The decline in metal prices did not prevent climate change, biodiversity loss, or social inequality. The trends that Ehrlich and Schneider later bet on—warming temperatures, rising CO2, shrinking forests—did indeed get worse. The second bet, which Simon refused to take, might have told a different story.

The wager also highlights the limitations of using markets as the sole measure of environmental health. Prices reflect scarcity, but they do not reflect the intrinsic value of a forest or the moral imperative to protect a species. They do not account for the suffering of the poor or the long-term consequences of pollution. The market is a powerful tool, but it is not a perfect oracle.

In the end, the Simon-Ehrlich wager is more than a historical anecdote about a check for $576.07. It is a mirror in which we can see our own hopes and fears about the future. Do we believe that the human mind is powerful enough to overcome the limits of the earth? Or do we believe that we are treading a thin line, and that our consumption is pushing us toward a cliff?

The events of the 1980s and 1990s showed that the market could be surprisingly resilient. The prices of metals fell despite a massive increase in population. But the events of the 21st century have shown that the challenges of climate change and biodiversity loss are not so easily solved by market forces alone. The wager reminds us that the story of human progress is complex, filled with unexpected twists and turns. It is a story where luck plays a role, but where human creativity is the most powerful variable of all.

The check was cashed, the books were closed, but the conversation continues. Every time we debate the price of oil, the state of the forests, or the future of the climate, we are, in a sense, replaying the Simon-Ehrlich wager. We are asking the same question: Will we run out, or will we find a way? The answer, as Julian Simon would have it, lies in our ability to innovate, to adapt, and to see ourselves not as the problem, but as the solution. But the warning from Ehrlich remains: innovation is not a magic wand, and the costs of our progress must be carefully measured. The wager was a moment in time, but the stakes are eternal.
