Sourdough

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Sourdough

In 1898, prospectors heading into the frozen Yukon Territory during the Klondike Gold Rush carried something more precious than gold dust around their necks: small pouches of fermenting flour and water. They guarded these pouches fiercely, sleeping with them close to their bodies to prevent freezing. The pouch contained their sourdough starter—a living culture of wild yeast and bacteria that meant the difference between eating bread and going hungry in a land where commercial yeast simply wouldn't survive.

These grizzled miners became known as "sourdoughs" themselves, a nickname that stuck. To this day, an Alaskan old-timer is still called a sourdough.

The Oldest Bread in the World

Sourdough isn't just old. It's ancient in a way that connects you directly to the first farmers who ever grew wheat.

Archaeologists have found sourdough bread dating to 3700 BCE in Switzerland, but the technique almost certainly stretches back further—to the Fertile Crescent and Egypt, where agriculture itself was born. For most of human history, this was simply how bread was made. There was no other way. The packaged yeast you can buy at any grocery store? That's an invention of the late nineteenth century, less than one hundred fifty years old.

Think about that for a moment. Humans have been making sourdough for perhaps ten thousand years. We've been using commercial baker's yeast for barely a century and a half. In the grand sweep of bread history, the way most of us make bread today is a radical experiment.

What Makes Sourdough Different

To understand sourdough, you need to understand what makes bread rise in the first place.

When you mix flour and water, you create a paste. Add yeast—microscopic single-celled fungi—and something remarkable happens. The yeast eats the sugars in the flour and produces carbon dioxide gas as a byproduct. Those gas bubbles get trapped in the stretchy network of proteins in the dough (what bakers call gluten), and the bread puffs up. That's leavening.

Commercial baker's yeast is a single strain of yeast, carefully cultivated in laboratories and factories to be predictable and fast-acting. You tear open the packet, mix it in, and within an hour or two your bread is rising.

Sourdough takes a completely different approach. Instead of adding yeast from a packet, you cultivate a living ecosystem right in your kitchen. A sourdough starter is a mixture of flour and water that has been colonized by wild yeasts floating in the air and bacteria naturally present in the flour. Leave flour and water sitting out at room temperature for a week or two, and life finds a way in.

But here's the crucial difference: sourdough isn't just wild yeast. It's wild yeast living alongside lactobacillus bacteria—the same type of bacteria that makes yogurt tangy and gives kimchi its funk. These bacteria produce lactic acid as they ferment, which is where sourdough gets its distinctive sour taste.

The Symbiosis Inside Your Starter

The relationship between the yeast and bacteria in a sourdough starter is genuinely fascinating. They're not just coexisting—they're cooperating.

The yeast can eat some of the sugars in flour, but not all of them. The bacteria can eat different sugars that the yeast can't touch. And crucially, the bacteria produce maltose as a byproduct—which happens to be the yeast's favorite food. Meanwhile, the acid produced by the bacteria creates an environment that's hostile to other, unwanted microorganisms but perfectly comfortable for both partners in this microscopic alliance.

Each sourdough starter develops its own unique community of organisms. The wild yeasts floating in the air of San Francisco are different from those in Paris or Tokyo. A starter that's been maintained for decades will have a different microbial profile than one started last month. Bakers speak of their starters almost as pets, feeding them on regular schedules, giving them names, even worrying about them when they travel.

This isn't just sentimentality. The starter really is a living thing, a tiny ecosystem in a jar, with a history all its own.

San Francisco and the Science of Sour

French bakers brought sourdough techniques to California during the Gold Rush of 1849, and something magical happened. The bread they made in San Francisco developed a particularly pronounced sour flavor that became famous.

For years, people assumed there was something special about San Francisco's foggy air, its unique microclimate, that couldn't be replicated anywhere else. The city's signature sourdough became a point of local pride—the mascot of the San Francisco 49ers football team is named "Sourdough Sam."

Scientists eventually isolated the specific bacterium responsible for much of that characteristic tang. They named it Fructilactobacillus sanfranciscensis—literally, "the San Francisco lactobacillus." Alongside it lives a yeast that was originally called Candida milleri and is now known as Kasachstania humilis.

But here's the thing: that bacterium and yeast aren't actually unique to San Francisco. They've been found in sourdough starters around the world. The distinctive flavor of San Francisco sourdough has more to do with how the bread is made—the specific flour, the hydration levels, the fermentation times, the baking techniques—than with any irreplaceable local microbe.

Why Rye Needs Sourdough

In northern Europe—Germany, Scandinavia, Russia, Poland—bread made from rye flour has been a staple for centuries. And unlike wheat bread, rye bread essentially requires sourdough fermentation. It's not optional.

The reason is structural. Wheat flour contains abundant gluten, those stretchy proteins that trap gas bubbles and give bread its structure. Rye flour has very little gluten. Instead, rye bread relies on starches and other carbohydrates called pentosans to hold itself together.

Here's where the chemistry gets interesting. Rye contains an enzyme called amylase that breaks down starches. In wheat, this enzyme becomes inactive at the temperatures reached during baking—it dies off before it can do any damage. But rye amylase is hardier. It keeps working even in a hot oven, breaking down the very starches that are supposed to be holding the bread together. The result, if you're not careful, is a gummy, collapsed mess.

The acid produced by sourdough fermentation solves this problem. The low pH deactivates the amylase in a way that heat alone cannot. The starches remain intact, gel properly during baking, and the bread holds its structure.

This is why traditional German pumpernickel, Danish rugbrød, and Finnish ruisleipä are all made with sourdough. It's not just about flavor. Without the acid fermentation, you can't make proper rye bread at all.

The Art of the Starter

Making a sourdough starter from scratch is deceptively simple. Mix equal parts flour and water. Leave it out. Wait.

The waiting is where the magic happens. The naturally occurring enzyme amylase in the flour breaks down starches into simple sugars—glucose and maltose—that the wild yeasts and bacteria can eat. Within a few days, you'll start to see bubbles. The mixture will smell sour, maybe a little funky. Life has arrived.

Over the next week or two, you "feed" the starter regularly—discarding some and adding fresh flour and water. This refreshment process encourages the development of a stable microbial community. The organisms that thrive in the acidic environment will outcompete those that don't. Eventually, you'll have a vigorous culture that can reliably raise bread.

The details matter enormously. A wetter starter, kept warm, will produce more lactic acid—a smoother, more yogurt-like sourness. A drier starter, kept cool, will produce more acetic acid—that sharp, vinegary tang. The ratio of old starter to new flour and water (what bakers call the refreshment ratio) affects how acidic the culture becomes. Temperature, humidity, and even elevation play roles.

Professional bakers who use sourdough as their sole leavening agent may feed their starters several times per day. Home bakers who only bake on weekends need different strategies—you can slow a starter down by refrigerating it, or dry it out and store it like an heirloom.

Heirloom Starters and Living History

Some bakers maintain starters that have been passed down for generations.

Carl Griffith's starter, dating to 1847, became famous after his family shared it for free with anyone who sent a self-addressed stamped envelope. Thousands of home bakers now maintain descendants of that original culture, a continuous lineage of living organisms stretching back to before the American Civil War.

Ed Wood, a sourdough expert and author, isolated yeast samples from an ancient Egyptian bakery near the pyramids of Giza. The idea that you could bake bread using organisms that might descend from those that leavened bread for pharaohs is almost impossibly romantic—though proving actual continuity across millennia is scientifically tricky.

What we do know is that sourdough cultures are remarkably stable. The acid they produce acts as a natural preservative, preventing colonization by unwanted yeasts and bacteria. A well-maintained starter can theoretically persist indefinitely, evolving slowly as new organisms drift in from the air, but maintaining its essential character.

When bakers talk about their hundred-year-old starters, they're not just being sentimental. They're describing a genuine biological lineage.

The Pandemic Bread Boom

When COVID-19 lockdowns began in early 2020, something unexpected happened: commercial baker's yeast disappeared from store shelves. Suddenly, millions of people stuck at home wanted to bake bread, and the supply chain couldn't keep up.

Sourdough offered a solution. You don't need to buy anything special. Flour, water, salt, and time—that's all it takes. Across the world, people who had never baked bread before began cultivating starters on their kitchen counters. Social media filled with photographs of bubbly starters and golden loaves. Forums and Facebook groups dedicated to sourdough troubleshooting exploded with new members.

For many, it became more than a hobby born of necessity. There was something deeply satisfying about creating bread from nothing but flour and water and the invisible life floating in the air. In a time of uncertainty and isolation, tending a sourdough starter offered a small daily ritual, a living thing that depended on you.

The Pursuit of Purity

Sourdough baking attracts people who appreciate simplicity and self-sufficiency. The ingredient list is almost absurdly short: flour, water, salt. That's it. No additives, no preservatives, no mysterious enzymes or dough conditioners.

As one enthusiast described it: "If you take flour, water, wild yeast and salt, and play around with time and temperature, what comes out of the oven is something utterly transformed."

This minimalism is part of the appeal. In a world of processed foods with ingredient lists that read like chemistry experiments, sourdough represents a return to fundamentals. You know exactly what's in your bread because you put it there yourself.

But simplicity doesn't mean there isn't depth. Bakers obsess over flour types—whole wheat versus white, high-protein versus low-protein, freshly milled versus store-bought. They experiment with hydration levels, fermentation times, and shaping techniques. Some treat it as an art, guided by intuition and accumulated experience. Others approach it as science, taking pH readings and maintaining spreadsheets of their experiments, corresponding with professional microbiologists about optimal conditions.

Both approaches can produce extraordinary bread.

The Question of Authenticity

Here's a dirty secret of the bread world: not everything labeled "sourdough" is actually made with sourdough.

In many countries, there's no legal definition of what sourdough means. Some commercial bakeries use industrial bread labeled as sourdough that's actually leavened with ordinary baker's yeast, with sour flavoring added artificially. Others use a small amount of real sourdough starter alongside commercial yeast, getting a hint of sour flavor without the long fermentation times that true sourdough requires.

The Real Bread Campaign, a British advocacy group, has coined the term "sourfaux" for breads that trade on the sourdough name without the sourdough process. They argue that genuine sourdough fermentation does more than create flavor—it may also make bread easier to digest and break down certain compounds that some people find problematic in conventionally made bread.

For home bakers, this distinction matters less. You know what's in your starter because you made it. You know how long it fermented because you watched the clock. The question of authenticity resolves itself when you're the one doing the baking.

The Slow Bread Movement

Sourdough is inherently slow. You can't rush it.

Commercial baker's yeast can raise a loaf in an hour or two. Sourdough takes its time—often twelve to twenty-four hours or more from mixing to baking, sometimes several days. The wild yeasts in a sourdough culture are simply less vigorous than their laboratory-cultivated cousins.

But that slowness isn't just an inconvenience to be tolerated. It's part of what makes sourdough bread different.

During those long hours of fermentation, the bacteria are producing acids that develop flavor. Enzymes are breaking down proteins and starches, creating compounds that affect texture and taste. The gluten network is developing in ways that give sourdough its characteristic chewy crumb. The extended fermentation may even affect the bread's glycemic impact—how quickly it raises blood sugar—though the science on this is still being studied.

In a culture obsessed with speed and efficiency, sourdough offers a different relationship with time. You can't make the bread rise faster by wanting it to. You have to work with the rhythms of the organisms in your starter, adjusting your schedule to theirs rather than the other way around.

For many bakers, that's the point.

Practical Wisdom

If you want to start making sourdough, a few practical considerations are worth knowing.

Water matters. The chlorine or chloramines in most municipal tap water are added to kill microorganisms—exactly what you don't want when you're trying to cultivate a living culture. Bottled water works fine. You can also leave tap water uncovered for twenty-four hours to let chlorine dissipate, though chloramines are more persistent and may require a carbon filter to remove.

Flour matters too. Unbleached, unbromated flour contains more of the microorganisms you want to encourage. Whole wheat or rye flour, with the bran intact, provides an even richer variety of organisms and additional nutrients to feed them. Many bakers start their cultures with whole grain flour and then switch to white flour once the starter is established.

Temperature is crucial. Most sourdough organisms thrive between seventy-five and eighty-five degrees Fahrenheit (about twenty-four to thirty degrees Celsius). Cooler temperatures slow fermentation; warmer temperatures speed it up. A starter kept in the refrigerator goes dormant and needs only weekly feeding. A starter on a warm kitchen counter in summer may need attention twice a day.

Patience is not optional. A new starter typically takes one to two weeks to become stable enough to reliably raise bread. During that time, you'll see activity, then sometimes a period of apparent death, then activity again as the microbial community sorts itself out. Don't give up during the quiet periods.

The Gift That Keeps Giving

One of the beautiful things about sourdough culture is that it's inherently shareable. Every time you feed your starter, you're creating more of it. Most bakers end up discarding some with each feeding just to keep the quantities manageable.

That "discard" doesn't have to go in the trash. You can use it to make pancakes, crackers, pizza dough, or give it to a friend who wants to start their own sourdough journey. Passing along a bit of starter is passing along a living culture—literally, a gift that money can't buy in the same way.

Online communities have sprung up around the sharing of starters and techniques. People mail dried starter cultures across continents. They troubleshoot each other's problems, share photographs of their latest loaves, and celebrate successes.

In an age of mass production and industrial food systems, there's something almost subversive about this. A sourdough starter represents knowledge and living culture transmitted person to person, outside of any commercial system. Your great-grandparents could have understood it immediately. Their great-grandparents, too, stretching back to the first farmers who noticed that old dough made new bread rise.

When you bake a loaf of sourdough, you're participating in a tradition nearly as old as civilization itself. And that connection, that sense of continuity across millennia, might be the most remarkable thing about this simple mixture of flour and water and wild life.
