Gig worker

16 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Gig worker

The Musicians Who Named a Revolution

In 1952, Jack Kerouac was working part-time as a brakeman for the Southern Pacific Railroad. When he wrote about this job, he called it a "gig"—borrowing slang from jazz musicians who had long used the word to describe their one-night performances at clubs and dance halls. A gig was something temporary, something you did for cash between the real work of living.

Kerouac probably never imagined that seventy years later, this little piece of musician slang would describe how tens of millions of people make their living.

Today, gig workers are the people who deliver your food, drive you to the airport, assemble your IKEA furniture, and walk your dog. They're freelance graphic designers and temporary office workers. They're the invisible hands that complete small tasks online so that artificial intelligence systems can learn to recognize cats in photographs. Some do this work by choice, valuing flexibility above all else. Others do it because traditional employment has become harder to find or doesn't pay enough to live on.

What unites them is what they lack: a traditional employer-employee relationship with its steady paycheck, health insurance, paid sick days, and retirement contributions.

The Numbers Are Staggering—and Slippery

How many gig workers exist? The honest answer is that nobody knows for certain, partly because the definition keeps shifting.

A 2016 study by the McKinsey Global Institute found 162 million people doing some form of independent work across the United States and England combined. By 2018, roughly 36 percent of American workers were participating in the gig economy, either as their main source of income or as a side hustle.

In Europe, the figures are somewhat lower but still substantial. A 2017 survey found that nearly 10 percent of adults across 14 European Union countries were gig workers. When researchers broaden the definition to include all "independent or non-conventional workers," the estimates jump to somewhere between 20 and 30 percent of the working population in both the United States and Europe.

These aren't marginal numbers. We're talking about a quarter to a third of the workforce operating outside the traditional employment model that dominated the twentieth century.

How Did We Get Here?

The technology came first. The 2000s brought widespread internet access and then smartphones—pocket-sized computers that could pinpoint your location, process payments, and connect you instantly with anyone nearby who wanted to buy what you were selling or sell what you were buying.

Then came the platforms. Uber, launched in 2009, became so influential that its name spawned a new word: "uberization." This refers to the business model of using mobile applications to connect service providers directly with customers, cutting out traditional middlemen. A taxi company owns vehicles, employs dispatchers, and maintains radio systems. Uber owns an app. The drivers bring their own cars. The customers bring their own phones. The company sits in the middle, taking a percentage of each transaction.

This model has spread far beyond transportation. DoorDash connects hungry customers with people willing to pick up restaurant orders. TaskRabbit connects homeowners with people who will assemble furniture or hang pictures. Fiverr connects businesses with freelance designers, writers, and programmers. Amazon's Mechanical Turk—named after a famous eighteenth-century chess-playing automaton that secretly hid a human operator inside—connects companies with workers willing to perform simple digital tasks for pennies apiece.

The economics are genuinely different from traditional business. A taxi company must buy vehicles, maintain them, insure them, and pay drivers whether or not customers show up. A ride-sharing platform has none of these costs. Its expenses are primarily software development and marketing. When demand drops, it doesn't pay workers to sit idle. When demand surges, it doesn't need to hire more employees—it just sends notifications to existing drivers offering them higher rates.

The Flexibility Bargain

Ask gig workers why they do it, and flexibility tops the list.

Traditional employment means showing up at a specific place at a specific time, staying for a set number of hours, and requesting permission to deviate from this schedule. Gig work, in theory, means working when you want, where you want, for as long as you want.

This appeals especially to people whose lives don't fit neatly into nine-to-five boxes. Parents managing childcare. Students working around class schedules. People caring for elderly relatives. People with chronic illnesses whose energy levels fluctuate unpredictably. People pursuing artistic careers who need income but can't commit to rigid schedules.

Digital nomads represent one extreme of this flexibility. These are people who've structured their entire lives around location independence, traveling the world while earning money through remote gig work. They need only a laptop, an internet connection, and marketable skills. Today they might be writing code from a café in Lisbon; next month, designing websites from a beach in Thailand.

But flexibility cuts both ways.

The Dark Side of "Be Your Own Boss"

When companies classify workers as independent contractors rather than employees, they shed significant costs and obligations.

Traditional employees in the United States receive protections under laws like the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938, which mandates minimum wage and overtime pay, and the Wagner Act of 1935, which guarantees the right to organize unions. Employers must contribute to Social Security, provide workers' compensation insurance, and in many cases offer health insurance.

Independent contractors receive none of this. No minimum wage guarantee. No overtime pay. No employer contribution to retirement. No health insurance. No paid sick days. No unemployment insurance if work dries up. No workers' compensation if they're injured on the job.

The companies argue this is fair. Independent contractors set their own schedules, work for multiple clients, and have entrepreneurial freedom. They're not employees; they're small business owners.

Critics point out that this independence is often illusory. Drivers can't set their own prices—the platform does. They can't negotiate terms—take it or leave it. Their customer relationships are mediated entirely through the platform, which can "deactivate" them at any time, effectively firing them without the legal protections that firing an employee would require.

In 2020, California voters approved Proposition 22, creating a novel third category: gig worker drivers who remain classified as independent contractors but receive some benefits, including a minimum wage floor and mileage reimbursement. This compromise satisfied neither side completely. Companies wanted the flexibility of the contractor model. Workers and their advocates wanted full employee protections. What emerged was a hybrid that may become a template for other jurisdictions—or may prove too awkward to replicate.

Zero Hours and Ghost Work

The gig economy has cousins that share some family traits while remaining distinct.

Zero-hour contracts, a term used primarily in the United Kingdom, refer to employment arrangements where the employer has no obligation to provide any minimum amount of work. Workers are technically employees, paid by the hour when work is available, but with no guarantee that work will be available. They receive some employment protections but face the same income instability as gig workers.

The key difference is in how payment works. Gig workers are typically paid per task—per delivery completed, per ride given, per job finished. Zero-hour workers are paid for time worked. A gig worker who spends an hour waiting for a delivery assignment earns nothing. A zero-hour worker called in for a shift earns hourly wages for that shift, even during slow periods.

Ghost work occupies an even more invisible corner of this landscape. Ghost workers complete small digital tasks that help train artificial intelligence systems—labeling images, transcribing audio, flagging inappropriate content. The work is typically paid per task at rates of pennies to a few dollars. Workers have no relationship with the companies whose AI systems they're training beyond receiving task assignments through a platform.

The term captures something important: these workers are invisible to end users. When you ask a voice assistant a question and it understands you, you might assume pure machine intelligence is at work. In reality, thousands of ghost workers may have listened to similar queries and labeled them to teach the system what phrases like "set a timer for ten minutes" actually mean.

The Cybertariat

The scholar Ursula Huws coined the term "cybertariat" to describe a new class of precarious workers whose labor is controlled by algorithmic systems. The word blends "cyber" with "proletariat"—the industrial working class that Karl Marx analyzed in the nineteenth century.

The cybertariat can work anytime and from anywhere, which sounds like freedom. But they can also be hired and fired at will, work mostly anonymously, and are subject to decision-making systems they cannot see or appeal to. An algorithm determines which workers receive job offers. An algorithm calculates their ratings. An algorithm decides when they've violated terms of service and should be deactivated.

This represents something genuinely new in the history of work. Previous generations of workers were managed by humans—foremen, supervisors, managers—whose decisions could be questioned, negotiated with, or at least understood. The cybertariat is managed by software that offers no explanations and accepts no appeals.

The Health Toll

In 2021, the World Health Organization and the International Labour Organization released a sobering report. They found that the expansion of gig work correlated with a significant increase in deaths among workers who put in more than 55 hours per week.

The numbers: 600,000 such deaths in 2000. By 2016, 750,000.

That's a 25 percent increase in deaths attributable to overwork. The researchers found that roughly 9 percent of the world's population was working these excessive hours, with men and workers in the Western Pacific and Southeast Asia regions most affected.

The connection to gig work isn't hard to see. When you're paid per task rather than per hour, and when there's no guaranteed minimum, the incentive is to work as much as possible. There's no overtime pay kicking in after 40 hours to discourage excess. There's no manager telling you to go home. There's only the calculation of whether you've earned enough to cover rent, food, and the bills that never stop arriving.

Research has also documented poor mental health outcomes among gig workers. The combination of income instability, social isolation, irregular hours, and lack of workplace community creates conditions that correlate with anxiety and depression.

Women in the Gig Economy

The gig economy's flexibility attracts women disproportionately—and often exploits them disproportionately too.

In the United States, women make up about 55 percent of gig workers. In India, the figure is 28 percent. These numbers reflect different cultural contexts but a shared reality: women often need flexible work to balance employment with domestic responsibilities that still fall primarily on their shoulders.

When DoorDash surveyed its female workers, 80 percent said flexibility was their main reason for doing gig work. Many are monetizing skills they already use at home—food shopping, cooking knowledge, caregiving expertise—while working around childcare schedules.

The work itself remains highly gendered. Studies show that male gig workers concentrate in transportation, driving for Uber and Lyft. Women cluster in food shopping, care work, cleaning, and creative freelance work like graphic design and writing.

Delivery work has emerged as something of an exception. Women now make up nearly half of Uber Eats delivery workers and 58 percent of DoorDash drivers. Many prefer delivery to ride-sharing because it feels safer—you're not trapped in a car with a stranger.

That safety concern isn't hypothetical. A 2019 Uber safety report documented 6,000 incidents of sexual assault between 2017 and 2018, experienced by both riders and drivers. Female drivers face particular risks: drunken passengers, harassment, situations where they're alone with potentially dangerous individuals and have minimal support from the platform.

The Rating Trap

Platforms like Uber assign work based on customer ratings. High ratings mean more job offers. Low ratings mean fewer offers or outright removal from the platform.

This creates a perverse incentive structure, especially for women. Tolerating some level of harassment might be preferable to risking a low rating from an entitled customer. Defending yourself firmly might cost you your livelihood.

Some platforms have implemented safety measures. Uber now requires drivers to complete anti-sexual violence training and includes a "panic button" that connects directly to 911 dispatchers. But these measures are widely regarded as insufficient. Workers facing dangerous situations often find themselves on their own, with minimal guidance from platforms about how to respond.

When assault or harassment does occur, the financial burden falls entirely on the worker. Traditional employees can access sick leave to recover from traumatic experiences. Independent contractors cannot. They either keep working through their distress or lose income they may desperately need.

The Pay Gap Persists

One might hope that the gig economy's algorithmic assignment of work would eliminate gender discrimination. If an app assigns tasks based purely on availability and location, surely men and women would earn equally?

The data says otherwise. One analysis of Uber drivers found women earning about 7 percent less than men. On Amazon's Mechanical Turk, women earned roughly 10.5 percent less per hour of work.

Some of this reflects different choices—women were more likely to take breaks between tasks, perhaps because they were simultaneously managing domestic responsibilities. Some reflects the continuation of the same biases that plague traditional employment. Customer ratings may be influenced by unconscious gender bias. Men and women may still be steered toward different types of tasks with different pay rates.

The gender pay gap in the gig economy typically mirrors the gap in traditional employment, running somewhere between 7 and 15 percent depending on the platform and methodology. The dream that algorithmic work assignment would create equality remains unrealized.

India's First Women-Led Gig Worker Strike

In October 2021, roughly 100 women gathered outside the offices of Urban Company in Gurugram, India. Urban Company is a platform that connects customers with at-home service providers—beauticians, cleaners, repair workers.

The women were protesting low wages, high commission rates taken by the platform, and poor safety conditions. They were, quite simply, demanding better treatment.

Urban Company's response was revealing. The company filed a lawsuit against its own workers, accusing them of "instigating violence." The lawsuit explicitly described Urban Company as merely an "aggregator" connecting customers with independent workers—a legal framing designed to avoid responsibility for those workers' conditions. The company sought a permanent court order prohibiting any future protests.

The protest was eventually called off after authorities imposed Section 144 of the Criminal Procedure Code in Gurugram, a legal provision that prohibits public gatherings of more than four people. The workers dispersed, but they had made their point visible.

A Planetary Labor Market

One of the gig economy's most profound effects is also one of its least visible: workers now compete globally.

A graphic designer in Mumbai can bid for the same project as a designer in Minneapolis. A content writer in Manila competes with writers in London. A programmer in Lagos offers services to the same clients as programmers in San Francisco.

This "planetary labor market" has obvious benefits for customers, who can access talent worldwide and often at lower prices. It has obvious benefits for workers in lower-cost countries, who can earn international rates while living on local expenses.

But it creates intense downward pressure on wages in higher-cost countries. Why pay $50 an hour for a task when someone equally qualified will do it for $10? The gig worker in a wealthy nation increasingly must compete with the entire world—and the entire world, on average, is willing to work for less.

The Classification Battle

At the heart of almost every gig economy controversy lies a single question: Are these workers employees or independent contractors?

The answer determines everything. Employees get minimum wage, overtime, benefits, and the right to organize. Independent contractors get none of these things but theoretically gain freedom and flexibility.

Companies have powerful incentives to classify workers as contractors. Labor advocates have powerful incentives to push for employee classification. The battles play out in courtrooms, legislatures, and ballot initiatives across the world.

California's Proposition 22 suggested a possible middle path—a third classification with some protections but not all. Whether this compromise spreads or collapses remains an open question. What's clear is that laws written in the 1930s, designed for a world of factories and offices and clear employment relationships, struggle to address a twenty-first-century reality where someone might deliver groceries for three hours, drive for Uber for two hours, and complete freelance design work for four hours, all in a single day, for three different platforms, with none of them claiming any employer obligations.

The Future Is Being Negotiated

Every time you order delivery or hail a ride through an app, you're participating in an ongoing experiment about the future of work.

The gig economy offers genuine benefits: flexibility, accessibility, the democratization of opportunity. Someone with a car can earn money driving. Someone with a skill can find clients worldwide. Someone whose life doesn't fit traditional employment patterns can still earn a living.

But it also represents genuine dangers: the erosion of workplace protections that took generations to establish, the atomization of workers who can't organize because they never meet each other, the transfer of risk from companies to individuals who lack the resources to absorb it.

Jack Kerouac, working his gig on the Southern Pacific Railroad in 1952, probably didn't worry much about health insurance or retirement savings. He was young, he was passing through, and the gig was temporary. That's what gigs were supposed to be—interludes between more permanent arrangements.

For millions of workers today, the gig has become the arrangement. Whether that represents liberation or exploitation—or, as is usually the case with complex social changes, some measure of both—depends on whose story you're telling and what alternatives were actually available.

The musicians who gave us the word understood something important: a gig is what you do when you need the money and the real thing hasn't come along yet. The question hanging over the gig economy is whether, for more and more workers, there is no "real thing" coming along anymore—whether the gig is all there is, and we're just now beginning to reckon with what that means.
