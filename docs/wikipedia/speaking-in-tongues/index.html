Speaking in tongues

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Speaking in tongues

In 1972, William J. Samarin, a linguist from the University of Toronto, stepped into the chaotic, fervent heart of a Pentecostal gathering in the Bronx. He was not there to pray, but to record. For five years, he had been capturing the vocalizations of believers across Italy, the Netherlands, Jamaica, Canada, and the United States. His subjects ranged from the Puerto Rican faithful in New York to the snake handlers of the Appalachians and the Molokan refugees from Russia living in Los Angeles. Samarin sought to answer a question that had divided theologians and scientists for centuries: When a person speaks in tongues, are they channeling a divine, unknown language, or are they merely making noise? His conclusion, published that year, was a definitive blow to the literal interpretation held by many believers. He found that while the speech possessed the rhythm, accent, and intonation of human language, it was, in his words, "only a facade of language."

This phenomenon, known to scholars as glossolalia, is the fluid vocalizing of speech-like syllables that lack any readily comprehensible meaning. To the believer, it is often a divine language, a direct line to the holy. To the linguist, it is a fascinating psychological and phonological construction that mimics the surface structures of human speech without containing its internal logic. The term itself is a borrowing from the Greek glossolalía, a compound of glossa, meaning "tongue" or "language," and laleō, meaning "to speak," "talk," or even "prattle." It is a practice deeply embedded in the fabric of Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity, yet its roots stretch back to the very birth of the Christian movement and echo through the corridors of ancient philosophy and medieval mysticism.

To understand the modern phenomenon, one must first descend to the source: the New Testament. The exact phrase "speaking in tongues" has been used since at least the 14th century, appearing in the Wycliffe Bible, the first complete translation of the Bible into Middle English. However, the concept originates in the Greek text of the books of Acts and First Corinthians. In Acts 2, the narrative is dramatic and immediate. The followers of Christ are gathered, and they receive the Holy Spirit. Suddenly, they begin to speak in languages they do not know. The text specifies that these were not gibberish, but the actual languages of at least fifteen different countries or ethnic groups, allowing the listeners from various nations to hear the wonders of God in their own native tongues. This was xenolalia, or xenoglossy—a distinction made by modern scholars to describe the belief that the speaker is using a natural language previously unknown to them, as opposed to glossolalia, which is the utterance of non-human, or at least non-comprehensible, speech.

The linguistic analysis of this behavior has evolved significantly over the last century. Before the 20th century, the focus was almost exclusively on the theological implications. Frederic Farrar first introduced the specific term "glossolalia" into the English lexicon in 1879, attempting to categorize the phenomenon scientifically. However, it was not until the mid-20th century that the empirical study of the sound itself began in earnest. Samarin's work in 1972 remains the benchmark. He recorded hundreds of hours of speech, analyzing the phonetics with a microscope's precision.

What he found was a paradox. The speakers were not producing random noise. Their utterances were structured. They used accent, rhythm, and pauses to break the stream of sound into distinct units, much like sentences. Each unit was composed of syllables, and those syllables were built from consonants and vowels found in the speaker's native language. A speaker from Jamaica would use the phonemes of Jamaican English; a speaker from Mexico would use the sounds of Spanish. There was no evidence of alien phonetics. As Samarin noted, the speech consisted of strings of syllables made up of sounds the speaker already knew, put together more or less haphazardly. Yet, because of the realistic, language-like rhythm and melody, the result emerged as word-like and sentence-like units.

"It is verbal behaviour that consists of using a certain number of consonants and vowels ... in a limited number of syllables that in turn are organized into larger units that are taken apart and rearranged pseudogrammatically ... with variations in pitch, volume, speed and intensity."

This finding was corroborated by other researchers. Felicitas Goodman, a psychological anthropologist and linguist, conducted her own extensive studies. She traveled to Pentecostal communities in the United States, the Caribbean, and Mexico, comparing English, Spanish, and Mayan-speaking groups. She did not stop there. Goodman also recorded non-Christian rituals in Africa, Borneo, Indonesia, and Japan. Her methodology was rigorous, examining both the segmental structure (the sounds and syllables) and the supra-segmental elements (the rhythm and intonation). Her conclusion was striking: there was no distinction between the glossolalia of Pentecostal Protestants and the ecstatic speech of followers in other religious traditions. The human voice, when in a state of religious ecstasy, seems to default to a universal pattern of phonological organization, regardless of the specific theology involved.

However, the resemblance to human language stops at the surface. Samarin concluded that glossolalia is "meaningless but phonologically structured human utterance." The critical failure point for it being a true language is the lack of a systematic relationship between the units of speech and concepts. In human language, a word or phrase maps to a specific meaning or idea. In glossolalia, there is no such map. The stream of speech is not internally organized to convey information. It does not communicate. Therefore, linguists argue, it is not a specimen of human language because it is neither internally organized nor systematically related to the world man perceives.

This scientific perspective contrasts sharply with the historical and theological understanding of the phenomenon, where it has often been accorded the highest status. In the ancient world, the idea that divine beings spoke languages different from humans was commonplace. Historians of religion have identified references to esoteric speech in Greco-Roman literature that bear a striking resemblance to glossolalia. These utterances were often explained as angelic or divine languages, a direct transmission from the gods to the prophet.

One of the most vivid examples comes from the Testament of Job, a non-canonical elaboration of the biblical Book of Job. In this text, the daughters of Job are described as receiving sashes that enable them to speak and sing in angelic languages. This was not seen as a defect or a psychological quirk, but as a mark of divine favor. Dale B. Martin, a historian, notes that glossolalia was highly valued in the ancient world precisely because of this association with the divine. The prophet Alexander of Abonoteichus, a figure of controversy in the 2nd century, may have exhibited glossolalia during his episodes of prophetic ecstasy, using the phenomenon to cement his authority as a messenger of the gods.

The Neoplatonist philosopher Iamblichus took this a step further, linking glossolalia directly to the mechanics of prophecy. He wrote that prophecy was a form of divine spirit possession. In his view, the prophet did not generate the words through their own intellect. Instead, the divine energy took over the body, causing the prophet to "emit words which are not understood by those that utter them." Iamblichus described the prophet as speaking with an "insane mouth" (mainomenō stomati), wholly subservient to the energy of the predominating God. The lack of understanding was not a bug; it was a feature. It proved that the source of the speech was external, supernatural, and beyond human comprehension.

Yet, not all ancient observers were so reverent. The Greek philosopher Celsus, writing in his polemic against early Christianity, offers a scathing critique of the phenomenon. In his account of early Christian prophets in Palestine and Phoenicia, Celsus describes how these believers would brandish threats and then descend into incomprehensible, incoherent utterances. He argued that the meaning of these words could not be discovered by any intelligent person. To Celsus, they were meaningless and nonsensical, a dangerous opening for "any fool or sorcerer to take the words in whatever sense he likes." His critique highlights the tension that has always existed around speaking in tongues: is it a window to the divine, or a mask for deception?

The history of the Church Fathers reveals a complex relationship with the gift. References to speaking in tongues are surprisingly rare in their writings. Irenaeus, in the 2nd century, mentions that many in the church spoke "all kinds of languages through the Spirit," suggesting a widespread practice. Tertullian, writing in 207 AD, also references the spiritual gift of the interpretation of tongues being encountered in his day. Beyond these two, there are no known first-hand accounts, and very few second-hand accounts in the extensive writings of the early church. This silence suggests that while the phenomenon may have existed, it was perhaps not the central pillar of worship that it would later become in the Pentecostal movement.

As the centuries rolled on, the interpretation of the gift shifted. In the 12th century, Bernard of Clairvaux, a towering figure of the Cistercian order, argued that the gift of tongues was no longer necessary. He believed that the miracles of the apostolic age had ceased because the Church had been established. The "greater miracles" now were the transformed lives of believers, a moral and spiritual renewal that rendered the ecstatic speech obsolete.

However, the gift was not entirely forgotten. Hildegard of Bingen, the 12th-century mystic and polymath, is said to have possessed the gift of visions and prophecy. Her case was unique; she was reportedly able to speak and write in Latin without having learned the language. Unlike the glossolalia of the Pentecostals, which linguists analyze as a facade of language, Hildegard's ability was viewed as a true xenoglossy—a mastery of a natural language acquired through divine intervention. This distinction between the "unknown language" of the mystic and the "speech-like sounds" of the ecstatic worshiper has persisted throughout history.

Thomas Aquinas, the great theologian of the 13th century, weighed in on the matter in 1265. Writing about the gift of tongues in the New Testament, Aquinas understood it as an ability to speak every language, given specifically for the purposes of missionary work. He offered a pragmatic explanation for why the gift seemed to have disappeared: Christ himself did not possess this gift because his mission was primarily to the Jews. Furthermore, Aquinas argued that "no one speaks in the tongues of all nations" in his own time because the Church itself had already spread and "speaks the languages of all nations." The need for a miraculous linguistic gift had been superseded by the organic growth of the Church across the globe.

The Reformation and the centuries that followed saw a resurgence of the phenomenon, often accompanied by controversy. In the 15th century, the Moravians were accused by their detractors of speaking in tongues. John Roche, a contemporary critic, claimed that the Moravians "commonly broke into some disconnected Jargon, which they often passed upon the vulgar, 'as the exuberant and resistless Evacuations of the Spirit'." The language of the critic reveals the fear and misunderstanding that often accompany the phenomenon: it is seen as an "evacuation," a loss of control, something messy and unrefined.

By the 17th century, the French Prophets, also known as the Camisards, brought the phenomenon back into the public eye. These Protestant refugees in the Cévennes region of France claimed to speak in languages unknown to them. James Du Bois of Montpell noted that "several persons of both sexes" among them spoke in these foreign tongues. The Camisards were a militant group, and their ecstatic speech was often a prelude to prophetic declarations of judgment and deliverance. For them, the gift was not a private spiritual experience but a public declaration of resistance against religious persecution.

The modern era has seen the phenomenon explode, particularly with the rise of the Pentecostal movement in the early 20th century. Today, millions of Christians around the world practice glossolalia as a standard part of their worship. The linguistic debates continue, but the personal experience of the believer remains unchanged. For the practitioner, the sound is not meaningless. It is a prayer of the spirit, a language of the heart that bypasses the intellect. Samarin's finding that the speech is "meaningless" in a linguistic sense does not negate the meaning it holds for the speaker. It is a meaning that exists in the emotional, the spiritual, and the communal, rather than the semantic.

The distinction between glossolalia and xenoglossy remains a crucial one for understanding the spectrum of this experience. While linguists have largely debunked the idea that Pentecostal glossolalia is a natural language previously unknown to the speaker, the possibility of xenoglossy—the ability to speak a real language without learning it—has never been entirely ruled out by the scientific community, though it remains a rarity. The vast majority of cases fall into the category of glossolalia: a phonologically structured, emotionally charged, and spiritually significant utterance that mimics the form of language without its function.

The story of speaking in tongues is a story of the human voice pushed to its limits. It is a phenomenon where the boundary between the self and the divine seems to dissolve. Whether one views it as the "facade of language" described by Samarin, the "angelic language" of the Testament of Job, or the "insane mouth" of the Neoplatonist prophet, it remains a powerful testament to the human capacity for transcendence. It is a practice that has survived millennia, adapting to the theological needs of each era while retaining its core mystery.

In the end, the silence of the Church Fathers and the noise of the Pentecostal congregation are two sides of the same coin. They both grapple with the same fundamental question: How does the human being communicate with the infinite? For some, the answer is found in the transformed life, in the quiet work of the spirit. For others, the answer is found in the cacophony of the unknown, in the rhythmic, syllable-striking sounds that rise from the throat, defying analysis but affirming faith. The linguist may hear only a rearrangement of known sounds, but the believer hears a new language, a language of the spirit that speaks directly to the soul.

The history of this practice is not just a history of religion; it is a history of psychology, linguistics, and the enduring human desire to reach beyond the known world. From the 1st-century streets of Jerusalem to the 21st-century megachurches of the Americas, the sound persists. It is a sound that challenges the intellect, unsettles the skeptic, and comforts the faithful. It is a reminder that language, in its most profound form, may not be about the communication of facts, but the expression of the ineffable.

As we look back at the centuries of debate, from Celsus's mockery to Iamblichus's reverence, from Aquinas's pragmatism to Samarin's phonological charts, one thing becomes clear: the phenomenon of speaking in tongues is as much about the listener as it is about the speaker. It forces us to decide what we value in language. Is it the system of meaning, the structure of syntax, the utility of communication? Or is it the raw power of the voice, the rhythm of the spirit, the ability to say something that cannot be said?

The answer depends on who you ask. To the linguist, it is a fascinating anomaly, a "facade." To the believer, it is the voice of God. And perhaps, in that very tension, in the space between the data and the faith, lies the true mystery of glossolalia. It is a practice that defies easy categorization, a sound that is at once human and divine, known and unknown, meaningless and full of meaning. It is a phenomenon that continues to shape the landscape of religious experience, a testament to the enduring power of the spoken word to bridge the gap between the earthly and the eternal.

The journey of understanding speaking in tongues is far from over. As new communities form and new rituals emerge, the voices will continue to rise. They will carry the accents of their native lands, the rhythms of their cultures, and the melodies of their faith. And as they do, they will continue to challenge our understanding of what it means to speak, to listen, and to believe. The sound of glossolalia is not just a relic of the past; it is a living, breathing reality in the present, a reminder that there are still mysteries in the world that cannot be fully explained, only experienced.

In the end, the story of speaking in tongues is a story of the human spirit's refusal to be silenced. It is a story of the search for a language that can carry the weight of the divine. Whether that language is found in the structured syntax of the ancient world or the fluid, rhythmic sounds of the modern church, the impulse remains the same. It is the impulse to reach out, to speak, and to be heard by something greater than oneself. And in that impulse, we find the heart of the phenomenon, beating strong and clear, across the centuries.
