Short (finance)

19 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Short (finance)

In 1992, George Soros made over a billion dollars in a single day by betting that the British pound would collapse. He wasn't buying anything. He was selling something he didn't own—and that counterintuitive maneuver is the essence of short selling, one of the most powerful and controversial techniques in finance.

Most people understand investing intuitively: you buy something, wait for it to become more valuable, then sell it for a profit. Buy low, sell high. But short selling flips this logic entirely on its head. You sell first, then buy later. And if the price drops in between, you pocket the difference.

How is that even possible? How can you sell something you don't have?

The Mechanics of Betting Against

The answer involves borrowing. A short seller borrows shares from someone who already owns them, sells those borrowed shares immediately at the current market price, and then waits. If the price falls, the short seller buys replacement shares at the lower price, returns them to the lender, and keeps the difference as profit.

Think of it this way. Imagine you believe that a particular vintage of wine is overpriced at one hundred dollars a bottle, and you expect the price to drop soon. You borrow a bottle from a friend who has a cellar full of them. You immediately sell that bottle to someone else for one hundred dollars. A month later, the price has indeed fallen to sixty dollars. You buy a replacement bottle for sixty dollars, give it back to your friend, and pocket the forty-dollar difference. Your friend has their bottle back—not the exact same bottle, but one that's identical and interchangeable. And you've made money from a price decline.

This works because securities like stocks and bonds are what economists call fungible. One share of Apple stock is exactly the same as any other share of Apple stock, just as one ten-dollar bill is interchangeable with any other ten-dollar bill. When you borrow fungible assets, you don't need to return the exact same item—just an equivalent one.

Contrast this with borrowing your neighbor's bicycle. You have to return that specific bicycle, not just any bicycle of the same model. Securities don't work that way, and that fungibility is what makes short selling possible.

An Illustrative Trade

Let's walk through a concrete example. Shares in a company called ACME are trading at ten dollars each. A short seller borrows one hundred shares and immediately sells them, receiving one thousand dollars in cash.

Now the short seller waits.

If the price drops to eight dollars per share, the short seller can buy one hundred replacement shares for only eight hundred dollars, return them to the lender, and keep the two hundred dollar profit. Minus whatever borrowing fees they owed the lender, of course—nothing is free.

But here's where it gets dangerous. What if the price doesn't fall? What if it rises instead?

Suppose those ACME shares climb from ten dollars to twenty-five dollars. The short seller still owes one hundred shares to the lender. They have no choice but to buy replacement shares at the new, higher price. That costs two thousand five hundred dollars. Since they only received one thousand dollars from the initial sale, they've lost fifteen hundred dollars.

And this asymmetry reveals the terrifying mathematics of short selling.

The Infinite Loss Problem

When you buy a stock the normal way—going "long" in trader parlance—your maximum possible loss is capped at what you paid. If you invest ten thousand dollars in a company and it goes bankrupt, you lose ten thousand dollars. Painful, but finite.

Short selling has no such ceiling on losses.

A stock's price can theoretically rise forever. If you short a stock at ten dollars and it rises to one hundred dollars, you've lost ninety dollars per share. If it rises to one thousand dollars, you've lost nine hundred ninety dollars per share. There is no upper bound. The stock could become the next Apple or Amazon, multiplying in value by hundreds of times, and your losses would multiply right along with it.

This is why short sellers live in a state of perpetual anxiety that long investors never experience. Time is not their friend. Every day that passes with the stock price stubbornly rising is another day of accumulating losses. And unlike a long investor who can simply hold through turbulence and wait for recovery, a short seller may be forced to close their position at the worst possible moment.

Margin Calls and Forced Exits

Because short selling can generate theoretically unlimited losses, brokers require short sellers to maintain collateral. This is called posting margin. If you borrow shares to sell short, you must deposit cash or securities with your broker as a guarantee that you can cover your obligations if the trade goes against you.

If the stock price rises and your potential losses grow, the broker will demand additional margin. If you can't provide it, the broker will forcibly close your position by buying shares on your behalf at the current market price. This is called a margin call, and it can be devastating.

Here's why margin calls create a vicious cycle. When short sellers are forced to buy shares to close their positions, that buying pressure pushes the stock price even higher. This triggers more margin calls for other short sellers, forcing them to buy as well, pushing the price higher still. This self-reinforcing spiral is called a short squeeze, and it can send stock prices skyrocketing far beyond any rational valuation.

The most famous recent short squeeze occurred in January 2021, when retail investors coordinated on social media to buy shares of GameStop, a struggling video game retailer. Hedge funds had heavily shorted the stock, betting on its decline. As the price rose, short sellers faced massive margin calls. Their forced buying pushed the price from around twenty dollars to nearly five hundred dollars in a matter of weeks. Some hedge funds lost billions.

Where Do Borrowed Shares Come From?

For short selling to work, someone has to be willing to lend their shares. Who are these lenders, and why would they do it?

The shares typically come from large institutional investors like pension funds, mutual funds, and insurance companies. These institutions often hold enormous portfolios of stocks for the long term. Since they're not planning to sell anytime soon, they can earn extra income by lending their shares to short sellers and collecting borrowing fees.

Your own shares might be lent out without your knowledge. If you have a margin account—a brokerage account that allows you to borrow money to buy stocks—the fine print almost certainly gives your broker permission to lend your shares to short sellers. You might own Apple stock, but the physical shares sitting in your account could be on loan to someone who is actively betting that Apple's price will fall.

The lender doesn't lose their ownership rights in any meaningful way. If Apple pays a dividend while the shares are on loan, the short seller must reimburse the lender for that dividend. If the lender wants to sell their shares, the broker can simply recall the loan or substitute shares from another customer's account. In a well-functioning market, the original owner barely notices.

Before executing a short sale in the United States, brokers must perform what's called a "locate"—confirming that shares are actually available to borrow. This requirement exists to prevent failures to deliver, situations where a seller cannot actually produce the shares they've sold.

Beyond Borrowing: Other Ways to Go Short

Physical short selling—borrowing and selling actual shares—is the most direct way to profit from falling prices, but it's not the only way. Financial derivatives offer alternative routes to the same destination.

A futures contract is an agreement to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price on a specific future date. If you believe oil prices will fall, you can enter a futures contract obligating you to sell oil at today's price three months from now. If prices do fall, you can buy oil cheaply in the market and deliver it at the higher contracted price, pocketing the difference. You've achieved a short position without ever borrowing a barrel of oil.

Options work similarly but with more flexibility. A put option gives you the right, but not the obligation, to sell an asset at a specified price. If you buy a put option on a stock and the price falls below your strike price, you profit. If the price rises instead, you simply let the option expire and lose only the premium you paid for it. This caps your maximum loss in a way that physical short selling cannot.

Contracts for difference, commonly called CFDs, provide yet another route. These are agreements between two parties to pay each other the difference in an asset's price between when the contract opens and when it closes. If you enter a CFD betting that a stock will fall, and it does, your counterparty pays you the difference. No shares ever change hands.

These derivative instruments are often "cash-settled," meaning the underlying asset is never actually delivered. The parties simply exchange money based on price movements. This makes short exposure accessible in markets where physical short selling would be impractical.

A Four-Century History

Short selling is not a modern invention. Its origins trace back to 1609 and the Dutch East India Company, one of the first publicly traded corporations in history.

Isaac Le Maire was a wealthy Dutch merchant and one of the company's original major shareholders. After a falling out with the company's management, Le Maire became convinced that its shares were overvalued. He began systematically shorting the stock, selling borrowed shares in large quantities while spreading negative rumors about the company's prospects. The share price dropped, and Le Maire profited.

The company's other shareholders were not amused. They successfully lobbied the Dutch government to ban short selling in 1610, making the Netherlands the first jurisdiction to restrict the practice. This established a pattern that would repeat across centuries and continents: short sellers profit, prices fall, other market participants blame the short sellers rather than underlying fundamentals, and governments respond with restrictions.

The pattern emerged dramatically in London in 1772. The banking house of Neal, James, Fordyce and Down had taken massive short positions in the British East India Company, apparently using customer deposits to cover their losses when the trade went wrong. When the bank collapsed, it triggered a crisis that brought down almost every private bank in Scotland and caused a liquidity crunch in both London and Amsterdam, the world's two leading financial centers at the time.

The Wall Street crash of 1929 intensified hostility toward short sellers. They became convenient scapegoats for the market's collapse. Congress enacted the "uptick rule," which prohibited short selling a stock unless the previous trade had been at a higher price. The logic was that short sellers could no longer pile on during a freefall, pushing prices down further. This rule remained in effect for over seven decades until the Securities and Exchange Commission removed it in 2007.

President Herbert Hoover publicly condemned short sellers. Even J. Edgar Hoover, the director of the Federal Bureau of Investigation, announced an investigation into short selling's role in prolonging the Great Depression. Short sellers had become enemies of the state.

The Birth of Hedge Funds

But short selling wasn't only a tool for speculators betting on disaster. In 1949, a former journalist named Alfred Winslow Jones realized that short selling could serve a fundamentally different purpose: reducing risk.

Jones created what is generally considered the first hedge fund. His insight was simple but revolutionary. Instead of just buying stocks he thought would rise, he would simultaneously short stocks he thought would fall. If the overall market dropped, his short positions would generate profits that offset losses on his long positions. If the market rose, his long positions would cover any losses on his shorts.

By combining long and short positions, Jones could "hedge" against market-wide movements and focus purely on picking winners and losers among individual stocks. His fund wasn't betting on the market going up or down—it was betting on the ability to distinguish good companies from bad ones, regardless of what the broader market did.

This hedging function explains why short selling persists despite persistent criticism. Portfolio managers use short positions to reduce their exposure to risks they don't want to take. A fund might be bullish on technology stocks generally but think one particular tech company is overvalued. By shorting that company while going long on others, they can profit if their analysis is correct without being exposed to sector-wide swings.

The 2008 Crisis and Regulatory Response

The 2008 financial crisis renewed the ancient debate about short selling's role in market instability. As financial stocks plummeted, critics argued that short sellers were accelerating the collapse of banks like Lehman Brothers and Morgan Stanley. By betting against these firms, short sellers supposedly created self-fulfilling prophecies, destroying confidence and driving the institutions toward failure.

Defenders of short selling countered that the financial firms failed because of their own poor decisions—excessive leverage, toxic assets, inadequate risk management—not because of short sellers. The shorts were merely recognizing reality sooner than others.

Regulators didn't wait to adjudicate the debate. In September 2008, the Securities and Exchange Commission took the extraordinary step of temporarily banning short sales in financial stocks entirely. The ban lasted several weeks.

It didn't work. Studies conducted afterward found that the ban failed to stabilize prices and may have actually impaired market functioning by reducing liquidity and widening bid-ask spreads. When you prohibit trading activity, markets become less efficient, not more.

The 2008 crisis also brought increased scrutiny to "naked" short selling—selling shares short without first borrowing them or even confirming that they can be borrowed. In a naked short sale, the seller simply promises to deliver shares at settlement, typically one or two days after the trade. If they cannot locate shares to borrow in that window, they "fail to deliver."

Regulators viewed naked shorting as particularly problematic because it allowed traders to flood the market with sell orders without the natural constraint of having to find shares to borrow. Several countries, including Australia and Germany, moved to ban the practice entirely. Others imposed stricter locate requirements.

The United Kingdom, France, Germany, Italy, and other European nations implemented their own temporary short selling bans during the crisis. These too proved largely ineffective at supporting prices. Academic research consistently found that bans on short selling failed to achieve their stated goals while imposing real costs on market quality.

The Case Against Short Sellers

Why does short selling attract such persistent hostility? Several arguments recur across centuries of criticism.

First, short selling seems to profit from others' misfortune. When a company struggles and workers lose their jobs, short sellers make money. This feels morally problematic to many observers, even if the short sellers didn't cause the problems they're profiting from.

Second, short sellers may engage in manipulation. A trader who builds a large short position has an incentive to spread negative information about the company, whether true or false, to drive down the price. Isaac Le Maire did exactly this in 1609. Modern "short and distort" schemes use research reports, social media, and press contacts to attack companies and profit from the resulting price declines. While publishing genuinely fraudulent information is illegal, the line between aggressive criticism and market manipulation can be blurry.

Third, short selling can accelerate price declines during crises. Even if short sellers don't cause problems, their selling pressure during moments of panic can intensify downward spirals. In fragile markets, this additional pressure might push otherwise viable companies or institutions over the edge.

Fourth, short selling creates what some view as an unnatural relationship with securities. In a world without short selling, there's a rough correspondence between the number of shares a company has issued and the number of people who own them. But when shares are borrowed and sold short, multiple parties have legitimate claims on the same shares. The original owner still has beneficial ownership rights, but so does the person who bought the borrowed shares. This "shadow ownership" can distort corporate governance and create uncertainty about who really controls a company.

The Case for Short Sellers

Defenders of short selling offer equally powerful counterarguments.

Most fundamentally, short sellers contribute to price discovery. Markets function by aggregating information from many participants. If only optimists can easily express their views through buying, while pessimists face barriers to selling short, prices will systematically skew upward. Short sellers ensure that negative information gets incorporated into prices, making markets more accurate reflectors of reality.

History provides dramatic examples. Short sellers identified problems at Enron before the energy giant's fraud became public. They spotted the unsustainable subprime mortgage bubble before 2008. They flagged issues at Wirecard, the German payments company that collapsed in a massive fraud in 2020. In each case, short sellers were among the first to recognize that celebrated companies were not what they seemed.

Short sellers also provide liquidity. When prices are falling, many investors want to sell. Short sellers, looking to buy back shares to close their positions profitably, become natural buyers during downturns. This provides a cushion that can actually moderate price declines, contrary to the common assumption that short sellers only push prices down.

Academic research generally supports the pro-short-selling position. Studies find that banning short selling increases price inefficiency, reduces liquidity, and fails to support prices in the long run. Markets function better when participants can express negative views as easily as positive ones.

There's also an ethical dimension that defenders emphasize. Short sellers often expose fraud and wrongdoing. They have powerful financial incentives to uncover hidden problems. While this means they profit from others' losses, it also means they perform a valuable investigative function that regulators, auditors, and journalists sometimes miss.

Disclosure and Transparency

In recent years, regulators have generally moved away from outright bans toward disclosure requirements. Rather than prohibiting short selling, many jurisdictions now require short sellers to publicly report their positions above certain thresholds.

In the European Union, significant short positions must be disclosed to regulators, and very large positions must be publicly announced. The idea is that markets benefit from knowing the extent of short interest in particular stocks. If heavy short interest signals informed skepticism about a company, other investors deserve to know.

Stock exchanges report "short interest" data—the total number of shares currently held short in a particular stock, expressed as a percentage of the company's outstanding shares or as a multiple of average daily trading volume. High short interest can signal that informed traders see problems ahead. It can also indicate potential for a short squeeze if positive news forces short sellers to cover.

This information is valuable but imperfect. The data typically reports with a delay, and in markets with significant naked short selling, the reported figures may understate true short exposure. Nevertheless, most observers consider transparency a reasonable middle ground between unrestricted short selling and outright prohibition.

Short Selling in Different Markets

Short selling is common in public equity markets, where shares are standardized and liquid. It's also widespread in futures markets, where traders routinely take short positions on commodities, currencies, and financial indices. Currency markets are particularly active, as George Soros demonstrated when he bet against the pound sterling.

But short selling is uncommon or impossible in many other contexts. Real estate, for instance, cannot practically be sold short. You can't borrow a house, sell it, and buy a replacement later. The same applies to most physical goods, private company shares, and illiquid securities. Short selling requires fungibility—the ability to substitute equivalent assets—and reasonable liquidity—confidence that you can find assets to buy when you need them.

During the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, several countries reimposed temporary restrictions on short selling as stock markets plunged. European regulators tightened rules "in an effort to stem the historic losses arising from the coronavirus pandemic." Once again, the measures proved largely ineffective at supporting prices, but they remained politically popular as a response to crisis.

The Eternal Scapegoat

More than four centuries after Isaac Le Maire pioneered the practice, short selling remains controversial. Each market crisis renews calls for restrictions. Each time, academic evidence suggests the restrictions don't work as intended. Yet the cycle continues.

Perhaps this is because short selling violates deep intuitions about how investment should work. We understand buying things and hoping they increase in value. We're less comfortable with the inverse—profiting when things fall apart. The short seller's gain feels like someone else's loss in a way that the long investor's gain does not, even though both are simply participants in a market pricing mechanism.

Short sellers will likely remain convenient scapegoats when markets decline. They are visible, they profit from falling prices, and their activities are easy to restrict. That the restrictions consistently fail to achieve their goals matters less than the political satisfaction of doing something.

Meanwhile, the arguments for short selling remain compelling. Price discovery requires pessimists as well as optimists. Liquidity requires buyers during downturns. Fraud detection benefits from investigators with strong financial incentives. Markets that prohibit short selling tend to be less efficient, less liquid, and more prone to bubbles than those that permit it.

The practice that began in seventeenth-century Amsterdam continues to serve these functions today, despite four hundred years of suspicion and periodic suppression. As long as markets exist, some participants will want to bet against the crowd. And as long as they do, others will want to stop them.
