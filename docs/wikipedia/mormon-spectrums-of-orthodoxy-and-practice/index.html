Mormon spectrums of orthodoxy and practice

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Mormon spectrums of orthodoxy and practice

In the quiet, sun-drenched suburbs of Utah and the sprawling diaspora of Latter-day Saint communities worldwide, a silent schism has been widening for decades. It is not marked by the clashing of armies or the tearing down of temples, but by the internal friction of belief itself. Here, in the heart of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS Church), exists a complex topography of faith where the lines between orthodoxy and skepticism are drawn not in ink, but in the daily choices of millions of individuals. To understand Mormonism today is to understand that it is no longer a monolith; it is a vast spectrum of practice, a dynamic interplay of those who hold fast to every doctrinal tenet, those who navigate a middle ground of cultural attachment, and those who have fundamentally rejected the supernatural claims while retaining the community. This is the story of the Mormon spectrums of orthodoxy and practice, a narrative defined by the tension between institutional expectation and personal integrity.

The foundation of this spectrum lies in the definition of identity itself. For generations, being Mormon was synonymous with active membership: attending sacrament meetings every Sunday, holding temple recommends, and adhering to a strict code of health and conduct known as the Word of Wisdom. Yet, a significant demographic has emerged that defies this binary categorization. These are the cultural Mormons. They may have been raised in the faith, converted young, or spent decades serving in full-time missions, only to find their theological beliefs diverging from official church doctrine. Despite this intellectual drift, they do not abandon the culture. They celebrate Christmas and Easter with a distinct Mormon flavor, they maintain strong family ties within the ward, and they often possess a profound appreciation for the moral lessons and love experienced during their years of active membership.

Cultural Mormons are not necessarily defined by anti-Mormon sentiment. In fact, many remain supportive of the church's humanitarian goals and community service initiatives. They simply no longer subscribe to the metaphysical framework that undergirds the institution. This creates a unique social phenomenon where individuals can be deeply embedded in Mormon life without being doctrinally aligned. They are the neighbors who bring casseroles when someone dies, yet they might privately doubt the historicity of the Book of Mormon or the divine authority of the priesthood. For some, this identity is lifelong; they carry the Mormon imprint on their psyche like a birthmark, visible to those who know where to look, but fading for others as they drift further from the center.

To map these beliefs, sociologists and church observers often utilize a left-center-right axis, though this political analogy only goes so far. On the left side of the religious spectrum sit the secular and progressive Mormons. These are individuals who view their faith through a lens of humanism or social justice, prioritizing ethical living over doctrinal purity. They may question the historical narratives of early church leaders or the church's stance on gender and sexuality. In the center reside the mainstream Mormons, the typical active members whose beliefs align closely with official teachings and who participate fully in the sacraments and temple rites. On the right, however, lie the religious dissidents. These are not the secularists but rather fundamentalists and those within the Remnant movement who believe the church has strayed from "original teachings." They often advocate for a return to polygamy or other practices discontinued by the mainstream LDS Church in the 20th century. This right wing is distinct; while they disagree with modern changes, their disagreement stems from an excess of belief rather than a lack thereof.

The LDS Church leadership has long recognized this variance in belief. Officially, the church teaches that doubt is a natural part of the human experience and can be resolved through "instruction, study, and prayer." The prevailing theological argument is that these practices result in increased testimony, which in turn drives out further doubts. This is the ideal trajectory: a journey from questioning to certainty. However, reality is often messier. While the church tolerates a certain amount of private disbelief—understanding that faith requires agency—it draws a hard line at public opposition or fundamental denial of core doctrines.

These core doctrines are non-negotiable pillars. They include the role of Jesus Christ as savior and redeemer, the necessity of the Atonement, and the prophetic calling of church leaders. To deny these is to place oneself outside the bounds of full fellowship. Disbelief in these areas prevents a member from participating in sacred ordinances, such as receiving blessings from those holding the priesthood or entering the temple for marriage sealings and endowments. The temple, in particular, serves as the ultimate gatekeeper of orthodoxy. To enter, one must pass an interview where they affirm their belief in specific doctrines. Those who cannot answer "yes" to questions about the Book of Mormon's divinity or the living prophet are turned away.

This creates a profound psychological dilemma for many faithful members. Faced with growing doubts that challenge these core tenets, some choose silence. They keep their struggles secret to maintain their standing in the community and to avoid conflict with family and friends who remain fervently active. This is the "silent spectrum," where individuals attend services, hold callings (volunteer positions), and serve on committees while internally harboring significant skepticism. The fear of stigmatization is real; in close-knit wards, a public admission of doubt can lead to social isolation or even disciplinary action.

The church's response to public dissent is codified in its disciplinary policies. Apostasy, defined as publicly opposing church doctrines or organizing groups that teach contrary beliefs, can result in formal disciplinary councils. These meetings can range from informal counsel to disfellowshipment (suspension of privileges) or excommunication (removal from membership). The threat of these consequences drives many toward anonymity. In an era before the internet, such dissent was often whispered in private homes. Today, it has found a robust home online.

The digital landscape has become the primary sanctuary for those navigating these spectrums. Internet communities allow Mormons to discuss their issues without fear of immediate social repercussion. One of the most significant of these groups is the New Order Mormons. The name itself is telling, patterned after the "New Order Amish," a group that maintains cultural ties to the Amish religion while rejecting certain core tenets like the ban on modern technology. Similarly, New Order Mormons are current and former members who no longer believe at least some of the LDS Church's tenets but choose not to completely separate themselves from the faith. They maintain membership for family reasons, social connections, or a deep-seated love for the culture.

"A forum for those who have chosen to remain connected with the LDS church for personal reasons and in spite of church history or present practices."

This quote, often found on New Order Mormon discussion boards, encapsulates the movement's ethos. It is not about rebellion; it is about persistence. These individuals navigate a path that allows them to honor their past and their community while refusing to compromise their intellectual honesty. They engage in a form of "faithful skepticism," holding two contradictory ideas in mind: a love for the Mormon people and a rejection of certain church claims.

Closely related to this is Humanistic Mormonism, a movement that takes the secular spectrum even further. This group comprises freethinkers, cultural Mormons, disfellowshipped members, and independents who emphasize Mormon culture and history while explicitly rejecting the demand for belief in a supernatural god. For humanistic Mormons, the Book of Mormon is not a divine record but a work of literature or historical fiction; Jesus may be seen as an ethical teacher rather than a resurrected deity. They focus on the social cohesion and moral framework that the religion provides, stripping away the miraculous elements.

The Society for Humanistic Mormonism serves as an organized hub for this perspective, offering a space where members can explore their heritage without the pressure of supernatural belief. Their existence challenges the traditional definition of what it means to be Mormon, suggesting that culture and community can survive even when theology dissolves. This is a radical proposition in a faith that hinges on the reality of modern revelation.

Yet, for many, there comes a point where the spectrum cannot hold them any longer. The "Post-Mormon Community" represents those who have left the LDS Church entirely and no longer believe in its foundational claims. These individuals often experience a profound sense of loss, not just of faith, but of community, identity, and family dynamics that were once centered around the church. Leaving is rarely a clean break; it is a process of untangling years of social programming. For some, this leads to a complete severance from Mormon culture. They may change their names, move away from predominantly LDS areas, or distance themselves from family members who cannot understand their departure.

The tension between these groups creates a dynamic, often painful, reality for the modern Latter-day Saint. The church's stance remains one of seeking unity through doctrinal conformity. Leaders teach that doubt is a problem to be solved, not a state to be embraced. Yet, the human experience suggests otherwise. Doubt is often the catalyst for deeper reflection and growth. The existence of these spectrums proves that faith is not static; it evolves, fractures, and reforms in response to new information and personal experience.

The consequences of this fragmentation are felt on a deeply personal level. Families find themselves divided across the spectrum, with parents holding orthodox views while children grapple with skepticism, or vice versa. Weddings may be celebrated in civil ceremonies rather than temples because one partner cannot affirm the necessary doctrines. Funerals become complex negotiations between traditional rites and the wishes of those who no longer believe in the afterlife as taught by the church. The human cost of this doctrinal rigidity is measured in these quiet moments of separation, where love struggles against dogma.

Despite the challenges, the cultural Mormon identity remains resilient. Even for those who have left the faith or moved to the far edges of the spectrum, the "Mormon" label often sticks. It influences their parenting styles, their social networks, and their worldview. The lessons of service, family centrality, and moral living are deeply ingrained. This cultural residue acts as a bridge, allowing for dialogue between the orthodox and the skeptical. It prevents the complete alienation that might otherwise occur in other religious schisms.

The spectrum is not merely a theoretical construct; it is a lived reality for millions. From the fundamentalist communities in the American Southwest to the progressive atheists in Salt Lake City, from the secret doubters in the pews of suburban wards to the open critics on internet forums, Mormonism is defined by its diversity of belief. The church's ability to tolerate a certain amount of private disbelief has allowed this spectrum to widen without causing an immediate collapse of the institution. However, as information becomes more accessible and the younger generation increasingly questions historical narratives, the pressure on these boundaries continues to grow.

Ultimately, the story of Mormon spectrums is a story about agency. It is about individuals exercising their right to believe or not believe, to stay or to leave, to conform or to dissent. It challenges the notion that religion must be an all-or-nothing proposition. In the Mormon context, one can be fully immersed in the culture while holding no supernatural beliefs, or one can be a devout believer while questioning specific historical details. The spectrum offers a space for this complexity, acknowledging that faith is rarely black and white.

As we look to the future of the Latter-day Saint movement, these spectrums will likely continue to expand. The "New Order" and "Humanistic" movements are not fringe anomalies; they represent significant portions of the faithful who are redefining what Mormonism means in the 21st century. They are building new communities based on shared values rather than shared dogma. Whether the institutional church can adapt to this reality, or whether it will maintain its strict boundaries at the cost of losing these members, remains one of the defining questions of modern religious history.

The silence of the doubter and the noise of the dissident are both part of the same chorus. They are not enemies of the faith, but rather participants in a long, ongoing conversation about truth, meaning, and community. In the end, the Mormon spectrum is a testament to the enduring power of belief, even when that belief takes forms the institution never intended. It is a reminder that religion is not just what is taught from the pulpit, but what is lived in the heart, in all its messy, contradictory, and deeply human complexity.
