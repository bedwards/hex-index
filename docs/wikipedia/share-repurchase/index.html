The $1 Trillion Magic Trick

In 2018 alone, American corporations spent over one trillion dollars buying back their own stock. That's a number so large it's almost meaningless—roughly five thousand dollars for every adult in the country, vanishing into a financial maneuver that most people have never heard of.

What exactly is happening here?

Imagine you own a pizza shop with three equal partners. The shop earns $120,000 a year in profit. Each partner gets $40,000. Now imagine one partner wants out. Instead of finding a new investor, the remaining two partners buy that third stake using company profits. Suddenly, only two people split the earnings—$60,000 each.

Did the pizza shop get better? Make more pizza? Serve more customers? No. The same business now just enriches fewer people more richly.

This is essentially what a share repurchase does—a company uses its cash to buy back its own stock from shareholders, reducing the number of shares floating around in the market. It's also called a stock buyback or share buyback, and it has quietly become one of the most consequential financial practices in the modern economy.

Why Companies Do This Instead of Just Paying Dividends

Companies that make money face a choice. They can reinvest that money in new equipment, research, or expansion. They can save it for a rainy day. Or they can return it to the people who own the company—the shareholders.

Historically, returning money meant dividends. The company writes a check to every shareholder, proportional to their stake. Simple and direct.

But buybacks offer something dividends can't: tax advantages.

When you receive a dividend, you owe taxes on it that year. There's no escaping it. When a company buys back shares instead, something different happens. You still own your shares—you just own a larger percentage of a smaller pie. The stock price rises because fewer shares exist. If you never sell, you never pay tax. And when you eventually do sell, you pay capital gains tax, which is often lower than the tax rate on dividends.

For wealthy shareholders who don't need the cash immediately, this is enormously valuable. They get richer on paper, year after year, while deferring the tax bill indefinitely.

The Mechanics: How Buybacks Actually Work

There are several ways a company can repurchase its shares, but one method dominates so completely that the others are almost afterthoughts. More than 95 percent of all buyback programs worldwide happen through open-market repurchases.

Here's how it works: a company announces it will buy back shares over the coming months or years, then quietly purchases its own stock on the open market—the same stock exchange where you or I could buy shares. The company acts like any other investor, placing orders through brokers.

To prevent companies from manipulating their stock price through aggressive buying, the United States Securities and Exchange Commission (the SEC, the federal agency that regulates stock markets) created Rule 10b-18. This rule says a company can't buy more than 25 percent of its average daily trading volume. If a stock typically trades a million shares a day, the company can only buy 250,000 shares as part of its buyback that day.

This creates a strange situation. A company might announce a $10 billion buyback program, but it can take years to complete. The announcement itself often moves the stock price—investors hear "buyback" and assume the company thinks its stock is undervalued, so they buy. The company hasn't even started purchasing yet, and the stock is already rising.

When Speed Matters: Accelerated Buybacks

Sometimes companies want to buy back stock quickly. Maybe they've just sold a division and are sitting on a mountain of cash. Maybe their CEO believes the stock is deeply undervalued and wants to act before the market figures it out.

Enter the accelerated share repurchase, or ASR. The company hands a large sum of cash—sometimes billions of dollars—to an investment bank. The bank immediately delivers a huge block of shares to the company. But here's the twist: the bank doesn't actually own those shares yet.

The bank borrows the shares from other investors, hands them over, then spends weeks or months buying shares on the open market to replace what it borrowed. The final price adjusts based on what the bank actually paid. It's essentially financial time travel—the company gets its shares now, but the transaction settles later at whatever price prevails.

The Tender Offer: Making Shareholders an Offer

Before open-market buybacks dominated, companies used tender offers. Instead of quietly buying shares on the exchange, the company makes a public announcement: "We will buy up to X million shares at Y dollars per share, which is 15 percent above the current market price."

Shareholders then decide whether to "tender" their shares—to offer them up for purchase at that premium price. If too many shareholders want to sell, everyone who tendered gets their shares bought on a proportional basis. If too few shareholders want to sell, the company might extend the deadline or simply cancel the whole thing.

The twist? Company executives and directors usually can't participate. They can't sell their own shares back to the company through a tender offer. This prevents the obvious conflict of interest where insiders would set a price designed to benefit themselves.

The Dutch Auction: Finding the Right Price

In 1981, a company called Todd Shipyards invented a clever variation. Instead of picking one price for its tender offer, the company specified a range—say, $50 to $60 per share.

Shareholders could then tender their shares at any price within that range. "I'll sell at $52." "I'll sell at $57." The company collected all these offers and built what economists call a demand curve—a picture of how many shares were available at each price point.

The company then picked the lowest price that would get them enough shares. Everyone who had offered at or below that price sold their shares, all at the same final price. This method, called a Dutch auction (named after a traditional flower auction technique used in the Netherlands), helps the company pay fair market value rather than guessing at the right premium.

The Strategic Game

Buybacks aren't just about returning cash. They're chess moves in the corporate strategy game.

Consider a company sitting on $5 billion in cash with nothing compelling to invest in. That cash pile makes the company a target. A competitor or private equity firm might look at that balance sheet and think: "We could borrow money to buy this company, then use their own cash to pay down our debt." It's called a leveraged buyout, and it has devoured countless cash-rich companies.

By spending cash on buybacks, companies keep their balance sheets lean and their stock prices high. Both make hostile takeovers more difficult and expensive.

Buybacks also send signals—or at least, companies hope they do. When management announces a buyback, they're implicitly saying: "We think our stock is undervalued. We're so confident we're willing to bet the company's money on it."

But here's the problem with signals: they're easy to fake.

Unlike dividends, which commit the company to actual cash outlays, buyback announcements are often just... announcements. A company can declare a $5 billion buyback program and never complete it. There's no penalty. No enforcement. Researchers have found that many announced buybacks never happen at all, or happen only partially.

This is what economists call "cheap talk." The signal costs nothing to send, so it doesn't reliably indicate anything.

The Dark Side: Where Executive Compensation Enters

This is where stock buybacks get controversial, and where they connect directly to the question of excessive CEO pay.

Many executives receive compensation tied to earnings per share (EPS) targets or stock price performance. Earnings per share is calculated by dividing total profits by the number of shares outstanding. Here's the math that matters: when a company buys back shares, the denominator shrinks. Even if profits stay flat, earnings per share go up.

Let's say a company earns $1 billion and has 500 million shares outstanding. That's $2 per share. The CEO's bonus kicks in if EPS hits $2.50. Without any improvement in the actual business, the company could buy back 100 million shares, leaving 400 million outstanding. Same $1 billion in profits now equals $2.50 per share. Bonus achieved.

The CEO got richer. Shareholders who stayed got richer on paper. But did the company actually perform better? Did it innovate more? Hire more people? Serve customers better?

Not necessarily.

This creates what economists call a misalignment of incentives. The CEO's interests diverge from the long-term health of the company. A CEO nearing retirement might push for aggressive buybacks that boost the stock price today, even if that means underinvesting in research and development that would pay off in five years.

The Case Study Everyone Cites: Hewlett-Packard

Between 1999 and 2011, the technology company Hewlett-Packard (now known as HP) spent $67 billion buying back its own stock. That's not a typo—sixty-seven billion dollars.

During this period, HP cycled through several CEOs. Carly Fiorina took over in 1999, championing the controversial merger with Compaq. Mark Hurd followed, focusing relentlessly on cost-cutting. Leo Apotheker finished this era with a brief and tumultuous tenure.

What happened to HP during these buyback-heavy years? The company's business performance was, charitably, mixed. Its position in personal computers eroded. Its printer business faced commoditization. Innovation—the lifeblood of any technology company—largely vanished.

Critics point to HP as Exhibit A in the case against buybacks. Here was a company that could have invested $67 billion in developing new products, acquiring innovative startups, or entering new markets. Instead, it funneled that money into buying its own shares, enriching shareholders in the short term while hollowing out its competitive position.

What might have been different if even half that money had gone into research? We'll never know.

The Regulatory Vacuum

Stock buybacks haven't always been common. In fact, for most of American corporate history, they were rare and somewhat suspect.

The Securities and Exchange Commission spent years in the 1970s worrying that large-scale buybacks would allow companies to manipulate their own stock prices. Think about it: a company has enormous inside information about its own prospects. It knows things that public investors don't. What's to stop it from buying shares when it knows good news is coming, artificially inflating the price?

Then came 1982 and Rule 10b-18.

This rule didn't legalize buybacks—they weren't exactly illegal before. Instead, it created what lawyers call a "safe harbor." If companies followed certain guidelines about timing, price, and volume of their purchases, they would be presumed innocent of market manipulation. They would have legal cover.

Critics argue this opened the floodgates. Buybacks went from a rarely used tool to the dominant method of returning cash to shareholders. In 2018, buybacks exceeded dividends for the first time. Companies that had been around for a century, companies known for their generous dividends, started replacing those dividends with buybacks.

The criticism goes deeper than just tax preferences. Lenore Palladino, an economist at the Roosevelt Institute (a progressive think tank focused on economic policy), argues that buybacks are "one of the drivers of our imbalanced economy, in which corporate profits and shareholder payments continue to grow while wages for typical workers stay flat."

The logic is straightforward: every dollar spent on buybacks is a dollar not spent on wages, or training, or factories, or research. When companies prioritize returning cash to shareholders above all else, workers get squeezed.

The International Perspective

America leads the world in buybacks, but it's not alone. The practice started later in Europe and other markets, partly because regulations were stricter and partly because dividend cultures were stronger.

The United Kingdom, for instance, requires shareholder approval for selective buybacks—cases where the company only offers to buy from certain shareholders rather than everyone equally. This requires either unanimous approval or a special resolution with a 75 percent supermajority. Crucially, the shareholders who are selling can't vote in favor. This prevents insiders from using company money to buy themselves out at premium prices.

Countries approach buybacks differently based on their broader views about corporations. In stakeholder-oriented economies like Germany or Japan, where companies are expected to balance the interests of workers, communities, and shareholders, buybacks face more skepticism. In shareholder-primacy economies like the United States and United Kingdom, where maximizing shareholder returns is seen as the paramount corporate duty, buybacks are more accepted.

The Arithmetic of Buybacks

One common misconception: buybacks don't make the overall stock market go up through direct buying pressure. The purchases are too small relative to daily trading volumes to move prices much in the short term.

The real effect is arithmetic, not mechanical. If a company has 100 shares worth $10 each (a $1,000 market capitalization) and buys back 10 shares for $100, it now has 90 shares. The total company is now worth $900. Each share is still worth... $10.

Wait, that doesn't seem right.

Here's the key: the company used cash to buy those shares. If the company had $200 in cash before, it now has $100. The company's total value didn't change—it just shifted from cash to a smaller share count. Each share represents a slightly larger claim on the company's business operations and a smaller claim on its cash pile.

The share price really does rise when buybacks are tied to earnings per share improvements, because investors value stocks based on EPS multiples. If EPS goes up 10 percent, many investors will pay 10 percent more per share. The stock market is, in many ways, a machine for capitalizing earnings.

Employee Share Buybacks: A Special Case

Not all buybacks happen on the open market. Companies often buy shares from their own employees or directors.

When employees exercise stock options or receive shares as compensation, the company often provides a convenient way to sell. An "employee share scheme buyback" allows the company to repurchase these shares directly, avoiding brokerage fees and market impact for the employee.

These buybacks require only a simple majority vote of shareholders (an "ordinary resolution"), less than the 75 percent supermajority needed for selective buybacks from outside shareholders. The logic is that employee compensation programs serve everyone's interest by aligning incentives.

There are also "minimum holding buybacks" for cleaning up tiny stakes. If someone owns seven shares of a company worth $80, they're a nuisance to administer. Annual reports, dividend checks, voting materials—all for $80? Companies can offer to buy out these unmarketable parcels without even holding a shareholder vote.

The Fundamental Question

Every financial practice serves someone's interests. The question is whose.

Buybacks clearly benefit shareholders who want to defer taxes. They benefit executives whose compensation is tied to stock prices or earnings per share. They benefit investment banks who earn fees structuring accelerated repurchases. They may benefit the economy by getting cash out of low-return corporate treasuries and into the hands of investors who can redeploy it.

But do they benefit workers? Do they benefit innovation? Do they benefit the long-term health of companies and the economy?

This is where buybacks connect to broader questions about capitalism's purpose. If corporations exist solely to maximize shareholder value, buybacks might be exactly the right tool. If corporations have broader obligations—to workers, to communities, to future generations—then diverting $1 trillion a year into buybacks might be a monumental misallocation of resources.

The debate isn't going away. If anything, as income inequality grows and corporate cash piles swell, the scrutiny of buybacks will only intensify. What happens next depends on whether policymakers decide the current rules serve the economy—or just serve those who already have the most.
