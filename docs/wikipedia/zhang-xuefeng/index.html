Zhang Xuefeng

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Zhang Xuefeng

On March 24, 2026, the digital silence that followed the sudden cardiac death of Zhang Xuefeng in Suzhou was deafening. At forty-one years old, the man who had become the de facto oracle for millions of Chinese families regarding their children's futures was gone. He died just as his influence was reaching a fever pitch, leaving behind a legacy that was as contentious as it was transformative. For nearly a decade, Zhang had been the voice of a generation grappling with an economic reality that felt increasingly hostile to the idealistic dreams of the past. He was not an academic in the traditional sense; he was a pragmatist, a salesman of survival, and a man who dared to tell students that their passion might be a liability in the job market. His death did not just end a life; it marked the closing of a distinct chapter in China's educational history, a period where the utilitarian calculation of a degree's value was stripped of all pretense and laid bare on the table.

Zhang Xuefeng was born Zhang Zibiao on May 18, 1984, in Fuyu County, Qiqihar, in the frosty reaches of Heilongjiang province. His ancestral roots lay in Linqing, Shandong, but his formative years were shaped by the gritty reality of a working-class family in the Northeast. His father worked in the railway sector, a job that offered stability but little glamour, while his mother, having been laid off from her previous position, sold goods in a local market. This upbringing instilled in Zhang a profound, almost visceral understanding of economic fragility. He did not come from a background of academic privilege; he came from a world where a wrong turn could mean a lifetime of struggle. He was admitted to Zhengzhou University to study water supply and drainage engineering, a technical discipline that promised a steady career. Yet, in a move that would foreshadow his later philosophy, Zhang did not complete his degree. He left the university system before graduation, a decision that would later be framed by critics as a failure and by supporters as the first step toward a more honest education.

For nearly a decade after leaving university, Zhang worked within the graduate examination training industry, a sector dedicated to helping students navigate the grueling postgraduate entrance exams, known as kǎoyán. It was a high-stakes environment where the difference between a good job and unemployment often hinged on a single score. Zhang honed his craft here, learning the intricacies of the exam system and, more importantly, observing the desperate needs of the students who walked through his doors. He saw the anxiety, the confusion, and the crushing weight of expectation. In 2016, the trajectory of his life changed irrevocably. A video of one of his lectures on postgraduate entrance examinations was uploaded to the internet and, almost overnight, went viral. In the clip, Zhang spoke with a directness that was alien to the polished, often evasive rhetoric of traditional educators. He did not coddle his audience; he dissected the market. He told them that the path they were on might lead to a dead end. That video was the spark that ignited a firestorm, turning Zhang from a regional tutor into a national phenomenon.

The core of Zhang's appeal lay in his refusal to engage in the polite fictions that often surround higher education. In a society where the Gaokao (college entrance exam) was traditionally viewed as the great equalizer, Zhang introduced a variable that many found uncomfortable: the market value of the major itself. He rose to prominence by championing a utilitarian approach to education that prioritized employment prospects over disciplinary passion. To Zhang, a university degree was not merely a pursuit of knowledge; it was an investment, and like any investment, it required a calculated return. He became the architect of a new educational philosophy for the middle class: "Direction matters more than effort." This mantra, which would later become the title of one of his bestselling books, encapsulated his worldview. He argued that no amount of hard work could compensate for a choice made in a sinking ship.

His advice was blunt, often provocative, and relentlessly specific. He categorized university majors into two distinct buckets: those that led to financial security and those that led to precarious employment. Fields such as economics, finance, accounting, business management, computer science, IT, engineering, law, Chinese studies, and medical/health-related majors were, in his view, the safe havens. These were the paths that led to stable incomes and social mobility. Conversely, he actively cautioned students against what he termed "unmarketable" majors. Biology, chemistry, environmental science, materials science, civil engineering, journalism, humanities, liberal arts, and the arts were frequently characterized as offering limited career opportunities. He did not mince words. He described these fields as traps for the idealistic, warning that a degree in biology or environmental science often resulted in a career of low wages and high frustration.

This rhetoric, delivered in a style that was often emotionally charged and sometimes bordering on alarmist, resonated deeply with parents and students who were increasingly anxious about the future. The Chinese job market had become fiercely competitive, and the promise that a degree guaranteed a good life had long since evaporated. Zhang gave a name to their fears and, more importantly, offered a strategy to navigate them. He became a savior to many, a man who was willing to tell the hard truths that other educators were too polite or too entrenched in the system to voice. His livestreams on platforms like Douyin and Kuaishou became virtual town halls where families debated their futures. By the 2020s, he had amassed a following that was nothing short of staggering. On Douyin alone, he had over 26.7 million followers. His presence on Kuaishou reached 9.4 million, while Weibo, Bilibili, and RedNote added millions more to his total reach. He was not just an influencer; he was an institution.

However, the very clarity that made Zhang popular also made him a target. His approach, while effective for many, was not without its critics. Education commentators and academics argued that his rhetoric oversimplified the complexity of the labor market and the intrinsic value of education. By dismissing entire fields of study as "unmarketable," critics claimed, he was distorting students' understanding of the world. They argued that his focus on immediate employability ignored the broader educational, scientific, and cultural contributions of the humanities and sciences. His comments on liberal arts education, which he frequently described as leading primarily to service-sector jobs, generated widespread debate. Academics in these fields felt their life's work was being reduced to a cost-benefit analysis, their intellectual contributions dismissed in favor of a spreadsheet.

The controversy reached a fever pitch regarding his comments on journalism. Zhang famously argued that the field was in decline and that students should avoid it, a statement that sparked a public debate and drew sharp responses from media professionals and scholars. He was accused of "scaremongering," of reinforcing negative stereotypes, and of discouraging young people from pursuing careers that were essential for a healthy society. Yet, for every critic, there was a parent who thanked him for saving their child from a lifetime of debt and underemployment. Zhang's philosophy was a mirror held up to a society in transition, reflecting the harsh realities of an economy that was no longer forgiving of idealism. He did not create the anxiety; he merely articulated it with a precision that others lacked.

Beyond his online presence, Zhang built a formidable commercial empire. He founded education companies in Suzhou and Beijing, expanding from a single tutor into a business that reportedly generated substantial revenue. He secured venture capital investment, signaling that his model was not just a viral sensation but a viable commercial enterprise. His business model was built on the trust of millions of families who were willing to pay for his guidance. In 2016, he published You Are Only One Book Away from Graduate Exam Success, a guide that capitalized on his early viral fame. This was followed by a series of books that would become staples in the Chinese educational canon: Direction Matters More Than Effort: Renowned Teacher Zhang Xuefeng's Guide to Passing the Postgraduate Entrance Examination (2021), Choice Matters More Than Effort: Renowned Teacher Zhang Xuefeng's Step-by-Step Guide to Filling Out College Application Forms (2021, revised 2023), Winning at University: How You Should Spend Your College Years (2024), and Choosing Majors from an Employment Perspective (2025). These titles were not merely descriptive; they were manifestos. They distilled his philosophy into actionable advice, offering a roadmap for survival in a competitive world.

Zhang's influence extended beyond the digital and commercial spheres into the political realm. On January 9, 2023, he was elected as a deputy to the 14th Jiangsu Provincial People's Congress. This appointment was a significant validation of his status, transforming him from a private sector influencer into a public figure with a voice in the governance of his region. It signaled that his message had been absorbed into the mainstream, even if it remained controversial. He was no longer just a critic of the system; he was part of the system. Yet, even as his influence grew, the platforms that had made him famous began to crack down on his style. In September 2025, his accounts were restricted from gaining new followers following reported violations of platform rules during his livestreams. The algorithmic gates that had once amplified his voice were suddenly closed, a stark reminder of the volatility of digital fame and the limits of free expression in the Chinese media landscape.

Behind the millions of followers and the viral videos was a man with a deeply personal life. Zhang was married to Li Lijing (also known as Li Xuejing), a historian and university associate professor. Their marriage was a union of two very different worlds: the pragmatic, market-driven educator and the academic historian. They had one daughter together. The contrast between his public persona and his private life was striking. While he preached a gospel of cold calculation on the internet, he was a father and a husband navigating the same complexities of life that he analyzed for others. His father's work in the railway and his mother's struggle in the market were the silent architects of his worldview. He knew what it meant to be vulnerable, to be on the outside looking in, and he used that knowledge to build a fortress of advice for others.

The suddenness of his death on March 24, 2026, left a void that was impossible to fill. He died in Suzhou, the city where he had built his empire, at the age of 41. The cause was sudden cardiac death, a condition that struck without warning and ended the life of a man who had spent his career warning others about the unpredictability of the future. His passing was met with an outpouring of grief from his millions of followers, many of whom saw him not just as an advisor, but as a protector. He had been their guide through the fog of uncertainty, the one who told them where the ice was thin and where the ground was solid.

In the wake of his death, the debate over his legacy began in earnest. Was he a cynic who reduced education to a transaction? Or was he a realist who saved countless families from the pitfalls of a broken system? The answer likely lies somewhere in the middle. Zhang Xuefeng was a product of his time, a man who rose to prominence because he was willing to speak the language of a society that had lost its faith in the traditional promises of education. He did not invent the pressure; he just gave it a voice. He did not create the disparity between majors; he just highlighted it with a flashlight that many found blinding.

His life story is a testament to the power of directness in an age of evasion. From a boy in Fuyu County who left university to become a tutor, to the most influential education consultant in China, Zhang Xuefeng's journey was one of relentless adaptation. He understood the market better than the academics, and he understood the fears of the parents better than the politicians. He built a business on the premise that direction matters more than effort, a lesson that resonated with millions who felt that no amount of hard work was enough to guarantee a future.

As the dust settles on his passing, the books he wrote remain on the shelves, their titles serving as a reminder of the philosophy he championed. Choice Matters More Than Effort. Direction Matters More Than Effort. These were not just slogans; they were the principles that guided his life and the lives of those who followed him. He died young, but his impact was profound. He forced a national conversation about the value of education, about the role of the humanities, and about the harsh realities of the job market. Whether one agreed with him or not, he could not be ignored. He was the man ordinary Chinese chose to trust, the voice of the pragmatic majority, and the architect of a new way of thinking about the future.

The legacy of Zhang Xuefeng is complex, layered, and undeniably significant. He was a figure who polarized opinion, who challenged the status quo, and who provided a lifeline to millions. In a world where education is often treated as a sacred, untouchable ideal, Zhang treated it as a tool, a means to an end. And perhaps, in the end, that was his greatest contribution. He stripped away the romance of education and replaced it with the reality of survival. He reminded us that in a competitive world, the choice of direction is the most critical decision of all. As the world moves forward, the questions he raised will remain. How do we value education? What is the purpose of a degree? And how do we navigate a future that is increasingly uncertain? Zhang Xuefeng may be gone, but the conversation he started will continue for years to come.

The story of Zhang Xuefeng is not just the story of a man; it is the story of an era. It is the story of a generation that grew up in the shadow of economic uncertainty, searching for answers in a world that seemed to have none. It is the story of a father who sold goods in a market and a son who became the voice of millions. It is a story of pragmatism, of controversy, and of the enduring human desire to secure a better future for the next generation. Zhang Xuefeng died on March 24, 2026, but his voice, his warnings, and his wisdom will echo long after the silence has settled. He was the man who told the truth, even when it hurt, and in doing so, he became the man who was trusted.
