Energy density

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Energy density

The Sandwich That Beats Dynamite

Here's a fact that sounds wrong but isn't: a peanut butter and jelly sandwich contains more energy per unit mass than a stick of dynamite.

This isn't a trick of measurement or a physicist's joke. It's a real consequence of how we think about energy density—the amount of energy packed into a given amount of stuff. And understanding why this apparent paradox is true opens up a fascinating window into how energy works, from the food on your plate to the nuclear reactions powering distant stars.

What Energy Density Actually Means

Energy density is simply a measure of how much energy you can squeeze into a given volume or mass. When we talk about volume, we're asking: how much energy fits in a liter of this substance? When we talk about mass, we're asking: how much energy does a kilogram of it contain? These two measures—volumetric energy density and gravimetric energy density (also called specific energy)—tell us different things and matter in different situations.

If you're designing a car, both matter enormously. You want fuel that packs a lot of energy into a small tank (high volumetric density), but you also don't want that fuel to weigh down your vehicle (so you need high gravimetric density too). This is why gasoline has been so hard to replace. It's remarkably good at both.

Consider this: one kilogram of diesel fuel, when burned with the oxygen in about fifteen kilograms of air, releases a tremendous amount of energy. Liquid hydrocarbons—gasoline, diesel, kerosene—remain the densest practical way we know to store and transport chemical energy at scale.

The Hidden Ingredient in Energy Calculations

Now back to that sandwich beating dynamite. The key lies in a detail that's easy to overlook: oxygen.

When we burn food or fuel, we need oxygen to make it happen. Your body doesn't carry oxygen around in your sandwiches—it pulls oxygen from the air you breathe. Similarly, a car engine doesn't store oxygen in the fuel tank; it draws in air through the intake manifold.

But dynamite is different. Explosives like TNT and gunpowder carry their own oxidizer built right into their chemical structure. They have to, because they need to release their energy in a fraction of a second, with no time to wait for air to mix in.

This changes everything about how we calculate energy density. When we measure a sandwich's energy content, we don't count the weight of the oxygen you'll breathe to metabolize it. But when we measure dynamite, all that oxidizer weight is already included. The oxidizer takes up space and adds mass, but it doesn't contribute energy—it absorbs some of the combustion energy just to break apart and release its oxygen atoms.

So the sandwich's energy density looks artificially high, and the dynamite's looks artificially low. In reality, dynamite releases its energy vastly faster—which is what makes it explosive—but spread over the same duration, your lunch actually contains more total energy per gram.

A Hierarchy of Energy Sources

Energy comes in different flavors, and they're not created equal. Roughly speaking, from most energy-dense to least, the hierarchy runs: nuclear, chemical, electrical, pressure, and electromagnetic.

Chemical energy is what most of us interact with daily. It's the energy released when atoms rearrange themselves into different molecules. Burning wood, digesting food, running a car engine—all chemical reactions. Electrochemical reactions are a subset of these, powering everything from the battery in your phone to the neurons firing in your brain as you read this.

Electrical energy storage, like in capacitors, tends to be much less dense than chemical storage, which is why batteries (chemical) have largely won out over supercapacitors (electrical) for most applications, despite supercapacitors being able to charge and discharge much faster.

But chemical energy is nothing compared to nuclear energy.

The Nuclear Leap

A single uranium fuel pellet, about the size of the tip of your finger—roughly an inch tall—contains as much energy as one ton of coal. Or one hundred twenty gallons of crude oil. Or seventeen thousand cubic feet of natural gas.

This isn't a small difference. Nuclear fuels typically have energy densities tens of thousands of times higher than chemical fuels. In a light-water nuclear reactor, one kilogram of natural uranium, after appropriate enrichment and processing, delivers the energy equivalent of nearly ten thousand kilograms of oil or fourteen thousand kilograms of coal.

Why such an enormous gap? Because nuclear reactions tap into a fundamentally different energy source: the binding energy that holds atomic nuclei together. Chemical reactions merely rearrange electrons around atoms. Nuclear reactions rearrange the protons and neutrons inside atoms themselves. It's the difference between redecorating a house and demolishing it to build something new from the rubble.

The famous equation from Einstein, E equals mc squared, tells us that mass itself is a form of energy. The speed of light squared is an enormous number—about ninety quadrillion in standard units—which means even tiny amounts of mass represent staggering amounts of energy. Nuclear fission converts roughly 0.1 percent of the fuel's mass into energy. Nuclear fusion does better, converting about 1 percent.

And if you could somehow annihilate matter with antimatter? That would convert 100 percent of the mass into pure energy.

The Fusion Promise and Fission Reality

Fusion is the process that powers the sun, where hydrogen nuclei slam together with enough force to overcome their electrical repulsion and fuse into helium, releasing enormous energy in the process. The sun has been doing this for about four and a half billion years and will continue for billions more.

Humans have been trying to replicate this process in a controlled way since the 1950s. As of now, sustained fusion power production remains elusive. The joke in the physics community is that fusion power is always thirty years away—and has been for the past sixty years. The challenge is creating conditions where the fusion reactions release more energy than it takes to confine and heat the plasma to the hundreds of millions of degrees required.

Fission, by contrast, has been generating electricity commercially since the 1950s. Uranium and thorium, the primary fission fuels, exist in sufficient quantities on Earth to power civilization for many decades or even centuries. The full potential of nuclear fission would require breeder reactors—designs that create more fissile material than they consume—but apart from a couple of Russian reactors called the BN-600 and BN-800, these aren't yet in widespread commercial use.

The Terrifying Density of Nuclear Power

The flip side of nuclear energy's extraordinary density is the extraordinary challenge of controlling it.

A typical nuclear power plant generates about one gigawatt of electrical power, which corresponds to roughly three gigawatts of thermal power (the difference being conversion losses). This thermal energy is concentrated in a remarkably small space—the reactor core might be only thirty cubic meters, with the entire primary cooling circuit perhaps three hundred cubic meters.

This means you might have ten to one hundred megawatts of thermal energy packed into every cubic meter of cooling water. That's an almost unimaginable concentration of heat that must be continuously removed by high-velocity water flow at all times.

This is not optional. Even after you shut down a reactor—even after you've stopped the nuclear chain reaction entirely—the radioactive decay of fission products in the fuel continues generating substantial heat. This "decay heat" can be enough to melt the reactor core if cooling fails.

This is exactly what happened at Fukushima in 2011. The earthquake triggered automatic shutdown of the three operating reactors, just as designed. The nuclear chain reactions stopped. But then the tsunami knocked out both the external power supply and the backup diesel generators that powered the cooling pumps. Without cooling, decay heat accumulated. Within hours, three reactor cores melted.

No coal plant, no gas plant, no chemical facility of any kind faces this challenge. Once you stop burning coal, the fire goes out. Nuclear fuel keeps producing heat whether you want it to or not, for days and weeks after shutdown. This unique characteristic explains why nuclear plants require such extensive redundancy and safety systems—not because nuclear engineers are paranoid, but because the physics genuinely demands it.

The Battery Problem

Given gasoline's remarkable energy density, anyone trying to replace it faces a steep challenge.

Consider electric vehicles. The best lithium-ion batteries today store perhaps one percent of the energy per kilogram that gasoline does. If you simply swapped out a car's gas tank for the same mass of batteries, you'd have a vehicle with about two percent of the original range.

This is why electric cars need such massive battery packs. A Tesla Model S battery weighs about half a ton—roughly ten times what a full gas tank weighs in a comparable conventional car. The battery has to be that big just to achieve a range that gasoline cars take for granted.

This also explains the intense interest in alternative energy storage technologies. Hydrogen, for instance, has excellent gravimetric energy density—more than twice that of gasoline per kilogram. But hydrogen is a gas at normal conditions, so its volumetric energy density is terrible. Compressing it or liquefying it helps, but adds weight, complexity, and energy costs.

Supercapacitors offer another approach, excelling at rapid charging and discharging, but their energy density remains far below batteries, let alone chemical fuels.

No single storage technology wins on all dimensions. There's always a tradeoff between how much energy you can store, how much it weighs, how much space it takes, how quickly you can charge and discharge it, and how efficiently the energy converts to useful work.

Beyond Fuels: Energy Density in Physics

Energy density isn't just about fuels. The concept appears throughout physics in forms that might seem less intuitive.

Electric and magnetic fields themselves store energy. The stronger the field, the more energy is packed into the space it occupies. This energy density is proportional to the square of the field strength—double the field strength, and you quadruple the energy density.

Interestingly, energy per unit volume has the same physical units as pressure. This isn't just a coincidence of mathematics—in many situations, energy density really does behave like pressure. A magnetic field exerts a pressure on its surroundings. Compress a gas, and you're storing energy in it, which manifests as increased pressure.

In cosmology and general relativity, energy density takes on even more profound significance. Einstein's field equations relate the curvature of spacetime to the energy and momentum present in a region. The stress-energy tensor—the mathematical object that encodes all forms of energy, momentum, and pressure—is what tells spacetime how to curve. Here, even the rest mass of matter contributes to energy density, following that famous E equals mc squared.

Hot and Cold: The Two Heats of Combustion

When chemists and engineers measure how much energy a fuel contains, they actually have two different numbers to report, and the difference matters.

The higher heating value, sometimes called the gross heat of combustion, includes all the energy released as combustion products cool to room temperature and any water vapor condenses back to liquid. This captures the absolute maximum energy available.

The lower heating value, or net heat of combustion, excludes the energy that would be released by condensing water vapor. It assumes the combustion products remain gaseous.

Why does this matter? Because water vapor carries significant energy in the form of latent heat—the energy required to evaporate it in the first place. If your application can capture that condensation energy (like a condensing boiler), the higher value is relevant. If the water vapor escapes up a chimney, the lower value is what you'll actually get.

For hydrogen fuel, this distinction is particularly significant. Burning hydrogen produces only water. The lower heating value is about 120 megajoules per kilogram; the higher heating value is about 142 megajoules per kilogram. That's an 18 percent difference, which matters a lot when you're designing fuel systems or comparing efficiency claims.

The Ultimate Energy Source

If you want to find the absolute theoretical maximum of energy density, you have to go beyond ordinary matter entirely.

Matter-antimatter annihilation converts one hundred percent of the mass involved into energy. When an electron meets its antimatter twin, a positron, they both vanish in a flash of gamma rays. No ash, no waste, no inefficiency—pure conversion of mass to energy.

The energy density of this process depends entirely on how much matter and antimatter you can pack together. A neutron star, with its inconceivable density—a teaspoon would weigh billions of tons—would represent something close to the maximum possible.

Black holes present an even stranger case. Though denser than neutron stars, they don't have antimatter counterparts in any meaningful sense. But through a process called Hawking radiation, black holes slowly convert their mass into energy and radiate it away. For small black holes, this process could be spectacular—a black hole with the mass of a mountain would radiate energy at the rate of billions of nuclear reactors.

Of course, we have no way to make antimatter in useful quantities—it takes far more energy to create than it releases when annihilated. And we certainly can't manufacture black holes or neutron stars. These represent not practical energy sources but the theoretical limits of what the universe allows.

Why This Matters

Understanding energy density helps explain why our world looks the way it does.

It explains why we still burn fossil fuels despite knowing their drawbacks—nothing else packs energy so conveniently for transportation. It explains why electric vehicles need such enormous batteries and why hydrogen cars remain uncommon despite hydrogen's excellent energy-per-kilogram numbers. It explains why nuclear power generates so much electricity from so little fuel, and why nuclear accidents, though rare, can be so severe.

It explains, in short, why our energy infrastructure exists in its current form, what's so hard about changing it, and what physics permits and prohibits as we imagine alternatives.

And it explains why your sandwich contains more energy than dynamite—a fact that's both completely true and deeply misleading, in a way that teaches us how carefully we must think about energy if we want to understand it at all.
