London Has Fallen

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: London Has Fallen

On March 4, 2016, the global film industry watched as a cinematic nightmare unfolded in the heart of the British capital, a scenario so meticulously planned and so violently executed that it left audiences breathless and critics baffled. London Has Fallen, the second installment in the Has Fallen franchise, did not merely ask viewers to suspend their disbelief; it demanded they witness the systematic dismantling of the world's most secure city by a coalition of ghosts from the past. Directed by Babak Najafi and written by a quartet of screenwriters including Creighton Rothenberger and Katrin Benedikt, the film stars Gerard Butler as Secret Service agent Mike Banning, tasked with a mission that defies all logic: protecting United States President Benjamin Asher while London itself becomes a death trap. The film grossed $205.9 million worldwide, a commercial triumph that stood in stark contrast to the critical reception, which was overwhelmingly negative, yet the narrative it constructed remains a fascinating case study in the evolution of the modern action thriller.

To understand the stakes of London Has Fallen, one must first understand the genesis of the conflict, a conflict that began not in the bustling streets of the UK, but in the shadowed corners of international arms trafficking. The story opens with a chilling display of American technological dominance turned to devastating effect. G8 intelligence services, operating with the precision of a surgical strike, locate Pakistani arms trafficker and terrorist mastermind Aamir Barkawi at his compound. The authorization for a drone strike comes down, and it is executed during his daughter's wedding. The strike is successful in its primary objective of eliminating a high-value target, but the collateral damage is catastrophic. Barkawi's family is wiped out, and while Barkawi himself is presumed dead, the seeds of a far more intricate and personal revenge are sown in the ashes of that ceremony.

Two years pass, and the geopolitical landscape shifts. British Prime Minister James Wilson dies suddenly, a death that will later be revealed as the first domino in a complex chain reaction. The world's leaders, the G7, converge on London for his funeral. It is a gathering of the elite, a moment of solemnity and diplomacy. Into this high-stakes environment steps Secret Service Director Lynne Jacobs, played with steely resolve by Angela Bassett. She assigns Agent Mike Banning to lead the security detail for President Asher. The personal cost for Banning is immediate and palpable; his wife, Leah, is shortly to give birth, a detail that adds a layer of human vulnerability to the agent's professional armor. As Air Force One lands at Stansted Airport, the rhythm of the film accelerates. Banning, ever the visionary, arranges for an earlier arrival at Somerset House via Marine One, a decision that inadvertently alters the course of history.

The attack begins not with a whisper, but with a roar that shatters the silence of the British morning. As the President's limousine approaches St. Paul's Cathedral, the city itself turns against its guests. An army of mercenaries, led by Kamran Barkawi—the son of the man killed two years prior—launches a coordinated assault that is nothing short of apocalyptic. The attackers are not faceless insurgents; they are disguised as the very symbols of British authority. They wear the uniforms of the Metropolitan Police, the ceremonial garb of the Queen's Guardsmen, and the attire of other first responders. This camouflage is the key to their success, allowing them to infiltrate the security perimeters with terrifying ease.

The carnage is swift and indiscriminate. The leaders of Canada, Germany, Japan, Italy, and France are killed in a series of orchestrated explosions and ambushes that target every major landmark in the city. Major infrastructure is damaged, and mass panic grips the population. The sheer scale of the violence is designed to overwhelm, to turn the city's own geography into a weapon. The attack on St. Paul's Cathedral is the centerpiece of this chaos, a symbolic strike against the heart of the city. However, Banning's decision to arrive early proves to be the only saving grace for the American President. Because Asher and his party are already inside Somerset House, they miss the initial wave of destruction that claims the other world leaders. Banning immediately extracts Asher and Director Jacobs, returning them to Marine One, only to find that the sky itself has become hostile.

Islamic terrorists, armed with Stinger missiles, unleash a barrage that forces the presidential helicopter to crash-land in Hyde Park. The impact is violent, and the cost is high; Director Jacobs is killed in the crash, leaving Banning as the sole guardian of the President. The city is plunged into darkness as the power grid is severed, and residents are forced to shelter indoors, turning London into a ghost town of terrified citizens. In Washington, D.C., Vice President Allan Trumbull, played by Morgan Freeman, takes the helm of the crisis response. He works tirelessly with British authorities to investigate the incident, trying to track down the missing President while managing the geopolitical fallout. It is in this moment of desperation that the true scope of the villain's plan is revealed.

Trumbull receives a call from Sanaa, Yemen. The voice on the other end is Aamir Barkawi, alive and well, having survived the drone strike that killed his family. He reveals that he poisoned Prime Minister Wilson to lure the G7 leaders to London, turning a funeral into a slaughterhouse. Barkawi's promise is simple and terrifying: he will livestream the execution of President Asher once his son Kamran captures him. The stakes are no longer just about survival; they are about the spectacle of American power being dismantled in real-time for the world to see. Trumbull orders his staff to locate Barkawi's businesses, searching for any connection to the attack, while British authorities make the desperate move of standing down all first responders. This counter-intuitive order is designed to identify the terrorists, who are still disguised as police and guards, by leaving them exposed in the open.

Banning, operating in the shadows of a blacked-out London, leads Asher through the city's underbelly. They leave a sign to be picked up by satellite, a desperate signal to their allies. Banning leads Asher to a safe-house where they are briefed by MI6 Agent Jacquelin Marshall. Marshall receives a voice message from Trumbull confirming that the signal has been seen and an extraction team is en route. The tension mounts as security monitors display an approaching Delta Force team. But Banning, with the instincts of a former Army Ranger, realizes something is wrong. The team is not American; it is Barkawi's men. The ensuing firefight is brutal and intimate. Banning eliminates the threat and drives away with Asher, but their escape is cut short. Their car is struck before they can reach the safety of the U.S. Embassy, and in the chaos, President Asher is captured.

Banning is rescued by an SAS extraction team, who, amidst the confusion, suspect a mole within the British government. Meanwhile, in Washington, Trumbull's staff identifies a building owned by one of Barkawi's front companies. The building is seemingly unoccupied, yet it draws massive amounts of power, a tell-tale sign of a high-tech operation. The deduction is swift: this is Kamran's headquarters. The climax of the film unfolds as Banning infiltrates the building seconds before Kamran can kill Asher on live coverage. The action is a blur of movement and violence, a testament to Banning's resilience. Kamran attempts to escape when his brother, Sultan Mansoor, drops a grenade before being killed, forcing Banning to shield Asher from the blast. They escape just as the building is destroyed by the SAS, using an explosive Banning had planted earlier. The explosion kills Kamran and the remaining terrorists, bringing an end to the immediate threat.

The aftermath is a race against time to restore order and uncover the final conspirator. MI6 Agent Jacquelin Marshall helps British authorities restore London's security system, and in the process, they discover the identity of the mole: MI5 Intelligence Chief John Lancaster. Lancaster is revealed to be Barkawi's inside man, a betrayal that allowed the attack to happen. When Lancaster resists arrest, Marshall kills him, ending the conspiracy from within. Back in the United States, Trumbull informs Barkawi that his plan has failed. In a final act of poetic justice, a drone strike kills Barkawi, ending the cycle of revenge that began two years prior.

Two weeks later, as London begins to recover from the devastation, the focus shifts to the personal cost of the conflict. Banning is home with his wife Leah and their newborn child, who they have named Lynne in honor of the fallen Director Jacobs. As he drafts a resignation letter, overwhelmed by the trauma and the toll the job has taken on his life, he watches Vice President Trumbull speak on television about the recent events. The President's words, full of resolve and gratitude, inspire Banning. He deletes the resignation letter, choosing to stay on, to continue the fight. It is a moment of quiet heroism in a film defined by loud explosions.

The cast of London Has Fallen is a who's who of Hollywood talent, brought together to breathe life into this high-octane narrative. Gerard Butler, as the relentless Mike Banning, anchors the film with a performance that balances physical prowess with emotional depth. Aaron Eckhart brings a gravitas to the role of President Asher, portraying a leader who is both vulnerable and resilient. Morgan Freeman, as Vice President Trumbull, provides the steady hand of command, while Angela Bassett's Lynne Jacobs serves as the moral compass of the Secret Service. The supporting cast is equally impressive, with Alon Moni Aboutboul delivering a chilling performance as the vengeful Aamir Barkawi. The film also features a host of international actors portraying world leaders, including Alex Giannini as the Italian Prime Minister, a role that marked his final performance before his death in October 2015. The attention to detail in the casting extends to the background, with real-life news anchor Lawrence O'Donnell appearing uncredited as a news anchor, reprising his role from the first film to provide a sense of continuity and realism.

The production of London Has Fallen was a complex logistical feat, mirroring the chaos depicted on screen. Filming began in London on October 24, 2014, a date that marks the start of a rigorous schedule that would see the city transformed into a war zone. The production faced several challenges, including a change in directors. Antoine Fuqua, who directed the first film, Olympus Has Fallen, did not return due to his commitments with The Equalizer. Charlie Countryman director Fredrik Bond was announced as the replacement in August 2014, but he left the film just six weeks before shooting was set to begin. It was then that Babak Najafi signed on to direct, bringing his own vision to the project. The script was a collaborative effort, written by Creighton Rothenberger, Katrin Benedikt, Chad St. John, and Christian Gudegast, who worked to ensure that the narrative remained tight and the action sequences were coherent.

The film was released in the United States by Focus Features under their recently revived Gramercy Pictures label on March 4, 2016. The marketing campaign was aggressive, highlighting the high-stakes action and the star-studded cast. The film's performance at the box office was strong, grossing $205.9 million worldwide, a testament to the enduring appeal of the action thriller genre. However, the critical reception was mixed to negative, with critics citing the film's reliance on clichés, its excessive violence, and its simplistic portrayal of international politics. Despite the critical backlash, the film found a large audience, and its commercial success paved the way for a third installment, Angel Has Fallen, which was released on August 23, 2019.

The legacy of London Has Fallen is complex. On one hand, it is a film that pushed the boundaries of the action genre, delivering spectacle on a massive scale. On the other hand, it is a film that has been criticized for its lack of subtlety and its sensationalized depiction of terrorism. Yet, it remains a significant entry in the Has Fallen series, a franchise that has carved out a unique niche in the world of political action thrillers. The film's exploration of revenge, loyalty, and the cost of duty resonates with audiences, even as it challenges them with its relentless pace and high body count.

As the dust settles on the events of London Has Fallen, one is left with the image of a city recovering from the brink of destruction, and a man who chose to stay in the line of fire. The story of Mike Banning is not just a tale of survival; it is a testament to the resilience of the human spirit in the face of overwhelming odds. It is a story that reminds us that even in the darkest of times, there is hope, and that sometimes, the greatest act of heroism is simply refusing to give up. The film may have been criticized for its excesses, but it succeeded in one crucial area: it told a story that could not be ignored, a story of a city under siege and the agent who refused to let it fall.

The narrative of London Has Fallen is a testament to the power of cinema to transport us to places we have never been and to show us things we have never seen. It is a film that challenges our perceptions of security, of leadership, and of the cost of peace. It is a film that, despite its flaws, manages to capture the imagination of audiences around the world. And as we look back on the events of March 4, 2016, we are reminded that sometimes, the most dangerous place in the world is the one we call home, and the most powerful weapon in the fight for freedom is the courage to stand up and fight back.

In the end, London Has Fallen is more than just an action movie; it is a reflection of our times, a mirror held up to the anxieties and fears of a world that feels increasingly unstable. It is a film that asks us to consider the cost of our security and the price of our freedom. And while the critics may have panned it, the audience has embraced it, finding in the chaos of London a story that resonates with their own hopes and fears. The film may not be perfect, but it is undeniably compelling, a reminder that in the world of cinema, sometimes the most dangerous stories are the ones that are worth telling.
