List of horses of the American Civil War

17 min read

Based on Wikipedia: List of horses of the American Civil War

More than three million horses were pressed into service by the Union and Confederate armies during the American Civil War, a staggering mobilization of living machinery that defined the conflict's pace, scale, and brutality. While modern historians often fixate on the strategic genius of generals like Grant and Lee or the political machinations in Washington and Richmond, the war was ultimately fought in the mud and dust of the American landscape, guided by the hooves of animals that were as vital to victory as any rifle or cannon. These creatures were not merely beasts of burden; they were the primary engine of communication, the elevated vantage points from which commanders surveyed the chaos, and the most potent symbols of authority on the battlefield. Without them, the war as we know it would have been a static, foot-bound struggle of attrition rather than the mobile, thunderous clash of empires that reshaped a nation.

To understand the sheer magnitude of this equestrian army, one must look past the romanticized image of the lone cavalryman and examine the logistical reality. The American Saddlebred, the Morgan, and the Thoroughbred were the preferred breeds, prized for their stamina, speed, and temperaments suited to the rigors of war. Yet, the demand was insatiable. Cavalry regiments consumed the vast majority of these resources. Consider the organizational math: the war saw 206 Confederate and 137 Union cavalry regiments, and each unit required a minimum of 1,200 horses to function. This was not a one-to-one ratio. The hierarchy of command dictated a tiered distribution of mounts that revealed the status of the rider. Every upper officer was entitled to three horses, while each lieutenant was allowed two. But the true secret to cavalry endurance lay in the replacement system. Regiments were forced to maintain a surplus of extra horses to account for animals lost in the heat of battle or those that simply collapsed from exhaustion after forced marches. On average, every cavalryman rode a carousel of four or more replacement horses in addition to his primary mount. This rotisserie of living beings ensured that a rider could swap a weary beast for a fresh one, keeping the unit mobile when the alternative was paralysis.

The generals, operating at the apex of this hierarchy, required an even more robust supply chain to maintain their rapid maneuverability across the theater. The 425 Confederate and 583 Union generals needed a standing reserve of at least 4,032 horses at all times to ensure they could traverse the battlefield with the speed necessary to issue orders or exploit a breakthrough. This number does not even account for the artillery, the heavy artillery of the age that relied entirely on animal power. The 636 Confederate and Union artillery batteries each required around 120 horses. The most critical calculation involved the guns themselves; moving a single cannon piece required a team of six horses, perfectly harnessed and trained to move in unison. If that team faltered, the gun fell silent. The difference between a battery that could redeploy in minutes and one that was captured lay in the strength and training of these teams.

Managing this colossal force required strict discipline and detailed regulation, a fact captured in the bureaucratic efforts of the war's commanders. On October 1, 1862, Robert E. Lee issued Order No. 155, a document that meticulously detailed the care of Confederate horses. The order assigned specific responsibility for the welfare of artillery horses to individual officers and listed explicit punishments for neglect, acknowledging that the loss of a horse was a loss of combat power as severe as the loss of a soldier. The Union, under the command of William Tecumseh Sherman, issued similar directives. Sherman's orders were specific about rations: each artillery horse was to receive twelve pounds of grain and fourteen pounds of hay daily. These were not arbitrary numbers but the caloric requirements necessary to sustain a large animal pulling heavy loads through mud and under fire. Yet, the romantic ideal of the well-fed warhorse often collided with the grim reality of supply lines. The food and water required by Sherman's orders were not always available. During campaigns deep into enemy territory or during sieges, the rations were slashed, and the horses began to starve, their ribs protruding through their coats, their gaits becoming shuffling and weak. The gap between policy and reality was often measured in the life expectancy of the animal.

The mortality rate among these horses was catastrophic, a statistic that dwarfs many human losses. Between 1.2 and 1.5 million horses died in active service during the war, representing roughly fifty percent of the total population mobilized. They died as their riders did, from the wounds of combat, pierced by minie balls or crushed by artillery shrapnel. But they also died from diseases that ravaged the ranks, from pneumonia, colic, and the various ailments that flourish in crowded, unsanitary conditions. Starvation was a silent killer, claiming more lives than the bayonets of the enemy. It was a common refrain in the accounts of commanders and generals to note, "his horse was shot out from beneath him," a phrase that became a cliché of the era because it happened so frequently. The horse was not just a vehicle; it was the soldier's shield. When the horse fell, the rider was often exposed, vulnerable, and forced to fight on foot or flee.

The toll on the most famous cavalrymen was particularly high, a testament to their aggressive tactics and the sheer danger of their trade. Cavalry commander George Armstrong Custer, known for his flamboyant leadership, lost eleven horses during the war. General Philip Sheridan, whose cavalry raids were legendary, saw twenty-four of his mounts die. But it was Nathan Bedford Forrest, the master of the lightning raid, who suffered the most profound losses, with thirty-nine of his horses killed in action. These numbers are not just statistics; they represent the constant, grinding attrition of the cavalryman's life. Forrest and his men were constantly in motion, engaging and disengaging, their horses pushed to the absolute limit of their endurance. The high turnover rate meant that a commander was never truly attached to a single mount, but rather to a stream of animals that were consumed by the war effort.

In the face of this attrition, the moral dilemmas faced by soldiers were harrowing. On some occasions, soldiers were ordered to kill their exhausted horses rather than leave them behind for enemy troops. This was a cruel calculus: a horse that could no longer carry a rider or pull a gun was a liability. If left alive, it would be captured by the enemy, replenishing their ranks, or it would suffer a slow, agonizing death in the wilderness. The mercy killing of a trusted companion, an animal that had carried the soldier through the hell of Gettysburg or Chancellorsville, was a moment of profound psychological trauma. The average life expectancy of a cavalry horse during the Civil War was a mere four months. An artillery horse, perhaps working slightly less intensely than the cavalry, averaged seven and a half months. In the span of a single campaign, a horse was expected to work itself to death.

The procurement of these millions of animals was a massive logistical undertaking that fell to the Quartermaster Corps. For the Union troops, the United States Army Quartermaster Corps, under the leadership of Brigadier General Montgomery C. Meigs, was responsible for procuring, feeding, and equipping the horses. Meigs was a master of logistics, understanding that the war would be won or lost on the supply lines. The Confederacy had its own Quartermaster-General's Department, tasked with the same impossible mission but operating with fewer resources and a crumbling infrastructure. Despite these central efforts, the reality of the battlefield often devolved into a policy of "self-mounting." Many officers and Confederate cavalrymen rode their personal horses, bringing animals from their farms and estates into the war. This was a pragmatic solution to the shortage of government-supplied mounts. Confederate soldiers were paid forty cents a day for the use of their horse, a compensation that acknowledged the loss of their property to the state. However, this system also meant that the quality of the mounts varied wildly, depending on the wealth and breeding stock of the individual soldier.

Both sides frequently resorted to the formal impressment of horses. When the armies needed mounts and could not purchase them, they would simply take them. Officers would issue a receipt to the owner, promising future payment for the confiscated animal. This practice was legal under military law, but it was deeply resented by the civilian population. A farmer could lose his only draft horse, the backbone of his livelihood, to a passing army, leaving him with a piece of paper that might never be redeemed. The economic devastation of this policy was immense, stripping the countryside of the very animals needed to plant and harvest crops, exacerbating the food shortages that plagued both the North and the South.

Beyond the logistics and the statistics, the horses themselves had to be trained for a unique and terrifying existence. Depending on their role, military horses required specific characteristics that went beyond simple strength. Cavalry horses needed to be able to ride in synch with other horses, forming a cohesive unit that could charge or retreat as one. They had to follow their rider's instructions with precision, even in the chaos of a melee. Most importantly, they had to tolerate the deafening sounds of battle. The roar of cannon, the crack of rifle fire, and the screaming of men were a sensory assault that would terrify a civilian animal. Remarkably, many of these horses learned to respond to bugle calls without an additional prompt from their rider. They could distinguish the charge call from the retreat call, moving instinctively to the sound of the horn. This level of training was a testament to the bond between horse and rider and the rigorous discipline of the cavalry.

However, the psychological toll of war on these animals was profound. Horse historian Earl J. Hess notes that some of the misbehavior observed in war horses—biting, kicking, running off with their riders—may have been related to wartime trauma. Just as soldiers developed "shell shock" or combat fatigue, horses developed their own versions of these conditions. They had a harder time learning to move in tight formations when the sounds of battle became overwhelming. The sights and sounds of the battlefield could trigger a panic response that no amount of training could suppress. Soldiers described horses that had behavioral issues, animals that had become unpredictable and dangerous. A horse that had been steady and calm in training could become a frantic, bucking nightmare in the face of artillery fire. The line between a brave warhorse and a broken one was often thin, determined by the severity of the trauma it had witnessed.

The end of the war brought a moment of unexpected grace to the story of these animals. When Union General Ulysses S. Grant agreed to Confederate General Robert E. Lee's terms of surrender at Appomattox Court House, the fate of the Confederate horses became a point of contention. Lee, aware that the South was in ruins and that the farmers needed their animals to survive, negotiated a specific term. Grant agreed to allow Confederate soldiers to keep their personal horses. This was not a gesture of military necessity but one of humanitarian foresight. Grant understood that the soldiers, many of whom were farmers, needed their horses to plow the fields and plant the spring crops. Without their mounts, the return to civilian life would be impossible, and the South would descend into even deeper poverty. This decision ensured that the horses, which had been weapons of war, could once again become tools of peace and agriculture.

The legacy of these millions of horses is etched into the landscape of the United States and the collective memory of the nation. There are memorials dedicated to the Civil War horses in Middleburg, Virginia; Fort Riley, Kansas; Four Oaks, North Carolina; and Murfreesboro, Tennessee. These monuments stand as silent tributes to the animals that fought, suffered, and died alongside men. In addition, many of the memorial statues of Civil War notables are depicted on horseback, immortalizing the bond between the commander and his mount. The bronze sculpture of Robert E. Lee on Traveller, created by Alexander Phimister Proctor, once stood in Turtle Creek Park in Dallas, Texas, capturing the dignified stoicism of the general and his famous horse. The "Cavalry Charge" by Henry Merwin Shrady and the Ulysses S. Grant Memorial by Henry Shrady both depict General Grant on horseback, a reminder that the Union victory was mounted on the backs of these animals.

These statues and memorials serve as a counterpoint to the grim statistics of the war. They remind us that the history of the American Civil War is not just a history of battles, politics, and laws, but a history of living beings. The three million horses that were used by the Union and Confederate Armies were not interchangeable parts; they were individuals with names, temperaments, and stories. From the American Saddlebred and the Morgan to the Thoroughbred, they brought speed, power, and life to the conflict. They were the symbols of authority, the messengers of command, and the victims of a war that consumed everything in its path. The fact that they are remembered in bronze and stone is a testament to their essential role in the making of modern America. Without them, the war would have been a different story, a slower, quieter, and perhaps even more brutal struggle. But with them, the war became a thunderous, dynamic clash that swept across the continent, driven by the rhythm of millions of hooves beating against the earth.
