Flow (2024 film)

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Flow (2024 film)

On May 22, 2024, inside a theater in Cannes, the silence was not empty; it was heavy with the collective breath of an audience witnessing a miracle born entirely from code and conviction. When the credits rolled on Flow, there were no actors to applaud, no director stepping out for a standing ovation that required spoken words to justify. Instead, the applause erupted for a dark grey cat, a capybara, and a lemur who had just navigated the end of the world without uttering a single syllable. This was not merely a film; it was a five-and-a-half-year testament to the power of visual storytelling, an independent animated dystopian adventure that would go on to shatter records in its native Latvia, sweep the Golden Globes, and become the first Latvian film in history to win an Academy Award for Best Animated Feature. Directed by Gints Zilbalodis, a man who refused to let his small nation's animation scene be defined by its size, Flow (Latvian: Straume) stands as a monument to what can happen when technology is wielded not just to mimic reality, but to deepen our empathy for the creatures with whom we share this fragile planet.

The genesis of this cinematic journey began long before the first frame was rendered in 2019. It started in 2012 with Aqua, a short film by Zilbalodis that explored a cat's fear of the ocean. That initial spark contained the DNA of what would become his magnum opus: a creature confronting the overwhelming forces of nature. But the leap from a three-minute short to a feature-length epic spanning five and a half years was not a matter of simple scaling. It required a complete reinvention of Zilbalodis's workflow. Up until his 2019 film Away, he had relied on Maya, the industry-standard software for 3D animation. Yet, seeking something more fluid, more immediate, he made a radical pivot to Blender, a free and open-source program. This was not just a technical switch; it was a philosophical one. By embracing Blender's real-time renderer, EEVEE, Zilbalodis and his small team of collaborators could explore scenes as they built them, placing animals into the environment and "exploring them with the camera" rather than rigidly adhering to pre-drawn blueprints.

The result was a film that defied the mechanical precision often associated with computer-generated imagery. There were no storyboards for Flow. There were no deleted scenes. Every moment captured on screen existed because it felt necessary in the moment of its creation, a direct translation of instinct into visual language. This approach allowed for a spontaneity rarely seen in high-budget animation, where every move is often choreographed months in advance. Zilbalodis was not working alone in a vacuum; he was buoyed by a growing international coalition. In 2022, Take Five and Sacrebleu Productions joined the fold, bringing their expertise in character animation and sound design to the project. The financial backbone of this ambitious endeavor was a patchwork of support from the National Film Centre of Latvia, the State Culture Capital Foundation, the Centre national du cinéma et de l'image animée in France, ARTE France, Eurimages, RTBF, and the Belgian Tax Shelter. It was a true co-production between Latvia, France, and Belgium, proving that great art often knows no borders.

"No storyboards were used."

This single fact changes everything about how Flow feels to watch. Without the safety net of a rigid plan, the film breathes. The narrative follows a dark grey cat wandering through a forest, an ordinary existence shattered by the arrival of a pack of dogs. In a world that seems poised for apocalypse, conflict is immediate and visceral. The dogs chase the cat after it takes a fish they had fought over. It is a primal scene, stripped of moralizing dialogue, driven entirely by survival instinct. The cat escapes, only to stumble upon a deer stampede just as the world literally begins to end. A massive flood rises with terrifying speed, swallowing the landscape.

The survival of this small grey protagonist becomes the axis around which the entire film rotates. The cat and the dogs survive the initial deluge, reaching higher ground, but the old hierarchies are meaningless in the face of rising waters. A yellow Labrador Retriever from the pack follows the cat to an abandoned cabin, a place marked by wooden cat sculptures that hint at a human world long departed. As the water swallows their sanctuary, the Labrador makes a choice to join its own kind on a passing boat, leaving the cat behind. The cat is forced onto a giant statue of another cat, watching as the floodwaters climb higher until they reach the statue's head. In that moment of desperation, as the statue is submerged, the cat leaps into an approaching sailboat where a capybara waits in silence.

This boat becomes the film's central stage, a microcosm of society floating through a drowned world. The next morning reveals a forest partially underwater, a landscape of haunting beauty and hidden danger. When the cat falls overboard while trying to evade a white secretarybird, it is saved from drowning by a mutated whale. This whale, originally intended to be realistic but redesigned to look more mythical, serves as a guardian figure in this new mythology. Yet, salvation is fleeting; another secretarybird seizes the cat, carrying it through the air where it glimpses massive stone pillars in the distance before being released back onto the boat. The narrative rhythm here is relentless, mirroring the rising tides.

As the water continues to climb, the crew of the sailboat expands, not by choice, but by necessity and chance. A ring-tailed lemur with a basket of trinkets is invited aboard by the capybara. The lemur adds a mirror found on the boat to its collection, an object that will later serve as a poignant symbol of self-reflection in a world without people. The cat sleeps, haunted by nightmares of being circled by deer, looking toward those massive stone pillars before being swept away again by another flood. When they finally land on shore, they are joined by the Labrador, and the group dynamic shifts from solitary survival to tentative community.

However, peace is fragile. They encounter a flock of secretarybirds, creatures that have adapted with hostility to this new world. The birds show aggression, forcing the cat to flee. In a scene of brutal beauty, a younger secretarybird pleads with its leader for the cat's life. The plea fails. A duel ensues, and the young bird loses, its wing injured. Abandoned by its flock, it joins the crew of the boat, a scarred outcast finding solace among other survivors. The diversity of this makeshift family—the cat, the Labrador, the capybara, the lemur, and the secretarybird—creates a tapestry of interspecies cooperation that feels both miraculous and inevitable in the face of extinction.

The journey leads them to a half-submerged city near the base of those massive stone pillars. Here, the tension between the characters comes to a head. The Labrador attempts to play fetch with the secretarybird using a glass float belonging to the lemur. The bird kicks it off the boat in anger. The lemur retaliates, fighting the secretarybird who is steering the vessel. In the struggle for control, the boat's mast gets caught in a tree, leaving them stranded. It is the whale that saves them once again, breaching out of the water to produce waves strong enough to free the ship from its wooden prison.

"The sound of a tiger pitched down was used for the whale."

This detail from the production highlights the care taken to ground these mythical creatures in sonic reality. Sound designer Gurwal Coïc-Gallas used actual animal sounds for every character, yet he also knew when to bend the truth to serve the emotional core of the film. The cat's voice is that of Miut, Coïc-Gallas's own pet, providing a genuine feline texture to the protagonist's internal life. For the capybara, known for its calm demeanor, the natural sound of a real capybara being tickled was deemed too high-pitched and unpleasant; instead, they chose the gentle grunt of a baby camel. These choices were not arbitrary; they were calculated decisions to ensure that every sound reinforced the character's personality rather than undermining it.

As the storm intensifies, the boat sails through the huge stone pillars. The secretarybird flies away, leaving the group in turmoil. The cat falls off but swims ashore, climbing the stairs of a pillar to find the bird there, standing on a labyrinth-like carving. In a surreal sequence that defies physics and logic, gravity vanishes. A bright portal opens above them. For a brief moment, they are weightless, suspended between the earth and something beyond. The cat floats back down to the ground, while the secretarybird flies toward the light and disappears. This is not a death in the traditional sense; it is a transcendence, a release from the burden of survival that has driven the entire narrative.

The cat rushes back to the boat, but it is too far away. It finds the lemur's glass float and uses it as a lifeline. As if in response to this act of desperation, the water level drops rapidly. Massive fault lines open in the earth, draining the ocean and leaving the world transformed once more. The group roams through the forest, finding the boat hanging precariously from a tree. The dogs jump off, but as the capybara prepares to exit, the tree begins to fracture. In a display of teamwork, the cat climbs the tree, passing the rope to the lemur and the dogs, who pull the boat to safety. But in a cruel twist of fate, a rabbit scampers by, triggering a primal instinct in the other dogs. They abandon the Labrador and its friends to chase the prey, leaving the core group isolated once again. The cat falls but is caught by the capybara. Together, they jump off just as the tree collapses into a ravine below.

The celebration of survival is short-lived. Another deer stampede occurs. The cat flees, only to stop in its tracks when it comes upon the whale, now beached and dying in the forest. It is a moment of profound stillness amidst chaos. The cat approaches the massive creature, offering comfort as it passes away. Saddened by this loss, the cat looks at its reflection in a puddle of water, joined by the lemur, the capybara, and the Labrador. They are alone, but they are together. In a post-credits scene, a whale surfaces on the ocean, a silent promise that life continues even after the end of the world.

The critical reception to Flow was not just positive; it was reverent. When it premiered at the Cannes Film Festival in the Un Certain Regard section, it was clear that something special had emerged from Riga. The film broke several box-office records in Latvia, becoming the most-viewed film in the country's history. It resonated deeply with audiences who saw their own resilience reflected in the struggles of these animals. But its reach extended far beyond its small homeland. At the 97th Academy Awards, Flow won Best Animated Feature, a stunning victory for an independent production that relied on open-source software and a tiny team. It was also nominated for Best International Feature Film as Latvia's submission, marking the first time a Latvian film had ever received both a nomination and a win at the Oscars.

The Golden Globe Award for Best Animated Feature Film followed, cementing its status as a global phenomenon. Both statuettes are now on display at the Latvian National Museum of Art, physical symbols of a cultural shift that took place over five years of grueling work. The film's success was not just a victory for Zilbalodis; it was a triumph for the entire Latvian creative community. It proved that a nation with a population of less than two million could produce art that competed on the world stage, challenging the dominance of massive studios in Los Angeles and Tokyo.

The inspirations behind Flow are as eclectic as its production history. Zilbalodis cited Jacques Tati, the French filmmaker known for his visual comedy and focus on the human condition without dialogue, as a major influence. He also drew inspiration from Future Boy Conan, the anime series by Hayao Miyazaki that blends adventure with environmental themes. These influences merged to create something entirely new: a film that feels both timeless and modern, grounded in the physical reality of animal movement yet elevated by a mythic scope. The boat itself was inspired by Mediterranean feluccas, grounding the fantastical setting in a tangible historical reality.

The production process was a masterclass in problem-solving and artistic integrity. With no deleted scenes, every frame had to be perfect from the start. The team studied capybaras and lemurs at zoos like the Izu Shaboten Zoo in Japan to understand their movements and behaviors. They redesigned the whale to look more mythical, ensuring it felt like a creature of legend rather than just a biological specimen. They changed the secretarybird from a seagull because the smaller bird lacked the visual impact needed for the film's dramatic confrontations. Every decision was made with intentionality, driven by a desire to create an immersive experience that would resonate on an emotional level.

"Seven hours of music was composed by Zilbalodis and 50 minutes of it was used."

The musical score, composed by Zilbalodis and Rihards Zaļupe and mixed by Mikko Raita, plays a crucial role in the film's emotional landscape. With no dialogue to guide the viewer, the music becomes the primary vehicle for storytelling, shifting from tense, rhythmic pulses during moments of danger to soaring, ethereal melodies during moments of wonder. The fact that only 50 minutes of seven hours of composed music made it into the final cut speaks to the rigorous editing process and the precision with which every second was chosen.

The documentary Aizliegtais paņēmiens (The Forbidden Technique), released in March 2025, offered a glimpse behind the curtain, detailing the hardships and breakthroughs of the production. It showed a team working in isolation, driven by a shared vision that refused to compromise. In an era where animation is often dominated by formulaic franchises and risk-averse studio mandates, Flow stands as a defiant act of independence. It reminds us that great stories do not require billions of dollars or thousands of employees; they require passion, persistence, and the courage to try something new.

The film's impact on Latvia has been profound. Before Flow, Latvian cinema was often defined by its struggle for visibility in a global market dominated by Hollywood giants. Now, the world knows that Riga is a hub of innovation and creativity. The success of Flow has inspired a new generation of animators in Latvia and across Europe to pick up their tablets and open-source software, to dream big regardless of their resources. It has shown that the barriers to entry are lower than ever before, provided one has the vision to see what is possible.

In the end, Flow is more than a story about animals surviving a flood. It is a meditation on community, adaptation, and the resilience of life in the face of overwhelming odds. It asks us to consider our relationship with the natural world, not as conquerors, but as participants in a vast, interconnected web of existence. The silent journey of the cat, the capybara, and their friends serves as a mirror for our own struggles, reflecting back a truth that is often lost in the noise of modern life: we are all just trying to stay afloat, waiting for the next wave to either carry us forward or wash us away. And sometimes, if we are lucky, we find others who will help us swim.

The film's legacy will endure long after the box office numbers fade and the awards gather dust in museums. It will be remembered as the moment independent animation found its voice without speaking a word, as the moment Latvia took its place on the world stage, and as the story that reminded us all of the beauty and fragility of our shared home. In a world increasingly divided by language, politics, and culture, Flow offers a universal language of survival and hope, proving that sometimes the most powerful stories are the ones told in silence.
