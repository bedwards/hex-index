Decision of 1789

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Decision of 1789

On May 15, 1789, the newly convened First United States House of Representatives faced a question that would define the balance of power in America for centuries. They were not debating taxes or borders, but the very soul of the executive branch: Could the President fire his own cabinet officers without the Senate's permission? The debate that followed was not a mere procedural squabble; it was a high-stakes constitutional reckoning that occurred just months after the Constitution itself had taken effect. This month-long confrontation, now known as the Decision of 1789, has been called "the first significant legislative construction of the Constitution." It emerged from the practical necessity of creating three new executive departments—the Department of the Treasury, the Department of War, and the Department of Foreign Affairs. While the Treasury was largely self-evident in its structure, the creation of the Department of Foreign Affairs, the precursor to today's State Department, sparked a firestorm over who held the ultimate authority to dismiss the Secretary of that department. The question seemed technical at first glance, a matter of statutory language regarding the custody of papers after a secretary "shall be removed from office by the President." Yet beneath this dry phrasing lay a fundamental disagreement about whether the American executive was a unified commander or a figurehead shackled by legislative and senatorial consent.

The stakes were existential for the young republic. If the President could not remove his subordinates at will, he would be forced to serve alongside cabinet officers appointed by rivals in the Senate, effectively creating an executive branch with multiple masters. This was the fear that drove James Madison, the primary architect of the Constitution and a leader of the House, to abandon his earlier inclinations and spearhead the argument for unilateral presidential removal power. The debate lasted from May 19 to June 23, 1789, filling the pages of the Congressional Register with hours of impassioned rhetoric. Four distinct theories on the proper authority to remove officers emerged, each representing a different vision of American governance.

The most dominant view, which would eventually carry the day, was the executive power theory. Proponents like Madison, Fisher Ames, and John Vining argued that because Article Two of the Constitution vested "the executive Power" in the President alone, the authority to remove executive officers must logically follow. Their reasoning was structural: if the Constitution intended for removals to require Senate approval, it would have said so explicitly, just as it did for appointments. The Constitution listed exceptions to presidential power, such as the requirement for Senate concurrence in making treaties or appointing ambassadors. Since removal was not listed among these exceptions, and since removing an official is inherently an executive function, the power must reside with the President unilaterally. To argue otherwise, they posited, would allow Congress to modify the core authority of the executive branch on its own whim, fracturing the unity that the Constitution demanded.

This coalition was not without internal friction. While the majority agreed on the outcome—that the President had the right to remove—they squabbled over the statutory language needed to convey this power. Some wanted explicit wording stating the President could fire at will; others, wary of setting a precedent that might be misunderstood as Congress granting a power it didn't possess, preferred language that simply acknowledged removal happened by the President without explicitly defining the source of that authority in the text of the law itself.

Opposing this view was the congressional delegation theory. Supported by at least seven Representatives, with perhaps nine more remaining silent but sympathetic, this camp argued that Congress held the power to create executive offices and therefore possessed the authority to set the terms of their existence. Under this theory, the Necessary and Proper Clause granted Congress the right to delegate removal powers as it saw fit. Some supporters believed this was a formality—that Congress was morally obliged to grant the President these powers—but others insisted it was a genuine choice. The logic here was one of legislative supremacy over the machinery of government: if Congress builds the office, Congress decides how the occupant leaves. However, this theory faced a fatal flaw in the eyes of its own proponents, including Madison, who realized that if Congress could delegate removal power to anyone, it might eventually decide to vest that power in itself or a committee, thereby crippling the President's ability to supervise the executive branch. Madison and others abandoned this view as they recognized its potential to undermine the very presidency they were trying to empower.

A third perspective, the advice and consent theory, was voiced by about six Representatives who drew a direct line between the Appointments Clause and the removal power. They argued that since the Constitution required Senate "advice and consent" for appointments and treaties, it implicitly required similar consultation for removals. If the Senate had to agree to put someone in office, they reasoned, it should also have to agree to take them out. This view sought to preserve a strong role for the Senate in the executive branch's lifecycle, ensuring that cabinet officers were not merely the President's personal agents but representatives of a broader consensus.

The fourth and most marginal theory was the impeachment theory, championed by William Smith and supported by only two or three Representatives. Smith argued with a legalistic fervor that an office was akin to property for the officer holding it. Just as one could not strip a citizen of their property without due process, one could not deprive an official of their "property" (the office) without a formal finding of malfeasance through impeachment. This view would have made removal incredibly difficult, effectively insulating cabinet officers from presidential oversight unless they committed high crimes or misdemeanors. It was the least popular argument, reflecting a fear of executive tyranny that most in the room were willing to tolerate less than the chaos of a fragmented executive.

The result of this month-long debate was a legislative compromise that was anything but clear-cut. Congress enacted three departmental acts for Foreign Affairs, War, and Treasury. None of these laws contained language expressly granting the President removal power. Instead, they discussed the custody of departmental papers after a secretary "shall be removed from office by the President of the United States." This phrasing was a masterclass in legislative ambiguity, allowing both sides to claim victory while leaving the constitutional question technically open. However, one act included a proviso urged by Madison that many scholars believe "was meant to imply recognition that the Secretary would be removable by the President at will." The silence on the source of the power, coupled with the explicit mention of the act of removal by the President, became the bedrock of the traditional legal view.

For over a century, the traditional legal view held sway: the Decision of 1789 was a definitive construction of the Constitution that placed full executive removal power in the hands of the President. This interpretation was championed by some of America's most towering figures. Alexander Hamilton, writing under the pseudonym Pacificus in 1793, stated unequivocally that the First Congress had construed the Constitution to vest this power solely with the Executive. Chief Justice John Marshall, in his biography of George Washington, echoed this sentiment, cementing the decision as a foundational pillar of presidential authority.

This interpretation was not merely academic; it became the law of the land through Supreme Court precedent. In 1926, Chief Justice William H. Taft, writing for the majority in Myers v. United States, leaned heavily on the Decision of 1789 to strike down a statute that required Senate approval for the removal of postmasters. Taft argued that the First Congress had settled the issue, establishing a broad presidential removal power necessary for the faithful execution of the laws.

The relevance of this 230-year-old debate surged into the modern era with renewed intensity. In Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (2010), Chief Justice John Roberts invoked the Decision to limit the independence of multi-layered agencies, arguing that the President must have control over those who execute his policy. The same logic appeared in Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (2020), where Roberts again cited the 1789 debates to invalidate limits on the President's power to remove the director of an independent agency. Justice Samuel Alito followed suit in Collins v. Yellen (2021), using the Decision of 1789 to strike down statutory restrictions on the removal of the Federal Housing Finance Agency director. For Originalist jurists, the First Congress's silence on Senate involvement was a loud declaration of presidential supremacy.

Yet, a dissenting view, often labeled as revisionist or judicial minimalist, has gained significant traction in recent decades. This perspective argues that the Decision of 1789 did not resolve the issue but rather deferred it. In Seila Law, Justice Elena Kagan challenged Roberts's characterization of the event, noting that the First Congress "was deeply divided" on the President's removal power and "never squarely addressed" the central constitutional question. She argued that the ambiguity of the statutory language was a feature, not a bug—a conscious choice to avoid settling a contentious issue that could fracture the new government.

Modern legal scholars have engaged in fierce debates over this historical moment. Professors Jed Shugerman and Saikrishna Prakash have become prominent voices on opposite sides. Prakash adopts the traditional view, arguing that the debate was conclusive and that the President's removal power is inherent and broad. Shugerman, conversely, opposes this conclusion, suggesting that the First Congress intentionally left the door open for legislative constraints on removal. Their discourse highlights how a single month of debate in 1789 continues to shape the legal battles of the twenty-first century.

The legacy of the Decision of 1789 is not just a matter of historical curiosity; it dictates the rhythm of American politics today. When a President fires a cabinet secretary, when Congress passes laws limiting that power, or when the Supreme Court strikes down those limits, they are all dancing to the music first played in that sweltering room in Philadelphia in May and June of 1789. The debate revealed a profound tension at the heart of American democracy: the need for a strong, unified executive capable of decisive action versus the fear of monarchical overreach. The First Congress chose ambiguity, perhaps wisely, allowing future generations to adapt the balance of power as circumstances changed.

However, the human cost of this abstract legal maneuvering is often overlooked in favor of constitutional theory. While Madison and his colleagues debated the nature of executive authority, they were essentially deciding whether the President could be held accountable for the actions of his subordinates or if those subordinates would act with a degree of independence that could frustrate the popular will. The stakes were not just about legal precedent but about who ultimately answers to the people. If a department secretary acts in a way that harms citizens, can the President remove them immediately to stop the harm? Or must they wait for a Senate vote, or worse, an impeachment trial?

The Tenure of Office Act (1820) and later the Tenure of Office Act (1867) were direct attempts by Congress to revisit this question. The 1820 act required Senate approval for the removal of certain officers, a move that eventually led to the impeachment of President Andrew Johnson in 1868 after he attempted to remove Secretary of War Edwin Stanton in violation of the law. That conflict was a violent eruption of the tensions first hashed out in 1789, proving that the decision was far from settled. The 1867 act was eventually repealed and declared unconstitutional in part by the Myers decision, but its existence showed how deeply the removal power could destabilize the government when left unresolved.

The Decision of 1789 remains a mirror reflecting our current anxieties about executive power. In an era where the presidency commands vast bureaucratic machinery, the question of who controls that machine is more urgent than ever. The First Congress's decision to leave the statutory language ambiguous was a gamble on the prudence of future leaders and the wisdom of the courts. As Chief Justice Roberts and his colleagues continue to cite 1789 as the ultimate authority on presidential removal, they are reinforcing a vision of the presidency that Madison fought for in those long summer days.

But the revisionist challenge remains potent. If the First Congress was truly "deeply divided," then perhaps there is no single constitutional answer, only a political one that shifts with the times. The silence of the statute may not be a declaration of presidential power but a reminder that the Constitution leaves room for negotiation between the branches. The debate in 1789 was not just about words on paper; it was about the nature of accountability in a democracy. It asked whether the President is the master of his cabinet or merely its first among equals. The answer, as Madison and his contemporaries knew, would shape the character of the American experiment for generations to come.

The events of 1789 remind us that constitutional principles are not static monuments but living arguments. The decision was made in a room with open windows and high ceilings, where the heat of the debate matched the humidity of the summer air. It was a moment when the future of the executive branch hung in the balance, resolved not by a clear command but by a complex interplay of theory, political necessity, and legislative compromise. As we look at the modern presidency, with its expanded reach and intensified scrutiny, the ghosts of that debate are still present. The question of removal power is a proxy for the larger question of control: who holds the reins in the American government? The Decision of 1789 suggests there is no final answer, only an ongoing conversation that began two centuries ago and continues to this day.

The legacy of Madison's pivot from the congressional delegation theory to the executive power theory illustrates the fluidity of constitutional interpretation. He recognized that while Congress could create offices, the efficiency and unity of the government required a single head who could direct and dismiss subordinates without obstruction. This realization was not just legalistic; it was pragmatic. A government where the President cannot remove his own cabinet members would be paralyzed by internal conflict, unable to respond swiftly to crises or implement policy coherently. The decision to vest this power in the President, even if ambiguously expressed, was a recognition of the dangers of a fractured executive.

Yet, the dissenting voices in 1789 were not wrong to fear tyranny. The removal power is a blunt instrument. If unchecked, it can be used to punish political enemies or protect allies who have committed misconduct. The tension between the need for a strong executive and the need for checks and balances is as relevant today as it was in 1789. The fact that this debate resurfaces in cases like Seila Law and Collins v. Yellen proves that the First Congress's compromise was insufficient to settle the issue permanently.

In the end, the Decision of 1789 stands as a testament to the complexity of founding a new government. The Founders were not omniscient; they were human, grappling with a text they had just written and trying to make it work in the real world. Their decision to leave the removal power somewhat ambiguous was a strategic choice, allowing the system to evolve through practice and precedent rather than rigid dogma. As we navigate the political landscape of 2026, looking back at that summer of 1789 offers clarity on the enduring nature of these struggles. The debate continues, not in the halls of Congress, but in the courtrooms, the newsrooms, and the minds of citizens who wonder where power truly lies.

The Decision of 1789 was more than a legislative maneuver; it was the first great test of the Constitution's durability. It forced the new nation to confront the reality of executive authority before it even had time to settle into a routine. The debates were fierce, the theories complex, and the outcome ambiguous. Yet, from that ambiguity emerged a working model of government that has endured for over two centuries. Whether we view this as a triumph of pragmatism or a failure to resolve a fundamental conflict depends on one's perspective. But there is no denying its significance. It set the stage for the presidency as we know it, defining the relationship between the President and his cabinet in a way that continues to influence American law and politics today.

As the Supreme Court grapples with these issues in the modern era, the echoes of 1789 are unmistakable. The arguments made by Madison, Ames, and Smith have been refined and repackaged, but their core logic remains intact. The question of who controls the executive branch is as vital now as it was then. The Decision of 1789 reminds us that the Constitution is not a dead letter but a living document, shaped by the debates of its first interpreters and the challenges of every subsequent generation. It is a story of power, principle, and the eternal struggle to balance efficiency with liberty.
