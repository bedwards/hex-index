Coalescing (computer science)

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Coalescing (computer science)

In the high-speed architecture of the NVIDIA Blackwell GPU, where thousands of threads execute in lockstep and memory bandwidth is the ultimate currency, a seemingly mundane operation determines the difference between a world-record performance and a catastrophic stall. It is the act of merging. When a programmer releases a block of global memory, that space does not simply vanish into the ether; it becomes a ghost, a gap in the physical address space. If the memory manager is passive, these gaps accumulate like debris in a riverbed, choking the flow of data for subsequent allocations. To prevent this, systems employ a technique known as coalescing. This is the deliberate, algorithmic merging of two adjacent free blocks of computer memory into a single, larger contiguous block. It is the fundamental defense against external fragmentation, a phenomenon that plagues dynamic storage allocation from the earliest mainframes to the most advanced tensor cores of 2026.

To understand why coalescing is not merely an optimization but a necessity, one must first grasp the nature of memory fragmentation. In a perfect world, memory is a single, unbroken river of bytes. A program requests a bucket of water; the manager hands it over. The program finishes and returns the bucket. The river flows on, unbroken. Reality, however, is far more chaotic. Programs request memory in varying sizes and at varying times. They release it unpredictably. Over time, the river of memory becomes a series of isolated pools separated by dry land. This is external fragmentation: the total amount of free memory may be sufficient to satisfy a new request, but because that free memory is scattered in non-contiguous chunks, the request cannot be fulfilled.

Consider a scenario where a memory allocator has released three blocks: 4KB, 8KB, and 4KB, sitting side by side. A new process requests a contiguous 12KB block. Without coalescing, the allocator sees three separate islands. It cannot bridge the gap, even though 16KB of free space exists. The request fails, triggering a costly system call to expand the heap or, in the worst case, causing the program to crash. Coalescing solves this by erasing the boundaries. As soon as the 8KB and the adjacent 4KB blocks are freed, the allocator merges them into a 12KB block. The islands become a continent. This transformation turns a failed allocation into a success, reclaiming space and maintaining the fluidity of the memory pool.

The decision of when to perform this merge is a critical architectural trade-off, balancing the immediate cost of CPU cycles against the future cost of fragmentation. There are two primary strategies: eager coalescing and deferred coalescing. Eager coalescing, as the name suggests, occurs the moment a block is freed. If Block A is released and its neighbor Block B is already free, the system immediately merges them. The advantage is clear: the memory landscape is kept pristine, and the likelihood of a future allocation failing due to fragmentation is minimized. However, the cost is high. Every free operation now requires a check of the neighbor's status, a calculation of the new size, and a pointer update. In a system with millions of allocations per second, this overhead can become a bottleneck.

Deferred coalescing takes the opposite approach. It ignores the immediate merging of blocks. When a block is freed, it is left as is, even if its neighbor is free. The system only performs the merge when allocation pressure demands it—when a new request for a large block arrives and the allocator is forced to scan the free list to find a suitable candidate. At that moment, it may coalesce several small blocks to form a large one. This defers the computational cost to the time of allocation, which might be less frequent or more manageable. However, the risk is that if the system runs low on memory and cannot find a large enough block even after scanning, it may trigger a garbage collection cycle or a page fault that could have been avoided. The choice between eager and deferred is not binary; it is a spectrum tuned to the specific workload. In real-time systems where latency is king, eager coalescing is often preferred to guarantee allocation speed. In general-purpose servers where throughput matters more, deferred coalescing might save precious CPU cycles.

The efficiency of coalescing is heavily dependent on the underlying allocation algorithm. The Buddy System, a classic memory management scheme, exemplifies a design where coalescing is not just efficient but mathematically elegant. In the buddy system, memory is allocated in blocks of sizes that are powers of two (1KB, 2KB, 4KB, 8KB, etc.). Every block of size $2^k$ has exactly one "buddy" of the same size. The location of this buddy is uniquely determined by a simple bit manipulation of the block's address. If the address of a block is even, its buddy is the next block; if odd, the previous one. When a block is freed, the system instantly checks if its buddy is also free. If it is, they are merged into a single block of size $2^{k+1}$. This process can cascade recursively, merging two 4KB blocks into 8KB, then 16KB, and so on, up to the size of the entire heap. The overhead is negligible because the buddy's location is known without searching. This structural guarantee makes the buddy system a favorite for operating system kernels and embedded systems where predictability is paramount.

Contrast this with the Slab Allocation strategy, which represents a philosophical departure from the coalescing paradigm. In the slab allocator, memory is not managed as a continuous stream of bytes but as a collection of caches, each dedicated to objects of a specific size. When a program requests a 64-byte structure, it is taken from the 64-byte slab. When that structure is freed, it is returned to the 64-byte slab cache, not merged with its neighbors. The slab allocator avoids coalescing entirely. Why? Because the cost of splitting and merging variable-sized blocks is deemed too high for the use case. Slab allocation trades the flexibility of variable-size allocation for speed and reduced fragmentation within the cache. Since objects are reused from the cache, the memory remains in a state of readiness. The fragmentation that might occur between different slab caches is acceptable because the allocator never attempts to satisfy a request by combining two different caches. This approach is ubiquitous in the Linux kernel, where the speed of allocating network packets or inode structures is critical. The trade-off is explicit: lose the ability to merge across size classes to gain the speed of a direct cache hit.

The history of memory management is a chronicle of these trade-offs. Early allocators, like the original `malloc` in Unix, relied on simple linked lists of free blocks. Coalescing was performed eagerly but required traversing the list to find adjacent blocks, leading to $O(n)$ complexity for the free operation. As systems grew more complex, the need for efficiency drove the development of Doug Lea's malloc (`dlmalloc`), a landmark allocator designed in the early 1990s that optimized coalescing through a combination of bins and a tree structure. `dlmalloc` uses a segregated free list approach, grouping free blocks by size and using a "coalesce on free" strategy that is highly optimized for modern cache hierarchies. It recognizes that the cost of a cache miss often outweighs the cost of an extra pointer chase, a lesson that remains relevant in the era of Blackwell GPUs.

The implications of coalescing extend far beyond the CPU cache and into the parallel computing realm, particularly in the context of NVIDIA's CUDA architecture. In a GPU, thousands of threads access global memory simultaneously. The efficiency of this access is governed by memory coalescing, a concept that shares a name but a distinct mechanism with the memory management coalescing discussed here. In CUDA, memory coalescing refers to the hardware's ability to combine multiple memory requests from threads in a warp into a single, efficient memory transaction. If threads in a warp access consecutive memory addresses, the hardware coalesces these requests into one burst transfer. If they access scattered addresses, the hardware must make multiple transactions, drastically reducing bandwidth. While this is a hardware-level optimization for data access patterns, it mirrors the software-level goal of reducing fragmentation. Both concepts aim to transform a scattered, inefficient state into a contiguous, efficient one. In the context of Blackwell, where the memory bandwidth is a primary constraint for training massive language models, the alignment of data structures to ensure hardware coalescing is just as critical as the software coalescing of free blocks to prevent fragmentation.

Garbage collection (GC) in modern languages like Java, Go, or Rust also relies heavily on coalescing and related techniques. When a garbage collector identifies objects that are no longer reachable, it reclaims their memory. In a non-compacting collector, these objects are simply marked as free, potentially leaving a fragmented heap. To mitigate this, some garbage collectors employ a "mark-compact" algorithm. This process not only reclaims space but also moves live objects together to form a single, contiguous block of free space at the end of the heap. This is an extreme form of coalescing, often deferred until the system is under significant memory pressure. The cost is high—moving objects requires updating all pointers to them—but the benefit is a heap that is entirely free of external fragmentation. The decision to compact is a complex calculation involving the cost of moving data versus the cost of allocation failure. In systems with real-time constraints, this compaction is often avoided or spread out over many cycles to prevent latency spikes.

The theoretical underpinnings of coalescing are well-documented in academic literature, such as the textbook "Operating Systems: Three Easy Pieces" (OSTEP), which provides a rigorous analysis of fragmentation, splitting, and coalescing. The text highlights that while coalescing is a powerful tool, it is not a panacea. It cannot eliminate fragmentation entirely if the allocation pattern is adversarial. A program that allocates and frees blocks in a specific alternating pattern can still create a fragmented heap even with eager coalescing. This is known as the "fragmentation hole" problem. Furthermore, the overhead of coalescing itself can become a performance bottleneck in highly concurrent systems. If multiple threads are freeing memory simultaneously, they may contend for the locks required to merge blocks, leading to serialization and reduced parallelism. Modern allocators address this by using thread-local caches, where each thread maintains its own small pool of free blocks, performing coalescing locally and only interacting with the global pool when the local cache is empty or full. This design, pioneered by allocators like `tcmalloc` (Thread-Caching Malloc), significantly reduces the contention and overhead associated with global coalescing.

The evolution of memory management is a testament to the enduring challenge of resource allocation. From the early days of redlining in city maps—a social analogy that, while different in context, shares the theme of defining boundaries and access—to the intricate bit-manipulation of the buddy system, the goal remains the same: to make the best use of limited space. In the context of the Blackwell GPU, where the memory hierarchy is a multi-level puzzle of HBM3e, L2 caches, and register files, the principles of coalescing are more relevant than ever. The GPU's memory controller must constantly decide whether to merge free pages, whether to defer the merge to a background thread, and how to align data to ensure that the massive parallelism of the tensor cores is not throttled by fragmented memory accesses.

The interplay between coalescing and other techniques like splitting is also crucial. When a large block is allocated but the request is for a smaller size, the allocator must split the large block into a used portion and a free remainder. This free remainder is then subject to coalescing. The cycle of split and merge is the heartbeat of dynamic memory management. An allocator that splits too aggressively creates many small fragments; one that merges too aggressively incurs too much overhead. The art of allocator design lies in finding the sweet spot. Doug Lea's notes on `dlmalloc` emphasize this balance, detailing how the allocator uses a size-based binning system to minimize the number of splits and merges required. By grouping blocks of similar sizes, the allocator ensures that splits result in free blocks that are likely to be coalesced with existing bins, rather than creating new, unusable fragments.

As we look toward the future of computing, the challenges of memory management will only intensify. With the advent of heterogeneous computing, where CPUs, GPUs, and specialized accelerators share memory pools, the complexity of coalescing increases exponentially. A memory block freed by a GPU thread must be coalesced in a way that is visible to the CPU, requiring a unified virtual address space and sophisticated coherence protocols. The principles of coalescing remain the same, but the scale and the stakes have changed. The efficiency of coalescing will determine the viability of next-generation AI models, the responsiveness of real-time systems, and the stability of cloud infrastructure.

In the end, coalescing is more than a technical detail; it is a fundamental strategy for managing chaos. It is the mechanism by which a system transforms the inevitable waste of dynamic allocation into a resource. It is the difference between a system that grinds to a halt and one that flows with the grace of a well-oiled machine. Whether it is the buddy system's elegant bit-twiddling, the slab allocator's cache-driven efficiency, or the deferred coalescing of a high-throughput server, the goal is always to reclaim space and reduce fragmentation. As we push the boundaries of what is possible with hardware like Blackwell, the algorithms that manage the memory beneath the silicon will continue to evolve, ensuring that the digital world remains a place of infinite possibility, not limited by the fragmentation of its own making.

Coalescing is the bridge between the abstract needs of the software and the physical realities of the silicon. It is the silent guardian of performance, working in the background to ensure that every byte of memory is ready to serve. In the grand architecture of computing, it is a small operation with a massive impact, proving once again that the details matter most.

It is a lesson in the power of structure, the cost of chaos, and the relentless pursuit of efficiency. As we stand on the precipice of a new era in computing, defined by the sheer scale of data and the complexity of algorithms, the principles of coalescing will remain as relevant as they were in the days of the earliest mainframes. It is the heartbeat of the digital age, a rhythm that keeps the machines running, one merged block at a time.
