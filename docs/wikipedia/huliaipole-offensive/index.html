Huliaipole offensive

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Huliaipole offensive

By late 2025, the city of Huliaipole had become a subterranean ghost town, a place where the concept of 'home' was redefined as a concrete basement shielded from the sky. Of the thousands who once called this Zaporizhzhia Oblast hub their residence, only 500 civilians remained, huddled in the dark as the Russian Armed Forces tightened a noose that had been slowly constricting since the full-scale invasion began in 2022. The line of contact, which had stagnated just south of the city for years, finally began to move, and it moved with a violence that turned the region into a grinding meat grinder. The Huliaipole offensive, a brutal military campaign that spanned from September 2025 to January 2026, was not merely a battle for territory; it was a systematic dismantling of a frontline city, culminating in its capture by Russian forces in the dead of winter.

To understand the magnitude of this collapse, one must look back to the summer of 2025. Following the Russian capture of Velyka Novosilka in January of that year, the momentum of the southern front shifted irrevocably. The Russian army, having secured a foothold, did not pause to consolidate. Instead, they turned their gaze westward, aiming for the Dnipropetrovsk region and the strategic heartland beyond. By September 1, the advance had already breached the administrative border, with Russian troops seizing the village of Komyshuvakha. This was the spark. Komyshuvakha sits at a critical junction where the Donetsk, Dnipropetrovsk, and Zaporizhzhia oblasts meet, a geographic choke point that, once taken, allowed the Russian war machine to fan out into three directions simultaneously.

The pace of the advance in September was staggering. Within just eleven days, Russian forces had pushed deep into Ukrainian territory, capturing the villages of Sosnivka and Ternove on September 12. The very next day, Moscow announced the seizure of Novomykolaivka. These were not isolated skirmishes but a coordinated, multi-axis thrust designed to stretch Ukrainian defenses to the breaking point. Further south, the pressure intensified as Russian units advanced west of Velyka Novosilka, securing the northeastern outskirts of the Zaporizhzhia region by taking Olhivske. The road connecting Huliaipole to Velyka Novosilka, a vital artery for Ukrainian logistics, became the focus of relentless assaults. By September 19, the Russian army had captured Novoivanivka, a move that brought their frontline uncomfortably close to the city's northern approaches.

The human cost of this maneuvering was immediate and devastating. On that same day, September 19, Ivan Fedorov, the governor of Zaporizhzhia Oblast, reported a grim incident in Huliaipole itself. A Russian drone struck a civilian car, killing a married couple. This was not collateral damage in the abstract; it was a targeted strike on the last remnants of normalcy in a city that had already been reduced to a siege zone. As the weeks turned into a month, the Russian grip tightened. In eastern Dnipropetrovsk, elements of the Russian 36th Motorized Rifle Brigade began a campaign of symbolic and tactical domination, raising Russian flags in multiple locations of Berezove, situated west of Ternove. By the final week of September, the Russian army had increased its control around Berezove, taking Kalynivske and Stepove, gains that the Ukrainian military officially denied but which geolocated evidence suggested were real.

October brought a new phase of the offensive, characterized by deeper penetrations and the systematic erosion of Ukrainian defensive lines. In the first days of the month, Russian assault units captured Verbove, pushing further west. Simultaneously, they launched assaults north of Malynivka, advancing into the eastern parts of Poltavka. The Ukrainian command attempted to stem the tide. On October 7, units of the Ukrainian 141st Mechanized Brigade launched a desperate counterattack in eastern Dnipropetrovsk. For a brief moment, it seemed to work; they recaptured Sosnivka and Sichneve. But the Russian response was overwhelming. In northeastern Zaporizhzhia, Russian troops pushed into Novovasylivske and Novohryhorivka. The following week, they seized Oleksiivka, a village north of Verbove, and continued their push west of Verbove, capturing Pryvillia and reaching the Yanchur river.

The Yanchur river was a natural barrier, a potential stopping point for an advance. But the Russian 36th Motorized Rifle Brigade had no intention of stopping. By October 19, they had taken full control of Poltavka. Geolocated footage soon confirmed the worst fears of Ukrainian commanders: Russian forces had established a bridgehead across the Yanchur river by seizing Zlahoda, formerly known as Pershotravneve. This was a critical tactical victory. It meant the Russians were no longer attacking from the east; they were now positioned on the other side of a major water obstacle, threatening the rear of Ukrainian positions.

The final week of October saw the Russian army secure a string of settlements on the right bank of the Yanchur river: Pavlivka, Pryvilne, and Krasnohirske. Further north, the 36th Motorized Rifle Brigade raised their flags over Vyshneve and Novooleksandrivka, signaling a new reality on the map. Just a week later, they gained a second foothold across the river by capturing Uspenivka. The situation was deteriorating rapidly. Units of the 37th Motorized Rifle Brigade leveled the frontline along the left bank, seizing Rybne. The Ukrainian lines were fracturing. Dense fog and intense pressure forced Ukrainian forces to withdraw from a series of settlements in eastern Zaporizhzhia: Nove, Novomykolaivka, Novouspenivske, Okhotnyche, and Solodke.

The scale of the Russian artillery barrage was industrial in nature. Vladyslav Voloshyn, a spokesman for Ukraine's Southern Defense Forces, told ABC News that Russia was launching over 400 artillery strikes per day on Ukrainian positions. This was not an artillery duel; it was an artillery hammer. Ukrainian Commander-in-Chief Oleksandr Syrskyi acknowledged the gravity of the situation, admitting that the Huliaipole sector had deteriorated significantly. He cited "numerically superior" Russian forces capturing three settlements and engaging in fierce clashes in Rivnopillia and Yablukove. The human toll continued to mount; Governor Fedorov reported that a 56-year-old civilian was killed by a Russian FPV drone in Huliaipole.

By mid-November, the collapse of the northern defensive line became inevitable. On November 12, the Ukrainian army pulled back from Rivnopillia. The very next day, the DeepStateMap, a widely followed conflict monitoring tool, showed that Russian forces had taken full control of Rivnopillia and Yablukove, located to the northeast of Huliaipole. The strategic implication was clear: the capture of Rivnopillia gave Russian forces the operational room to advance directly on Huliaipole from the north. The fighting, previously held at a distance, was now knocking on the city's front door. Residents who had remained through the shelling of the previous years began a frantic evacuation.

The chaos on the ground was exacerbated by internal fractures within the Ukrainian command structure. Military analysts at DeepState described a chaotic retreat of the Ukrainian 102nd Territorial Defense Brigade, triggered by Russian troops infiltrating its rear positions. Amidst the confusion, one battalion lost command and control and retreated without orders, leading to tragic incidents of friendly fire with the 225th Assault Battalion. The brutality of the conflict was also evident in the treatment of prisoners; reports emerged of Russian forces executing four captured Ukrainian drone operators. The New York Times reported that by November, the Russian army had seized 75 square miles around Huliaipole, a massive swath of territory that effectively encircled the city.

Governor Ivan Fedorov announced on November 20 that the regional administration was beginning a formal evacuation of civilians from Huliaipole. The rate was agonizingly slow, around 10 to 20 people per day. At the time of the announcement, only an estimated 400 people remained inside the city. The evacuation was a desperate race against a closing door. Meanwhile, Russian forces continued their advance along the T-0401 Pokrovske–Huliaipole highway, reaching the eastern bank of the Haichur river by gaining footholds in Nechaivka, Radisne, and Nove Zaporizhzhia. Simultaneously, Russian units advanced east of Huliaipole, seizing Vesele.

The intensity of the combat was reflected in the casualty figures. Vladyslav Voloshyn claimed that 150 Russian soldiers were killed every day in the Huliaipole sector, representing half of the daily Russian casualties on the entire southern front. The Southern Defense Forces reported eliminating at least 250 Russian soldiers and 50 pieces of military equipment daily. They denied reports of a full encirclement, but the tactical reality was undeniable. By November 27, the Russian army had captured the remaining villages of Vysoke, Zelenyi Hai, and Zatyshshia to the east of Huliaipole. These victories placed Russian troops on both the eastern and northern flanks of the city, cutting off the primary escape routes and supply lines.

The beginning of December marked the final assault. Russian forces advanced in the fields north of Huliaipole toward Varvarivka while simultaneously assaulting the city itself from the north and east. Russian units penetrated into the eastern and northeastern parts of the city. Geolocated footage confirmed advances in southeastern Huliaipole as well. By December 14, Russian assault groups were spotted in the villages of Dobropillia and Varvarivka along the T-0401 highway, further tightening the vise. In the second half of December, the Russian army crossed the Haichur river and advanced into central Huliaipole.

The fall of the city was marked by a grim discovery. The Russian army captured the headquarters of the 1st Battalion of the Ukrainian 106th Territorial Defense Brigade. Video footage circulating on social media showed Russian soldiers inside the abandoned command post, accessing multiple laptops and an unlocked smartphone. The breach of the command structure was complete. The brutality of the final days was underscored by a report that on December 20, Russian forces executed three captured Ukrainian soldiers. The offensive, which had begun with the seizure of a border village in September, had now consumed a major city, leaving behind a landscape of destruction and a population that had been forced into the shadows.

The Huliaipole offensive was a microcosm of the wider war in Ukraine: a story of attrition, technological adaptation, and the sheer grinding weight of numbers. It demonstrated how a stalemate could be broken not by a single breakthrough, but by the relentless application of pressure across multiple axes, forcing a defender to retreat until there was nowhere left to go. The city of Huliaipole, once a symbol of Ukrainian resilience on the southern front, became a testament to the brutal arithmetic of modern warfare, where 500 civilians hiding in basements were the last witnesses to a city's fall.

The strategic implications of the Huliaipole offensive extended far beyond the city limits. By capturing Huliaipole, Russian forces secured a critical logistical hub and opened the door for further advances into the Dnipropetrovsk region. The road network that had been the lifeline of the Ukrainian defense in the south was severed. The fall of Huliaipole also had a psychological impact, signaling a shift in the momentum of the southern front. It showed that even heavily fortified positions could be overcome with sufficient resources and time. The battle was a reminder that in this war, the line between victory and defeat was often just a few kilometers of contested terrain, and that the cost of holding that terrain was measured in the lives of soldiers and civilians alike.

As the dust settled on the Huliaipole offensive in January 2026, the map of the southern front had been redrawn. The Russian army had achieved its objective, capturing the city and the surrounding area. But the victory came at a high price. The Russian forces had sustained heavy casualties, and the Ukrainian forces, despite the retreat, had inflicted significant damage. The war continued, with the front line now shifted, but the lessons of Huliaipole would not be forgotten. It was a battle that defined the nature of the conflict: a war of attrition where the ground was bought with blood, and the only certainty was the relentless advance of the artillery.

The story of Huliaipole is not just a military history; it is a human tragedy. It is the story of the 500 civilians who stayed, the married couple killed by a drone, the 56-year-old man who lost his life to a FPV strike, and the soldiers who fought and died in the basements and the streets. It is a story of a city that was reduced to a frontline, and then to a memory. The Huliaipole offensive serves as a stark reminder of the cost of war, and the fragility of peace in a world where the guns never truly fall silent.
