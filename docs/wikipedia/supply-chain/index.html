Supply chain

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Supply chain

In 1905, a British expedition to Tibet stumbled not over a mountain pass or a hostile army, but over a logistical failure. An article in The Independent described the excruciating difficulty of "keeping a supply chain with India unbroken." That single phrase, born of military necessity in the Himalayas, would eventually grow to describe the invisible nervous system of the modern global economy. Today, a supply chain is no longer just a line of soldiers carrying rations; it is a complex, living logistics system that converts raw materials into finished products and distributes them to end consumers, encompassing every facility, truck, warehouse, and handoff required to get an object from a mine in Chile to a living room in Ohio. Yet, while the scope has exploded from a colonial military footnote to the backbone of global civilization, the fundamental tension remains unchanged: how do we move things efficiently without breaking the people and the planet that make the movement possible?

To understand the supply chain, one must first discard the image of a simple, linear line. While the term "chain" suggests a rigid, sequential link, the reality is a dynamic, often chaotic network. At its core, a supply chain is divided into two primary stages: production and distribution. In the production stage, raw components are forged, grown, or extracted in manufacturing centers. These semi-finished parts are then transported to assembly plants where they are stitched together into a final product. The distribution stage follows, a sprawling web of central and regional hubs that ferry these goods to the ultimate destination: the end customer. However, this is not a one-way street. In sophisticated modern systems, the concept of a "circular" flow is gaining ground. Used products are reintroduced into the supply chain at any point where their residual value is recyclable, turning waste back into raw material and challenging the traditional "take-make-dispose" model.

The architecture of these networks is hierarchical, organized into tiers that often stretch back decades into the past. First-tier suppliers, known as "direct suppliers," stand in the front line, delivering goods or services directly to the client. But who feeds them? Second-tier suppliers supply the first tier, and third-tier suppliers feed the second, creating a deep, often opaque hierarchy. A smartphone in your pocket is the culmination of this cascade; its screen glass comes from one tier, its lithium batteries from another, its microchips from a third, all assembled by a fourth. This structure creates a phenomenon known as "profit layering." In this scenario, every single tier adds a margin to their costs before passing the product up the line. The result is a bloated final price that reflects not just the value of the goods, but the accumulated profit of every intermediary who touched them.

Consider the stark example of the UK's Ministry of Justice in 2015. The government had awarded lift maintenance and refurbishment contracts to a main contractor. That contractor, rather than doing the work, sub-contracted the labor to a specialist lift company. The result was a massive inefficiency: the Ministry was paying for the main contractor's overhead and profit margin, only for the actual work to be done by a specialist who received a fraction of the total cost. The Ministry eventually realized the absurdity of this arrangement and began contracting directly with the specialist contractors, bypassing the unnecessary profit layer. This is the hidden tax of complexity. When a supply chain is too long, too many hands reach in to take a cut, and the final consumer pays the price.

Academics have long debated the precise definition of where a supply chain begins and ends. Mentzer et al. argue that at least three entities are required for a true "supply chain" to exist: a supplier, a company, and a customer. Without this triad, there is merely a transaction, not a chain. Yet, the boundaries are fluid. Alan Harrison and Janet Godsell, prominent voices in the field, argue that supply chain processes should not be designed in a vacuum. Instead, they must be coordinated to focus entirely on "end customer buying behaviour." They posit that "customer responsiveness" is the ultimate indicator of a healthy chain. If materials cannot flow through a sequence of processes quickly enough to meet the shifting whims of the customer, the chain has failed, regardless of how efficient the factory floor is.

This focus on the customer reveals a critical flaw in how many businesses view their operations. Many of the exchanges in a supply chain take place between companies that are laser-focused on maximizing their own revenue within their specific sphere of interest. They often have little or no knowledge, and even less interest, in the players upstream or downstream. A factory manager might optimize for speed, unaware that their decision to switch to a cheaper, faster shipping method is causing a bottleneck for the retailer downstream, or that their raw material sourcing is fueling environmental degradation upstream. This siloed thinking is why the term "supply chain" is increasingly being criticized as a misnomer. The "chain" implies a linear, predictable structure that is "harder to relate to the way supply networks really operate." In reality, it is a loosely coupled, self-organizing network of businesses, a concept now often referred to as the "extended enterprise."

The distinction between "tightly coupled" and "loosely coupled" systems is perhaps the most strategic decision a company can make. Tightly coupled, or "hard-wired," collaboration represents a close, almost symbiotic relationship between a buyer and a supplier. The Chartered Institute of Procurement & Supply suggests the aim here is to reduce inventory and avoid stock-outs by synchronizing production schedules down to the minute. It is efficient, but it is fragile. If one link breaks, the whole system halts. Cutting-edge companies are now swapping these rigid ties for loosely coupled models. A loosely-coupled link implies low interdependency between buyer and seller, offering greater flexibility. In a volatile world, flexibility often trumps efficiency. If a supplier in one region faces a strike or a natural disaster, a loosely coupled system can pivot to another partner without collapsing. The trade-off is clear: tight coupling minimizes waste but maximizes risk; loose coupling embraces redundancy to ensure survival.

Marshall L. Fisher, in his seminal 1997 article "Which is the right supply chain for your product?", identified that there is no one-size-fits-all solution. He, along with Naylor, Naim, and Berry in 1999, proposed that supply chain strategies must match the nature of the product. For "functional" products like toilet paper or basic staples, where demand is predictable and margins are thin, the strategy must be "efficient." The goal is to minimize cost at every step. For "innovative" products like the latest fashion trends or cutting-edge tech, where demand is volatile and the risk of obsolescence is high, the strategy must be "responsive." Here, speed and adaptability are worth the extra cost. A mismatch is fatal. Trying to run a fast-fashion supply chain with the efficiency metrics of a grocery store will result in massive waste; trying to run a utility supply chain with the responsiveness of a luxury brand will bankrupt the company.

To measure the success of these complex systems, the industry relies on models like the SCOR (Supply-Chain Operations Reference) model. Developed by a consortium of industry leaders and the non-profit Supply Chain Council, SCOR became the de facto standard for defining the scope of supply-chain management. It measures performance from "the supplier's supplier to the customer's customer." It is a holistic view that includes delivery and order fulfillment performance, production flexibility, warranty and returns processing costs, and inventory turns. It forces companies to look beyond their four walls and evaluate the health of the entire network. Without such a framework, optimization becomes myopic, solving a local problem while creating a global disaster.

However, the most profound shift in supply chain thinking in the last two decades has not been technological, but ethical. The invisibility of the supply chain has long been a shield for socially irresponsible practices. When a consumer buys a cheap shirt, they rarely see the farm where the cotton was picked or the factory where it was sewn. A lack of transparency bars consumers from knowing where their purchases originated, facilitating exploitation. In recent years, large companies and global brands have been forced to integrate codes of conduct and guidelines into their corporate cultures. They are now making demands on their suppliers—farms, cleaning services, security firms, canteens—and verifying compliance through social audits.

The pressure is real. In 2018, the Loyola University Chicago's Supply and Value Chain Center found that 53% of supply chain professionals considered ethics to be "extremely" important to their organizations. This is a dramatic shift from the era where the bottom line was the only metric that mattered. Yet, the challenge remains immense. How does a brand ensure that a second-tier supplier in a remote region is not using child labor when they don't even know the second-tier supplier exists? The "extended enterprise" is often too vast to police effectively. When a major scandal erupts—be it forced labor in a cotton field or unsafe working conditions in a textile mill—it shatters the illusion that the supply chain is a neutral, mechanical process. It is, in fact, a reflection of human choices, and those choices have human costs.

The complexity of these networks also means that individual actors can wear multiple hats. A bank, for instance, might play a supporting role in a clothing supply chain, providing financing for the manufacturer. But in the supply chain for security printing, that same bank is the customer, ordering the checks and credit cards. Mentzer et al. remind us that the focus of a supply chain is on the product or service in its end state. We speak of "the supply chain for candy" or "the supply chain for clothing" because the journey is defined by the destination. Fazel Zarandi et al. add a crucial third component to the mix: "buyer-seller relations." It is not just about the flow of goods or information; it is about the human and corporate relationships that bind the chain together. Trust, negotiation, and conflict resolution are as vital as the logistics software.

As we look toward the future, the definition of a supply chain continues to expand. It is no longer just about moving physical goods. It is about the flow of information, the flow of finances, and the flow of responsibility. The SCOR model and other frameworks help us measure the efficiency, but they cannot measure the morality. The question is no longer just "How fast can we get this to the shelf?" but "At what cost?" The 1905 expedition to Tibet failed because the supply chain was broken by geography and logistics. Today, supply chains are broken by a different set of forces: political instability, ethical failures, and the sheer complexity of a globalized world that has outpaced our ability to manage it.

The break-even point for a supply chain has changed. In the earlier stages, raw material processing and manufacturing, the break-even is determined by the cost of production relative to the market price. In the later stages, wholesale and retail, it is determined by the velocity of sales and the cost of holding inventory. But in the modern era, there is a third break-even point: the social and environmental cost. If a supply chain is efficient but built on exploitation, it is not a sustainable model. If it is cheap but reliant on a fragile, tightly coupled network that collapses at the first sign of trouble, it is not a resilient model.

The journey from a simple line of soldiers in the Himalayas to the hyper-connected, data-driven, ethically charged networks of the 21st century is a testament to human ingenuity. It has allowed for the distribution of life-saving medicines to remote villages, the availability of fresh food year-round, and the democratization of technology. But it has also created a system where the human cost is often hidden in the fine print of a contract, buried under layers of profit and distance. As we move forward, the challenge for the next generation of supply chain managers is to build networks that are not only efficient and responsive but also transparent and humane. The chain must be strong enough to hold the weight of the world, but flexible enough to bend without breaking under the pressure of our own expectations. The supply chain is not just a logistical system; it is the story of how we live, how we trade, and how we treat one another in the process. And like any good story, it requires more than just a plot; it requires a conscience.
