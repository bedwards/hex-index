Surrogacy laws by country

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Surrogacy laws by country

In 2006, Australian senator Stephen Conroy and his wife Paula Benson made a public announcement that sent shockwaves through the legal and medical communities of their nation: they had arranged for a child to be born through egg donation and gestational surrogacy. Uniquely, Conroy was placed directly on the birth certificate as the father, bypassing the grueling, years-long adoption process that had been the standard for intended parents in Australia for decades. This singular event did more than just add a baby to the Conroy family; it forced a reckoning with the archaic machinery of parenthood laws that treated surrogacy arrangements as void rather than valid. It highlighted a fundamental truth about the modern world: the biological act of creating a child has long since outpaced the legal frameworks designed to protect it.

The legal landscape of surrogacy is a fractured mosaic, a patchwork of jurisdictions where the very definition of "family" shifts with the crossing of a border. In any specific country, the law tends to hinge on a few piercing questions that determine the fate of every arrangement. Is the contract between the intended parents and the gestational carrier enforceable, or is it a piece of paper destined to be ignored by the courts? Does the financial nature of the deal matter—specifically, does it make a difference if the woman carrying the child is paid a fee for her service, known as commercial surrogacy, or if she is merely reimbursed for her medical and living expenses, known as altruistic surrogacy? Furthermore, does the technology used change the legal outcome? Is there a distinction between traditional surrogacy, where the carrier is genetically related to the child, and gestational surrogacy, where she is not? Perhaps most critically, is there a legal pathway for the intended parents to be recognized as the child's parents immediately, or must they navigate the humiliating and uncertain route of post-birth adoption?

These questions yield answers that vary wildly from one jurisdiction to another, creating a global map of opportunity and prohibition. In nations that permit surrogacy, the rules often demand that the intended parents or the surrogate hold specific residency or citizenship status. But where these barriers are lowered, a new phenomenon emerges: fertility tourism. Countries without strict residency requirements become magnets for those from abroad, turning surrogacy into a global industry where parents cross borders to find the legal clarity their home nations deny them.

The tension between commerce and biology is perhaps most starkly defined in Europe. The European Union has drawn a hard line in the sand. Commercial surrogacy, defined as a transaction where an individual is paid to carry a baby or surrender it to others, is strictly illegal across the bloc. This prohibition is not merely a matter of local statute but is rooted in the fundamental human rights framework of the continent. Article 3 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union explicitly states that in the fields of medicine and biology, the human body and its parts must not, as such, be a source of financial gain. This ethical stance is reinforced by the Oviedo Convention, a treaty ratified by thirty countries. Article 21 of this convention leaves no room for ambiguity: "The human body and its parts shall not, as such, give rise to financial gain." The message from Europe is clear: the womb is not a commodity.

Yet, even within a continent that rejects commercial surrogacy, the landscape is not entirely uniform. In Australia, a nation that has wrestled intensely with these issues, the law draws a sharp distinction between altruism and commerce. All jurisdictions in Australia allow altruistic surrogacy, where the surrogate is reimbursed for approved expenses but receives no profit. However, commercial surrogacy is a criminal offense. The penalties for violating this ban are severe and vary by state, reflecting the gravity with which the Australian legal system views the commodification of pregnancy. In the Australian Capital Territory, entering into an international commercial surrogacy arrangement can result in imprisonment for up to one year. In New South Wales, the maximum penalty climbs to two years. In Queensland, it reaches three years. These are not mere administrative fines; they are criminal charges that can end careers and ruin lives.

The evolution of Australian law tells a story of adaptation. The Australian Capital Territory was the first to make only altruistic surrogacy legal in 2004. The catalyst for broader change was the public revelation by Senator Conroy in 2006. Before his announcement, the system was broken; couples who used surrogacy arrangements in Australia were forced to adopt their own genetic children after the birth, treating the gestational carrier as the legal mother by default. It was a legal fiction that denied intended parents their rights until the courts intervened. Following the Conroy case, the legal tide turned. Victoria passed the Assisted Reproductive Treatment Act 2008, which came into effect on January 1, 2010, cementing the ban on commercial surrogacy and establishing a framework for altruistic arrangements. Western Australia followed in 2009, but with a discriminatory twist; their law allowed only altruistic surrogacy for opposite-sex couples, explicitly prohibiting single people and same-sex couples from participating. It was not until 2010 that Queensland and New South Wales aligned with the national trend of allowing only altruistic surrogacy. Tasmania joined in 2013 with the Surrogacy Act No 34. Progress continued in 2017 when South Australia passed a bill granting gay couples equal access to surrogacy and IVF, receiving royal assent on March 15 and taking effect on March 21 of that year. Finally, in 2022, the Northern Territory passed legislation permitting altruistic surrogacy, completing the national shift. Yet, a gap remains in the safety net: a Medicare rebate is not available for IVF treatment specifically for surrogacy, leaving a financial burden on parents who have already navigated a complex legal maze.

The situation in Brazil presents a paradox of law versus reality. Commercial surrogacy is banned by Brazilian law, yet altruistic surrogacy is technically allowed. In practice, however, the distinction is often blurred, and the practice is performed illegally throughout the country. The gap between the statute books and the operating rooms is wide, creating a shadow market where the rules are ignored.

To the north, Canada offers a model of regulated altruism. The Assisted Human Reproduction Act (AHRC) permits only altruistic surrogacy. Gestational carriers may be reimbursed for approved expenses, but the payment of any other consideration or fee is strictly illegal. The law is clear: you can pay for the costs of the pregnancy, but you cannot pay for the pregnancy itself.

In contrast, the People's Republic of China has taken a hardline stance on the books. Surrogacy is forbidden by the Regulation of Human Assisted Reproductive Technology law. The Ministry of Health has established "departmental rules" that prohibit medical professionals from performing surrogacy procedures. The penalty for violation is a fine, but notably, it does not carry criminal liabilities for the doctors or the parents. Despite the ban, surrogacy arrangements are common in mainland China. An underground market for commercial surrogacy has flourished, estimated to encompass between 400 and 500 agencies as of 2012. This underground industry has grown significantly in the 21st century, leading to significant complications for children, surrogates, and parents alike. The law prohibits the procedure, but it has failed to stop the demand, creating a black market that operates with little oversight.

In South America, specifically Colombia, the legal landscape is defined by a lack of definition. As of today, there are no clear rules regarding surrogacy, and a significant loophole persists. The current laws applied are those governing natural childbirth. This means that, by default, the child must be registered with the surnames of the gestational carrier and her partner or spouse, if she has any. The intended parents are not recognized as legal parents at birth. To claim their child, they must engage in a challenging paternity lawsuit before a judge, a process that may include genetic tests and can be emotionally and financially draining. In an attempt to close this ambiguity, a right-wing political party, the Democratic Center, introduced a bill in July 2016 to determine the concept of surrogacy and forbid all types of it. However, the legislative process has been slow and contentious. Only altruistic surrogacy is allowed after a referendum over a new family law passed, and this became available in September 2022. The gap between the old loopholes and the new regulations remains a source of legal uncertainty.

The Czech Republic offers a curious case of silence. Surrogacy is not legally regulated, and so it is generally considered legal by default. The only mention of the phrase "surrogate motherhood" can be found in § 804 of the law n. 89/2012, where the law designates an exception to the ban of adoption by siblings for siblings carried by a surrogate mother. This is a narrow, technical acknowledgment that suggests the practice exists but is not fully integrated into the legal code.

France stands as a bastion of prohibition. Since 1994, Article 16-7 of the Code Civil has stipulated that any arrangement, commercial or altruistic, relating to procreation or surrogacy is void, meaning it has no legal effect. The law does not sanction the woman who gives birth or the sponsoring parents criminally, but it legally sanctions anyone who acts as an intermediary to arrange a surrogacy or makes any attempts to facilitate it. The French Court of Cassation took this point of view in 1991, holding that such agreements are illegal based on articles 6, 353, and 1128 of the Code Civil. The message is absolute: all surrogacy arrangements, both commercial and altruistic, are illegal and unenforceable.

Germany is currently in a state of legislative flux. While the German Civil Code maintains that the legal mother is always the woman who gave birth to the child, the political landscape is shifting. The German Free Democratic Party has expressed a desire to allow altruistic surrogacy, signaling a potential future where the strict prohibition might be relaxed. For now, however, the principle that the birth mother is the legal mother remains the governing rule.

In the Caucasus, Georgia emerges as a surprising haven. Surrogacy is legally permitted in Georgia, making it one of the few countries in the region where intended parents can engage in both altruistic and commercial surrogacy agreements. The legislation, established in the early 1990s, explicitly allows surrogacy for both domestic and international intended parents. This favorable legal environment has made Georgia a destination for fertility tourism. Since 2022, Georgia has absorbed a significant number of clients from Ukraine, which was the major surrogacy hub in Europe prior to the Russian invasion. Many Ukrainian clinics reopened in Georgia, and surrogates moved there to avoid conflict and to take advantage of the favorable legislation and lower costs. In 2023, the Georgian government announced some plans to reconsider its stance regarding commercial surrogacy for foreign citizens, perhaps in response to the sheer volume of international traffic. However, no further action was taken, and all forms of surrogacy remain fully legal. The country continues to offer programs involving egg donor programs, shipped embryos, and self-cycles. Under Georgian law, surrogacy is available to couples that are married or in a domestic relationship for at least one year. The legislation is protective of the intended parents: a surrogate cannot be an egg donor at the same time, and donor materials can still be used in surrogacy programs. Most importantly, the law stipulates that surrogates have no parental rights over the child born through surrogacy. Once the child is born, the intended parents are automatically recognized as the legal parents, and the surrogate has no claims to the child.

The complexity of these laws creates a world where a child's legal identity can depend entirely on where they are born. In the United States, Canada, and Australia, laws vary by state or territory, adding another layer of confusion. A couple might be legal parents in one state but strangers to their own child in the next. The residency requirements in many countries further complicate the picture, turning surrogacy into a game of geography. Those without requirements attract the world's childless, creating a global flow of people seeking the one thing the law in their home country denies them.

The core conflict remains the same everywhere: the tension between the biological reality of reproduction and the legal fiction of parenthood. In some places, the law tries to freeze time, insisting that the woman who gives birth is the only mother. In others, the law adapts, recognizing that the intent to parent can be as binding as the DNA that creates the child. From the criminal penalties in Australian states to the underground markets in China, from the strict prohibitions in France to the open markets in Georgia, the world is divided by a simple question: can a woman sell her womb? The answer determines not just the legality of a transaction, but the very existence of a family.

The journey from the 2006 Conroy announcement to the 2022 legislative shifts in South Australia and the Northern Territory shows that the law is not static. It bends under the pressure of social change, medical advancement, and the desperate needs of families. Yet, the gaps remain. The underground markets thrive where the law is too rigid. The legal battles continue where the law is too vague. And in the warzones of the world, where the conflict is not just political but biological, the search for a safe harbor for surrogacy continues. The laws by country are more than just statutes; they are the boundaries of human hope, drawn in ink that is often too thin to hold back the tide of human desire.

The distinction between commercial and altruistic surrogacy is the fault line upon which these legal systems fracture. In the European Union, the prohibition on making the human body a source of financial gain is a moral absolute. In the United States, particularly in states like California, commercial surrogacy is a thriving industry, protected by contract law. In between lies a vast array of jurisdictions where the rules are a confusing mix of bans, allowances, and gray areas. The Oviedo Convention's Article 21 stands as a monument to the idea that human dignity cannot be bought. But the reality of the 21st century is that human dignity is often tested by the very need to create life. The laws of Georgia, with their automatic recognition of intended parents, offer a solution that prioritizes the family unit over the biological act. The laws of France, with their absolute voidance of any surrogacy agreement, prioritize the protection of the woman from exploitation, even at the cost of denying intended parents their children. There is no consensus, only the ongoing struggle to define what it means to be a parent in a world where technology has decoupled conception from birth.

As we look to the future, the trend seems to be moving toward greater regulation and recognition, albeit at a glacial pace. The 2022 changes in Colombia, the 2017 shifts in South Australia, and the ongoing debates in Germany suggest that the world is slowly waking up to the reality of surrogacy. But until there is a global consensus, the fate of the surrogacy child will remain a gamble of geography. A child born in Georgia is the legal child of their intended parents from the moment of birth. A child born in France is a legal orphan until the courts intervene. A child born in China may never have their parents' names on their birth certificate. The laws by country are not just rules; they are the architects of destiny for the most vulnerable members of society. And as long as the laws differ, the world will remain a place where a child's rights are determined by the zip code of their birth.
