America's Stonehenge

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: America's Stonehenge

In the dense, pine-covered woods of Salem, New Hampshire, a thirty-acre landscape of scattered boulders and constructed stone walls stands as a testament to a century-long battle between archaeological reality and the human desire for mystery. To the untrained eye, the site presents a bewildering array of megaliths, trenches, and chambers that seem to whisper of an ancient, pre-Columbian civilization. Visitors often arrive expecting to walk through the footsteps of Irish monks or Phoenician sailors who supposedly crossed the Atlantic long before Christopher Columbus set sail. They are drawn by the name, a rebranding effort from 1982 that christened the location "America's Stonehenge," a moniker intended to elevate the site from a roadside curiosity to a monument of global historical significance. Yet, the stones themselves tell a more complicated, and perhaps more fascinating, story of 18th-century farming, 20th-century insurance executives, and the persistent allure of pseudoarchaeology.

The site, originally known as "Mystery Hill," was first documented in print in the 1907 History of Salem, N.H., where it was described with a blend of reverence and confusion. The text noted the presence of Jonathan Pattee's Cave, recalling a man who lived in the woods seventy years prior and utilized the rough boulders and soft pines for shelter and storage. The author of that historical marker described the area as a "wild but beautiful spot" where "the most weird and fantastic tale might be woven." This early description captured the essence of the location that would later captivate the imagination of William Goodwin. In 1937, Goodwin, an insurance executive with a penchant for the esoteric, purchased the property. He was not merely a landowner; he was a visionary who became convinced that the stone structures scattered across his land were not the random results of colonial agriculture but the deliberate architectural achievements of Irish monks, specifically a group known as the Culdees, who he believed had settled in North America centuries before the Viking or Spanish arrivals.

Goodwin's conviction was rooted in a specific historical mythos: the legend of St. Brendan, an Irish priest said to have sailed to the New World in the late 500s or early 600s. This narrative, while compelling to the romantic mind, lacks any archaeological corroboration. Goodwin, however, was undeterred by the absence of evidence. In fact, his enthusiasm led him to actively reshape the landscape to fit his theory. He rearranged massive stones, moving them to align with his interpretation of astronomical phenomena and religious significance. He sought to prove that the site was a sacred temple, complete with a "sacrificial table" and an astronomical calendar capable of predicting lunar and solar events. The stones he moved, and the new configurations he created, became the primary features that visitors would see for decades. Archaeologist David Starbuck, in later years, would summarize this era of the site's history with a stark assessment: "It is widely believed that Goodwin may have 'created' much of what is visible at the site today."

The physical evidence of Goodwin's intervention is etched into the very surface of the rock. Many of the stones bear drill marks, scars left by the quarrying operations that took place on the site long before Goodwin's arrival. The area was not a pristine, untouched wilderness but a working landscape that had been altered by human industry. Before Goodwin, the stones were likely part of the agricultural infrastructure of New England farmers in the 18th and 19th centuries. The so-called "sacrificial stone," a flat-topped boulder with deep grooves that some claim channeled blood during ritual sacrifices, bears a striking resemblance to "lye-leaching stones" found on countless old farms across the region. These stones were used by colonial farmers to extract lye from wood ashes, the crucial first step in the manufacture of soap. The grooves, far from being ritualistic, were functional, designed to collect the runoff liquid. Similarly, the structure often identified as a temple or a calendar could easily be a cider press bed stone, a common tool in New England agriculture where the grooves served to collect the pressed juice. The line between a sacred altar and a farm tool is often blurred by the lens of modern imagination, but the archaeological record points decisively toward the practical.

Despite the lack of evidence for a pre-Columbian European presence, the allure of the site has never waned. The theory that the stones were built by Celts, Phoenicians, or Irish monks gained traction in the public consciousness, fueled by the work of writers and self-styled experts who were eager to rewrite the history of the Americas. In 1976, Barry Fell, a marine biologist by training who turned his attention to archaeology and epigraphy, published America BC: Ancient Settlers in the New World. Fell claimed to have found evidence of pre-Columbian occupation, citing astronomically linked positioning of stones and what he identified as Phoenician inscriptions written in Ogham, an ancient Irish script. His theories were widely disseminated and captivated the public, lending a veneer of academic legitimacy to the pseudoarchaeological claims. However, the expert consensus among professional archaeologists was swift and dismissive. Fell's specialty was marine biology, not the study of ancient stone tools or inscriptions, and his writings were widely deemed to be pseudoarchaeological. As archaeologist Curtis Runnels stated, "No Bronze Age artifacts have been found there. ... In fact, no one has found a single artifact of European origin from that period anywhere in the New World."

The scientific reality of the site is grounded in the presence of Indigenous peoples, not European monks. Radiocarbon analysis of charcoal found on the site confirms that humans occupied the area approximately 4,000 years ago. In 1982, David Stewart-Smith, the director of restoration at Mystery Hill, led an excavation of a megalith found in a stone quarry to the north of the main site. Under the supervision of the New Hampshire state archaeologist, his team uncovered hundreds of chips and flakes from the stone. The analysis of these fragments revealed that the stone had been quarried using percussion techniques, a method consistent with American Indigenous lithic traditions. The surface of the stone suggested it was modeled by Indigenous stone workers using stone tools, rather than the metal tools that would have been used by European settlers. Further evidence of Indigenous presence includes the remains of a Native American wigwam found in the area, now accompanied by a replica for visitors. Associated with the wigwam were two fire pits dating back 2,000 years. A canoe, dated to approximately 300 years ago, is now housed in the site's museum, alongside various Native American tools and pottery. These artifacts provide a tangible link to the true history of the land, a history that predates the arrival of any European by millennia.

The transformation of the site from a private property to a public attraction has been a journey of rebranding and reinterpretation. The name "Mystery Hill" served the site well for decades, capturing the air of intrigue that Goodwin cultivated. However, in 1982, the owners decided to rename it "America's Stonehenge." This was a calculated move to separate the site from the category of roadside oddities and to reinforce the idea that it was an ancient archaeological site of global importance. The name was not invented for the rebranding; it had been coined in a news article in the early 1960s, reflecting the growing public fascination with the site's supposed antiquity. The choice of name was deliberate, invoking the image of the famous stone circle in England to lend the New Hampshire site a similar gravity. Yet, as the site's own history attests, there is no evidence of a cultural or historical connection between the two. The name is a marketing tool, a hook to draw in visitors who are eager to believe in the extraordinary.

The site has not been immune to the darker currents of modern conspiracy theories. In 2019, the tranquility of the woods was shattered by an act of vandalism that shocked the community. Power tools were used to carve inscriptions into the so-called "sacrificial table." The markings included the QAnon slogan "WWG1WGA," which stands for "Where we go one, we go all," and "IAMMARK," the Twitter handle of the perpetrator. On March 4, 2021, the New Hampshire State Police arrested Mark L. Russo, a member of the online QAnon group, and charged him with criminal mischief. The investigation revealed a disturbing motivation behind the act. Russo and another QAnon follower had both lost adult sons. They had become convinced that the "sacrificial table" was a real artifact of an ancient ritual and that a worldwide conspiracy led by Hillary Clinton had killed their sons to extract adrenochrome, a substance they believed could renew life. The police investigation, aided by social media researcher Chris Walters who used a pseudonym to find photographs of the site and items later recovered by law enforcement, uncovered the depth of this delusion. The vandalism was not just an act of destruction; it was a physical manifestation of a conspiracy theory that had taken hold in the minds of grieving parents, turning a stone structure of colonial or Indigenous origin into a stage for their personal tragedy.

The literary world has also found its place in the lore of Mystery Hill. H.P. Lovecraft, the renowned American horror writer known for his fascination with the occult and the ancient, was an enthusiast for New England megalith sites. He visited Mystery Hill sometime between 1928 and the 1930s, a period when the site was still under the ownership of Goodwin and the "Irish monk" theory was being actively promoted. Popular attribution often links Lovecraft's visit to his 1929 story "The Dunwich Horror," suggesting that the eerie stone structures inspired the tale. However, scholars have placed Lovecraft's visit too late to have been the direct inspiration for the story, which was written before he could have seen the site in its Goodwin-era configuration. Despite this chronological discrepancy, the association persists, adding a layer of literary mystique to the already complex history of the place.

Television has played a significant role in amplifying the myths surrounding America's Stonehenge. The site has been featured in numerous programs, from the In Search of... series, which aired an episode in 1977 speculating about an ancient Minoan origin, to the History Channel's Secrets of the Ancient World in 2002. In the latter, Boston University archaeology professor Curtis Runnels appeared on screen to refute the theory that the site was built by Celts in ancient history. These programs have served to broadcast the conflicting narratives to a mass audience, often giving equal weight to the fringe theories and the scientific consensus. The visual spectacle of the stones, combined with the dramatic storytelling of television, has helped to cement the image of America's Stonehenge as a place of ancient secrets, even as the archaeological evidence points to a much more grounded reality.

Today, the site operates as a privately owned tourist attraction, open to the public for a fee. It has evolved into a recreational area that includes snowshoe trails and an alpaca farm, blending the historical intrigue with family-friendly activities. The museum houses the artifacts that tell the true story of the site, including the Native American canoe and tools. A replica of the wigwam stands next to the trail, offering a glimpse into the lives of the people who truly inhabited the land. The site is mentioned on New Hampshire Historical Marker No. 72, acknowledging its place in the state's history, even if that history is more about the 20th-century imagination than the pre-Columbian past.

The story of America's Stonehenge is ultimately a story about the human need to find meaning in the landscape. It is a place where the practical needs of colonial farmers, the spiritual convictions of an insurance executive, the scientific rigor of archaeologists, and the conspiracy theories of the internet age have all collided. The stones remain, silent and enduring, bearing the marks of their many interpreters. They are a reminder that history is not just a record of what happened, but also a reflection of what we believe, what we fear, and what we desire to be true. As visitors walk the trails, they are walking through a palimpsest of human history, where the lines between fact and fiction are as blurred as the grooves on the "sacrificial table."

The debate over the site's origins is unlikely to be settled by a single discovery. The absence of Bronze Age artifacts and the presence of 18th-century quarry marks and 20th-century rearrangements make the case for a pre-Columbian European settlement extremely difficult to prove. Yet, the allure of the mystery remains. The site continues to attract those who wish to believe that the history of the Americas is more complex and more magical than the standard narrative suggests. In this way, America's Stonehenge serves as a mirror, reflecting the hopes and fantasies of those who come to see it. It is a place where the past is constantly being rewritten, where the stones are not just rocks, but canvases for our collective imagination.

The legacy of William Goodwin is inextricably linked to the site. His vision, however misplaced, transformed a collection of farmstones into a destination that continues to draw thousands of visitors each year. He created a narrative that was so compelling that it has survived for nearly a century, outlasting the original structures he moved and the quarrying that scarred the landscape. His belief in the Irish monks may have been a fiction, but the impact of that fiction on the cultural landscape of New Hampshire is a reality. The site stands as a monument to the power of storytelling, a place where the line between history and myth is as thin as the veneer of truth that covers the stones.

As the sun sets over the thirty acres of Salem, the shadows lengthen across the stone walls and chambers. The wind rustles through the pines, carrying the whispers of the past. Whether those whispers are of Irish monks, Native American hunters, or colonial farmers depends on the listener. But the stones themselves remain silent, waiting for the next generation to interpret their silent language. In the end, America's Stonehenge is not just a collection of rocks; it is a testament to the enduring human quest to understand our place in the world, to find connection with the past, and to weave our own stories into the fabric of history. The mystery remains, not because of what is hidden in the stones, but because of what we project onto them.
