Soviet occupation of Bessarabia and Northern Bukovina

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Soviet occupation of Bessarabia and Northern Bukovina

Between June 28 and July 3, 1940, the map of Eastern Europe was redrawn not by the clash of armies in the field, but by the stroke of a pen and the threat of a gun barrel. In a span of six days, the Soviet Union absorbed 50,762 square kilometers of territory, displacing the sovereignty of Romania and incorporating 3,776,309 souls into the Soviet machine. This was not a war in the traditional sense; it was a forced retrocession, a diplomatic strangulation that turned a sovereign state's border into a Soviet frontier overnight. The territories in question, Bessarabia and Northern Bukovina, had been Romanian for barely two decades, yet their loss marked the beginning of a geopolitical tragedy that would echo through the rest of the 20th century.

To understand the magnitude of this event, one must first grasp the sheer speed of the collapse. The Soviet Union had prepared for a full-scale invasion, mobilizing massive forces along the border, ready to crush any resistance. Instead, the Romanian government, paralyzed by the sudden isolation of its allies and the terrifying specter of the Nazi advance in the West, chose to capitulate without firing a shot. The ultimatum delivered on June 26, 1940, gave Romania less than forty-eight hours to comply or face total destruction. By the time the deadline passed, the Red Army was already rolling across the Prut River, meeting no organized opposition. The occupation was swift, the annexation absolute, and the international community largely silent.

The legal fiction surrounding this annexation is as fascinating as it is disturbing. While the use of force had been declared illegal by the Conventions for the Definition of Aggression in July 1933, the Soviets managed to cloak their aggression in the garb of consent. The new status of the annexed territories was officially based on a formal agreement where Romania, under duress, consented to the retrocession of Bessarabia and the cession of Northern Bukovina. However, the devil was in the details. The ultimatum had specifically mentioned Bessarabia and Northern Bukovina, but it had said nothing about the Hertsa region. Yet, the Soviets occupied Hertsa anyway. Similarly, six Romanian islands on the Chilia branch of the Danube, covering 23.75 square kilometers, were seized on October 26, 1940, despite not being part of the original demand. These were not accidents of war; they were calculated expansions of a pre-determined imperial design.

The Shadow of the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact

The seeds of this catastrophe were sown long before the ultimatum was ever written. On June 24, 1940, just two days before the Soviet ultimatum, Nazi Germany was made aware of the planned Soviet move. This was no surprise to Berlin; the 1939 Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact had included a secret protocol acknowledging Soviet interest in Bessarabia. However, the Germans, bound by their own strategic calculations and unwilling to antagonize their new ally, did not inform the Romanian authorities. They offered no support, leaving Bucharest to face the Soviet juggernaut alone.

The timing was not coincidental. On June 22, 1940, France, the traditional guarantor of Romanian borders and a key member of the Entente, fell to the Nazi advance. The collapse of the French Third Republic sent shockwaves through the diplomatic world. It signaled the end of the old order and left Romania exposed on all fronts. The Soviets, watching the French defeat with keen interest, seized the moment. With France out of the picture, the balance of power in Europe had tilted decisively toward the Axis powers, but Moscow moved with its own agenda. The fall of France is considered the critical factor that emboldened the Soviet leadership to issue the ultimatum. They knew Romania was isolated, desperate, and unable to rely on its traditional Western protectors.

Yet, the Soviet invasion of Bukovina in 1940 was a brazen violation of the very pact that had enabled it. The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact had defined spheres of influence, and Northern Bukovina had never been included in the Soviet sphere agreed upon with the Axis. By taking it, Stalin overreached, expanding his empire beyond the negotiated limits. This was a signal to the world that the pact was merely a tactical pause, not a binding constraint on Soviet ambition.

The Human Cost and the New Republics

The administrative reorganization that followed the occupation was swift and ruthless. On August 2, 1940, the Moldavian Soviet Socialist Republic (MSSR) was proclaimed as a constituent republic of the Soviet Union. This new entity was a patchwork quilt, encompassing most of Bessarabia and a portion of the Moldavian Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic, which had been located on the left bank of the Dniester River in the Ukrainian SSR (an area known today as the breakaway region of Transnistria).

The Soviet administration did not stop at drawing new borders. They engaged in a systematic campaign of political persecution that would define the region for decades. Executions, deportations to labor camps, and mass arrests became the daily reality for the inhabitants of these newly annexed lands. The Soviet state viewed the local population with deep suspicion, seeing in them a potential for anti-Soviet resistance. The elite, the intelligentsia, and anyone with ties to the previous Romanian administration were targeted first.

The ethnic demographics of the region played a crucial role in how the Soviets administered the territory. The Hertsa region and the areas inhabited by Slavic majorities, such as Northern Bukovina and the northern and southern parts of Bessarabia, were not incorporated into the new Moldavian SSR. Instead, they were handed over to the Ukrainian SSR. This decision reflected the Soviet policy of dividing and ruling, ensuring that no single ethnic group could form a cohesive national bloc that might challenge Moscow's authority. The ethnic Russians, Ukrainians, and other Slavic minorities were often favored in the new administration, while the Romanian majority was marginalized.

The Historical Roots: From Empire to Union

To understand why Bessarabia was such a contested prize, one must look further back than 1940. The historical region of Bessarabia was the eastern part of the Principality of Moldavia. In 1812, under the terms of the Treaty of Bucharest, the Ottoman Empire, to which Moldavia was a vassal state, ceded the region to the Russian Empire. This was the beginning of the "Bessarabian question," a conflict that was both political and national in nature.

The demographic shifts over the century of Russian rule were stark. According to the 1897 census, Bessarabia, then a guberniya of the Russian Empire, had a population that was 47.6% Romanians, 19.6% Ukrainians, 8% Russians, 11.8% Jews, 5.3% Bulgarians, 3.1% Germans, and 2.9% Gagauz. These figures showed a significant decrease in the proportion of Moldovans and Romanians compared to the census of 1817, conducted shortly after the Russian annexation. In that earlier survey, Moldovans and Romanians represented 86% of the population. The decline was the direct result of Russian policies of settling other nationalities in the region and a systematic campaign of Russification. The Russian Empire sought to dilute the Romanian character of the land, bringing in Ukrainians, Russians, Bulgarians, and Germans to create a more pliable, multi-ethnic population that would be less likely to rally around a national cause.

The Russian Revolution of 1917 brought a brief period of hope and self-determination. A national assembly known as Sfatul Țării was formed in Bessarabia to manage the province. This body initiated several national and social reforms and, on December 2/15, 1917, declared the Moldavian Democratic Republic an autonomous republic within the Russian Federative Democratic Republic. However, the revolution also unleashed the forces of Bolshevism. The Rumcherod, an Odessa-based soviet council loyal to the Petrograd Soviet, formed in late December and decided to take action against the authority of Sfatul Țării.

By January 1/14, 1918, the Front Section of the Rumcherod, made up of Bolsheviks, captured strategic locations in Chisinau. They attempted to seize power by arresting elected deputies and abolishing Sfatul Țării, replacing it with a self-proclaimed Moldavian Soviet. The contrast between the two bodies was telling: none of the members of the new Soviet were ethnic Moldavians, whereas in Sfatul Țării, ethnic Moldovans constituted about 70% of the membership.

It was in this chaotic vacuum that Romanian troops entered Bessarabia in early January 1918. With the consent of the Allies and based on calls for intervention, the Romanian army pushed the Soviet forces back over the Dniester by February. The intervention was not without consequence; Soviet Russia broke off diplomatic relations with Romania and confiscated the Romanian Treasure, which had been stored in Moscow for safekeeping during the war. To calm the situation, Entente representatives in Iași issued a guarantee that the presence of the Romanian army was a temporary military measure for stabilization, without affecting the political life of the region.

The political landscape shifted again in January 1918 when the Ukrainian People's Republic declared its independence from Russia, physically isolating Bessarabia from the Petrograd government. This isolation paved the way for the declaration of independence of the Moldavian Republic on January 24/February 5. Following several Soviet protests, Romanian Prime Minister General Alexandru Averescu signed a treaty with the Soviet representative Christian Rakovsky on February 20/March 5. The treaty provided for the evacuation of Romanian troops within two months in exchange for the repatriation of Romanian prisoners of war. However, after the White Army forced the Soviets to withdraw from Odessa and the German Empire agreed to the Romanian annexation of Bessarabia in a secret agreement (part of the Buftea Peace Treaty) on March 5/18, Romanian diplomacy repudiated the treaty, claiming the Soviets were unable to fulfill their obligations.

On March 27/April 9, 1918, Sfatul Țării voted for the Union of Bessarabia with Romania, conditional upon the fulfillment of an agrarian reform. The vote was 86 in favor, 3 against, 36 abstentions, and 13 absent. This vote remains controversial among historians, with Romanian scholars like Cristina Petrescu and Sorin Alexandrescu questioning the conditions under which it was held. On April 18, Georgy Chicherin, the Soviet Commissar for Foreign Affairs, sent a note of protest against the incorporation of Bessarabia into Romania, setting the stage for the eventual Soviet reversal of this union.

The Axis Invasion and the Holocaust

The Soviet occupation of 1940 was short-lived in military terms, though its political consequences were permanent. In July 1941, as part of the Axis invasion of the Soviet Union (Operation Barbarossa), Romanian and German troops re-occupied Bessarabia, Northern Bukovina, and the Hertsa region. A military administration was established, and the region became a theater of horrific violence. The Jewish population of the region was targeted for extermination. Thousands were executed on the spot, while others were deported to Transnistria, a region between the Dniester and the Southern Bug rivers, where large numbers were killed in mass shootings and by disease and starvation. This period marked one of the darkest chapters in the history of the region, with the Romanian administration playing a central role in the Holocaust.

The tide of war turned in August 1944 during the Soviet Second Jassy–Kishinev Offensive. The Axis war effort on the Eastern Front collapsed under the weight of the Soviet advance. The coup of August 23, 1944, caused the Romanian army to cease resistance and join the fight against Germany. Soviet forces advanced from Bessarabia into Romania, capturing much of the standing army as prisoners of war and occupying the country. On September 12, 1944, Romania signed the Moscow Armistice with the Allies. The armistice and the subsequent peace treaty of 1947 confirmed the Soviet-Romanian border as it was on January 1, 1941.

Bessarabia, Northern Bukovina, and Hertsa remained part of the Soviet Union until its collapse in 1991. When the Soviet Union dissolved, these territories became part of the newly independent states of Moldova and Ukraine. The Declaration of Independence of Moldova on August 27, 1991, explicitly declared the Soviet occupation illegal, a statement that resonates with the historical memory of the region.

The story of Bessarabia and Northern Bukovina is one of shifting borders, broken promises, and the enduring struggle for national identity. From the Russian Empire's Russification policies to the Soviet ultimatum of 1940, and from the brief union with Romania to the Holocaust and the eventual independence of Moldova and Ukraine, the region has been a crucible of 20th-century history. The events of June and July 1940 were not merely a territorial adjustment; they were a fundamental transformation of the region's destiny, one that continues to shape the geopolitics of Eastern Europe today. The human cost of these decisions—measured in lives lost, families separated, and cultures suppressed—remains a stark reminder of the fragility of sovereignty in a world dominated by great power politics.

The use of force had been made illegal by the Conventions for the Definition of Aggression in July 1933, but from an international legal standpoint, the new status of the annexed territories was eventually based on a formal agreement through which Romania consented to the retrocession of Bessarabia and cession of Northern Bukovina.

This sentence, with its cold legalistic tone, encapsulates the tragedy of the era. It describes a process where the will of a nation was overridden by the threats of a superpower, and where the language of diplomacy was used to sanitize the reality of conquest. The Soviet occupation of Bessarabia and Northern Bukovina was a pivotal moment in history, a moment where the fate of millions was decided in a matter of days, leaving a legacy that continues to reverberate in the modern world. The regions that were once part of Romania are now integral parts of Moldova and Ukraine, but the memory of their lost sovereignty and the violence that accompanied their transfer remains a powerful force in the collective consciousness of the people who live there. The story is not just about borders; it is about the human cost of empire, the resilience of nations, and the enduring quest for self-determination in a world that often seems indifferent to the plight of the small and the vulnerable.

The demographic engineering of the Russian Empire, the political maneuvering of the post-WWI era, the brutal annexation of 1940, and the subsequent tragedies of World War II have all left their mark on the landscape of Bessarabia and Northern Bukovina. The region is a testament to the complexity of history, where the lines between friend and foe, occupier and liberator, are often blurred. The events of 1940 were a stark reminder that in the game of great powers, the small nations are often the first to lose, and their losses are the most profound. Yet, the story does not end in tragedy. The declaration of independence in 1991, the establishment of new nations, and the ongoing efforts to reconcile with the past are a testament to the resilience of the human spirit. The people of Bessarabia and Northern Bukovina have endured much, but they have also shown a remarkable capacity to rebuild, to remember, and to hope for a future where their sovereignty is never again threatened by the whims of empires. The history of this region is a cautionary tale, but it is also a story of survival and the enduring power of national identity.
