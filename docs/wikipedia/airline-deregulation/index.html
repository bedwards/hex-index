Airline deregulation

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Airline deregulation

In 1938, the United States government decided that the most efficient way to run an airline was to stop it from competing. Under the stewardship of the Civil Aeronautics Board (CAB), the federal government treated the sky not as a market, but as a carefully curated garden where every seed had to be approved, every price fixed, and every path pre-ordained. For four decades, this system held sway, creating a stable but stagnant industry where the "Big Four"—United, American, Eastern, and TWA—dominated the skies, accounting for 94% of air mail revenue and effectively controlling the nation's mobility. It was a system designed to protect the industry from itself, a paternalistic structure that assumed the government knew better than the market where planes should fly and what they should cost. But as the 1970s wore on, the cracks in this gilded cage became impossible to ignore. Inflation was spiraling, fuel costs were skyrocketing, and the promise of a stable, efficient travel network had curdled into a monopoly that stifled innovation and kept prices artificially high for the American public.

The story of airline deregulation is not merely a tale of legislative maneuvering in Washington; it is the story of a fundamental shift in how we understand freedom, competition, and the very fabric of modern travel. When the Airline Deregulation Act was finally signed into law in 1978, it did not just remove red tape; it shattered a decades-old social contract. It was a gamble that the chaos of the free market would produce a better outcome than the order of the state. The results were immediate, profound, and deeply uneven. While the average ticket price plummeted and the number of passengers exploded, the human cost of this efficiency was paid by the workers who built the system and the communities that found themselves bypassed by the new logic of profitability. The dream of a stable industry gave way to a ruthless reality where survival depended on the ability to cut costs at every turn, leading to a transformation of the American landscape that continues to define our travel experience today.

The Architecture of Control

To understand the magnitude of the 1978 act, one must first appreciate the sheer density of the regulatory web that preceded it. The roots of this control stretched back to the Great Depression and the administration of President Herbert Hoover. Walter Folger Brown, the Postmaster General from 1929 to 1933, was the architect of the early airline industry. In 1930, through the McNary-Watres Act (also known as the Air Mail Act), Brown revolutionized the way aviation was funded. He shifted the system from paying airlines based on the weight of mail they carried to paying them based on the space in their aircraft. This subtle change was a masterstroke of industrial policy: it incentivized airlines to build larger, passenger-capable planes rather than just efficient mail carriers. Brown used his power to award contracts in a way that deliberately consolidated the industry, effectively birthing the four major domestic carriers: United, American, Eastern, and TWA. He even ensured that Pan American held a monopoly on international routes.

By 1938, this consolidation was formalized under the Civil Aeronautics Authority, which soon became the Civil Aeronautics Board (CAB). The CAB was granted sweeping powers that extended far beyond safety. It controlled the entry and exit of airlines from the market, meaning no new competitor could enter a route without the board's explicit permission. It set fares, ensuring that airlines could not undercut each other on price. It dictated schedules and routes, creating a rigid network that prioritized stability over efficiency. The prevailing wisdom of the era, articulated clearly in a CAB report, was that there was "no inherent desirability of increasing the present number of carriers merely for the purpose of numerically enlarging the industry." In other words, competition was viewed as a threat to the public good, a dangerous force that could lead to instability. The government believed that by preventing price wars and limiting entry, it could guarantee a safe, reliable, and universally accessible service.

This system worked for a time. As jets were integrated into the market in the late 1950s and early 1960s, the industry grew dramatically. By the mid-1960s, airlines were carrying roughly 100 million passengers annually. By the mid-1970s, that number had doubled to over 200 million. But this growth came at a cost. The rigid regulatory framework could not adapt to the changing economic landscape. The 1970s brought a perfect storm of economic malaise: high inflation, low growth, falling productivity, and a massive spike in fuel costs due to the oil crises. Under the old system, airlines could not respond by cutting prices to stimulate demand or by abandoning unprofitable routes. They were trapped. Labor costs rose, fuel prices soared, and the government-mandated fare structure prevented the market from clearing. The result was an industry that was bloated, inefficient, and on the brink of collapse.

Market analysts began to sound the alarm. They pointed out that the system was riddled with monopolistic practices that artificially inflated ticket prices. Incumbent airlines, protected by the CAB, had little incentive to innovate or reduce costs. The barriers to entry for fledgling airlines were insurmountable. If a new carrier wanted to serve a city pair that was already dominated by a legacy airline, the CAB would almost always deny the application, citing the need to protect the existing carrier from "destructive competition." The government's response to these concerns was slow and inadequate. By the late 1970s, the consensus had shifted. The experiment in regulation had failed. The goal was no longer stability at any cost, but efficiency and affordability.

The Great Unshackling

The turning point came with the Airline Deregulation Act of 1978. This legislation was not a minor adjustment; it was a radical dismantling of the entire regulatory apparatus. The act removed the government's power to control routes, fares, and market entry. Overnight, the CAB lost its authority to decide which airlines could fly where and for how much. The concept of a "flag carrier" was dismantled. The air cargo industry had already seen a taste of this freedom with the Air Cargo Deregulation Act of 1977, which removed limitations on competition in domestic freight. Now, the passenger market was opened to the same forces.

The immediate aftermath was a period of chaos and opportunity. Incumbent airlines, suddenly stripped of their protective shields, scrambled to adapt. They could no longer rely on the CAB to protect them from new entrants. They could no longer charge high prices on uncompetitive routes. The market was flooded with new players, and the old giants had to reinvent themselves or die. The most significant strategic shift was the adoption of the hub-and-spoke system. Under the old point-to-point model, airlines flew directly between cities, often with low load factors on less popular routes. The new model consolidated traffic through major "hubs." Flights from smaller cities (the spokes) would funnel into a central airport, where passengers would transfer to other flights. This allowed airlines to fill larger planes with passengers from multiple origins, drastically increasing capacity utilization and lowering the cost per seat mile.

But the real revolution was the rise of the low-cost carrier (LCC). Before deregulation, airlines like Southwest were restricted by the CAB to flying only within Texas, as they were considered intrastate carriers and thus outside federal jurisdiction. Deregulation blew these boundaries wide open. Southwest and other new entrants could now choose their own routes, set their own fares, and design their own schedules. They bypassed the expensive hub-and-spoke model, returning to a simplified point-to-point system that focused on high-frequency flights between major cities. They stripped away the frills—meals, assigned seats, complex loyalty programs—to offer fares that were a fraction of the legacy carriers' prices.

The impact on the consumer was immediate and dramatic. Base ticket prices began a steady decline that has continued for decades. The inflation-adjusted 1982 constant dollar yield for airlines fell from 12.3 cents per mile in 1978 to 7.9 cents in 1997. By 2011, the real price of flying had dropped by nearly 45% since the act was passed. This wasn't just a statistical curiosity; it was a transformation of American life. Flying was no longer a luxury reserved for the wealthy or the business elite. It became a viable mode of transportation for the average family. The number of potential customers exploded, and with it, the demand for air travel surged. Passenger miles flown jumped from 250 million in 1978 to 750 million in 2005. The sky, once the domain of a select few, became the great connector of the nation.

The Human Cost of Efficiency

Yet, for all the gains in affordability and volume, the story of deregulation is not a simple triumph of free-market economics. The drive for efficiency came with a steep human price tag that is often glossed over in the celebratory narratives of lower fares. The hub-and-spoke system, while economically efficient for airlines, created a new kind of bottleneck for workers and communities. As airlines consolidated, they began to close down service to smaller cities that were no longer profitable. The "monopoly" that the CAB had once protected was replaced by a new kind of monopoly: the hub. If you lived in a city that was not a hub, your options for direct flights vanished. You were forced to connect through a major hub, adding hours to your travel time and increasing the complexity of your journey.

For the workers, the transition was brutal. The legacy airlines, facing intense competition from low-cost carriers, began a relentless drive to cut costs. This meant wage cuts, benefit reductions, and a systematic erosion of job security. The unions, which had once enjoyed strong protections under the regulated regime, found themselves fighting a rearguard action to preserve the gains of the past. Bankruptcies became a common tool for airlines to shed legacy costs, forcing workers to accept concessions or face the loss of their jobs. The stability that the regulated industry had promised was replaced by a precarious existence where the threat of bankruptcy loomed over every major carrier.

The service quality, too, suffered. As airlines squeezed margins to survive, the passenger experience deteriorated. Seats became smaller, legroom was reduced, and fees for checked bags and carry-ons were introduced. The "frills" that had once been standard were the first to go. In 2008, the American Customer Satisfaction Index ranked major US airlines last among all industries surveyed. It was a stark indictment of a system that had prioritized cost-cutting over customer satisfaction. The airlines had become efficient machines, but they had forgotten that they were in the business of moving people, not just cargo. The human element of travel—the comfort, the dignity, the sense of care—was sacrificed on the altar of the bottom line.

The impact on smaller communities was particularly acute. The CAB had once mandated that airlines serve small towns, ensuring that even the most remote communities had access to the national network. Deregulation removed this obligation. Suddenly, a town that had been connected to the rest of the country for decades found itself isolated. Airlines would drop routes as soon as they became unprofitable, leaving communities with no air service at all. The "public service" aspect of aviation, which had been a cornerstone of the regulated era, was effectively abandoned. The market had spoken, and it had decided that some people were not worth flying.

A Global Perspective and the Road Ahead

The United States was not alone in this experiment. Globally, the trend toward deregulation gained momentum, though the approaches varied. The European Union, Australia, the United Kingdom, and Scandinavia all moved to deregulate their domestic markets, though often with a less laissez-faire approach than the US. State-supported airlines remained common in many parts of the world, maintaining control over ticket prices and route entry, but the pressure to compete was undeniable. The US experiment served as a blueprint, a cautionary tale, and a proof of concept all at once.

Today, the legacy of the Airline Deregulation Act is a complex tapestry of gains and losses. On one hand, the democratization of air travel is undeniable. Millions of Americans who would never have flown in 1978 now travel by air every year. The economy has been transformed by the mobility of the workforce and the ease of connecting markets. The innovation driven by competition has led to safer, more efficient aircraft and a more dynamic industry.

On the other hand, the human cost of this efficiency remains a lingering shadow. The erosion of labor rights, the decline in service quality, and the isolation of small communities are real consequences that cannot be ignored. The airline industry is once again facing a period of consolidation, with the "Big Four" dominating the skies much as the "Big Four" did before deregulation. The hub-and-spoke model has created a network that is highly efficient but incredibly fragile, vulnerable to disruptions that ripple through the entire system. The promise of endless competition has given way to an oligopoly that often behaves like the regulated monopolies of the past, charging high prices for limited options.

The lesson of Spirit's bankruptcy, as many recent analysts have noted, is not that deregulation failed, but that the market has a natural tendency to consolidate when the pressure for efficiency is high enough. The cycle of entry, competition, consolidation, and monopoly is repeating itself. The question for the future is whether the government will step in to correct these imbalances, or whether the market will be allowed to run its course, regardless of the human cost. The history of airline deregulation suggests that the answer is rarely black and white. It is a story of trade-offs, of choices made in the name of progress that have left some behind. As we look to the future of aviation, we must remember that the goal of any system should not just be efficiency, but the well-being of the people it serves. The sky is not just a market; it is a shared space, and how we manage it reflects our values as a society.

The debate over the role of government in aviation is far from over. As the industry faces new challenges—from climate change to the rise of supersonic travel—the lessons of the past must inform our future decisions. We cannot simply return to the rigid controls of the CAB era, nor can we blindly accept the unchecked chaos of the unregulated market. We need a new framework, one that balances the benefits of competition with the need for stability, fairness, and human dignity. The story of airline deregulation is a reminder that progress is not a straight line. It is a winding path, full of detours and dead ends, where the cost of innovation is often paid by the vulnerable. It is up to us to ensure that the next chapter of this story is written with a clearer eye toward the human cost of our choices.
