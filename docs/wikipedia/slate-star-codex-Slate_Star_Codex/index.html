Slate Star Codex

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Slate Star Codex

In June 2020, a single thread of anxiety pulled on a digital tapestry that had taken seven years to weave, causing Scott Alexander to delete every post he had ever written. The blog Slate Star Codex (SSC), a sprawling repository of over nine thousand pages of text covering medicine, philosophy, and the future of artificial intelligence, vanished from the internet in an instant. It was not a server crash or a gradual sunset; it was a preemptive strike against what Alexander perceived as "doxing." A technology reporter for The New York Times had reportedly informed him that their policy required using real names for influential figures, and Alexander, fearing the exposure of his identity as a San Francisco Bay Area psychiatrist, chose to erase his life's work rather than risk it. The site went dark. Comments were disabled. The archive was wiped. Yet, the silence did not last long enough to be permanent; by July 2020, fragments of the content reappeared without commentary, and in January 2021, a successor blog, Astral Codex Ten (ACX), launched on Substack, continuing the conversation that had made Alexander one of the most significant intellectual figures of the early twenty-first century.

To understand the weight of this digital exodus, one must first grasp what Slate Star Codex actually was. It was not merely a blog; it was a nexus for the rationalist community, a subculture dedicated to applying rigorous reason to debates about ideas, moral quandaries, and the nature of reality itself. The New Statesman described it as a place where "others who seek to apply reason" gathered, while The New Yorker called Alexander's arguments "often counterintuitive and brilliant." Tyler Cowen, the economist, noted his profound influence on other writers. But these accolades only scratch the surface of an engine that produced content at a staggering velocity. An e-book compilation of all posts would run longer than the entire corpus of many classic authors, yet it was written by one man operating under a pen name to protect his privacy and his patients.

The core of Alexander's appeal lay in his unique method of inquiry, often prefaced by a disclosure of "epistemic status." This was not just academic humility; it was a calibration tool for the reader. Before diving into complex medical literature or game theory, Alexander would explicitly state how confident he was in his own conclusions. Was this a hard fact? A strong hypothesis? Or a speculative leap based on incomplete data? This transparency allowed readers to navigate the dense thicket of his writing with a map in hand. He treated his audience as intellectual peers capable of handling nuance, refusing to dumb down complex topics like psychiatric disorders or the intricacies of AI safety research.

One of the most striking examples of this method was his March 2020 post, "Face Masks: Much More Than You Wanted To Know." While early guidance from the CDC suggested masks were unnecessary for the general public, Alexander dissected the available medical literature with a forensic eye. He analyzed studies on droplet transmission, asymptomatic spread, and the mechanics of cloth barriers. The result was a conclusion that ran counter to official narrative: masks were likely an effective protection measure under certain conditions. This essay did not just offer an opinion; it offered a roadmap for how to think about a pandemic in real-time, weighing conflicting data points without succumbing to panic or denial. It became a touchstone for those trying to navigate the fog of war that was the early COVID-19 crisis.

Beyond the immediate crises of public health, Alexander's blog served as a primary venue for exploring the long-term trajectory of humanity, particularly regarding artificial intelligence. He was a vocal advocate for AI safety research, arguing that the risks posed by advanced AI should be understood through the lens of game theory and human cooperation failures. In his landmark essay "Meditations On Moloch," he wove together concepts from economics, biology, and mythology to explain why societies often fail to cooperate even when it is in their best interest. He described "Moloch" as a metaphor for the competitive dynamics that force individuals and organizations into harmful behaviors—like an arms race or environmental degradation—simply because they cannot trust others to hold back.

This framework was not just theoretical; it was applied directly to the future of technology. In 2025, Alexander collaborated with AI researcher Daniel Kokotajlo to publish "AI 2027," a detailed scenario forecast describing potential developments in artificial intelligence over a two-year window. The essay did not rely on sci-fi tropes but grounded its predictions in current trends and technical constraints, offering a chillingly plausible look at how rapidly the landscape could shift. These were not the musings of a doomsayer but the calculated projections of someone who understood the mechanics of the systems he was analyzing.

The influence of Slate Star Codex extended far beyond the pages of its essays; it shaped the very language of online discourse. Alexander coined terms that became shorthand for complex social dynamics within the rationalist community and beyond. "Lizardman's Constant," coined in a 2013 post, refers to the approximate four percent of responses in any poll or survey that are not sincere. This concept emerged from a Public Policy Polling statement claiming four percent of Americans believed lizardmen were running the Earth. Alexander argued this was not evidence of mass delusion but rather a statistical artifact: people answering carelessly, out of politeness, anger, or amusement. He suggested that polls should include absurd options to weed out trolls, a simple heuristic that has since been applied in data science and polling methodology to separate signal from noise.

Another term, "Shiri's Scissor" (or the "scissor statement"), introduced in the short story "Sort By Controversial," describes a statement so provocative that it forces people into opposing camps based on wildly divergent interpretations of its meaning. These statements generate conflict not because they are false, but because they serve as a catalyst for tribal signaling. This concept was used to explain why certain controversies spread with viral intensity while others fade away. In "The Toxoplasma of Rage," Alexander analyzed how memes that generate disagreement spread further because they offer members of different groups an opportunity to signal their commitment to their cause. He pointed to PETA's controversial campaigns as a prime example, noting they were better known than more effective organizations like Vegan Outreach precisely because the outrage they generated made them visible.

The blog was also a significant force in the Effective Altruism movement, ranking fourth on a 2017 survey of how people first heard about the philosophy. Alexander regularly wrote about moral offsets, the ethical treatment of animals, and the trade-offs involved in pursuing systemic change versus direct charity. His writing helped popularize the idea that one's life choices could be optimized for maximum positive impact, a concept that resonated deeply with a generation of technologists and philosophers.

However, the shadow that hung over Slate Star Codex was always the tension between public influence and private safety. Alexander used his first and middle name alone to maintain anonymity, a decision rooted in his dual life as a psychiatrist. He could not risk his patients' privacy or his professional license being jeopardized by the vitriol of internet debates. This anonymity allowed him to write with a candor that would have been impossible under his real name, exploring sensitive topics in psychiatry and politics without fear of personal retribution.

The crisis of June 2020 brought this tension to a breaking point. When The New York Times reporter intended to publish an article revealing Alexander's full identity, the reaction was immediate and drastic. Alexander argued that forcing his name into the public sphere constituted "doxing," a violation of the privacy he had meticulously guarded. The newspaper responded with a standard policy statement: their goal was always to give readers all accurate and relevant information about newsworthy figures. The Verge cited sources suggesting that not a word of the story had actually been written at the time Alexander deleted his blog, raising questions about whether the threat was real or perceived.

This event sparked a fierce debate within the tech and media industries. The Daily Beast reported on internal debates among New York Times staff regarding the ethics of revealing names without clear justification. Commentators like Jasper Jackson in The New Statesman argued that it was difficult to see how Alexander's full name was integral to any story, while others cautioned that criticism should not be based solely on his word. The clash reflected a fundamental disagreement over values: the media's drive for transparency versus the individual's right to privacy in an age where online harassment can have devastating real-world consequences.

Despite the deletion of the original site, the spirit of Slate Star Codex endured. The community did not scatter; it migrated. Meetups organized by readers continued in about two hundred cities around the world, creating a physical network for the digital tribe. In 2026, Sam Kriss writing for Harper's Magazine described Alexander as a "titanic figure" in this scene, noting that his fans included some of the most powerful figures in Silicon Valley who considered him the only intellectual of our time who might be remembered in a thousand years. Kriss observed a paradox: while Alexander could theoretically start a cult with such devoted followers, in person he was "almost comically gentle."

The legacy of Slate Star Codex is not just in the thousands of posts it produced, but in the way it changed how people think about thinking itself. It championed the idea that being wrong is okay, as long as you are honest about why and willing to change your mind. It treated politics not as a team sport but as a complex system of incentives and game theory. It approached AI not with fear or hype, but with a rigorous analysis of probability and risk. And it demonstrated that even in an era of digital fragmentation, a single voice could build a global community dedicated to reason.

The deletion of the site was a trauma for its readers, a reminder of how fragile digital spaces can be when they intersect with real-world power structures. Yet, the rebirth of Astral Codex Ten on Substack signaled resilience. The new platform allowed Alexander to continue his work with the same depth and breadth, albeit in a slightly different format. The comment sections remained disabled initially, a lingering scar from the controversy, but the essays continued to flow. From "The Anti-Reactionary FAQ," which critiqued the neoreactionary movement and its desire for feudalism, to his fiction book Unsong, which was revised and republished in 2024, Alexander's output remained a beacon of intellectual curiosity.

The story of Slate Star Codex is ultimately a story about the price of truth in the information age. It asks whether an anonymous thinker can have more influence than a named one, and what happens when the demand for transparency collides with the need for safety. It highlights the tension between the media's role as an observer and its power to shape reality by naming names. And it shows that ideas, once released into the world, take on a life of their own, surviving even when the blog they originated from is scrubbed from existence.

Alexander's work continues to resonate because it speaks to a fundamental human desire: the need to understand the world as it is, not as we wish it to be. In an era of polarization and misinformation, his commitment to nuance, his willingness to change his mind, and his deep empathy for the complexity of the human condition stand out. Whether he is analyzing the spread of rage on social media or forecasting the future of artificial intelligence, he does so with a gentle hand and a sharp mind. The blog may have been deleted, but the questions it raised remain urgent, and the answers it sought continue to guide those willing to look closely at the data.

The rationalist community that coalesced around this blog has become a significant force in Silicon Valley and beyond, influencing how technology companies approach safety, how charities evaluate their impact, and how individuals navigate moral dilemmas. The "Lizardman's Constant" is now taught in statistics classes; the concept of "scissor statements" is used by political scientists to analyze polarization; the "Meditations on Moloch" are required reading for anyone interested in systems theory. These ideas have permeated the culture, proving that a blog written by one man under a pseudonym can shift the trajectory of public discourse.

Yet, the shadow of June 2020 remains. It serves as a cautionary tale about the fragility of online anonymity and the power of traditional media to expose individuals. It reminds us that in the digital age, privacy is not a given but something that must be fiercely defended. The clash between Alexander and The New York Times was not just about one person's name; it was about the boundaries of public discourse and the rights of those who contribute to it. As we move further into an era where AI can generate infinite content and algorithms determine what we see, the human element—represented by Alexander's gentle voice and his commitment to truth—becomes more valuable than ever.

The story ends not with a bang, but with a continuation. The blog is gone, but the conversation goes on. Astral Codex Ten stands as a testament to the resilience of ideas and the enduring power of a community built on reason. It is a reminder that even when the platform changes, the pursuit of understanding remains constant. And in a world that often feels chaotic and divided, that pursuit is perhaps the most important work we can do. The legacy of Slate Star Codex is not just what was written, but who it made us: a community willing to think deeply, argue honestly, and strive for a better future, even when the odds are stacked against us.
