Friedrich Nietzsche

8 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Friedrich Nietzsche

In 1889, a man who had proclaimed that God was dead suffered a catastrophic mental breakdown in Turin. He collapsed in a street, howling like a wounded animal. When he emerged from weeks of paralysis and complete loss of his mental faculties, he would never be the same again—and neither would Western philosophy.

Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche, born on October 15, 1844, in the small Prussian town of Röcken near Leipzig, arrived into a world that would soon bear his intellectual mark like few others. His life unfolded with an almost mythological sense of inevitability: named after King Friedrich Wilhelm IV of Prussia, who turned forty-nine on the day of Nietzsche's birth—itself a curious omen for a thinker who would spend his career questioning the foundations of authority and tradition.

The Making of a Rebel

Nietzsche grew up in the provincial world of nineteenth-century Germany, surrounded by the influences of his family. His father, Carl Ludwig Nietzsche, was a Lutheran pastor and former teacher who died in 1849 after a year of agonizing brain disease—leaving the four-year-old Friedrich without a father. The boy's younger brother, Ludwig Joseph, died six months later at age two. The household then consisted of Nietzsche, his mother Franziska, his sister Elisabeth, and the maternal grandmother in Naumburg—a setting that would later become the Nietzsche-Haus museum.

The young Nietzsche attended Schulpforta, a prestigious boarding school that offered him what he needed most: languages. He immersed himself in Greek, Latin, Hebrew, and French, developing the philological tools that would later define his precision as a thinker. His academic records reveal something telling: his grades were not stellar—a 1 (the highest grade) in Religion and German at his end-of-semester exams in March 1864; a 2a in Greek and Latin; a 2b in French, History, and Physics; and a "lackluster" 3 in Hebrew and Mathematics. The scholarship offered to him due to his father being a pastor was not the result of exceptional competence—the claim was later debunked.

Yet it was at Schulpforta that Nietzsche discovered Friedrich Hölderlin, who became "my favourite poet," writing essays about raising consciousness to what he called "the most sublime ideality." He also encountered Ernst Ortlepp, an eccentric, blasphemous, and often drunken poet who introduced him to Richard Wagner's music. Under Ortlepp's influence, Nietzsche and a student named Richter returned to school drunk and encountered a teacher, resulting in Nietzsche's demotion from first in his class and the end of his status as a prefect.

After graduation in September 1864, Nietzsche began studying theology and classical philology at the University of Bonn—not with religious conviction, but with growing skepticism. He became briefly involved with the Burschenschaft Frankonia, but after one semester—and to the anger of his mother—he abandoned his theological studies. His loss of faith was complete.

As early as his 1862 essay "Fate and History," Nietzsche argued that historical research had discredited the central teachings of Christianity. David Strauss's Life of Jesus also seemed to have profoundly affected the young man. Ludwig Feuerbach's work would complete this intellectual shift.

The Academic Years

By 1869, at age twenty-four, Nietzsche was appointed Professor of Classical Philology at the University of Basel—a position that began his formal career as an academic. He had turned from philology to philosophy early in his academic trajectory, though he remained a scholar's scholar.

Plagued by health problems for most of his life—migraines, digestive troubles, and an ill-defined condition that led him to seek climate relief across Switzerland, Italy, and southern France—he resigned from the university in 1879. The resignation marked the end of his academic career and the beginning of his true life's work: independent writing.

Nietzsche lived in relative solitude and financial insecurity during the following decade, moving between nations in search of environments that might alleviate his condition. It was during this period that he completed much of his core writing—the aphoristic, poetic, deeply challenging works that would reshape European thought.

The Philosophy of Rupture

Nietzsche's philosophical contributions cannot be reduced to a single doctrine, but certain themes emerge with particular force:

His critique of truth favored what he called perspectivism—the idea that there is no neutral, objective vantage point from which to assess reality; all knowledge is embedded in perspective, interest, and power.

His genealogical critique of Christian morality attacked the foundations of European ethics: he argued that Christian morality was not timeless or natural but historically constructed, emerging from specific conditions—specifically, the "slave morality" that valued meekness, humility, and obedience. Against this, Nietzsche proposed the possibility of "master morality," which affirmed strength, power, and earthly joy.

The famous proclamation "God is dead" appeared in Thus Spoke Zarathustra—not as celebration but as a diagnosis of the modern condition. With traditional Christian values weakening, what remained was passive nihilism: the absence of meaning that Nietzsche saw spreading through European culture like a disease. His response was not despair but "the affirmation of life," the possibility of creating new values, what he called the transvaluation of values.

He introduced the notion of Apollonian and Dionysian duality—order versus chaos in art, structure versus orginal energy. And he characterized the human subject as an expression of competing wills, collectively understood as the will-to-power—not power over others in crude terms but creative drive, self-overcoming, expansion.

The Übermensch and Eternal Return

Nietzsche's later work developed concepts that would haunt twentieth-century thought. The Übermensch—a figure beyond conventional morality, capable of creating values rather than following them—became one of his most misinterpreted ideas. His unique formulation of eternal return suggested not cyclical time but the intensification of existence: could one's life be lived again, exactly as it was, for all eternity?

His body of work touched art, philology, history, music, religion, tragedy, culture and science. He drew inspiration from Greek tragedy—particularly the works of Hölderlin—from Arthur Schopenhauer, from Ralph Waldo Emerson's American individualism, and above all from Richard Wagner, whose opera cycles Nietzsche worshipped until his own break with that tradition.

The Sister's Betrayal

After Nietzsche's death in 1900, his sister Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche became the curator and editor of his manuscripts. Her editions were catastrophic for his intellectual legacy: she edited his unpublished writings to fit her German ultranationalist ideology—fitting precisely the anti-Semitic, militaristic nationalism that Nietzsche explicitly opposed.

Through her published editions, Nietzsche's work became associated with fascism and Nazism—the very movements he had criticized as decadent. His explicit opposition to antisemitism and nationalism was obscured by his sister's distortions.

Twentieth-century scholars such as Walter Kaufmann, R.J. Hollingdale, and Georges Bataille defended Nietzsche against this interpretation. Corrected editions emerged, clarifying that Nietzsche's writings advocated for individual creativity and opposed mass movements like fascism.

The Legacy

The works of Nietzsche achieved renewed popularity in the 1960s—during the era of counterculture, civil rights, and existentialist revival. His ideas have since had widespread impact on twentieth- and twenty-first-century thinkers across philosophy—especially in schools of continental philosophy such as existentialism, postmodernism, and post-structuralism.

His influence extends beyond academic philosophy into art, literature, music, poetry, politics, and popular culture. From the existentialist reading of his works by Jean-Paul Sartre to the post-structuralist interpretations of Michel Foucault, Nietzsche's questions about power, language, and the construction of meaning have become foundational.

Nietzsche was an amateur composer—he worked on musical compositions beginning in 1858 at Schulpforta. Richard Wagner was dismissive, allegedly mocking a birthday gift of piano composition sent by Nietzsche in 1871 to Wagner's wife Cosima. German conductor Hans von Bülow described another piece as "the most undelightful and the most anti-musical draft on musical paper that I have faced in a long time." His music remains a footnote—but his philosophical compositions became revolutionary.

The Final Truth

He was not a systematic thinker in any conventional sense. Nietzsche wrote poetry, cultural criticism, philosophical essays—displaying a fondness for aphorisms and irony that made him simultaneously brilliant and dangerous. He attacked Christianity, yet he was not simply pessimistic: his "gay science" offered creative affirmation, the joy of becoming who one might be.

He spent his final eleven years in complete paralysis and vascular dementia under his family's care until his death at age forty-five—or perhaps more accurately, his death at fifty-five—depending on how one counts. The man who declared God dead became the most contested figure in modern philosophy: twisted by his sister, weaponized by movements he despised, and ultimately rediscovered as a thinker about human possibility rather than authoritarian certainty.

His core insight remained constant across all his work: there are no facts, only interpretations—and with that radical humility came an invitation to create.
