Riemann hypothesis

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Riemann hypothesis

There's a million dollars waiting for anyone who can solve it. The Riemann hypothesis has stood unconquered for over 160 years, tantalizing the greatest mathematical minds of every generation. It's not just any unsolved problemâ€”many mathematicians consider it the single most important open question in pure mathematics. And yet, at its heart, it asks something deceptively simple: where exactly do certain special numbers equal zero?

To understand why this matters, we need to talk about prime numbers.

The Irregularity of Primes

Prime numbers are the atoms of arithmetic. Every whole number greater than one can be built by multiplying primes together, and there's only one way to do it. The number 60 is 2 times 2 times 3 times 5. The number 17 is just 17â€”it's prime, indivisible, fundamental.

Here's the frustrating thing about primes: they seem to follow no pattern. They thin out as numbers get larger, but irregularly. After 7 comes 11, a gap of 4. After 23 comes 29, a gap of 6. After 89 comes 97, a gap of 8. Sometimes primes cluster together, like 41 and 43, separated by just 2. Sometimes they're maddeningly sparse.

Mathematicians have long sought to understand the distribution of primes. How many primes are there below a million? Below a billion? Is there a formula that tells us approximately how many primes we should expect up to any given number?

There is. It's called the prime number theorem, and it says that the number of primes less than a given number x is approximately x divided by the natural logarithm of x. But this is only an approximation. The Riemann hypothesis, if true, would tell us exactly how good this approximation is. It would reveal the hidden order in the seeming chaos of the primes.

A Function That Knows About Primes

In 1859, a German mathematician named Bernhard Riemann published a short paper with a long title: "On the Number of Primes Less Than a Given Magnitude." It was only eight pages. Those eight pages changed mathematics forever.

Riemann studied a mathematical function that had been investigated decades earlier by the great Leonhard Euler. This function, now called the Riemann zeta function, looks innocent enough in its original form. You pick a number, call it s. Then you calculate 1 divided by 1 to the power of s, plus 1 divided by 2 to the power of s, plus 1 divided by 3 to the power of s, and so on forever. Add up all these fractions, and you get the zeta function of s.

When s equals 2, this sum equals pi squared divided by 6â€”a beautiful and surprising result that Euler proved in 1735, solving what was known as the Basel problem. When s equals 4, you get pi to the fourth power divided by 90. These connections to pi hint at deep structure lurking beneath the surface.

Euler discovered something remarkable about this function. It can be rewritten as a product involving all the prime numbers. This is called the Euler product, and it's a direct bridge between the smooth world of calculus and the jagged world of primes. The zeta function, it turns out, encodes information about every prime number that exists.

Venturing Into the Complex Plane

Here's where things get interestingâ€”and strange.

The original formula for the zeta function only works when s is greater than 1. Try to plug in s equals 1, and you get the harmonic series: 1 plus one-half plus one-third plus one-quarter, continuing forever. This sum doesn't converge to any finite numberâ€”it grows without bound.

But Riemann wasn't content to study the zeta function only where the original formula worked. Using a technique called analytic continuation, he extended the function to work with complex numbersâ€”numbers that have both a "real" part and an "imaginary" part.

Complex numbers might sound like mathematical fiction, but they're essential tools that appear throughout physics and engineering. The imaginary unit, denoted i, is defined as the square root of negative oneâ€”a number that doesn't exist among ordinary numbers, yet proves indispensable for describing everything from electrical circuits to quantum mechanics. A complex number like 3 plus 2i has a real part of 3 and an imaginary part of 2.

Once Riemann extended the zeta function to the complex plane, he could ask: where does this function equal zero?

The Trivial and the Mysterious

The zeta function equals zero at the negative even integers. Plug in negative 2, negative 4, negative 6, and so onâ€”the function returns zero every time. These zeros are called "trivial" not because they're unimportant, but because mathematicians understand them completely. They're predictable, well-behaved, boring.

The interesting zeros live elsewhere. They inhabit a vertical strip in the complex plane where the real part lies between 0 and 1. This region is called the critical strip. Within it, the zeta function has infinitely many zeros, and these "nontrivial" zeros are intimately connected to the distribution of prime numbers.

Riemann calculated the first few nontrivial zeros by hand. The first one has its imaginary part at approximately 14.135. The second at about 21.022. The third at roughly 25.011. Each zero corresponds to a wave that contributes to the overall pattern of how primes are distributed.

And here's what Riemann noticed: every nontrivial zero he computed had its real part equal to exactly one-half.

The Hypothesis

Riemann's hypothesis states that this pattern holds universally. Every single nontrivial zero of the zeta function, without exception, has its real part equal to one-half. These zeros all lie on a single vertical line in the complex plane, called the critical line.

In his 1859 paper, Riemann wrote (in German): "It is very probable that all roots are real. Of course one would wish for a rigorous proof here; I have for the time being, after some fleeting vain attempts, provisionally put aside the search for this, as it appears dispensable for the immediate objective of my investigation."

Those "fleeting vain attempts" have now stretched across more than a century and a half. The proof Riemann set aside has eluded every mathematician who has sought it since.

Why It Matters

The zeros of the zeta function control the oscillations of primes around their expected positions. Riemann derived an exact formula connecting the two. If you want to know precisely how many primes exist below a given number, you need to sum contributions from all the nontrivial zeros. Each zero creates a wave, and the superposition of infinitely many waves produces the actual distribution of primes.

If the Riemann hypothesis is true, these waves are as well-behaved as possible. The prime number theorem's approximation is about as accurate as it could theoretically be. The primes, for all their apparent randomness, are distributed in the most orderly way consistent with their fundamental nature.

If the hypothesis is falseâ€”if even a single zero strays from the critical lineâ€”then the distribution of primes is wilder than we thought. There would be larger deviations from the expected counts, more irregularity in the prime landscape.

The Search for Proof

When Riemann died in 1866, just seven years after publishing his famous paper, a note was found among his effects. It said that certain properties of the zeta function were "deduced from an expression of it which, however, I did not succeed in simplifying enough to publish it." We still have no idea what that expression might have been.

Jacques Hadamard, one of the mathematicians who proved the prime number theorem in 1896, later wrote that some thirty years elapsed before he could prove all but one of the properties Riemann had simply stated without proof. That remaining unproven property? The Riemann hypothesis itself.

In 1900, the German mathematician David Hilbert listed 23 unsolved problems that he believed would shape the development of mathematics in the twentieth century. Problem number 8 concerned the Riemann hypothesis and related conjectures about prime numbers. When asked what he would do if he woke from a 500-year slumber, Hilbert reportedly said his first question would be whether the Riemann hypothesis had been proved.

In 2000, the Clay Mathematics Institute designated the Riemann hypothesis as one of seven Millennium Prize Problems, offering one million dollars for a solution. As of this writing, it remains unsolvedâ€”one of only six Millennium Problems still open.

Evidence Without Proof

The circumstantial evidence for the Riemann hypothesis is overwhelming. Computers have verified that the first ten trillion nontrivial zeros all lie on the critical line. Not a single counterexample has ever been found.

But in mathematics, trillions of examples prove nothing. There exist mathematical statements that are true for astronomically large numbers of cases before finally failing. The history of mathematics is littered with plausible conjectures that turned out to be false. Without a proof, the hypothesis remains just thatâ€”a hypothesis.

Various mathematicians have proved weaker results. We know, for instance, that infinitely many zeros lie on the critical lineâ€”but not that all of them do. We know that the proportion of zeros on the critical line is at least 41 percentâ€”but not that it's 100 percent. These partial results tantalizingly suggest that the full hypothesis might be true, but they fall short of proving it.

Connections to Physics

One of the strangest twists in the story emerged in the 1970s. The mathematician Hugh Montgomery was studying the statistical distribution of the zeros when he met the physicist Freeman Dyson at tea. Montgomery mentioned his findings, and Dyson immediately recognized the pattern: it matched the statistics of energy levels in heavy atomic nuclei.

This was bizarre. Why should the zeros of a number-theoretic function behave like energy states in quantum mechanics? The connection has been explored extensively since then, with physicists and mathematicians finding deep parallels between the zeta function and random matrix theoryâ€”a branch of mathematics developed to study complex quantum systems.

Some researchers have speculated that the Riemann hypothesis might ultimately be proved using ideas from physics. Perhaps the zeros are eigenvaluesâ€”characteristic numbersâ€”of some undiscovered quantum mechanical operator. If we could identify this hypothetical operator and prove its properties, the Riemann hypothesis might fall out as a consequence. This remains speculation, but it's intriguing speculation.

The Opposite Possibility

What if the Riemann hypothesis is false? What if, against all evidence, some zero lurks off the critical line, waiting to be discovered?

The consequences would be profound but not catastrophic. Many theorems in number theory are stated in two forms: an unconditional form and a stronger form that assumes the Riemann hypothesis. If the hypothesis fails, we would lose the stronger versions of these results. Our understanding of prime distribution would need revision.

But here's the thing: no one seriously believes the hypothesis is false. The accumulated evidence is simply too strong. The real question is not whether it's true, but how to prove it.

The Human Story

Behind the mathematics lies a human drama. Riemann himself was a shy, sickly genius who died of tuberculosis at 39. He left behind revolutionary ideas in geometry, complex analysis, and number theoryâ€”any one of which would have secured his legacy.

Generations of mathematicians have devoted years to the Riemann hypothesis. Some have claimed proofs that turned out to be flawed. Others have made genuine progress on related problems without cracking the central mystery. The problem has a reputation for swallowing careers.

And yet mathematicians keep trying. The hypothesis sits at the intersection of so many mathematical fieldsâ€”number theory, complex analysis, harmonic analysis, random matrix theoryâ€”that progress on it often yields insights even when a full proof remains elusive.

A Challenge for Artificial Intelligence?

In the age of artificial intelligence and automated theorem proving, some have wondered whether computers might eventually settle the Riemann hypothesis. Machine learning systems have already discovered new mathematical results, and automated proof assistants have verified complex proofs that humans find difficult to check.

But the Riemann hypothesis seems to require a genuinely new ideaâ€”not just computation or verification, but insight into why the zeros should all lie on the critical line. So far, no one has found the right perspective, human or artificial.

The hypothesis remains what it has been for 166 years: a profound mystery, a million-dollar challenge, and perhaps the deepest open question about the numbers we use every day.

Somewhere on a vertical line in the complex plane, infinitely many zeros are keeping their secrets. The first ten trillion have been checked. The next one is surely on the line too. But until someone proves that all of them are, the Riemann hypothesis will continue to stand as mathematics' most tantalizing unsolved problemâ€”a testament to how much we still don't understand about numbers as simple as 2, 3, 5, and 7.
