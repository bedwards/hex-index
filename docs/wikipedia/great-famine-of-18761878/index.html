Great Famine of 1876–1878

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Great Famine of 1876–1878

In March 1877, the Viceroy of India, Lord Lytton, stood before a government that had just overseen the export of a record-breaking 6.4 million hundredweight of wheat from the Indian subcontinent to England. While the ships sailed westward laden with grain, the Deccan Plateau was dying. The drought that had shattered the harvest in 1876 had turned the soil to dust, yet the colonial administration insisted that the market must remain free. The logic was rigid: to intervene was to distort the economy. The result was a catastrophe that would claim the lives of between 5.6 and 9.6 million people, with modern demographic analysis settling firmly on 8.2 million fatalities. This was not a failure of nature alone; it was a failure of policy, ideology, and a deliberate choice to prioritize financial prudence over human survival.

The Great Famine of 1876–1878 was a complex tragedy rooted in the intersection of global climate patterns and British imperial economics. It began in the southern and southwestern presidencies of Madras and Bombay, as well as the princely states of Mysore and Hyderabad. The trigger was a severe drought, part of a larger, terrifying atmospheric phenomenon. A strong El Niño event, combined with an active Indian Ocean Dipole, had disrupted rainfall across the globe, affecting not just India but also China, South America, and parts of Africa. In India, the rains simply did not come. The Deccan Plateau, the breadbasket of the south, saw its crops wither in the fields. Lakes dried up, and the ragi crops of Kolar and Bangalore, already damaged by heavy rains two years prior, failed completely.

Yet, the land was not barren of food. The region had produced enough grain to sustain its population, had that grain been allowed to remain in the region. Instead, the colonial machinery operated with a relentless focus on trade and revenue. The regular export of grain continued unabated, swelling to record numbers precisely as the local harvest collapsed. The British administration, under the guise of laissez-faire economics, refused to halt the flow of foodstuffs to Britain. The market, they argued, would regulate itself. This belief in the invisible hand proved to be a deadly instrument for the millions of Indians who were suddenly priced out of their own food supply.

The human toll was staggering. The famine affected an area of 670,000 square kilometers, reaching into the Central Provinces, the North-Western Provinces, and even a small area of Punjab by 1877. It touched the lives of 58.5 million people. In the princely state of Mysore alone, the population decreased by 874,000 souls compared to the 1871 census. The death toll was not merely a statistic of starvation; it was a demographic reset that neutralized natural population growth in the Madras and Bombay presidencies for the entire decade between the 1871 and 1881 censuses.

The Philosophy of Starvation

The tragedy of the 1876 famine was not that the British government did nothing; it was that they did something specific, and it was the wrong thing. The administration was haunted by the memory of the Bihar famine of 1873–74. In that previous crisis, the Lieutenant-Governor of Bengal, Sir Richard Temple, had overseen a relief effort that critics deemed too generous. They argued that the government had spent too much money, creating a culture of dependency among the Indian populace. When the new famine struck in 1876, Temple was appointed Famine Commissioner for the Government of India. He was determined to ensure that history did not repeat itself, but his interpretation of "learning from the past" led to policies of extreme austerity.

Temple's approach was rooted in a prevailing Victorian belief that excessive aid would "demoralize" the native population. He insisted on a strict policy of laissez-faire regarding the grain trade. He believed that the government should not interfere with the price of food, even as prices skyrocketed beyond the reach of the poor. More insidiously, he imposed rigorous standards for receiving relief. The goal was to disburse the smallest sum of money possible, a directive that Lord Lytton, the Viceroy, explicitly supported.

"Everything must be subordinated to the financial consideration of disbursing the smallest sum of money."

This was the guiding principle of the relief effort. Temple divided the relief into two categories: "relief works" for the able-bodied and "gratuitous relief" for the vulnerable—the elderly, the infirm, and small children. However, the conditions for the relief works were designed to be so grueling that only the desperate would accept them, and even then, the compensation was calculated to be barely sufficient for survival.

The Temple Wage

In January 1877, Temple made a decision that would become infamous. He reduced the wage for a day's hard labor in the relief camps in Madras and Bombay. This reduction, known as the "Temple wage," consisted of 450 grams (1 pound) of grain plus one anna for a man. For women and working children, the ration was slightly lower. This was the compensation for a "long day of hard labour without shade or rest."

The rationale was cold and calculating. Temple believed that paying more would encourage idleness. He argued that if the wage were too high, it would create a "dependency" among the famine-stricken population. The logic ignored the physiological reality: a man performing strenuous labor in the scorching heat of the Indian summer, while already malnourished, required far more calories than 450 grams of grain could provide.

Critics within the administration saw the flaw immediately. William Digby, a journalist and official, and Dr. W. R. Cornish, the Sanitary Commissioner for the Madras Presidency, vehemently opposed Temple's measures. Cornish, a physician, understood the biological imperative. He argued for a minimum of 680 grams (1.5 pounds) of grain, supplemented with vegetables and protein, specifically for those performing strenuous labor. He knew that without adequate nutrition, the laborers would not survive the work, let alone the famine.

"A famine can scarcely be said to be adequately controlled which leaves one-fourth of the people dead."

This was Digby's scathing assessment of Temple's claim in early 1877 that he had "put the famine under control." Temple's confidence was a delusion born of bureaucratic metrics. He was counting the number of people in the camps, not the number of people dying outside of them. The "Temple wage" was a death sentence for thousands. It forced families to choose between starvation and a labor camp where the work itself would consume what little energy they had left.

In the Bombay Presidency, the rigidity of the qualification tests led to strikes by relief workers. They could not work under conditions that offered no hope of survival. In Madras, the provincial government eventually increased the ration in March 1877, moving halfway toward Cornish's recommendations to 570 grams of grain and adding 43 grams of protein in the form of daal (pulses). By then, however, it was too late for many. The delay had cost countless lives.

The Mechanics of Collapse

The famine did not respect the boundaries of British administration; it swept through the princely states with equal ferocity. In Mysore, the situation was dire. Two years before the great drought, heavy rains had destroyed ragi crops. The subsequent year brought scant rainfall, drying up the lakes that stored the region's water. When the famine hit, the government of Mysore, led by Dewan C. V. Rungacharlu, attempted to act. They established relief kitchens and imported large quantities of grain from the neighboring British-ruled Madras Presidency.

The relief effort in Mysore was a logistical nightmare. Large numbers of people journeyed to Bangalore, hoping to find food. In exchange for grain, they were required to work on the Bangalore–Mysore railway line. The state government allowed temporary grazing in forests and began constructing new water tanks while repairing old ones. The cost was astronomical for the state: Rungacharlu estimated the expense at 160 lakhs, leaving the state with a debt of 80 lakhs. Yet, despite these efforts, the death toll in Mysore was severe. Sir Richard Temple was sent as a Special Famine Commissioner to oversee these works, bringing his ideology of austerity with him.

The broader picture was even bleaker. In the United Provinces and other regions where relief was meagre or non-existent, mortality was catastrophic. The famine was compounded by a secondary killer: disease. In the second half of 1878, an epidemic of malaria swept through the region. It found a population already ravaged by malnutrition, a group with no immune resistance left to fight. The combination of starvation and disease created a feedback loop of death that the government was ill-equipped to break.

The Cost of Austerity

The financial accounting of the famine reveals a stark disparity between the perceived cost and the human cost. The Government of India spent Rs. 30 million in relief for British India and another Rs. 7.2 million for the princely states of Mysore and Hyderabad. They also deferred tax payments amounting to Rs. 6 million. Charitable donations from Great Britain and the colonies added another Rs. 8.4 million.

On paper, these were significant sums. But when broken down per capita, the expenditure was minuscule. The cost incurred in the Bombay Presidency was less than one-fifth of that in the Bihar famine of 1873–74, a smaller crisis that had lasted for a shorter time. The administration was obsessed with the bottom line, treating the famine as a budgetary problem rather than a humanitarian crisis. The "financial consideration" that Lord Lytton prioritized was a consideration of the imperial treasury, not the Indian stomach.

This frugality had a long-term impact on the economy of the region. The famine neutralized population growth, effectively erasing a decade of demographic progress. It also triggered a massive wave of emigration. In the aftermath, a large number of agricultural laborers and handloom weavers in South India were forced to leave their homes. They became indentured laborers, shipped off to British tropical colonies to work on plantations. The famine had not just killed millions; it had uprooted a generation, scattering them across the empire as a cheap labor force.

The Legacy of Silence

The Great Famine of 1876–1878 did more than reshape the demographics of India; it shattered the political consensus of the British Raj. The official reaction to the famine, and the subsequent stifling of debate regarding the best form of relief, created a deep sense of disillusionment among a specific class of British administrators and Indian intellectuals.

Men like William Wedderburn and A. O. Hume were unsettled by what they had witnessed. They saw a system that prioritized the export of grain over the lives of its subjects. They saw a bureaucracy that refused to acknowledge its own culpability. Their horror at the "Temple wage" and the export policies of Lord Lytton became a catalyst for political organization. Less than a decade after the famine ended, Wedderburn and Hume founded the Indian National Congress.

The famine became a cornerstone for the emerging nationalist movement. Leaders like Dadabhai Naoroji and Romesh Chunder Dutt would spend the rest of their lives analyzing the economic drain of India, using the famine as their primary evidence. They argued that the British economic policies were not just misguided but were actively predatory. The famine proved that the "benevolent" empire was willing to let millions die to balance a ledger.

"The Great Famine had a lasting political impact on events in India."

The cultural memory of the event also persisted. In Tamil and other literary traditions, the famine was immortalized. A large number of Kummi folk songs describing the suffering, the hunger, and the loss were documented. These songs served as a counter-narrative to the official reports, preserving the truth of the experience for future generations. They spoke of the empty bowls, the silent villages, and the roads filled with the dead.

The Birth of the Famine Codes

The sheer scale of the mortality and the public outcry that followed forced the British government to confront the reality of their failure. The "excessive mortality" led directly to the formation of the Famine Commission of 1880. This commission was tasked with investigating the causes of the famine and recommending measures to prevent a recurrence. Their work led to the eventual adoption of the Indian Famine Codes.

These codes were a significant, albeit flawed, step forward. They established a framework for famine relief, defining the stages of famine and the corresponding government responses. They mandated the construction of relief works and the provision of food at subsidized rates. However, the codes were also a testament to the limitations of the colonial mindset. They were designed to manage famine, not necessarily to prevent the economic conditions that caused it. The focus remained on the mechanics of relief rather than the structural inequalities of the colonial economy.

The 1876–1878 famine remains one of the darkest chapters in the history of the British Empire. It stands as a stark reminder of the dangers of ideological rigidity in the face of human suffering. The officials who presided over the crisis were not monsters in the traditional sense; they were bureaucrats who believed they were doing the right thing by adhering to the principles of free trade and fiscal responsibility. They believed that the market would solve the problem, and that government intervention was a sin.

In the end, the market did not solve the problem. It amplified it. The grain that should have fed the starving was sold to the highest bidder, and the people were left to die. The "Temple wage" was not a solution; it was a confession of indifference. The famine of 1876–1878 was not an act of God. It was a man-made disaster, engineered by the convergence of drought, drought, and the cold calculus of empire.

The story of the Great Famine is a story of choices. It was the choice to export grain when the fields were dry. It was the choice to pay a wage that could not sustain life. It was the choice to prioritize the treasury over the taxpayer. And it was the choice to believe that the suffering of millions was a necessary price for the stability of the market.

Today, as we look back at the 8.2 million lives lost, we see the ghost of a policy that still haunts our modern world. The debate over free trade, the tension between market forces and human rights, and the question of how much a government should spend to save its citizens are as relevant now as they were in 1876. The famine taught us that when the ideology of the market overrides the humanity of the people, the result is catastrophe.

The legacy of the famine is not just in the numbers, but in the silence that followed. It was a silence broken only by the songs of the survivors and the growing roar of the nationalist movement. It was the moment when the Indian people realized that their survival depended not on the benevolence of their rulers, but on their own political power. The Great Famine was the crucible in which the Indian National Congress was forged, and the lesson it taught was one that the empire could never fully forget: that you cannot starve a people and expect them to remain loyal.

The drought of 1876 was a natural event. The famine was a political one. The wind and the rain were indifferent to human life, but the government was not. The government made a decision. And that decision killed millions. It is a decision that history has not forgotten, and it is a lesson that we must never stop learning. The grain that was exported to England could have saved lives. The wage that was reduced could have been increased. The relief that was denied could have been given. These were not acts of nature. They were acts of policy. And they were acts of violence.

The Great Famine of 1876–1878 stands as a monument to the cost of indifference. It is a reminder that in the face of disaster, the choice to do nothing is a choice to do harm. The 8.2 million dead are not just statistics. They are a warning. A warning that when we prioritize the ledger over the life, we pay a price that no amount of gold can ever repay. The famine is over, but the question remains: will we learn?
