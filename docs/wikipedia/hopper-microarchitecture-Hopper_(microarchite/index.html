Hopper (microarchitecture)

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Hopper (microarchitecture)

In January 2024, Raymond James Financial analysts estimated that a single Nvidia H100 GPU was trading in the $25,000 to $30,000 range, yet on eBay, individual units commanded prices exceeding $40,000. By February of that same year, the situation had escalated to a point where Nvidia was reportedly shipping these processors to data centers in armored cars. This was not mere speculation or the fevered dreams of a bubble; it was the tangible reality of the Hopper microarchitecture, a silicon breakthrough that had become the most coveted commodity in the global economy. Just months prior, during a dinner with Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang, Oracle's Larry Ellison, Tesla's Elon Musk, and the team behind xAI found themselves in the absurd position of "begging" for these chips. "I guess is the best way to describe it," Ellison later recalled of that hour of sushi and pleading. The Hopper architecture was not just a new generation of graphics cards; it was the engine of the artificial intelligence revolution, a physical manifestation of the world's desperate hunger for computation.

Named for Grace Hopper, the United States Navy rear admiral and computer science pioneer who was one of the first programmers of the Harvard Mark I, the Hopper microarchitecture represents a quantum leap in datacenter design. While the name pays homage to a legacy of coding excellence, the architecture itself is a marvel of modern engineering, officially revealed in March 2022 after being leaked as early as November 2019. It was designed to sit alongside the Lovelace microarchitecture, but while Lovelace served the gaming and creative professional markets, Hopper was forged exclusively for the datacenter. It is the successor to the Turing and Ampere architectures, but the improvements were not merely iterative; they were structural, fundamental, and designed to solve the specific bottlenecks that were beginning to choke the exponential growth of AI models.

The Silicon Foundation

At the heart of the Hopper architecture lies the H100 GPU, a monolithic slab of silicon that pushes the boundaries of what is physically possible in mass production. Manufactured by TSMC using the N4 process, this chip contains a staggering 80 billion transistors. To put that density into perspective, if each transistor were the size of a grain of sand, the H100 would cover a football field. This massive transistor count is distributed across up to 144 streaming multiprocessors (SMs), the fundamental building blocks of Nvidia's parallel computing power.

The physical form of the H100 is as critical as its internal logic. The architecture offers two primary configurations: a PCIe version for standard server insertion and the SXM5 socket version for high-performance clusters. The distinction is vital. Due to the increased memory bandwidth provided by the SXM5 socket, the H100 in this configuration delivers significantly superior performance compared to its PCIe counterpart. This is not a minor optimization; it is a difference in kind. The SXM5 design allows for a thermal design power (TDP) of up to 700 watts in the SXM5 form factor, enabling the chip to run at frequencies and power levels that would melt a standard card.

The memory subsystem of Hopper is perhaps its most revolutionary feature. The H100 supports HBM3 and HBM2e memory, with capacities reaching up to 80 GB. The HBM3 memory system alone supports a bandwidth of 3 TB/s. This represents a 50% increase over the 2 TB/s bandwidth of the previous generation, the Nvidia Ampere A100. In the world of deep learning, memory bandwidth is often the limiting factor, not the raw compute speed. Models are so large that the processor spends most of its time waiting for data to arrive. By widening the data highway to 3 TB/s, Hopper ensures that the 80 billion transistors are constantly fed, eliminating the starvation that plagued earlier generations.

Reimagining the Streaming Multiprocessor

The streaming multiprocessor (SM) is the workhorse of the GPU, and Hopper reengineered it from the ground up. While the maximum number of concurrent warps per SM remains at 64, identical to the Ampere architecture, the internal efficiency of these warps has been dramatically enhanced. Hopper improves upon its predecessors by introducing a new level of concurrency and memory management that allows applications to perform tasks that were previously impossible or inefficient.

One of the most significant additions is the Tensor Memory Accelerator (TMA). This hardware unit supports bidirectional asynchronous memory transfer between shared memory and global memory. In simpler terms, TMA allows the GPU to move data around while it is simultaneously crunching numbers, without the CPU or the programmer having to micromanage every byte. Under TMA, applications can transfer up to 5D tensors, a capability that is essential for the complex data structures used in modern neural networks.

When writing data from shared memory to global memory, the TMA can perform elementwise reduction and bitwise operators. This is a massive efficiency gain. By avoiding the need to use registers and SM instructions for these operations, the architecture frees up valuable compute resources. It enables users to write warp-specialized codes, where specific threads handle data movement while others handle computation, all orchestrated by hardware rather than software overhead. This feature is exposed to developers through the `cuda::memcpy_async` API, allowing them to harness this power without rewriting their entire codebase from scratch.

Parallelization in Hopper is further enhanced by the introduction of thread block clusters. In traditional GPU programming, thread blocks operate somewhat in isolation. In Hopper, developers can group thread blocks into clusters, allowing them to perform atomics in the shared memory of other thread blocks within the same cluster. This is known as distributed shared memory. It effectively creates a shared workspace for a group of processing units, allowing them to communicate and synchronize with a latency and bandwidth that mimics local memory.

Distributed shared memory may be used by an SM simultaneously with the L2 cache. When used to communicate data between SMs, this can utilize the combined bandwidth of both systems. The maximum portable cluster size is 8, but the H100 can support a cluster size of 16 by using the `cudaFuncAttributeNonPortableClusterSizeAllowed` function. This flexibility comes at a cost—a potential reduction in the number of active blocks—but for applications that require massive inter-block communication, it is a game-changer. With L2 multicasting and distributed shared memory, the required bandwidth for dynamic random-access memory (DRAM) read and writes is significantly reduced, lowering power consumption and heat generation while increasing throughput.

The Transformer Engine and Precision

Perhaps the most defining feature of the Hopper architecture, and the one that cemented its status as the AI standard, is the Transformer Engine. Hopper was the first Nvidia architecture to implement this technology, which fundamentally changes how neural networks are trained and inferred. The Transformer Engine accelerates computations by dynamically reducing them from higher numerical precisions, such as FP16, to lower precisions, like FP8, when the loss in precision is deemed acceptable.

This dynamic scaling is not a simple rounding error; it is a sophisticated, runtime decision-making process. The engine is capable of dynamically allocating bits in the chosen precision to either the mantissa or the exponent at runtime to maximize precision where it is needed most. This allows the GPU to perform calculations with the speed of FP8 while maintaining the accuracy of FP16. For large language models, which require processing trillions of parameters, this efficiency gain is not just helpful; it is essential. Without the Transformer Engine, the training times for the models dominating the 2024-2025 AI landscape would have been prohibitive.

The architecture also maintains support for TensorFloat-32 (TF-32) arithmetic, a format introduced in the Ampere generation. The mapping pattern for both architectures is identical, ensuring a smooth transition for developers, but the underlying hardware in Hopper is optimized to handle these formats with greater throughput. Additionally, Hopper features improved single-precision floating-point format (FP32) throughput, delivering twice as many FP32 operations per cycle per SM compared to its predecessor. This makes Hopper not only the king of AI but also a formidable tool for high-performance scientific computing.

New instructions were added to the instruction set to support specific algorithms. The architecture adds support for the Smith–Waterman algorithm, a critical tool in bioinformatics for comparing nucleotide or protein sequences. The math application programming interface (API) exposes functions in the SM such as `__viaddmin_s16x2_relu`, which performs the per-halfword maximum of `(a + b, c)` clamped to zero. In the Smith–Waterman algorithm, `__vimax3_s16x2_relu` can be used, a three-way min or max followed by a clamp to zero. Similarly, Hopper speeds up implementations of the Needleman–Wunsch algorithm. These specific optimizations demonstrate that Hopper was designed with a deep understanding of the workloads it would serve, from generative AI to genomics.

Cache, Compression, and Coherence

The memory hierarchy of the H100 has been rethought to minimize latency and maximize bandwidth. The capacity of the combined L1 cache, texture cache, and shared memory has been increased to 256 KB. Like its predecessors, Hopper combines the L1 and texture caches into a unified cache designed to be a coalescing buffer. The attribute `cudaFuncAttributePreferredSharedMemoryCarveout` may be used to define the carveout of the L1 cache, giving developers granular control over how this memory is allocated between different types of operations.

Hopper allows CUDA compute kernels to utilize automatic inline compression, including in individual memory allocation. This allows the GPU to access memory at higher bandwidths by compressing data on the fly. It is important to note that this feature does not increase the amount of memory available to the application, because the data—and thus its compressibility—may be changed at any time. The compressor will automatically choose between several compression algorithms to find the best fit for the specific data pattern. This dynamic approach ensures that the memory bandwidth is utilized as efficiently as possible without requiring the programmer to manually compress data.

Communication between chips is handled by an enhanced version of NVLink. The Hopper architecture introduces a new generation of NVLink with faster overall communication bandwidth. This is critical for multi-GPU systems where the speed of the network often becomes the bottleneck. However, asynchrony in such high-speed systems introduces complexity. Some CUDA applications may experience interference when performing fence or flush operations due to memory ordering. Because the GPU cannot know which writes are guaranteed and which are visible by chance timing, it may wait on unnecessary memory operations, thus slowing down fence or flush operations.

For example, when a kernel performs computations in GPU memory and a parallel kernel performs communications with a peer, the local kernel will flush its writes, resulting in slower NVLink or PCIe writes. In the Hopper architecture, the GPU can reduce the net cast through a fence operation. This subtle but powerful optimization ensures that the communication links are not held up by unnecessary synchronization, allowing the data to flow freely between accelerators in a cluster.

The GH200 and the CPU-GPU Convergence

The evolution of the datacenter is not just about better GPUs; it is about better integration between the CPU and the GPU. The GH200 module represents this convergence. It combines a Hopper-based H100 GPU with a Grace-based 72-core CPU on a single module. The total power draw of the module is up to 1000 W, a massive amount of energy that requires advanced cooling solutions.

The CPU and GPU are connected via NVLink, which provides memory coherence between CPU and GPU memory. This is a paradigm shift. In traditional systems, data must be copied between the CPU's memory and the GPU's memory, a process that introduces latency and consumes bandwidth. In the GH200, the CPU and GPU share a unified memory space. They can access the same data without copying, effectively acting as a single, massive processing unit. This architecture is particularly beneficial for large-scale AI workloads where the CPU is responsible for data preprocessing and the GPU for training, allowing them to operate in a tightly coupled loop.

Geopolitics and the Global Chip War

The story of Hopper is not just one of engineering; it is a story of geopolitics. The architecture became a focal point in international trade disputes involving U.S. export policy and Chinese import controls. In late 2022, due to U.S. regulations limiting the export of chips to the People's Republic of China, Nvidia adapted the H100 chip to the Chinese market with the H800. This model had lower bandwidth compared to the original H100 model, a deliberate throttling designed to comply with U.S. export controls while still providing a viable product for the Chinese market.

In late 2023, the U.S. government announced new restrictions on the export of AI chips to China, including the A800 and H800 models. This led to Nvidia creating another chip predicated on the Hopper microarchitecture: the H20. The H20 was a modified version of the H100, further adjusted to meet the new regulations. By 2025, the H20 had become the most prominent chip in the Chinese market, a testament to the resilience of the Hopper architecture and the sheer demand for its capabilities, even in a compromised form.

By early 2026, the Hopper-based H200 AI accelerator became a focal point in international trade disputes. Although the U.S. government approved the limited export of H200 chips to China under specific security conditions, reports indicated that Chinese customs officials prevented shipments of the processors from entering the country despite the U.S. clearance. This led suppliers to pause production of H200 components amid uncertainty. The Hopper architecture, born in a lab in Santa Clara, had become a pawn in a global chess game, its fate determined as much by diplomats as by engineers.

A Legacy of Demand

The demand for Hopper chips was insatiable. In 2023, during the AI boom, H100s were in great demand. Larry Ellison of Oracle Corporation described the struggle to acquire them, noting that he, Elon Musk, and Jensen Huang spent an hour "begging" for chips over sushi. The scarcity was real, and the price reflected it. The H100 was not just a piece of hardware; it was a key to the future of artificial intelligence.

The Hopper microarchitecture was leaked in November 2019, a testament to the intense interest in what came next. It was officially revealed in March 2022 at the Nvidia GTC, where it was unveiled as the successor to Ampere. The naming convention, honoring Grace Hopper, was a nod to the history of computing, but the architecture itself was a look into the future. It featured a new streaming multiprocessor, a faster memory subsystem, and a transformer acceleration engine that would redefine the limits of what computers could do.

The H100, with its 80 billion transistors and 3 TB/s of memory bandwidth, was a masterpiece of the TSMC N4 process. It was designed for the SXM5 socket, where it could unleash its full potential, but it was also available in PCIe form for broader adoption. The improvements over Turing and Ampere were not just in numbers; they were in the fundamental way data was moved, processed, and stored. The Tensor Memory Accelerator, the distributed shared memory, the Transformer Engine, and the enhanced NVLink all worked in concert to create a system that was greater than the sum of its parts.

As of 2026, the Hopper architecture remains the backbone of the AI industry. The H200, the latest iteration, continues to drive innovation, even as geopolitical tensions threaten to disrupt its supply chain. The story of Hopper is the story of the modern era: a race against time, a battle for resources, and a relentless pursuit of intelligence. It is a story of 80 billion transistors working in harmony to power the next generation of human thought. And it is a story that is far from over. The Hopper architecture has set the stage, and the world is watching to see what comes next. The demand for more, faster, and more efficient chips shows no sign of abating. The H100, the H800, the H20, and the H200 are not just products; they are milestones in the journey toward artificial general intelligence. And at the center of it all is the legacy of Grace Hopper, the woman whose name now graces the most powerful computer chip ever made.
