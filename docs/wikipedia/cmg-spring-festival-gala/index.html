CMG Spring Festival Gala

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: CMG Spring Festival Gala

In 1983, a television studio in Beijing, cramped and barely 600 square meters in size, became the stage for a radical experiment that would eventually command the attention of a billion people. There was no budget for editing, no safety net for live television, and the airwaves were filled with the nervous energy of improvisation. The director, Huang Yihe, had proposed a televised party to celebrate the Lunar New Year, a concept that seemed almost frivolous given the political and economic fragility of China at the time. The studio could hold only sixty staff members and two hundred guests, yet the ambition was national in scale. Four telephones sat on a desk, their lines open to the entire country, inviting viewers to call in with their requests. It was a gamble of monumental proportions, broadcast live to a nation just beginning to emerge from the Cultural Revolution's shadows.

The first Spring Festival Gala, or Chunwan as it is affectionately known, was not merely a variety show; it was a cultural barometer for a changing society. The hosts—Liu Xiaoqing, Ma Ji, Jiang Kun, and Wang Jingyu—navigated a landscape where the boundaries of acceptable entertainment were still being drawn. The most daring moment of the inaugural broadcast came when the singer Li Guyi took the stage. In the early 1980s, pop music was viewed by hardline ideologues as "spiritual pollution," and Li's signature song, Hometown Love, was officially banned. Yet, with the silent but decisive permission of Wu Lengxi, the Minister of Radio and Television, who was seated in the audience, Li Guyi performed the forbidden track. It was a quiet rebellion that resonated loudly across the country, signaling that the Gala would be a space where the rigid dogma of the past could occasionally give way to the emotional needs of the present.

The success of that first night was immediate and overwhelming. The audience response was so potent that Wu Lengxi was tasked with directing the following year's edition, a decision that would alter the course of Chinese television history. By 1984, the geopolitical landscape was shifting; China and Britain were deep in negotiations regarding the future of Hong Kong. In a move that defied convention and met with fierce resistance from conservative officials, Huang Yihe insisted on inviting Cheung Ming-man, an amateur singer from Hong Kong. It was unprecedented for a Hong Kong entertainer to perform on mainland Chinese television. Huang lobbied relentlessly, arguing that the performance could bridge a cultural divide during a tense political moment. His persistence paid off. When Cheung performed the patriotic ballad "My Chinese Heart," the song became an anthem, turning the singer into a household name and cementing the Gala's role as a unifying force for the Chinese nation, regardless of where its people lived.

The Ritual of the Nation

As the decades turned, the Spring Festival Gala evolved from a televised party into a ritual of national significance. The viewership numbers grew exponentially, a testament to the show's embedding into the fabric of daily life. By 2018, the broadcaster claimed an audience of one billion viewers, a figure that secured its place in the Guinness World Records as the most watched national network TV broadcast in history. This was not merely a statistic; it represented a collective experience shared across the vast expanse of China. On Chinese New Year's Eve, the typical scene in millions of homes was identical: three generations of a family gathered around a single television set. The air would be thick with the scent of dumpling dough as families prepared their first meal of the year, while the Gala provided the soundtrack to their celebration.

The show became the backdrop for laughter, discussion, and shared joy, a digital hearth around which the nation warmed itself. Even in rural areas where television had been a novelty just a few years prior, great gatherings were organized specifically to watch the program. The Gala was no longer just entertainment; it was the glue holding the family unit together during the most important holiday of the year. It was a tradition so ingrained that to miss it was to be absent from the national conversation. The broadcast transformed the private act of dining into a public spectacle of unity, where the boundary between the viewer and the performer blurred in a shared moment of cultural affirmation.

The Evolution of Content and Politics

The content of the Gala has always been a reflection of the political winds blowing through Beijing. In its earliest years, the 1980s, the show focused almost exclusively on arts and entertainment. The programming was remarkably free of heavy political rhetoric, mirroring the general openness of Chinese society as it moved away from Maoist dogma. The political establishment, however, watched with keen interest. In 1984, General Secretary Hu Yaobang viewed the show and was so moved by Cheung Ming-man's performance that he resolved to learn the song himself. By 1990, the line between the state and the stage began to thin visibly. Communist Party General Secretary Jiang Zemin and Premier Li Peng appeared on the Gala, ostensibly to join the celebration rather than to lecture. Jiang delivered a six-minute speech of well-wishes, the only time in the program's history that national leaders participated directly in the festivities. It was a moment of genuine connection, a fleeting instance where the hierarchy of power seemed to dissolve into the warmth of the holiday.

However, as the audience swelled and the Gala's prestige grew, so too did the state's desire to control its message. The 1990s marked a turning point where the show began to shift from a "year-end tea party" to a "conference for disseminating political propaganda," as noted by the Takungpao newspaper. Segments were increasingly devoted to celebrating the previous year's "national achievements" and previewing the significant events of the coming year. The show became a ritualized event of national significance, experiencing increased state involvement in its production. In 2008, during the year of the Beijing Summer Olympics, the atmosphere was charged with national pride. State media reported that major officials from the Publicity Department of the Chinese Communist Party and the State Administration of Radio, Film, and Television were on-site during rehearsals to supervise the production. In a gesture that highlighted the state's focus on social harmony, a segment featuring migrant workers was inserted into the show on the direct recommendation of Premier Wen Jiabao.

The political evolution of the Gala has been a pendulum swing over the years. Beginning in the 1990s, video montages featuring Communist Party leaders became a staple, accompanied by stirring background music. These segments consistently honored paramount leaders such as Mao Zedong, Deng Xiaoping, Jiang Zemin, and Hu Jintao. In 2007 and 2008, the footage expanded to include the entire Politburo Standing Committee, a visual assertion of unity and continuity. Yet, there were periods of restraint. Between 2011 and 2014, imagery of national leaders was notably absent from the show, and the political content varied significantly from year to year. The 2011 edition, directed by Ha Wen, wife of host Li Yong, marked a distinct break with tradition. The announcements of embassies sending New Year's greetings were removed, as was the "My Favorite New Year's Gala Act" voting announcement. For the first time, the show did not conclude with the traditional rendition of "Can't Forget Tonight."

The Rise of the Ordinary and the Nationalist Turn

Coinciding with the removal of overt political imagery in the early 2010s was a surge in the popularity of "ordinary people" performers. In 2011, the show featured Dashan alongside several foreign nationals of various ages, all engaging in fluent Mandarin conversation. The segment included a Russian, an Australian, and a Kenyan, showcasing a cosmopolitan China that was confident in its global interactions. The 2011 show also highlighted performers selected by popular vote in a TV competition months prior, a trend that continued into 2012. These amateur performers brought a sense of authenticity and grassroots energy to the stage, contrasting sharply with the polished, state-sanctioned acts of previous decades. This era suggested a brief opening for a more diverse and less ideologically rigid cultural expression.

However, the pendulum swung back. By 2014, the Gala was once again peppered with political enhancements. The show paid homage to General Secretary Xi Jinping's "Chinese Dream" ideology, weaving nationalistic themes throughout the performance lineup. The songs were more patriotic, the messaging more direct. The 2015 show reportedly continued this trend, reinforcing the Gala's role as a vehicle for state ideology. Officials in charge of propaganda and media control, including Ding Guangen, Li Changchun, and Liu Yunshan, maintained a close watch on the production teams, visiting to ensure the tone remained aligned with the party's objectives. The narrative of the show shifted from the personal and the celebratory to the collective and the political, reminding the audience of their place within the broader arc of national history.

A Global Stage

While the domestic audience remains the core of the Gala's power, the show's reach has expanded far beyond the borders of mainland China. Beginning in the 1990s, the broadcast was extended to the Chinese diaspora, connecting millions of overseas viewers with their cultural roots. With the rise of the internet and digital media, the reach has only grown. Since 2016, dedicated simulcasts for foreign viewers have been aired on the CGTN network, ensuring that the Spring Festival Gala is a global event. The show celebrated its silver jubilee in 2008, a milestone that coincided with the hype and excitement of the Beijing Summer Olympics, with CCTV serving as the official national broadcaster of the Games. The pearl jubilee was marked in 2013, and the ruby jubilee in 2023, each milestone reinforcing the show's longevity and its status as an enduring institution.

The Gala's journey from a low-budget, live improvisation in a 600-square-meter studio to a billion-dollar global phenomenon is a testament to its unique ability to adapt. It has survived political upheavals, technological revolutions, and shifting social mores. It has been a platform for banned songs, a bridge to Hong Kong, a stage for migrant workers, and a megaphone for national ideology. Through it all, the core experience remains unchanged: on Chinese New Year's Eve, families gather, dumplings are made, and the nation watches together. The Gala is more than a television program; it is the heartbeat of the Chinese New Year, a pulse that beats in unison with the hopes, fears, and dreams of a billion people. It is a mirror reflecting the complexities of modern China, a place where the personal and the political, the traditional and the modern, dance together on the same stage.

The story of the CMG Spring Festival Gala is the story of China itself in the late 20th and early 21st centuries. It is a narrative of opening and closing, of risk and reward, of a society struggling to define its identity in a rapidly changing world. From the four telephones on the desk in 1983 to the digital streams reaching every corner of the globe today, the Gala has remained a constant, a familiar presence in a world of flux. It is a reminder that even in the most rigid of systems, there is a space for celebration, for connection, and for the enduring power of a shared story. The show may have changed its hosts, its songs, and its political messages, but its purpose remains the same: to bring the nation together, if only for a few hours, in the warmth of a shared tradition.

As we look back at the history of the Gala, we see a tapestry woven with threads of creativity, politics, and human connection. The early days were marked by a sense of possibility, a belief that television could be a force for joy and unity. The middle years saw the show grow into a giant, taking on the weight of national expectations and political demands. The recent years have seen a complex interplay between the desire for entertainment and the need for ideological reinforcement. Yet, through it all, the audience remains. They are the reason the show exists, the reason the directors take risks, and the reason the singers perform their hearts out. The Spring Festival Gala is, ultimately, a reflection of the people it serves, a mirror that shows us not just who we are, but who we aspire to be. It is a celebration of life, a tribute to the past, and a hope for the future, broadcast live to a nation that refuses to look away.

The legacy of the Gala is secure. It has broken records, defied expectations, and become a cultural touchstone that transcends generations. It is a phenomenon that cannot be fully understood through statistics alone; it must be felt in the laughter of a family gathered around a TV, in the silence of a nation holding its breath for a political announcement, and in the joy of a singer performing a song that was once banned. The CMG Spring Festival Gala is a testament to the power of television to shape culture, to influence politics, and to bring people together. It is a story that is still being written, with each New Year's Eve adding a new chapter to an epic tale of a nation's journey. As the show continues to evolve, it will undoubtedly face new challenges and new opportunities, but its place in the hearts of the Chinese people is assured. It is, and always will be, the voice of the nation, singing its song to the world.
