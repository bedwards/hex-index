Iran International

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Iran International

In May 2017, a satellite signal began to pierce through the heavy veil of state censorship that had long choked the airwaves inside Iran. From a studio in London, funded by a coalition within the Saudi royal court, a new Persian-language voice emerged: Iran International. It was not merely another news channel; it was a geopolitical weapon, a digital lifeline for a dissatisfied populace, and a direct challenge to the Islamic Republic's monopoly on truth. Its stated mission was to provide "a fair and balanced view" of what happens inside Iran, yet its practical function quickly evolved into something far more confrontational: a platform actively promoting former Crown Prince Reza Pahlavi as the nation's future ruler and broadcasting the raw, unfiltered anger of the Iranian opposition to a global audience.

To understand the magnitude of this enterprise, one must first grasp the environment it was designed to disrupt. For decades, the Iranian government has treated independent information as an existential threat, jamming satellite signals and criminalizing the consumption of foreign media. Iran International was built to bypass these barriers, beaming news into living rooms in Tehran, Mashhad, and Isfahan via the TürkmenÄlem 52°E / MonacoSAT satellite, while also maintaining a shortwave radio relay that could reach even the most remote valleys where internet access is impossible. It arrived at a time when the Iranian public was increasingly isolated from the global narrative, hungry for an outlet that would not dance around the regime's red lines regarding human rights abuses, economic collapse, or the suppression of LGBTQ+ communities.

The channel's genesis was deeply rooted in the shifting sands of Middle Eastern power dynamics. While it claimed to be a neutral broadcaster for the diaspora and domestic audiences alike, its financial and strategic DNA traced back to Riyadh. According to UK corporate records and sources familiar with the venture, the initiative was launched by members of the Saudi royal court circle. Scholar Elisheva Machlis placed this founding moment squarely within the context of "a Saudi effort to gain influence inside Iran," a calculated move to counter Tehran's regional expansionism by weaponizing information. Negar Mortazavi, the channel's former Washington correspondent, described it as part of an expensive Saudi push for "influence and credibility." This was not charity; it was statecraft conducted through the lens of 24-hour news cycles.

Headquartered in London but operating with a global reach, Iran International assembled a team of journalists who had defected from or been pushed out by other major Persian-language outlets like Manoto, Radio Farda, BBC Persian, and Voice of America. These were not novices; they were veterans of the information war, seasoned by years of navigating the treacherous waters of exile journalism. The network expanded its footprint with bureaus in Istanbul, Paris, Kabul, and Washington D.C., creating a web of reporting that could track events from the streets of Tehran to the corridors of power in the West. In 2021, Volant Media UK launched a sister channel, Afghanistan International, signaling an ambition to become the primary voice for Persian-speaking peoples across borders.

The channel's editorial stance was immediately polarizing. It did not shy away from its identity as an opposition media organization, a fact acknowledged by critics and supporters alike. Kourosh Ziabari of Al-Monitor noted that the outlet "does not shy away from presenting itself as an opposition media organization," frequently providing a microphone to guests who offered scathing critiques of the Iranian government. It became known in the West as an "Iranian exile news outlet," but inside Iran, its presence was felt as a breach in the dam holding back public dissent. The channel consistently raised the profile of Reza Pahlavi, the son of the last Shah, framing him not just as a historical figure but as a viable, necessary alternative to the clerical establishment. Through constant coverage and repeated high-profile interviews, Iran International worked tirelessly to establish him as the potential leader of a future Iran, a narrative that the regime viewed as nothing short of treasonous.

The human cost of this information war became starkly apparent in September 2018, following a devastating terrorist attack on a military parade in Ahvaz, Khuzestan Province. The blast killed 25 civilians and military personnel, leaving families shattered and the city reeling with grief. In the chaotic aftermath, Iran International aired an interview with Yaqoub Hor Altostari, presented as a spokesman for the separatist group claiming responsibility. The spokesperson described the attack on his own people not as a tragedy, but as "resistance against legitimate targets."

This decision triggered a diplomatic firestorm. Iran's ambassador to the UK lodged a formal complaint with Ofcom, the British media regulator, arguing that the channel was amplifying terrorism and violating broadcasting standards. The investigation was long and contentious, pitting freedom of speech principles against the realities of state-sponsored violence. Ultimately, Ofcom ruled that Iran International had not breached any rules, a decision that underscored the complex legal tightrope walked by exile broadcasters. Yet, for the families of those 25 dead in Ahvaz, the distinction between "reporting" and "inciting" was a cold comfort; the airwaves they relied on had given a platform to the voice justifying their loss.

The tension escalated dramatically with the eruption of the Mahsa Amini protests in late 2022. When a young woman died in police custody, sparking nationwide uprisings that shook the foundations of the regime, Iran International became one of the primary conduits for images and stories from the streets. The Iranian government, unable to contain the narrative internally, turned its fire outward. On November 9, 2022, Ismail Khatib, the Minister of Intelligence, announced a chilling declaration: Iran International was now designated a "terrorist organization."

The implications were immediate and severe. Khatib warned that any cooperation with the channel would be considered an act of terrorism and a threat to national security. This was not mere rhetoric; it was a direct threat to the physical safety of journalists, their families in Iran, and the London staff who reported from afar. The regime's message was clear: to listen to this voice was to betray the state. In response, Iran International recognized that the threat environment had shifted irrevocably. The abstract danger of censorship had morphed into a concrete risk of assassination or sabotage.

The channel took drastic measures to protect its staff and infrastructure in London. Concrete barriers, engineered to stop a 7.5-ton truck traveling at 50 miles per hour, were installed around the studio perimeter, mirroring the defensive protocols used by the UK government against vehicular attacks. Checkpoints were established, and vehicles entering the premises were monitored with intense scrutiny. The atmosphere inside the newsroom transformed from one of professional urgency to one of siege mentality.

By February 18, 2023, the pressure had become unsustainable. Following a significant escalation in state-backed threats and explicit advice from the Metropolitan Police that the security situation was untenable, Iran International made the reluctant decision to close its London studios temporarily. The broadcast operations were uprooted and moved thousands of miles away to Washington D.C., severing the physical connection to their historic home but attempting to preserve the signal. It was a humiliating retreat, forced by the reach of a regime that refused to let go of its enemies even on foreign soil.

However, the geopolitical landscape shifted once again. In March 2023, in a surprise diplomatic breakthrough brokered in Beijing, Iran and Saudi Arabia agreed to re-establish full diplomatic relations. The details of this accord remained largely behind closed doors, but reports soon emerged suggesting that Saudi Arabia had made concessions regarding its media arm. It was rumored that as part of the deal, Riyadh agreed to tone down coverage of the Mahsa Amini protests on Iran International or even halt funding entirely. This revelation cast a long shadow over the channel's independence, raising questions about whether its editorial line would be dictated by the whims of foreign monarchs rather than the needs of the Iranian people.

Despite these geopolitical machinations, the channel found itself back in London by September 2023, resuming broadcasting from a new location. The return was not without incident; just months prior, in February 2023, Magomed-Husejn Dovtaev had been charged with terrorism for photographing areas surrounding the studios. Found guilty and sentenced to three and a half years in jail, his case served as a grim reminder of the lengths to which hostile actors would go to infiltrate and intimidate the network.

The digital front of this conflict proved equally volatile. In July 2025, the Handala group, which Iran International linked directly to Iranian intelligence services, executed a massive cyberattack on the channel's internal systems. The breach was catastrophic. Over two terabytes of data were exfiltrated and leaked online, exposing sensitive information on more than 71,000 users, employees, financial records, contracts, and internal messages. For a news organization operating in such a hostile environment, this was an existential crisis. The leak threatened the safety of every source who had entrusted the channel with their identity, potentially handing the Iranian intelligence apparatus a roadmap to dismantle dissent networks across the globe. Iran International confirmed the authenticity of the leaked data, acknowledging that the digital walls they relied upon had been breached.

Yet, amidst these threats and breaches, the channel continued its work, driven by a relentless focus on human stories that official narratives tried to erase. It became a primary source for reporting on the plight of political prisoners and the struggle for justice. The network reported exclusively on the extension of Nazanin Zaghari-Ratcliffe's furlough, keeping her case in the global spotlight while her husband, Richard Ratcliffe, appeared as a frequent guest, pleading for his wife's release. It was the first to report that Iranian rapper Amir Tataloo faced deportation from Turkey back to a potentially fatal fate, and it covered the sentencing of acclaimed actress Taraneh Alidoosti, giving voice to the cultural repression that defined life under the regime.

During the COVID-19 pandemic, when the Iranian government attempted to downplay the severity of the outbreak and its own failures in managing medical supplies, Iran International dug deep. The channel published exclusive documents alleging high-level corruption within the procurement process for medical equipment. These documents purported to show how regime figures intervened to steer contracts toward South Korean companies under dubious circumstances, a revelation that aligned with reports from The Independent and others suggesting that the crisis was exacerbated by graft rather than just resource scarcity. The human cost of this corruption was measured in lives lost, families denied treatment, and a healthcare system pushed to the brink.

The channel's scope extended beyond politics into culture and sports, producing documentaries that were nominated for awards by the Association for International Broadcasters. It broadcast performances of Iranian singers flown in from the United States for the Winter at Tantora Festival in 2020, celebrating the resilience of Persian art even as the nation crumbled under political and economic pressure. By 2018, the channel was not running television advertisements, but by 2026, the financial landscape had shifted; ads promoting Reza Pahlavi were now a feature of the broadcast schedule, further cementing the channel's role as a political vehicle for the monarchist movement.

The geopolitical stakes remained high even as new conflicts emerged. Following the Twelve-Day War in June 2025, Iran International conducted a series of interviews with top Israeli politicians, including Benjamin Netanyahu, Benny Gantz, and Yair Lapid. These discussions placed the channel at the center of the region's most volatile flashpoints, offering a platform for Israeli perspectives that were otherwise inaccessible to Persian-speaking audiences. This move drew further ire from Tehran, which viewed any normalization of relations or open dialogue with Israel as an unforgivable betrayal.

Throughout its history, Iran International has been accused by the Iranian government of inciting violence and acting as a mouthpiece for foreign powers. The regime's narrative paints the channel as a tool of imperialism, designed to destabilize the Islamic Republic from within. Yet, for the millions of Iranians who tune in via satellite or stream online despite the risk of arrest, the channel represents something different: a window into a world where free speech exists, where corruption is exposed, and where the possibility of a different future is debated openly.

The tragedy of Iran International is that its existence highlights the deep fractures within Iranian society. It is a symbol of hope for some and a symbol of treason for others, a entity caught between the strategic interests of Saudi Arabia, the security apparatus of the United Kingdom, and the desperate aspirations of the Iranian people. The 71,000 users whose data was leaked in 2025 were not just numbers; they were individuals who believed that the truth was worth the risk. The 25 civilians killed in Ahvaz were not just statistics in a terror claim; they were victims of a cycle of violence that the channel both reported on and, according to its critics, exacerbated.

As of June 9, 2026, the channel continues to broadcast from London and Washington, navigating a landscape defined by cyber warfare, diplomatic maneuvering, and state-sponsored threats. It remains a testament to the power of media in the modern age—a tool that can shape regimes, protect dissidents, or destroy lives depending on who wields it and how it is used. The story of Iran International is not just about a television station; it is about the struggle for truth in an era where information is both the most valuable commodity and the deadliest weapon. It is a reminder that while governments can build walls, jam signals, and designate enemies as terrorists, they cannot easily silence the human desire to know what is happening in the world, especially when that knowledge holds the key to freedom.

The journey of this channel from its Saudi-funded inception to its current status as a fortified bastion of opposition journalism illustrates the evolving nature of information warfare in the 21st century. It has weathered cyberattacks, physical intimidation, diplomatic betrayals, and legal challenges, yet it persists. In doing so, it serves as a mirror to the Iranian state's insecurities and the people's resilience. Whether viewed as a liberator or an agitator, its impact is undeniable. The airwaves are no longer silent, and for many inside Iran, that noise is the sound of change, however dangerous it may be to hear.
