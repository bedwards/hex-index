Amicus curiae

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Amicus curiae

In 1823, a man named Henry Clay stood before the Supreme Court of the United States, not as a litigant, but as a guest of the judiciary itself. He was there to offer wisdom on a dispute between Kentucky and Indiana, a case known as Green v. Biddle. Clay did not win the argument, but he established a precedent that would ripple through two centuries of legal history: the right of a non-party to step into the courtroom and speak truth to power. He was the first recorded amicus curiae, a Latin phrase meaning "friend of the court," and his appearance marked the formalization of a mechanism that allows the law to hear voices beyond the narrow, adversarial clash of the plaintiff and the defendant.

The concept is deceptively simple, yet its application has become one of the most dynamic forces in modern jurisprudence. An amicus curiae is an individual or organization that has no stake in the outcome of a specific legal case, yet is granted permission to assist the court by offering information, expertise, or insight that bears on the issues at hand. Unlike a witness who testifies to facts, or a lawyer who argues for a client, the amicus is a neutral scholar, a concerned citizen, or a representative of a broader industry, invited to expand the court's horizon. Whether a court accepts this help is entirely at its discretion, a gatekeeping function that transforms the amicus from a bystander into a strategic player in the highest courts of the land.

The Anatomy of a Friend

To understand the amicus, one must first understand the rigid architecture of the adversarial system. In a typical lawsuit, two parties face off. Each is motivated by self-interest, presenting a version of the facts and law that is meticulously curated to favor their client. The judge or jury is left to sift through these competing narratives, often constrained by the procedural boundaries set by the litigants themselves. This system works well for resolving binary disputes, but it struggles when a case carries implications that extend far beyond the two people standing in the courtroom.

When a decision could reshape an entire industry, redefine a constitutional right, or alter the landscape of civil liberties, the narrow arguments of the litigants may not be enough. This is where the amicus curiae steps in. They are the broad public interest advocates, the technical experts, and the policy wonks who ensure that the court sees the full picture. In the United States, the term "amicus" is often shorthand for the "amicus brief," a written submission that articulates these broader concerns.

The origins of the phrase are rooted in the dusty corridors of legal history, dated to the period between 1605 and 1615. The language itself is Latin, the lingua franca of the legal and cultural elites of the Anglo-Saxon world for centuries. While some scholars have attempted to trace the figure back to the Roman consiliarius, the Italian academic Giovanni Criscuoli argues that the amicus is a creature of exclusive Anglo-Saxon blood. Starting in the 9th century, the concept was woven into the fabric of English common law, eventually spreading to most common law systems across the globe. It was later transplanted into the realm of international law, particularly concerning human rights, and has since found its way into civil law systems, including Argentina's law system and Honduras's 2010 civil procedures code.

The role is not merely to support one side or the other. As Lord Justice of Appeal Cyril Salmon eloquently described in Allen v Sir Alfred McAlpine & Sons Ltd:

"I had always understood that the role of an amicus curiae was to help the court by expounding the law impartially, or if one of the parties were unrepresented, by advancing the legal arguments on his behalf."

This definition highlights a duality in the amicus function. In some contexts, they are the voice of the unrepresented, ensuring that justice is not denied simply because a party lacks a lawyer. In others, they are the voice of the public, ensuring that the law does not develop in a vacuum, detached from the reality of the society it governs.

The American Phenomenon

In the United States, the amicus curiae has evolved from a rare judicial courtesy into a staple of appellate litigation. The Supreme Court of the United States, in particular, has witnessed a staggering surge in these filings. In the 2019–2020 term, a remarkable 97% of cases included amicus briefs. This represents a sharp increase from the earlier decades of the 20th century, where such interventions were few and far between. The trajectory of these filings mirrors the increasing complexity of American law and the growing recognition that high-stakes litigation often transcends the immediate parties.

The rules governing these briefs are strict, designed to maintain order and relevance. Supreme Court Rule 37 mandates that an amicus brief must cover "relevant matter" not dealt with by the parties which "may be of considerable help." The cover of the brief must explicitly state which party is being supported, or if the brief supports a simple affirmance or reversal of the lower court's decision. Furthermore, transparency is paramount: all non-governmental amici must identify those providing a monetary contribution to the preparation or submission of the brief. This is a crucial safeguard against hidden agendas. Briefs must be prepared in booklet format, and 40 copies must be served with the Court, a physical reminder of the weight these documents carry.

The landscape of who files these briefs is dominated by organizations with sizable legal budgets. In the United States, non-profit legal advocacy groups are the primary drivers. The American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU), the Landmark Legal Foundation, the Pacific Legal Foundation, the Electronic Frontier Foundation, the American Center for Law and Justice, and the National Organization for the Reform of Marijuana Laws (NORML) are just a few of the heavy hitters that frequently submit briefs to advocate for or against specific legal changes.

These organizations do not simply offer opinions; they offer data, historical context, and predictive modeling. If a decision could affect an entire industry, companies that are not litigants may wish to have their concerns heard. In McDonald v. Chicago, a landmark case regarding the Second Amendment, thirty-two states filed amicus briefs under the aegis of Texas, with California filing independently. These states were not the primary litigants, but the outcome of the case would directly impact their laws and interests. By filing as amici, they ensured the Court understood the federalism implications of the ruling.

The federal government itself is a frequent amicus, particularly in cases involving the constitutionality of state laws. However, the most extraordinary aspect of the American system is the court's willingness to appoint its own amicus curiae. If neither party supports the decision of the lower court, or if the Court feels a critical legal argument is being missed, it can appoint a lawyer to argue the case. The Supreme Court has done this at least 44 times, a testament to its commitment to a fully developed record.

The Canadian Distinction

While the American model focuses on the "brief" as a tool for advocacy, the Canadian model retains a more literal interpretation of the "friend." In Canadian law, an amicus curiae is rarely an outside organization or a lobby group. Instead, it is a lawyer appointed by the court itself. This distinction is vital. The Canadian amicus is not an advocate for a specific interest group; they are an officer of the court, tasked with ensuring that the legal issues are properly canvassed.

This mechanism is most often triggered in criminal cases where the accused is unrepresented and ineligible for or refuses to apply for legal aid. If a judge determines that the lack of representation puts the accused at a significant disadvantage and risks a miscarriage of justice, they may appoint a lawyer as amicus curiae. This lawyer is not retained by the accused, nor do they represent the accused in the traditional sense. Their duty is not to the client, but to the court.

The responsibility of the Canadian amicus is to ensure that points of law of importance to the party's case are brought to the attention of the court. In a criminal trial, for instance, the amicus has the duty to ensure the accused's right to make a full answer and defense is protected. They ask the questions that the accused cannot ask, challenge the prosecution's evidence, and ensure that the trial proceeds fairly. This model strips the amicus of the political baggage often associated with the American version, returning the role to its roots as a neutral helper of the judiciary.

The Global Reach

The influence of the amicus curiae has transcended national borders, becoming a fixture in international law. In the realm of human rights, where the stakes are often existential, the ability of non-state actors to provide insight is invaluable. The European Court of Human Rights, the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights, the Inter-American Court of Human Rights, the Court of Justice of the European Union, and the Special Tribunal for Lebanon all utilize legal submissions by intervenors, known as amicus curiae observations.

These international bodies recognize that human rights cases often involve complex cultural, social, and political dimensions that the litigants may not fully articulate. An NGO specializing in the rights of indigenous peoples, a medical association providing data on health impacts, or a historical society offering context on colonial legacies can all submit observations that shape the court's understanding of the case. This global adoption underscores the universality of the need for a broader perspective in the administration of justice.

The De Facto Amicus

Not all "friends of the court" file briefs. In the modern information age, a new category of amicus has emerged: the de facto amicus. These are academics, historians, economists, and sociologists who publish their analyses in the print media, on social media, and in op-eds. They do not submit materials to the Court, nor do they ask for leave. They have no guarantee that their words will be read by a justice. Yet, their influence is undeniable.

If a case hinges on a history of legislation, a historian may evaluate the claim from their specialized expertise. If the case involves economic regulation, an economist may provide data that contradicts the assumptions of the litigants. Newspaper editorials, blogs, and opinion pieces have the capability to influence Supreme Court decisions by shaping the intellectual climate in which the justices operate. While they are not technically amici curiae, they function as such, bringing specialized knowledge and public perspective to bear on the legal dispute.

This phenomenon highlights a shift in the nature of judicial influence. The court is no longer an isolated fortress; it is part of a larger ecosystem of public discourse. The arguments that eventually appear in an amicus brief often originate in the broader public sphere, refined and formalized by legal advocates. The line between the "de facto" and the "de jure" amicus is increasingly blurred, as the court pays attention to the "relevant matter" being discussed everywhere, not just in the filing cabinets of the Supreme Court.

The Mechanics of Influence

The mechanics of filing an amicus brief are a dance of procedure and strategy. In the United States Supreme Court, unless the brief is being filed by the federal government or a U.S. state, permission is generally required. This permission is granted either by the consent of the parties or by a motion for leave to the Court. This gatekeeping role ensures that the court is not inundated with frivolous or repetitive arguments.

Allowing an amicus curiae to present oral argument is considered "extraordinary." It is a rare privilege, reserved for cases where the written brief is insufficient to convey the complexity of the issue. The court prefers to let the litigants do the heavy lifting, using the amicus only to fill the gaps. This restraint is a testament to the court's desire to maintain the adversarial process while still benefiting from external expertise.

Religious groups are among the most frequent filers of amicus briefs at the U.S. Supreme Court. They have filed on both sides of divisive cultural issues, such as same-sex marriage and expansive conceptions of gender identity. These briefs often reflect deep theological and moral reasoning, providing the court with a window into the religious community's understanding of the law. Muslim organizations and individuals, for example, have filed briefs on these issues, ensuring that the diversity of religious thought is represented in the Court's deliberations.

The Evolution of a Concept

The story of the amicus curiae is one of adaptation. From its origins in the 9th century English common law to its current status as a global legal instrument, the concept has evolved to meet the changing needs of society. In the early days, it was a mechanism for ensuring that the unrepresented were not left to fend for themselves. Today, it is a tool for ensuring that the law is informed by the full spectrum of human knowledge and experience.

The increase in amicus filings in the United States, from a handful in the early 20th century to nearly every case today, reflects the increasing complexity of the legal landscape. As society becomes more interconnected and issues more multifaceted, the need for a broader perspective in the courtroom grows. The amicus curiae is the embodiment of this need, a reminder that justice is not a zero-sum game between two parties, but a collective endeavor to find the right answer.

The debate over the origins of the term continues, with scholars divided on whether it is a Roman inheritance or an Anglo-Saxon innovation. But the utility of the figure is beyond debate. Whether it is a lawyer appointed in a Canadian criminal court to protect the rights of the accused, or a coalition of thirty-two states filing a brief in the U.S. Supreme Court to protect federalism, the amicus curiae serves the same fundamental purpose: to help the court see what the parties cannot see.

The Future of the Friend

As we look to the future, the role of the amicus curiae is likely to expand further. The rise of digital media and the increasing accessibility of legal information mean that more voices can be heard, even if they do not file a formal brief. The "de facto" amicus will continue to play a crucial role in shaping the intellectual environment of the courts.

However, challenges remain. The sheer volume of amicus briefs can be overwhelming, and the risk of "brief fatigue" is real. Courts must continue to exercise discretion, ensuring that only the most relevant and helpful submissions are considered. The requirement for transparency regarding funding is a vital tool in this regard, allowing the court and the public to understand the motivations behind the arguments.

The amicus curiae is a testament to the resilience and adaptability of the legal system. It is a mechanism that allows the law to breathe, to absorb new ideas, and to remain connected to the society it serves. In a world where the law is often criticized for being slow, rigid, and out of touch, the amicus curiae offers a glimpse of a different possibility: a legal system that listens, learns, and evolves.

The next time you read about a landmark case in the news, look for the list of amici. Behind the headlines and the legal jargon, there is a story of a diverse array of voices—historians, economists, religious leaders, industry giants, and public interest groups—all trying to help the court make the right decision. They are the friends of the court, and their contributions are a vital part of the justice system's ongoing quest for truth.

In the end, the amicus curiae is more than just a legal technicality. It is a philosophical statement about the nature of justice. It asserts that the pursuit of truth is a collective effort, and that no single party, no matter how powerful, has a monopoly on wisdom. By inviting the friend of the court to speak, the judiciary acknowledges that the law belongs to everyone, and that the best decisions are made when all relevant voices are heard.

The legacy of Henry Clay, the first recorded amicus, lives on in every brief filed, every oral argument granted, and every de facto contribution made in the public sphere. He may not have prevailed in Green v. Biddle, but he won something far more important: a permanent place for the friend of the court in the halls of justice. And as long as there are complex issues to be resolved and broad public interests to be protected, that place will remain open.
