National People's Congress

9 min read

Based on Wikipedia: National People's Congress

At precisely 9:00 AM each spring, something remarkable happens in the shadow of Tiananmen Square. In the Great Hall of the People—a monumental structure on the western edge of the square where Chairman Mao once hoisted the triumph of a new nation—nearly three thousand people file into their seats. They represent every province, every ethnic minority, every profession and stratum of Chinese society. Within fourteen days, they will have reshaped the legal landscape of the world's most populous nation. This is the National People's Congress, and it is, by sheer number, the largest legislative body on Earth.

The Supreme Organ of Power

The National People's Congress—NPC in international shorthand—isn't merely another parliament. It is the supreme organ of state power of the People's Republic of China, the vessel through which the machinery of Chinese governance flows. To understand its role requires understanding a fundamental principle embedded in China's political architecture: the Chinese Communist Party, constitutionally enshrined as having the "leading role" in the country's communist state system.

The NPC wields powers that extend far beyond what Western observers typically associate with legislatures. It possesses the authority to amend China's constitution, to enact legislation that becomes binding law across fourteen hundred million people, and to oversee the operations of every major state organ. When it gathers each year—from typically mid-March until the end of the month—the NPC votes on landmark pieces of legislation and personnel assignments that will shape the country for years ahead.

Critically, however, this supreme organ does not govern alone. It operates within a division of labour with inferior state organs—the State Council, the Supreme People's Court, the Supreme People's Procuratorate, the National Supervisory Commission, and the Central Military Commission—all unified under what Beijing calls the "unified state apparatus." The relationship between the NPC and these subordinate organs defines how power flows through China's system.

How Power Flows Upward

The mechanics of appointment reveal much about who truly controls this legislature. The deputies to the National People's Congress are not directly elected by popular vote. Instead, they emerge from provincial-level people's congresses, which themselves are indirectly elected—meaning that the electors are chosen by other electors, a cascading chain of representation that begins at the local level and rises upward.

The Chinese Communist Party controls both nomination and election processes at every level of the people's congress system. This isn't hidden or denied; it's simply how the system functions. The party determines who may stand, vetches candidates through its apparatus, and ensures that the final composition reflects its priorities. When the NPC gathers each year in the Great Hall of the People, it represents a carefully curated assembly of China's elites.

In 2025, the NPC comprises 2,904 members—more than any legislative body on Earth. The United States Congress, British Parliament, and French National Assembly all fall short of this number by several hundred seats. And yet, unlike those bodies, membership is largely honorary. Delegates serve part-time with virtually no compensation. They hold their positions alongside other government roles, often simultaneously sitting in provincial people's congresses or other organs of state.

The Real Legislature: Where Power Actually Lives

Here's the secret that Western observers often miss: when the NPC finishes its fourteen-day annual session each spring, it doesn't actually govern China for the remainder of the year. That role falls to an entirely different body—the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress.

The Standing Committee consists of approximately 170 legislators who meet bi-monthly in Beijing—six times a year, regularly enough to ensure continuous exercise of state power when the full NPC is not convened. These positions are full-time and carry salaries, unlike the part-time deputies who serve without pay. The Standing Committee members are explicitly prohibited from simultaneously holding positions in executive, judicial, prosecutorial, or supervisory posts—a rule designed to prevent conflicts of interest and ensure professional legislative focus.

The Standing Committee wields nearly all the lawmaking powers of the NPC itself—except for two crucial exceptions: it cannot amend the constitution and cannot appoint or remove national-level personnel. Otherwise, it passes the vast majority of China's laws, conducts oversight over governmental organs, ratifies treaties, grants special amnesties, confers state honours, and manages the appointment of top-level personnel that fall below national-level positions.

The Two Sessions: Where China Deliberates

Each spring marks a peculiar spectacle in Beijing. The NPC gathers at the same time as another body—the National Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference, or CPPCC. This consultative organ represents various social groups across Chinese society and meets simultaneously with the NPC.

The combined sessions have become known as "Lianghui," or the Two Sessions—a term that carries enormous weight in China's political calendar. The parallelism is deliberate: when these two bodies gather, they hear reports from the premier of the State Council, the president of the Supreme People's Court, and the prosecutor general. For fourteen days each year, China essentially deliberates its future direction through this dual mechanism.

But there's more to these sessions than formal deliberation. Before each plenary session of the NPC, a preparatory meeting occurs where a Presidium and Secretary-General are elected. The Presidium presides over daily affairs during the Congress—determining schedules, deciding whether to list delegates' bills on the agenda, hearing deputy deliberations, nominating candidates for top state offices, and organizing constitutional oath ceremonies.

Special Committees: The Real Legislative Engine

Beyond the Standing Committee, ten special committees exist under the NPC to handle specific domains. These committees include:

  • Ethnic Affairs Committee
  • Constitution and Law Committee
  • Supervisory and Judicial Affairs Committee
  • Financial and Economic Affairs Committee
  • Education, Science, Culture and Public Health Committee

These committees meet regularly with full-time staff, drafting legislative work and policy proposals. A large portion of China's legislative work is effectively delegated to these committees in between the bimonthly plenary sessions of the Standing Committee—a system that keeps the legislative engine running year-round.

The Ghost of 1931

The current NPC traces its lineage back to something far more obscure than its modern incarnation. The First National Congress of the Chinese Soviets of Workers', Peasants' and Soldiers' Deputies took place on November 7, 1931, in Ruijin, Jiangxi—on the fourteenth anniversary of the Russian October Revolution.

At that moment in the early 1930s, China was a fractured nation battling against itself—the new Communist movement testing its strength against both domestic rivals and foreign threats. A subsequent Soviet Congress took place in Fujian on March 18, 1932—the sixty-first anniversary of the Paris Commune—marking the early institutional forms of what would eventually become this modern legislature.

The official Second National Congress took place from January 22 to February 1, 1934, with only 693 deputies elected and the Chinese Red Army holding 117 seats. These were humble beginnings for an institution that would eventually command nearly a billion people.

The Turning Point: 1949

The most significant transformation came not in Beijing but in Chongqing—a temporary capital of the Republic of China during the war against Japan.

In September 1949, at the conclusion of the Second Sino-Japanese War, a Political Consultative Conference convened that would reshape Chinese history. This conference approved what became known as the Common Program—serving as the de facto constitution for the next five years—and approved the new national anthem, flag, capital city, and state name.

More importantly, it elected the first government of the People's Republic of China. It was a de facto legislature during those crucial first years of PRC existence—functioning before the NPC officially received constitutional authority in 1954 when China's Constitution formally transferred this function to the National People's Congress.

The Rubber Stamp Debate

External observers have long characterized the NPC as little more than a "rubber stamp" for decisions already made at the highest levels of the Chinese Communist Party. The characterization is partially accurate—the NPC's annual sessions typically vote on legislation that has already been drafted and approved through party channels.

However, this understanding misses nuance. The annual meetings provide an opportunity for officers of state to review past policies and present future plans to the nation—and to do so publicly, in broad daylight, before nearly three thousand representatives from every corner of Chinese society. Whether this constitutes meaningful deliberation or ceremonial ratification depends entirely on how one defines the purpose of a legislature.

The sessions are timed precisely to occur with meetings of the CPPCC—creating what Beijing calls "the Two Sessions" that form the core of China's annual political cycle. When the premier of the State Council delivers his report, when the president of the Supreme People's Court addresses the body, and when the prosecutor general speaks—these aren't merely formalities. They represent moments where China publicly interrogates its own governance.

The Giant's Scale

The numbers remain staggering. With 2,904 members in 2025, the NPC dwarfs every legislative body on Earth—not just in raw size but in scope. It is elected for a term of five years, and each year it gathers in the Great Hall of the People to deliberate laws affecting over a billion citizens.

The Great Hall itself sits on the western edge of Tiananmen Square—precisely where Chairman Mao proclaimed the founding of the People's Republic on that fateful October day in 1949. In those halls, China's future has been debated, shaped, and determined. Whether one views this as meaningful participation or elaborate ceremony depends entirely on perspective.

What cannot be disputed is scale: within this body lies the legislative engine for nearly a fifth of humanity. The NPC meets each spring, passes laws, approves appointments, and oversees the apparatus of state—all while operating under the firm control of the Chinese Communist Party, which determines who may serve and what they may debate.

This is China's supreme organ of state power: vast in number, constrained in function, yet undeniably consequential in shaping the nation's trajectory.
