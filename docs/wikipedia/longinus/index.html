Longinus

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Longinus

In the year 586, in a scriptorium in Edessa, a Syrian monk named Rabulas illuminated a gospel manuscript that would eventually find its home in the Laurentian Library in Florence. Amidst the gold leaf and vibrant pigments depicting the Crucifixion, he painted a soldier holding a spear. Beside this figure, he wrote a name in horizontal Greek letters: ΛΟΓΙΝΟϹ, or Loginos. This single, contemporaneous inscription marks the first known appearance of the name Longinus in the historical record. Before this moment, the soldier who pierced the side of Jesus Christ remained a faceless instrument of divine will, a nameless Roman centurion mentioned only in the canonical Gospel of John. It was not the Bible that gave him an identity, but the apocryphal Gospel of Nicodemus, a text that would weave a tapestry of legend, miracle, and martyrdom around a man who, according to the Gospels, was never named at all.

The story of Longinus is a testament to the human need to personalize the divine. In the Gospel of John, the narrative is stark and theological. After Jesus has died, a soldier thrusts a lance into his side, and immediately blood and water flow out. This act creates the last of the Five Holy Wounds of Christ, a physical testament to the reality of his death and the fullness of his sacrifice. The soldier is unnamed. The centurion present at the cross, who declares, "Truly this man was the Son of God," is also unnamed in the synoptic Gospels, though early Christian tradition eventually merged these two figures into one. This synthesis transformed a Roman executioner into a convert, a man of the sword who became a soldier of Christ. The name Longinus, likely a Latinized cognomen from the gens Cassia, is derived from the Greek word lónchē, meaning spear. It is a linguistic echo of the very instrument of his crime, a name that binds his identity inextricably to the wound he inflicted.

The Evolution of a Legend

The transformation of Longinus from a silent bystander to a central figure of Christian hagiography was neither immediate nor uniform. In the earliest traditions, Longinus was not a saint. In fact, some early accounts viewed his act with profound ambivalence or even condemnation. A sixth- or seventh-century pseudepigraphal text, the Letter of Herod to Pilate, offers a grim alternative to the redemptive arc found in later centuries. This text claims that Longinus suffered a horrific fate for his role in the Crucifixion. He was condemned to a cave where, every night, a lion would emerge to maul him. By dawn, his body would miraculously heal, only to be torn apart again when night fell. This cycle of torture and restoration was said to repeat until the end of time, a purgatorial loop of pain rather than a path to salvation.

It was not until the eighth century that the narrative shifted decisively toward sanctification. The patriarch Germanus of Constantinople, in 715, is credited with introducing the name Longinus into Greek tradition. Before this, the Greeks had no knowledge of the name. As the noted hagiographer Sabine Baring-Gould observed with characteristic skepticism, "The name of Longinus was not known to the Greeks previous to the patriarch Germanus, in 715. It was introduced among the Westerns from the Apocryphal Gospel of Nicodemus. There is no reliable authority for the Acts and martyrdom of this saint." Despite Baring-Gould's warning, the legend grew with a life of its own, absorbing new details and deepening its theological significance.

The most dramatic addition to the Longinus legend emerged after the tenth century: the story of his blindness. While the canonical Gospels mention nothing of the soldier's physical state, later hagiographical tradition, influenced by writers like Petrus Comestor, introduced the idea that Longinus was blind or "dim-sighted" at the moment of the Crucifixion. The Golden Legend, a medieval compilation of saints' lives that became the most widely read religious text after the Bible, elaborated on this theme. It posited that Longinus suffered from an eye ailment caused by illness or age, perhaps a condition that had plagued him for years.

The miracle that followed was instantaneous and visceral. As Longinus thrust the lance into the side of the dying Christ, blood and water gushed forth. Some of this sacred fluid fell directly into Longinus's eyes. In that moment, the blindness that had obscured his vision for so long was cured. The text suggests he saw celestial signs, a revelation of the divine nature of the man he had just wounded. This physical healing became the catalyst for his spiritual conversion. The Roman soldier, once an agent of death, became the first Christian centurion, a man who saw the truth in the blood of the victim. The spear, once a tool of execution, became the instrument of his enlightenment.

The Holy Lance and the Spear of Destiny

The object at the center of Longinus's story, the lance itself, took on a life of its own, transcending its biblical function to become one of the most revered and debated relics in Christendom. In Catholic Christianity, it is known as the lancea or the Holy Lance. In the language of the later Middle Ages and in occult circles, it acquired the ominous and powerful title of the "Spear of Destiny." The reverence for this object was well-established by the sixth century, with reports of its veneration in Jerusalem, though these early accounts did not invoke the name of the centurion or the specific name "Longinus."

The spear's journey through history is a saga of loss, rediscovery, and fragmentation. It is said that the body of Longinus was lost twice before being discovered in Mantua, Italy. There, it was found alongside the Holy Sponge, stained with the blood of Christ. The legend extended Longinus's role further, claiming that he had assisted in cleansing the body of Jesus after it was taken down from the cross, a detail that softened his image from executioner to compassionate servant. The relics of Mantua enjoyed a revived cult in the late 13th century under the patronage of the Bonacolsi, the ruling family of the city.

However, the physical reality of the Holy Lance is as fragmented as the legends surrounding it. The relics were eventually divided and distributed to various locations across Europe. A significant portion found its way to Prague, where it was housed in the St. Peter and Paul Basilica at Vyšehrad. Other fragments appeared in churches throughout Germany and beyond. The most famous fragment, a spearpoint, is said to be conserved in the Basilica of Saint Peter in the Vatican City. This fragment is displayed in a reliquary, a tangible link to the event that changed the course of Western history.

The spear also features prominently in the legends of the Holy Grail. In the Arthurian cycles, the Lance of Longinus is often depicted as a weapon of immense power, capable of bringing either healing or destruction depending on the virtue of its wielder. This duality reflects the dual nature of Longinus himself: a man who caused the ultimate wound but received the ultimate grace. The spear became a symbol of divine authority, a physical manifestation of the power that flows from the side of Christ.

Veneration and Feast Days

Longinus is venerated as a saint in the Roman Catholic Church, the Eastern Orthodox Church, and the Armenian Apostolic Church, though the specifics of his veneration vary by tradition. In the Roman Martyrology, his feast day is celebrated on October 16. The entry is notably restrained, mentioning him "without any indication of martyrdom" in the following terms: "At Jerusalem, commemoration of Saint Longinus, who is venerated as the soldier opening the side of the crucified Lord with a lance." This phrasing reflects the Church's careful navigation of the historical uncertainties surrounding his life. The pre-1969 feast day in the Roman Rite was March 15, a date that has since been shifted to align with other liturgical calendars.

The Eastern Orthodox Church also commemorates him on October 16, maintaining the connection to the date of the event in the liturgical year. In the Armenian Apostolic Church, his feast is observed on October 22, reflecting the distinct liturgical traditions of the Armenian Church. Despite the variations in date, the core of his veneration remains consistent: he is honored as the soldier who pierced the side of Christ and, in doing so, became the first to see the truth of the Resurrection in the flow of blood and water.

Greek sources assert that Longinus suffered martyrdom in Cappadocia, adding a final chapter to his life of service. While the canonical Gospels do not record his death, the tradition of his martyrdom solidified his status as a saint who gave his life for the faith he embraced. The Russian Orthodox Cathedral of St. John the Baptist in Washington, D.C., purports to hold a holy relic, a fragment of bone, of Saint Longinus. This relic serves as a focal point for the faithful in the Americas, connecting the modern believer to the ancient martyr.

Art, Culture, and the Folkloric Longinus

The legacy of Longinus extends far beyond the liturgy, permeating the visual arts, festivals, and folk traditions of the Christian world. One of the most magnificent representations of the saint is the statue by Gian Lorenzo Bernini, one of the four colossal figures in the niches beneath the dome of Saint Peter's Basilica in Vatican City. Bernini's Longinus is a masterpiece of Baroque sculpture, capturing the moment of revelation. The figure is depicted with the spear in hand, his face turned upward in a moment of spiritual ecstasy, the light of the divine illuminating his features. The statue stands as a testament to the enduring power of the Longinus legend to inspire awe and devotion.

In the Philippines, the legend of Longinus is brought to life in the Moriones Festival, held during Holy Week on the island of Marinduque. Participants dress as Roman soldiers, wearing colorful masks and armor, reenacting the story of the Crucifixion and the conversion of the centurion. The festival is a vibrant celebration of faith, blending religious devotion with cultural expression, and keeping the story of Longinus alive in the hearts of the Filipino people.

The veneration of Longinus also found a unique expression in Brazil. There, he is known as São Longuinho, a diminutive form of his name that reflects his close connection to the common people. In Brazilian folklore, Saint Longinus is attributed the power of finding missing objects. This association stems from a folk tale that explains his short stature. According to the story, when Longinus was a soldier in Rome, he was of small height, which allowed him to see under tables in crowded parties where others could not. He would find and return objects that had been dropped on the ground, earning him a reputation as a helper in times of loss.

The ritual to summon his aid is a charming blend of faith and playfulness. The chant goes: "São Longuinho, São Longuinho, se eu achar [missing object], dou três pulinhos!" which translates to "O Saint Longinus, Saint Longinus, if I find [missing object], I'll hop three times!" The three hops are said to be a deference to an alleged limp of the saint or a plea to the Holy Trinity. This folk tradition transforms the solemn figure of the centurion into a patron of lost keys, missing rings, and misplaced documents, bridging the gap between the divine and the mundane.

The spiritual legacy of Longinus even extends into the realm of spiritualism. Chico Xavier, a renowned Brazilian medium, wrote the book Brasil, Coração do Mundo, Pátria do Evangelho (Brazil, Heart of the World, Homeland of the Gospel) under psychographic authorship attributed to the spirit of Humberto de Campos. In this text, Saint Longinus is claimed to have been reincarnated as Pedro II, the last Emperor of Brazil. This narrative connects the ancient saint to the history of a nation, suggesting a continuous thread of spiritual mission from the days of the Crucifixion to the modern era.

The Longinus Cross and Regional Devotions

In the Black Forest region of Germany, a special form of the Arma Christi cross, known as the Longinuskreuz (Longinus Cross), is found. This cross typically depicts the instruments of the Passion, with the spear prominently featured. The Longinuskreuz serves as a reminder of the centrality of Longinus's act in the theology of the Passion. These crosses, though primarily localized to South Germany, occasionally appear in other regions, marking the spread of his veneration across the continent.

In Croatia, hagiographical fragments from the 11th to 13th centuries found in Dubrovnik indicate that the veneration of Saint Longinus was well-established in the Middle Ages. These fragments serve as historical evidence of the saint's reach, extending his influence from the Mediterranean to the Adriatic. In Veli Lošinj, a 17th-century altarpiece depicting Saint Longinus and Saint Gaudentius hangs in the parish church of St. Anthony the Great, further illustrating the enduring artistic and devotional interest in the saint.

The story of Longinus is also preserved in the fresco of the Nea Moni Church in Chios, Greece, where he is depicted alongside Christ on the Cross, the three Marys, and John the Evangelist. In Bom Jesus do Monte, Portugal, a statue of Saint Longinus stands as a testament to the global reach of his cult. The fresco in the Basilica of St. Peter and St. Paul in Vyšehrad (Prague) and the painting The Crucifixion by Jan Provoost in the Groeningmuseum of Bruges all contribute to a vast visual archive of the saint's life and legend.

The Unnamed Becoming Named

The journey of Longinus from an anonymous soldier to a named saint is a microcosm of the development of Christian tradition itself. It reflects the human desire to give faces to the figures of the Bible, to provide names to the nameless, and to find personal connection in the grand narrative of salvation. The name Longinus, first written by Rabulas in 586, has become a symbol of redemption. It represents the idea that no act is too great, and no sinner too lost, to be transformed by the grace of Christ.

The legend of Longinus is not merely a collection of facts and dates; it is a living story that continues to evolve. From the Letter of Herod to Pilate to the modern chants of Brazilian children, the story of the centurion who pierced the side of Jesus has taken on countless forms. It is a story of blindness and sight, of violence and healing, of death and resurrection. It is a story that reminds us that the divine is often found in the most unexpected places, even in the hands of a Roman soldier.

The facts of his life may be shrouded in the mists of history, with no reliable authority for his martyrdom and no name in the canonical Gospels. Yet, the power of the legend remains undeniable. Longinus stands as a testament to the transformative power of faith. He is the soldier who saw the blood of Christ and saw the truth. He is the man who was blind and then saw. He is the saint who, despite his violent beginnings, became a symbol of hope and redemption for millions of believers around the world.

In the end, the story of Longinus is about the human capacity for change. It is a reminder that even the most unlikely figures can become vessels of grace. The name Longinus, once just a label on a spear, has become a beacon of light for those who seek the truth. Whether in the grand basilicas of Rome, the vibrant festivals of the Philippines, or the quiet prayers of a Brazilian home, the story of Saint Longinus continues to resonate, a timeless tale of a soldier who found his way to the heart of God.

The relic of the Holy Lance, the statue by Bernini, the frescoes of Chios, and the chants of São Longuinho are all threads in the same tapestry. They weave together the history, the art, and the faith of the Christian world, creating a rich and complex portrait of a man who was once unknown but is now known to all. The story of Longinus is a story of the sublime, of the moment when the infinite touches the finite, and the ordinary becomes extraordinary. It is a story that, like the blood and water that flowed from the side of Christ, continues to flow through the ages, nourishing the faith of believers and inspiring the imagination of all who hear it.
