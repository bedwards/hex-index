Blackwell (microarchitecture)

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Blackwell (microarchitecture)

In March 2024, amidst the roar of a keynote that would define the next decade of computing, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang declared that the company had "created a processor for the generative AI era." The processor in question was not a chip, but an architecture named Blackwell. This wasn't merely an incremental update to the silicon that had fueled the AI boom of 2023; it was a fundamental reimagining of how data moves, how math is calculated, and how the digital world scales. Named after David Blackwell, the first African American scholar inducted into the National Academy of Sciences, the architecture carries the weight of a legacy in game theory, probability, and information theory—fields that are the very bedrock of the transformer models driving the current AI revolution. The name itself was a quiet leak in 2022, confirmed in October 2023, and fully unveiled at the Graphics Technology Conference (GTC) on March 18, 2024. It signaled a shift from the two-year cadence that had governed the semiconductor industry for decades to a frantic, year-over-year sprint, with the B100 and B200 accelerators leading the charge.

The context for Blackwell's arrival was a market in a state of near-panic. Throughout 2023, the demand for Nvidia's Hopper architecture, specifically the H100 accelerator, was so insatiable that lead times for servers stretched from 36 to 52 weeks. Nvidia sold half a million H100 units in the third quarter of 2023 alone, a figure that propelled the company's market capitalization past the $2 trillion mark, leaving it trailing only Microsoft and Apple in the global hierarchy of value. The Hopper architecture had solved the immediate problem of training massive models, but the industry was already looking over the horizon. The "Hopper-Next" roadmap item was a placeholder for a solution that needed to be orders of magnitude more efficient. Blackwell was that solution, designed to bridge the gap between the current limitations of silicon physics and the insatiable hunger for generative AI.

The Physics of the Impossible

To understand the magnitude of Blackwell, one must first understand the wall it hit. In semiconductor fabrication, there is a physical constraint known as the reticle limit. This is the maximum size of a pattern that a lithography machine can etch onto a silicon wafer in a single exposure. It is a hard ceiling. Previous generations of Nvidia chips, like the GH100 based on Hopper, had nearly grazed this ceiling with a die size of 814 square millimeters. The successor, the GB100 die used in Blackwell, pushed even harder, reaching 1,040 billion transistors and hitting the absolute limit of what can be printed as a single piece of silicon.

Nvidia could not simply make the die bigger; the machines wouldn't allow it. They could not simply move to a smaller manufacturing process node to pack more transistors in, because the industry was not yet ready for a major node advancement that would yield significant efficiency gains on its own. The 4NP process node used for datacenter Blackwell chips was an enhancement of the 4N node used for Hopper and Ada Lovelace, likely adding metal layers to the standard TSMC N4P technology to squeeze out extra performance. But the real genius of Blackwell lay not in the lithography, but in the packaging.

Nvidia decided to break the monolithic rule. Instead of trying to print a die larger than the reticle limit, they printed two GB100 dies and fused them together. This dual-die package totals 208 billion transistors, a staggering increase over the 80 billion found in the Hopper GH100. These two massive chips are placed on top of a silicon interposer, a specialized layer of silicon produced using TSMC's CoWoS-L 2.5D packaging technique. This interposer acts as a high-speed highway, connecting the two dies with a link so fast it defies intuition: the NV-High Bandwidth Interface (NV-HBI). Running at 10 terabytes per second, NV-HBI is based on the NVLink 7 protocol, allowing the two separate dies to function as a single, massive monolithic piece of silicon with full cache coherency. The result is a system where the memory and processing power are effectively unified, eliminating the bottlenecks that usually plague multi-chip designs.

Jensen Huang revealed in an interview with CNBC that Nvidia spent approximately $10 billion in research and development specifically for this NV-HBI interconnect. It was a bet-the-company investment in a proprietary system to bypass the limitations of the silicon itself. Not everyone was convinced by the price tag. Jim Keller, a veteran semiconductor engineer known for his work on AMD's Zen architecture, criticized the expenditure, claiming that the same outcome could be achieved for $1 billion by utilizing Ultra Ethernet rather than Nvidia's proprietary NVLink system. Whether one views it as a brilliant engineering feat or an excessive display of dominance, the NV-HBI is the nervous system that makes Blackwell possible, allowing the architecture to scale in ways that single-die designs never could.

The Architecture of Efficiency

The transition to Blackwell was not just about raw power; it was about efficiency in the face of the energy crisis that threatens to cap AI growth. The architecture introduced fifth-generation Tensor Cores, specifically designed to handle the floating-point calculations required by generative AI. In the datacenter, this meant native support for sub-8-bit data types, a critical evolution for running models at lower precisions without sacrificing accuracy. Blackwell added support for new Open Compute Project (OCP) community-defined formats, specifically MXFP6 and MXFP4 microscaling formats.

The previous Hopper architecture had introduced the Transformer Engine, a software layer that facilitated the quantization of higher-precision models (like FP32) down to lower precisions to increase throughput. Blackwell's second-generation Transformer Engine takes this further. By using 4-bit data (FP4), the architecture achieves a massive leap in efficiency and throughput for model inference. Nvidia claims that the dual-GPU GB200 superchip can deliver 20 petaflops of FP4 compute, a figure that excludes the additional gains from sparsity. This is not just a number; it represents the difference between a model that takes days to train and one that takes hours, or between an AI that can run in real-time and one that requires a massive server farm to generate a single sentence.

The efficiency gains are driven by the underlying architectural changes, as the process node itself offered only incremental improvements. The GB100 die is at the reticle limit, so every gain in performance per watt had to come from smarter design. The architecture introduced an AI Management Processor (AMP), a dedicated scheduler chip built on a RISC-V core. This is a significant departure from previous generations, where the CPU was heavily burdened with scheduling tasks for the GPU. The AMP offloads this scheduling, allowing the GPU to control its own resources with greater autonomy. This is utilized through Windows Hardware-Accelerated GPU Scheduling (HAGS) in consumer applications, but in the datacenter, it allows for a level of orchestration that is essential for managing the complex workloads of trillion-parameter models.

The impact of these changes was immediate and felt across the industry. By November 2024, Morgan Stanley reported that the "entire 2025 production" of Blackwell silicon was already sold out. The demand was so high that even before the chips were fully deployed, the supply chain was locked up for the next year. The architecture had become the bottleneck, not the lack of it.

The Consumer Divide and the Gaming Gap

While the datacenter received the lion's share of the attention, Blackwell was also designed with a split personality. It is an architecture built for both the massive scale of the cloud and the high-performance needs of gamers and workstation users. On the consumer side, the largest die, the GB202, measures 750 square millimeters, making it 20% larger than the AD102 die found in the Ada Lovelace architecture. This is the largest consumer die Nvidia has designed since the TU102 die in 2018, based on the Turing microarchitecture.

The GB202 contains 24,576 CUDA cores, a 28.5% increase over the 18,432 cores in its predecessor. However, the gap between the flagship GB202 and the mid-tier GB203 has widened significantly compared to previous generations. In the past, the difference between tiers was often marginal, but in Blackwell, the flagship features more than double the number of CUDA cores than the GB203. This strategy creates a distinct tiering, separating the professional and enthusiast market from the mainstream in a way that was not seen with the AD102 and AD103.

For gamers, Blackwell introduces the fourth generation of ray tracing cores. These are not just faster; they are smarter. The new Triangle Cluster Intersection Engine is designed for "Mega Geometry," allowing for the rendering of incredibly complex scenes with millions of objects. The Linear Swept Spheres feature accelerates ray tracing for finer details, such as hair and fur, which have historically been a nightmare for real-time rendering. These improvements are critical for the next generation of gaming, where photorealism is no longer a luxury but an expectation.

Yet, the consumer side of Blackwell was notably absent from the GTC 2024 keynote. Jensen Huang's presentation focused entirely on the datacenter, the enterprise, and the future of AI. The gaming market, while a massive revenue driver for Nvidia, was not the primary narrative for Blackwell's launch. This was a strategic choice. The company's dominance in the AI sector had become the primary story, and the consumer market was expected to follow the technological lead of the datacenter. The announcement at CES 2025 in January would later fill in the gaps, revealing that the foundation models for Blackwell would include contributions from Black Forest Labs (Flux), Meta AI, Mistral AI, and Stability AI, further cementing the architecture's role in the generative AI ecosystem.

The Roadmap and the Future

The release of Blackwell marked a fundamental shift in Nvidia's release strategy. The company moved from a two-year cadence for datacenter products to a yearly release schedule, targeting both x86 and ARM systems. This acceleration was driven by the sheer pace of innovation in AI. The Hopper architecture, announced in March 2022, had dominated 2023, but the industry was already moving on. The Blackwell roadmap, updated in October 2023, included references to the B100 and B40 accelerators, signaling that the successor to Hopper was not a distant future concept but an imminent reality.

The B100 and B200 accelerators are the core of the datacenter push, but the ecosystem extends far beyond the chip itself. The eight-GPU HGX B200 board and the 72-GPU NVL72 rack-scale system represent the physical manifestation of Blackwell's power. These are not just servers; they are supercomputers in a rack, designed to handle the training of the world's largest models. The endorsements from the CEOs of Google, Meta, Microsoft, OpenAI, and Oracle during the GTC keynote were a testament to the architecture's importance. These companies, the very architects of the AI revolution, had staked their futures on Blackwell.

However, the road to Blackwell was not without its challenges. In October 2024, reports emerged of a design flaw in the architecture that required a fix in collaboration with TSMC. Jensen Huang later clarified that the flaw was "functional" and had caused yields to be low. The fact that such a significant issue was identified and resolved before mass production is a testament to the rigorous testing and the deep collaboration between Nvidia and its manufacturing partner. The flaw did not derail the launch; it was a hurdle that was cleared, and by November 2024, the production was fully sold out.

The naming of the architecture after David Blackwell adds a layer of historical significance to the technical achievement. Blackwell was a pioneer in fields that are now central to the operation of AI: game theory, probability theory, information theory, and statistics. His work laid the mathematical groundwork for the algorithms that power the transformers and generative models that Blackwell is designed to accelerate. By naming the architecture after him, Nvidia acknowledged the intellectual lineage of the technology they were building. It was a reminder that the silicon in the datacenter is the physical embodiment of decades of mathematical discovery.

The Legacy of Silicon

The story of Blackwell is a story of limits and the human drive to break them. It is a narrative of hitting the reticle limit and finding a way around it through 2.5D packaging. It is a story of the energy crisis and the need for efficiency through sub-8-bit precision and dedicated schedulers. It is a story of market dominance, where a single architecture can dictate the pace of the entire industry.

From the initial leak in 2022 to the official announcement in 2024, and the subsequent rush to deploy in 2025, Blackwell has become the cornerstone of the generative AI era. It is a processor that was built for a specific moment in history, a moment where the demand for compute outstripped the supply of silicon, and where the only way forward was to reinvent the way chips are built. The $10 billion spent on NV-HBI, the 208 billion transistors in a dual-die package, and the 20 petaflops of FP4 compute are not just specifications; they are the metrics of a new age.

As the industry looks toward the future, the lessons of Blackwell will remain relevant. The move to yearly release cycles, the integration of ARM-based Grace CPUs, and the focus on efficiency over raw scale are trends that will define the next decade of computing. The architecture has set a new standard, one that requires not just better transistors, but better systems, better packaging, and better algorithms.

The Blackwell architecture is more than a product; it is a statement. It declares that the era of incremental improvement is over, and the era of systemic innovation has begun. As the 2025 production runs out and the demand for 2026 and beyond begins to form, the shadow of Blackwell will loom large over the semiconductor industry. It is the architecture that saved the AI boom from its own success, providing the capacity, efficiency, and power needed to sustain the next wave of innovation. And in doing so, it has etched its name, alongside David Blackwell, into the history of technology.

The gap between the GB202 and the rest of the consumer line, the specialized support for hair and fur in ray tracing, and the dedicated AI management processors all point to a future where the line between datacenter and consumer is increasingly blurred. The technology that trains the models that power the chatbots and image generators is the same technology that will render the next generation of games and simulations. Blackwell is the bridge between these worlds, a unified architecture that serves the needs of the enterprise and the enthusiast with equal vigor.

In the end, the story of Blackwell is a story of ambition. It is the story of a company that looked at the limits of physics and decided to build a new set of rules. It is the story of a market that was willing to pay any price for the future, and a company that was willing to spend $10 billion to deliver it. As we move further into the 2020s, the impact of Blackwell will be felt in every corner of the digital world, from the data centers that power the cloud to the desktops that power the next generation of creativity. It is a legacy that will be measured not just in transistors, but in the possibilities it unlocks.
