Gadsden Mall

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Gadsden Mall

On July 31, 1974, the doors to the Gadsden Mall swung open for the first time, marking a seismic shift in the commercial heartbeat of Etowah County, Alabama. It was not merely the opening of a new building; it was the formal migration of a city's economic soul from the historic, pedestrian-scale downtown to the automobile-centric expanse of the southern flats. Located at the strategic interchange of Interstate 759 and U.S. Route 411, the mall rose from what was historically known as the flats near Barrel Springs, a transformation that would take decades to fully play out. Today, the 502,591-square-foot structure stands as a testament to the cyclical nature of American retail, a place where anchors have fallen and risen, where the ghosts of a senatorial scandal linger in the public imagination, and where the concrete reality of a community's identity is constantly being renegotiated.

To understand the Gadsden Mall, one must first understand the landscape it displaced. Before the first shovel broke ground in September 1972, the area was defined by its geography rather than its commerce. The site was the "flats," a low-lying area near Barrel Springs, historically distinct from the bustling, albeit aging, commercial core of downtown Gadsden. The decision to build there was a calculated bet on the future of American consumption: the future was not in walking, but in driving. Developed by the Colonial Properties Corporation, a name that would eventually become synonymous with the mall itself, the project represented a massive infusion of capital into a region that had long relied on its downtown district for its retail lifeblood. Construction began in earnest in March 1973, a period of intense activity that promised to modernize the city's infrastructure and offer a new kind of social gathering space.

When the mall officially opened on July 31, 1974, it was the first of its kind in Etowah County. The opening was a moment of triumph for the developers and a moment of existential crisis for the downtown merchants. The mall was anchored by two giants: Sears and Belk Hudson. These were not small, local boutiques; they were national powerhouses with deep roots in the local community. Their decision to relocate from their long-standing downtown locations to the new mall signaled a profound change. As the anchors moved, they dragged the ecosystem of smaller merchants with them. Downtown Gadsden, once the undisputed center of gravity, began a slow, decades-long decline as the population and commerce migrated south to the air-conditioned, parking-lot-rich environment of the mall.

The early years of the Gadsden Mall were defined by this dominance of the anchor tenants. It was a place of relative stability, a regional hub that drew shoppers from across the county and beyond. The name "Gadsden Mall" was not just a label; it was a brand that carried the weight of the city's progress. However, the retail landscape is notoriously volatile, and the mall would not be immune to the forces of change. In 1991, JCPenney arrived, adding a third major anchor to the mix. For a decade, the mall operated with this robust trio of department stores, serving as the primary destination for families, teenagers, and shoppers seeking the latest trends. But the retail world was beginning to shift beneath its feet.

The first major crack in the foundation appeared in 2001, when JCPenney closed its doors at the mall. This was not an isolated incident but a precursor to a broader trend of consolidation and contraction that would plague the department store model for the next two decades. The departure of a major anchor is always a blow to a shopping center, as it reduces the foot traffic that sustains the smaller, inline stores. Yet, the mall was resilient. In 2008, JCPenney returned. This was not a simple reopening; it was part of a significant remodeling effort that created a new anchor location. The return of the department store suggested a confidence in the mall's future, a belief that the property could adapt to the changing times. The name of the mall was also changed during this era, officially becoming "The Colonial Mall," a nod to its developer, Colonial Properties. This rebranding was an attempt to refresh the property's image, to distance it from the generic label of "Gadsden Mall" and align it with the developer's portfolio.

But the tide was turning, and the momentum was irreversible. The year 2017 would prove to be a watershed moment for the mall, a year that would strip away the illusion of permanence that had surrounded it for forty-three years. On March 17, 2017, the announcement came: JCPenney would be closing its store. It was part of a massive, nationwide plan to shutter 138 stores. The closure was not a failure of the local management or the specific location; it was a symptom of a collapsing business model. The rise of e-commerce, the changing habits of consumers, and the financial struggles of traditional department stores had converged to make the JCPenney at the Colonial Mall untenable. The store closed its doors on July 31, 2017, exactly forty-three years after the mall had first opened. The date was a cruel symmetry, marking the end of an era that had begun with such fanfare.

The loss of JCPenney left a gaping hole in the mall's layout, a physical void that represented the erosion of the mall's economic viability. But the damage was far from done. Just over a year later, on October 15, 2018, Sears followed suit. The announcement that the historic anchor would close was a devastating blow. Sears had been a pillar of the mall since its inception, a symbol of the American middle class's purchasing power. Its closure was part of a plan to shutter 142 stores nationwide, a final gasp for a company that had once defined the concept of the department store. With Sears gone, Belk remained as the sole anchor. A mall with only one anchor is a precarious entity; it lacks the gravitational pull to draw in the masses. The Colonial Mall was no longer a destination; it was a remnant.

Yet, the story of the Gadsden Mall did not end with the hollowing out of its anchors. In a twist that defied the narrative of total decline, the mall began a slow process of reinvention. On January 24, 2024, the former Sears location, a massive, cavernous space that had sat empty for years, found new life. The supermarket chain Food City opened its doors, transforming a department store into a grocery store. This was a significant shift in the mall's DNA. It signaled a move away from the purely discretionary shopping model toward a necessity-based model. Food City, along with the existing anchors of Belk and Martin's Family Clothing, formed a new, more modest triad. The mall was no longer trying to be everything to everyone; it was becoming a community hub for daily needs and essential goods. The transformation of the Sears space into a supermarket was a pragmatic response to the reality of the modern retail landscape, where convenience and necessity often trump luxury and leisure.

While the physical transformation of the mall has been a story of adaptation and survival, the social history of the Gadsden Mall has been marked by a different kind of turbulence. In November 2017, the mall became the center of a national controversy that had nothing to do with retail and everything to do with politics and scandal. The subject was Roy Moore, a U.S. Senate candidate who had once frequented the mall in the early 1980s. During that time, Moore was a young district attorney, and his behavior at the mall became the subject of intense scrutiny and accusation. Reports emerged that Moore had approached teenage females for dates, engaging in conduct that many locals found deeply inappropriate and disturbing.

The allegations regarding Moore's behavior at the mall were not merely gossip; they were part of a broader pattern of conduct that would eventually derail his Senate campaign. The narrative that Moore had been "banned" from the mall took hold in the public consciousness. Locals claimed that the mall management had formally barred him from the premises due to his interactions with minors. This claim became a focal point of the media coverage surrounding Moore's candidacy, adding a layer of moral judgment to the political discourse. The story of the "banned" candidate turned the Gadsden Mall into a stage for a national drama, a place where the private conduct of a public figure was scrutinized against the backdrop of a suburban shopping center.

However, the reality of the situation was more nuanced than the headlines suggested. Despite the widespread belief and the local anecdotes, no formal ban has ever been proven. There is no official record, no signed document, no public statement from the mall management confirming that Roy Moore was ever formally banned. The story of the ban appears to be a piece of local lore, a narrative that gained traction because it fit the broader narrative of Moore's unfitness for office. This distinction is crucial. It highlights the power of rumor and the way in which public perception can be shaped by stories that, while compelling, may not be grounded in verifiable fact. The Gadsden Mall, in this instance, became a symbol of the line between public memory and documented reality.

The Roy Moore scandal serves as a poignant reminder of the mall's role as a social space. It is not just a collection of stores; it is a community gathering place where the lives of residents intersect. The allegations against Moore were not just about a man's behavior; they were about the safety and dignity of the teenage girls who visited the mall. The fact that these incidents occurred in a public, family-oriented space like the Gadsden Mall added a layer of tragedy to the story. It was a violation of the trust that communities place in their public spaces. The mall was supposed to be a safe haven, a place where families could shop and socialize without fear. The allegations against Moore challenged that fundamental assumption.

The aftermath of the scandal saw the mall continue its slow evolution. The controversy, while intense, did not derail the mall's operations. The business of shopping continued, even as the political landscape shifted around it. The mall's ability to move forward despite the scandal speaks to its resilience. It is a place that has seen the rise and fall of anchors, the changing of names, and the ebb and flow of local politics. Through it all, the Gadsden Mall has remained a constant presence in the lives of Gadsden residents.

The current state of the Gadsden Mall is a reflection of the broader challenges facing the American shopping mall. The era of the massive, department-store-dominated mall is over. The future lies in adaptation, in finding new uses for old spaces, and in serving the immediate needs of the community. The opening of Food City in the former Sears space is a perfect example of this evolution. It is a practical solution to a practical problem. The mall is no longer trying to compete with the online giants or the suburban lifestyle centers. It is embracing its role as a local resource, a place where residents can buy groceries, clothes, and essentials.

The history of the Gadsden Mall is a microcosm of the American experience. It is a story of growth, of decline, of adaptation, and of resilience. It is a story of how a city changes, how its economy evolves, and how its people navigate the shifting tides of history. From the flats near Barrel Springs to the bustling commercial hub of the 1970s, from the decline of the department store to the rise of the supermarket, the mall has been a witness to it all. It has been a place of celebration and a place of controversy, a place of progress and a place of loss.

As we look at the Gadsden Mall today, we see a building that is far more than the sum of its parts. It is a repository of memories, a symbol of a changing era, and a testament to the enduring spirit of a community. The anchors may have changed, the name may have been altered, and the scandals may have faded, but the mall remains. It stands on U.S. Route 411, a landmark in the southern section of Gadsden, reminding us that while the world changes, some things endure. The story of the Gadsden Mall is not finished. It is a story that is still being written, one by the residents who shop there, one by the businesses that call it home, and one by the history that has shaped it.

The journey of the Gadsden Mall from its inception in 1974 to its current form in 2026 is a testament to the complexity of American commerce and culture. It is a story that is rich with detail, filled with the names of people, the dates of events, and the numbers of square feet. But it is also a story that is deeply human. It is about the hopes and dreams of a community, the fears and anxieties of a changing world, and the resilience that allows us to move forward. The Gadsden Mall is not just a place to shop; it is a place to remember, to reflect, and to look toward the future. And as the sun sets over the parking lot on Rainbow Drive, the mall stands as a silent sentinel, watching over the city of Gadsden, waiting for the next chapter to begin.
