Kids (film)

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Kids (film)

In the summer of 1994, a nineteen-year-old writer named Harmony Korine sat down to pen a screenplay that would dismantle the glossy, sanitized image of American adolescence. He did not consult focus groups, nor did he seek the approval of Hollywood gatekeepers. He wrote Kids in a fever state, channeling the raw, unfiltered energy of the New York City streets he knew too well. The result was a film that would premiere in 1995 to a storm of controversy, an NC-17 rating that was ultimately ignored by its distributors, and a box office performance that defied all logic. It grossed $20.4 million on a budget of merely $1.5 million, a figure that turned a small independent project into a cultural phenomenon. This was not a movie about teenagers; it was a movie about the absence of adults, a visceral chronicle of a single day in the lives of a group of libertines who treated sex, drugs, and violence as casual pastimes.

Larry Clark, the film's director, had spent the previous two decades photographing the underbelly of youth culture. His camera had captured the grit of skate parks and the intimacy of drug-fueled parties with a detached, almost anthropological eye. When he approached the Kids project, his ambition was monumental: he wanted to create the "Great American Teenage Movie," a film with the weight and significance of the Great American Novel. To achieve this, Clark and Korine abandoned the traditional Hollywood casting couch. They did not want polished actors who could recite lines with perfect diction. They wanted the real thing. They scoured the streets of New York City, finding their leads among the very subjects Clark had photographed for years. Leo Fitzpatrick, a scrawny sixteen-year-old skateboarder, was cast as Telly after Clark witnessed him cursing in frustration while failing to land a skate trick. Justin Pierce, a charismatic and chaotic presence, became Casper. Chloë Sevigny, who had initially been cast in a minor role, was promoted to the lead after the original actress, Mia Kirshner, was deemed incompatible with the first-time actors. The cast was a mosaic of newcomers, including Rosario Dawson, who would go on to become a major star, but here they were simply playing versions of themselves, or perhaps, darker reflections of the city they inhabited.

The narrative structure of Kids is deceptively simple, unfolding over the course of a single day, yet it operates with the relentless momentum of a train heading toward a collision. The story begins with Telly, a sixteen-year-old with a predatory fixation on virginity. He is seen making out with a twelve-year-old girl, Sarah Henderson, in a bedroom devoid of adult supervision. The scene is not romanticized; it is clinical, almost documentary in its presentation. Telly, scrawny and unremarkable, talks the girl into sex, revealing his specific fetish for taking virginity. He does this not out of love or attraction, but as a point of conquest, a way to validate his own existence in a world that offers him little else. Afterward, he meets his best friend, Casper, and they dissect the encounter with a chilling nonchalance. They are not monsters in the traditional sense; they are children who have been abandoned by the structures of society, left to navigate a moral vacuum where their impulses are the only law.

The film's opening sequence sets the tone for what follows. Telly and Casper enter a local store, where Casper shoplifts a 40-ounce bottle of malt liquor and two peaches from a Korean fruit vendor. This act of petty theft is performed with such casual ease that it feels mundane, a routine part of their day. They are looking for drugs, food, and a place to hang out, eventually gravitating toward the apartment of a friend named Paul, a person they claim to dislike but tolerate because he is available. As they sit, watching skateboarding videos on television, Casper inhales whippets, the nitrous oxide canister hissing in the silence of the room. The atmosphere is heavy with a sense of aimlessness. The boys boast about their sexual prowess, their conversations laced with a dangerous ignorance regarding venereal diseases. One boy casually dismisses the existence of HIV and AIDS, a statement that serves as the film's ticking time bomb. This is the central tension of Kids: the disconnect between the characters' reckless behavior and their catastrophic lack of knowledge about the consequences.

"Lord of the Flies with skateboards, nitrous oxide and hip-hop... There is no thunderous moral reckoning, only observational detachment."

This description by Ben Detrick of The New York Times captures the essence of the film perfectly. There is no narrator to guide the viewer, no moralizing voice to condemn the actions on screen. The camera simply observes, capturing the boys and girls in their natural habitat. While the boys engage in their posturing and drug use, a parallel narrative unfolds among a group of girls. Their conversations about sex stand in stark contrast to the boys' bravado. They discuss the significance of the individuals to whom they lost their virginity, the emotional weight of their experiences. Yet, even here, the innocence is fraying. Two of the girls, Ruby and Jennie, reveal that they have recently been tested for STDs. Ruby tests negative, a relief that is short-lived given her multiple encounters. Jennie, however, tests positive for HIV. The revelation is delivered with a quiet devastation that cuts through the film's chaotic energy. She tells the nurse that she has had sex only once, with Telly. The connection is immediate and terrifying. Jennie, distraught, realizes that the boy she trusted, the boy she thought was special, is the vector of a disease that could destroy her life. Her desperate search for Telly to warn him—and to warn others he might infect—becomes the emotional core of the film's second half.

As the day wears on, the characters drift further into a world of escalating violence and depravity. Telly and Casper head to Telly's house to steal money from his mother, a transaction that happens with the ease of breathing. They purchase marijuana from a Rastafarian dealer, an encounter that is fleeting and transactional. The group then gathers, and in a moment that highlights the toxic masculinity permeating their world, they taunt a gay couple passing by on the street. The tension escalates quickly when Casper, riding his skateboard, carelessly bumps into a man. The man, angry and threatened, pushes Casper. The response is immediate and disproportionate. Casper's friend Harold strikes the man in the back of the head with a skateboard, knocking him to the ground. What follows is a brutal beating. Several other skaters join in, their aggression fueled by the adrenaline of the moment and the drugs in their systems. The man is beaten until he is unconscious, the final blow delivered by Casper himself. This scene is not a moment of heroism or justice; it is a display of collective violence, a mob mentality that consumes the individual. The man is left lying on the pavement, a casualty of a day that has already spiraled out of control.

Jennie's journey continues as she hails a cab, seeking solace and answers. The taxi driver, a stranger who offers a moment of paternal advice on how to be happy, provides a brief respite before the chaos resumes. Meanwhile, Telly and his friends pick up Darcy, a thirteen-year-old girl and the virginal younger sister of an acquaintance. Telly's interest in her is immediate and predatory. He wants to have sex with her, driven by his obsession with virginity. Darcy shows some restraint, a flicker of innocence in a world that seems intent on crushing it. The group sneaks into a pool, swimming with friends in an unsupervised setting where Harold showboats his genitals, a grotesque display of power and vulnerability. The pool scene is a microcosm of the film's themes: a space of supposed freedom that is actually a breeding ground for danger and exploitation.

The climax of the film takes place at an unsupervised party at the house of a friend named Steven. The atmosphere is thick with drugs, alcohol, and sexual tension. Jennie arrives at the house, her mind already fogged by a pill that a boy named Fidget had shoved into her mouth in a backroom at a club. She drifts in and out of consciousness, a passive observer in a world that is actively consuming her. She learns that Telly is at Steven's house. The journey to the house is a blur of confusion and fear. When she finally arrives, she finds Casper, drunk and singing nonsense, who tells her where Telly is. What she finds is the culmination of the film's tragic arc. She witnesses Telly having sex with Darcy, the young girl she had hoped to protect. Jennie is visibly numb, paralyzed by the realization of what Telly has done. She does not intervene. She cannot. In that moment of inaction, Telly exposes Darcy to HIV, perpetuating the cycle of disease and destruction that has already claimed Jennie. Jennie cries, her body and spirit broken, and passes out among the other partygoers, lost in a sea of indifference.

The film's ending is as bleak as its beginning. In the morning, the partygoers begin to stir. A still inebriated Casper, unaware of the gravity of his actions, begins to rape an also inebriated and unconscious Jennie. The irony is suffocating: in his attempt to take what he wants, he exposes himself to the very virus that Jennie carries. The cycle completes itself. A montage of homeless people and drug users in New York City plays out, a visual reminder of the world that exists just outside the bubble of these teenagers. The final voiceover comes from Telly, who reflects on his life with a disturbing clarity. He says that sex is the only worthwhile thing in his life. It is a statement that encapsulates the emptiness of his existence, a life devoid of purpose, love, or future. As the sun rises, a naked Casper awakes, his face a mask of confusion and regret. "Jesus Christ, what happened?" he asks. The question hangs in the air, unanswered. The film offers no resolution, no redemption, no moral lesson. It simply ends, leaving the viewer to grapple with the horror of what they have witnessed.

The production of Kids was as unconventional as the film itself. Harmony Korine wrote the screenplay in 1992 at the age of 19, drawing on his own experiences and the stories of the kids he knew. Principal photography took place during the summer of 1994, with the cast and crew moving through the streets of New York, capturing the city in its rawest form. The film was shot in a quasi-documentary style, blurring the line between fiction and reality. Clark insisted on casting actual street kids, people who had lived the lives they were portraying. This authenticity is what gives the film its power, its ability to shock and disturb. It is not a performance; it is a reflection of a reality that many would prefer to ignore.

The journey to get the film made was fraught with obstacles. Gus Van Sant was initially attached as a producer but left the project after failing to generate sufficient interest. Under the leadership of new producer Cary Woods, the film found independent funding. Harvey Weinstein of Miramax Films, wary of the reaction from his parent company, The Walt Disney Company, initially declined to get involved. However, after viewing the final cut, Miramax paid $3.5 million to buy the worldwide distribution rights. This decision would prove to be a gamble, as the film's NC-17 rating threatened to limit its theatrical release. The MPAA's strict censorship standards meant that the film could not be shown in many theaters, and the Weinstein brothers were forced to buy back the film from Disney and create a separate distribution company, Shining Excalibur Films, to release it. Despite these challenges, the film found an audience. It became a cult classic, a film that is studied, debated, and revered for its unflinching look at the darker side of adolescence.

The legacy of Kids extends far beyond its box office numbers. It changed the landscape of independent cinema, proving that films could be made on a shoestring budget with non-professional actors and still achieve massive critical and commercial success. It paved the way for a new generation of filmmakers who were unafraid to tackle difficult subjects and push the boundaries of what was acceptable on screen. The film's impact on pop culture is undeniable, influencing everything from music videos to fashion to the way teenagers are portrayed in media. It remains a time capsule of a specific moment in time, a snapshot of the anxiety and alienation that defined a generation in the mid-1990s.

Harmony Korine and Larry Clark's collaboration did not end with Kids. Sevigny and Korine would go on to make Gummo (1997) and Julien Donkey-Boy (1999), further exploring the fringes of American society. Korine's cameo in the club scene as Fidget, the kid who gives Jennie the pill, is a subtle nod to his role as the architect of this chaotic world. The film's minor characters, cast with the same rigor as the leads, added layers of authenticity that made the world of Kids feel lived-in and real. The casting of a double amputee and a nursing woman as Telly's mother and brother, though ultimately unsuccessful in finding a ten-year-old for a flashback scene, speaks to the lengths Clark and Korine went to in their pursuit of truth.

In the end, Kids is a film that refuses to look away. It forces the viewer to confront the reality of a world where children are left to fend for themselves, where the safety nets of family and society have collapsed. It is a film about the loss of innocence, not in a sentimental way, but in a brutal, unforgiving manner. The characters are not villains; they are victims of a system that has failed them. They are children who have been given too much freedom and too little guidance, left to navigate a world that is hostile and indifferent to their plight. The film's enduring power lies in its ability to make us feel the weight of that abandonment, to see the world through the eyes of those who have nothing left to lose. It is a warning, a cautionary tale, and a masterpiece of American cinema. It is a film that stays with you, long after the credits have rolled, a haunting reminder of the fragility of youth and the devastating consequences of a world without adults.
