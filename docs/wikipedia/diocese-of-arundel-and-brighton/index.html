Diocese of Arundel and Brighton

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Diocese of Arundel and Brighton

On Saturday, 21 March 2015, the historic Arundel Cathedral in Sussex became the stage for a moment that would echo through the next decade of English Catholicism. It was the fiftieth anniversary of the Diocese of Arundel and Brighton's creation, yet the ceremony was not merely a look back at a half-century of survival, but a launchpad for a future that would see its current shepherd ascend to the highest office in the English Church. On that day, Bishop Richard Moth, previously the Catholic Military Ordinary for Great Britain, was installed as the fifth bishop of the diocese. Just a decade later, on 19 December 2025, the very man who had shepherded the coastal faithful through the 2020s was appointed the 12th Archbishop of Westminster, leaving the diocese he had revitalized suddenly vacant. This rapid trajectory—from the quiet coastal counties of Sussex and Surrey to the thunderous corridors of Lambeth—encapsulates the unique, often turbulent, and deeply significant history of a Catholic jurisdiction that has served as a crucible for the faith in southern England.

The Diocese of Arundel and Brighton is not merely a line on a map; it is a spiritual geography that encompasses the administrative counties of West and East Sussex, the sprawling suburbs of Surrey (specifically excluding Spelthorne, which remains tethered to the Diocese of Westminster), and the vibrant unitary authority of Brighton & Hove. Situated in the South East of England, this territory is a microcosm of the region's demographic and spiritual complexity. It stretches from the ancient, mist-shrouded villages of the Weald to the high-density urban sprawl of central Surrey, and down to the sun-drenched, culturally diverse coastal strip running from Chichester to the Kent border. It is one of 22 Roman Catholic dioceses in England and Wales, yet its story is distinct, born not of ancient apostolic tradition but of a deliberate restructuring of the modern Church.

The diocese was officially erected on 28 May 1965 by Pope Paul VI. To understand the significance of this date, one must understand the landscape that preceded it. Prior to 1965, this territory was part of the massive Diocese of Southwark. On that same fateful day in May, the Vatican performed a surgical operation on the ecclesiastical map of southern England: the Diocese of Southwark was elevated to an archdiocese, forming a new ecclesiastical province, while the new Diocese of Arundel and Brighton was carved out of its northern and eastern reaches. This was an era of renewal in the Catholic Church, the period immediately following the Second Vatican Council, where the Church in England and Wales was reorganizing itself to better serve a growing and diversifying Catholic population. The choice of Arundel, a small market town, and Brighton, a bustling seaside resort, as the dual titular seats was a masterstroke of symbolism, bridging the gap between the ancient, feudal history of the Sussex hills and the modern, cosmopolitan energy of the coast.

The leadership of this new diocese has been defined by a remarkable succession of men who would go on to shape the entire English Church. The first bishop, David Cashman, was consecrated on 14 June 1965, mere weeks after the diocese's creation. He was a man of the moment, tasked with building the administrative and spiritual foundations of a brand-new entity. However, his tenure was cut short by death in March 1971, leaving a vacancy that would soon be filled by a figure of immense future influence. He was succeeded by Bishop Michael George Bowen, who arrived in April 1977. Bowen's time in Arundel and Brighton was relatively brief, serving as a stepping stone to greater heights; he was translated to head the Archdiocese of Southwark in 1977, the very see from which his diocese had been carved.

Then came the bishop who would arguably define the modern era of the diocese: Cormac Murphy-O'Connor. Appointed in 1977, he led the diocese for over two decades, a period of profound change and consolidation. His leadership was characterized by a steady hand through the cultural shifts of the 1980s and 1990s. In March 2000, his tenure ended not with resignation or death, but with a promotion that would resonate globally: he became the 10th Archbishop of Westminster and, shortly thereafter, a Cardinal. Murphy-O'Connor's departure marked the end of an era, leaving behind a diocese that had matured from a newborn into a robust institution.

The succession continued with Bishop Kieran Conry, who served from 2001 until his resignation in 2014. Conry's tenure was marked by the difficult realities of the early 21st century, including the shadow of the sexual abuse scandals that would eventually plague the diocese's public reputation. His resignation in 2014 paved the way for the arrival of Bishop Richard Moth in 2015, a man whose background as the Military Ordinary brought a unique perspective of discipline and service to the coastal diocese. Moth's installation on 28 May 2015 was a celebration of the diocese's golden jubilee, a moment of renewal. Yet, history moved quickly. On 19 December 2025, Pope Francis appointed Moth as the 12th Archbishop of Westminster, the spiritual leader of Catholics in England and Wales. The diocese he had led for a decade was left vacant, a testament to the transient nature of episcopal leadership and the constant motion of the Church's hierarchy.

The geography of the diocese is as varied as its history. It is a place where the ancient meets the contemporary. The administrative counties of West and East Sussex are home to some of the most beautiful landscapes in England, yet they also contain some of the most densely populated Catholic communities in the country. The diocese covers highly populated parts of Surrey, a region where the Catholic population has exploded in recent decades due to migration and suburban expansion. In central Sussex and the coastal region, the faithful are scattered across a mix of historic parishes and modern housing estates. This diversity is reflected in the numbers: the diocese boasts a weekly Mass attendance of 43,377 persons. This figure is not merely a statistic; it represents approximately one quarter of the total Catholic population in the region, a significant portion of the faithful gathering every Sunday to sustain the life of the Church.

There are 116 parishes within the diocese, a network of communities that serve as the local anchors of the Catholic faith. But the diocese does not stop at the parish church doors. There are a number of other centres where Mass is celebrated regularly, extending the reach of the sacraments to the peripheries. The clergy who serve these communities are a dedicated force. As of April 2005, the diocese counted 182 diocesan priests and 97 religious priests, alongside 16 permanent deacons. These numbers, while from a specific point in time, illustrate the density of pastoral care required to serve a region that spans from the rural quiet of the Weald to the urban roar of Brighton. There are many religious institutes of both men and women living and working in the diocese, engaged in a wide variety of apostolates. From education and healthcare to social work and direct evangelization, these religious orders form the backbone of the diocese's charitable and spiritual outreach.

Education is a cornerstone of the diocese's identity. With a total of 90 Catholic schools, the Diocese of Arundel and Brighton is a major player in the educational landscape of southern England. These schools are not just places of academic instruction; they are communities of faith where the next generation is formed. The sheer number of schools—nearly one hundred—speaks to the commitment of the diocese to providing a Catholic education to its children, a commitment that has survived the changing tides of public policy and social attitude.

One of the most distinctive features of the diocese is the annual Arundel and Brighton Pilgrimage. Founded by the diocese itself, this pilgrimage is a testament to the enduring spirit of the faithful. Though it is largely Roman Catholic in character, it is in fact ecumenical, welcoming Anglican attendants and fostering a spirit of Christian unity. The first pilgrimage took place in 1975, a modest event that circled around the diocese. Since then, it has evolved into a grander undertaking, with cross-country routes through England and Wales. These pilgrimages are not mere walks; they are journeys of faith, with different themes and stops at various churches and cathedrals, connecting the local community to the wider body of the Church. The pilgrimage serves as a reminder that the diocese is part of a larger story, a story that stretches back through the centuries and forward into the future.

However, the history of the Diocese of Arundel and Brighton is not without its shadows. In the early 2000s, the diocese was engulfed by the sexual abuse scandal that swept through the Catholic Church in England and Wales. This was a period of profound trauma for the local community, hurting the public's trust in the work of local diocesan officials. The scandal forced a reckoning, a painful examination of the structures and cultures that had allowed such abuses to occur. It is a chapter that cannot be ignored, for it is part of the diocese's reality. The response to this crisis, the efforts at healing, and the ongoing work of safeguarding have become integral to the diocese's identity in the 21st century. The trust of the faithful had to be rebuilt, brick by brick, in the face of deep disappointment and hurt.

The leadership of the diocese has also been marked by the careers of auxiliary bishops who have gone on to serve in other capacities. Michael George Bowen, before his translation to Southwark, served as a bishop in the 1970s. Maurice Noël Léon Couve de Murville, who served as an auxiliary, was later appointed Archbishop of Birmingham in 1982. Bernard Longley, another auxiliary, was appointed auxiliary bishop of Westminster in 2003. These figures represent the diocese's role as a training ground for leadership, a place where bishops are formed and then sent out to serve the wider Church. The list of bishops is short, yet the impact of each one has been profound. David John Cashman laid the foundations. Michael George Bowen provided stability. Cormac Murphy-O'Connor brought vision and prominence. Kieran Conry navigated the crises of the new millennium. And Charles Phillip Richard Moth, from 2015 to 2025, provided the leadership that would eventually lead to his elevation to Westminster.

The diocese is part of the Metropolitan Province of Southwark, which is currently presided over by John Wilson, by virtue of his office of Archbishop of Southwark. This relationship places Arundel and Brighton within a larger ecclesiastical family, a network of dioceses that work together to serve the Catholic faithful in the South East of England. The province structure ensures that there is a sense of unity and shared purpose, even as each diocese maintains its own unique identity and challenges.

The Catholic Children's Society, an organization with deep roots in the diocese, continues to play a vital role in supporting vulnerable children and families. This work is a reflection of the diocese's commitment to the poor and the marginalized, a commitment that is central to the Gospel message. The society's presence in the diocese underscores the importance of social justice and charity in the life of the Church.

As we look at the Diocese of Arundel and Brighton, we see a community that is both ancient and new. It is a place where the stone of Arundel Cathedral stands as a witness to centuries of faith, while the streets of Brighton buzz with the energy of a modern, diverse society. It is a diocese that has faced its share of challenges, from the trauma of abuse scandals to the complexities of a changing world, yet it has endured. The weekly attendance of over 43,000 people is a testament to the resilience of the faith. The 90 schools are a testament to the commitment to the next generation. The annual pilgrimage is a testament to the enduring spirit of the faithful.

The recent departure of Bishop Moth to Westminster leaves the diocese in a state of transition, a "vacant" period that is both a challenge and an opportunity. It is a time for the faithful to reflect on the past, to pray for the future, and to prepare for the next chapter in the story of Arundel and Brighton. The history of the diocese is a story of continuity and change, of stability and upheaval, of joy and sorrow. It is a story that continues to be written, day by day, by the thousands of Catholics who call this diocese home.

The diocese's connection to the broader Catholic Church in England and Wales is undeniable. It is one of 22 dioceses, each with its own unique character, but all united in faith and mission. The Diocese of Arundel and Brighton is a vital part of this tapestry, a region where the faith is lived out in the everyday moments of life, in the schools, the parishes, the hospitals, and the homes. It is a place where the countercultural message of the Gospel is proclaimed, often in the face of a secularizing society.

The numbers tell a story, but they do not tell the whole story. The 116 parishes are not just buildings; they are communities of people who gather to worship, to support one another, and to serve. The 182 diocesan priests and 97 religious priests are not just employees; they are shepherds who dedicate their lives to the care of souls. The 16 permanent deacons are not just assistants; they are servants who bring the Gospel to the margins. And the 90 schools are not just institutions; they are places where the future is shaped.

The Diocese of Arundel and Brighton is a place of pilgrimage, both literal and metaphorical. It is a place where the faithful journey through the valleys of doubt and the peaks of joy, guided by the light of faith. It is a place where the past is honored, the present is embraced, and the future is anticipated with hope. The story of this diocese is a story of faith in action, of a community that has stood the test of time and continues to thrive in the modern world.

As the diocese moves forward from the vacancy left by Bishop Moth's elevation, it does so with the weight of history on its shoulders and the promise of the future in its heart. The legacy of its bishops, the strength of its parishes, and the dedication of its people will guide it through the challenges and opportunities that lie ahead.
