WARP (systolic array)

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: WARP (systolic array)

In April 1987, a machine priced at $350,000 sat in a laboratory, offering a computational speed that seemed impossible for its era. It was not a supercomputer in the traditional sense, housing rows of massive mainframes; it was a linear array of ten tiny, specialized processors working in a rhythm so precise it mimicked a beating heart. This was the PC-Warp, the second generation of a radical experiment born from the collision of academic theory and industrial ambition. While today's GPU architects obsess over tensor cores and memory bandwidth to accelerate deep learning, the seeds of this revolution were sown decades earlier in the halls of Carnegie Mellon University, where H. T. Kung and his collaborators were building the first true systolic arrays. The Warp machines were not merely faster computers; they were a fundamental rethinking of how data moves, challenging the von Neumann bottleneck by forcing processors to pass data like a baton in a relay race, calculating as they ran.

The story of Warp begins with a specific problem: the inefficiency of general-purpose processors when faced with the repetitive, data-intensive demands of signal processing and image analysis. In the mid-1980s, the standard computing model involved a central processor fetching instructions and data from memory, performing a calculation, and then writing the result back. This constant shuffling created a traffic jam that limited performance. H. T. Kung, a visionary at Carnegie Mellon University (CMU), proposed a different architecture. He envisioned a systolic array, a term borrowed from the circulatory system of the human body. Just as the heart pumps blood through the body in a rhythmic, pulsating manner, a systolic array pumps data through a grid of processing elements (PEs). Each PE performs a simple calculation on the data it receives, passes the result to its neighbor, and repeats. The data flows through the system like a wave, with every processor active at every clock cycle.

This was not a mere theoretical exercise. The project was launched in 1984, fueled by the strategic foresight of the U.S. Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA). DARPA recognized that the future of high-performance computing lay not just in raw clock speed, but in parallelism and efficiency. To turn this vision into reality, CMU forged an unlikely alliance with three industrial titans: General Electric, Honeywell, and Intel. Each partner brought a different strength to the table, creating a unique ecosystem where academic research could be rapidly prototyped and manufactured. The goal was to create three generations of increasingly general-purpose systolic array processors, a trajectory that would see the machines evolve from rigid, lock-step devices into flexible, high-performance workhorses.

The Purest Form: WW-Warp

The first iteration, known as WW-Warp (Wire Wrap Warp), was the purest embodiment of the systolic ideal. Completed as a two-cell prototype at CMU in June 1985, this machine was a marvel of disciplined engineering. It forced a "lock step" sequencing of stages. In this architecture, every processing element had to wait for the previous one to finish its task before proceeding. If one PE was delayed, the entire array stalled. This rigidity severely restricted programmability; the software had to be choreographed with the precision of a ballet, where every dancer knew exactly when to move and where to stand.

However, this constraint was the price of purity. WW-Warp was a linear array of ten or more programmable processing elements, each capable of 10 MFLOPS (Millions of Floating-point Operations Per Second) in single-precision. The simplicity of the design allowed researchers to validate the core concept of systolic computing: that by eliminating the overhead of fetching and decoding complex instructions for every operation, one could achieve massive throughput for specific algorithms. The machine was built using wire-wrap technology, a method of constructing electronics that was becoming obsolete for mass production but ideal for rapid prototyping. It was a proof of concept, a laboratory beast that proved the math worked, even if it was difficult to program.

Two essentially identical ten-cell WW-Warp systems were produced in 1986 to move beyond the prototype phase. One was built by Honeywell, the other by General Electric. The GE system arrived at CMU in February 1986, followed by the Honeywell unit in June. These machines were not just academic curiosities; they were the first real-world testbeds for systolic algorithms. They demonstrated that a linear array of simple processors could outperform a general-purpose supercomputer on specific tasks like convolutions and filtering, which are foundational to computer vision. Yet, the lock-step nature of WW-Warp meant that if the data stream was interrupted or the algorithm didn't fit the rigid pipeline, the performance plummeted. The next generation needed to loosen the coupling between processors, trading some of that architectural purity for greater flexibility.

The Bridge to Reality: PC-Warp

If WW-Warp was the theoretical ideal, PC-Warp (Printed Circuit Warp) was the bridge to reality. Delivered by GE in April 1987, the PC-Warp represented a significant redesign that loosened the strict lock-step requirements of its predecessor. While it still maintained the linear array structure of ten or more processing elements, the coupling between them was less rigid. This change allowed for a more flexible flow of data, enabling the machine to handle a broader range of algorithms without the catastrophic stalls that plagued the WW-Warp.

The performance leap was tangible. Each processing element in the PC-Warp was upgraded to 20 MFLOPS, doubling the computational power of the original. More importantly, the architecture began to separate the concerns of computation and communication. A single PE in the Warp design consisted of two main agents: a Computation Agent and a Communication Agent. The Computation Agent was the brain, responsible for processing data with a power of 20 MFLOPS and 20 MIPS (Millions of Instructions Per Second). It had access to local memory with a bandwidth of 160 MBytes/sec, a massive improvement over the global memory access of traditional systems.

The Communication Agent was the nervous system, handling data transfer between the PE and its neighbors. Each physical port on this agent boasted a bandwidth of 40 MBytes/sec. This separation was crucial. In a systolic array, the speed of the computation is often limited not by how fast the processor can calculate, but by how fast it can receive the next piece of data. By dedicating a specific agent to communication, the Warp machines ensured that the Computation Agent never had to wait. Data flowed in, the calculation happened, and the result flowed out, all while the next piece of data was already on its way.

About twenty production models of the PC-Warp were produced and sold by GE between 1987 and 1989. Priced at around $350,000 per machine, they were accessible only to well-funded research institutions, government agencies, and industrial labs. These machines found their niche in applications that demanded high throughput on repetitive tasks. The originally intended application was low-level computer vision, including convolutions and filtering, as well as the simulation of artificial neural networks. But the utility of the Warp architecture quickly expanded. Researchers began using it for magnetic resonance image processing, repetitive image texture analysis, and complex linear algebra problems.

The software ecosystem for these machines was as innovative as the hardware. A research compiler for a language known as "W2" targeted all three machine designs. It was the only compiler available for the WW-Warp and PC-Warp, serving as a critical tool for the research community. W2 allowed programmers to describe the flow of data through the array in a high-level manner, leaving the intricate details of scheduling and communication to the compiler. This abstraction was vital, as manually coding the data flow for a systolic array was an error-prone and tedious task. The W2 compiler translated these high-level descriptions into the specific instructions needed to drive the Computation and Communication agents, effectively hiding the complexity of the hardware from the user.

The Integrated Future: iWarp

While GE and Honeywell were refining the printed circuit boards, Intel was preparing a revolution in silicon. In 1986, Intel was selected through competitive bidding to be the industrial partner for the integrated circuit implementation of Warp. This marked a shift from discrete components and printed circuit boards to a single-chip solution. The result was the iWarp (integrated circuit Warp), a name that conveniently played on Intel's "i" prefix while signaling the next evolutionary step in the systolic lineage.

The iWarp was a masterpiece of VLSI design. It was based on a single-chip custom microprocessor containing 700,000 transistors, a staggering number for the time. This chip was designed specifically for the Warp project, utilizing a long-instruction-word (LIW) format that allowed multiple operations to be specified in a single instruction. This design choice maximized the efficiency of the processor, ensuring that the Computation and Communication agents could operate in perfect harmony without the overhead of instruction decoding. The chip tightly integrated communications with the computational processor, a feature that had been a separate concern in the earlier generations.

The first iWarp system, a 12-node configuration, became operational in March 1990. After a number of stepping of the part to refine the design, Intel produced and sold about 39 machines in 1992 and 1993. These machines were sold to universities, government agencies, and industrial research laboratories, continuing the legacy of Warp as a tool for cutting-edge research. The standard configuration for iWarp machines arranged the nodes in a 2m x 2n torus. Unlike the linear arrays of the WW-Warp and PC-Warp, the torus topology allowed data to wrap around, creating a grid where every node had neighbors in all directions. All iWarp machines included "backedges," ensuring that the torus structure was complete and that data could flow in multiple directions simultaneously.

The performance of the iWarp was a quantum leap over its predecessors. The processing elements in the iWarp ran at 20 MFLOPS (SP), but the architectural improvements meant that the effective throughput was far higher. The production compiler for iWarp was a C and Fortran compiler based on the AT&T pcc compiler for UNIX. Ported under contract for Intel and then extensively modified and extended by the company, this compiler brought the power of high-level languages to the systolic architecture. It allowed researchers to write standard C or Fortran code, which was then optimized by the compiler to map efficiently onto the systolic array. This was a critical step in making systolic computing accessible to a wider audience of programmers who were not specialists in hardware architecture.

Benchmarking the Revolution

The true measure of the Warp machines was not in their specifications, but in their performance on real-world problems. The 10-cell Warp (not iWarp) computer was benchmarked on performing a forward-backward propagation on the NETtalk, a famous neural network used for speech recognition. The results were staggering. The machine achieved 16.5 MC/s (million connections per second). This meant that to run one forward and one backward pass over NETtalk's 18,629 weights took a fraction of a second. Specifically, the time required was calculated as 18629 divided by 16.5 million seconds.

This performance represented an 8x speedup over a backpropagation algorithm running on the Connection Machine-1, a leading parallel computer of the era. Even more impressively, it was a 340x speedup over the original implementation on the Ridge 32, a traditional vector supercomputer. The Warp machines were not just faster; they were exponentially more efficient at the specific task of neural network training, which involves massive amounts of matrix multiplication and data movement.

When the 10-cell iWarp came online, the researchers ran the same backpropagation benchmark with essentially the same implementation. The results were even more dramatic. The iWarp ran at 36 MC/s, a 760x speedup over the Ridge 32. This demonstrated the power of the integrated circuit approach and the torus topology. The iWarp could handle more complex data flows and execute the neural network algorithms with unprecedented efficiency. These benchmarks were not just numbers on a page; they were a validation of the systolic paradigm. They showed that for specific classes of problems, a specialized architecture could outperform general-purpose supercomputers by orders of magnitude.

The software development for all models of Warp machines was done on Sun workstations, which were the standard UNIX-based workstations of the time. This separation of concerns allowed researchers to develop and test their algorithms on a familiar platform before deploying them to the specialized Warp hardware. The Warp machines were attached to these Sun workstations, which acted as the host, managing the data flow and providing the user interface. This architecture mirrored the modern GPU model, where a host CPU manages the workload for a specialized accelerator.

The Legacy of the Systolic Array

The Warp project yielded more than just hardware. It produced a wealth of research results, publications, and advancements in general-purpose systolic hardware design, compiler design, and systolic software algorithms. The three generations of machines—WW-Warp, PC-Warp, and iWarp—told a story of evolution. They started as rigid, lock-step devices that proved the concept, moved to flexible printed circuit arrays that found practical applications, and culminated in highly integrated silicon chips that redefined the limits of parallel computing.

The influence of Warp can be seen in the modern landscape of high-performance computing. Today's GPUs, with their thousands of cores and specialized tensor units, are the spiritual successors to the systolic arrays of the 1980s. The concept of separating computation and communication, the use of local memory to reduce bandwidth bottlenecks, and the focus on data flow rather than instruction flow are all hallmarks of the Warp architecture. The work done by H. T. Kung and his team at CMU, in partnership with GE, Honeywell, and Intel, laid the groundwork for the deep learning revolution that is reshaping the world today.

The Warp machines were a testament to the power of collaboration between academia and industry. They showed that with the right combination of theoretical insight, engineering prowess, and strategic funding, it was possible to build machines that defied the conventional wisdom of the time. The $350,000 price tag of the PC-Warp might seem exorbitant, but for the researchers who used it, it was an investment in the future of computing. They were the pioneers who proved that the heart of a computer could beat in a rhythm of its own, pumping data through a systolic array to solve problems that seemed impossible.

The story of Warp is a reminder that progress in technology is often non-linear. It is not just about making things faster; it is about rethinking the fundamental principles of how things work. The Warp machines did not just accelerate existing algorithms; they enabled new ones. They allowed researchers to simulate artificial neural networks with a speed that was previously unimaginable, paving the way for the AI applications we rely on today. The 760x speedup on the NETtalk benchmark was not just a record; it was a glimpse into a future where machines could learn and adapt in real-time.

In the end, the Warp project was a triumph of vision. It took a complex, abstract idea—the systolic array—and turned it into a reality that changed the course of computer history. From the wire-wrapped prototypes of 1985 to the silicon tori of 1992, the Warp machines proved that sometimes, the fastest way forward is to let the data flow. The legacy of H. T. Kung and the Warp team lives on in every neural network that trains, every image that is processed, and every complex calculation that is accelerated by the principles they established. They built the engine for the future, one systolic beat at a time.
