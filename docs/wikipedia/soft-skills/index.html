Soft skills

5 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Soft skills

In 1972, a US Army training manual quietly introduced a term that would eventually reshape how employers evaluate candidates worldwide. The word was "soft skills"—and for half a century, it remained as vague and contested as the day it first appeared on military paperwork.

The concept emerged from a practical problem: the Army realized that winning wars required more than technical proficiency with weapons and machinery. Leadership matters. Morale matters. The ability to motivate soldiers and make quick decisions under pressure—these human qualities mattered enormously, yet they resisted easy classification. So the military did what it does best: it codified them.

By 1972, Dr. PG Whitmore presented findings at the CONARC Soft Skills Conference that defined soft skills as "job-related skills that involve little or no interaction with machines and whose application on the job is quite generalized." The definition was deliberately broad—perhaps too broad. One critic noted the paradox: "those job functions about which we know a good deal are hard skills and those about which we know very little are soft skills."

The term has never quite recovered from that honesty.

What Actually Makes a Soft Skill

Unlike technical abilities—hard skills—that can be measured through certifications, tests, and demonstrable outputs, soft skills occupy murkier territory. They include critical thinking, problem solving, collaboration, public speaking, professional writing, teamwork, digital literacy, leadership, strategic vision, creativity, open-mindedness, professional attitude, adaptability, work ethic, career management, and intercultural fluency.

This is not a checklist. These are ways of thinking—the psychological and social dispositions that determine how someone navigates complexity. Psychologist Nicholas Humphrey famously argued that social intelligence, rather than raw quantitative intelligence, defines what makes us human. It defines our success in life.

The Nobel Prize-winning economist James Heckman has spent decades studying this phenomenon. "Soft skills predict success in life," he wrote, "that they casually produce that success, and that programs that enhance soft skills have an important place in an effective portfolio of public policies." This is not inspirational poetry. It is empirical economics.

The Market Speaks

Here is the measure that matters: three-quarters of employers have a hard time finding graduates with the soft skills their companies need.

A 2019 survey by the Society for Human Resource Management revealed an uncomfortable truth. Companies couldhire based on grade point averages—GPAs—once considered the most important factor in hiring decisions. Hard skills were quantifiable and measurable from educational background, work experience, or interview performance. Success at work seemed related solely to technical ability.

But something shifted.

The trend changed in recent years due to more businesses adopting hybrid work environments. Hard skills still represent a fundamental aspect—coding, engineering, accounting—but soft skills now equal them for importance. According to leadership professor Robert Lavasseur, most researchers he interviewed "rated soft skills higher than technical skills."

In employment sectors that have seen rapid growth—technology, healthcare, consulting—employers have stated that newly graduated employees possess a skill gap. This gap resides between soft and hard skills. They possess the hard skills required and expected but are lacking the soft skills.

Europe's Wake-Up Call

The European Commission launched its program Agenda for new Skills and Jobs in 2012 specifically to train young adults in this new set of skills. The huge amount of funding companies and worldwide organizations have invested in training and developing this field demonstrates renewed interest.

Research shows the effect of poorer soft skills on life outcomes, and how improving these can fill skills gaps or increase individuals' own circumstances.

Measuring What Cannot Be Measured

Studies by the OECD in 2015 suggested soft skills can be meaningfully measured within cultural and linguistic boundaries. Such measures include a combination of methods: self-reported personality, behavioral surveys, and objective psychological assessments.

These measurements improve by collecting data from multiple sources across learning contexts—the school environment, family context, wider community—and triangulating the data. This is because surveys can be subject to bias. Having multiple sources such as self, teacher, peer, and parental reporting provides unique perspectives on a student's skills as well as infer latent personality.

Anchoring vignettes is another method that lessens biases and increases data quality while improving cross-cultural comparability of soft skill assessments.

The Modern Differentiator

In the 21st century, soft skills are a major differentiator—a sine qua non for employability and success in life. This has nothing to do with warm feelings or touchy-feely management philosophy. The evidence is overwhelming: workers with strong soft skills adapt more readily to changing environments. Their ductility helps "people to adapt and behave positively so that they can deal effectively with the challenges of their professional and everyday life."

The problem is not limited to young people looking for jobs. It is also actual employees—those who cannot navigate interpersonal complexity, cannot inspire collaboration, cannot manage time or lead teams.

The high request, and broadly diffused confusion about meaning and training, represent two elements that can explain the lack of soft skills in the job market. Employers struggle to find leaders and workers able to keep up with evolving markets. Soft skills make people flexible in a world which keeps changing—and that is precisely why they matter.
