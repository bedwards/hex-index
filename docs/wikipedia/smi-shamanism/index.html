Sámi shamanism

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Sámi shamanism

In the stark, wind-swept landscapes of Northern Scandinavia, a specific geological formation known as a sieidi stands not as a mere curiosity of erosion, but as a living entity with a voice. These are not random boulders; they are stones with unusual shapes—perhaps resembling a man, a reindeer, or a bird—perched precariously on mountain tops, hidden in caves, or rising from the waters of lakes. For centuries, the Sámi people did not merely look at these formations; they engaged in a daily, intimate dialogue with them. Families would spread fresh twigs beneath a sieidi in the biting cold of winter, or lay down green grass in the short, vibrant summer, offering these simple gifts to spirits that governed the very success of their hunt and the safety of their herds. This was not a religion of distant dogmas written in stone tablets, but a vibrant, animistic reality where the boundary between the human and the non-human was porous, fluid, and deeply sacred.

To understand Sámi shamanism is to abandon the Western notion of a religion confined to a temple or a specific day of the week. Traditional Sámi spiritual practices are rooted in a complex tapestry of animism, polytheism, and what anthropologists classify as shamanism, yet these terms only scratch the surface of a worldview that treats the entire natural world as a community of persons. The core tenet is simple yet profound: every significant natural object possesses a soul. This includes the animals that provide sustenance, the plants that offer medicine, the rocks that anchor the landscape, and the winds that dictate travel. From a polytheistic perspective, this spiritual ecosystem is populated by a multitude of spirits, each with distinct personalities, domains, and demands. The religion is not static; it varies considerably from region to region within Sápmi, the vast territory stretching across northern Norway, Sweden, Finland, and the Kola Peninsula of Russia. A spirit revered in one valley might be unknown in the next, and the names of deities shift like the aurora borealis overhead.

At the heart of this belief system lies a relationship with the dead that would eventually cause an irreconcilable rift with Christian missionaries. The Sámi regarded the living and the departed as two halves of the same family. This was not a metaphorical connection but a fundamental social and spiritual reality. The ancestors were not gone; they were present, participating in the daily life of the kin-group, offering guidance, and requiring respect. As one author noted, this belief transformed society into a living dialogue with the ancestors. When Protestant Christian missionaries arrived, they found this concept absolutely unacceptable. To the missionaries, the dead were dead, cut off from the realm of the living, and any interaction with them was tantamount to necromancy or idolatry. The missionaries discounted any possibility of the dead having anything to do with the living, viewing the Sámi's continuous relationship with their forebears as a barrier to salvation. This clash was not merely theological; it was a collision between two fundamentally different understandings of what constitutes a human life and the continuity of the family line.

The spiritual landscape of the Sámi is populated by a pantheon as diverse as the terrain itself, though the deities often overlap and shift depending on the local dialect and tribal tradition. In one region, a single deity might fracture into several distinct spirits, while in another, multiple spirits might be consolidated into a singular entity. This fluidity can be confusing to the outside observer, but for the Sámi, it reflects the localized nature of their spirituality. Among the most prominent figures is Radien-attje, the Creator and high god, the head divinity who created the world. Yet, in a twist that might seem passive to modern sensibilities, Radien-attje is often described as sleeping or inactive in the daily religious practice. He created the souls of human beings with his spouse, Raedieahkka, and while he is the ultimate source of existence, he is not the spirit one petitions for a good hunt or a safe journey. That role falls to his son, Radien-pardne, who acts as the proxy, carrying out the will of the passive father.

The active gods of the Sámi are far more immediate. Beaivi, the goddess of the sun, is often viewed as the mother of human beings, a figure of warmth and life-giving energy. However, in some regions, the sun is male, and her counterpart, Bieggagallis, becomes the father of humanity. The winds are personified as Bieggolmai, the "Man of the Winds," a force that can be both a guide and a destroyer. The thunder god, Horagalles, whose name may derive from a compound meaning "Thor-man," is a figure of immense power, also known as Bajanolmmai, Dierpmis, or Tordöm. He is the "Grandfather" of the storm, a deity who commands the sky and the thunder that shakes the earth. Then there is Rana Niejta, the daughter of Radien-attje and Raedieahkka, known as "the daughter of the green, fertile earth." Her name, Rana, meaning "green" or "fertile," was so popular that it became a common name for Sámi girls, symbolizing the deep connection between the divine and the cycles of nature.

The hunt, the central pillar of traditional Sámi subsistence, is governed by a complex hierarchy of spirits. Laib Olmai, the forest spirit, is the master of the wild animals, which he regards as his personal herds. He holds the power to grant either good or bad luck in hunting, and his favor was so critical that believers offered prayers and sacrifices to him every morning and every evening. His domain is the forest, a place of both danger and abundance. Similarly, the Storjunkare are spirits described sometimes as stones with a likeness to a man or an animal, set up on mountain tops or near rivers. These entities held power over all animals, fish, and birds, and every clan had its own hill of sacrifice where reindeer were offered up to ensure the bounty of the next season. The relationship with the reindeer, the animal that sustains the people, is paramount to the kin-group. It is a relationship of reciprocity; the reindeer gives its life, and the Sámi give their respect and offerings to the spirits that control the herds.

But the world is not only filled with benevolent guardians. The Sámi cosmology acknowledges the presence of malevolent forces. Ruohtta is the god of sickness and death, terrifyingly depicted riding a horse through the land, bringing illness and ending life. Stallo are the feared cannibal giants of the wilderness, monstrous beings that lurk in the shadows of the mountains. The god of evil, Mubpienålmaj, represents a darker force, influenced later by the Christian concept of Satan, yet rooted in the ancient fear of chaos and destruction. Even the moon has a deity, known as Mano, Manna, or Aske, watching over the night and the tides of the human spirit. The goddess of the dead, Jahbme akka, rules the underworld, the saivo, a realm that is not a place of punishment but a continuation of existence, a parallel world where the ancestors reside.

The mediator between these two worlds—the human world and the saivo—is the noaidi. The noaidi is the shaman, the healer, the prophet, and the keeper of tradition. This figure does not merely pray; they travel. Using a Sámi drum, often intricately decorated with symbols of the cosmos, and a domestic flute called a fadno, the noaidi enters a trance state. In this altered state of consciousness, they journey to the underworld to negotiate with spirits, retrieve lost souls, or diagnose the cause of an illness. The drum is not just an instrument; it is a vessel, a map of the spiritual world that guides the noaidi on their journey. The fadno, a simple reed flute, adds a haunting melody that bridges the gap between the physical and the metaphysical. The noaidi acts on behalf of the community, a role that requires immense spiritual strength and knowledge. They are the ones who can navigate the dangerous waters of the spirit world to bring back blessings or warnings.

The deities of the Sámi are not monolithic; they are deeply intertwined with the specific needs of the people. Áhkká is a group of fertility goddesses, including Maderakka, Sarakka, Juksakka, and Uksakka. Maderakka, the wife of Madder-Attje, is the mother of the tribe. While her husband, Madder-Attje, gives newborns their souls, it is Maderakka who gives them their bodies. This division of labor in creation highlights the complementary roles of the divine in Sámi thought. Beaivi, the sun goddess, has a daughter, Biejjenniejte, who is the goddess of healing and medicine. She is the one who cures the sick, drawing on the healing power of the sun. The god of the hunt, Lieaibolmmai, is the patron of adult men, guiding them through the forests and over the fells. Tjaetsieålmaj, "the man of water," governs the lakes and the rivers, the domain of fishing, ensuring that the nets are full and the waters are safe.

The concept of Ipmil, meaning "God," is an interesting example of linguistic and cultural adaptation. Originally a native term, it was adopted by the Sámi as a name for the Christian God, much like the Finnish word Jumala. This linguistic shift reflects the complex history of Christianization, where indigenous terms were repurposed to fit new theological frameworks, often obscuring the original meaning. The noaidi and the community did not simply abandon their old gods; they attempted to integrate the new or resist them, creating a syncretic spiritual landscape where the old and the new vied for dominance. The noaidi became the focal point of this resistance, as their role was so deeply embedded in the community's survival that to eliminate the noaidi was to dismantle the social fabric of the Sámi people.

The landscape itself is a testament to this spiritual history. Throughout Northern Scandinavia, the sieidis remain as silent witnesses to a time when the mountains spoke. These places of unusual landforms were considered to have spiritual significance, and each family or clan had its local spirits to whom they made offerings for protection and good fortune. The sieidi was not just a place of worship; it was a landmark, a meeting point, and a source of identity. The act of spreading fresh twigs or grass was a ritual of renewal, a way of acknowledging the cyclical nature of life and the constant need for reciprocity with the natural world. The sieidi were the anchors of the Sámi spiritual world, grounding their beliefs in the very soil they walked upon.

The Christianization of the Sámi people was a long and painful process that sought to erase this animistic worldview. Missionaries viewed the Sámi's relationship with the dead, their worship of natural objects, and their use of the noaidi as pagan superstitions that had to be purged. They saw the sieidis as idols and the noaidi as sorcerers. The missionaries' campaign was relentless, and it succeeded in suppressing many of the public expressions of the old religion. Drumming was banned, noaidi were persecuted, and the sieidis were often destroyed or Christianized. Yet, the core of the Sámi worldview proved resilient. The belief that the living and the dead are part of the same family could not be easily extinguished. It survived in the folklore, in the stories told by the fire, and in the quiet reverence paid to the natural world.

The variations in Sámi religion between regions and tribes add another layer of complexity to this spiritual tapestry. While the deities are similar, their names vary. In one region, a deity might appear as several separate entities, while in another, several deities might be united into just a few. This fluidity means that the pantheon is not a rigid hierarchy but a dynamic network of relationships. The deities can be confused with each other by outsiders, but for the Sámi, the distinctions are clear and meaningful. The noaidi was the one who understood these nuances, the one who knew which spirit to call upon in which situation, and how to navigate the complex web of spiritual obligations.

The legacy of Sámi shamanism is not just a historical footnote; it is a living tradition that continues to influence Sámi culture today. The noaidi may no longer travel to the underworld in a trance, but the role of the spiritual leader remains. The sieidis are still respected, and the connection to the land is still profound. The Sámi have reclaimed their identity, and with it, their spiritual heritage. The old gods, the noaidi, and the sieidis are no longer just relics of a pagan past; they are symbols of resilience, of a people who refused to let their worldview be erased. The animism, the polytheism, and the shamanism of the Sámi are a testament to the depth of human connection to the natural world, a connection that transcends the boundaries of time and culture.

The story of the Sámi is a story of survival in the face of overwhelming odds. It is a story of a people who looked at the world and saw not just resources to be exploited, but relatives to be honored. The noaidi with their drums and flutes, the sieidis with their silent power, and the Áhkká with their nurturing presence are all part of a spiritual legacy that challenges the modern world to remember what it means to be human. The Sámi religion is not just a set of beliefs; it is a way of life, a way of being in the world that emphasizes reciprocity, respect, and the enduring bond between the living and the dead. In the end, the Sámi shamanism stands as a powerful reminder that the world is alive, that every stone has a soul, and that the ancestors are always with us, watching, waiting, and guiding us through the darkness and the light.
