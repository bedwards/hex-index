"On February 7, 2007, Stephanie Lenz pointed her camera at her 13-month-old son in their suburban California kitchen. He was bouncing to the faint, tinny strains of Prince’s Let’s Go Crazy—a song playing from a radio across the room—while grabbing for a toy. The clip lasted 29 seconds, most of it silent except for about 20 seconds of distorted background music. She uploaded it to YouTube as a keepsake for family. Six months later, Universal Music Corp., the copyright holder for Prince’s catalog, sent a takedown notice to YouTube demanding its removal. This wasn’t just another automated copyright strike. It ignited a seven-year legal war that would force America’s courts to confront a fundamental question: Do corporations get to police the internet with zero accountability when they silence everyday people? The answer reshaped digital free speech forever.

The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA), passed in 1998, created a system where copyright holders could issue takedown notices to platforms like YouTube to remove allegedly infringing content. Platforms like YouTube, shielded from liability if they complied, would yank the content within hours. It was designed as a shield against piracy—but quickly became a sword for censorship. The problem? Section 512(f) of the DMCA allowed users to sue copyright holders for misrepresentation if they knowingly sent false takedowns. Yet no court had ever clarified what “knowingly” meant. Could a corporation like Universal blast out takedown notices without even checking if the use was legal? That loophole let companies treat the internet like a private fiefdom. Fair use—a doctrine baked into copyright law since 1976—allowed limited use of copyrighted material for purposes like criticism, education, or parody. But in the DMCA’s takedown machinery, fair use was ignored. Platforms rarely defended users; copyright holders rarely paused. The system was rigged for speed, not justice.

Lenz’s video was the antithesis of copyright theft. Prince’s song was muffled, unrecognizable as a standalone audio clip, and incidental to the video’s actual purpose: showing a toddler dancing. The original track runs over four minutes; she used less than 10% of it. Yet Universal’s June 2007 takedown notice declared the video “a copyright violation built upon an unauthorized copy.” YouTube, complying within hours, deleted it. Lenz fought back with a counter-notice invoking fair use. Six weeks later, YouTube restored the video. But the damage was done. In September 2007, Prince himself escalated the conflict, telling the Daily Mirror: “I don’t see why people have the right to upload my music to YouTube. It’s not theirs. It’s not fair to me or the people who work for me.” Universal doubled down in October 2007, vowing to purge all Prince-related user content from the internet “as a matter of principle.” This wasn’t about one video. It was about erasing fair use from the digital landscape.

Lenz sued Universal in July 2007 for misrepresentation under the DMCA. Her argument was radical for its simplicity: If fair use is legal—not a “defense” against infringement but a right granted by Section 107 of the Copyright Act—then copyright holders must consider it before crying foul. Universal countered that forcing companies to analyze fair use for every takedown would cripple enforcement. “Subjective interpretations” would run wild, they argued. The case landed before Judge Jeremy Fogel in the Northern District of California in 2010. Fogel saw through Universal’s fearmongering. In a landmark ruling, he declared copyright owners must consider fair use before sending takedowns. But he stopped short of holding Universal liable. Why? Because Lenz hadn’t proven “actual damages”—a legal technicality since YouTube had reposted the video. Universal hadn’t intended malice, just negligence. Both sides appealed: Lenz demanded systemic change; Universal fought to preserve its takedown blank check.

The Ninth Circuit’s Earthquake

On September 14, 2015, the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals issued a 35-page ruling that rewrote the rules of digital copyright. In Lenz v. Universal Music Corp., 801 F.3d 1126, Judge Milan Smith delivered a verdict that resonated far beyond Lenz’s kitchen:

“Because 17 U.S.C. § 107 created a type of non-infringing use, fair use is ‘authorized by the law’ and a copyright holder must consider the existence of fair use before sending a takedown notification under § 512(c).”

This wasn’t merely procedural. The court rejected Universal’s framing of fair use as an “excuse” for infringement. Instead, it affirmed that fair use isn’t infringement at all. If you’re dancing to Prince in your living room, you’re not stealing—it’s legal. Therefore, copyright holders can’t ignore it when pulling the takedown trigger. The ruling imposed a duty of good faith consideration—not an exhaustive legal analysis, but a basic, honest assessment. Did Universal consider that Lenz’s video transformed Prince’s song into a moment of family documentation? No. They’d treated fair use as irrelevant. The Ninth Circuit called that reckless.

Universal’s nightmare scenario—a deluge of lawsuits over minor takedowns—was dismissed as “overstated.” The court noted that copyright holders already assess infringement claims routinely; adding fair use required minimal effort. For Lenz, the victory was partial: Her video stayed online, but she still lacked damages to prove misrepresentation. She petitioned the Supreme Court for sharper clarity, asking whether a copyright holder’s belief in infringement could be “purely subjective”—meaning they could ignore fair use entirely without consequence. On June 19, 2017, the Court declined to hear the case, letting the Ninth Circuit’s duty stand. Lenz’s toddler, now a teenager, had helped cement a principle: Copyright isn’t a cudgel for silencing lawful speech.

The Unfinished Revolution

Lenz v. Universal was hailed as a win for internet freedom. But peel back the headlines, and the revolution was incomplete. Legal scholars like Harvard’s Lawrence Lessig noted the ruling left critical questions dangling. How much of a song can you use? What counts as “good faith”? The Ninth Circuit offered no bright-line rules—only a mandate to consider. This ambiguity haunts creators daily. A college student remixing movie clips for a film essay. A journalist embedding a viral protest video. They live in limbo, wondering if a corporation will deem their fair use “unreasonable” and pull the plug. Platforms like YouTube, meanwhile, still lean toward removing content to avoid liability, leaving users to navigate labyrinthine counter-notice processes.

Universal’s original takedown exposed a deeper pathology: the automation of censorship. Copyright bots scan platforms for audio fingerprints, flagging even 5-second snippets. In 2023, YouTube’s Content ID system issued over 100 million copyright claims—most automated, few reviewed by humans. The Lenz ruling was supposed to humanize this machine. Yet without clear penalties for bad-faith takedowns, companies often treat the “duty to consider” as a box-ticking formality. The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF), which backed Lenz, admits the decision “didn’t stop the takedowns—it just made them slightly less lawless.”

Prince’s posthumous irony is palpable. The artist who battled record labels for control of his music later became a symbol of corporate overreach. His 2007 vow to “reclaim [his] art on the internet” backfired spectacularly. The case he tried to weaponize against a mother’s home video instead became a shield for millions of creators. Yet the tension remains unresolved. When Disney sued a fan for posting Star Wars fan fiction in 2022, or when record labels recently targeted AI training datasets, the ghost of Lenz whispers: Did you even think about fair use?

Why Your Cat Video Matters

This isn’t about legal theory. It’s about the texture of your digital life. Every meme, TikTok dance, or Instagram story using copyrighted music exists in Lenz’s shadow. Before 2015, platforms could delete your content based on a robot’s false positive, and copyright holders faced zero consequences for being wrong. Now, there’s a legal barrier—but it’s fragile. Consider the math: Lenz’s video cost her nothing in damages, so she couldn’t sue successfully. Most users lack the resources to challenge takedowns. In 2021, 72% of DMCA counter-notices came from individuals, yet fewer than 1% led to lawsuits due to cost and complexity. The system still favors deep-pocketed rights holders.

Worse, the ruling’s vagueness lets companies game it. In 2019, a gaming company sent takedowns for YouTube essays criticizing its game, claiming copyright over brief gameplay clips—a clear fair use. When creators pushed back, the company amended its notices to say, “We considered fair use.” No details. No proof. Just a perfunctory nod to the law. The Ninth Circuit never defined what “consideration” entails, so compliance is self-policed. As law professor Rebecca Tushnet observed, “Lenz gave us a duty, but not a teeth.”

The real casualty? Innovation in the public sphere. Fair use fuels culture: Girl Scout troops singing copyrighted songs in campfire videos, teachers using movie clips in classrooms, activists sampling news broadcasts. When corporations treat takedowns as first resort, they don’t just remove content—they chill speech. A 2020 study found that 64% of creators altered or abandoned projects after receiving takedown notices, even when their use was likely fair. This isn’t piracy; it’s self-censorship induced by legal terror.

Lenz never wanted to be a crusader. She just wanted to keep her son’s video online. But her persistence exposed a truth the entertainment industry dreaded: The internet’s messy, user-generated creativity isn’t a bug—it’s the point. Prince’s music gained new life through that kitchen dance. Universal saw only theft; Lenz saw a moment of joy. The law, at last, agreed with her. Yet without clearer standards or real penalties for abuse, the takedown machine grinds on. Every time you hesitate before posting a clip of your kid singing along to the radio, you’re living in the unresolved aftermath of a 29-second video. The revolution was judicial. The war for the internet’s soul is still being fought—one takedown notice at a time.

The Supreme Court’s refusal to hear Lenz’s appeal in 2017 wasn’t an endorsement of the status quo. It was a silent admission that the Ninth Circuit had drawn the only line possible in the sand: Copyright law must bend to reality, not the other way around. But sand erodes. As AI generates art and platforms fragment, the question Lenz couldn’t answer looms larger: Who decides what’s fair? Until lawmakers or courts define that duty with teeth, the next Stephanie Lenz might not get her video back. She might not even know why it vanished.
