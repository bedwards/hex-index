PaR Systems

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: PaR Systems

In the shadow of the Chernobyl sarcophagus, where radiation levels remain high enough to kill a human in minutes, a machine performs a dance of impossible delicacy. Suspended from a bridge spanning ninety-six meters, a Mobile Tool Platform hangs like a spider on a wire, utilizing six degrees of freedom to manipulate robotic arms that dismantle a reactor building. This is not science fiction; it is the daily reality of PaR Systems, a firm headquartered in Shoreview, Minnesota, that has spent over sixty years engineering the unseeable and the unmanageable. While the average observer might associate Minnesota with agriculture or retail, the state is also a quiet powerhouse of high-stakes industrial engineering, a fact that PaR Systems has demonstrated with every crane it lifts and every hot cell it designs.

The story of PaR Systems is, at its core, a story of metamorphosis. It began not in the world of heavy industry, but in the cereal aisle. In 1961, the company was spun off from General Mills, the global food giant, under the name Programmed and Remote Systems Corporation. It is a surreal image to conjure: the engineers who would later build the machinery to contain nuclear meltdowns and launch aircraft off carrier decks once cut their teeth optimizing the logistics of corn flakes. This transition from food processing to nuclear engineering was not a random pivot but a testament to the company's foundational DNA: a rigorous, systems-engineering approach to solving complex automation problems. When the General Mills corporate structure shifted, the engineers took their methodology with them, applying the same precision required to package breakfast food to the far more volatile and high-consequence challenges of the nuclear and aerospace sectors.

By the time the company rebranded to PaR Systems, it had established a foothold in the most demanding environments on Earth. The firm operates on a simple but profound principle: if a human cannot safely do a job, a machine must be built to do it instead. This philosophy drives their work in the nuclear field, where they have become the premier designer of equipment for hot cells, process facilities, and decommissioning applications. A hot cell is a shielded room, typically lined with lead and concrete, designed to handle highly radioactive materials. Inside, remote manipulators allow operators to work as if they were in the room, while massive shielding keeps the radiation at bay. PaR Systems does not just build these rooms; they engineer the entire ecosystem of movement within them, ensuring that fuel rods, waste, and experimental materials can be moved with absolute certainty and zero error.

The scope of their nuclear portfolio is global, with significant projects spanning Japan, the United Kingdom, and the United States. However, no project looms larger in the annals of their history than the New Safe Confinement at Chernobyl. When the world gathered to seal the doomed reactor, it was PaR Systems that was awarded the subcontract for the Main Crane System (MCS). This was not a standard piece of lifting equipment. The system consists of two massive overhead bridge cranes, each supporting two 50-ton trolley hoists. But the true marvel is the Mobile Tool Platform (MTP). Suspended from a third trolley via a wire rope tensile truss with three paired winches, the MTP can move in any direction with six degrees of freedom. It is a platform of pure agility in a zone of static danger. Equipped with a wide variety of tools, including sophisticated manipulator arms, the MTP is tasked with the grim but necessary job of dismantling the original Sarcophagus and the reactor building itself. The goal is to move the radioactive material to more stable containment, a process that will take decades. The bridges themselves are engineering giants, running on a 150-meter long runway track with six rails. Along this track, shielded garages allow tools to be exchanged without exposing the machinery or the operators to unnecessary risk. Engineering, manufacturing, and assembly were all handled in-house by PaR Systems and its marine division, PaR Marine, a seamless integration of capability that few firms can match.

This level of specialization extends beyond the nuclear sector into the high-speed, high-precision world of aerospace. In the skies, where a fraction of a millimeter can mean the difference between flight and failure, PaR Systems provides the tools for precision cutting, trimming, drilling, coating, scanning, and non-destructive testing. The aerospace industry demands a level of quality that borders on the religious, and PaR Systems meets it with a suite of certifications that serve as their badge of honor: ISO9001 for general quality management, AS9100 for aerospace, ISO13485 for medical devices, and ASME NQA-1 for nuclear quality assurance. These are not merely stickers on a wall; they are the result of a culture that treats every bolt, every weld, and every line of code as a potential point of failure that must be anticipated and eliminated.

The reach of PaR Systems is perhaps most visible in the ocean, where their subsidiary, Jered LLC, has become a titan of marine equipment and cargo handling. Jered's claim to fame is written in the steel hulls of the world's most powerful warships. They were the primary contractor for the aircraft elevators on the Nimitz-class aircraft carriers, the massive vessels that project American power across the globe. But their footprint is even wider. They engineered the elevators for the Tarawa-class and Wasp-class amphibious assault ships, as well as half of the Iwo Jima-class ships for the US Navy. Their influence extends beyond American waters; the French aircraft carrier Charles de Gaulle and the Spanish aircraft carrier Príncipe de Asturias rely on Jered's engineering. An aircraft elevator on a carrier is a feat of mechanical engineering that must move tons of aircraft between the hangar deck and the flight deck with the speed and reliability of a factory floor, all while the ship pitches and rolls in the middle of an ocean. The margin for error is non-existent. If the elevator jams, the carrier's air wing is grounded. PaR Systems understands that their machines are the heartbeat of these floating cities.

Another subsidiary, Ederer LLC, focuses on the custom and specialty cranes that keep the world moving. These are not the stock cranes found in a home improvement store; these are bespoke giants designed for unique industrial challenges. Whether it is lifting a specialized component in a power plant or handling delicate cargo in a port, Ederer's cranes are the muscles of heavy industry. Then there is CAMotion, the division specializing in advanced motion control and Cartesian palletizers. In the modern world of logistics, speed is currency. CAMotion's systems organize, move, and palletize goods with a speed and accuracy that human hands cannot match, optimizing the flow of materials in warehouses and manufacturing plants. Finally, Oak River Technology brings this expertise to the medical field, specializing in automated manufacturing and testing equipment for medical devices. Here, the stakes are biological rather than structural, but the requirement for precision remains the same. Every syringe, every implant, and every diagnostic tool that rolls off an Oak River line is a testament to the company's ability to adapt its core engineering principles to the most sensitive industry of all.

The company's product lines are a mix of these custom marvels and standardized solutions. One of their most recognizable standardized products is the Vector brand line of 5-axis waterjet cutters. While the name sounds mundane, the technology is extraordinary. These cutters use high-pressure water, often mixed with an abrasive, to slice through materials with the precision of a laser but without the heat that can warp metals. The 5-axis capability allows the cutting head to move in any direction, enabling the cutting of complex, three-dimensional objects. A car part, a turbine blade, or a piece of art can be cut with a single pass, no matter how twisted or curved the geometry. This versatility makes the Vector line a staple in industries ranging from automotive to aerospace, proving that PaR Systems can scale its technology from the bespoke to the mass-market.

Ownership of such a specialized firm often changes hands, but the narrative of PaR Systems is one of continuity and local roots. In 2017, the company was acquired by the Minneapolis-based Pohlad family. The Pohlads are a name synonymous with Minnesota business, best known for owning the Minnesota Twins baseball team and a vast portfolio of real estate and investments. The acquisition of PaR Systems by a local family firm signals a commitment to keeping this high-tech industrial capability in the Twin Cities ecosystem. It ensures that the engineering talent cultivated in Shoreview continues to drive innovation without being diluted by distant corporate mandates. The Pohlad family's involvement suggests a long-term vision, recognizing that the company's expertise in nuclear decommissioning, aerospace manufacturing, and marine logistics is not just a business line, but a strategic asset.

The history of PaR Systems is a chronicle of solving problems that others deemed impossible. When they were spun off from General Mills in 1961, they were a small firm with a big idea: that remote systems could solve problems humans couldn't. Six decades later, that idea has grown into a global empire of engineering excellence. From the depths of the Chernobyl exclusion zone to the flight decks of Nimitz-class carriers, from the clean rooms of medical device manufacturing to the high-speed lines of automotive parts production, PaR Systems is there. They are the silent partners in our most critical infrastructure, the unseen hands that move the world's most dangerous and valuable materials.

The rhythm of their work is defined by the tension between the massive and the microscopic. They build bridges that are ninety-six meters long, yet they design manipulator arms that can handle a radioactive fuel rod with the delicacy of a surgeon. They operate in environments where a single mistake can lead to catastrophe, yet they achieve a level of reliability that allows them to serve as the primary contractor for the most advanced navies in the world. This duality is the essence of systems engineering. It is the understanding that the whole is greater than the sum of its parts, and that every component, from the tiniest sensor to the largest crane, must function in perfect harmony.

As the world moves toward a future of nuclear decommissioning, advanced manufacturing, and increasingly complex aerospace challenges, the role of firms like PaR Systems will only grow. The need to safely dismantle the reactors of the past, to build the aircraft of the future, and to handle the materials of a high-tech economy requires a level of engineering sophistication that few possess. PaR Systems, with its diverse portfolio of subsidiaries and its deep roots in Minnesota, is uniquely positioned to meet these challenges. They have proven that they can adapt to any industry, any environment, and any scale. From the cereal factories of the 1960s to the nuclear graveyards of the 21st century, their journey is a testament to the power of human ingenuity when applied with precision and purpose.

The legacy of PaR Systems is not just in the machines they build, but in the safety and efficiency they provide. They allow humans to stay out of the radiation, out of the danger, and out of the way, while their machines do the heavy lifting. They are the guardians of the high-risk frontier, ensuring that progress does not come at the cost of safety. In a world that often feels chaotic and unpredictable, the work of PaR Systems offers a glimpse of order and control. They take the most chaotic environments imaginable—the heart of a nuclear reactor, the storm-tossed deck of an aircraft carrier—and impose a system of perfect order upon them. It is a quiet, unglamorous, but absolutely essential work. And as long as there are difficult problems to solve, PaR Systems will be there to solve them, one automated system at a time.

The story of PaR Systems is far from over. With the Pohlad family at the helm and a legacy of innovation stretching back to 1961, the company is poised for the next chapter. Whether it is developing new technologies for the next generation of nuclear power, creating smarter aerospace manufacturing lines, or building the next class of shipboard cranes, the future of PaR Systems is as bright and complex as the problems they solve. They have shown that with the right engineering, the impossible becomes routine, and the dangerous becomes manageable. In the end, that is the true measure of a systems engineering firm: not just the machines they build, but the confidence they instill in the world that those machines will work, every single time.
