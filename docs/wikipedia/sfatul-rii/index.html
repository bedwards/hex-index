Sfatul Țării

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Sfatul Țării

In the chaotic spring of 1917, a massive crowd of over 10,000 Bessarabian soldiers gathered in the port city of Odessa, their faces turned not toward the front lines of a grinding World War I, but toward a future that seemed to be evaporating in the dust of the collapsing Russian Empire. They were not merely an army; they were a nation in waiting, a swelling tide of peasants and intellectuals who had been drafted into a foreign war but were rapidly discovering a shared identity that transcended the borders of Tsarist Russia. This gathering was the catalyst for Sfatul Țării, the "Council of the Country," a body that would emerge from the ashes of imperial order to declare the Moldavian Democratic Republic and, within eighteen months, engineer one of the most significant geopolitical shifts of the 20th century: the union of Bessarabia with Romania.

To understand the magnitude of Sfatul Țării, one must first grasp the sheer volatility of the region. By August 1914, the First World War had already shattered the quiet of the Governorate of Bessarabia. The war machine demanded bodies, and Russia answered by mobilizing 300,000 Bessarabians into its army. This was not a voluntary enlistment of patriots but a desperate conscription that stripped the countryside of its young men just as the military situation on the Eastern Front began to deteriorate. By March 1917, the front had stagnated into a bloody stalemate, and the rear guard became a cauldron of political ferment. The Tsar had abdicated in March, leaving a vacuum filled by the Provisional Government, a hesitant administration that had not yet even proclaimed the Empire a Republic—a move that would not happen until September.

In this liminal space between monarchy and republic, the soldiers' conferences began to dominate the landscape. These were not quiet gatherings of military discipline but raucous demands for a new world order. The soldiers, many of whom were illiterate peasants thrust into the chaos of modern warfare, articulated a clear vision: they wanted social and economic transformation. They called for the annulment of noble privileges and, most urgently, an agrarian reform that would finally hand the land to the peasants who tilled it. Despite the crumbling state of the Russian Empire, the army did not simply disband. Units remained stationed, but the chain of command had been severed. Officers were no longer appointed by the Tsar; they were replaced by elected leaders chosen by the men themselves. The old army continued to exist, but it moved only with the consent of the general command and the political will of the soldiers.

This political awakening was inextricably linked to language and identity. The soldiers began to make demands that went beyond economics; they insisted on the use of the national language in administration, courts, education, and church services. For the Bessarabian soldiers, this was not an abstract academic debate. Many had interacted with soldiers from the Kingdom of Romania and ethnic Romanians from Transylvania and Bukovina, who had been taken as prisoners of war by the Austrians and subsequently organized into regiments fighting on the side of the Entente. These contacts had been strengthening for years. Even before 1914, a trans-imperial network of Romanian intelligentsia was at work, sharing tactics and visions for a national homeland. Some dreamed of uniting all Romanian lands under the Austrian crown; others envisioned an independent state, torn between the gravitational pull of the Central Powers and the Entente.

The Kingdom of Romania, at the time, was a small nation with almost no influence over the two massive empires flanking it. Less than half of all Romanians lived within its borders. The majority were scattered across the empires of Austria-Hungary and Russia, a fact that forced the intelligentsia in Transylvania, Bukovina, and Bessarabia to seek political empowerment through their own means. The flow of ideas was relentless. Transylvanian newspapers like Ardealul, edited by Onisifor Ghibu, circulated widely in Bessarabia, despite the Russian authorities' attempts to suppress local publications like Cuvânt moldovenesc and Viața Basarabiei. When the war recruited these intellectuals into the ranks of low-rank officers, the dissemination of nationalist ideas became even more potent.

The February Revolution in Petrograd acted as a detonator. In the wake of the Tsar's fall, "Moldavian Revolutionary Committees of Soldiers" sprang up in every major city where Bessarabian recruits were concentrated: Odessa, Kiev, Sevastopol, Kherson, Novogeorgievsk, and Moroski in Russia, as well as Iași, Roman, and Bârlad on the Romanian front. Their banners were emblazoned with slogans that would define the era: "Land and liberty," "Down with the war," and "Peoples' right to self-determination." April 1917 was the peak of this mobilization, a month where the political consciousness of the region crystallized.

The Odessa meeting on May 2 (Old Style: April 18) stands as a defining moment. More than 10,000 Bessarabian soldiers convened, joined by students and intellectuals like Emanoil Catelli and Baluță. The resolution they adopted was a masterclass in political pragmatism. It demanded political autonomy for Bessarabia and, crucially, the organization of specific Moldavian military units, or cohorts. This was a direct response to the anarchy that was engulfing the region. As the Russian army disintegrated, deserting irregulars were terrorizing the local population. The soldiers, far from home, were terrified for the fate of their families. The resolution also secured permission for Romanian history and literature courses at the University of Odessa, a small but vital step in reclaiming the cultural narrative.

Following this, the momentum shifted from the barracks to the villages. With the cessation of active hostilities on the front, a wave of meetings and congresses swept through Bessarabia. Led by teachers and local intellectuals, committees were formed in virtually every village. These local bodies fed into county-wide and province-wide congresses representing the major pillars of society: peasants, teachers, cooperators, and clergy. On April 19–20, 1917, the First Congress of Cooperatives of Bessarabia met in Chișinău. They voted a motion that was remarkably comprehensive, demanding political, administrative, educational, religious, and economic autonomy. They called for the formation of a legislative assembly, explicitly naming it Sfatul Țării.

This was not a solitary voice. The momentum was cumulative. On May 2–5, 1917, a Congress of Clergy gathered in Chișinău, demanding a Moldavian archbishop to lead the church in Bessarabia and establishing a High Council as a national legislative and executive body. Similar motions echoed through all nine counties of the province. The General Congress of Bessarabian Teachers, also held in Chișinău, passed a resolution that would reshape the cultural landscape: primary education would switch from Russian to Romanian, and the Latin alphabet would replace the Cyrillic script. These were not mere administrative tweaks; they were acts of cultural reclamation.

The Congress of Moldavian Teachers on June 7–10, 1917, became a battleground for identity. Alexei Mateevici delivered a stirring speech urging Bessarabians to identify as "Romanians" rather than "Moldavians," while Iulie Frățiman argued that the areas beyond the Dniester inhabited by Romanians should be administered by Bessarabia. Yet, the path to unity was not without friction. Several delegates protested the label "Romanian," insisting they were "Moldavian." This internal debate highlighted the complex tapestry of identities in the region, where local pride and national aspiration often overlapped and clashed. Despite these divergences, the call for self-rule was unanimous.

Throughout April, May, and June, Peasant Congresses proliferated at local levels, demanding land reform and social justice. The culmination of this grassroots movement came on October 14–15, 1917, with the First General Congress of Peasants of All Bessarabia in Chișinău. The report presented to the Congress painted a grim picture: the land was being overrun by an "anarchic and public disorder current" that the Kerensky administration in Petrograd could no longer control. The chaos was real. The government had lost its grip. The Congress decided that the time for waiting was over. They founded a Provisional Council of 100 members, deliberately composed of 70 Moldavians and 30 representatives from minority groups, acknowledging the region's diversity while centering the majority will.

The formation of this council was the direct precursor to Sfatul Țării as a formal legislative body. The organizational commission was set up, though the initial steps were fraught with difficulty. The political vacuum left by the February Revolution had been filled by a new governor, but the real power lay with the people's committees. When the governor of Bessarabia stepped down, he passed his legal powers to Constantin Mimi, the President of the Cooperative Union, a move that signaled the transfer of authority from the imperial appointee to the local leadership.

The months that followed were a blur of revolutionary fervor and diplomatic maneuvering. The Bolshevik Revolution in October 1917 added a new layer of complexity. As the Bolsheviks seized power in Petrograd, the situation in Bessarabia became increasingly precarious. The anarchy that the Peasant Congress had feared was now a reality. The Russian army was dissolving, and bands of deserters were looting and pillaging. In this context, the demand for a Moldavian military force became a matter of survival. Sfatul Țării, which had been envisioned in the spring, was now a necessity. It was established in December 1917, officially creating the Moldavian Democratic Republic as part of the Russian Federative Republic.

The declaration of the republic was a bold stroke of political theater and reality. It was an attempt to carve out a space of order amidst the chaos of the collapsing empire. Sfatul Țării was not just a council; it was the embodiment of the will of the Bessarabian people. It represented the convergence of the soldier's demand for land and safety, the teacher's demand for language and culture, and the priest's demand for spiritual autonomy. The council was composed of representatives from all walks of life, a microcosm of the society it sought to govern.

The journey from the congresses of 1917 to the union in 1918 was rapid and dramatic. The council faced immense challenges. The Russian Empire was gone, replaced by a civil war that threatened to swallow the region. The Bolsheviks were advancing, and the Romanian army was moving north to stabilize the border. Sfatul Țării had to navigate these treacherous waters with skill and determination. They declared the union with Romania in April 1918 (Old Style: March), a decision that was both a rejection of Russian chaos and an embrace of a shared national destiny.

The union was not without its critics and complications. The minority groups, represented in the council, had their own concerns. The international community, particularly the Great Powers, viewed the union with a mix of skepticism and strategic interest. Yet, the momentum was undeniable. The people of Bessarabia had spoken, first in the soldiers' meetings in Odessa, then in the village congresses, and finally in the halls of Sfatul Țării. They had demanded autonomy, land, and language, and they had achieved a union that would define the region's future for decades.

The story of Sfatul Țării is a testament to the power of grassroots political organization. It shows how a disparate group of soldiers, peasants, teachers, and priests could come together to forge a national identity and reshape the map of Europe. It was a movement born of necessity, fueled by the chaos of war, and sustained by the unwavering belief in the right to self-determination. The council was more than a legislative body; it was the voice of a nation finding its own voice.

In the end, the legacy of Sfatul Țării is complex. It was a short-lived experiment in democracy, overshadowed by the geopolitical storms of the 20th century. But its achievements were real. It established the Moldavian Democratic Republic, it secured the union with Romania, and it laid the groundwork for the cultural and political life of the region. The demands made in 1917—land, language, autonomy—were not just slogans; they were the foundation of a new society. The council's story is a reminder that history is not just made by kings and generals, but by the people who dare to imagine a different future.

The events of 1917 and 1918 were not inevitable. They were the result of choices made by thousands of individuals who refused to accept the status quo. They chose to organize, to speak, and to act. They chose to build a council of the country, a body that would stand as a symbol of their aspirations. Sfatul Țării remains a powerful example of what can be achieved when a people unite around a common vision. It is a story of courage, of conviction, and of the enduring quest for freedom.

The transition from the chaos of the Russian Empire to the union with Romania was a pivotal moment in the history of the region. It marked the end of an era and the beginning of another. The council that emerged from the fires of revolution was a testament to the resilience of the Bessarabian people. It was a body that dared to dream of a future where the land belonged to the peasants, the language belonged to the people, and the country belonged to itself. That dream, though challenged by the storms of history, remains a vital part of the region's identity today.

The legacy of Sfatul Țării is not just in the laws it passed or the borders it drew, but in the spirit it unleashed. It was a spirit of self-determination, of cultural pride, and of political engagement. It showed that even in the darkest times, when empires crumble and armies disintegrate, the human capacity for organization and hope can create something new. The council was a beacon in the storm, guiding the people of Bessarabia toward a future of their own making. And in doing so, it left an indelible mark on the history of the world.

The story of Sfatul Țării is a story of a people rising up to claim their destiny. It is a story of soldiers, peasants, teachers, and priests who came together to build a nation. It is a story of a council that dared to say "enough" to chaos and "yes" to freedom. And it is a story that continues to resonate, reminding us of the power of the people to shape their own history.
