Lewis F. Powell Jr.

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Lewis F. Powell Jr.

The Memo That Rewired American Politics

In the summer of 1971, a successful corporate lawyer in Richmond, Virginia sat down to write a confidential memo for the United States Chamber of Commerce. He had no idea he was drafting a blueprint that would reshape American political life for the next half-century.

Lewis Powell was sixty-three years old. He had spent decades defending tobacco companies, sat on the board of Philip Morris, and watched with growing alarm as consumer advocates, environmentalists, and liberal intellectuals seemed to be winning the battle for America's soul. His memo, titled "Attack on the American Free Enterprise System," would become one of the most consequential political documents of the twentieth century—more influential, arguably, than many Supreme Court decisions he would later write.

Two months after writing it, Richard Nixon appointed Powell to the Supreme Court.

A Virginia Gentleman's Path to Power

Powell came from the old South, born in Suffolk, Virginia in 1907. He was the kind of student who collected accolades the way others collected stamps: president of his fraternity at Washington and Lee University, managing editor of the student newspaper, graduated magna cum laude with Phi Beta Kappa honors. He earned his law degree as class valedictorian, then picked up a Master of Laws from Harvard for good measure.

At a leadership conference during his college years, Powell befriended another ambitious young man named Edward R. Murrow—the same Murrow who would later become America's most celebrated broadcast journalist and famously take down Senator Joseph McCarthy on national television. The friendship endured, though the two men's views on American institutions would diverge considerably.

When World War II erupted, Powell tried to join the Navy but was rejected for poor eyesight. The Army Air Forces proved less discriminating about his vision, and he spent the war as an intelligence officer. His service took him from North Africa during Operation Torch to Sicily during the Allied invasion, and eventually to the Ultra project—one of the war's most closely guarded secrets.

Ultra was the code name for intelligence derived from breaking German and Japanese encryption. Powell's job was to ensure that when military commanders used this intercepted information, they did so without revealing its source. If the enemy discovered their codes had been cracked, they would change them, and the advantage would evaporate. It was painstaking, high-stakes work that required understanding how small decisions could cascade into enormous consequences.

After the bombing of Dresden in February 1945, Powell wrote with striking candor: "Personally, I consider this very fortunate indeed as the German people are being taught for the first time in modern history what it means to have war on their own soil." He advanced to colonel and collected a Legion of Merit, Bronze Star, and French Croix de Guerre before returning to civilian life.

The Corporate Lawyer's World

Powell spent the next quarter-century at Hunton, Williams, Gay, Powell and Gibson—a white-shoe Virginia law firm now known as Hunton Andrews Kurth. He specialized in corporate law: business acquisitions, securities regulation, bankruptcy, real estate, railroad litigation. His clients included the Tobacco Institute and various tobacco companies.

This matters enormously for understanding the memo he would later write.

By the mid-1960s, scientific evidence linking cigarettes to cancer was becoming overwhelming. The Surgeon General's landmark 1964 report had declared smoking a cause of lung cancer. Consumer advocates were demanding warning labels, advertising restrictions, and corporate accountability.

Powell, representing the tobacco industry, argued that these measures violated his clients' First Amendment rights. He complained that news organizations weren't giving fair coverage to the industry's cancer denials. He saw the growing consumer movement not as a reasonable response to corporate malfeasance, but as a step toward socialism.

Then Ralph Nader came along.

The Consumer Crusader Who Alarmed Corporate America

In 1965, a young lawyer named Ralph Nader published "Unsafe at Any Speed," an exposé arguing that American automakers were prioritizing profits over safety. The book focused particularly on the Chevrolet Corvair, whose rear-engine design made it prone to dangerous handling problems that General Motors knew about but refused to fix.

General Motors responded by hiring private investigators to dig up dirt on Nader. They tailed him, tried to trap him with prostitutes, and investigated his personal life. When this surveillance became public, it backfired spectacularly. GM's president was forced to apologize before a Senate committee. Nader sued and won, using the settlement money to fund new consumer advocacy organizations.

The American consumer movement was born.

To Powell, watching from his Richmond law office, this looked like the beginning of the end. Here was the American economic system—the system that had made him wealthy, that had created the corporations he represented, that had built the most prosperous nation in human history—under sustained attack. And the attackers weren't just wild-eyed radicals.

"The most disquieting voices joining the chorus of criticism," Powell would write, "came from perfectly respectable elements of society: from the college campus, the pulpit, the media, the intellectual and literary journals, the arts and sciences, and from politicians."

Respectable people were turning against business. That was the crisis.

The Memo

On August 23, 1971, Powell's neighbor Eugene Sydnor Jr.—education director of the U.S. Chamber of Commerce—asked him to write a confidential memorandum. Sydnor wanted a strategy document. What should American business do about the growing chorus of criticism?

Powell's response ran to several thousand words and laid out a comprehensive plan for corporate America to reshape American thought.

The enemy, as Powell saw it, wasn't just Nader or a few troublesome professors. It was an entire intellectual and cultural infrastructure that had turned hostile to free enterprise. The solution, therefore, had to be equally comprehensive. Business couldn't just defend itself case by case. It needed to build a counter-infrastructure.

Powell called for "constant surveillance" of textbook content to identify and purge left-wing elements. He urged business leaders to fund scholars who would produce research favorable to free enterprise, to publish books and articles, to create magazines and journals, to influence public opinion systematically.

Television, in particular, needed attention. Powell noted that the national networks reached "the homes of millions of Americans every night" and argued that business should demand equal time to respond to criticism, monitor programming for anti-business bias, and cultivate friendly voices in the media.

Universities were another battleground. Powell proposed establishing speakers' bureaus, endowing professorships, funding research centers, and ensuring that business perspectives received representation in curricula that he saw as dominated by critics of capitalism.

Perhaps most consequentially, Powell emphasized the courts. American business, he argued, had been too passive in litigation, allowing labor unions and civil rights groups to shape constitutional law to their advantage. Business needed its own public-interest law firms, its own legal theorists, its own pipeline of judges and clerks who understood that the Constitution protected economic liberty as surely as it protected free speech.

The memo was blunt about power: "Business must learn the lesson... that political power is necessary; that such power must be assiduously cultivated; and that when necessary, it must be used aggressively and with determination."

From Memo to Movement

Powell sent his memorandum to Sydnor, who circulated it within the Chamber of Commerce. Two months later, Nixon nominated Powell to the Supreme Court. The memo remained confidential—for about a year.

Then Jack Anderson, an investigative columnist at the Washington Post, got hold of it. Anderson published excerpts and accused Powell of trying to undermine democracy. The revelation generated some controversy but no lasting damage to Powell's reputation. By then he was already on the Court, confirmed 89-1 by the Senate.

But the memo's ideas were spreading.

Wealthy conservative foundations—the Earhart Foundation (oil money), the Smith Richardson Foundation (cough medicine money), the Carthage Foundation (Mellon banking fortune)—began funding exactly the kind of institutions Powell had envisioned. The Heritage Foundation was established in 1973. The Cato Institute followed in 1977. The Manhattan Institute for Policy Research came in 1978. The American Legislative Exchange Council, or ALEC, which would later draft model legislation for state governments across the country, gained new momentum.

The Business Roundtable organized in 1972, bringing together CEOs of major corporations for coordinated political action. The Chamber of Commerce, which had been a relatively sleepy trade association, transformed into an aggressive lobbying force.

Within a decade, the infrastructure Powell had imagined was largely in place. Conservative think tanks were producing policy papers, training young intellectuals, placing op-eds, and building the network of ideas and personnel that would staff the Reagan administration and shape American politics for the next forty years.

Historians have given this phenomenon various names. David Harvey, a geographer at the City University of New York, traces the rise of neoliberalism in America directly to the Powell Memo. Gary Gerstle, a historian, calls it "a neoliberal call to arms." Political scientist Aaron Good uses a more provocative term: "an inverted totalitarian manifesto."

Justice Powell's Constitutional Vision

Meanwhile, Lewis Powell was busy on the Supreme Court.

He served from 1972 to 1987, mostly under Chief Justice Warren Burger. Powell quickly became the Court's crucial swing vote—the justice whose position often determined which side prevailed in close cases. This gave him enormous influence over American constitutional law.

Several of his opinions directly reflected the concerns of his pre-Court memo.

In First National Bank of Boston v. Bellotti (1978), Powell wrote for the majority that corporations had First Amendment rights to spend money on ballot initiatives, even when the issues at stake had nothing to do with their business operations. The case arose when a Massachusetts bank wanted to spend money opposing a state income tax referendum. The state said corporations couldn't spend on issues unrelated to their commercial interests. Powell said the state was wrong.

The First Amendment, Powell argued, protects not just speakers but the value of speech to listeners. It doesn't matter whether the speaker is a person or a corporation. What matters is that the public has access to the ideas. Corporate speech about political matters deserves the same protection as individual speech.

This reasoning would reappear three decades later in Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission, the 2010 decision that struck down limits on corporate and union spending in elections. The Citizens United majority relied heavily on Bellotti. Powell, in his memo, had called for business to fight back through the courts. In Bellotti, he delivered.

The Contradictions

Powell was not a simple ideologue. His record on the Court contains genuine surprises.

He joined the majority in Roe v. Wade (1973), the landmark decision establishing a constitutional right to abortion. He wrote the controlling opinion in Regents of the University of California v. Bakke (1978), a case about affirmative action in university admissions, where he struck down rigid racial quotas but upheld the use of race as one factor among many—a compromise that shaped affirmative action law for the next quarter-century.

In Plyler v. Doe (1982), Powell joined the majority in ruling that Texas could not deny free public education to undocumented immigrant children. In Solem v. Helm (1983), he wrote an opinion striking down a life sentence without parole for a man whose most serious offense was writing a bad check for $100—finding the punishment constitutionally disproportionate.

But he also joined the majority in Bowers v. Hardwick (1986), upholding a Georgia law criminalizing sodomy between consenting adults. He later expressed regret about that vote, telling a group of law students in 1990 that he had "probably made a mistake." The Court overruled Bowers in 2003.

In McCleskey v. Kemp (1987), Powell wrote for a 5-4 majority rejecting a challenge to Georgia's death penalty. The defendant, Warren McCleskey, presented statistical evidence showing that defendants in Georgia were far more likely to receive the death penalty if their victim was white rather than Black. Powell acknowledged the statistics but held that McCleskey needed to prove intentional discrimination in his own case—a nearly impossible burden. The decision effectively insulated systemic racial disparities in capital punishment from constitutional challenge.

Powell later told his biographer that if he could change his vote in any case, it would be McCleskey. He had come to believe the death penalty was unconstitutional. But by then he had retired, and his regret changed nothing.

The School Board Years

Before the Supreme Court, before the memo, Powell had faced the central moral crisis of his generation of white Southerners: the desegregation of public schools.

He served on the Richmond School Board from 1951 to 1961, chairing it from 1952 onward. This meant he presided over Richmond's schools during the period of "Massive Resistance"—Virginia's organized defiance of Brown v. Board of Education, the 1954 Supreme Court decision declaring school segregation unconstitutional.

Virginia's response to Brown was extraordinary. The state enacted laws closing any public school that tried to integrate, cutting off state funds to integrated schools, and providing tuition grants for white students to attend private "segregation academies." In Prince Edward County, the public schools closed entirely for five years rather than integrate, from 1959 to 1964.

Powell's law firm, though not Powell personally, represented one of the school districts sued in the original Brown case—Davis v. County School Board of Prince Edward County, which was consolidated with Brown. Powell himself had clients who were resisting integration. His institutional position was clear.

Yet Powell did not join the firebrands. He maintained relationships with Black leaders, including civil rights lawyer Oliver Hill. He never gave fiery speeches defending segregation. When Nixon nominated him to the Supreme Court in 1971, several Black leaders in Richmond supported his confirmation.

Was this moral courage or careful calculation? Genuine moderation or strategic positioning? The historical record permits multiple readings. Powell did not lead the resistance. But he did not oppose it either. He presided over a segregated school system for a decade, navigating carefully, offending no one powerful, and emerging with his reputation intact.

In 1990, Powell swore in Douglas Wilder as Virginia's first Black governor—a ceremony rich with historical irony.

The Legacy

Powell retired from the Supreme Court in 1987, during Ronald Reagan's administration. He was succeeded, after a dramatic confirmation battle that rejected Robert Bork, by Anthony Kennedy.

He died in 1998, at ninety years old. His wife Josephine had died two years earlier.

By then, the vision he had articulated in his 1971 memo had been realized beyond anything he might have imagined. The conservative intellectual infrastructure he called for—the think tanks, the legal foundations, the academic centers, the media outlets—had become the dominant force in American political life. The Chamber of Commerce had become one of the most powerful lobbying organizations in Washington. Corporate speech rights had expanded dramatically.

During the 2024 presidential election, journalists and scholars pointed to the Powell Memo as a direct intellectual ancestor of Project 2025—the Heritage Foundation's comprehensive plan for a potential Republican administration. The through-line was clear: Powell had called for building an institutional infrastructure to advance conservative ideas and train conservative personnel for government. Half a century later, that infrastructure was producing detailed transition plans and personnel databases.

Whether this represents the vindication of Powell's vision or its corruption depends, of course, on your politics. He saw himself as defending the American system against its critics. His critics saw him as subverting democracy on behalf of corporate power.

What seems beyond dispute is the memo's influence. A confidential strategy document written for a trade association by a lawyer about to join the Supreme Court became, in the words of historian Kim Phillips-Fein, "a blueprint for the resurgence of the American right." It showed that ideas, systematically funded and strategically promoted, could reshape political reality. It demonstrated that building institutions matters as much as winning elections.

Powell understood something about power that his opponents were slow to learn: the battle for American politics would be won not just in voting booths but in think tanks, law schools, newspaper op-ed pages, and foundation boardrooms. The memo was his battle plan. The decades that followed were the war.
