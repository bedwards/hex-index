People's commune

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: People's commune

In the summer of 1958, the Chinese countryside underwent a transformation so radical that the very concept of a family home began to vanish. Across vast swathes of rural China, individual households were dismantled, their walls torn down to feed the kilns of the Great Leap Forward, while families were herded into massive communal residences where men and women, strangers and kin alike, lived in segregated dormitories. The private kitchen, the hearth of domestic life, was abolished in favor of colossal public mess halls where the state served meals on a scale previously unimaginable. This was not a gradual shift in policy; it was a violent, sweeping reordering of human existence known as the People's Commune. Between 1958 and 1983, this system served as the highest administrative level in rural China, a colossal machine designed to extract labor, pool resources, and forge a new society. It was an experiment that promised to accelerate history, to leapfrog from agrarian feudalism directly into full communism, but it ultimately became a monument to the dangers of imposing ideological purity upon the complex realities of human life and agriculture.

To understand the magnitude of the commune, one must first understand the soil from which it sprang. Before the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) seized power in 1949, rural China was a landscape of stark inequality and fragmented ownership. Landlords held almost half of the arable land, leasing it to tenant farmers who tilled the earth under the shadow of debt and insecurity. Most farms were small, family-operated enterprises, vulnerable to the whims of the harvest cycle. Farmers experienced a rhythm of life defined by intense busyness during harvest and a grinding idleness during the off-seasons. The CCP, upon establishing the People's Republic, recognized that this structure was not only economically inefficient but politically unstable. Their initial response was a thorough land reform, a revolutionary process that identified and classified the rural population, stripping landlords of their holdings and redistributing the land to middle and poor peasants. For a brief, shining moment, individual families owned the land they farmed. They paid taxes as households and sold their grain at state-set prices, a system that offered a semblance of stability.

But the CCP had never intended for this individual ownership to last. The party's vision was always one of total collectivization. Soon after announcing its "general line for the transition to socialism" in 1953, the state began a systematic march toward the elimination of private property in the countryside. The process was incremental, designed to acclimatize the peasantry to the loss of autonomy. First came the Mutual Aid Teams, small groups of up to 68 million families by 1954 that coordinated the sharing of labor, tools, and technology. These were followed by Agricultural Producer Cooperatives (APCs), larger institutions at the village level where land and capital were pooled. By June 1956, the pace accelerated dramatically. Over 60% of rural households had been consolidated into higher-level agricultural producers' cooperatives (HAPCs), a structure modeled on the Soviet kolkhoz. In these cooperatives, tens of households pooled their land and draft animals. The individual's right to the fruit of their labor was replaced by a complex system of work points. Adult members were credited based on the quantity and type of labor they provided. At the year's end, the collective deducted taxes, fulfilled state quotas at fixed prices, and set aside seed and welfare funds. Only the remainder was distributed to households, split partly by work points and partly by a standard rate based on age and sex. Even then, a small concession remained: cooperatives lent tiny plots of land back to individual households for personal consumption or market sale.

It was within this framework of growing state control that Mao Zedong, in 1958, decided to discard the gradualism of the past. The political atmosphere had shifted. Following the Hundred Flowers Campaign and the subsequent Anti-Rightist Campaign, Mao turned his focus from steady economic growth toward the rapid, violent establishment of communism. The goal was no longer just to build socialism, but to bypass it entirely and arrive at the final stage of human history. For Mao, achieving communism required not only economic abundance but the total eradication of "feudal" habits and the creation of a new human consciousness. Propaganda outlets began to publicize the emergence of a giant collective in Xushui, Hebei, where the traditional concept of the household had been dissolved. Here, peasants supposedly no longer worried about money or food, living in a state of pure communal abundance. Mao visited Xushui and similar units in Henan, declaring, "People's communes are good." This endorsement was all the signal the party needed. The construction of communes became the central pillar of the Great Leap Forward, a movement that sought to industrialize China overnight and surpass Britain in steel production.

The consolidation was swift and total. Over the summer of 1958, the existing agricultural producers' collectives were merged into massive new entities. The CCP leadership called these giant administrative and economic regions "People's Communes." The term itself was heavy with ideological weight, tracing back to Western Europe and the concept of autonomous cities. Influenced by Robert Owen, Friedrich Engels had used the term to describe the basic unit of a Communist society, and Karl Marx viewed it as a form of proletariat governance. Mao adopted this vision, intending the commune to be the fundamental building block of Chinese society, ruled by the working class. He famously explained the logic of the new structure: "They're called people's communes, first, because they're big and, second because they're public. Lots of people, a vast area of land, large scale of production, and all their undertakings are done in a big way. They integrate government administration with commune management to establish public mess halls, and private plots are eliminated."

The scale of these units was staggering. By the end of 1958, the state had consolidated thousands of smaller cooperatives into roughly 26,000 communes. Each commune was a microcosm of a city-state, containing on average 4,500 hectares of land, 24,000 people, and 5,200 households. Some communes were even larger, encompassing up to one hundred previous cooperatives and a market town with its surrounding villages. In total, there were between 50,000 and 90,000 such communes operating across the country. These were not merely agricultural units; they were the highest level of administration in rural China, a fusion of government and economic management. The commune administration held absolute authority over the lives of its members. Land, tools, and resources were pooled together, and working hours and farming practices were completely dictated by the CCP. The individual's connection to the land was severed; the family's connection to its own home was severed. The state now owned everything, and the people were merely cogs in a vast, mobilized machine.

The logic behind this massive consolidation was twofold: industrialization and militarization. The communes were designed to be the engines of China's industrial boom. The sheer scale of the units allowed administrations to extract income from the rural population and invest it in large-scale mechanization, infrastructure projects, and, most infamously, backyard steel furnaces. The belief was that by pooling labor, the state could mobilize millions of people to work on irrigation projects, build roads, and smelt steel, achieving productivity levels that small, family farms could never hope to match. This was the era of "surpassing Britain and catching up to the US," a race against time and the West. But the communes were also defined by a second, more insidious characteristic: the militarization of society. Commune members were mobilized through military-style campaigns, exhorted to act with rigid discipline, devotion, and selflessness. The countryside became a barracks, and the farmers, soldiers in a war against nature and scarcity. The communal living arrangements, where men and women were often segregated regardless of their relationships, were intended to break the bonds of the traditional family and replace them with loyalty to the collective.

"They integrate government administration with commune management to establish public mess halls, and private plots are eliminated."

The implications of this system for daily life were profound and often devastating. The abolition of individual homes was not just a logistical change; it was a psychological assault on the rural population. Many individual homes were demolished, their bricks and timber repurposed for the commune's infrastructure. Families were uprooted and placed in communal residences, often with multiple families crammed into single structures. The public mess halls, intended to liberate women from housework and free them for production, became centers of control. Food was distributed based on the whims of the administration and the state's grain quotas. The promise that "peasants" would no longer worry about food was a lie; in reality, the centralization of food distribution, combined with the unrealistic production targets of the Great Leap Forward, led to catastrophic famines. The system's inability to accurately measure production or respond to local conditions meant that grain was often requisitioned even when harvests failed, leaving the commune members to starve.

Despite the grandiose ambitions, the People's Communes failed to meet their long-term goals. They did not facilitate the construction of full communism in the rural areas; instead, they entrenched a system of poverty and coercion. They did not fully liberate women from housework; the lack of infrastructure and the burden of production often left them with double the workload, now in the fields and now in the communal kitchens. They did not create sustainable agriculture practices; the focus on steel production and the disregard for ecological limits led to soil degradation and the destruction of natural ecosystems. The communes ranged in number from 50,000 to 90,000, but they were not diverse in their success. They were uniformly rigid, uniformly controlled, and uniformly unable to adapt to the complexities of the land. The militarization of society created an atmosphere of fear and suspicion, where deviation from the party line was met with severe punishment. The "work point" system, intended to be a fair measure of contribution, often became a tool for political control, rewarding obedience over skill.

During the Cultural Revolution, the communes took on an even more complex role, serving as the primary vehicle for the CCP's political and social campaigns. They were the stage upon which the struggle between the masses and the "bourgeois" elements played out. The communes were not just economic units; they were the organs of political power in the countryside. They enforced the ideological purity of the party, purging dissenters and ensuring that the rural population remained aligned with Mao's vision. This political function often took precedence over economic reality. The drive to achieve ideological perfection led to policies that were economically ruinous. The commune system became a symbol of the party's absolute control over the rural populace, a control that was maintained through a combination of propaganda, coercion, and the sheer impossibility of escape.

Yet, the story of the People's Commune is not just one of failure; it is also a testament to the sheer audacity of the Chinese Communist Party. They attempted to reshape human society on a scale that had never been seen before. They sought to erase the family, the private home, and the individual's connection to the land, replacing them with a collective identity that was entirely dependent on the state. The experiment lasted for twenty-five years, from 1958 to 1983. It was a period of immense suffering, but also of immense change. The communes forced the rural population to adapt to a new reality, one where the state was the ultimate provider and the ultimate authority. They laid the groundwork for the eventual transition to the township system, which would replace the communes in 1983. The dissolution of the communes marked the end of an era, but the scars they left on the Chinese countryside remain visible to this day. The legacy of the People's Commune is a warning about the dangers of ideological rigidity, the limits of state power, and the resilience of the human spirit in the face of overwhelming odds.

The transition away from the communes began as the failures of the Great Leap Forward became undeniable. The famine that swept the country in the early 1960s forced the state to reconsider its approach. While the communes were not immediately dismantled, their power was curtailed, and the system was adjusted to allow for more local autonomy. The public mess halls were closed, and the private plots were restored. The strict militarization of society was relaxed. But the core structure of the commune remained in place for two more decades, serving as the administrative backbone of rural China. It was only in the early 1980s, under the leadership of Deng Xiaoping, that the system was finally abandoned. The Household Responsibility System replaced the commune, returning land to individual families and allowing them to sell their surplus on the open market. This shift marked the beginning of China's economic miracle, a transformation that was made possible by the very failure of the commune system.

The People's Commune remains one of the most ambitious and tragic experiments in human history. It was a vision of a utopia that turned into a dystopia, a promise of liberation that resulted in subjugation. It was a system that sought to create a new man, but instead created a generation of survivors. The lessons of the commune are clear: that human societies cannot be engineered from the top down, that the complexity of agriculture cannot be reduced to a simple formula, and that the cost of ideological purity is often paid in human suffering. As we look back on this period, we see not just a failure of policy, but a failure of imagination. The leaders of the CCP believed that they could transcend the laws of economics and the nature of human beings. They were wrong. The People's Commune stands as a monument to that error, a reminder of the price of hubris and the enduring power of the human spirit to endure even the most crushing of systems.

The story of the commune is a story of numbers, of 26,000 giant units, of 24,000 people per commune, of 5,200 households. But it is also a story of individuals, of families torn apart, of children growing up in dormitories, of women working in the fields and the kitchens, of men mobilized for campaigns that made no sense. It is a story of a country that tried to leap forward and fell, only to rise again. The legacy of the People's Commune is woven into the fabric of modern China. It shaped the political landscape, the economic structure, and the social consciousness of a nation. It is a chapter in history that cannot be forgotten, a warning that must be heeded. The communes are gone, but their memory lingers, a ghost in the machine of the Chinese state, a reminder of the cost of trying to build a paradise on earth.

In the end, the People's Commune was a testament to the power of the state and the fragility of the individual. It was an attempt to create a society where everyone was equal, but where no one was free. It was a system that promised abundance but delivered scarcity. It was a vision of the future that was rooted in the past, a dream of communism that was crushed by the reality of human nature. The story of the commune is a story of hope and despair, of ambition and failure. It is a story that continues to resonate today, as China and the world grapple with the challenges of inequality, governance, and the search for a better way of life. The People's Commune was a mistake, but it was a mistake that taught us a great deal about the limits of power and the resilience of the human spirit. It is a lesson that we must never forget.

The dissolution of the communes in 1983 was not just a bureaucratic change; it was a recognition of the failure of the entire experiment. The township system that replaced them was a return to a more traditional form of local governance, one that allowed for greater autonomy and flexibility. The Household Responsibility System that accompanied it was a return to the individual, to the family, and to the land. It was a recognition that the path to prosperity lay not in the collective, but in the individual. The legacy of the commune was a warning that the state cannot replace the family, that the collective cannot replace the individual, and that the future cannot be engineered by the will of a few. The People's Commune was a chapter in the history of China that was written in blood and tears, but it is a chapter that has been closed. The story of the commune is over, but the lessons it taught remain relevant. It is a story that reminds us that the path to a better world is not paved with good intentions, but with hard work, humility, and respect for the complexity of human life. The People's Commune was a failure, but it was a failure that helped China to succeed. It is a paradox that defines the modern Chinese experience, a paradox that continues to shape the nation's future.
