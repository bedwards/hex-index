Corpus Christi College, Cambridge

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Corpus Christi College, Cambridge

In 1381, the streets of Cambridge ran red with a specific kind of irony: a mob of townspeople, led by their own mayor, stormed the very institution they had helped fund, carrying away its silver plate and burning its founding charter to ash. This was not an act of foreign invasion or royal decree, but the Peasants' Revolt, and its epicenter within the university was a place that defied the norm. While every other college in Cambridge stood as a bastion of clerical privilege, detached from the common folk, this one was the child of the townspeople themselves. It was the only college in the history of the University of Cambridge founded not by a king, a bishop, or a wealthy noble, but by the local guilds of the city.

This institution, known today as Corpus Christi College, carries a history that reads less like a dry academic record and more like a saga of survival, rebellion, and the relentless accumulation of treasure. To understand the weight of its existence, one must look back to the year 1349, a time when the Black Death had swept through England, leaving a landscape of corpses and a society in the throes of existential terror. It was in this crucible of mortality that the seeds of the college were sown. Three men—William Horwode, Henry de Tangmere, and John Hardy—founded the Guild of Corpus Christi in Cambridge. Their motivation was born of the immediate horror of the plague, a desire to pray for the dead and the living in a world where death was the only certainty.

Later that same year, a merger occurred that would define the college's identity for centuries. The newly formed Guild of Corpus Christi united with an older organization, the Guild of the Blessed Virgin Mary, which had been decimated by the same plague that terrified the city. This union of survivors created a formidable entity with a singular ambition: to found a new college in the University of Cambridge. They secured the patronage of the Duke of Lancaster, who applied to King Edward III for a royal licence. In 1352, the licence was granted, and the "College of Corpus Christi and the Blessed Virgin Mary" was born.

The construction of the college began immediately. It was a modest affair, a single court built near the parish church, designed to house a Master and two fellows. By 1356, the statutes were drawn up, laying down the rigid rules that would govern the behavior of the fellows for centuries. However, there was a curious omission in these early statutes: students were not mentioned. The foundation was strictly for the fellows. It would take another two hundred years before students were formally brought within the scope of the college's governance. The newly constructed Old Court could house only 22 fellows and students, a tiny fraction of the hundreds who walk its grounds today.

For the first few centuries, the college was known by a different name, one that reflects the turbulent religious tides of England. From the early 16th century until the 19th century, it was commonly called Bene't College, or St Benet's College. This name derived from the nearby St Bene't's Church, which had served as the college's de facto chapel since its founding. The connection was so intimate that when Thomas Cosyn became Master in the late 15th century, he built a gallery linking the college directly to the church. The name "Corpus Christi" had fallen out of favor, deemed too Roman Catholic for the sensibilities of a post-Reformation England. It was a name that whispered of popes and processions, while "Bene't" sounded safe, Anglican, and local.

The college's early history was marked by a stark reality: it was poor. Unlike the grand, stone-walled foundations of kings, Corpus Christi had to make do with what it had. It could not afford to build new courts or expand its architecture. As a result, Old Court, the original structure from 1356, has survived to the present day, a rare architectural time capsule. For centuries, the college had no chapel of its own. The members worshipped next door in St Bene't's, and for a time, services were also held at St Botolph's. The college's wealth would not manifest in stone, but in something far more portable and enduring.

The defining moment of the college's early identity was its grandest ceremony: the annual Corpus Christi procession. This was a spectacular parade through the streets of Cambridge, stretching from the college to Magdalene Bridge. The host, the consecrated bread of the Eucharist, was carried by a priest, followed by the Master and fellows carrying several of the college's most precious treasures. The procession concluded with an extravagant dinner, a feast of excess in a world often defined by scarcity. This parade was a public declaration of the college's status and piety. It continued until 1535, when the Master, William Sowode, put a stop to it. Sowode's decision was a pragmatic one, made in the wake of the English Reformation, when such overtly Catholic displays could be dangerous. Yet, the tradition of the feast day remains. The college still holds a grand dinner on the feast of Corpus Christi, the Thursday after Trinity Sunday, a silent echo of the procession that once dominated the city streets.

The relationship between the college and the city of Cambridge was never entirely peaceful. The Peasants' Revolt of 1381 remains a unique footnote in the history of the university. While the revolt swept through the countryside, burning manor houses and challenging the feudal order, it found a specific target in Cambridge. The college was sacked by a mob that included some of its own students. The grievances were specific: the college collected "candle rents," a rigid tax on the town that had become a symbol of oppression. The mob, led by the mayor, gutted the buildings, stole the silver, and burned the charter. The college claimed £80 in damages, a sum equivalent to roughly £50,000 in modern terms. It was the only university college to be attacked, a testament to the fact that its foundation by the townspeople had not guaranteed it their loyalty.

The Wars of the Roses brought a different kind of threat. In 1460, as the civil war tore England apart, the college found itself in the crossfire. Fearing a "tempestuous riot," the college paid for armaments, including artillery and arrows, and protective clothing to defend its treasures. It was a time when the college's survival depended not on prayer, but on the barrel of a gun. During this period, the college found a powerful ally in Elizabeth, Duchess of Norfolk, and her sister, Lady Eleanor Botelar. Lady Eleanor is rumored by some historians to have been the secret wife of King Edward IV. Regardless of the truth of her marriage, her financial support was real. She endowed the college with scholarships and financed crucial repairs to the buildings. As a monument to this patronage, a 'talbot', the heraldic dog of the Talbot family, was placed on the gable of Old Court, where it still stares out over the courtyard today.

The college's most significant transformation came in the 16th century, driven by a man who would become its greatest benefactor: Matthew Parker. Parker, who served as Master of the college, was a scholar of immense ambition and a collector of unparalleled passion. He is celebrated as the savior of the college's intellectual and material wealth. In an era where religious turmoil threatened to erase history, Parker began to amass a library that would become unrivalled. He donated his entire collection of books and manuscripts to the college, along with a vast amount of silver plate.

Parker's genius lay not just in his acquisitions, but in his foresight. He knew that the college might one day be tempted to sell off parts of his collection to pay for debts or repairs. To prevent this, he inserted a clever condition into his endowment. He stipulated that if more than a certain number of books were lost, the remainder of the collection would pass first to Gonville and Caius College, and then to Trinity Hall. The same condition applied to the silver. This "Parker's Clause" meant that the college could not sell a single book or a single piece of silver without losing the entire collection. The result is that Corpus Christi College retains to this day the entirety of Parker's library and silver collection. Every few years, representatives from Gonville and Caius and Trinity Hall ceremonially inspect the collection to ensure no losses have occurred. It is a system of checks and balances that has preserved a cultural treasure for nearly five centuries.

Parker's assiduousness in acquiring books has even given rise to the phrase "Nosey Parker," a testament to his relentless curiosity. His legacy is everywhere in the college. The silver collection, one of the largest in Cambridge, is a visual representation of the college's wealth. In terms of fixed assets, Corpus Christi ranks among the wealthiest colleges in the university. The endowment was valued at £99.7 million at the end of June 2023, while its net assets stood at £239 million. This wealth, however, is not just a number; it is a physical reality of gleaming silver and ancient manuscripts that have survived fire, riot, and religious upheaval.

The college's academic success is a direct reflection of this stability and the quality of its community. With around 300 undergraduates and 200 postgraduates, it boasts the second smallest student body of the traditional colleges, after Peterhouse. This small size fosters a tight-knit community where academic excellence is the norm. In the unofficial Tompkins Table, which ranks colleges by the class of degrees obtained by their undergraduates, Corpus has consistently punched above its weight. In 2012, it placed third, with 32.4% of its undergraduates achieving first-class degrees. Its average position between 2003 and 2012 was ninth, but in the 2024 rankings, it surged back to third place. In 2024, the college also ranked first overall for finalists, a testament to its continued dominance in the academic sphere.

The college has produced a roster of distinguished alumni who have shaped the course of history. Perhaps the most celebrated is Christopher Marlowe, the playwright who matriculated in 1580. Though his life was cut short in a tavern brawl, his time at Corpus Christi laid the foundation for a literary legacy that would rival Shakespeare. Other notable figures include Richard Reynolds, a martyr who was executed by Henry VIII for his refusal to accept the king's supremacy over the church, and Thomas Dusgate and George Wishart, who were burned as Protestants during the religious turmoil of the 16th century. The college has produced adherents and martyrs to both sides of the great religious divide, a microcosm of the nation's struggle.

The name "Corpus Christi" eventually returned to prominence in the early 19th century, shedding the "Bene't" moniker that had served it for so long. The college re-embraced its original identity, acknowledging the medieval roots that had survived the Reformation and the Enlightenment. The processions of the past were gone, but the spirit of the guilds that founded it remained. The college had evolved from a modest court for twenty-two fellows into a powerhouse of academic and cultural significance.

It survived the Peasants' Revolt, the Wars of the Roses, and the Reformation. It weathered the loss of its charter and the burning of its buildings. It thrived through the patronage of powerful women and the intellectual vision of men like Matthew Parker. It transformed from a poor, modest institution into one of the wealthiest and most academically successful colleges in the world.
