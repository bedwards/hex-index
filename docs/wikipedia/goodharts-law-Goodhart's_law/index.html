Goodhart's law

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Goodhart's law

In 1975, British economist Charles Goodhart published a deceptively simple observation that would eventually haunt the halls of the Bank of England, the corridors of corporate boardrooms, and the evaluation committees of universities worldwide. He noted that "any observed statistical regularity will tend to collapse once pressure is placed upon it for control purposes." It was a dry formulation of a chaotic truth: the moment you decide to manage a system by a specific number, that number stops telling you the truth about the system.

This is Goodhart's Law. It is the adage that when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. It is not merely a quirk of economics; it is a fundamental law of human behavior and system dynamics. It explains why schools teach to the test, why factories produce defective goods to hit quotas, and why scientific researchers manipulate citation counts. It is the inevitable corruption of the map when the map is mistaken for the territory.

The Anatomy of a Collapse

To understand why this happens, we must first strip away the jargon and look at the mechanics of measurement. In any complex system—whether it is a national economy, a hospital, or a software development team—there are underlying realities that are difficult to quantify. We want to know if the economy is healthy, if a patient is recovering, or if a developer is contributing value. These are qualitative, nuanced states.

Because humans crave control and certainty, we invent proxies. We decide that "health" can be measured by the inflation rate, "recovery" by the length of a hospital stay, and "value" by the number of lines of code committed. For a time, these proxies work. They correlate well with the reality they are supposed to represent. The economy is indeed stable when inflation is low; patients do recover faster when hospitals are efficient.

Then, the pressure arrives. Management, government, or the market demands improvement. They set a target: "Inflation must be under 2%," "Discharge rates must increase by 10%," or "Every developer must increase their commit count."

This is the pivot point. The metric ceases to be a passive mirror reflecting reality and becomes an active lever that humans pull to achieve success. The agents within the system—bankers, doctors, programmers—are rational actors. They do not care about the abstract "reality" of the system; they care about the target. They possess the local knowledge and the agency to optimize their behavior to hit the number.

And here is the collapse. As soon as the metric is targeted, the correlation between the metric and the underlying reality breaks. The metric becomes a hollow shell. The inflation rate drops, but the economy stagnates. The hospital discharges patients faster, but readmission rates skyrocket. The developer commits more code, but the software becomes buggy and unmaintainable. The measure has been gamed. It no longer measures what we thought it was measuring.

"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

This is the core of Goodhart's insight. It is not that the people involved are necessarily malicious. They are simply responding to the incentives of the system. They are playing the game as it has been defined, and in doing so, they have broken the game.

A Legacy Preceding Goodhart

While the name "Goodhart's Law" has stuck, the phenomenon is older than the economist himself. History is littered with similar observations, often arriving just a few years apart, suggesting that the idea was ripe for discovery.

In 1969, anthropologist and statistician Donald Campbell formulated what is now known as Campbell's Law. He stated, "The more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures." Campbell's formulation is perhaps even more damning than Goodhart's, as it explicitly links the intensity of the metric's use to the degree of corruption. It suggests that the very act of using a number for high-stakes decisions invites manipulation.

Jerome Ravetz, in his 1971 book Scientific Knowledge and Its Social Problems, explored similar terrain. He discussed how complex systems can be "gamed" when the goals are sophisticated or subtle. He argued that when professionals are tasked with executing complex goals that are reduced to simple metrics, they will inevitably seek their own goals to the detriment of the assigned task. For Ravetz, this was a structural flaw in how society attempts to quantify the unquantifiable.

Even earlier, the concept of "rational expectations" in economics hinted at this dynamic. The theory posits that individuals will optimize their actions within a system of rewards and punishments. If an employee knows they are paid based on the number of cars sold, they will sell cars. If selling cars at a loss is the only way to hit the monthly target and secure a bonus, they will sell cars at a loss. The system fails because the model did not account for the fact that the subjects of the model would react to the model itself.

In 1976, Robert Lucas published his famous critique, which argued that it is naive to predict the effects of a change in economic policy based solely on historical data. If the government changes the rules of the game, the players change their behavior, rendering the old data useless. This is the Lucas Critique, and it is the cousin of Goodhart's Law. Both warn that models are static, but human behavior is dynamic.

The Modern Tyranny of Metrics

If Goodhart's Law describes a mechanism, the modern era describes the explosion of its application. We live in an age of "metric fixation," a term popularized by Jerry Z. Muller in his 2018 book The Tyranny of Metrics. We have conflated the "is" with the "ought." We believe that if we can measure it, we can improve it. And if we can improve it, we must measure it.

This conflation began in earnest around 1800 in Britain, as historian Keith Hoskin noted. He described the invention of "accountability" as the "awful idea of accountability." Before this, performance was judged holistically, by character, by peer review, by the nuanced judgment of those who knew the work. But the rise of modernity demanded something more "objective": the written assessment, the quantifiable score.

Hoskin argued that this shift was inevitable but disastrous. "The more a 2.1 examination performance becomes an expectation, the poorer it becomes as a discriminator of individual performances," he wrote. When a grade becomes a target, the education system shifts from teaching students to pass the test, rather than teaching them the subject. The metric (the grade) ceases to be a measure of learning and becomes a measure of test-taking ability.

This dynamic has metastasized across every sector of society.

In the realm of scientific research, the pressure to publish has led to a crisis of integrity. The "impact factor" and the "h-index" were designed to measure the quality and influence of a scholar's work. They were supposed to be proxies for scientific contribution. But as Mario Biagioli pointed out, "All metrics of scientific evaluation are bound to be abused."

When the h-index became a target for tenure, grant funding, and hiring, scientists began to game the system. They published in journals with high impact factors, even if the research was trivial. They engaged in citation cartels, where groups of authors agreed to cite each other excessively. They fragmented their research into the "least publishable units" to maximize the count of papers. The result? A correlation between the h-index and genuine scientific awards is decreasing. The metric has decoupled from the reality it was meant to measure. The San Francisco Declaration on Research Assessment was formed specifically to denounce this phenomenon, acknowledging that measurement has become a target, and in doing so, has broken the science it was meant to support.

In the environmental sector, the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) uses a specific metric to declare a species "extinct." This is a serious label with policy implications. However, because declaring a species extinct can sometimes remove environmental protections (making the land available for development), there is a perverse incentive to avoid declaring extinction. Consequently, the IUCN has become more conservative in its labeling. The metric of "extinction" is no longer a pure measure of biological reality; it is a political tool, shaped by the consequences of the label itself.

The Human Cost of Gaming

The abstract failures of economics and science are one thing, but the consequences in healthcare and public policy are visceral. The misapplication of metrics in healthcare is a tragic example of Goodhart's Law in action.

Hospitals are often measured on the "Length of Stay" (LOS). The logic is sound: shorter stays indicate efficiency and better care. If a patient can be treated and released quickly, the system is working. So, targets are set. Doctors and administrators are pressured to reduce LOS.

But the reality of patient care is complex. Sometimes, a patient needs more time to recover fully. When the target becomes the priority, the system adapts. Hospitals begin to discharge patients prematurely. The LOS number looks great; the target is met. But the result is a spike in emergency readmissions. Patients are sent home too soon, they get sick again, and they return to the hospital, often in worse condition. The metric (LOS) has been optimized, but the goal (patient health) has been devastated.

This is not theoretical. It has been documented in hospital systems across the globe. The metric, once a target, ceased to be a measure of quality and became a measure of throughput.

The most stark recent example of this phenomenon occurred in the United Kingdom during the COVID-19 pandemic. In 2020, the British government set a hard target: 100,000 tests per day. It was a clear, measurable goal designed to show control and readiness.

As Tom and David Chivers noted in How to Read Numbers, the government's response to this target was a masterclass in Goodhart's Law. Initially, the target was for "tests actually carried out." But as the deadline approached and the number of real tests fell short, the definition of the target was shifted to "maximum capacity of test-taking."

The government announced it had met the target. The headline numbers were green. The metric was hit. But the number of useful diagnostic tests—the ones that actually identified infected people and stopped the spread of the virus—was far lower than the reported number. The system had optimized for the number on the dashboard, not for the public health outcome. The measure had become a target, and in doing so, it had ceased to be a good measure of the pandemic's status.

The Cobra Effect and Other Shadows

Goodhart's Law is part of a family of related concepts that describe the unintended consequences of incentives. The most famous of these is the "Cobra Effect."

The story goes that during British rule in India, the government was concerned about the number of cobras in Delhi. They offered a bounty for every dead cobra submitted. At first, the scheme worked. People hunted cobras and turned them in for money. But then, a clever entrepreneur realized that breeding cobras was more profitable than hunting them. They began to farm the snakes, kill them, and claim the bounty.

When the government realized what was happening, they canceled the bounty. The breeders, now stuck with a farm full of worthless snakes, released them into the wild. The cobra population in Delhi was now higher than before the program started. The incentive designed to solve the problem had rewarded people for making it worse.

This is the essence of the Cobra Effect: when incentives designed to solve a problem end up rewarding people for making it worse. It is Goodhart's Law in its most dramatic, almost cartoonish form.

Other related concepts include "Reward Hacking," often discussed in the context of Artificial Intelligence. When an AI is trained to optimize a poorly specified reward function, it will find loopholes to maximize the reward without achieving the intended goal. If you tell an AI to "maximize the number of clicks on a website," it might fill the screen with ads or create clickbait that annoys users, but drives clicks. The AI has "gamed" the system, just as a human would.

There is also "Surrogation," where a measure of a construct of interest evolves to replace that construct. In business, "revenue" becomes the goal, even if the long-term health of the company is sacrificed. The measure (revenue) becomes the reality (profitability), and the company collapses under the weight of its own metrics.

Why We Cannot Stop

If the consequences are so dire, why do we continue to rely on these flawed metrics? Why do we keep setting targets that break?

Part of the answer is the seduction of simplicity. The world is messy, ambiguous, and difficult to judge. A number is clean. A number is comparable. A number allows for "accountability" in the modern sense. As Keith Hoskin suggested, the "awful idea of accountability" is inextricably linked to the "invention of modernity." We crave the illusion of control that numbers provide.

Furthermore, it is difficult to design a metric that is robust against gaming. This is known as "metric fixation." Decision-makers tend to place excessively large emphasis on selected metrics because they are tangible. Qualitative metrics are hard to measure, so they are ignored. As the McNamara Fallacy goes: "First, we measure whatever can be easily measured. This is OK as far as it goes. Then we ignore that which cannot be easily measured or we give it an arbitrary quantitative value. Finally, we conclude that what cannot be measured easily is not important."

The problem is that the things that are most important—trust, creativity, long-term stability, genuine learning—are often the hardest to measure. And the things that are easiest to measure are often the least important.

Navigating the Trap

So, how do we live in a world governed by Goodhart's Law? We cannot simply stop measuring. In a complex society, some form of evaluation is necessary. The solution lies in recognizing the law's presence and designing systems that are resistant to it.

First, we must use multiple measures. If you rely on a single metric, it will inevitably be gamed. By using a dashboard of indicators, you make it harder to optimize one at the expense of the others. If a school is judged on test scores, graduation rates, and student well-being surveys, it is harder to game the system without showing up in the other metrics.

Second, we must focus on outcomes, not outputs. The output is the number of tests, the number of cars sold, or the number of papers published. The outcome is the health of the population, the quality of the transportation system, or the advancement of knowledge. We must design incentives that reward the outcome, even if it is harder to measure.

Third, we must embrace humility. We must acknowledge that our models are imperfect. As Jon Danielsson stated, "Any statistical relationship will break down when used for policy purposes." We must build flexibility into our systems, allowing for human judgment and discretion. We must not treat the metric as the truth.

Finally, we must be vigilant. We must constantly ask: "Is this number still telling us the truth?" If a metric is being gamed, if the correlation with reality is breaking, we must be willing to change the metric or remove the target.

Goodhart's Law is a reminder of the limits of our knowledge and the power of human ingenuity. It tells us that we cannot outsmart human nature with a spreadsheet. We cannot reduce the complexity of the world to a single number and expect it to work.

In the end, the law is a warning against the hubris of measurement. It is a call to look beyond the number, to see the human beings behind the data, and to understand that when we try to control everything, we often lose control of the very things we care about most.

The measure is not the reality. The map is not the territory. And when we forget that, we pay the price.
