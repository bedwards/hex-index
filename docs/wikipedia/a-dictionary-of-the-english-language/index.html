A Dictionary of the English Language

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: A Dictionary of the English Language

On April 15, 1755, a book of such monumental scale that it nearly broke the printing press was unveiled in London. It stood nearly twenty inches wide and eighteen inches tall, a physical behemoth of paper and ink that required two volumes to contain its contents. This was not merely a reference book; it was a declaration of war against chaos. For the first time, the English language, a sprawling, unregulated beast of dialects, slang, and borrowed terms, was being hunted, tamed, and pinned to the page with surgical precision. Samuel Johnson had done it. He had taken a task that the greatest scholars of the age deemed impossible for a single man and completed it in a London attic, armed only with a pen, a vast memory, and a stubborn refusal to yield to the disorder of the world.

The story of A Dictionary of the English Language is not just a chronicle of lexicography; it is a testament to the human capacity to impose order on entropy. To understand why Johnson's work was such a seismic event, one must first understand the linguistic landscape of mid-eighteenth-century Britain. It was a time of explosion, but not the kind that brings clarity. The middle of the 1700s saw a dramatic rise in literacy rates among the general public. The mechanics of printing had advanced, and bookbinding became cheaper, meaning that for the first time, texts, maps, pamphlets, and newspapers were flooding the market at prices the average person could afford.

But this explosion of the printed word created a crisis. Without a standard, the language was fracturing. Writers were spelling words differently; grammatical rules were fluid; technical jargon was obscuring meaning. The public, hungry to read and write, demanded a set pattern of grammar, definition, and spelling. They needed a map for a territory that was constantly shifting beneath their feet. In 1746, a consortium of London's most successful printers, including the formidable Robert Dodsley and Thomas Longman, recognized this void. They knew that no single bookseller could afford the risk of such a massive undertaking alone. So, they pooled their resources and approached Samuel Johnson.

The contract signed in June 1746 was a gamble of epic proportions. Johnson was paid the staggering sum of 1,500 guineas (approximately £1,575 at the time), a figure that would equate to roughly £290,000 in 2023. In exchange, he was to produce a dictionary that would define the English language. Johnson, perhaps buoyed by youthful optimism or a desire to secure the funds quickly, confidently claimed he could finish the work in three years. The reality, however, was far more grueling. It would take him seven years to complete the masterpiece, and he did so almost entirely single-handedly. He had no team of scholars, no committee of experts, and no government grant. He had only clerical assistants to help copy the illustrative quotations he had marked in books, while he, the sole architect, bore the weight of every definition and every etymology.

The Ghosts of Previous Dictionaries

Johnson was not the first to attempt this task, nor was he even among the first dozen. The previous 150 years had seen more than twenty dictionaries published in England, yet none had succeeded in capturing the true spirit of the language. The earliest efforts were little more than "wordbooks." Sir Thomas Elyot's Latin-English compilation from 1538 was a bridge between languages, not a guide to English itself. Richard Mulcaster, a headmaster, had compiled a table of 8,000 words in 1583, noting that it would be "verie praise worthy" to gather all words used in the English tongue into one dictionary, but he never quite achieved the comprehensive scope he envisioned.

By 1598, John Florio published an Italian-English dictionary that introduced a revolutionary concept: the use of quotations, or "illustrations," to give meaning to words. Before Florio, dictionaries offered no actual definitions; they simply listed words. Robert Cawdrey's Table Alphabeticall, published in 1604, was the first monolingual English dictionary, but it was a tiny thing, containing only 2,449 words and notably excluding any word beginning with W, X, or Y. It was a list of "hard words"—technical, foreign, obscure, or antiquated terms that the common man might not understand.

This was the fundamental flaw of the early lexicographers. As historian Henry Hitchings observed, they "failed to give sufficient sense of [the English] language as it appeared in use." They treated English as a static list of difficult terms to be decoded, rather than a living, breathing organism that evolved through the daily speech of writers, merchants, and poets. Benjamin Martin's Lingua Britannica Reformata in 1749 and Ainsworth's Thesaurus Linguae Latinae in 1737 made strides by defining entries in separate senses, and Nathan Bailey had prepared a dictionary of 40,000 words in 1721, but none could match the breadth or style that Johnson would eventually bring to the table. The problem remained: the dictionaries were poorly organized, poorly researched, and disconnected from the reality of how people actually spoke and wrote.

The Plan and the Patron

Johnson prepared his dictionary at 17 Gough Square in London, an eclectic household that became the laboratory for this great experiment. By 1747, he had written his Plan of a Dictionary of the English Language, a document that spelled out his intentions and his methodology with brilliant clarity. Johnson saw the process not as an act of invention, but of discovery. He viewed the rules of style as parallel to legal precedents.

"I shall therefore, since the rules of stile, like those of law, arise from precedents often repeated, collect the testimonies of both sides, and endeavour to discover and promulgate the decrees of custom, who has so long possessed whether by right or by usurpation, the sovereignty of words."

This was a radical shift. Instead of prescribing how English should be used, Johnson sought to record how it was used. He would look to the "best writers" of the past and present to find the precedents that governed the language. This approach required a massive amount of reading and annotation. Johnson spent years marking passages in books, extracting quotations that would serve as evidence for the definitions he was crafting.

The Plan attracted the attention of Philip Stanhope, the 4th Earl of Chesterfield, a man of immense influence and literary taste. Stanhope offered his patronage, a relationship that was standard for the time but fraught with potential for conflict. For seven years, Stanhope offered little more than a distant nod. He did not provide financial support beyond the initial contract, nor did he offer the encouragement a man working in isolation so desperately needed. It was only seven years later, when the dictionary was nearly complete and Johnson was on the verge of publication, that Chesterfield decided to act.

He wrote two anonymous essays in The World, a periodical, praising Johnson's work and advocating for him as the "dictator" of the English language.

"We must have recourse to the old Roman expedient in times of confusion, and choose a dictator. Upon this principle, I give my vote for Mr Johnson to fill that great and arduous post."

The tone was condescending, the timing opportunistic. Johnson, who had spent years struggling without a single act of assistance or a word of encouragement, was incensed. He felt that Chesterfield was trying to claim credit for a work that had been born of his own solitary labor. In a letter that has since become one of the most famous pieces of correspondence in literary history, Johnson dismantled the concept of patronage with a withering precision.

"Seven years, my lord, have now past since I waited in your outward rooms or was repulsed from your door, during which time I have been pushing on my work through difficulties of which it is useless to complain, and have brought it at last to the verge of publication without one act of assistance, one word of encouragement, or one smile of favour. Such treatment I did not expect, for I never had a patron before ... Is not a patron, my lord, one who looks with unconcern on a man struggling for life in the water, and when he has reached ground, encumbers him with help? The notice which you have been pleased to take of my labours, had it been early, had been kind: but it has been delayed till I am indifferent and cannot enjoy it; till I am solitary and cannot impart it; till I am known and do not want it."

This letter marked the end of the old system of patronage and the beginning of a new era where the author, not the aristocrat, held the power. It was a declaration of independence for the intellectual class.

The Physical Marvel

When the first edition of A Dictionary of the English Language finally hit the shelves, it was a physical marvel. It was printed in in-folio, a format so large that the pages measured 18 inches in height and nearly 20 inches in width. The paper was of the finest quality available, and the cost of the paper alone ran to nearly £1,600, a sum that exceeded the entire fee Johnson had been paid to write the book. Johnson himself described the volume as Vasta mole superbus—"Proud in its great bulk."

The title page was a masterpiece of design, with the words "Samuel Johnson" and "English Language" printed in bold red ink against a backdrop of black text. The typography was equally impressive. The preface and headings were set in 4.6 mm "English" type, while the double-columned text was set in 3.5 mm pica. No bookseller could have attempted such a project without the backing of a consortium, and outside of a few special editions of the Bible, no book of this heft and size had ever been set to type in England.

The first edition contained a list of 42,773 words. While Johnson would add a few more in subsequent editions during his lifetime, this initial count represented a universe of vocabulary that had never before been comprehensively documented. The dictionary was not just a list; it was a history of the language. The title page promised that the words would be "deduced from their ORIGINALS, and ILLUSTRATED in their DIFFERENT SIGNIFICATIONS by EXAMPLES from the best WRITERS." It also included a History of the Language and an English Grammar, positioning the dictionary as the ultimate authority on the structure and evolution of English.

The Legacy of a Single Man

The impact of Johnson's dictionary was immediate and enduring. It was viewed as the pre-eminent English dictionary until the completion of the Oxford English Dictionary 173 years later. Walter Jackson Bate, a renowned biographer of Johnson, stated that the Dictionary "easily ranks as one of the greatest single achievements of scholarship, and probably the greatest ever performed by one individual who laboured under anything like the disadvantages in a comparable length of time."

What made Johnson's work so superior to its predecessors? It was the depth of his research and the humanity of his definitions. Johnson did not just define words; he captured their essence. He understood that language was not a rigid code but a reflection of human experience. His definitions were often witty, sometimes biting, and always insightful. He included quotations from the greatest writers of the English canon—Shakespeare, Milton, Dryden, Pope—to show how words were used in context. This method gave the dictionary a literary quality that turned it into a work of art as well as a reference tool.

The dictionary also cemented Johnson's reputation as a literary giant. It was a work that required a mind of encyclopedic scope and a heart of immense compassion. Johnson was a man who knew the struggle of poverty and the pain of loneliness, and these experiences infused his work with a profound sense of empathy. He understood the weight of words and the power they held to shape the world.

In the decades that followed, the dictionary became a staple of English-speaking households. It was used by scholars, writers, and students alike. It provided a standard for spelling and grammar that helped to unify the English language across the British Empire and eventually the world. The chaos of the mid-eighteenth century gave way to a sense of order and stability, thanks in no small part to the seven years of solitary labor at 17 Gough Square.

The story of A Dictionary of the English Language is a reminder that great things can be accomplished by a single individual who refuses to give up.
