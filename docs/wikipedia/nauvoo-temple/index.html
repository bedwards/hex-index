Nauvoo Temple

18 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Nauvoo Temple

On April 30, 1846, the Nauvoo Temple stood in a state of suspended animation, a monument to a people in desperate flight. Just weeks earlier, the bulk of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints had begun a harrowing exodus from their Illinois headquarters, fleeing mob violence and legal persecution. Yet, a small, determined crew of workers remained behind in the near-empty city. They were not there to loot or to escape; they were there to finish the interior pulpits and benches of the first-floor assembly hall. Their mission was singular: to complete the structure just enough to hold a formal dedication. On that spring morning, with the windows still bare and the city silent, the building was dedicated in private services, followed by public ceremonies the next day. It was a triumph of faith over circumstance, but it was also a funeral for a dream. The temple had been in active use for less than three months. Within months, the remaining faithful would be driven out, the building vandalized, sold for a fraction of its worth, burned to the ground, and finally torn apart by a tornado. The story of the Nauvoo Temple is not merely a record of architecture; it is a chronicle of a community's frantic race against time, a clash between spiritual vision and brutal reality, and the enduring power of a symbol that refused to stay buried.

The seeds of this architectural marvel were sown shortly after the church established its headquarters in Nauvoo, Illinois, in 1839. The site was a riverfront city of ambitious potential, and the church leadership saw in it a place to build a house of worship that would dwarf their previous efforts. In the winter of 1836, they had completed their first temple in Kirtland, Ohio, a structure of immense significance to the early believers. But the Nauvoo project was conceived on a grander scale. By late 1840, the church purchased four acres of land for the temple site, paying $1,100—a sum that reflected the value of the land but perhaps underestimated the cost of the vision to come.

On April 6, 1841, the cornerstone was laid in a ceremony of profound gravity. Joseph Smith, the church's founder and president, directed the proceedings, while Sidney Rigdon delivered the principal oration. The building that rose from the foundation was a masterpiece of the Greek Revival style, designed by architect William Weeks under Smith's direct supervision. The dimensions were staggering for the American frontier: 128 feet long and 88 feet wide at the base. A clock tower and weathervane capped the structure, pushing its total height to 165 feet. This was a 60% increase in size compared to the Kirtland Temple, signaling a new era of ambition and permanence.

The interior layout mirrored the theological complexity of the church. Like its predecessor, the temple contained two assembly halls: a lower court on the first floor and an upper court on the second. Both levels were flanked by classrooms and offices in the attic. However, the Nauvoo Temple introduced a critical innovation: a full basement. This subterranean level housed a massive baptismal font, supported by the carved backs of twelve oxen. This basement was not an afterthought; it was central to the new ordinances the church was preparing to administer.

The design of the temple was far more than a structural exercise; it was a theological text written in stone. Weeks, working closely with Smith, incorporated distinctively Latter Day Saint motifs into the facade. The building was adorned with sunstones, moonstones, and starstones. For over a century, a common misconception persisted that these stones represented the three degrees of glory in the Latter Day Saint conception of the afterlife. This interpretation, however, did not hold up to scrutiny. The stones appeared in an order that contradicted the theological hierarchy of the afterlife.

The true meaning was clarified by Wandle Mace, the foreman for the framework of the temple. Mace explained that the design was intended to be "a representation of the Church, the Bride, the Lamb's wife." He pointed to the biblical text in Revelation 12:1, which describes a "woman clothed with the sun, and the moon under her feet, and upon her head a crown of twelve stars." This scriptural imagery dictated the placement of the stones. The starstones were positioned at the very top of the temple, representing the "crown of twelve stars." The sunstones occupied the middle section, symbolizing the figure being "clothed with the sun." The moonstones were placed at the bottom, depicting the "moon under her feet." Every chiseled detail was a deliberate invocation of this celestial bride, transforming the building into a physical manifestation of the church's identity.

Construction was a race against a gathering storm. By the time Joseph Smith was murdered in June 1844, the temple was only half complete. The succession crisis that followed Smith's death left the church in turmoil, with Brigham Young emerging as the leader sustained by the majority of the Saints in Nauvoo. The political climate in Illinois was deteriorating rapidly. Mob violence increased throughout the summer of 1845, creating an atmosphere of imminent danger. Despite the looming threat of expulsion, Young made a strategic and spiritual decision: he encouraged the Latter-day Saints to complete the temple even as they prepared to abandon the city.

The logic was urgent. If the temple could be finished, even partially, it could be used to perform essential ordinances before the church was scattered. The basement with its font was completed first, allowing for the administration of baptisms for the dead. As the winter of 1845–1846 approached, the temple began to be used for a flurry of sacred activities. It became a hive of religious intensity, hosting the Nauvoo-era endowment, sealings, and adoptions. The building was a sanctuary in a city turning into a war zone.

The temple's operational life was shockingly brief. In February 1846, the exodus began. Most of the Latter-day Saints left Nauvoo, crossing the frozen Mississippi River into the harsh winter of Iowa. A small crew remained behind, working feverishly to finish the first floor so the building could be formally dedicated. Once the pulpits and benches were installed, the dedication took place. But the end was already written. In September 1846, the remaining Latter-day Saints were finally driven from the city.

What followed was a systematic destruction of the physical symbol of the church. Vigilantes from neighboring regions, including Carthage, Illinois, entered the near-empty city and began to vandalize the temple. The church's agents attempted to lease the structure, first offering it to the Catholic Church and then to private individuals, but no one would take it. When leasing failed, they attempted to sell the temple, asking up to $200,000—a price that reflected its monumental value to the church but was a fantasy to the market. The effort met with no success.

The turning point came on March 11, 1848. The church's agents finally sold the building to David T. LeBaron for a mere $5,000. It was a fire sale of a sacred monument. Hope flickered briefly when the New York Home Missionary Society expressed interest in leasing the building as a school. But on the night of October 8–9, 1848, the temple's fate was sealed. Around midnight, an unknown arsonist set the building on fire.

Nauvoo's residents attempted to fight the blaze, but the fire was too intense. The temple was gutted, leaving only the four exterior walls standing. James J. Strang, the leader of the Strangite faction of Latter-day Saints, accused Brigham Young's agents of setting the fire, claiming it was a calculated act of destruction. However, Strang's charges were never proven, and the true identity of the arsonist remains a mystery to this day.

The ruins were then sold again. On April 2, 1849, LeBaron sold the damaged structure to Étienne Cabet for $2,000. Cabet was a French philosopher and utopian socialist whose followers were known as Icarians. They hoped to establish Nauvoo as a communistic utopia. Cabet and his followers began the arduous task of clearing the rubble and attempting to reconstruct the interior of the temple. But the building was cursed by both human malice and natural disaster.

On May 27, 1850, a major tornado struck Nauvoo. The storm seemed to single out the temple. It felled one of the remaining walls with a roar heard miles away, crushing eight stonemasons who were working on the reconstruction. The violence of the storm left the structure even more precarious. In the interests of public safety, Cabet ordered the demolition of two more walls, leaving only the façade standing. The Icarians repurposed the temple's stone, using much of it to build a new school building on the southwest corner of the lot.

By 1857, most of Cabet's followers had left Nauvoo. Over time, the stones of the temple were scavenged and used in the construction of other buildings throughout Hancock County. The physical presence of the temple was slowly erased from the landscape. In February 1865, the Nauvoo City Council ordered the final demolition of the last standing portion—the lone corner of the façade. Soon afterwards, all evidence of the temple disappeared, except for a hand pump over a well that had supplied water to the baptismal font. The grand structure, the "Bride of the Lamb," had been reduced to memory and scattered stones.

Three of the original sunstones survived this long march of destruction. They are now on display, serving as silent witnesses to the temple's history. One is on loan to the LDS Church's visitors' center in Nauvoo. Another resides in the Smithsonian Institution in Washington, D.C., a testament to its national historical significance. The third, the only one that has not been restored, is displayed along with the only moonstone on display at the Joseph Smith Historic Center of the Community of Christ. These stones are the physical remnants of the "woman clothed with the sun," preserved against the odds.

The story of the Nauvoo Temple did not end with its destruction. For decades, the lot sat empty, a scar on the landscape of the city. Between 1937 and 1962, the LDS Church began a decades-long effort to reacquire the square on which the temple had stood. In an uncontested public auction on February 19, 1937, the church purchased the exact portion of the block where the temple had been located for $900. The property had been listed and protected by the bank at auction several times previously because the prices were lower than expected, but the church was finally in a position to act.

Wilford C. Wood, a dedicated historian and preservationist, bought the majority of the remainder of the temple square in 1940, 1941, and 1951, transferring it to church ownership. The church continued to buy the remainder of the property through purchases in 1959, 1961, and 1962. The acquisition was complete. Between the temple's destruction and the acquisition of the square, many different buildings had been constructed on the property. After the church acquired most of the block, they cleared many of the structures and began archaeological excavations to locate any remnants of the temple. Melvin L. Fowler was hired to lead the preliminary investigation, which began in December of the relevant year, searching for the foundations of the house of worship that had once dominated the skyline.

The rediscovery of the site paved the way for a resurrection. In 2000, the church began to build a temple on the same site. The new structure was a marvel of modern engineering and historical reverence. Its exterior is a meticulous replica of the original Greek Revival design, complete with the sunstones, moonstones, and starstones that once adorned the facade. It stands 165 feet tall, a direct echo of the original. However, the interior is laid out like a modern Latter-day Saint temple, designed to meet the liturgical needs of the contemporary church while honoring the spiritual legacy of the past.

On June 27, 2002, the temple was dedicated by the LDS Church as the Nauvoo Illinois Temple. The date was not chosen at random; it coincided with the 158th anniversary of the death of Joseph and Hyrum Smith. It was a moment of profound closure and renewal. The temple that had been burned, torn down, and scattered had risen again. The cycle of destruction and rebirth was complete.

The journey of the Nauvoo Temple from a vision of stone to a ruin, and finally to a modern sanctuary, mirrors the journey of the people who built it. It is a story of resilience in the face of persecution, of a community that refused to let its spiritual center be erased. The original temple was a symbol of a people's hope and their fear of the future. The new temple is a symbol of their endurance and their commitment to the past.

The architectural details, once misunderstood as mere decoration, are now recognized as a profound statement of faith. The sun, moon, and stars are no longer just stones; they are a narrative of the church's identity, a visual theology that has survived fire and tornado. The baptismal font, once hidden in a basement, is now a focal point of the new temple's design, connecting the modern believers to the rituals performed in the winter of 1846.

Today, visitors to Nauvoo can stand on the same ground where Joseph Smith laid the cornerstone in 1841. They can look up at the replica of the original temple, its clock tower reaching toward the sky just as it did in the 19th century. They can touch the sunstones and see the reflection of the stars that once crowned the original structure. The story of the Nauvoo Temple is not just about a building; it is about the human capacity to create beauty in the midst of chaos, to find meaning in destruction, and to rebuild what was lost.
