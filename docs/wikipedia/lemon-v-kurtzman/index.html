Lemon v. Kurtzman

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Lemon v. Kurtzman

In 1968, the Pennsylvania legislature passed a law that seemed, on its face, like a straightforward act of educational thrift. The state would reimburse private schools for the salaries of teachers who instructed students in secular subjects—math, science, history—using only public textbooks and materials. The goal was noble: to improve education across the board by lightening the financial load on nonpublic institutions, most of which were Roman Catholic. Yet beneath this veneer of fiscal efficiency lay a constitutional earthquake waiting to happen. By 1971, the United States Supreme Court would strike down this Pennsylvania statute, along with a nearly identical one from Rhode Island, not merely because they subsidized religion, but because they created a relationship between government and faith so tangled that it could no longer be untangled without tearing the fabric of the First Amendment apart.

The case arrived at the nation's highest court under the name Lemon v. Kurtzman, 403 U.S. 602 (1971). It was a consolidation of two separate challenges, one from Pennsylvania led by David Kurtzman and the other from Rhode Island led by Alton Lemon. The stakes were deceptively simple: could the state pay the salaries of teachers working in religious schools without violating the Establishment Clause? For decades, American jurisprudence had danced around this question with cautious ambiguity. But the Supreme Court, in an 8–0 decision for Pennsylvania and an 8–1 decision for Rhode Island, decided that enough was enough. The statutes were unconstitutional. And to explain why, Chief Justice Warren Burger, writing for the majority, invented a new framework that would dominate First Amendment litigation for half a century: the Lemon test.

The logic of the case began with a stark reality check regarding the demographics of private education in Pennsylvania. A three-judge district court panel had found that while only 25% of elementary students attended private schools, an overwhelming 95% of those students were enrolled in Roman Catholic institutions. The sole beneficiaries of the new reimbursement acts were not a diverse array of independent academies but exactly 250 teachers at Roman Catholic parochial schools. The Court acknowledged that these schools served a vital community function, yet it also recognized a fundamental truth: the parochial school system was an integral part of the religious mission of the Catholic Church. To subsidize the salaries of teachers in such an environment, even for secular subjects, required the state to engage deeply with a religious enterprise.

The act at issue stipulated that eligible teachers must teach only courses offered in public schools, use only public materials, and agree not to teach religion. On paper, this sounded like a firewall. In practice, the Court saw it as an invitation for disaster. How could the state ensure that a teacher paid with public funds was not subtly weaving religious doctrine into a history or science lesson? To verify compliance, the government would have to inspect classrooms, review lesson plans, and monitor teachers on a near-continuous basis. This constant surveillance would create what Chief Justice Burger termed "excessive entanglement" between government and religion.

The Court held that for a statute to survive scrutiny under the Establishment Clause, it had to pass a three-pronged test. First, the Purpose Prong: the statute must have a secular legislative purpose. Second, the Effect Prong: the principal or primary effect of the statute must neither advance nor inhibit religion. Third, and perhaps most damning in this specific case, the Entanglement Prong: the statute must not result in excessive government entanglement with religion.

In Lemon, the statutes failed on all three counts, but it was the third prong that proved fatal to the state's defense. The Court reasoned that maintaining a system of checks and balances to ensure that public money was not supporting religious instruction would require an intrusive bureaucracy. It would force government officials into the inner sanctum of religious education, fostering a relationship of mutual dependency and suspicion. This was not the neutrality the Constitution demanded; it was a corrosive mixture of state power and ecclesiastical authority.

The ruling did not happen in a vacuum, nor did its legacy remain static for long. As legal scholars and jurists debated the boundaries of church and state over the subsequent decades, the Lemon test became both a shield and a sword, invoked to strike down prayer in schools and defended against attempts to blur the lines of secular governance. In 1985, in Wallace v. Jaffree, the Supreme Court clarified that if a law lacked any obvious secular purpose, there was no need to even examine the effect or entanglement prongs; the absence of a legitimate reason for the state's involvement was dispositive.

However, the Court also began to nuance its understanding of "purpose." In 1987, in Corporation of Presiding Bishop of Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints v. Amos, the justices wrote that the requirement of a secular purpose did not mean a law's intent had to be completely unrelated to religion. Citing the 1952 case Zorach v. Clauson, they warned against a standard that would require the government to show "callous indifference" toward religious groups. Instead, the Court clarified that Lemon's purpose requirement aimed to prevent the government from abandoning neutrality and acting with the specific intent of promoting a particular point of view. If the government acts with an ostensible and predominant purpose of advancing religion, it violates the central value of official religious neutrality.

By 1997, in Agostini v. Felton, the Supreme Court attempted to streamline the test. The justices combined the effect prong and the entanglement prong into a single analysis, leaving the purpose prong unchanged but modifying how the primary effect was evaluated. The new criteria for determining if government action advanced religion focused on three factors: whether there was government indoctrination, whether recipients were defined by their religion, and whether excessive entanglement persisted. This shift signaled that the Court was willing to look at the practical realities of aid distribution rather than relying solely on rigid theoretical barriers.

Yet, the Lemon test never achieved universal acceptance among the justices. Conservative jurists, in particular, viewed it as an impediment to a more natural ordering of church and state relations. Justice Antonin Scalia famously disparaged the test in his 1993 concurrence in Lamb's Chapel, comparing it to a "ghoul in a late night horror movie" that repeatedly returned from the grave despite being buried time and again. He argued that the test was unworkable, subjective, and contrary to historical tradition.

The tension between strict separationists and those favoring accommodation played out in high-profile cases for decades. In Santa Fe Independent School Dist. v. Doe (2000), the Court applied Lemon to strike down student-led prayer at football games. In McCreary County v. ACLU (2005), the Court refused to overturn Lemon, even when urged to do so, reaffirming that government neutrality was paramount. The test also became central in the landmark district court case Kitzmiller v. Dover, where it helped defeat a school board's attempt to teach intelligent design alongside evolution.

As the 21st century progressed, the political and legal landscape shifted dramatically toward religious accommodation. The Fourth Circuit Court of Appeals applied Lemon in 2017 to uphold an injunction against President Donald Trump's travel ban targeting majority-Muslim countries, highlighting the test's continued utility for protecting minority faiths from government hostility. But the tide was turning within the Supreme Court itself.

In American Legion v. American Humanist Association (2019), the case concerning the constitutionality of a massive World War I cross on public land in Maryland, the conservative justices made their dissatisfaction with Lemon unmistakable. Justice Samuel Alito, writing for the plurality, stated that the test had "shortcomings" and had become incapable of resolving modern Establishment Clause cases. He argued that rigid adherence to Lemon failed to account for historical context and long-standing traditions. Justice Brett Kavanaugh went further in his concurrence, noting that the Court "no longer applies the old test articulated in Lemon v. Kurtzman" and asserting that the decision was not good law.

Justice Clarence Thomas, a consistent critic of the separationist approach, declared that he would take the logical next step and overrule Lemon entirely. He viewed the test as an invention with no basis in the text or history of the First Amendment. Even Justice Neil Gorsuch labeled Lemon v. Kurtzman a "misadventure," claiming it had been effectively shelved by the Court's subsequent jurisprudence. Justice Elena Kagan, however, stood in defense of the test, arguing that while rigid application might not solve every problem, its focus on purpose and effect remained crucial for evaluating government action.

The final nail in Lemon's coffin came with the 2022 decision in Kennedy v. Bremerton School District. In a majority opinion written by Justice Gorsuch, the Court did not explicitly cite the overturning of Lemon, but it instructed lower courts to disregard the test entirely. Instead, they were told to evaluate religious actions in public schools based on history and tradition. This marked a definitive pivot away from the three-pronged analysis that had governed the nation for fifty years.

The end of Lemon was formalized in 2023 with Groff v. DeJoy, a unanimous case regarding religious accommodations in the workplace. In his opinion, Justice Alito described Lemon v. Kurtzman and the test it spawned as "now abrogated." The era of using a three-part checklist to determine if the government was crossing a line into religion had officially ended.

The journey from 1971 to 2023 reveals a profound evolution in how the United States understands the relationship between church and state. When Chief Justice Burger struck down the Pennsylvania and Rhode Island statutes, he was driven by a fear that public funds would inevitably become entangled with religious indoctrination. The solution was a bright-line test designed to keep the two spheres separate. But as the decades wore on, the Court's composition changed, and its philosophy shifted toward a more flexible, historically grounded approach.

For those seeking to understand the current legal landscape regarding LGBTQ rights, education, and religious freedom, the legacy of Lemon is essential context. The original case was about money and teachers; it was about whether the state could pay for secular instruction in a religious environment without compromising its neutrality. Today's debates over whether public schools can accommodate religious objections to LGBTQ inclusion echo the same fundamental tension: where does the right to free exercise end, and where does the government's obligation to remain neutral begin?

The Lemon test provided a structured way to answer that question for half a century. It forced courts to ask not just what the law did, but why it was passed and how it interacted with religious institutions. While the test is no longer the law of the land, its spirit—the insistence on examining government purpose and effect—remains embedded in the fabric of constitutional analysis. The abrogation of Lemon does not mean the end of scrutiny; it simply means that the scrutiny will now be guided by history and tradition rather than a rigid formula.

The human cost of these legal battles is often abstract, reduced to dollars and cents or procedural technicalities. But behind every statute like the one in Pennsylvania was a real teacher, a real family, and a real community trying to navigate the complex intersection of faith and civic duty. The teachers at those Catholic schools were not merely beneficiaries of state aid; they were employees caught in a constitutional tug-of-war between their religious mission and their public funding source. The Court's decision in 1971 protected them from becoming pawns in a political game, even as it stripped away the financial support they relied on.

As we look back at Lemon v. Kurtzman, it stands as a monument to a specific moment in American history—a time when the fear of government entanglement with religion was so profound that it demanded a strict, almost surgical separation. The fact that this standard has now been discarded reflects not just a change in legal theory, but a shift in the national consensus on what neutrality actually looks like. Whether one views this shift as a liberation or a retreat from constitutional principles depends largely on which side of the aisle one stands.

But the core question remains unchanged: How do we build a society where the government respects religion without endorsing it? Lemon tried to answer that with a checklist. The new jurisprudence tries to answer it with history. Both approaches seek the same elusive goal: a nation where faith is free, but not forced; where the state is neutral, but not indifferent.

The story of Lemon is not over. Even as abrogated, its shadow looms large over every case involving religious expression in public spaces. From the display of crosses on government property to the funding of charter schools with religious affiliations, the echoes of Burger's three-pronged test can still be heard. The Court may have put the ghoul to rest, but the questions it raised about power, faith, and money continue to haunt the American experiment.

In the end, Lemon v. Kurtzman teaches us that constitutional principles are not static statutes carved in stone, but living conversations that evolve with the times. The 1971 decision was a necessary correction to a specific legislative overreach. Its eventual obsolescence is a testament to the dynamic nature of American democracy. As we navigate the complex terrain of religious freedom today, understanding the rise and fall of the Lemon test offers more than just historical trivia; it provides a roadmap for the ongoing struggle to define the boundaries of our nation's conscience.

The specific numbers matter: 8-0 votes in 1971, 250 teachers affected, 95% of private students in Catholic schools. These are not just data points; they are the anchors that keep the legal theory grounded in reality. Without them, the debate becomes pure philosophy, detached from the people it affects. With them, we see the human stakes of every judicial decision.

As Justice Kagan noted, the focus on purpose and effects is crucial. Even if the test itself is gone, the inquiry remains. Why did the government do this? What does it accomplish? Who benefits? And who gets left behind? These are the questions that Lemon asked, and they are the questions we must continue to ask.

The abrogation of Lemon in 2023 marks a new chapter, but the story began with a simple act of reimbursement for teachers in Pennsylvania. It is a reminder that the most profound constitutional shifts often start with the smallest details of daily life: a teacher's salary, a textbook, a classroom door.

In the end, the legacy of Lemon v. Kurtzman is not just a legal test, but a lasting question about the nature of our republic. Can we be one people, united by law, while remaining distinct in faith? The answer changes with every generation, but the struggle to find it defines us.

The Supreme Court may have moved on from Lemon, but the tension between church and state remains the defining feature of American public life. As new cases arise—concerning prayer at graduations, funding for religious schools, or exemptions from anti-discrimination laws—the ghost of Lemon will continue to whisper its warnings about entanglement, even as the justices move forward with a new standard. The past is never fully dead; it shapes the present in ways we are only beginning to understand.

And so, as we close the book on Lemon v. Kurtzman, we must remember that the case was never just about money. It was about the soul of the nation and the delicate balance required to keep both government and religion free from each other's dominion. The test may be gone, but the principle remains: the state must walk a tightrope, careful not to favor one faith over another, or faith itself over unbelief. That is the enduring lesson of 1971, carried forward into an uncertain future.
