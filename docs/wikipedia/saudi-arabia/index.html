Saudi Arabia

10 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Saudi Arabia

In 1932, a royal decree issued by King Abdulaziz fused two distinct territories, Hejaz and Nejd, into a single entity named al-Mamlaka al-ʿArabiyya as-Suʿūdiyya. The translation is often rendered as "the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia," but a literal reading reveals a more potent truth: "the Saudi Kingdom of Arabia." This linguistic nuance was no accident; it was a declaration that the nation was the personal possession of the Al Saud dynasty, a family name derived from Saud ibn Muhammad ibn Muqrin, the 18th-century patriarch whose lineage would come to dominate the Arabian Peninsula. Today, that kingdom stands as the fifth-largest country in Asia, sprawling across 2.15 million square kilometers of the Middle East's heart, a landmass larger than all of Western Europe combined.

To understand the geopolitical weight of Saudi Arabia, one must first visualize its geography. It is a country defined by its borders and its isolation. To the west, the Red Sea carves a deep blue scar along the horizon, separating the kingdom from Egypt and Israel via the Gulf of Aqaba. To the east, the Persian Gulf laps against a coastline shared with Bahrain, Qatar, and the United Arab Emirates. Saudi Arabia holds a unique distinction in the region: it is the only nation with a coastline along both the Red Sea and the Arabian Gulf. This dual access has historically dictated its fate, serving as a bridge between continents and a barrier against invasion.

The interior, however, tells a different story of harshness and resilience. Most of the terrain is a vast tapestry of arid desert, lowland, and steppe, punctuated by mountain ranges that rise abruptly from the dust. This is not merely empty space; it is the stage for the world's most significant religious and political transformations. The capital, Riyadh, sits deep within the Najd plateau, a modern metropolis of glass and steel that contrasts sharply with the ancient dunes surrounding it. Yet, the spiritual gravity of the kingdom pulls toward the west, to Jeddah, the gateway to the two holiest cities in Islam: Mecca and Medina. Here, the population of 32.2 million gathers, making Saudi Arabia the fourth most populous nation in the Arab world, a demographic force that is rapidly shifting as nearly half the population is under the age of 25.

The narrative of Saudi Arabia cannot begin with the 20th century. It must begin in the deep past, in a time when the Arabian Peninsula was a cradle for humanity itself. Evidence suggests human habitation in this region dates back approximately 125,000 years. A pivotal study in 2011 traced the migration of the first modern humans leaving Africa, crossing the Bab-el-Mandeb strait into Arabia roughly 75,000 years ago. This land was not a barrier but a corridor, central to the dispersal of humanity across Asia. The environment was far different then, subject to extreme fluctuations during the Quaternary period that drove profound evolutionary changes.

By the Lower Paleolithic era, the region was marked by Oldowan-like sites, indicating significant early hominin colonization of Eurasia. As the Neolithic period dawned, a culture known as Al-Magar flourished in the southwestern Najd. Often described as a "Neolithic Revolution," Al-Magar represents one of the world's first instances of widespread animal domestication, particularly of the horse. The people of this culture carved statues from local stone, fixing them in central buildings that likely served as the nexus of social and religious life. The discovery of hunting scenes featuring domesticated dogs, resembling the Canaan Dog, in northwestern Saudi Arabia in 2017 pushed the timeline of human-animal partnership back more than 8,000 years, making them the earliest depictions of dogs in the world.

As the 4th millennium BC closed and the Bronze Age began, metals became the currency of power. The era was characterized by towering 2-meter burials and temples adorned with free-standing sculptures originally painted in vivid reds. In May 2021, archaeologists announced the discovery of a 350,000-year-old Acheulean site named An Nasim in the Hail region, potentially the oldest human habitation site in northern Saudi Arabia, with artifacts mirroring those found in the Nefud Desert. The sedentary culture of the Ubaid period at Dosariyah eventually gave way to aridity, only to be revived in the early 3rd millennium by the Dilmun civilization. Known to the Sumerians as a source of copper and imported woods, Dilmun's settlements, such as Umm an-Nussi, Umm ar-Ramadh, and the coastal Tarout, anchored the eastern province in a network of ancient trade that connected Mesopotamia to the wider world.

Yet, the defining moment of the region's history arrived in the early 7th century with the emergence of Islam. In what is now Saudi Arabia, the prophet Muhammad united the disparate tribes of the Arabian Peninsula into a single religious polity. Following his death in 632, the momentum of this new faith was unstoppable. Within decades, his followers expanded Muslim rule far beyond the peninsula, conquering territories in North Africa, Central Asia, South Asia, and even Iberia. The Arab dynasties originating from the lands of modern Saudi Arabia founded the Rashidun (632–661), Umayyad (661–750), Abbasid (750–1517), and Fatimid (909–1171) caliphates, shaping the political and cultural landscape of three continents for centuries.

The modern state, however, is a product of the 20th century. Following the collapse of the Ottoman Empire and the fragmentation of the region, King Abdulaziz began a relentless campaign in 1901 to reclaim his ancestral lands. Through a series of military and political maneuvers, he united Hejaz, Najd, parts of Eastern Arabia, and South Arabia (Asir) into a cohesive entity. On September 23, 1932, the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia was officially born. Since its founding, the country has been governed as an absolute monarchy under the House of Saud. The Basic Law defines the nation as a sovereign Arab Islamic state, with Islam as the official religion and Arabic as the official language.

For much of its modern history, the kingdom's political and cultural identity was forged in the fires of the Wahhabi movement, an ultraconservative branch of Sunni Islam that became the prevailing force in the 20th century. This religious doctrine was not merely a matter of belief but the bedrock of the state's legitimacy and social order. However, the 21st century has witnessed a dramatic pivot. Since the mid-2010s, the Saudi government, under the leadership of Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, has instituted a series of limited but significant reforms aimed at liberalizing social, cultural, and religious life. Women are now driving, cinemas have reopened, and the strict social codes of the past are being gradually dismantled in a bid to modernize the economy and society.

These changes are driven by necessity as much as ambition. The discovery of petroleum in 1938 transformed Saudi Arabia from a desert kingdom into a global energy superpower. Today, it stands as the world's second-largest oil producer and the leading oil exporter, controlling the second-largest oil reserves and sixth-largest gas reserves on the planet. This dependence on hydrocarbons has made the kingdom a central player in global economics, categorizing it as a World Bank high-income economy and the only Arab country among the G20 major economies. Its economy is the largest in the Middle East, ranking as the world's nineteenth-largest by nominal GDP and seventeenth-largest by purchasing power parity.

The wealth generated by oil has allowed the state to build a robust social safety net. Saudi Arabia offers free university tuition, imposes no personal income tax, and provides free universal health care. These benefits are underpinned by a unique demographic reality: the kingdom has the world's third-largest immigrant population. Foreign-born residents comprise roughly 40% of the total population, a testament to the kingdom's reliance on foreign labor to build its infrastructure and run its industries. This influx of talent has created a cosmopolitan layer beneath the surface of a deeply traditional society.

Politically, Saudi Arabia occupies a complex space in the international arena. It is considered both a regional and middle power, wielding influence that far exceeds its population size. It maintains a status as a major non-NATO ally of the United States, a relationship forged in the fires of World War II and solidified by the oil-for-security pact. Domestically, the kingdom has faced intense scrutiny for its human rights record, restrictions on political and civil liberties, and its military intervention in the Yemeni Civil War. These criticisms have often put the kingdom at odds with Western democracies, even as its strategic importance keeps it firmly within the US sphere of influence.

Despite these tensions, Saudi Arabia remains a key member of the global community. It is a founding member of the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC), the United Nations, the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC), the Arab League, and OPEC. It has also established itself as a dialogue partner of the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation, signaling a desire to diversify its geopolitical alliances beyond the West. The kingdom's foreign policy is increasingly driven by its Vision 2030 initiative, an economic roadmap designed to reduce dependence on oil and transform the nation into a global investment powerhouse.

The story of Saudi Arabia is one of contrasts. It is a land where ancient rock engravings of domesticated dogs sit alongside hyper-modern skyscrapers. It is a nation where the strictures of a conservative religious past are colliding with the aspirations of a young, tech-savvy generation. It is a country that controls the flow of the world's most vital commodity while grappling with the environmental consequences of that very industry. From the ancient hominins of the Nefud Desert to the oil fields of the Eastern Province, the narrative of the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia is the narrative of the Middle East itself: a story of survival, adaptation, and the relentless pursuit of power.

The name "Saudi" itself is a reminder of this dynastic control. Formed from the nisba of the Al Saud family, it expresses the view that the country is the patrimony of the royal house. Saud ibn Muhammad ibn Muqrin, the ancestor after whom the dynasty is named, laid the groundwork in the 18th century, but it was his descendant, Abdulaziz, who forged the modern state. The decree of 1932 was not just a political act; it was a coronation of a family's claim to the land. This personal possession of the state has shaped every aspect of its governance, from the succession of kings to the implementation of social reforms.

As the kingdom looks to the future, the challenges are immense. The dependence on foreign labor creates a fragile social fabric, while the youth bulge demands jobs and opportunities that the traditional oil economy cannot provide. The environmental degradation of the desert, the geopolitical tensions with neighbors like Iran and Yemen, and the pressure to reform without losing the cultural identity that anchors the regime are the defining issues of the next decade. Yet, the resilience of the Saudi people, forged over 125,000 years of habitation, suggests a capacity for adaptation that is difficult to underestimate.

The Saudi Arabia of today is a country in transition. It is no longer just the custodian of Islam's holiest sites or the world's oil pump. It is an emerging global player, seeking to carve out a new identity in a changing world order. The reforms of the mid-2010s were merely the opening chapter. The story of how this ancient land will navigate the complexities of the 21st century remains unwritten, but the foundations—laid by the first hominins, the prophets of old, and the kings of the 20th century—are as solid as the mountains of the Hijaz.
