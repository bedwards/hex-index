The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in the Lesser Antilles

Based on Wikipedia: The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in the Lesser Antilles

In September 1979, the doors of the Puerto Rico San Juan Mission swung open to a new frontier, marking the official beginning of organized missionary work in Barbados. It was a moment born not of geopolitical strategy or economic calculation, but of a single conversation between a man from Scotland and his new friends on a Caribbean island. That modest exchange rippled outward, eventually establishing a presence that, by the end of 2024, would include 1,205 members gathered across three congregations. This is the story of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in the Lesser Antilles—a narrative woven from the threads of isolated island communities, the quiet persistence of converts, and the complex administrative tapestry that binds them together under the Caribbean sun.

To understand the Church's footprint in the Lesser Antilles, one must first discard the image of a monolithic institution spreading uniformly across a landscape. The reality is far more granular, a mosaic of distinct national and cultural identities, each with its own history of arrival, growth, and organizational structure. As of 2024, the Church reported a total of 10,276 members scattered across 34 congregations throughout the archipelago. These islands, a chain of volcanic and coral formations stretching from the Virgin Islands to Trinidad, are part of the broader Caribbean Area, yet they are administratively fractured into three distinct missions. This fragmentation is not a sign of weakness, but a reflection of the linguistic, political, and geographical realities that define the region. The Church does not merely exist here; it adapts to the specific contours of each island nation, often serving as the only unifying religious structure for dispersed communities.

The ABC Islands and the Trinidad Hub

The administrative heart of this region beats in Port of Spain, Trinidad and Tobago, which serves as the headquarters for the Trinidad Port of Spain Mission. This mission oversees a diverse cluster of islands, most notably the ABC Islands: Aruba, Bonaire, and Curaçao. Here, the Church's presence is defined by a central district centered in Oranjestad, Aruba. By the close of 2024, Aruba alone accounted for 661 members across two congregations, located in the capital city of Oranjestad and the southern town of San Nicolás. Both of these meetinghouses host family history centers, vital hubs for members seeking to trace their genealogical roots—a practice that holds profound significance in Latter-day Saint theology.

The organizational reach of the Oranjestad district extends beyond Aruba's sandy shores to encompass Bonaire and Curaçao. In Bonaire, a small island nation, the Church maintains a single branch with a reported 80 members as of 2015, gathering in Kralendijk. While the numbers are modest, the presence is constant, anchored by a meetinghouse that also houses a family history center. The narrative of growth here is one of consolidation rather than rapid expansion, a testament to the enduring nature of small, tight-knit congregations.

Curaçao presents a more robust picture. In Willemstad, the capital, the Curaçao Branch reported 602 members in 2024. Like its neighbors in the district, the Willemstad meetinghouse serves as a repository for family history resources. These three islands, though distinct in their colonial histories and languages, are united in the LDS administrative structure under the ABC Islands District. This arrangement highlights a pragmatic approach to ecclesiastical governance: grouping smaller populations to ensure they have access to the same resources and leadership structures, preventing the isolation that can plague small congregations in remote locations.

Barbados: A Timeline of Persistence

If the ABC Islands represent the northern anchor of the mission, Barbados stands as a historical cornerstone of the Church's work in the eastern Caribbean. The story of the Church in Barbados is one of remarkable patience. International church leaders had visited the island as early as the 1950s, yet for decades, the seeds planted during these visits lay dormant. The breakthrough did not come from a grand campaign but from the simple, human act of sharing faith. In 1978, a convert from Scotland shared his beliefs with friends on the island, resulting in the first baptism. It was a quiet beginning, but it ignited a fire that would not be extinguished.

The following year, in September 1979, the Puerto Rico San Juan Mission officially opened Barbados to full-time missionary work. The momentum was immediate. By the next month, the first congregation was organized in Christ Church. The growth trajectory was steady and deliberate. By 1983, both seminary and institute programs—the Church's religious education systems for youth and young adults—were operational, signaling a deepening commitment to the community's spiritual development. In 1988, Elder Marvin J. Ashton, a leader of the Quorum of Twelve Apostles, formally dedicated the islands of the West Indies for missionary work, a ritual that underscored the spiritual significance of the region.

The Church's engagement in Barbados has always extended beyond the pulpit. Since 1985, the Church has conducted twelve humanitarian projects in the country, including eight community initiatives and four major wheelchair donation drives. These projects serve as a tangible expression of the Church's values, addressing physical needs while building bridges of goodwill within the wider community. As of May 2025, the ecclesiastical structure in Barbados is organized under the Christ Church Barbados District, comprising the Black Rock Branch, the Christ Church Branch, and the Oistins Branch. It is a structure built on the foundation of those early converts, growing from a single conversation into a network of three distinct congregations serving a total of 1,205 members.

The Guadeloupe District: A Multilingual Mosaic

Moving northward into the French Caribbean, the organizational landscape shifts to accommodate a different linguistic and cultural reality. The Guadeloupe District, headquartered in Les Abymes, serves as a critical hub for the Barbados Bridgetown Mission. This district is unique in its scope, covering not only Guadeloupe itself but also French Guiana, Martinique, and Sint Maarten. It is a testament to the Church's ability to transcend language barriers, as the congregations here operate primarily in French, yet remain administratively linked to English-speaking leadership in Bridgetown.

In Guadeloupe, the Church reported 585 members in 2024, distributed across three congregations located in Basse-Terre, Lamentin, and Les Abymes. The meetinghouse in Les Abymes doubles as the district center and houses a family history center, serving as the administrative and spiritual anchor for the wider region. The presence of a family history center in a French-speaking territory underscores the universal nature of the Church's genealogical focus, regardless of the language spoken by the congregation.

Martinique, the sister island to Guadeloupe, has a single congregation with 273 members as of December 2024. The Fort-de-France Branch, organized on October 10, 1985, serves the entire island nation. Like its counterpart in Les Abymes, the Fort-de-France meetinghouse contains a family history center. The establishment of these branches in the mid-1980s coincided with a period of expansion for the Church in the Caribbean, suggesting a strategic push to formalize the presence of the faith in the French territories.

Sint Maarten, the northern portion of the island of Saint Martin, presents a fascinating linguistic complexity. The Philipsburg Branch, organized on November 26, 1985, serves a diverse population spanning Anguilla, Saba, Saint Barthélemy, Saint Martin, and Sint Maarten. With 289 members reported in 2024, this branch operates in a trilingual environment, conducting services and activities in English, French, and Spanish. This polyglot nature makes the Philipsburg Branch a unique microcosm of the Caribbean, where cultural and linguistic boundaries are fluid, and the Church's message must be delivered with equal fluency across three major languages.

The Eastern Chain: From Grenada to Saint Kitts

The southern and eastern reaches of the Lesser Antilles offer their own distinct stories of community building. In Grenada, the Church's presence is centered in St. George's. The St. George's Branch, created on September 3, 1985, serves the entire island nation. By the end of 2024, it reported 420 members. Grenada falls under the Kingstown St. Vincent District, which is part of the Barbados Bridgetown Mission, linking it administratively to the larger eastern Caribbean network.

Just to the north, Saint Vincent and the Grenadines boasts a slightly larger congregation with 728 members spread across three meetinghouses in Calliaqua, Georgetown, and Kingstown. Kingstown serves as the district center for the Kingstown St. Vincent District, a role that encompasses Grenada, Saint Lucia, and Saint Vincent and the Grenadines. The concentration of resources in Kingstown, including a family history center, reflects the strategic importance of the capital in serving the wider district.

Saint Lucia, situated between Martinique and Barbados, maintains two congregations in Castries and Vieux Fort, with a combined membership of 406 in 2024. Both meetinghouses feature family history centers, a consistent theme across the region. The Church in Saint Lucia is also part of the Kingstown St. Vincent District, reinforcing the interconnectedness of these island nations within the LDS administrative framework.

Further north, Saint Kitts and Nevis presents a story of consolidation. The St. Kitts Branch, organized on September 10, 1985, meets in Basseterre and serves the entire nation of Saint Kitts and Nevis, as well as members from the nearby island of Sint Eustatius. With 244 members reported in 2024, the branch represents a significant presence for such a small population. The inclusion of Sint Eustatius members highlights the Church's willingness to cross political boundaries to serve its members, a practice that is essential in the scattered geography of the Lesser Antilles.

Dominica and the Northern Outposts

In the rugged, volcanic landscape of Dominica, the Church's presence is concentrated in a single branch. The Portsmouth Branch was created on January 4, 2007, a relatively recent addition to the regional map compared to the branches established in the 1980s. Despite its later founding, the branch serves the entire island nation, reporting 173 members in 2024. The establishment of the Portsmouth Branch in the 21st century suggests a continued, albeit slower, expansion of the Church into areas that may have been previously overlooked or too difficult to reach.

Antigua and Barbuda, located further east, has a single congregation serving the entire nation. The St. John's Branch was created on January 15, 1985, and by 2024, it reported 342 members. The meetinghouse in St. John's houses a family history center, ensuring that even in this isolated outpost, members have access to the Church's genealogical resources. The longevity of the St. John's Branch, established in the same wave of expansion as many of its neighbors, speaks to the stability of the Church's early efforts in the region.

Trinidad and Tobago: A Case Study in Strategic Growth

While the Lesser Antilles islands form a chain of small, scattered congregations, Trinidad and Tobago represents the most significant concentration of Latter-day Saints in the Caribbean. The Church's presence here is so substantial that it is organized as a stake—the highest level of local church organization, encompassing multiple congregations. The Port of Spain Trinidad and Tobago Stake covers the entire country, a designation that reflects the depth of the Church's roots in the nation.

The history of the Church in Trinidad is a compelling narrative of persistence against bureaucratic hurdles. The story begins in late 1940, when missionaries returning from South Africa stopped in Trinidad briefly and taught a congregation. However, it would take decades for the work to take root. In 1955, Ezra Taft Benson, then a member of the Quorum of Twelve Apostles, visited Trinidad during a tour of the Caribbean and South America. He noted that a church member was serving as one of the secretaries of the consulate, a detail that hints at the early, informal connections the Church had with the island's elite.

The turning point came in the 1970s, driven by the personal journey of Elizabeth Anne Rogers. Born in Trinidad, she traveled to England where she married Emil Paul Dopson, a member of the LDS Church. Baptized two months after her marriage, she returned to Trinidad in 1976. Recognizing the need for formal missionary work, she wrote a letter to Church President Spencer W. Kimball, requesting that full-time missionaries be sent to the island. Her initiative, combined with the willingness of the Trinidad government to allow missionaries to enter, broke the deadlock.

The first sacrament meeting was held in November 1976, marking the formal rebirth of the Church in Trinidad. Among the first converts were Lucy Josephine Payne and Blasil D. and Felicia Borde, baptized in 1977 by Daniel Rector and Michael Willis. These individuals became the foundation of the new community. On June 5, 1980, the Trinidad Branch was organized in Port of Spain, with Errol O. Balfour as its first branch president. This was the first official congregation in the nation, a milestone that paved the way for the eventual formation of a stake.

The 1980s and 1990s were a period of strategic growth and community integration. In the mid-1980s, missionaries organized a city-wide cross-country race, a visible and energetic way to introduce the Church to the broader public. In 1987, Frank and Arline Talley, church representatives from Puerto Rico, organized a health fair in Trinidad to teach hygiene and the principles of the Word of Wisdom. This event was more than just a religious outreach; it was a demonstration of the Church's commitment to public health and well-being.

One of the most significant outcomes of the health fair was the conversion of Kevin Diaz. At the time, Diaz was the chief executive of the Boy Scouts of Trinidad and the head of civil service training for the government. After learning about the LDS Church at the fair, he was baptized. His conversion had profound implications for the Church's future in the country. Diaz discovered that missionaries were restricted to short-term tourist visas, limiting their effectiveness. Using his high-level government contacts, he arranged for up to ten missionaries to stay for one-year periods, with special cases allowing for even longer durations. This administrative breakthrough transformed the mission from a series of short visits into a sustained, long-term effort.

Diaz's career was a testament to the integration of faith and public service. For 14 years, prior to his retirement, he served as the manager of organization planning and development for British West Indian Airways. Beyond his professional achievements, he was active in the Boy Scouts and served in Red Cross hurricane disaster relief. His dedication to public service was recognized with the award of Member of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire. After his retirement, he continued to serve the Church as a first counselor, bridging the gap between his civic leadership and his religious commitment.

Today, the Church in Trinidad and Tobago stands as a model of growth in the region. As of 2024, there are 3,497 members in 10 congregations, all under the umbrella of the Port of Spain Stake. This concentration of members and the existence of a stake reflect a maturity and stability that is rare in the smaller, more isolated islands of the Lesser Antilles. The story of Trinidad is one of how individual initiative, strategic community engagement, and administrative innovation can transform a small group of converts into a thriving, organized community.

The Human Tapestry of Faith

The story of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in the Lesser Antilles is not merely a collection of statistics or a timeline of organizational milestones. It is a human story, woven from the lives of individuals like Elizabeth Anne Rogers, who dared to write a letter that changed the course of her nation's religious history. It is the story of Kevin Diaz, who used his government influence to secure the future of missionary work. It is the story of the Scottish convert who started it all in Barbados, and the thousands of members who now gather in meetinghouses from Kralendijk to Castries, from Willemstad to Port of Spain.

These congregations, whether they are small branches with a few dozen members or large stakes with thousands, share a common purpose. They are communities of faith that have adapted to the unique challenges of island life. They navigate linguistic barriers, political boundaries, and geographical isolation to create spaces where members can worship, serve, and connect. The presence of family history centers in almost every meetinghouse underscores a shared desire to connect with the past, to understand one's roots, and to build a future that honors those who came before.

The Church's humanitarian efforts, from wheelchair donations in Barbados to health fairs in Trinidad, demonstrate a commitment to the well-being of the entire community, not just its members. These projects are a testament to the idea that faith is not just a private belief but a public force for good. They show that the Church is willing to roll up its sleeves and work alongside the people of the Lesser Antilles, addressing the practical needs of the islands while nurturing the spiritual lives of its members.

As the Church continues to grow and evolve in the region, it faces new challenges. The shifting political landscapes, the impact of climate change on small island nations, and the changing demographics of the Caribbean all present new opportunities and obstacles. Yet, the foundation built over the last few decades remains strong. The stories of the early converts, the strategic innovations of leaders like Kevin Diaz, and the quiet persistence of missionaries and members alike have created a resilient network of faith that is uniquely suited to the Caribbean.

In the end, the presence of the Church in the Lesser Antilles is a reminder of the power of human connection. It is a story of how a few individuals, armed with nothing but their faith and their willingness to serve, can build a community that spans islands, languages, and cultures. It is a story of adaptation, of resilience, and of the enduring hope that binds people together in the face of adversity. As the sun sets over the Caribbean, casting a golden glow on the meetinghouses of the Lesser Antilles, it illuminates a community that is as vibrant and diverse as the islands themselves, ready to face the future with the same courage and commitment that brought them to this point.
