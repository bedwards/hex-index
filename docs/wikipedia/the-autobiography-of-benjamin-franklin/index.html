The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin

16 min read

Based on Wikipedia: The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin

In the summer of 1771, sixty-five-year-old Benjamin Franklin sat in the quiet estate of Twyford, England, and began a letter to his son, William. He was not writing a will, nor a political manifesto, but something far more intimate: a record of his own life. He called it his Memoirs. History, however, would rename it The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin, a title that now stands as the definitive label for one of the most influential works of non-fiction ever penned. Written in fits and starts between 1771 and 1790, the manuscript remained unfinished, trailing off at the year 1758 when Franklin was fifty-two. It is a strange artifact, a life story halted decades before the end, yet it captures the very essence of the American experiment in the making. As F. W. Pine noted in his 1916 introduction, Franklin provided the "most remarkable of all the remarkable histories of our self-made men," serving as the ultimate exemplar of a life constructed not by birthright, but by grit, intellect, and relentless self-improvement.

The narrative is not a seamless flow but a mosaic of four distinct parts, separated by years of silence and the violent tides of history. The first part, addressed to his son William, who was then the forty-one-year-old Royal Governor of New Jersey, opens with the humblest of origins. Franklin details his parents and grandparents, painting a portrait of a family rooted in the soap-boiling trade of England before their migration to the New World. He describes a childhood defined not by privilege, but by an insatiable hunger for books. This literary voracity would soon collide with his professional training as an apprentice to his older brother, James Franklin, a printer and publisher of the New-England Courant in Boston.

It was here that the young Franklin's genius for subversion first ignited. A devoted reader of Joseph Addison and Sir Richard Steele's Spectator, Franklin decided to test his brother's press. Under the cover of night, he slipped anonymous essays under the door of the printing house, writing them in the voice of a fictional widow named Silence Dogood. James Franklin, unaware of the author's identity, was delighted by the wit and published the pieces. The Courant became a sensation, and the "Silence Dogood" essays were later collected, marking Franklin's first public triumph. When the ruse was finally revealed, the reaction was not one of paternal pride but of brotherly fury. James was enraged that his apprentice had bested him in his own medium. This conflict escalated into a series of disputes that culminated in Franklin's decision to break his indenture. In a twist of irony, James was jailed by the authorities for publishing seditious material, leading him to have the paper continue under Benjamin's name to evade the ban. However, James attempted to draft secret indenture papers to bind Benjamin to the trade for another term. When a fresh disagreement arose, Franklin, correctly judging his brother would not produce these secret papers, abandoned his post.

Franklin later admitted, "It was not fair in me to take this Advantage... and this I therefore reckon one of the first Errata of my life." Yet, the breach was irreparable. James made it impossible for Benjamin to find work anywhere else in Boston. The path forward required a radical break. Without the knowledge of his father or brother, the seventeen-year-old Franklin sneaked onto a ship bound for New York City. His destination was a printer named William Bradford, but upon arrival, he found no work. Bradford, however, directed him to his son Andrew in Philadelphia, a printer who had recently lost an employee. Thus began Franklin's arrival in Philadelphia, a city that would become his home and the stage for his greatest achievements.

In Philadelphia, Franklin found work under Samuel Keimer, a printer of questionable character. His fortunes seemed to shift when Sir William Keith, the Governor of Pennsylvania, took notice of the young man's intellect. Keith offered to set Franklin up in business for himself, promising letters of introduction and credit in London. It was a seductive offer, and Franklin, eager to escape the drudgery of Keimer's shop, sailed for London. The moment he arrived, the promise evaporated. Keith had written no letter of recommendation. As Franklin later discovered, "no one who knew him had the smallest Dependence on him." The Governor was a man of empty words, and Franklin was left stranded in a foreign city with little work and no support.

He eventually returned to Philadelphia, working briefly as an assistant to Thomas Denham, a Quaker merchant. When Denham died unexpectedly, Franklin returned to Keimer's shop, only to quit once more over a dispute regarding wages. This time, he was ready to strike out on his own. He formed a partnership with Hugh Meredith, a former co-worker whose father provided the necessary capital. While Meredith's father funded the venture, it was Franklin who did the heavy lifting; Meredith, prone to drinking, was not much of a worker. Their ambition was to launch a newspaper. Keimer, sensing the threat, rushed out a paper of his own, the Pennsylvania Gazette. It was a failure. Franklin, seizing the opportunity, bought the Gazette from Keimer and transformed it into a highly profitable enterprise. The Saturday Evening Post would later trace its lineage directly to this publication. The partners also secured the lucrative appointment as printers for the Pennsylvania Assembly.

Financial setbacks eventually forced Meredith's father to withdraw his support, but friends loaned Franklin the capital to keep the operation alive. When Meredith decided to relocate to North Carolina, the partnership was dissolved amicably, leaving Franklin as the sole proprietor of a thriving business. By 1730, he had married Deborah Read. With his business stable, Franklin turned his attention to the community. With the help of the Junto, a club of mutual improvement he had founded, he drafted the proposals for the Library Company of Philadelphia, creating the first lending library in America. Part One of the Autobiography ends abruptly with a simple note from Franklin: "The Affairs of the Revolution occasion'd the Interruption."

The silence that followed was not merely a pause in writing; it was a chasm created by the American Revolution. The second part of the manuscript begins in the early 1780s, with Franklin residing in Paris. He had received letters from correspondents who had read Part One and urged him to continue. The context, however, was profoundly different from the first. The father and son had been torn apart by the war. William Franklin remained a Loyalist, serving as the British Governor of New Jersey, while Benjamin had become a leading figure of the American Revolution. This breach is never explicitly mentioned in the text, but it hangs heavy over the narrative. In 1784, at Passy, a suburb of Paris, Franklin resumed his writing. He provided a more detailed account of his public library plan and then introduced what would become the most famous section of the entire work: his "bold and arduous Project of arriving at moral Perfection."

Franklin did not simply list virtues; he engineered a system. He identified thirteen virtues he wished to cultivate, including Temperance, Silence, Order, Resolution, Frugality, Industry, Sincerity, Justice, Moderation, Cleanliness, Tranquility, Chastity, and Humility. To track his progress, he created a small book with a page for each virtue, divided into columns for the days of the week. Each evening, he would review his conduct and mark a black spot against any virtue he had failed to uphold. It was a mechanical approach to the spiritual, a scientific method applied to the soul. He noted that Order was the most difficult virtue to maintain, admitting that his memory for details and schedules was naturally chaotic. Franklin eventually realized that absolute perfection was unattainable, yet the attempt itself made him "a better and a happier man." The exercise was not about the destination but the discipline of the journey.

Beginning in August 1788, after returning to Philadelphia, Franklin noted that he could not rely on his papers as much as he had hoped. The Revolutionary War had scattered or destroyed many of his records. Undeterred, he quoted from writings of the 1730s that had survived, including the "Substance of an intended Creed." This document outlined what he considered the "Essentials" of all religions, a rationalist attempt to distill faith down to its core moral truths. He had once intended this as the basis for a new sect but ultimately abandoned the project. Instead, he turned his gaze to his literary successes. In 1732, he had first published Poor Richard's Almanack, a work that became a massive success, filling the shelves of colonists with proverbs and weather forecasts. He continued his profitable newspaper and engaged in complex social dynamics, such as his support for the Reverend Samuel Hemphill, a preacher from County Tyrone, Ireland. When Hemphill was exposed for plagiarizing his sermons, Franklin rationalized the act, arguing that he would rather hear good sermons taken from others than poor ones of the man's own composition.

Franklin's life in these years was a tapestry of intellectual and civic expansion. He studied languages, reconciled with his brother James, and suffered the profound tragedy of losing a four-year-old son to smallpox. His Junto club grew and eventually splintered into subordinate clubs, spreading its influence throughout the city. In 1736, he was appointed Clerk of the General Assembly, marking his formal entry into politics. The following year, he became Comptroller to the Postmaster General, a role that significantly improved his ability to gather news and fulfill newspaper subscriptions. He proposed improvements to the city's watch and fire prevention regulations, laying the groundwork for organized public safety in Philadelphia. When the famed preacher George Whitefield arrived in 1739, Franklin, despite their significant theological differences, assisted him by printing his sermons and journals and lodging him in his house. As Franklin's success grew, he began providing the capital for several of his workers to start printing houses of their own, fostering a generation of entrepreneurs.

The third and fourth parts of the Autobiography, though less detailed in their biographical narrative than the first two, offer a window into Franklin's later years as a statesman and diplomat. Written in the 1780s and 1790s, these sections reflect a man looking back on a life that had shaped a nation. The narrative continues seamlessly from Part Three into Part Four, bridging the gap without an authorial break. The work concludes with events from 1758, leaving the reader with a sense of a life in full bloom, just as the storm of revolution was about to break. Franklin would die in 1790 at the age of eighty-four, long after he stopped writing this memoir, yet the text remains a living testament to his philosophy.

The Autobiography is more than a history of a man; it is a history of an idea. It is the story of how a boy from a soap-boiler's family in Boston could become a printer, an inventor, a scientist, a diplomat, and a founding father. Franklin's account is divided by the breaks in his writing, but the thread that binds the four parts is the relentless drive for self-betterment and public service. He presents himself not as a saint, but as a work in progress, acknowledging his "errata" and his failures. This honesty is what makes the work so enduring. It invites the reader to believe that if Franklin could rise from such humble beginnings, then perhaps they, too, could shape their own destinies.

The publication history of the work was as tortuous as the writing of it. Franklin died before he could see it in print. The manuscript passed through various hands, and it was not until decades later that it was published in French, then English, and eventually became a global phenomenon. The various editions and translations have cemented its place in the canon of world literature. It is a book that has inspired generations of self-made men and women, from Napoleon to Ralph Waldo Emerson, to pursue their own projects of moral and intellectual perfection. The Autobiography is a testament to the power of the written word to transcend time, to bridge the gap between the past and the future, and to offer a roadmap for a life well-lived.

In the end, Franklin's Memoirs is a conversation across time. He speaks to his son, to his contemporaries, and to us. He speaks of the importance of industry, of the value of education, of the necessity of civic engagement, and of the possibility of moral growth. He does not offer a perfect life, but a life of effort. He shows us that perfection is not a state of being, but a practice of becoming. The black spots on his virtue chart are not failures; they are reminders of the work that remains. And so, the Autobiography remains unfinished, not because Franklin stopped writing, but because the project of self-improvement never ends. It is a story that continues as long as there are readers willing to pick up the book and ask themselves: what are my virtues? What are my errata? And what will I do about them?

The legacy of Franklin's Autobiography is its ability to speak to the universal human desire for a better life. It is a guidebook for the ambitious, a comfort for the struggling, and a challenge for the complacent. It reminds us that history is not just made by kings and generals, but by printers, inventors, and thinkers who dare to imagine a better world and work tirelessly to build it. Franklin's life, as he tells it, is a blueprint for the American dream, but it is also a universal story of human potential. It is a story that began in a Boston print shop and ended in the hearts of millions around the world.
