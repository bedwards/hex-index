List of Huawei products

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: List of Huawei products

In the winter of 2012, a device called the Huawei Ascend G300 landed on store shelves, marking the beginning of a relentless, decade-long campaign to redefine what a smartphone could be. It was a humble beginning, a mid-range handset with a 4-inch screen that felt utilitarian compared to the sleek glass rectangles emerging from California and South Korea. Yet, this device was the first brick in a wall that would eventually tower over the global technology landscape. From that initial G-series entry to the futuristic, triple-folding Mate XT of 2024, the history of Huawei is not merely a chronicle of product releases; it is a narrative of strategic evolution, from a chaotic proliferation of models to a disciplined ascent into the ultra-premium tier, and finally, a rebranding that signaled a new era of independence.

The early days of Huawei's smartphone era were defined by an almost aggressive breadth of options. The G-series, which dominated the market from 2012 through 2016, was a testament to a strategy of volume and segmentation. The Ascend G300, G312, and G330 all launched in the same year, 2012, creating a confusing but comprehensive net for consumers. By 2013, the lineup had swelled to include the G510, G525, and the massive Ascend Mate, a device that introduced the "phablet" concept to a wider audience before Samsung had fully cemented the Galaxy Note's dominance. The G-series was the workhorse, the volume driver. It was the Huawei that people bought when they needed a reliable phone without breaking the bank. The G6, G7, and G8 followed in quick succession, each iteration chipping away at the perception that Huawei was a budget brand. The G8, released in 2015, was a turning point, bridging the gap between the entry-level G-series and the emerging premium aspirations of the company.

But as the company grew, so did the complexity of its branding. The same device would wear different masks in different markets. The Honor 4X, launched in 2015, was the Huawei G Play in some regions. The Honor 4C was the G Play Mini. The Honor 5X became the GR5. This was not mere marketing confusion; it was a calculated strategy to test waters, to segment the youth market under the "Honor" banner while keeping the core "Huawei" brand focused on professional and premium users. The Honor brand, originally a sub-brand aimed at the youth, became a vehicle to pack advanced features into lower-cost devices. The Honor 6, released in 2014, and its successor, the Honor 7 in 2015, were critical in this regard. They offered the build quality and camera technology of the flagship P and Mate lines at a price point that undercut competitors by hundreds of dollars. The Honor 7i, with its unique rotating camera, and the Honor 8, with its iridescent glass back, showed that even the budget-conscious segment demanded style.

While the G and Honor lines chased volume, the Ascend P-series was chasing perfection. Launched in 2012 with the Ascend P1, this line was Huawei's attempt to capture the design-conscious consumer. The P1 was thin, but the P6, released a year later, was a revelation. At just 6.18mm thick, it was the thinnest smartphone in the world at the time, a physical feat that garnered headlines globally. The P-series became the style icon of the Huawei ecosystem. The P7, the P8, and the P9 continued this legacy. The P9, released in 2016, was particularly significant. It was the first Huawei phone to feature a dual-camera system developed in partnership with Leica, a collaboration that would become the cornerstone of Huawei's camera reputation. The P9 Pro, the P9 Lite, and the various iterations of the P8 Max and P8 Lite showed a company willing to experiment with form factors, from massive tablets to compact, stylish slabs.

However, the true revolution in Huawei's product history came with the Mate series. If the P-series was about style, the Mate series was about power. It began with the Ascend Mate in 2013, a device that was larger and more powerful than anything else on the market. The Mate 7, released in 2014, was a critical success, particularly in China and Europe, establishing Huawei as a serious contender in the high-end market. But it was the Mate 8 and Mate 9 that truly cemented the brand's status. The Mate 9, launched in 2016, introduced the Leica dual-camera system to the premium large-screen category, offering a zoom lens and a monochrome sensor that produced images with a depth and clarity previously unseen in smartphones.

The trajectory of the Mate series is a story of relentless innovation. The Mate 10, released in 2017, brought the Kirin 970, Huawei's first AI-enabled mobile processor, changing how phones processed data. The Mate 20 series in 2018 pushed the boundaries of battery life and camera aperture. The Mate 30 series in 2019, released amidst the growing geopolitical tensions between the US and China, was a statement of defiance. It featured a circular camera array that became an instant icon, and it was the first major flagship to launch without Google Mobile Services, relying instead on Huawei's own AppGallery. The Mate 40, released in 2020, was a masterpiece of engineering, featuring the Kirin 9000, a chip that many experts considered the most advanced in the world at the time. It was the last major flagship to be built with a full supply chain of Western components, a point that would soon become a focal point of global trade wars.

The evolution did not stop at traditional smartphones. Huawei's foray into foldable technology with the Mate X in 2019 was a gamble that paid off in terms of prestige. The Mate X, followed by the Mate Xs, X2, X3, X5, and X6, represented a technological leap that few competitors could match. The Mate Xs 2, released in 2022, refined the folding mechanism, making the device thinner and more durable. The Mate X3 and X5 continued this trend, proving that foldable phones were not a gimmick but a viable future. The Mate XT, released in 2024, took this a step further, becoming the world's first commercial tri-fold smartphone. It was a device that could unfold into a 10-inch tablet, a technological marvel that showcased Huawei's ability to innovate even under extreme pressure.

The rebranding of the P-series to the Pura series in 2024 marked a new chapter in Huawei's history. The Pura 70 series, with its unique triangular camera design, signaled a shift towards a more distinct, artistic identity. The Pura 70 Ultra, with its retractable lens, was a technical feat that pushed the limits of what a smartphone camera could do. The Pura 80 series, announced for 2025, and the Pura 90 series, slated for 2026, promise to continue this legacy of innovation. The Pura X, a foldable device, and the Pura X Max, a larger variant, suggest that Huawei is doubling down on the foldable form factor as a core part of its premium strategy.

The Mate series also continued to evolve, with the Mate 60 series in 2023 making headlines for its return to 5G capabilities despite US sanctions. The Mate 60 Pro, with its Kirin 9000S chip, was a symbol of technological resilience. The Mate 70 series, launched in 2024, and the Mate 80 series, planned for 2025, represent the next generation of Huawei's flagship devices. The Mate 80 Pro Max, with its advanced camera system and AI capabilities, is expected to set new standards for the industry. The Mate 70 RS Ultimate Design and the Mate 80 RS Ultimate Design, in collaboration with Porsche Design, continue the tradition of luxury smartphones, offering a blend of high-end materials and exclusive design.

The impact of Huawei's product strategy extends beyond the devices themselves. The company's ability to innovate under pressure, to develop its own operating system, HarmonyOS, and to build a robust ecosystem of devices, has changed the global tech landscape. The P series, the Mate series, the Nova series, and the foldable devices are not just products; they are testaments to a company's vision and determination. The Nova series, aimed at mobile selfie enthusiasts, has become a favorite among younger consumers, with devices like the Nova 2i, Nova 3e, and Nova 6 SE offering high-quality cameras at accessible prices. The Y series, though now discontinued, played a crucial role in establishing Huawei's presence in the entry-level market, with devices like the Y200, Y300, and Y500 providing affordable connectivity to millions.

The story of Huawei is also a story of adaptation. The company has navigated the complexities of global trade, the challenges of supply chain disruptions, and the shifting dynamics of the smartphone market. From the early G-series devices to the cutting-edge foldables of today, Huawei has consistently demonstrated its ability to innovate and adapt. The Mate Xs 2, the Pura 70 Ultra, and the Mate 80 Pro Max are not just phones; they are symbols of a company that refuses to be defined by external constraints.
