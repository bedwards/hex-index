1998 Cavalese cable car crash

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: 1998 Cavalese cable car crash

At 15:13 on February 3, 1998, the silence of the Dolomites was shattered not by the roar of an avalanche or the crash of a storm, but by the sound of steel shearing steel. An American military aircraft, an EA-6B Prowler flying at nearly 540 miles per hour and far below the legal minimum altitude, sliced through the support cables of a cable car in the Italian ski resort of Cavalese. In the seconds that followed, a cabin carrying twenty people plummeted more than 80 meters down a steep mountainside, leaving no survivors. The crash, known in Italy as the Strage del Cermis or "Cermis Massacre," was a catastrophic failure of military procedure that instantly transformed a winter vacation into a scene of international tragedy and a decades-long legal battle over justice, accountability, and the limits of sovereign immunity.

The victims were not abstract statistics; they were families, couples, and friends enjoying a rare afternoon of leisure. Among the nineteen passengers and one operator who perished were seven Germans, five Belgians, three Italians, two Poles, two Austrians, and one Dutch citizen. They were ordinary people caught in a routine that had been rendered fatal by a decision made thousands of miles away in a briefing room. The cable car, a vital link for skiers navigating the slopes, was severed at a height of 360 feet. The wreckage of the cabin scattered across the snow, a grim testament to the speed and force of the impact. The pilot, Captain Richard J. Ashby, and his navigator, Captain Joseph Schweitzer, managed to fly their damaged aircraft back to Aviano Air Base in northeastern Italy, leaving behind a valley filled with smoke, the wreckage of a lifeless cabin, and a community in shock.

The aircraft involved was a specialized electronic warfare plane, BuNo 163045, callsign Easy 01, belonging to Marine Tactical Electronic Warfare Squadron 2 (VMAQ-2). On that fateful afternoon, it was conducting a low-altitude training mission, a practice common in military aviation to avoid radar detection and simulate combat conditions. However, the parameters of this specific flight were dangerously outside the bounds of safety and regulation. The plane was traveling at 470 knots, or roughly 870 kilometers per hour, and was flying at an altitude between 80 and 100 meters above ground level. In the narrow valley where the crash occurred, this altitude was not just low; it was suicidal for anyone on the ground. Military regulations at the time strictly required a minimum flying height of 2,000 feet (610 meters) in that region, a rule that had been recently reinforced by the Italian government. The aircraft was flying 1,000 feet lower than the absolute minimum legal limit and nearly 1,500 feet lower than the safety zone the pilots claimed they believed they were in.

When the right wing of the Prowler struck the cables, the physics of the event were brutal and immediate. The cable, designed to hold the weight of a suspended cabin, could not withstand the kinetic energy of a half-ton aircraft wing moving at supersonic speeds relative to the ground. The cable snapped, and gravity took over. The cabin, known as the "Cermis" lift, fell freely, impacting the ground with such force that it was destroyed. The sheer suddenness of the event left no time for reaction, for prayer, or for farewell. The international composition of the victims highlighted the global nature of the tragedy; this was not a localized incident but a moment where the boundaries of nations dissolved into a single point of shared grief.

In the immediate aftermath, the response from the United States government was one of formal apology and financial reparation. President Bill Clinton offered an official apology, acknowledging the gravity of the loss, and promised monetary compensation to the victims' families. Thomas M. Foglietta, the U.S. Ambassador to Italy at the time, traveled to the crash site, a somber scene of twisted metal and snow. In a gesture that resonated deeply with the Italian public, he knelt in prayer, offering apologies on behalf of his nation. These gestures, while necessary, could not undo the loss of life, nor could they immediately quell the rising tide of anger and confusion that swept through Italy.

The legal and diplomatic fallout was immediate and complex. Italian prosecutors, driven by the outrage of their citizens and the evident negligence of the flight crew, sought to have the four men on the plane stand trial in Italy. They argued that the crime had occurred on Italian soil, and that Italian courts were the appropriate venue for such a heinous act. However, the situation was complicated by the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) Status of Forces Agreement. This treaty, which governs the legal status of military personnel from one country stationed in another, granted the United States jurisdiction over its military personnel for crimes committed in the line of duty. An Italian court ultimately recognized this legal framework, ruling that the trial must take place within the United States military justice system. This decision, while legally sound under the treaty, was perceived by many Italians as a shield protecting American pilots from the consequences of their actions.

The charges brought against the pilots were severe. Captain Richard J. Ashby and Captain Joseph Schweitzer were charged with twenty counts of involuntary manslaughter and negligent homicide. The other two crew members, Captains Chandler P. Seagraves and William L. Raney, were initially charged but later received testimonial immunity in exchange for their cooperation. The trial of Ashby took place at Marine Corps Base Camp Lejeune in North Carolina, a location far removed from the snow-covered slopes of the Dolomites. During the proceedings, the defense presented a narrative of technical failure and miscommunication. Ashby testified that the maps on board the aircraft did not clearly indicate the location of the cable car lines. He claimed he believed they were flying at 1,000 feet, well above the cables, and that the aircraft's altimeter, a critical instrument for measuring altitude, had been malfunctioning. Furthermore, he asserted that he was unaware of the specific speed restrictions for that area.

The prosecution, on the other hand, painted a picture of gross negligence and willful disregard for safety. Evidence presented showed that the EA-6B was flying significantly faster and considerably lower than allowed by military regulations. The restrictions were clear: a minimum height of 2,000 feet was mandatory. The cable was cut at a height of 360 feet. The discrepancy between the claimed altitude and the actual altitude was stark. The defense's argument about the maps was scrutinized, as the flight had been planned using obsolete documents, ignoring new directives that had been distributed to the squadron. These directives, which forbade flights below 2,000 feet in the Trentino-Alto Adige region, had been received by all pilots in the squadron. A copy of the directive was later found in the cockpit of the EA-6B, unopened, alongside maps that clearly marked the cable car wire route.

In March 1999, the jury at Camp Lejeune delivered a verdict that sent shockwaves through Italy and across the world. Captain Ashby was acquitted of all charges of involuntary manslaughter and negligent homicide. The acquittal was a source of profound outrage for the Italian public, who felt that the military justice system had failed to hold the pilots accountable for the deaths of twenty innocent civilians. The manslaughter charges against Captain Schweitzer were subsequently dropped. The verdict seemed to validate the defense's claims of technical error and lack of intent, but it ignored the broader context of systemic failure and the disregard for safety protocols that had led to the disaster.

However, the story did not end with the acquittal. A new layer of scandal emerged in August 1998, months before the trial concluded, when military investigators discovered the existence of a videotape recorded on the aircraft on the day of the disaster. The tape, which would have provided a crucial record of the flight's altitude, speed, and the crew's actions, had been destroyed. The existence of this tape and its subsequent destruction came to light only after the other two crew members, Seagraves and Raney, chose to disclose "the truth about everything" under their grant of testimonial immunity. They revealed that Ashby and Schweitzer had replaced the original tape with a blank one and burned the original.

This revelation shifted the focus of the military justice system from the crash itself to the cover-up. Ashby and Schweitzer were court-martialed a second time, this time charged with obstruction of justice and conduct unbecoming an officer and a gentleman. The charge of obstruction of justice was based on the deliberate destruction of evidence, an act that undermined the integrity of the investigation and the trust of the public. In May 1999, the military jury found both men guilty. The consequences were severe: both were dismissed from the Marine Corps, effectively ending their military careers. Captain Ashby was sentenced to six months in prison, from which he was released after four and a half months for good behavior. Captain Schweitzer, who had entered into a plea agreement that was not fully disclosed until after the jury deliberated, avoided prison time but received the same dismissal from service.

The aftermath of the trial and the cover-up exposed deep fractures in the relationship between the United States and Italy. The acquittal on the manslaughter charges, followed by the conviction for obstruction of justice, left many Italians feeling that the American military had prioritized the protection of its own over the pursuit of truth and justice. The incident strained diplomatic ties and fueled a national debate in Italy about the presence of foreign military forces and the rules governing their operations. Low-level flight exercises, which had already been a source of contention, were now under intense scrutiny. Politicians in Italy called for a re-evaluation of the rules or a complete ban on such exercises, arguing that the risk to civilian life was too great.

A formal investigation report, led by Lieutenant General Michael DeLong and signed by Lieutenant General Peter Pace on March 10, 1998, provided a comprehensive account of the events leading up to the crash. The report, which was kept secret for over a decade, was eventually obtained by the Italian newspaper La Stampa and published on July 13, 2011. The document, redacted in parts, agreed with the findings of the Italian officers: the U.S. aircrew was flying too low and too fast, putting themselves and others at risk. The report concluded that the U.S. bore full blame for the disaster and that the victims' relatives were entitled to monetary compensation.

The investigation uncovered a chain of command failures that contributed to the tragedy. The squadron had been deployed to Aviano Air Base in August 1997, before the Italian government issued new directives forbidding flights below 2,000 feet. Although all pilots had received a copy of the directive, the squadron commander, Lieutenant Colonel Muegge, and his assistants failed to alert the navigator about the new flight altitude limitations. This failure to communicate critical safety information was a significant factor in the navigator's use of obsolete documents for flight planning. The report noted that the pilots were generally well-behaved and sane, with no history of drug abuse or psychological stress, but on January 24, just days before the crash, they had received a formal warning for flying too low after a training take-off. This warning was apparently ignored or not taken seriously enough to alter their subsequent behavior.

The morning of the disaster also saw maintenance issues with the aircraft. The "G-meter," which measures g-forces, had malfunctioned and was replaced. The radar altimeter was checked, but the underlying issues with the crew's adherence to regulations and the command structure's failure to enforce them remained. The report suggested that disciplinary measures should be taken against the flight crew and the commanding officers, highlighting a systemic culture that prioritized mission completion over safety.

In the years following the crash, the pilots continued to seek redress. Ashby and Schweitzer appealed their convictions, challenging their dismissals in order to be eligible for military benefits. They argued that during the first trial, the prosecution and the defense had secretly agreed to drop the manslaughter charges in exchange for the obstruction of justice charge, a deal designed to satisfy the political demands of Italy. This appeal was denied in November 2007, and decisions from the Court of Appeals for the Armed Forces were made available in August 2009. The legal battles continued for years, a testament to the enduring impact of the tragedy on the lives of those involved.

Decades later, the human cost of the Strage del Cermis remains the most poignant aspect of the story. In an interview for the National Geographic series Seconds from Disaster, Captain Schweitzer admitted to replacing the original tape with a blank one and burning the original. This was the only time he spoke publicly regarding the tape, a confession that came too late to bring back the twenty lives lost. The admission served as a grim reminder of the human capacity for denial and the lengths to which individuals will go to avoid accountability, even when the cost is measured in human lives.

The crash of the Cavalese cable car was not an isolated incident of bad luck or a simple accident. It was the result of a confluence of factors: outdated maps, ignored regulations, a failure of communication within the chain of command, and a willingness to cut corners on safety. The legal proceedings that followed highlighted the complexities of international military law and the difficulties of achieving justice in a system that often prioritizes the protection of its own. The acquittal of the pilots on the manslaughter charges, while legally defensible under the specific circumstances of the trial, failed to address the moral and ethical dimensions of the tragedy. The conviction for obstruction of justice was a small step toward accountability, but it could not undo the damage done to the families of the victims or the trust between two allied nations.

The legacy of the Strage del Cermis is a warning about the dangers of complacency and the importance of rigorous adherence to safety protocols. It serves as a reminder that in the world of military aviation, where the margin for error is often razor-thin, the consequences of negligence can be catastrophic. The twenty people who died in the cable car were not collateral damage in a military exercise; they were the victims of a preventable tragedy. Their deaths continue to resonate, a somber reminder of the human cost of military operations and the enduring need for transparency, accountability, and respect for civilian life.

The story of the Cavalese crash is not just about a plane cutting a cable. It is about the fragility of life in the shadow of military power and the enduring struggle for justice in the face of institutional inertia. The snow in the Dolomites has long since melted and refrozen, but the memory of that day remains etched in the history of both Italy and the United States. It is a story that demands to be told, not just as a record of events, but as a testament to the lives lost and the lessons that must never be forgotten. The tragedy of Cavalese is a stark reminder that when the rules are broken, the cost is often paid in blood, and the search for truth is a journey that can take decades to complete.
