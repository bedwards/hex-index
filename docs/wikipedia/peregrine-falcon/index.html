Peregrine falcon

12 min read

The peregrine falcon (Falco peregrinus) is the fastest member of the animal kingdom, capable of diving at speeds exceeding 320 kilometers per hour (200 mph) to strike prey in mid-air. It is a bird of prey in the family Falconidae, a creature of such terrifying velocity and precision that it has captivated human imagination for millennia. A large, crow-sized falcon, it presents a striking silhouette: a blue-grey back, barred white underparts, and a distinctive black head. As is typical for avivores—raptors that specialize in eating birds—the peregrine displays marked sexual dimorphism. The females are considerably larger than the males, a biological reality that often dictates the dynamics of their survival and hunting strategies.

Historically, the bird has carried different names in different lands, reflecting its ubiquity and the local cultures that observed it. In Australia, it was known as the "black-cheeked falcon," while in North America, it earned the moniker "duck hawk." These names are not merely colloquial; they hint at the bird's range and its diet. The breeding range of the peregrine spans land regions from the Arctic tundra to the tropics. It is found nearly everywhere on Earth, with only a few exceptions: extreme polar regions, very high mountains, and most tropical rainforests remain out of reach. The only major ice-free landmass from which it is entirely absent is the islands of New Zealand.

This distribution makes the peregrine the world's most widespread raptor and one of the most widely found wild bird species. In fact, the only land-based bird species found over a larger geographic area is the domestic and feral pigeon. The success of the pigeon, however, is owed entirely to human-led introduction, a stark contrast to the peregrine's ancient, natural spread. Yet, this human intervention has created a bizarre symbiosis. Feral pigeons are a major prey species for peregrine populations. Due to their greater abundance in cities than most other birds, feral pigeons now support many peregrine populations as a staple food source, especially in urban settings.

The peregrine is a highly successful example of urban wildlife in much of its range. It has adapted with remarkable agility to the concrete canyons of modern civilization, taking advantage of tall buildings as nest sites that mimic the cliff edges of their ancestors. In these vertical cities, an abundance of prey such as pigeons and ducks ensures their survival. Both the English and scientific names of the species mean "wandering falcon," a direct reference to the migratory habits of many northern populations that traverse continents in their seasonal journeys.

The Architecture of a Hunter

The peregrine is a creature of specific proportions and adaptations. It has a body length of 34 to 58 cm (13–23 in) and a wingspan ranging from 74 to 120 cm (29–47 in). The male and female share similar markings and plumage, but the difference in size is significant. The female measures up to 30% larger than the male. Males weigh between 330 to 1,000 g (12–35 oz), while the noticeably larger females weigh 700 to 1,500 g (25–53 oz). In most subspecies, males weigh less than 700 g (25 oz) and females weigh more than 800 g (28 oz). Cases where females weigh about 50% more than their male breeding mates are not uncommon. This size disparity is not merely a curiosity; it is a strategic necessity in a world where hunting success and the ability to defend a nest are paramount.

The standard linear measurements of peregrines reveal a body built for aerodynamics. The wing chord measures 26.5 to 39 cm (10.4–15.4 in), the tail measures 13 to 19 cm (5.1–7.5 in), and the tarsus measures 4.5 to 5.6 cm (1.8–2.2 in). The back and the long pointed wings of the adult are usually bluish black to slate grey with indistinct darker barring. The wingtips are black, slicing through the air. The white to rusty underparts are barred with thin clean bands of dark brown or black, providing camouflage against the sky and ground alike. The tail, colored like the back but with thin clean bars, is long, narrow, and rounded at the end with a black tip and a white band at the very end.

The head is a study in contrast. The top of the head and a "moustache" along the cheeks are black, contrasting sharply with the pale sides of the neck and white throat. This "moustache," or malar stripe, has long been a subject of fascination. For years, ornithologists debated its function. A 2021 study of photos from around the world finally provided clarity. The study showed that the malar stripe is larger in areas that receive more sunlight. The conclusion was definitive: the stripe serves to improve the falcon's vision by reducing glare, acting as a natural pair of sunglasses that allows the bird to track high-speed prey without being blinded by the sun.

The cere, the fleshy part at the base of the beak, is yellow, as are the feet. The beak and claws are black. The upper beak is notched near the tip, an adaptation that enables falcons to kill prey by severing the spinal column at the neck with a single, precise bite. An immature bird presents a different picture entirely, being much browner with streaked, rather than barred, underparts. It also possesses a pale bluish cere and orbital ring, a visual signature that signals youth and inexperience until the adult plumage takes hold.

A History of Names and Classifications

The peregrine falcon was first described under its current binomial name by English ornithologist Marmaduke Tunstall in his 1771 work Ornithologia Britannica. However, the scientific name Falco peregrinus is far older, a Medieval Latin phrase used by Albertus Magnus in 1225. The etymology is revealing. Peregrinus is Latin, meaning "one from abroad" or "coming from foreign parts." It is likely the name was applied because juvenile birds were often taken while journeying to their breeding location, rather than from the nest itself, as falcon nests are notoriously difficult to access.

The Latin term for falcon, falco, is related to falx, meaning "sickle." This is a reference to the silhouette of the falcon's long, pointed wings in flight, which curve like a blade when the bird glides. The peregrine belongs to a genus whose lineage includes the hierofalcons and the prairie falcon (F. mexicanus). Genetic analysis suggests this lineage probably diverged from other falcons towards the end of the Late Miocene or in the Late Pliocene, about 3–8 million years ago. As the peregrine-hierofalcon group includes both Old World and North American species, it is likely that the lineage originated in western Eurasia or Africa.

The taxonomy of the peregrine has been a subject of debate, complicated by the fact that widespread hybridization confounds mtDNA sequence analyses. One genetic lineage of the saker falcon (F. cherrug) is known to have originated from a male saker ancestor producing fertile young with a female peregrine ancestor, with the descendants further breeding with sakers. This fluidity in genetics makes the definition of species boundaries a complex task. Numerous subspecies have been described, with 18 accepted by the IOC World Bird List, and 19 accepted by the 1994 Handbook of the Birds of the World.

A significant point of contention has been the Barbary falcon. Disagreement existed in the past over whether this distinctive bird was represented by two subspecies of Falco peregrinus or was a separate species, F. pelegrinoides. However, the difference in their appearance is very small, as is their genetic difference, being only about 0.6–0.8% genetically differentiated. That indicates the divergence is relatively recent, occurring during the Last Ice Age. Consequently, all major ornithological authorities now treat the Barbary falcon as a subspecies.

Among the subspecies, Falco peregrinus anatum, described by Bonaparte in 1838, holds a special place in history. Known as the American peregrine falcon or "duck hawk," its scientific name means "duck peregrine falcon." It was formerly common throughout North America between the tundra and North Mexico. It is mainly found in the Rocky Mountains, though it was once widespread. Most mature F. p. anatum, except those that breed in more northern areas, winter in their breeding range. Most vagrants that reach western Europe seem to belong to the more northern and strongly migratory F. p. tundrius, which was only considered distinct since 1968. It is similar to the nominate subspecies but is slightly smaller.

The Diet and the Hunt

The diet of the peregrine consists almost exclusively of medium-sized birds. It is an avivore, a specialist in the air. However, the peregrine is not a rigid feeder; it will sometimes hunt small mammals, small reptiles, or even insects when the opportunity arises. The method of hunting is what defines the species. The peregrine is a highly successful example of urban wildlife, taking advantage of tall buildings as nest sites and an abundance of prey such as pigeons and ducks.

In the wild, the peregrine hunts by spotting its prey from a high perch or while in flight. It then dives, tucking its wings to become a bullet of feathers and bone. The speed achieved in this stoop is unmatched in the natural world. The impact is often lethal before the talons even touch the prey. The bird kills by striking the prey with its closed talons, knocking it out of the air, or by severing the spinal column with its beak. This efficiency allows the peregrine to sustain itself on a diet that would be impossible for slower predators.

Reaching sexual maturity at one year, the peregrine mates for life. The bond is strong, and the partnership is essential for the survival of the offspring. They nest in a scrape, normally on cliff edges or, in recent times, on tall human-made structures. The choice of nesting site is critical. The peregrine requires a high vantage point to launch its hunts and to spot predators. In the urban environment, skyscrapers and bridges have replaced the cliffs of the past. This adaptation has been crucial to the bird's recovery in recent decades.

The Scourge of DDT and the Road to Recovery

The peregrine falcon's history in the 20th century is a cautionary tale of human impact on the natural world. The bird became an endangered species in many areas because of the widespread use of various pesticides, especially DDT. The story of DDT is one of unintended consequences. Introduced in the 1940s as a powerful insecticide, DDT worked its way up the food chain. As predators like the peregrine consumed contaminated prey, the chemical accumulated in their bodies.

The effect was devastating. DDT caused the eggshells of peregrines to become so thin that they would break under the weight of the incubating parents. This led to a catastrophic collapse in breeding success. Populations plummeted, and the bird vanished from large parts of its range. In North America, the American peregrine falcon (F. p. anatum) was listed as endangered. The silence in the skies where once the scream of the falcon could be heard was a stark reminder of the fragility of ecosystems.

The turning point came in the early 1970s. Following a ban on DDT in the United States and other countries, the path to recovery began. The ban was a hard-won victory for environmentalists who had documented the decline of the bird and linked it directly to the pesticide. Since the ban, populations have recovered, supported by large-scale protection of nesting places and releases to the wild.

Recovery efforts were not just about banning a chemical; they involved active human intervention. Conservationists worked tirelessly to protect nesting sites from disturbance and to establish captive breeding programs. Birds were bred in captivity and released into the wild, their numbers bolstered by the absence of DDT in the environment. The peregrine falcon's comeback is one of the great conservation success stories of the modern era. It stands as a testament to the resilience of nature when given the chance to heal.

Cultural Significance and the Future

The peregrine falcon is a well-respected falconry bird due to its strong hunting ability, high trainability, versatility, and availability via captive breeding. It is effective on most game bird species, from small to large. This utility has ensured its survival in the eyes of humans for centuries. Falconry, the art of hunting with birds, has a long history, and the peregrine is often considered the pinnacle of the practice. Its speed and power make it a formidable hunter, and its intelligence makes it a responsive partner.

Beyond falconry, the peregrine has been used as a religious, royal, or national symbol across many eras and civilizations. Its image appears on coats of arms, in religious iconography, and as a national emblem. The bird's association with speed, freedom, and the sky has made it a potent symbol of power and transcendence.

As we look to the future, the peregrine faces new challenges. Climate change, urbanization, and the loss of habitat in some regions threaten its stability. Yet, the bird's adaptability is its greatest asset. It has already proven its ability to thrive in the most unlikely of environments, turning skyscrapers into cliffs and city pigeons into a reliable food source. The story of the peregrine falcon is not just a story of a bird; it is a story of human impact, human error, and human redemption.

The peregrine reminds us that the natural world is interconnected. The chemicals we spray on our crops can travel up the food chain to the highest predator. The buildings we construct can become the new homes of ancient species. And the laws we pass can bring a species back from the brink of extinction. The peregrine falcon, with its malar stripe shielding its eyes from the glare of the sun and its wings cutting through the air, is a symbol of this complex relationship. It is a wanderer, a hunter, and a survivor, flying over our cities and our wilds, a reminder of what we have lost and what we can regain.
