Great Famine of 1315–1317

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Great Famine of 1315–1317

In August 1315, King Edward II of England stopped at the Abbey of St Albans, a place of immense wealth and spiritual refuge, and found himself unable to secure a loaf of bread. This was not a momentary lapse in supply chain logistics; it was a stark revelation that the very foundation of European society had begun to crumble. For a king, whose entourage was accustomed to the finest provisions, to face such a scarcity was a rare and terrifying anomaly. Yet, this single incident at St Albans was merely the opening chapter of a catastrophic saga that would strip the continent of its food, its livestock, and eventually, its humanity. The Great Famine of 1315–1317 was the first of a series of large-scale crises that would shatter the medieval world, marking the violent end to centuries of growth and prosperity.

To understand the magnitude of this disaster, one must first appreciate the context of the preceding era. From the 11th to the 13th centuries, Europe was in the throes of the Medieval Warm Period. During these centuries, the climate was remarkably favorable, allowing agriculture to flourish in ways previously unseen. The population exploded, reaching levels that would not be matched in some regions until the 19th century. In parts of rural France, the density of inhabitants in the early 14th century remains unmatched to this day. The yield ratios of wheat—the critical metric of agricultural success—had been climbing. Under ideal conditions, a farmer could plant one seed and harvest seven. A 7:1 ratio meant that after setting aside enough grain to plant the next year's crop, there was a massive surplus to feed families and trade.

But beneath this veneer of abundance, the seeds of catastrophe were being sown. By 1280, the yield ratios had begun a slow, steady decline. Favourable harvests might still produce a 7:1 return, but unfavourable ones dropped to a precarious 2:1. In that worst-case scenario, for every seed planted, only two were harvested: one to consume, and one to replant. There was no margin for error. Modern farming, with its industrial machinery and chemical inputs, operates on ratios of 30:1 or higher. Medieval agriculture, dependent on the whims of nature and oxen-drawn plows, existed on a razor's edge. The population had grown to the absolute limit of what the land could sustain, leaving no buffer against failure.

The failure arrived not with a whisper, but with a deluge. The Great Famine was precipitated by a dramatic shift in climate patterns, occurring as the Medieval Warm Period gave way to the Little Ice Age. Some historians suggest a volcanic event may have triggered this sudden change, plunging Northern Europe into a state of meteorological chaos. Between 1310 and 1330, the continent endured some of the worst and most sustained periods of bad weather in the Middle Ages. The winters were severe, but it was the summers that proved fatal.

In the spring of 1315, unusually heavy rain began to fall across much of Europe. It did not stop. Throughout the spring and into the summer, the rain continued relentlessly, while temperatures remained cool. The soil became so saturated that it was impossible to plow the fields for planting. Grain could not ripen in the cold, wet gloom. The straw and hay required for animal fodder could not be cured, leaving it to rot in the fields. Farmers resorted to bringing grain indoors, storing it in urns and pots in a desperate attempt to keep it dry, but the damage was already done. In England, the lowlands of Yorkshire and Nottingham were flooded, and the stew ponds on the River Foss were washed away, destroying the local infrastructure.

The economic consequences were immediate and devastating. As the harvest failed, food prices began to skyrocket. In England, prices doubled between spring and midsummer. Salt, the only effective method for curing and preserving meat, became a luxury item because the brine could not be evaporated in the wet weather. Its price surged from 30 to 40 shillings. In Lorraine, the price of wheat rose by 320%, rendering bread unaffordable for the peasantry. The stores of grain, which had been built up over centuries of good harvests, were limited to the reserves of royalty, lords, nobles, wealthy merchants, and the Church. For the 95% of the population who were peasants, there were no reserves. When the harvest failed, hunger arrived instantly.

As the summer of 1315 dragged on without relief, the desperation deepened. People began to strip the forests of every available resource, harvesting wild edible roots, grasses, nuts, and even stripping bark from trees to mix with flour. The social fabric began to fray. Chroniclers of the time recorded incidents that would haunt history. In Bristol, the city chronicles reported a "great Famine of Dearth with such mortality that the living could scarce suffice to bury the dead." The horror escalated to the point where horse flesh and dog flesh were considered good meat. More chillingly, the chronicles stated that "some eat their own children." The breakdown of law and order was total; thieves in prison were reported to have "plucked and tore in pieces" newly arrived prisoners, devouring them half alive.

The crisis was not confined to the local. In France, King Louis X attempted to invade Flanders, a campaign that was doomed by the weather before a single sword was drawn. In the low-lying areas of the Netherlands, the fields were so soaked that the army became bogged down in the mud. They were forced to retreat, burning their provisions where they abandoned them because they could not carry the wet, heavy loads away. A military campaign, usually a symbol of state power, was rendered impotent by the rain.

Spring 1316 brought no reprieve. The rain continued to fall on a population already deprived of energy and reserves. The effects rippled through every stratum of society, from the nobility to the peasantry, though the latter bore the brunt of the suffering. With no food left in the fields, people were forced to make impossible choices. To survive the immediate moment, farmers slaughtered their draft animals, the very engines of future agriculture. They ate the seed grain that was meant for the next planting, mortgaging the future for the present. Families abandoned children to fend for themselves in the woods, a grim reality that likely inspired the folktale of "Hansel and Gretel." Among the elderly, voluntary starvation became a noble sacrifice, allowing the younger generation the strength to continue working the fields.

The psychological and physical toll was compounded by disease. The weakened bodies of the population became susceptible to a host of illnesses. Pneumonia, bronchitis, and tuberculosis swept through the starving masses. The "Great Bovine Pestilence" also struck, decimating the livestock population. Weakened cattle were susceptible to disease, and the resulting plague caused sheep and cattle numbers to fall by as much as 80 per cent. This was a catastrophic blow, as livestock provided not only meat and dairy but also the essential labor for plowing and the manure for fertilizing fields. The loss of the herds meant that even when the weather eventually improved, the agricultural system was crippled.

The height of the famine was reached in 1317. The wet weather persisted, and the human body, pushed beyond its limits, began to fail in droves. It was in this year that the most horrific rumors of cannibalism were most prevalent. Chroniclers noted that people would open the graves of the newly dead, claiming the bodies as food. As the historian Lynn H. Nelson cautiously noted, "one can never tell if such talk was not simply a matter of rumor-mongering," but the sheer volume of such reports suggests a societal collapse where the boundaries of human decency had been obliterated by starvation.

By the summer of 1317, the weather finally returned to normal patterns. The rain stopped, the sun returned, and the crops began to ripen. But the recovery was agonizingly slow. The population was so weakened by disease, and so much of the seed stock had been consumed, that it was not until 1322 that the food supply returned to relatively normal levels. Some estimates suggest it took until 1325 for the population to begin increasing again. The Great Famine had lingered for years, a slow, grinding horror that contrasted sharply with the swift devastation that would come later in the century.

The human cost of this catastrophe was staggering. Historians debate the exact figures, but it is estimated that 10–25% of the population of many cities and towns died. In the south of England, the loss was between 10–15%. Northern France lost about 10% of its population. The famine was restricted to Northern Europe, including the British Isles, Northern France, the Low Countries, Scandinavia, Germany, and western Poland. It also affected some of the Baltic states, though the far eastern Baltic was only indirectly impacted. The southern boundary of the famine was drawn by the Alps and the Pyrenees, where the climate remained different enough to spare those regions from the worst of the deluge.

The 1314–1316 period was the fifth-wettest three-year interval in Europe from 1300 to 2012. The four wetter periods all occurred after 1710, a time when agricultural techniques had evolved to better withstand such anomalies. In 1315, the technology and social structures of the Middle Ages were ill-equipped to handle such a meteorological assault. The ineffectiveness of medieval governments in dealing with the crisis was profound. There were no central food reserves, no coordinated relief efforts, and no understanding of the scale of the disaster until it was too late.

The Great Famine was a watershed moment in European history. It marked a clear end to the period of growth and prosperity that had defined the 11th, 12th, and 13th centuries. It was the first of a series of large-scale crises that would strike parts of Europe in the 14th century. Famines were familiar occurrences in medieval Europe, but none matched the scale and duration of the Great Famine. In the Kingdom of France, for instance, localized famines occurred in 1304, 1305, 1310, 1330–1334, 1349–1351, 1358–1360, 1371, 1374–1375, and 1390. In the Kingdom of England, the most prosperous kingdom affected, there were additional famines in 1321, 1351, and 1369.

The demographic impact was captured starkly in the life expectancy statistics of the English royal family, a group that usually enjoyed the best of everything. In 1276, the average life expectancy at birth was 35.28 years. Between 1301 and 1325, during the Great Famine, it plummeted to 29.84 years. The situation would worsen with the arrival of the Black Death. Between 1348 and 1375, during the plague, life expectancy dropped to a mere 17.33 years. While these figures are skewed by naturally high child mortality rates even in non-famine years, they demonstrate the steep population drop that occurred. The period between 1348 and 1375 saw a population decline of about 42%.

The historian Jean-Pierre Leguay noted that the Great Famine "produced wholesale slaughter in a world that was already overcrowded, especially in the towns, which were natural outlets for rural overpopulation." Unlike the Black Death of 1347–1351, which often swept through an area in a matter of months, the Great Famine lingered for years, prolonging the suffering of the populace. The Black Death was a sudden, violent blow; the Great Famine was a slow, suffocating embrace.

The consequences of the famine extended far beyond the immediate loss of life. It had profound effects on the Church, the state, and European society. The inability of religious and secular authorities to provide relief eroded trust in traditional institutions. The social hierarchy was strained as the nobility, too, faced hunger, blurring the lines between the haves and the have-nots. The crisis set the stage for the future calamities of the 14th century, leaving a population that was weaker, more vulnerable, and more desperate.

The memory of the Great Famine faded, but its scars remained. It was a reminder of the fragility of human civilization in the face of nature's indifference. The Medieval Warm Period had allowed Europe to flourish, but it had also led to a population density that the land could no longer support once the climate shifted. The transition to the Little Ice Age was not just a change in temperature; it was a fundamental disruption of the agricultural cycle that sustained millions.

The story of the Great Famine is one of resilience and horror in equal measure. It is a story of kings unable to eat, of parents abandoning children, of neighbors eating neighbors, and of a society that nearly collapsed under the weight of the rain. It serves as a grim prelude to the Black Death, a warning of what happens when the margin for error is eliminated. The 14th century would be defined by these crises, a century of death and rebirth that would reshape the map of Europe.

But the legacy of the Great Famine is not just in the deaths it caused. It is in the way it altered the trajectory of history. The population drop, while tragic, eventually led to changes in land use, labor relations, and social structures. The scarcity of labor that followed the famines and the plague would eventually empower the peasantry, contributing to the end of serfdom in many parts of Europe. The Great Famine was a catalyst for change, forcing a reevaluation of how society organized itself around the land and its resources.

In the end, the Great Famine of 1315–1317 was a perfect storm of climate, population pressure, and agricultural limitation. It was a time when the rain did not stop, the crops did not grow, and the people did not eat. It was a time when the world turned upside down, and the laws of nature seemed to conspire against humanity. The memory of that time, captured in the chronicles of the era, stands as a testament to the resilience of the human spirit in the face of overwhelming odds. And yet, it also serves as a stark reminder of the vulnerability of our civilization. When the weather turns, and the harvest fails, the thin line between order and chaos is easily crossed.

The Great Famine was a turning point. It ended the medieval golden age and ushered in an era of uncertainty and decline. It was a time of extreme levels of crime, disease, mass death, and even cannibalism and infanticide. It was a time when the future was mortgaged, when the seed was eaten, and when the draft animals were slaughtered. It was a time when the world seemed to be ending, and perhaps, in many ways, it was. But from the ashes of that famine, a new world would eventually rise, shaped by the lessons of survival and the memory of the rain that never stopped.

The story of the Great Famine is not just a historical footnote; it is a profound narrative of human endurance. It reminds us that our history is written not just in the victories of kings and the triumphs of armies, but in the quiet, desperate struggles of ordinary people trying to survive against the elements. It is a story that resonates even today, as we face our own climate challenges and the fragility of our food systems. The lessons of 1315 are as relevant now as they were then: that no society is immune to the forces of nature, and that the margin for error is always thinner than we think.

As we look back at the Great Famine, we see a world that was on the brink. We see a society that was pushed to its limits and forced to confront its own mortality. We see the darkness of human nature, but also the light of survival. The Great Famine of 1315–1317 was a catastrophe, but it was also a moment of transformation. It changed the course of history, and its echoes can still be heard in the world we live in today.

The Great Famine was the first of the great crises of the 14th century, a decade of death that would come to define the era. It was a time when the rain fell for years, when the crops failed, and when the people starved. It was a time of despair, but also of resilience. It was a time when the world changed, and the old order crumbled. And in the end, it was a time that would shape the future of Europe, for better or for worse.

The legacy of the Great Famine is complex. It was a tragedy of immense proportions, but it was also a catalyst for change. It forced Europe to confront its limitations and to adapt to a new reality. It was a time of suffering, but it was also a time of growth and transformation. The Great Famine of 1315–1317 was a dark chapter in history, but it was also a necessary one, one that would lead to a new understanding of the world and our place in it.
