2009 NBA draft

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: 2009 NBA draft

On June 25, 2009, the air inside The Theater at Madison Square Garden in New York City hummed with a specific kind of electric anticipation that only a new era can generate. It was not merely a ceremony to distribute contracts; it was a ritualistic passing of the torch, a moment where the future of professional basketball was codified into a list of sixty names. The National Basketball Association (NBA) gathered its thirty franchises to take turns selecting eligible players from the amateur ranks of U.S. colleges and international leagues. This was the 2009 NBA draft, a pivotal gathering that would eventually redefine the geometry of the game itself. While the night belonged to the momentary thrill of the selection, the true narrative arc stretched far beyond the final buzzer of that evening, revealing how a single group of young men would go on to shatter scoring records, win championships, and alter the very philosophy of offensive basketball for generations to come.

The stage was set by the whims of probability and misfortune earlier in the spring. On May 19, 2009, the draft lottery, a mechanism designed to give struggling teams a chance at elite talent, concluded with a decisive result: the Los Angeles Clippers had won the first overall pick. It was a rare stroke of luck for a franchise that had long been synonymous with mediocrity. The front office did not hesitate. They reached out and selected Blake Griffin from the University of Oklahoma. Griffin was a physical specimen, a power forward whose explosive dunking ability had captivated college audiences across America. He represented the ideal modern athlete: strong enough to battle in the paint yet agile enough to soar above it.

The draft is often described as a lottery, but for teams like the Clippers, it felt more like a rescue mission.

However, the trajectory of Griffin's career, and indeed the immediate impact of the 2009 class, was thrown into chaos before a single regular-season game could be played. During the pre-season, Griffin suffered a devastating injury to his left kneecap. The surgery required was extensive, and the recovery time was total. He would miss the entire 2009–10 season. While the world waited for Griffin's eventual emergence, the draft class continued to produce its own immediate stars, proving that the night in New York was not dependent on a single savior.

The second pick belonged to the Memphis Grizzlies, who selected Hasheem Thabeet from the University of Connecticut. Standing at an imposing height, Thabeet carried the weight of history on his shoulders as he stepped onto the stage. He became the first player born in Tanzania ever to be drafted by an NBA team. His selection was a testament to the increasingly global nature of the league, a signal that the scouting net had widened far beyond the traditional American high school and college circuit. Yet, Thabeet's journey would also highlight the gap between potential and execution, as he struggled to translate his collegiate dominance into professional consistency.

Three spots later, the Oklahoma City Thunder made a selection that would eventually be viewed as one of the most transformative in franchise history. They chose James Harden, the 3rd overall pick. This moment held a peculiar significance for the organization itself; it was the first time the franchise had drafted anyone under the banner of "Oklahoma City," having relocated from Seattle just a year prior in 2008. The Thunder were a newborn entity looking to build an identity, and they found their cornerstone in Harden, a player known for his unique scoring ability and the creation of difficult shots that defied conventional basketball logic.

The Sacramento Kings followed with the fourth pick, selecting Tyreke Evans. If there was any doubt about the offensive firepower of this draft class early on, Evans silenced it immediately. In his rookie season, he became only the fourth player in NBA history to average at least 20 points, 5 rebounds, and 5 assists per game. He joined an elite and almost mythical club that included Oscar Robertson (1960), Michael Jordan (1984), and LeBron James (2003). His performance was not just a statistical anomaly; it was a declaration of arrival that earned him the 2009–10 NBA Rookie of the Year award. Evans proved that this draft was not just about potential; it was about immediate, high-level production.

The International Wave and the Trailblazers

While American college stars dominated the early headlines, the 2009 draft served as a critical inflection point for international talent. Ricky Rubio, a Spanish teenager with a vision that seemed to operate on a different frequency than his peers, was drafted 5th overall by the Minnesota Timberwolves. Rubio's selection was historic in its own right; he became the fifth-highest-drafted international player who had never played U.S. college basketball. He entered this exclusive company alongside legends like Yao Ming (1st in 2002), Andrea Bargnani (1st in 2006), Darko Miličić (2nd in 2003), and Pau Gasol (3rd in 2001). Rubio's path was not linear; the logistics of importing a teenager from Europe required patience and cultural adaptation, but his presence signaled that the NBA was no longer just an American export but a global destination.

The narrative of international breakthrough continued further down the board. The 23rd pick, Omri Casspi, made history as the first Israeli player to be selected in the first round of an NBA draft. His selection paved the way for his future status as the first Israeli to actually play in the league. These picks were not mere tokens; they represented a fundamental shift in how teams scouted talent. The assumption that one must play NCAA basketball to reach the NBA was crumbling, replaced by a recognition of skill developed in diverse environments around the world.

Sons of Giants and New Paths

The 2009 draft also offered a unique glimpse into the genetic and cultural legacy of the sport. For the first time ever, three sons of former NBA players were selected within the top 15 picks. This clustering was more than a statistical curiosity; it highlighted the deepening roots of basketball families. Stephen Curry, son of Dell Curry, was drafted 7th by the Golden State Warriors. His selection would eventually be viewed as the catalyst for one of the greatest dynasties in sports history. Gerald Henderson Jr., son of Gerald Henderson, followed at 12th to the Charlotte Bobcats, and Austin Daye, son of Darren Daye, went 15th to the Detroit Pistons. These young men carried names that resonated with basketball fans, yet they were forced to forge their own identities in a league that had already been shaped by their fathers' generation.

Perhaps the most radical departure from tradition was the selection of Brandon Jennings at 10th overall by the Milwaukee Bucks. Jennings had made a bold choice prior to the draft: he skipped college entirely to play professional basketball with Lottomatica Roma in Italy. Before him, no former high school player who had bypassed U.S. college basketball to play professionally overseas had ever been selected in an NBA draft. His path was fraught with skepticism, as many analysts questioned whether a teenager playing against grown men in Europe could translate that experience to the NBA. The Bucks' decision to take him was a gamble on a new model of player development, one that valued international pro experience over the collegiate system.

The demographics of the 60 players selected told their own story of this transition. Of the total class, only four were freshmen, while nine were sophomores and twelve were juniors. The majority, twenty-two players, were seniors who had exhausted their college eligibility. Yet, thirteen of the draftees were international players with no U.S. college experience. This balance represented a league in flux, trying to bridge the gap between the traditional American development pipeline and the global talent pool.

The Mechanics of Acquisition

To understand the depth of the 2009 draft, one must look beyond the podium and into the complex machinery of trades that preceded it. The draft is rarely just about who picks whom; it is about how teams position themselves to make those picks. The Minnesota Timberwolves emerged as the most active team in this regard, holding a league-high four first-round draft picks. It was the first time in franchise history they held two top-10 selections. Their aggression extended to the second round as well, giving them a total of six picks—the most of any team that year.

The Timberwolves' stockpile of assets was the result of a series of calculated moves. On June 24, 2009, just one day before the draft, they acquired the 5th pick from Washington in exchange for Randy Foye and Mike Miller. That 5th pick became Ricky Rubio, the centerpiece of their international vision. Their other first-round picks were equally hard-won. One was re-acquired on July 31, 2007, as part of a massive trade with Boston that brought Kevin Garnett to Minnesota; this pick eventually fell to them and was used on Wayne Ellington at number 28. Another came from Miami on October 24, 2007, in a deal for Ricky Davis, which allowed them to select Ty Lawson at number 18.

Other teams were equally active in manipulating the board. The Portland Trail Blazers engaged in a swap with Dallas on June 24, 2009, acquiring the 22nd pick from Dallas in exchange for the 24th and 56th picks plus a future second-round selection. Portland used their new asset to draft Víctor Claver, while Dallas selected Byron Mullens and Ahmad Nivins with their acquired picks. The Sacramento Kings had secured their 23rd pick (Omri Casspi) through a trade with Houston on August 14, 2008, which involved sending Ron Artest away.

The Oklahoma City Thunder's acquisition of the 25th pick was a direct result of a February 19, 2009 trade between Chicago and Denver involving Thabo Sefolosha, which had its roots in earlier deals with Oklahoma City and Denver. These trades were not random; they were strategic maneuvers by general managers trying to maximize value in a draft that promised high-impact talent. It is worth noting that the Houston Rockets and Orlando Magic were the only two teams without a first-round pick of their own, a testament to the aggressive trading that had stripped them of their immediate future assets for present-day gains.

The Long Arc: From Draft Night to History

In the moment of the draft, predictions are often speculative at best. The true measure of a class like 2009 only reveals itself over the long arc of careers. As we look back from 2026, the magnitude of this specific group becomes undeniable. Of the sixty players selected that night, six have been selected as NBA All-Stars. Among them, two—Stephen Curry and James Harden—have won the league's most prestigious individual honor: the MVP award for the regular season.

The impact extends far beyond individual accolades to the very trophies that define success in the NBA. Nine players from this draft have stood on a team that won an NBA championship. The list reads like a roll call of modern basketball greats: Danny Green, Roddy Beaubois, Stephen Curry, Austin Daye, Jrue Holiday, Jodie Meeks, Patty Mills, Jeff Ayres, and Jeff Teague. The depth of their success is staggering. Danny Green has won three championships; Stephen Curry has four; Jrue Holiday has two. These are not just participants in winning teams; they have been pivotal figures in the era's greatest dynasties. Stephen Curry's legacy was further cemented when he was named MVP of the 2022 NBA Finals, a crowning achievement for a player who entered the league as the son of a journeyman shooter and left it as one of the most influential athletes of his generation.

The longevity of this class is equally remarkable. As of June 2026, seven players from the 2009 draft remain active in the NBA: James Harden, Stephen Curry, DeMar DeRozan, Jrue Holiday, Garrett Temple, Joe Ingles, and Taj Gibson. It is a rare feat for any draft class to have multiple players still competing at an elite level nearly two decades later. The endurance of these careers speaks to their work ethic, adaptability, and the foundational skills they possessed on that night in 2009. Even those who were not selected initially, like Temple and Ingles, found ways to carve out significant roles, though their presence in the league today is a testament to their perseverance rather than the draft board itself.

The University of North Carolina's Dominance

While the international players and the sons of stars drew headlines, the academic powerhouse of the University of North Carolina also had its moment in the sun. The Tar Heels dominated the 2009 class with more selections than any other university. Three players were chosen in the first round, and one in the second round. This marked only the second time in history that four Tar Heels were selected within the first two rounds of a single draft. It was a validation of North Carolina's coaching system and development pipeline, proving once again that their program remained the gold standard for preparing young men for the professional level.

The 2009 NBA Draft was a confluence of history, strategy, and raw talent. It was a night where the Clippers bet on a broken knee that would heal into a legend; where the Timberwolves gambled everything on teenagers from Spain and Italy; where the Thunder found their identity in a scorer who defied convention; and where the Warriors selected a shooter who would change how the game is played forever. It was a draft defined by its diversity—geographic, stylistic, and generational.

From the moment the lottery balls bounced to the final second-round pick, every selection carried weight. The trades that reshaped the board, the historic firsts for international players, and the legacy of NBA sons all wove together into a tapestry that is still being written today. When we analyze the landscape of the modern NBA, with its emphasis on spacing, shooting, and global talent, we are looking at the direct descendants of that summer in 2009. The draft was not just a list of names; it was the blueprint for the next decade of basketball evolution.

"The draft is where potential meets probability."

In 2009, the probability favored the extraordinary. The players selected that night did not just fill rosters; they filled eras. They won MVPs, championship rings, and All-Star selections in numbers that few drafts have ever matched. As the league continues to evolve, the fingerprints of Blake Griffin's power, James Harden's step-back, Stephen Curry's range, and Ricky Rubio's vision remain on every game played. The 2009 draft was a singular event, but its echoes are still reverberating through the halls of Madison Square Garden and arenas across the globe, reminding us that sometimes, the most important moments in sports happen not when the ball goes in the hoop, but when a name is called on a stage in New York City.

The narrative of this draft serves as a reminder that talent is often scattered, waiting to be discovered by those willing to take risks. Whether it was the risk to trade away future assets for immediate picks, or the risk to draft an international teenager who had never stepped foot on American soil, the teams that embraced uncertainty were the ones that reaped the rewards. The 2009 class stands as a testament to the idea that in basketball, as in life, the path to greatness is rarely straight, but it is always worth the journey.

As we reflect on this history, it becomes clear that the 2009 NBA draft was more than just a transactional event for the league. It was a cultural reset. It introduced us to players who would become household names, icons of a generation, and the architects of modern basketball strategy. The stories of these sixty young men are intertwined with the history of the sport itself, a reminder that every great era begins with a single draft night, where the future is written in ink and hope.
