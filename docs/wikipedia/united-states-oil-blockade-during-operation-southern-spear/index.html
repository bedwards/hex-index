United States oil blockade during Operation Southern Spear

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: United States oil blockade during Operation Southern Spear

On December 10, 2025, a Very Large Crude Carrier (VLCC) named the Skipper drifted in the international waters between Grenada and Trinidad, its engines humming a deceptive rhythm. To the casual observer, or perhaps the automated sensors of a distant satellite, the ship appeared to be en route to Asia, its transponder broadcasting a false location from October through December to mask its true trajectory. But the United States Navy had been watching. The vessel, carrying 1.8 million barrels of crude oil extracted from the state-owned Petroleos de Venezuela (PDVSA), had just offloaded a portion of its cargo to a companion ship, the Neptune 6, near Curaçao in a classic shadow fleet maneuver. Moments later, the silence of the Caribbean was shattered not by the roar of combat, but by the sudden, calculated deployment of force. Ten personnel from the United States Coast Guard's Maritime Security and Response Team, joined by ten special operations Marines and helicopter-borne tactical units from the USS Gerald R. Ford, descended upon the Skipper. They boarded the vessel with a seizure warrant that had been ticking down its final hours. There was no resistance. There were no casualties. But the implications of that quiet boarding in the Caribbean Sea would ripple across the global economy, destabilizing the already fragile Venezuelan state and marking the most aggressive naval deployment in the region since the Cuban Missile Crisis of 1962.

This event was not an isolated incident of law enforcement; it was the opening salvo of Operation Southern Spear, a concerted US strategy to enforce a comprehensive blockade on sanctioned oil tankers traveling to and from Venezuela. The operation represented a fundamental shift in American foreign policy, moving beyond the diplomatic sanctions of the past into a kinetic, maritime enforcement regime that tested the very limits of international law. To understand the magnitude of this moment, one must look at the machinery of the Venezuelan economy, which has been hemorrhaging vitality for decades. Since the early 2000s, oil production in Venezuela has plummeted, a victim of a toxic triad: endemic corruption, the mismanagement of PDVSA, and the relentless pressure of United States sanctions. Yet, despite this collapse, the Maduro government's survival remains inextricably linked to the export of crude. It is the lifeblood that funds the regime, the currency with which it pays its debts, and the leverage it hopes to use against its enemies.

The United States, under the administration of President Donald Trump, decided that the era of waiting was over. The catalyst for the escalation was a series of actions that began well before the Skipper was seized. On August 7, 2025, the Department of Justice dramatically raised the reward for the arrest of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro to $50 million. Maduro, already indicted in a US federal court in 2020 on charges of narcoterrorism and conspiracy to import cocaine, was now the primary target of a global manhunt. The Trump administration's rhetoric hardened, accusing the Maduro government of flooding the United States with drugs and issuing an ultimatum: step down or face the full weight of American power. This was not merely posturing. By March 2025, President Trump had signed Executive Order 14245, a legislative hammer that imposed a punitive 25% tariff on any nation that imported Venezuelan oil. The order declared Venezuela "very hostile," effectively attempting to strangle the country's oil market by making its product toxic to global buyers.

The Venezuelan response was swift and militarized. In August 2025, Defense Minister Vladimir Padrino López announced that the Bolivarian Navy would be deployed to protect the nation's main oil hubs. By October, President Maduro went further, claiming that the United States intended to seize Venezuela's oil reserves and ordering the mobilization of the Bolivarian Army to repel a potential land invasion. The stage was set for a confrontation that would play out not on the ground, but on the high seas. The United States Navy responded by launching its largest naval deployment in the Caribbean since 1962. This was no longer a presence; it was a blockade. By September 2025, the US had begun launching air strikes against alleged drug trafficking vessels in the Caribbean. The toll was immediate and severe. By December 10, the United States had conducted 22 air strikes, killing at least 87 people. The waters were no longer neutral ground; they had become a zone of active enforcement.

The seizure of the Skipper was the culmination of months of intelligence gathering and legal maneuvering. The vessel, originally named Toyo when it was built by Imabari Shipbuilding in Japan in 2005, had undergone a metamorphosis into a ghost of the global oil trade. Owned by Triton Navigation Corp. of the Marshall Islands and operated by Nigeria-based Thomarose Global Ventures, the Skipper was a 310,309 DWT supertanker, too large for many ports and perfectly suited for the shadowy world of illicit trade. In November 2022, the US Department of the Treasury had already sanctioned the vessel, then known as Adisa, for its alleged involvement in an oil smuggling network that financed both the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) and Hezbollah. This network was allegedly coordinated by Viktor Sergiyovitch Artemov, a Russian oil magnate and Ukrainian citizen living in Switzerland, who was himself sanctioned by the Office of Foreign Assets Control.

The Skipper was part of what experts call the "global dark fleet," a loose coalition of vessels that falsify their location, change flags at sea, and engage in ship-to-ship transfers to evade detection. A New York Times analysis of satellite imagery confirmed that the Skipper had issued false transponder readings for months, a digital ghost story that ended on December 10. Moments before the US boarding party arrived, the tanker was flying the flag of Guyana, despite not being registered there—a classic tactic of the trade. The ship had docked in Iran in July and August 2025, and in Hong Kong, before making its fateful run to Venezuela. When the US forces boarded, the ship was carrying a mix of Venezuelan and Iranian crude, a physical manifestation of the sanctions-busting network that the US had vowed to dismantle.

The legal justification for the seizure was rooted in a warrant issued by a federal judge two weeks earlier, which expired on the very day of the boarding. US Attorney General Pam Bondi, in a video posted to X, declared the operation "conducted safely and securely" and framed it as a necessary step to prevent the transport of sanctioned oil. However, the narrative was complex. Anonymous US officials told The New York Times that the warrant was issued specifically because of the ship's history of smuggling Iranian oil, not necessarily because of a direct link to the Maduro government at that exact moment. Yet, Bondi clarified that the vessel was indeed transporting both Venezuelan and Iranian crude, blurring the lines between the two illicit economies. The message was clear: the US was no longer distinguishing between the specific source of the oil, but was targeting the mechanism of the shadow fleet itself.

The Venezuelan government's reaction was one of outrage. Caracas condemned the seizure of the Skipper, labeling it an "act of international piracy." The language was deliberate, invoking the historical imagery of state-sponsored theft on the high seas. The United Nations Security Council urged restraint, and several representatives condemned the naval blockade, arguing that the US actions were testing the boundaries of maritime law. Analysts warned that the precedent being set could have far-reaching consequences for the freedom of navigation, a cornerstone of the global trading system. If a superpower could board and seize commercial vessels in international waters based on suspicion of sanctions violations, what protections remained for merchant ships of any kind?

The operation did not stop with the Skipper. The US had planned additional seizures, and the follow-up was rapid and relentless. On December 20, just ten days after the Skipper was taken, the US Coast Guard boarded a second vessel, the Panama-flagged Centuries. Carrying 1.8 million barrels of crude oil off the coast of Venezuela, the Centuries was reported as "intercepted" by Reuters. Unlike the Skipper, the Centuries and its owner were not sanctioned entities. The boarding was based on a "right to visit" law, which allows a warship to board and inspect a vessel suspected of engaging in illicit activity. The trigger was a history of the vessel transporting oil to China and a pattern of turning off its transponder while sailing with crude, a violation of Panamanian maritime law. Panama, the vessel's flag state, later confirmed the violation. The Centuries was eventually moored at the Galveston Offshore Lightering Area in February 2026, its fate uncertain.

The pressure continued to mount. The US had been pursuing a third VLCC, the Bella 1 (later renamed Marinera), since December 20, 2025. This tanker, which had previously transported Iranian oil, was en route to load Venezuelan oil purchased by a company associated with Pedro Carretero, a Panamanian who had recently been sanctioned. The hunt for these vessels became a global game of cat and mouse. On January 7, 2026, the US successfully boarded and seized the Russian oil tanker Marinera in the Atlantic near Iceland, as well as the Panama-flagged M Sophia in the Caribbean Sea. Both vessels had been sanctioned by the Treasury Department. The reach of Operation Southern Spear was global; no ocean was too vast, and no jurisdiction too distant to prevent the US from enforcing its will.

The economic impact of these actions was profound. The Venezuelan economy, already in a state of collapse, faced the prospect of total isolation. With the US Navy effectively blockading the country's oil exports, the flow of revenue that sustained the Maduro government was cut off. The US strategy was explicit: weaken the government by undermining its oil market. The deployment of the Navy, the air strikes, and the seizures were all calculated to create an environment where the only choice for the regime was to negotiate or face total economic strangulation. The Russian tanker Seahorse, sanctioned by the European Union and the UK, had attempted to deliver naphtha—a critical product needed to export Venezuela's heavy crude—to Venezuela. It was forced several times by the USS Stockdale to turn back towards Cuba. Although it eventually reached Venezuela in November 2025, the message was sent: the US would not allow the flow of essential supplies that could keep the Venezuelan oil industry running.

The events of late 2025 and early 2026 marked a new chapter in the long saga of US-Venezuela relations. The blockade was not just a military operation; it was a declaration that the rules of the road had changed. The United States had moved from imposing sanctions to enforcing them with kinetic force, turning the Caribbean Sea into a theater of war. The seizure of the Skipper, the Centuries, the Marinera, and the M Sophia were not merely arrests; they were the physical manifestation of a geopolitical strategy that prioritized regime change over the traditional norms of maritime commerce. As the year 2026 unfolded, the world watched to see if the Maduro government could withstand the pressure, or if the blockade would finally break the back of the Venezuelan state. The answer would depend on whether the regime could find a way to bypass the US Navy, or if the shadow fleet could evolve faster than the American enforcement apparatus. For now, the waters remained dangerous, and the silence of the Skipper in a US port was a stark reminder of the cost of defiance.

The legal and diplomatic fallout was just beginning. The UN's calls for restraint fell on deaf ears in Washington, where the administration viewed the operation as a necessary defense of national security and a fight against narcoterrorism. The distinction between law enforcement and warfare was blurring, and the international community was left to grapple with the implications. Could a nation be blockaded by a single power in international waters? Was the "right to visit" being expanded into a right to seize? These questions hung in the air, unanswered, as the US Navy continued its patrols. The operation was a high-stakes gamble, a test of whether the United States could impose its will on a region that had long resisted its influence. The events of December 2025 and January 2026 suggested that the United States was willing to pay the price, to risk the legal and diplomatic fallout, to achieve its objectives. The blockade was on, and the world was watching.

The story of the Skipper and the Operation Southern Spear is a testament to the power of naval force in the modern age. It is a story of technology, of satellite imagery, of transponder spoofing, and of the relentless pursuit of a target. It is a story of a regime on the brink, of a government that has relied on the export of oil for its survival, and of a superpower that has decided that the cost of inaction is too high. The seizure of the Skipper was not just a capture of a ship; it was a capture of a moment, a moment when the abstract concept of sanctions became a concrete reality on the deck of a supertanker. The implications will be felt for years to come, reshaping the landscape of international law and the future of oil trade in the Western Hemisphere. The blockade is not just a military operation; it is a statement of intent, a declaration that the United States will no longer tolerate the smuggling of sanctioned oil, no matter the cost. And as the ships of the US Navy patrol the Caribbean, the message is clear: the era of impunity for the shadow fleet is over.
