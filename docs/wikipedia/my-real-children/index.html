My Real Children

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: My Real Children

In 2015, an eighty-nine-year-old woman named Patricia sits in a nursing home, her memory fractured into two distinct, irreconcilable realities that she insists are equally true. She is not suffering from the confabulations of dementia or the delusions of psychosis, but rather exists at the terrifying intersection of a branching timeline where history has split like a fissioning atom. In one version of her life, she is "Trish," a woman who escaped an unhappy marriage to become a political activist in a world that narrowly avoided nuclear annihilation after President John F. Kennedy was killed by a bomb in 1963. In the other, she is "Pat," a travel writer living with her lesbian partner, Bee, raising three children in a world where the Cuban Missile Crisis escalated into a catastrophic nuclear war that incinerated Miami and Kyiv. Both sets of memories possess the visceral texture of lived experience; both sets of children visit her bedside to ask for stories. The central horror and beauty of Jo Walton's 2014 novel, My Real Children, lies not in the mechanics of time travel or alternate history theory, but in this specific, crushing weight of a consciousness that holds two complete lives within a single skull.

Published by Tor Books on May 20, 2014, the Welsh-Canadian author's work stands as a profound meditation on how individual choices ripple outward to reshape the fate of nations, and conversely, how geopolitical forces dictate the contours of private happiness. Walton describes her project as an "attempt to meld the genres of women's fiction and SF," a synthesis that results in a narrative where the epic scale of nuclear proliferation is measured against the quiet, devastating details of domestic life. To read this book is to understand that the "what if" of history is not a game for armchair strategists, but a visceral reckoning with the fragility of human existence.

The Divergence Point

The fracture in Patricia's reality occurs at two distinct moments in 1963 and 1964. In the timeline where she becomes Trish, John F. Kennedy is assassinated not by Lee Harvey Oswald with a rifle, but by a bomb in Dallas. This event triggers a different political cascade. The United States, reeling from a more traumatic loss of confidence in its leadership and facing a different Cold War dynamic, accelerates toward nuclear disarmament rather than escalation. The Soviet Union, under this new pressure, liberalizes sooner than in our own history. They withdraw peacefully from the Warsaw Pact without intervening militarily in Hungary or Czechoslovakia when those nations seek independence.

This is a world where the space race takes on a different complexion. In Trish's memory, the Soviet Union lands the first humans on the Moon in 1967. The United States struggles to catch up, pouring resources into constructing a space station and eventually a moonbase, but always playing second fiddle to a more stable, if distant, Moscow. It is a timeline marked by a strange sense of relief; the shadow of nuclear winter lifts early, replaced by the cold logic of détente and the slow, arduous work of rebuilding trust between superpowers. Trish herself finds a constructive role in this world. After leaving her husband Mark—a man she describes as obnoxious and closeted gay—she becomes deeply involved in politics, finding purpose in the lives of her four children and grandchildren. The pain of her personal life is mitigated by the relative stability of the public sphere.

In stark contrast, the timeline where Patricia remains "Pat" begins with a failure of diplomacy that mirrors our own fears most closely. Here, Kennedy chooses not to run for re-election in 1964 following an escalated Cuban Missile Crisis. The tension does not de-escalate; it detonates. Over a fifty-year interval, the world is subjected to nuclear attacks on a scale that defies imagination. Miami, Kyiv, Delhi, Tel Aviv, and several unspecified cities in China are subjected to orbital bombardment and ground detonations. The incineration of Miami is not just a geopolitical event; it is a cultural amputation of American history, turning the United States into an isolationist power that refuses to become actively involved in the Vietnam War.

The consequences for Pat's life are immediate and devastating. She marries Bee, a woman who becomes her partner for decades, but their idyllic relationship exists under the constant threat of annihilation. They raise three children together, navigating a world where the taboo against the use of nuclear weapons has been shattered. In this reality, the European Union consolidates more rapidly than in our timeline, perhaps as a desperate survival mechanism, yet the old colonial powers refuse to pursue decolonization, clinging to empires that are already burning. The human cost is absolute. Pat and Bee watch in horror as their world shrinks, as cities vanish from maps and families are wiped out in seconds of light and fire.

The Geography of Grief

The genius of Walton's narrative structure is that she does not treat these timelines as abstract alternatives. She grounds them in the specific geography of pain and joy. In the "Pat" timeline, the nuclear proliferation is a living, breathing entity. It breathes down the necks of every character. The book details the slow, agonizing erosion of hope over five decades. We see the aftermath of the attacks not through the lens of military strategy, but through the eyes of civilians trying to survive in the rubble.

The incineration of Miami is a recurring trauma for Pat. It represents the moment the United States ceased to be the global hegemon and became a frightened island. The decision to stay out of Vietnam was born not of moral clarity, but of a paralyzing fear that any engagement could trigger another exchange. This isolationism leaves a vacuum in the world order, one filled by other, often more brutal, powers. The European colonial powers, refusing to decolonize, create a different kind of violence, one rooted in the refusal to let go of the past even as the present burns around them.

But it is the personal losses that carry the heaviest weight. Pat and Bee's relationship is tested by the physical toll of living in a post-apocalyptic world. Bee suffers a disabling injury during an IRA bombing campaign, a reminder that even in this nuclear-hobbled world, regional conflicts continue to claim bodies. The disease that eventually claims them is anaplastic thyroid cancer, a brutal illness that sweeps through their family circle, killing several relatives and ultimately taking Bee's life. This disease serves as a grim counterpoint to the nuclear blasts; while the bombs kill instantly and spectacularly, the cancer kills slowly and invisibly, a constant reminder of the fragility of the human body in a poisoned world.

In Trish's timeline, the absence of these specific tragedies creates a different kind of sorrow. She remembers Mark not as a victim of war, but as an obnoxious husband she left. Her children are alive, growing up in a world where the sun still rises without fear of fire from the sky. Yet, there is a lingering question: did her happiness come at the cost of a deeper moral ambiguity? The Soviet Union's peaceful withdrawal and early space dominance might seem like victories for peace, but they also imply a shift in global power that left other nations vulnerable. Trish's life is easier, safer, perhaps even happier, but it exists alongside the memory of a world that could have been much darker.

The Mechanics of Memory

The narrative device Walton employs is deceptively simple: she moves back and forth between these two realities as Patricia sits in her nursing home. The transition is seamless, often triggered by a sensory detail—a smell, a sound, or the arrival of one set of children versus the other. This technique forces the reader to experience the disorientation of the protagonist. We are not told which timeline is "real" and which is "fake." Both feel completely real because both are built on the foundation of human memory, emotion, and consequence.

The fact that both sets of children visit her complicates the distinction further. They are distinct individuals in each timeline, yet they share a name, a connection to their mother, and a love for her. Trish's four children bring news from a world where nuclear disarmament worked, where the space race continued with less urgency but more cooperation. Pat's three children bring memories of a world where they grew up under the shadow of the bomb, where their lives were defined by loss and survival.

This duality challenges the reader to consider what makes a life "real." Is it the objective truth of the events that occurred? Or is it the subjective experience of the person living them? Patricia's mind refuses to choose. She holds both lives simultaneously, creating a unique form of psychological endurance. She has lived eighty-nine years in one reality and eighty-nine years in another, accumulating two lifetimes of joy, grief, love, and loss. The burden of this dual existence is immense, yet it is also the source of her profound wisdom.

The novel does not offer a resolution where one timeline is proven to be an illusion. Instead, it suggests that reality itself may be more fluid than we dare to admit. The branching paths of history are not merely theoretical constructs; they are lived experiences in the mind of someone who has seen too much. Patricia's condition becomes a metaphor for the collective human memory of the Cold War era—a time when the world stood on the brink of two possible futures, one where we learned to live together and one where we destroyed ourselves.

Critical Reception and Cultural Resonance

When My Real Children was published in 2014, it garnered immediate acclaim for its ability to balance high-concept science fiction with intimate character study. Lev Grossman described the novel as a "quiet triumph," drawing parallels between Walton's prose and the works of Alice Munro, known for her deep psychological insight into ordinary lives. He also noted the comparison to Philip K. Dick's The Man in the High Castle, another seminal work of alternate history that explores the nature of reality under oppressive regimes. However, where Dick often leaned toward paranoia and metaphysical confusion, Walton grounded her exploration in emotional truth.

Robert Wiersema praised the book for its "achingly beautiful prose and carefully crafted characters," highlighting how Walton manages to make the grand sweep of geopolitical change feel personal and immediate. Cory Doctorow went further, calling it a "standout" even among Walton's other acclaimed works. He described the reading experience as one that "literally kept [him] up all night, weeping uncontrollably with the most astounding mixture of joy and sorrow." This reaction underscores the novel's power to evoke deep empathy; it is not just an intellectual puzzle about time travel, but a story that breaks the heart.

At NPR, Amal El-Mohtar argued that calling the book "elegant" was insufficient to capture its impact. The term "elegant" suggests a cold, polished surface, but My Real Children is raw and visceral. It deals with the incineration of cities, the slow death by cancer, the pain of divorce, and the terror of nuclear war. Its elegance lies in how it handles these heavy subjects without sensationalism or melodrama.

The novel's success was recognized by several prestigious awards. It won the 2014 James Tiptree, Jr. Award, which honors works that expand and explore our understanding of gender. This is particularly fitting given the book's focus on Patricia/Trish's life as a woman navigating different social structures in two timelines—one where she escapes a closeted gay marriage to find political agency, and another where she builds a family with her lesbian partner in a world that is both more tolerant in some ways and more brutal in others. It was also a finalist for the 2015 World Fantasy Award—Novel, the Aurora Award for Best Novel, the 2015 Sunburst Award for Adult Novel, and the 2015 Sidewise Award for Alternate History. These accolades confirm that Walton has achieved something rare: a book that satisfies fans of hard science fiction while resonating deeply with readers of literary fiction.

The Human Cost of History

At its core, My Real Children is a meditation on the human cost of history. In many alternate history novels, the focus is on the mechanics of change—how technology differs, how borders shift, how leaders rise and fall. Walton flips this script. She is less interested in the political maneuvering that led to the nuclear war or the disarmament treaties than she is in what those events meant for a mother watching her children grow up.

The description of the nuclear attacks on Miami, Kyiv, Delhi, Tel Aviv, and Chinese cities is not a list of strategic objectives; it is a catalog of human suffering. The novel forces us to confront the reality that behind every geopolitical decision lies a body count. The incineration of Miami is not just a plot point; it is the destruction of a city full of people, families, and memories. The fact that Pat and Bee watch this happen "aghast" emphasizes the helplessness of individuals in the face of state violence.

Similarly, the depiction of anaplastic thyroid cancer serves as a reminder that death does not always come with the flash of an explosion. It comes quietly, stealing loved ones one by one, leaving behind a trail of grief that is just as real as the radiation burns. The disease kills Bee in Pat's timeline, ending her partnership and leaving Patricia to face the end of her life alone in a world that has already lost so much. This personal tragedy stands in sharp contrast to the grand narrative of nuclear war, reminding us that even in the shadow of global catastrophe, individual lives are lived with their own unique intensity and sorrow.

The novel also challenges the official framing of historical events. In Trish's timeline, the Soviet Union is depicted as liberalizing sooner and withdrawing from Eastern Europe peacefully. This narrative might be seen by some as a triumph of diplomacy, but Walton does not shy away from the complexities. The space race becomes a source of tension rather than cooperation, with the United States struggling to catch up. The "peace" in this timeline is fragile, built on a foundation of compromise and fear. It is not a utopia, but it is a world where nuclear war was avoided, and that avoidance comes at a cost.

In Pat's timeline, the failure of diplomacy leads to a world that is darker than our own. The European Union consolidates rapidly, but this consolidation does not bring peace; it brings a different kind of oppression. The colonial powers refuse to decolonize, perpetuating cycles of violence and exploitation. The United States becomes isolationist, abandoning its role on the global stage and leaving others to suffer. The novel suggests that there is no perfect timeline, only a series of choices with varying degrees of tragedy.

A Legacy of Two Worlds

As Patricia sits in her nursing home in 2015, surrounded by two sets of children who love her but know nothing of the other life she has lived, the novel reaches its emotional climax. She is the keeper of two histories, a living archive of what might have been and what actually was. Her existence challenges the linear nature of time and memory. She proves that the past is not fixed, but fluid, shaped by the choices we make and the chances we take.

The book leaves the reader with a profound sense of empathy for the human condition. We are all living in a world where history could have gone differently. Every decision we make has the potential to alter the course of our lives and the lives of those around us. My Real Children asks us to consider the weight of these decisions, not just on a personal level, but on a global one. It reminds us that the fate of nations is inextricably linked to the fate of individuals.

In a world where nuclear proliferation remains a threat and where political polarization continues to fracture societies, Walton's novel feels more relevant than ever. It serves as a warning against complacency and a call to remember the human cost of our choices. The incineration of Miami is not just a fictional event; it is a possibility that we have narrowly avoided in our own timeline. The peaceful withdrawal of the Soviet Union from Eastern Europe was not guaranteed; it required courage, diplomacy, and luck.

Ultimately, My Real Children is a testament to the resilience of the human spirit. Whether in a world of nuclear war or a world of uneasy peace, Patricia/Trish finds ways to love, to raise children, to find purpose, and to endure. Her story is one of hope in the face of despair, a reminder that even when the world seems broken, there is beauty to be found in the connections we make with each other. The novel does not offer easy answers or simple solutions. Instead, it offers a mirror, reflecting our own fears and hopes back at us, urging us to consider what kind of world we want to build for the future.

The final image of Patricia, holding two sets of memories in her mind, is one of profound sadness and profound beauty. She has lived two lives, loved two families, and witnessed two worlds. Her story is a reminder that history is not just a record of events; it is a tapestry woven from the threads of human experience. And in that tapestry, every thread matters, no matter how small or insignificant it may seem in the grand scheme of things.
