Levelized cost of electricity

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Levelized cost of electricity

In 2026, as the global energy grid transitions from fossil dominance to a complex mosaic of renewables, the single most cited number in boardrooms from Beijing to Berlin remains the Levelized Cost of Electricity, or LCOE. It is the financial Rosetta Stone of the energy sector, a metric designed to translate the chaotic, decades-long reality of power generation into a single, comparable dollar figure per kilowatt-hour. Yet, for all its ubiquity, this number is a treacherous guide. It tells you how much it costs to build and run a machine, but it often fails to tell you how much that machine is actually worth to the grid at 2:00 AM on a windless Tuesday. To understand why geothermal failed in China, or why solar sometimes floods the market with negative prices, one must first understand that LCOE is not the price of electricity. It is an estimation of production cost, a static snapshot of a dynamic, breathing system, and relying on it without context is like judging a marathon runner by their average speed over a single mile while ignoring the hills, the wind, and the fatigue.

The concept of LCOE was born out of necessity. Before its widespread adoption, comparing a nuclear plant with a 60-year lifespan against a natural gas peaker plant with a 20-year life and a volatile fuel supply was an exercise in financial chaos. LCOE solved this by calculating the average net present cost of electricity generation over a generator's entire lifetime. It asks a fundamental question: what constant price per unit of energy would yield a net present value of zero for the project? More technically, it is the cost that, if assigned to every unit of energy produced or saved by the system over the analysis period, will equal the total life-cycle cost when discounted back to the base year. This formula acts as a great equalizer, allowing investors, governments, and policymakers to stack wind turbines, solar arrays, and coal plants on the same spreadsheet and see which one looks cheapest on paper.

However, the elegance of the formula hides a labyrinth of assumptions. The cost of electricity production is not a fixed variable; it is a function of costs during the expected lifetime of the generator and the total amount of electricity that generator is expected to produce. This is where the danger lies. For any given technology, the LCOE can vary significantly from region to region. A solar farm in the sun-drenched Atacama Desert will have a radically different LCOE than one in the overcast skies of Northern Europe, not because the panels are different, but because the natural resource economics dictate the output. Similarly, for non-renewable generators, the availability of fuel over the lifespan of the plant is a moving target. Geopolitical shocks, such as the energy crises of the 1970s, or the gradual depletion of reserves, can upend the fuel cost assumptions that anchor the LCOE calculation. If the price of gas doubles in year ten of a twenty-year project, the LCOE calculated at year zero becomes a historical curiosity rather than a predictive tool.

"The levelized cost of electricity is an estimation of the cost of production of electricity and not the price of electricity."

This distinction is the first crack in the foundation. The price of electricity is influenced by market markup, price controls, transmission constraints, and the whims of supply and demand. LCOE ignores all of this. It is a supply-side metric, designed to support decisions on capacity replacement and expansion. It assumes a world where the only variable is the cost to build and fuel the generator. It does not account for the value of that electricity at the moment it is generated. This omission creates a phenomenon known as the "energy efficiency gap." While LCOE is the dominant tool for analyzing supply-side generation, it largely ignores demand-side energy conservation. The metric suggests that the cheapest energy is the energy we generate, rather than the energy we don't use. In investment strategies and energy policy, this bias can lead to massive over-investment in new capacity when the most cost-effective solution might be simply reducing waste on the demand side.

The limitations of LCOE become most glaring when we attempt to rank-order energy-generation alternatives. For LCOE to be a valid comparator, it must be calculated in "real" terms, adjusting for expected inflation. But even with inflation adjustments, the metric struggles to control for time effects associated with matching electricity production to demand. This is the problem of dispatchability. A coal plant or a nuclear reactor is dispatchable; it can come online, go offline, or ramp up and down quickly to match demand swings. A solar farm is not. It produces when the sun shines, regardless of whether the grid needs power. If the costs of matching grid energy storage are not included in the LCOE of variable renewable energy sources like wind and solar, the metric produces a dangerous illusion. It suggests that a solar panel is cheaper than a gas turbine. In reality, if that solar panel produces electricity when it is not needed, the value of that electricity may be lower than if it was produced at another time, or even negative.

In modern grids, we are seeing the rise of negative spot prices. When solar generation peaks during mid-day, particularly in hot countries where air conditioning is a major consumer, the grid can become oversaturated. The sun is shining, the panels are working perfectly, but there is no one to buy the power. In some markets, generators are actually paid to turn off. LCOE does not capture this. It assumes a constant utilization rate for the lifetime of the plant. For fossil fuel and nuclear plants, this "capacity factor" is often assumed to be high and steady. But in a grid increasingly dominated by cheap solar and wind, these dispatchable plants are forced to run at lower capacity factors, ramping up and down to fill the gaps left by the sun and wind. Their fixed costs are spread over fewer megawatt-hours, causing their real-world LCOE to skyrocket, even if the initial calculation looked favorable.

The response to this blindness has been the development of more nuanced metrics. System LCOE and Levelized Full System Costs of Electricity (LFSCOE) have been proposed to factor in the cost of required grid energy storage or redundant dispatchable power plants needed to balance demand during intermittency periods. These metrics attempt to answer the question that standard LCOE ignores: what does it cost to make this intermittent power reliable? If a solar farm requires a battery system to store its mid-day excess for evening use, the cost of that battery must be included in the true cost of that electricity. Without this adjustment, LCOE significantly overvalues dispatchable power plants in the short term while undervaluing the hidden costs of integrating variable sources.

Furthermore, some analyses fail to adequately consider the indirect costs of generation. These include the social cost of greenhouse gas emissions, other environmental externalities like air pollution, and the grid upgrade requirements necessary to handle new types of generation. A coal plant might have a low LCOE on paper, but if the calculation does not include the cost of carbon emissions or the health impacts of particulate matter, it is a flawed metric. Conversely, LCOE often ignores potential co-benefits. Solar photovoltaic systems are not just electricity generators; they are multi-functional assets. Agrivoltaics, where crops are grown under solar panels, can produce more food while generating power. Floating solar, or floatovoltaics, reduces water evaporation from reservoirs, a critical benefit in arid regions. Building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) and solar canopies provide shade, cooling buildings and cars, which reduces the demand for air conditioning. These are economic values that LCOE fails to capture, leading to an undervaluation of emerging solar technologies that offer more than just electrons.

The bias inherent in LCOE also extends to scale. The LCOE for a given generator is often inversely proportional to its capacity. Larger power plants tend to have lower LCOE than smaller ones due to economies of scale. This creates a structural bias in investment decisions, favoring massive, centralized installations while overlooking opportunities for distributed energy and energy efficiency. If a comparison is omitted or incomplete, LCOE may not give a comprehensive picture of potential options available for meeting energy needs. A small, local solar array might have a higher LCOE than a massive, distant wind farm, but it eliminates transmission losses and increases grid resilience. The metric, in its simplicity, pushes us toward the biggest, not necessarily the best.

The amount of electrical energy generated, or estimated to be generated, is dependent upon a large number of factors that LCOE struggles to encapsulate. Natural resource economics and the availability of economically viable resources are variable per generation technology. For variable renewable energy generators, wind resource assessment and solar potential assessment are critical methods used to determine the raw material available. For non-renewable generators, fuel availability is a game of geopolitical chess. The 1970s energy crisis serves as a stark reminder that fuel availability can be temporarily impacted by geopolitical factors or the gradual depletion of reserves. The methods used to assess long-term availability, such as oil and gas reserve quantification, are themselves subject to uncertainty.

Grid balancing adds another layer of complexity. The electricity market effects may require a load-following power plant to curtail generation if the grid does not demand energy, or allow a generator with high variable costs to be brought online to dispatch into a grid with significant unmet demand and high spot prices. Battery storage, run-of-the-river hydroelectricity with pondage, and natural gas turbines are examples of dispatchable generators that can respond to these signals. But seasonal diurnal cycles, climatology, and short-term meteorological events have significant impacts on grid balancing from both supply and demand perspectives. The geographical region within which LCOE is being assessed, the mix of generators in the grid, and the proportion of variable to dispatchable sources all dictate the true value of the electricity produced.

Significant caution must be applied to the use of LCOE because outputs are highly sensitive to the selection of input values. The ability to interpret and compare LCOE model outputs is dependent upon the level of detailed justification provided for input values and the results of sensitivity analysis. A small change in the discount rate, the assumed capacity factor, or the fuel price can swing the LCOE of a project by tens of percentage points. This sensitivity makes LCOE a tool for feasibility study decisions and investment strategy, but a poor tool for absolute truth. It is a model, and like all models, it is a simplification of reality.

In the context of China's geothermal failure, the limitations of LCOE provide a crucial lens. China possesses vast geothermal resources, particularly in the Tibetan Plateau and surrounding regions. On paper, the LCOE for geothermal energy in these resource-rich areas should be competitive. Geothermal offers baseload power, a feature that solar and wind lack. It is dispatchable. It does not suffer from intermittency. Yet, the sector has struggled to scale. Why? Because the LCOE calculation, often focused on the cost of the well and the turbine, fails to capture the geological risks and the specific market conditions that make the project unviable.

The failure is not in the technology, but in the application of a metric that assumes a level playing field that does not exist. In China, the cost of drilling deep wells is high, and the geological uncertainty is significant. If the LCOE model assumes a standard capacity factor based on ideal conditions, it overestimates the output. If it fails to account for the specific grid integration costs in remote, mountainous regions where these resources are located, it underestimates the total system cost. Furthermore, the Chinese grid, with its massive scale and heavy reliance on coal and hydro, has different dynamics than the European or American grids where LCOE is often benchmarked. The value of baseload power in a grid already saturated with hydro and coal may be lower than the LCOE suggests. The "price" of the electricity, influenced by market controls and the dominance of state-owned utilities, may not reflect the "cost" of production in a way that allows private or even state-backed geothermal projects to be profitable.

The energy efficiency gap phenomenon suggests that demand-side energy conservation should also be considered in investment strategies. In China, the push for efficiency has often been secondary to the massive build-out of supply-side generation. If LCOE is used as the sole metric, the focus remains on building more power plants rather than improving the efficiency of the buildings and industries that consume the power. This bias toward supply-side expansion, driven by the simplicity of LCOE, can lead to a cycle of over-investment in generation capacity that the market cannot absorb, or that does not align with the actual demand profile.

Decisions about investments in energy generation technologies may need to be guided by other measures such as the levelized cost of storage (LCOS) and the levelized avoided cost of energy (LACE), in addition to the LCOE. The LACE metric, for instance, measures the value of the electricity to the grid, rather than the cost to produce it. If the LACE is higher than the LCOE, the project is valuable. If the LCOE is lower than the LACE, it is cheap. But when LCOE is lower than the market price, but the electricity is produced at a time when the market price is negative, the project loses money. This is the reality of variable renewables in high-penetration scenarios.

The story of LCOE is the story of the modern energy transition. It is a tool that brought order to chaos, allowing the world to compare apples to oranges, or rather, coal to wind. But as the grid becomes more complex, more variable, and more interconnected, the limitations of this tool become more apparent. It is a static measure in a dynamic world. It ignores the time value of electricity, the cost of reliability, and the hidden benefits of co-generation. It favors the large over the small, the supply-side over the demand-side, and the visible cost over the invisible externality.

To navigate the future of energy, we must use LCOE not as a gospel, but as a starting point. We must demand sensitivity analyses that test the robustness of the numbers. We must look for metrics that capture the full system cost, including storage and grid upgrades. We must value the co-benefits of clean energy, from water conservation to food production. And we must recognize that the cheapest electricity on paper is not always the most valuable electricity in reality. The transition to a sustainable energy future requires a nuanced understanding of cost, one that goes beyond the simple arithmetic of LCOE to embrace the complex, interconnected reality of the power grid.

The lesson from China's geothermal struggles is a warning for the rest of the world. Do not let a single number dictate your energy strategy. The LCOE is a powerful tool, but it is not the whole story. It is a measure of production cost, not value. It is a snapshot of a moment in time, not a prediction of the future. As we move forward, we must build our investment strategies and energy policies on a foundation that recognizes these limitations. We must look at the full picture, including the cost of storage, the value of reliability, and the potential for efficiency. Only then can we make the right decisions for a sustainable, resilient, and affordable energy future.

"The levelized cost of electricity is the constant price per unit of energy that would yield a net present value of zero for the project."

This definition is the anchor, but it is also the cage. It cages the complexity of energy into a single variable. To break free, we must expand our view. We must look at the system, not just the generator. We must look at the demand, not just the supply. We must look at the time, not just the total. The future of energy depends on our ability to see beyond the LCOE, to understand the true cost of power in all its dimensions. The numbers on the page are important, but they are not the truth. The truth is in the grid, in the wind, in the sun, and in the people who use the power. And that truth is far more complex than any formula can capture.

In the end, the LCOE remains the dominant tool for assessing solar photovoltaics and other energy source economics due to its simplicity. But simplicity is a double-edged sword. It makes the metric easy to use, but it also makes it easy to misuse. It allows for quick comparisons, but it obscures the deep, structural challenges of the energy transition. As we stand on the brink of a new era in energy, we must use this tool with care, with skepticism, and with a deep understanding of its limitations. We must remember that the goal is not just to generate electricity at the lowest cost, but to generate the right electricity, at the right time, in the right place, with the right value. The LCOE can help us get there, but it cannot get us there alone. We need a broader vision, a more comprehensive metric, and a more nuanced approach. The future of our energy system depends on it.
