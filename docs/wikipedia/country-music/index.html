Country music

7 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Country music

In June 1923, a Georgia fiddler named Fiddlin' John Carson walked into an Okeh Records studio in New York City and recorded "Little Log Cabin in the Lane"—the first commercial recording of what would become country music. It was a ragged, warbling voice singing over a fiddle, nothing like the polished pop that dominated American radio. Yet within a decade, this obscure genre born in the Southern Appalachian Mountains would transform American culture. By 2023, nearly half of all Americans report listening to country music—a resurgence that has reconnected the nation with its roots.

The Music of the Mountains

Country music didn't emerge from nowhere. It grew from nearly three centuries of musical migration into the southern Appalachian region, where European and Mediterranean traditions blended with American folk forms. The genre's DNA is a fusion of old-time Southern Appalachian music, cowboy ballads from the Western frontier, African-American spirituals, and blues modes that had been developing since the earliest days of colonization.

The genre historian Bill C. Malone described country music as "a Southern phenomenon," and the evidence supports this claim. The Mississippi River and Louisiana became crossroads where Cajun music flourished. In the Southwestern United States, the Rocky Mountains, the American frontier, and the Rio Grande provided the backdrop for Native American, Mexican, and cowboy ballads—giving rise to New Mexico music and Western sounds directly related to Red Dirt, Texas country, and Tejano styles.

Birth of a Genre

The term "country music" itself was popularized in the 1940s to replace the earlier (and more pejorative) label of "hillbilly music." But before these modern designations took hold, pioneer settlers in the Great Smoky Mountains region had developed a rich musical heritage that would eventually become one of America's most influential art forms.

The discovery that mattered most came on August 1, 1927, when a family from Scott County, Virginia walked into a Bristol, Tennessee recording studio. The Carter Family—consisting of Ayers, A.P, and their relatives—had learned to sight-read hymnals and sheet music using solfege. Their session with talent scout Ralph Peer captured what would become the foundation of modern country music: old-time ballads, traditional tunes, gospel hymns representative of America's southeastern folklore.

That same year also witnessed the historic Bristol recording sessions that officially earned Bristol, Tennessee its formal recognition by the U.S. Congress as "the Birthplace of Country Music." Historians have also noted the influence of the lesser-known Johnson City sessions of 1928 and 1929, and the Knoxville sessions of 1929 and 1930—each contributing to the genre's early sound.

The First Recordings

Before the Carter Family's historic session, however, others had already begun the work of recording country music. On June 30, 1922, fiddlers Henry Gilliland and A.C. (Eck) Robertson recorded "Arkansas Traveler" and "Turkey in the Straw" for Victor Records—an early example of what was considered instrumental country style. These recordings were released in April 1923.

Then came Fiddlin' John Carson's June 14, 1923 session with Okeh Records, recording "Little Log Cabin in the Lane"—the first widely considered country song featuring vocals and lyrics. The genre had found its voice.

The following year brought further milestones. In May 1924, Vernon Dalhart recorded "Wreck of the Old 97"—becoming the first country singer to have a nationwide hit. Its flip side, "Lonesome Road Blues," also gained popular attention. By April 1924, "Aunt" Samantha Bumgarner and Eva Davis had become the first female musicians to record and release country songs.

The 1920s saw hillbilly musicians recording blues songs well into the 1930s—including yodeler Cliff Carlisle—while James Gideon "Gid" Tanner, an American old-time fiddler, became one of country music's earliest stars with his string band, the Skillet Lickers.

The Rise of the Stars

Two figures emerged as the most important early country musicians: Jimmie Rodgers and the Carter Family. Their approaches differed, but together they established what would become the genre's core identity.

Rodgers fused hillbilly country, gospel, jazz, blues, pop, cowboy, and folk into a remarkably cohesive sound. His compositions—particularly "Blue Yodel," which sold over a million records—established him as the premier singer of early country music. From 1927 onward, his hybrid approach influenced countless musicians.

The Carter Family, meanwhile, began their recording career in 1927 and continued for seventeen years, producing roughly 300 old-time ballads, traditional tunes, country songs, and gospel hymns—all representing America's southeastern folklore and heritage.

Radio Changes Everything

The invention of the radio in the 1920s gave country music—still known as hillbilly music at the time—an increased audience it had never before possessed. The largest country music radio show was the Grand Ole Opry, aired starting in 1925 by WSM in Nashville and continuing to present day.

Record labels followed suit. Okeh Records, a New York City record label, began issuing hillbilly records in 1923. Columbia Records joined in 1924, followed by RCA Victor Records in 1927. Atlanta's country music scene outside of New York was also important, launching many early recording artists' careers.

The steel guitar entered country music as early as 1922, when Jimmie Tarlton met famed Hawaiian guitarist Frank Ferera on the West Coast—a meeting that would permanently alter the genre's sonic signature.

During the Great Depression, record sales declined but radio became an even more popular source of entertainment. "Barn dance" shows featuring country music were popularized, beginning in the Southern United States and spreading north to Chicago and west to California.

Nashville's Ascent

The Grand Ole Opry remained the most important country music venue, though it was initially conservative about instrumentation. Early stars on the Opry included Uncle Dave Macon, Roy Acuff, and African American harmonica player DeFord Bailey—demonstrating that the genre had room for diverse voices despite its regional origins.

Drums were looked down upon by early country musicians as being too loud and not pure for the country sound. But by 1935, Bob Wills added drums to his Western Swing band, Texas Playboys, despite opposition from the Grand Ole Opry. In contrast, Louisiana Hayride—a less conservative country music venue—kept a house drummer backstage as late as 1956.

By the 1960s, it had become rare for a country band not to have a drummer—a complete transformation from the genre's earlier purism.

Bob Wills was also among the first country musicians known to have added an electric guitar to his band in 1938. A decade later, in 1948, Arthur Smith recorded "Guitar Boogie," which crossed over to the US pop charts, introducing many people to the potential of the electric guitar.

For several decades, Nashville session players preferred the warm tones of Gibson and Gretsch archtop electrics. But a "hot" Fender style—using guitars that became available beginning in the early 1950s—eventually prevailed as the signature guitar sound of country music.

The Genre's Legacy

Country music has remained an integral part of the American music scene, with recent revitalization since the early 2020s. In 2023, 45% of Americans reported listening to country music—an uptick in the genre's popularity that reflects its enduring resonance.

The main components of modern country music date back to music traditions throughout the Southern and Southwestern United States. Its place in American popular music was established in the 1930s during the early days of recording, when a generation of musicians transformed regional folk sounds into national phenomena.

Today, Nashville remains the common hotspot for many country artists—continuing the work begun by the Carter Family and Jimmie Rodgers nearly a century ago. The Mountain City Fiddlers Convention, held in 1925, helped inspire modern country music, and its legacy echoes through every fiddler, singer, and songwriter who carries forward this distinctly American tradition.

The genre that began as hillbilly music—the product of British immigrants, African American spirituals, cowboy ballads, and Southern Appalachian folk—has become one of the most influential musical traditions in the world. From Fiddlin' John Carson to the modern revival, country music tells the story of America itself: messy, diverse, resilient, and constantly evolving.
