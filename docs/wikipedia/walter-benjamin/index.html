Walter Benjamin

18 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Walter Benjamin

The Philosopher Who Knew Everyone

Walter Benjamin was in the audience when Kurt Gödel announced the incompleteness theorem, one of the most profound discoveries in the history of mathematics. He once took a class on the Ancient Mayans from the legendary poet Rainer Maria Rilke. He attended the same university seminar as Martin Heidegger when both were still students, and the ideas they encountered there shaped the rest of their careers. He was a regular houseguest of Bertolt Brecht, reading early drafts of plays that would transform modern theater.

And yet, when Benjamin died in 1940, almost nobody had heard of him.

Today he is considered one of the most important cultural critics of the twentieth century. His essays on art, history, and technology are assigned in universities around the world. But during his lifetime, he couldn't secure a teaching position. He struggled to support his family. He bounced between Berlin and Paris, between friends' couches and his parents' home, always on the periphery of the intellectual movements he helped shape.

Benjamin's story is partly about ideas—big ones, about how mechanical reproduction changes the nature of art, about history as something the oppressed might one day reclaim. But it's also about timing, about being in exactly the right places at exactly the wrong moment, about a brilliant mind caught in the gears of a catastrophe he saw coming but couldn't escape.

A Berlin Childhood

Walter Bendix Schönflies Benjamin was born in Berlin on July 15, 1892, into a wealthy family of assimilated Ashkenazi Jews. His father Emil had been a banker in Paris before relocating to Germany, where he reinvented himself as an antiques trader and art dealer. Among his investments were several ice skating rinks around Berlin—a detail that somehow captures the eclectic, slightly eccentric quality of the Benjamin family's bourgeois prosperity.

The intellectual pedigree ran deep. Walter's uncle, William Stern, was a pioneering child psychologist who developed the concept we now call the intelligence quotient, or IQ. His cousin Günther Anders would become a philosopher and prominent anti-nuclear activist, studying under the phenomenologists Edmund Husserl and Martin Heidegger. Through his mother's side, Walter could claim the classical archaeologist Gustav Hirschfeld as a great-uncle.

Walter himself was a fragile child. When he was thirteen, his parents sent him away to a progressive rural boarding school in the Thuringian countryside, part of a movement that believed country air and reform pedagogy could strengthen weak constitutions. He stayed two years before returning to Berlin to finish his secondary education.

The frailty never entirely left him. It would reappear later, conveniently, when he needed to avoid military service.

The Youth Movement and a Fateful Friendship

In 1912, twenty-year-old Benjamin enrolled at the University of Freiburg, then transferred to the University of Berlin. There he encountered Zionism for the first time—an ideology that had played no part in his liberal, assimilated upbringing.

Benjamin's response was characteristically original. He rejected political Zionism, the movement to establish a Jewish state in Palestine. Instead, he developed what he called a "cultural Zionism"—a commitment to Jewish ethics and identity as a contribution to European civilization. In a formulation that would remain central to his self-understanding, he wrote: "My life experience led me to this insight: the Jews represent an elite in the ranks of the spiritually active... For Judaism is to me in no sense an end in itself, but the most distinguished bearer and representative of the spiritual."

This wasn't assimilation in the sense of abandoning Jewish identity. It was something stranger and more ambitious: a claim that Jewish thought was essential to Europe, that being Jewish meant bearing responsibility for European culture itself.

It was during this period that Benjamin became a prominent voice in the German Youth Movement, a loose collection of student organizations dedicated to cultural and educational reform. He was elected president of the Free Students Association. He wrote essays for Der Anfang, a controversial youth magazine so radical it was banned in all Bavarian schools. The magazine's guiding figure, Gustav Wyneken, believed that young people must lead the way to revolutionary cultural change—a thesis Benjamin enthusiastically promoted.

Among the young intellectuals drawn to this milieu was Gershom Scholem, who would become Benjamin's closest friend and, eventually, his literary executor. Their meeting was one of those accidents that shape intellectual history. Scholem was drawn to mysticism; Benjamin to aesthetics and cultural criticism. Their decades-long correspondence would influence both of their life's work in ways neither could have predicted.

Dodging the War

When World War One erupted in August 1914, Benjamin tried to volunteer. The army rejected him.

It's unclear whether this rejection reflected genuine health problems or whether Benjamin was already beginning to develop the strategic hypochondria that would characterize his relationship with military service. What's certain is that as the war dragged on, he became increasingly creative in his efforts to avoid conscription. He feigned illnesses. He obtained dubious medical exemptions. Eventually, he fled to Switzerland entirely, ostensibly for health reasons that conveniently allowed him to continue his studies and his translations of the French poet Charles Baudelaire.

This conspicuous draft-dodging would haunt him. After the war, when Benjamin sought academic employment, the question of his wartime service—or rather, his wartime absence—was a likely factor in his repeated failures to secure a position. The German academy remembered who had served and who had hidden in Bern.

But the Swiss exile wasn't entirely wasted. In 1917, Benjamin transferred to the University of Bern, where he met two people who would transform his life. One was Ernst Bloch, a philosopher writing an ambitious work called The Spirit of Utopia, with whom Benjamin developed a friendship he later described as "almost too close." The other was Dora Sophie Pollak, whom he married that same year.

Their son, Stefan Rafael, was born in 1918. A year later, Benjamin completed his doctoral dissertation, earning his PhD with the highest honors—summa cum laude—for a study of art criticism in German Romanticism.

The Heidegger Problem

In 1920, Benjamin set out to write his habilitation thesis, the second major academic work required for a university teaching career in Germany. He hit upon an intriguing idea: to legitimize his theory of language by connecting it to the largely forgotten medieval writings of Duns Scotus, the thirteenth-century Scottish philosopher.

There was just one problem. Martin Heidegger had already done it.

When Scholem gently informed Benjamin that Heidegger had published a habilitation thesis on precisely this topic—Duns Scotus's theory of categories and meaning—Benjamin was furious. Here began one of the strangest intellectual rivalries of the twentieth century.

Benjamin and Heidegger had crossed paths as students at Freiburg in the summer of 1913, attending the same seminar. Now they were developing parallel philosophical projects, arriving at similar insights through different routes. Both were interested in language, in history, in the relationship between past and present. Both rejected the dry academic philosophy of their teachers in favor of something more urgent and existential.

But where Heidegger would become the most celebrated philosopher in Germany, Benjamin would remain forever on the outside. Benjamin's friends later described him as considering Heidegger a "nemesis"—a rival whose success seemed to mock Benjamin's own marginality.

The Intellectual Nomad

Unable to support himself and his growing family, Benjamin moved back in with his parents in Berlin. It was 1921. He was nearly thirty years old, married with a child, possessed of a brilliant doctorate, and living in his childhood home.

He published an essay called "Toward the Critique of Violence," a dense meditation on law, power, and revolutionary politics that would later be read as prophetic. He became acquainted with Leo Strauss, the political philosopher who would eventually emigrate to America and become enormously influential among American conservatives. Benjamin remained an admirer of Strauss's work for the rest of his life—a fact that complicates any simple political categorization of Benjamin himself.

The pattern of Benjamin's adult life was now established. He was a maven, a connector, someone who happened to know everyone important in the left-intelligentsia of interwar Europe. Acquaintance with Walter Benjamin was a thread linking major figures in philosophy, theology, visual arts, theater, literature, radio, and politics. He was perpetually present on the outskirts of important events, interpreting them in his writing while never quite achieving the recognition or stability he craved.

In 1923, the Institute for Social Research was founded in Frankfurt—the institution that would later be known as the Frankfurt School, home to critical theorists like Theodor Adorno and Max Horkheimer. Benjamin never held a formal position there, but he became closely associated with its members. He published his translation of Baudelaire's Tableaux Parisiens. He befriended Georg Lukács, whose book The Theory of the Novel had influenced his thinking about culture and form.

That same year, the hyperinflation that devastated the Weimar Republic made it increasingly difficult for Emil Benjamin to support his son's family. And at the end of 1923, Scholem emigrated to Palestine.

Scholem would spend the next decades urging Benjamin to join him, to leave Europe for the relative safety of the British Mandate. Benjamin never went. He couldn't quite bring himself to abandon the European culture he believed Jews were meant to preserve and perfect.

The Essay That Ruined Everything

In 1924, the literary magazine Neue Deutsche Beiträge published Benjamin's essay on Goethe's third novel, Elective Affinities. According to the literary critic Burkhardt Lindner, this essay represents one of Benjamin's three major philosophical-aesthetic treatises from his early period, alongside his doctoral dissertation and his habilitation thesis.

It's a strange piece. Partly it meditates on the "free love" experiments that Benjamin and Dora were conducting in their own marriage at the time. The dedication to Julia Cohn, a woman Benjamin had unsuccessfully courted, suggests the essay emerged from personal turmoil. But these biographical details were merely tangential to the real problem.

The problem was that Benjamin attacked Friedrich Gundolf.

Gundolf was the most prominent academic member of the George-Kreis, a circle of post-symbolist poets centered around Stefan George. The circle had a mystically conservative, medievalist aesthetic—what Benjamin, in private letters, would later identify as an anticipation of fascist aesthetics, regardless of the horror with which George Circle members later greeted the Nazi regime.

Gundolf had written his own book on Goethe, a work of established scholarly authority. Benjamin's essay was, in part, a polemical assault on Gundolf's interpretation. This was a spectacular miscalculation.

According to Hannah Arendt, the essay on Goethe destroyed Benjamin's only real chance at an academic career. You could be brilliant in Weimar Germany. You could be eccentric. You could even be a Jew, at least for a while longer. But you could not publicly humiliate a member of the academic establishment and expect to be welcomed into that establishment yourself.

Capri and the Habilitation Disaster

Later in 1924, Benjamin traveled to the Italian island of Capri with Ernst Bloch. There he worked on what he hoped would finally secure his academic future: Ursprung des deutschen Trauerspiels, which translates as The Origin of German Tragic Drama.

The Trauerspiel—literally "mourning play"—was a genre of baroque German drama from the seventeenth century, largely forgotten by Benjamin's time. His habilitation thesis attempted to rehabilitate these plays and, through them, to develop a new theory of allegory and historical meaning. It was dense, allusive, and deliberately difficult. Benjamin's prose style, as one scholar later noted, seemed designed to resist understanding on first reading.

The University of Frankfurt rejected it.

The professors who evaluated the thesis couldn't understand what Benjamin was trying to say. The work was too strange, too unconventional, too far outside the boundaries of normal academic philosophy. Benjamin withdrew his application before facing formal rejection, preserving a shred of dignity at the cost of his academic dreams.

He would never hold a university position. For the rest of his life, he would remain what the Germans call a Privatgelehrter—a private scholar, an independent intellectual, a man of letters without institutional support.

The Web of Connections

What Benjamin lacked in institutional standing, he made up for in relationships. The list of his acquaintances reads like a syllabus for twentieth-century intellectual history.

There was Bertolt Brecht, the playwright and poet, whose Berlin cabaret theater Benjamin frequented. Benjamin served as an early reader of Brecht's scripts, a comrade, a favorable critic, and a promoter of his work. The two men's friendship—between the mystically inclined critic and the hard-edged Marxist dramatist—puzzled their mutual friends, but it was genuine and lasting.

There was Martin Buber, the Jewish philosopher of dialogue, who took an early interest in Benjamin's career. Benjamin declined to contribute to Buber's journal, finding it too esoteric—an ironic objection from a man whose own writing was famously obscure. Nevertheless, Buber financed Benjamin's trip to Moscow and promoted his work in other ways. He commissioned Benjamin to write an article on Moscow for his journal Die Kreatur, though Benjamin missed his deadline by several years.

There was Carl Schmitt, the legal theorist who would later become known as the "crown jurist of the Third Reich." Benjamin's relationship with Schmitt was that of intellectual rivals engaging with overlapping concerns about sovereignty, law, and political theology. It's an uncomfortable connection for those who want to place Benjamin cleanly on the left—a reminder that intellectual influence doesn't respect political boundaries.

And there were the casualties. One of Benjamin's high school best friends, another German Jew, killed himself with gas at the outbreak of World War One. Another friend became one of the Jewish liaisons who gave Nazi diplomats a tour of Palestine in the years when the Third Reich was still pretending that European Jews would merely be forcibly emigrated rather than exterminated.

The Rescue of the Kabbalah

Benjamin's lifelong correspondence with Gershom Scholem deserves special attention, because Scholem's subsequent career gives Benjamin's influence an unexpected legacy.

Scholem, from his new home in Jerusalem, would devote his life to resurrecting the Kabbalah—the tradition of Jewish mysticism that had been marginalized, ignored, and dismissed by mainstream Jewish scholars as an embarrassing relic of medieval superstition. Scholem disagreed. He believed the Kabbalah represented a vital strand of Jewish thought, a tradition of symbolic interpretation and mystical insight that deserved serious academic study.

To recover these texts, Scholem scoured private libraries and ancient document repositories called Genizot—storage rooms in synagogues where worn-out sacred texts were deposited rather than destroyed. As books and manuscripts flooded into Mandatory Palestine during the years before, during, and after the Holocaust, Scholem gathered and cataloged what remained of a nearly lost tradition.

His work transformed Jewish studies. Today, thanks largely to Scholem, the Kabbalah is taken seriously as a subject of academic inquiry. And the letters between Scholem and Benjamin—their decades of discussion about Judaism, mysticism, language, and meaning—shaped the intellectual framework through which Scholem approached this project.

Benjamin himself described his research as "theological," even as he rejected traditional metaphysical appeals to revealed authority. His thinking combined elements of German idealism, Jewish mysticism, Western Marxism, and post-Kantian philosophy in ways that defied easy categorization. Was he a philosopher? His older friend Scholem said unequivocally yes. His younger colleagues Hannah Arendt and Theodor Adorno said no. Benjamin himself might have appreciated the contradiction.

The Works That Endure

Benjamin's most famous essays were written in the 1930s, during his years of exile in Paris.

"The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction," published in 1935, argues that photography and film fundamentally change the nature of art. When a painting can be infinitely reproduced, it loses what Benjamin called its "aura"—the sense of uniqueness and authenticity that comes from standing in the presence of an original work. This might sound like a lament for lost tradition, but Benjamin's argument was more ambivalent. The loss of aura, he suggested, also democratized art, freeing it from its ritual and religious origins and opening it to political use.

The essay has been endlessly discussed, critiqued, and applied to phenomena Benjamin never lived to see: television, the internet, digital media, artificial intelligence. Its central insight—that technologies of reproduction change not just how we consume art but what art fundamentally is—remains generative nearly a century later.

"Theses on the Philosophy of History," written in 1940, the year of Benjamin's death, offers a revolutionary theory of historical time. Against the progressive view of history as continuous improvement, Benjamin proposed that the past is filled with unredeemed possibilities, moments of hope and resistance that were defeated but not destroyed. The task of the historian, he argued, was to "brush history against the grain," to rescue these suppressed possibilities from the triumphalist narratives of the victors.

The "Theses" are fragmentary, almost gnomic. Their most famous image is of the "angel of history," based on a Paul Klee painting that Benjamin owned. The angel is blown backward into the future by a storm from Paradise, watching helplessly as catastrophe piles up before his eyes. "What we call progress," Benjamin wrote, "is this storm."

Beyond these essays, Benjamin produced major critical studies of Kafka, Baudelaire, Goethe, and Proust. He translated sections of Baudelaire's Les Fleurs du mal and parts of Proust's In Search of Lost Time. He developed a theory of translation that remains influential among literary scholars. Each work added another facet to a body of thought that resists systematic summary—a feature Benjamin would have considered a virtue rather than a flaw.

Flight

By 1940, Benjamin had remained in Europe far too long. The Third Reich had conquered France. For a German Jew associated with the Marxist left, there was no longer any safe refuge on the continent.

Benjamin obtained transit visas that would allow him to travel through Spain to Portugal, where he could board a ship to America. On September 25, 1940, he joined a small group of refugees attempting to cross the Pyrenees on foot, evading the official border crossing at Port Bou where German agents might be waiting.

The crossing was brutal. Benjamin, forty-eight years old and in poor health, struggled up the mountain paths. When the group finally reached the Spanish border town of Port Bou, they learned that Spain had just that day closed its borders to refugees without French exit visas—documents Benjamin did not possess.

The Spanish authorities announced they would return the group to France the next morning. For Benjamin, this meant certain capture by the Nazis.

That night, Benjamin took his own life with an overdose of morphine tablets he had been carrying for just such an emergency.

The irony—if irony is even the right word for something so bitter—is that his death shocked the Spanish authorities into reversing their decision. Impressed and shaken by Benjamin's suicide, they allowed his traveling companions to continue into Spain. They eventually reached safety and survived the war.

Benjamin's body was buried in Port Bou. The location of his grave is uncertain.

Afterlife

For fifteen years after his death, Benjamin remained obscure. A few friends and colleagues remembered his work. Scholem preserved his papers and letters. But the wider world had never heard of him.

Then, in 1955, Suhrkamp Verlag began publishing Benjamin's collected works in Germany. A new generation of readers encountered his essays and fragments. But true global recognition came only in 1968, when Hannah Arendt edited a collection of Benjamin's writings translated into English and introduced him to readers in the Anglosphere.

Arendt and Benjamin had been connected by marriage—her first husband, Günther Anders, was Benjamin's cousin—but their friendship had developed in Paris after she separated from Anders. Both were students of the same philosophical tradition. Both were German Jews forced into exile. Both wrote with a density and difficulty that rewarded patient reading.

Arendt's introduction painted Benjamin as a tragic figure, a man out of time, an exemplar of the catastrophe that befell German-Jewish intellectuals under the Third Reich. This framing has shaped Benjamin's reception ever since. He appears in popular culture and left journalism as a symbol of what was lost—the brilliant minds destroyed, the works unfinished, the possibilities foreclosed.

But there's something unsatisfying about reducing Benjamin to a symbol of tragedy. His ideas—about art and technology, about history and memory, about language and translation—remain alive in ways that transcend his personal fate. Scholars still argue about what he meant. Artists still draw on his concepts. The questions he raised about mechanical reproduction feel more urgent now, in an age of artificial intelligence and digital manipulation, than they did in the age of photography and film.

Benjamin was a philosopher, or wasn't. He was a mystic, or a materialist, or somehow both. He was a failure in his lifetime and a success after his death. He knew everyone and belonged nowhere. He saw the catastrophe coming and couldn't escape it.

As Scholem observed, "The peculiar aura of authority emanating from his work tended to incite contradiction." Perhaps that's the most fitting epitaph for a thinker who distrusted easy answers: his work continues to provoke, to puzzle, to resist any final interpretation.
