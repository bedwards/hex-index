The Missile That Changed Modern Warfare

In January 1991, as the Gulf War began, television viewers around the world watched something unprecedented: cruise missiles threading through the streets of Baghdad, turning corners around buildings, and striking their targets with eerie precision. These were Tomahawk missiles, and they announced a new era of warfare—one where a ship hundreds of miles away could reach out and destroy a specific building with the accuracy of a surgeon's scalpel.

The Tomahawk isn't just a weapon. It's a flying robot that thinks for itself.

What Exactly Is a Cruise Missile?

Before diving into the Tomahawk specifically, it helps to understand what makes a cruise missile different from other missiles. Most missiles you've heard of—intercontinental ballistic missiles, surface-to-air missiles—follow a ballistic arc. They're essentially thrown at their target, like a very fast, very expensive rock. Once launched, gravity and momentum do most of the work.

A cruise missile is different. It flies like an airplane. It has wings, an engine, and sophisticated navigation systems that let it adjust its course throughout its entire flight. The Tomahawk cruises at about 550 miles per hour—roughly the speed of a commercial airliner—and can travel over a thousand miles to reach its target. It flies low, often just a few hundred feet off the ground, hugging terrain to avoid radar detection.

Think of the difference between throwing a paper airplane and flying a remote-controlled drone. The paper airplane goes where physics takes it. The drone goes where you tell it to go, adjusting constantly along the way.

Birth of the Tomahawk

The Tomahawk emerged from the Applied Physics Laboratory at Johns Hopkins University in the 1970s, developed under the direction of James H. Walker near Laurel, Maryland. The United States military wanted a medium- to long-range missile that could fly low enough to avoid radar while still hitting targets with precision. The solution was a modular design—meaning the basic missile could be fitted with different warheads and guidance systems depending on the mission.

General Dynamics built the first Tomahawks. Testing happened in some of the harshest conditions imaginable: between 1983 and 1993, twenty-three missiles were fired over northern Canada in a joint program with Canadian forces. Why Canada? The frozen landscapes and rugged terrain mimicked what missiles might encounter if they ever needed to strike the Soviet Union. The tests also helped the North American Aerospace Defense Command—better known as NORAD—figure out how to detect and intercept enemy cruise missiles if they ever came our way.

The manufacturing history reads like a corporate merger flowchart. General Dynamics was the sole supplier in the 1970s. McDonnell Douglas took over from 1992 to 1994. Then Hughes Aircraft bought General Dynamics' missile division and outbid McDonnell Douglas. Hughes and Raytheon ran a joint venture until Raytheon acquired Hughes in 1997. Today, Raytheon remains the sole manufacturer.

How Does a Flying Robot Find Its Target?

This is where the Tomahawk becomes genuinely fascinating. The missile doesn't just fly toward coordinates—it navigates through a combination of systems that would have seemed like science fiction a few decades ago.

The original guidance system, introduced in the early 1980s, was called the Digital Scene Matching Area Correlator, or DSMAC. It was essentially an early form of artificial intelligence running on computers with less processing power than today's digital watches. Here's how it worked: before launch, operators would program the missile with a series of images showing what the terrain along its flight path should look like from above. These images came from spy satellite photographs, processed by mainframe computers to estimate what the ground would appear like during low-level flight.

As the missile flew, onboard cameras would photograph the terrain below and compare what they saw to what they expected to see. The problem was that the computers were so limited they couldn't directly analyze the photographs. Instead, they would compute contrast maps—essentially simplified versions of the images—combine multiple maps into a buffer, and compare averages. It was a clever workaround for primitive technology.

Seasons change. Lighting changes. Snow covers the ground in winter but not summer. Shadows fall differently at noon versus dawn. DSMAC had to filter out these differences and find the similarities that remained constant. It was designed to ask: "Does this generally look like what I expected?" rather than "Is this an exact match?"

The missile even carried an extremely bright strobe light. At night, it could fire brief flashes to illuminate the ground below, photograph what it saw, and continue navigating. It knew that nighttime terrain would look different than daytime photographs and compensated accordingly.

Later versions added the Global Positioning System—GPS—the same satellite navigation technology now in every smartphone. Modern Tomahawks can navigate using GPS alone, DSMAC alone, or both together for maximum accuracy. GPS allows faster mission planning with some reduced precision. DSMAC takes longer to prepare but delivers better terminal accuracy. Combined, they're remarkably precise.

The Many Faces of Tomahawk

Because of its modular design, the Tomahawk has worn many hats over its fifty-year career. Some variants are now retired. Others continue evolving.

The nuclear version, designated TLAM-N, carried a W80 nuclear warhead. It was quietly retired between 2010 and 2013, though reports from 2018 suggested the Navy was considering bringing back some form of nuclear-armed cruise missile. The ground-launched nuclear version, called the Gryphon, was stationed at bases throughout Europe during the Cold War. These were withdrawn in 1991 to comply with the Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces Treaty—an agreement between the United States and Soviet Union to eliminate certain categories of nuclear weapons.

An anti-ship version existed briefly, using active radar to home in on enemy vessels. It was withdrawn in 1994 and the missiles were converted to land-attack variants. The Navy apparently wasn't satisfied with its ability to distinguish between friendly and enemy ships from long distances—a rather important capability for a weapon that can't be recalled once launched.

The conventional land-attack versions have gone through continuous refinement. Early models carried a 992-pound warhead filled with a high explosive mixture called Picratol. Starting in 1993, this was replaced by a lighter 690-pound warhead, which allowed engineers to enlarge the fuel tank and extend the missile's range. Some variants dispensed cluster munitions—166 small bomblets that would scatter across an area, each containing just over half a pound of high explosive.

One particularly clever variant carried a warhead designed specifically to disable electrical grids. First used in the Gulf War, these missiles would detonate above power stations and release clouds of carbon fiber filaments. These threads would drape across power lines and transformers, causing massive short circuits without destroying the underlying infrastructure. The idea was elegant: knock out an enemy's electricity without having to rebuild the power plants after the war.

The Missile That Loiters

The Block IV Tomahawk, introduced in 2006, added capabilities that transformed the weapon from a precision strike tool into something more flexible: a missile that could wait.

Traditional missiles are one-way, one-target weapons. You launch them at something, and they either hit it or they don't. The Block IV can loiter—fly in circles over a designated area, waiting for further instructions. Commanders can redirect it to any of fifteen preprogrammed alternate targets, or to entirely new coordinates transmitted via satellite. A missile launched at dawn could be given a completely different target by noon.

Even more remarkably, the missile can see. Equipped with electro-optical sensors, Block IV Tomahawks can photograph their targets moments before impact and transmit those images back to commanders via satellite link. This provides real-time battle damage assessment—did the missile hit what we aimed at? What does the destruction look like? If the first missile wasn't enough, should we send another?

The two-way satellite data link also lets the missile report its own health and status. Commanders know where each missile is, whether its systems are functioning properly, and whether it's tracking toward its target correctly.

Making the Missile Smarter

In 2010, engineers tested something called the Joint Multi-Effects Warhead System, or JMEWS. The goal was to give the Tomahawk bunker-busting capabilities—the ability to penetrate hardened structures like concrete bunkers before detonating. In the test, the warhead punched a hole large enough for its follow-through element to completely penetrate a concrete target. By 2014, this programmable warhead was being integrated into the Block IV, allowing a single missile design to work against both soft targets like buildings and hardened targets like command bunkers.

Around the same time, Raytheon began working on something arguably more transformative: giving the Tomahawk the ability to hit moving targets at sea. The original anti-ship version, retired years earlier, had relied on the Harpoon missile's seeker—technology that struggled to distinguish between targets at long range. The new approach uses passive radar detection, meaning the missile can pick up electromagnetic signals from a target's own radar systems and follow them home. It also has millimeter-wave active radar for final target discrimination—sending out its own signal in the moments before impact to confirm it's about to hit the right thing.

This maritime strike capability came fully online with the Block V version, which began deployment in 2021. Block Va is specifically designated for striking moving ships. Block Vb carries the bunker-busting JMEWS warhead. All older Block IV missiles are being upgraded to Block V standard, while the remaining Block III missiles are being retired and demilitarized.

The Network-Centric Battlefield

Modern Tomahawks are designed to work as part of a larger system. This concept, called network-centric warfare, means the missile can receive targeting data from multiple sources: aircraft, unmanned drones, satellites, ground forces, tanks, and ships. Any sensor that can detect a target can potentially feed information to a Tomahawk in flight.

The relationship works both ways. The missile isn't just receiving data—it can transmit what its own sensors detect back to these other platforms. A Tomahawk flying low over enemy territory becomes, in effect, a reconnaissance asset that happens to also be a weapon.

The Physics of Going Faster

One limitation of the Tomahawk has always been its speed. At 550 miles per hour, it's subsonic—slower than the speed of sound. This makes it easier to intercept than faster missiles. Engineers have considered developing a supersonic version using a ramjet engine that could push the missile to Mach 3, roughly 2,300 miles per hour.

The challenge is geometric. Every ship capable of launching Tomahawks uses the same size launch tubes: 21 inches in diameter and 20 feet long. Rather than modify the launch systems on every ship, submarine, and weapons platform, any new Tomahawk variant needs to fit within those existing dimensions. Cramming a ramjet engine into that space while still leaving room for fuel and a warhead is a significant engineering challenge.

Turning Leftover Fuel into a Weapon

In 2016, Los Alamos National Laboratory began working on a surprisingly elegant idea: using the missile's unburned fuel as an additional explosive. Tomahawks burn JP-10, a specialized jet fuel. When a missile reaches a nearby target, it arrives with fuel to spare—fuel that would otherwise just be extra weight at impact.

The concept turns this leftover fuel into a fuel-air explosive. At the moment of detonation, the JP-10 mixes with atmospheric oxygen and burns rapidly in a thermobaric explosion. This burning fuel acts as a secondary warhead. For short-range targets where the missile arrives with significant fuel remaining, this thermobaric effect can actually be more powerful than the conventional warhead itself.

In 2020, Los Alamos took this further, announcing plans to produce Tomahawk fuel from corn ethanol rather than petroleum. Beyond any agricultural policy considerations, the corn-based fuel is easier to manufacture—it doesn't require the harsh acids needed to produce petroleum-based JP-10.

How Do You Launch Something This Complex?

Each Tomahawk lives inside a pressurized canister that serves three purposes: it protects the missile during transport and storage, maintains the proper environment for the missile's delicate electronics, and functions as the launch tube when it's time to fire.

These canisters have been deployed on an impressive variety of platforms. The four reactivated Iowa-class battleships—USS Iowa, USS New Jersey, USS Missouri, and USS Wisconsin—carried them in Armored Box Launchers. Eight Spruance-class destroyers had them. So did the four Virginia-class cruisers and the nuclear cruiser USS Long Beach.

Most modern surface ships use vertical launching systems, where the canisters stand upright and missiles blast straight up before tilting toward their targets. Submarines have two options: capsule launch systems in newer Los Angeles-class and Virginia-class submarines, or launching directly from torpedo tubes in other configurations.

From Cold War to the Present

The Tomahawk has served in nearly every American military conflict since its introduction. It announced itself to the world in the Gulf War, then appeared in Yugoslavia, Afghanistan, Iraq, Libya, Syria, and Yemen. The United States Navy remains the primary operator, but the missile now also serves with the Royal Navy, the Royal Australian Navy, and the Royal Netherlands Navy.

What began as a Cold War weapon designed to strike Soviet targets has evolved into something far more versatile. The modern Tomahawk can hit moving ships at sea, penetrate hardened bunkers, loiter over battlefields awaiting targets, transmit reconnaissance imagery, and receive new mission orders mid-flight. It thinks for itself, adapts to changing conditions, and reports back to its commanders.

A missile that started life with computers barely capable of matching simple terrain photographs now carries artificial intelligence systems that would have seemed miraculous to its original designers. The basic concept—a jet-powered flying robot that navigates itself to distant targets—remains unchanged. Everything else has transformed beyond recognition.

The Tomahawk represents more than fifty years of continuous development, a weapon that has been reinvented and upgraded through every advance in computing, navigation, and communications technology. It's no longer just a cruise missile. It's a node in a global network of sensors and weapons, a robotic scout and striker that extends the reach of naval power across oceans and continents. And unlike many military programs that come and go, the Tomahawk shows no signs of retirement. Each new block brings new capabilities, and the assembly lines at Raytheon keep producing them.

Somewhere right now, in a canister aboard a destroyer or submarine, a Tomahawk waits. It might wait for years. But if the order comes, it will fly.
