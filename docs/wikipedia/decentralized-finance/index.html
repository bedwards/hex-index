Decentralized finance

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Decentralized finance

In September 2024, a major financial institution rebranded itself as 'Sky,' shedding the name MakerDAO that had become synonymous with the early days of crypto lending. Simultaneously, its stablecoin, DAI, was renamed USDS. This was not a corporate acquisition in the traditional sense, but a strategic pivot within a financial ecosystem that operates without a headquarters, a board of directors, or a central bank. As of March 2025, the combined circulating supply of these tokens stood at approximately $9 billion, a testament to a system where code has largely replaced the teller window. This is the world of Decentralized Finance, or DeFi, a paradigm shift that provides financial instruments and services through smart contracts on a programmable, permissionless blockchain. It is a sector where the need for intermediaries such as brokerages, exchanges, or banks is not just reduced; it is actively engineered out of existence.

To understand the magnitude of this shift, one must first understand the architecture that supports it. DeFi is not a monolithic application but a highly composable ecosystem built on a layered architecture. Think of it as a digital financial stack where each layer serves a well-defined purpose, much like the physical infrastructure of a city. At the foundation lies the Settlement layer, typically a blockchain like Ethereum, which records transactions and ensures consensus. Above this sits the Asset layer, where tokens representing value reside. The Protocol layer contains the smart contracts—the immutable rules of the game—and the Application layer is where users interact, often through a browser extension like MetaMask. This modularity allows for a phenomenon unique to this space: composability. Just as a developer can plug a camera module into a smartphone, a DeFi developer can plug a lending protocol into a trading protocol. These building blocks are open-source, allowing anyone to inspect, fork, or combine them to create new financial services.

The catalyst for this revolution was the popularization of smart contracts on the Ethereum blockchain in 2017. While Bitcoin had proven that a distributed ledger could store value, Ethereum proved that a distributed ledger could run programs. This capability birthed the first wave of DeFi applications. Before this, if you wanted to borrow money against your crypto assets, you had to go to a centralized company, undergo a credit check, and trust them with your collateral. With DeFi, the process is automated. In June 2020, Compound Finance, a protocol enabling users to lend or borrow cryptocurrency, introduced a mechanism that would fundamentally change how value flows in this ecosystem. It began rewarding lenders and borrowers with a cryptocurrency called Comp. This was not merely an incentive; it was a governance token, giving holders the right to vote on the protocol's future. Other platforms quickly followed suit, leading to a frenzy of activity known as 'yield farming' or 'liquidity mining.' Speculators began shifting cryptocurrency assets between pools and platforms, chasing the highest yields, which were a composite of interest, trading fees, and the value of newly minted reward tokens.

The scale of this growth was meteoric and volatile. By September 2020, Bloomberg reported that DeFi made up two-thirds of the cryptocurrency market in terms of price changes, with collateral levels reaching $9 billion. The momentum continued unabated until the total collateral levels across DeFi protocols peaked at a staggering $178 billion in November 2021. This represented a massive migration of capital from traditional finance into code-based protocols. However, the path was not linear. By 2023, amid broader downturns in the cryptocurrency market, these levels had declined to under $40 billion. Yet, the infrastructure remained. As of May 2025, the lending platform Aave alone held approximately 45% of the total value locked in DeFi, with around $25.4 billion in TVL. This resilience suggests that despite the volatility, the utility of these systems has taken root.

The core innovation that enables these services is the smart contract, a self-executing program that governs the terms of an agreement. Take the case of MakerDAO, established in 2017. It allowed users to borrow DAI, a stablecoin pegged to the US dollar, by depositing other cryptocurrencies as collateral. The smart contracts governed the entire lifecycle of the loan: the borrowing, the repayment, and the liquidation. If the value of the collateral dropped too low, the code automatically sold it to pay back the loan, maintaining the stability of DAI in a fully decentralized and autonomous manner. This eliminated the need for a bank to assess risk or manage the liquidation process. The protocol automatically adjusted interest rates based on the demand for the asset, a mechanism that would be incredibly difficult to replicate with human operators at the speed and scale of the blockchain.

Perhaps the most audacious application of this technology is the 'flash loan.' Popularized by the Aave Protocol, though originally invented by Max Wolff and released by Marble Protocol in 2018, flash loans allow users to borrow an arbitrary amount of cryptocurrency without any collateral, provided the loan is taken out and paid back within a single blockchain transaction. If the borrower cannot repay the loan by the end of the transaction, the entire transaction is reverted, as if it never happened. This sounds like a magic trick, but it is a feature of atomic transactions on a blockchain. These loans have been used for legitimate arbitrage, allowing traders to exploit price differences between exchanges. However, they have also been weaponized. Many exploits of DeFi platforms have utilized flash loans to manipulate cryptocurrency spot prices, temporarily inflating the value of an asset to trigger favorable outcomes in other smart contracts before draining the funds. The technology is neutral; the intent of the user determines the outcome.

Decentralized exchanges, or DEXs, represent another pillar of this ecosystem, offering an alternative to the centralized exchanges (CEXs) that have historically dominated crypto trading. In a traditional exchange, users must transfer their assets to the platform, which acts as a custodian. This centralization creates a single point of failure, making these platforms prime targets for hacking. DEXs, such as Uniswap, operate differently. They do not hold user funds. Instead, they use a mechanism known as an Automated Market Maker (AMM). Uniswap pays users to form liquidity pools, allowing traders to swap tokens directly against these pools in exchange for a percentage of the fees. Because no centralized party runs Uniswap and the platform is governed by its users, there is no entity to check the identities of the people using the platform. This lack of a central intermediary means the platform cannot meet Know Your Customer (KYC) or Anti-Money Laundering (AML) regulations in the traditional sense. As of 2020, the regulatory position on such platforms remained ambiguous, creating a gray zone where innovation raced ahead of legislation.

The architecture of these exchanges has also evolved. While basic DEXs operate on a single protocol, DEX aggregators have emerged with an additional 'Aggregator layer.' This layer allows them to connect and interact with multiple DEXs via smart contracts, finding the best possible price for a trade across the entire ecosystem. This further enhances the efficiency of the market, ensuring that liquidity is not fragmented but accessible through a unified interface. The distinction between CEXs, DEXs, and aggregators is becoming increasingly nuanced, yet the fundamental principle remains: the blockchain replaces the middleman.

Despite the allure of high interest rates and the promise of financial sovereignty, the DeFi ecosystem is fraught with risk. The very openness that allows for innovation also exposes the system to vulnerabilities. Coding errors and hacks are a common challenge. Unlike a traditional bank, where a security breach might be mitigated by insurance or government bailouts, a bug in a smart contract can lead to the irreversible loss of funds. The risk is not theoretical; it is a daily reality for participants. Furthermore, the degree of decentralization varies wildly across protocols. Truly decentralized protocols can act as neutral infrastructure, resistant to censorship and manipulation. However, false decentralization leaves protocols open to manipulation, fraud, or eventual regulation as financial intermediaries. If a small group of developers retains control over the upgrade keys of a 'decentralized' protocol, that protocol is effectively centralized, regardless of its branding.

The financialization of these risks has attracted significant attention from the investment world. DeFi has drawn venture capitalists such as Andreessen Horowitz and Michael Novogratz, who see the potential for a new financial layer of the internet. The Economist, in 2022, framed the future of digital finance as a 'three-way fight' between Big Tech giants like Facebook (now Meta) building their own digital wallets, 'big rich countries' testing Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs), and the software developers building applications to decentralize finance. This tripartite struggle highlights the strategic importance of DeFi. It is not merely a niche for crypto enthusiasts; it is a battleground for the future of how value is stored, transferred, and created.

Regulators, particularly in the United States, have faced a daunting challenge in handling the risks presented by crypto-assets valued at $2.5 trillion. The borderless and pseudonymous nature of DeFi complicates enforcement. In July 2020, The Washington Post described the techniques and risks involved, noting the potential for systemic issues. Yet, the growth has been undeniable. Ethereum saw a surge in developers during 2020, driven by the opportunities DeFi presented. The ecosystem has matured from a collection of experimental protocols to a complex financial machine. The rise of alternative Layer 1 protocols, such as Radix, which utilize sharded consensus mechanisms, attempts to resolve the scalability issues inherent in earlier blockchains like Ethereum, suggesting that the infrastructure is still evolving to meet the demands of global finance.

The user experience of DeFi has also become increasingly sophisticated. DApps are typically accessed through browser extensions or mobile applications that act as digital wallets. These wallets allow users to sign transactions and interact with the blockchain without needing to deposit funds with a third party. The integration of blockchain oracles allows these DApps to source external, off-chain data, such as the real-time price of an asset, ensuring that the smart contracts have the information needed to execute correctly. This connectivity bridges the gap between the digital and physical worlds, allowing the blockchain to react to real-world events.

As we look toward the future, the question is no longer whether DeFi is viable, but how it will coexist with traditional finance. The rebranding of MakerDAO to Sky in 2024 signaled a shift toward a more mature, perhaps more institutional identity, even while maintaining its decentralized roots. The sheer volume of capital locked in these protocols—tens of billions of dollars—indicates that a significant portion of the global financial system is now operating on a parallel track. The 'three-way fight' predicted by The Economist is already underway. Big Tech is integrating wallets; governments are exploring digital currencies; and developers are continuing to build a more open, transparent, and accessible financial system.

The promise of DeFi is a world where financial services are not a privilege granted by institutions, but a public utility provided by code. It is a world where a farmer in a developing nation can access the same lending rates as a hedge fund in New York, provided they have an internet connection. But it is also a world where the margin for error is zero, where a single line of bad code can wipe out fortunes, and where the regulatory framework is still being written. The journey from the first flash loan in 2018 to the $9 billion stablecoin ecosystem of 2025 has been a whirlwind of innovation, speculation, and risk. As the technology continues to mature, the distinction between 'traditional' finance and 'decentralized' finance may eventually blur, leaving behind only the question of whether the system is built on trust in people or trust in math.

The narrative of DeFi is still being written. The layers of the stack are being reinforced, the protocols are being audited, and the regulations are being debated. What remains clear is that the genie cannot be put back in the bottle. The smart contract has proven that money can be programmable, and that financial services can be automated. The challenges of security, scalability, and regulation are significant, but they are not insurmountable. As the ecosystem continues to evolve, the lessons learned from the early days of MakerDAO, Compound, and Uniswap will shape the financial infrastructure of the future. The question for the next decade is not if DeFi will survive, but how it will transform the very nature of money itself.

In the end, the story of DeFi is a story of trust. In the traditional system, we trust banks, governments, and intermediaries to act in our best interests. In the DeFi system, we trust the code. We trust that the smart contract will execute as written, that the consensus mechanism will remain secure, and that the open-source nature of the software will allow the community to identify and fix vulnerabilities. It is a bold experiment, one that challenges the fundamental assumptions of how we organize economic life. Whether this experiment succeeds or fails, it has already irrevocably changed the landscape of global finance. The future of finance is no longer just about moving money; it is about rewriting the rules of the game.
