Port of Primorsk

10 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Port of Primorsk

In September 2025, a cloud of smoke, stretching three hundred kilometers across the Baltic Sea, blotted out the sun over the Gulf of Finland. It was not a natural phenomenon, nor a result of industrial negligence, but the direct consequence of a targeted strike by Ukrainian forces using exploding drones against the Port of Primorsk. The fires that erupted that day were long-lasting, choking the air and halting the flow of a commodity that has dictated the geopolitical fortunes of the region for decades. Just six months later, in March 2026, the attack was repeated, causing significant fresh damage and further delaying oil shipments. The number of casualties remains unknown, lost in the haze of a conflict that has turned a quiet industrial outpost into a frontline of energy warfare.

To understand why this port is worth burning, one must look far back before the drones, before the fires, and even before the collapse of the Soviet Union. Primorsk is not merely a dock; it is the end point of the Baltic Pipeline System and the largest Russian oil-loading port in the entire Baltic Sea. Located on the Björkösund mainland of the Gulf of Finland, five kilometers southeast of the town of Primorsk, it sits at the strategic choke point where Russian oil meets the open ocean. But for most of the 20th century, this specific patch of coastline was not the primary artery of Russian trade. In the 1980s, the Soviet Union's foreign trade shipments on the Baltic Sea flowed predominantly through the Baltic States. Russia's own ports—Kaliningrad, the Great Port of Saint Petersburg, Vyborg, and Vysotsk—accounted for a mere 25% of the total cargo turnover.

The map changed in 1991. The USSR collapsed, and the Russian Federation emerged on the former Soviet territory alongside a constellation of new, independent nations. The geopolitical reality of the Baltic Sea was instantly rewritten. Four out of the nine major Soviet Baltic ports were now situated on Russian soil, but this was a Pyrrhic victory of geography. The remaining ports, which had handled the bulk of the trade, were now in Latvia, Lithuania, and Estonia. Worse still, the Kaliningrad port found itself in a peculiar limbo, lacking direct overland transport with the Russian mainland, effectively turning it into an exclave dependent on foreign transit.

The new Russian state faced an immediate crisis of sovereignty and logistics. The capacity of the ports that remained within its borders was woefully insufficient to handle the volume of trade the Soviet economy had generated. More critically, Russia was forced to pay foreign governments for the use of their harbors and the right to transfer cargoes across their territory. This was an unacceptable vulnerability for a nation that viewed energy exports as its primary lever of global power. The need to minimize dependency on foreign harbors became a matter of national security.

In 1993, the government made a definitive decision to break this stranglehold. They initiated a plan to build three new ports in Leningrad Oblast. The strategic objective was clear: one of these new facilities would serve as a direct, sovereign alternative to Ventspils in Latvia, which had been the Soviet Union's biggest oil-loading port. The goal was to create an infrastructure that could handle the massive tankers of the global market without asking permission from a neighbor.

The construction of the Primorsk oil-loading port began in earnest in 2000. The timeline was aggressive, driven by the urgent need for energy independence. By December 2001, the first row of docks, comprising two berths, was operational. It was a start, but far from the finish line. The second and third rows of docks came online in 2004 and 2006, respectively. With each phase, the port's capabilities expanded exponentially, with the number of docks for tankers growing by four times. A dedicated terminal for transferring light petroleum products was also opened, diversifying the cargo mix beyond crude oil.

The physical scale of the operation is staggering. The port's land area covers 2.5 square kilometers, while its water area spans 32.3 square kilometers. This is not a shallow harbor; it is engineered to handle the giants of the maritime world. The docks are equipped to service tankers with a deadweight of up to 150,000 tons. These vessels are massive, measuring 307 meters in length and 55 meters in width, with a submersion depth of 15.5 meters. These are vessels capable of crossing oceans, yet they are pushing the absolute limits of what is physically possible to navigate into the Baltic Sea.

The geography of the Baltic Sea imposes strict constraints on maritime traffic. The Danish straits, the gateway between the Baltic and the North Sea, have limited depths. This natural barrier prevents true supertankers—the massive VLCCs (Very Large Crude Carriers) that dominate global trade—from entering the Baltic. Consequently, the ships that dock at Primorsk are the largest vessels that can physically make the journey. They are the ceiling of the Baltic's capacity, operating right at the maximum submersion limit. To handle these leviathans, the port relies on a specialized fleet of six azimuth tugboats. Three of these are Project 16609 vessels, each generating 2.6MW of power and delivering a bollard pull of 40 tons-force. Two are the more powerful Project 21110 models, pushing 4MW and 65 tons-force. A single Project 1233 tugboat rounds out the fleet with 3.7MW and 64 tons-force of pulling power. These machines are the muscle required to guide the 150,000-ton tankers into their berths with precision.

Once the ships are docked, the infrastructure for storage and transfer must be equally robust. The site features 18 storage tanks for petroleum, each with a capacity of 5,000 tons. In addition, there are specialized tanks for light petroleum products and a couple of emergency discharge tanks designed to handle spills or overflows. The total holding capacity of the facility is immense: 921,000 tons for petroleum and 240,000 tons for light petroleum products. This storage buffer allows the port to function as a critical node in the global supply chain, smoothing out the fluctuations between pipeline supply and maritime demand.

The port's operational history, prior to the recent conflicts, was one of steady growth and strategic importance. By March 2012, the throughput figures were measured in millions of tons, cementing Primorsk's role as the backbone of Russia's western energy exports. It was a monument to the post-Soviet ambition to reclaim control over the Baltic trade routes. But the geopolitical landscape is never static. The very independence that the port was built to secure has now become the source of its greatest vulnerability.

The strikes in September 2025 and March 2026 marked a terrifying new chapter in the history of the Baltic Sea. The Ukrainian army's decision to target Primorsk with exploding drones was a calculated move to strike at the heart of Russian energy logistics. The first strike, in September 2025, caused immediate damages and forced a halt to oil shipments for two days. It was a warning shot, a demonstration that the port was not impregnable. But the second strike, in March 2026, was far more devastating. The damage was significant, the fires long-lasting, and the environmental impact catastrophic. The 300-kilometer cloud of smoke was a visible scar across the region, a testament to the sheer volume of fuel being consumed by the blaze.

The consequences of these attacks extended far beyond the physical destruction of the docks and storage tanks. The cancellations and delays in oil shipments rippled through the global market, disrupting supply chains and altering the flow of energy to Europe and beyond. The port, designed to ensure Russia's energy independence, had become a focal point of international conflict. The uncertainty surrounding the number of casualties adds a human tragedy to the industrial devastation, a reminder that in modern warfare, energy infrastructure is as much a target as any military installation.

The story of Primorsk is a microcosm of the broader struggle for control over the Baltic region. It began as a Soviet project, evolved into a post-Soviet necessity, and has now become a battleground in a war that has reshaped the map of Europe. The port's ability to handle the largest tankers in the Baltic, its massive storage capacity, and its strategic location were once seen as guarantees of stability. Now, they are liabilities in a war of attrition. The fires that burned in 2025 and 2026 did not just destroy oil; they destroyed the illusion that economic infrastructure can remain neutral in a time of total war.

As of the current date in April 2026, the port is still reeling from the March attack. The smoke has not fully cleared, and the operations have not returned to full capacity. The world watches to see if Primorsk can be rebuilt, or if it will remain a smoldering symbol of a failed strategy. The Russian government's decision in 1993 to build this port was driven by the fear of foreign dependency. Today, that fear has been realized in a different form: the dependency on a single point of failure that can be struck from hundreds of kilometers away by a drone.

The physical details of the port—the 18-meter depth, the 921,000 tons of storage, the 150,000-ton tankers—remain the same. But the context has shifted irrevocably. What was once a symbol of Russian industrial might is now a testament to the fragility of supply chains in an age of asymmetric warfare. The Baltic Pipeline System, once the lifeblood of the region's economy, now faces a future defined by uncertainty. The port of Primorsk stands on the edge of the Gulf of Finland, a place where the tides of history have washed over concrete and steel, leaving behind a legacy that is as much about destruction as it is about construction.

The events of 2025 and 2026 will be studied for decades. They serve as a stark reminder that in the 21st century, the front lines are not always defined by trenches and barbed wire. Sometimes, they are defined by the flow of oil, the depth of the water, and the reach of a drone. The Port of Primorsk, with its massive tanks and deep-water docks, has become a stage for one of the most dramatic acts of the ongoing conflict. The smoke that rose from its shores in March 2026 was a signal to the world: the Baltic Sea is no longer a safe harbor for commerce. It is a war zone, and the stakes have never been higher.

The journey of Primorsk from a neglected coastline in the 1990s to a global energy hub in the 2010s, and finally to a target in the 2020s, is a narrative of ambition, engineering, and ultimately, conflict. It is a story that began with the need to break free from foreign ports and ended with the realization that no port is truly safe when the world is at war. The fires may eventually burn out, and the smoke may clear, but the impact of these strikes on the global energy market and the geopolitical balance of power will be felt for a long time. The Port of Primorsk is no longer just a port; it is a monument to the volatility of the modern world.
