Lanchester's laws

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Lanchester's laws

The Mathematics of Who Wins Wars

In 1916, an English engineer named Frederick Lanchester sat down to answer a deceptively simple question: if you know how many soldiers each side has and how effective their weapons are, can you calculate who will win a battle?

The answer was yes. And the math was surprisingly elegant.

What Lanchester discovered would become one of the most influential ideas in military theory, still forming the backbone of combat simulations used by the United States Army more than a century later. His equations revealed something counterintuitive about modern warfare: doubling your forces doesn't just double your advantage. It quadruples it.

This insight has implications far beyond the battlefield. Researchers have used Lanchester's laws to study everything from gang warfare to ant colonies. The equations help explain why concentration of force matters so much in combat, why smaller armies sometimes adopt guerrilla tactics, and why the shift from swords to guns fundamentally changed the mathematics of war.

Two Ways to Fight: The Linear Law

Lanchester actually developed two different laws, one for ancient combat and one for modern warfare. Understanding the difference between them reveals something profound about how technology changes the nature of conflict.

Imagine two ancient armies facing each other, soldiers armed with spears and shields arranged in tight formations called phalanxes. In this kind of combat, each soldier can only fight one enemy at a time. You're locked in a duel with the person directly in front of you. You can't reach past them to attack someone else, and their comrades can't reach past them to attack you.

In this scenario, the math is simple. If each soldier kills exactly one enemy before dying, then the battle is essentially a series of one-on-one duels happening simultaneously. The army with more soldiers will win, and the number of survivors will be the difference between the two armies.

Say the Red army has 1,000 soldiers and the Blue army has 800. After the battle, Red will have roughly 200 soldiers left standing. This is Lanchester's linear law: advantage scales directly with numbers.

The linear law also applies in a less obvious situation: when soldiers fire into an area without aiming at specific targets. Think of archers lobbing arrows over a wall into an enemy encampment. The rate at which you hit enemies depends on how densely they're packed into the target area. If both sides are shooting randomly into areas of equal size with equal weapons, they'll inflict roughly equal casualties until one side runs out of soldiers.

The Revolution of Aimed Fire: The Square Law

Everything changes when soldiers can aim.

With firearms, a soldier can shoot at any visible enemy, not just the one directly in front of them. They can receive fire from multiple directions simultaneously. The battlefield becomes a web of potential engagements rather than a series of isolated duels.

This transforms the mathematics completely.

Consider a situation where 1,000 Red soldiers face 500 Blue soldiers, all armed with identical rifles. In ancient combat under the linear law, Red would win and have 500 survivors. But under modern conditions?

Each Red soldier can fire at any of the 500 Blue soldiers. That means Blue is receiving fire from 1,000 rifles. Meanwhile, each Blue soldier can fire at any of the 1,000 Red soldiers. But there are only 500 Blue soldiers shooting.

Blue is absorbing twice as much firepower while dishing out half as much. The situation is far worse for them than a simple two-to-one disadvantage would suggest.

Lanchester worked out the precise relationship: combat power scales with the square of the number of soldiers, not the number itself. An army twice as large isn't twice as powerful. It's four times as powerful. An army three times as large is nine times as powerful.

This is why military strategists obsess over concentration of force. Dividing your army in half doesn't just halve your strength at each location. It quarters it.

What the Square Law Tells Us

The square law leads to several conclusions, some obvious and some surprising.

If both sides have equal firepower per soldier and equal numbers, the battle could go either way. Tie game.

If both sides have equal numbers but unequal firepower, the side with better weapons wins. Also fairly obvious.

If one side has more soldiers and better weapons, they win. Obvious to the point of tautology.

But here's where it gets interesting: what if one side has more soldiers but the other side has better weapons?

The math reveals that numbers matter more than firepower, dramatically so. To overcome a two-to-one disadvantage in numbers, you need a four-to-one advantage in firepower. To overcome a three-to-one disadvantage in numbers, you need nine times the killing power per soldier.

This explains why quantity has a quality all its own, as the saying goes. Superior technology can only compensate for so much numerical inferiority before the math becomes insurmountable.

The Limits of the Model

Like all models, Lanchester's laws are simplifications of reality. They work well in specific circumstances but fail when their assumptions break down.

The square law assumes continuous combat where casualties accumulate gradually over time. It models gunfire as a steady stream, like water from a hose, gradually wearing down the enemy. Each bullet has a small chance of hitting its target and does limited damage.

This breaks down in several situations.

Machine guns and artillery don't fit the model because a single weapon can inflict many casualties. The equations assume each soldier can only kill one enemy at a time, but a well-placed artillery shell might kill a dozen.

Nuclear weapons obviously shatter all the assumptions. One bomb, one city. The mathematics of attrition warfare don't apply when single weapons can end wars.

The model also assumes all troops are actively engaged in combat. Real armies don't work that way. Tactical deployment means reserves, flanking maneuvers, and troops held back from the fighting. A general who commits all forces simultaneously is usually making a mistake, even if the math suggests more soldiers equals more firepower.

And the equations don't capture situations where combat is instantaneous rather than gradual. If two snipers spot each other simultaneously and one gets their shot off first, the engagement is over before any continuous differential equation could describe it.

From Bullets to Missiles: The Salvo Model

Modern naval warfare introduced a new problem: cruise missiles.

Unlike bullets, missiles are fired in small numbers. Each one has a high probability of hitting its target and carries enough explosive power to cripple or sink a ship. The continuous-fire assumption of Lanchester's equations doesn't fit.

Military analysts developed what they call the salvo combat model to address this. Instead of modeling firepower as a continuous stream, salvo equations treat it as discrete pulses. A ship fires a salvo of missiles, then waits to assess damage before firing again.

The salvo model also introduced something Lanchester's equations ignore: defense. You can't intercept bullets in meaningful numbers, so Lanchester didn't bother modeling defensive capability. But missiles can be shot down by surface-to-air missiles and close-in weapons systems. Modern naval combat is as much about defense as offense.

This creates interesting dynamics. A ship's combat effectiveness depends on both how many missiles it can launch and how many incoming missiles it can intercept. Two ships might have identical offensive capability but very different defensive systems, leading to asymmetric outcomes the original Lanchester equations couldn't predict.

Testing the Theory Against History

Researchers have applied Lanchester's laws to historical battles to see how well the mathematics match reality.

Pickett's Charge at the Battle of Gettysburg in 1863 provides a classic case study. Confederate infantry marched across open ground toward entrenched Union positions, enduring constant rifle and artillery fire. The situation closely matches Lanchester's assumptions: aimed fire, continuous engagement, soldiers unable to kill more than one enemy at a time. The casualty figures roughly align with what the square law predicts.

The Battle of Britain in 1940 offers another test case. British and German aircraft engaged in sustained combat over months, with daily sorties and gradual attrition. Again, the mathematics provide reasonable approximations of actual outcomes.

The Battle of Kursk in 1943, the largest tank battle in history, has also been analyzed through the Lanchester lens. Thousands of tanks on each side engaged in a grinding battle of attrition, the kind of warfare the equations were designed to model.

When Neither Law Fits: The Exponent of 1.5

Real battles rarely fit neatly into either the linear law or the square law. Ancient combat wasn't perfectly linear because commanders could concentrate forces at weak points. Modern combat isn't perfectly square because not all troops are engaged simultaneously.

Military analysts have found that an exponent of 1.5 often works better than either 1 (linear) or 2 (square) for modeling modern warfare. This represents a blend: some aspects of combat follow linear dynamics while others follow square dynamics.

Guerrilla warfare is a particularly interesting case. Insurgents deliberately avoid the kind of force-on-force engagements where the square law would crush them. They use hit-and-run tactics, ambushes, and asymmetric attacks specifically designed to nullify the mathematical advantage of larger conventional forces.

The mathematics explain why occupying powers so often struggle against insurgencies despite massive numerical superiority. If guerrillas can force combat into situations closer to the linear law, they neutralize the square-law advantage that should guarantee their defeat.

Lanchester's Laws in the Animal Kingdom

Perhaps surprisingly, researchers have applied Lanchester's laws to conflicts between animals.

Chimpanzee warfare follows patterns remarkably similar to human combat. Groups of male chimpanzees conduct lethal raids on neighboring territories, with outcomes that roughly match Lanchester's predictions. The chimps even seem to instinctively understand the mathematics: they only attack when they have overwhelming numerical superiority, typically three-to-one or better.

Ant warfare provides another testing ground. Australian meat ants and Argentine ants engage in battles that follow the square law reasonably well. The larger colony wins, and the relationship between numbers and victory follows the expected quadratic pattern.

Fire ants, however, don't fit the model. Their combat dynamics differ enough from the Lanchester assumptions that the equations fail to predict outcomes. Not all animal conflict follows the same rules.

Measuring Battles: The Helmbold Parameters

Military historians face a challenge: how do you compare battles that occurred in different times and places with different technologies and tactics? Saying one battle was "bigger" or "more decisive" than another requires some quantitative framework.

The Helmbold Parameters provide such a framework. Named after the analyst who developed them, these indices offer precise numerical measures for comparing battles along two dimensions: bitterness and advantage.

Bitterness captures how costly the battle was relative to the forces engaged. A battle where both sides suffer heavy casualties is more bitter than one where the victor wins decisively with light losses.

Advantage measures how much one side dominated the other. A battle that was nearly a draw has low advantage; a one-sided victory has high advantage.

Importantly, while the mathematical form of the Helmbold Parameters comes from Lanchester's equations, their actual values depend only on the initial and final strengths of the opposing forces. You don't need to know anything about the combat dynamics during the battle itself. You just need to know how many troops each side started with and how many survived.

This makes the parameters practical tools for historical analysis, where detailed information about combat dynamics may be lost to time.

A Tale of Two Hypothetical Battles

Consider two imaginary battles to see how the Helmbold Parameters work in practice.

In the Major Battle, 220,000 attackers face 195,000 defenders. The attackers lose 3,025 soldiers while the defenders lose 2,650. Heavy forces, light casualties.

In the Minor Battle, 12,000 attackers face 15,000 defenders. The attackers lose 525 soldiers while the defenders lose 360. Much smaller forces, but proportionally higher casualties.

Which battle was more significant? The raw numbers favor the Major Battle, but the Helmbold analysis reveals something different.

The Major Battle has a bitterness parameter of about 0.014 and an advantage parameter of about 1.006. Translation: it was a gentle affair, practically a standoff. Neither side pressed hard enough to take serious casualties, and neither gained meaningful advantage.

The Minor Battle has a bitterness parameter of about 0.024 and an advantage parameter of about 1.35. This was a real fight. The defenders won decisively.

Numbers alone can deceive. A huge battle with light casualties may matter less than a smaller engagement where one side was clearly beaten.

Still Relevant After a Century

In 2016, the RAND Corporation used Lanchester's laws to analyze what might happen if Russia invaded the Baltic states of Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania. A century after Lanchester scribbled his differential equations, military planners still turn to his mathematics when they need to think seriously about force ratios and combat outcomes.

The United States Army's combat simulations continue to use modified versions of Lanchester's equations as their analytical foundation. When war games pit Red Team against Blue Team in hypothetical conflicts, the underlying calculations often trace their lineage back to that English engineer pondering the mathematics of World War One.

The laws have their limitations. They're better at describing attrition warfare than maneuver warfare, better at modeling conventional combat than counterinsurgency, better at handling gradual engagements than decisive moments. No equation captures the fog of war, the breakdown of morale, the flash of tactical genius, or the catastrophic failures of command.

But within their domain, Lanchester's laws reveal something true about the nature of modern warfare. When you can shoot at any enemy and receive fire from any direction, numbers matter in a particularly powerful way. The mathematics don't just add up. They multiply.

And that insight, however simple it sounds, has shaped military thinking ever since.
