Vision Zero

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Vision Zero

Here's a number that should stop you cold: the human body can survive being hit by a car traveling at about 30 kilometers per hour. That's roughly 19 miles per hour—about the speed of a casual bicycle ride. Go much faster than that, and the physics become unforgiving. Bones break. Organs rupture. People die.

This grim arithmetic sits at the heart of Vision Zero, a road safety philosophy that emerged from Sweden in the 1990s and has since spread to cities across the globe. Its central premise is startling in its simplicity: no one should die or be seriously injured while using the road system. Not some people. Not fewer people. Zero.

The Ethical Revolution

Most transportation systems operate on a quiet bargain. Engineers calculate acceptable death rates. Economists assign monetary values to human lives—around $10 million per statistical life in the United States, less in poorer countries. These figures get plugged into cost-benefit analyses that determine how much to spend on guardrails, how wide to make lanes, whether to install a traffic light at a dangerous intersection.

Vision Zero rejects this bargain entirely.

The Swedish Parliament approved the framework in October 1997, codifying what its architects called an "underlying ethical principle": it can never be acceptable that people are killed or seriously injured while moving within the road transport system. Not as an aspirational goal. As an ethical absolute. Life and health cannot be exchanged for other benefits within society—not for faster commutes, not for cheaper infrastructure, not for anything.

This might sound like obvious moralizing, the kind of thing any politician would nod along to before approving another highway expansion. But Vision Zero's designers meant it literally, and the implications ripple outward in ways that transform how we think about roads.

Who's Responsible When Things Go Wrong?

In most transportation systems, the answer is straightforward: the road user. If a pedestrian gets hit crossing the street, we ask why they weren't paying attention. If a cyclist dies in traffic, we wonder whether they were wearing a helmet. If a driver plows into a tree, we check whether they were speeding or drinking.

Vision Zero inverts this assumption. Yes, people make mistakes—they always have and always will. They get distracted. They misjudge distances. They take chances. Any safety system that requires perfect human behavior is a system designed to fail.

The responsibility, therefore, shifts to the system designers. If a road allows speeds that turn human error into human death, the road is poorly designed. If an intersection creates conflicts between cars and pedestrians, the intersection is the problem. The question isn't "why did that person make a mistake?" but "why did our system allow that mistake to be fatal?"

This is a profound reorientation. It's the difference between designing airplanes that are safe when pilots do everything perfectly versus airplanes that remain safe when pilots inevitably don't.

The Speed Equation

Vision Zero's designers worked backward from human fragility. If the body can withstand a 30 kilometer per hour collision with a car, then anywhere pedestrians might cross the street needs to be designed for that speed—maximum. Not suggested. Not encouraged. Designed.

This creates a hierarchy of road types, each with its own speed ceiling based on what kinds of collisions are possible.

At the top sit motorways—what Americans call freeways and Germans call Autobahns. These roads eliminate the most dangerous collision types entirely. Crash barriers separate opposing traffic, preventing head-on crashes. Limited access points and grade-separated interchanges prevent side impacts. Slower and more vulnerable road users—pedestrians, cyclists, mopeds—are prohibited entirely. With these risks designed out, speeds above 100 kilometers per hour become tolerable.

Below these come roads with physical barriers dividing opposing traffic but without full grade separation. Sweden developed "2+1 roads," an elegant solution where three lanes alternate direction: two lanes going one way, then one lane going the other, then back again. Barriers prevent head-on collisions while allowing reasonable throughput. These roads can safely accommodate speeds around 90 kilometers per hour.

Urban streets sit at the bottom of the hierarchy. Here, the fundamental conflict between fast-moving vehicles and vulnerable humans cannot be engineered away. People need to cross streets. Cyclists need to share space. Children need to walk to school.

The only solution is slower speeds.

The German Paradox

Germany offers an instructive comparison. In 2010, German rural roads—typically limited to 100 kilometers per hour—killed 7.7 people per billion kilometers traveled. Urban streets, with their 50 kilometer per hour limits, killed 5.2. But the famous Autobahn, much of it without any speed limit at all? Just 2.0 deaths per billion kilometers.

The Autobahn carried nearly a third of Germany's motorized road traffic while accounting for only 11 percent of traffic deaths. The safest roads weren't the slowest ones—they were the ones designed to eliminate the possibility of the deadliest crash types.

This isn't an argument against speed limits. It's an argument that speed limits alone aren't enough. A rural road with a 100 kilometer per hour limit and an Autobahn with no limit at all operate under completely different risk profiles. One allows head-on collisions between drivers traveling in opposite directions. The other makes them physically impossible.

The lesson: separation matters more than signage.

The Dutch Variation

The Netherlands, famous for its cycling culture and dense urban areas, developed a parallel approach they call "sustainable safety." Where Vision Zero emphasizes the ethical imperative against death, the Dutch framework starts from a different premise: humans will always make mistakes, so roads should be designed to be "self-explaining."

A self-explaining road makes its intended use obvious. The design tells you how to behave without requiring signs or enforcement. Wide lanes suggest higher speeds. Narrow lanes suggest slower ones. Roundabouts force everyone to slow down and yield. Separate paths for different users prevent conflicts between mismatched masses and speeds.

The Dutch approach prevents dangerous differences from mixing. On a typical 50 kilometer per hour road, roundabouts create crossing points slow enough—30 kilometers per hour—for pedestrians and cyclists to navigate safely. Above 30 kilometers per hour in built-up areas, mopeds and bicycles get their own dedicated paths, separated from car traffic entirely. Buses often get dedicated lanes too, preventing their substantial mass from conflicting with smaller vehicles.

More recently, the Dutch added another principle: roads should be "forgiving." When crashes happen—and they will—the road environment should minimize the damage. Flexible barriers instead of rigid concrete. Breakaway poles instead of fixed obstacles. Cushioned edges instead of hard curbs.

These principles now inform both Dutch and Swedish approaches, converging on a shared recognition: you cannot rely on human perfection, so you design for human fallibility.

Twenty Is Plenty

In the early 2000s, a movement emerged in the United Kingdom pushing for 20 mile per hour speed limits in residential areas. "Twenty's Plenty for Us," the campaigners called it. At that speed—just over 30 kilometers per hour—a pedestrian hit by a car has roughly a 90 percent chance of surviving. At 30 miles per hour, that number drops to about 50 percent. At 40, it plummets below 20.

The campaign spread to the United States by 2010. New York City adopted it. San Francisco did too. London eventually imposed 20 mile per hour limits across its central roads following a public consultation in 2019.

Critics argued that lower speed limits would increase congestion, frustrate drivers, and make little practical difference since few actually obeyed them anyway. Supporters countered that even modest speed reductions changed outcomes dramatically in the crashes that did occur, and that design changes—narrower lanes, raised crosswalks, speed bumps—could enforce compliance better than signs.

The American Experiment

American cities began adopting Vision Zero in earnest around 2014. New York City led the way when Mayor Bill de Blasio announced the city's plan in January of that year, complete with a long list of initiatives targeting the most dangerous streets and intersections.

San Francisco followed the same month. Los Angeles announced its plan in September. Austin, Portland, Seattle, San Jose, San Diego, Boston, and Washington, D.C. all signed on within the next two years. By 2016, Vision Zero had become something approaching a movement.

The results have been mixed.

In New York, traffic deaths decreased in the first four years of implementation—even as crashes and injuries actually increased. More people were getting hurt, but fewer were dying. This might reflect success: the same number of mistakes happening at lower speeds, converting fatalities into survivable injuries. Or it might reflect something else entirely: more cars, more conflicts, more danger, partially offset by safety improvements.

San Francisco set an ambitious goal of eliminating all traffic deaths by 2024. As that deadline approached, the city remained far from zero—though deaths had fallen from their pre-Vision Zero peaks. The target, critics argued, was always aspirational rather than achievable. Supporters countered that the point was never really to hit zero by an arbitrary date, but to orient every decision around the goal of doing so eventually.

What Actually Works

The evidence points toward several interventions that consistently reduce deaths.

Physical separation works. Barriers between opposing traffic eliminate head-on collisions. Protected bike lanes eliminate cyclist-vehicle conflicts. Pedestrian refuges give people crossing wide streets a safe place to wait midway.

Lower speeds work—but only when enforced. Signs suggesting slower speeds accomplish little. Automated enforcement—speed cameras, red light cameras—changes behavior. So does physical design: narrower lanes, raised crosswalks, chicanes that force drivers to slow down.

Fixing dangerous intersections works. Most traffic deaths cluster at a small number of locations. Redesigning these hotspots—adding signals, improving sight lines, separating conflicting movements—yields outsized returns.

What doesn't work reliably is education. Campaigns urging people to drive safely, look both ways, and share the road generate awareness without generating behavioral change. People already know they should be careful. Knowing isn't the problem.

The Political Pendulum

In New Zealand, the government released its "Road to Zero" strategy in 2019, modeled directly on Vision Zero. Speed limits dropped. Road safety features multiplied. By early 2022, the transportation authority had launched public awareness campaigns and announced reviews of speed limits around schools.

Then the pendulum swung.

A new government took power in 2024 and promptly announced it would reverse the previous administration's speed limit reductions. The new policy raised limits by 20 kilometers per hour in many areas, introduced variable speed limits around schools rather than permanent reductions, and—crucially—required that speed limit decisions weigh economic criteria alongside safety ones.

This last change strikes at Vision Zero's philosophical core. The whole point of the framework was to declare that safety could not be traded off against economic efficiency. Now it could.

The reversal illustrates a persistent tension. Vision Zero asks society to accept constraints—slower speeds, more expensive infrastructure, designs that prioritize safety over convenience—in exchange for preventing deaths that remain statistically rare. Each individual weighs the odds. Each politician calculates the votes. Most people won't die on the roads this year, or next year, or ever. The abstract promise of zero feels less compelling than the immediate frustration of a lower speed limit on your daily commute.

Sweden's Record

What about Sweden itself, where all this began?

Swedish road deaths were already declining before 1997 and continued falling after Vision Zero's adoption. The country built new roads to Vision Zero standards and retrofitted older ones. It implemented speed cameras and lowered urban limits. It targeted the most dangerous roads and intersections.

But the trajectory flattened around 2013. Deaths stopped decreasing. The zero that gave the vision its name remained stubbornly out of reach.

This plateau sparked debate. Critics argued it showed Vision Zero's limits—that the easy gains had been captured and further progress would require increasingly expensive interventions yielding diminishing returns. Supporters countered that the plateau reflected broader trends, including the rise of distracted driving from smartphones, and that without Vision Zero's framework, deaths would have risen rather than held steady.

The honest answer may be that nobody knows. Road safety involves too many variables—economic conditions, vehicle design, driver demographics, weather patterns, enforcement intensity—to isolate the effect of any single policy framework. Vision Zero countries have made progress. So have some non-Vision Zero countries. Untangling causation from correlation remains difficult.

The Question Beneath the Question

Ultimately, Vision Zero asks a question that goes beyond traffic engineering: what obligations do we have to design systems that protect people from the consequences of their own mistakes?

We accept this obligation in other domains. Pharmaceutical companies must childproof their bottles even though responsible parents should keep medicine away from children. Building codes require sprinkler systems even though responsible people shouldn't start fires. Aviation regulators demand multiple redundancies even though pilots should fly correctly.

We call these requirements when they protect against dramatic, concentrated risks—a plane crash, a building fire—but balk at them for diffuse, routine ones. Car crashes kill more people than plane crashes. Road fatalities dwarf building fires. But they happen one at a time, scattered across thousands of streets, each one seemingly the fault of someone who should have been more careful.

Vision Zero insists this is an accounting error. The individual mistakes are individual. The system that allows those mistakes to be fatal is collective. We built the roads. We set the speed limits. We chose not to install the barriers or the cameras or the separated paths. Every death is a choice—not by the victim, but by the society that designed the space where they died.

Whether you find this argument compelling probably depends less on your views about traffic engineering than on your views about the relationship between individual and collective responsibility. Vision Zero isn't really a transportation policy. It's an ethical stance that happens to have implications for how we build roads.

The stance is this: some mistakes shouldn't be fatal. And when they are, we should blame the design, not the human.

Where We Are Now

Vision Zero has spread from one country's parliamentary resolution to a global movement spanning dozens of cities and multiple continents. Edmonton became the first Canadian city to officially adopt it in 2015. Toronto followed a year later. Haryana became the first Indian state to embrace the framework in 2017. London, long one of the world's most traffic-dense cities, now operates its central roads at 20 miles per hour.

But adoption varies wildly in meaning. Some cities treat Vision Zero as a binding operational framework, redesigning streets and accepting slower traffic as the price of safety. Others treat it more as an aspiration—a slogan to invoke while continuing business as usual. The gap between Vision Zero as implemented and Vision Zero as practiced can be enormous.

Meanwhile, the forces pushing in the other direction remain powerful. Drivers want to move quickly. Businesses want their goods delivered efficiently. Politicians want to avoid the backlash that comes with speed reductions. The economic logic that Vision Zero explicitly rejected—trading some deaths for faster, cheaper transportation—remains embedded in how we actually make decisions.

The vision endures. Zero, as a number, remains stubbornly far away.

But perhaps that's the point. A vision isn't a prediction. It's a direction. The question isn't whether we'll ever reach zero—we probably won't—but whether orienting every decision around that goal produces a better system than the alternative. Whether it saves lives we would otherwise have lost. Whether it forces us to see deaths we would otherwise have accepted.

The human body can survive being hit by a car traveling 30 kilometers per hour. Everything else is engineering. And ethics. And choices.

Mostly choices.
