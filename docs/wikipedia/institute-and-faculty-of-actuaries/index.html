Institute and Faculty of Actuaries

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Institute and Faculty of Actuaries

On June 1, 2025, the Continuous Mortality Investigation (CMI) released Working Paper 201, introducing the CMI_2024 mortality projections model. Calibrated to population data ending just months prior, on December 31, 2024, this document did not merely update a spreadsheet; it recalibrated the financial lifeblood of pension funds, insurance companies, and sovereign wealth strategies across the globe. While the world was still reeling from the climate shocks described in recent reports, a quieter, more calculated shift was occurring in the offices of London, Oxford, and Edinburgh. The Institute and Faculty of Actuaries (IFoA), the United Kingdom's chartered professional body, had just adjusted the mathematical lens through which the future of human longevity is viewed. This is not abstract mathematics. It is the architecture of solvency. When the IFoA speaks, the markets listen, because the numbers it produces determine whether a pension fund can pay out in thirty years or whether an insurer will collapse under the weight of its promises.

The IFoA is the guardian of a profession that sits at the intersection of risk, time, and money. Representing and regulating more than 34,000 members worldwide, the organization is the central nervous system of the actuarial world. Its influence extends far beyond the British Isles, operating through a network that includes regional offices in Beijing, Malaysia, and Singapore, ensuring that the principles of risk management are applied with consistent rigor from the City of London to the financial districts of Asia. But to understand the weight of the IFoA today, one must look at the machinery of its governance and the specific, often invisible, crises it navigates. The profession is overseen in the UK by the Financial Reporting Council (FRC), an independent body that sets technical actuarial standards and monitors the IFoA's regulation of its members. This dual-layer oversight ensures that the actuaries, who are the gatekeepers of financial stability, are themselves kept in check by a rigorous framework of accountability.

The IFoA as it exists today is a product of a significant consolidation in the modern era, born on August 1, 2010. Before this date, the profession was split between two historic bodies: the Institute of Actuaries, founded in 1848, and the Faculty of Actuaries, established in 1856 in Scotland. For over a century, these two organizations operated in parallel, a historical artifact of the divide between England and Scotland. The merger that created the IFoA was not merely a administrative housekeeping exercise; it was a strategic realignment implemented through amendments to the Institute's Royal Charter. The combined body assumed the assets and liabilities of the former organizations, creating a unified voice for a profession that had become increasingly global and complex.

The Architecture of Regulation

To call the IFoA a "professional body" is to understate its role. It is a regulatory powerhouse. The organization is incorporated by Royal Charter, a mark of prestige and authority that places it in the company of the oldest and most respected institutions in the land. However, its power is not absolute; it is carefully distributed. The IFoA is governed by a Council, which delegates authority to a board and a system of policy and regulatory committees. This structure is designed to prevent the concentration of power and to ensure that the voice of the profession is balanced by the needs of the public.

The regulatory oversight is a unique split between the profession and the state. The FRC sets and maintains the technical actuarial standards, acting as the ultimate arbiter of what constitutes "good" actuarial work. Simultaneously, the FRC oversees the IFoA's regulation of actuaries through its Actuarial Monitoring Programme. This programme is not a passive review; it is an active scrutiny of how actuaries apply their skills in the real world. Within the IFoA, the Regulatory Board includes a significant proportion of lay members—individuals who are not actuaries. This is a crucial design feature. It ensures that the regulation of a highly technical profession is not an insular process, shielded from the public interest by a wall of jargon. The lay members bring the perspective of the consumer, the policyholder, and the taxpayer to the table, ensuring that the technical standards serve the wider economy.

This regulatory framework was put to the test in the wake of the gilt-market stress that rocked the UK in September and October of 2022. The crisis exposed vulnerabilities in liability-driven investment (LDI) strategies used by UK defined benefit pension schemes. As the Bank of England described, emergency measures were required to stabilize the market. In the aftermath, parliamentary committees and commentators examined the role actuaries played in these strategies. The House of Lords took evidence on LDI oversight and market structure, and a range of views on lessons for pension governance were expressed, including letters to national media. The IFoA found itself at the center of a storm where mathematical models met market reality. The crisis underscored the fact that actuarial science is not just about predicting the future; it is about understanding the fragility of the systems built upon those predictions.

The Evolution of the Profession

The IFoA is not a static relic; it is an evolving organism that adapts to the changing landscape of risk. In recent years, the organization has repositioned its research and thought-leadership activity. The Actuarial Research Centre (ARC) was closed, a move that signaled a shift away from centralized, academic research toward member-led and industry-collaboration projects. This decentralization allows the profession to be more agile, responding to emerging risks like climate change and cyber threats with the collective intelligence of its entire membership rather than a single research unit.

The most significant recent change in the identity of the profession occurred in November 2024, when the IFoA introduced the "Chartered Actuary" designation. This was a pivotal moment. For the first time, both Fellow and Associate members could adopt the Chartered Actuary title after meeting specific eligibility criteria. This designation aligns the UK qualification with international standards, elevating the status of the profession and providing a clear, recognizable mark of excellence for employers and clients. It was a strategic move to ensure that the "Chartered" title carried the same weight as Chartered Accountants or Chartered Engineers, reinforcing the idea that actuaries are the strategic advisors of the 21st century.

The qualification structure itself was overhauled with the introduction of Curriculum 2019. This new structure comprises Core Principles (CS1–CS2, CM1–CM2, CB1–CB3), Core Practices (CP1–CP3), Specialist Principles (SP), and Specialist Advanced (SA) subjects. The curriculum is rigorous, requiring not just mathematical proficiency but also Personal and Professional Development (PPD) and professional skills training. The goal was to produce actuaries who are not just number-crunchers but holistic problem-solvers. Since 2019, new students have normally qualified first as Associates before progressing to Fellowship, a change that aligns the UK system with international standards and ensures that the initial qualification is robust and recognized globally.

The Human Element and Leadership

Behind the charts, the Royal Charters, and the regulatory frameworks are the people. The IFoA is led by a President, a role that rotates every two years, bringing a fresh perspective and specific area of expertise to the forefront of the profession. As of July 2025, the President is Paul Sweeting, who began his two-year term succeeding Kartina Tahir Thomson. The succession of presidents reads like a roll call of the profession's most distinguished leaders. The list includes Ronnie Bowie (2010–11), Jane Curtis (2011–12), Philip Scott (2012–13), David Hare (2013–14), Nick Salter (2014–15), Fiona Morrison (2015–16), Colin Wilson (2016–17), Marjorie Ngwenya (2017–18), Jules Constantinou (2018–19), John Taylor (2019–20), Tan Suee Chieh (2020–21), Louise Pryor (2021–22), Matt Saker (2022–23), Kalpana Shah (2023–24), and Kartina Tahir Thomson (2024–25).

Each of these leaders has steered the IFoA through distinct challenges, from the global financial crisis to the pandemic and the climate emergency. Under their guidance, the IFoA has expanded its global footprint. In 2024, the organization established new member committees in Greater China and the Middle East to support regional engagement. This expansion is not just about increasing membership numbers; it is about understanding the unique risk profiles of different markets and ensuring that the IFoA's standards are applied with cultural and economic sensitivity.

The human element is also central to the IFoA Foundation, the profession's registered charity. The Foundation supports scholarships, mentoring, and widening participation initiatives in the UK and internationally. It is the engine of diversity and inclusion, ensuring that the next generation of actuaries reflects the society they serve. In a profession that has historically been perceived as exclusive, the Foundation's work is critical to breaking down barriers and fostering a more diverse pipeline of talent.

The Code and the Standard

Every member of the IFoA is bound by the Actuaries' Code, a document that sets the core professional principles of the profession. The Code is not merely a set of rules; it is a moral compass. Version 3.2 of the Code took effect on September 25, 2025, accompanied by updated guidance that reflects the evolving nature of the profession. The Code mandates that actuaries act with integrity, competence, and objectivity. It requires them to consider the public interest in all their work. Within the UK geographic scope, members must also comply with the FRC's Technical Actuarial Standards (TASs). These include TAS 100 (General) and the relevant Specific TASs, such as TAS 200 for Insurance, TAS 300 for Pensions, and TAS 400 for Funeral Plans.

These standards are the bedrock of trust. When an actuary signs off on a pension valuation or an insurance premium, they are not just doing math; they are making a public statement that the numbers are sound and the assumptions are reasonable. The IFoA operates the Quality Assurance Scheme (QAS) for organizations employing actuaries. As of October 31, 2024, there were 42 accredited organizations, with additional firms covered by the QAS CPD scheme. This scheme ensures that the firms where actuaries work also adhere to high standards of practice. The IFoA also acts as a Designated Professional Body under Part XX of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000, licensing firms for certain exempt regulated activities. This dual role as both a professional body and a licensing authority gives the IFoA a unique position in the UK financial regulatory landscape.

The Future of Risk

The world is changing faster than ever. Climate change, pandemics, cyber warfare, and geopolitical instability are creating new types of risks that traditional actuarial models struggle to capture. The IFoA is acutely aware of this. The Continuous Mortality Investigation (CMI), which the IFoA supports, provides mortality and morbidity experience analyses and practical tools widely used by actuaries. The CMI Mortality Projections Model is updated regularly, with the latest iteration, CMI_2024, calibrated to data up to the end of 2024. The CMI also publishes regular mortality monitors for England and Wales and periodic updates on recent experience. These tools are essential for actuaries to adjust their models to the reality of a changing world.

The IFoA's research and thought-leadership activity has been repositioned to focus on these emerging challenges. The closure of the Actuarial Research Centre was not a retreat but a reallocation of resources toward member-led and industry-collaboration projects. This approach allows the profession to leverage the collective expertise of its 34,000 members to tackle complex, multi-disciplinary problems. The IFoA's monthly membership magazine, The Actuary, published in print and online by Redactive Media Group, serves as a platform for these discussions, keeping members informed and engaged with the latest developments.

The IFoA's role is more critical now than at any point in its 177-year history. As the world grapples with the consequences of climate change and the fragility of global financial systems, the need for rigorous, ethical, and forward-looking risk management has never been greater. The actuaries of the IFoA are the ones who are tasked with building the models that will guide us through this uncertain future. They are the ones who will determine how we fund our pensions, how we insure our homes against floods, and how we prepare for the next pandemic.

The IFoA is not just a professional body; it is a vital institution in the infrastructure of modern society. Its work is often invisible to the public, but its impact is profound. When a pension fund pays out its benefits, when an insurance company honors its claims, when a government sets its fiscal policy, the IFoA is there, in the background, ensuring that the numbers add up. The organization's commitment to high standards, its rigorous regulatory framework, and its focus on the public interest make it a cornerstone of financial stability.

As we look to the future, the IFoA faces new challenges. The government's intention to place the FRC's oversight and regulation of the actuarial profession on a statutory footing as part of wider reforms, including the proposed Audit, Reporting and Governance Authority, will further formalize the profession's role. This move will reinforce the importance of actuarial oversight and ensure that the profession remains accountable to the public. The IFoA is well-positioned to meet these challenges, with a leadership team that is experienced, a membership that is diverse and global, and a culture that is committed to excellence.
