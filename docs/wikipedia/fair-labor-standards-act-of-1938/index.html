Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938

6 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938

On a sweltering August afternoon in 1938, President Franklin D. Roosevelt sat at his desk in the White House, pen in hand, preparing to sign legislation that would fundamentally reshape the relationship between employer and employee across the United States. The Fair Labor Standards Act had emerged from years of political struggle, failed proposals, and a nation desperate for meaningful reform during the Great Depression.

The law's core premise was simple yet revolutionary: no worker should be expected to labor more than forty hours per week without fair compensation. Roosevelt signed the bill on that August day, creating what economists call "time-and-a-half" overtime pay—meaning anyone working beyond the forty-hour threshold would receive one and a half times their regular hourly wage for each additional hour labored.

This provision marked a decisive shift in American labor philosophy. The traditional eight-hour workday had become enshrined in law, and workers could now earn premium compensation when employers demanded more than the standard workweek.

The Act also established federal minimum wage standards, though these remained modest by modern standards. As of 2009, the federal floor held steady at $7.25 per hour—though state variations meant that workers in places like Seattle or Los Angeles could command significantly higher compensation based on local cost-of-living calculations.

Child labor prohibitions formed another critical component. The law explicitly banned "oppressive child labor," generally forbidding employment for children under fourteen or fifteen, with specific exemptions granted for non-hazardous occupations such as the entertainment industry. Those exemptions allowed younger children to work under strict regulations governing education and working hours.

The Human Story Behind Labor Reform

The Fair Labor Standards Act did not emerge from nowhere—it descended from a decade of failed attempts at meaningful reform.

In 1932, Senator Hugo Black of Alabama proposed requiring employers to adopt a thirty-hour workweek. The proposal met fierce resistance from business interests and legislative opposition, failing to pass both houses of Congress. This initial defeat taught reformers the necessity of compromise.

The revised version that finally passed in 1938 found powerful allies. Frances Perkins—a former Columbia University professor who would become the first female Cabinet Secretary—provided crucial support alongside Clara Mortenson Beyer from the Bureau of Labor Standards within the Department of Labor. Congresswoman Mary T. Norton contributed her political acumen to the effort.

The final legislation adopted the eight-hour day and forty-hour workweek framework, allowing premium pay for overtime hours while establishing baseline protections for workers across interstate commerce.

When Roosevelt signed the Act into law, the immediate impact proved substantial: approximately 700,000 workers received wage increases, demonstrating that reform could succeed through patient legislative effort.

Who Belongs to the Act?

The law's applicability clause established clear boundaries. The Fair Labor Standards Act covers "employees engaged in interstate commerce or in the production of goods for commerce, or employed by an enterprise engaged in commerce or in the production of goods for commerce"—unless employers could claim specific exemptions.

In practical terms, businesses with at least $500,000 in annual revenue or gross sales typically satisfy the Act's coverage requirements. Workers for those employers become subject to Fair Labor Standards Act protections unless additional exemptions apply.

Several exemptions relieve employers from meeting statutory minimum wage and overtime requirements. The most significant include so-called "white collar" exemptions applying to professional, administrative, and executive employees, though exemptions also cover roles like movie theater workers.

Crucially, exemptions are narrowly construed—employers must prove that employees fit "plainly and unmistakably" within exemption terms. Courts consistently require employers to demonstrate clear, documented compliance rather than assuming exemptions apply automatically.

One of the most prominent exemption factors involves how specific employees are compensated. If an employee receives a salary rather than hourly compensation—that employee becomes exempt because their pay does not depend on hours worked. They can labor beyond forty hours without receiving overtime premium pay.

The Question of Independent Contractors

The Fair Labor Standards Act applies "to any individual employed by an employer"—but explicitly excludes independent contractors and volunteers, who are not considered "employees" under the Act's language.

This exclusion has generated significant litigation. Courts consistently examine whether employers have improperly classified workers as independent contractors to avoid wage obligations. The analysis looks at the "economic reality" of the relationship between putative employers and workers.

Similar tests determine whether a worker was concurrently employed by multiple entities—commonly referred to as "joint employers." Consider agricultural workers: labor contractors might handle recruitment, transportation, payroll, and hour-tracking while growers monitor work quality, control workload volume, impose quality standards, and possess power to fire or discipline workers. In such scenarios, courts may find joint employment exists.

During World War II, the Army-Navy "E" Award for excellence in war production specifically required maintaining the fair labor standards established under the Act—demonstrating government commitment to worker protections even during national emergencies.

Tips and Minimum Wage

The Fair Labor Standards Act mandates that employers pay each employee at least minimum wage—unless employees engage in occupations where they "customarily and regularly receive more than $30 a month in tips." In such cases, if the employee's wage plus tips does not equal minimum wage, employers must make up the difference.

Employees may keep all tips—whether distributed individually or through tip pools. Tip pools can include only employees who customarily and regularly receive tips. The phrase "customarily and regularly" signifies frequency greater than occasional but less than constant.

Job titles alone do not determine tip-pool eligibility, though certain roles have explicit validation. Busboy positions perform integral customer service without direct interaction—thus requiring visibility to customers. Service bartenders must meet minimal thresholds sufficient to incentivize customers to "customarily and regularly" recognize their services through tipping.

Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

When Roosevelt signed the Fair Labor Standards Act in 1938, he addressed not merely wage concerns but broader questions of worker dignity and economic fairness. The Act set minimum wage, overtime pay, recordkeeping standards, and child labor prohibitions—establishing frameworks that remain relevant today.

The legislation prohibited dangerous jobs for children under eighteen while banning mining or manufacturing work during school hours for those under sixteen. These provisions reflected Congressional recognition that certain occupations posed risks to developing young people.

Though the Act did not cover executives, seasonal employees, and certain other groups—it established fundamental protections for millions of American workers. Those protections continue evolving through court decisions, regulatory interpretations, and legislative amendments.

The Fair Labor Standards Act emerged from specific political circumstances: an economy devastated by Depression, workers desperate for protection, reformers learning from earlier failures. Its passage demonstrated that meaningful worker protection could succeed through patient advocacy and legislative compromise—lessons that remain vital as income inequality, automation, and gig economy employment continue reshaping American workplaces.
