Mint julep

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Mint julep

In the summer of 1793, Reverend Harry Toulmin sat in Virginia and described a drink that was already becoming a regional obsession: "a tumbler of rum and water, well sweetened, with a slip of mint in it." It was a simple concoction, far removed from the elaborate, ice-crusted spectacles we recognize today, yet it captured the essence of a beverage that would eventually define the American South. The mint julep is not merely a cocktail; it is a historical artifact, a medicinal remedy, a symbol of aristocratic leisure, and the undisputed ambassador of the Kentucky Derby. To understand the julep is to understand the complex evolution of Southern identity, the global trade of sugar and ice, and the specific alchemy required to transform four simple ingredients—mint, bourbon, sugar, and water—into a drink that has survived nearly three centuries of changing tastes and technologies.

The core of the mint julep remains stubbornly consistent, even as the world around it has shifted. At its heart, it is a bourbon-based cocktail, inextricably linked to the cuisine and culture of the American South. While recipes vary wildly from one bartender to another, the traditional formula relies on four pillars: fresh mint leaves, bourbon whiskey, simple syrup, and crushed or shaved ice. In the Southern states, and particularly in Kentucky, spearmint is the varietal of choice, prized for its sharp, clean flavor profile that cuts through the richness of the spirit. The preparation is a ritual that often sparks debate among purists. The drink belongs to a loose family of beverages known as "smashes," a category that includes the brandy smash and the mojito. The defining characteristic of a smash is the muddling process, where fresh mint and other ingredients are crushed in the bottom of the vessel. This is not a gentle stir; it is a mechanical release of essential oils and juices, intensifying the flavor profile before the spirit is ever added. The goal is to wake up the mint, to extract its soul and bind it to the sugar and ice before the bourbon ever touches the glass.

The Silver Vessel and the Art of the Frost

One cannot discuss the mint julep without addressing the vessel in which it is served, for the cup is as much a part of the drink's identity as the liquid within. Traditionally, the mint julep is served in a silver or pewter cup. This is not an aesthetic choice born of vanity, but a functional necessity rooted in thermodynamics. The metal cup is held only by the bottom and top edges, a specific hand placement that serves a dual purpose. First, it prevents the heat of the human hand from transferring to the cup, which would prematurely melt the ice and dilute the drink. Second, and more visually striking, this isolation allows the extreme cold of the crushed ice to draw heat from the surrounding air, causing a thick, crystalline frost to form on the exterior of the metal.

This "stalactites of ice," as they were poetically described in the 19th century, is the hallmark of a properly prepared julep. The frost acts as an insulator, keeping the drink cold while the ice slowly melts, gradually watering down the spirit to a perfect, sippable strength. British Captain Frederick Marryat, in his 1840 book Second Series of A Diary in America, noted this phenomenon with the eye of a connoisseur: "Epicures rub the lips of the tumbler with a piece of fresh pine-apple, and the tumbler itself is very often incrusted outside with stalactites of ice. As the ice melts, you drink." While the silver cup remains the traditional standard for the purist, the modern era has shifted the landscape. Today, the mint julep is most commonly encountered in a tall old-fashioned glass, a Collins glass, or a highball glass, almost always accompanied by a straw. The straw is essential, allowing the drinker to bypass the mint leaves and the slush of ice at the bottom to access the liquid, while also preventing the frost from melting the drinker's fingers.

The word "julep" itself carries a weight of history that predates the bourbon-based cocktail by centuries. It is generally defined as a sweet drink, often used as a vehicle for medicine. The etymology is a journey through language and trade: the term derives from the Spanish julepe, which came from Spanish Arabic, and ultimately from the Persian word Golâb, meaning rosewater. In its earliest iterations, English juleps were medicinal concoctions—lightly alcoholic, often containing camphor, and designed to soothe the stomach or cure ailments. The American mint julep was a radical departure from this medical origin, evolving into a recreational beverage that retained the "cure" aspect only in the sense of curing a hot summer day or a social awkwardness.

From Medicine to Morning Ritual

The origins of the mint julep in the southern United States are believed to date back to the eighteenth century, though pinpointing the exact moment of invention is impossible. The earliest known mentions appear in 1770, a time when the American colonies were on the brink of revolution. In Robert Munford's satirical play The Candidate, a drunkard character named "Mr. Julip" appears, suggesting that the drink was already a recognizable trope of indulgence. That same year, a poem titled "A Short Poem on Hunting," published in the Williamsburg Virginia Gazette, described the julep as a concoction "Which doctors storm at, and which some adore." This duality—the doctor's disapproval versus the drinker's adoration—perfectly encapsulates the drink's early reputation.

Further evidence of the julep's medicinal roots can be found in the 1784 text Medical communications, which records a prescription for a patient suffering from "sickness at the stomach, with frequent retching, and, at times, difficulty of swallowing." The physician's remedy included an emetic, some opening powders, and a mint julep. By 1793, the drink had evolved, as noted by Reverend Toulmin, but it was still a humble mixture of rum, water, sugar, and mint.

The transition from a medicinal tumbler to a morning ritual for the gentry began in earnest in the early 19th century. In 1803, John Davis, an English traveler, published a book in London describing the drink as "a dram of spirituous liquor that has mint steeped in it, taken by Virginians of a morning." Davis interviewed a local who confessed that his love for whiskey stemmed from his daily preparation of the drink. This is a crucial detail: the julep was not just a party drink; it was a daily habit, a way to start the day. The infrastructure of the South began to adapt to this demand. Virginia taverns, such as J. Pryor's Haymarket Inn in Richmond, began installing ice-houses in the 1780s, a significant investment in technology that allowed for the preservation of ice year-round, enabling the creation of iced cocktails. The first specific mention of an "Iced Julep" appeared in an advertisement for the Wig-Wam Gardens in Norfolk on May 4, 1807, marking the moment the drink shed its room-temperature origins and embraced the chill of the ice.

The drink's popularity spread beyond the borders of Virginia. U.S. Senator Henry Clay of Kentucky, a man of immense influence and a notorious drinker, is credited with introducing the julep to the nation's capital. During his residence in Washington, D.C., Clay would frequent the Round Robin Bar in the famous Willard Hotel, where he likely popularized the bourbon-based version among the political elite. By the 1820s, the julep was a legacy in Virginia and had been adopted as a key item of local identity. It was a symbol of Southern hospitality and sophistication.

However, the early julep was not exclusively a bourbon drink. In the 19th century, Americans enjoyed gin-based juleps made with genever, an aged gin, alongside their bourbon versions. The first acknowledged master of the mint julep, however, was a man of profound historical significance: Jasper Crouch. From Richmond, Crouch identified himself as a Free Person of Color and was celebrated in the 1830s for his mastery of the craft. His story highlights the often-overlooked role of Black craftsmen in the development of Southern culinary and mixological traditions. In New York during the same decade, the drink appeared in a variation known as the "hailstone" or "hailstorm" julep, which utilized small, pounded lumps of ice to create a texture resembling a winter storm in a glass.

The Golden Age and the Bourbon Shift

The mid-19th century marked the codification of the mint julep. In 1840, Captain Frederick Marryat provided a detailed recipe in his travelogue, distinguishing the "real mint julep" from its imitators. He noted that while there were varieties composed of claret, Madeira, and other wines, the authentic version required a dozen sprigs of tender mint, a spoonful of white sugar, and equal proportions of peach and common brandy to fill the tumbler one-third of the way. The rest was filled with rasped or pounded ice. Marryat's description captures the sensory experience of the drink: the scent of the mint, the crunch of the ice, and the slow dilution that transforms the drink over time.

Jerry Thomas, the father of American mixology, cemented the julep's place in the canon in his 1862 Bar-Tenders Guide: How to Mix Drinks or The Bon-Vivant's Companion. Thomas included five distinct recipes for the mint julep, allowing for Cognac, brandy, gin, whiskey, or even sparkling Moselle. Yet, even in this period of variety, Thomas noted that the mint julep was "a peculiarly American beverage," a statement that acknowledged its unique status in the global lexicon of drinks. He also noted that it was introduced into England by Captain Marryat, completing the circle of trans-Atlantic cultural exchange.

By 1916, the drink had found a new home in the luxury of the "Old White" hotel in White Sulphur Springs, West Virginia. The description of the barroom is evocative: a dark, cool room approached by a spiral staircase, scented with great masses of fragrant mint lying upon mountains of crushed ice. Here, the "White Sulphur mint julep" and the Virginia toddy were created, famous the world over. The recipe at this time was described as a composite of the purest French brandy, limestone water, old-fashioned cut loaf sugar, crushed ice, and young mint so fresh its foliage touched the drinker's ears. This era represented the height of the julep's aristocratic prestige, a drink of the elite, served in settings of opulence and cool.

Beginning in the 20th century, a decisive shift occurred. The gin-based and brandy-based juleps began to fade, and the bourbon-based julep decisively eclipsed them all. This shift was not accidental; it was the result of the consolidation of bourbon as the "native spirit" of the United States and the rising cultural power of Kentucky. The song "Lift the Juleps to Your Two Lips," featured in the 1929 talkie musical On with the Show!, captured the public imagination and linked the drink to the era of jazz and glamour.

The Derby and the Modern Icon

The final transformation of the mint julep from a regional favorite to a global icon is inextricably linked to the Kentucky Derby. The promotion of the drink by Churchill Downs began in earnest in 1938, and it has never looked back. The association is so strong that the mint julep is now synonymous with the race itself. As of 2009, the scale of this consumption was staggering: approximately 120,000 juleps were served at Churchill Downs over the two-day period of the Kentucky Oaks and the Kentucky Derby. Virtually all of these were served in specially made, collectible Kentucky Derby glasses, turning the act of drinking into a form of souvenir hunting.

The commercialization of the julep has been a carefully orchestrated dance between the race and the beverage industry. In a contract arrangement that has lasted more than 18 years, the Brown-Forman Corporation became a key player. For a long time, the Early Times Mint Julep Cocktail was designated the "official mint julep of the Kentucky Derby." However, a technicality in whiskey law meant that the Early Times sold within the United States was technically a Kentucky whiskey, not a bourbon, because it was aged in used, rather than new, oak barrels. This distinction, while legalistic, mattered deeply to the bourbon purists.

In 2015, the landscape shifted again. Old Forester, also produced by Brown-Forman, became "the official drink of the Kentucky Derby," specifically when sold as the Old Forester Mint Julep Ready-to-Serve Cocktail. This move aligned the official drink more closely with the bourbon tradition. Furthermore, Woodford Reserve, another Brown-Forman sister brand, was designated the "official bourbon" of the Derby. Under the terms of the current marketing agreement, these brands are inextricably linked to the event's identity.

The stakes of the mint julep have also been raised to absurd heights. Since 2006, Churchill Downs has served extra-premium custom-made mint juleps at the Kentucky Derby for a cost of $1,000 each. These are not mere drinks; they are performances of luxury. Served in gold-plated cups with silver straws, these juleps are crafted from Woodford Reserve bourbon, mint imported from Ireland, spring water ice cubes from the Bavarian Alps, and sugar from Australia. The proceeds from these exorbitant drinks support charitable causes dedicated to retired race horses, adding a layer of philanthropy to the extravagance. In May 2008, Churchill Downs unveiled the world's largest mint julep glass, a 6-foot tall structure (7.5 feet with the mint sprig) fabricated by the Weber Group in conjunction with Brown-Forman. This monolithic glass stands as a testament to the drink's status as a cultural monument.

The Enduring Spirit of the Julep

The journey of the mint julep is a microcosm of American history. It began as a medicinal remedy in the 18th century, evolved into a morning ritual for the Southern gentry, traveled to the nation's capital to influence politics, and eventually became the centerpiece of the world's most famous horse race. Its ingredients have changed—rum to brandy to gin to bourbon—but its soul remains the same: a balance of sweet, cold, and aromatic, served with a specific reverence for the ice and the vessel.

The debate over preparation continues, with bartenders arguing over the degree of muddling, the type of ice, and the ratio of sugar to spirit. Yet, the essence of the drink is its ability to adapt while retaining its core identity. Whether served in a pewter cup with frost clinging to the sides, in a highball glass with a straw, or in a gold-plated vessel with ice from the Alps, the mint julep remains a symbol of the American South. It is a drink that demands to be sipped slowly, allowing the ice to melt and the flavors to evolve, much like the history it represents.

The mint julep is more than a cocktail; it is a story of resilience and reinvention. From the satirical plays of the 1770s to the modern marketing campaigns of multi-billion dollar corporations, the drink has survived wars, economic shifts, and changing tastes. It has been a cure for sickness, a morning wake-up call, a symbol of aristocracy, and a symbol of celebration. Today, as millions of people raise their glasses at the Kentucky Derby, they are participating in a tradition that spans nearly three centuries. They are drinking the same basic formula that Henry Clay sipped in Washington, that Jasper Crouch perfected in Richmond, and that Captain Marryat described in 1840. The mint julep is a living history, a frozen moment in time that is constantly melting and reforming, yet always remaining the same.

The next time you hold a mint julep, whether in a silver cup or a glass with a straw, consider the journey of the ingredients. The mint from the garden, the sugar from the tropics, the ice from the freezer or the Alps, and the bourbon from the barrel. Consider the hands that have held it for generations, from the free person of color in 19th-century Richmond to the millionaires at the Derby today. The mint julep is a testament to the power of a simple recipe to become a complex cultural phenomenon. It is a drink that invites you to slow down, to savor the cold, and to appreciate the history in every sip. As the ice melts and the mint releases its oils, you are not just drinking a cocktail; you are tasting the history of the American South.
