Peterhouse, Cambridge

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Peterhouse, Cambridge

In 1884, a gong rang through the Hall of Peterhouse, not merely to summon diners, but to herald a technological revolution that had arrived at Cambridge three years prior. When the six-hundredth anniversary of the college was celebrated, Lord Kelvin personally oversaw the installation of electric lighting in the Hall and Combination Room, making Peterhouse the first institution in the University of Cambridge, and only the second building in all of Britain after the Palace of Westminster, to be lit by electricity. It was a moment where ancient tradition brushed shoulders with the cutting edge of human innovation, a fitting metaphor for an institution that has survived since 1284 not just by preserving its past, but by constantly reinventing the terms of its existence. To understand Peterhouse is to understand a microcosm of British history itself: a place of profound wealth and academic dominance, yet one forged in the fires of religious schism, civil war, and internal ideological warfare that would make any modern political drama seem tame.

Founded by Hugh de Balsham, the Bishop of Ely, Peterhouse holds the distinction of being the oldest constituent college of the University of Cambridge. Its story begins not with a grand cathedral or a royal charter, but with a dispute over housing. In 1280, letters patent from King Edward I allowed Balsham to maintain scholars in the Hospital of St John in Burgh, Suffolk. However, friction inevitably arose between the scholarly community and the Brethren of the Hospital. The result was a separation that would define the college's future trajectory. By 1284, Balsham transferred his scholars to their current site just outside what was then Trumpington Gate, purchasing two modest houses to accommodate a Master and fourteen "worthy but impoverished Fellows." They were to live under rules modeled on Merton College, utilizing the nearby Church of St Peter without Trumpington Gate for their spiritual needs.

The early decades were defined by scarcity. Bishop Balsham died in 1286, leaving a bequest of 300 marks, which was immediately directed toward buying land to the south of St Peter's Church to construct the college Hall. The financial reality of the time is starkly evident in the earliest surviving statutes, granted by Bishop Simon Montacute in 1344. While these rules were based on those of Merton, they explicitly highlighted the lack of resources available to the fledgling institution. Yet, it was precisely this statutory framework that allowed Peterhouse to defend its autonomy in 1345, successfully repelling an attempt by King Edward III to appoint his own candidate as a scholar against the college's wishes. The college began to find its feet through strategic land acquisitions; between 1354 and 1392, a trust established by William Moschett secured nearly 70 acres of land at Fen Ditton, a significant economic asset that would provide stability for centuries. Further relief came in 1401 when the college acquired the advowson and rectory of Hinton, and during the reign of Elizabeth I, it absorbed Volney's Croft, which today constitutes St Peter's Terrace, the William Stone Building, and the Scholars' Garden.

The history of Peterhouse is inextricably linked to the volatile religious landscape of England, a relationship that often turned the college into a battleground for the nation's soul. In 1553, Andrew Perne was appointed Master, a man whose career became a byword for pragmatic survivalism during the Reformation. His ability to navigate the treacherous waters between Mary I and Elizabeth I was legendary; he was favored by both monarchs despite their diametrically opposed religious policies. A contemporary joke captured his duality perfectly: the letters on the weathervane of St Peter's Church could be read as "Andrew Perne, Papist" or "Andrew Perne, Protestant" depending on which way the wind blew. Yet, beneath this veneer of adaptability lay a capacity for brutality that has left an indelible mark on his legacy. Having previously been close to the reformist Martin Bucer, Perne later served as Vice-Chancellor and orchestrated the exhumation and burning of Bucer's bones in Market Square during the reign of Mary I. The Protestant martyrologist John Foxe condemned this act as "shameful railing," and the story goes that there is a hole burnt through the page describing this event in Perne's own copy of Foxe's Actes and Monuments. Perne died in 1589, leaving behind a legacy that funded fellowships and an extensive library collection known today as the Perne Library, a testament to his ability to leave a mark regardless of one's moral judgment.

The religious turbulence continued into the 17th century. Between 1626 and 1634, Matthew Wren served as Master, having previously accompanied Charles I on his ill-fated journey to Spain to negotiate the Spanish Match. Wren was a staunch supporter of Archbishop William Laud, transforming Peterhouse into a center of Arminianism. His successor, John Cosin, who took office in 1634, continued this trajectory, making significant changes to the college Chapel to align with Laud's vision of the "beauty of holiness." These aesthetic and theological shifts were not merely matters of taste; they were political statements that would soon invite violent retribution. On March 13, 1643, in the early stages of the English Civil War, Cosin was expelled by a Parliamentary ordinance from the Earl of Manchester, accused of opposing Parliament and engaging in "scandalous acts." Later that year, on December 21, the Puritan zealot William Dowsing led a committee into the college to strip the Chapel of its statues and decorations. The physical violence against the sacred space mirrored the broader disintegration of the English state, with the college serving as a casualty of a conflict that would ultimately lead to the execution of a king and the establishment of a republic.

Despite these historical traumas, Peterhouse emerged as a powerhouse of intellectual achievement, particularly in the natural sciences. The list of its alumni reads like a roll call of the Industrial Revolution and the birth of modern physics: Lord Kelvin, Henry Cavendish, Charles Babbage, James Clerk Maxwell, James Dewar, and Frank Whittle. The college has produced five Nobel Prize winners in science: Sir John Kendrew, Sir Aaron Klug, Archer Martin, Max Perutz, and Michael Levitt. This scientific dominance is reflected in the college's modern standing; it is one of the wealthiest colleges in Cambridge, with assets exceeding £350 million, ranking third in net assets per student. This financial muscle supports a rigorous academic environment where around 300 undergraduate and 175 graduate students are taught by 54 fellows. The academic performance has been consistently strong; in the past five years, Peterhouse has sat within the top ten of the Tompkins Table, reaching fourth place in both 2018 and 2019.

The culture of the college is defined by its traditions, which blend the medieval with the modern in a way that demands participation rather than mere observation. Members are strongly encouraged to attend communal dinners known as "Hall." These take place in two sittings, but it is the second, known as "Formal Hall," that captures the essence of the Peterhouse experience. This is not a casual meal; it is a three-course candlelit affair where students must wear suits and gowns. The ritual begins with the procession of the fellows into the room, prompting the students to rise in respect. A gong rings, signaling the moment, followed by the reading of two Latin graces before the meal commences. This adherence to formality is not simply about nostalgia; it is a mechanism for community building, a daily reinforcement of the bonds between generations of scholars. The social calendar extends to May Week, where the college hosts a biennial white-tie ball, an event that draws on the tradition of Cambridge's most exclusive celebrations.

The 20th century introduced new layers of complexity to Peterhouse's identity, particularly in the realm of politics and gender. During World War II, the college demonstrated its intellectual depth by simultaneously housing four professors from the university's faculty of history: Herbert Butterfield, David Knowles, Michael Postan, and Denis Brogan. This concentration of historical expertise cemented a reputation for the teaching of history that began with Harold Temperley. However, it was in the 1980s that Peterhouse became the epicenter of a fierce ideological struggle that would define its modern character. The college acquired an association with Conservative politics, driven by influential fellows such as Maurice Cowling and Roger Scruton. They were described as key figures in the "Peterhouse right," an intellectual movement linked to philosophical conservatism.

This political alignment was not without internal friction. The period coincided with the mastership of Hugh Trevor-Roper, a renowned historian who would find himself in a brutal, seven-year feud with Cowling and his allies. The conflict was not merely academic; it was a battle for the soul of the institution. Cowling's clique was accused by critics of fostering an atmosphere where fellows wore mourning on the anniversary of General Franco's death, attended parties in SS uniforms, and insulted black and Jewish guests at high table. Trevor-Roper, described as an "anti-clerical Whig with a preference for free speech over superstition," fought to suppress this insurgency and modernize the college. His reforms included the admission of women in 1983, a move urged by him despite the resistance from traditionalist elements within the fellowship.

The struggle between Trevor-Roper and Cowling was characterized by "grotesque rudeness and insubordination," as journalist Neal Ascherson later summarized it. It became a campaign to drive the Master out of the college, a proxy war for broader debates about British identity, tradition, and the role of the university in society. While often associated with Thatcherite politics—Conservative politicians Michael Portillo and Michael Howard both studied at Peterhouse—the extent to which Margaret Thatcher's economic liberalism was admired within the "Peterhouse right" movement was actually quite limited. The conflict highlighted a deep fracture: one side seeking to preserve an insular, traditionalist vision of England, the other pushing for a more open, albeit still conservative, engagement with the modern world. Neither side won the struggle outright; instead, the college emerged scarred but resilient, forced to confront its own contradictions.

The legacy of Peterhouse is not just in its buildings or its bank balance, but in the sheer diversity of its human output. Beyond the scientists and politicians, the college has produced important poets like Thomas Gray, the first Anglican Bishop of New York Samuel Provoost, and the first British Fields Medallist Klaus Roth. In the arts and entertainment world, Oscar-winning film director Sam Mendes and comedian David Mitchell are among its notable alumni. The political reach extends globally as well, with figures such as Augustus FitzRoy, 3rd Duke of Grafton, who served as British Prime Minister, and Elijah Mudenda, the second prime minister of Zambia, having studied within its walls.

The financial strength of Peterhouse allows it to maintain this high standard, yet the human element remains central to its identity. The "worthy but impoverished Fellows" of 1284 have evolved into a community that is now one of the wealthiest in Cambridge, but the ethos of communal living and rigorous scholarship remains. The transition from the candlelit halls of the 14th century to the electric lighting of the late 19th century, and finally to the digital age of the 21st, has not eroded the college's core mission. Instead, it has allowed it to adapt, surviving religious wars, political purges, and internal civil wars to remain a leading institution in one of the world's most prestigious universities.

The story of Peterhouse is a reminder that institutions are not static monuments but living entities shaped by the conflicts and collaborations of their members. From the pragmatic survivalism of Andrew Perne to the ideological battles of the 1980s, the college has always been a place where ideas are tested, often violently, against the realities of power and tradition. The wealth, the Nobel prizes, and the formal dinners are the surface manifestations of a deeper current: a relentless drive to define what it means to be a scholar in an ever-changing world. As students rise at Formal Hall to greet their fellows, they are participating in a ritual that has survived six centuries of upheaval, a testament to the enduring power of community and the pursuit of knowledge.

The physical space of the college itself tells this story of adaptation and resilience. The purchase of two houses outside Trumpington Gate in 1284 was just the beginning of an expansion that would eventually encompass the William Stone Building and the Scholars' Garden. Each brick, each acre of Fen Ditton land, represents a negotiation between the past and the future. The college's ability to acquire resources, from the 70 acres secured by William Moschett to the advowson of Hinton in 1401, demonstrates a strategic acumen that has ensured its survival when other institutions have faded into obscurity.

In recent years, the ranking of Peterhouse as one of the highest-achieving colleges serves as validation of this long-term strategy. While academic performance varies year to year due to its small population, the consistent presence in the top ten of the Tompkins Table underscores a culture of excellence that permeates every aspect of student life. The alumni network, stretching from the natural sciences to the highest offices of state and the arts, provides a global reach that amplifies the college's influence far beyond the borders of Cambridge.

Yet, the human cost of this history should not be overlooked. The expulsion of John Cosin, the burning of Bucer's bones by Perne, the violent iconoclasm of William Dowsing—these were not abstract events but moments of profound personal and communal trauma. They remind us that the pursuit of truth and tradition often comes with a heavy price. The "Peterhouse right" conflict of the 1980s was a less bloody but equally intense struggle, where the very identity of the college was at stake. The fact that neither side achieved total victory suggests a healthy tension, a balance between preservation and progress that continues to define the institution.

As we look at Peterhouse today, with its £350 million in assets and its prestigious alumni, it is easy to see only the success stories. But a deeper examination reveals a history marked by struggle, adaptation, and an unyielding commitment to its mission. From the "worthy but impoverished Fellows" of the 13th century to the Nobel laureates and world leaders of today, Peterhouse has remained a beacon of learning, navigating the turbulent waters of history with a steady hand. The gong that rang in 1884 still echoes, not just as a signal for dinner, but as a reminder of the enduring legacy of an institution that has refused to be silenced by the winds of change.
