Sandwich panel

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Sandwich panel

In 2017, a fire at Grenfell Tower in London consumed the building with terrifying speed, turning a residential high-rise into an inferno within minutes. The tragedy was not merely a failure of electrical wiring or evacuation protocols; it was a structural failure born from the very material chosen to clad the building's exterior. The aluminium composite panels (ACP) used on the facade contained a core of polyethylene, a highly combustible plastic that turned the building's skin into a chimney for the flames. This catastrophic event cast a long shadow over an entire industry, forcing architects, engineers, and regulators to confront the dual nature of sandwich panels: materials engineered for unparalleled strength and lightness, yet capable of becoming instruments of disaster when their fire-safety properties are compromised.

To understand how a material designed to save weight could contribute to such loss of life, one must first strip away the jargon and look at the fundamental physics of a sandwich panel. At its core, this technology is an exercise in structural efficiency, mimicking the logic of nature and the classic I-beam found in steel construction. Imagine holding a thin sheet of cardboard; it bends easily under the slightest pressure. Now, glue two sheets of that cardboard to either side of a thick block of foam or honeycomb material. Suddenly, you have a structure that is incredibly difficult to bend, despite being made largely of soft, light materials.

This is the essence of the sandwich panel: three distinct layers working in concert. There is a low-density core—often polyisocyanurate (PIR), mineral wool, or extruded polystyrene (XPS)—sandwiched between two thin, rigid skin layers. The structural functionality relies on a division of labor that is as elegant as it is effective. The outer face sheets, typically metal, act like the flanges of an I-beam; they are positioned far apart to resist in-plane and lateral bending loads, providing stiffness and strength. Meanwhile, the thick, lightweight core acts like the web of the I-beam, primarily resisting shear forces that try to slide the layers past one another.

The genius of this design lies in its ability to increase overall thickness without adding significant weight. By using a light but thick core and strong but thin skins, engineers can achieve bending stiffness that would otherwise require a solid block of heavy material. The result is a structural element that offers high rigidity at a fraction of the mass. This principle has made sandwich panels ubiquitous in applications where every kilogram counts, from the fuselages of aircraft to the chassis of modern automobiles.

The versatility of this technology means it is not limited to a single formula. The core can be anything from a rigid foam to a honeycomb structure, and even a solid filling. When these layers are enclosed in a specific way, they are termed cassettes. In the aviation industry, where mechanical performance and weight-saving are existential imperatives, sandwich structures have long been the standard. But their reach extends far beyond the sky. They appear in transportation, automotive design, and most visibly, in the built environment.

In construction, these prefabricated products serve as building envelopes for a staggering variety of structures: industrial warehouses, office complexes, clean rooms for pharmaceutical manufacturing, cold storage facilities, and even private residences. Whether used in new builds or renovations, they offer a unique combination of high-quality finish and design flexibility. They are not merely functional; they are aesthetic. The metal skins can be coated with polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF), fluoropolymer resins (FEVE), or polyester paint, allowing architects to specify virtually any color. The panels can mimic the look of wood, marble, or stone, providing a visual richness that belies their lightweight nature.

Yet, as the Grenfell Tower tragedy demonstrated, the choice of core material is not just an aesthetic or structural decision; it is a matter of life and death. The standard ACP core for decades was polyethylene (PE) or polyurethane (PU). These materials offered excellent insulation and ease of fabrication but possessed abysmal fire-resistant properties unless specially treated. In many jurisdictions, the use of these combustible cores in high-rise buildings has since been banned entirely.

The physics of fire in a sandwich panel is brutal. When a core made of polyethylene ignites, it does not simply char; it melts and burns with intense heat, releasing toxic fumes. Arconic, the owner of the Reynobond brand, issued stark warnings to prospective buyers long after the fact was clear. In their brochure, Fire Safety in High-rise Buildings: Our Fire Solutions, they presented a graphic of a building engulfed in flames with a chilling caption: "[a]s soon as the building is higher than the firefighters' ladders, it has to be conceived with an incombustible material." The graphic illustrated a hierarchy of safety based on height. For buildings up to roughly 10 meters, a standard polyethylene product might be deemed acceptable. From there to about 30 meters—the maximum reach of many fire truck ladders—a fire-retardant (FR) product with approximately 70% mineral core was required. Above that altitude, only the European A2-rated product, containing roughly 90% mineral core, was considered safe.

This distinction is critical because the Grenfell Tower fire involved panels with a highly combustible PE core. The cladding acted as a wick, drawing flames up the facade and allowing the fire to spread rapidly to upper floors that were far from the ignition point. This pattern of disaster has not been unique to London. Similar high-rise building fires involving ACP have occurred in Melbourne, Australia; in France; in the United Arab Emirates; in South Korea; and across various cities in the United States. In each case, the rapid spread of flame was facilitated by the combustible nature of the panel's core.

The industry response has been a push toward fire-rated cores, often designated as "FR" by manufacturers. These safer alternatives limit polyethylene content to a maximum of 30%, allowing the material to self-extinguish in the absence of heat or ventilation. However, compliance is complex. Building codes in the USA and elsewhere impose rigorous requirements on wall assemblies depending on the specific materials used and the building type. When these codes are followed, FR core products are generally considered safe. But it is a dangerous fallacy to assume that all sandwich panels carry the same risk profile.

A crucial distinction must be made between Aluminium Composite Panels (ACP) and Insulated Metal Panels (IMP). The term ACP does not apply to sandwich panels with mineral wool cores; those fall under the category of IMPs. Mineral wool is non-combustible by nature, offering a level of fire safety that polyethylene-based cores simply cannot match. The confusion between these categories has often led to dangerous misapplications in construction.

The history of this material is relatively short but explosive. Aluminium composite materials were invented in 1964 as a joint invention by 3A Composites (then Alcan Composites & Alusuisse) and BASF. Commercial production of the first product, Alucobond, commenced in 1969, with the patent following in 1971. That patent expired in 1991, triggering a surge in competition. Immediately after, major players like Reynobond (1991), Alpolic by Mitsubishi Chemicals (1995), and etalbond (1995) entered the market. Today, it is estimated that more than 200 companies across the globe are producing ACP, making it one of the most widely used cladding materials in modern architecture.

Famous structures have relied on the flexibility, low weight, and durability of these panels. Spaceship Earth at Epcot, the VanDusen Botanical Garden in Vancouver, and the Leipzig branch of the German National Library all utilized ACP to achieve innovative designs with increased rigidity. The material's ability to be easily formed and processed allowed architects to create sweeping curves and sharp angles that would have been prohibitively expensive or heavy using traditional materials. It has even found a niche in the art world, serving as a lightweight backing for mounting fine art photography, often with acrylic finishes using techniques like Diasec.

However, the evolution of sandwich panel technology is not limited to the past four decades of cladding history. In recent years, the advent of 3D printing has sparked a flourishing of research into new geometric complexities previously impossible to fabricate. Researchers are exploring energy absorption capabilities, the use of natural fibers alongside continuous synthetic fibers, and vibration damping applications. The promise of this technology lies in its ability to create internal lattice structures within the core that can be tailored for specific mechanical responses, pushing the boundaries of what a sandwich panel can achieve.

In the realm of packaging, the technology has also found widespread use. Fluted polypropylene boards and polypropylene honeycomb boards are now common, replacing heavier materials in shipping and protective casing. These applications highlight the material's ability to provide structural integrity without adding bulk.

Yet, for all its innovation, the construction sector remains the primary battleground for sandwich panel regulation. In Europe, the CE mark is mandatory for these panels to be sold, ensuring they meet specific safety standards. The governing standard is EN14509:2013, which covers self-supporting double-skin metal-faced insulating panels made in factories. Quality certification can also be achieved through the EPAQ quality level.

The thermal performance of these panels remains a primary driver for their adoption. Sandwich panels boast impressive lambda ($\lambda$) values, ranging from 0.024 W/(m·K) for polyurethane to 0.05 W/(m·K) for mineral wool. This means they can achieve very low U-values (a measure of heat loss), making them highly energy-efficient. In a world increasingly focused on sustainability and carbon reduction, the ability of a single panel to provide structural support, insulation, and a finished surface is invaluable.

The installation systems for these panels have also evolved, becoming faster and more precise. The panels are often prefabricated with integrated seals and fixings, reducing the time required on-site. This speed of construction is a major economic benefit for developers, but it must never come at the cost of safety verification.

The human cost of ignoring fire safety in these materials cannot be overstated. The 2017 Grenfell Tower fire claimed 72 lives. Families were torn apart in moments, their homes consumed not just by heat, but by a material choice made for economy and aesthetics over survival. Victims included young children, elderly residents, and entire families who had no way to escape the rising flames that raced up the building's facade. The tragedy was a stark reminder that engineering decisions have moral dimensions.

Following the disaster, inquiries revealed that cost-cutting measures often prioritized cheaper combustible cores over fire-retardant alternatives. The "mystery" of why such dangerous materials were used in high-rises was not a mystery at all; it was a calculated risk taken by developers and approved by lax regulatory environments. The fire exposed a systemic failure where the financial incentives for speed and low cost outweighed the imperative to protect human life.

Since Grenfell, the regulatory landscape has shifted dramatically. In the UK and many other countries, the use of combustible cladding on high-rise residential buildings has been effectively banned. Manufacturers have had to reformulate their products, introducing mineral wool cores and fire-retardant additives. The industry is now under intense scrutiny, with rigorous testing required before any new panel can be certified for use in tall structures.

The story of the sandwich panel is one of duality. It represents a triumph of materials science, offering lightweight strength that has revolutionized everything from aviation to architecture. It allows buildings to soar with sleek facades and thermal efficiency that old masonry cannot match. But it also serves as a cautionary tale about the consequences of prioritizing form over function, or cost over safety.

As we look to the future, the integration of 3D printing and new composite materials offers exciting possibilities for even more efficient structures. Yet, the lesson from Grenfell must remain etched into the industry's collective memory. A sandwich panel is only as safe as its core. In a world where buildings are getting taller and denser, the choice of that core material determines whether a structure protects its inhabitants or endangers them.

The versatility of sandwich panels ensures they will remain a cornerstone of modern construction. They appear in clean rooms protecting sterile environments, in cold storage preserving food supplies, and in iconic landmarks defining skylines. But their legacy is now permanently intertwined with the memory of those lost to fire. It serves as a reminder that in engineering, there are no small decisions. Every layer, every bond, and every material specification carries weight. The strength of a sandwich panel lies not just in its stiffness or its lightness, but in the integrity of its design to serve human needs without compromising human life.

The path forward requires a continued commitment to rigorous standards, transparent testing, and an unwavering focus on fire safety. As building codes evolve and new technologies emerge, the industry must ensure that the pursuit of innovation never again outpaces the necessity of protection. The sandwich panel is a marvel of modern engineering, but it demands respect. It is a material that can build the future, provided we remember the lessons of the past.
