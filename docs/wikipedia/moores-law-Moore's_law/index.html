Moore's law

5 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Moore's law

In 1965, a quiet engineer at Fairchild Semiconductor made a prediction that would reshape the world. Gordon Moore, then director of research and development, was asked to contribute to a special anniversary issue of Electronics magazine. His response was a brief article entitled "Cramming more components onto integrated circuits"—and within it lay an observation so accurate it would become the heartbeat of modern computing.

Moore had noticed something remarkable: the number of components per integrated circuit had been doubling every year. He extrapolated this trend forward, speculating that by 1975, a single quarter-square-inch of semiconductor—roughly 1.6 cm2—could contain up to 65,000 components. "The complexity for minimum component costs has increased at a rate of roughly a factor of two per year," he wrote. "Certainly over the short term this rate can be expected to continue, if not to increase." The phrase would become legendary—not because it was a physical law, but because it described an empirical reality that held for decades.

What Moore had captured was essentially a log-linear relationship between device complexity and time: as circuit density increased, costs per unit decreased. In a 2015 interview, Moore admitted frankly about his 1965 article: "I just did a wild extrapolation saying it's going to continue to double every year for the next 10 years." The boldness of that guess—thrown off the top of his head—became one of the most influential observations in technological history.

The observation carries Moore's name not because he discovered some immutable principle of nature, but because he documented what was already happening. One historian has noted this is partly due to Stigler's law of eponymy: the regular doubling of components was known to many working in the field, but Moore was the one who published it at the right moment. The name stuck.

By 1975, Moore revised his forecast. Looking at the decade ahead at the IEEE International Electron Devices Meeting, he predicted semiconductor complexity would continue doubling every year until about 1980—after which it would slow to approximately every two years. This compound annual growth rate of 41% became the benchmark. He outlined several factors driving this exponential behavior: the advent of metal-oxide-semiconductor technology, the exponential increase in die sizes coupled with decreased defective densities, finer minimum dimensions, and what he called "circuit and device cleverness."

Shortly after, Caltech professor Carver Mead popularized the term Moore's law. It became a rallying cry for the semiconductor industry—a target to aim for, a promise to keep. Intel and AMD didn't just follow it; they cited it as competitive doctrine. The observation transformed from description into aspiration.

Moore viewed his eponymous law with surprising humility: "Moore's law is a violation of Murphy's law. Everything gets better and larger." It was even seen as self-fulfilling prophecy—the industry oriented itself around doubling precisely because Moore had named it.

The doubling period is often misquoted as 18 months, and this confusion comes from an Intel executive named David House. In 1975, House noted that Moore's revised law of doubling transistor count every two years implied computer chip performance would roughly double every 18 months with no increase in power consumption. Mathematically, the combination of shrinking dimensions and Dennard scaling—Robert H. Dennard's formulation that as MOS transistors get smaller, their power density stays constant—meant energy efficiency roughly doubled every 18 months.

This relationship held for decades. As the cost of computing power fell for consumers, the cost for producers followed an opposite trend: R&D, manufacturing, and test costs increased steadily with each new generation of chips. The cost of extreme ultraviolet lithography machines—EUVL, the primary tools for manufacturing chips at modern scales—doubles roughly every four years.

This led to what became known as Moore's second law, or Rock's Law: the capital cost of a semiconductor fabrication plant increases exponentially over time. Each generation costs more than the last, pushing ever fewer companies to pursue cutting-edge manufacturing.

But the trend has shown signs of exhaustion. Microprocessor architects report that since approximately 2010, semiconductor advancement has slowed industry-wide, slightly below the pace predicted by Moore's law. Brian Krzanich, former CEO of Intel, cited Moore's 1975 revision as precedent for the current deceleration, calling it "a natural part of the history of Moore's law"—a result of technical challenges.

In September 2022, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang declared Moore's law dead. Intel's then-CEO Pat Gelsinger disagreed violently. The industry remains divided on when—or whether—the observation will end.

The evidence is mixed. As of 2019, leading manufacturers TSMC and Samsung Electronics claimed to keep pace with Moore's law, with 10, 7, and 5 nanometer nodes in mass production. But Dennard scaling—a principle that power consumption per area remained constant as chips shrank—ended in the mid-2000s.

Much of the industry has shifted focus toward major computing applications rather than pure semiconductor scaling. Yet Moore's observation remains foundational to how we understand modern technology: the phones in our pockets, the cameras capturing moments, the memory storing memories—all trace their lineage to this quiet insight born in 1965.
