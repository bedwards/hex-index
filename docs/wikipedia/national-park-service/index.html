National Park Service

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: National Park Service

On August 25, 1916, President Woodrow Wilson signed a piece of legislation that would fundamentally alter the relationship between the American people and their own land. The National Park Service Organic Act did not merely create an administrative body; it codified a moral promise into federal law. It mandated that this new agency "conserve the scenery and the natural and historic objects and wildlife therein, and to provide for the enjoyment of the same in such manner and by such means as will leave them unimpaired for the enjoyment of future generations." This phrase, often recited in visitor centers across the country, carries a weight that extends far beyond bureaucratic protocol. It is a covenant between the living and those yet unborn, a recognition that the wild places of North America are not infinite resources to be consumed, but fragile legacies to be guarded.

Today, the National Park Service (NPS) stands as one of the most recognizable and beloved agencies within the United States Department of the Interior. It is an entity of staggering scale, employing roughly 20,000 people who maintain a portfolio of 433 units spanning over 85 million acres. These lands stretch across all fifty states, the District of Columbia, and U.S. territories, encompassing everything from the frozen peaks of Denali to the sun-baked canyons of Arizona. But the story of how we came to protect these places is not a linear march of progress. It is a saga of conflicting philosophies, shifting scientific understanding, and the relentless pressure of human demand against ecological limits.

The concept of setting aside land for its own sake was radical in the 19th century. While European powers had long treated wilderness as something to be conquered or tamed, a lone American artist saw it differently. George Catlin, during his travels through the Dakotas in 1832, observed the encroaching destruction of indigenous civilizations and wildlife by settlers and traders. He foresaw a future where the "wild[ness] and freshness" of nature would be lost unless government intervention created a sanctuary.

A nation's Park, containing man and beast, in all the wild[ness] and freshness of their nature's beauty!

Catlin's vision was prophetic, but it took decades for it to become policy. The catalyst came with Yellowstone National Park in 1872. At that time, Wyoming was still a U.S. territory without a state government capable of managing such a vast wilderness. The federal government stepped in, but not with rangers or biologists; they sent the Army. For years, the protection of Yellowstone fell to cavalry units, including the famed African American Buffalo Soldier regiments. These soldiers, fighting for a country that often denied them their own civil rights, became the first guardians of the national park system. They patrolled the geysers and guarded the wildlife, establishing a precedent of federal stewardship that would eventually evolve into the modern NPS.

However, the parks were initially managed with little coordination or public support. It took the energy of a business magnate named Stephen Mather to change the trajectory. In 1915, Mather, backed by journalist Robert Sterling Yard, launched a publicity campaign designed to sell the idea of national parks to the American public and the political elite. They did not just write about scenery; they wrote about the spiritual and educational necessity of these lands. Their articles framed the parks as essential for the nation's soul, arguing that access to such grandeur was a civic right.

Their efforts culminated in the 1916 Organic Act, and Mather himself became the first director of the new service. Under his leadership, the NPS began to define its identity. But the agency was always balancing two competing mandates: preservation and public use. The law demanded that resources be left unimpaired, yet it also required them to be available for enjoyment. This tension would only grow more complex as the century wore on.

The Expansion of Stewardship

The scope of the NPS was not static. In 1933, a significant reorganization shifted the balance of power within the federal government. President Herbert Hoover signed the Reorganization Act of 1933, which granted the president the authority to transfer national monuments between departments. That summer, President Franklin D. Roosevelt used this power to reshape the landscape of American conservation.

On the suggestion of Horace M. Albright, who served as Deputy Director under Mather, Roosevelt issued two executive orders that transferred a massive portfolio of sites to the NPS. The War Department relinquished control of historic Civil War battlefields, while the Department of Agriculture handed over national monuments. Additionally, parks in and around Washington, D.C., previously managed by an independent federal office, were consolidated under the NPS umbrella.

This consolidation was more than administrative housekeeping; it signaled a new era where history and nature were seen as interconnected narratives. The NPS was no longer just about protecting geysers and forests; it was becoming the guardian of American memory itself. The agency now held the keys to Gettysburg, Antietam, and Vicksburg, places where the cost of national unity had been paid in blood.

The human dimension of these sites cannot be overstated. When rangers walk the fields of Gettysburg today, they are walking on ground soaked with the sweat, fear, and final moments of young men who died for a fractured nation. The NPS's role is to preserve the physical integrity of these battlefields while honoring the profound human tragedy that occurred there. This requires a sensitivity that goes beyond mere maintenance. It demands an acknowledgment that these landscapes are gravesites as much as they are recreational areas.

The Post-War Boom and Mission 66

The end of World War II brought a surge in domestic travel, fueled by the GI Bill, the expansion of the highway system, and a growing middle class with leisure time. The parks became the destination for millions of Americans seeking to reconnect with nature. But this popularity was a double-edged sword. By the early 1950s, the infrastructure of the national park system was crumbling under the weight of unprecedented demand. Roads were unpaved or non-existent, lodging was scarce, and sanitation facilities were inadequate. The parks were beloved, but they were broken.

In 1951, Conrad Wirth became director of the NPS with a clear mandate: fix the system. With the full support of President Dwight D. Eisenhower, Wirth launched "Mission 66" in 1952. This was a decade-long, billion-dollar initiative designed to upgrade and expand park facilities in time for the agency's 50th anniversary in 1966.

Mission 66 succeeded in bringing the parks up to modern standards. Visitor centers were built, roads were paved, and campsites were developed. The program allowed more people than ever before to experience the majesty of places like Grand Canyon and Yosemite. Yet, it also cemented a model of development that would later be criticized for prioritizing convenience over conservation. The very infrastructure that made parks accessible also brought them closer to destruction, introducing more vehicles, more noise, and more human impact into fragile ecosystems.

As the 1960s progressed, the philosophy of the NPS began to shift again. In 1966, under Director George Hartzog, the emphasis moved beyond preserving "great and wonderful scenery" toward making parks accessible for diverse recreational uses. The creation of National Lakeshores and National Recreation Areas signaled a broadening of the definition of what a national park could be. These new designations often allowed for more human activity and development than traditional parks, reflecting a society that valued recreation as a public good but struggled to define the limits of that access.

The Leopold Report: A New Science of Management

While infrastructure was being built, a quiet revolution was taking place in how the NPS thought about ecology. In 1963, Secretary of the Interior Stewart Udall appointed an advisory board on Wildlife Management to address growing concerns about how animals were being managed in the parks. The result was a fourteen-page document that would become the most influential report in the history of American conservation: the "Wildlife Management in the National Parks," commonly known as the Leopold Report, after its principal author A. Starker Leopold.

The Leopold Report was a watershed moment because it challenged the prevailing notion that parks were static museums where nature should be frozen in time. It argued that ecosystems are dynamic and that management must reflect scientific reality rather than sentimental ideals. The report tackled difficult issues head-on, such as the overpopulation of elk in Yellowstone, which was decimating vegetation, and the suppression of natural fires in California's Sequoia and Yosemite parks, a practice that had inadvertently created conditions for catastrophic wildfires.

The goal of managing the national parks and monuments should be to preserve, or where necessary to recreate, the ecologic scene as viewed by the first European visitors.

This sentence from the Leopold Report established a historical baseline that would guide ecological restoration for decades. It was a noble ambition: to return parks to how they looked before industrial expansion. But it also revealed a fundamental tension. Which "first European visitors"? When did their arrival begin? And more importantly, could an ecosystem ever truly be returned to a past state in a world that had changed so dramatically since the 19th century?

The report's recommendations were radical for their time. It suggested that fire suppression was a mistake and that wildlife populations should be managed through natural means or regulated culling if necessary. It forced park managers to confront the uncomfortable reality that "unimpaired" did not mean "untouched." Human intervention, carefully guided by science, was often required to maintain ecological integrity.

Revisiting Leopold in a Changing World

For nearly fifty years, the Leopold Report remained the bedrock of NPS policy. But as the 21st century dawned, the world began to change at an accelerating pace. Climate change, invasive species, and urban sprawl were altering landscapes faster than managers could adapt. The static baseline of "first European visitors" was becoming increasingly untenable.

In response, Director Jonathan Jarvis charged a twelve-member Science Committee to re-examine the ecological foundations of the NPS. Their findings, published in 2012 as "Revisiting Leopold: Resource Stewardship in the National Parks," acknowledged that the old rules no longer applied. The report called for a new approach, one that embraced uncertainty and managed for change rather than trying to hold back time.

New and emerging scientific disciplines — including conservation biology, global change science, and genomics — along with new technological tools like high-resolution remote sensing can provide significant information for constructing contemporary tactics for NPS stewardship.

The 2012 report was a call to action, urging the agency to become "science-informed at all organizational levels." Yet, it stopped short of providing specific tactics for the most pressing challenge of the era: climate change. It mentioned the phenomenon three times and the concept of "climate refugia" once, but it did not offer a roadmap for how rangers should respond when the very ecosystems they were sworn to protect began to disappear or transform beyond recognition.

The RAD Framework: Managing the Unmanageable

The gap between scientific understanding and management action was finally addressed in 2021 with the release of "Resist–Accept–Direct (RAD): A Framework for the 21st-century Natural Resource Manager." This ten-author report, a collaboration between the NPS, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, and the U.S. Geological Survey, marked a paradigm shift in conservation philosophy.

The RAD framework admitted what many had feared: that we can no longer simply resist change to maintain historical baselines. In some cases, managers must "Resist" human-induced changes to preserve specific ecological conditions. In others, they must "Accept" that change is inevitable and allow ecosystems to transition to new states. And in yet other scenarios, managers must "Direct" the change toward a desired future condition, actively intervening to guide the ecosystem through its transformation.

The convention of using baseline conditions to define goals for today's resource management is increasingly untenable, presenting practical and philosophical challenges for managers.

This framework acknowledges that the parks are not isolated islands but part of a rapidly changing global system. It requires park managers to make difficult, often heartbreaking decisions. Do we spend millions trying to keep a mountain lake ice-covered in a warming world? Or do we accept that the lake will melt earlier and adapt our management strategies for the species that depend on it? The RAD framework provides the language and tools to navigate these choices with integrity.

The Human Cost of Conservation

As the NPS evolves, it must also contend with the human stories embedded in its lands. The parks are not just collections of trees and rocks; they are places where indigenous peoples were displaced, where workers toiled in grueling conditions, and where visitors have found solace or tragedy.

The history of the Buffalo Soldiers is a testament to the complex racial dynamics of American conservation. These African American soldiers protected the parks while facing segregation and discrimination within their own ranks and society. Their contributions were often erased from the historical narrative until recent decades, when the NPS began to actively seek out and honor these stories.

Similarly, the creation of many parks involved the forced removal of indigenous communities. The "wilderness" that early visitors marveled at was often a landscape that had been managed for millennia by Native peoples, only to be emptied of its human inhabitants to create the illusion of pristine nature. Acknowledging this history is crucial for the NPS as it moves forward. It requires a reimagining of stewardship that includes indigenous knowledge and respects the deep connections between people and place.

The Volunteer Force

Today, the NPS relies not just on its 20,000 employees but on a massive volunteer force. In 2019 alone, more than 279,000 volunteers contributed their time and skills to the parks. From trail maintenance to visitor education, these individuals are the lifeblood of the agency. They represent a diverse cross-section of American society, united by a belief in the value of public lands.

This volunteerism reflects a broader cultural commitment to the idea that national parks belong to everyone. It is a democratic institution where citizens have a direct role in preservation. In a time when many public institutions face skepticism and underfunding, the NPS remains one of the few places where people across the political spectrum can find common ground.

A Future of Uncertainty and Hope

As we look toward the future, the National Park Service faces challenges that would have been unimaginable to Stephen Mather or Conrad Wirth. Climate change threatens to reshape the very landscapes they sought to protect. Sea-level rise endangers coastal parks like Everglades and Gateway National Recreation Area. Droughts and wildfires are becoming more frequent and severe in the West.

Yet, the core mission remains unchanged: to leave these places unimpaired for future generations. But what does "unimpaired" mean in a world of rapid change? The answer lies in flexibility, science, and a deep commitment to the public trust. It requires the courage to accept that some things cannot be saved in their current form and the wisdom to direct change toward resilient futures.

The story of the National Park Service is still being written. Every ranger who patrols a trail, every scientist who monitors a glacier, and every volunteer who picks up trash contributes to this narrative. They are the guardians of a promise made in 1916, a promise that our children and their children will have the opportunity to stand in awe of the same wonders we do today.

In the end, the parks are more than just government property. They are a mirror reflecting who we are as a society. Do we value short-term convenience over long-term survival? Do we see nature as a resource to be exploited or a legacy to be cherished? The choices made by the NPS and the public it serves will determine the answer. As we move further into the 21st century, the need for thoughtful, adaptive, and compassionate stewardship has never been greater. The parks await us, wild and fresh, ready to teach us what it means to be part of a world that is constantly changing.
