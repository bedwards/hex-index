Spinal Tap II: The End Continues

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Spinal Tap II: The End Continues

On December 14, 2025, the world lost Rob Reiner, a titan of American cinema, and his wife in a brutal double homicide, an event that forever altered the legacy of his final directorial effort. Just days prior, on December 15, Forbes Magazine had published a list of the top ten most streamed films on HBO Max, where Reiner's last project, Spinal Tap II: The End Continues, sat comfortably at number five. This placement was a bittersweet victory for a film that had struggled at the box office, grossing a mere $3.2 million against a production budget of $22.6 million, yet found a fervent second life in the digital realm after its premium video-on-demand debut on September 30, 2025. The film, released by Bleecker Street in the United States and internationally by Sony Pictures Releasing, was not merely a sequel to the 1984 cult classic This Is Spinal Tap; it was the final chapter in a decades-long saga involving the fictional heavy metal band Spinal Tap, written and directed by the original creative quartet: Reiner, Christopher Guest, Michael McKean, and Harry Shearer.

To understand the weight of The End Continues, one must first understand the legal odyssey that made its existence possible. For forty years following the original film's release, the creative team was embroiled in a protracted legal struggle over rights and profits, a conflict that nearly extinguished the band's legacy entirely. In 2016, Harry Shearer filed a landmark lawsuit against Vivendi and its StudioCanal division, alleging "Hollywood accounting" practices, trademark mismanagement, and grossly inadequate earnings. The numbers he presented were staggering in their absurdity: Shearer claimed to have received only $81 from merchandising and $98 from music sales over the course of decades. The lawsuit initially sought $400 million in damages and the return of rights under the Copyright Act, while a parallel suit against Universal Music Group targeted soundtrack royalties. It was not until a settlement was reached in 2019, and after four years of litigation, that the legal fog cleared. By late 2020, it was determined that from January 1, 2021, all rights, including the characters and trademarks, were finally secured for the original creators, paving the way for Reiner to pick up the camera again.

The Reunion of the Disparate

The narrative of Spinal Tap II begins where the first left off, but with the heavy, dust-mote-laden air of forty years of silence. Director Marty DiBergi, played by Rob Reiner himself, returns to the fold. In the intervening years, DiBergi has been unable to secure work in Hollywood, relegated to teaching at the Ed Wood School of Cinematic Arts, a detail that adds a layer of poignant meta-commentary to the film. His mission is to document the reunion and final show of the legendary rock band Spinal Tap, a task that requires him to track down the three original members, who have drifted into lives so bizarre they seem scripted by a fever dream.

Marty's journey begins in the north of England, specifically Berwick-upon-Tweed. Here, he finds Nigel Tufnel, the band's former lead guitarist and the man famous for his amplifiers that go to eleven. Tufnel no longer rocks out in stadiums; he runs a cheese-and-guitar shop, a business that perfectly encapsulates his eccentricity. He plays electric guitar in a local folk band, an instrument he shares time with a girlfriend named Moira and a repertoire that includes penny whistles and mandolins. The sight of Nigel, the master of distortion, gently cooing into a mandolin, is the first signal to the audience that the world of Spinal Tap has shifted from the manic to the melancholic.

Next, DiBergi travels to Morro Bay, California, to find David St. Hubbins. Once the charismatic frontman with the flowing hair and the voice of an angel, St. Hubbins has traded the stage for the sound booth. He now produces music for true-crime podcasts, specifically The Trouble with Murder, and composes the droning, soulless melodies for on-hold phone music. His personal life has undergone an even more radical transformation; his wife, Jeanine, has become a nun. The juxtaposition of the former rock god creating background noise for callers waiting for customer service against the backdrop of his wife's religious devotion creates a surreal tension that permeates the film.

The final member, Derek Smalls, is found in London, where he serves as the curator of the New Museum of Glue. Smalls, the bassist whose riffs once anchored the band's sound, has composed a symphonic work titled "Hell Toupee" and performs with a philharmonic orchestra. The image of Derek, the man of the low end, standing before a display of industrial adhesives and discussing the viscosity of glue, is a masterclass in the mockumentary's ability to find humor in the mundane. Despite the tension between Nigel and David, a tension that has festered for decades, the trio agrees to perform one last show. However, a significant hurdle remains: they need a drummer. As the film reminds the audience with grim humor, all of their previous drummers have mysteriously died.

The Catalyst and the Promoter

The catalyst for this reunion is not a sudden burst of inspiration, but a legal obligation inherited by Hope Faith, the daughter of the band's original manager, Ian Faith. Ian Faith, a pivotal character in the first film, died in 2021, and Hope, played by Valerie Franco, inherits a contract that mandates Tap perform one final concert. To navigate this, the band turns to a sleazy promoter named Simon Howler, a man who, as the film hilariously points out, does not understand music. Howler, played by Chris Addison, is the antithesis of a music industry veteran; he is a man of pure transaction, devoid of artistic sensibility.

The band travels to New Orleans to rehearse for the concert, which is set to fill an arena slot vacated by the adult film star Stormy Daniels. The choice of venue and the context of the slot underscore the band's descent from rock royalty to a curiosity act. Finding a drummer proves to be a nightmare. Questlove, Chad Smith, and Lars Ulrich all turn down requests to join the band, their refusals serving as a stark reminder of the band's diminished status. Auditions go badly until the band finds Didi Crockett, a spirited young rocker woman who brings a fresh, chaotic energy to the group. She is joined by keyboardist CJ "Caucasian Jerry" Vanston, a character whose very name is a joke on the band's history of racial insensitivity.

As the band prepares, the ghosts of their past return in the form of former manager Bobbi Flekman and PR man Artie Fufkin. Bobbi, played by Fran Drescher, reveals that the stress of managing Spinal Tap was so immense that she became a Buddhist, seeking peace in a world that once demanded her chaos. Artie, played by Paul Shaffer, has pivoted to selling tube men to car dealerships, a job that requires him to wave inflatable figures in the wind, a fitting metaphor for his former role as a hype man for a band that could barely stay upright.

The Ghost House and the Hall of Fame

The rehearsal phase is spent living in a "ghost house" tourist attraction, a setting that forces the band to endure being served a whole alligator supper while tourists with "ghost meters" wander through, measuring the spectral density of the house. It is in this surreal environment that the band reflects on aging and death. Derek Smalls, the bassist, composes a song titled "Rockin' in the Urn," a title that captures the band's morbid obsession with their own mortality.

The film also addresses the band's long-standing desire for recognition from the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame. In a moment of pure, unadulterated satire, it is revealed that the Hall of Fame rejected Tap's membership with a letter that read, "Fuck off, sincerely yours." This rejection leads Hope and Simon to propose that the band establish their own hall of fame. The design for this new institution resembles an IHOP site, featuring themselves as the primary exhibits, a self-aggrandizing move that highlights their inability to understand their own place in history.

Despite the friction, the band finds moments of connection. Nigel shows Marty a cavity in his guitar, a secret compartment designed for storing cheese and a grater, a detail that brings a smile to the audience's face even as it underscores Nigel's inability to let go of his quirks. As the band reflects on their legacy, they are visited by some of the biggest names in music. Paul McCartney drops by, offering advice and telling Marty that "Big Bottom" is "almost literature," a high praise that elevates the band's absurdity to the realm of art. Elton John also visits, though Simon mocks him. Elton, in a moment of grace, sings Tap's "Flower People" and agrees to sing "Stonehenge," the band's most famous (and disastrous) song.

The Final Show and the Tragedy

The climax of the film takes place in the New Orleans arena. The stage is set with a huge model of a woman's buttocks for the performance of "Big Bottom," a prop that emits flatulent sounds and annoys the band. Didi, the new drummer, requests that her drum platform be moved closer to the musicians, a small adjustment that will have catastrophic consequences. Derek makes an unsuccessful pass at Didi, who promptly calls her girlfriend into the room, a scene that highlights the band's continued inability to connect with the modern world.

Simon Howler, the promoter, tries to make the band exercise with a personal trainer, wanting them to dance like a K-pop boy band, a suggestion that is met with bewilderment and disgust. He had earlier proposed that at least one of the band members could die on stage to produce a financially valuable tribute opportunity, a plan that is eventually abandoned when Simon claims he must visit his birth mother and leaves the band.

The tension between Nigel and David comes to a head when David accuses Nigel of adultery with Jeanine, David's ex-wife and a nun. Nigel denies the accusation, but the fracture is deep. David, frustrated, takes a walk in New Orleans and sees a bluesman singing a song that reminds him of the good old days with Nigel. He returns to tell Nigel he forgives him, although Nigel still pleads his innocence. The reconciliation is fragile, a thin thread holding the band together as they prepare for the final show.

As the concert begins, the crowd cheers for Tap's performance of their old hits. The atmosphere is electric, a testament to the enduring appeal of the band. However, the disaster is inevitable. As "Stonehenge" begins, with Elton John at the piano, a huge stone monument descends from the ceiling. The monument teeters precariously on the edge of Didi's relocated drum platform. Two dwarves in druid robes, played by Brad Williams and Wee Man, accidentally knock it down. The stone crashes onto the piano, crushing Elton John and the other musicians. Elton screams, "Fuck Spinal Tap!", a line that serves as the film's final punchline before the tragedy.

The band recovers in a hospital room, battered but alive. In a final twist, Derek tells Marty that he was the one who had the affair with Jeanine, not Nigel. The truth, like so many things in the world of Spinal Tap, is a matter of perspective and miscommunication. As Marty congratulates Didi on being the only surviving Tap drummer, she chokes on her meal. Marty gives her the Heimlich maneuver, saving her life in a moment that is both life-affirming and absurd.

The Legacy of the End

Spinal Tap II: The End Continues is more than a comedy; it is a meditation on legacy, aging, and the absurdity of the music industry. It captures the essence of the original film while pushing the narrative into new, darker territory. The film's financial failure, grossing only $3.2 million on a $22.6 million budget, mirrors the band's own decline from rock gods to a forgotten curiosity. Yet, its success on HBO Max, where it reached number five in the top ten list, suggests that the spirit of Spinal Tap is alive and well, even in the digital age.

The film is a tribute to the four men who created it: Rob Reiner, Christopher Guest, Michael McKean, and Harry Shearer. Their collaboration, which began in 1984, has endured for forty years, surviving legal battles, personal changes, and the shifting tides of the entertainment industry. The fact that Reiner's final film was a Spinal Tap sequel, released just days before his untimely death, adds a layer of poignancy to the work. It is a final bow, a last laugh, and a reminder that even in the face of tragedy, the music goes on.

The cast of the film is a who's who of the original creative team and a host of new talent. Christopher Guest, Michael McKean, and Harry Shearer reprise their roles as Nigel, David, and Derek, bringing a depth of experience and a sense of history to their characters. Rob Reiner, as Marty DiBergi, provides the narrative anchor, guiding the audience through the band's journey. The supporting cast, including Valerie Franco, Kerry Godliman, June Chadwick, Fran Drescher, Paul Shaffer, and Chris Addison, adds layers of comedy and heart to the story.

The film also features a host of musicians appearing as themselves, including Paul McCartney, Elton John, Garth Brooks, Trisha Yearwood, Questlove, Chad Smith, Lars Ulrich, and Little Freddie King. Their appearances serve as a bridge between the fictional world of Spinal Tap and the real world of rock and roll, blurring the lines between fact and fiction in a way that only a mockumentary can.

In the end, Spinal Tap II: The End Continues is a testament to the enduring power of comedy and the human spirit. It is a film that makes you laugh, cry, and think, a rare combination that elevates it above the typical comedy sequel. It is a fitting end to a career that spanned decades and a final tribute to a band that never really existed, but whose impact on the world of music is undeniable. As the credits roll, the audience is left with a sense of loss and a sense of joy, a complex emotional response that is the hallmark of great art. The film is a reminder that even in the face of death and tragedy, there is always room for a little bit of rock and roll. And as the final scene shows, even a stone monument can't bring down the spirit of Spinal Tap. They may be old, they may be broken, and they may be a little bit crazy, but they are still Spinal Tap, and that is enough.
