Seraph Young Ford

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Seraph Young Ford

On February 14, 1870, a twenty-three-year-old schoolteacher named Seraph Young stepped into Council Hall in Salt Lake City, Utah, and cast a ballot. She was not a celebrity, a political firebrand, or a seasoned activist. She was a grandniece of the Mormon pioneer leader Brigham Young, the eldest of nine children, and a woman who had spent her life in the shadow of the Great Basin's harsh winters and the rigid structures of a theocratic society. Yet, in that single, quiet act of walking to a polling place and dropping a piece of paper into a box, she became the first woman in the United States to vote under a law that explicitly granted women equal suffrage to men. This was not a theoretical victory debated in the halls of Congress; it was a tangible, legal reality enacted in a territory that would soon become a state. While the rest of the nation remained entrenched in the belief that the ballot was the exclusive domain of men, Utah had just passed a law that shattered that assumption, and Seraph Young was the human face of that rupture.

The story of Seraph Young Ford is often reduced to a trivia question or a footnote in the broader narrative of the American women's suffrage movement, overshadowed by the more famous figures like Susan B. Anthony or Elizabeth Cady Stanton. But to dismiss her as merely the "first" is to ignore the profound context of her life and the sheer improbability of her action. Her vote was not an isolated incident of rebellion but the culmination of a specific legal and cultural convergence in the Utah Territory. It was a moment where the territorial legislature, driven by a complex mix of political maneuvering and genuine, albeit limited, belief in women's capabilities, unanimously passed a law extending voting rights to women. Acting territorial governor Stephen A. Mann signed the bill into law on February 12, 1870. Just forty-eight hours later, on February 14, the municipal election in Salt Lake City was held. Seraph Young, then teaching at the model school at the University of Deseret, was ready.

To understand the magnitude of this moment, one must first understand the world Seraph was born into. She arrived on November 6, 1846, in Winter Quarters, Nebraska, a temporary settlement for thousands of Mormon refugees fleeing persecution in the Midwest. Her parents were Cedenia Clark and Brigham Hamilton Young, a brother of the church president, Brigham Young. The following year, in a journey that defined the American West, the Young family migrated to the Great Basin, arriving in Salt Lake City in October 1847. They were part of the vanguard of the Mormon exodus, a people seeking religious freedom in the arid isolation of the desert. Seraph grew up in a society that was simultaneously insular and revolutionary. In Utah, women were given roles in the church and community that were unheard of elsewhere in the United States. They spoke in church meetings, ran businesses, and held positions of influence. Yet, they were still legally subordinate to men in the eyes of the federal government.

The passage of the women's suffrage law in 1870 was a strategic move as much as it was a moral one. The federal government was increasingly hostile toward the practice of polygamy in Utah, and the territorial legislature hoped that granting women the vote would demonstrate that Mormon women were not oppressed slaves but willing participants in their society. They believed that Mormon women, if given the ballot, would vote in support of their husbands and the church, thereby shielding the territory from federal interference. The law was passed unanimously, a rare display of political unity. But the legislature did not anticipate that the first person to exercise this new right would be a young teacher who had no intention of becoming a national symbol.

Seraph Young was twenty-three years old, a prime age for a woman in that era to be defining her future. She was intelligent, educated, and deeply embedded in the community. She taught at the university, a role that placed her at the intellectual center of the territory. When the election was called, she did not hesitate. She walked to Council Hall, the building that served as both the city hall and the seat of the territorial legislative assembly. The building itself was a testament to the ambition of the early pioneers, constructed of local materials and housing the machinery of government. Inside, the atmosphere was likely charged with a mixture of curiosity and skepticism. Men watched as women lined up to vote, many for the first time in their lives. Seraph Young was the first. Her vote was recorded, not as a spectacle, but as a matter of legal procedure.

The significance of Seraph's vote lies in the timing and the legal framework. While the Wyoming Territory had passed a women's suffrage law in 1869, the first election in Wyoming did not take place until September 6, 1870. Utah's municipal election on February 14, 1870, preceded the Wyoming vote by nearly seven months. This distinction is crucial. Seraph Young was not just the first woman to vote in Utah; she was the first woman to vote in the United States under a law that made women's voting rights equal to men's. The Wyoming law was passed earlier, but the actual casting of the ballot happened later. In the annals of history, the act of voting is the tangible proof of the right, and Seraph Young provided that proof first.

"Young's simple action made history."

This quote from the source material captures the essence of her achievement. It was a simple action, yes, but one that required a specific set of circumstances to occur. It required a law to be passed, a governor to sign it, an election to be scheduled, and a woman to be willing to walk into the room. Seraph Young did not set out to be a pioneer in the way that suffragists in the East did. She did not go on to lead a national movement, to organize marches, or to engage in the fiery rhetoric that characterized the fight for the 19th Amendment. She was a woman who exercised a right that had been given to her, and then she lived her life.

After her historic vote, Seraph Young's life took a different trajectory. In 1872, she married Seth L. Ford, a printer from Buffalo, New York, and a veteran of the Union Army during the American Civil War. Seth Ford was a man who had seen the brutality of war, having served in the conflict that tore the nation apart. His experiences in the war would haunt him for the rest of his life. The couple settled in Salt Lake City, where they had three children. Two of them survived to adulthood, a statistic that reflects the high mortality rates of the era even in the settled territories.

The story of Seraph Young Ford does not end with her marriage or her historic vote. It becomes a story of resilience, of care, and of the quiet, unglamorous labor that holds families together in the face of tragedy. As her husband's health deteriorated, the burden of supporting the family fell heavily on Seraph. Seth Ford had been wounded during the Civil War, and these wounds eventually left him blind and paralyzed. The man who had once marched to war for the Union now lay helpless in their home, unable to see or move. Seraph, the first woman to vote in the United States under equal suffrage, became a caregiver, a breadwinner, and a protector.

The couple moved east, leaving the familiar landscape of the Great Basin for the more humid and crowded cities of New York and Maryland. They lived most of their married life in these states, far from the Utah community that had witnessed her historic act. The financial strain was immense. With Seth unable to work and the costs of caring for a disabled veteran rising, the family struggled to make ends meet. For a time, they resorted to singing and playing the harmonica on street corners to raise money. It is a poignant image: Seraph Young Ford, the woman who had cast a ballot in a territory where women were equal to men, standing on a street corner in New York or Maryland, playing music to feed her children and care for her husband.

This period of her life highlights the disconnect between the legal victories of the suffrage movement and the daily realities of women's lives. Being granted the right to vote did not automatically grant women economic security or social power. Seraph Young Ford faced the same struggles that faced countless other women in the 19th and early 20th centuries. She had to navigate a world that was often hostile to women working outside the home, a world that offered little support for families with disabled members. Yet, she persevered. She cared for Seth until his death in 1910, a testament to her devotion and strength.

Seraph Young Ford died on June 22, 1938, at the age of ninety-one. She was buried next to her husband in Arlington National Cemetery, a final resting place that honored her husband's service to the country. Her headstone, however, bore a mistake that would go unnoticed for decades. It read "Serath" instead of "Seraph." It was not until 2020, more than eighty years after her death, that the error was corrected. This small detail serves as a metaphor for her place in history. For much of the 20th century, her name was misspelled, her story was obscured, and her contribution was minimized. She was a footnote, a minor character in a play that was supposed to be about the great suffragists.

But the tide has turned. In recent years, there has been a concerted effort to bring Seraph Young Ford's story to the forefront. In 2019, the Utah legislature recognized her vote and passed a bill designating February 14 as Women's Voter Registration Day. This is a fitting tribute, linking the day of her historic act to the ongoing work of ensuring that women continue to exercise their right to vote. The choice of Valentine's Day, a day associated with love and affection, adds a layer of irony and depth to the designation. It reminds us that the right to vote is an act of love for one's country, a commitment to its future, and a declaration of one's worth as a citizen.

On February 14, 2020, the 150th anniversary of Seraph Young's vote, a remembrance walk was held to Council Hall. The event was led by Better Days 2020, a public history non-profit dedicated to commemorating the centennial of the 19th Amendment and the role of Utah women in the suffrage movement. Salt Lake City mayor Erin Mendenhall spoke at the event, emphasizing the importance of Utah women's leadership in the suffrage movement and encouraging citizens to register and vote. The walk was a physical reclamation of the space where Seraph had once stood. It was a moment of collective memory, a way for the community to acknowledge the debt they owe to the woman who took that first step.

The visual legacy of Seraph Young Ford is also growing. A mural depicting her vote, created by artist David Koch, hangs in the House of Representatives chamber of the Utah State Capitol. This mural ensures that her image is visible to the lawmakers who craft the laws of the state. It is a constant reminder that the right to vote was not granted by the federal government, but by the people of Utah, and that the first person to exercise that right was a local woman. Furthermore, in 2020, a sculpture designed by Utah artists Kelsey Harrison and Jason Manning was installed on the lawn in front of Council Hall. This sculpture, approved by the Utah Capitol Preservation Board in 2019, serves as a permanent monument to her achievement. It stands in the very spot where the history was made, inviting passersby to reflect on the courage it took to walk into that building in 1870.

The story of Seraph Young Ford challenges the traditional narrative of the women's suffrage movement. That narrative often focuses on the women of the East Coast, the women who marched in the streets of Washington D.C., the women who were arrested and force-fed in prison. While those women are undeniably important, their story is incomplete without the story of Seraph Young Ford. She represents a different kind of suffragist, one who was not part of a national organization, one who did not seek fame or glory, but who simply did what was required of her. She voted because she could, and in doing so, she paved the way for millions of women who would follow.

Her life also serves as a reminder of the complexities of the suffrage movement. The law that allowed her to vote was passed in a territory that was practicing polygamy, a practice that was deeply controversial and eventually led to the disenfranchisement of Utah women in 1887 under the Edmunds-Tucker Act. The federal government revoked the voting rights of Utah women as a way to pressure the church to abandon polygamy. Women lost the right they had gained, and it was not until 1895, when Utah was admitted as a state with a new constitution, that women regained the right to vote. Seraph Young Ford's vote was a fleeting victory in the long struggle, but it was a victory nonetheless. It proved that women could vote, that they could participate in the political process, and that they were capable of making decisions that affected the future of their communities.

The human cost of the struggle for suffrage is often overlooked. It was not just a matter of passing laws; it was a matter of changing hearts and minds. It was a matter of women facing ridicule, hostility, and even violence for daring to assert their rights. Seraph Young Ford may not have faced the same level of public opposition as the suffragists in the East, but she still had to overcome the ingrained beliefs of her society. She had to believe that her vote mattered, that her voice was worth hearing, even when the world told her otherwise. Her ability to do so is a testament to her character and the strength of the community that supported her.

In the end, Seraph Young Ford's story is a story of quiet courage. It is a story of a woman who did not seek to be a hero but who became one by virtue of her actions. She was a teacher, a wife, a mother, and a caregiver. She was a woman who lived through the Civil War, the expansion of the West, and the fight for women's rights. She was a woman who faced personal tragedy with grace and resilience. And she was the first woman to vote in the United States under a law that granted women equal suffrage.

Her legacy is not just in the historical record, but in the lives of the women who followed her. Every woman who votes today stands on the shoulders of Seraph Young Ford. Every woman who runs for office, who speaks out, who demands her rights, is continuing the work that she began. The mural in the Capitol, the sculpture on the lawn, the designation of February 14 as Women's Voter Registration Day—these are not just tributes to a woman from the past. They are invitations to the present to remember the cost of freedom and the importance of participation.

The correction of her headstone in 2020 was a small act, but it was a significant one. It was a way of saying that Seraph Young Ford matters, that her name is important, and that her story should not be forgotten. It was a way of fixing a mistake that had persisted for too long. And it was a reminder that history is not just a collection of facts, but a living, breathing thing that is constantly being reinterpreted and rewritten. Seraph Young Ford's story is still being written, and it is up to us to ensure that it is told with the accuracy, respect, and depth that it deserves.

In a world that often feels divided and uncertain, the story of Seraph Young Ford offers a beacon of hope. It reminds us that change is possible, that progress can be made, and that one person can make a difference. It reminds us that the right to vote is a precious gift, one that was fought for and earned by women like Seraph. And it reminds us that the struggle for equality is not over, that there is still work to be done, and that we must all play our part in ensuring that the voices of all women are heard.

Seraph Young Ford did not live to see the 19th Amendment, which would finally grant women the right to vote nationwide in 1920. She died in 1938, nearly two decades after that victory. But her vote in 1870 was the spark that helped ignite the fire. It was the first step on a long journey, a journey that continues to this day. And as we look back on her life, we see not just a woman who voted, but a woman who believed in the power of the ballot, in the potential of her fellow citizens, and in the possibility of a better future. That belief is her true legacy, and it is one that we must carry forward.
