Single-family zoning

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Single-family zoning

In 1916, the Elmwood neighborhood of Berkeley, California, became the testing ground for a legal innovation that would eventually reshape the American landscape. Duncan McDuffie, a real estate developer, was not worried about traffic congestion or noise pollution. He was worried about a dancehall owned by a Black resident and the presence of Chinese laundries. McDuffie feared that if these entities were allowed to exist near the homes he was trying to sell to white families, property values would plummet. His solution was not to negotiate with the neighbors or to improve the quality of his own developments, but to lobby for a new type of land-use restriction that would legally forbid anything other than a detached house with a yard on that specific plot of land. This moment marked the birth of single-family zoning in the United States, a policy born not from a desire for orderly streets, but from a calculated effort to exclude minorities and maintain racial segregation through the power of the state.

Today, this obscure 20th-century mechanism stands as the single most powerful driver of housing inequality in North America. It is a form of exclusionary zoning that disallows townhomes, duplexes, and multifamily housing from being built on any plot of land carrying its designation. The result is a rigid landscape where 75% of the land zoned for residential use in many United States cities is reserved exclusively for single-family detached homes. In California, that figure exceeds 66%. In San Jose, it is 94%. In Arlington, Texas, 89%. In Seattle, 81%. Even in cities known for density, the footprint of this restriction is massive: 79% of Chicago and 75% of Los Angeles are locked into this single-use model. By contrast, Washington, D.C. sits at 36%, and New York City at a mere 15%. This is not an accident of geography or market forces; it is the result of deliberate legislative choices that have turned the American suburb into a fortress of class and race.

The Legal Architecture of Exclusion

To understand the persistence of single-family zoning, one must understand how it evolved to survive legal challenges. The early 20th century was a period of intense racial anxiety for white elites. Before 1916, many cities used explicit race-based zoning laws to keep Black residents out of white neighborhoods. However, the U.S. Supreme Court struck this down in 1917 in the case of Buchanan v. Warley, declaring that such statutes were unconstitutional violations of the Fourteenth Amendment. The court ruled that the government could not legally segregate neighborhoods by race.

But the architects of segregation were not deterred. They simply changed the language. They stopped talking about race and started talking about "property values" and "neighborhood character." In 1926, the Supreme Court delivered the green light for this new strategy in Euclid v. Ambler. In this landmark decision, the Court upheld a zoning ordinance in the village of Euclid, Ohio, that banned apartment buildings from residential zones. Justice George Sutherland, writing for the majority, famously described an apartment complex as a "mere parasite" on a neighborhood, suggesting that these structures were inherently detrimental to the quality of life for single-family homeowners. This ruling effectively validated single-family zoning as a legitimate exercise of police power.

The genius, and the tragedy, of this legal maneuver was its camouflage. By banning "density" rather than "Black people," municipalities could achieve the exact same demographic results without violating the Constitution. As Sonia Hirt, a professor of landscape architecture and urban planning at the University of Georgia, notes, "In the early 1900s, the racially and ethnically charged private restrictions of the late nineteenth century were temporarily overshadowed by the rise of municipal zoning ordinances with the same explicit intent." This shift allowed the machinery of segregation to continue operating under the guise of urban planning.

Even as courts began to dismantle other tools of segregation, single-family zoning remained the last line of defense. For decades, homeowners and neighborhood associations used restrictive covenants in property deeds to explicitly forbid the sale of homes to people of color. These covenants were legal and enforceable until the Supreme Court's 1948 decision in Shelley v. Kraemer, which ruled that courts could not enforce such private agreements. The Fair Housing Act of 1968 finally made the inclusion of these covenants illegal. Yet, by that time, the damage was done. The physical and legal barriers of single-family zoning had already been erected, creating a landscape where segregation was baked into the very soil of the city.

"Single-family zoning became basically the only option to try to maintain both race and class segregation." — Jessica Trounstine, associate professor of political science at the University of California, Merced.

The Economics of Scarcity

The human cost of this legal architecture is measured in dollars, but the mechanism is one of artificial scarcity. Single-family zoning restricts the supply of housing units on any given plot of land. If a neighborhood is zoned strictly for single-family homes, an owner cannot subdivide their large lot to build three smaller, more affordable homes. They cannot build a duplex to house two families. They are legally compelled to build only one. When demand for housing outstrips supply, prices rise. This is basic economics, but the suppression of supply is not a market force; it is a government mandate.

This restriction has profound consequences for the people who live in these cities. It pushes development into poor, minority communities or to the far fringes of the city, forcing lower-income residents to commute longer distances and pay more for transportation. It creates a scenario where local public employees—teachers, firefighters, and police officers—are often priced out of the very communities they serve. Even in municipalities that require these workers to live within city limits, the housing stock is so expensive that it becomes impossible for them to afford a home. The only exceptions are rarely granted, and when they are, they come with strings attached, such as requiring priority for existing residents, which does little to solve the systemic shortage.

The myth that density destroys property values has long been the primary argument used to defend single-family zoning. Homeowners believe that if their neighbors build an apartment building or a row of townhomes, their own home value will drop. Consequently, they view city planners not as public servants, but as wealth managers whose primary job is to protect their investment. According to Andrew Whittemore, a professor of city and regional planning at the University of North Carolina-Chapel Hill, this belief system has turned city planners into enforcers of asset inflation rather than guardians of the public good.

"In the United States, private profit as a result of zoning ordinances that preserved and enhanced 'investment values' was not only fully expected, it was a major zoning goal." — Sonia Hirt, professor of landscape architecture and urban planning at the University of Georgia.

This dynamic is unique to the American experience. In many other advanced nations, the detached single-family home is not considered incompatible with other forms of urbanization. As Sonia Hirt points out, "I could find no evidence in other countries that this particular form — the detached single-family home — is routinely, as in the United States, considered to be so incompatible with all other types of urbanization as to warrant a legally defined district all its own, a district where all other major land uses and building types are outlawed." In Europe and other parts of North America, a house might sit next to a small apartment building without a second thought. In the United States, the law demands a buffer, a buffer that costs money and excludes the poor.

The American Dream as a Wealth Transfer Mechanism

The reliance on single-family zoning is deeply intertwined with the American conception of the "Dream." In the United States, homeownership is not merely a way to secure shelter; it is the primary vehicle for wealth accumulation. For many families, a home is the only significant asset they will ever own. It is the source of their retirement security, their ability to pay for college, and their legacy for their children. Monica Prasad, a professor of sociology at Northwestern University, describes homeownership as a "lynchpin for the long-term distribution of resources," acting in many ways as a replacement for a robust welfare state.

Because the value of this asset is so critical to the stability of the middle class, protecting it becomes a moral imperative for voters. When zoning laws restrict supply and drive up prices, the existing homeowners see their net worth increase. They view this appreciation as a reward for their hard work and a sign of a healthy neighborhood. They do not see it as a transfer of wealth from the younger generation, the working class, and minorities to themselves. This creates a political feedback loop where the people who benefit from exclusionary zoning are the ones most likely to vote against changing it.

This system creates a stark divide between those who can afford to buy a house and those who cannot. The goal of limiting certain neighborhoods to only single-family homes meant that only families who could afford to buy an entire house could live there. There was no option to subdivide housing so that families who could not afford to buy the whole property could live in smaller, more affordable units. This effectively priced out social groups deemed "less desirable" by the developers and planners of the early 20th century, and it continues to do so today. The result is a society where wealth is increasingly concentrated in the hands of those who already own property, while those without it are locked out of the opportunity to build wealth.

The Rise of the YIMBY Movement

As the housing crisis has worsened, with rents soaring and homelessness becoming a visible crisis in major cities, the political tide has begun to turn. The "Not In My Backyard" (NIMBY) mindset, which has dominated local politics for decades, is facing a new challenger: the "Yes In My Backyard" (YIMBY) movement. These urbanists and activists argue that the only way to solve the housing shortage is to allow more housing to be built, specifically in the neighborhoods that have been locked down by single-family zoning.

The YIMBY argument is simple but radical: upzoning does not require that new housing be built; it merely allows for it. Changing a single-family zoning district to a multifamily residential district does not mandate that a homeowner tear down their house. It does not prohibit new single-family homes. It simply gives owners of those lots the flexibility to subdivide their property or build something other than a single-family home if they choose. It is a matter of removing a legal barrier, not a mandate to build.

Beginning in the late 2010s, this movement has gained significant traction. In 2019, Minnesota became the first state to effectively eliminate single-family zoning in Minneapolis, requiring cities to allow two units on every residential lot. California followed suit with a series of bills that require cities to approve two units and, under certain conditions, up to four units on single-family lots. Oregon has passed similar legislation. Cities like Charlottesville, Virginia, have started investigations into reforming their land-use regulations. These efforts are not just about economics; they are explicitly about addressing the racial inequities that arise from housing segregation. By allowing more diverse housing types, these reforms aim to break down the class and racial barriers that single-family zoning erected a century ago.

The resistance to these changes is fierce. Local homeowners often argue that increased density will change the "character" of their neighborhood, bring in more traffic, and lower property values. However, evidence suggests that these fears are often unfounded. In many cases, larger, more heterogeneous suburbs are more responsive to calls for change, likely because they already have a population that is harmed by these policies. The people who are priced out of their own neighborhoods are the ones pushing hardest for reform.

A Uniquely American Problem

The persistence of single-family zoning highlights a fundamental flaw in the American approach to urban planning. While other nations have managed to integrate density with quality of life, the United States has chosen a path of segregation and scarcity. This is not just a policy failure; it is a moral failure that has deepened the divide between rich and poor, and between white and non-white communities.

The legacy of Duncan McDuffie and the developers who followed him is a landscape where 75% of residential land in many cities is reserved for a single, expensive type of housing. This land use policy has turned cities into wealth managers, prioritizing the investment values of existing homeowners over the need for shelter for all. It has forced public servants to live outside the communities they serve and has made the American Dream inaccessible to millions of families.

The path forward requires a fundamental rethinking of what a neighborhood is supposed to be. It requires acknowledging that the single-family home is not the only valid form of housing, and that allowing diversity in housing types does not degrade a community; it enriches it. It requires recognizing that the "parasite" described by Justice Sutherland was not the apartment building, but the exclusionary mindset that sought to keep people out. As cities across the country begin to dismantle these barriers, they are not just changing zoning laws; they are attempting to heal the wounds of a century of segregation. The battle for housing justice is being fought in city councils and planning commissions, but the stakes are nothing less than the future of the American city itself.

The story of single-family zoning is a story of how a legal tool, designed to exclude, became the foundation of the American suburban ideal. It is a story of how the fear of the "other" was codified into law, and how that law continues to shape the lives of millions. As the 21st century unfolds, the question remains: will the United States continue to cling to a zoning system that prioritizes exclusion over inclusion, or will it finally embrace a more flexible, equitable, and diverse future? The answer lies in the hands of the voters, the planners, and the politicians who decide how the land is used. The time for change is now, and the cost of inaction is too high to ignore.

The data is clear, the history is documented, and the need is urgent. From the dancehalls of 1916 Berkeley to the modern housing crises of 2026, the thread is unbroken. Single-family zoning remains one of the most potent tools of segregation in the modern world, a legacy of a time when the law was used to enforce a racial hierarchy. Breaking that legacy requires more than just new laws; it requires a new vision of what a community can be. It requires a willingness to share space, to accept density, and to prioritize human need over property value. The road ahead is long, but the first step is to recognize that the walls we built were never necessary, and that they can be taken down.
