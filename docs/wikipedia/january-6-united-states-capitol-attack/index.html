January 6 United States Capitol attack

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: January 6 United States Capitol attack

They came with zip ties, tactical vests, and radios tuned to coordinated channels. Some carried flagpoles sharpened into spears. Others wore body armor emblazoned with neo-Nazi slogans. On January 6, 2021, a mob of several thousand stormed the United States Capitol building—the seat of American democracy—in a violent attempt to prevent the peaceful transfer of presidential power.

It was the only attempted coup d'état against the federal government in American history.

The Unraveling

To understand that chaotic afternoon, you have to rewind two months. On November 3, 2020, Americans voted in a presidential election that pitted incumbent Donald Trump against challenger Joe Biden. The race wasn't particularly close. Biden won 306 electoral votes to Trump's 232—ironically, the same margin by which Trump had defeated Hillary Clinton four years earlier, a result Trump had then called a "landslide."

But Trump refused to accept the outcome.

Within hours of polls closing, while millions of ballots were still being counted, Trump declared himself the winner and demanded that counting stop. This was unprecedented. American presidents who lose elections—from John Adams in 1800 to George H.W. Bush in 1992—concede gracefully. Even in genuinely disputed elections, like the razor-thin 2000 contest between George W. Bush and Al Gore, the loser eventually accepts the result and participates in the peaceful transfer of power.

Trump chose a different path.

Sixty Losses in Court

What followed was the most extensive legal challenge to a presidential election in American history. Trump's campaign and allies filed sixty lawsuits across the country, alleging fraud, irregularities, and misconduct. Two cases reached the Supreme Court, where three of the nine justices had been appointed by Trump himself.

Every single lawsuit failed.

The cases weren't dismissed on technicalities. Courts found that Trump's attorneys couldn't produce evidence of significant fraud because no such evidence existed. In some cases, Trump's own lawyers admitted in court that they weren't actually alleging fraud—they just wanted it to sound that way in press conferences. Federal and state officials, many of them Republicans, certified the results. Trump's own attorney general, William Barr, publicly stated the Justice Department had found no evidence of fraud that would change the election outcome.

When the legal strategy collapsed, Trump turned to pressure campaigns.

The Phone Calls

In early January, a recording emerged of Trump on the phone with Brad Raffensperger, Georgia's Republican Secretary of State. "I just want to find 11,780 votes," Trump said—one more than Biden's margin of victory in the state. Trump alternated between flattery, threats, and conspiracy theories during the hour-long call. Raffensperger refused to manufacture votes.

Trump made similar overtures to Republican governors, state legislators, and election officials across the country. He pushed a scheme to create fake slates of electors—essentially counterfeit documents claiming that Trump, not Biden, had won key states. These fake certificates would be sent to Washington where, Trump hoped, they would create enough confusion to derail the final certification of election results.

That certification was scheduled for January 6.

How Electoral Votes Work

Every four years, Americans technically don't vote directly for president. Instead, they vote for slates of "electors" who then cast the official votes. This system, called the Electoral College, is a quirk of the Constitution—a compromise between those who wanted Congress to choose the president and those who wanted direct popular election.

After each state certifies its results, the electors meet in their state capitals in December to formally cast their votes. Those certificates are then sent to Congress, where they're officially counted in a joint session on January 6. The sitting vice president presides over this count and announces the winner.

In 2021, that vice president was Mike Pence.

Trump fixated on this ceremony. He convinced himself—or at least claimed to believe—that Pence had the power to simply reject electoral votes from states Biden had won, or to send them back to state legislatures for "reconsideration." Constitutional scholars across the political spectrum called this theory absurd. The vice president's role in the count is purely ceremonial, like a referee announcing the final score of a game that's already been played.

Pence knew this. He told Trump he wouldn't do it. But Trump kept pushing.

"Be There, Will Be Wild"

On December 18, 2020, Trump posted on Twitter: "Big protest in D.C. on January 6th. Be there, will be wild!"

The message electrified his most fervent supporters. Planning began immediately on forums, social media platforms, and encrypted messaging apps. Far-right groups that had supported Trump throughout his presidency—the Proud Boys, the Oath Keepers, the Three Percenters—started coordinating. These weren't spontaneous expressions of political frustration. They were organized paramilitary operations.

The Oath Keepers, a militia group that recruits current and former law enforcement and military personnel, held encrypted conference calls outlining plans to use force to stop the transfer of power. They established a "Quick Reaction Force" with an arsenal of weapons staged in Alexandria, Virginia, just across the Potomac River from Washington. They even researched boat transportation in case police closed the bridges into the city.

The Proud Boys, a self-described "Western chauvinist" organization that the Federal Bureau of Investigation classifies as an extremist group, raised money through crowdfunding to purchase tactical equipment and radios. In mid-December, some members were photographed wearing shirts with the slogan "6MWE"—shorthand for "Six Million Wasn't Enough," a reference to the Holocaust. Their leader, Enrique Tarrio, coordinated with Oath Keepers leader Stewart Rhodes.

None of this was secret.

Planned in Plain Sight

In the weeks before January 6, there were over one million mentions of storming the Capitol on social media. Users shared maps of the building's layout, including the tunnel system connecting it to House and Senate office buildings. They discussed bringing weapons, "physically removing" members of Congress, and attacking police.

On December 28, someone posted a tactical map marking Capitol entrances and exits. Black X's indicated where forces would position themselves "ready for action." The operator of a website about the Capitol tunnels noticed a sudden spike in traffic and contacted the FBI to warn of a likely attack.

The FBI's field offices issued warnings from late December through early January: armed protests were planned at every state capitol, and Washington, D.C. was the centerpiece. One widely-shared post from December 30 read: "I'm thinking it will be literal war on that day. Where we'll storm offices and physically remove and even kill all the D.C. traitors and reclaim the country."

News organizations published stories about the threats. The Daily Beast detailed plans to bring guns and attack federal buildings. Organizations that monitor online extremism issued urgent warnings. D.C. Mayor Muriel Bowser told residents to avoid downtown. Some businesses near the planned protest route closed for safety.

Capitol Police intelligence circulated an internal memo three days before the attack: Trump supporters "see the day of the Electoral College vote count as the last opportunity to overturn the results of the presidential election" and could use violence against "Congress itself."

Yet somehow, the Capitol remained lightly defended.

The Morning of January 6

Washington woke to an unusual chill. Tens of thousands of Trump supporters had flooded into the city, filling hotels and spilling onto the National Mall. Many wore red MAGA hats and waved American flags. Others wore tactical gear and carried weapons—some openly, others concealed.

At 10 a.m., about a hundred Proud Boys gathered at the Washington Monument. They'd been ordered to dress in plain clothes rather than their usual black and yellow, to blend in with the crowd. Leaders distributed radios programmed to specific channels. Then they marched toward the Capitol—not to the rally on the Ellipse where Trump would speak, but directly to their target.

At noon, Trump took the stage at the "Save America" rally on the Ellipse, a park just south of the White House. For over an hour, he repeated false claims about election fraud. He named specific enemies: Democrats, the media, weak Republicans, Mike Pence. He told the crowd that Pence had the power to change the outcome but might lack the courage to use it.

"If you don't fight like hell," Trump said, "you're not going to have a country anymore."

He told them to march to the Capitol.

The Breach

By early afternoon, thousands had gathered at the Capitol's west side. The building sits atop a hill, its white dome visible across the city—a symbol of American democracy recognizable around the world. That day, it looked like a medieval castle under siege.

The first barriers fell around 1 p.m. Rioters overwhelmed the thin line of Capitol Police, pushing through metal fencing and surging onto the Capitol grounds. Officers were beaten with flagpoles, sprayed with chemicals, and crushed against doors. Some rioters erected a gallows on the lawn. Others chanted "Hang Mike Pence."

Inside the building, Congress had just begun the ceremonial counting of electoral votes. Vice President Pence presided from the dais. When Arizona's results came up, Republican members objected, triggering a two-hour debate. The proceedings were mundane, procedural—and about to be violently interrupted.

At 2:11 p.m., rioters smashed a window on the Senate side of the Capitol and climbed through. Within minutes, the building was breached at multiple points. Secret Service agents rushed Pence to a secure location; he came within 40 feet of the mob. Capitol Police evacuated the Senate chamber and began barricading the House, where representatives were still on the floor.

The FBI estimates that between 2,000 and 2,500 people entered the Capitol during the attack.

Inside the Siege

For several hours, the Capitol—the building where Congress has met since 1800, which has survived the Civil War and two world wars—belonged to the mob.

Rioters wandered through Statuary Hall, posing for photos with marble figures of American heroes. They ransacked offices, stealing laptops and documents. One man put his feet up on House Speaker Nancy Pelosi's desk and left a note: "We will not back down." Others smeared feces on the walls. In the Senate chamber, a shirtless man wearing a horned fur headdress and face paint posed at the vice president's desk.

But the violence was real. Capitol Police officer Brian Sicknick collapsed after being assaulted by rioters who sprayed him with a chemical irritant; he died of a stroke the following day. Rioters beat officers with hockey sticks, crutches, and poles flying American and Confederate flags. One officer lost an eye. Another was stabbed with a metal fence stake. Over 140 police officers were injured.

On the House side, rioters came within feet of the chamber where members were sheltering. A group tried to break through a barricaded door. On the other side, Capitol Police officer Lieutenant Michael Byrd watched as a woman in the mob attempted to climb through a broken window into the Speaker's Lobby. He fired once. Ashli Babbitt, a 35-year-old Air Force veteran and QAnon follower, fell dead.

Elsewhere in the Capitol complex, pipe bombs had been planted the night before at the headquarters of both the Democratic and Republican National Committees, just blocks from the Capitol. They were discovered that afternoon but did not detonate. A truck parked near the Capitol contained Molotov cocktails—homemade firebombs—and an assault rifle.

Where Was Help?

As the Capitol fell, frantic calls went out for reinforcements. The National Guard, D.C. police, FBI tactical teams—everyone was needed. But for hours, they didn't come.

The delays remain controversial. What's clear is that Trump resisted sending help. Multiple officials later testified that they couldn't get authorization to deploy the National Guard. When aides urged Trump to call off the mob, he refused. When they asked him to send troops, he declined. He sat in the White House watching the chaos on television.

At 4:17 p.m., more than two hours after the breach, Trump finally released a video message. He told the rioters, "We love you. You're very special." He repeated his false claims about the election. And then, finally, he told them to go home.

It wasn't until evening that National Guard troops from D.C., Virginia, and Maryland arrived in force. The Capitol was cleared and secured by 8 p.m. Members of Congress, shaken but determined, reconvened to complete the electoral count. At 3:41 a.m. on January 7, Vice President Pence announced the final tally: Joe Biden had won.

The Aftermath

Five people died within 36 hours of the attack. Babbitt was shot by police. Another rioter died of a drug overdose. Three others, including officer Sicknick, died of natural causes related to the day's events. In the following months, four police officers who responded to the attack took their own lives.

Damage to the Capitol exceeded $2.7 million.

A week after the attack, the House of Representatives impeached Trump for "incitement of insurrection." It was his second impeachment—making him the only president in American history to be impeached twice. The Senate trial, held after Trump had left office, resulted in a 57-43 vote for conviction. That was a bipartisan majority, with seven Republicans joining all Democrats, but it fell short of the two-thirds required to convict. Trump was acquitted.

Criminal prosecutions moved faster. As of early 2025, over 1,400 people had been charged with federal crimes related to the attack. More than a thousand pleaded guilty. Sixty-four percent of those sentenced received jail time. Leaders of the Proud Boys and Oath Keepers were convicted of seditious conspiracy—a rarely-used Civil War-era charge for attempting to overthrow the government by force. Enrique Tarrio received 22 years in prison, the longest sentence.

Then, on January 20, 2025—exactly four years after Biden's inauguration and the day Trump returned to the presidency following his 2024 election victory—Trump granted clemency to everyone convicted of January 6 crimes, including those who had committed violent attacks on police officers.

The Memory Wars

Perhaps the strangest aspect of January 6 is what happened to its memory.

Within months, many Republican politicians who had condemned the attack began minimizing it. Some called the rioters "tourists." Others suggested the real insurrectionists were Antifa activists disguised as Trump supporters—a conspiracy theory contradicted by mountains of evidence and the identities of those arrested. Trump himself called the convicted rioters "hostages" and "patriots," promising pardons if reelected.

The House select committee investigating the attack—composed of seven Democrats and two Republicans—held public hearings in 2022 that laid out evidence of Trump's role in organizing and encouraging the mob. They voted to refer criminal charges against Trump to the Justice Department. A special counsel investigation followed, resulting in a four-count federal indictment of Trump.

Those charges were dismissed after Trump won the 2024 election.

What It Means

January 6 exposed something about American democracy that most citizens prefer not to think about: its fragility.

The peaceful transfer of power—a president losing an election and handing authority to his successor—is not guaranteed by law alone. It depends on norms, on shared agreement that the system's legitimacy matters more than any individual's hold on power. Every American president before Trump had honored this norm, even when losing hurt.

John Adams, the second president, lost to Thomas Jefferson in 1800 in a bitter, ugly campaign. He left the White House before dawn on Inauguration Day rather than attend the ceremony, but he left. Gerald Ford, who had never been elected to national office and desperately wanted his own term, lost to Jimmy Carter in 1976. He conceded graciously. George H.W. Bush, blindsided by his 1992 loss to Bill Clinton, wrote a letter wishing his successor well: "Your success is now our country's success. I am rooting hard for you."

These weren't just courtesies. They were the connective tissue of democracy—the mutual agreement that while we fight fiercely over power, we accept the verdict of voters.

On January 6, 2021, that agreement was tested as it had never been tested before. A sitting president encouraged a violent mob to prevent his successor from taking office. The mob got closer to its goal than many people realize. Had Pence been captured by rioters chanting for his execution, had members of Congress been taken hostage, had the building burned—any of these outcomes was possible that afternoon.

American democracy survived January 6. Whether it learned from it remains an open question.
