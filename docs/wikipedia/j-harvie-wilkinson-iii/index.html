J. Harvie Wilkinson III

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: J. Harvie Wilkinson III

In 1959, a young James Harvie Wilkinson III sat in a Richmond classroom while the state of Virginia was actively dismantling its public school system to avoid desegregation. His father, J. Harvie Wilkinson Jr., CEO of State Planters Bank, stood on the other side of the ideological divide from the segregationist hardliners. Alongside figures like Stuart Saunders and Lewis F. Powell, Wilkinson Sr. supported Governor J. Lindsay Almond in his controversial decision to break with the Byrd Organization and comply with federal court rulings that declared Massive Resistance unconstitutional. This was not a distant political maneuver for the young Wilkinson; it was the texture of his childhood, a lesson in how deeply law could penetrate the fabric of daily life and family loyalty. Born on September 29, 1944, in New York City but raised in the heat of Richmond's racial battles, Wilkinson would grow up to become one of the most influential conservative jurists of the modern era, serving on the United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit since 1984.

The path from that turbulent classroom in Virginia to the federal bench was neither straight nor accidental. It was forged through a series of deliberate choices that often placed Wilkinson at odds with his peers or the prevailing political winds of his youth. After attending St. Christopher's School during the height of the segregation crisis, he moved north to the Lawrenceville School in New Jersey before entering Yale University in 1965. At Yale, Wilkinson was not merely a student; he was a leader who embraced the conservative movement at a time when it was still finding its footing on elite campuses. He chaired the Conservative Party of the Yale Political Union and eventually became its president. But his intellectual curiosity outpaced his political organizing. By 1967, having graduated with honors with a Bachelor of Arts degree, he had already penned his first book, Harry Byrd and The Changing Face of Virginia Politics, 1945–1966. At age twenty-three, this young man was dissecting the very political machine that had once dominated his home state, analyzing how the old guard of Virginia politics was fracturing under the weight of federal pressure and social change.

The late 1960s were a time of national reckoning, and Wilkinson felt the pull to serve. In 1968, he enlisted in the United States Army, serving until 1969. Yet, even as he left the military, his focus remained fixed on law and governance. He enrolled at the University of Virginia School of Law in Charlottesville, but his legal education was interrupted by a bold, if ill-fated, political experiment. In 1970, at the age of twenty-five, Wilkinson dropped out of law school to run for the United States House of Representatives against David E. Satterfield III, a three-term Democratic incumbent. The race was a mismatch in resources and experience, and Wilkinson lost by a significant margin. He later recalled the humiliation with dry humor, noting that Satterfield had erected billboards urging voters to send him back to law school rather than to Congress. That defeat, however, was a crucial pivot point. It taught Wilkinson the limits of electoral politics and reinforced his belief in the power of institutions. He returned to UVA, completed his Juris Doctor in 1972, and passed the Virginia bar exam.

His post-law school trajectory was defined by an unusual refusal to join the corporate legal elite. Instead of a high-powered firm in Washington or New York, Wilkinson chose to clerk for Justice Lewis F. Powell Jr., a family friend who had just joined the Supreme Court. From 1972 to 1973, Wilkinson watched the highest court in the land from the inside. This experience was transformative, offering him a view of judicial philosophy that went beyond theory. He captured these insights in his second book, Serving Justice: A Supreme Court Clerk's View (1974). But unlike many clerks who used their service as a springboard to lucrative private practice, Wilkinson returned to Charlottesville. For five years, he served as an associate professor at the University of Virginia School of Law, immersing himself in academia and continuing his role as a public intellectual.

Wilkinson's commitment to understanding the practical mechanics of society led him away from the ivory tower for three years between 1978 and 1981. He took a job with The Virginian-Pilot in Norfolk, eventually becoming its editorial page editor. This stint was not merely a career detour; it was a deliberate immersion into the pulse of the community he would later serve as a judge. He credited this period with broadening his practical experience of government at all levels and connecting him with people from every walk of life. It also sharpened his time management skills and honed his ability to communicate complex legal ideas to a general audience. During this era, he published From Brown to Bakke in 1979, a critical examination of affirmative action and the evolving landscape of civil rights law. The book demonstrated his deep engagement with the most contentious social issues of the time, arguing for a nuanced approach that respected individual merit while acknowledging historical realities.

By 1982, Wilkinson had returned to the legal arena, joining the Civil Rights Division of the U.S. Department of Justice as deputy assistant attorney general. His resume was a mosaic of law, academia, journalism, and public service. In 1983, he briefly returned to teaching at UVA as a full professor before President Ronald Reagan nominated him to the United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit on November 10, 1983. The seat had been vacated by Judge John D. Butzner Jr., and the nomination process was far from smooth. Wilkinson faced controversy during his hearings, which took place in November 1983 and February 1984. Critics questioned his conservative leanings and his past political activities. Yet, on August 9, 1984, the United States Senate confirmed him by a vote of 58–39. He received his commission four days later.

Upon taking his seat on the Fourth Circuit, Wilkinson quickly established himself as a jurist of significant intellect and moral seriousness. He was not a judge who sought to be a political actor from the bench; rather, he viewed the judiciary as a guardian of constitutional principles that often required resisting political pressure from all sides. From 1996 to 2003, he served as chief judge of the circuit, a role that demanded administrative acumen and judicial leadership. During his tenure as chief judge, he wrote One Nation Indivisible: How Ethnic Separatism Threatens America (1997), a book reflecting his deep concern for national unity in an increasingly polarized society.

The post-9/11 era presented Wilkinson with some of the most difficult legal questions of his career. In 2003, he wrote the majority opinion in Hamdi v. Rumsfeld (before it went to the Supreme Court), upholding the right of the United States government to detain Yaser Esam Hamdi indefinitely without access to counsel or a court. Hamdi was a U.S. citizen captured during the invasion of Afghanistan and labeled an "enemy combatant." Wilkinson's decision reflected a deference to executive power in times of war, a stance that prioritized national security concerns over traditional due process rights for citizens detained domestically. The Supreme Court ultimately overturned this specific aspect of his ruling, asserting that Hamdi had a right to contest his detention before a neutral decision-maker. This reversal highlighted the tension between Wilkinson's judicial philosophy and the broader constitutional consensus on civil liberties during wartime.

For years, Wilkinson was viewed as a potential candidate for the Supreme Court. As early as August 2000, speculation mounted that he could be a nominee if George W. Bush won the presidency. When Chief Justice William Rehnquist fell ill in late 2004, Wilkinson's name remained on the shortlist as a potential replacement. After Bush's re-election in 2004, he was widely considered one of the president's favorites for the position of Chief Justice. However, his candidacy took a fateful turn when he agreed to an interview with The New York Times. The interview, intended to showcase his judicial temperament, reportedly undermined his standing within the Bush inner circle, and the nomination eventually went to John Roberts.

Despite missing the Supreme Court appointment, Wilkinson continued to shape American law through his opinions on the Fourth Circuit. His jurisprudence was often marked by a conservative restraint, but also by a distinct moral clarity that sometimes alienated him from the most rigid factions of his own party. In 2006, he penned an op-ed in The Washington Post on the issue of gay marriage, castigating both the political left and right for their approaches to the debate. He argued that the "American constitutional tradition" had become a casualty in the struggle over same-sex marriage. Rather than rushing to constitutionalize the dispute through amendments or federal mandates, Wilkinson believed marriage should be regulated through ordinary legislative means. This position reflected his deep skepticism of judicial activism, regardless of which side of the political spectrum it favored.

Wilkinson's views on abortion were equally firm and often controversial. On June 24, 2008, in a concurring opinion in Richmond Medical Center For Women v. Herring, he upheld a Virginia ban on partial-birth abortions. His language was stark and unflinching. He wrote:

"The fact is that we—civilized people—are retreating to the haven of our Constitution to justify dismembering a partly born child and crushing its skull. Surely centuries hence, people will look back on this gruesome practice done in the name of fundamental law by a society of high achievement. And they will shudder."

This passage revealed the visceral moral weight Wilkinson brought to his judicial work. He did not see abortion as merely a legal abstraction but as a profound ethical issue with tangible human consequences. His voice carried the gravity of someone who had spent decades studying the intersection of law and morality.

In 2012, Wilkinson published his fifth book, Cosmic Constitutional Theory: Why Americans Are Losing Their Inalienable Right to Self-Governance. The title itself was a critique of what he saw as an overreach by the judiciary into areas that should be left to democratic processes. He argued against the idea of "living constitutionalism" that allowed judges to impose their own values under the guise of interpretation. For Wilkinson, the Constitution was a fixed document that required restraint from those who interpreted it.

His commitment to these principles extended to diverse legal issues. In 2013, he wrote an opinion upholding the Baltimore Ravens' use of their "Flying B" logo in videos and photographs as fair use, navigating the complex intersection of intellectual property and sports branding. But his most significant recent battles involved religious freedom and the Establishment Clause. In 2016, Wilkinson dissented when a panel of the Fourth Circuit, led by Judge G. Steven Agee, found that sectarian prayers offered by Rowan County, North Carolina commissioners at their meetings did not violate the Constitution. The full circuit en banc later rejected this judgment in a 10–5 vote, with Wilkinson writing for the majority, while Agee and Paul V. Niemeyer dissented. This reversal was later challenged, and in June 2018, the Supreme Court denied review of the case over the written dissent of Justice Clarence Thomas, joined by Neil Gorsuch.

The controversy surrounding sectarian prayers continued to define Wilkinson's later years on the bench. In March 2018, he wrote a powerful dissent when the circuit denied an en banc rehearing to a divided panel that had concluded the Bladensburg Peace Cross memorial from World War I violated the Establishment Clause. The cross, a massive memorial to fallen soldiers, stood at a busy intersection in Maryland. Wilkinson argued that removing such historic monuments would be a violation of history and tradition, severing the connection between the nation's past and its present identity. He believed that the court had misapplied the Constitution by ignoring the historical context of the cross as a symbol of sacrifice rather than an endorsement of religion. The Fourth Circuit's judgment was eventually reversed by the U.S. Supreme Court in American Legion v. American Humanist Association (2019), vindicating Wilkinson's position.

Even in his later years, Wilkinson remained intellectually active and willing to engage with complex constitutional questions. In August 2018, he wrote for a panel majority that found the Constitution's Eighth Amendment did not prevent Virginia from prosecuting certain actions, continuing to apply his rigorous legal analysis to contemporary issues. His work on the bench has been characterized by a consistent theme: a deep respect for the rule of law and a belief that the judiciary should be careful not to usurp the role of the people in a democracy.

Wilkinson's personal life has also reflected his commitment to service and family. He is married to Lossie Wilkinson, and they have two children. Their daughter, Porter Wilkinson, followed in her father's footsteps into the legal profession, clerking for Chief Justice John Roberts at the Supreme Court from 2007 to 2008. She is now a prominent appellate attorney, married to Jeff Wall, another leading figure in constitutional law. The Wilkinson family name has become synonymous with a certain breed of thoughtful, principled conservatism that values both tradition and the rule of law.

James Harvie Wilkinson III's career spans more than half a century of American history, from the segregation crises of the 1950s to the polarized politics of the 2020s. He has been a clerk, a professor, an editor, a government official, and a judge. Throughout it all, he has maintained a consistent voice, one that challenges easy answers and demands a deeper engagement with the moral and legal complexities of our time. Whether writing about the dangers of ethnic separatism, the sanctity of life in abortion cases, or the preservation of historical monuments, Wilkinson has always sought to ground his arguments in a broader understanding of human dignity and constitutional order.

His legacy is not just in the cases he decided but in the questions he forced others to ask. He challenged judges to consider the long-term consequences of their rulings on society. He urged politicians to respect the limits of their power. And he reminded the public that the Constitution is a living document only if the people who interpret it remain faithful to its original spirit while addressing the needs of a changing world.

In an era where legal decisions are often viewed through the lens of political victory or defeat, Wilkinson stands as a reminder that the law is something more. It is a framework for human coexistence, a set of principles that must be applied with wisdom and compassion. His journey from a boy in Richmond during the Massive Resistance crisis to one of the most respected judges on the Fourth Circuit illustrates the power of a life dedicated to public service. He has shown that it is possible to hold firm convictions without losing the capacity for nuance, and to advocate for conservative principles without sacrificing the human element at the heart of every legal dispute.

As he continues his tenure on the bench, Wilkinson remains a vital voice in the American legal landscape. His writings, both in books and opinions, offer a roadmap for navigating the turbulent waters of modern jurisprudence. They remind us that the law is not just a set of rules but a reflection of our collective values. And in a world that often seems to be losing its way, his steady hand and clear vision provide a beacon for those who believe in the enduring power of the Constitution.
