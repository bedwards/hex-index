New Spain

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: New Spain

In 1521, Hernán Cortés stood in the smoking ruins of Tenochtitlan, the Aztec capital, and decided to build a new world on top of the old one. He called it New Spain. Within three centuries, this territory would stretch from the jungles of Central America to the frozen coasts of Alaska, from the Caribbean to the Philippines. It was not merely a colony. It was an empire within an empire, and its silver would reshape the global economy in ways Cortés could never have imagined.

The Birth of a Viceroyalty

Cortés had a problem. He had just conquered one of the most sophisticated civilizations in the Americas, and now Queen Isabella's heirs wanted to take it away from him.

The Spanish Crown had learned a hard lesson from Christopher Columbus. Give a conquistador too much power, and he might start thinking he's a king. So in 1535, fourteen years after the conquest, Spain created the Viceroyalty of New Spain. They appointed Antonio de Mendoza as viceroy, meaning "in place of the king." Mendoza was an aristocrat, loyal to the monarchy, chosen specifically because he was not Hernán Cortés.

This wasn't personal spite. It was policy. The Crown wanted direct control over what was clearly going to be an extraordinarily valuable territory. And valuable it was. New Spain became the first of only two viceroyalties Spain would establish in the Americas for the next two centuries. The second was Peru, created in 1542 after the conquest of the Inca Empire. Both territories had something Spain desperately wanted: dense indigenous populations who could be put to work, and vast deposits of silver waiting to be pulled from the earth.

An Empire of Staggering Scope

It's difficult to grasp how much territory New Spain claimed. The viceroyalty included all of modern Mexico, of course. But it also encompassed Central America down to Panama. The entire American Southwest: California, Arizona, New Mexico, Nevada, Utah, Colorado, Texas. Florida. Louisiana and the vast Mississippi River basin. The Caribbean islands, including Cuba, Hispaniola, Puerto Rico, and Jamaica.

And that's just the Americas.

New Spain also governed the Philippines, Guam, the Mariana Islands, the Caroline Islands, parts of Taiwan (which the Spanish called Formosa, meaning "beautiful island"), and chunks of the Moluccas, the legendary Spice Islands of Indonesia. The sun quite literally never set on this dominion.

Here's the thing about claiming territory, though: saying you own something and actually controlling it are very different matters. Spain asserted sovereignty over this vast realm, but huge swaths of it remained beyond effective control. The British, French, and Dutch all established colonies in territories Spain insisted belonged to the Spanish Crown. The northern borderlands, what American historians would later call the "Spanish borderlands," attracted few settlers. They were arid, mountainous, and populated by nomadic indigenous groups who had no interest in being conquered.

In a particularly cruel irony of history, California's massive gold deposits were discovered in 1848, immediately after Mexico lost the territory to the United States in the Mexican-American War. Spain had controlled California for centuries without ever finding what lay beneath its soil.

The Silver That Changed Everything

The 1540s brought a discovery that would transform New Spain from a conquest into an economic engine. Silver. Enormous deposits of it, centered on the city of Zacatecas in the mountainous north.

Silver mining didn't just enrich Spain. It fundamentally altered the global economy. Spanish silver pieces became the world's first truly international currency, accepted from London to Beijing. The Manila galleon, a fleet of ships that sailed annually between Acapulco on Mexico's Pacific coast and Manila in the Philippines, created the first regular transpacific trade route in history. Chinese silk and porcelain flowed east; Mexican silver flowed west. New Spain became the crucial link connecting Spain's Atlantic empire with its Pacific possessions.

The mines demanded labor. Lots of it. The indigenous population of central Mexico, which may have numbered around twenty million at the time of conquest, plummeted to perhaps one million within a century, devastated by European diseases like smallpox and measles against which they had no immunity. This catastrophic population collapse would have lasting consequences for the colony's labor systems and its treatment of indigenous peoples.

A Land of Regions

New Spain wasn't a monolithic territory. It developed distinct regional characters shaped by climate, geography, proximity to ports, the nature of local indigenous populations, and whether or not there was silver in the ground.

Central and southern Mexico had the densest indigenous populations. These were the descendants of the Aztecs, Maya, Zapotecs, and dozens of other civilizations with complex social and political structures. They became the workforce for Spanish haciendas and enterprises, but they also maintained their own communities, languages, and modified versions of their cultural practices.

The north was a different world entirely. Arid, mountainous, hostile to agriculture, and populated by indigenous groups the Spanish found impossible to subdue. The Chichimeca War, fought between 1550 and 1590, pitted Spanish settlers and their indigenous allies against the nomadic peoples of the northern frontier. Unlike the conquest of the Aztec Empire, which took just two years, this forty-year conflict demonstrated the limits of Spanish power against mobile, unconquered peoples.

To establish some presence in the north, Spain relied on a combination of religious missions and military forts called presidios. Franciscan friars would attempt to convert indigenous peoples to Christianity and settle them into mission communities. Soldiers in the presidios provided protection and, when necessary, coercion. This dual system extended Spanish influence into what is now California, Arizona, New Mexico, and Texas, though control remained tenuous.

How New Spain Was Governed

The viceroy, ruling from the capital of Mexico City, sat atop an elaborate administrative hierarchy. But the viceroyalty was too vast for one person to manage directly. Power was delegated through multiple layers of government, each with its own powers and jealously guarded prerogatives.

The audiencias were high courts that combined judicial, administrative, and legislative functions. Think of them as regional supreme courts that also issued regulations and oversaw local governance. The first audiencia in New Spain was established in 1527, before the viceroyalty itself existed. Its first president, Nuño de Guzmán, was a bitter rival of Cortés who used his position to strip the conqueror of power and property. The Crown eventually dissolved this corrupt tribunal and established a second, more respectable audiencia.

Below the audiencias were captaincies general, semi-autonomous military and political commands in regions facing security threats. Guatemala and the Philippines were organized as captaincies general, giving their commanders significant independence from the viceroy in Mexico City. The viceroy himself served as captain-general for provinces remaining under his direct command.

At the local level, more than two hundred districts were governed by officials called corregidores or by town councils called cabildos. These local governments handled the day-to-day administration of justice and community affairs. In indigenous communities, traditional leaders often continued to govern under Spanish supervision, creating a hybrid system that preserved some elements of pre-conquest political organization.

The Bourbon Reforms: When Spain Tried to Tighten Its Grip

For nearly two centuries, this sprawling administrative system worked well enough. The Crown received its silver, the Church converted souls, and local elites accumulated wealth and influence. But by the eighteenth century, Spain had a new ruling dynasty, the Bourbons, and they had new ideas about how an empire should be run.

The Bourbon Reforms were a sweeping attempt to modernize and centralize colonial administration. The Crown created new administrative units called intendancies, with officials whose broad fiscal powers cut into the authority of viceroys, audiencias, and local councils alike. The goal was to break the power of entrenched local elites and extract more revenue for the Crown.

As you might expect, these reforms were not popular with the people whose power they threatened. Viceroys resisted. Audiencias protested. Church officials objected to Crown interference in economic matters. The reforms created resentment among the American-born Spanish population, the criollos, who found themselves increasingly marginalized in favor of administrators sent directly from Spain.

In a particularly dramatic move, the Crown expelled the Jesuits from all Spanish territories in 1767. The Society of Jesus had accumulated enormous wealth and influence in the Americas, running missions, schools, and haciendas. Their sudden removal, carried out with military precision, shocked colonial society and removed one of the most capable organizations from the educational and economic landscape.

Ironically, the intendancy boundaries that the Bourbons created would outlast Spanish rule entirely. They became the basis for the states of independent Mexico and the nations of Central America. The administrative lines drawn to strengthen colonial control ended up defining post-colonial identities.

Society in New Spain: Who Ruled, Who Served

Colonial society was organized into a rigid hierarchy, at least in theory. At the top sat the peninsulares, Spanish-born subjects who had crossed the Atlantic. Just below them were the criollos, people of Spanish descent born in the Americas. Then came a complex array of mixed-race categories: mestizos (Spanish and indigenous), mulatos (Spanish and African), and dozens of other classifications that Spanish colonial law attempted to codify.

Indigenous peoples, though legally protected as subjects of the Crown and theoretically not eligible for enslavement, occupied subordinate positions. Africans, brought to New Spain as enslaved laborers, occupied the bottom of the hierarchy.

In practice, this system was messier than the legal categories suggested. Wealth could blur racial lines. Indigenous communities maintained internal hierarchies and negotiated directly with Spanish authorities. By the eighteenth century, demographic changes and economic shifts had created new opportunities for social mobility, even as the official categories remained in place.

The colonial period also produced a distinctive legal culture. Courts handled an enormous volume of disputes, and both indigenous communities and Spanish settlers learned to use the legal system to assert their rights. Villages fought over land boundaries. Workers petitioned against abusive employers. The legal archives of New Spain contain millions of documents recording these negotiations and conflicts, evidence of a society where even the marginalized had some access to formal mechanisms of redress.

The End of New Spain

Three centuries of colonial rule ended not with a whimper but with a prolonged and bloody war.

The crisis began in 1808, when Napoleon invaded Spain and forced the Spanish king, Charles IV, to abdicate. This created a constitutional emergency throughout the Spanish Empire. If the legitimate king was a prisoner, who held sovereignty? In New Spain, the viceroy José de Iturrigaray attempted to convene a congress to address this question. Spanish-born peninsulares, fearing that the American-born criollos would seize power, staged a coup and arrested the viceroy.

This resolved nothing. Resentment continued to build until September 16, 1810, when a parish priest named Miguel Hidalgo y Costilla issued his famous Grito de Dolores, the Cry of Dolores, calling for an uprising against Spanish rule. What followed was eleven years of brutal warfare.

The insurgency went through multiple phases and leaders. Hidalgo was captured and executed in 1811. José María Morelos, another priest and brilliant military commander, took up the cause before he too was executed in 1815. By 1820, the insurgency seemed to be failing.

Then came an unexpected twist. Agustín de Iturbide, a royalist military officer who had spent years fighting the insurgents, switched sides. He allied with the remaining insurgent commander, Vicente Guerrero, and together they launched a final campaign for independence. In 1821, New Spain ceased to exist. The Mexican Empire was born, with Iturbide as its first emperor.

The Legacy of Three Centuries

What was New Spain? It was an experiment in governing a territory so vast that messages could take months to travel from one end to the other. It was a laboratory for mixing cultures, languages, and peoples in ways both creative and coercive. It was an economic engine that pumped silver into the global economy and connected Asia, the Americas, and Europe in new commercial networks.

The boundaries established under colonial rule still mark the maps of North and Central America. The legal traditions, architectural styles, and cultural practices that developed during those three centuries continue to shape life in Mexico and beyond. The Spanish language that spread throughout this territory remains the daily speech of hundreds of millions of people.

And perhaps most striking of all: the descendants of those who built, resisted, and survived New Spain are still here, still negotiating questions of identity, territory, and sovereignty that began when Hernán Cortés looked at the ruins of Tenochtitlan and decided to give it a Spanish name.
