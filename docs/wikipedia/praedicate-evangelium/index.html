Praedicate evangelium

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Praedicate evangelium

On March 19, 2022, Saint Joseph's Day, the ninth anniversary of Pope Francis's own papal inauguration, the Vatican issued a document that would fundamentally alter the machinery of the Catholic Church for the first time in over three decades. Titled Praedicate evangelium—Latin for "Preach the gospel"—this apostolic constitution did not merely tweak the existing bureaucracy; it replaced the 1988 framework established by Pope John Paul II, known as Pastor bonus, with a vision that prioritizes missionary zeal over administrative preservation. The document, which took effect on June 5, 2022, the Feast of Pentecost, represents the culmination of a nine-year effort by the Council of Cardinals, a body Pope Francis created in 2013 specifically to oversee the reform of the Roman Curia. This was not a sudden decree but the result of a sustained, often painstaking, dialogue that began even before Francis ascended to the throne of Peter, when cardinals at pre-conclave meetings in 2013 explicitly requested a restructuring of the Church's central government.

To understand the gravity of Praedicate evangelium, one must first understand what the Roman Curia is and how it had functioned for centuries. Traditionally, the Curia has been described as the "institution which the Roman Pontiff ordinarily makes use of in the exercise of his supreme pastoral office and his universal mission in the world." For decades, under the shadow of Pastor bonus, this institution operated with a distinct hierarchy where the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith reigned supreme. It was the guardian of orthodoxy, the internal watchdog, and the most powerful department in the Vatican. Its prefect was, by custom, the second most powerful man in the Church after the Pope. This structure reflected a Church that was primarily defensive, focused on maintaining doctrinal purity and administrative order. Praedicate evangelium shatters this paradigm. In the new order, the most important department is no longer the guardian of doctrine, but the Dicastery for Evangelization. Doctrine has been relegated to a secondary, albeit still critical, position, closely followed by the Dicastery for the Service of Charity. This reordering is not merely a change in the table of contents; it is a declaration that the primary identity of the Church is missionary. The document explicitly states that its goal is to "better harmonize the present exercise of the Curia's service with the path of evangelization that the church, especially in this season, is living."

The journey from the initial idea to the final promulgation was a marathon of ecclesiastical diplomacy. After establishing the Council of Cardinals in 2013, Pope Francis and his advisors quickly realized that the planned changes were too significant to be mere amendments to Pastor bonus. The council decided early on that a completely new constitution was necessary. The work began in earnest in 2014, with the first draft approved in 2018. This draft was not kept in the vaults of the Vatican; it was sent out into the world in the spring of 2019 to the heads of current Vatican offices, bishops' conferences globally, and various experts. The feedback loop was rigorous. By February 2020, the Council of Cardinals had re-read the constitution, incorporating revisions recommended by the dicasteries themselves and "some experts." This iterative process continued through the pandemic. In December 2020, the council met online to study amendments proposed by the consulted dicasteries, ensuring that the voices of the various departments were heard even as the structure they inhabited was being dismantled and rebuilt.

Cardinal Pietro Parolin, the Secretary of State, served as a key voice throughout this transition. In a January 29, 2021 interview, he noted that "considerable progress" had already been made, particularly regarding Vatican finances. He pointed to the creation of the Council for the Economy, the Secretariat for the Economy, and the Office of the Revisor General as evidence that the administrative skeleton was already shifting before the legal skin was fully applied. Parolin also hinted at specific structural consolidations that would be formalized in the new constitution: the fusion of the Congregation for the Evangelization of Peoples with the Pontifical Council for Promoting the New Evangelization, and the merging of the Congregation for Catholic Education with the Pontifical Council for Culture. His assessment was clear: "Now it is a question of giving homogeneity to all the reforms which have been made." By July 2021, the text was in the hands of canonists, working to adapt the terminology to ensure the document possessed the proper juridical character. The promise was made that the constitution would be published before the end of that year, a timeline that was met with the promulgation on March 19, 2022.

The immediate impact of Praedicate evangelium was felt in the very language used to describe the relationship between the Pope, the bishops, and the Curia. The document makes a radical theological and structural claim: "The Roman Curia is not placed between the pope and the bishops, rather it is at the service of both according to the modalities that are proper to each." This sentence dismantles the historical tendency of the Curia to act as a gatekeeper or a filter between the universal pontiff and the local churches. Instead, the Curia is redefined as a service body, a mechanism to facilitate the mission of both the Pope and the bishops. Furthermore, the constitution establishes a new principle of equality among the various bodies of the Curia. It states that the Roman Curia is composed of the Secretariat of State, the Dicasteries, and other bodies, "all juridically equal to each other." This eliminates the rigid hierarchy that had previously distinguished Congregations from Councils, streamlining the bureaucracy into a more fluid and responsive structure.

Perhaps the most striking departure from the past is the opening of the highest offices of the Vatican to the laity. Under Pastor bonus, leadership positions within the Curia were effectively reserved for bishops and cardinals. Praedicate evangelium changes this entirely. It states that baptised lay Catholics, both men and women, can be appointed to head dicasteries or other bodies of the Roman Curia. The document goes so far as to describe the role of the Catholic laity in the governing of the Curia as "essential." The rationale is grounded in the unique perspective that laypeople bring: their familiarity with family life and "social reality." While the constitution allows for this openness, it includes a caveat: the specific statutes of those departments can still mandate that they be headed by bishops or cardinals if necessary. However, the default position has shifted from exclusion to inclusion. This change reflects a broader vision where the Church's administration is not a clerical enclave but a reflection of the entire People of God.

The criteria for selecting these officials have also been overhauled to prioritize merit and transparency. The constitution mandates that Curial officials be selected "according to objective and transparency criteria." It requires that appointees possess "an adequate number of years of experience in pastoral activities" before they are brought to the center of the Church. This is a direct counter to the historical practice of promoting administrators who had never set foot in a parish or engaged in direct pastoral work. The document also emphasizes the universality of the Church, stating that department officials should, as much as possible, "[reflect] the universality of the Church" by hailing from different countries around the world. The goal is to ensure that the Roman Curia is not a reflection of a single cultural or geographic perspective but a truly global body.

Term limits were introduced to prevent the entrenchment of power and to encourage the circulation of talent. Heads of each Vatican department—prefects, secretaries, undersecretaries, and other major officials—can now only exercise their function for a five-year term. This is a significant departure from the life-tenure or indefinite terms that were common in the past. Lower-level officials are also subject to a five-year term, after which they are expected to return to their diocese or religious institute. This rotation is designed to keep the Curia connected to the reality of the local Church. The Pope retains the discretion to extend any of these terms, but the default expectation is a renewal of personnel every half-decade. To ensure that this new structure functions with unity and purpose, the constitution mandates that all heads of each Vatican department meet regularly with the Pope. Previously, the custom was that only the heads of the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith and the Congregation for Bishops had this privilege. Now, the entire leadership of the Curia is summoned to the Pope's table, fostering a culture of collective responsibility and direct communication.

The document also institutionalizes collaboration between different departments. Regular internal meetings of departments and meetings between heads of different departments are to take place. These inter-department meetings are to be organized by the Secretary of State with the approval of the Pope. This mechanism is designed to break down the silos that had allowed various dicasteries to operate in isolation, often pursuing conflicting agendas. By forcing interaction and coordination, the constitution aims to create a more coherent and unified Vatican administration. The text confirms changes that Pope Francis had already made, such as the restructuring of the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith in February 2022, effectively codifying his early reforms into permanent law. The initial release of the document was in Italian, with the official "typical edition" published in L'Osservatore Romano on March 31, 2022. This edition contained some differences from the version initially released, highlighting the precision and legal weight of the final text.

The significance of the order of dicasteries in the new constitution cannot be overstated, even if the text itself claims the order has no juridical effect. During the press conference presenting the document, speaker Marco Mellino noted that while the list of dicasteries was not legally binding, the placement of the first three was likely symbolic and significant. Evangelization comes first, followed by Doctrine, and then Charity. This sequence tells a story: the Church exists to preach, it must be faithful to the truth, and it must serve the poor. The Dicastery for Evangelization, created by merging two previous entities, sits at the apex of the Curia's priorities. It is no longer an afterthought or a specialized branch; it is the driving force of the entire apparatus. This shift answers the question of why the reform was necessary. The old Curia, built for a Church that was perhaps more defensive and administrative, was ill-suited for a Church called to be missionary. Praedicate evangelium is the legal instrument that reorients the entire Vatican toward the mission of the Gospel.

The reform also addresses the economic and administrative aspects that had long been a source of controversy and scandal within the Vatican. Cardinal Parolin had already pointed to the creation of the Council for the Economy and the Secretariat for the Economy as evidence that the reform was "already underway" in these areas. The new constitution enshrines these changes, providing a coherent framework for the financial and administrative transparency that Pope Francis has sought to achieve. The emphasis on "missionary conversion" is not just spiritual; it is organizational. It implies that the way the Church manages its resources, its personnel, and its relationships must be converted to serve the mission of evangelization. The document is a comprehensive blueprint for a Church that is no longer content to maintain the status quo but is eager to engage with the modern world.

In the months following the promulgation, the implementation of Praedicate evangelium has continued to reshape the landscape of the Vatican. The fusion of dicasteries, the appointment of lay leaders, and the introduction of term limits are all unfolding according to the schedule set out in the constitution. The document has effectively abrogated and replaced Pastor bonus, ending an era that began in 1988. It is a testament to the fact that the Roman Curia is not a static institution but a living body that must evolve to meet the needs of the time. The nine years of work by the Council of Cardinals, the input of bishops' conferences, and the guidance of the Pope have resulted in a document that is both legally robust and spiritually profound. It is a document that looks forward, setting the course for the Church's governance in the 21st century.

The story of Praedicate evangelium is also a story of patience and persistence. From the initial request by cardinals in 2013 to the final promulgation in 2022, the process was marked by delays, revisions, and careful deliberation. It was not a top-down imposition but a collaborative effort that sought to balance the need for reform with the stability of the Church. The result is a constitution that is deeply rooted in the tradition of the Church while boldly charting a new course. It is a document that speaks to the heart of Pope Francis's papacy: a Church that is poor, missionary, and open to the world. The reordering of the dicasteries, the inclusion of the laity, and the emphasis on synodality are not mere administrative details; they are the expression of a new ecclesiology. The Roman Curia, once a fortress of tradition, is now being transformed into a bridge for the Gospel.

As the Church moves forward under this new framework, the impact of Praedicate evangelium will be felt in the daily operations of the Vatican and in the lives of Catholics around the world. The shift in priority from doctrine to evangelization changes the way the Church speaks to the world. The opening of leadership to the laity changes the way the Church is governed. The emphasis on transparency and term limits changes the way the Church manages its resources. These changes are not without their challenges, and the road ahead will require vigilance and commitment. However, the foundation has been laid. The constitution has been promulgated, the structure has been redefined, and the mission has been clarified. The Church is now equipped with a new instrument for its universal mission, one that is designed to be more effective, more transparent, and more faithful to the call of the Gospel. The era of Pastor bonus has ended, and the era of Praedicate evangelium has begun.

The document's title, "Preach the Gospel," serves as a constant reminder of the purpose of the Curia. It is not an end in itself but a means to an end. The ultimate goal is the evangelization of the world. Every meeting, every appointment, every financial decision, and every policy must be measured against this standard. The constitution provides the legal and spiritual framework for this mission, ensuring that the Roman Curia remains a servant of the Church and not its master. In a world that is increasingly complex and challenging, the Church needs a Curia that is agile, responsive, and deeply committed to its mission. Praedicate evangelium is the answer to that need. It is a document that looks to the future with hope and confidence, trusting that the Holy Spirit will guide the Church in its mission to preach the Gospel to all nations. The journey of reform continues, but the direction is now clear. The Church is on the move, and the Curia is its engine, retooled and ready for the work ahead.
