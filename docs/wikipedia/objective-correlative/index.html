Objective correlative

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Objective correlative

In 1919, T.S. Eliot dropped a phrase into a literary essay that would haunt the corridors of criticism for over a century, a phrase he later admitted had taken on a life of its own that astonished him. The phrase was "objective correlative." It appeared in his essay "Hamlet and His Problems," republished in his collection The Sacred Wood: Essays on Poetry and Criticism, and it offered a seemingly simple, yet devastatingly precise solution to the messy, uncontrollable nature of human emotion in art. Eliot argued that the only way to express emotion in the form of art is by finding an "objective correlative"; in other words, a set of objects, a situation, a chain of events which shall be the formula of that particular emotion. When the external facts, which must terminate in sensory experience, are given, the emotion is immediately evoked.

This concept, which Eliot claimed was the secret to artistic "inevitability," was not entirely his invention. The term was actually coined decades earlier by the American painter and poet Washington Allston (1779–1843), a figure known for his romantic landscapes and his influence on the American literary scene. However, it was Eliot who introduced the term, rather casually, into the lexicon of high modernism, and whose specific application of it became the standard against which generations of poets and playwrights would be measured.

The core of the objective correlative is a rejection of the direct description of feeling. It is a mechanism that relies on the external world to trigger an internal response. Eliot, who is associated with the literary group called the New Critics, believed that an author could not simply state that a character is sad, or angry, or terrified, and expect the audience to feel it. Instead, the author must construct a specific cluster of circumstances—a broken mirror, a cold dinner, a recurring sound—that acts as a formula. When the reader or viewer encounters this formula, the emotion is not described; it is summoned.

The Ghost in the Machine

To understand the power and the controversy of this theory, one must look at the text that spawned it: Shakespeare's Hamlet. Eliot's assessment of the play is famously harsh, describing it as "most certainly an artistic failure." This was a bold claim for a critic to make about the most famous play in the English language, but it was based entirely on the mechanics of the objective correlative.

Eliot felt that the feelings of Hamlet, the protagonist, were not sufficiently supported by the story and the other characters surrounding him. In the play, Hamlet is paralyzed by a grief and a horror that seem to exceed the facts of the situation. He has a father murdered, a mother who remarried too quickly, and an uncle who is the murderer. Yet, the emotion Hamlet displays—the profound melancholy, the existential dread, the manic energy—feels to Eliot to be disproportionate to the "facts" provided by the plot.

According to Eliot, the feelings of Hamlet are not sufficiently supported by the story and the other characters surrounding him. He argues that Shakespeare "tackled a problem which proved too much for him." The Prince's emotions, Eliot suggests, "exceeded the facts" of the play. There was no objective correlative in the text that could fully contain or explain the magnitude of Hamlet's internal turmoil. The external events were not adequate to the emotion being portrayed.

"The artistic 'inevitability' lies in this complete adequacy of the external to the emotion…."

This quote from Eliot, referring to Lady Macbeth, serves as the counter-example to Hamlet. For Eliot, Lady Macbeth is a triumph of the objective correlative. Her sleepwalking scene, where she obsessively tries to wash imaginary blood from her hands, provides a perfect external formula for her internal guilt. The action of scrubbing, the smell of the blood, the darkness of the night—these are the "objects" and "chain of events" that perfectly match the emotion of guilt. When the audience sees this, they do not need to be told she is guilty; the sensory experience evokes the emotion immediately.

In contrast, Hamlet's internal state remains a mystery to the audience because the play fails to provide the necessary external equivalents. The tragedy, for Eliot, is that Shakespeare could not find the formula. He tried to represent a state of mind that was too complex for the dramatic tools available to him. Eliot acknowledged that such a circumstance—where emotion exceeds the facts—is "something which every person of sensibility has known," but he felt that in trying to represent it dramatically, Shakespeare failed.

From Plato to the New Critics

The objective correlative is not just a modern invention; it is a refinement of an ancient distinction. The concept reaches back to the roots of Western aesthetics, specifically to the work of Plato. As Peter Barry notes in his book Beginning Theory: An Introduction to Literary and Cultural Theory, the objective correlative is perhaps little more than the ancient distinction (first made by Plato) between mimesis and diegesis.

Mimesis is showing. Diegesis is telling. The objective correlative is the ultimate tool of mimesis. It is the literary equivalent of "show, don't tell," but elevated to a structural principle of the work. The purpose of the objective correlative is to express the character's emotions by showing rather than describing feelings. It moves the burden of emotional transmission from the narrator's voice to the world of the story itself.

According to Formalist critics, this action of creating an emotion through external factors and evidence linked together should produce an author's detachment from the depicted character. This detachment is crucial. If the author is too close to the character, if they simply pour their own feelings into the text without the filter of an objective formula, the result is often sentimentality or self-indulgence. The objective correlative unites the emotion of the literary work by grounding it in the concrete, shared reality of the objects and events. It creates a bridge between the subjective experience of the character and the objective experience of the reader.

This theory became a cornerstone of the New Criticism movement, which dominated literary analysis in the mid-20th century. The New Critics were obsessed with the text itself, treating it as a self-contained object that could be analyzed without reference to the author's biography or the historical context. The objective correlative fit perfectly into this framework. It was a technique that could be identified, isolated, and evaluated within the text. If the external facts matched the emotion, the work was successful. If they did not, it was a failure.

The Formula and the Fallacy

While Eliot's theory provided a powerful tool for analysis, it also invited criticism. The central assumption of the objective correlative is that there is a one-to-one correspondence between an external set of facts and an internal emotional state. Eliot argued that there is a verbal formula for any given state of emotion which, when found and used, will evoke that state and no other.

This assumption has been challenged by critics who argue that human emotion is too fluid, too culturally specific, and too individual to be captured by a single "formula." Balachandra Rajan, as quoted in David A. Goldfarb's New Reference Works in Literary Theory, pointed out the rigidity of Eliot's view. Rajan suggested that Eliot's theory assumes that an author's intentions concerning expression will be understood in one way only. But do a broken mirror and a rainy day always mean sadness? Or can they mean relief? Or nostalgia?

The problem lies in the universality of the formula. If the objective correlative is to work, the reader must share the same cultural and psychological framework as the author. What constitutes a "formula" for grief in one culture might be meaningless in another. Furthermore, the theory risks reducing the complexity of human experience to a mechanical equation. It suggests that art is a matter of finding the right variables to produce a predictable output.

Despite these criticisms, the influence of the objective correlative remains undeniable. It has shaped how writers think about structure, imagery, and emotional resonance. It has taught generations of writers that the most powerful way to make a reader feel is not to tell them what to feel, but to build a world in which that feeling is the only logical response.

A Haiku of Cold and Bone

The power of the objective correlative is perhaps best illustrated in the brevity of a haiku. Consider the famous work by the Japanese poet Yosa Buson, entitled The Piercing Chill I Feel.

The piercing chill I feel: my dead wife's comb, in our bedroom, under my heel...

In just a few lines, Buson constructs a perfect objective correlative. He does not write "I am grieving" or "I feel a cold, sharp sorrow." He does not describe the abstract nature of his loss. Instead, he presents a specific set of objects and a chain of events. There is a comb. It belongs to his dead wife. It is in their bedroom. It is under his heel.

The "chill" is the emotion, but the "chill" is also a physical sensation caused by the cold floor, the cold object, the cold reality of death. The external facts—the comb, the heel, the bedroom—terminate in a sensory experience that immediately evokes the emotion of loss and the physical sensation of cold. The formula is complete. The emotion is inevitable. There is no gap between the object and the feeling. This is the objective correlative in its purest form.

From Literature to Film and Song

The reach of Eliot's theory extends far beyond the pages of poetry and the stage of Shakespeare. It has permeated the broader landscape of storytelling, from cinema to popular music. In the Clint Eastwood movie Jersey Boys, the songwriter Bob Gaudio of The Four Seasons is asked who the girl is in his song "Cry For Me." His answer is a direct nod to the concept of the objective correlative.

Gaudio refers to T.S. Eliot's topic, "the Objective Correlative," as the subject being every girl, or any girl. In this context, the "girl" in the song is not a specific person from Gaudio's life. She is a formula. She is a set of circumstances and emotions that the audience can project their own experiences onto. By allowing the "girl" to be an objective correlative for "every girl," the author allows himself the literary license to step outside the scope of his personal experience and to conjecture about the emotions and responses inherent in the situation.

Gaudio utilizes a third-party perspective in the first-person presentation. He speaks as if he is a specific man with a specific heartbreak, but the "object" (the girl) is constructed in such a way that it resonates with anyone who has felt that specific type of longing or betrayal. The song works because it provides a formula for the emotion of unrequited love that the listener can instantly recognize and feel, without needing to know the biographical details of the songwriter.

This application of the theory demonstrates its versatility. Whether it is a 17th-century play, a 19th-century haiku, or a 20th-century pop song, the objective correlative remains a fundamental mechanism of artistic communication. It is the bridge between the artist's inner world and the audience's inner world, built from the bricks of the external world.

The Occasion of Montale

The concept of the objective correlative also found a unique resonance in the works of Eugenio Montale, an Italian poet who won the 1975 Nobel Prize in Literature. The works of T.S. Eliot were translated into Italian by Montale, creating a direct line of influence between the two. Montale developed his own variation of the concept, which he called the "occasion."

For Montale, the "occasion" was a specific, often mundane, event or object that triggered a deeper, almost mystical reflection on existence. Like the objective correlative, the occasion was an external fact that evoked an internal state. But Montale's approach was perhaps more subtle, more rooted in the specific landscape of the Ligurian coast and the Italian experience. He used the "occasion" to explore the gaps in human communication and the silence that exists between people.

Montale's use of the objective correlative (or the occasion) shows how the theory can be adapted to different cultural and philosophical contexts. While Eliot was often concerned with the failure of communication and the chaos of the modern world, Montale used the objective correlative to find moments of clarity and truth within that chaos. The "occasion" was a way to anchor the self in a world that often felt unmoored.

The Limits of the Formula

As we move further into the 21st century, the question remains: is the objective correlative still relevant? In an era of hyper-reality, where emotions are often mediated through screens and algorithms, does the concept of an "objective" formula for feeling still hold?

Some critics argue that the theory is too rigid for the fluid, fragmented nature of contemporary experience. The idea that there is a single "formula" for a specific emotion seems antithetical to the complexity of the modern psyche. We live in a world where the same object can mean a thousand different things depending on who is looking at it.

However, others argue that the objective correlative is more necessary than ever. In a world saturated with direct, often manipulative, appeals to emotion, the indirect, sensory-based approach of the objective correlative offers a way to reclaim the authenticity of feeling. It reminds us that true emotion is not something that can be shouted or stated; it is something that must be felt through the texture of the world.

The debate continues, but the legacy of T.S. Eliot's insight is secure. He gave us a name for a technique that artists had been using for centuries, and in doing so, he provided a lens through which we can understand the mechanics of art. Whether it is the blood on Lady Macbeth's hands, the comb under Buson's heel, or the girl in Gaudio's song, the objective correlative remains a powerful reminder that the most profound emotions are often found not in the heart, but in the world around us.

The Detachment of the Artist

One of the most profound implications of the objective correlative is the requirement for the artist's detachment. To create a successful objective correlative, the author must step back from their own raw emotions and construct a world that can stand on its own. This detachment is not a lack of passion; it is a discipline. It is the difference between screaming in pain and building a monument to that pain that allows others to feel it.

Formalist critics argue that this detachment is essential for the unity of the literary work. If the author is too present, too visible, the work becomes a diary entry rather than a piece of art. The objective correlative allows the author to disappear, leaving the objects and events to speak for themselves. It creates a space where the reader can enter and experience the emotion directly, without the filter of the author's voice.

This principle is evident in the best works of literature. We do not remember the author telling us how sad a character is; we remember the specific, concrete details that made us sad. We remember the rain, the empty chair, the cold tea. These are the objective correlatives that have stayed with us long after the plot has faded.

Conclusion

The objective correlative is more than just a literary term; it is a philosophy of art. It asserts that emotion is not an abstract, internal phenomenon that must be described, but a concrete, external reality that can be evoked through the careful arrangement of the world. From Washington Allston's coinage to T.S. Eliot's famous critique of Hamlet, from the haiku of Yosa Buson to the songs of Bob Gaudio, the objective correlative has proven to be a versatile and enduring tool for artists.

It challenges us to look at the world not just as a backdrop for our feelings, but as the very source of them. It reminds us that the external world is not separate from our internal lives; it is the formula for them. And in a world that often feels chaotic and unmoored, the objective correlative offers a way to find meaning, to find connection, and to find the specific, inevitable emotion that lies hidden in the objects and events of our daily lives.

As we read the great works of literature, or watch the great films, or listen to the great songs, we are constantly encountering these formulas. We are being asked to see the world through the eyes of the artist, to feel the emotion not because we are told to, but because the world has been arranged in such a way that we have no choice but to feel it. This is the power of the objective correlative. It is the art of making the invisible visible, the internal external, and the personal universal.

And perhaps, as Eliot himself noted, it is a principle that astonishes us because it is so simple, yet so profound. It is the realization that the key to the human heart lies not in the heart itself, but in the world we live in.
