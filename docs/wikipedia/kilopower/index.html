Kilopower

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Kilopower

In March 2018, at the Nevada National Security Site, a device no larger than a paper towel roll began to hum with the power of a star. Inside a concrete bunker, engineers watched as a solid cast alloy of uranium-235 and molybdenum reached temperatures of 850 °C (1,560 °F). For twenty-eight hours, this compact reactor, code-named KRUSTY, churned out 5.5 kilowatts of thermal energy, converting it into electricity through a series of silent, free-piston engines. It was a moment of quiet revolution, marking the first time since 1965 that the United States had successfully ground-tested a space nuclear reactor. The project, known as Kilopower, was not merely a technical demonstration; it was the birth of a new era for human exploration, promising to untether our deepest space ambitions from the fickle whims of the sun.

For decades, the gold standard for powering spacecraft beyond the inner solar system has been the Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generator (RTG). These devices, which famously powered the Voyager probes and the Curiosity rover, rely on the natural decay of plutonium-238 to generate heat, which is then converted directly into electricity. They are reliable, silent, and incredibly durable. But they have a fatal flaw: scarcity. The global supply of plutonium-238 is dwindling, and the production infrastructure is fragile. Furthermore, RTGs are limited in how much power they can produce, typically capping out at a few hundred watts. This is sufficient for a robotic arm or a camera, but it is woefully inadequate for the massive energy demands of a human crew on Mars or a high-thrust nuclear electric propulsion system. We needed something more. We needed Kilopower.

The Architecture of a Mini-Reactor

The Kilopower concept, launched in October 2015 as a joint venture between NASA and the Department of Energy's National Nuclear Security Administration (NNSA), represents a fundamental shift in how we think about space power. The goal was deceptively simple: create a fission reactor capable of producing between 1 and 10 kilowatts of electrical power (1–10 kWe) continuously for a lifespan of twelve to fifteen years. To put this in perspective, a standard household might use 1 to 2 kilowatts on average, but a space mission requires that power to be delivered without interruption, regardless of whether the spacecraft is in the shadow of a moon or dusted by a Martian sandstorm.

The heart of the Kilopower reactor is a solid core, not a vessel of liquid fuel. This core is an alloy composed of 93% uranium-235 and 7% molybdenum. The uranium-235 provides the fissile material necessary to sustain a chain reaction, while the molybdenum adds structural integrity to withstand the intense heat and radiation. The core itself is roughly the size of a paper towel roll, yet it contains the potential to power a small village for a decade. Surrounding this core is a shell of beryllium oxide, acting as a neutron reflector. This is a critical design element. The reflector bounces escaping neutrons back into the core, ensuring the chain reaction continues efficiently, and simultaneously acts as a shield, reducing the emission of gamma radiation that could fry the sensitive electronics of the spacecraft.

One of the most brilliant aspects of the Kilopower design is its method of heat transfer. Traditional nuclear reactors use pumps and coolants to move heat from the core to the turbine. In the vacuum of space, mechanical pumps are points of failure; if they seize, the reactor melts down. Kilopower eliminates this risk entirely by using passive sodium heat pipes. These are sealed tubes containing liquid sodium. The melting point of sodium is 98 °C (208 °F), but it boils at a much higher temperature, allowing it to flow freely as a liquid between 400 °C and 700 °C (750 °F and 1,300 °F). As the fission core heats up, the sodium inside the heat pipes boils, carrying the thermal energy away from the core to the conversion system without any moving parts. It is a system of pure thermodynamics, driven by the simple physics of phase change.

At the other end of these heat pipes sit the Stirling converters. These engines take the intense heat from the sodium and convert it into mechanical motion. Inside a Stirling engine, a piston moves back and forth (reciprocating motion) as the gas inside expands and contracts with temperature changes. In the Kilopower configuration, this motion is used to drive a linear electric generator, which produces electricity directly. The beauty of this system is its simplicity. There are no turbines spinning at thousands of RPMs, no complex gearboxes, and no lubrication issues. Just a piston, a heat source, and a generator. This mechanical simplicity is the key to the reactor's legendary reliability.

The Physics of Self-Regulation

Perhaps the most counterintuitive and vital feature of the Kilopower reactor is its intrinsic safety. In the world of nuclear engineering, safety is often a matter of active systems—emergency cooling pumps, control rods that drop automatically, and redundant power supplies. Kilopower, however, is safe by design. It relies on a negative temperature reactivity coefficient, a property that makes the reactor self-regulating in a way that feels almost biological.

Imagine the reactor core as a living organism that reacts to its own temperature. The core is designed with specific geometry and materials such that as it gets hotter, it physically expands. This expansion causes the fuel to become less dense, which in turn allows more neutrons to escape the core rather than striking other uranium atoms. The result is that the nuclear reaction naturally slows down as the temperature rises. Conversely, if the temperature drops, the core contracts, the fuel becomes denser, and the reaction speeds up to generate more heat.

This creates a passive feedback loop that prevents a meltdown under almost any conceivable scenario. If the heat pipes were to fail and the temperature spiked, the core would expand, shutting down the reaction before catastrophic damage could occur. If the power demand on the spacecraft drops and the reactor runs cooler, the core contracts, increasing power output to meet the need. This dynamic balance means the reactor does not need complex computer systems to manage its safety; the laws of physics do the work for it. As David Poston, the leader of the Compact Fission Reactor Design Team, and Patrick McClure, the manager for small nuclear reactor projects at Los Alamos National Laboratory, noted during the early phases of the project, this design allows for nuclear testing to be accomplished with reasonable cost and schedule within the existing infrastructure. The reactor is not just powerful; it is inherently stable.

Control over the reactor's output is still possible, though. A single rod made of boron carbide, a potent neutron absorber, sits in the center of the core. To start the reactor, this rod is pulled out, allowing the chain reaction to begin. To adjust the power level or shut the reactor down, the rod is inserted to varying depths, absorbing more neutrons and dampening the reaction. Crucially, the reactor is designed to be launched "cold." The fission process does not begin until the spacecraft has reached its destination and the boron rod is removed. This ensures that no highly radioactive fission products are created during the launch phase, eliminating the risk of a nuclear accident during ascent.

From DUFF to KRUSTY: A History of Proof

The road to the successful 2018 test of KRUSTY was paved by a series of daring experiments that proved the concept long before the full-scale reactor was built. The journey began in September 2012 with an experiment known as DUFF, or Demonstration Using Flattop Fissions. This was a bold, almost audacious test that utilized an existing nuclear assembly called Flattop as a heat source. The goal was simple: prove that a Stirling engine could be powered by nuclear fission and that heat pipes could transport that energy effectively.

DUFF was conducted at the Device Assembly Facility at the Nevada Test Site. The results were immediate and staggering. It became the first Stirling engine ever powered by fission energy and the first use of a heat pipe to transport heat from a reactor to a power conversion system. The success of DUFF shattered the skepticism surrounding the viability of small, Stirling-driven fission reactors for space. It demonstrated that the physics worked in the real world, not just in computer simulations.

Building on this foundation, the team moved to the construction of the KRUSTY test reactor. Completed in 2017, KRUSTY was designed to produce up to 1 kilowatt of electric power and stood about 6.5 feet (1.9 meters) tall. The goal was to replicate the operational parameters of a deep space mission as closely as possible. The first tests utilized a core made of depleted uranium, manufactured by the Y-12 National Security Complex in Oak Ridge, Tennessee. While depleted uranium is not fissile in the same way as highly enriched uranium, it is chemically and physically identical, allowing engineers to validate the thermal and mechanical performance of the system without the regulatory complexities of a fully active core.

The transition to a fully active core marked the true beginning of the high-stakes testing phase. In November 2017, testing began at the Nevada National Security Site, aiming to reach Technology Readiness Level (TRL) 5. This phase involved rigorous thermal, materials, and component validation. The team subjected the reactor to a gauntlet of stress tests, simulating everything from the loss of the heat removal system to the failure of the Stirling engines. They wanted to see how the reactor would behave when things went wrong, ensuring that the passive safety systems would hold up.

The climax of this testing campaign arrived on March 20, 2018. For twenty-eight hours, the KRUSTY reactor ran at full power. The 28 kg uranium-235 core reached temperatures of 850 °C (1,560 °F) and generated approximately 5.5 kW of fission power. The test was a masterclass in engineering validation. The team simulated various failure scenarios, including shutting down the Stirling engines mid-operation, adjusting the control rod, and thermal cycling. The reactor responded to every challenge with stability, proving its self-regulating capabilities. The experiment concluded with a "scram" test, where the control rod was fully inserted to shut down the reaction, confirming that the reactor could be safely and reliably turned off when necessary.

The Gateway to Mars and Beyond

The implications of the Kilopower success are profound, particularly for the future of human spaceflight. For years, the vision of a crewed mission to Mars has been held back by a simple equation: how do you keep six astronauts alive for years in a hostile environment? Solar power is unreliable on Mars, where dust storms can block sunlight for months, and the sun is only about 43% as bright as it is on Earth. Chemical batteries are too heavy to store enough energy for the long nights and dust storms. RTGs provide power, but not enough to run the heavy machinery required for life support, habitat heating, and fuel production.

NASA studies have determined that a 40 kWe reactor system would be sufficient to support a crew of between 4 and 6 astronauts. Kilopower is the stepping stone to this capability. The technology demonstrated by the 1 kWe KRUSTY prototype can be scaled up. The space-rated 10 kWe Kilopower reactor, intended for Mars missions, is expected to have a total mass of 1500 kg, with a core mass of 226 kg and containing 43.7 kg of uranium-235. By clustering multiple 10 kWe units, engineers could easily reach the 40 kWe target needed for a human outpost.

The applications for this power extend far beyond keeping the lights on. One of the most critical uses of Kilopower on Mars would be the production of propellant. To return to Earth, astronauts need fuel, specifically methane and oxygen. The process of extracting oxygen from the Martian atmosphere (which is 95% carbon dioxide) and synthesizing fuel requires massive amounts of energy. A Kilopower reactor would provide the steady, high-volume electricity necessary to run the electrolysis and chemical synthesis equipment, effectively manufacturing the fuel for the return journey while the crew is still on the surface.

Once the astronauts arrive, the reactor would power their life-support systems, air recyclers, water purifiers, and habitat heating. It would allow for the operation of rovers and drilling equipment to search for water and resources. In the event of a solar flare or a prolonged dust storm, the nuclear reactor would provide an uninterrupted power supply, a literal lifeline for the crew. The reliability of Kilopower means that the astronauts would not have to worry about their power source failing, allowing them to focus on exploration and science.

Furthermore, Kilopower opens the door to nuclear electric propulsion. Current chemical rockets are limited by the energy density of their fuel. A nuclear electric propulsion system, powered by a Kilopower reactor, could use that electricity to ionize propellant and accelerate it to extremely high speeds. This would drastically reduce the travel time to Mars and other destinations in the solar system, making interplanetary travel more efficient and reducing the exposure of astronauts to cosmic radiation.

The choice of uranium-235 over plutonium-238 is also a strategic one. Uranium is more abundant and easier to produce in the quantities needed for large-scale space programs. By moving away from the reliance on plutonium, NASA and the DoE are ensuring a sustainable supply chain for future missions. The solid cast uranium core avoids the uncertainties and geopolitical complexities associated with the supply of radioisotopes, providing a stable foundation for long-term exploration planning.

A New Chapter in Exploration

The success of the Kilopower project, and specifically the KRUSTY test, represents a turning point in space history. It is the first time since the SNAP-10A reactor in 1965 that the United States has conducted ground tests on a space reactor. That gap of over fifty years highlighted a stagnation in nuclear space technology, a reliance on aging methods that were no longer sufficient for the ambitious goals of the 21st century. Kilopower has broken that stagnation, proving that small, safe, and powerful fission reactors are not only possible but ready for deployment.

The journey from the DUFF experiment in 2012 to the full-power run of KRUSTY in 2018 was a testament to the power of focused engineering and the willingness to take calculated risks. The team at Los Alamos National Laboratory and NASA did not just build a reactor; they built a system that is inherently safe, mechanically simple, and scalable. They proved that nuclear power could be tamed for the specific needs of space travel, offering a solution that is both powerful and reliable.

As we look toward the future, the Kilopower reactor stands as a beacon of what is possible. It is the key that will unlock the doors of the Moon and the Red Planet. It is the engine that will drive the next generation of robotic explorers to the icy moons of Jupiter and Saturn, where sunlight is too faint to power solar panels. It is the promise of a future where humanity is no longer limited by the distance from the sun, but empowered by the boundless energy of the atom.

The tests in Nevada were just the beginning. The data gathered from KRUSTY has paved the way for the development of larger, more capable reactors that will one day power the first human habitats on Mars. The technology is mature, the physics are sound, and the need is urgent. Kilopower is not just a reactor; it is a gateway to abundant power for exploration, a technological leap that will define the next chapter of human history. The sun may be our ancient guide, but it is the atom that will light our way into the deep unknown.

"The test was considered to be a highly successful demonstration."

Those words, spoken by the engineers after the 28-hour run, were an understatement. They were the first words of a new era, a confirmation that we have finally mastered the art of bringing a star to the void. The path to the stars is no longer a dream of the distant future; it is a reality being built today, one kilogram of uranium at a time.
