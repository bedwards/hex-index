Dicastery for Evangelization

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Dicastery for Evangelization

On June 5, 2022, the administrative heart of the Catholic Church underwent a surgical rearrangement that sent ripples through the corridors of the Vatican, signaling a shift in theological and operational priority that had not been seen in five centuries. For the first time since the era of the Reformation, the entity responsible for spreading the Gospel was placed at the very top of the hierarchy, eclipsing the long-standing supremacy of the body tasked with guarding the purity of dogma. This was not merely a bureaucratic reshuffling of filing cabinets or a semantic game of rearranging the furniture in St. Peter's Basilica. It was the birth of the Dicastery for Evangelization, a new department of the Roman Curia forged from the merger of two distinct historical forces: the Pontifical Council for Promoting the New Evangelization and the Congregation for the Evangelization of Peoples. The creation of this dicastery, mandated by Pope Francis through the apostolic constitution Praedicate evangelium, fundamentally altered the architecture of the Vatican, placing the act of proclamation above the act of preservation.

To understand the magnitude of this change, one must first grasp the sheer inertia of the institution that was displaced. For roughly 500 years, the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith—and its antecedents, the Holy Office and the Inquisition—had held the number one spot on the list of Curial departments. This was the office that defined orthodoxy, that policed the boundaries of belief, and that ensured the faithful did not stray into heresy. Its primacy was a reflection of a Church that, for centuries, viewed itself as a fortress under siege, prioritizing the defense of truth above all else. The new order, codified in the spring of 2022, flipped this script. By listing the Dicastery for Evangelization first, the Vatican declared that the primary mission of the Church is not to sit behind the ramparts and guard the treasure, but to leave the walls, step into the world, and offer that treasure to the hungry. The sequence of the first three dicasteries was no accident. It was a deliberate narrative arc: first, the announcement of the Good News; second, the clarification of what that news means; and third, the tangible act of charity that flows from it. As Bishop Marco Mellino noted during the press conference unveiling the new constitution, while the order held no strict juridical weight, the symbolic sequence of evangelization preceding doctrine was a clear statement of intent. The Church was redefining its identity from a fortress to a field hospital, and from a gatekeeper to a herald.

The mechanics of this new entity are as complex as they are ambitious. The Dicastery for Evangelization is not a monolith; it is a dual-natured organism designed to handle two vastly different, yet interconnected, challenges. The constitution Praedicate evangelium divides the dicastery into two distinct sections, each with its own mandate and terrain. The first section deals with the "fundamental questions regarding evangelization in the world." This is the domain of the New Evangelization, a concept that gained traction in the late 20th century to address the secularization of historically Christian nations. Here, the dicastery confronts the reality that in places like Europe and the Americas, the Gospel is no longer a new story but a forgotten one. The challenge is not to find people who have never heard of Jesus, but to rekindle a faith that has grown cold, to re-announce the message to those who have drifted away, and to find new languages for an ancient truth in a post-Christian culture.

The second section of the dicastery is tasked with "first evangelization and the establishment of new particular churches." This is the traditional domain of the old Congregation for the Evangelization of Peoples, often known by its Latin acronym, Propaganda Fide. This section operates in the mission lands, in parts of Africa, Asia, and the Americas where the Church is still in its infancy or facing violent persecution. Its work involves the planting of seeds, the founding of dioceses, the training of local clergy, and the navigation of the delicate political and cultural landscapes where Christianity is a minority faith. Crucially, this section is responsible for the establishment, accompaniment, and support of new particular churches, though it must do so without prejudice to the competence of the Dicastery for the Eastern Churches, which guards the unique traditions of the Eastern Catholic rites. This bifurcation allows the dicastery to speak two languages simultaneously: the language of cultural re-evangelization in the West and the language of missionary expansion in the Global South.

Perhaps the most striking feature of the Dicastery for Evangelization is its leadership structure, which breaks a centuries-old precedent. In the traditional hierarchy of the Roman Curia, every dicastery is presided over by a Cardinal Prefect, a high-ranking churchman appointed by the Pope to serve as the administrative head. The Dicastery for Evangelization, however, is unique. Praedicate evangelium stipulates that the Pope himself serves as its Prefect. This is a radical concentration of authority, placing the pontiff as the direct chief executive of the Church's missionary work. It underscores the idea that evangelization is not a side project or a departmental concern, but the very essence of the papacy itself. The Pope is not merely the Vicar of Christ; in this dicastery, he is the chief missionary.

To assist the Pope in this monumental task, the structure provides for two pro-prefects. This is a departure from the single-head model seen elsewhere in the Curia and reflects the dual nature of the dicastery's mandate. The first pro-prefect is tasked with overseeing the section on fundamental questions of evangelization, while the second pro-prefect manages the section on first evangelization and the establishment of new churches. This dual leadership ensures that neither the "New Evangelization" nor the "First Evangelization" is neglected. The first pro-prefect since the dicastery's inception was Archbishop Rino Fisichella, a theologian and historian known for his work on the New Evangelization and a close collaborator of Pope Francis. The second pro-prefect was Cardinal Luis Antonio Tagle, often described as the "Asian Francis," a charismatic bishop from the Philippines who had previously led the Congregation for the Evangelization of Peoples. The pairing of Fisichella and Tagle represented a perfect synthesis of the dicastery's goals: the intellectual depth required to re-engage the secular West and the pastoral heart needed to expand the Church in the developing world.

The timeline of leadership in this new dicastery offers a fascinating glimpse into the transition of the papacy itself. Pope Francis, who initiated the merger and signed Praedicate evangelium, served as the first Prefect of the Dicastery from its inception on June 5, 2022, until his death on April 21, 2025. His tenure was marked by a relentless push to make the Church outward-looking, a theme that permeated his entire pontificate. He viewed the merger not just as an administrative efficiency but as a theological correction, a way to ensure that the Church's energy was focused on the peripheries of the world and the peripheries of the human heart. His passing in the spring of 2025 marked the end of an era and the beginning of a new chapter for the dicastery.

On May 8, 2025, following the conclave that elected Pope Leo XIV, the leadership of the Dicastery for Evangelization passed to the new pontiff. Pope Leo XIV, a figure whose election was anticipated to bring a new dimension to the Church's global outreach, immediately assumed the role of Prefect. The continuity of the dicastery's mission under a new pope highlighted the institutional stability of the change; the structure created by Francis was robust enough to survive the transition of the papacy. The two pro-prefects, Fisichella and Tagle, remained in their posts, providing a bridge between the papacies and ensuring that the momentum of the New Evangelization was not lost. Their continued presence signaled that the dicastery was not a personal project of a single pontiff but a permanent reorientation of the Curia. The presence of Tagle, in particular, was significant. As a cardinal from the Global South, his retention ensured that the voice of the majority of Catholics, who live outside the traditional centers of European power, remained central to the dicastery's strategy.

The impact of the Dicastery for Evangelization extends far beyond the walls of the Vatican. By placing evangelization at the forefront, the dicastery has begun to reshape the priorities of local churches around the world. Bishops in Europe are now being urged to rethink their strategies for engaging a secularized society, moving away from a defensive posture to one of proactive engagement. In Africa and Asia, the dicastery's focus on the establishment of new particular churches has accelerated the process of inculturation, allowing local churches to take greater ownership of their mission. The dicastery serves as a clearinghouse for ideas, a place where the experiences of missionaries in remote villages can inform the strategies of theologians in Rome, and vice versa. It is a mechanism for ensuring that the Church speaks with a single voice, even as it adapts to a thousand different contexts.

The creation of the dicastery also had profound implications for the internal culture of the Curia. For decades, the Curia had been criticized for being overly bureaucratic, inward-looking, and focused on control rather than mission. The merger of the Pontifical Council for Promoting the New Evangelization and the Congregation for the Evangelization of Peoples was a direct response to these criticisms. By combining these two entities, the Vatican signaled that the distinction between "mission to the nations" and "mission to the baptized" was artificial. In a globalized world, the challenges of the secular West and the mission fields of the developing world are increasingly intertwined. The dicastery was designed to break down the silos that had separated these two worlds, fostering a more holistic approach to the Church's mission.

The authority of the Dicastery for Evangelization is derived not just from its position at the top of the list, but from the specific powers granted to it by Praedicate evangelium. It is responsible for fundamental questions regarding evangelization in the world, a mandate that gives it the power to set the agenda for the Church's global mission. It can issue guidelines, commission studies, and coordinate the efforts of various Catholic organizations. It is tasked with the establishment of new particular churches, a role that involves navigating complex canonical and political landscapes. The dicastery's influence is felt in the appointment of bishops, the funding of mission projects, and the training of clergy. It is the engine room of the Church's missionary activity, driving the forward momentum of the Gospel.

Yet, the dicastery is not without its challenges. The dual nature of its mandate means it must constantly balance the needs of two different worlds. The resources required for the New Evangelization in the West are vastly different from those needed for first evangelization in the mission lands. The dicastery must ensure that the wealthier churches of the North do not dominate the agenda at the expense of the churches of the South, and vice versa. It must navigate the tensions between the intellectual demands of theology and the pastoral needs of the faithful. It must also contend with the reality that in many parts of the world, evangelization is a dangerous and even deadly endeavor. The dicastery must be a voice of courage, encouraging the faithful to stand firm in their witness even in the face of persecution.

The leadership of Rino Fisichella and Luis Antonio Tagle has been instrumental in navigating these challenges. Fisichella, with his deep theological background, has provided the intellectual framework for the dicastery's work, emphasizing the importance of a renewed encounter with Christ. Tagle, with his pastoral warmth and global perspective, has ensured that the dicastery remains connected to the lived reality of the faithful. Their collaboration has been a model of the dicastery's own mission: a partnership that bridges the gap between the Vatican and the world, between the past and the future, between the West and the rest. The dicastery under their guidance has become a hub of innovation, where new methods of evangelization are tested and where the Church's mission is constantly reimagined.

The legacy of the Dicastery for Evangelization is still being written. Its creation in 2022 was a bold step, a recognition that the Church of the 21st century must be a missionary Church, not just in name but in structure and practice. By placing evangelization at the top of the Curial hierarchy, the Vatican has made a statement that resonates far beyond the Vatican walls. It has declared that the primary task of the Church is to proclaim the Gospel, to bring the Good News to all corners of the earth, and to build a community of faith that transcends all boundaries. The dicastery is the instrument of this mission, a dynamic and evolving entity that will continue to shape the future of the Catholic Church. As Pope Leo XIV leads the dicastery forward, the focus remains clear: to evangelize, to establish, and to accompany. The fortress is open, the gates are wide, and the mission continues.

The story of the Dicastery for Evangelization is a story of transformation. It is a story of a Church that is willing to rethink its structures to better serve its mission. It is a story of a Pope who saw the need for change and acted with decisiveness. It is a story of leaders who have dedicated their lives to the work of evangelization. And it is a story that is far from over. As the dicastery continues to grow and evolve, it will face new challenges and new opportunities. But its core mission remains unchanged: to bring the light of the Gospel to a world that is often in darkness. The Dicastery for Evangelization is not just a department of the Roman Curia; it is the heartbeat of the Church, beating with the rhythm of the mission that Jesus Christ entrusted to his disciples. It is a reminder that the Church is not a museum of the past, but a living, breathing organism that is always on the move, always seeking, and always proclaiming. The work of the dicastery is the work of the Church itself, and as long as there are people to reach and hearts to heal, the work will never be done.
