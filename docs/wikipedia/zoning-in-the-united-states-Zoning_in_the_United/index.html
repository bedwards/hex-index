Zoning in the United States

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Zoning in the United States

In 1904, the Los Angeles City Council passed Ordinance 9774, a piece of legislation that did not merely regulate land use but codified a specific racial hierarchy into the city's physical fabric. The law prohibited laundries and wash houses from operating in designated residential districts. While the text of the ordinance spoke of zoning, the target was explicit: Chinese-owned laundries, which were ubiquitous in the city, were effectively banished from neighborhoods where white families lived. This was not an isolated anomaly of urban planning but the opening salvo in a century-long campaign where the American city was divided not by rivers or rail lines, but by lines drawn on maps to enforce class, ethnic, and racial segregation. Today, as the housing crisis deepens and homelessness becomes a defining feature of American metropolitan life, the legacy of these early ordinances looms large, not as a relic of the past, but as the active architecture of modern inequality.

Zoning, in its most technical definition, is a system of laws that divides a jurisdiction's land into districts, or zones, and limits how land in each district can be used. It is the mechanism by which state governments, often delegating this authority to local municipalities, exercise their "police power" over privately owned real property. To the uninitiated, this sounds like a bureaucratic necessity, a way to keep a factory from being built next to a school. But the history of zoning in the United States reveals a far more complex and often darker reality. It is a story of how the dream of the single-family home was weaponized to exclude, how the desire for order was used to justify oppression, and how a legal framework designed to protect property values ended up constraining the supply of housing and inflating prices for millions.

The roots of this system can be traced back to the rapid, chaotic urbanization of the turn of the twentieth century. Cities like New York were swelling with immigrants and industry, creating a pressure cooker of congestion, disease, and industrial pollution. Skyscrapers rose to unnatural heights, their cornices projecting into the street, shutting out light and air. The lower floors needed artificial light even during the day. Business centers were intensely congested rather than rationally spread out, overwhelming transit and street facilities. Edward M. Bassett, the author of the first comprehensive zoning ordinance in the United States, captured this sense of crisis in 1922, describing a city choking on its own growth. The perceived need for formal zoning arose as a legal means to combat these issues, to stabilize property values, and to protect residents from the hazards of crowded living conditions.

However, the path from managing congestion to enforcing segregation was short and well-traveled. Before the comprehensive zoning resolutions that would eventually sweep the nation, cities experimented with narrower ordinances. The Modesto, California ordinance of 1885, which banned wash houses from certain areas, is often cited as America's first true zoning ordinance. But it was the 1904 Los Angeles law that set a dangerous precedent. The prohibition against laundries had a distinct racial component, targeting Chinese residents and citizens. When the California Supreme Court upheld such rules in Yick Wo (1886), it opened the door for a wave of discriminatory legislation. Existing nuisance laws had already prohibited dangerous businesses like warehousing explosives or odorous land uses like slaughterhouses, but the new zoning laws expanded the scope of prohibited industries to include anything that could be used as a pretext for exclusion.

The momentum for segregationist zoning accelerated after the Supreme Court declared racial ordinances unconstitutional in 1917. In Buchanan v. Warley, the Court ruled that race-based zoning violated the Fourteenth Amendment, striking down a Louisville ordinance that mandated racial separation on a block-by-block basis. Before this ruling, cities across the South and border states had eagerly adopted such measures. Baltimore passed a race-based law in December 1910, creating a block-by-block scheme that formalized the de facto segregation of neighborhoods like Druid Hill. Richmond followed in 1908, and its 1915 decision in Hopkins v. City of Richmond upheld its own race-based zoning law. Atlanta, New Orleans, St. Louis, and Oklahoma City all passed similar laws between 1910 and 1918. The Court's decision in Buchanan did not end the practice; it merely forced municipalities to find a new, more subtle vehicle for the same goal.

That vehicle was found in the New York City 1916 Zoning Resolution. While New York's ordinance is celebrated as the first comprehensive zoning law in the United States, its adoption was influenced by the same class and racial anxieties that drove the earlier, explicitly discriminatory laws. The 1916 resolution was designed to control the height and bulk of buildings, but it also established a precedent for separating land uses in a way that would effectively segregate communities. The constitutionality of these broader zoning ordinances was finally cemented by the Supreme Court in 1926 in Village of Euclid, Ohio v. Ambler Realty Co. This landmark decision upheld the power of local governments to restrict land use, even when it diminished the value of private property. The Court essentially declared that the community's interest in orderly development outweighed the rights of individual landowners to use their property as they saw fit.

With the legal framework secured, the American city began to be reshaped by the principle of single-family zoning. According to the New York Times, "single-family zoning is practically gospel in America." A vast number of cities now zone land extensively for detached single-family homes, a practice that is far more predominant in the United States than in any other country. This is not a neutral preference for a certain type of architecture; it is a policy choice with profound economic and social consequences. In suburban localities, zoning often mandates single-family housing to the exclusion of anything else. Though not always explicitly discriminatory on the surface, these ordinances were often applied in ways that prevented African Americans from moving into predominantly white neighborhoods. The underlying belief was that their presence would lower property values, a self-fulfilling prophecy rooted in racism and classism.

The impact of this system on the human landscape is staggering. Studies indicate that strict zoning regulations constrain the supply of housing, inflate housing prices, increase homelessness, and contribute to inequality. By prioritizing single-family housing, cities have effectively frozen the demographic and economic mobility of their residents. The housing shortage in many metropolitan areas is not a result of market forces alone but is a direct consequence of political decisions made in city halls. The result is a nation where the ability to find a home is increasingly determined by the zoning code of the neighborhood one seeks to enter.

The political dynamics surrounding zoning reveal a striking consistency across the ideological spectrum. Support for local zoning against multifamily housing is concentrated among white, affluent homeowners. There are no substantial differences between liberal and conservative homeowners in their opposition to the construction of dense housing in their neighborhoods. This phenomenon, often called NIMBYism (Not In My Backyard), transcends party lines. Whether a homeowner identifies as a Democrat or a Republican, the instinct to protect property values and the perceived character of the neighborhood often leads to a fierce resistance to density. However, among the mass public and elected officials, Democratic constituencies are more likely to support dense, multi-family housing. This disconnect highlights the gap between the stated values of political parties and the actions of their most vocal constituents.

The economic toll of this resistance is equally severe. Some estimates suggest that restrictive zoning in the United States may result in hundreds of billions to over a trillion dollars per year in lost economic output. When housing is scarce and expensive, workers cannot move to where the jobs are. Innovation slows, productivity drops, and the economy as a whole suffers. The cost is not just measured in dollars but in the lives of those who are priced out of their communities. The housing shortage has become a defining challenge of the 21st century, coupled with racial residential segregation that continues to plague American cities. The public attention and political debate on zoning laws are growing, but the entrenched interests of single-family homeowners make change difficult.

The origins of this system can be traced further back than the American experience. Some scholars trace the roots of land use separation to German urban planner Reinhard Baumeister, who developed a system that influenced later zoning practices. Frankfurt's nineteenth-century zoning plans served as models for urban planning in the United States and other countries in Western Europe. But while the European models focused on separating industry from residential areas for reasons of public health and order, the American adaptation quickly took on a racial and class character. The perceived need for formal zoning in America arose at the turn of the twentieth century, as rapidly growing cities faced challenges that seemed insurmountable without legal intervention.

The early zoning laws were narrow in scope, focusing on specific nuisances. The Los Angeles ordinance of 1904, for instance, did not assign all parts of the city to a zoning map as later ordinances would. It simply established three residential districts where laundries and wash houses were prohibited. It was a targeted strike against a specific industry owned by a specific racial group. By 1908, the scope expanded. Ordinance 16170 established six industrial districts, drawn mainly in areas that had already hosted significant industrial development, such as corridors along the freight railroads and the Los Angeles River. A new ordinance in 1909 established that all land not part of an industrial district would become a residence district. This binary classification—industrial or residential—became the standard for American zoning, a rigid dichotomy that ignored the complexity of urban life.

Between 1909 and 1915, the Los Angeles City Council responded to requests from business interests by creating exceptions to industrial bans within residential districts. They did this through the legal device of "districts within districts." While some of these exceptions were benign, such as motion picture districts, others were polluting, such as poultry slaughterhouse districts. This flexibility allowed the system to adapt to economic pressures while maintaining the appearance of segregation. By 1913, there were 22 cities with zoning ordinances. The model was spreading, driven by a combination of genuine concern for urban health and a desire to maintain racial and class boundaries.

The legacy of these early laws is still visible in the American landscape today. The single-family home, once a symbol of the American Dream, has become a barrier to entry for millions. The zoning laws that prioritize this housing type have raised concerns regarding housing availability, affordability, and environmental harms. In a world where the climate crisis demands denser, more efficient cities, the American commitment to low-density residential zoning seems increasingly anachronistic. Yet, the political will to change it remains elusive. The resistance to multifamily housing is concentrated among those who benefit most from the status quo: white, affluent homeowners who view their neighborhoods as private enclaves rather than public spaces.

The human cost of this system is measured in the lives of those who are forced to live in overcrowded conditions, in the families who spend half their income on rent, and in the communities that are segregated by design. The zoning laws that were once used to exclude Chinese laundry owners and Black families are now used to exclude anyone who cannot afford a single-family home in a desirable neighborhood. The mechanism has changed, but the outcome remains the same: a divided society where opportunity is determined by geography.

As the debate over zoning intensifies, the need to understand its history becomes critical. The story of zoning in the United States is not just a story of land use; it is a story of power, exclusion, and the struggle for a more equitable future. The early ordinances in Los Angeles, Baltimore, and Richmond set the stage for a century of segregation. The Supreme Court's decision in Euclid provided the legal shield for this system. And the political inertia of homeowners has kept it in place. To change the future, we must first confront the past. We must acknowledge that the single-family home is not a natural state of being but a policy choice, one that has come at a tremendous cost to the American people. The challenge for the next generation of planners and politicians is to dismantle the barriers that were built a century ago and to create a system that allows all people to thrive, regardless of their race, class, or income. The path forward requires not just new laws, but a new understanding of what a city is for. It is not a collection of private fortresses, but a shared space where the community can grow and flourish together. The history of zoning shows us that when we prioritize exclusion over inclusion, we all lose. The time to change that trajectory is now.
