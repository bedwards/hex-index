Polyethylene terephthalate

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Polyethylene terephthalate

The Plastic That Conquered the World

In 1973, a DuPont engineer named Nathaniel Wyeth solved a problem that would reshape how humanity consumes beverages. He figured out how to make a plastic bottle strong enough to contain the pressure of carbonated drinks without exploding, yet light enough to be economically shipped around the world. That invention—the PET bottle—would go on to become so ubiquitous that humans now produce 56 million tons of this single material every year.

PET stands for polyethylene terephthalate, though you've almost certainly never called it that. When it shows up in your wardrobe, you call it polyester. When it holds your sparkling water, you call it plastic. And when you flip over that bottle and see the number 1 inside a recycling triangle, that's the code specifically assigned to this one material.

What makes PET remarkable isn't any single property—it's the combination. It's transparent. It's strong. It resists impact. It can be stretched into fibers thin enough for delicate clothing or molded into bottles tough enough to survive being dropped on concrete. And unlike many plastics, it can theoretically be recycled, broken down and reformed into new products.

Theoretically.

From Laboratory Curiosity to Global Commodity

The story of PET begins not with bottles but with fabric. In 1941, two British chemists named John Rex Whinfield and James Tennant Dickson were working for the Calico Printers' Association in Manchester, England—a company that, despite its quaint name, was deeply invested in the future of synthetic materials. They patented a new polyester compound that could be drawn into fibers.

The timing was terrible for commercialization. World War II consumed all available industrial resources for the next four years. But by 1950, the American chemical giant DuPont had begun producing PET fiber under the trade name Dacron. A year later, they trademarked Mylar for PET film. Both names became so associated with their products that many people still use them generically, the way we say Kleenex for tissue or Xerox for photocopies.

Meanwhile, on the other side of the Iron Curtain, Soviet scientists at the Institute of High-Molecular Compounds in Leningrad were independently developing the same material. They called it Lavsan—an acronym derived from the Russian name of their institute. The Cold War gave the world two names for an identical plastic, each side convinced they had invented something unique.

For decades, PET remained primarily a textile material. More than sixty percent of all PET produced today still becomes fiber for clothing, carpets, and upholstery. Polyester fabric revolutionized fashion in the 1970s, promising wrinkle-free convenience to a generation tired of ironing. It fell out of fashion, then came roaring back as performance wear for athletes and as fleece jackets for everyone else.

But the invention that truly unleashed PET on the world was Wyeth's bottle.

How to Make a Molecule Hold Your Soda

To understand why PET bottles work so well, you need to understand a bit about how the material behaves at a molecular level. PET is what chemists call a thermoplastic polymer, which means two things: it's made of long chains of repeating molecular units (that's the polymer part), and those chains can be softened by heat and reshaped (that's the thermoplastic part).

The repeating unit in PET has the chemical formula C10H8O4. If you remember anything from chemistry class, you might recognize that this is mostly carbon and oxygen, with some hydrogen thrown in. In fact, PET is essentially a mixture of carbon and water in its most stable form—a property that scientists recently exploited in an experiment using laser-driven shock compression to create tiny diamonds. But that's getting ahead of ourselves.

The magic of PET lies in what happens when you heat it and then cool it down. Cool it quickly, and the molecular chains freeze in place before they can organize themselves. You get an amorphous solid—literally meaning "without form"—where the molecules are jumbled randomly like a pile of tangled headphone cords. Amorphous PET is perfectly transparent.

Cool it slowly, and something different happens. The molecules have time to arrange themselves into orderly crystalline structures called spherulites. These crystalline regions scatter light as it passes through, making the material translucent or even opaque. A PET bottle that's been heated by accident—left in a hot car, say, or held over a candle—will turn cloudy as its molecules rearrange into crystals.

Bottle manufacturers exploit this dual nature deliberately. They start by injection molding what's called a preform—a small, thick-walled tube that looks like a test tube with screw threads at the top. This preform is then reheated and blown into its final bottle shape using high-pressure air, a process called stretch blow molding. The stretching aligns the molecular chains in a way that makes the material both stronger and more transparent. The result is a bottle that's crystalline enough to block gases and aromas, yet clear enough to show off its contents.

The Oxygen Problem

For all its virtues, PET has a fundamental weakness: it lets gases through. Oxygen can seep in; carbon dioxide can leak out. This is why your plastic soda bottle goes flat faster than a glass one, and why certain products can't use simple PET packaging at all.

Beer is particularly vulnerable. Exposure to oxygen causes beer to develop stale, cardboard-like flavors within weeks. Glass bottles and aluminum cans provide excellent barriers, but plastic would be lighter and cheaper. The solution is to sandwich the PET between layers of other materials—typically polyvinyl alcohol or polyamide—that block oxygen more effectively. This multilayer construction lets brewers use plastic while preserving their product's flavor.

There's also the reverse problem: PET absorbs things it shouldn't. The material has what chemists politely call an "affinity for hydrophobic flavors"—meaning it sucks up certain aromatic compounds from whatever liquid it contains. Beverage formulators often have to add extra flavoring to products destined for PET bottles to compensate for what the plastic will absorb.

This gets even more complicated with reusable bottles. In some European countries, thick PET bottles are collected, washed, and refilled. But because the plastic absorbs flavors from each use, bottles must pass a "sniffer test" before being refilled—someone literally smells them to ensure that, say, a bottle that once held orange soda won't contaminate the next batch of mineral water.

The Chemistry of Creation

Making PET requires two main ingredients: terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol. The names sound technical, but the concept is simple. Terephthalic acid provides the stiff, ring-shaped molecular structures that give PET its strength. Ethylene glycol provides the flexible links between them. Join them together in alternating sequence, and you get a long chain that's rigid enough to hold its shape but flexible enough to be drawn into fibers or blown into bottles.

As of now, both ingredients come from petroleum. Ethylene glycol is made from ethylene found in natural gas. Terephthalic acid comes from a compound called para-xylene, which is extracted from crude oil. This means every PET bottle traces its origins to fossil fuels extracted from deep underground.

The manufacturing process itself is an elegant chain of chemical reactions. In one common method, you start with dimethyl terephthalate and excess ethylene glycol, heating them together at 150 to 200 degrees Celsius with a catalyst. Methanol vapor boils off—that's the byproduct telling you the reaction is working. Then you crank up the temperature to 270 or 280 degrees, apply vacuum to pull out more ethylene glycol, and the molecules keep linking together into longer and longer chains.

How long should those chains be? It depends on what you're making. Chemists measure chain length indirectly through something called intrinsic viscosity—essentially, how thick and syrupy a solution of the plastic becomes. Fiber applications need chains of moderate length, with intrinsic viscosities around 0.65 deciliters per gram. Bottles require longer chains, around 0.75 to 0.85. Industrial strapping and engineering plastics push even higher. Different chain lengths give different properties: longer chains mean stronger material but harder processing.

Variations on a Theme

Pure PET is rarely pure. Manufacturers routinely add other ingredients to tune the material's properties for specific uses.

One common modification is called PETG—the G stands for glycol-modified. Instead of using only ethylene glycol, chemists substitute some of it with a larger molecule called cyclohexanedimethanol. This bigger building block disrupts the orderly crystalline structure, producing a material that stays amorphous and transparent even without rapid cooling. PETG has become hugely popular in 3D printing because it combines the ease of printing found in simpler plastics with much better strength and temperature resistance. You'll find it in everything from surgical equipment to automotive parts to LED lighting fixtures.

Another modification uses isophthalic acid alongside the standard terephthalic acid. Where terephthalic acid creates straight, rigid links, isophthalic acid introduces angles into the molecular chain. This again disrupts crystallization, making the material easier to mold into complex shapes. Small amounts in bottle formulations slow crystallization just enough to allow stretch blow molding while still producing a finished product with good barrier properties.

Manufacturers might also add antimony or titanium compounds as catalysts, phosphite stabilizers to prevent degradation, and cobalt salts as bluing agents. Why bluing agents? Because PET has a slight tendency to yellow, and adding a tiny bit of blue dye counteracts this—the same principle behind grandmother's laundry bluing or the blue tint in some "bright white" paper.

A Material of Many Faces

The versatility of PET means it shows up in places you'd never expect.

Biaxially oriented PET film—BOPET, sold under brand names like Mylar—can be coated with an incredibly thin layer of aluminum vapor. This metallized film is nearly opaque, highly reflective, and far less permeable to gases than plain PET. It insulates those crinkly space blankets in emergency kits. It forms the reflective backing of flexible food packaging. It protects the backsides of solar panels from moisture and environmental degradation.

PET fiber, twisted into rope, protects the parts of church bell ropes that pass through ceilings—the repeated friction would wear through natural fibers quickly, but polyester resists abrasion.

High-pressure gas cylinders, the kind that hold compressed hydrogen or natural gas, now use PET as an internal liner because it blocks oxygen far better than the polyethylene previously used.

And then there's glitter. That sparkly stuff on greeting cards and children's crafts is typically made of three layers: a plastic core, a thin aluminum coating for reflectivity, and another plastic layer on top. For decades, PET was the plastic of choice. Recently, however, environmental concerns have pushed manufacturers toward biodegradable alternatives—glitter is essentially microplastic by design, and festival organizers have grown uncomfortable with participants literally throwing plastic confetti into the environment.

The Promise and Paradox of Recycling

When you see that number 1 recycling symbol on a PET bottle, it represents both a technological truth and a complicated reality.

The truth: PET genuinely can be recycled. Unlike many plastics, its molecular structure survives the recycling process relatively intact. Collected bottles can be washed, shredded, melted, and reformed into new products. Some recycled PET goes back into bottles. Much of it becomes polyester fiber for clothing—a fleece jacket might be made from 25 or 30 recycled bottles.

The reality: most PET isn't recycled. Collection rates vary wildly by region. Contamination with other plastics, food residue, or incompatible materials renders many loads unrecyclable. The economics often favor virgin plastic over recycled material. And each recycling cycle tends to shorten the molecular chains slightly, eventually degrading the material to the point where it can't be made back into bottles.

This is the paradox that makes PET central to debates about plastic pollution. It's technically one of the most recyclable plastics we have. Yet the sheer volume produced—billions of bottles manufactured daily, worldwide—overwhelms recycling infrastructure. The material's durability, so useful in products, becomes a curse when those products escape into the environment.

Bio-PET and the Future

One emerging approach tries to break PET's dependence on fossil fuels. Bio-PET uses ethylene glycol derived from plant sugars—typically from sugarcane ethanol—instead of natural gas. The resulting plastic is chemically identical to petroleum-based PET, behaves identically in products, and can be recycled in existing streams.

But bio-PET is only partially plant-based. The ethylene glycol component represents roughly 30 percent of the material by weight. The terephthalic acid still comes from petroleum. Researchers have proposed making terephthalic acid from furfural—a compound easily obtained from agricultural waste—but this hasn't yet achieved commercial scale.

A fully bio-based PET would still have the same end-of-life challenges as conventional PET. It would still persist in the environment if improperly disposed. It would still shed microfibers when made into clothing. The carbon in those plant-derived molecules might technically be "renewable," but the plastic products made from them would be just as durable and just as problematic if they ended up in oceans or landfills.

The Nanodiamonds Footnote

Here's a final, wonderfully strange fact about PET that circles back to its basic chemistry.

Remember that PET is essentially carbon combined with water in fixed proportions? Scientists realized they could exploit this by subjecting PET to extreme conditions—specifically, laser-driven shock compression that mimics the environment deep inside icy planets like Neptune and Uranus. Under these conditions, the water separates from the carbon, and the carbon atoms rearrange themselves into diamond. Tiny diamonds, just nanometers across, but diamonds nonetheless.

This isn't just a laboratory curiosity. It might actually represent a practical route to manufacturing nanodiamonds at commercial scale. The universe, it seems, has found one more unexpected use for the plastic that humans invented to make wrinkle-free shirts.

From wartime laboratory curiosity to fiber for fashion to bottles holding your beverages to potential feedstock for industrial diamonds, PET has become one of the most consequential materials humans have ever created. Whether that consequence is ultimately positive or negative depends largely on what we choose to do with it after we're done—a choice that 56 million tons of annual production makes more urgent every year.
