Jeremy Lin

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Jeremy Lin

On August 23, 1988, in a hospital in Torrance, California, a baby boy was born to parents who were only six feet tall combined. His father, Gie-ming, and his mother, Shirley, stood at 5 feet 6 inches each, a genetic reality that led many to dismiss the idea of their son ever playing professional basketball. They were immigrants from Taiwan, carrying the weight of engineering degrees earned in the American Midwest and the quiet determination of a family navigating a new culture. They could not have known then that this child would grow up to shatter the glass ceiling of the National Basketball Association, becoming the first person of Chinese or Taiwanese descent born in the United States to play in the league. By February 2012, Jeremy Shu-How Lin would lead an unlikely seven-game winning streak with the New York Knicks, sparking a global cultural phenomenon known as "Linsanity." Yet, the story of his rise is not merely one of sudden stardom; it is a testament to resilience against a system designed to overlook him.

The road to the NBA for Jeremy Lin was paved with rejection, a stark contrast to the narrative of the athletic prodigy who breezes through high school on a full scholarship. Born into a devout Christian family in Palo Alto, California, Lin's upbringing was defined by discipline and parental support that bordered on the unconventional. His mother, Shirley, established a local youth basketball program specifically to nurture her son's talent, working tirelessly with coaches to ensure his playing time did not compromise his academic rigor. While friends criticized her for letting him play so much, she recognized a spark in her son that others missed. By his senior year of high school (2005–06), Lin was captain of the Palo Alto High School team, leading them to a 32–1 record and a California state title, where they upset the nationally ranked Mater Dei. He averaged 15.1 points, 7.1 assists, 6.2 rebounds, and 5.0 steals per game, earning first-team All-State honors.

Despite these statistical feats, the collegiate recruitment landscape remained hostile to Lin's profile. He sent résumés and highlight DVDs to every Ivy League school, his dream schools of Stanford and UCLA, and the University of California, Berkeley. The response was a collective shrug. No athletic scholarship offers came from the Pac-10 conference. In a system that prioritizes immediate physical dominance over potential or skill, Lin was deemed too small, too slow, or simply not what recruiters were looking for. Harvard and Brown were the only universities that guaranteed him a spot on their teams, yet even then, they offered no athletic scholarships because of Ivy League rules. The basketball world had effectively closed its doors.

"Most colleges start recruiting a guy in the first five minutes they see him because he runs really fast, jumps really high, does the quick, easy thing to evaluate." — Rex Walters, former NBA player and coach.

The rejection stung deeply, but it also forged a path that would eventually define his legacy. Harvard assistant coach Bill Holden initially dismissed Lin as a "Division III player," unimpressed by what he saw on the court. It took a shift in perspective—a single game where Lin displayed "instincts of a killer," driving to the basket with relentless aggression—to change Holden's mind. The coaches feared Stanford, located literally across the street from his high school, would swoop in, but Stanford did not offer him a scholarship. Golden State Warriors owner and Stanford booster Joe Lacob later admitted the university's failure was "really stupid," noting that if you cannot recognize talent right in front of your face, you have a fundamental problem. UCLA assistant Kerry Keating echoed this sentiment in hindsight, suggesting Lin likely would have become the starting point guard for the Bruins had he been recruited properly.

Lin chose Harvard not as a fallback, but as an opportunity to prove himself on a stage that demanded excellence both academically and athletically. The transition was grueling. As a freshman, he was described as "the physically weakest guy on the team," a fact that seemed to confirm the doubts of scouts. But Lin did not fold; he evolved. By his sophomore season (2007–08), he averaged 12.6 points and earned a spot on the All-Ivy League Second Team. His junior year (2008–09) was nothing short of historic. He became the only NCAA Division I men's basketball player ranked in the top ten for his conference across scoring, rebounding, assists, steals, blocked shots, field goal percentage, free throw percentage, and three-point shooting. He led Harvard to an 82–70 victory over the 17th-ranked Boston College Eagles, posting 27 points, six rebounds, and eight assists just days after those same Eagles had defeated the number one team in the nation.

His senior year (2009–10) cemented his status as a generational talent within the Ivy League. He averaged 16.4 points, 4.4 rebounds, 4.5 assists, and 2.4 steals, earning unanimous All-Ivy League First Team selection for the second consecutive year. He was one of thirty midseason candidates for the prestigious John R. Wooden Award and an eleven finalist for the Bob Cousy Award. A defining moment came in a game against the 12th-ranked Connecticut Huskies, where he scored a career-high 30 points and grabbed nine rebounds on the road. Hall of Fame coach Jim Calhoun, known for his toughness, praised Lin's composure: "I've seen a lot of teams come through here, and he could play for any of them. He's got great, great composure on the court. He knows how to play." Harvard set numerous program records that season, but when the final buzzer sounded in 2010, Lin faced his next hurdle: the NBA Draft.

Despite his dominance at one of the world's most prestigious universities, Jeremy Lin went undrafted. The machine of professional basketball had once again failed to see him. He was offered a spot as an undrafted free agent by the Golden State Warriors, the team based in his hometown. For a brief moment, it seemed the dream might be realized. But reality quickly intervened. In his rookie season (2010–11), Lin seldom played. He was relegated to the NBA Development League, now known as the G League, where he was treated as an afterthought. The pattern continued into the 2011 off-season. The Warriors waived him in December 2011. In a twist of fate, he signed with the Houston Rockets just days later, only to be waived again before the season truly began. He was now 23 years old, a former college star without a job, living on the fringes of the sport he loved.

He found his way to the New York Knicks in late January 2012, but even there, his role was marginal. The Knicks were struggling, and Lin was seen as a depth piece at best. He spent more time in the G League than on the court with the big club. Then came February 4, 2012. With injuries decimating the Knicks' roster, coach Mike D'Antoni made a desperate move: he inserted Jeremy Lin into the starting lineup against the Utah Jazz. What happened next defies logical explanation in the cold calculus of sports analytics.

Lin did not just play; he orchestrated. He scored 25 points and dished out seven assists, leading the Knicks to a victory that felt like a seismic shift. The following night, against the Denver Nuggets, he dropped 28 points. By February 14, after a win over the Minnesota Timberwolves, Lin had led the Knicks on a historic seven-game winning streak. The media frenzy was instantaneous and overwhelming. "Linsanity" was born. It was a term that captured the surreal nature of the moment: an Asian American player, who had been told he didn't belong, was now carrying one of the most famous franchises in sports on his back. The phenomenon transcended basketball. Lin appeared on the covers of Sports Illustrated and Time magazine. He was named to the Time 100 list as one of the most influential people in the world. In July 2012, he won the ESPY Award for Breakthrough Athlete of the Year.

The cultural impact was profound because it struck a nerve that had been dormant for decades. Lin was not just a basketball player; he was a symbol of possibility for Asian Americans and anyone who had ever been told they were too small, too slow, or not "the right look" to succeed. His success challenged the racial stereotypes that had long permeated American sports, particularly in basketball, where the narrative often excluded players of Asian descent from high-level competition. For a brief, shining moment, the world stopped to watch a Harvard graduate make history on the hardwood.

However, the nature of professional sports is brutal and unforgiving. The magic of Linsanity could not be sustained indefinitely against the backdrop of injuries and the intense scrutiny of the league. After his tenure with the Knicks, Lin moved to the Houston Rockets, where he experienced a period of genuine success, leading the team in assists and showing flashes of his old brilliance. He later played for the Los Angeles Lakers, Charlotte Hornets, Brooklyn Nets, Atlanta Hawks, and finally, the Toronto Raptors. In 2019, while with the Raptors, he won an NBA championship, becoming the first Asian American player to ever win the title. It was a crowning achievement, a validation of his perseverance after years of being waived and overlooked.

Yet, the journey did not end there. As injuries began to mount in subsequent seasons, Lin found himself once again on the outside looking in. In August 2019, he made the bold decision to leave the NBA for the Chinese Basketball Association (CBA), signing with the Beijing Ducks. There, he became an All-Star, proving that his skills translated beyond American borders. He attempted a return to the NBA in 2021, playing for the Warriors' development team, but the door remained elusive. He returned to the CBA, playing for both the Beijing Ducks and the Guangzhou Loong Lions before departing midseason in 2022–23.

The narrative then shifted to Taiwan, where Lin's connection to his heritage ran deep. In 2023, he joined the Kaohsiung 17LIVE Steelers of the P. League+ (PLG), where he led the league in assists that season. His journey came full circle when he signed with the New Taipei Kings later that same year. Under his leadership, the team secured the PLG championship in 2024 and followed it up with a Taiwan Professional Basketball League (TPBL) championship in 2025. Lin had not only returned to his roots; he had become a pillar of the league, bridging the gap between American professional basketball and the burgeoning markets in Asia.

The story of Jeremy Lin is one of contradictions. He is a man who was rejected by every major college athletic program yet went on to dominate the Ivy League. He was waived twice before the season even started and then became a global icon within weeks. He was told he was too small for the NBA, only to win an NBA championship, and later told his time was up, only to lead teams in Taiwan to titles years after leaving the American league. His physical stature—6 feet 3 inches (1.91 meters) and 200 pounds (91 kg)—was not what scouts wanted, yet he possessed a "killer instinct" that no measurement could quantify.

His family's history is woven into every aspect of his career. His parents' journey from Taiwan to the United States in the mid-1970s mirrors Lin's own path through the American sports system: navigating barriers, overcoming prejudice, and proving worth through sheer hard work. His mother's insistence on balancing academics with athletics created a player who could think as well as he played. His father taught him the fundamentals at the local YMCA, instilling a love for the game that persisted even when the world turned its back.

"I've seen a lot of teams come through here, and he could play for any of them. He's got great, great composure on the court. He knows how to play." — Jim Calhoun, Hall of Fame coach.

This quote from Jim Calhoun after Lin's performance against UConn is perhaps the most telling assessment of his career. It was not about the stats or the records; it was about the composure and the understanding of the game. In a league often defined by raw athleticism and physical dominance, Lin succeeded through intelligence, precision, and an unyielding work ethic. He forced the world to re-evaluate what a basketball player could look like and where they could come from.

The legacy of Jeremy Lin extends far beyond the box scores. He opened doors for future generations of Asian American athletes who saw in him a reflection of their own potential. Before Lin, the idea of an Asian American star in the NBA was considered a near impossibility. After Linsanity, it became a reality he had carved out with his own hands. His journey from the streets of Palo Alto to the courts of New York, Houston, Toronto, Beijing, and Taipei is a testament to the power of belief in oneself when no one else believes in you.

As we look back at his career, spanning from 2010 to 2025, it becomes clear that Jeremy Lin's story was never just about basketball. It was about identity, resilience, and the refusal to accept the limits imposed by others. He was a player who defied the odds not once, but repeatedly. From the "Division III player" label at Harvard to the NBA champion with the Raptors, and finally to the champion in Taiwan, Lin's path was never linear. It was a winding road filled with detours, setbacks, and triumphs that redefined what it meant to be an underdog.

The cultural phenomenon of Linsanity may have lasted only weeks, but its impact has lasted for over a decade. Jeremy Lin proved that talent is universal, even if opportunity is not always distributed equally. He showed that sometimes the best player in the room is the one nobody was looking at. And he reminded us all that success is not defined by where you start, but by how far you are willing to go when the world tells you to stop.

In 2026, as Jeremy Lin looks back on a career that has spanned continents and championships, his story remains a powerful reminder of the human spirit's capacity to overcome. He is no longer just the guy who led the Knicks to a winning streak; he is a pioneer, a champion, and a symbol of everything that is possible when passion meets perseverance. The "Linsanity" moment was fleeting, but the legacy it built is permanent, etched into the history books of basketball and the hearts of fans around the world who saw themselves in his journey.

The narrative of Jeremy Lin is a complex tapestry of high achievement and deep struggle. It challenges the traditional narratives of sports success, which often favor the physically gifted over the intellectually prepared. Lin's success at Harvard was not an anomaly; it was a preview of a career defined by outworking opponents who were bigger and faster than him. His time in the G League was not a failure; it was a necessary crucible that forged the resilience required to survive the NBA.

Every step of his journey, from the rejection letters he received as a high school senior to the championship banners he helped raise in Taiwan, tells a story of a man who refused to be defined by the limitations others placed upon him. He is a player who proved that the game belongs to anyone willing to fight for it, regardless of their background or their height. And in doing so, Jeremy Lin changed the game forever.
