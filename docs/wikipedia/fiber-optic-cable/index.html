Fiber-optic cable

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Fiber-optic cable

In September 2012, a team at NTT in Japan achieved something that sounded like science fiction but was, in fact, a triumph of material engineering: they transferred data at a rate of one petabit per second over a distance of 50 kilometers. To put that staggering figure into perspective, a single fiber strand in that experiment was moving enough data in one second to fill the entire digital library of the world's largest streaming service, and then some. This was not a hypothetical future; it was a concrete demonstration of the physical limits of light being pushed to their absolute breaking point. Yet, the average user, scrolling through a feed or loading a webpage, rarely pauses to consider the physical vessel carrying their digital life. They see a screen, not the glass thread beneath the ocean floor or buried in the trench outside their home. The fiber-optic cable is the silent, invisible artery of the modern world, a deceptively simple assembly that has fundamentally rewired human civilization.

At its heart, a fiber-optic cable is an assembly that mimics the form of an electrical cable but replaces the copper conductors with something far more ephemeral: light. Inside the protective sheathing lie one or more optical fibers, thin strands of glass or plastic designed to guide photons with ruthless efficiency. The physics governing these strands is a masterclass in the manipulation of the natural world. An optical fiber consists of a core and a cladding layer, a dual structure selected specifically to exploit total internal reflection. This phenomenon occurs because of a precise difference in the refractive index between the two layers. When light enters the core at a shallow angle, it hits the boundary with the cladding and bounces back in, trapped in a perpetual zig-zag journey down the length of the fiber. It does not escape; it does not dissipate into the surrounding air. It is a prison of light, a highway where the vehicle never leaves the road.

To ensure this delicate dance of photons survives the harsh realities of installation and environment, the optical fiber is rarely left naked. In practical applications, the cladding is coated with a layer of acrylate polymer or polyimide. This coating is a guardian; it protects the microscopic glass from physical damage and environmental stress, though it contributes nothing to the optical waveguide properties themselves. The light travels through the glass, not the plastic. Beyond this immediate protection, individual coated fibers are gathered into bundles or ribbons, and a tough resin buffer layer or core tube is extruded around them to form the cable core. This is just the beginning of the armor. Several layers of protective sheathing are added, tailored to the specific application. For a cable running through the air-conditioned safety of a server room, the construction is lightweight and flexible. For a cable being dragged across the ocean floor, it is a feat of heavy engineering.

The complexity of these cables often hides in the details of their internal architecture. In rigid fiber assemblies, engineers sometimes insert light-absorbing, or "dark," glass between the fibers. This is not an empty space; it is a deliberate barrier. Its purpose is to prevent light that leaks out of one fiber from entering another, a phenomenon known as crosstalk. In imaging applications, this leakage would cause flare, blurring the picture. In data transmission, it corrupts the signal. The solution is to swallow the stray light before it can cause trouble. For indoor applications, the jacketed fiber is generally enclosed with a bundle of flexible fibrous polymer strength members, such as aramid fibers—better known by their trade names, Twaron or Kevlar. These are the muscles of the cable, providing tensile strength without adding weight, all encased in a lightweight plastic cover to form a simple, robust cable.

The ends of these cables are the interface between the physical world and the digital one. Each end may be terminated with a specialized optical fiber connector, a precision-engineered device that allows the cable to be easily connected and disconnected from transmitting and receiving equipment. This modularity is essential for the maintenance and expansion of global networks. However, the journey from the factory to the field is rarely smooth. For use in more strenuous environments, a much more robust cable construction is required, leading to the dominance of the "loose-tube" design. In this construction, the fiber is laid helically into semi-rigid tubes. This helical layout is a stroke of genius; it allows the cable to stretch, bend, and contract with temperature changes without ever stretching the fiber itself. The fiber floats, suspended in a protective tube, free from the tension that would otherwise snap it.

These loose-tube fibers may be dry block or gel-filled. The gel-filled variety offers superior protection against moisture and mechanical stress but comes at a higher cost and makes the cable messier to work with. Dry block options are cheaper but offer less protection, a trade-off that network planners must weigh against the specific risks of the deployment site. Alternatively, the fiber may be embedded in a heavy polymer jacket, a construction known as "tight buffer." This design is common for breakout and distribution cables, where flexibility and direct termination are paramount. Breakout cables are the heavy lifters of the indoor world, containing a ripcord, non-conductive dielectric strengthening members, aramid yarn, and 3 mm buffer tubing with an additional layer of Kevlar surrounding each fiber. The ripcord is a critical, often overlooked component: a parallel cord of strong yarn situated under the jacket, designed to be pulled to slice through the sheath cleanly during installation, sparing the delicate fibers inside from the knife.

Distribution cables take a slightly different approach, featuring an overall Kevlar wrapping, a ripcord, and a 900 micrometer buffer coating surrounding each fiber. These units are commonly bundled with additional steel strength members, again with a helical twist to allow for stretching. But perhaps the most critical concern in outdoor cabling is not tension, but water. Water is the enemy of optical fibers, capable of degrading the glass over time through a process called hydrolysis. This is accomplished by the use of solid barriers, such as copper tubes, and water-repellent jelly or water-absorbing powder surrounding the fiber. The cable becomes a fortress against the elements. Finally, the cable may be armored to protect it from environmental hazards, such as construction work, backhoe blades, or gnawing animals.

The stakes are even higher for undersea cables, the true backbone of the internet. These are more heavily armored in their near-shore portions to protect them from boat anchors, fishing gear, and even sharks. It is a documented fact that sharks are attracted to the electrical power carried in the cable to power amplifiers or repeaters. The cable, in effect, becomes a beacon for marine life, necessitating steel wire armor that can withstand a bite or a snag. Modern cables come in a wide variety of sheathings and armor, designed for applications ranging from direct burial in trenches to dual use as power lines, installation in conduit, lashing to aerial telephone poles, submarine installation, and insertion in paved streets. The jacket material is application-specific, determining the mechanical robustness, chemical resistance, and UV radiation resistance. Common materials include LSZH (Low Smoke Zero Halogen) for indoor safety, polyvinyl chloride for general use, polyethylene for outdoor durability, and polyurethane for flexibility.

The materials themselves tell a story of trade-offs. There are two main types of material used for optical fibers: glass and plastic. They offer widely different characteristics and find uses in very different applications. Generally, plastic fiber is used for very short-range and consumer applications, where cost and ease of termination matter more than distance. Glass fiber, however, is the workhorse of telecommunications, used for short and medium-range multimode applications and long-range single-mode connections. The physics of glass allows for distances and speeds that plastic simply cannot match. But the field is evolving. In 2025, a breakthrough occurred with the double-nested antiresonant nodeless fiber (DNANF). This hollow-core fiber achieved a record transmission loss of 0.091 dB/km at 1,550 nm, a figure lower than the best solid-core silica fibers, which hover around 0.14 dB/km. In this new design, light travels through air rather than solid glass. Field trials in China demonstrated an 800 Gbit/s hollow-core link over 20 km with fusion losses as low as 0.05 dB and an average cable loss of 0.6 dB/km. These hollow-core fibers reduce latency because light propagates faster in air than in glass, and they also suppress nonlinear effects and dispersion, offering a glimpse of a future where the speed of light is approached even more closely.

The capacity of these cables is nearly unfathomable. While larger cables are available, the highest strand-count single-mode fiber cable commonly manufactured is the 864-count, consisting of 36 ribbons each containing 24 strands of fiber. These high fiber count cables are the lifeblood of data centers and the distribution networks of HFC and PON systems. Yet, a curious economic reality often accompanies this abundance: in some cases, only a small fraction of the fibers in a cable may actually be in use. These unused fibers, known as "dark fiber," represent a vast, latent resource. Companies can lease or sell this unused capacity to other providers looking for service in or through an area. Depending on specific local regulations, companies may overbuild their networks for the specific purpose of having a large network of dark fiber for sale, reducing the overall need for trenching and municipal permitting. It is a strategy of abundance, where the cost of digging the trench is the same whether you lay one fiber or a thousand, making the marginal cost of extra capacity almost zero. Alternatively, some may deliberately under-invest to prevent their rivals from profiting from their investment, a darker economic strategy that contrasts with the collaborative spirit of open networks.

Despite their strength, optical fibers are not invincible. They are very strong, but their strength is drastically reduced by unavoidable microscopic surface flaws inherent in the manufacturing process. These flaws are the weak points where the glass can snap under stress. The initial fiber strength, as well as its change with time, must be considered relative to the stress imposed on the fiber during handling, cabling, and installation for a given set of environmental conditions. There are three basic scenarios that can lead to strength degradation and failure by inducing flaw growth: dynamic fatigue, static fatigue, and zero-stress aging. To combat these risks, the industry relies on rigorous standards. Telcordia GR-20, titled "Generic Requirements for Optical Fiber and Optical Fiber Cable," contains reliability and quality criteria to protect optical fiber in all operating conditions, with a specific focus on the outside plant (OSP) environment. For the indoor plant, similar criteria are outlined in Telcordia GR-409. These documents are the bibles of cable engineering, ensuring that the physical medium can withstand the rigors of decades of service.

The performance of these cables is measured in terms that sound abstract but have concrete impacts on our daily lives. Optical cables transfer data at the speed of light in glass. This is the speed of light in a vacuum divided by the refractive index of the glass used, typically resulting in speeds of around 180,000 to 200,000 km/s. This translates to a latency of 5.0 to 5.5 microseconds per kilometer. Thus, the round-trip delay time for 1000 km is around 11 milliseconds. In the world of high-frequency trading or real-time gaming, these milliseconds are the difference between victory and defeat. Signal loss in optical fiber is measured in decibels (dB), a logarithmic scale that quantifies the attenuation of the light signal. A loss of 3 dB across a link means the light at the far end is only half the intensity of the light that was sent into the fiber. A 6 dB loss means only one quarter of the light made it through. Once too much light has been lost, the signal is too weak to recover, and the link becomes unreliable and eventually ceases to function entirely. The exact point at which this happens depends on the transmitter power and the sensitivity of the receiver. Typical modern multimode graded-index fibers have a loss of 3 dB per kilometer, a figure that defines the maximum span between amplifiers in a network.

The story of fiber-optic cable is one of relentless refinement. From the simple concept of guiding light through glass to the complex, armored, gel-filled, hollow-core systems of today, the technology has evolved to meet the insatiable demand for data. It is a technology that operates on the edge of the physical world, where the laws of optics and the realities of material science intersect. It is the invisible infrastructure that allows the world to talk to itself, to trade, to learn, and to connect. As we look toward the future, with the promise of hollow-core fibers and petabit-per-second capacities, the fiber-optic cable remains the most significant physical achievement of the information age. It is a testament to human ingenuity that we have learned to trap light, to guide it across oceans, and to use it to build a world that is faster, more connected, and more complex than ever before. The next time you send a message, remember the journey it takes. It travels through a glass thread, protected by layers of polymer and steel, guided by the laws of physics, racing at the speed of light, all to reach you in the blink of an eye.
