Operation Mersad

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Operation Mersad

In the sweltering heat of July 1988, a column of 7,000 militants crossed the border from Iraq into western Iran, convinced that the Iranian government was on its knees and that their moment to reclaim the homeland had finally arrived. They called their offensive "Operation Eternal Light," a poetic name for a brutal incursion that would ultimately become the last major land battle of the eight-year-long Iran–Iraq War. What they did not know was that the Iranian leadership, having just swallowed the bitter pill of accepting a UN ceasefire, had been waiting in the shadows for exactly this moment. The ambush that followed, code-named Operation Mersad, or "Operation Ambush," was not merely a military defeat for the People's Mujahedin of Iran (MEK); it was a cataclysm that shattered the group's military capacity and triggered a wave of violence so severe that it would haunt the region for decades. To understand the geopolitical landscape of the Middle East today, where proxy wars and shadowy alliances define relations between Tehran, Baghdad, and Washington, one must first understand the blood-soaked dust of those few days in late July 1988.

The stage for this final act was set by a year of crushing defeats for the Islamic Republic. By the summer of 1988, the Iran–Iraq War had dragged on for nearly eight years, draining the blood and treasure of both nations. The tide had turned decisively against Iran following a series of disastrous Iraqi offensives. In early 1988, the Second Battle of Al Faw saw Iranian forces expelled from the strategic peninsula in the south, a humiliating blow that shattered the morale of the Revolutionary Guards. This was followed by Operation Tawakalna ala Allah, a massive Iraqi push along the central border that stripped Iran of territory and exposed the vulnerability of its defenses. The Iranian economy was in tatters, its cities were under constant bombardment, and the international community, weary of the stalemate, had long since moved on. On July 18, 1988, just six days before the MEK's incursion, Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini made a decision that would alter the course of history: he publicly announced Iran's formal acceptance of United Nations Security Council Resolution 598, effectively agreeing to end the war.

This acceptance of the ceasefire was viewed by many as a sign of weakness, a capitulation that invited opportunists to strike. The MEK, a secular opposition group that had been based in Iraq for years and was supported by Saddam Hussein's regime, saw the announcement as their golden ticket. They believed the Iranian state was fractured, its military exhausted, and its political will broken. On July 26, 1988, the MEK launched Operation Eternal Light. Their plan was audacious and straightforward: use their armored columns to spearhead an invasion from Iraqi territory, capture key cities like Kermanshah, and march on Tehran to topple the government. They were not just a ragtag guerrilla force; they were a conventional army equipped with tanks, artillery, and air support, numbering in the thousands.

The initial days of the operation appeared to confirm the MEK's worst fears and highest hopes. As the Iraqi air force provided cover, MEK columns rolled across the border, meeting what seemed to be negligible resistance. The Iranian Revolutionary Guards, stretched thin and demoralized by months of setbacks, were quickly pushed back. The MEK captured the towns of Kerend-e Gharb and Islamabad-e Gharb with alarming speed. They seized key strongholds along the vital Baghdad-Tehran highway, a logistical artery that could have allowed them to pour reinforcements deep into Iranian territory. Within 48 hours, they had advanced 145 kilometers (90 miles) into Iran, their vanguard threatening the provincial capital of Kermanshah, a city of 500,000 people. The Iranian Kurdish fighters offered some resistance, slowing the advance just enough to buy time, but the picture looked dire. The regime in Tehran appeared to be on the brink of collapse.

But the Iranian leadership had no intention of crumbling. In a move that would later be described as a masterstroke of military deception, they allowed the MEK to advance. They did not commit their full force immediately. Instead, they let the enemy stretch their supply lines, overextend their front, and commit their best units to a deep penetration that left them vulnerable. The Iranian commander who orchestrated this trap was Lieutenant-General Ali Sayad Shirazi, a brilliant tactician who understood the terrain and the psychology of the enemy better than anyone. Shirazi coordinated a seamless, if brutal, integration of the Iranian Army and the Revolutionary Guard, two forces that had often clashed in the past. He prepared a massive ambush, waiting for the MEK to reach the kill zone near Kermanshah before springing the trap.

The counteroffensive, Operation Mersad, began in earnest on July 30. The Iranian strategy was simple but devastating: cut off the MEK's retreat and destroy them with overwhelming air power. The Iranian Air Force, which had been largely grounded or ineffective earlier in the war, suddenly came alive. F-4 Phantom jets roared overhead, bombing the MEK convoys trapped on the Kermanshah highway. They were joined by Army Aviation helicopters, which swooped in with anti-tank missiles to pick off the exposed armored vehicles. The scene on the highway was apocalyptic. It was, in many ways, a miniature version of the "Highway of Death" that would occur in the Persian Gulf War just two years later, where retreating Iraqi forces were decimated by coalition air power. The MEK, caught in a pincer movement with no escape route, faced annihilation. Their tanks were destroyed, their vehicles burned, and their command structure shattered.

The effectiveness of the counterattack was staggering. Iranian forces reported destroying 200 tanks and 700 other vehicles. The MEK's advance was halted, and their forces were thrown into chaos. On July 31, Iranian troops retook the towns of Qasr-e Shirin and Sarpol-e Zahab, which the MEK had briefly occupied. The MEK claimed in their propaganda that they had "voluntarily withdrawn" from these towns, a lie that was easily dismantled by the visual evidence of burning wrecks and abandoned equipment littering the roads. The battle was over in a matter of days. The Iranian military claimed that 4,500 MEK soldiers were killed, while their own losses amounted to roughly 400 soldiers. Many senior MEK commanders were killed in the fighting, and those who surrendered were often not given quarter. The ambush had turned the MEK's "Eternal Light" into a funeral pyre.

The military victory of Operation Mersad, however, was only the prelude to a darker chapter. The defeat of the MEK on the battlefield did not end the conflict; it merely shifted it from the open desert to the prisons and interrogation centers of Tehran. The Iranian regime, emboldened by their military success and terrified of the MEK's internal support, decided to launch a campaign of retribution that would shock the world. Hussein-Ali Montazeri, a senior cleric and a key figure in the early days of the revolution, later stated that the Ministry of Intelligence had used the events of Operation Mersad as a pretext to carry out mass killings that "had been under consideration for several years." The logic was cold and calculating: the MEK had dared to attack Iran on the eve of the ceasefire, and the regime would respond by eradicating the opposition from within.

What followed was a systematic execution of thousands of political prisoners across Iran. The victims were not just MEK members, but also members of the Tudeh Party (the Iranian Communist Party) and other opposition groups. The estimates of the death toll vary wildly, ranging from 5,000 to 30,000 people. Some accounts suggest the number was even higher, particularly for those executed by frontline courts-martial or those who died in the chaos of prison transfers. The killings were swift, often conducted by "death commissions" that offered prisoners no real chance of defense. The events of 1988 remain one of the darkest stains on the history of the Islamic Republic, a period where the state turned its full apparatus of violence against its own citizens.

The geopolitical consequences of Operation Mersad extended far beyond the immediate aftermath. It marked the definitive end of the Iran–Iraq War's active land phase. The last notable combat actions occurred on August 3, 1988, when the Iranian navy fired on a freighter in the Persian Gulf and Iraq launched chemical attacks on Iranian civilians, killing an unknown number and wounding 2,300. On August 8, the UN Secretary-General announced that a ceasefire had been agreed upon, effective August 20. UN peacekeepers from the UNIIMOG mission were deployed to the border, remaining there until 1991 to monitor the cessation of hostilities. The war was over, but the scars it left would define the region for generations.

In the aftermath of the ceasefire, Iraq turned its attention to its own internal enemies. Using 60,000 troops, helicopter gunships, and chemical weapons, Saddam Hussein launched a brutal campaign to crush the Kurdish resistance in northern Iraq. The campaign, which lasted through August and early September 1988, saw the destruction of 15 villages and the forced relocation of tens of thousands of Kurds to government-controlled settlements. The death toll was horrific: 400 Iraqi soldiers and an estimated 50,000 Kurdish civilians and soldiers were killed. This campaign of terror, known as the Anfal campaign, drove many Kurdish civilians to flee across the border into Iran, adding to the humanitarian crisis in the region.

The legacy of Operation Mersad and the subsequent executions is complex and enduring. For the MEK, it was a catastrophic defeat from which they never fully recovered as a military force. Their base in Iraq was eventually dismantled, and they became a marginalized group, often viewed with suspicion by both the West and the region. For Iran, the victory solidified the power of the Revolutionary Guard and the security apparatus, cementing their role as the ultimate guardians of the regime. The events of 1988 also cemented the deep-seated animosity between the Iranian state and the MEK, a rivalry that continues to influence Iranian politics and foreign policy to this day.

Lieutenant-General Ali Sayad Shirazi, the architect of the ambush, became a national hero in Iran. His coordination of the Army and the Revolutionary Guard set a precedent for the integrated military command structure that exists today. However, his story also serves as a grim reminder of the cyclical nature of violence in the region. On April 1, 1999, an MEK operative posing as a road sweeper assassinated Shirazi outside his home in Tehran. The attack was a stark illustration of the fact that while the battle of 1988 had ended, the war of ideology and retribution had not. The shadow of Operation Mersad stretched across the decades, a testament to the brutal reality that in the Middle East, the past is never truly dead; it is simply waiting for the right moment to ambush the present.

The story of Operation Mersad is often overlooked in the broader narrative of the Iran–Iraq War, overshadowed by the chemical warfare, the tanker wars, and the diplomatic maneuvering of the UN. Yet, it was a pivotal moment that defined the trajectory of Iran's internal security and its external relations. It demonstrated the resilience of the Iranian state, its willingness to use extreme violence to maintain power, and the fatal miscalculation of its enemies who believed that the regime's moment of weakness was its moment of collapse. For those seeking to understand the current tensions in the Middle East, the events of July 1988 offer a crucial case study. They reveal a region where alliances are fragile, where military victories can lead to moral catastrophes, and where the ghosts of the past are always ready to strike.

The numbers tell a part of the story, but they cannot capture the human cost. The 4,500 MEK fighters who died on the highways of Kermanshah, the thousands of prisoners who were executed in the basements of Tehran, the 50,000 Kurds who perished in the Anfal campaign—these are not just statistics. They are the remnants of a war that ended in 1988 but whose echoes still reverberate through the streets of Tehran, Baghdad, and Washington. The decision to accept the ceasefire, the gamble of the MEK's incursion, and the ferocity of the Iranian response created a new reality in the region, one where the lines between friend and foe, victory and defeat, and justice and revenge were drawn in blood. As we look at the current geopolitical landscape, with its complex web of proxies and alliances, it is impossible to ignore the foundational events of that summer. The ambush of 1988 was not just a battle; it was a turning point that reshaped the destiny of a nation and a region.

The story of Operation Mersad is a reminder that in the theater of war, the most decisive moments often happen in the silence before the ambush, in the strategic patience of the commander, and in the brutal aftermath of victory. It is a story of hubris and tragedy, of tactical brilliance and moral failure. It is a story that demands to be told, not as a dry recitation of dates and numbers, but as a cautionary tale of the cost of war and the enduring power of the past to shape the future. The dust of the Kermanshah highway may have settled, but the questions it raises about power, violence, and justice remain as urgent today as they were in 1988.
