Free public transport

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Free public transport

The Day the Turnstiles Opened

On February 29, 2020—a leap day, fittingly—Luxembourg did something no country had ever done. It made every bus, tram, and train in the entire nation free to ride. No tickets. No fare inspectors. No fumbling for exact change. Just walk on and go.

This wasn't a promotional gimmick or a pandemic emergency measure. It was permanent policy.

The idea of free public transport—sometimes called zero-fare transit or fare-free public transit—sounds almost utopian. We're so accustomed to paying for things that the notion of a government simply removing a barrier feels radical. But Luxembourg wasn't the first to experiment with this concept. It was just the first to go all in, nationwide.

What "Free" Actually Means

Nothing, of course, is truly free. Someone pays. The question is who, and how.

When we talk about free public transport, we mean that passengers don't pay at the point of use. The buses still need fuel. The drivers still earn wages. The trains still require maintenance. The funding comes from somewhere else—usually taxes, sometimes commercial sponsorship, occasionally a combination of both.

Think of it like public roads. You don't insert coins into a slot before driving down a city street. The infrastructure is funded collectively through taxation, and then everyone can use it. Free transit extends this logic to buses and trains. The public pays once, through taxes, and then rides as often as they like without additional charges.

Some systems take a hybrid approach. In parts of Estonia, for instance, you might get a card that grants free access—but only if you're a registered resident. Tourists still pay. Other places offer free transit only to certain groups: seniors, students, people under a certain age.

The Map of Zero-Fare Cities

Luxembourg may have grabbed headlines, but the idea has been percolating in cities around the world for decades.

Hasselt, a Belgian city of about 77,000 people, abolished fares in 1997. The result was dramatic: ridership increased by as much as thirteen times over the following decade. People who had never considered taking the bus suddenly found themselves using it for grocery runs, commutes, and weekend outings.

Tallinn, Estonia's capital, took the plunge in 2013 after putting the question to a public vote. With more than 420,000 residents, it became one of the largest cities anywhere to offer free transit to its inhabitants. The key word there is inhabitants—tourists and non-residents still pay, a common approach that targets the benefit toward locals who contribute to the tax base.

But as of 2022, the largest city in the world with universal free public transport is Kharkiv, Ukraine. With a population of 1.42 million, Kharkiv introduced free transit for everyone that year—a decision made amid the chaos and hardship of war, when removing one more barrier to movement became a humanitarian necessity as much as a transportation policy.

Belgrade, Serbia, joined the club in 2025, offering free local transport to its 1.38 million residents.

Malta, the small Mediterranean island nation, made its bus network free in October 2022—but only for residents, following the Luxembourg model of reserving the benefit for those who live there and pay taxes.

The American Experiments

The United States, with its car-centric culture and fragmented transit systems, might seem an unlikely place for fare-free experiments. Yet they're happening, often in unexpected corners.

Washington State has been particularly active. Fourteen rural transit systems there have adopted zero-fare policies, some permanently, others as pilot programs. Since September 2022, everyone eighteen and under rides free on most local and inter-city transit systems across the state—including Amtrak trains operated by the state and even the Washington State Ferries. That last one catches people off guard. The ferry system, which carries cars and passengers across Puget Sound, is one of the largest ferry operations in the country. Free rides for youth on ferries represents a meaningful subsidy.

Tribal governments in the Pacific Northwest have been pioneers as well. The Muckleshoot, Spokane, Umatilla, and Yakama reservations all offer free bus service within their lands—a practical recognition that reliable transportation is essential for communities that often lack other options.

University systems have also experimented with fare-free approaches. When the University of California, Los Angeles began covering transit fares for its community, ridership jumped 56 percent in the first year. Solo driving dropped by 20 percent. These aren't marginal effects. When you remove the cost barrier, behavior changes substantially.

Why Do It?

The arguments for free transit fall into several categories, and they're more compelling than you might expect.

Speed. Every time someone boards a bus and pays a fare—whether with cash, card, or app—it takes time. Those seconds add up. Multiply by thousands of passengers and dozens of stops, and fare collection becomes a significant drag on the system. Buses run slower. Schedules slip. Reliability suffers. When fares disappear, boarding becomes nearly instantaneous. Operators can tighten schedules, run more frequent service, and actually keep to their timetables.

Safety. This one surprised me. In 2008, bus drivers in Essonne, France, went on strike demanding zero-fare transport—not because they wanted to give riders a break, but because they were tired of being assaulted. They claimed that 90 percent of the aggression they faced was related to fare disputes: passengers refusing to pay, arguing about change, or getting angry when their card didn't work. Remove the fare, and you remove the flashpoint. The drivers wanted free transit as a workplace safety measure.

Equity. Public transportation, at its best, serves as an equalizer. It allows people without cars to access jobs, healthcare, education, and social connections. But fares create a barrier. For someone living paycheck to paycheck, a few dollars per ride adds up. A family of four taking a round trip can easily face a twenty-dollar charge. Over a month, that's hundreds of dollars—money that could go to food, rent, or medicine. Free transit removes this barrier entirely, making mobility a public good rather than a purchased commodity.

Environment. Every trip taken on a bus or train is a trip not taken in a car. On average, cars emit about one pound of carbon dioxide per mile driven—280 grams per kilometer. Public transit, by consolidating many people into one vehicle, dramatically reduces per-person emissions. Free transit nudges more people toward this cleaner option. A study in Santiago, Chile, found that fare-free access increased overall travel by 12 percent, with off-peak travel jumping 23 percent. People were leaving their cars at home more often.

Simplicity. Fare systems are expensive to operate. You need ticket machines, fare gates, card readers, validators, enforcement officers, revenue collection staff, and entire departments to manage pricing, zone structures, and exemptions. When Portland, Oregon, eliminated its downtown "Fareless Square" in 2012, the transit agency actually saved money despite losing fare revenue—because they also eliminated all the costs associated with collecting that revenue.

The Economics of Free

Here's a number that might surprise you: in the United States, fare collection typically generates only about 10 percent of a transit system's revenue. The other 90 percent comes from taxes, government subsidies, and advertising. Fares are, in many cases, almost a rounding error.

This raises an obvious question: if fares contribute so little to the budget, why bother collecting them at all?

The answer is partly psychological. There's a belief—not entirely unfounded—that people value things more when they pay for them. Free transit, critics argue, might be seen as "less than" and attract rowdiness or misuse. There's also the principle that users should contribute something to the services they consume.

But those concerns have to be weighed against the real costs of fare collection: not just the equipment and staff, but the enforcement, the court cases for fare evasion, even the prison time served by people who couldn't pay their fines. Eliminating fares eliminates fare-hopping, which eliminates the need for enforcement, which reduces demands on the criminal justice system. It's a cascading effect.

Swedish activists have taken this logic to its conclusion. A network called Planka.nu—roughly translated as "fare-dodge dot now"—operates as a kind of insurance cooperative. Members pay monthly dues into a common fund. If any member gets caught riding without a ticket and receives a fine, the fund pays it. The logic is that it's cheaper to pay occasional fines collectively than it would be for everyone to buy tickets individually. It's civil disobedience as financial optimization.

The Countries Leading the Way

Beyond the headline-grabbing Luxembourg story, several countries have embraced free or heavily subsidized transit as national policy.

Estonia embarked on an ambitious plan to make the entire country zero-fare. Between 2018 and 2024, buses ran free in eleven of Estonia's fifteen counties. Tallinn, as mentioned, had been free for residents since 2013. But in January 2024, the country pulled back somewhat—free local transport in most counties was abolished, though it remains available for anyone under nineteen or over sixty-three. It's a reminder that these policies can shift with changing governments and budgets.

The United Kingdom offers free bus travel to senior citizens nationwide, though the age threshold varies: sixty and over in Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland, and Greater London; state pension age elsewhere in England. Scotland went further in 2022, extending free bus travel to everyone under twenty-two. The logic is straightforward: young people are often poor, they're forming transportation habits, and getting them on buses now might keep them riding for life.

Romania gives free transit—including buses, subways, and inter-city trains—to all pre-university students. University students get a 50 percent discount instead, which seems like an odd place to draw the line, but education systems are full of arbitrary boundaries.

The Netherlands takes a different approach for students. Those enrolled in universities or applied science institutions receive free nationwide transit—trains, trams, buses, metro—but there's a catch. You have to finish your degree within ten years of starting it. Take longer, and you owe the government for all those free rides. It's an interesting incentive structure: the transit benefit becomes a soft deadline for graduation.

Spain, facing the economic shock of energy price spikes after Russia's invasion of Ukraine, made all commuter and medium-distance train trips (under 300 kilometers) free from September 2022 through June 2025. The condition: you had to use your multi-trip ticket at least once a week on average. It was a response to a crisis, but it also demonstrated that fare-free transit could be implemented quickly when political will existed.

Hungary's national railway, MÁV, stopped charging passengers sixty-five and older, or fourteen and younger, starting in March 2024. The country's bus system, Volánbusz, followed suit. It's a targeted approach, focusing on groups that often have limited incomes or are considered deserving of public support.

Emergency Free Transit

Some of the most interesting zero-fare experiments have happened during crises.

When wildfires tore through Northern California's wine country in 2017 and again in 2019, the Sonoma-Marin Area Rail Transit system—a commuter rail line that had only opened in 2017—temporarily dropped fares. Roads were clogged with evacuees, air quality was hazardous, and people needed alternatives. The train became a lifeline.

Snow emergencies have prompted similar responses. The Central Ohio Transit Authority and King County Metro, among others, have offered free rides during severe winter weather. The reasoning is practical: every car that stays off the road makes it easier for plows to clear the streets and reduces the risk of accidents.

The COVID-19 pandemic triggered the most widespread fare-free experiment in history. A study of 263 public transit agencies found that 63.5 percent suspended fare collection at some point during the pandemic. The reasons varied: concern that the virus could spread through touching fare machines, a desire to keep passengers from passing close to drivers, and the practical need to allow rear-door boarding on buses. Smaller cities, where fares represented a lower percentage of revenue, were more likely to go fare-free. Larger urban systems, more dependent on farebox income, generally maintained some form of fare collection.

The Other Side of Free

Not everyone thinks zero-fare transit is a good idea.

Critics point to the experience of some systems that went free and then struggled with overcrowding, schedule adherence, and what's delicately called "rowdiness"—usually meaning teenagers with too much energy and nowhere else to go. When transit becomes free, it also becomes a de facto public space, attracting people who aren't necessarily traveling anywhere. This can create friction with riders who are trying to commute to work or get to an appointment.

There's also an argument that free transit can actually hurt low-income residents if it leads to service cuts. If fare revenue disappears and isn't fully replaced by other funding, agencies might reduce service—cutting routes, decreasing frequency, or deferring maintenance. A free bus that comes once an hour is less useful than a cheap bus that comes every ten minutes.

Some economists argue that the money spent on making transit free would be better spent on improving transit: more buses, better routes, faster service, cleaner vehicles. A system that's excellent and charges a modest fare might attract more riders than a mediocre system that's free.

And then there's the question of who benefits. Research by Stroud and Bekhit, published in 2025, found that only 25 percent of studies on fare-free public transport significantly considered how the policy affected marginalized communities. We know less than we should about whether free transit actually helps the people who need it most, or whether the benefits flow disproportionately to those who already have other options.

The Road Ahead

Free public transport exists on a spectrum. At one end, you have Luxembourg's comprehensive approach: everyone rides free, everywhere, all the time. At the other end, you have targeted programs: students in Romania, seniors in the United Kingdom, youth in Washington State.

Most places are somewhere in between, experimenting with pilot programs, emergency measures, and partial implementations. The trend, at least in Europe, seems to be toward more free transit, not less. But trends reverse. Estonia's recent pullback shows that zero-fare policies can be scaled back when priorities shift.

What's clear is that the idea is no longer fringe. When a country as prosperous and well-organized as Luxembourg makes all transit free, it becomes harder to dismiss the concept as impractical idealism. When a city of 1.4 million people like Kharkiv implements universal free transit—even amid war—it demonstrates that necessity can drive innovation.

The Swedish activists at Planka.nu, the bus drivers in Essonne striking for their safety, the UCLA students riding free and leaving their cars at home, the tribal governments running buses across reservations—they're all part of a broader conversation about what transportation is for. Is it a commodity to be bought and sold? Or is it a public good, like roads and sidewalks, that enables everything else a society wants to do?

The answer, increasingly, seems to be: it depends on where you live. And that answer might be changing.
