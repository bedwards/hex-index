Technological utopianism

13 min read

The Dream That Refuses to Die

In 1989, as the Berlin Wall crumbled and the Soviet empire collapsed, Ronald Reagan offered a triumphant explanation: "The Goliath of totalitarianism will be brought down by the David of the microchip." It was a perfect encapsulation of an idea that has captivated humanity for centuriesâ€”that our machines will set us free.

This belief has a name: technological utopianism. And despite being declared dead after every catastrophe from world wars to financial crashes, it keeps coming back, each time wearing slightly different clothes.

What Technological Utopianism Actually Means

At its core, technological utopianism is the conviction that advances in science and technology canâ€”and shouldâ€”create an ideal society. Not just make things somewhat better, but fundamentally transform the human condition. We're talking about the elimination of scarcity, the end of suffering, perhaps even the defeat of death itself.

This is different from simply believing technology is useful. Most people appreciate antibiotics and smartphones without thinking they'll lead to paradise. Technological utopians take a much bolder position: they see technology as the primary engine of human progress, capable of solving not just technical problems but social, political, and even moral ones.

The opposite viewâ€”technological dystopianismâ€”warns that our creations will destroy us. Think of Frankenstein's monster, the Terminator, or countless science fiction tales where humanity's inventions turn against their makers. Between these poles lies a more moderate position called technorealism, which tries to evaluate each technology on its actual merits without the grand narratives of salvation or doom.

The Radical Origins

Technological utopianism wasn't always associated with Silicon Valley billionaires. Its roots lie in some of the most radical movements of the nineteenth century.

Karl Marxâ€”yes, that Karl Marxâ€”believed science and democracy were "the right and left hands" of human liberation. He argued that scientific advances helped delegitimize the divine right of kings and break the political power of the Christian Church. For Marx, technology wasn't neutral; it was revolutionary, laying the groundwork for entirely new forms of society.

Early socialists were obsessed with the transformative power of industry. Robert Owen, Charles Fourier, and Henri de Saint-Simon inspired communal movements with visions of scientific and technological evolution. They saw reason and progress as inseparable, and factory production as the key to abundance for all.

Edward Bellamy's 1888 novel "Looking Backward" depicted a socialist utopia so technologically advanced that it inspired hundreds of political clubs across the United States and even a national political party. For Bellamy and the Fabian Socialists in Britain, the socialist future would arrive almost automatically as a "painless corollary" of industrial development. Build enough factories, they believed, and equality would follow.

The Soviet Experiment

No society embraced technological utopianism more fervently than the Soviet Union.

From its founding in 1917, the Bolshevik regime imagined "a world of magnificent factories and mechanized agriculture that produced all of society's necessities." This wasn't just propagandaâ€”it was a genuine obsession. Historian Asif Siddiqi calls technological utopianism a "millenarian mantra" in Soviet thinking, comparing it to religious prophecy about the end times.

The Soviets believed technology was essentially apoliticalâ€”a tool that served capitalist profit under one system but could benefit all humanity under socialism. This led to massive efforts to develop domestic technological capacity independent of the West, supported by self-sufficient engineering communities and supply chains.

Soviet science fiction reflected this vision, endlessly depicting futures where technological utopia and socialist utopia had merged into one. The genre served both as entertainment and as a form of ideological education, teaching citizens to associate communist governance with technological marvels.

The irony, of course, is that the Soviet system ultimately fell behind the capitalist West in most technological domains. The dream of "technology for the masses" never quite materialized as promised.

The Dark Side

Technological utopianism has a disturbing shadow history that its modern proponents rarely discuss.

In the early twentieth century, some technological utopians embraced eugenicsâ€”the idea that humanity could be improved through selective breeding. Studies of families with names like the Jukes and Kallikaks claimed to prove that traits like criminality and alcoholism were hereditary. The conclusion seemed obvious to many progressives of the era: prevent "defective" people from reproducing, and society would improve.

This wasn't a fringe belief. Forced sterilization programs were implemented across several American states, affecting tens of thousands of people deemed unfit to reproduce. The logic was impeccable if you accepted the premise: technology and science should be used to optimize humanity itself.

Then came the Holocaust.

The philosopher Theodor Adorno argued that the Nazi death camps shattered the Enlightenment ideal that scientific progress automatically meant social progress. Here was the most technologically sophisticated nation in Europe using its industrial capacity for systematic genocide. The gas chambers were, in their horrible way, technological marvelsâ€”efficiently engineered for mass murder.

After World War II, faith in technological salvation seemed naive at best, complicit at worst. The dream went dormant.

The California Resurrection

It didn't stay dormant for long.

In the 1990s, a new form of technological utopianism emerged from an unlikely fusion: the counterculture of the 1960s meeting the personal computer revolution of the 1980s. The birthplace was Northern California, and the result became known as the Californian Ideology.

This new synthesis combined bohemian anti-authoritarianism with libertarian economics and digital evangelism. Its adherents believed that networked computers would fundamentally transform human societyâ€”not through government programs or collective action, but through individual empowerment.

Wired magazine, founded in San Francisco in 1993, became the movement's bible. Its pages overflowed with breathless predictions about how digital technology would free individuals from "the rigid embrace of bureaucratic big government." Traditional hierarchies would become obsolete. "Self-empowered knowledge workers" would escape not just their cubicles but the modern city itself, which Wired writers dismissed as "an obsolete remnant of the industrial age."

The movement claimed to transcend conventional politics. Right versus left? Obsolete categories for a digital age. In practice, though, this new techno-utopianism attracted adherents overwhelmingly from the libertarian right. Hostility toward government regulation and faith in free markets were core tenets. Prominent evangelists included George Gilder and Kevin Kelly, whose books and articles shaped the worldview of a generation of tech entrepreneurs.

Boom and Bust

The late 1990s dot-com bubble provided perfect conditions for techno-utopian fever.

Stock prices for internet companies soared to absurd heights. Commentators proclaimed "the end of the business cycle" and "a new era of permanent prosperity." For the small percentage of the population holding tech stocks or working at startups, the future had arrived ahead of schedule.

Then came the crash of 2000.

The NASDAQ lost nearly 80 percent of its value. Trillions of dollars in paper wealth evaporated. Companies that had been valued in the billions disappeared entirely. Many techno-utopians had to "rein in some of their beliefs in the face of the clear return of traditional economic reality," as one historian delicately put it.

But here's the remarkable thing: the dream survived. Within a few years, a new wave of tech companiesâ€”Google, Facebook, Amazonâ€”would generate even more spectacular wealth and even grander visions of technological transformation. Wikipedia itself, that remarkable experiment in collective knowledge-building, emerged from this period. As The Economist noted, it "has its roots in the techno-optimism that characterised the internet at the end of the 20th century"â€”the belief that "ordinary people could use their computers as tools for liberation, education, and enlightenment."

The Current Landscape

Today's technological utopianism has evolved but not disappeared.

Transhumanism argues that technology should be used to enhance human capabilities beyond their biological limitsâ€”extending lifespan, augmenting intelligence, perhaps eventually uploading consciousness into machines. Singularitarianism, associated with figures like Ray Kurzweil, predicts an approaching moment when artificial intelligence surpasses human intelligence, triggering changes so rapid and profound that they're impossible to predict.

A movement called effective accelerationism, often abbreviated as "e/acc," takes an even more aggressive stance, advocating for technological progress "at all costs." Its adherents argue that slowing down or attempting to regulate transformative technologies is not just futile but morally wrongâ€”that maximum acceleration toward technological capability is humanity's best path forward.

The philosopher Nick Bostrom has tried to think systematically about what a technologically mature civilization might look like. In his book "Deep Utopia: Life and Meaning in a Solved World," he cataloged technologies that are theoretically achievable: cognitive enhancement, reversal of aging, self-replicating spacecraft, the precise control of motivation and mood and personality. The question Bostrom grapples with is not whether such technologies are possible, but what human life would mean in a world where all our traditional problems had been solved.

The North Korean Exception

Perhaps the strangest contemporary example of technological utopianism exists in North Korea.

The country's official Juche ideology promotes the pursuit of advanced technology as integral to building an independent, self-reliant state. But North Korean technological utopianism has a peculiar flavor. It rejects consumer society and cultureâ€”no one is dreaming of better smartphones. Instead, it emphasizes heavy industry and grand infrastructure projects, many of which have primarily symbolic importance.

Nuclear weapons and space rockets feature prominently in state propaganda, portraying North Korea as a modern regional power despite its grinding poverty. Most technological resources flow into massive, resource-intensive projects rather than anything that might improve daily life.

It's a reminder that technological utopianism can take wildly different forms depending on who's doing the dreaming.

The Believers' Creed

The philosopher Bernard Gendron identified four core principles shared by modern technological utopians:

First, we are currently experiencing a revolutionary transformation in technologyâ€”often called the "post-industrial revolution" or the "digital revolution" or the "AI revolution," depending on the era.

Second, technological growth will continue at least at its current pace, and probably accelerate.

Third, this sustained growth will eventually eliminate economic scarcity. There will be enough for everyone.

Fourthâ€”and this is the crucial leapâ€”eliminating economic scarcity will eliminate every major social evil. Poverty causes crime; eliminate poverty, eliminate crime. Scarcity causes conflict; eliminate scarcity, achieve peace.

The media theorist Douglas Rushkoff documented additional beliefs common in techno-utopian circles: that technology reflects and encourages humanity's best aspectsâ€”communication, collaboration, sharing, community. That technology democratizes society by spreading knowledge and reducing inequalities of power. That technological progress is inevitable and its unforeseen impacts are generally positive. That new technologies can solve the problems created by old technologies.

The Critics

Not everyone is convinced.

Cultural critic Imre Szeman argues that technological utopianism is fundamentally irrationalâ€”a form of faith rather than reason. There is, he points out, no actual evidence that technological progress leads to social progress. The twentieth century saw both unprecedented technological advancement and unprecedented technological barbarism. The correlation between better machines and better societies simply doesn't exist in the historical record.

Critics from the left point out that modern techno-utopianism, particularly the Silicon Valley variety, tends to focus obsessively on "government interference" while ignoring or dismissing the role of business regulation in protecting workers, consumers, and the environment. The environmental impact of technologyâ€”the e-waste, the energy consumption, the resource extractionâ€”rarely features in utopian visions.

There's also the uncomfortable fact that most of the world's population has yet to benefit from the digital revolution. The global digital divide means that techno-utopian dreams are largely the province of wealthy nations and wealthy individuals within those nations. For a farmer in sub-Saharan Africa or a factory worker in Bangladesh, talk of post-scarcity futures can seem obscenely disconnected from reality.

Perhaps the most troubling critique concerns dependency. In 2011, during riots in British cities, Prime Minister David Cameron suggested the government should have the power to shut down social media during civil unrest. A poll asked Twitter users whether they would accept a temporary shutdown to help contain violence. Overwhelmingly, respondents said noâ€”they wanted to keep discussing the reality television show "The X-Factor."

The anecdote suggests something uncomfortable about technological utopia: we might become so dependent on our technologies that we can't give them up even when doing so would serve the greater good. The tools of liberation become the chains of addiction.

The Persistent Dream

So why does technological utopianism keep coming back?

Part of the answer may be that it reflects something deep in modern consciousnessâ€”what Szeman calls our "faith in narratives of progress and technology overcoming things, despite all evidence to the contrary." We want to believe that our problems have solutions, that the future will be better than the past, that our species is going somewhere.

Technology provides a tangible vehicle for these hopes. Unlike political revolutions or religious salvation, technological progress is visible and measurable. We can hold the smartphone in our hands. We can watch the rocket launch. We can see the AI generate images that no human could draw.

And sometimesâ€”often, evenâ€”technology does make things better. Vaccines prevent suffering. Agricultural advances feed billions. Communication technologies connect people across distances that would have seemed magical to our ancestors. The techno-utopians aren't entirely wrong; they're just prone to extrapolating too far from genuine achievements.

The question isn't whether technology can improve human lifeâ€”it obviously can and does. The question is whether technology alone can solve our deepest problems: inequality, conflict, meaning, mortality. Whether the David of the microchip can really slay every Goliath.

History suggests skepticism. But the dream persists, updated for each new technological generation, promising that this timeâ€”with artificial intelligence, with brain-computer interfaces, with nanotechnology, with whatever comes nextâ€”we really will transcend our limitations and build the world we've always imagined.

Maybe they're right. Or maybe each generation of believers is simply finding new reasons to hope for an old salvation that never quite arrives.
