Liquefied natural gas

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Liquefied natural gas

In 1944, a quiet industrial neighborhood in Cleveland, Ohio, became the site of one of the most devastating industrial disasters in American history. The East Ohio Gas Company had constructed a facility to store natural gas in liquid form, a revolutionary concept designed to buffer the city against the biting cold of winter. On October 20, the cylindrical tank containing the super-chilled fuel ruptured. Thousands of gallons of liquefied natural gas (LNG) spilled onto the ground, evaporated instantly, and ignited. The resulting fire consumed the plant and the surrounding neighborhood, claiming 130 lives. For years, this catastrophe cast a long shadow over the industry, delaying the widespread adoption of cryogenic storage. Yet, the failure of that tank did not kill the idea; it merely forced a reckoning with the physics of the impossible. The disaster was a painful lesson in the sheer power of transforming a gas into a liquid, a process that shrinks the volume of natural gas to one-six-hundredth of its original size while demanding a temperature of -162 °C (-260 °F). Today, that same physics underpins a global energy architecture that connects the deserts of Qatar to the power grids of Japan, turning a once-local byproduct into a geopolitical weapon.

To understand the magnitude of this shift, one must first grasp the fundamental nature of the substance itself. Natural gas is predominantly methane (CH4), a simple molecule of one carbon and four hydrogen atoms, often accompanied by smaller amounts of ethane (C2H6). In its natural state, it is odorless, colorless, non-toxic, and non-corrosive. It is the cleanest-burning fossil fuel, yet in its gaseous form at standard temperature and pressure, it is incredibly diffuse. Storing it in massive quantities is a logistical nightmare; it requires vast, high-pressure tanks or miles of pipeline, and even then, leakage is a persistent risk. The breakthrough that changed the calculus of global energy was the realization that if you could cool this gas down to near absolute zero, you could compress it into a liquid state that is manageable, storable, and transportable.

The process of liquefaction is a feat of thermodynamic engineering that begins long before the gas hits the cryogenic temperatures. The raw natural gas stream is a chaotic mixture of impurities that would wreak havoc on the liquefaction equipment. Dust, acid gases like carbon dioxide and hydrogen sulfide, helium, water vapor, and heavy hydrocarbons must be stripped away. These components, if left in the mix, would freeze and clog the machinery or cause corrosion downstream. Once purified, the gas enters the heart of the plant, where it is condensed into a liquid at close to atmospheric pressure. The cooling process is relentless, driving the temperature down to approximately -162 °C (-260 °F). At this temperature, the gas molecules slow down enough to coalesce into a liquid, occupying a volume roughly 1/600th of what they held as a gas. This density reduction is the magic trick of the industry. It transforms a fuel that was once trapped by geography into a commodity that can sail across oceans.

The engineering challenge does not end with the cooling. The liquefaction process is incredibly energy-intensive, requiring specialized infrastructure and a level of precision that few industries can match. The resulting LNG is stored in tanks designed with a "Thermos bottle" architecture, a concept patented by Godfrey Cabot in 1915. This design features a cold inner tank suspended within an outer tank, separated by layers of advanced insulation to prevent the heat of the outside world from warming the cryogenic liquid. The maximum transport pressure is kept low, around 127 kPa (18 psi), which is only about 1.25 times atmospheric pressure. This low-pressure storage is a key safety feature, contrasting sharply with the high-pressure tanks needed for compressed natural gas (CNG). The gas stream is often separated into fractions; the heavier butane and propane are stored as liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) at relatively low pressures, while the lighter ethane and methane fractions make up the bulk of the LNG shipped globally.

The history of this technology is a tapestry of scientific curiosity and industrial necessity. Long before the first commercial plant, scientists were unraveling the secrets of gas behavior. By the mid-seventeenth century, Robert Boyle had derived the inverse relationship between the pressure and volume of gases. Around the same time, Guillaume Amontons was investigating the effect of temperature on gases. In the early nineteenth century, Cagniard de la Tour discovered the critical temperature, the point above which a gas could not be liquefied regardless of pressure. It was not until 1886 that Karol Olszewski successfully liquefied methane, proving the primary constituent of natural gas could be tamed.

The first large-scale application of this science in the United States occurred in 1918, driven not by energy markets but by military necessity. The U.S. government liquefied natural gas to extract helium, a small component of certain gas fields. This helium was intended for use in British dirigibles during World War I. The LNG produced was not stored; it was immediately regasified and fed into the gas mains. The concept of storing natural gas as a liquid was still in its infancy, and the idea of transporting it across oceans was unthinkable. The key patents that would eventually enable the modern industry date from 1915 and the mid-1930s. In 1915, Godfrey Cabot patented his double-walled storage design. In 1937, Lee Twomey received patents for a large-scale liquefaction process intended to support peak energy loads during cold snaps. Twomey utilized a variant of the Linde process, which relies on the Joule-Thomson effect—a principle developed by James Joule and William Thomson. In this method, gas is cooled regeneratively by continually passing and expanding it through an orifice until it reaches the temperature at which it liquefies. A variation known as the Claude process is also used today.

The modern era of LNG began to take shape in the 1960s with the development of the cascade process. Engineers at Phillips Petroleum Company developed the Optimized Cascade Process, which employed three successive refrigeration cycles: propane for precooling, ethylene (or ethane) for intermediate cooling, and methane for final liquefaction. This stepwise approach dramatically improved thermodynamic efficiency and operational stability. It was first applied commercially at the Kenai LNG plant in Alaska in 1969, marking the debut of the first baseload LNG export facility in the United States. However, the first true commercial plant was built earlier, in 1940, by the East Ohio Gas Company in Cleveland. This facility, the first of its kind in the world, featured three spheres, each approximately 63 feet (19 m) in diameter, capable of holding the equivalent of 50 million cubic feet of natural gas. A fourth, larger cylindrical tank was added in 1942. For three years, the plant operated successfully, regasifying the liquid fuel to meet demand during cold snaps and preventing the denial of gas to customers. The tragedy of 1944, however, halted progress. The fire delayed the implementation of LNG facilities for several years, but it also spurred a wave of research into low-temperature alloys and better insulation materials. The industry did not die; it evolved.

The revival of the industry in the late 1950s marked the transition from local storage to global trade. In 1959, a U.S. World War II Liberty ship, the Methane Pioneer, was converted to carry LNG. It made a historic delivery from the U.S. Gulf Coast to energy-starved Great Britain, proving that the fuel could survive an ocean voyage. In June 1964, the world's first purpose-built LNG carrier, the Methane Princess, entered service. Soon after, the discovery of a massive natural gas field in Algeria opened the floodgates for international trade. The technology had finally caught up with the geography, allowing nations to decouple their energy security from domestic reserves.

Before the late 20th century, natural gas was largely viewed as a byproduct of oil production, often flared off or vented into the atmosphere because it was too difficult to transport. The development of production processes, cryogenic storage, and specialized transportation made it possible to commercialize natural gas, creating a global market that now competes with oil and coal. The ability to store natural gas in liquid form for extended periods solved the reliability issues that had plagued gas networks. Unlike simple tank storage used for other fuels, natural gas could not be stored for long periods as a gas due to leakage. Large-scale cryogenic storage made it possible to create reliable long-term reserves. These reserves could be quickly deployed through regasification processes, becoming the main means for networks to handle local peak shaving requirements—meeting the surge in demand during the coldest days of winter.

The economics of LNG are driven by its energy density. The heating value of LNG depends on the source of the gas and the specific liquefaction process used, with a range that can span ±10 to 15 percent. A typical value for the higher heating value of LNG is approximately 50 MJ/kg (21,500 BTU/lb), while the lower heating value is around 45 MJ/kg (19,350 BTU/lb). When expressed as energy per volume, the density of LNG is roughly 0.41 to 0.5 kg/litre, depending on temperature, pressure, and composition. Using a median value of 0.45 kg/litre, the typical energy density is 22.5 MJ/litre based on the higher heating value. This volumetric energy density is approximately 2.4 times that of compressed natural gas (CNG), which is why it is economical to transport natural gas by ship. The energy density of LNG is comparable to propane and ethanol, though it is only 60 percent that of diesel and 70 percent that of gasoline. Despite being less dense than liquid petroleum fuels, the sheer volume reduction of liquefaction makes it the only viable option for long-distance maritime transport of natural gas.

Today, the production and trade of LNG are concentrated in a handful of nations, creating a hyper-concentrated global supply chain. As of 2023, the United States, Australia, and Qatar held the most capacity for exporting LNG, while China, Japan, and South Korea were the biggest importers. This concentration creates significant geopolitical leverage. A 2025 report by the International Energy Agency (IEA) projected that more capacity would come online in the upcoming decade, but the risks of this concentration are already manifesting. The 2026 War in Iran, for instance, affected Qatar's LNG exports, causing a ripple effect in global access and cost. These choke points in the supply chain highlight the fragility of a system that relies on a few key nodes to power a significant portion of the global economy.

The hazards of LNG are real and must be managed with rigorous discipline. While the liquid itself is non-toxic and non-corrosive, the hazards include flammability after vaporization, extreme freezing temperatures, and asphyxiation risks. If a spill occurs, the liquid rapidly vaporizes, creating a cloud of gas that can ignite if it encounters a spark. The freezing capability of the liquid can damage materials not designed for cryogenic service, and the displacement of oxygen by the vaporizing gas can create asphyxiation hazards in confined spaces. Yet, the industry has matured significantly since the tragedy in Cleveland. The lessons learned from that fire led to the development of robust safety protocols and engineering standards that have allowed the industry to expand to a scale that would have been unimaginable a century ago.

The story of LNG is one of human ingenuity overcoming the constraints of nature. It began with the observation of Boyle and Amontons, moved through the experimental liquefaction of Olszewski, and survived the tragedy of Cleveland to become a pillar of the global energy system. It transformed a local nuisance into a global commodity, reshaping the geopolitical landscape and providing a bridge between the fossil fuel past and a lower-carbon future. As the world grapples with the complexities of energy transition, LNG remains a critical, albeit controversial, component of the energy mix. The physics of the process have not changed; the gas still shrinks to 1/600th of its volume at -162 °C, and the ships still sail across the oceans carrying the promise of energy security. What has changed is the scale and the stakes. In a world where energy access is a matter of national security, the ability to liquefy, transport, and regasify natural gas is not just an industrial capability; it is a strategic imperative. The future of this technology will be defined by how well the world can manage the risks of concentration and the environmental impacts of production, but the fundamental breakthrough—the ability to turn a gas into a liquid and move it across the world—remains one of the most significant engineering achievements of the modern era.

The narrative of LNG is far from over. As new fields are discovered and new markets open, the infrastructure continues to expand. The technology has evolved from the simple spheres of Cleveland to the massive, floating production, storage, and offloading (FPSO) units that now dot the coastlines of the world. The efficiency of the liquefaction processes has improved, reducing the energy penalty of converting gas to liquid. The safety record has improved, with incidents becoming rare anomalies rather than industry-defining tragedies. Yet, the core challenge remains: balancing the benefits of a versatile, clean-burning fuel with the risks of a highly concentrated, energy-intensive supply chain. The events of 2026 serve as a stark reminder that in a globalized energy system, a disruption in one corner of the world can be felt in every other. The liquefaction of natural gas is a triumph of engineering, but it is also a testament to the interconnectedness of the modern world, where a tank rupturing in Ohio or a conflict in the Persian Gulf can send shockwaves through the global economy. The story of LNG is the story of how humanity learned to tame the invisible, making the impossible possible, and in doing so, reshaping the world.
