Chagos Marine Protected Area

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Chagos Marine Protected Area

On April 1, 2010, the British government signed an order creating one of the largest protected areas on Earth. It covered 640,000 square kilometers of the central Indian Ocean, a contiguous swath of blue that included seven atolls and roughly 70 tiny islands. To the environmentalist, this was a miracle: a pristine sanctuary for 220 species of coral and nearly 800 species of fish, a place where the waters were so clean and the reefs so healthy they offered a glimpse of what the oceans looked like before the industrial age. To the historian of human rights, however, this same date marked the culmination of a decades-long strategy of erasure. The establishment of the Chagos Marine Protected Area (MPA) was not merely an act of conservation; it was a geopolitical maneuver designed to ensure that no human being would ever set foot on the islands again. As a WikiLeaks cable later revealed, a US State Department official, reflecting on the views of British ministers, noted that the reserve would be the "most effective long-term way to prevent any of the Chagos Islands' former inhabitants or their descendants from resettling." The ocean was saved, but at the cost of a people.

To understand the weight of this paradox, one must first understand the geography and the stakes. The Chagos Archipelago sits in the heart of the Indian Ocean, a strategic pivot point located about 1,500 kilometers from the southern tip of India, 3,400 kilometers east of Africa, and 3,000 kilometers west of Indonesia. Politically, these islands constitute the British Indian Ocean Territory (BIOT), a remnant of the British Empire that exists today almost entirely as a military zone. The largest island, Diego Garcia, hosts a joint UK-US military base, a facility of such critical importance to American global power projection that its presence dictates the political reality of the entire region. Before the MPA was declared, the islands had been stripped of their human population. In the late 1960s and early 1970s, the Chagossian people were forcibly expelled from their homes to make way for the US base. This expulsion, which involved herding families onto boats and leaving them in Mauritius and Seychelles with little more than the clothes on their backs, has been described by legal scholars and human rights advocates as ethnic cleansing. By the time the MPA was proposed in 2010, the islands had been empty for nearly forty years, a ghost archipelago where the only residents were the military personnel and the wildlife.

The conservation argument for the MPA was, on its surface, irrefutable. The area encompasses the Great Chagos Bank, the world's largest coral atoll. It contains one of the healthiest reef systems on the planet, supporting nearly half of the good quality reefs in the entire Indian Ocean. The biodiversity here is staggering. Ninety percent of the United Kingdom's total biodiversity lies within its Overseas Territories, and the Chagos Archipelago is the crown jewel of this collection. The habitats range from extensive shallow limestone reefs to about 300 seamounts and a deep-sea trench that plunges more than 4,900 meters (16,000 feet) into the abyss. The coral cover is dense even in deep water and on the steep outer slopes of the reefs, a phenomenon that defies the usual patterns of degradation seen elsewhere. Scientists have identified the Ctenella chagius, a variety of brain coral believed to be endemic to the atoll, and vast populations of staghorn coral that play a crucial role in protecting low-lying islands from wave erosion. The fish populations are equally robust, with 784 species identified, including the Chagos clownfish, a species found nowhere else on Earth. In a world where coral reefs are bleaching and fish stocks are collapsing, the Chagos MPA represented a "natural laboratory," a benchmark for conservation that allowed researchers to study complex ecosystems in a state of near-perfect equilibrium. The strict no-take policy meant that fishing was banned, allowing populations of manta rays, whale sharks, tuna, and the globally threatened silky shark to thrive without human interference.

Yet, the path to this sanctuary was paved with political calculation rather than pure environmental altruism. The case for a large-scale marine reserve was first formally put forward in March 2009 by a consortium of conservation organizations led by the Chagos Environment Network. This group, a collaboration of nine leading scientific bodies, launched a report titled "The Chagos Archipelago: Its Nature and the Future," which laid out the scientific imperative for protection. The momentum was genuine among conservationists. From November 2009 to March 2010, the UK government conducted a public consultation, asking whether an MPA should be established and, if so, to what degree. The response was overwhelming. Over 250,000 people submitted views, with more than 90% declaring support for greater marine protection. The majority of these respondents believed the area should be a fully no-take marine reserve. However, there was a significant economic hurdle: the UK government was earning approximately £800,000 a year from licenses issued for commercial tuna fishing. To designate the area as a no-take zone would mean sacrificing this revenue. It was in this financial gap that the true nature of the political alliance emerged. The Bertarelli Foundation, a Swiss philanthropic organization, stepped in to offer to fill the funding gap left by the absence of fisheries income. They pledged to contribute to the costs of an enforcement boat for a period of five years. This financial backstop cleared the way for the British government to act.

But the timing of the declaration was suspicious. The British Foreign Secretary instructed the BIOT Commissioner to establish the Marine Protected Area on April 1, 2010. This date was not chosen at random; it was essentially the last day the Commissioner could issue the order before the dissolution of Parliament prior to the 2010 British general election. By pushing the decision through at the eleventh hour, the government ensured that the issue would not be debated in the new Parliament, effectively burying the controversy in the administrative dust of a political transition. The resulting MPA was a fully no-take area, with one glaring exception: a 3-mile zone around the island of Diego Garcia. This exemption allowed people from the US military base to continue fishing. In 2010 alone, more than 28 tonnes of fish were caught for the use of personnel on the base. The terms of the reserve explicitly stated that the BIOT, including Diego Garcia, was reserved for military uses. A leaked cable from the US State Department explained the dual purpose of the reserve with chilling clarity. It noted that the establishment of the marine park had the potential to be a "win-win situation in terms of establishing situational awareness" of the BIOT. The goal was to create a situation where the environmental protection served as a shield for military operations, ensuring "no constraints on military operations" as a result of the park's creation.

The legal repercussions of this maneuver were swift and severe. The MPA did not go unchallenged. Mauritius, which had been separated from the Chagos Archipelago by the British in the lead-up to its own independence, had long contested the legality of the UK's administration of the territory. On March 18, 2015, the Permanent Court of Arbitration issued a landmark ruling. The court held unanimously that the establishment of the marine protected area was illegal under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea. The judges found that the UK had failed to fulfill its obligations to Mauritius, which held legally binding rights to fish in the waters surrounding the Chagos Archipelago, to an eventual return of the archipelago, and to the preservation of any minerals or oil discovered in the area prior to its return. The court's decision was final and binding, a rare moment where international law pushed back against the geopolitical machinations of a superpower. The ruling stripped the MPA of its legal foundation, transforming it from a symbol of conservation success into a testament to the limits of imperial overreach. The MPA is now under negotiation between the UK government and the government of Mauritius, a diplomatic stalemate that reflects the deeper, unresolved conflict over the sovereignty of the islands.

The administration of the Chagos MPA remains a complex and controversial endeavor. It is managed with the assistance of the Chagos Trust, an organization funded by donations and the British Government. Despite the designation as a protected area, tourist visitors are generally not permitted to enter. The strict nature reserve status means that the islands remain largely inaccessible to the public, preserving their isolation. However, as of 2016, private yachts sailing across the Indian Ocean may apply for mooring permits, though these are restricted to areas outside the strict nature reserve zones. This policy maintains the dichotomy of the Chagos: it is a sanctuary for nature, but a fortress for the state. The conservation success is undeniable. The no-take policy has freed the region's fish populations from the pressure of overfishing, creating a refuge for pelagic species that are threatened elsewhere. The deep-water trench, a geological wonder, is believed to harbor a variety of previously undiscovered species, waiting to be cataloged by science. The coral reefs, dense and diverse, serve as a buffer against erosion and a home to endemic species like the Chagos clownfish. But the human cost of this success remains a scar on the landscape.

The story of the Chagos MPA is a story of two competing narratives. One is the narrative of environmental salvation. It is a story of how a coalition of scientists, conservationists, and philanthropists managed to protect a unique and fragile ecosystem from the ravages of industrial fishing. It is a story of how the world's largest coral atoll was given the breathing room it needed to thrive, creating a benchmark for the rest of the planet. The other narrative is the story of displacement and denial. It is a story of how a government used the rhetoric of environmentalism to cement a military occupation and prevent the return of a displaced people. The WikiLeaks cables exposed the cynicism at the heart of the project, revealing that the "win-win" situation was a win for the military and the environment, but a loss for the Chagossians. The exemption for the US base, the timing of the declaration, and the explicit goal of preventing resettlement all point to a calculated strategy. The MPA is not just a park; it is a political tool.

The legal victory of Mauritius in 2015 did not immediately change the physical reality on the ground. The MPA remains in place, its boundaries drawn on the maps of the Indian Ocean. The coral continues to grow, the fish continue to swim, and the sharks continue to patrol the deep waters. But the political status of the area remains in flux. The UK government has opposed the area being proposed as a World Heritage Site, likely due to the sensitivity of the sovereignty dispute, yet it has agreed to treat it as such in order to preserve its environmental value. This contradiction highlights the fragility of the current arrangement. The protection of the Chagos is contingent on a political settlement that has yet to be reached. Until the question of sovereignty is resolved, the MPA will remain a symbol of both the best and the worst of human ambition. It is a place where nature is thriving because humans have been pushed away, but where the human presence that was pushed away is the very reason the humans were pushed away in the first place.

The Chagos Marine Protected Area stands as a complex monument to the 21st century. It is a place where the lines between conservation, geopolitics, and human rights are blurred. The Great Chagos Bank, with its 300 seamounts and deep trenches, offers a glimpse of a world that can exist without human exploitation. But the history of the islands serves as a reminder that the absence of humans is not always a natural state. It is often the result of force, calculation, and the prioritization of strategic interests over human dignity. The MPA protects the biodiversity of the United Kingdom, but it also protects the military base that makes the territory a source of contention. It is a no-take zone, but it is also a no-return zone for the Chagossians. The debate over the MPA is not just about fish or coral; it is about who owns the ocean, who has the right to live on the islands, and whether the protection of nature can ever be separated from the politics of power. As the negotiations between the UK and Mauritius continue, the world watches to see if the legal victory of 2015 will translate into a resolution that honors both the environment and the people. The coral reefs will survive regardless, but the human story of the Chagos remains unresolved, waiting for a conclusion that justice demands but history has yet to provide.
