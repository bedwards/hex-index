Anti-corruption campaign under Xi Jinping

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Anti-corruption campaign under Xi Jinping

In December 2012, shortly after assuming the mantle of General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party, Xi Jinping oversaw the abrupt dismissal of Li Chuncheng, the Deputy Party Secretary of Sichuan province. It was a quiet, administrative removal that signaled a seismic shift in the political architecture of the world's second-largest economy. Li was not merely a local bureaucrat; he was the first casualty of a campaign that would come to define a generation of Chinese governance. This was not a routine personnel shuffle. It was the opening salvo of the most extensive and systematic anti-corruption effort in the history of the Communist Party, a purge that would eventually swallow hundreds of generals, dozens of ministers, and five national leaders, fundamentally altering the balance of power within the state.

When Xi Jinping took office following the 18th National Congress of the CCP, the party was grappling with a crisis of legitimacy. Since the economic reforms of 1978 had unleashed a flood of wealth, political corruption had metastasized, becoming the primary threat to the regime's survival. The types of offenses were as varied as they were brazen: local businesses trading bribes for massive government contracts, subordinates paying for promotions, and officials trading state assets for personal luxury. At the 18th Party Congress, both outgoing leader Hu Jintao and Xi Jinping issued stark warnings. Xi, in his inaugural address on November 15, 2012, made the stakes explicit. He warned his colleagues on the Politburo that corruption would "doom the party and state."

The strategy he deployed was deceptively simple in its rhetoric but devastating in its execution. He vowed to crack down on "tigers and flies." The "flies" were the petty civil servants, the grassroots officials who extracted small bribes from citizens daily. The "tigers" were the high-ranking elites, the untouchable princes of the party who had grown fat on decades of unchecked power. For the first time in the party's history, the distinction between the two was rendered moot by the sheer scale of the investigation. The campaign was not a sporadic cleanup; it was a total war on the party's internal rot, administered with a cold, mechanical precision that left no province or institution untouched.

The Architecture of Power

To understand the sheer magnitude of this campaign, one must first understand the machinery that drove it. The primary engine was the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection (CCDI). Formally, the CCDI is an internal party agency tasked with enforcing party discipline, combating malfeasance, and punishing members who commit offenses. It does not, on paper, possess judicial authority. It is a watchdog within the party, not a court of law. Its official mandate is to investigate, gather evidence, and, when necessary, forward its findings to judicial organs like the Supreme People's Procuratorate, which then charges the accused and moves the case to trial.

However, in the hands of Xi Jinping, the CCDI became a weapon of unprecedented reach. From 2012 to 2017, the agency was led by Wang Qishan, a member of the seven-man Politburo Standing Committee and a politician with a deep background in the financial sector. Wang was the architect of the day-to-day execution of the purge. He brought a financial auditor's rigor to political investigations, treating the party like a corporation rife with embezzlement. Under his leadership, the CCDI's power was centralized, effectively undermining the original functions of local Discipline Inspection Commissions. The old guard, who might have protected local allies, were bypassed.

The ultimate oversight of this agency, nominally the highest representative body of the party, fell directly under the purview of Xi Jinping. As General Secretary and Chairman of the Central Military Commission, Xi held the keys to both the party and the army. While formal disciplinary measures against high-ranking officials, such as former Politburo members, required ratification by the sitting Politburo, the power of the anti-corruption drive was concentrated in the Politburo Standing Committee itself. This centralization allowed Xi to bypass traditional bureaucratic hurdles and strike directly at the heart of the opposition.

The mechanism for this strike was the "central inspection teams" (中央巡视组). These were not permanent fixtures but surgical units dispatched to specific regions, government ministries, or state-owned enterprises. Led by the Central Leading Group for Inspection Work, also under Wang Qishan's command, these teams would "station" themselves in an organization for a few months. Their mandate was a thorough audit of conduct and organizational practice. They would pore over financial records, interview whistleblowers, and scour internal communications.

Once the inspection teams completed their audits, they sent their findings to the CCDI. This triggered the formal investigative procedures, most notably the practice known as Shuanggui. Shuanggui is a unique, extra-legal detention method where party members are held incommunicado for investigation. They are removed from the public eye, often to remote locations, and subjected to intense interrogation until they confess to their crimes. It is a process that operates outside the standard legal framework, relying on the party's internal authority to detain its own members. The results of these interrogations provided the ammunition for the judicial bodies to step in and file criminal charges.

The Fall of the Giants

The true test of the "tiger" strategy lay in the willingness to prosecute the most powerful men in the country. In the history of the CCP, national leaders had rarely, if ever, been held accountable for corruption after leaving office. Xi Jinping shattered this taboo. The campaign targeted sitting and former national-level leaders with a ruthlessness that stunned the political elite.

The first major casualty of this new era was Zhou Yongkang. A former member of the Politburo Standing Committee and the national security chief, Zhou was considered one of the most powerful men in China. His network of influence was vast, spanning the national oil sector, where he had once been a chief executive, Sichuan province, where he served as party chief, and the security organs under the Central Political and Legal Affairs Commission. In the latter half of 2013, a separate operation began specifically to investigate officials connected to Zhou. The campaign dismantled his network piece by piece, targeting the three sectors he controlled. Zhou was eventually investigated, expelled from the party, and sentenced to life in prison. His fall sent a shockwave through the entire system: if Zhou Yongkang could be taken down, no one was safe.

The purge did not stop there. It moved on to the military, an institution that had long been a fortress of its own interests. Former Politburo members and Central Military Commission (CMC) vice chairmen Xu Caihou and Guo Boxiong were investigated and removed. Their cases were particularly sensitive, as they involved the upper echelons of the People's Liberation Army (PLA). The investigation into Xu Caihou, who had been accused of selling military promotions, exposed deep rot within the officer corps. Guo Boxiong followed, further demonstrating that the military was not exempt from Xi's vision of a clean party.

The campaign also targeted sitting Politburo members, proving that tenure and current power offered no shield. Sun Zhengcai, the Party Secretary of Chongqing, was investigated in 2017. His removal was a stark reminder that even the youngest and most promising members of the elite could be cast aside if they fell afoul of the campaign. The reach continued to expand into the future, with reports of investigations into CMC vice chairmen He Weidong and Zhang Youxia in 2025 and 2026 respectively, suggesting that the campaign's momentum had not waned but rather evolved into a permanent feature of governance.

The scale of the prosecutions is difficult to comprehend. As of 2023, approximately 2.3 million government officials had been prosecuted. The numbers include dozens of ministerial-level officials, hundreds of deputy ministerial-level officials, and executives of state-owned enterprises. The campaign was not just about punishing individuals; it was about reshaping the culture of the party. It was a message to every civil servant, from the lowest clerk to the highest general: the era of impunity was over.

The Constitutional Shift

As the campaign progressed, it became clear that the existing legal and political framework was insufficient to sustain the new reality. The CCDI, as an internal party body, operated in a gray area between party discipline and state law. To solidify the anti-corruption drive, Xi Jinping and his allies sought to embed it into the very fabric of the state.

In February 2023, proposed constitutional changes were published that would fundamentally alter the structure of China's anti-corruption apparatus. These changes envisioned the creation of a new state agency: the National Supervisory Commission. This body was designed to merge the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection with various anti-corruption government departments. The result was a powerhouse that held the same constitutional status as the State Council, the Supreme People's Court, and the Supreme People's Procuratorate.

The National Supervisory Commission was not merely a parallel body; it was intended to be the highest state supervisory body in the country. By outranking the courts and the office of the prosecutor in terms of supervisory authority, it signaled a shift where anti-corruption efforts were no longer just a party internal matter but a constitutional imperative. This move effectively institutionalized the power of the CCDI, giving it the full weight of the state behind it. It meant that the "tigers and flies" strategy was no longer a temporary campaign but a permanent feature of the Chinese political landscape.

The creation of the National Supervisory Commission also addressed the jurisdictional gaps that had previously allowed officials to slip through the cracks. By merging party and state functions, the new agency could investigate any public official, regardless of whether they were a CCP member. This expanded the reach of the campaign to include non-party public servants, further tightening the net around corruption. The constitutional changes were a testament to Xi Jinping's determination to leave a legacy of a clean, disciplined, and unified party.

The Cost of Purity

The anti-corruption campaign under Xi Jinping has been a defining feature of his political legacy. It has restored a degree of order and discipline to the party, curbing the rampant graft that had threatened to destabilize the regime. The message was clear: the party would survive, but only if it was pure. The campaign has become a central hallmark of Xi's term in office, with the majority of media reporting highlighting his direct involvement in managing the purge.

However, the campaign has also come with significant costs. The sheer scale of the investigations has created an atmosphere of fear and uncertainty within the bureaucracy. Officials, terrified of being accused of corruption, have become risk-averse, hesitant to make decisions or take initiatives for fear of crossing an invisible line. The "Shuanggui" process, while effective in extracting confessions, has raised concerns about due process and the rule of law. The centralization of power in the hands of the Politburo Standing Committee and the CCDI has weakened the checks and balances that existed within the party, concentrating authority in the hands of a few.

The impact on the economy has been mixed. On one hand, the reduction in corruption has improved the business environment, making it harder for companies to secure contracts through bribery. On the other hand, the uncertainty and the risk-averse behavior of officials have slowed down infrastructure projects and economic initiatives. The campaign has also strained the relationship between the party and the people, as the line between political purges and genuine anti-corruption efforts can sometimes blur.

Despite these challenges, the campaign has achieved its primary goal: it has cemented Xi Jinping's authority and ensured the survival of the CCP. By cracking down on both "tigers and flies," he has demonstrated that no one is above the law of the party. The campaign has reshaped the political landscape of China, creating a new normal where corruption is met with swift and severe punishment.

A Legacy of Discipline

The anti-corruption campaign is more than just a list of names and prison sentences. It is a profound transformation of the Chinese political system. It has redefined the relationship between the party and the state, between the leader and the led. It has shown that the CCP is capable of self-correction, of rooting out the rot that threatens its very existence.

The campaign began with the promise of a clean slate and has evolved into a permanent institution. The creation of the National Supervisory Commission ensures that the fight against corruption will continue long after Xi Jinping leaves office. The "tigers and flies" strategy has become a mantra of the party, a reminder of the cost of betrayal.

As the campaign moves into its second decade, the questions remain: Can the party maintain this level of discipline indefinitely? Will the fear of punishment lead to a stagnation of innovation and initiative? And most importantly, can the party truly claim to be free of corruption when the power to judge is so centralized?

For now, the answer lies in the continued success of the campaign. The hundreds of officials who have been prosecuted, the billions of dollars in assets recovered, and the restored faith of the people in the party's ability to govern are testaments to its effectiveness.
