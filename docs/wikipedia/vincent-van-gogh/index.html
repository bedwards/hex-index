Vincent van Gogh

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Vincent van Gogh

On a rainy Tuesday in late March 1853, a child was born in Groot-Zundert, a small village in the predominantly Catholic province of North Brabant, Netherlands. The parents, Theodorus van Gogh, a minister of the Dutch Reformed Church, and his wife Anna Cornelia Carbentus, named him Vincent Willem. They did not know it then, but they were naming their son after a ghost: a brother who had been stillborn exactly one year prior. This first Vincent, the grandfather's namesake and the family's lost heir, left a void that the living Vincent would spend his entire life trying to fill, not with words or money, but with a color so intense it seemed to burn through the canvas. He was the oldest surviving child of a modest but respected upper-middle-class family, a boy who would grow up to be quiet, serious, and thoughtful, yet increasingly haunted by a mind that refused to stay still.

The Weight of a Name and the Shadow of Failure

To understand Vincent van Gogh, one must first understand the gravity of his lineage. He was born into a world of religious duty and artistic commerce, a strange duality that would fracture his soul. His grandfather, Vincent (1789–1874), was a theology graduate from the University of Leiden and a prominent art dealer, a man whose name carried weight in both the pulpit and the gallery. Three of this grandfather's six sons became art dealers, following a path of trade that the family seemed destined to walk. Vincent's mother, Anna, came from a prosperous family in The Hague, and the union of these two lines produced a household that, while not wealthy in the grand sense, was comfortable. Theodorus's salary as a minister was modest, but the Church provided a house, a maid, two cooks, a gardener, and even a carriage with a horse.

Yet, within this orderly, duty-bound existence, signs of instability began to whisper. Vincent was born with a heavy inheritance: the name of a dead brother. His mother instilled in her children a profound sense of duty, a rigid adherence to the expectations of their station. But Vincent, the firstborn, seemed to carry a melancholy that was not merely youthful introspection. He was serious, almost to a fault. While his younger brother Theo, born on May 1, 1857, would grow to be the steady anchor of the family, Vincent drifted. He had a brother named Cornelis, known as "Cor," and three sisters: Elisabeth, Anna, and Wil. In later life, only Wil and Theo remained in his orbit, the others fading into the polite distance of Victorian propriety.

Vincent's early attempts to find his place in the world were a catalogue of well-intentioned failures. He drew as a child, but his path to adulthood was not the straight line of a painter's apprenticeship. Instead, he entered the world of his uncles and grandfather: the art trade. He worked as an art dealer, a job that required him to travel, to speak the language of commerce, and to navigate the social currents of the art market. But the commercial world, with its calculated assessments of value and taste, clashed violently with Vincent's internal landscape. He was transferred to London, a city of fog and industry, where he fell into a deep depression. The isolation of a foreign city, combined with a temperament that could not easily bend to the demands of a sales ledger, broke him. He turned away from the gallery and toward the pulpit.

The Missionary and the Madman

If the art dealer was a failure, the missionary was a tragedy. In his quest for spiritual redemption, Vincent traveled to the coal-mining region of southern Belgium. He threw himself into the lives of the miners with a ferocity that bordered on the pathological. He did not just preach; he lived as they lived. He gave away his clothes, his food, and his bedding, sleeping on straw and starving himself to better understand the suffering of his flock. He wanted to be a Christ figure among the poor, but the church hierarchy saw only a madman. They deemed his behavior excessive, his lack of hygiene and dignity a stain on the ministry. He was dismissed.

This rejection was a blow from which he never fully recovered. He drifted into ill-health and solitude, a man cast out by both the secular world of business and the sacred world of the church. It was in this valley of despair, while back with his parents, that he finally turned to painting. It was 1881. He was twenty-eight years old, nearly thirty, and had wasted a decade of his life in failed careers and broken spirits. But the canvas offered something the church and the shop could not: a place where his internal chaos could be externalized, where his pain could be translated into something tangible.

His early works from this period are dark, somber, and heavy. They are mostly still lifes and depictions of peasant labourers, painted in browns and grays, echoing the grim reality of the miners he once served. He was keenly aware of modernist trends in art, even as he struggled to find his own voice. But he was not alone. His younger brother Theo, the one constant in his life, became his lifeline. Theo, an art dealer, provided financial support, emotional stability, and access to the contemporary art scene. Their relationship was the backbone of Vincent's existence, a bond forged in the fires of shared history and unspoken love. They maintained a long correspondence, a torrent of letters that would become the primary source of our understanding of the artist.

"There are more than 600 letters from Vincent to Theo and around 40 from Theo to Vincent."

These letters are not mere business correspondence. They are eloquent, expressive, and possess a "diary-like intimacy" that reads in parts like autobiography. As the translator Arnold Pomerans noted, their publication adds a "fresh dimension to the understanding of Van Gogh's artistic achievement, an understanding granted to us by virtually no other painter." In these pages, Vincent dissected his own thoughts, his theories of art, his despair, and his fleeting moments of hope. He wrote to Theo about the color of the sky, the texture of bread, and the crushing weight of loneliness. He wrote to his sister Wil, to the painter Anthon van Rappard, and to Émile Bernard. Some letters were illustrated with sketches, tiny maps of his mind. Many were undated, but art historians have managed to place most in chronological order, creating a map of a life lived at a fever pitch.

The Light of Arles

In 1886, Vincent moved to Paris. It was a move that would change everything. The city was the center of the artistic avant-garde, a place where the old rules of Impressionism were being shattered. Here, he met members of the artistic community, including Émile Bernard and Paul Gauguin. These men were seeking new paths, new ways to see the world. Vincent was frustrated in Paris, overwhelmed by the noise and the competition, but he was also inspired. He saw a growing spirit of artistic change and collaboration that ignited a spark in his soul.

In February 1888, seeking a place where he could establish an artistic retreat and commune, Vincent moved to Arles in southern France. The change in his work was immediate and dramatic. The dark, muddy tones of his Dutch period vanished. Once in Arles, his paintings grew brighter, exploding with yellow, blue, and green. He turned his attention to the natural world, depicting local olive groves, wheat fields, and sunflowers with a passion that bordered on the religious. He saw the sun not as a distant star, but as a living force, a symbol of life and death.

"His bold use of colour, expressive line and thick application of paint inspired avant-garde artistic groups like the Fauves and German Expressionists in the early 20th century."

Vincent's vision was one of pure emotion. He did not paint what he saw; he painted what he felt. The sunflowers were not just flowers; they were symbols of gratitude and light. The wheat fields were not just crops; they were the earth breathing. He invited Gauguin to join him in Arles, eager to build an "Studio of the South," a utopian community of artists where they could work together and support each other. He eagerly anticipated Gauguin's arrival in late 1888, dreaming of a future where art and life could be one.

But the dream was fragile. Vincent suffered from psychotic episodes and delusions. He worried constantly about his mental stability, and often neglected his physical health. He did not eat properly, drank heavily, and pushed his body to the brink. His friendship with Gauguin, which began with high hopes, ended in a confrontation that would become legend. In a rage, fueled by alcohol and a mind unraveling, Vincent mutilated his own left ear with a razor. It was a moment of pure, unadulterated tragedy, a physical manifestation of the war raging inside him.

The Final Act

After the incident with Gauguin, Vincent was admitted to a psychiatric hospital. He spent time in Saint-Rémy, where he continued to paint, producing some of his most famous works, including The Starry Night. But the depression persisted. The hospital became a prison, a place where he was confined to protect himself and others. He discharged himself and moved to the Auberge Ravoux in Auvers-sur-Oise, near Paris. There, he came under the care of the homeopathic doctor Paul Gachet, who was also an amateur artist. Gachet tried to help, but the darkness was too deep.

On July 29, 1890, Vincent van Gogh died. He was thirty-seven years old. He had shot himself in the chest with a revolver, a final act of despair that ended a life of brilliant, painful creation. In the decade between 1880 and 1890, he had created approximately 2,100 artworks, including around 860 oil paintings. Most of these were created in the last two years of his life. His oeuvre includes landscapes, still lifes, portraits, and self-portraits, most of which are characterized by bold colors and dramatic brushwork that contributed to the rise of expressionism in modern art.

But at the time of his death, his work was only beginning to gain critical attention. During his lifetime, only one of Van Gogh's paintings, The Red Vineyard, was sold. He died a failure in the eyes of the world, a man whose genius was unrecognized and whose pain was misunderstood. It was not until after his death that his art and life story captured the public imagination. His widowed sister-in-law, Johanna van Gogh-Bonger, played a crucial role in this transformation. She tirelessly promoted his work, arranged for the publication of his letters, and fought to establish his legacy.

"Vincent's work began to attract critical artistic attention in the last year of his life. After his death, his art and life story captured public imagination as an emblem of misunderstood genius."

His bold use of color and expressive line inspired the Fauves and the German Expressionists. He became a lasting icon of the romantic ideal of the tortured artist, a figure whose suffering was seen as the price of his genius. Today, Van Gogh's works are among the world's most expensive paintings ever sold. His legacy is celebrated by the Van Gogh Museum in Amsterdam, which holds the world's largest collection of his paintings and drawings.

The Letters and the Legacy

The story of Vincent van Gogh is inextricably linked to the story of his letters. Without them, we would know only the myth, the madman with the cut ear. With them, we know the man. The most comprehensive primary source on Van Gogh is his correspondence with his younger brother, Theo. Their lifelong friendship, and most of what is known of Vincent's thoughts and theories of art, are recorded in the hundreds of letters they exchanged from 1872 until 1890.

Theo kept all of Vincent's letters to him, but Vincent kept only a few of the letters he received. After both had died, Theo's widow Jo Bonger-van Gogh arranged for the publication of some of their letters. A few appeared in 1906 and 1913; the majority were published in 1914. These letters reveal a man who was deeply thoughtful, deeply observant, and deeply human. He wrote about the influence of Jules Breton, a highly paid contemporary artist whose work he admired. In 1875, Vincent mentioned seeing Breton and discussing his paintings at a Salon. In 1884, he discussed a poem by Breton that had inspired one of his own paintings. In 1885, he described Breton's famous work The Song of the Lark as "fine."

In March 1880, Vincent set out on an 80-kilometer trip on foot to meet Breton in the village of Courrières. He was intimidated by Breton's success and the high wall around his estate, and he returned without making his presence known. It appears Breton was unaware of Van Gogh or his attempted visit. There are no known letters between the two artists, and Van Gogh is not one of the contemporary artists discussed by Breton in his 1891 autobiography. This small, failed encounter speaks volumes about Vincent's insecurity and his longing for recognition from those he admired.

The letters also reveal the gap in the record when he lived in Paris. As the brothers lived together, they had no need to correspond. But the letters from Arles are particularly rich, with Vincent writing around 200 letters in Dutch, French, and English. Problems in transcription and dating remain, mainly with those posted from Arles, but the sheer volume of his writing is a testament to his need to communicate, to connect, to explain the world to the one person who understood him.

The Unfinished Symphony

Vincent van Gogh was a man who lived in the margins, who saw the world in colors that others could not see. He was a man who loved deeply, who suffered deeply, and who created beauty out of his pain. His life was short, but his impact was eternal. He created a body of work that continues to move us, to challenge us, to inspire us. His story is a reminder that genius is often a lonely journey, that the path to greatness is paved with failure and heartbreak.

But it is also a story of hope. It is a story of a man who, despite all odds, refused to give up. He refused to give up on his art, on his brother, on the world. And in doing so, he gave us something that no one else could: a window into the soul of a human being who felt everything, saw everything, and loved everything with a ferocity that still burns bright today.

"His bold use of colour, expressive line and thick application of paint inspired avant-garde artistic groups like the Fauves and German Expressionists in the early 20th century."

Today, when we look at The Starry Night or Sunflowers, we are not just looking at a painting. We are looking at a moment in time, a snapshot of a mind in motion, a cry for help that was finally heard. We are looking at the legacy of a man who turned his pain into art, and whose art turned the world upside down. Vincent van Gogh is no longer the madman of Arles. He is the master of color, the poet of light, the voice of the misunderstood. And his voice, carried on the wings of his letters and his paintings, will never be silent.

In the end, the story of Vincent van Gogh is not just about a painter. It is about the human condition. It is about the struggle to find meaning in a chaotic world, the struggle to connect with others, the struggle to create something that lasts. It is a story that resonates with anyone who has ever felt alone, anyone who has ever dreamed of something more, anyone who has ever tried to make sense of the world through the lens of their own experience.

Vincent van Gogh died in 1890, but he is still with us. His art is in museums all over the world, his words are in books, his spirit is in the hearts of those who love him. He is a testament to the power of art, the power of love, and the power of the human spirit. And as long as there are people who can see the beauty in a sunflower, the magic in a starry night, and the truth in a letter to a brother, Vincent van Gogh will live on.
