Vatican City

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Vatican City

In 1929, a single treaty signed in a room of the Lateran Palace carved a sovereign nation out of the heart of Rome, creating a state so small it could be walked across in twenty minutes, yet so powerful it commands the spiritual allegiance of over a billion souls. This was not the restoration of an ancient empire, but the birth of a modern anomaly: the Vatican City State. While the world often conflates the physical territory with the spiritual authority it houses, the distinction is vital. The Vatican is a landlocked city-state, an enclave within the city of Rome, Italy, governed by the See of Rome—the Holy See. The Holy See is the sovereign entity under international law, the central governing body of the Catholic Church, and the entity that maintains the temporal power, diplomacy, and spiritual independence that has defined the papacy for centuries. The city-state itself is merely the stage upon which this drama unfolds, a creation of the Lateran Treaty rather than a mere vestige of the sprawling Papal States that once ruled much of Central Italy.

To understand the Vatican is to grasp a paradox of scale. With an area of just 48.99 hectares (121 acres) and a population of approximately 882 people as of 2024, it stands as the smallest sovereign state in the world by both metrics. It is the only country on Earth with a population under 1,000, a city of priests, Swiss Guards, and lay staff where the streets are as quiet as a monastery cloister. Yet, the density of its influence is infinite. Governed by the Holy See, the Vatican City State is an ecclesiastical or sacerdotal-monarchical state. It is ruled by the Pope, who serves simultaneously as the Bishop of Rome and the head of the Catholic Church. Every high-ranking functionary within the state is a Catholic cleric, drawn from every corner of the globe, creating a unique political entity where citizenship is often tied to office rather than birth. This is not a nation of families and neighborhoods; it is a nation of service, a floating island of sovereignty in the Mediterranean of geopolitics.

The Holy See, the true sovereign power, traces its lineage back to the dawn of Christianity. It is the principal episcopal see of the Catholic Church, an institution that, by 2018, counted roughly 1.329 billion baptized Catholics across the Latin Church and 23 Eastern Catholic Churches. For centuries, the seat of this power shifted. Following the Avignon Papacy (1309–1377), when the popes resided in France, the pontiffs returned to Rome and largely settled within the walls of the Apostolic Palace, now the heart of Vatican City. Before the 1929 treaty, however, the Pope was a prisoner in his own palace, a status born of the turbulent 19th century. The independent state of Vatican City is a direct response to the "Roman Question," the diplomatic impasse that lasted from 1861 to 1929 after the newly formed Kingdom of Italy annexed the Papal States and captured Rome in 1870.

The Shadow of the Circus

Long before it was a state, the land was a swamp, a place the Romans found ominous and uninhabited. The name "Vatican" itself is a relic of this ancient past, derived from the Etruscan settlement of Vatica or Vaticum. The Romans called the general area the Ager Vaticanus, or "Vatican territory." It was a marshy stretch on the west bank of the Tiber, situated between the Janiculum, the Vatican Hill, and Monte Mario, extending down to the Aventine Hill. Its proximity to Rome's archenemy, the Etruscan city of Veii, and its susceptibility to the Tiber's floods gave the area a reputation for being dismal and cursed. The Roman poet Martial, writing in the 1st century AD, mocked the particularly low quality of the wine produced there, a sentiment that persisted even after the land was reclaimed.

The historical record paints a grim picture of the site during the chaotic Year of the Four Emperors in AD 69. The historian Tacitus noted that when the northern army supporting the emperor Vitellius arrived in Rome, a large proportion of the troops camped in the "unhealthy districts of the Vatican." The heat, combined with the inability of the Gauls and Germans to withstand the Italian climate and their subsequent greed for drinking from the Tiber, left their bodies weakened and susceptible to disease, resulting in many deaths. It was a place of death and decay, far removed from the spiritual epicenter it would become.

The transformation began with the imperial family. Agrippina the Elder, mother of Caligula, drained the area in the early 1st century AD, laying out magnificent gardens. Her son, the emperor Caligula, followed by constructing a circus for charioteers within these gardens around AD 40. This was later completed by Emperor Nero and named the Circus Gaii et Neronis, commonly known as the Circus of Nero. It was here, on the spina of this circus, that the Vatican Obelisk was erected. Brought from Heliopolis in Roman Egypt by Caligula, the obelisk stands today in the center of St. Peter's Square, the last visible remnant of the pagan spectacle. In 1586, Pope Sixtus V had it moved to its current position using a method devised by the architect Domenico Fontana, a feat of engineering that symbolized the triumph of the Church over the imperial past.

The Circus of Nero became a site of profound tragedy and martyrdom. After the Great Fire of Rome in AD 64, the circus was the venue for the persecution of Christians. Tradition holds that it was within this very arena that Saint Peter was crucified upside-down, an act of defiance against the Roman expectation that he was unworthy of dying in the same manner as his master. Opposite the circus lay a cemetery separated by the Via Cornelia, a necropolis filled with funeral monuments, mausoleums, and altars to pagan gods. Even after the Constantinian Basilica of St. Peter was built in the first half of the 4th century, a shrine dedicated to the Phrygian goddess Cybele and her consort Attis remained active nearby, a ghost of the old religions haunting the new faith.

The systematic excavation of this ancient necropolis, ordered by Pope Pius XII between 1939 and 1941, revealed the layers of history buried beneath the Basilica. The Constantinian Basilica, constructed in 326, was built directly over what was believed to be the tomb of Saint Peter. From that moment, the landmass began to populate, shifting from a desolate marsh to a hub of religious activity. A palace was constructed nearby as early as the 5th century during the pontificate of Pope Symmachus (reigned 498–514), marking the beginning of the papal residence at the Vatican.

The Roman Question and the Birth of a State

For over a millennium, the popes ruled the Papal States, a vast territory covering much of the Italian peninsula. Yet, for most of this time, they did not live at the Vatican. The Lateran Palace, on the opposite side of Rome, was their habitual residence for a thousand years. It was only after the return from Avignon in 1377 that the popes began to favor the Vatican, though they moved to the Quirinal Palace in 1583 after renovations under Pope Paul V were completed.

The trajectory of the Vatican changed irrevocably in 1870. As the Kingdom of Italy moved to unify the peninsula, Italian forces captured Rome, bringing the nominal resistance of the papal forces to an end. The Pope's holdings were left in an uncertain legal limbo. The "Roman Question" was born: the Pope, who had ruled as a temporal monarch, was now a captive in his own palace, while the Italian state claimed sovereignty over Rome. The Quirinal Palace was confiscated by the King of Italy, and the popes retreated to the Vatican, refusing to recognize the Italian state's authority.

Between 1861 and 1929, the popes considered themselves "prisoners in the Vatican." Italy made no attempt to interfere with the Holy See within the Vatican walls, but it confiscated church property elsewhere, and the relationship remained strained. The resolution came on February 11, 1929, with the signing of the Lateran Treaty between the Holy See and the Kingdom of Italy. This agreement did not merely return property; it created a new sovereign state. The Treaty of Lateran established the Vatican City State, a creation rather than a restoration. It spoke of the Vatican as a new entity, distinct from the lost Papal States, designed to guarantee the spiritual independence of the Pope and the Holy See.

The name "Vatican City" was first used in this treaty. The Italian name, Città del Vaticano or formally Stato della Città del Vaticano, means "State of Vatican City." Its Latin name, Status Civitatis Vaticanae, is used in all official documents. The term "Vatican" itself, once referring to a marshy district, now denotes a sovereign capital. The treaty ended the Roman Question, giving the Pope a territory where he could exercise temporal power without being subject to any other government.

A City of Art and Economy

Today, the Vatican City State is a unique blend of the sacred and the secular, a museum and a government in one. It contains some of the world's most revered religious and cultural sites. St. Peter's Basilica, the Sistine Chapel, the Vatican Apostolic Library, and the Vatican Museums are not just tourist attractions; they are the physical manifestations of two millennia of history. These institutions house some of the most famous paintings and sculptures in human history, from Michelangelo's ceiling in the Sistine Chapel to the Laocoön group in the Vatican Museums. The Basilica itself, a masterpiece of Renaissance architecture, stands on the site of the ancient Circus of Nero, a testament to the continuity of the site from pagan martyrdom to Christian pilgrimage.

The economy of this micro-state is as unique as its politics. It has no taxes, and items are duty-free. The Vatican City State is supported financially by a diverse array of sources: donations from Catholic believers around the world, the sale of postage stamps and souvenirs, fees for admission to its world-class museums, and sales of publications. The postal service alone is a significant revenue stream, with the Vatican's stamps being highly sought after by collectors globally. The economy is small, self-contained, and entirely dependent on the global Catholic community and the cultural capital of the city itself.

The population of 882 is a reflection of its function. There are no permanent residents in the traditional sense; citizenship is granted based on residence and office. The citizens include cardinals resident in the city, the diplomatic corps of the Holy See, the Swiss Guard, and a small number of lay staff who maintain the infrastructure. The population fluctuates with the needs of the Church, making it a city of transient residents who are there to serve the universal mission of the papacy.

The Enduring Legacy

The history of Vatican City is a story of survival and reinvention. From the marshy, ominous Ager Vaticanus of the Roman Republic to the Circus of Nero, from the tomb of Saint Peter to the palace of the popes, and finally to the sovereign state of 1929, the site has constantly adapted to the tides of history. The Lateran Treaty of 1929 was the culmination of this evolution, securing the independence of the Holy See in a world of nation-states.

The Vatican remains the only country in the world with a population under 1,000, a tiny speck on the map that commands the attention of the world. It is a place where the past is never truly past. The obelisk in St. Peter's Square still bears the scars of its journey from Egypt; the walls of the Sistine Chapel still echo with the voices of the Renaissance; the foundations of the Basilica still rest on the tombs of martyrs. The Vatican City State is not just a political entity; it is a living museum, a spiritual capital, and a testament to the enduring power of faith and diplomacy.

As the world changes, the Vatican remains a constant, a sovereign island in the river of time. Its existence is a reminder that power does not always come from size or military might, but from the depth of its influence and the resilience of its institutions. The "Roman Question" is long resolved, but the questions it raised about the relationship between church and state, between spiritual authority and temporal power, continue to resonate. The Vatican City State stands as the answer to those questions, a small state with a large purpose, a city that is both a home and a symbol, a place where the past and the future meet in the present.

The name "Vatican" has traveled a long road. From a marshy, flood-prone district feared by Roman soldiers to the center of the Catholic world, it has shed its ominous reputation to become a beacon of faith. The transformation is complete, yet the layers of history remain visible in every stone and every painting. The Vatican City State is the latest chapter in a story that began with an Etruscan settlement and a Roman circus, a story that continues to be written by the Pope and the faithful of the world. It is a city-state that defies the logic of modern geopolitics, proving that in the realm of ideas and faith, the smallest states can have the largest impact.
