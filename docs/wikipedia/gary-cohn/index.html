Gary Cohn

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Gary Cohn

In the high-stakes theater of the Trump White House, few figures were as anomalous as Gary Cohn. Here was a man who had spent twenty-five years mastering the opaque, ruthless mechanics of global finance, ascending to the presidency of Goldman Sachs, only to walk into the Oval Office and attempt to steer a populist movement toward the very globalist principles he had championed on Wall Street. Born Gary David Cohn on August 27, 1960, to an Eastern European Jewish family in Shaker Heights, Ohio, his journey from a struggling student with undiagnosed dyslexia to the 11th Director of the National Economic Council is a narrative of intellectual grit, strategic maneuvering, and a profound collision of ideologies.

Cohn’s early life was defined not by privilege, but by the struggle to decode a world that was not built for his mind. His father, Victor, worked as an electrician before becoming a real estate developer, and the family environment was one of modest ambition. Yet, Cohn's own education was a chaotic shuffle; diagnosed with dyslexia at a young age, he attended four different schools before reaching the sixth grade. The cognitive dissonance of trying to learn to read and write while the world moved at a speed his brain could not match became a defining trauma and a catalyst for his future resilience. Canadian journalist Malcolm Gladwell would later feature Cohn's childhood as a central case study in David and Goliath, illustrating how perceived disabilities can forge unique pathways to success. Cohn eventually found his footing at Gilmour Academy, graduating in 1979, before earning a Bachelor of Science in business administration from American University in 1982.

His entry into the workforce was as unglamorous as his education was turbulent. Cohn began at the U.S. Steel home products division in Cleveland, Ohio, but the corporate bureaucracy of the steel industry offered no outlet for his restless intellect. Within months, he abandoned the stable path to become an options dealer on the floor of the New York Mercantile Exchange. The transition was abrupt and terrifying; Cohn had no formal training in derivatives. He taught himself the mechanics of options trading by frantically reading about them in the days between his interview and his first day on the floor. This self-made education was the first of many instances where Cohn would outmaneuver the system not by following the rules, but by mastering the logic beneath them faster than anyone else.

In 1990, Cohn joined Goldman Sachs, a firm that would become his professional home for a quarter-century. His rise was meteoric. By 1994, he was a partner. In 1996, he took the helm of the commodities department. The trajectory of his ascent was a map of the firm's most profitable divisions: in 2002, he led the massive Fixed Income, Currency, and Commodities (FICC) division; in 2003, he co-headed Equities; and by January 2004, he was co-head of global securities businesses. By June 2006, he had reached the apex of the corporate ladder, becoming President and Co-Chief Operating Officer. He was not just a trader; he was a strategist, serving on the firm's board of directors and chairing the Firmwide Client and Business Standards Committee.

However, the shadow of the 2008 financial crisis loomed over his tenure. In 2010, Cohn found himself on Capitol Hill, testifying before Congress about Goldman Sachs' role in the meltdown. The atmosphere was hostile, the questions accusatory. Cohn’s defense was stark and data-driven. >"During the two years of the financial crisis, Goldman Sachs lost $1.2 billion in its residential mortgage-related business," Cohn testified, his voice cutting through the political theater. >"We did not 'bet against our clients', and the numbers underscore this fact." The statement was a declaration of a specific moral code within the high-velocity world of investment banking—a code that valued the bottom line and the letter of the contract over the broader social fallout, yet insisted on technical honesty.

When Donald Trump won the presidency in 2016, the political landscape of Washington was upended. On January 20, 2017, Cohn stepped into the White House as the Director of the National Economic Council (NEC), a position that required no Congressional confirmation. The move was seismic. Cohn, the archetypal globalist Wall Street titan, was now the chief economic advisor to a candidate who had built his campaign on protectionism and anti-establishment rhetoric. The press immediately noticed the tension. By February 11, 2017, The Wall Street Journal had already anointed him an "economic-policy powerhouse," while The New York Times labeled him Trump's "go-to figure on matters related to jobs, business, and growth."

Cohn's arrival filled a vacuum created by the slow confirmation process for Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin. With the administration's economic team in flux, Cohn pushed forward on the administration's most ambitious agenda items: tax reform, infrastructure investment, financial deregulation, and the dismantling of the Affordable Care Act. He was not merely a bystander; he was the engine. Yet, his presence in the administration came with a price tag that seemed to mock the populist austerity of his boss. Cohn had left Goldman Sachs with a $285 million severance package, a staggering sum that highlighted the gulf between the Washington elite and the working-class base of the Trump movement. Furthermore, he had sold a stake in the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China, valued at $16 million, shortly before entering public service, a transaction that would later be scrutinized by ethics watchdogs.

Inside the White House, Cohn became a lightning rod for ideological conflict. He was a staunch supporter of global trade and free markets, a stance that earned him the derisive nicknames "Globalist Gary" and "Carbon Tax Cohn" from the administration's populist wing. Alongside Jared Kushner, Ivanka Trump, and Dina Powell, he was grouped into what critics called the "Wall Street wing" of the Trump administration. This faction was in constant, often bitter, conflict with the nationalist populists led by White House Chief Strategist Steve Bannon and trade advisor Peter Navarro. Navarro, a hawk on China who favored aggressive tariffs, viewed Cohn with open contempt, later describing him as "one of the worst and most treacherous misfits of the entire Trump administration."

The friction was not merely theoretical; it was daily, grinding, and personal. Cohn led the charge to pass the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, a cornerstone of the administration's legislative agenda. In a 2019 reflection in The Wall Street Journal, Cohn pointed to the tangible results of this policy, citing an increase of $6,000 in real disposable personal income per household and the creation of seven million jobs as evidence of success. But the policy victories could not mask the cultural chasm that was widening within the residence at 1600 Pennsylvania Avenue.

The breaking point for Cohn's conscience came in August 2017, following the white nationalist rally in Charlottesville, Virginia. The tragedy, which left one woman dead and dozens injured, exposed the raw nerve of racial hatred in American society. When President Trump refused to unequivocally condemn the neo-Nazis and white supremacists, instead speaking of "very fine people on both sides," Cohn was horrified. He reportedly considered resigning immediately. >"Citizens standing up for equality and freedom can never be equated with white supremacists, neo-Nazis, and the KKK," Cohn said in an interview with Jim Sciutto for the book The Madman Theory. He believed the administration had a moral imperative to speak clearly against hatred, a standard that Trump seemed unwilling to meet.

The tension between Cohn's globalist worldview and Trump's protectionist instincts finally snapped in early 2018. Cohn had spent months trying to dissuade Trump from imposing steep tariffs on steel and aluminum imports, arguing that such measures would harm American manufacturers and ignite a trade war. In a desperate final attempt to sway the President, Cohn arranged a meeting with end-users of steel and aluminum—companies that would be devastated by the tariffs. But Trump cancelled the meeting. Days later, on March 6, 2018, Cohn announced his resignation. His departure was a clear signal that the populist faction had won; by September 2017, the Office of Trade and Manufacturing Policy had already been folded into Cohn's NEC, giving Navarro a reporting line that bypassed Cohn's authority. Cohn's exit solidified Navarro's influence and marked the end of the "Globalist Gary" experiment.

Bob Woodward's 2018 book, Fear: Trump in the White House, revealed the extent of Cohn's behind-the-scenes efforts to moderate the President's more erratic impulses. Woodward detailed two instances where Cohn physically removed draft letters from Trump's desk to prevent them from being signed. One letter would have unilaterally withdrawn the United States from the NAFTA trade agreement with Canada and Mexico. The other would have canceled a key trade agreement with South Korea. Cohn was not just an advisor; he was a dam holding back a flood of isolationism. Despite their clashes, Trump's opinion of Cohn remained surprisingly positive after the resignation. He called him a "rare talent" who had done a "superb job," a testament to Cohn's ability to navigate the chaotic presidency without losing his mind or his dignity.

Following his departure from the White House, Cohn did not fade into retirement. Instead, he pivoted with the same agility that had defined his trading career. He became a venture capital investor and advisor, focusing on the sectors that would define the next century: cybersecurity, blockchain infrastructure, and medical technology. In January 2021, he was appointed Vice Chairman of IBM's board of directors, a role that bridged his financial expertise with the world's largest technology firm. He joined the Board of Advisors for Hoyos Integrity, a startup utilizing biometric blockchain technology for secure communications and digital payments, and became a key advisor for Machine Zone and Abryx, a biomaterial science company.

Cohn's post-White House career has been a series of calculated bets on the future of digital trust. He sits on the Board of Spring Labs, a startup using blockchain to share consumer credit data, and Sotera Digital Security, which has developed a mobile phone specifically designed for secure communications. At Sotera Digital, he is deeply involved in the strategy and rollout of a next-generation secure mobile phone aimed at government and corporate customers, addressing a growing global concern over data privacy and surveillance. In April 2020, he was appointed to the Risk & Governance Advisory Board of Starling, a technology company in applied behavior sciences. In August 2020, he and investor Clifton S. Robbins launched Cohn Robbins Holdings Corp, a vehicle to invest in transformative companies.

Yet, Cohn's relationship with the political world remained complex. During the 2024 Republican Party presidential primaries, he supported other candidates, distancing himself from the very movement he had once served. But when Donald Trump won the 2024 United States presidential election, the dynamic shifted once again. Cohn was brought back into the fold, not as a policymaker, but as a "Trump ambassador" to Wall Street, tasked with smoothing over appointments and policy for the business community. It was a role that required a delicate balance: advocating for the administration's interests while maintaining the credibility of a man who had once stood in the way of its most protectionist impulses.

The story of Gary Cohn is not just a biography of a financier or a politician; it is a story of the collision between two Americas. It is the story of a man who believed in the power of global markets and the rule of law, trying to impose those values on a movement fueled by nationalism and disruption. It is the story of a dyslexic boy from Ohio who taught himself to read the complex language of derivatives and then tried to read the mind of a president, often failing, but never losing his nerve. From the trading floors of the New York Mercantile Exchange to the halls of the White House, and now into the digital frontiers of blockchain and cybersecurity, Cohn has remained a figure of immense influence, navigating the currents of history with a mix of pragmatism and principle. His legacy is a testament to the idea that even in the most polarized times, there are those who try to build bridges, even if the other side is determined to burn them.

His support for reinstating the Glass-Steagall legislation, which would separate commercial and investment banking, stands as a curious footnote to his career. It suggests that even as a beneficiary of the deregulation that followed its repeal, Cohn recognized the systemic risks that had nearly destroyed the global economy. It is a reminder that his loyalty was not to any single ideology, but to the stability of the system itself. In a world increasingly defined by fragmentation, Cohn's career serves as a reminder of the cost of that fragmentation and the difficulty of holding a coherent vision in the face of chaos.

As we look at the landscape of 2026, Cohn's role continues to evolve. He is no longer the man trying to stop the train; he is the man helping to build the new tracks. Whether it is through the secure communications of Sotera Digital or the strategic guidance he offers to the Trump administration's economic team, Cohn remains a central figure in the intersection of finance, technology, and politics. His journey from a struggling student to a global economic advisor is a reminder that the path to power is rarely a straight line, and that the most effective leaders are often those who have learned to adapt to the world as it is, while never forgetting how it could be. The dyslexia that once threatened to derail his future became the very trait that allowed him to see patterns others missed, to find opportunities in chaos, and to navigate the treacherous waters of the Trump White House with a unique blend of caution and courage. In the end, Gary Cohn is not just a participant in history; he is a lens through which we can understand the complexities of the modern economic and political landscape, a man who has spent a lifetime trying to make sense of a world that often makes no sense at all.
