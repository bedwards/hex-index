Creativity (religion)

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Creativity (religion)

In the summer of 1993, a 75-year-old man named Ben Klassen sat alone on the grounds of his compound in Otto, North Carolina, and ended his own life. He did not die in a hospital bed, nor did he pass in a moment of quiet reflection. He took an overdose of sleeping pills, adhering to a theological tenet of his own making: that suicide is not a sin, but a strategic and sometimes necessary act for the preservation of the self and the race. Klassen was the founder of the Church of the Creator, a religious movement that would later shed its ecclesiastical veneer to become known simply as Creativity. He was a man who had spent two decades preaching that the white race was nature's finest creation, destined for a holy war against a world he viewed as decadent and populated by enemies. His death did not mark the end of his ideology; rather, it served as a grim catalyst for a fractured movement that continues to operate in the shadows of the United States, Australia, and Europe, classified by the Southern Poverty Law Center and the Anti-Defamation League as a hate group. To understand Creativity is to understand a belief system built entirely on the paradox of atheism and religious fervor, a cult of personality that replaced the divine with the biological, and the soul with the gene pool.

The origins of this movement are rooted in the fertile, if toxic, soil of 1970s American racism. In 1973, in Lighthouse Point, Florida, Ben Klassen self-published a book titled Nature's Eternal Religion. This was not a theological treatise in the traditional sense; it contained no gods, no afterlife, and no spiritual redemption. Instead, it was a manifesto of scientific racism, antitheism, and white separatism. Klassen, a former naval officer and a man of immense, albeit twisted, charisma, founded the Church of the Creator that same year. He sought to create a religion for those who rejected the Christian God, whom he viewed as a tool of Jewish subjugation, and instead worshipped the concept of the White race itself. The adherents of this new faith called themselves "Creators," a term Klassen deliberately borrowed from Adolf Hitler's Mein Kampf. In Hitler's tripartite classification of humanity, the white race was the "Creator" race, the only ones capable of building civilization, while others were merely destroyers or parasites. Klassen adopted this hierarchy not as a metaphor, but as the literal, immutable law of the universe.

Klassen was not merely a theorist; he was an activist who understood the power of branding and organization. He attempted to recruit heavily from the neo-Nazi community, yet he maintained a distinct distance from Nazism itself. His objections were not moral; they were strategic. He despised Nazism's embrace of "Positive Christianity," its hyper-nationalism which prioritized the German state over the global white race, and its specific anti-Slavic sentiments which alienated other European ethnicities. Klassen wanted a global white solidarity that transcended borders. He revered Hitler as a "great pioneer" and viewed the Nazi era as an admirable, albeit flawed, attempt at racial purity. This nuanced, yet deeply hateful, positioning allowed him to court a specific demographic: those who wanted the racial ideology of Nazism without the baggage of the Third Reich's specific failures. He cultivated a relationship with William Luther Pierce, the author of The Turner Diaries and leader of the National Alliance. Klassen met Pierce twice in 1975, and they maintained an "on and off" relationship for nearly two decades. While Klassen publicly praised Pierce as "a great man and an outstanding intellectual thinker," he privately confessed in his memoir, Trials, Tribulations, and Triumphs, that he never understood the logic of Pierce's "Cosmotheism." For Klassen, the only religion that mattered was racial loyalty. Anything else was distraction.

The movement grew, but it also attracted the attention of the communities where it tried to plant its flag. In 1982, Klassen established the headquarters of his church in Otto, North Carolina. He and his family had expected resistance, but they were unprepared for the ferocity of the local backlash. The local newspaper, the Franklin Press, ran a headline on May 13, 1982, that read: "Pro-Hitler, anti-Christ Leader Headquarters Here." The town of Otto, a small rural community, became a battleground. The opposition was not just political; it was visceral. The locals saw through the religious language to the core of the message: a call for the subjugation and hatred of non-white people. Despite the hostility, Klassen held his ground for over a decade, using the compound as a base to distribute literature and recruit followers. However, the weight of the world, combined with personal tragedies, began to crush him. His wife died, the church faced mounting legal and financial troubles, and Klassen was diagnosed with cancer. In August 1993, at the age of 75, he committed suicide on the very grounds he had fought so hard to secure. He was buried in a section of the property he had designated as the "Ben Klassen Memorial Park," a final monument to his obsession.

The death of the founder did not kill the movement; it merely fractured it. The vacuum of leadership led to a succession struggle that would define the next two decades. In 1996, Matthew F. Hale, a young and charismatic lawyer from Illinois, emerged as the leader. Hale reorganized the group as the "World Church of the Creator." He was a man of law, which gave him a veneer of legitimacy that Klassen's self-published ramblings lacked. Hale was ambitious, expanding the organization's reach and attempting to legitimize its rhetoric through the courts and the media. However, the movement's trajectory was destined to crash against the reality of its own violent ideology. In January 2003, the facade crumbled. Hale was arrested and charged with soliciting the murder of Judge Joan Lefkow, a federal judge who had ruled against his organization in a trademark infringement case. The case was a direct result of Hale's attempt to use the name "Church of the Creator," which had been lost to an unaffiliated religious organization in Ashland, Oregon. Hale's conviction was swift and severe. He was sentenced to 40 years in prison, effectively decapitating the World Church of the Creator.

Following Hale's imprisonment, the movement splintered into two distinct factions, both claiming the legacy of Klassen and Hale. The first was the Creativity Alliance (CA), also known as the Church of Creativity, co-founded by an Australian man named Colin Campbell. The second was the Creativity Movement. Despite the organizational schism, the core tenets of the religion remained unchanged. Both groups continue to promote the same hateful worldview: a naturalistic and racialistic philosophy based on the "survival, expansion and advancement of the White race." They claim this worldview is derived from the "eternal laws of nature, the experience of history, on logic and common sense." To a member of the Creativity Movement, there is no higher moral authority than the white race. They believe in a "racial holy war," a perpetual conflict between the white race and all non-white peoples, including Jews, Black people, and mixed-race individuals. This is not a metaphorical war; it is a call to action, a directive to separate, to hate, and to fight.

The theology of Creativity is rigid, codified, and unforgiving. Adherents are expected to recite the "Sixteen Commandments" and the "Five Fundamental Beliefs" five times a day. These creeds are the liturgy of the race. One fundamental belief states that "their race is their religion." Another declares that the white race is "nature's finest creation." A third asserts that racial loyalty is the "greatest honor," while racial treason is the "worst crime." The moral universe of a Creator is binary and absolute: anything beneficial to white people is good; anything detrimental to white people is bad. This extends to the most mundane aspects of life. The movement advocates for white people to avoid all business dealings with Jews and to refuse to "employ niggers or other coloreds." The language is not accidental; it is deliberate and weaponized.

Ben Klassen was explicit about his use of slurs. In his book Little White Book, he repudiated other racist religions like Christian Identity and Wotanism, declaring Creativity the "one and only, true and revolutionary White racial religion." But his most disturbing requirement was his insistence on the use of the N-word. Klassen hated Black people with a visceral, all-consuming passion. In his public discourse and writings, he consistently referred to Black people as "niggers." He demanded that all followers use this term, rejecting non-derogatory alternatives like "Black" or "African American." He even condemned other racists who used the term "negro," finding it too polite. In a chilling passage from his writings, Klassen explained his reasoning: "Furthermore, in looking up the word in Webster's dictionary I found the term 'nigger' very descriptive: 'a vulgar, offensive term of hostility and contempt for the black man'. I can't think of anything that defines better and more accurately what our position... should be... If we are going to be for racial integrity and racial purity... we must take a hostile position toward the nigger. We must give him nothing but contempt." This was not just a preference for one word over another; it was a commandment to dehumanize an entire group of people. It was an attempt to strip away the humanity of Black people through language, to force his followers to view them through a lens of pure hostility.

The movement's worldview is one of paranoia and decadence. Creativity teaches that American culture is collapsing, becoming "more decadent" by the day. It cites the increase in "black crimes," the growing acceptance of homosexuality, the prevalence of interracial marriage, and the rise of drug use as evidence of this decline. For a Creator, the lack of racial identity among white people is the ultimate tragedy. They are taught that they are living in a time of existential threat, where the white race is being replaced and erased. This narrative of displacement fuels their separatism. White separatism is fundamental to the faith. Adherents are taught to hate non-whites and to avoid social interactions with them at all costs. They are expected to refute homosexuality, miscegenation, and even the act of "complaining," which is seen as a sign of weakness. Membership in the Creativity Movement is strictly restricted to persons whose genetic heritage is "wholly or predominantly" from Europe. It does not matter where you live; if you are not white, you are not a Creator. The leader of the entire religion is called the "Pontifex Maximus," a title derived from Ancient Rome meaning "Greatest Priest." The first Pontifex Maximus was Ben Klassen, and the title has been passed down through the fractured leadership, a reminder of the imperial ambition that drives the movement.

Today, the movement operates in several states across the U.S., as well as in Australia, Eastern Europe, and the United Kingdom. The two main organizations, the Creativity Alliance and the Creativity Movement, continue to promote the ideology, though they are often marginalized and monitored by law enforcement and civil rights groups. The legacy of Ben Klassen and Matthew Hale is one of failure and violence, yet the ideas they propagated persist. The movement's classification as a hate group by the Southern Poverty Law Center and the Anti-Defamation League is not a bureaucratic formality; it is a reflection of the tangible harm they seek to inflict. Their theology is a roadmap for hatred, a system that justifies prejudice, segregation, and violence under the guise of religious freedom and natural law. They claim to be fighting for the survival of the white race, but their methods are rooted in the very destruction they claim to fear. They preach a gospel of division in a world that is increasingly interconnected. They call for a holy war, but their only victories are in the echo chambers of their own making.

The story of Creativity is a stark reminder of how religion can be weaponized. It is a case study in the creation of a faith that has no god, no soul, and no redemption, only a race and a war. It shows how a man like Ben Klassen, driven by his own hatred and fears, could construct a belief system that continues to inspire others to hate. The suicide of Klassen in 1993 was not an end, but a transformation. It transformed a man into a martyr for his cause, a symbol of the ultimate sacrifice for the race. His grave in Otto, North Carolina, remains a pilgrimage site for the few who still believe in the "eternal laws of nature" that he preached. But the reality of the movement is far less romantic. It is a collection of isolated individuals and small cells, preaching a message of death and division. They are the remnants of a movement that once sought to conquer the world but could not even conquer the town of Otto. Yet, as long as there are those who feel threatened by a changing world, the ideology of Creativity will find new recruits, new followers, and new ways to spread its poison. The war they speak of is not a battle for the future; it is a war against the present, a refusal to accept the reality of a multiracial, multicultural world. And in that refusal, they have created a religion of pure hatred, a faith that has no future but the one they are trying to destroy.

The persistence of this movement in the modern era, despite its legal defeats and the imprisonment of its leaders, speaks to a deeper, more insidious problem. It is not just about the individuals who join; it is about the ideas that attract them. The ideas of white separatism, of racial purity, of a world divided by blood, are seductive to those who feel left behind, who feel that their identity is under attack. Creativity offers them a simple explanation for complex problems: it is not the economy, or the government, or the social system; it is the other. It is the non-white, the Jew, the mixed-race. This simplicity is dangerous. It provides a target for anger, a scapegoat for failure, and a cause for which to fight. The movement's rhetoric is designed to trigger a primal fear, to tap into the deepest insecurities of its followers. It tells them that they are the victims, the persecuted, the last line of defense against extinction. This narrative is powerful, and it is why the movement continues to exist, even as its leaders rot in prison and its headquarters are abandoned. The ideas of Creativity are not bound by geography or organization. They are bound by the human capacity for hatred, and as long as that capacity exists, the religion of the Creator will have a home. The legacy of Ben Klassen is not just a set of books or a failed compound in North Carolina; it is a living, breathing ideology that continues to poison the well of public discourse. It is a testament to the fact that hatred, once codified into a religion, can outlive its founder, its leaders, and even its followers. It is a reminder that the battle for the soul of a society is not won by laws or courts, but by the stories we tell ourselves about who we are and who we are not. And in the story of Creativity, the story is one of endless war, endless hate, and endless division.
