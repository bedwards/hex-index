Foreign policy of the United States

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Foreign policy of the United States

In January 2025, the United States unveiled a National Security Strategy that stripped away decades of diplomatic ambiguity to declare a singular, unapologetic objective: the preservation of American preeminence. The document was not merely a statement of intent but a manifesto of survival, explicitly tasked with halting and reversing the economic damage inflicted by foreign actors while ensuring the Indo-Pacific remained free and open. It demanded the prevention of any adversarial power from dominating the Middle East and insisted that "U.S. technology and U.S. standards" must remain the global gold standard. This was a pivot from the abstract ideals of the post-Cold War era to a hard-nosed realism that viewed the world not as a community to be built, but as a competitive arena where American dominance is the only guarantee of safety.

To understand the weight of this 2025 declaration, one must look past the immediate headlines and into the architectural foundations of American statecraft. Since its independence from Britain, liberalism has been the DNA of U.S. foreign policy, a belief system that posits the spread of democracy and free markets as inherently good for the world and, crucially, for America itself. However, the machinery used to execute this vision has evolved into a massive, interconnected engine of global power. Since the conclusion of World War II, the United States has operated under a grand strategy characterized by scholars as "primacy," "deep engagement," or "liberal hegemony." This is not a passive stance; it is an active, expensive, and relentless campaign to maintain military predominance across every ocean.

The scale of this engagement is difficult to overstate. The strategy entails building and maintaining an extensive network of allies that serves as the backbone of the Western order. This network is exemplified by the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO), a military alliance that has expanded far beyond its original Cold War mandate, alongside a web of bilateral alliances and a sprawling constellation of foreign U.S. military bases. These bases are not mere outposts; they are the forward operating nodes of American power, projecting force and assurance simultaneously. Beyond the military, the United States has worked tirelessly to integrate other states into international institutions designed in Washington. The International Monetary Fund, the World Bank, and the World Trade Organization (formerly GATT) are not neutral arbiters of global commerce; they are structures built on U.S. economic models and legal frameworks, ensuring that the global economy functions in a way that favors American interests.

This structural dominance is matched by a relentless focus on non-proliferation. The United States has long sought to limit the spread of nuclear weapons, viewing the atomic bomb as the ultimate disruptor of the global balance of power. The U.S. House Committee on Foreign Affairs codifies these ambitions into specific jurisdictional goals that read like a checklist for global management. Their mandate includes export controls to prevent the spread of nuclear technology and hardware, measures to foster commercial interaction while safeguarding American business abroad, international commodity agreements, and the protection of American citizens on foreign soil. They even oversee the mechanics of expulsion, a diplomatic tool used to eject hostile actors from the American sphere.

Yet, for all its power, the execution of this foreign policy is a constant tug-of-war within the American government itself. The Constitution, specifically Article Two, grants the primary power of foreign policy to the President. This executive authority is vast, encompassing the power to command the military, negotiate treaties, and appoint ambassadors. The Department of State acts as the President's primary instrument, conducting the day-to-day diplomacy that keeps the global machine running. However, the State Department is perpetually caught in a vice grip between the wishes of the sitting President and the legislative will of Congress. While the President drives the car, Congress holds the keys to the fuel tank.

The Department of Defense carries out the military dimensions of this policy, while the Central Intelligence Agency operates as an independent shadow, gathering the intelligence necessary to inform decision-making. But the checks and balances are real and often contentious. Treaties negotiated by the President are not binding until ratified by a two-thirds majority of the Senate. This high bar ensures that no President can unilaterally lock the nation into a long-term commitment without broad bipartisan support. Similarly, ambassadorial nominations require Senate approval, meaning the President cannot simply appoint their own foreign ministers without the legislature's consent.

Perhaps the most significant check is the power to declare war. While the President is the Commander-in-Chief of the Armed Forces, the Constitution explicitly grants Congress the power to declare war. In practice, this line has blurred. Military actions are often authorized through congressional resolutions rather than formal declarations, but the requirement for approval by both chambers remains a fundamental constraint on executive power. The President appoints the Secretary of State, who acts as the foreign minister, and other ambassadors, but these appointments are subject to the "advice and consent" of the Senate. This friction between the branches is not a bug in the system; it is a feature designed to ensure that the nation's foreign policy reflects a consensus rather than the whims of a single leader.

Despite the friction and the varying personalities of different administrations, there is a remarkable consistency in the goals of U.S. foreign policy over the last century. While the rhetoric may shift from idealism to realism and back again, the underlying objectives remain stubbornly similar. Political scientists generally categorize the approaches to these goals into four distinct schools of thought.

The first is Neo-Isolationism. Proponents of this view argue that the United States should maintain a very narrow focus, prioritizing domestic issues and avoiding entanglement in the affairs of the rest of the world. They believe that the cost of global engagement outweighs the benefits and that America should retreat from its role as the world's policeman.

The second school is Selective Engagement. This approach is more moderate, advocating for involvement only in conflicts that directly threaten American security. It avoids unnecessary entanglements and is semi-restrictive, seeking to engage only when the strategic payoff is clear and the risks are manageable.

The third is Cooperative Security. This school requires a higher level of involvement, arguing that threats to the international order are threats to the United States regardless of their origin. It calls for active participation in global institutions and occasional military intervention to counter threats before they reach American shores.

Finally, there is the idea of Primacy. This is the most ambitious and aggressive school, seeking to advance the United States well beyond all other nations. It places America first in all matters, believing that only by maintaining absolute superiority can the U.S. ensure its own security and the stability of the global system. The 2025 National Security Strategy is a clear embodiment of the Primacy school, rejecting the notion of retreating or merely reacting, and instead demanding proactive dominance.

The legal mechanisms through which the United States enacts these policies are as complex as the strategies themselves. International agreements are the tools of the trade, governed by the Treaty Clause of the Constitution. This clause dictates a rigorous process: the President negotiates and signs, but the Senate must approve by a two-thirds vote. Typically, these treaties are first scrutinized by the Senate Committee on Foreign Relations. If the committee approves, the full Senate votes. If successful, the United States exchanges instruments of ratification with the relevant foreign states, and the treaty becomes binding.

The history of this process is one of high stakes and high standards. Between 1789 and 1990, the Senate approved more than 1,500 treaties. However, the rejection rate was also significant; 21 treaties were outright rejected, and 85 were withdrawn without further action. As of 2019, 37 treaties were still pending Senate approval, sitting in the legislative limbo that can stall major diplomatic initiatives for years.

But the Constitution offers other pathways. The President can unilaterally make executive agreements, which have the force of law to the degree that they are made within the scope of the President's existing authority. The Supreme Court, in decisions like United States v. Pink (1942) and Reid v. Covert (1957), has upheld the validity of these agreements, though their legal standing is distinct from treaties. Then there are Congressional-executive agreements. In these cases, the executive branch negotiates the language, but the agreement is approved by Congress as regular legislation, requiring only a simple majority in both houses rather than the supermajority needed for treaties. This procedure has been used frequently to enshrine international compacts into federal law. While federal courts have upheld this method, some scholars question its constitutionality, arguing that it bypasses the explicit Senate ratification procedure intended by the Founders.

The State Department maintains that the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties represents established law, providing a framework for how these agreements are interpreted and enforced. Once a treaty is ratified, it is incorporated into the body of U.S. federal law. However, this incorporation comes with a twist: Congress retains the power to modify or repeal treaties after they are ratified. This means that the United States can legally overrule an agreed-upon treaty obligation, even if doing so is seen as a violation of international law. Several court rulings have confirmed this understanding. The Supreme Court, in Paquete Habana (1900) and Reid v. Covert (1957), along with lower court rulings like Garcia-Mir v. Meese (1986), has affirmed that domestic law supersedes international obligations in the U.S. legal system.

This reality has led to a unique diplomatic quirk. Following the Reid v. Covert decision, the United States adds a reservation to the text of every treaty it ratifies. This reservation effectively states that while the U.S. intends to abide by the treaty, if the treaty is found to be in violation of the U.S. Constitution, the United States is legally unable to abide by it because the American signature would be ultra vires—beyond its legal power. This clause serves as a constitutional safety valve, ensuring that no international agreement can override the supreme law of the land.

The trajectory of American foreign policy since the Revolution is a story of transformation from a regional power to a global hegemon. In the early years, the U.S. adhered to a policy of non-interventionism, a stance that persisted even after the First World War. The country was content to look inward, focusing on its own growth and development. But the cataclysm of World War II shattered this isolation. The United States emerged from the conflict not just as a victor, but as the undisputed leader of the free world. Throughout the Cold War, the U.S. assumed the role of global hegemon, a position it has maintained, albeit with varying degrees of intensity, ever since.

This shift from isolationism to global leadership was accompanied by a change in philosophy. Since the 19th century, the U.S. has moved from the realist school of thought, which prioritizes power and national interest above all else, to the idealistic or Wilsonian school. Named after President Woodrow Wilson, this approach believes that the United States has a moral obligation to promote democracy, human rights, and the rule of law around the world. This idealism has often clashed with realism, creating a tension that defines American foreign policy to this day.

Presidential doctrines have often served as the vessels for these shifting themes. George Washington's Farewell Address remains the foundational text of American foreign policy thinking. In it, Washington urged the nation to observe good faith and justice towards all nations and to cultivate peace and harmony. He warned against "inveterate antipathies against particular nations" and "passionate attachments for others," arguing that such emotional entanglements could draw the young republic into unnecessary wars. His most famous admonition was to "steer clear of permanent alliances with any portion of the foreign world." This advice shaped U.S. policy for nearly two centuries, guiding the nation through its formative years and into the modern era.

Yet, the world Washington described is unrecognizable today. The interdependence of the global economy, the nature of modern warfare, and the complexity of international threats have rendered the isolationist path impossible. The United States cannot simply "steer clear" of the world; it is inextricably linked to it. The 2025 National Security Strategy acknowledges this reality, shifting the focus from avoiding alliances to managing them in a way that ensures American preeminence. The goal is no longer just to be safe, but to be dominant.

The debate over U.S. foreign policy and foreign aid remains one of the most contentious in American political life. Critics argue that the cost of maintaining a global network of allies and military bases is unsustainable, while others contend that the cost of withdrawal would be far higher. The criticism comes from both domestic and international sources. Abroad, the U.S. is often accused of hypocrisy, promoting democracy at home while supporting authoritarian regimes abroad when it suits its interests. At home, the public is frequently skeptical of the endless cycle of intervention and the trillions of dollars spent on foreign aid and defense.

Despite the criticism, the institutions of American foreign policy remain robust. The Department of State, the Department of Defense, and the CIA continue to operate as the pillars of U.S. power. The Congress continues to exercise its constitutional powers, sometimes checking the President, sometimes enabling him. The courts continue to interpret the boundaries of executive authority, ensuring that the rule of law prevails even in the chaotic realm of international relations.

The history of U.S. foreign policy is not a straight line; it is a series of pivots, adjustments, and recalibrations. From the isolationism of the early republic to the global hegemony of the 21st century, the United States has constantly redefined its role in the world. The 2025 strategy is just the latest iteration of this ongoing process. It reflects a nation that is confident in its power but wary of its rivals, committed to its ideals but willing to use force to protect them.

In the end, the foreign policy of the United States is a reflection of the nation itself: complex, contradictory, and powerful. It is a policy that seeks to balance the ideal of a just world order with the reality of national self-interest. It is a policy that is shaped by the Constitution, challenged by Congress, and executed by a bureaucracy that spans the globe. As the world continues to change, the United States will continue to adapt, but the core goal remains the same: to ensure that the United States remains the preeminent power in a world that is increasingly uncertain.

The machinery of American power is vast, but it is not invincible. The checks and balances that define the domestic system also apply to foreign policy, ensuring that no single branch of government can dictate the nation's course without oversight. The Senate's power to ratify treaties, the Congress's power to declare war, and the courts' power to interpret the Constitution all serve to keep the U.S. foreign policy in check. These mechanisms are designed to prevent the nation from plunging into reckless adventures or becoming trapped in permanent entanglements.

Yet, the challenge of the 21st century is that the threats are no longer confined to traditional battlefields. They are economic, technological, and ideological. The 2025 strategy recognizes this, placing a premium on U.S. technology and standards. The battle for the future is not just for territory, but for the rules that govern the digital world, the flow of data, and the standards of innovation. In this new arena, the United States must maintain its preeminence if it is to secure its future.

The story of U.S. foreign policy is a story of ambition. It is a story of a nation that looked at the world and decided it could not stand aside. From Washington's warning against permanent alliances to the 2025 declaration of preeminence, the United States has consistently sought to shape the world in its image. Whether this ambition will lead to a stable and prosperous world or to endless conflict remains to be seen. But one thing is certain: the United States will not retreat. It will continue to engage, to lead, and to fight for its place at the top of the global order.

The reader, having just finished a report on the losses in Ukraine and the resilience of its fighting forces, now sees the broader canvas. The conflict in Eastern Europe is not an isolated event; it is a manifestation of the great power competition that defines the current era. The U.S. strategy of preventing an adversarial power from dominating the Middle East or the Indo-Pacific is the same logic that drives support for Ukraine. It is a strategy of containment, of maintaining a balance of power that favors the United States and its allies. The history of U.S. foreign policy is the history of this struggle, a struggle that shows no sign of ending.

As the United States moves forward, it will face new challenges and new adversaries. The tools of foreign policy will evolve, but the fundamental goal will remain unchanged. The United States will continue to seek preeminence, to protect its economy, and to ensure that the world remains free and open. It is a daunting task, but it is one that the United States has undertaken before, and it is one that it will undertake again. The story is far from over, and the next chapter is being written today.
