Affordable Care Act

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Affordable Care Act

The Law That Changed How America Gets Sick

In 2010, roughly fifty million Americans had no health insurance. Not because they didn't want it, but because insurance companies could simply refuse to cover them. Got cancer five years ago? Denied. Born with a heart defect? Denied. Took antidepressants in college? That's a preexisting condition—denied.

Then came the Affordable Care Act.

Signed into law by President Barack Obama on March 23, 2010, the Affordable Care Act—often called the ACA or, by both supporters and detractors, Obamacare—represented the most significant overhaul of American healthcare since Medicare and Medicaid were created in 1965. It didn't replace the existing system. Instead, it rewrote the rules of the game while keeping most of the players on the field.

The Problem It Was Trying to Solve

Before the ACA, the American health insurance market had a fundamental flaw that economists call adverse selection. Here's how it worked: insurance companies made money by collecting premiums and avoiding paying claims. The best way to avoid paying claims was to avoid insuring sick people in the first place.

So they got very good at it.

Companies employed teams of underwriters whose entire job was to comb through your medical history looking for reasons to deny you coverage or charge you more. A young woman who wanted to start a family might pay higher premiums simply because she could become pregnant. An older man paid more than a younger one, even if both were equally healthy. And if you had ever been seriously ill—ever—many insurers wanted nothing to do with you.

This created what economists call a death spiral. Healthy people, seeing high premiums, would often choose to go without insurance. Why pay hundreds of dollars a month when you rarely get sick? But this meant the pool of insured people got sicker on average, which drove premiums higher, which drove more healthy people away, which made the pool sicker still. Round and round it went.

The result was a country where having a job with good benefits wasn't just nice—it was the difference between getting treatment for a serious illness and going bankrupt trying to pay for it.

The Grand Bargain

The ACA tried to break this cycle through what amounted to a three-legged stool. Remove any one leg, and the whole thing tips over.

Leg one: Insurance companies could no longer deny coverage based on preexisting conditions or charge sick people more than healthy people. This is called guaranteed issue and community rating. An insurer had to accept you, period, and could only vary your premium based on your age (with limits—older people couldn't be charged more than three times what younger people paid) and where you lived.

Leg two: If you force insurers to cover everyone at similar prices, healthy people might wait until they get sick to buy insurance. Why pay premiums for years when you can sign up after your diagnosis? To prevent this, the law included an individual mandate—a requirement that most Americans carry health insurance or pay a penalty. This kept healthy people in the risk pool, which kept premiums manageable.

Leg three: But what about people who simply couldn't afford insurance, even with the new rules? The law created subsidies—federal money to help lower-income and middle-income Americans pay their premiums. These subsidies were available to households earning between 100 and 400 percent of the federal poverty level, which in practice meant a family of four earning up to roughly $100,000 a year could get some help.

This was the grand bargain. Insurers got millions of new customers. Americans got protection from the worst practices of the insurance industry. The federal government got higher taxes and Medicare cuts to pay for it all.

What Actually Changed

By 2014, when the major provisions took effect, the American health insurance landscape looked dramatically different.

The most visible change was the creation of health insurance exchanges—online marketplaces where individuals and families could shop for coverage. Some states built their own exchanges; others let the federal government run theirs through HealthCare.gov. The rollout of that federal website became infamous for its technical failures, with the site crashing repeatedly in its first weeks. But eventually, the exchanges stabilized and became functional.

The law also standardized what insurance actually meant. Before the ACA, some "insurance" plans were barely better than nothing—they might cover doctor visits but cap your total benefits at $50,000, leaving you exposed to catastrophic costs. The ACA required all plans to cover ten essential health benefits: hospitalization, prescription drugs, maternity care, mental health treatment, preventive services, and more. It banned lifetime and annual limits on coverage. It required that preventive care—vaccinations, cancer screenings, contraception—be provided without copays or deductibles.

Plans were sorted into metal tiers based on how much they paid versus what you paid out of pocket. Bronze plans had the lowest premiums but highest out-of-pocket costs, covering about 60 percent of expenses on average. Silver covered 70 percent. Gold covered 80 percent. Platinum covered 90 percent. This gave consumers a clear way to compare apples to apples.

Medicaid Gets Bigger—Sort Of

The ACA also expanded Medicaid, the government insurance program for low-income Americans. Previously, Medicaid eligibility varied wildly by state and typically excluded childless adults entirely, regardless of how poor they were. You could be destitute, but if you didn't have kids, Medicaid wasn't for you.

The law changed this by extending Medicaid to all adults earning up to 138 percent of the federal poverty level—roughly $20,000 a year for an individual. The federal government would pay 100 percent of the cost for newly eligible people through 2016, then gradually reduce its share to 90 percent by 2020.

But then the Supreme Court intervened.

In 2012, in a case called National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius, the Court upheld most of the ACA but ruled that the federal government couldn't force states to expand Medicaid. States could choose to participate or not. Republican-controlled states largely chose not, leaving millions of their poorest residents in a coverage gap—earning too much for traditional Medicaid but too little for exchange subsidies, which started at 100 percent of the poverty level.

This created the absurd situation where a single mother earning $12,000 a year in Kentucky could get heavily subsidized insurance, while the same woman earning the same amount in neighboring Tennessee got nothing.

The Politics of Healthcare

No piece of legislation in modern American history has been more politically contentious than the Affordable Care Act.

Republicans opposed it from the start. Not a single Republican in either chamber of Congress voted for it. They called it a government takeover of healthcare, though it actually preserved the private insurance system. They warned it would destroy jobs, explode the deficit, and lead to rationed care. "Repeal and replace" became a rallying cry that powered Republican electoral victories in 2010, 2014, and 2016.

The law survived multiple repeal attempts, including a dramatic late-night Senate vote in 2017 when Senator John McCain, dying of brain cancer, walked onto the floor and voted no, killing the latest Republican effort with a simple thumbs-down gesture.

What Republicans did manage, through the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, was to set the individual mandate penalty to zero starting in 2019. You were still technically required to have insurance, but there was no longer any consequence for going without it. Critics warned this would destabilize the insurance markets. The death spiral, they said, would return.

It hasn't, at least not yet. Insurance markets have remained relatively stable, though premiums have risen and some rural areas have only one insurer offering exchange plans.

Did It Work?

By the simplest measure—how many people have health insurance—the ACA was a clear success. The uninsured rate dropped from roughly 16 percent in 2010 to about 9 percent by 2016. Between 20 and 24 million Americans gained coverage, roughly split between those who got Medicaid and those who bought private insurance on the exchanges.

Healthcare spending growth also slowed after the law took effect, though economists debate how much credit the ACA deserves for this versus other factors like the lingering effects of the 2008 recession. Premiums for employer-sponsored insurance—the kind most working Americans have—grew more slowly than in the decade before the law.

The Congressional Budget Office, the nonpartisan agency that analyzes legislation's fiscal impact, repeatedly found that the ACA reduced the federal deficit. It did this through a combination of new taxes (on high-income earners, medical device companies, and insurance plans) and reductions in Medicare payments to hospitals and private insurers.

But the law fell far short of universal coverage, the goal of healthcare reformers since at least the 1940s. Roughly 27 million Americans remained uninsured as of 2021. Some were undocumented immigrants, explicitly excluded from the law's benefits. Others lived in states that refused Medicaid expansion. Still others found even subsidized premiums too expensive or simply chose to go without.

The Subsidy Cliff and Other Quirks

The ACA's subsidy structure created some strange incentives. Subsidies were designed to cap your premium at a percentage of your income, with that percentage rising as your income did. Someone earning 150 percent of the poverty level might pay only 4 percent of their income for a silver plan. Someone earning 350 percent might pay 9.5 percent.

But at 400 percent of the poverty level, the subsidies stopped entirely. This created what analysts called the "subsidy cliff." A family earning $99,000 might get substantial help with their premiums. A family earning $101,000 got nothing, and suddenly faced the full cost of insurance—potentially $20,000 or more per year.

This meant that for some Americans, earning a little more money actually made them worse off, a perverse outcome that the law's authors hadn't fully anticipated.

The 2021 American Rescue Plan and subsequent legislation temporarily extended subsidies beyond the 400 percent cliff and made them more generous overall. Whether these changes become permanent remains a matter of ongoing political debate.

The Insurance Industry's Uneasy Bargain

When the ACA first took effect, some insurers saw the exchanges as an opportunity and jumped in aggressively. Others held back, wary of the new rules and uncertain about who would sign up.

The law included three programs to manage the risk insurers faced in these early years. The risk corridor program promised to compensate insurers who lost money and take back some profits from those who did well—essentially training wheels for a new market. The reinsurance program helped cover the costs of the sickest enrollees. The risk adjustment program transferred money from plans with healthier enrollees to plans with sicker ones.

The risk corridor program became a flashpoint. Many insurers did lose money in the early years, and they expected the government to cover those losses as promised. But Congress, controlled by Republicans opposed to the law, prohibited the government from using general funds to make risk corridor payments. Insurers who had relied on that promise found themselves with billions in unpaid claims.

Some sued. In 2020, the Supreme Court ruled in Maine Community Health Options v. United States that the government had broken its promise and was obligated to pay. The insurers eventually got their money—years late, after some had already gone out of business.

Young Adults and the Twenty-Six Rule

One of the ACA's most popular provisions allowed young adults to stay on their parents' health insurance until age 26. Before the law, most plans kicked children off when they turned 19 or graduated from college, whichever came first.

This change addressed a real gap. Young adults have traditionally had the highest uninsured rates of any age group. They're often too old for their parents' plans, too young to have established careers with benefits, and too healthy to think they need insurance—until they do.

The provision was relatively cheap for insurers since young adults are generally healthy, and it was easy for families to understand. It became law in 2010, four years before most other provisions, giving the ACA its first taste of broad public approval.

What the ACA Didn't Do

It's worth being clear about what the Affordable Care Act left untouched.

It didn't create a single-payer system like Medicare for All. Americans still got insurance primarily through their employers, just as they had before. The private insurance industry remained firmly in place, profit motive and all.

It didn't directly control healthcare prices. Hospitals could still charge what they wanted. Drug companies could still set any price the market would bear. America's healthcare spending remained the highest in the developed world by a wide margin, with outcomes that are middling at best.

It didn't fundamentally alter the employer-based insurance system that covers most working Americans. If anything, it reinforced that system by imposing penalties on large employers who didn't offer coverage.

And it didn't solve the underlying cost problem that makes American healthcare so expensive—the administrative bloat, the fee-for-service incentives that reward doing more rather than doing better, the lack of price transparency, the regional monopolies that allow hospital systems to charge whatever they want.

The ACA was reform within the existing system, not replacement of it. Whether that represents pragmatic incrementalism or a failure of ambition depends on your politics.

The ACA Today

More than a decade after its passage, the Affordable Care Act remains the law of the land. Republicans no longer seriously talk about repealing it; instead, they've largely accepted its basic framework while trying to chip away at specific provisions.

The individual mandate, while technically still on the books, carries no penalty. The Medicaid expansion, once fiercely resisted, has been adopted by 40 states—including conservative strongholds like Oklahoma and Missouri, often through ballot initiatives that bypassed Republican legislatures.

Roughly 20 million Americans get their insurance through the ACA exchanges. Another 15 million or so gained coverage through Medicaid expansion. The preexisting condition protections, once controversial, are now so popular that even Republicans claim to support them.

But the law's future remains uncertain. Subsidies have been temporarily enhanced, but those enhancements require Congressional action to continue. The Supreme Court, now dominated by conservative justices, has twice turned back challenges to the law, but future cases could chip away at its provisions. And the fundamental question of whether healthcare is a right or a privilege remains unresolved in American politics.

What the ACA proved, perhaps more than anything, is that major social legislation is hard to pass and even harder to repeal. Once millions of people gain a benefit, taking it away becomes politically treacherous. The law that was supposed to be Barack Obama's Waterloo has instead become the baseline from which all future healthcare debates must begin.

The question is no longer whether to have an Affordable Care Act. It's what comes next.
