Arms control

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Arms control

In 1675, France and the Holy Roman Empire signed the Strasbourg Agreement, a quiet but revolutionary document that marked the first international treaty explicitly limiting the use of chemical weapons. Specifically, it banned poison bullets. It was a modest beginning in a long, fraught history of nations attempting to handcuff their own capacity for destruction, yet it established a precedent that would ripple through centuries: the idea that warfare could, and should, be subject to rules. This was not merely a moral plea; it was a strategic calculation that certain tools of war were too dangerous to be left entirely to the whims of the battlefield.

Arms control, in its modern essence, is the practice of imposing international restrictions on the development, production, stockpiling, proliferation, and usage of weapons. These range from the small arms carried by infantry to the devastating conventional weapons of armies, and finally to the apocalyptic potential of weapons of mass destruction. It is a discipline born of diplomacy, where consenting participants agree to limit their own military appetites through treaties and agreements. The goal is rarely the total elimination of force, but rather the avoidance of costly, counter-productive arms races that drain national treasuries and destabilize the global order.

The logic is deceptively simple. When nations fear one another, they build more weapons. This building triggers fear in their neighbors, who respond in kind, leading to a spiral where everyone is more heavily armed but no one is safer. This is the security dilemma. Arms control seeks to break this cycle. It offers a trade: nations agree to halt the spread of certain technologies, such as nuclear weaponry or advanced missile systems, in exchange for assurances that they will not become victims of those very technologies. It is a mechanism to limit the damage done by warfare, protecting civilians and the environment from the ravages that would be bad for all participants, regardless of who claims victory.

"Arms control is meant to break the security dilemma. It aims at mutual security between partners and overall stability."

Yet, for all its noble intentions, arms control is often viewed with cynicism by the very states that sign these treaties. For many participants, these agreements are not primarily about peace, but about economics. They are ways to limit the astronomical costs of developing and building weapons systems. Furthermore, they reduce the costs associated with war itself. Perhaps most pragmatically, arms control can be a way of maintaining the viability of military action. By limiting weapons that would make war so catastrophic and destructive that it ceases to be a viable tool for national policy, nations ensure that they can still fight wars if necessary, but only wars they can win without mutual annihilation.

The history of enforcement reveals the fragility of these pacts. Most agreements rely entirely on the continued desire of participants to abide by the terms. When that desire fades, the treaty crumbles. Nations do not always openly withdraw; instead, they often seek to covertly circumvent the terms or simply ignore them. This was vividly illustrated by the Washington Naval Treaty of 1922 and the subsequent London Naval Treaty. These agreements attempted to cap naval tonnage to prevent a naval arms race before World War II. The result was a masterclass in gaming the system.

The United States, bound by weight limits, focused on developing superior technology to get better performance from their ships while technically staying within the weight restrictions. The United Kingdom, ever the pragmatist, exploited loopholes in the treaty's wording to expand their capabilities. The Italians, in a brazen act of deception, simply misrepresented the weight of their vessels. And Japan, when the limits became too constraining for their strategic vision, simply left the treaty. The nations that violated the terms suffered no great consequences. Within little more than a decade, the entire edifice was abandoned, and the world marched toward a devastating conflict.

The Geneva Protocol, which banned the use of chemical and biological weapons, has lasted longer and enjoyed more respect, yet it too has been violated at will when nations felt the need. Enforcement has historically been haphazard, driven more by political expediency than by adherence to the terms. Sanctions and punitive measures are often advocated primarily by a violator's natural political enemies, while their allies ignore the breaches or offer only token measures. This double standard undermines the very foundation of international law.

Modern treaties have attempted to address these weaknesses by including more stringent terms on enforcement and, crucially, verification. Verification is the process of determining whether a nation is complying with the agreement. It involves a delicate dance of information release and physical examination, allowing participants to inspect each other to confirm the truth of their claims. This process often involves as much negotiation as the limits themselves. In some cases, the question of verification has led to the complete breakdown of treaty negotiations. The Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty (CTBT), for instance, faced significant opposition in the United States, where verification concerns were cited as a major obstacle, ultimately preventing ratification.

States often find themselves in a paradox: they wish to remain in a treaty to keep their competitors bound by its limits, even as they seek to break those limits themselves. To openly defy an agreement and withdraw carries heavy diplomatic repercussions and paints a nation as an aggressor in the court of public opinion. However, if a nation remains in the agreement while secretly violating it, it forces its competitors to continue adhering to the restrictions. If they were to withdraw, those competitors would be released from the constraints, allowing them to match the violator's developments and negating any advantage the violator sought to gain.

Scholars and practitioners such as John D. Steinbruner, Thomas Schelling, Morton Halperin, Jonathan Dean, and Stuart Croft have worked extensively to provide the theoretical backing for this complex field. They distinguish arms control from disarmament. Disarmament often takes a stance of peace without weapons. Arms control, by contrast, accepts the maintenance of stability through mutually controlled armament. It is a defensive strategy in principle; transparency, equality, and stability are concepts that do not fit easily into an offensive strategy.

A 2020 study published in the American Political Science Review highlighted a fundamental reason why successful arms control is so rare. It involves a difficult trade-off between transparency and security. For an agreement to be effective, there must be a way to thoroughly verify compliance, often requiring intrusive inspections. However, states are often reluctant to submit to such inspections. They fear that inspectors will use their presence not to verify the treaty, but to gather intelligence on the state's broader military capabilities, which could be used against them in a future conflict. The very mechanism designed to ensure safety can be perceived as a threat to national security.

The roots of this practice stretch back much further than the 17th century. One of the first recorded attempts at arms control occurred in ancient Greece, governed by the Amphictyonic Leagues. These leagues laid down rules specifying how war could be waged, with breaches punishable by fines or by war itself. It was an early recognition that war required boundaries.

Centuries later, in the 8th and 9th centuries AD, the Frankish Empire produced swords and chain mail armor of such superior quality that they were highly sought after across Europe. Charlemagne, ruling from 768 to 814, recognized the danger of these weapons falling into the hands of his enemies, including the Moors, the Vikings, and the Slavs. He made the sale or export of this equipment illegal, punishable by the forfeiture of property or even death. This was a blunt, unilateral form of arms control, designed to limit the possession of lethal technology by specific groups.

The Church also played a pivotal role as a trans-national organization, using its moral authority to limit the means of warfare. The Peace of God, proclaimed in 989 and extended in 1033, protected noncombatants, agrarian and economic facilities, and church property from the ravages of war. The Truce of God, established in 1027, attempted to prevent violence between Christians altogether by designating specific times when fighting was forbidden. The Second Lateran Council in 1139 went a step further, prohibiting the use of crossbows against other Christians, though it notably did not prevent their use against non-Christians. These were attempts to humanize warfare, to draw lines that even in the heat of battle, should not be crossed.

The development of firearms in subsequent centuries led to a dramatic increase in the devastation of war. The brutality of these conflicts spurred efforts to formalize the rules of war, ensuring humane treatment for prisoners of war and the wounded, and establishing rules to protect non-combatants from pillaging. However, until the beginning of the 19th century, few formal arms control agreements were recorded, aside from theoretical proposals or treaties imposed on defeated armies.

The Strasbourg Agreement of 1675 stands out as a rare exception, banning poison bullets between France and the Holy Roman Empire. But it was the Rush–Bagot Treaty of 1817 that truly marked the beginning of the modern era of arms control. Signed between the United States and the United Kingdom, it led to the demilitarization of the Great Lakes and Lake Champlain region of North America. This was a profound shift, moving from temporary truces to a permanent structural limitation on military power in a specific region, setting the stage for the complex web of international agreements that would follow.

The history of arms control is a history of tension. It is the tension between the desire for safety and the need for power, between the need for transparency and the instinct for secrecy. It is a field where the best-laid plans often unravel due to the very human tendency to seek advantage. Yet, despite the failures, the circumventions, and the broken treaties, the impulse to control the tools of destruction remains. It is a testament to the recognition that while war may be a part of the human condition, the total absence of rules leads to a darkness from which there is no return. The struggle is not to eliminate war entirely, but to ensure that when it comes, it does not bring about the end of civilization itself. The challenge for the future, particularly as we face new frontiers like artificial intelligence and cyber warfare, is to apply the hard-lessons of the past to technologies that move faster than our diplomacy can keep up. The stakes are higher than ever, and the margin for error has never been smaller.

The difficulty lies in the fact that arms control is not a static state but a dynamic process. It requires constant negotiation, constant verification, and a constant willingness to trust, even when trust is in short supply. It is a reminder that in a world of sovereign states, peace is not a gift; it is a construct, built piece by piece through treaties, inspections, and the difficult, often thankless work of diplomacy. When it works, it is invisible. When it fails, the consequences are written in history books in the blood of millions. The story of arms control is the story of our attempt to keep the former true and avoid the latter.

As we look toward the future, the lessons of the Washington Naval Treaty and the Geneva Protocol remain relevant. They teach us that treaties are only as strong as the political will to enforce them. They teach us that loopholes will be found and exploited if the incentives are strong enough. And they teach us that verification is the linchpin of any successful agreement. Without the ability to know that your adversary is abiding by the rules, the rules are meaningless. But with verification, we face the risk of espionage and the erosion of security. It is a precarious balance, one that nations have been trying to strike for centuries.

The 2020 study in the American Political Science Review underscores that this trade-off is the central obstacle. We want to know what our enemies are doing, but we are terrified of showing them what we are doing. This paradox is the engine of the security dilemma. Arms control is the only mechanism we have to resolve it, yet it is the very mechanism that requires us to expose our vulnerabilities. It is a high-wire act without a net, performed on a global stage where a single misstep can have catastrophic consequences. The history of arms control is a testament to human ingenuity in the face of this dilemma, a record of our attempts to build a ladder out of the abyss of total war. Whether we succeed in the future depends on our ability to learn from the past, to accept the limitations of our own power, and to recognize that in a world of interconnected threats, our security is inextricably linked to the security of our adversaries.

The evolution from the Peace of God to the modern non-proliferation regimes shows a clear trajectory: a growing recognition that the methods of war must be constrained by the methods of peace. It is a recognition that the cost of unrestricted warfare is too high for anyone to pay. As nations continue to develop new and more destructive technologies, the need for effective arms control becomes more urgent. The question is no longer whether we need it, but whether we can find the political will to make it work. The history suggests it is possible, but difficult. The future depends on our ability to rise to that challenge.
