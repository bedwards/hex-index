Causes of income inequality in the United States

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Causes of income inequality in the United States

Here's a puzzle that should keep you up at night: American workers today produce roughly twice as much per hour as they did in the 1970s. Yet for most of them, paychecks haven't budged much in real terms. Where did all that extra output go?

The answer reveals one of the most consequential economic transformations of our time—and it's not a simple story.

The Great Decoupling

For about three decades after World War Two, something remarkable happened in the American economy. As workers became more productive, their compensation rose in lockstep. If you produced more, you got paid more. It seemed like an iron law of capitalism.

Then, around the mid-1970s, the lines diverged. Productivity kept climbing. Compensation flatlined.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics has documented this "productivity-compensation gap" across virtually every corner of the economy. When researchers examined 183 different industries from 1987 to 2015, they found that productivity growth outpaced compensation growth in 83 percent of them. Not a few outliers—the vast majority.

The information industry offers perhaps the starkest example. Productivity there grew at about 5 percent annually over nearly three decades. Compensation? Roughly 1.5 percent. That's a gap of 3.5 percentage points, year after year, compounding relentlessly.

Manufacturing showed a 2.7 percent gap. Retail trade, 2.6 percent. Even transportation and warehousing—moving physical goods from place to place—saw a 1.3 percent divergence between what workers produced and what they took home.

So where did the money go?

The Usual Suspects

Economists have proposed roughly seven major explanations for America's growing income inequality. Each captures part of the truth. None captures all of it.

The Congressional Budget Office put it diplomatically: "the precise reasons for the rapid growth in income at the top are not well understood." Translation: even the experts aren't entirely sure what's happening.

But let's examine the leading theories.

Globalization: The World Got Flat

In 2000, about 5 million Chinese households earned between $11,500 and $43,000 in today's dollars—what economists would call a middle-class income. By 2015, that number had exploded to 225 million households.

This represents one of the greatest reductions in absolute poverty in human history. It also represents fierce new competition for American workers.

Economist Branko Milanovic analyzed global income changes between 1988 and 2008 and found a striking pattern. The biggest winners were the global top 1 percent (whose real incomes rose about 60 percent) and the emerging middle classes of China, India, Indonesia, Brazil, and Egypt (who saw gains of 70 to 80 percent). The biggest losers? The middle classes of developed countries—people around the 75th to 90th percentile of global income in 1988. In plain terms: the American middle class.

Here's an interesting wrinkle, though. By 1995, imports from low-wage countries totaled less than 3 percent of American economic output. It wasn't until 2006 that America imported more manufactured goods from developing countries than from wealthy ones. The timing doesn't quite match up with the inequality trend, which began accelerating in the early 1970s.

Author Timothy Noah estimates trade explains about 10 percent of what he calls "the Great Divergence" in income distribution. Important, but not the whole story.

The Robots Are Coming (And They're Picking Winners)

If you had to pick a consensus explanation among economists in the mid-2000s, it would be something called "skill-biased technological change." The term sounds technical, but the concept is intuitive.

Imagine you're running a company in 1970. You need workers who can operate machinery, answer phones, file documents, and handle routine tasks. A high school diploma provides adequate preparation for most of these jobs.

Now imagine you're running the same company in 2020. Much of that routine work has been automated. The machinery operates itself. Phone systems route calls automatically. Documents live in the cloud. What you need now are workers who can program the machines, analyze data, manage complex systems, and solve problems that software can't handle.

This shift dramatically increased demand for educated workers while reducing demand for those without advanced skills. The result was a widening gap between what college graduates earn and what high school graduates earn—economists call this the "college premium."

Between 1980 and 2005, the college premium roughly doubled. Economist David Autor estimates that about two-thirds of rising income inequality during this period came from the increased returns to education.

One scholarly gathering of prominent labor economists estimated that technological change alone accounted for over 40 percent of the increase in inequality—more than any other single factor.

The Superstar Economy

There's another way to think about what technology has done to compensation: it's turned many sectors of the economy into winner-take-all tournaments.

Consider the difference between a local accountant in 1960 and a software company today. The accountant could only serve clients within driving distance. No matter how talented, there was a ceiling on how much business one person could handle. Competition was inherently local.

Now consider a software company. Once you've written the code, serving one customer or serving a million customers costs roughly the same. The best products don't just win their local markets—they win globally. The runner-up gets not a silver medal but obscurity.

This "superstar" dynamic has transformed compensation across many industries. The rewards increasingly flow to the very top performers and the very top firms.

Eric Posner and Glen Weyl argue this explains much of what we observe. They point out something often overlooked in discussions of inherited wealth: of the Forbes 400 richest Americans, 69 percent are first-generation wealth creators, up from 40 percent in 1982. Only 35 people from the original 1982 list remain on it today. The rich are different from you and me—but many of them weren't rich a generation ago.

The superstar effect extends to corporations themselves. Dominant firms in concentrated industries pay their workers substantially more than industry averages. But as fewer firms capture more market share, fewer workers benefit from these premium wages.

The Union Question

In the decades after World War Two, about one-third of American workers belonged to labor unions. Today that figure hovers around 12 percent.

Unions did more than negotiate higher wages for their members. They compressed wage distributions, meaning they reduced the gap between the highest and lowest paid workers. They also established norms that influenced non-union employers. When the local factory paid union wages, other businesses had to compete for workers.

The decline of unionization removed both the direct wage effects and these ripple effects throughout the economy. Some economists estimate declining union membership accounts for 10 to 15 percent of rising inequality.

Tax Policy: America's Choice

Here's a fact that might surprise you: before taxes and government transfers, income inequality in the United States looks similar to other developed countries. It's after taxes and transfers that America stands out as dramatically more unequal.

This suggests something important. High pre-tax inequality might be an inevitable feature of modern capitalism. High post-tax inequality is a policy choice.

Emmanuel Saez, an economist at the University of California, Berkeley, has documented striking international comparisons. Japan, Sweden, and France did not experience significant increases in income inequality during the 1979–2010 period, even as the United States did. The top 1 percent continued to receive less than 10 percent of income in those countries, while in America the share rose from 10 percent to over 20 percent.

"Differences across countries rule out technical change and globalization as the sole explanation," Saez wrote in 2014. "Policies play a key role in shaping inequality—tax and transfer policies, regulations, education."

Social Norms: What We'll Tolerate

In the 1970s, the typical chief executive officer earned about 40 times what the average worker at their company earned. By the early 2000s, that ratio had climbed to over 350 times.

Did CEOs become nine times more valuable over three decades? Did the skills required to run a company change that dramatically?

Some researchers argue that what changed was not the objective value of executive work but social norms about appropriate compensation. In an era when boards of directors, compensation consultants, and shareholders accepted modest pay packages, CEO compensation stayed modest. As those constraints loosened, compensation ratcheted upward.

Ed Dolan points to another factor: moral hazard. Executive compensation increasingly came through bonuses tied to short-term performance, without adequate "clawback" provisions for losses. Executives could capture enormous gains while pushing risks onto shareholders, creditors, or ultimately taxpayers.

Immigration and Education

Two more factors deserve mention, though their effects are harder to isolate.

Since 1965, the United States has experienced relatively high levels of immigration, particularly of less-skilled workers. Some economists argue this increased competition at the bottom of the labor market, reducing wages for native-born workers without high school diplomas. Others dispute the magnitude of this effect.

Education tells a more straightforward story, though one with troubling implications. In the 1950s through 1970s, annual tuition at public universities averaged about 4 percent of median family income. At private universities, it was about 20 percent. By 2005, those figures had risen to 10 percent and 45 percent respectively.

Colleges raised prices because they could—demand for education was soaring as the college premium grew. But the rising costs meant fewer families could afford the credential that would grant access to higher incomes. The ladder of upward mobility developed missing rungs.

The Deeper Puzzle

Researchers have identified something called the "word gap" that illustrates how inequality perpetuates itself across generations. Children in low-income families are exposed to roughly 636 words per hour during their first four years of life. Children in high-income families hear about 2,153 words per hour.

That difference—hearing more than three times as many words during the critical years of brain development—shows up later as differences in academic achievement, which translate into differences in educational attainment, which become differences in income. The cycle continues.

Psychologists have also documented how poverty itself carries stigma that creates barriers. At the same time, some research suggests those in poverty tend to attribute wealth to luck or illegal behavior rather than effort—a belief that might reduce motivation to pursue traditional paths to success. Breaking these cycles requires addressing not just economic policies but deeply ingrained social attitudes.

Three Forces, One Outcome

The Bureau of Labor Statistics, in its analysis of declining labor share, identifies three underlying forces that vary in importance across different industries.

First, globalization. Income that might once have flowed to American workers now flows to workers in other countries through offshoring of production and services.

Second, automation. As machines perform more tasks, a larger share of business income goes to capital—meaning the owners of those machines—rather than to workers.

Third, faster capital depreciation. This one is subtle but important. Modern businesses rely heavily on information technology assets—software, data systems, digital infrastructure. These assets lose their value much faster than the machinery of previous eras. A factory might operate for decades; software becomes obsolete in years. This faster depreciation may require businesses to allocate more revenue to capital replacement, leaving less for workers.

What This Means for the Modern Workplace

The connection to current debates about work—including where and how it happens—becomes clearer when you understand these underlying dynamics.

Journalist James Surowiecki observes that the sectors now providing the most employment in America—major retailers, restaurant chains, supermarkets—are ones with lower profit margins and less pricing power than the dominant employers of the 1960s. Meanwhile, high-profit sectors like technology employ relatively few people.

This is the world that return-to-office debates are happening within. Not a world where productivity gains naturally flow to workers, but one where the connection between what workers produce and what they earn has been fundamentally transformed.

Understanding why this happened doesn't automatically tell us what to do about it. But it does suggest that simple explanations—blame the robots, blame trade, blame immigrants—miss the complexity of what's actually occurred.

The American economy underwent a transformation beginning around 1970. Workers became dramatically more productive. Most of them didn't share in the gains. The explanations involve technology, globalization, policy choices, social norms, and educational access—all interacting in ways that even experts struggle to untangle.

Paul Krugman, the economist and Nobel laureate, offers a provocative prediction about where current trends might lead. The surge in inequality so far, he argues, has been driven mainly by "supersalaries"—enormous compensation for top executives and professionals. But as this generation of high earners passes their wealth to their children, America could transform into what he calls "a rentier-dominated society"—one where the primary economic division is not between workers and executives, but between those who own capital and those who don't.

Two decades from now, he suggests, America could be more unequal than Belle Époque Europe—that gilded age before World War One when hereditary aristocracies still dominated European society.

That's the stakes of the inequality debate. Not just who gets what today, but what kind of society we're building for tomorrow.
