Learning curve

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Learning curve

The Phrase Everyone Gets Wrong

Here's a delicious irony: the phrase "steep learning curve" means the opposite of what most people think it means.

When someone says an activity has a steep learning curve, they usually mean it's difficult—that you'll struggle and make little progress despite great effort. But look at an actual learning curve graph. The vertical axis shows proficiency. The horizontal axis shows experience or time. A steep curve means proficiency shoots up quickly. In other words, a steep learning curve represents rapid progress, not difficulty.

What people actually mean when they say "steep learning curve" is something more nuanced: an activity where the basics come easily but mastery proves elusive. The curve starts steep—you learn the fundamentals fast—then flattens into a frustrating plateau where additional effort yields diminishing returns.

But by now, the misnomer has become so embedded in everyday speech that fighting it feels futile. Language, after all, evolves through collective misunderstanding just as often as through precision.

Memory, Nonsense, and the Birth of a Concept

The learning curve emerged from one of psychology's strangest experiments. In 1885, a German psychologist named Hermann Ebbinghaus decided to study how humans learn and forget. His method was deliberately absurd: he memorized lists of nonsense syllables—meaningless combinations like "DAX" and "BUP" and "ZOL"—then tested himself repeatedly to see how his recall changed over time.

Why nonsense? Because Ebbinghaus wanted to strip away meaning. Real words carry associations, emotional weight, connections to other memories. Nonsense syllables are pure information, uncontaminated by prior knowledge. They let him study the mechanics of memory itself.

When Ebbinghaus plotted his results—success rates against trial numbers—he noticed something unexpected. Learning didn't proceed in a straight line. Sometimes his scores jumped. Sometimes they dropped. Sometimes they leveled off for a while before climbing again. The path from ignorance to mastery was anything but smooth.

Ebbinghaus didn't actually use the term "learning curve." That phrase wouldn't appear until 1903, when psychologists Bryan and Harter published their study of telegraph operators learning Morse code. They noticed a characteristic pattern: rapid improvement at first, then a period of slower progress, with the curve bending away from the vertical axis like a ski slope leveling into a plateau.

From Psychology to the Factory Floor

The learning curve might have remained a curiosity of academic psychology if not for Theodore Paul Wright. In 1936, Wright was studying the aircraft industry when he noticed something remarkable about production costs.

Building the first airplane of a new design is expensive. Engineers are still figuring out the manufacturing process. Workers are learning new techniques. Mistakes are common. But with each subsequent plane, the cost drops—not just a little, but by a predictable percentage.

Wright discovered a pattern that would come to be known as Wright's Law: every time total production doubles, the cost per unit falls by about twenty percent. Build ten planes, and each costs less than when you'd only built five. Build a hundred, and costs drop further still. The curve never quite reaches zero—there are irreducible material costs—but it keeps declining with experience.

This wasn't just workers getting faster. The whole system was learning. Engineers refined designs. Managers optimized workflows. Suppliers improved their processes. Institutional knowledge accumulated, was shared, and compounded.

During World War Two, the United States Air Force began tracking this phenomenon systematically. They collected data on how many labor hours it took to build airframes—the structural skeletons of aircraft—from 1940 through mid-1945. The results confirmed Wright's observations. Learning curves were real, measurable, and predictable enough to use for planning.

The Experience Curve and the Consulting Revolution

In 1968, Bruce Henderson of the Boston Consulting Group took Wright's insight and ran with it. Henderson wasn't interested in just aircraft manufacturing. He suspected the learning curve applied broadly—to any production process, any industry, any organization that repeated similar tasks over time.

He was right. The Boston Consulting Group studied industry after industry throughout the 1970s and found experience curve effects everywhere. The reductions weren't as dramatic as in aircraft manufacturing—most industries showed cost decreases of ten to twenty-five percent for each doubling of cumulative production—but the pattern held.

Henderson gave this phenomenon a new name: the experience curve. And he expressed it mathematically using what's sometimes called Henderson's Law—a power law relationship where cost follows a smooth, predictable decline against cumulative production.

The implications for business strategy were profound. If costs fall predictably with experience, then market share becomes a competitive weapon. The company that produces the most units accumulates the most experience, achieves the lowest costs, and can undercut competitors on price while maintaining margins. This insight helped launch the modern consulting industry and influenced corporate strategy for decades.

The Mathematics of Getting Better

Beneath the business strategy lies elegant mathematics. Several models have emerged to describe how learning unfolds.

Wright's original model is the simplest. The cost of making the thousandth unit equals the cost of the first unit multiplied by the total number made, raised to some negative exponent. That exponent captures the strength of learning. A larger negative exponent means faster improvement. Wright found this exponent corresponded to roughly an eighty percent learning rate in aircraft manufacturing—meaning costs dropped to eighty percent of their previous level with each doubling of production.

But reality often proves messier than a simple power law.

The Plateau Model acknowledges that improvement can't continue forever. At some point, you hit a floor—the minimum achievable cost given current technology and constraints. No amount of additional experience will push costs lower. The mathematics adds a minimum threshold: costs follow the power law until they reach this floor, then stay flat.

The Stanford-B Model accounts for prior experience. Workers don't always start from zero. A factory hiring experienced employees begins partway up the learning curve. The mathematics adds a constant representing accumulated prior knowledge before the measurement period began.

DeJong's Model separates human and machine contributions. Machines, unlike humans, don't learn. A factory that's fifty percent automated can only improve the human portion of its costs. The mathematics partitions production into learnable and fixed components.

The S-Curve Model combines everything. Learning starts slow as workers figure out what they're doing. It accelerates as techniques crystallize and spread. Then it slows again as the easy improvements are exhausted and the process approaches its practical limits. Plot this against time and you get a shape like a stretched S—technically called a sigmoid function.

Why Getting Better Makes Things Bigger

Here's where learning curves intersect with one of economics' most counterintuitive principles.

In the 1860s, the English economist William Stanley Jevons noticed something puzzling about coal consumption. Steam engines were becoming dramatically more efficient. Each improved engine extracted more work from every pound of coal. Logic suggested that total coal consumption should fall—you needed less fuel to accomplish the same tasks.

Instead, coal consumption soared.

The explanation became known as the Jevons Paradox: efficiency improvements lower the cost of using a resource, which increases demand, which often overwhelms the efficiency gains entirely. Better steam engines made coal-powered industry more economical, which led to more industry, which consumed more coal than the inefficient engines ever had.

Learning curves create the same dynamic. As organizations learn to do things more cheaply, they can do more of those things. A factory that halves its per-unit cost doesn't pocket the savings and maintain production—it expands. The efficiency gains fund expansion, and expansion generates more learning, which enables more efficiency, which funds more expansion. The flywheel spins faster and faster.

This is why learning curves matter for industrial leapfrogging. Countries or companies that start from behind can sometimes jump ahead by riding the learning curve of emerging technologies. They don't need to recapitulate every step that incumbents took. They can learn from others' accumulated experience, avoid their mistakes, and reach cost parity faster than anyone expected.

Learning Curves in Silicon

Perhaps no industry demonstrates learning curve effects more dramatically than semiconductor manufacturing.

In 1965, Gordon Moore observed that the number of transistors on an integrated circuit doubled roughly every two years, while costs stayed constant or fell. This observation—Moore's Law—has held for nearly six decades, driving the exponential improvement in computing power that reshaped the modern world.

Moore's Law isn't a law of physics. It's a learning curve.

Each generation of chips requires new manufacturing techniques, new materials, new precision engineering. But the semiconductor industry has organized itself around the expectation of continuous improvement. Enormous research and development investments pay off because everyone expects the learning to continue. The prediction becomes self-fulfilling.

The learning isn't just technical. It includes supply chains that improve quality and reduce costs, manufacturing equipment that becomes more precise and reliable, design tools that automate increasingly complex tasks, and organizations that accumulate institutional knowledge about what works and what doesn't.

Recently, researchers have begun using learning curve mathematics to predict how long Moore's Law can continue and what might replace semiconductor scaling when physical limits finally bite.

The Human Side of Learning

Learning curves create odd social dynamics. Consider a factory that pays workers by the piece—more widgets produced means higher pay. Now introduce a new team member.

The newcomer is slow. They're still learning. But their slow work doesn't just affect their own output—it disrupts the rhythm of experienced workers who must train them, answer questions, and compensate for mistakes. Team productivity temporarily drops.

Workers paid by the piece have a perverse incentive to resist new hires. The existing team would earn more, at least in the short term, if they could keep the newcomer out.

Some companies have addressed this by building learning curves into compensation. New workers earn a premium relative to their output while they're learning. Experienced workers receive bonuses for training that reflect the temporary productivity loss. The mathematics of learning becomes the basis for labor relations.

Similar dynamics play out when workers change roles. Moving an experienced employee to a new station means temporarily losing their hard-won expertise. The organization as a whole may benefit from cross-training—employees who understand multiple functions can respond more flexibly to changing demands—but individual workers and their immediate teams often bear the short-term costs of relearning.

Approaching the Impossible

Every learning curve eventually flattens. The laws of thermodynamics guarantee it.

Consider trying to perfect any process—eliminating waste, reducing errors, approaching some theoretical ideal. The first improvements come easily. Obvious inefficiencies get spotted and fixed. But as you approach perfection, each incremental improvement requires geometrically more effort.

Reducing defects from ten percent to one percent might take a year of focused work. Reducing defects from one percent to one-tenth of a percent might take another year. Getting from one-tenth of a percent to one-hundredth might take a decade. You're always approaching the limit but never quite reaching it.

This isn't just a metaphor. The second law of thermodynamics tells us that no process can be perfectly efficient. There's always some irreducible waste, some minimal energy loss, some floor below which no amount of learning will push performance. The mathematics of learning curves captures this physical reality.

Understanding these limits matters for strategic planning. A company investing in process improvement needs to recognize when it's in the steep part of the curve—where investment yields dramatic returns—and when it's approaching the plateau—where the same investment produces diminishing gains. Sometimes the right move is to abandon the current approach entirely and start a new learning curve with a fundamentally different technology or process.

The Shape of Progress

Zoom out far enough and nearly all learning follows an S-curve. Early progress is slow as pioneers explore and fail and gradually figure out what works. Then comes the acceleration phase—techniques crystallize, knowledge spreads, investment floods in, improvement compounds on improvement. Finally, progress slows as the approach nears its practical limits and the remaining improvements become increasingly difficult to achieve.

This pattern appears at every scale. Individual skills follow S-curves. Organizational capabilities follow S-curves. Technologies follow S-curves. Entire industries follow S-curves. Perhaps civilizations do too.

What looks like sudden revolutionary change—the explosive growth phase that grabs headlines and transforms industries—is often just the middle section of an S-curve that began long before anyone was paying attention. The years of slow experimentation, failed attempts, and incremental refinement laid the groundwork. Then conditions aligned, the curve went steep, and everything seemed to change overnight.

This perspective suggests patience during the early slow phase and caution during the explosive middle. The pioneers struggling through early failures are building the knowledge base that will enable later acceleration. And the triumphant growth phase won't last forever—eventually every curve flattens.

Learning to Learn

The deepest question about learning curves isn't mathematical. It's strategic: how do you move along the curve faster?

Some organizations seem to learn more efficiently than others. They extract more improvement from each unit of experience. Their curves are steeper. Their plateaus come later and higher. What makes the difference?

Part of the answer is systematic. Organizations that deliberately capture and share knowledge—through documentation, training programs, and cultural norms that encourage asking for help—compound their learning more effectively than those where knowledge stays locked in individual heads.

Part of it is psychological. A culture that treats mistakes as learning opportunities rather than failures to punish will experiment more freely and discover improvements faster. Fear suppresses the risk-taking that accelerates learning.

Part of it is structural. Small teams with clear communication paths often learn faster than large organizations where information gets filtered through layers of hierarchy. But small teams lack the resources for major investments. The optimal structure depends on where you are on the curve.

And part of it remains mysterious—the organizational equivalent of natural talent. Some groups just seem to click, their members' skills complementing each other in ways that produce learning greater than any individual could achieve alone.

The Curve Ahead

Learning curves are more than academic abstractions or consulting frameworks. They're a lens for understanding how the world gets better over time—and why that improvement sometimes stalls, accelerates, or takes unexpected turns.

For anyone interested in industrial development, in technological progress, in the economic fortunes of nations and companies and individuals, learning curves offer a vocabulary and a mathematics for thinking about improvement. They remind us that experience matters, that early costs predict later costs, and that the path from expensive and crude to cheap and refined follows predictable patterns.

They also remind us that every curve eventually flattens. The limit always waits. The question is never whether progress will slow, but when—and whether you can jump to a new curve before the old one runs out.
