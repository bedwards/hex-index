Maundy Thursday

16 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Maundy Thursday

In the quiet hours before dawn on the Thursday of Holy Week, a peculiar silence descends upon the cathedrals of Europe and the Americas. It is a silence so profound that it is often described not as an absence of sound, but as a heavy, expectant presence. This is the moment the church bells, which have rung with such exuberant joy for the final days of Lent, are stripped of their clappers or simply fall silent. They will not ring again until the explosion of the Easter Vigil, leaving the faithful to navigate the remaining hours of the Triduum in a hush that feels like the holding of breath before a plunge. This specific day, the fifth day of Holy Week, stands as the dramatic hinge upon which the entire Christian drama of salvation swings. It is Maundy Thursday, a day that simultaneously celebrates the intimate warmth of the Last Supper and foreshadows the crushing isolation of the Cross, marking the precise beginning of the Paschal Triduum.

The very name of this day is a linguistic fossil, a layered artifact that reveals centuries of theological evolution and cultural adaptation. "Maundy" is not a word one encounters in everyday conversation, yet it carries the weight of a divine imperative. It derives from the Latin mandatum, meaning "commandment." This etymology points directly to the words of Jesus in the Gospel of John, chapter 13, where, after washing the feet of his disciples, he speaks a line that would define the ethics of the Christian community for two millennia: "Mandatum novum do vobis ut diligatis invicem sicut dilexi vos." Translated, this is the "new commandment": "I give you a new commandment, that you love one another as I have loved you." The English word "mandate" shares this root, but in the context of the church, "Maundy" is softer, more personal. It is the commandment of love enacted through the humble, dusty act of footwashing.

Yet, the naming of this holy day is a testament to the diversity of the Christian tradition, a Babel of terms that all point to the same sacred event. In the Roman Rite of the Catholic Church, the modern liturgical books prefer "Holy Thursday," though the Latin texts retain the ancient Feria quinta in Cena Domini—the "Thursday of the Supper of the Lord." The spelling of Cena has shifted over time; before the 1955 decree Maxima redemptionis, the medieval spelling Cœna was standard, a visual nod to the archaic roots of the Latin language. In the United States, the Episcopal Church, a province of the Anglican Communion, clings to "Maundy Thursday," as does the Church of England's mother publication, the Book of Common Prayer. However, within the broader Anglican Communion, a curious linguistic divergence exists: in many parts of the world, "Holy Thursday" is reserved for Ascension Day, creating a potential for confusion that is only resolved by knowing the specific liturgical calendar of one's region.

The complexity deepens when one looks beyond the Anglican and Roman spheres. The United Methodist Church has oscillated in its terminology; their 1965 Book of Worship for Church and Home used "Maundy Thursday," while the 1992 Book of Worship shifted to "Holy Thursday," though both terms persist in official usage. The Lutheran Church and various Reformed denominations use both names interchangeably. The Eastern traditions offer their own distinct titles. In the Byzantine Rite, the day is known as "Great and Holy Thursday" or simply "Holy Thursday." The Coptic Orthodox Church refers to it as "Covenant Thursday" or "Thursday of the Covenant," emphasizing the new agreement between God and humanity. In the Maronite and Syriac Orthodox Churches, it is the "Thursday of Mysteries," a title that captures the ineffable depth of the events unfolding in the upper room. Even the Western Rite Orthodox communities, who use the Western liturgy while remaining in communion with the East, may call it "Maundy Thursday," "Holy Thursday," or both.

This linguistic richness is mirrored by an ancient, almost forgotten English name: Shere Thursday, or Sheer Thursday. The word "shere" comes from an Old English root meaning "clean" or "bright." This name likely refers to the practice of cleansing the church for the final time before Easter, or perhaps to the custom of switching liturgical colors from the somber tones of Lent to the white of the resurrection. Some historians suggest it refers to the practice of shearing beards, a physical act of purification that marked the day. This tradition of "cleaning" or "shearing" is not unique to England; it echoes across the Nordic languages. In Danish, it is skærtorsdag; in Swedish, skärtorsdag; in Norwegian, skjærtorsdag. The Faroese call it skírhósdagur or skírisdagur, the Icelandic skírdagur, and the Finnish kiirastorstai. These cognates suggest a pan-European understanding of the day as a time of spiritual and physical purification, a cleansing of the soul in preparation for the passion.

The date of Maundy Thursday, like Easter itself, is a moving target, shifting with the phases of the moon and the specific calendar a community follows. It is the fifth day of Holy Week, preceded by Holy Wednesday, often called Spy Wednesday for the betrayal of Judas, and followed immediately by Good Friday. The calculation of the date depends entirely on whether the Gregorian calendar or the Julian calendar is in use. The Roman Catholic Church and most Protestant denominations follow the Gregorian calendar, while the Eastern Orthodox Church generally adheres to the Julian system. This divergence means that for a significant portion of the year, the Christian world is celebrating different feasts on different days, a fragmentation that is particularly visible during Holy Week.

At the heart of the day's observance is the rite of the Maundy, the footwashing ceremony. This is not merely a symbolic gesture; it is a re-enactment of the most radical act of humility recorded in the Gospels. In the ancient world, washing feet was the task of the lowest servant, a job no Jew would typically ask another to perform, and certainly not a Rabbi. Yet, Jesus, the Master, took off his outer garments, tied a towel around his waist, and washed the feet of his disciples. The Mandatum ceremony, as it is known in the Roman Rite, is sung as an antiphon during the Mass or as a separate event. The priest or bishop, acting in persona Christi, washes the feet of a selected group of people. Historically, this group was strictly male, consisting of twelve men to represent the twelve apostles. However, the liturgy is a living thing, and in 2016, the Roman Missal was revised to allow women to participate in the rite, a significant theological shift that broadened the image of the apostolic community to include all of the faithful. Previously, only males had been permitted to partake in this specific ritual act.

The rite of footwashing is practiced across a vast spectrum of Christian denominations, including the Anglican, Episcopal, Armenian, Ethiopian, Lutheran, Methodist, Eastern Catholic, Schwarzenau (German Baptist) Brethren, Mennonite, Presbyterian, and Roman Catholic traditions. It is a visual sermon, a tangible expression of the "new commandment" that defines the day. In the Catholic Church, the Mass of the Lord's Supper, celebrated in the evening to align with the Jewish tradition of the day beginning at sunset, begins with the usual solemnity. However, the Gloria is accompanied by the ringing of the church bells, a final, joyous peal before the silence falls. After the homily, the footwashing takes place. Then, the Blessed Sacrament is not left on the main altar. It is processed in a solemn, often candlelit parade to a place of reposition, a side chapel or altar where it will be adored throughout the night. The main altar is then stripped bare, the candles removed, the linens taken away, leaving the sanctuary stark and empty. This stripping is a powerful visual metaphor for the stripping of Christ on the Cross, a pre-emptive enactment of the violence to come.

Beyond the sacred rituals of the church, the day has seeped into the very fabric of civil law and royal tradition, particularly in the United Kingdom. The "Royal Maundy" is a ceremony that dates back to the Middle Ages, with roots extending to the reign of King Edward I. In this ancient custom, the British Monarch distributes "alms" to deserving senior citizens. The number of recipients is precise: one man and one woman for each year of the Sovereign's age. The gifts are distributed in distinctive red and white purses. The red purse contains regular currency, given in place of food and clothing that was once provided. The white purse contains Maundy coins, specially minted pieces of silver: 1, 2, 3, and 4 pence. These are not mere souvenirs; they are legal tender, though their silver content far exceeds their face value. The tradition has evolved over centuries; until the death of King James II in 1701, the Monarch would personally wash the feet of the selected poor. There is even an old sketch, drawn from life, depicting Queen Elizabeth I performing this act of humility. Today, the Monarch distributes the coins but does not wash feet, though the symbolism remains potent. The service rotates around churches in England and Wales, though in a historic expansion in 2008, the ceremony was held for the first time in Northern Ireland at Armagh Cathedral.

The etymology of "Maundy" is not limited to the Latin mandatum. There is a competing, though less likely, theory that the name arises from "maunds," which were baskets or purses used to hold the alms distributed by the King. In this view, "maund" is connected to the Latin mendicare and the French mendier, meaning "to beg." The King's distribution of alms to the poor was a royal duty, and the baskets in which the money was carried became synonymous with the day itself. Whether the name comes from the commandment to love or the baskets of charity, the result is the same: a day of giving, of service, and of deep spiritual reckoning.

It is crucial to distinguish Maundy Thursday from what is sometimes erroneously called "Easter Thursday." In many parts of the world, particularly in Australia, "Easter Thursday" refers to the Thursday after Easter Sunday. This is a different day entirely, often marked by secular events such as the Australian Football League's "Easter Thursday" game. Maundy Thursday, by contrast, is the Thursday before Easter Sunday, the day that initiates the Paschal Triduum. The Triduum is not a series of separate days but a single, continuous liturgical celebration that spans from the evening of Maundy Thursday to the evening of Easter Sunday. It includes Good Friday, the day of Christ's death, and Holy Saturday, the day of the tomb, and culminates in the Easter Vigil. The Mass of the Lord's Supper on Maundy Thursday is the opening movement of this great symphony.

The liturgical color of the day is white, symbolizing purity and the institution of the Eucharist, yet the mood is one of somber anticipation. The Synoptic Gospels (Matthew, Mark, and Luke) place the Last Supper on the feast of Passover, while the Gospel of John suggests a slightly different chronology, placing the crucifixion at the time the Passover lambs were being slaughtered. This theological tension is part of the richness of the day, as the church holds both the celebration of the Passover meal and the foreshadowing of the ultimate sacrifice in the same moment. The Eucharist, or Holy Communion, is the central sacrament of the day. Christian denominations that observe this day universally celebrate the institution of this sacrament, teaching that Jesus took bread and wine, gave thanks, and gave them to his disciples as his body and blood, commanding them to "do this in memory of me."

The silence that follows the Mass is not merely a lack of noise; it is a theological statement. In the Catholic Church, the Lutheran Church, and certain Anglican congregations, the bells that rang for the Gloria are silenced. They are often "muted" or their clappers are removed, a physical act that signifies the departure of the bells to "Rome" or their own silence in mourning. This silence extends until the Easter Vigil, when the bells ring out again to announce the resurrection. This gap in sound creates a sensory void that mirrors the spiritual desert of the tomb. The faithful are left with nothing but the memory of the footwashing, the taste of the Eucharist, and the stark reality of the empty altar.

The day also carries a profound social dimension. The washing of feet is an act of leveling. It strips away hierarchy, status, and power. In a world obsessed with status and achievement, Maundy Thursday demands a radical reversal. The priest washes the feet of the layperson; the King washes the feet of the beggar (historically); the Master washes the feet of the servant. It is a challenge to the structures of society, a reminder that in the Kingdom of God, the last are first, and the greatest is the one who serves. This is why the rite has been so enduring, surviving the Reformation, the Enlightenment, and the modern era. It speaks to a fundamental human need for connection, for humility, and for love that transcends social barriers.

The diversity of names and practices surrounding Maundy Thursday is not a sign of confusion, but of a rich, living tradition that has adapted to countless cultures while holding fast to its core mystery. Whether it is called "Maundy Thursday" in the Anglican Communion, "Great and Holy Thursday" in the Byzantine Rite, or "Thursday of Mysteries" in the Syriac tradition, the essence remains the same. It is the night of the new commandment, the night of the Last Supper, the night of the footwashing, and the beginning of the journey to the Cross. It is a day that demands more than just attendance; it demands participation. It asks the faithful to strip away their own pride, to wash the feet of their neighbors, and to sit in the silence of the waiting.

The history of the day is also a history of the church's struggle to define itself. The shift from "Maundy" to "Holy Thursday" in some denominations reflects a desire to emphasize the sanctity of the day over the specific rite of the footwashing. The retention of "Maundy" in others, particularly those with an Anglican patrimony, suggests a desire to preserve the specific ritual and the commandment it embodies. The revision of the Roman Missal in 2016 to include women in the footwashing ceremony is a modern example of the church grappling with its own traditions and seeking a more inclusive expression of the "new commandment." These changes are not trivial; they are the living breathing of a faith that is constantly reinterpreting its past to speak to its present.

As the sun sets on Maundy Thursday, the world does not end. The business of the day goes on, the traffic flows, and the markets open. But for the church, the world has changed. The silence has fallen. The bells are still. The altar is bare. The faithful are left with the memory of a cup and a loaf, a towel and a basin, and a commandment that is as challenging today as it was two thousand years ago. "Love one another as I have loved you." It is a simple sentence, but it is the axis on which the Christian life turns. And on Maundy Thursday, the church pauses to remember that this love was not just a theory, but a practice. It was washed feet. It was a broken body. It was a shed blood. And it is a commandment that is still in force, still being tested, and still being lived out in the quiet acts of service that define the day.

The Paschal Triduum is a journey, and Maundy Thursday is the first step. It is the step from the safety of the upper room into the storm of Gethsemane. It is the step from the warmth of the Last Supper into the cold of the cross. It is a step that requires courage, for it means embracing the vulnerability of the servant. It means accepting the silence of the waiting. It means trusting in the commandment of love, even when the world seems to be falling apart. And in that trust, the church finds its strength. It finds its identity. It finds its reason for being. Maundy Thursday is not just a day in the calendar. It is a mirror in which the church sees itself, and in which it is called to see its Master. It is a day of deep background, of profound history, and of eternal significance. And it is a day that, no matter how the names change or the calendars shift, will always be the day of the new commandment.

The silence of the bells is a heavy thing, but it is a necessary one. It forces the faithful to listen to the quiet voice of the commandment. It forces them to hear the sound of the towel being tied, the water being poured, the feet being washed. It forces them to confront the reality of their own need for love and their own call to love. In a world that is often loud and chaotic, the silence of Maundy Thursday is a gift. It is a space for reflection, for repentance, and for renewal. It is a space where the new commandment can be heard, understood, and embraced. And as the night deepens, and the faithful gather in the church to adore the Blessed Sacrament, they are reminded that they are not alone. They are with the Master, who has gone before them, who has washed their feet, and who has given them a new commandment. They are with the community of the faithful, who are called to love one another as he has loved them. And they are with the future, which is waiting to be born from the silence of this night.

The story of Maundy Thursday is the story of the church itself. It is a story of humility and service, of love and sacrifice, of silence and song. It is a story that has been told for two thousand years, and it is a story that will be told for two thousand years more. It is a story that is as relevant today as it was in the upper room, and as relevant tomorrow as it is today. And it is a story that begins with a commandment: "Love one another as I have loved you." It is a commandment that is simple, yet profound. It is a commandment that is easy to say, yet hard to do. It is a commandment that is the heart of the Christian faith, and the soul of Maundy Thursday. And it is a commandment that is still being lived out, in the quiet acts of service, in the silent prayers, and in the loving hearts of the faithful.
