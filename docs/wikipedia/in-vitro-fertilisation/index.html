In vitro fertilisation

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: In vitro fertilisation

In July 1978, a baby girl named Louise Brown was born at Dr Kershaw's Cottage Hospital in Royton, Oldham, England, marking the first time in human history that a child had been conceived outside the womb. Her arrival was not merely a medical milestone; it was a seismic shift in the definition of life's beginning. Louise was the product of natural-cycle in vitro fertilisation, a procedure performed without the hormonal stimulation that defines modern protocols, yet it proved a fundamental truth: life could be nurtured in a glass vessel before being entrusted to a human body. Her physiologist, Robert G. Edwards, would eventually be awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2010 for this breakthrough, though his colleagues Patrick Steptoe and Jean Purdy were excluded due to the Nobel Committee's strict rule against posthumous awards. The world had changed forever.

The term in vitro itself is a linguistic fossil from a simpler time in the laboratory. Derived from Latin, it literally translates to "in glass," a nod to the early biological experiments where tissues were cultivated outside the living organism in beakers, test tubes, or Petri dishes. This stands in stark contrast to in vivo procedures, where biological processes occur within the living body. In the context of fertility, in vitro refers to the specific act of combining an egg and sperm in a culture medium within a laboratory setting, rather than the natural union that typically occurs within the fallopian tubes. While the colloquial term "test tube baby" persists in the public imagination, evoking images of slender glass cylinders, the reality of the procedure is far more nuanced. The fertilisation actually takes place in Petri dishes—shallow, wide containers that provide a more stable environment for the delicate early stages of embryonic development than the narrow neck of a test tube ever could.

The process is a meticulously choreographed dance of biology and technology. It begins with the monitoring and stimulation of the ovulatory process, a phase where physicians guide the ovaries to produce multiple eggs rather than the single one typically released in a natural cycle. These eggs, or ova, are then retrieved from the ovaries and placed in a culture medium where they meet sperm. Here, the laboratory becomes a proxy for the fallopian tube. After fertilisation occurs, creating a zygote, the embryo is cultured for two to six days. It is during this critical window that the embryo undergoes rapid cell division, transforming from a single cell into a blastocyst. Only then is it transferred via a catheter into the uterus, with the singular, high-stakes intention of establishing a successful pregnancy.

The applications of this technology have expanded far beyond the original scope of treating blocked fallopian tubes. Today, IVF is a cornerstone of assisted reproductive technology (ART), serving as a lifeline for a diverse array of individuals and couples. It enables gestational surrogacy, allowing a woman who cannot carry a pregnancy to have a genetically related child by using her own eggs and a partner's sperm, which are fertilised and then implanted into a surrogate. In these complex arrangements, the resulting child is genetically unrelated to the woman who carries them to term, a reality that has reshaped family structures and legal frameworks globally. Furthermore, when combined with pre-implantation genetic testing, IVF offers a powerful shield against the transmission of severe genetic conditions, allowing parents to select embryos free from specific hereditary diseases before implantation occurs.

The history of IVF is not a singular Western narrative, but a global race of discovery. While Louise Brown's birth in the UK is the most celebrated, the second successful birth of a 'test tube baby' occurred just 70 days later, on October 3, 1978, in India. The child, named Durga, was conceived through a method developed independently by Subhash Mukhopadhyay, a physician and researcher from Hazaribag. Mukhopadhyay's work was a testament to ingenuity under constraint; he performed his experiments using primitive instruments and a household refrigerator, far removed from the high-tech facilities of the West. Tragically, state authorities in India prevented him from presenting his work at scientific conferences, and his monumental contribution was largely ignored for many years, a stark reminder of how geography and politics can obscure scientific truth.

The demographics of IVF have shifted dramatically since those early days. In 2018, it was estimated that eight million children had been born worldwide using these techniques. By 2023, that number had swelled to twelve million. These are not abstract statistics; they represent millions of families who have navigated the complexities of infertility. The procedure has opened doors for women who have reached menopause, those with infertile partners, and individuals suffering from idiopathic female-fertility issues, where no specific cause can be identified. When assisted by egg donation, women who can no longer produce viable eggs can still experience pregnancy and childbirth, challenging the biological clock in ways previously thought impossible. Adriana Iliescu, a Romanian woman, held the record for the oldest woman to give birth using IVF and a donor egg when she delivered a baby at the age of 66 in 2004, a record that was surpassed in 2006.

However, the path to parenthood via IVF is not without its hurdles, and the technology is not a guaranteed solution. Success rates are a complex metric, often misunderstood by those desperate for a child. The "live birth rate" is the gold standard, representing the percentage of IVF cycles that result in a live birth, excluding miscarriages and stillbirths. It is distinct from the "pregnancy rate," which counts confirmed pregnancies regardless of outcome. A 2021 summary compiled by the Society for Assisted Reproductive Technology (SART) in the United States revealed a stark correlation between maternal age and success. Using non-donor eggs, women aged 21 and younger saw success rates of 35.3%, while the rate for those over 48 was effectively zero. The data showed a gradual decline, with 37-year-olds seeing a success rate of 27.4%.

This age barrier has given rise to a new phenomenon: fertility tourism. As the availability of IVF is restricted or banned in certain countries due to ethical or religious regulations, couples find themselves crossing borders to access treatment. Financial cost and age remain the two primary gatekeepers. The procedure is expensive, often requiring multiple cycles to achieve success, and the cost is rarely covered fully by insurance. For women over 41, the likelihood of success with their own eggs diminishes significantly, making donor eggs a more viable, though emotionally and financially complex, alternative. The decision to proceed often involves a grueling calculation of risk, cost, and hope.

The medical community continues to refine the protocols, constantly evaluating which adjuncts truly aid success and which are merely placebo or potential harm. A 2019 study that evaluated the use of ten different adjuncts with IVF—including screening hysteroscopy, DHEA, testosterone, growth hormone, aspirin, heparin, antioxidants, seminal plasma, and platelet-rich plasma (PRP)—offered a sobering conclusion. With the exception of hysteroscopy, the study suggested that these additional treatments should be avoided until there is more robust evidence proving their safety and efficacy. In the world of fertility treatment, where every hope is precious, the temptation to try "everything" is strong, but science demands restraint.

The evolution of IVF has also been marked by the development of Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection (ICSI), a technique that revolutionized the treatment of male infertility. In cases where sperm quality is poor, or where sperm numbers are critically low, the natural process of fertilisation is impossible. ICSI involves the direct injection of a single sperm cell into an egg, bypassing the need for the sperm to penetrate the egg's outer layer on its own. This technique has been found to increase success rates significantly when indicated, turning previously hopeless cases of male infertility into viable paths to parenthood.

Guidelines from major health bodies, such as the UK's National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE), attempt to bring order to the chaos of infertility. They recommend IVF for cases of unexplained infertility after two years of regular, unprotected intercourse without conception. For those with anovulation, where the ovaries do not release eggs, IVF is suggested as an alternative after seven to twelve failed cycles of ovulation induction, a process that can be expensive and difficult to control. These guidelines reflect a balance between the emotional toll of waiting and the medical necessity of intervention.

The success of IVF is also influenced by the reproductive history of the patient. Those who have been previously pregnant are often more successful with IVF treatments than those who have never conceived. This suggests that the body's ability to carry a pregnancy, once proven, remains a positive predictor even in the face of subsequent infertility. Yet, the technology is not without its risks. In an effort to maximize success rates, some clinics may place multiple embryos into the uterus, a practice that increases the chance of multiple births. While twins and triplets may seem like a bonus, they carry significant health risks for both the mother and the children, which is why modern protocols have shifted toward the transfer of fewer embryos. The multiple birth rate has decreased from 25% to 8% as clinics have prioritized the safety of single-embryo transfers, even if it means a slightly lower success rate per cycle.

The societal impact of IVF extends beyond the individual family unit. It has forced a re-evaluation of what constitutes a family, challenging traditional notions of genetic relatedness and parenthood. The ability to use donor eggs, donor sperm, and surrogates has created a mosaic of family structures that defy simple categorization. A child conceived through IVF with donor gametes and carried by a surrogate is the genetic child of two donors but the social child of the intended parents. This complexity has led to a proliferation of legal battles and ethical debates, as laws struggle to keep pace with scientific capability.

The journey of IVF from a fringe experiment to a mainstream medical procedure is a testament to human resilience and ingenuity. It began in a cottage hospital in Oldham with a single egg and a microscope, and it has grown into a global industry touching millions of lives. The story of Louise Brown is no longer just a story of one baby; it is the prologue to a chapter of human history where the boundaries of reproduction are constantly being redrawn. Yet, despite the advancements, the core of the experience remains deeply human. It is a story of hope, of the desperate desire to create life, and of the profound emotional journey that every patient undertakes.

The future of IVF holds promises of even greater precision and accessibility. As genetic testing becomes more sophisticated, the ability to screen for a wider range of conditions will only increase. The integration of artificial intelligence into embryo selection may soon provide even higher success rates, reducing the number of cycles required for a live birth. However, the fundamental ethical questions will persist. How far should we go in selecting the traits of our children? Who has the right to access these technologies, and who is left behind? The answers to these questions will shape the next generation of families just as profoundly as the first successful IVF birth shaped the one before it.

In the end, the story of IVF is a story of glass and glass-like fragility. It is the story of life nurtured in a dish, protected from the harshness of the outside world until it is ready to take its place in the human community. It is a reminder that while science can provide the tools, the drive to create life is a force that transcends technology, rooted in the most primal and enduring of human desires. The twelve million children born through these techniques are living proof that what was once impossible is now routine, and what was once the stuff of science fiction is now a reality for families around the world.

The narrative of IVF is not a straight line of progress, but a winding road marked by triumphs, tragedies, and the relentless pursuit of a better future. From the primitive refrigerator of Subhash Mukhopadhyay to the high-tech laboratories of the twenty-first century, the journey has been one of constant evolution. It is a journey that continues today, with every new birth adding a new chapter to the story. As we look back at the history of IVF, we see not just a medical procedure, but a mirror reflecting our deepest hopes and our most profound challenges. The glass vessel that once seemed a strange and unnatural container for life has become a symbol of possibility, a testament to the enduring power of human ingenuity to overcome the barriers of biology.

The financial and emotional toll of IVF cannot be overstated. For many, it is a journey that drains savings, strains relationships, and tests the limits of endurance. Yet, the success rate, while variable, offers a beacon of hope. The fact that some couples are able to conceive naturally after undergoing IVF treatment suggests that the procedure may sometimes reset the biological clock or provide a psychological boost that facilitates natural conception. This phenomenon, though not fully understood, adds another layer of complexity to the medical narrative. It suggests that the mind and body are inextricably linked in the process of reproduction, and that the act of seeking help can sometimes be the catalyst for the solution.

As we move forward, the focus must remain on the safety and efficacy of the procedures. The 2019 study on adjuncts serves as a cautionary tale against the allure of "more is better." In a field where desperation can lead to poor decision-making, the role of the medical professional is to guide patients with evidence-based practices, not to sell hope through unproven methods. The integrity of the field depends on this commitment to science, even in the face of intense emotional pressure.

The story of IVF is far from over. With each passing year, the techniques become more refined, the success rates higher, and the access wider. The legacy of Robert Edwards, Patrick Steptoe, Jean Purdy, and Subhash Mukhopadhyay lives on in every child born through this technology. They showed us that life could be nurtured in the laboratory, that the boundaries of nature could be expanded, and that the desire for children is a force strong enough to drive the most ambitious scientific endeavors. As we look to the future, the challenge will be to ensure that this miracle of medicine remains accessible, ethical, and focused on the well-being of the child and the family. The glass vessel has become a home for millions, and its story is one of the greatest achievements of modern medicine.
