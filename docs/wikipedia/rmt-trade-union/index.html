RMT (trade union)

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: RMT (trade union)

In the smog-choked dawn of 1990, two distinct legacies of British labor history collided to form a singular, formidable force: the National Union of Railwaymen and the National Union of Seamen. The National Union of Railwaymen (NUR) had spent decades fighting for the men who kept the iron arteries of the empire pulsing, while the National Union of Seamen (NUS) represented the sailors who bridged the gap between island and continent. When they merged, they did not merely combine membership rolls; they forged the National Union of Rail, Maritime and Transport Workers, universally known as the RMT. This was not a bureaucratic consolidation but a strategic unification of the transport sector's most vital workers, creating an entity that would soon become one of Britain's fastest-growing and most politically volatile trade unions.

To understand the RMT is to understand the shifting tectonic plates of British politics over the last thirty-five years. From its inception, the union was steeped in the radical history of the labor movement. Its predecessor unions were founding members of the original Labour Representation Committee, the very bedrock upon which the modern Labour Party was built. Consequently, the RMT did not just join the political fray; it became a defining voice on the left, often pulling the party's center of gravity toward more militant socialist policies. For decades, the RMT was the conscience of the transport sector, a guardian of pensions, safety, and the very dignity of the working class in an industry increasingly viewed through the lens of shareholder value rather than public service.

The trajectory of the union changed dramatically under the leadership of Bob Crow. Taking the helm in a period of intense privatization and austerity, Crow transformed the RMT from a defensive organization into an aggressive advocate for its members. Under his stewardship, membership surged. In 2002, the union counted 57,000 members. By 2019, that number had swelled to almost 81,500. This was not merely growth; it was a resurrection. Crow's leadership resonated with workers who felt abandoned by the establishment, turning the RMT into a beacon for those resisting the erosion of workers' rights. Although Crow passed away in 2016, his legacy of confrontation and solidarity remains etched into the union's DNA, influencing its current leadership under General Secretary Eddie Dempsey and President George Welch.

The Great Schism with New Labour

The relationship between the RMT and the Labour Party has never been a smooth romance; it has been a marriage of convenience marked by bitter divorces and reluctant reconciliations. The most dramatic rupture occurred in the early 2000s, a period when the Labour Party, under Tony Blair, had pivoted sharply toward the center with its "New Labour" agenda. The RMT, however, remained committed to traditional socialist principles, particularly the renationalization of the railways, a policy Blair had abandoned.

The tension came to a head in 2003. A group of Scottish branches within the RMT, driven by frustration over the party's direction, voted to donate union funds to the Scottish Socialist Party (SSP). This was a direct protest against Blair's policies, signaling that the union was willing to financially support political alternatives that aligned more closely with its core values. The reaction from the Labour Party headquarters was swift and severe. In early 2004, citing breaches of party rules regarding financial independence and political alignment, the Labour Party expelled the RMT. It was a seismic event in British political history: a major union, historically linked to the party's founding, cast out for refusing to toe the new centrist line.

This expulsion did not silence the RMT; it liberated it. Freed from the constraints of party discipline, the union doubled down on its political activism. In 2009, the RMT announced it would stand a slate of candidates in the European Parliament elections under the banner of "No to EU – Yes to Democracy." This was not a standard election run but a broad left-wing alter-globalization coalition designed to offer a stark alternative to the pro-business policies of the UK Independence Party and the establishment parties. The message was clear: the RMT would not compromise its vision of a democratic, socialist Europe for the sake of electoral convenience.

The union's political evolution continued with the formation of the Trade Unionist and Socialist Coalition (TUSC). As a founding member of this left-wing political party, the RMT ensured that its voice was heard in the general elections of 2010 and 2015. TUSC became the vehicle for the union's dissent, contesting seats and challenging the duopoly of the Conservative and Labour parties with a platform rooted in anti-austerity and workers' rights. Even in 2022, when the RMT finally disaffiliated from TUSC, its history of political independence remained a defining characteristic, distinguishing it from the more compliant unions of the era.

The Brexit Conundrum

The 2016 referendum on the United Kingdom's withdrawal from the European Union presented the RMT with a complex ideological puzzle. Officially, the union supported the UK's withdrawal, a stance that seemed to align with the broader Brexit movement. However, the RMT's support was deeply nuanced, rooted in a specific critique of the European Union as a pro-business entity that prioritized free markets over workers' protections. The union feared that remaining in the EU meant accepting a framework that undermined public ownership and facilitated austerity.

Yet, the official Leave campaigns were dominated by voices the RMT deeply distrusted. In a striking display of internal dissent and principled independence, RMT members Sean Hoyle, Peter Pinkney, Paul McDonnell, and John Reid signed a letter in The Guardian, voicing their opposition to the Electoral Commission's decision to designate Leave.EU, Vote Leave, or Grassroots Out as the official groups advocating for Brexit. Their argument was a masterclass in socialist critique of the right-wing Brexit movement.

"We call on the commission not to give taxpayers' money to the Tory and Ukip-dominated Vote Leave, Leave.EU or Grassroots Out campaigns, or any amalgam of them ... We believe there are millions of trade unionists, young people, anti-austerity campaigners and working-class voters, whose opposition to the big business-dominated EU would not be represented by these organisations. ... We call on the Electoral Commission to recognise that a significant proportion of those who will vote against the EU do so because they support basic socialist policies of workers' rights, public ownership, and opposition to austerity and racism."

This letter captured the essence of the RMT's position: they were leaving the EU not to join a nationalist, right-wing project, but to reclaim the possibility of a socialist economy free from the shackles of European free-trade directives. It was a reminder that for the RMT, political alliances were always secondary to the principles of class struggle and workers' rights.

The Battle for the Underground

While the RMT was fighting political battles on the national stage, it was simultaneously engaged in a relentless war on the tracks of London. The union represents the vast majority of London Underground staff, as well as countless workers across the broader public transport network. This position placed it at the epicenter of some of the most contentious industrial disputes in the capital's history. The adversaries were not just the government but the privatized entities that had been handed the keys to the kingdom: Transport for London (TfL), Metronet, and Tube Lines.

The disputes were never about trivialities. They were fought over the fundamental pillars of the working life: pay, safety, pensions, and job security. For the RMT, the privatization of the Underground was a betrayal of the public trust, a system designed to extract profit at the expense of the workforce and the passengers. The union's resistance often manifested in industrial action, leading to periods of travel disruption that rattled the capital. These strikes were not mere inconveniences; they were strategic interventions designed to halt the machinery of privatization and force a renegotiation of the social contract.

The tension reached a fever pitch in the years leading up to the pandemic. The union's confrontations with management became a regular feature of London life, with the RMT frequently accused of being an obstacle to modernization while management was accused of eroding safety standards. The RMT argued that the relentless drive for efficiency was a code for cutting corners on safety and degrading the conditions of the men and women who kept the trains running. The result was a decade of friction, a testament to the union's refusal to let the London Underground become a purely commercial enterprise.

The Modern Era of Disruption

The landscape of industrial relations shifted dramatically in the wake of the global pandemic. As the world locked down, the railways fell silent, and passenger numbers plummeted. When the trains finally began to run again, the sector was in a state of flux. The government and rail companies, facing a financial crisis, emphasized the urgent need to modernize the sector and drastically reduce its cost base. The narrative from the top was clear: fewer passengers meant a smaller, leaner railway.

For the RMT, this narrative was a direct threat to the livelihood of its members. In July 2015, the union had endorsed Jeremy Corbyn's campaign in the Labour Party leadership election, signaling its continued alignment with the left wing of the party. But by the time the post-pandemic reality set in, the focus had returned to the tracks. In May 2022, the union began balloting 40,000 members on strike action, a move that might be joined by the Transport Salaried Staffs' Association (TSSA). The stakes were incredibly high. Both managers and operators would be affected, and the potential for disruption was immense.

Rail insiders accused the union leaders of balloting prematurely, claiming that the union had not exhausted all avenues of negotiation. However, the union's concerns were palpable and immediate. The primary drivers of the dispute were the looming job cuts at Network Rail and a desperate demand for pay rises to offset the soaring cost of living. As inflation ate into wages, the workers found themselves unable to make ends meet, while the rail companies sought to cut costs to balance their books.

The balloting results were unequivocal. Union members backed industrial action overwhelmingly. The RMT and TSSA scheduled 24-hour strikes for June 21, 23, and 25, warning of a level of disruption not experienced since the general strike of 1926. The threat was not empty. Rail companies, anticipating the chaos, began drawing up emergency plans to prioritize freight delivery over passenger services. The logic was stark: keep the supermarkets stocked, even if it meant leaving millions of commuters stranded. This prioritization highlighted the tension between the economic necessity of the supply chain and the social necessity of public transport.

A Network of Solidarity

Beyond the picket lines and the ballot boxes, the RMT operates as a vast network of solidarity, affiliated with a multitude of political and trade union confederations. In the United Kingdom and Ireland, the union is a cornerstone of the Trades Union Congress (TUC), the Scottish Trades Union Congress, the Wales Trades Union Congress, and the Irish Congress of Trade Unions. These affiliations provide a platform for collective bargaining and political advocacy on a national scale.

Internationally, the RMT's reach is equally extensive. It is affiliated with the European Transport Workers' Federation, the International Transport Workers' Federation, and the World Federation of Trade Unions. These connections allow the RMT to coordinate with workers across borders, recognizing that in a globalized economy, the struggles of transport workers are shared. Whether it is fighting against the deregulation of the railway sector in Europe or supporting maritime workers in the Americas, the RMT sees itself as part of a global movement.

Politically, the union's affiliations have been a source of both strength and controversy. While it disaffiliated from TUSC in 2022, it remains affiliated with the Labour Representation Committee, a pressure group dedicated to promoting traditional socialist principles within the Labour Party. This dual approach allows the RMT to maintain a presence within the Labour Party while retaining the freedom to critique it when necessary. It is a delicate balancing act, one that has defined the union's relationship with the political establishment for decades.

The Weight of History

The RMT is not just a union of the present; it is the custodian of a deep and often tragic history. The union has provided 23,000 trade union cases to the Railway Work, Life and Death Project. This data is not merely a statistical archive; it is a memorial to the thousands of members of the Amalgamated Society of Railway Servants and the National Union of Railwaymen who suffered accidents and disabilities during the late 19th and early 20th centuries. It is a record of the human cost of industrialization, a reminder that every safety regulation and every pension benefit was won through struggle and sacrifice.

This historical awareness informs the union's modern approach to safety. The RMT does not view safety as a bureaucratic checkbox but as a moral imperative. The stories of those who came before, the men and women who were injured or killed on the job, serve as a constant warning against complacency. In an era of cost-cutting and privatization, this historical perspective is a powerful tool, grounding the union's demands in the lived experience of generations of workers.

The Financial Backbone

To support its members in times of need, the RMT established the RMT Credit Union Limited in 2004. This savings and loans co-operative is more than a financial institution; it is an expression of the union's values. It provides a safe haven for members' savings and offers loans at fair rates, free from the predatory practices of commercial banks. The credit union is a member of UKCreditUnions Limited, a national trade association that represents smaller credit unions and support organizations.

The RMT Credit Union is authorized by the Prudential Regulation Authority and regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority and the PRA. Like the banks and building societies, members' savings are protected against business failure by the Financial Services Compensation Scheme. This ensures that the financial security of the members is not compromised by the volatility of the market. The credit union is a tangible example of the union's commitment to the economic well-being of its members, providing a safety net that extends beyond the picket line.

The Road Ahead

As the RMT looks to the future, it faces a transport sector in flux. The pandemic has changed the way people travel, with fewer passengers using the railways and a greater emphasis on digital connectivity. The government and rail companies are pushing for modernization, often at the expense of traditional roles and job security. The RMT must navigate this new landscape, balancing the need for innovation with the protection of its members.

The union's history of political activism, its willingness to confront the establishment, and its deep commitment to socialist principles will continue to shape its strategy. Whether it is fighting against job cuts, demanding fair pay, or advocating for the renationalization of the railways, the RMT remains a formidable force. It is a union that understands the power of collective action and the importance of standing together in the face of adversity.

From the merger of the NUR and NUS in 1990 to the strikes of 2022, the RMT has been a constant in the British labor movement. It has weathered political storms, survived expulsions, and emerged stronger. As the transport sector continues to evolve, the RMT will undoubtedly remain at the forefront, defending the rights of its members and fighting for a fairer, more equitable future. The story of the RMT is not just the story of a trade union; it is the story of the working class in Britain, a story of struggle, resilience, and hope.
