Solemn assembly

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Solemn assembly

On April 4, 2026, the air inside the Conference Center in Salt Lake City held a stillness that felt less like silence and more like a held breath. The gathering was not a standard quarterly meeting, but a solemn assembly, a distinct and sacred procedure within The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints designed to elevate the purpose of the occasion above the mundane. In that room, under the direction of D. Todd Christofferson, the second counselor in the First Presidency, the church formally sustained Dallin H. Oaks as its eighteenth president. The ritual was a continuation of a lineage stretching back nearly two centuries, a direct line of spiritual authority connecting the modern congregation to the earliest days of the faith. The business of the assembly extended beyond the presidency; it included the sustaining of newly called apostles Gérald Caussé and Clark G. Gilbert, marking a pivotal moment of transition and continuity for a global religious organization.

To the uninitiated observer, the term "solemn assembly" might sound like a generic description of a serious meeting. In the context of the LDS Church, however, it is a technical term with specific theological weight and a rigid historical structure. It is a formal, sacred procedure conducted to give added emphasis to the purpose of the occasion. These assemblies are not held for routine business. They are reserved for three primary purposes: the dedications of temples, specially-called meetings to provide instruction to church leaders, and the sustaining of a new church president. The latter is the most frequent occurrence in the modern era, taking place at the first general conference following the death of a president. It is here that the church members, who regard their president as a prophet, seer, and revelator, formally act to sustain him, adhering to the law of common consent.

The roots of this practice are deeply embedded in the church's founding narrative. In 1831, only a year after Joseph Smith established the Church of Christ, members believe he received a revelation instructing him to "call your solemn assembly, that your fastings and your mourning might come up into the ears of the Lord of Sabaoth." This directive was not seen as a novel invention but as a restoration of ancient biblical practices. Members understand the modern solemn assembly as a direct continuation of the gatherings held in ancient Israel on special occasions of major religious importance. The logic is one of spiritual gravity; just as the ancient Israelites assembled for covenant renewal or national crisis, the modern church assembles to bind itself to divine authority and human leadership in a moment of profound covenant-making.

The first solemn assembly connected to a temple dedication in the modern era occurred on March 30, 1836, during the dedication of the Kirtland Temple, the first temple built in the Latter Day Saint movement. The preparation for this event was meticulous. Prior to the dedication, Joseph Smith taught the church members with a sense of urgent necessity: "We must have all things prepared, and call our solemn assembly as the Lord has commanded us, that we may be able to accomplish His great work, and it must be done in God's own way. The House of the Lord must be prepared, and the solemn assembly called and organized in it, according to the order of the House of God." This instruction set the template for all future dedications. The Kirtland event introduced many elements that remain integral to temple dedications today, including the Hosanna Shout and the singing of "The Spirit of God Like a Fire Is Burning," a hymn written by W. W. Phelps that captures the fervor of the occasion. Since that day in Kirtland, solemn assemblies have been held in connection with the dedications of every LDS Church temple, creating a consistent liturgical rhythm across the movement's history.

When the assembly shifts from temple dedication to the sustaining of a president, the focus moves from the sanctity of the building to the sanctity of the covenant between the people and their leaders. The procedure is a carefully choreographed sequence of voting by distinct groups. Historically, the order of the sustaining groups has followed a strict hierarchy: the First Presidency, the Quorum of the Twelve, the Quorums of Seventy and Presiding Bishopric, the remaining Melchizedek priesthood holders, Aaronic priesthood holders, and finally, all church members together. This order reflects the ecclesiastical structure of the church, moving from the highest authority down to the individual member.

The evolution of this voting order in recent decades highlights the changing dynamics of church membership and leadership. In more recent solemn assemblies, female church members aged 18 and older who constitute the Relief Society, and female church members aged 12 to 18 who constitute the Young Women organization, have been asked to stand and vote as distinct groups. This marks a significant shift from the traditional male-dominated voting structure of the priesthood quorums. The April 2018 Solemn Assembly, held to sustain Russell M. Nelson, saw a slight modification to the order. The sustaining by Melchizedek Priesthood holders was followed by the Relief Society, then the Aaronic Priesthood, the Young Women, and the church at large. This adjustment ensured that the voices of the women's organizations were heard with distinct clarity before the final, collective vote of the entire congregation.

The culmination of the procedure is the final vote. After the First Presidency and the various groups have voted in turn, all members of the church together, including those who have already voted in their specific quorums, are asked to stand wherever they may be at the time. They are called to vote in a single, unified gesture to sustain, or oppose, the new president, along with his counselors and the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles. This final moment is the essence of the law of common consent. It is not merely a rubber stamp; it is a public, collective affirmation of the new leadership. The procedure is observed with equal gravity at satellite locations around the world. General authorities watch from gatherings on Temple Square, such as the Salt Lake Tabernacle and the Assembly Hall, while members of stake presidencies oversee the voting at local meetinghouses globally. In these local settings, observers are explicitly asked to invite anyone who opposes the sustaining to meet with their stake president, ensuring that dissent is addressed personally and pastorally rather than suppressed.

The spiritual significance of this ritual was articulated with clarity by N. Eldon Tanner during the solemn assembly held on April 6, 1974, when Spencer W. Kimball was sustained as the church's twelfth president. Tanner described the occasion as a "sacred, sober, and reverent occasion when the saints assemble under the direction of the First Presidency." He emphasized that the act of sustaining the president is not a passive acknowledgment but an active covenant. "When we sustain the president of the church by our uplifted hand," Tanner explained, "it not only signifies that we acknowledge before God that he is the rightful possessor of all the priesthood keys; it also means that we covenant with God that we will abide by the direction and the counsel that come through His prophet. It is a solemn covenant." This covenant is the theological anchor of the assembly; it transforms a political or administrative succession into a spiritual agreement between the congregation and the divine.

The history of these sustainings is a timeline of the church's growth and resilience. Joseph Smith and his counselors in the original First Presidency were sustained in a solemn assembly in the Kirtland Temple on March 27, 1836. Brigham Young followed, sustained in a solemn assembly on December 27, 1847, in the Kanesville Tabernacle in Council Bluffs, Iowa. The first solemn assembly sustaining to take place in the Salt Lake Tabernacle occurred on October 10, 1880, when John Taylor was sustained as the church's third president. For the next century, the Salt Lake Tabernacle served as the stage for the sustaining of twelve presidents of the church, a physical testament to the church's establishment in Utah.

However, the timeline is not without interruptions, reflecting the broader historical forces that have touched the church. The solemn assembly sustaining for Heber J. Grant, the seventh church president, was postponed to June 1, 1919, because of the worldwide flu pandemic in 1918-1919. This delay underscores that even sacred procedures must yield to the realities of human health and safety. The pandemic, which claimed millions of lives globally, forced the church to pause its traditional rituals, demonstrating the adaptability of the solemn assembly to the circumstances of the times.

The venue for these ceremonies also shifted in the late twentieth century. Gordon B. Hinckley, the church's fifteenth president, was the last to be sustained in a solemn assembly in the Salt Lake Tabernacle on April 2, 1995. The Tabernacle, with its historic significance, had served the church for over a century. Subsequent solemn assembly sustainings have taken place in the Conference Center near Temple Square in Salt Lake City, a larger and more modern facility capable of accommodating the growing global membership. This transition from the historic Tabernacle to the modern Conference Center mirrors the church's expansion from a regional movement to a global institution.

The business of the solemn assembly has also evolved in its scope. Until the spring general conference of 1973, solemn assemblies included a vote for the sustaining of the Patriarch to the Church, formerly known as the Presiding Patriarch, an office that was abolished in 1979. Local seventies were explicitly included as part of the Melchizedek Priesthood voting group as well, until the 1986 dissolution of local quorums of seventy at the stake level. These administrative changes altered the composition of the voting groups, streamlining the process as the church's organizational structure became more centralized. Until these changes, the entire procedure often lasted a half hour, given that the voting had been done separately for each of the positions being called upon. The modern assembly, while still lengthy, reflects a more consolidated approach to leadership succession.

The list of solemn assemblies to sustain presidents of the LDS Church serves as a chronicle of the faith's leadership. Beginning with Joseph Smith in 1836 at the Kirtland Temple, the list includes Brigham Young in 1847 at the Kanesville Tabernacle, and John Taylor in 1880 at the Salt Lake Tabernacle. It continues through the generations: Wilford Woodruff in 1889, Lorenzo Snow in 1898, Joseph F. Smith in 1901, Heber J. Grant in 1919, George Albert Smith in 1945, David O. McKay in 1951, Joseph Fielding Smith in 1970, Harold B. Lee in 1972, Spencer W. Kimball in 1974, Ezra Taft Benson in 1986, and Howard W. Hunter in 1994. Each of these dates marks a moment of profound spiritual and organizational significance, a point where the church collectively reaffirmed its commitment to its chosen leader.

The solemn assembly is more than a procedural necessity; it is a ritual of unity. In a world often fractured by division, the act of a global congregation rising together to sustain a single leader is a powerful statement of shared purpose. The raising of the hand is a physical manifestation of a spiritual agreement. It is a moment where the individual merges with the collective, where the personal conviction of the member aligns with the institutional authority of the church. The solemnity of the occasion ensures that this is not taken lightly. The silence, the music, the specific order of voting, and the final collective stand all serve to heighten the awareness of the sacred nature of the covenant being made.

As the church moves forward, the solemn assembly remains a fixed point in its calendar, a recurring reminder of its origins and its beliefs. The most recent assembly in April 2026, sustaining Dallin H. Oaks, continues this unbroken chain. The presence of new apostles and the changing demographics of the voting groups ensure that the ritual remains dynamic, adapting to the needs of a modern, diverse membership while retaining its ancient roots. The solemn assembly is a bridge between the past and the future, connecting the Kirtland Temple of 1836 to the Conference Center of 2026, binding the faithful in a shared covenant that transcends time and geography.

The gravity of the occasion is not lost on the participants. When N. Eldon Tanner spoke of the "solemn covenant," he was describing a bond that goes beyond mere organizational loyalty. It is a spiritual commitment that carries with it the weight of eternal consequences. In the context of the assembly, the church members are not just voting for a leader; they are affirming their own identity as a people bound by divine law. The ritual serves as a touchstone, a moment of reflection and recommitment that occurs every time the presidency changes.

The evolution of the solemn assembly from the Kirtland Temple to the modern era reflects the journey of the church itself. From the small, intimate gatherings of the early saints to the massive, global events of today, the core purpose remains unchanged: to call the faithful together to acknowledge their leader and renew their covenant with God. The specific details—the order of voting, the inclusion of women's organizations, the shift in venues—may change, but the underlying spirit of the solemn assembly endures. It is a testament to the enduring power of ritual to shape community, to reinforce belief, and to provide a sense of continuity in a changing world.

In the end, the solemn assembly is a story of faith in action. It is the story of a people who, despite the challenges of history, from the flu pandemic to the expansion of their ranks, have consistently gathered to affirm their shared destiny. The raising of the hand is a simple gesture, but in the context of the solemn assembly, it becomes a profound declaration of trust and unity. As the church continues to grow and evolve, the solemn assembly will remain a central pillar of its life, a sacred space where the past, present, and future converge in a moment of shared reverence.
