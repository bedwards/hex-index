Answer song

8 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Answer song

Big Mama Thornton’s raw, swaggering 1953 rendition of Hound Dog didn’t just top the charts—it detonated a cultural landmine. Within months, six answer songs rushed into the breach, most notably Rufus Thomas’s Bear Cat, which soared to number three. Thornton’s record label sued Sun Records for copyright infringement and won $35,000—a sum so devastating it allegedly pushed Sam Phillips to sell Elvis Presley’s contract to RCA. This wasn’t mere competition. It was musical warfare, and the answer song was its sharpest weapon.

An answer song—sometimes called a response record—isn’t a cover. It’s a direct rebuttal, a lyrical counterpunch fired across the radio waves at another artist’s hit. Think of it as the 20th century’s original viral clapback, where melody became ammunition. While today’s Twitter feuds play out in 280 characters, mid-century America settled scores in three-minute vinyl spins. And the stakes? Career-making, lawsuit-spawning, industry-altering.

The form exploded in the blues and R&B underground of the 1930s–1950s, where Black artists traded barbs with wit and grit. Thornton’s Hound Dog was the prime target, but the blueprint was older. Back in 1902, Arthur Collins recorded I Wonder Why Bill Bailey Don’t Come Home as a direct retort to his own earlier hit Bill Bailey, Won’t You Please Come Home. By 1909, Collins’s fellow vaudeville star Billy Murray doubled down, answering his 1908 smash I’m Afraid to Come Home in the Dark with I Used to Be Afraid to Come Home in the Dark—proving that even your own song could become your adversary.

Then came the golden age of country answer songs, where gender dynamics turned the dial. Male artists dominated the airwaves, but women fired back with precision. In 1952, Hank Thompson’s The Wild Side of Life lamented a woman chasing “the wild side of life,” painting her as a cautionary tale. Kitty Wells, dubbed the “Queen of Country Music,” didn’t just respond—she rewrote the narrative. Her It Wasn’t God Who Made Honky Tonk Angels flipped the blame onto unfaithful men: "It’s a shame that all for you men, we must pay / But you’ll find it’s true, honky tonk angels have no place to go." The song became the first No. 1 country hit by a solo female artist, shattering industry sexism while outselling Thompson’s original. Wells proved answer songs could be Trojan horses for revolution.

The Calculus of a Clapback

Why did these replies work? Jim Curtis, in Rock Eras: Interpretations of Music and Society, nailed it: "Answer songs rode on the coattails of the popularity of the first song... their success depended on a knowledge of the original." They exploited a listener’s instant recognition, turning familiarity into free marketing. Most were one-hit wonders by unknowns—Rufus Thomas aside, few answer artists survived beyond their retort. The performer didn’t matter; the dialogue did. This dissociation made the format uniquely powerful. As Curtis observed, "only the song, and not the performer, mattered."

Consider the absurd spectacle of 1923. When Yes! We Have No Bananas—a nonsense novelty hit—dominated the airwaves, Lew Brown, Robert King, and James F. Hanley fired back with I’ve Got The Yes! We Have No Banana Blues, mocking its gibberish lyrics. Stars like Eddie Cantor and Belle Baker queued to record it. The song’s entire purpose was to trade on the original’s ubiquity, then vanish like a punchline. This wasn’t art for art’s sake; it was calculated cultural parasitism.

The Battle of the Ballads

Long before rap beefs, poets dueled with quills. In 1599, Christopher Marlowe penned The Passionate Shepherd to His Love, a seductive fantasy of eternal pastoral bliss: "Come live with me and be my love... / And we will all the pleasures prove." Sir Walter Raleigh, ever the skeptic, shot back in 1600 with The Nymph’s Reply to the Shepherd: "The flowers do fade, and wanton fields / To wayward winter reckoning yields... / Thy gowns, thy shoes, thy beds of roses, / Thy cap, thy kirtle, and thy posies / Soon break, soon wither, soon forgotten— / In folly ripe, in reason rotten." This 400-year-old poetic tango—idealism versus realism—laid the template for every answer song after.

Music absorbed this tradition. Thomas Westendorf’s 1875 ballad I’ll Take You Home Again, Kathleen directly countered George W. Persley’s Barney, Take Me Home Again, replacing Irish longing with American promise. Woody Guthrie’s 1940 anthem This Land Is Your Land—originally titled God Blessed America for Me—was a socialist riposte to Irving Berlin’s patriotic 1938 hit God Bless America. Guthrie’s verses painted breadlines and private property signs where Berlin saw “alabaster cities.” One celebrated divine providence; the other demanded economic justice. Both defined American identity—one from the pulpit, the other from the Dust Bowl.

The Answer Song Industrial Complex

By the 1950s, the format had become a hit factory. Producers actively commissioned retorts, knowing radio would play both. When Rosemary Clooney’s Come on-a My House (1951) invited listeners into her bungalow, comedian Robert Q. Lewis fired back with Where’s-a Your House, mocking her invitation: "Where’s-a your house? / Is it on the hill? / Or way down in the valley?" It was cheeky, cheap, and charted.

Country’s gender wars intensified. Patsy Montana’s 1935 I Want to Be a Cowboy’s Sweetheart—the first million-selling hit by a female country artist—answered Stuart Hamblen’s Texas Plains by inserting a woman into the cowboy fantasy: "Ridin’ down the canyon, whoop-ee-ti-yi-yo! / I’m the roughest, toughest hombre that you ever saw." Decades later, Jeanne Black’s He’ll Have to Stay (1960) countered Jim Reeves’ He’ll Have to Go (1959), where a man demands his date silence another suitor. Black flipped the script: "If he wants to stay and talk to me, / Well, I guess he’ll have to stay." Skeeter Davis piled on with I’m Glad That You’re Falling—a triumphant middle finger to Hank Locklin’s pleading Please Help Me, I’m Falling.

But the most astonishing cycle began in 1954. Hank Ballard and The Midnighters’ R&B hit Work with Me, Annie described a seductive encounter. They followed it with Annie Had a Baby. Then the answer song avalanche hit: Annie’s Answer (The El Dorados), Annie Pulled a Humbug (The Midnights), Wallflower (Roll With Me Henry) (Etta James), and I’m the Father of Annie’s Baby (Danny Taylor). The Midnighters even recorded an answer to the answer: Henry’s Got Flat Feet (Can’t Dance No More). This six-song saga, all within 18 months, turned one suggestive tune into a cultural epic. It was musical improv at breakneck speed—imagine Game of Thrones rewritten as a doo-wop opera.

From Honky Tonk to Hip Hop

Rock ’n’ roll’s arrival didn’t kill answer songs—it mutated them. Carole King and Neil Sedaka, high school friends turned rivals, traded romantic jabs: Sedaka’s Oh! Carol (1959) inspired King’s Oh Neil!. When Annette Funicello chirped Tall Paul (1959), King fired back with Short Mort: "You can keep Tall Paul, I’ll take Short Mort." Even teen idols played the game.

But the true heir to the answer song throne emerged in the 1980s Bronx hip hop scene. The Roxanne Wars began when U.T.F.O.’s Roxanne, Roxanne (1984) dissed a girl who rejected them. Roxanne Shanté, just 14, recorded Roxanne’s Revenge in one take, dismantling U.T.F.O. with lethal wit: "You’re not man enough to handle me, U.T.F.O., / ’Cause I’m the queen of rap, and you’re just a zero." Over 100 answer tracks followed—by artists like Elease Jack, Sparky D, and UTFO’s own rebuttals—turning a local beef into a national phenomenon. This wasn’t just mimicry; it was lyrical combat where authenticity was armor.

Similarly, The Bridge Wars (1985–1989) saw Boogie Down Productions (Bronx) and MC Shan (Queensbridge) battle for borough supremacy. Shan’s The Bridge Is Over claimed Queensbridge invented hip hop; KRS-One’s South Bronx fired back: "You’re living in the past, I’m living in the present / South Bronx represent!" The feud birthed entire subgenres and defined rap’s territorial ethos. Hip hop didn’t just adopt answer songs—it elevated them to ideological warfare.

Why the Answer Song Endures

Today, the form thrives online. When Cardi B dropped Money (2018), critics called it tone-deaf during economic hardship. Young M.A.’s Big (2019) countered: "I got money, I got power / I got bitches, I got hours." It was a direct, uncredited rebuttal—proving the template still fits.

The answer song’s genius lies in its economy. It hijacks cultural real estate, demands immediate context, and turns passive listening into active debate. In an age of algorithmic isolation, it forces dialogue. When Kitty Wells sang Honky Tonk Angels, she didn’t just sell records—she told millions of women they weren’t the problem. When Roxanne Shanté spat her rhymes, she proved girls could dominate a boys’ club. These weren’t novelty tracks. They were cultural interventions disguised as pop.

The practice stretches from Marlowe’s shepherd to Drake’s diss tracks—a 400-year conversation about power, perspective, and who gets to speak next. Every answer song carries a silent question: Now what? That tension—the unresolved chord hanging in the air—is why we still lean in to listen.

The next time you hear a song that feels like a reply, check the liner notes. You might be witnessing history in the making.
