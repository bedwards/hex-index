Crow people

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Crow people

The Children of the Large-Beaked Bird

In 1805, a Crow camp welcomed a fur trader named François-Antoine Larocque to travel with them across the Northern Plains. They crossed the Little Missouri River, threaded through the Bighorn River valley, and eventually reached a point west of where Billings, Montana stands today. The next year, some Crow discovered a group of white men with horses camped along the Yellowstone River. By morning, those horses had vanished—taken silently in the night by people who had mastered the art of moving unseen across the vast grasslands they called home.

The Lewis and Clark Expedition never actually saw the Crow. They only knew them by their missing horses.

This is the story of the Apsáalooke—a name pronounced roughly "ap-SAH-ro-kay"—the people known to English speakers as the Crow. Their autonym means "children of the large-beaked bird," a name given to them by the Hidatsa, a neighboring tribe who spoke a related language. French interpreters translated this as gens du corbeau, "people of the crow," and the English name stuck. But the original bird wasn't necessarily a crow at all. Many Apsáalooke today believe the name refers to the mythical Thunderbird—a creature of immense spiritual power in the cosmologies of many Plains tribes.

A Journey Across Half a Continent

The Crow did not originate in Montana. Their ancestors lived in the Ohio Country, south of Lake Erie, where they were part of the Eastern Woodland peoples—settled communities who grew crops and lived in established villages. Sometime in the twelfth or thirteenth century, they migrated west, passing through what is now Illinois and Minnesota before settling south of Lake Winnipeg in present-day Manitoba, Canada.

For centuries they maintained their agricultural lifestyle there, growing corn and hunting bison from permanent villages. But pressure from the Ojibwe and Cree peoples—who had earlier and better access to European guns through the fur trade—pushed them further west to the Upper Missouri River.

This is a pattern worth understanding. The arrival of Europeans on the Atlantic coast set off a chain reaction of displacement that rippled westward for centuries. Tribes with access to guns pushed on tribes without them. Tribes who lost their territory pushed on others further west. The Crow were caught in this cascade of displacement, and they in turn would displace others.

Around the late seventeenth century, the Crow split from the Hidatsa. According to oral tradition, the separation happened because of a dispute over a bison stomach—which might sound trivial but likely represented deeper tensions between bands. The Hidatsa still call the Crow by a name that translates to "Those Who Pout Over Tripe."

A leader named No Intestines had received a vision and led his band on a long migratory search for sacred tobacco. They eventually settled in southeastern Montana, in the valley of the Yellowstone River and its tributaries.

Lords of the Yellowstone

The territory the Crow claimed was vast and magnificent. Their lands stretched from what is now Yellowstone National Park in the west to the mouth of the Yellowstone River where it joins the Missouri in the east—a distance of nearly five hundred miles. They called the Yellowstone "E-chee-dick-karsh-ah-shay," which means "Elk River."

To the north, their territory extended to the Musselshell River. To the south, it was bounded by the Wind River Range in present-day Wyoming. Within this territory lay some of the most dramatic landscapes in North America: the Bighorn Mountains, the Absaroka Range, the Pryor Mountains, and river valleys rich with game—the Judith, the Powder, the Tongue, the Big Horn, and the Wind.

But claiming this land meant fighting for it. The Crow drove westward bands of Shoshone people, including a group called the Bikkaashe, or "People of the Grass Lodges." They allied with Kiowa and Plains Apache bands who were passing through the region. When those allies later migrated southward toward Texas and Oklahoma, the Crow remained, dominant in their established territory through the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries.

Once established in the Yellowstone valley, the Crow divided into four distinct groups. The Mountain Crow lived in the Rocky Mountain foothills along the upper Yellowstone, near the present Wyoming-Montana border. The River Crow inhabited the lower elevations along the Yellowstone and Musselshell rivers. A group called the Kicked in the Bellies claimed the Bighorn Basin between the Bighorn Mountains and the Absaroka Range. And a fourth group, the Bilapiluutche—"Beaver Dries its Fur"—may have merged with the Kiowa and disappeared as a distinct Crow band.

The Horse Revolution

Around 1730, everything changed. The horse arrived on the Northern Plains.

Horses had been reintroduced to the Americas by Spanish conquistadors in the sixteenth century. Over the following two hundred years, horses spread northward through trade and capture, transforming Plains cultures as they went. Before horses, the Crow had used dog travois—poles attached to dogs that could drag small loads across the grasslands. Dogs limited how much a family could carry and how far they could travel.

Horses changed the calculus entirely. A people who had been semi-nomadic farmers in the northeastern woodlands became fully nomadic hunters, following the vast bison herds across the open plains. The Crow, along with their relatives the Hidatsa and their neighbors the Shoshone, became renowned as horse breeders and traders. They developed relatively large herds despite the harsh northern winters that made it difficult to keep horses alive through the cold months.

But horses also made the Crow a target. Tribes with fewer horses—the powerful Blackfoot Confederacy, the Gros Ventre, the Assiniboine, the Pawnee, the Ute—raided Crow camps to steal mounts. Later, the Lakota Sioux and their allies the Arapaho and Cheyenne would do the same. Horse raiding became both an economic activity and a way for young warriors to earn prestige. The Crow's wealth in horses made them perpetually embattled.

Their greatest enemies became the Blackfoot Confederacy to the north and west, and the Lakota-Cheyenne-Arapaho alliance to the south and east. These weren't occasional skirmishes. This was sustained, multi-generational warfare.

A Cascade of Violence

The historical records paint a picture of relentless conflict. In 1800-1801, the Crow killed at least thirty Lakota, according to Lakota winter counts—pictographic calendars that recorded the most significant event of each year. The next year, the Lakota and Cheyenne retaliated by killing all the men in a Crow camp of thirty tipis.

In 1813, Blood Blackfoot warriors from what is now Alberta launched a raid on Crow camps in the Bighorn area. The next year, Crow warriors near the Little Bighorn River killed a Blackfoot chief named Top Knot.

In 1819, a Crow camp wiped out thirty Cheyenne horse raiders. The following year, the Cheyenne and Lakota destroyed an entire Crow camp at Tongue River—likely the most devastating attack on the Crow in recorded history.

This wasn't the romanticized warfare of Hollywood westerns. It was a grinding struggle for survival in a world where territory meant access to bison, and bison meant life.

The Whites Will Be Dominant

By the 1850s, the Crow faced a strategic dilemma. They were badly outnumbered by their enemies. The Lakota and Cheyenne had established themselves just to the south and east of Crow territory and were actively pushing the Crow westward, taking over their hunting grounds by right of conquest.

Then came a vision.

A boy named Plenty Coups—who would later become the greatest Crow chief—received a vision that the tribal elders interpreted carefully. They concluded that the white Americans would become dominant over the entire country. Nothing could stop this. The question was not whether to resist—resistance would be futile—but how to preserve what they could.

The elders decided that if the Crow were to retain any of their land, they would need to remain on good terms with the whites. More than that, they would need to make themselves useful to the Americans.

This was not capitulation. It was strategic calculation based on a clear-eyed assessment of power dynamics. The Crow had been fighting the Lakota and Cheyenne for generations. Now the Americans were also fighting the Lakota and Cheyenne. The enemy of my enemy, the Crow reasoned, might help me survive.

Allies Against Common Enemies

The Treaty of Fort Laramie in 1851 seemed to confirm Crow hopes. The United States recognized as Crow lands a vast territory centered on the Big Horn Mountains—from the Big Horn Basin in the west to the Musselshell River in the north and the Powder River in the east. This was official American acknowledgment of what the Crow already knew: this was their homeland.

But the Lakota and Cheyenne had other ideas. For two centuries they had been migrating steadily westward across the plains, and they were not about to stop because the Americans had drawn lines on a map. They pressed hard on the Crow, taking the eastern hunting grounds including the Powder and Tongue River valleys.

Red Cloud's War from 1866 to 1868 was a Lakota challenge to American military presence on the Bozeman Trail, a route along the eastern edge of the Big Horn Mountains that led to Montana gold fields. The Lakota won this war decisively. The Treaty of Fort Laramie of 1868 confirmed Lakota control over all the high plains from the Black Hills westward to the crest of the Big Horn Mountains—territory that had been Crow land just a generation earlier.

For the Crow, this was a catastrophe. Their enemies had been officially recognized as owning Crow ancestral lands. Bands of Lakota led by Sitting Bull, Crazy Horse, and Gall, along with their Northern Cheyenne allies, now hunted and raided throughout eastern Montana and northeastern Wyoming with American blessing.

The Battle That Changed Everything

On June 25, 1876, the Lakota Sioux and Cheyenne achieved one of the most famous military victories in American history, annihilating the forces under Lieutenant Colonel George Armstrong Custer at the Battle of the Little Big Horn. The battle took place on the Crow Indian Reservation.

Crow warriors had enlisted with the United States Army for this campaign. They served as scouts, using their intimate knowledge of the terrain and their understanding of their enemies. When Custer made his catastrophic tactical decisions, Crow scouts were among those who warned him.

The Lakota victory was spectacular but pyrrhic. The Great Sioux War of 1876-1877 ended in the defeat of the Sioux and their Cheyenne allies. Some bands fled to Canada. Others were forced onto reservations in Montana and Nebraska. The Crow's strategic gamble had paid off—their enemies were broken, and the Crow retained a reservation of more than 3,600 square miles, large by the standards of what the federal government usually allowed.

Many other tribes were forced onto much smaller reservations far from their traditional lands. The Crow, by making themselves useful to the Americans and by never fighting against them, had preserved a homeland in the heart of their ancestral territory.

The Continuity of Culture

In 1918, the Crow organized a gathering to display their culture and invited members of other tribes to participate. This became the Crow Fair, now celebrated yearly on the third weekend of August. It has grown into one of the largest Native American gatherings in North America, drawing participants from tribes across the continent.

Today, the Crow have a federally recognized tribe—the Crow Tribe of Montana—with their headquarters at Crow Agency. The reservation lies in south-central Montana, south of Billings. The tribe operates Little Big Horn College, providing higher education on the reservation.

Of approximately 14,000 enrolled tribal citizens, an estimated 3,000 spoke the Crow language as of 2007. The language belongs to the Missouri River Valley branch of the Siouan language family, related to but distinct from Lakota. Efforts continue to preserve and teach the language to new generations.

Many Crow people still live on the reservation, but many others have moved to major Western cities while maintaining tribal connections. This is a common pattern among Native American communities—the tension between economic opportunity elsewhere and cultural continuity at home.

The Gift of the Medicine Doll

Sometime before 1765, the Crow held a Sun Dance—the most important religious ceremony for many Plains tribes, involving fasting, prayer, and often physical sacrifice. A poor Arapaho attended this ceremony.

A Crow with spiritual power gave this poor man a medicine doll. The gift transformed his life. He quickly earned status and acquired horses as no one else could.

At the next Sun Dance, some Crow stole the figure back, wanting to keep such powerful medicine within the tribe. Eventually the Arapaho man made a duplicate. Later in life he married a Kiowa woman and brought the doll with him to her people.

The Kiowa still use this figure during their Sun Dance and recognize it as one of their most powerful tribal medicines. They credit the Crow tribe for the origin of their sacred Tai-may figure.

This story captures something important about Plains Indian cultures. Spiritual power was not abstract theology—it was practical force in the world. Objects could hold it. It could be given, stolen, and duplicated. And even bitter enemies could share sacred traditions across the boundaries of tribal warfare.

Friendship and Alliance

The Crow were not at war with everyone. They maintained generally friendly relations with several northern Plains tribes: the Flathead (though sometimes they had conflicts), the Nez Perce, the Kutenai, the Shoshone, the Kiowa, and the Plains Apache.

But the powerful Iron Confederacy developed as their enemy. This was an alliance of northern Plains nations centered on the fur trade, named after its dominant peoples—the Plains Cree and Assiniboine. It later came to include the Stoney, the Saulteaux, and the Métis (people of mixed European and Indigenous ancestry who developed their own distinct culture).

The geography of alliance and enmity on the Plains was complex. It shifted over time as tribes migrated, as the fur trade changed patterns of power, and as the Americans entered the picture. But some hatreds ran deep and lasted for generations. The Crow fought the Blackfoot and the Lakota for more than a century.

What Remains

The Crow made a choice in the mid-nineteenth century. Facing enemies who outnumbered them, watching a new power sweep across the continent, they calculated their odds and decided that alliance with the Americans offered the best chance of survival.

Whether this was wisdom or betrayal depends on perspective. Some see the Crow as pragmatic survivors who did what was necessary to preserve their people and their homeland. Others see them as collaborators who helped the Americans defeat other Native peoples.

The historical record shows that the Crow acted in their own interests, as peoples generally do. Their enemies were not abstract. The Lakota and Cheyenne had killed Crow people, taken Crow land, and would have continued doing so regardless of what the Americans did. The Crow chose the side that aligned with their existing conflicts.

Today, the Crow Nation endures. They still live in the heart of their ancestral territory, in the valley of the Elk River, beneath the shadow of the Big Horn Mountains. Their language is still spoken. Their ceremonies continue. Their story—of migration, of adaptation, of strategic calculation, of survival—is one chapter in the long history of the peoples who called this continent home long before anyone else arrived.
