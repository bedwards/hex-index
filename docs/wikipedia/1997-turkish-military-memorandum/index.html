1997 Turkish military memorandum

17 min read

Based on Wikipedia: 1997 Turkish military memorandum

In the winter of 1997, the streets of Sincan, a district in Ankara, were not merely patrolled but occupied by the heavy treads of tanks. This was not a response to an invasion from a foreign power, nor a riot in the traditional sense. The tanks had been deployed by the Turkish military to suppress a municipal event celebrating "Al-Quds Night," a gathering organized to protest human rights violations in the Middle East. The municipality of Sincan, led by a local Islamist administration, had plastered the venue with posters of Hamas and Hezbollah. To the generals sitting in the command centers of the Turkish Armed Forces, these symbols were not merely political statements; they were an existential threat to the secular soul of the republic they had sworn to protect. General Çevik Bir, a key architect of the coming storm, would later describe this tank deployment not as an act of repression, but as a necessary "balance adjustment to democracy." It was a chilling euphemism that would define the next few months of Turkish history, a period where the state turned its gaze upon its own elected government and forced it to kneel without firing a single shot in anger. This event, known as the 28 February process, was the first time a modern military intervened in a democratic system without dissolving parliament or suspending the constitution, earning it the grim and precise title of a "postmodern coup."

To understand the weight of that February evening, one must first understand the fracture lines running through Turkey at the time. The country was in the grip of a deep ideological struggle, a battle between the Kemalist establishment—the military, the judiciary, and the secular bureaucracy who viewed themselves as the guardians of Mustafa Kemal Atatürk's legacy—and a rising tide of political Islam that sought to reshape the public sphere. At the helm of this new movement was Necmettin Erbakan, a charismatic and stubborn engineer turned politician who had become the Prime Minister of Turkey as the head of the Welfare Party (RP). Erbakan led a fragile coalition government, partnering with the center-right True Path Party (DYP) led by Tansu Çiller. On paper, they were allies. In practice, they were walking on a tightrope over a chasm of mutual suspicion.

The military, however, did not see a tightrope. They saw a cliff. For months, the Chief of the General Staff, General İsmail Hakkı Karadayı, had been compiling a list of grievances against the government. He believed the state was being slowly hollowed out by Islamist infiltration. On January 17, 1997, President Süleyman Demirel, the nation's head of state, requested a briefing from Karadayı at the General Staff headquarters. The atmosphere was tense. Karadayı presented a document containing 55 specific items of concern, ranging from the influence of religious groups in the education system to the perceived softening of secular laws. President Demirel, a seasoned politician himself, looked at the list and made a startling admission: half of these accusations were based on hearsay. He urged the General to communicate directly with the government to soften the language before proceeding. Demirel hoped for dialogue; he hoped for a political solution. But the military leadership had already moved past the point of negotiation.

The tension had been building for weeks prior to that meeting. The Sincan incident in early February was merely the catalyst. The military's response was swift and humiliating. Tanks rolled into the streets, their presence a silent, roaring reminder of who held the real power in the room. The message was clear: the secular order would not be compromised. General Çevik Bir, who would later become a central figure in the drama, articulated the military's philosophy with a metaphor that would haunt Turkish political discourse for decades. "In Turkey we have a marriage of Islam and democracy," Bir said. "The child of this marriage is secularism. Now this child gets sick from time to time. The Turkish Armed Forces is the doctor which saves the child. Depending on how sick the kid is, we administer the necessary medicine to make sure the kid recuperates." It was a paternalistic, authoritarian view of democracy, where the military claimed the right to diagnose the patient and prescribe the cure, regardless of the patient's own wishes.

The climax of this confrontation arrived on the morning of February 28, 1997. The National Security Council (MGK), the highest decision-making body in the country regarding security matters, convened for a marathon session. The room was filled with the country's top military brass: Admiral Güven Erkaya of the Navy, General Çevik Bir, and the other architects of the plan. Prime Minister Erbakan was there, along with his coalition partners. The meeting lasted nine hours. It was not a debate; it was an ultimatum. The generals laid out their demands with surgical precision, presenting a document that would effectively dismantle the policy platform of the elected government.

The decisions issued that day were devastating for Erbakan's vision. The military demanded the immediate closure of religious schools (imam hatip schools) that had been expanded during his tenure. They called for the abolition of tariqas, traditional civilian religious brotherhoods that had deep roots in Turkish society. They insisted on the enforcement of mandatory eight-year secular education to prevent children from entering religious vocational schools too early. They demanded strict enforcement of laws protecting the memory of Atatürk, cracking down on any speech that could be seen as diminishing the founder's legacy. They ordered the suppression of Iranian influence, which they claimed was fueling Islamist movements in Turkey. They demanded the purging of Islamist infiltration from the Turkish Armed Forces and the strict enforcement of dress codes on government property, a direct blow to the headscarf-wearing women who were beginning to enter the public workforce.

Prime Minister Erbakan was forced to sign the document. There was no room for negotiation. The military had drawn the line in the sand, and the elected government had to cross it. The resignation of Erbakan was not immediate, but the writing was on the wall. The "postmodern coup" had succeeded. The government was not overthrown by a tank column storming the parliament; it was strangled by a series of bureaucratic decrees, media campaigns, and judicial threats that left the coalition with no political space to breathe. The military had achieved its goal without ever having to declare martial law or dissolve the parliament. It was a coup that left the flag flying and the constitution technically intact, yet the spirit of democracy had been suffocated.

In the aftermath, the political landscape of Turkey was shattered. Although the DYP, RP, and the Nationalist Movement Party (BBP) attempted to form a new government with Tansu Çiller as the Prime Minister, President Demirel appointed Mesut Yılmaz of the Motherland Party (ANAP) to form a new coalition. Yılmaz, who had been approached months earlier by General Teoman Koman with a plan to install him or himself as Prime Minister after a coup, now found himself at the helm of a government that existed solely because the military had cleared the field. He formed a coalition with Bülent Ecevit of the Democratic Left Party and Hüsamettin Cindoruk, a politician who had been expelled from the DYP for opposing the military's heavy-handed tactics.

The consequences for the Welfare Party and its members were severe. In 1998, the Constitutional Court of Turkey closed the Welfare Party, ruling that it had violated the constitutional separation of religion and state. Necmettin Erbakan was banned from politics for five years. The party's members, including mayors and MPs, fled to the newly formed Virtue Party. Among them was Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, the charismatic mayor of Istanbul. Erdoğan's political career was soon to be halted in a manner that would define the next two decades of Turkish history. In 1997, at a public function in Siirt, Erdoğan read a nationalist and Islamist poem: "The minarets are our bayonets, the domes our helmets, the minarets our barracks, the believers our soldiers." The military and the secular establishment viewed this as a declaration of war. He was given a prison sentence and banned from politics for five years, just like Erbakan.

The 1999 general elections saw the Virtue Party win several seats, but it could not replicate the success of the Welfare Party in 1995. The political atmosphere remained toxic. A notorious incident occurred when Merve Kavakçı, an MP elected on the Virtue Party ticket, attempted to take her seat in parliament while wearing an Islamic headscarf. The act, which was a direct challenge to the secular dress codes enforced by the military and the judiciary, caused a massive commotion and led to her being barred from entering the chamber. The Virtue Party itself was closed by the Constitutional Court in 2001. The cycle of closure and banning seemed unbreakable. The military had successfully purged the overtly Islamist parties, but they had not solved the underlying social and political tensions.

However, the story did not end with the banishment of Erbakan and Erdoğan. The political vacuum created by the 28 February process forced a transformation within the Islamist movement. While the traditionalists formed the Felicity Party to maintain the old guard's ideology, Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, still banned from politics, began to forge a new path. He formed the Justice and Development Party (AKP), a reformist party that explicitly declared it was not an Islamist party. It declared it would not follow the path of the Welfare Party or the Virtue Party. It presented itself as a conservative democratic party, embracing the EU accession process and the language of Western democracy. This strategic pivot allowed the AKP to bypass the military's red lines and eventually win the 2002 general elections in a landslide, fundamentally altering the balance of power in Turkey. The "postmodern coup" had failed to kill the movement; it had only forced it to evolve, to become more subtle, more pragmatic, and ultimately more powerful.

The human cost of this political maneuvering was not just in the careers destroyed or the parties banned. It was in the deepening of the polarization that would tear Turkish society apart for decades. The military's intervention, justified as a defense of secularism, created a narrative of victimhood among the religious conservatives that fueled their political resurgence. The memory of 28 February became a rallying cry, a symbol of the "deep state" that would eventually be used by the AKP to dismantle the very military institutions that had orchestrated the coup. The generals who had planned the operation, including Karadayı and Bir, found themselves on the defensive. In April 2012, decades after the fact, Çevik Bir and 30 other officers were detained for their roles in the coup. In 2018, a Turkish court awarded life sentences to 21 senior military officials for their role in sabotaging democratic institutions. The court accused Karadayı and Bir of "masterminding" the coup. Sixty-eight individuals were acquitted, and Karadayı filed an appeal, but he died in 2020 while the process was ongoing. In a twist of irony, in 2024, President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, the man once banned by the system these generals protected, pardoned Bir, Doğan, and five other senior officers on account of ill health and old age.

The motivations behind the coup remain a subject of intense debate and speculation. While the official narrative focused on the threat of political Islam, some have speculated that the coup was actually directed against Erbakan's coalition partner, Tansu Çiller, who was implicated in the Susurluk scandal, a massive corruption and mafia scandal that rocked the government. It is alleged that Bülent Orakoğlu of the police intelligence, under Hanefi Avcı, learned about the coup plans. Necmettin Erbakan himself claimed that the process was planned by "Zionists," reflecting the deep conspiracy theories that permeate the discourse. In October 2016, Tuncay Özkan claimed that the 28 February process was prepared and organized by the Gülen movement, adding another layer of complexity to the historical record. The truth, as it often is in the murky waters of Turkish politics, is likely a mixture of all these factors: a genuine fear of Islamization, a desire to protect the secular state, a power struggle within the elite, and the personal ambitions of the generals involved.

General Çevik Bir's metaphor of the "sick child" remains the most haunting summary of the event. If the child is democracy, and the medicine is a military intervention that forces the elected government to resign, then the cure is arguably worse than the disease. The 1997 memorandum was a testament to the power of the Turkish military to shape the political destiny of the nation without a single bullet fired. It was a coup that left the institutions of democracy standing, but hollowed out from the inside. It demonstrated that in a country where the military views itself as the ultimate guardian of the state, the will of the voters could be overridden by the will of the generals. The events of that February day set the stage for the polarized, volatile, and transformative politics of modern Turkey, a legacy that continues to shape the nation's identity today. The tanks in Sincan, the 55 items on the list, the nine-hour meeting, and the forced resignation of Erbakan are not just historical footnotes; they are the foundation of a political earthquake that is still shaking the ground beneath Turkey's feet. The "postmodern coup" proved that the most effective way to overthrow a government is not to storm its gates, but to simply change the rules of the game until the government can no longer stand.

The legacy of 28 February is a stark reminder of the fragility of democratic institutions when they are held hostage by unelected power centers. It serves as a cautionary tale for any nation where the military believes it has the right to intervene in politics, even under the guise of protecting the constitution. The human cost of such interventions is measured not just in the lives lost in the streets, but in the careers destroyed, the parties banned, and the deep societal fractures that take generations to heal. The generals who planned the coup may have believed they were saving the child of democracy, but in doing so, they may have inflicted a wound that has never fully healed. The story of 1997 is a story of a nation holding its breath, waiting to see if the doctor would save the patient or kill it with the cure. In the end, the patient survived, but the scars remain, and the diagnosis of the disease continues to be a matter of fierce and unending debate. The 28 February process was a moment in time, but its echoes reverberate through every election, every court case, and every political speech in Turkey to this day. It is the ghost that haunts the Turkish political landscape, a reminder that in the struggle between the sword and the ballot, the sword has often held the upper hand. And yet, the ballot box, battered and bruised, has always found a way to speak again. This is the paradox of modern Turkey: a nation where the military has been both the guardian and the destroyer of democracy, and where the people, through their resilience, have constantly reinvented the game to ensure their voice is heard, no matter how many times the generals try to silence it.

The events of 1997 were not an isolated incident but part of a longer pattern of military interventions in Turkish history, including the coups of 1960, 1971, and 1980. Each intervention left a mark on the national psyche, shaping the political culture and the relationship between the state and its citizens. The 28 February process was unique in its subtlety, its use of legal and bureaucratic mechanisms rather than brute force, but its impact was no less profound. It was a coup that changed the course of history, forcing the Islamist movement to adapt and evolve, and ultimately paving the way for the rise of the AKP and the transformation of Turkey into a different kind of state. The story of 1997 is a story of power, ideology, and the enduring struggle for the soul of a nation. It is a story that continues to unfold, with each new chapter adding to the complex and often tragic narrative of Turkish democracy. The tanks in Sincan, the generals in the meeting room, and the Prime Minister who was forced to sign his own resignation are characters in a drama that has not yet reached its final act.
