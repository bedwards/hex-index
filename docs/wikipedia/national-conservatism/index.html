National conservatism

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: National conservatism

In 2018, Viktor Orbán stood before a crowd in Budapest and declared that his government was building a "illiberal state." This was not a slip of the tongue, nor a momentary rhetorical flourish; it was a precise ideological manifesto for a movement that has since swept across the Western world. While the traditional right had long spent its energy defending free markets and the sanctity of the individual against the encroachments of the left, a new faction emerged with a different enemy in mind: the very foundations of Enlightenment liberalism itself. This is national conservatism, a political force that prioritizes the defense of national and cultural identity above all else, often viewing the family as the primary model for the state and rejecting the universality of human rights in favor of the specific, the local, and the traditional.

It is a movement that has moved from the fringes of political discourse to the center of power, reshaping the landscape of Europe and the United States. To understand the current geopolitical tremors shaking the West, one must look beyond the standard left-right economic divide and examine this distinct ideological current that challenges the post-1945 consensus. It is a philosophy that does not merely seek to conserve the status quo but to actively dismantle the "liberal state" to restore what its adherents see as a lost moral and social order.

The Rejection of Universalism

At the heart of national conservatism lies a fundamental disagreement with the core precepts of the Enlightenment. Where classical liberalism and its modern iterations champion individualism, the universal application of human rights, and the idea that all humans are created equal regardless of their national or cultural context, national conservatives argue that these concepts are abstract fictions. They posit that human identity is inextricably bound to the nation, the family, and the specific historical traditions of a people.

This ideology departs sharply from the universalist dream of a borderless world. In the view of national conservatives, the Enlightenment project, particularly as articulated by thinkers like Jean-Jacques Rousseau and John Locke, created a "dream world" that has no place for the "Jewish and Christian world" or any other distinct cultural entity. Yoram Hazony, in his influential 2022 book Conservatism: A Rediscovery, argues that the political arena must be about the "recovery, restoration, elaboration, and repair of national and religious traditions." For Hazony and others in this camp, the nation is not just a political unit but a moral community, and the preservation of its unique character is the highest good.

This rejection of universalism manifests in a hostility toward the "1968 agenda," a shorthand for the cultural shifts of the late 1960s that emphasized gender emancipation, sexual liberation, and the separation of church and state. National conservatives view these developments as a catastrophic loss of moral bearing. They argue that liberalism's relativism has eroded the moral order necessary for a stable society. Instead of the autonomous individual, they advocate for the "common good," a concept loosely defined by scholars like Patrick Deneen as "integration" that often includes the reunification of church and state. Deneen has argued that the "current elite" must be replaced by "a better aristocracy brought about by a muscular populism" to advance this common good.

The family, in this worldview, is not merely a private arrangement but the bedrock of the state. National conservatives praise the family as a "home and a center of identity, solidarity, and tradition," as noted by Austrian political scientist Sieglinde Rosenberger. Consequently, the movement is fiercely critical of gender-related emancipation and advocates for traditional family values and gender roles. This is not a passive preference but an active political program aimed at reversing what they see as the disintegration of the social fabric.

The Economic Divergence

Perhaps the most surprising aspect of national conservatism to the casual observer is its relationship with economics. For decades, the global right was dominated by economic liberalism, the belief in free markets, deregulation, and tight government spending. National conservatism breaks from this tradition. It is not an advocate of laissez-faire capitalism; in fact, it often views unfettered capitalism with suspicion, seeing it as a force that erodes national identity and social cohesion.

The movement takes a pragmatic approach to regulatory economics, often embracing protectionism and a strong state role in the economy. This shift is driven by the works of philosophers like Leo Strauss and Eric Voegelin, whose ideas serve as building blocks for modern national conservative socio-economic policies. Strauss's indictment of capitalism as "economism"—the reduction of human needs to mere consumption—resonates deeply within the movement. It argues for "solidarism" and an increased statist role to bring about a moralizing "financial nationalism."

In practice, this means supporting a mixed economy, corporatism, and policies that prioritize the "pro-worker" and "pro-family" agenda over pure market efficiency. In post-communist Central and Eastern Europe, particularly in Poland and Hungary, national conservatives have developed an economic alternative to liberalism. This includes increased state social spending, the re-nationalization of strategic enterprises like banks, and opposition to tax breaks for the wealthy or corporations that do not align with national interests.

This economic stance creates a clear dividing line with liberal conservatives. As one commentator noted, "Most parties of the Right [today] are run by economically liberal conservatives who, in varying degrees, have marginalised social, cultural and national conservatives." The rise of "pro-worker conservatism" in the United States and the protectionist policies in Hungary illustrate a growing gap between the two wings of the right. National conservatives are willing to use the power of the state to protect domestic industries and workers, even if it means violating the principles of free trade. This is a form of "social nativism" that draws inspiration from East Asian state-led development models, where the economy is directed toward national goals rather than global market efficiency.

The economic philosophy of national conservatism is not uniform; it ranges from support for a mixed economy to a more laissez-faire approach depending on the country. However, the dominant trend is a rejection of the neoliberal consensus that has governed the West since the 1980s. The argument is that economic inequality engendered by neoliberalism contributed to the rise of national conservatism, as communities felt abandoned by a globalist economic system that prioritized capital mobility over national stability.

The Populist Surge and the "New Global Divide"

In the 2020s, national conservatism has been described as causing a "new global divide," particularly within the Western world. This movement has gained ground not just in the margins but in the heart of established democracies, fueled by a sense of grievance and a desire for a return to order. In Europe and America, national conservatives are majoritarian populists. They advocate for fewer limits on the power of elected representatives, arguing that these leaders must be empowered to "smash the liberal state" and restore the true will of the people.

This populist strain is evident in the rise of Trumpism in the United States, which is widely considered a variety of national conservatism. The movement gives its name to the National Conservatism Conference, organized by the Edmund Burke Foundation, which has become a central hub for the movement's intellectual and political networking. Similarly, in Hungary, Viktor Orbán's Fidesz party has explicitly described its ideology as national conservative. Orbán's government has been instrumental in funding and spreading national conservative institutions across Europe and the United States, including the Danube Institute, the Mathias Corvinus Collegium, The European Conservative magazine, and the National Conservatism Conference itself.

The demographic base of national conservatism often contrasts sharply with that of liberal-conservative parties. While liberal conservatives tend to draw support from urban centers, national conservative parties often have roots in rural environments. This divide reflects a deeper cultural schism between those who feel left behind by globalization and rapid social change and those who are the beneficiaries of the current order.

Costas Panayotakis has argued that the economic inequality engendered by neoliberalism was a catalyst for this shift. The feeling of abandonment, combined with a fear of cultural displacement, has created a potent political force. Several very close elections in recent years have pitted national conservative candidates against liberal incumbents, highlighting the intensity of this new political fault line. The movement is not merely about policy preferences; it is an existential struggle over the identity of the nation itself.

Sovereignty, Security, and the International Order

The foreign policy of national conservatism is defined by a fierce commitment to national sovereignty and a deep skepticism of internationalism. National conservatives lean towards militarism, unilateralism, and isolationism, rejecting the multilateralism that has characterized the modern age. They view international organizations like the United Nations and the European Union with suspicion, feeling that their multinational agendas erode unique national identities.

In Europe, this translates into a strong Euroscepticism. National conservatives argue that the European Union represents a supranational bureaucracy that undermines the sovereignty of individual nation-states. They oppose the free movement of people, viewing illegal immigration or immigration per se as a threat to national and cultural identity. This is not just a policy position but a core tenet of their ideology, which sees the defense of borders as essential to the preservation of the nation.

The movement also advocates for a strong military, viewing it as a necessary tool for defending national sovereignty. This stance is often accompanied by a rejection of the "liberal peace" that has underpinned Western foreign policy for decades. Instead, national conservatives favor a foreign policy that unapologetically upholds the interests of their own nation, even at the expense of international cooperation.

This isolationist turn is part of a broader opposition to modernity, secularism, liberalism, socialism, communism, and anarchism. National conservatives value Europe's Christian heritage and see themselves as the defenders of Western civilization against forces they perceive as destructive. They argue that liberalism and socialism are aspects of modernity that "disconnect human designs from normative order," and they call for the "restoration and order" of social, moral, and political structures.

The movement's view of history is also distinct. National conservatives disagree with liberalism and socialism over the interpretation of key historical developments, such as the Enlightenment, modernization, and emancipation. They view events like those of 1789, 1968, and 1989 not as triumphs of freedom but as moments of disruption that led to the current crisis of the West. For them, 1989, the year the Berlin Wall fell, was not just the end of communism but the beginning of a new era of globalist domination that must now be resisted.

The Shadow of Authoritarianism and the Distinction from Fascism

One of the most contentious aspects of national conservatism is its relationship with the darker chapters of political history, particularly fascism. While the movement distances itself from fascism, viewing it as another aspect of modernity that disrupts normative order, the line can sometimes appear blurred to outside observers. Some scholars, such as Roger Griffin, have identified forms of "para-fascism"—a type of authoritarian nationalism that seeks to preserve traditional structures rather than pursue a palingenetic (rebirth) ultranationalism—as aligning more closely with the tenets of national conservatism than with pure fascism.

National conservatives argue that they are not seeking a totalitarian revolution but a restoration of order. They reject the racial politics and extreme ultranationalism of historical fascism, instead focusing on cultural nationalism, patriotism, and the preservation of national identity. They favor assimilation into the dominant culture and strict law and order policies, but they maintain a rhetorical and often practical distinction from the totalitarianism of the 20th century.

However, the emphasis on a "muscular populism," the desire to "smash the liberal state," and the rejection of checks and balances in favor of majoritarian rule raise concerns about the potential for authoritarianism. The movement's focus on the "common good" and the "restoration of order" can be interpreted as a justification for concentrating power in the hands of a strong leader who claims to represent the true will of the people.

Despite these concerns, national conservatives insist that their ideology is a necessary corrective to the excesses of liberalism. They argue that the current elite has failed to protect the nation and that a new aristocracy, brought about by a populist movement, is needed to restore the moral and social fabric of society. This is not a call for chaos but for a new kind of order, one that is rooted in tradition and national identity.

The Intellectual Foundations and the Future

The intellectual underpinnings of national conservatism are rich and complex, drawing from a variety of sources. The works of Michael Oakeshott and Edmund Burke, while foundational to classical conservatism, are often sidelined or reinterpreted by national conservatives, who find their focus on gradual change insufficient for the current crisis. Instead, the movement looks to thinkers like Leo Strauss, Eric Voegelin, and Yoram Hazony for a more robust defense of national and religious traditions.

Hazony's The Virtue of Nationalism and Conservatism: A Rediscovery have become seminal texts for the movement, providing a philosophical justification for the rejection of universalism and the embrace of national sovereignty. These works argue that the nation is the highest form of human association and that the preservation of national identity is essential for human flourishing.

The movement is not static; it is evolving and adapting to the challenges of the 21st century. In the United States, the rise of "pro-worker conservatism" represents a shift away from the free-market orthodoxy of the past. In Europe, the spread of national conservative institutions and the influence of figures like Viktor Orbán suggest that this is a long-term trend rather than a temporary reaction to specific events.

The debate over national conservatism is far from over. It represents a fundamental challenge to the liberal order that has governed the West for decades. As the movement continues to gain ground, it will force a re-examination of the core values of Western democracy. Will the West continue down the path of globalism and individualism, or will it embrace a new vision based on national sovereignty and cultural identity?

The answer to this question will shape the future of the Western world. For now, national conservatism stands as a powerful and provocative force, demanding that the West confront the costs of its own success and consider the possibility of a different path. It is a movement that refuses to accept the status quo, arguing that the preservation of the nation and its traditions is the only way to ensure a stable and meaningful future.

In the end, national conservatism is not just a political party or a set of policies; it is a worldview that challenges the very foundations of modern liberal democracy. It asks difficult questions about the nature of the individual, the role of the state, and the meaning of community. Whether one agrees with its answers or not, it is impossible to ignore the force with which it is reshaping the political landscape of the 21st century. The "new global divide" is real, and it is being drawn along the fault lines of national identity, cultural heritage, and the definition of the common good.

The rise of national conservatism signals a shift in the global balance of power and ideology. It is a movement that has found a home in the hearts of those who feel that the liberal order has failed them, and it offers a vision of the future that is rooted in the past. As the world continues to grapple with the challenges of globalization, migration, and social change, the ideas of national conservatism will undoubtedly play a central role in the political debates of the coming decades. The question is no longer whether national conservatism will influence the future, but how deeply and in what direction it will steer the ship of state.

"In the political arena, conservatism refers to a standpoint that regards the recovery, restoration, elaboration, and repair of national and religious traditions as the key to maintaining a nation and strengthening it through time." — Yoram Hazony

This quote encapsulates the essence of the movement: a call to action, a rejection of the present, and a yearning for a restored past. It is a call to defend the nation, not just as a political entity, but as a moral community. And in doing so, national conservatism has become one of the most significant political forces of our time.
