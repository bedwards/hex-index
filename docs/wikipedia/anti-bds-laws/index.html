Anti-BDS laws

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Anti-BDS laws

In February 2020, Benjamin Netanyahu, then Prime Minister of Israel, took to Twitter with a declaration that would cement the legal reality of the American political landscape: his government was "advancing legislation in many countries" with the explicit goal of making it illegal to boycott Israel. This was not a diplomatic suggestion or a rhetorical flourish; it was a statement of strategic intent that had already begun to reshape the laws of thirty-eight American states. While the world watched the geopolitical tensions in the Middle East, a quiet but aggressive legislative campaign was unfolding in state capitals from South Carolina to New York, designed to penalize individuals and corporations for exercising their right to economic protest. These are the Anti-BDS laws, a complex web of statutes and executive orders that have turned the simple act of refusing to buy a specific product into a legal liability, sparking a fierce constitutional debate over the boundaries of free speech, the nature of antisemitism, and the power of foreign lobbying within the American democratic process.

To understand the weight of these laws, one must first understand the movement they seek to crush. The Boycott, Divestment, and Sanctions movement, known by the acronym BDS, was formally announced in 2005 by a broad coalition of Palestinian civil society groups. It emerged from the ashes of the failed Oslo Accords, a period where Palestinian human rights activists concluded that Western leaders were no longer committed to holding Israel accountable for alleged human rights violations in the territories it had occupied since 1967. The movement's strategy was modeled on the international campaigns that eventually dismantled apartheid in South Africa. Its demands were specific and rooted in international law: an end to the Israeli occupation of the West Bank and Gaza, the dismantling of the separation wall, and the recognition of the right of return for Palestinian refugees. The tactics were non-violent but economically potent: refusing to purchase goods produced in Israeli settlements, divesting pension funds from companies complicit in the occupation, and imposing cultural and academic sanctions.

The Israeli government and its supporters, however, have consistently framed the BDS movement not as a political protest, but as a form of antisemitism. They argue that the movement applies a double standard to Israel, holding it to a level of scrutiny no other nation faces, and that its ultimate goal is the delegitimization of the Jewish state itself. This narrative has become the cornerstone of the legal defense for anti-BDS legislation. Proponents of these laws argue that they are not regulating political speech but are instead legislating against hate speech, equating the boycott of Israel with the persecution of Jews. This conflation of anti-Zionism with antisemitism is the ideological engine driving the legislation, transforming a debate over land and sovereignty into a question of moral character and safety.

The result is a patchwork of laws that vary in their specific provisions but share a common DNA. Most of these statutes fall into two distinct categories: contract-focused laws and investment-focused laws. The contract-focused variety requires any entity wishing to do business with the state government to sign a certification promising that they are not currently boycotting Israel. If a company refuses to sign this pledge, it is effectively barred from receiving millions of dollars in public contracts. The investment-focused laws go a step further, mandating that state public pension funds and investment portfolios divest from any company that participates in a boycott of Israel. In essence, the state uses its massive financial leverage to force the private sector to align with the state's foreign policy preferences, punishing those who choose to take a moral stand against the occupation.

The proliferation of these laws across the United States is not a spontaneous organic development of state legislatures; it is the result of a highly organized, well-funded, and transnational lobbying effort. At the heart of this machinery is the Israel Allies Foundation (IAF), an Israeli non-profit organization that explicitly encourages the formation of pro-Israel caucuses in foreign parliaments and state legislatures. In 2015, following the passage of South Carolina's anti-BDS law, the IAF released a "model act," a template bill that combined the language of the South Carolina and Illinois legislation. This document was designed to be "copy, paste, and legislate." The strategy was to provide state lawmakers with a ready-made solution that required little to no modification, allowing them to pass complex legislation with minimal legislative friction.

The investigation into the spread of these laws, known as the "Copy, Paste, Legislate" project, revealed the sheer scale of this operation. It uncovered that in addition to the IAF, the American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC), the Israel Action Network, and local Jewish Federations were directly involved in lobbying for these measures. In states like Arizona, California, and Nevada, the efforts were spearheaded by Dillon Hosier, a lobbyist working for the Israeli-American Council, an organization funded by prominent Israeli-American billionaire Adam Milstein. The connections were direct and personal. When Ohio Governor John Kasich signed his state's anti-BDS bill into law, Gilad Erdan, then the Israeli Minister of Strategic Affairs, sent him an email of gratitude: "I sincerely appreciate your contribution." In 2016, Israel's then-ambassador to the UN, Danny Danon, boasted that his government was advancing legislation in many countries to make boycotting Israel illegal. The success rate was staggering: as of 2024, thirty-eight states had passed bills or executive orders designed to discourage boycotts of Israel, often with broad bipartisan support.

Yet, the legal and ethical foundations of these laws remain deeply contested. Critics, including the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) and the Council on American–Islamic Relations (CAIR), argue that these statutes constitute a clear violation of the First Amendment. The central legal argument is that the right to boycott is a form of political speech protected by the Constitution. In the landmark case NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co. (1982), the Supreme Court ruled that the right to boycott is "an integral part of the freedom of expression" and that individuals cannot be penalized for their political beliefs simply because those beliefs are unpopular. By conditioning government contracts or investment opportunities on a refusal to boycott, critics argue, the state is engaging in "viewpoint discrimination," punishing citizens for their political stance on a specific foreign policy issue.

The debate extends beyond the courtroom into the court of public opinion, where the data presents a picture of a deeply divided electorate. A University of Maryland Critical Issues Poll from October 2019 found that a significant majority of Americans—72%—opposed laws that penalized people for boycotting Israel, while only 22% supported such measures. The poll also highlighted a stark partisan divide: among those aware of the BDS movement, 76% of Republicans opposed the movement, compared to 52% of Democrats. However, the Data for Progress poll from the same year told a slightly different story, with 35% to 27% opposing anti-BDS laws. When broken down by party, 48% of Democrats opposed these laws, while 44% of Republicans supported them. Despite the legislative success of the anti-BDS movement, the general public remains largely skeptical of the idea that the government should dictate economic behavior based on foreign policy preferences.

The complexity of the issue was further illuminated by a 2022 Pew Research Center survey, which revealed a profound lack of familiarity with the movement itself. While 70% to 80% of Americans believed that boycotts were a legitimate tactic for political protest, only 5% of Americans said they supported the BDS movement specifically. Furthermore, 84% of respondents admitted they did not know much about the movement. This suggests that the fervor driving the legislation may be disconnected from the public's actual engagement with the issue. The lobbying machinery appears to be operating with a level of intensity that far exceeds the level of public awareness or support, creating a policy environment where a minority of highly motivated actors can shape the laws of dozens of states.

The context in which these laws operate has shifted dramatically in recent years, particularly following the July 2024 advisory opinion issued by the International Court of Justice (ICJ). In a landmark ruling, the ICJ declared that Israel's occupation of the Palestinian territories was illegal and must be brought to an end as quickly as possible. The court found that Israel was in violation of Article 3 of the International Convention on the Prevention of Racial Discrimination, which imposes a duty on states to "condemn racial segregation and apartheid" and to "prevent, prohibit and eradicate all practices of this nature in territories under their jurisdiction." The court further stipulated that member states should not recognize Israel's occupation as legal, nor should they render aid or assistance in maintaining it.

This ruling adds a layer of profound legal tension to the American anti-BDS statutes. If the highest court in the world declares the occupation illegal and calls on states to stop assisting it, does a state law that penalizes those who refuse to do business with the occupying power become an instrument of international law violation? A senior legal adviser for the European Center for Constitutional and Human Rights noted to the Washington Post that the ICJ ruling could "force companies and member states to differentiate between Israel and occupied territory when it comes to trade." The anti-BDS laws, which often treat Israel and the occupied territories as a single economic entity, may be directly at odds with the emerging international legal consensus. The laws were designed to make it difficult for people to boycott Israel, but the ICJ ruling creates a moral and legal imperative for states to ensure their economic policies do not support the occupation.

The origins of the modern BDS movement trace back to the failure of the Oslo Accords, which promised a path to peace but ultimately resulted in the continued expansion of settlements and the entrenchment of military rule. Palestinian activists, realizing that diplomatic avenues had stalled, conceived a new strategy: a global, peaceful movement to exert economic pressure. They began by refusing to buy goods from the occupied territories, particularly those made in settlements, and by calling for divestment from corporations that facilitated the occupation. The movement's formal launch in 2005 was a pivotal moment, marking the transition from isolated acts of resistance to a coordinated international campaign. It sought to mirror the global pressure that had previously been applied to South Africa, arguing that the international community had a moral obligation to isolate the occupier until rights were restored.

The Israeli response to this movement was swift and multifaceted, moving from public diplomacy to hard legal constraints. The government established the Ministry of Strategic Affairs specifically to combat the BDS movement, viewing it as an existential threat to Israel's legitimacy. This ministry, along with other state and non-state actors, poured resources into lobbying efforts in the United States and Europe. The "model act" strategy proved to be a masterstroke of political engineering, allowing a single narrative to be replicated across fifty different state legislatures with minimal friction. The narrative was simple: to boycott Israel is to be antisemitic, and to legislate against antisemitism is a moral imperative.

However, the legal challenges mounted by civil liberties groups have begun to chip away at the edifice of these laws. Courts have struck down certain provisions, ruling that the government cannot condition contracts on the surrender of constitutional rights. The argument that these laws are merely about "fairness" or "non-discrimination" has been tested against the reality that they single out one specific country for exclusion, a move that many legal scholars argue violates the principle of viewpoint neutrality. The tension between the state's interest in protecting its economic partners and the individual's right to political expression remains unresolved, creating a legal landscape that is as volatile as the geopolitical one it seeks to influence.

The involvement of private money in this legislative battle cannot be overstated. Figures like Adam Milstein have played a crucial role, funding organizations and lobbying efforts that have directly influenced state policy. The scale of this investment underscores the high stakes involved for the Israeli government and its allies. For them, the anti-BDS laws are not just about economics; they are about survival. In a world where the occupation is increasingly viewed through the lens of human rights violations, as evidenced by the ICJ ruling, the ability to maintain a stable economic and diplomatic relationship with the United States is paramount. The anti-BDS laws are a defensive fortification, designed to ensure that the American public and its institutions remain aligned with Israeli policy, regardless of the moral arguments raised by the occupation.

The debate over anti-BDS laws is ultimately a debate about the nature of democracy and the limits of state power. Can a government compel its citizens to support a foreign nation's policies? Can it penalize them for exercising their right to protest? The thirty-eight states that have passed these laws have answered "yes" to these questions, relying on the argument that the boycott is a form of hate speech. The ACLU, the courts, and a significant portion of the American public have answered "no," arguing that the First Amendment protects the right to dissent, even when that dissent is unpopular.

As the international legal landscape continues to evolve, with the ICJ ruling casting a long shadow over the legitimacy of the occupation, the relevance of these American laws is being put to the test. If the occupation is illegal under international law, then the laws that protect it from economic pressure may themselves be illegitimate. The contradiction is stark: a state passes a law to punish those who boycott an illegal occupation, effectively using the force of its own legal system to shield a violation of international law. This paradox lies at the heart of the anti-BDS debate, a conflict that transcends the borders of the Middle East and strikes at the very core of American civil liberties.

The future of these laws remains uncertain. With the Supreme Court yet to issue a definitive ruling on the constitutionality of state-level anti-boycott statutes, the legal battleground is wide open. Lower courts have issued conflicting rulings, some striking down the laws as unconstitutional, while others upholding them as valid exercises of state power. The outcome of these cases will determine whether the right to boycott remains a protected form of political speech or whether it becomes a privilege that can be revoked by the state.

In the meantime, the movement continues. The Israel Allies Foundation and its partners are not slowing down. They continue to draft new model acts, lobby new states, and push for federal legislation. The Combating BDS Act, reintroduced in 2023 by Senators Marco Rubio, Bill Cassidy, Mike Braun, Rick Scott, Bill Hagerty, and Steve Daines, represents the latest attempt to bring this fight to the federal level. While no federal law has been adopted yet, the momentum in the states suggests that the pressure is only increasing.

The story of anti-BDS laws is a story of power, money, and ideology. It is a tale of how a small but determined group of activists can reshape the laws of a nation, how the lines between foreign policy and domestic law can blur, and how the right to protest can be challenged in the name of combating hate. It is a reminder that in a democracy, the most dangerous laws are often those that are passed with the best of intentions, designed to protect the state from its critics, only to end up protecting the state from itself. The battle over the boycott is not just about Israel; it is about the soul of the American legal system and the enduring struggle to balance national security with individual liberty.

The events described here are not hypothetical. They are documented facts, recorded in legislative texts, court rulings, and the testimony of the lobbyists and activists who have shaped this era. From the model acts drafted in 2015 to the ICJ ruling of 2024, the trajectory is clear: the world is watching, the courts are deciding, and the right to boycott hangs in the balance. The question that remains is whether the American legal system will uphold the principle that the government cannot force its citizens to support a foreign policy they disagree with, or whether the pressure of the lobby will prevail, turning the marketplace of ideas into a marketplace of compliance. The answer to that question will define not just the future of the Middle East peace process, but the future of free speech in the United States.

The complexity of the issue demands that we look beyond the slogans and the soundbites. We must understand the history of the occupation, the origins of the BDS movement, the mechanics of the lobbying effort, and the constitutional principles at stake. We must recognize that while the Israeli government views these laws as a necessary defense, the American civil liberties community views them as a dangerous precedent. The tension between these two perspectives is unlikely to be resolved soon, but the dialogue must continue. For in the end, the strength of a democracy is measured not by its ability to silence its critics, but by its capacity to engage with them, even when their views are deeply unpopular. The anti-BDS laws test this capacity to its limit, challenging the very foundations of the American experiment.
