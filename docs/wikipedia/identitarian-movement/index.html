Identitarian movement

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Identitarian movement

In 2012, a group of young activists in France scaled a construction site in Grenoble, unfurling a banner that declared the site a "European territory" and demanding it be returned to its "original owners." They were not squatters in the traditional sense, nor were they left-wing anti-capitalists. They were the first public manifestation of Génération Identitaire, the youth wing of a movement that would soon reshape the landscape of European politics. Their message was stark, their aesthetic polished, and their ideology rooted in a specific, terrifying fear: that the white European identity was facing an existential threat of extinction. This was the birth of the modern Identitarian movement, a pan-European nationalist force that has since spread from the cafes of Paris to the digital forums of the United States, Australia, and New Zealand.

To understand the Identitarian movement, one must first discard the notion that it is merely a rebranding of old-school fascism, though the lineage is undeniable. It is something more subtle, more calculated, and arguably more dangerous in its adaptability. Emerging in the early 2000s from the ashes of France's far-right intellectual history, Identitarianism is a pan-European, ethno-nationalist ideology centered on the preservation of a white European identity. The movement posits that this identity is under siege from three distinct vectors: multiculturalism, mass immigration from extra-European regions, and the homogenizing forces of globalization. Unlike the blunt instruments of 20th-century authoritarianism, the Identitarians operate on a strategy of "metapolitics," seeking not immediate electoral victory, but a long-term cultural hegemony that will eventually make their vision of a racially homogeneous Europe seem like the only logical conclusion.

The Intellectual Architecture: From the Nouvelle Droite to the Great Replacement

The roots of Identitarianism are deep and tangled, stretching back to the interwar period of the 1920s and 30s, specifically to the German Conservative Revolution. This was a loose collection of thinkers who rejected both liberal democracy and traditional conservatism, seeking a "third way" that was anti-modern yet modern in its tactics. However, the direct progenitor of the Identitarian movement is the Nouvelle Droite (New Right), a French far-right political movement that crystallized in the 1960s. The Nouvelle Droite was born out of a desperate need to adapt illiberal, traditionalist politics to a post-World War II European context where Nazism and fascism were culturally and politically toxic.

The architects of this shift were men like Alain de Benoist, Dominique Venner, Pierre Vial, Guillaume Faye, and Renaud Camus. These essayists did not seek to revive the swastika; they sought to revive the idea of the ethno-cultural nation while discarding the baggage of the Holocaust. They understood that to survive, the far right had to stop shouting about racial supremacy and start whispering about "cultural preservation." This strategic pivot is the bedrock of Identitarianism. The movement adopted the Nouvelle Droite's core tenets: an anti-universalist, anti-globalist, anti-liberal, and anti-Islam worldview. They argue that ethnic, cultural, and racial identities are not social constructs but fundamental, biological realities that must be protected.

The movement's most potent and controversial contribution to political discourse is the theory known as the Great Replacement. Introduced to the mainstream by Renaud Camus and Guillaume Faye in the 2000s and 2010s, this conspiracy theory asserts that white Europeans are facing demographic and cultural extinction. The argument is that declining birth rates among white populations, combined with high rates of extra-European immigration and pro-diversity policies, are systematically erasing the indigenous European population. To the Identitarian, this is not a natural demographic shift but a deliberate, often shadowy conspiracy to replace the "native" population with non-European peoples. This narrative, which scholars describe as a form of "mixophobia" or the fear of ethnic mixing, serves as the movement's primary mobilizing tool. It transforms a complex sociological reality into a narrative of survival, framing the migrant not as an economic actor or a refugee, but as an agent of extinction.

The Strategy of Metapolitics and Cultural Hegemony

What distinguishes the Identitarian movement from its predecessors is its rejection of direct electoral politics. Influenced heavily by the tactics of the French New Right, Identitarians believe that winning an election is futile if the culture itself is hostile to their ideas. They look to the success of the left-wing movements of the mid-20th century, which achieved dominance not just through voting, but through the control of universities, media, and cultural institutions. Alain de Benoist and Guillaume Faye, through their think tank GRECE (Groupement de recherche et d'études pour la civilisation européenne), explicitly sought to imitate Marxist metapolitics. They aimed to use agitprop, entryism, and cultural warfare to shift the Overton window of public discourse.

This strategy is known as metapolitics. The goal is to provoke long-term social transformations that will eventually lead to cultural hegemony and popular adherence to Identitarian ideas. They do not want to be a political party; they want to be the water in which the political parties swim. By promoting concepts like ethnopluralism, they argue that different cultures should remain separate and distinct to preserve their unique identities. This sounds, on the surface, like a defense of diversity. However, the Identitarian interpretation is exclusionary: it demands that cultures not mix, effectively advocating for a world of rigid, segregated ethno-states. They oppose the mixing of cultures within a single society, viewing it as a form of "sclerosis and entropy," as Guillaume Faye famously put it.

This approach has allowed the movement to dress its racialist ideology in the language of environmentalism and cultural preservation. Just as one might protect a rare species of bird from extinction, the Identitarian argues one must protect the "European species." They advocate for pan-European nationalism, localism, and remigration—the controversial idea that non-European migrants should be encouraged or forced to return to their countries of origin. This is not merely a policy proposal; it is a call for the reversal of demographic trends. They promote the preservation of homogeneous ethno-cultural entities, generally to the exclusion of extra-European migrants and the descendants of immigrants. While they may claim to be anti-racist in the sense of opposing biological supremacy, their insistence on race as a defining and immutable category makes them racialist by definition.

The European Expansion and the Role of Key Figures

While the movement's intellectual nucleus was French, its physical expansion was rapid and pan-European. The original prominence of the French groups, such as Bloc Identitaire (founded in 2003) and its youth wing Génération Identitaire (founded in 2012), began to wane as a nebula of similar movements emerged across the continent. This network, often grouped under the term "European New Right," saw the rise of groups in Germany, Austria, Italy, and beyond. The movement spread more rapidly to the eastern part of the continent through conscious efforts of figures like Guillaume Faye, who saw the potential in regions with less entrenched liberal democratic traditions.

Among the most prominent figures to emerge from this expansion is Martin Sellner, an Austrian activist who became one of the biggest faces of the Identitarian movement. Sellner's ideology is heavily influenced by the German philosophers Martin Heidegger and Carl Schmitt, thinkers known for their critiques of liberal democracy and their emphasis on the friend-enemy distinction. Similarly, Daniel Friberg, a leading figure in the Swedish branch of the movement, has cited the influences of Ernst Jünger and Julius Evola, two of the most radical and esoteric thinkers of the 20th century. Evola, in particular, provided a spiritual and metaphysical dimension to the movement, linking the struggle for identity to a cosmic battle against the forces of modernity and decay.

The movement's expansion was not accidental; it was fueled by specific historical events. The Syrian civil war, which began in 2011, and the subsequent European migration crisis of 2015 created a perfect storm for Identitarian recruitment. As millions of refugees fled conflict zones, moving through the Balkans and into the heart of Europe, the Identitarians found their most potent imagery. They exploited widespread anti-immigration and anti-Islam sentiments, framing the migration not as a humanitarian crisis but as an invasion. The escalating instability and terrorism in the Middle East and North Africa, and its spillover into Europe, were used as proof of the incompatibility of cultures and the necessity of the "Great Replacement" narrative.

In 2019, the movement's status shifted from a fringe intellectual curiosity to a recognized security threat. The German Federal Office for the Protection of the Constitution classified the Identitarian Movement as a right-wing extremist organization. This was a significant escalation, acknowledging that the group's activities posed a danger to the democratic order. In France, the government took even more drastic action. In 2021, Génération Identitaire was banned for racial incitement, violence, and paramilitary ties. The ban was a direct response to their tactics, which often involved staging dramatic, militaristic demonstrations, such as the "Defend Europe" campaign, where activists chartered a boat to intercept migrant rescue vessels in the Mediterranean. These actions, designed to generate media coverage and provoke a reaction, were seen by authorities as a direct threat to public safety and social cohesion.

The Global Reach and the American Connection

Although rooted in Western Europe, the Identitarian movement has proven to be highly exportable. The ideas that resonated in the suburbs of Paris found a receptive audience among white nationalists in North America, Australia, and New Zealand. The movement's pan-European nationalism morphed into a broader pan-white nationalism in these contexts. In the United States, the Southern Poverty Law Center has classified many of these organizations as hate groups, describing them as racist, exclusionary, and in favor of ethnic separatism for whites. The American iteration of Identitarianism has been particularly influential in shaping the rhetoric of the alt-right, blending the European focus on ethno-cultural preservation with American grievances about political correctness and demographic change.

The movement's success in the digital age cannot be overstated. Unlike the street brawls of the 1980s neo-Nazis, the Identitarians are masters of the internet. They produce high-quality videos, memes, and podcasts that appeal to a younger, tech-savvy demographic. They have created a global network of chapters, offshoots, and like-minded groups that operate with a loose but effective coordination. This network has inspired movements as far afield as Chile, demonstrating the universality of their core message: the fear of being replaced.

However, the movement is not without its internal contradictions. Scholar Imogen Richards has noted that while Génération Identitaire is characteristic of the European New Right in its ideology, its tactics diverge significantly in the realm of economics. While traditional ENR thinkers are often anti-capitalist and critical of global trade, Identitarian spokespersons have enthusiastically embraced capitalism and commodification. They advocate for international trade and have built lucrative businesses selling merchandise, from t-shirts to books. This commercialization of the movement suggests a pragmatic adaptation to the very globalized world they claim to despise, prioritizing the spread of their ideas over ideological purity.

The Precursors and the Future of the Movement

To fully grasp the present, one must look at the precursors that laid the groundwork. The neo-Völkisch movement Terre et Peuple, founded in 1995 by Nouvelle Droite writers Pierre Vial, Jean Haudry, and Jean Mabire, is generally considered the direct precursor to the Identitarian movement. These groups began the work of redefining the concept of the nation, moving away from the "nation-state" to the "white nation." Dominique Venner and his magazine Europe-Action were instrumental in this shift, arguing that the nation was defined by blood and culture rather than borders or citizenship. From the 1990s onward, Venner, Vial, and Faye pushed for a stronger commitment to the Identitarian struggle, arguing that metapolitics alone was insufficient. They called for a cultural revolution against multiculturalism, Islam, and globalism.

The legacy of these thinkers is a movement that has successfully shifted the boundaries of acceptable political discourse. By framing their arguments in the language of identity, preservation, and survival, they have made the Great Replacement a talking point in mainstream political debates. They have forced politicians to address immigration and cultural change in ways that were previously taboo. The movement's ability to adapt, to move from the intellectual salons of the Nouvelle Droite to the digital streets of the 21st century, is a testament to its resilience.

Yet, the movement remains a paradox. It claims to defend tradition while embracing modern media tactics; it claims to be anti-racist while promoting racial separation; it claims to be pan-European while often relying on localist rhetoric. It is a movement built on the fear of death, not of individuals, but of a people. As the world becomes increasingly interconnected, the Identitarian vision of a world of segregated, homogeneous entities stands in stark opposition to the reality of global migration and cultural exchange. Whether this movement will fade into obscurity or evolve into a dominant political force remains to be seen. What is certain is that the ideas of Alain de Benoist, Guillaume Faye, and their successors have already left an indelible mark on the political landscape of the 21st century. The question is no longer whether the Identitarian movement exists, but how deeply its vision of a world without mixing has taken root in the collective consciousness of the West.

The movement's future will likely depend on its ability to navigate the tension between its radical roots and its desire for mainstream acceptance. As governments crack down on its activities, banning groups and classifying them as extremists, the Identitarians will be forced to adapt once again. They may retreat further into the digital realm, or they may seek to infiltrate mainstream conservative parties. Regardless of their path, the core of their message—the fear of replacement and the desire for a homogeneous identity—resonates with a significant portion of the population. In a world of rapid change and uncertainty, the Identitarian promise of a return to a static, pure past offers a seductive, if dangerous, comfort. The movement is not just a political entity; it is a symptom of a deeper anxiety about the future of identity in a globalized world. And as long as that anxiety persists, the Identitarian movement will have a home.
