Ethnopluralism

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Ethnopluralism

In 1973, German sociologist Henning Eichberg penned an essay that would inadvertently birth a modern political lexicon, coining the term Ethnopluralismus while attempting to critique both Western and European eurocentrism. He sought a language to describe a world where differences were preserved, not erased. Yet, within a decade, this term would be seized, repurposed, and weaponized by a different faction entirely. It became the intellectual engine of the European New Right, a sophisticated ideological project designed to replace the crude, biological racism of the mid-20th century with a "cultural" argument for segregation. Today, the concept known as ethnopluralism, or ethno-differentialism, stands as one of the most insidious and influential doctrines in contemporary far-right politics, offering a "right to difference" that, upon closer inspection, demands the absolute separation of peoples.

The core proposition of ethnopluralism is deceptively simple: distinct cultures must be kept separate to survive. Proponents argue that the mixing of cultures within a single territory leads to a homogenized global monoculture, a "one-world doctrine" that dilutes the unique essence of every ethnic group. To prevent this "progressive disappearance of diversity," they advocate for a world map redrawn along ethno-cultural lines, where borders are not just political but biological and cultural boundaries. According to this model, significant foreign cultural elements in a given region ought to be culturally assimilated to seek homogenization within that specific territory. The goal is to let different cultures thrive only in their respective geographical areas, creating a global mosaic of isolated, self-contained ethno-states.

"The greatest world danger is the progressive disappearance of diversity from the world, including the biodiversity of animals, cultures and peoples."

This quote, attributed to Alain de Benoist in 2002, captures the environmentalist veneer that often cloaks the ideology. De Benoist, the founding member of the Nouvelle Droite (New Right) and the primary architect of modern ethnopluralism, frames the preservation of human culture as an ecological imperative. He posits that just as we protect endangered species in their natural habitats, we must protect indigenous European cultures from the encroachment of non-European influences. To the uninitiated observer, this sounds like a plea for cultural conservation. However, the mechanism for achieving this conservation is the strict enforcement of ethnic borders and the rejection of multiculturalism at the national level.

The intellectual lineage of this movement is rooted in a deliberate strategic shift. In the 1950s and 1960s, the French neo-fascist activist René Binet articulated a concept he called "biological realism." Binet argued that inequalities between individuals and races were founded upon scientific observations. He railed against "interbreeding capitalism" (capitalisme métisseur), which he claimed aimed to create a "uniform barbary" (barbarie uniforme). For Binet, the only solution was "a true socialism" that could achieve "race liberation" through "absolute segregation at both global and national level." While Binet's language was blunt and explicitly racial, the architects of the New Right recognized that such overt racism had become politically toxic in the post-Holocaust era. They needed a new vocabulary.

This is where the "cultural turn" occurred. The GRECE think tank, led by de Benoist, began promoting "ethno-differentialism" from the 1970s onward. The goal was to imitate the egalitarian, anti-totalitarian, and antiracist discourses of the progressive movement while advancing a fundamentally similar agenda. Scholar Daniel Rueda describes this as the passage from biological and pseudoscientific racism to "alterophobic discourses based on culture and ethnicity." By shifting the debate from biology to culture, the New Right could claim to be fighting for diversity while advocating for what critics have rightly termed "global apartheid."

The semantic shifts were subtle but profound. The 1960s saw the rise of the magazine Europe-Action, where de Benoist worked as a journalist. This publication drew influence from the "Message of Uppsala," a text likely written in 1958 by French far-right activists related to the New European Order, a neo-fascist movement led by Binet. This text carried out a careful maneuver, shifting the focus from "inequality" to "differentialism." The argument was no longer that one race was superior to another in a biological sense, but that different cultures were so distinct that they could not coexist without destroying one another. This "right to difference" became the rallying cry, allowing proponents to claim support for cultural diversity at a worldwide level while vehemently opposing it at the national level.

The difficulty in defining ethnopluralism clearly lies in the fact that its proponents can oscillate between a genetic and a cultural definition of "difference." De Benoist himself exemplifies this fluidity. In the 1960s, he supported an ethno-biological perspective, even endorsing South African apartheid during the same decade. However, as the political climate shifted, he gradually adopted a more dual approach. Inspired by Martin Buber's philosophy of dialogue and the Ich und Du (I and You) concept, de Benoist redefined "identity" in his 2006 work We and the Others (Nous et les autres). He described identity as a "dialogical" phenomenon, composed of an "objective part" derived from one's background (ethnicity, religion, family, nationality) and a "subjective part" freely chosen by the individual.

In this revised framework, identity is a process in constant evolution rather than an immutable notion. This allowed de Benoist to distance himself from the crude racial determinism of the past. In 1992, he explicitly dismissed the Front National's use of ethnopluralism, arguing that it portrayed "difference as an absolute, whereas, by definition, it exists only relationally." Yet, the practical application of his theories remained deeply exclusionary. The "dialogical" identity did not imply an open door to integration; rather, it reinforced the idea that one's roots are fundamental and that mixing these roots leads to the loss of self.

This ideological flexibility has proven to be a powerful tool for the far right. In the 1970s, Guillaume Faye, a key figure in the movement, argued that "immigration, rather than immigrants, should be combated" to preserve cultural and biological identities on both sides of the Mediterranean. By 2000, however, Faye's rhetoric had hardened significantly. In his book The Colonization of Europe, he preached "total ethnic war" between "original" Europeans and Muslims, a stance that eventually led to his criminal conviction for hate speech. The evolution from Faye's earlier cultural preservationism to his later calls for war illustrates the underlying volatility of the ideology: the "cultural" argument is often a thin veil for a demand for ethnic cleansing.

Critics have been swift to identify the strategic nature of this discourse. Philosopher Pierre-André Taguieff has labeled ethnopluralism a "strategic attempt to disguise racial supremacist ideas behind an anti-totalitarian and egalitarian discourse." Political scientist Blair Taylor notes that many contemporary far-right groups have "traded in the language of overt white supremacy for ethnopluralism, a vision wherein distinct groups live separately but allegedly equal, free to pursue their ethnic interests." This shift has allowed the ideology to embed itself in the discourse of diversity, capitalizing on progressive associations with environmentalism and antiracism.

The logic of ethnopluralism leads to a world of "separate but equal" enclaves, a doctrine that critics describe as "global apartheid." In this vision, there is no agreement among proponents on the definition of group membership or where these hypothetical borders would lie. Some advocate for limiting Europe to "true Europeans," defined strictly by descent. Others propose much smaller divisions, similar to an ethnically-based communitarianism. De Benoist has proposed that ethnic and social territories should be as small as possible, suggesting a scenario where Muslims might be allowed some territories subordinated to Sharia law within the European continent. This is not a call for integration or coexistence; it is a call for partition.

"The concept emphasizes the separation of varying ethno-cultural groups, as opposed to cultural integration and intra-state multiculturalism."

The implications of this worldview are staggering. It rejects the idea of a shared public sphere where different cultures interact, negotiate, and evolve together. Instead, it envisions a static world where cultures are preserved in amber, isolated from one another to prevent the "contamination" of their purity. The "right to difference" is thus transformed into a right to separation. The "diversity" championed by ethnopluralists is not the vibrant, messy, and dynamic diversity of a multicultural city, but a curated, museum-like diversity of isolated exhibits.

Left-wing historian Rasmus Fleischer has offered a chilling speculation on the hidden logic of this map. He suggests that in the vision of these "multi-fascists," certain groups are implicitly absent. Jews and Roma, he argues, have no place in the ethnopluralist world map because they are minorities that do not have a defined territorial homeland. In the utopia of the ethnopluralists, these groups should be "eliminated in order to make room for a peaceful utopia." The dream of a world of pure, separated cultures inevitably requires the removal of those who do not fit neatly into the designated boxes.

The influence of ethnopluralism has spread far beyond the intellectual salons of Paris and Berlin. From the 1970s onward, these views have become popular among European far-right groups. More recently, the ideology has found fertile ground in the alt-right circles of North America. The language of "ethnopluralism" has been adopted by movements that seek to frame their exclusionary politics as a defense of local cultures against the homogenizing forces of globalization. It provides a sophisticated vocabulary for those who wish to oppose immigration without appearing to be racists. They do not say "white people are superior"; they say "cultures must remain separate to survive."

The connection to the environmental movement is particularly striking and deliberate. By framing cultural mixing as a threat to "cultural biodiversity," ethnopluralists have attempted to co-opt the moral authority of the environmentalist movement. Just as the introduction of invasive species can destroy a local ecosystem, they argue, the introduction of foreign cultures can destroy the local cultural ecosystem. This analogy is flawed, of course, as cultures are not biological species and human interaction is not ecological invasion. But the rhetorical power of the analogy is undeniable. It allows proponents to present themselves as guardians of the planet's cultural heritage, fighting against the "uniform barbary" of global consumerism.

Yet, the contradictions in the theory are numerous. If the goal is to preserve the uniqueness of every culture, how does one justify the dominance of one culture over another within its own territory? Ethnopluralism tends to rely on the cultural assimilation of foreign cultural norms to preserve the inherent forms of a given ethno-culture. This creates a paradox: to preserve the "purity" of the local culture, the foreigner must be forced to become like the local. But if they become like the local, they are no longer "different," and the "right to difference" is negated. If they remain different, they are a threat to the homogeneity of the territory. The only solution, in the ethnopluralist view, is to ensure they never arrive in the first place.

The concept of "indigenous" rights has also been hijacked by this movement. De Benoist and others have argued that indigenous cultures in Europe are being threatened by the liberal worldview promoted by the United States. They propose a pan-European nationalism founded on the idea of ethnopluralism as a defense mechanism. This framing is particularly insidious because it appropriates the language of indigenous rights, which is typically used by marginalized groups to resist colonization, and turns it around to justify the exclusion of immigrants. The "indigenous European" becomes the victim, and the immigrant becomes the colonizer.

The impact of this ideology on public discourse has been profound. It has forced a re-evaluation of what it means to be "multicultural." The traditional view of multiculturalism, which emphasizes the coexistence of different cultures within a single nation-state, is now under attack from a new quarter. The ethnopluralist alternative offers a vision of "diversity" that is spatially segregated. It is a diversity of regions, not of communities. In this world, a person's culture is determined by their geography, and their geography is determined by their ethnicity.

Scholars have highlighted the close ideological similarities between ethnopluralism and the ideas promoted by French neo-fascist activists in the 1950s and 1960s. The "Message of Uppsala" and the writings of René Binet laid the groundwork for the semantic shifts that would follow. The transition from "biological racism" to "cultural differentialism" was not a rejection of the old ideas but a refinement of them. The core belief in the necessity of separation and the inherent incompatibility of different groups remained intact; only the justification changed.

The legacy of this movement is now being felt in the political arenas of the 21st century. The "Identitarian movement," which is closely associated with the European New Right, has adopted ethnopluralism as its core tenet. These groups organize protests, publish manifestos, and run social media campaigns that promote the idea of "remigration" and the defense of European identity. They are the modern face of an old ideology, dressed in the clothes of the new right.

As we look at the world today, the shadow of ethnopluralism looms large. It offers a seductive narrative for those who feel threatened by the rapid changes of globalization. It promises a return to order, to purity, to a world where everyone knows their place. But the cost of this order is the loss of the very thing that makes human civilization dynamic and resilient: the ability to cross boundaries, to learn from others, and to create something new from the meeting of different worlds. The "right to difference" that ethnopluralists champion is, in the end, a right to remain in isolation, a right to fear the stranger, and a right to build walls where bridges should be.

The essay of ethnopluralism is still being written, but the plot is clear. It is a story of a movement that sought to reinvent racism for the modern age, trading the language of biology for the language of culture, and the rhetoric of supremacy for the rhetoric of diversity. It is a testament to the power of ideas to shape the world, and a warning of how easily the language of liberation can be twisted into the language of exclusion. The "global apartheid" of the future may not be enforced by barbed wire and guard dogs, but by the subtle, insidious logic of the "right to difference," a logic that demands we live apart to survive, and in doing so, ensures that we never truly live at all.

"Many contemporary far-right groups have traded in the language of overt white supremacy for ethnopluralism."

The challenge for those who believe in a truly diverse and integrated world is to see through the mask. To recognize that the "cultural" argument is often just the "racial" argument in a new suit. To understand that the preservation of culture does not require the isolation of people. And to realize that the only way to truly protect the "biodiversity" of human culture is to embrace the messiness of mixing, rather than seeking the sterile purity of separation. The ethnopluralist vision is a world of museums, where cultures are preserved behind glass, safe from the touch of the other. But a living culture is not a museum piece; it is a living, breathing, changing thing that grows only through contact with the world.

In the end, the history of ethnopluralism is a history of a failed experiment in human segregation. It is a reminder that the desire to keep the world "pure" is a desire to keep it dead. The true diversity of the human species lies in our ability to cross boundaries, to learn, to adapt, and to create a shared future. The ethnopluralist dream of separate but equal worlds is a nightmare of isolation and fear. It is a dream that must be awakened from, if we are to have any hope of a future that is truly plural, truly diverse, and truly human.

The intellectual journey from Henning Eichberg's 1973 essay to the alt-right rallies of the 2020s is a testament to the adaptability of hate. It shows how a concept can be stripped of its original context, repackaged, and sold as a new solution to old problems. It is a story of how the language of the left can be co-opted by the right, how the arguments of the progressive movement can be turned against it. And it is a story that is far from over. As long as there are those who fear the other, there will be those who offer them the comfort of separation. The task of the present is to reject that comfort, to embrace the difficulty of coexistence, and to build a world where difference is not a reason for walls, but a reason for bridges.

The concept of ethnopluralism, with its roots in the neo-fascist movements of the mid-20th century and its branches in the contemporary far right, remains a potent force in global politics. It is a doctrine that promises order in a chaotic world, but delivers only isolation. It promises to preserve culture, but in doing so, it kills the very thing that makes culture alive. It is a warning for our times, a reminder that the struggle for a just and diverse world is not just a struggle against overt racism, but against the subtle, insidious ideologies that seek to divide us in the name of preserving our differences. The future of our world depends on our ability to see through these deceptions and to choose a path of connection over separation, of unity over division, and of life over the sterile purity of the grave.
