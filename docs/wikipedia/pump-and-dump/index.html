Pump and dump

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Pump and dump

In 2000, the Securities and Exchange Commission filed a civil lawsuit against a stock manipulator who had made hundreds of thousands of dollars through false and misleading statements. The defendant was fifteen years old.

Jonathan Lebed had discovered something that Wall Street veterans had known for decades: with enough hype, you can make almost anyone believe almost anything about a stock. The teenager bought penny stocks—shares in tiny companies trading for less than five dollars—then flooded internet message boards with breathless predictions about their imminent rise. When other investors took the bait and started buying, the price climbed. Lebed sold. The new investors were left holding shares that promptly collapsed back to their original value, or lower.

This is the essence of a pump and dump scheme. It's one of the oldest frauds in financial markets, and despite centuries of regulation and enforcement, it remains stubbornly effective.

The Mechanics of Manufactured Excitement

A pump and dump works in two phases, exactly as the name suggests.

First, the pump. A fraudster acquires shares in a company—typically a small one with a low share price and thin trading volume. Then they begin promoting the stock through whatever channels they can access. In earlier eras, this meant cold calls from boiler rooms, those high-pressure telephone sales operations where rows of brokers worked the phones, pushing stocks on unsuspecting investors. The 2000 film Boiler Room dramatized this world with uncomfortable accuracy.

The internet changed everything. Suddenly, one person could reach millions of potential victims for essentially no cost. Spam emails became the weapon of choice. One study of 75,000 unsolicited emails found that about fifteen percent of all spam was devoted to stock promotion. The researchers calculated that spammers could earn an average return of 4.29 percent using this method, while the recipients who actually acted on the tips lost nearly 5.5 percent of their investment within just two days.

Today, social media platforms like Twitter, Telegram, and Discord have become the preferred hunting grounds. The technology changes, but the psychology remains constant: create excitement, suggest insider knowledge, imply that this is a rare opportunity that won't last.

Then comes the dump. Once enough new investors have bought in—driving the price up through their own demand—the fraudster sells everything. The price collapses. The new investors are left holding worthless or nearly worthless shares, having essentially transferred their money directly to the person who deceived them.

Why Penny Stocks Are Perfect Targets

Not all stocks are equally vulnerable to manipulation. The shares of large companies traded on major exchanges like the New York Stock Exchange or NASDAQ are relatively resistant to pump and dump schemes for several reasons.

First, there's volume. When millions of shares trade hands every day, no single actor can significantly move the price without enormous capital. Second, there's scrutiny. Large companies have analysts following them, journalists covering them, and regulatory attention focused on them. Any false claims about their business would be quickly challenged.

Penny stocks offer the opposite environment. These are shares in tiny companies—often called microcaps—that trade for just a few dollars or even cents. They're typically found on over-the-counter markets rather than formal exchanges. In the United States, this means venues like the OTC Bulletin Board or the Pink Sheets. In the United Kingdom, it's the Alternative Investment Market (known as AIM) or OFEX.

These stocks trade thinly. A few thousand dollars in purchases can meaningfully move the price. There are no analysts covering them, no journalists investigating them, and often little public information available about the companies at all. Into this information vacuum, the pump and dump operator pours false promises.

The Wolf and Other Famous Schemes

The most notorious pump and dump operation of the 1990s was run by a Long Island firm called Stratton Oakmont. Its co-founder, Jordan Belfort, orchestrated a sprawling fraud that artificially inflated stock prices through systematic deception, then sold at the peak while ordinary investors absorbed the losses.

Belfort was eventually convicted and served prison time. He later wrote a memoir about his crimes called The Wolf of Wall Street, which Martin Scorsese adapted into an Academy Award-nominated film starring Leonardo DiCaprio. The movie made Belfort something of a celebrity, a morally complex outcome for a story about financial predation.

But pump and dumps aren't always the work of stereotypical Wall Street villains. Consider the Enron case. Before the energy giant's spectacular collapse in 2001, twenty-nine of its executives sold their overvalued shares for more than a billion dollars combined. They knew the company's reported profits were fictional, inflated through accounting practices that bordered on or crossed into fraud. They pumped through press releases and analyst calls, then dumped while employees and ordinary shareholders were still being told everything was fine.

The Langbar International case demonstrated how the scheme could work on a truly grand scale. Originally called Crown Corporation, Langbar operated on the Alternative Investment Market in London. At its peak, the company was valued at over one billion dollars based on bank deposits in Brazil. Those deposits didn't exist. The fraud eventually unraveled, but remarkably, none of the chief conspirators were convicted. Investors lost as much as one hundred million pounds. They later sued one of the fraudsters and recovered thirty million—a significant sum, but less than a third of what they'd lost.

The Cryptocurrency Wild West

When cryptocurrency emerged as a new asset class, pump and dump operators found paradise.

The crypto market combines several features that make it extraordinarily vulnerable to manipulation. It's largely unregulated, meaning many of the rules that govern traditional securities don't apply. Ownership is highly concentrated, with a small number of wallets often holding large percentages of any given coin's total supply. Many cryptocurrencies, especially newer or smaller ones, have low trading volumes, making them easy to move with relatively modest capital.

And there's the culture. Cryptocurrency communities often celebrate volatility as opportunity. The line between genuine enthusiasm and coordinated manipulation can be difficult to discern, which provides cover for schemes that would be obviously suspicious in traditional markets.

Organized pump and dump groups now operate openly on platforms like Telegram and Discord. Some are remarkably brazen about their intentions, recruiting participants who understand they're joining a scheme. The early participants who buy before the announcement hope to sell to later participants who buy during the pump. It's a game of musical chairs where everyone believes they'll find a seat before the music stops.

Hack, Pump, and Dump: A Modern Variant

As online brokerage accounts became ubiquitous, fraudsters developed a new approach. Rather than just promoting a stock, they would first acquire shares, then hack into other people's brokerage accounts and use those compromised accounts to buy the same stock.

This is elegant in its ruthlessness. The fraudster doesn't need to convince anyone of anything. They simply use stolen credentials to force purchases, driving up the price through the victims' own money. Day traders seeing unusual activity might pile in, adding to the momentum. The original stockholder sells at the peak, and the hacked account holders discover their portfolios have been drained into worthless positions.

Related Schemes and Distinctions

Pump and dump is sometimes confused with other financial frauds, but there are important differences.

A Ponzi scheme—named after Charles Ponzi, who famously executed one in the 1920s—also uses new investor money to pay returns to earlier investors. But Ponzi schemes are typically private arrangements between people who know each other, promising specific returns over extended periods. They can last for years or even decades before collapsing. Bernie Madoff ran his Ponzi scheme for approximately seventeen years before it unraveled in 2008.

Pump and dumps are faster and more anonymous. They target public markets, involve strangers, and execute over days or weeks rather than years. They make general promises of profits rather than specific returns. And while Ponzi schemes sometimes begin as legitimate investments that fail to perform, pump and dumps are fraudulent from conception.

There's also a mirror-image variant called short and distort. In a regular pump and dump, the fraudster buys first, promotes second, and sells at the inflated price. In a short and distort, the fraudster bets against the stock first—through a mechanism called short selling, which profits when prices fall—then spreads negative information to drive the price down, then closes their short position at the lower price.

Scalping occupies related territory. A scalper takes a position in a stock, then recommends it to others without disclosing that they plan to sell. The recommendation creates buying pressure, the price rises, and the scalper exits. It's subtler than a full pump and dump, but it operates on the same principle of profiting from information asymmetry.

The Regulatory Response

Governments have tried to combat pump and dump schemes through both broad securities regulation and targeted penny stock rules.

In the United States, the Securities and Exchange Commission (the SEC, the federal agency that oversees securities markets) and the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA, a private self-regulatory organization for the brokerage industry) have developed specific regulations for penny stocks. These rules impose additional disclosure requirements and restrict certain sales practices for low-priced securities.

Georgia was the first state to enact comprehensive penny stock legislation. Max Cleland, who was then Georgia's Secretary of State and would later serve as a United States Senator, championed the law. When it was challenged in court, it survived, and became a template for other states.

The regulations have had some success. Firms that specialized in penny stock manipulation, like Blinder, Robinson & Company, were shut down or severely constrained. Meyer Blinder himself went to prison for securities fraud in 1992.

But enforcement faces inherent limitations. The regulations work best against registered brokers and dealers who operate within the formal financial system. They're far less effective against unregistered groups and individuals operating through spam emails or social media, especially when those operators are overseas or use anonymizing technologies.

The Spam That Ate Wall Street

Pump and dump spam has unusual characteristics that make it harder to combat than other types of unwanted email.

Most scam emails require some response from the victim. Nigerian prince schemes—more formally known as advance fee fraud—need the recipient to write back and eventually send money. Lottery scams need the victim to provide bank details. This creates traceability. The scammer must receive something, which creates a point of contact that investigators can follow.

Stock spam doesn't work this way. The spammer buys shares before sending the messages, then sells on the same day the messages go out. The victim never contacts the spammer at all. They simply log into their own brokerage account, buy shares of the promoted stock, and lose money when the price falls. There's no communication to trace, no money changing hands between victim and perpetrator.

This has led to what researchers call minimalist spam: messages that contain nothing but a small image file showing a stock ticker symbol. No text to analyze, no links to follow, no reply addresses to trace. Just a picture and an implied promise.

Why People Keep Falling For It

The persistence of pump and dump schemes across centuries and technologies tells us something uncomfortable about human psychology.

People want to believe in shortcuts. The promise of quick profits triggers something deep—greed, certainly, but also hope. The victims aren't necessarily stupid or uneducated. The Enron executives who sold their shares weren't naive retail investors; they were sophisticated business people who understood exactly what they were doing. But on the buying side, even intelligent people can be swept up in narratives about the next big thing.

There's also social proof at work. When a stock price starts rising—even if that rise is artificially manufactured—it looks like confirmation that something real is happening. Other people are buying! The price is going up! If you wait, you'll miss the opportunity! The urgency that pump and dump promoters create isn't entirely artificial; once a pump begins, the price really is rising, and hesitating really might mean missing the peak.

Of course, the peak is followed by a cliff.

The Strange Afterlife of Morrie Tobin

Some pump and dump stories lead to unexpected places.

In April 2018, a financier named Morrie Tobin was caught running a pump and dump scheme through offshore accounts. He and his associates had made over 165 million dollars. When federal agents questioned him, Tobin tried to bargain for leniency by offering information about another scheme entirely.

He told them about a soccer coach at Yale University who was accepting bribes.

That tip led investigators to uncover what became known as the 2019 college admissions bribery scandal—a massive scheme in which wealthy parents paid millions to get their children admitted to prestigious universities through fraud and bribery. Actresses, financiers, and business executives were eventually charged.

Tobin pleaded guilty to conspiracy and securities fraud in February 2019. A federal judge ordered him to forfeit four million dollars. But his pump and dump scheme, which had been his undoing, became a footnote to a different story entirely—one about the lengths some parents would go to secure advantages for their children.

The connections between different forms of fraud and privilege often reveal themselves in unexpected ways. The same mindset that sees nothing wrong with artificially inflating stock prices might also see nothing wrong with artificially inflating college applications. Both involve using asymmetric information and access to gain advantages that would be unavailable in a fair system.

The Eternal Return

Pump and dump schemes have existed as long as there have been markets to manipulate. The South Sea Bubble of 1720, which devastated English investors and contributed to Isaac Newton losing a fortune, contained elements of coordinated price manipulation. The tools change—from coffeehouse whispers to boiler room calls to spam emails to Telegram groups—but the underlying dynamic remains.

Someone acquires an asset cheaply. They convince others that asset is worth more than it is. They sell at the inflated price. The later buyers absorb the loss.

Regulation helps at the margins. Enforcement catches some perpetrators. Education makes some potential victims more skeptical. But as long as there are information asymmetries, as long as some people have knowledge or access that others lack, and as long as human beings are susceptible to greed and hope and the fear of missing out, the pump and dump will persist.

The teenager Jonathan Lebed settled with the SEC by paying back a fraction of his gains. He neither admitted nor denied wrongdoing. He simply promised not to manipulate securities in the future. He was fifteen years old and had already learned a lesson that many adults never fully absorb: markets are not purely rational mechanisms for price discovery. They are arenas where psychology, information, and manipulation interact in complex ways.

Understanding how pump and dump schemes work won't make you immune to them. But it might make you pause the next time someone suggests you need to buy something quickly, before the opportunity disappears. In financial markets as in life, urgency is often a warning sign. The deals that require immediate action, that can't wait for careful consideration, are frequently the deals most worth avoiding.
