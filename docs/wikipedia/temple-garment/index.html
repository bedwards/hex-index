Temple garment

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Temple garment

In 1906, Joseph F. Smith, then president of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, declared any attempt to alter the pattern of the temple garment a "grievous sin." He viewed the garment as sacred, unchanged, and unaltered from the very pattern in which God gave it in the 1840s. For decades, this one-piece union suit, extending to the wrists and ankles with an open crotch and a high collar, was the immutable standard for the faithful. Today, that specific garment is largely a relic of history, preserved only by fundamentalist groups who reject modernization. The mainstream Church, however, has quietly revolutionized the very fabric of this sacred undergarment, transforming it from a rigid, ankle-length cotton suit into a modern, two-piece undergarment that buttons and zips, while insisting that the spiritual core remains exactly as Smith intended.

To the uninitiated, the temple garment is often dismissed as "Mormon underwear." This reductive label, frequently wielded by critics and anti-Mormon activists, misses the profound theological architecture that supports it. For adherents of the Latter Day Saint movement, particularly the LDS Church, the garment is not merely clothing; it is a constant, tactile reminder of the covenants made with God. It is worn only after an individual has participated in the endowment ceremony, a ritual that serves as the gateway to the temple. Once endowed, the garment becomes a non-negotiable requirement for temple entry. Without it, the sacred doors remain closed. It is viewed as a symbolic and, in the minds of many believers, a literal source of protection against both spiritual and physical harm. The garment is given during the washing and anointing portion of the endowment, donned under the white temple robes during the ceremony, and then worn beneath everyday clothing for the rest of the wearer's life.

The duality of the garment's function is its defining characteristic: it is both a private symbol and a public commitment. Church leaders have long taught that the garment serves as an outward expression of an inward commitment to follow Jesus Christ. Carlos E. Asay, a former general authority, articulated this purpose clearly, stating that the garment "strengthens the wearer to resist temptation, fend off evil influences, and stand firmly for the right." It is a spiritual armor, a daily touchstone that anchors the believer in their promises. Yet, the nature of the protection it affords is where the theology becomes nuanced. Researchers who have interviewed Latter-day Saints report that while virtually all wearers believe in its "spiritual protection," the belief in physical protection varies. Some adherents assert with absolute certainty that the garment saved them from car wrecks, fires, and natural disasters, weaving a rich tapestry of folklore around the fabric. Others remain uncertain of any physical aspect, viewing the protection as purely spiritual. The Church itself has navigated this ambiguity carefully. In a 2014 explanatory video, the Church released photographs of the garments and temple robes, explicitly stating that "there is nothing magical or mystical about temple garments." This distinction is crucial to the modern LDS identity: the power lies not in the cotton or the symbols themselves, but in the faith and covenants of the person wearing them.

The Architecture of Symbols

The garment is a canvas of sacred geometry, bearing symbols that have evolved in their interpretation but remained central to the faith. In the 1840s, when the garment was first introduced in Nauvoo, Illinois, it was a one-piece undergarment made of unbleached cotton. It featured four distinct marks snipped directly into the cloth. These were not mere decorations; they were sacred symbols with specific theological meanings. On the right breast was a reverse-L-shaped symbol; on the left, a V-shaped symbol. Horizontal marks appeared at the navel and over the right knee.

The interpretation of these symbols has shifted and deepened over the decades, reflecting the evolving understanding of the Church's leaders. In 1883, Church President John Taylor provided a detailed exposition of these marks. He identified the reverse-L shape on the right breast as "The Square," representing the justice and fairness of Heavenly Father. It signified a "square deal," ensuring that members would receive all the good they earned and all the good that came to them. The V-shaped symbol on the left breast was "The Compass," representing the North Star, a guide for the faithful. Taylor also interpreted the collar as the idea that the Lord's "yoke is easy and [his] burden is light," or alternatively, the "Crown of the Priesthood." The double-knotted strings that held the garment together symbolized the Trinity and the marriage covenant. The mark at the navel represented "strength in the navel and marrow in the bones," a reference to vitality and spiritual sustenance, while the mark over the knee stood for the biblical prophecy that "every knee shall bow and every tongue confess that Jesus is the Christ."

By 1926, the understanding of these symbols had been refined by David O. McKay, who would later become Church President. In an updated description that was incorporated into the endowment ceremony, McKay expanded the symbolism of the Compass. It now represented "an undeviating course leading to eternal life," a constant reminder that desires, appetites, and passions must be kept within bounds, and that all truth may be circumscribed into one great whole. The Square, in McKay's view, stood for "exactness and honor" in keeping the commandments. The navel mark shifted slightly in meaning to represent the "need of constant nourishment to body and spirit." The knee mark retained its biblical significance. Notably, McKay did not describe the collar or the tie-strings in his 1926 explanation, as those elements had already been eliminated from the garment design in 1922, marking the beginning of the garment's physical transformation.

These symbols are sacred, and their treatment is governed by strict protocols. The garment is considered the most sacred of all things in the world, next to the wearer's own virtue and purity of life, a sentiment echoed by Joseph F. Smith. Consequently, members feel uncomfortable discussing the garment in casual or disrespectful manners. It is not to be displayed publicly. In fact, the disposal of old or torn garments is a solemn act. Members are instructed never to throw them in the trash. Instead, they must be cut into pieces with scissors or burned, ensuring that the sacred symbols are never treated with contempt. This reverence extends to the digital age as well, where church members have criticized the sale of garments on online auction sites, viewing the commodification of the sacred as a violation of its sanctity.

The Evolution of the Form

The journey of the temple garment from a 19th-century union suit to a modern undergarment is a story of doctrinal flexibility meeting practical necessity. For several decades after its introduction in the 1840s, the original pattern was considered unalterable. The garment was a one-piece suit with long sleeves and long legs, held together by strings in a double knot, featuring an open crotch and a high collar. It was a product of its time, resembling the union suits worn by men and women of the Victorian era. Home-made garments were common, and the pattern was viewed as divinely ordained and static.

However, the winds of change began to blow in the early 20th century. In 1906, Joseph F. Smith's strong defense of the 1840s pattern seemed to lock the design in place. Yet, just two decades later, the Church recognized the need for adaptation. In 1923, Church President Heber J. Grant sent a letter to stake and temple presidents announcing a unanimous decision by the First Presidency and the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles to permit specific modifications. This decision was reached after "careful and prayerful consideration." The changes were significant: sleeves could now end at the elbow, legs could be shortened to just below the knee, and buttons could replace the traditional strings. The collar was eliminated, and the open crotch was closed. These modifications were a direct response to the changing fashions and the practical needs of a growing membership, yet they were framed not as a rejection of tradition, but as a prudent stewardship of the garment's purpose.

The evolution did not stop there. Other changes were made after 1923, shortening the sleeves and legs further and eventually eliminating buttons in favor of zippers and other modern fasteners. The transition was gradual, and the Church established Beehive Clothing Mills in the 1930s to produce the garments, ensuring quality control and standardization. This factory production replaced the era of home-made suits, bringing a new level of uniformity to the faithful. While the fundamentalist branches of the Latter Day Saint movement denounced these changes as a betrayal of the "unchanged" pattern, the mainstream LDS Church continued to refine the design. The modern garment is a far cry from the 1840s union suit, yet it retains the essential elements: the white color, the specific symbols, and the requirement of being worn beneath clothing.

The color of the garment has also been a point of standardization. In 1893, the Church expressed an official preference for white garments, which has since become the universal standard for the LDS Church. White symbolizes purity and the cleansing nature of the covenants. The only exception to this rule is for members of the military. Military personnel are permitted to submit regulation military T-shirts of any color to the Church for the custom addition of the symbolic markings. This accommodation acknowledges the unique constraints of military service while maintaining the spiritual significance of the symbols. For a time, sand-colored or "coyote tan" garments were available, but the Church's preferred supplier has ceased manufacturing them, returning the focus to the traditional white.

The Social and Cultural Tension

The temple garment exists in a complex social space, caught between the private sanctity of the believer and the public curiosity of the world. For the wearer, it is a deeply personal relationship with the divine. Church leaders have compared the garment to the clerical vestments worn by clergy in other churches—robes, collars, and stoles that signify a sacred office. Just as a priest does not wear a cassock to a bar or a rabbi does not wear a tallit to a baseball game, the temple garment is intended to be worn under clothing, hidden from public view. It is an intimate connection between the individual and God, a "constant reminder" that does not need to be flaunted.

However, the garment has frequently become a flashpoint for controversy. Anti-Mormon activists have occasionally publicly displayed or defaced temple garments to express their opposition to the LDS Church. These acts of desecration are designed to mock the sacredness the believers attribute to the undergarment. For the Church, such actions are a violation of religious freedom and an attack on the dignity of the faithful. The Church has responded by emphasizing the non-magical nature of the garment, hoping to demystify it and reduce the targets for such attacks. By stating that the power lies in the covenant and not the cloth, the Church attempts to shift the focus from the physical object to the spiritual reality.

The tension is also evident within the community. The sale of garments on online auction sites has drawn criticism from members who feel that the garment should never be treated as a commodity. The instruction to cut or burn old garments rather than discard them highlights the reverence with which the garment is held. It is not trash; it is a sacred artifact that must be retired with dignity. This internal culture of reverence often clashes with the external world's perception of the garment as a curiosity or a symbol of eccentricity.

The Enduring Legacy

Despite the changes in pattern, the evolution of symbols, and the controversies surrounding its public perception, the temple garment remains a cornerstone of Latter-day Saint identity. It is a tangible link to the early days of the Church in Nauvoo, a connection to Joseph Smith and the original endowment ceremony. Yet, it is also a living tradition, adapting to the needs of the 21st century while maintaining its spiritual core. The garment serves as a reminder that faith is not static; it is a dynamic relationship that requires constant nurturing and, occasionally, adaptation.

For the millions of Latter-day Saints who wear the garment, it is more than a piece of clothing. It is a shield against temptation, a badge of covenant, and a symbol of their commitment to Jesus Christ. Whether they believe it offers physical protection from car accidents or purely spiritual protection from the pressures of the world, the garment is a daily anchor. It is a quiet, unassuming presence under the shirt and pants, a secret kept between the wearer and their God. In a world that often demands public displays of faith, the temple garment stands as a testament to the power of the private, the hidden, and the sacred. It is a reminder that the most profound commitments are often the ones worn closest to the skin, unseen by the world but felt deeply by the soul.

The story of the temple garment is not just a story of fabric and sewing patterns. It is a story of a community navigating the tension between tradition and modernity, between the sacred and the secular. From the unbleached cotton of the 1840s to the modern, two-piece designs of today, the garment has endured. It has weathered the storms of criticism, the rigors of war, and the changing tides of fashion. And as long as there are members of the Church who are willing to make the covenants in the temple, the garment will continue to be worn, a silent witness to the enduring power of faith.
