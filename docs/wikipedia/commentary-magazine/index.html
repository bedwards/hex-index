Commentary (magazine)

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Commentary (magazine)

In 1945, as the smoke over Europe had yet to fully clear and the world was grappling with the unspeakable horrors of the Holocaust, a small monthly journal appeared on newsstands in New York City. It was not a newspaper screaming for attention, nor was it a glossy lifestyle publication. It was Commentary, a magazine founded by the American Jewish Committee under the editorship of Elliot E. Cohen. Its mission was audacious and specific: to construct a new American Jewish identity while processing the trauma of a genocide, the birth of a new state in the Middle East, and the rising shadow of the Cold War. For decades, this publication would serve as the intellectual engine room for a profound political migration, guiding a generation of Jewish thinkers from the radical left to the neoconservative right, fundamentally altering the landscape of American politics in the process.

To understand the magnitude of Commentary's influence, one must first understand the soil from which it grew. The magazine was the direct successor to the Contemporary Jewish Record, a publication that ran from 1938 to 1945 under the editorship of Morris Waldman, the executive secretary of the AJC. By 1944, with Waldman retiring, the American Jewish Committee found itself at a crossroads. They needed a new voice, one that could speak to the changing realities of American Jewry. They turned not to the usual academic circles, but to a group of New York City intellectuals, including the towering figures of Daniel Bell and Lionel Trilling. These men did not suggest another insular community newsletter. They recommended Elliot Cohen, a man who had previously edited a Jewish cultural magazine and was working as a fundraiser. Cohen was the architect of a grand design. He envisioned a journal that would do more than report news; he wanted to reconnect assimilated Jews and Jewish intellectuals with the broader, more traditional, and deeply liberal Jewish community. Simultaneously, he sought to bring the ideas of the young Jewish New York intellectuals to a wider, mainstream American audience.

Cohen's vision was articulated with striking clarity in the very first issue of the magazine. With Europe in ruins, he declared, "there falls upon us here in the United States a far greater share of the responsibility for carrying forward, in a creative way, our common Jewish cultural and spiritual heritage...to harmonize heritage and country into a true sense of at-home-ness." This was not merely a statement of cultural pride; it was a political manifesto. It signaled a definitive break from the past. Many, if not most, of the editors and writers who would populate Commentary had once been socialists, Trotskyites, or even Stalinists. Those days were over. The magazine was designed to demonstrate that Jewish intellectuals, and by extension all American Jews, had turned their backs on their past political radicalism to embrace mainstream U.S. culture and values.

The early years of Commentary were defined by a fierce, unyielding anti-Communism that was distinct from the crude hysteria of the McCarthy era. The magazine was anti-Communist, yes, but it was also anti-McCarthyite. It walked a razor's edge, identifying and attacking any perceived weakness among liberals on Cold War issues while simultaneously backing President Harry Truman's policies, such as the Truman Doctrine, the Marshall Plan, and NATO. The Commentary writers viewed the "soft-on-Communism" position of the Congress of Industrial Organizations (CIO) and the candidacy of Henry A. Wallace with deep suspicion. They were willing to alienate their former allies on the left to preserve the integrity of the liberal anti-Communist stance.

This stance often created friction even among those who hated Joseph McCarthy. When the Senator was at the height of his controversy, Irving Kristol, a key figure in the magazine's early circle, wrote a piece that sent a shockwave through the liberal intelligentsia. Kristol argued that the American people knew one thing about McCarthy: "he, like them, is unequivocally anti-Communist. About the spokesmen for American liberalism, the people feel they know no such thing." It was a brutal critique that suggested the liberal left had lost its way, failing to defend the nation against the Soviet threat with the same vigor as the man they despised.

The intellectual caliber of the magazine was unmatched. Cohen brought on board a roster of editors and contributors who were giants in their respective fields. There was Irving Kristol, the "godfather of neoconservatism"; Clement Greenberg, the preeminent art critic; Robert Warshow, the insightful film and cultural critic; and Nathan Glazer, the renowned sociologist. The pages of Commentary also featured work by Hannah Arendt, Daniel Bell, Sidney Hook, and Irving Howe. These were not mere commentators; they were the people defining the terms of the debate.

However, the trajectory of the magazine was not without its tragedies. By the late 1950s, the quality of the publication began to sag. The founder, Elliot E. Cohen, struggled with severe mental illness and ultimately died by suicide. His death left a vacuum that would soon be filled by a man who would steer the magazine in a radically new direction. In 1960, Norman Podhoretz, a protégé of Lionel Trilling, took over as editor. He would remain at the helm until his retirement in 1995, a tenure spanning thirty-five years.

Podhoretz described the mission of Commentary in vivid, almost biblical terms. He said the magazine was founded to lead Jewish intellectuals "out of the desert of alienation...and into the promised land of democratic, pluralistic, and prosperous America." Under his leadership, the magazine became the primary vehicle for the intellectual shift that would define the late 20th century. While the early issues were focused on integrating Jewish identity with American liberalism, the Podhoretz era became defined by a fierce opposition to the cultural and political shifts of the 1960s and 1970s.

The catalyst for this shift was the emergence of the New Left. This movement, which was bitterly hostile to President Lyndon B. Johnson, capitalism, and the universities, enraged Podhoretz. He perceived the New Left as shallow, hypocritical, and dangerously hostile to Israel. The Six-Day War of 1967 was a watershed moment. The reaction of the New Left to Israel's victory, which Podhoretz and his circle viewed as a defensive necessity against annihilation, was seen as a betrayal. The magazine turned its fire on the New Left, attacking its positions on crime, the nature of art, drug culture, poverty, and the new egalitarianism. Commentary did not mince words: it labeled the New Left a dangerous anti-American, anti-liberal, and antisemitic force.

One of the most controversial figures to utilize the platform was Daniel Patrick Moynihan. In a series of articles that would later be compiled into his famous work The Politics of a Paradox, Moynihan used Commentary to attack the Watts riots and the liberals who defended the uprising as a "just revolution." This stance was politically explosive, alienating the magazine from the Democratic Party establishment and signaling a permanent realignment. As Podhoretz shifted from a liberal Democrat to a neoconservative in the 1970s and 1980s, he moved the magazine with him to the right and toward the Republican Party.

This transformation was not merely a change in policy preferences; it was a fundamental rethinking of the role of the intellectual in society. The "neoconservative" movement, which found its intellectual home in the pages of Commentary, argued that the welfare state and the cultural revolution of the 1960s had undermined the moral fabric of the nation. They believed that a strong foreign policy and a commitment to American exceptionalism were necessary to counter the threats of the Soviet Union and the internal decay of American society. Commentary provided the voice for this movement, articulating a vision that would eventually be embraced by the Reagan administration and shape American foreign policy for decades.

The magazine's influence extended far beyond the pages of political theory. It became a cultural touchstone, referenced in the popular imagination in ways that few other intellectual journals ever have. The magazine is mentioned in three Woody Allen films, a testament to its status as a symbol of the New York intellectual class. In 1971's Bananas, an issue of Commentary is seen on a rack in a pornographic bookshop, a juxtaposition that highlights the absurdity of the intellectual's place in the world. Later in the same film, as an old woman is threatened on a subway car, Allen's character hides his face by holding up that very issue of the magazine, using the intellectual journal as a shield against the chaos of the street. This specific image was so iconic that it was displayed at the New York Transit Museum in Brooklyn Heights.

In 1977's Annie Hall, the magazine's reputation for dense, serious political discourse is the punchline of a joke. Allen's character, Alvy Singer, jokes that he heard that Dissent magazine (the rival publication of the Jewish left) and Commentary had merged to form "Dysentery." It was a dark, witty acknowledgment of the two poles of Jewish intellectual life. In 1989's Crimes and Misdemeanors, an issue of Commentary lies on a character's bedside table, a silent prop suggesting the character's engagement with the high-stakes moral and political debates of the era. The magazine's presence in these films underscores its role as a marker of a certain type of urban, intellectual identity.

Beyond the silver screen, Commentary maintained a strong presence in television. Between 1989 and 1992, in the ABC sitcom Anything but Love, standup comedian Richard Lewis was often shown holding or reading a copy of Commentary. This visual cue was not accidental; it was a shorthand for the character's intellect and his alignment with a specific political worldview. The magazine had become a prop in the cultural narrative of America, a symbol of the "thinking person's" engagement with the world.

The relationship between Commentary and its founding organization, the American Jewish Committee, eventually reached a breaking point. In 2007, the magazine ended its affiliation with the AJC. Commentary, Inc., an independent 501(c)(3) nonprofit enterprise, took over as publisher. This move granted the magazine greater editorial independence, allowing it to continue its evolution without the constraints of an institutional parent. It was a recognition that the magazine had outgrown its origins and had become a force in its own right.

The legacy of Commentary is preserved in physical form at the Harry Ransom Center at the University of Texas at Austin. In 2011, the journal donated its archives from 1945 to 1995 to the center. These archives include letters, essay revisions, and the raw materials of intellectual history, offering a glimpse into the process by which ideas were forged and refined. The collection serves as a testament to the meticulous work that went into every issue, every argument, and every shift in political direction.

Historians and critics have long debated the magnitude of the magazine's impact. Benjamin Balint, an American-Israeli journalist and former Commentary editor, has called it the "contentious magazine that transformed the Jewish left into the neoconservative right." This transformation is perhaps the single most significant political realignment of the post-World War II era. It was not a spontaneous shift; it was a carefully cultivated intellectual movement, nurtured and articulated within the pages of Commentary.

Richard Pells, a historian and literary critic, offered an even broader assessment of the magazine's significance. He stated, "no other journal of the past half century has been so consistently influential, or so central to the major debates that have transformed the political and intellectual life of the United States." Pells' observation highlights the unique position Commentary occupied. It was not just a publication; it was a catalyst. It shaped the minds of policymakers, influenced the direction of the Democratic and Republican parties, and redefined the terms of the debate on Israel, the Cold War, and American culture.

The journey of Commentary from a post-war Jewish cultural journal to the leading voice of neoconservatism is a story of intellectual courage and political consequence. It began with a desire to harmonize Jewish heritage with American life, a goal that seemed both noble and necessary in the aftermath of the Holocaust. But as the world changed, and as the New Left rose to challenge the very foundations of the liberal order, the magazine adapted. It did not retreat into the shadows of its past; it marched forward, taking its readers with it.

The editors and writers of Commentary were not afraid to be unpopular. They were willing to alienate their former friends on the left to defend what they saw as the core values of American democracy and the survival of the Jewish people. This willingness to stand alone, to argue against the prevailing winds of their time, is what gave the magazine its power. It was a place where ideas were tested, where arguments were sharpened, and where the future of American politics was debated with a seriousness that is rare in the modern media landscape.

Today, as the political landscape continues to shift and the old alliances fracture, the legacy of Commentary remains a potent force. The magazine continues to be edited by John Podhoretz, the son of Norman Podhoretz, carrying on the tradition of his father and the generations of intellectuals who came before him. The magazine still grapples with the same fundamental questions: What is the role of the intellectual in a democracy? How do we balance heritage with assimilation? What are the obligations of a nation in a dangerous world?

The story of Commentary is not just a history of a magazine; it is a history of an idea. It is the story of a group of people who believed that ideas mattered, that words could change the world, and that the intellectual life was a vital component of the American experiment. From the pages of Commentary, a new American Jewish identity was forged, and a new political reality was born. It was a journey from the desert of alienation to the promised land of democratic, pluralistic, and prosperous America, a journey that continues to shape the nation today.

The impact of Commentary is undeniable. It transformed the Jewish left into the neoconservative right. It challenged the New Left and redefined the liberal consensus. It provided a voice for the anti-Stalinist left and later for the anti-Communist right. It was a magazine that was not afraid to be controversial, to be wrong, or to be right. It was a magazine that mattered. And in a world where media is often ephemeral and disposable, Commentary stands as a testament to the enduring power of the written word.

The archives at the University of Texas serve as a reminder of the work that went into this transformation. The letters, the drafts, the revisions—they are the fingerprints of a generation of thinkers who shaped the course of history. They are a reminder that the ideas we hold today were forged in the fires of debate and struggle. Commentary was the crucible in which these ideas were formed. It was the place where the past was reconciled with the present, and where the future was imagined.

As we look back on the history of Commentary, we see a magazine that was always ahead of its time. It anticipated the shifts in the political landscape before they happened. It understood the dangers of the Cold War before they were fully realized. It saw the flaws in the New Left before they became apparent. It was a magazine that was willing to take risks, to challenge the status quo, and to push the boundaries of what was possible.

The legacy of Commentary is not just in the ideas it promoted, but in the people it inspired. It was a magazine that attracted the best and the brightest, the most thoughtful and the most courageous. It was a magazine that mattered to its readers, who saw in its pages a reflection of their own beliefs and a guide for their own lives. It was a magazine that was not afraid to be part of the conversation, to be part of the struggle, and to be part of the history of the nation.

In the end, Commentary was more than just a magazine. It was a movement. It was a voice. It was a force for change. And its story is a story that is still being written. The questions it asked are still being asked. The debates it sparked are still raging. And the legacy it left behind is a legacy that will endure for generations to come.

The magazine's ability to evolve while maintaining its core mission is a lesson for all who seek to make a difference in the world. It shows that it is possible to change one's mind without losing one's integrity. It shows that it is possible to adapt to new circumstances without abandoning one's principles. And it shows that it is possible to be a leader in a time of change, to be a voice for the future, and to be a force for good.

Commentary remains a vital part of the American intellectual landscape. It continues to challenge us to think, to question, and to engage. It continues to be a place where ideas are tested and where the future is imagined. And it continues to be a reminder that the power of the written word is still with us, still capable of changing the world, and still capable of shaping the course of history.
