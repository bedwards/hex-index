Traditional animation

16 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Traditional animation

In 1935, a single frame of film cost money, ink, and the labor of a human hand, yet it moved with a life that defied the static nature of the paper it was drawn upon. This was the era when traditional animation, also known as classical or cel animation, reigned supreme as the dominant storytelling medium of the 20th century in the United States. Unlike the digital fluidity of the 21st century or the rigid mechanics of stop-motion, this technique required that every single frame be drawn by hand. It was a marriage of extreme patience and artistic brilliance, a process so labor-intensive that it eventually gave way to the computational power of 3D computer animation. Yet, the ghost of the hand-drawn line remains; even in an age of pixels, the digital ink and paint pipeline owes its very existence to the rigorous protocols established by the animators of the mid-century. To understand the magic of a character leaping from a cliff or a shadow falling across a face, one must first understand the industrial machine that made it possible, a machine that began not with a pencil, but with a story.

The genesis of any traditional animation film is a quiet, paper-based alchemy that happens long before a single line of movement is committed to film. The process begins only after a narrative has been solidified into an animation film script. From this text, the visual world is born through the creation of a storyboard. These are not merely sketches; they are a shot-by-shot breakdown of the entire film, appearing somewhat similar to comic book panels but serving a far more functional purpose. They map the staging, the acting choices, and the precise camera moves that will dictate the viewer's experience. The storyboard artists are the first architects of the film's flow, planning the composition of the imagery and the rhythm of the plot.

"The images allow the animation team to plan the flow of the plot and the composition of the imagery."

This planning phase is rarely a straight line. Storyboard artists engage in regular, often grueling, meetings with the director. A sequence may be redrawn, or "re-boarded," dozens of times before it meets the final approval of the studio. It is a process of elimination and refinement, ensuring that the visual language of the film is as strong as its dialogue. Only when the visual map is complete does the process move toward the auditory realm, a shift that highlights a fundamental truth about traditional animation: sound leads, and image follows.

Before the first frame of animation is drawn, a preliminary soundtrack, known as a "scratch track," is recorded. This is a critical technical constraint of the medium. Given the slow, frame-by-frame nature of traditional animation, it is exponentially easier to synchronize the movement of a character to a pre-existing soundtrack than it is to synchronize a soundtrack to pre-existing animation. If a character is to sing, dance, or speak with precise lip-sync, the audio must be there first. A completed cartoon soundtrack will eventually feature a full orchestra, intricate sound effects, and dialogue performed by professional voice actors. However, the scratch track used during production is a skeletal version of this final product. It typically contains only the dialogue, any songs the characters must sing along to, and temporary musical score tracks. The final score and the rich tapestry of sound effects are reserved for post-production, a luxury that allows the animators to focus entirely on the visual performance.

There is a fascinating historical exception to this rule of pre-synchronization. In the case of Japanese animation (anime) and most pre-1930 sound animated cartoons, the sound was post-synched. In this method, the soundtrack was recorded after the film elements were finished. Animators would watch the finished film and perform the dialogue, music, and sound effects to match the existing action. Some American studios, most notably the Fleischer Studios, continued to post-synch their cartoons through most of the 1930s. This unique approach allowed for a level of spontaneity that pre-synchronization often stifled, giving rise to the "muttered ad-libs" that flavor many classic Popeye the Sailor and Betty Boop cartoons. The voice actors could react to the physical comedy already on the screen, creating a dynamic interplay between the visual and the auditory that defined an era of comedy.

Once the storyboards are sent to the design departments, the abstract begins to take concrete form. Character designers prepare model sheets for every character and prop that will appear in the film. These documents are the bibles of the animation house, used to standardize appearance, poses, and gestures across a team of dozens or even hundreds of artists. Without model sheets, a character drawn by one artist might look unrecognizable when drawn by another. These sheets often include "turnarounds," which show a character or object in three dimensions from multiple angles, alongside standardized special poses and expressions. They serve as a rigid guide, ensuring that Mickey Mouse looks like Mickey Mouse, whether he is drawn by a lead animator in California or an inbetweener in a distant studio.

"Small statues known as maquettes may be produced so that an animator can see what a character looks like in three dimensions."

These maquettes are physical sculptures of the characters, providing a tangible reference for the artists. In a similar vein, background stylists work to define the world the characters inhabit. They create similar reference materials for any settings and locations present in the storyboard. Working alongside the art directors and color stylists, they determine the overarching art style and the specific color schemes to be used. This phase is about establishing the visual DNA of the film, ensuring that the lighting, the textures, and the emotional tone of the colors are consistent from the first frame to the last.

The technical bridge between the design phase and the animation phase is the timing director, who in many cases is the main director themselves. They take the animatic—a rough assembly of the storyboard images timed to the scratch track—and analyze exactly what poses, drawings, and lip movements will be needed on specific frames. From this analysis, an exposure sheet, or X-sheet, is created. This printed table is a complex grid that breaks down the action, dialogue, and sound frame-by-frame, serving as the ultimate instruction manual for the animators. If a film is driven heavily by music, a "bar sheet" may be prepared in addition to or instead of an X-sheet. Bar sheets show the precise relationship between the on-screen action, the dialogue, and the actual musical notation of the score, allowing animators to hit the beat with mathematical precision.

Layout is the stage where the film truly begins to look like a film. After the designs are completed and approved, the layout artists determine the camera angles, camera paths, lighting, and shading of every scene. This is the first time the film is viewed as a continuous visual sequence rather than a collection of static drawings. Character layout artists determine the major poses for the characters in the scene and create a drawing to indicate each pose. For short films, this heavy lifting is often the responsibility of the director. The layout drawings and storyboards are then spliced together with the audio to form an animatic.

"The term 'animatic' was originally coined by Walt Disney Animation Studios."

An animatic typically consists of pictures of the storyboard timed and cut together with the soundtrack. This rough reel allows the animators and directors to work out any script and timing issues that may exist with the current storyboard. The storyboard and soundtrack are amended if necessary, and a new animatic may be created and reviewed with the director until the storyboard meets the user's requirements. Editing the film at the animatic stage is a crucial cost-saving measure; it prevents the animation of scenes that would eventually be edited out of the film. Creating scenes that will never be seen by the audience is a waste of thousands of dollars in labor, and the animatic ensures that every frame drawn has a purpose.

While the animatic is being refined, the background artists are hard at work painting the sets over which the action of each animated sequence will take place. These backgrounds are generally done in gouache or acrylic paint, though some animated productions have famously used watercolor or oil paint to achieve specific atmospheric effects. Background artists follow the work of the background layout artists and color stylists very closely, usually referencing a compiled workbook. Their goal is to ensure that the resulting backgrounds are harmonious in tone with the character designs, creating a unified visual world. The background is not merely a backdrop; it is a character in its own right, setting the mood and defining the space in which the drama unfolds.

With the design, layout, and backgrounds in place, the heavy lifting of the animation process begins. In the traditional animation process, animators will begin by drawing sequences of animation on sheets of transparent paper perforated to fit the peg bars in their desks. These peg bars are essential tools used to keep the drawings in perfect alignment. The animators work one picture, or "frame," at a time, often using colored pencils to distinguish between different layers of the drawing.

A key animator, or lead animator, is the first to tackle a scene. They draw the key frames or key drawings, using the character layouts as a guide. The key animator does not draw every single frame; instead, they draw enough frames to get across the major poses within a character performance. These key frames define the extremes of the movement, the start and end of an action, and the critical moments of expression. While working on a scene, a key animator will usually prepare a pencil test of the scene.

A pencil test is a much rougher version of the final animated scene, often devoid of many character details and color. The pencil drawings are quickly photographed or scanned and synced with the necessary soundtracks. Originally, pencil tests were shot on film with animation cameras, a slow process where animators had to wait for the developed film to come back before they could see the results. This delay could cost days of production time. However, in the late 1970s, the industry switched to a video system which stored the drawings on analog tape, making it possible to see the tests immediately. At first, reel-to-reel tape was used, but was soon replaced by U-matic and VHS formats. By the early 1990s, the animation tests were stored digitally, and eventually as files. These tests allow the animation to be reviewed and improved upon before passing the work on to their assistant animators.

"These tests allows the animation to be reviewed and improved upon before passing the work on to their assistant animators."

The assistant animators are the unsung heroes who take the key frames and begin to fill in the gaps. Their work is reviewed, pencil-tested, and corrected until the lead animator is ready to meet with the director and have their scene "sweatboxed." This term, "sweatboxed," refers to a screening session where the director and senior staff review the scene in a dark room, scrutinizing every movement and expression until the lead animator is sweating under the pressure. Once the key animation is approved, the lead animator forwards the scene on to the clean-up department.

The clean-up department is made up of clean-up animators and inbetweeners, and their job is to transform the rough, sketchy pencil drawings into the final, polished lines that will be inked and painted. The clean-up animators take the lead and assistant animators' drawings and trace them onto a new sheet of paper, making sure to include all of the details present on the original model sheets. This step is vital for maintaining cohesiveness and consistency in art style. A rough drawing might have wobbly lines or inconsistent thickness, but the clean-up artist ensures that the line weight is perfect and the shapes are true to the model.

Following the clean-up, the inbetweeners take over. Their task is to draw in whatever frames are still missing in-between the other animators' drawings. This procedure is called tweening. If a character's hand moves from point A to point B in ten frames, and the key animator has drawn the hand at point A and point B, the inbetweener must draw the eight intermediate frames that show the hand moving smoothly between the two points. This is where the illusion of life truly happens; the tweening creates the fluidity of motion that makes a character feel alive. The resulting drawings are again pencil-tested and sweatboxed until they meet approval.

At each stage during pencil animation, approved artwork is spliced into the leica reel. This process, which was once a physical splicing of film, is now often a digital assembly, but the principle remains the same. The leica reel serves as the master timeline, a running record of the film's progress. It allows the directors to see how the individual scenes fit together and ensures that the pacing of the film remains consistent. The leica reel is the backbone of the production, holding the fragmented pieces of the animation together until they become a cohesive whole.

Once the pencil animation is fully approved and the leica reel is complete, the drawings move to the inking and painting stage. In the days of true traditional animation, this was a laborious manual process. Artists would place the clean-up drawings onto transparent sheets of celluloid, or "cels." They would then trace the outlines of the drawings onto the cels with ink. Once the ink was dry, the cels were flipped over, and the reverse side was painted with opaque paint. The color was applied only to the areas that needed to be filled, leaving the outlines in black ink. These painted cels were then placed on top of the hand-painted backgrounds and photographed frame by frame.

"The process remains commonly used primarily in the form of digital ink and paint for television and film, especially when outsourced."

Today, the physical inking and painting of cels has largely been replaced by digital ink and paint. The scanned pencil drawings are imported into software where artists use digital brushes to trace the lines and fill in the colors. This digital pipeline offers significant advantages: it is faster, more cost-effective, and allows for easier corrections. If a color is wrong, it can be changed in seconds rather than requiring the repainting of a cel. Furthermore, the digital process eliminates the risk of physical damage to the artwork, such as fingerprints or scratches, which were common in the analog era. Despite this shift, the fundamental workflow remains rooted in the traditions of the 20th century. The concept of key frames, inbetweening, and the separation of characters from backgrounds is still the standard, even in the most advanced computer animation pipelines.

The transition to digital has not diminished the importance of the hand-drawn aesthetic; rather, it has liberated it. Artists can now experiment with textures, lighting effects, and camera moves that were once impossible with physical cels. However, the core philosophy of traditional animation endures: the belief that movement is a series of distinct moments, carefully crafted and sequenced to create the illusion of life. The shift to computer animation in the industry, particularly the rise of 3D computer animation, did not erase the hand-drawn tradition but rather forced it to evolve. While 3D animation allows for a level of realism and spatial complexity that hand-drawn animation struggles to match, the emotional resonance of a hand-drawn line remains unique.

The legacy of traditional animation is found in the sheer volume of work produced during its golden age. Studios like Disney, Warner Bros., and MGM produced thousands of cartoons and feature films that have become cultural touchstones. These works were not the product of a single genius but of a highly specialized, industrialized process involving hundreds of artists, each with a specific role. From the storyboard artist who mapped the visual journey to the inbetweener who filled in the gaps, every person played a crucial part in the creation of the final image.

As we look back on the history of animation, it is clear that the techniques developed during the era of traditional animation laid the groundwork for everything that followed. The principles of timing, spacing, and the squash-and-stretch techniques developed by animators like Frank Thomas and Ollie Johnston are still taught in animation schools today. The digital tools used by modern animators are built upon the logic of the exposure sheet and the leica reel. Even as the medium shifts, the soul of the animation remains in the hand that draws the line.

The story of traditional animation is a story of human ingenuity overcoming technical limitations. It is a testament to the power of the human hand to create movement, emotion, and life from a blank sheet of paper. While the tools have changed, from the peg bar to the stylus, from the animation camera to the rendering farm, the fundamental goal remains the same: to tell a story that moves the audience, frame by frame. The shift to computer animation was inevitable, a result of technological progress and economic necessity, but the spirit of the hand-drawn line continues to pulse through the industry. It is a reminder that behind every pixel and every polygon, there is a human hand, a human mind, and a human heart.

In the end, the dominance of traditional animation in the 20th century was not just a matter of technology, but of artistry. It was a time when the limitations of the medium forced artists to be more creative, more precise, and more deliberate. Every frame was a decision, every line a choice. The result was a body of work that continues to captivate audiences nearly a century later. As we move further into the digital age, the lessons of traditional animation remain as relevant as ever. They remind us that no matter how advanced our technology becomes, the most powerful tool in animation is still the imagination of the artist. The hand that draws the frame is the same hand that shapes the story, and as long as there are stories to tell, the spirit of traditional animation will live on.

The process described here is not merely a historical footnote; it is the foundation upon which the entire industry of visual storytelling is built. From the initial spark of an idea in a storyboard meeting to the final polish of a digital clean-up, the journey of an animated film is a testament to the collaborative power of human creativity. The transition from the physical cels of the past to the digital files of the present is a story of adaptation, but the core truth remains unchanged: animation is the art of bringing the inanimate to life, one frame at a time.
