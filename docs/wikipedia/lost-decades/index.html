Lost Decades

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Lost Decades

In 1989, the Imperial Palace grounds in Tokyo were worth more than all the real estate in California. Japanese companies dominated global rankings, with thirty-two of the world's fifty largest firms by market value calling Japan home. The future belonged to Japan, or so it seemed to everyone watching.

Then everything collapsed.

What followed wasn't a recession—those typically last a year or two—but something far stranger and more unsettling: three decades of economic stagnation that economists have come to call the Lost Decades. Japan's experience represents one of the most dramatic reversals of fortune in modern economic history, and the questions it raises about growth, debt, and policy remain deeply relevant today.

The Anatomy of a Bubble

To understand how Japan lost its way, you first need to understand how thoroughly intoxicated its economy became during the 1980s. The Bank of Japan, the country's central bank, was guiding commercial banks to lend more and more money, using a mechanism called "window guidance"—essentially telling banks how much they should expand their loan portfolios each quarter.

As economist Paul Krugman put it, Japan's banks "lent more, with less regard for quality of the borrower, than anyone else's." Money flooded into real estate and stocks. Land prices tripled. Stock prices tripled. Companies borrowed against inflated asset values to buy more assets, pushing prices even higher in a self-reinforcing spiral.

This wasn't irrational exuberance in the abstract. People had good reasons to believe Japan's economic miracle would continue indefinitely. The country had transformed itself from postwar devastation into the world's second-largest economy through extraordinary discipline, innovation, and coordination between government and industry. Toyota, Sony, Panasonic—these weren't just brands but symbols of Japanese excellence that had conquered global markets.

The bubble, in hindsight, was partly a victim of Japan's own success story. If the past several decades proved anything, it was that betting on Japan paid off.

The Day the Music Stopped

In late 1989, worried about speculation and inflation, the Bank of Japan did what central banks do: it raised interest rates. The inter-bank lending rate shot up sharply. This was supposed to cool things down gradually.

Instead, it popped the bubble entirely.

The stock market crashed. Real estate values plummeted. Within a few years, asset prices had fallen sixty percent from their peaks. Banks and insurance companies that had lent against those assets suddenly found their books full of loans that borrowers couldn't repay—what financial professionals call bad debt.

Here's where Japan's response diverged from the textbook crisis playbook. Rather than forcing banks to recognize their losses immediately, write down bad loans, and let failing companies fail, the Japanese government chose a different path. Banks received government capital infusions and cheap credit from the central bank. More importantly, they were allowed to postpone recognizing how bad their loan portfolios really were.

This created what economists would later call "zombie banks"—financial institutions that were technically alive but too weakened to actually perform their core function of channeling savings into productive investment.

The Zombie Economy

Zombie banks had a particularly insidious effect. They kept lending money to unprofitable "zombie firms"—companies that couldn't grow or compete but also couldn't be allowed to fail because they owed the banks so much money. If these firms went bankrupt, the banks would have to acknowledge their losses.

So the zombies shuffled on, consuming credit that could have gone to dynamic new companies, blocking creative destruction, and keeping wages suppressed because they couldn't afford to pay more. Economist Michael Schuman argued that Japan's economy didn't begin to truly recover until this practice ended—but by then, enormous damage had been done.

Eventually, waves of consolidation swept through the banking sector. Japan went from having numerous major banks to just four national institutions. But the path to that consolidation was agonizingly slow, and every year of delay was a year of stunted growth.

The Deflation Trap

As the 1990s ground on, something strange started happening to prices: they stopped rising. Then they started falling.

This is called deflation, and it sounds like good news if you've never experienced it. Who doesn't want things to cost less? But deflation creates a vicious psychological trap. If prices are falling, the smartest thing to do with cash is... nothing. Just hold onto it. Your money becomes more valuable simply by sitting in your pocket.

This logic infected Japanese corporations. Why invest in new factories if the products you make will sell for less next year? Why hire workers if you can produce the same output with fewer people? Why spend on research and development when you could just accumulate cash reserves?

Companies began hoarding money. They cut wages. They reduced investment. They stopped borrowing, which seems prudent for any individual company but is catastrophic when the entire business sector does it simultaneously.

Economist Richard Koo calls this a "balance sheet recession"—a situation where companies have been so burned by debt that they prioritize paying down what they owe over growing their businesses, even when interest rates are essentially zero. Japanese corporations in aggregate became net savers rather than borrowers. Corporate investment, normally a key engine of economic growth, fell by twenty-two percent of the country's total economic output between 1990 and 2003.

The Zero Lower Bound

Central banks have a standard playbook for recessions: cut interest rates. Lower rates make borrowing cheaper, encouraging companies to invest and consumers to spend. This typically sparks some inflation, which is exactly what you want when the economy is sluggish.

But Japan had already cut rates to essentially zero by the mid-1990s, and they stayed there. As of 2012, the official interest rate was 0.1 percent. It had been below one percent since 1994.

This created what economists call the "zero lower bound"—you can't push interest rates much below zero because people would simply withdraw their money as cash rather than pay a bank to hold it. With prices falling, even a zero percent nominal interest rate actually meant a positive real interest rate. Money was effectively earning a return just by existing.

The Bank of Japan was pushing on a string. Its main policy tool had become useless.

Unconventional Measures

By 2013, Japanese policymakers were desperate enough to try something new. The Bank of Japan launched what it called Quantitative and Qualitative Monetary Easing—essentially creating massive amounts of new money to buy government bonds and other assets, trying to force inflation into existence through sheer volume of currency.

In 2016, they went even further, introducing negative interest rates of minus 0.1 percent. This meant banks actually had to pay to keep reserves at the central bank, theoretically encouraging them to lend the money out instead.

These policies achieved... mild inflation. Around zero to one percent in the late 2010s. Better than deflation, but far short of the two percent target the Bank of Japan had set.

It took a global shock—the inflation surge of 2021 to 2023 driven by pandemic disruptions and supply chain chaos—to finally push Japan's inflation above two percent. But this created its own problems. While every other major economy was desperately raising interest rates to fight inflation, Japan found itself in the bizarre position of hoping inflation would stick around.

The Yen's Collapse

Keeping interest rates low while other countries raise theirs has a predictable side effect: your currency weakens. If you can earn four or five percent on dollars but near zero on yen, investors sell yen to buy dollars.

By July 2024, the yen had fallen to 161 yen per dollar, its weakest point in thirty-seven and a half years. The real effective exchange rate—a measure that accounts for inflation and trade patterns—hit 68.36 in June 2024, the lowest level since Japan began keeping records in 1970.

A weak currency makes your exports cheaper, which can help manufacturers. But it also makes imports more expensive, which hurts consumers and companies that depend on foreign inputs. More symbolically, the yen's collapse meant that Germany—whose economy had been roughly half the size of Japan's a decade earlier—surpassed Japan as the world's third-largest economy in nominal terms.

The Human Cost

Statistics about GDP growth rates can feel abstract. The human toll of the Lost Decades is more visceral.

Real wages in Japan fell around thirteen percent from their 1997 peak to 2013, an unprecedented decline for a developed nation. Household income in 2010 had fallen back to 1987 levels—meaning Japanese families were, in real terms, no better off than they had been a quarter century earlier.

The labor market transformed in troubling ways. Companies replaced permanent employees with temporary workers who had little job security and fewer benefits. By 2009, these non-traditional employees made up more than a third of the workforce. The famous Japanese system of lifetime employment, which had provided social stability and encouraged worker loyalty, was crumbling.

Many borrowers couldn't get credit from traditional banks and turned to sarakin—literally "salary lenders," a polite term for what in other countries would be called loan sharks, charging interest rates far above what mainstream financial institutions offered.

The conspicuous consumption of the 1980s, when Japan was riding high, simply never returned. A generation grew up without experiencing economic optimism.

Japan's Shrinking Place in the World

In 1995, Japan's nominal Gross Domestic Product—the total value of goods and services produced—was $5.55 trillion. Japan accounted for 17.8 percent of the entire world's economic output. The country's GDP per capita of $44,210 ranked third in the world, behind only Luxembourg and Switzerland.

By 2025, nominal GDP had fallen to $4.27 trillion. Japan's share of global output had shrunk to 3.6 percent. GDP per capita had dropped to $34,713, ranking thirty-sixth globally. The country was economically smaller in absolute terms than it had been thirty years earlier, while the rest of the world had grown enormously.

The comparison with Australia is particularly striking. In 1991, Japan's real output per person was fourteen percent higher than Australia's. By 2011, it had fallen to fourteen percent below Australia's. A complete reversal in just two decades.

Labor productivity tells a similar story. In 1990, Japan ranked sixth among the Group of Seven major economies, ahead of the United Kingdom. By 2021, Japan had fallen to dead last in the G7 and ranked twenty-ninth among thirty-eight members of the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development, the club of wealthy nations.

The Corporate Giants Fall

Remember those thirty-two Japanese companies in the world's top fifty by market capitalization in 1989? By 2018, exactly one remained: Toyota.

Sony, Panasonic, Sharp, Toshiba—companies that had dominated consumer electronics and defined Japanese technological prowess from the 1960s through the 1990s—found themselves fighting desperate rearguard actions against rivals from South Korea and China. Samsung, LG, and Chinese manufacturers ate into market share that Japanese firms had considered their birthright.

The aggregate sales of all Japanese companies fell by nearly fourteen trillion yen between 2000 and 2010. This wasn't just stagnation; this was shrinkage.

The Debt Mountain

With the private sector refusing to borrow and spend, the Japanese government stepped in. It ran fiscal deficits every year starting in 1991, trying to stimulate the economy through government spending when businesses and consumers wouldn't.

This kept Japan from falling into something resembling the Great Depression of the 1930s, when American GDP fell by forty-six percent. Koo argues this massive fiscal stimulus was the right policy—it offset the collapse in corporate investment and allowed Japan to maintain its basic standard of living.

But it came at a cost. By 2013, Japanese government debt had reached approximately 240 percent of GDP, the highest of any nation on earth. To put that in perspective, the United States government debt is considered alarming at around 120 percent of GDP. Japan's was double that.

Japan's debt situation is unusual in that most of it is held domestically—by Japanese citizens and institutions, including the Bank of Japan itself—rather than by foreign creditors. This makes it more sustainable than it might otherwise be. But the sheer size of the debt requires enormous interest payments just to service, crowding out other government spending and raising questions about long-term fiscal sustainability.

Abenomics: A Shot at Recovery

When Shinzō Abe became Prime Minister in December 2012, he launched an ambitious reform program that became known as Abenomics. His "three arrows" targeted Japan's core problems: chronic low inflation, declining worker productivity, and demographic challenges from an aging population.

The financial markets responded enthusiastically. The Nikkei 225, Japan's main stock index, rallied from around 9,000 points in 2008 to 20,000 in May 2015. Investors wanted to believe Japan could finally turn the corner.

But the effects on ordinary Japanese people were harder to detect. A Kyodo News poll in January 2014 found that seventy-three percent of respondents hadn't personally noticed any effects from Abenomics. Only twenty-eight percent expected a pay raise. And nearly seventy percent said they were planning to cut spending in response to an upcoming increase in the consumption tax—the exact opposite of what the policy was supposed to achieve.

Abe's government faced a dilemma familiar to anyone who has studied Japan's Lost Decades: fixing the country's deep structural problems required policies that would cause short-term pain. Voters don't like short-term pain, especially when they've already endured decades of stagnation.

The Pandemic's "Final Blow"

Just as Japan was showing tentative signs of recovery in 2018, the COVID-19 pandemic hit. Jun Saito of the Japan Center for Economic Research called it "the final blow" to Japan's long-struggling economy.

The pandemic was only the latest in a series of external shocks that interrupted any recovery momentum. The 2008 global financial crisis, the 2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami, the Fukushima nuclear disaster—each time Japan seemed to be getting back on its feet, something knocked it down again.

Has Japan Finally Escaped?

In February 2024, the Nikkei 225 reached 39,098.68, finally surpassing its bubble-era peak from nearly thirty-five years earlier. Some commentators declared that Japan had finally escaped the Lost Decades.

Not everyone agrees. Kazuo Momma, executive economist at Mizuho Information & Research Institute, argues that the stock market gains reflect corporate reforms at Japanese companies rather than genuine economic growth. GDP growth rates and consumer spending haven't improved dramatically. The demographic crisis continues to deepen. Government debt remains staggering.

The stock market is one measure of economic health, but it's not the only one—and not necessarily the most important one for ordinary people trying to support their families.

What Went Wrong? The Debate Continues

Economists still argue about what caused Japan's extended slump and what could have fixed it.

Paul Krugman points to a liquidity trap—the situation where monetary policy can't push interest rates any lower because they're already at zero. The massive asset bubble, inflated by banks lending recklessly due to implicit government guarantees, created moral hazard and crony capitalism. When the bubble burst, conventional policy tools were useless.

Richard Koo emphasizes the balance sheet recession—companies paying down debt instead of investing, regardless of how cheap borrowing became. In this view, massive government spending was the only thing that prevented catastrophe.

Scott Sumner argues that monetary policy was simply too tight throughout the Lost Decades, prolonging unnecessary pain.

Fumio Hayashi and Edward Prescott offer a more controversial explanation: the real problem was low productivity growth, not credit constraints. They argue that Japanese companies couldn't find profitable investment opportunities, which is why they didn't borrow—not because banks wouldn't lend. If this is true, then monetary and fiscal stimulus would only transform Japan from a low-growth, low-inflation economy into a low-growth, high-inflation economy, which hardly seems like progress.

Stuck on a Local Maximum

Political scientist Ian Lustick offers a different lens: Japan got stuck on what he calls a "local maximum." Imagine climbing a hill in dense fog. You keep going up until you reach a peak and can't go any higher without first going down. But there might be a much higher peak nearby—you just can't get to it without descending first.

Japan's institutions, policies, and corporate practices were optimized for the economic landscape of the 1970s and 1980s. They achieved remarkable success in that environment. But when the landscape changed, Japan's institutional rigidity made adaptation nearly impossible. Everyone could see what needed to change, but making those changes required first making things worse.

No politician wants to tell voters that things must get worse before they get better. So Japan stayed stuck on its local maximum while the rest of the world climbed higher peaks.

The Questions That Remain

Japan's Lost Decades aren't just Japanese history. They're a cautionary tale about what can happen when asset bubbles burst, when debt burdens become crushing, when deflation takes hold, when populations age, and when institutions built for one era struggle to adapt to another.

Many developed nations face some combination of these challenges today. Japan's experience suggests that even wealthy, technologically advanced, well-governed societies can fall into traps that prove remarkably difficult to escape.

The most haunting aspect of the Lost Decades might be their inevitability once they began. Each year of delayed reform made the next year's reform harder. Each year of deflation made the next year's deflation more likely. Each year of stagnation convinced another cohort of young Japanese that this was simply how the economy worked.

Japan is still trying to escape. Whether it truly has—or whether the Lost Decades have merely transformed into something else—remains an open question that Japanese policymakers, and the Japanese people, continue to grapple with every day.
