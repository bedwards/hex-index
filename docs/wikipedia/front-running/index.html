Front running

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Front running

In the frantic, paper-choked corridors of early 20th-century Wall Street, a broker could literally outrun his colleagues. When a client handed over a slip of paper with a massive order to buy or sell stock, the routine was for a runner to carry that slip at a walking pace to the trading desk. But a dishonest broker could sprint ahead, placing a personal order in his own account just before the client's massive block trade hit the floor. By the time the client's order arrived, driving the price up or down, the runner was already positioned to cash in. This was the literal origin of "front running," a term born from the physical act of racing ahead of a crowd, which has since evolved from a sprint down a hallway into a sophisticated, algorithmic form of market manipulation that siphons billions from the public trust.

While the image of a broker in a three-piece suit sprinting down a marble corridor is charmingly archaic, the core mechanism remains a violation of the most fundamental contract in finance: the duty of loyalty. Front running, also known as tailgating, is the practice of entering into an equity trade, option, futures contract, derivative, or security-based swap to capitalize on advance, nonpublic knowledge of a large pending transaction. It is the financial equivalent of seeing the cards of the player before you and betting accordingly. In essence, it means using the knowledge of an impending trade to engage in a personal or proprietary securities transaction in advance of that trade.

The stakes are not merely theoretical; they represent a direct transfer of wealth from the uninformed to the informed, often at the direct expense of the very clients the trader is supposed to serve.

The Mechanics of the Squeeze

To understand why front running is so pernicious, one must first grasp the concept of market impact. When a large institution, such as a pension fund or a mutual fund, decides to buy a massive block of shares—say, 400,000 shares of a mid-cap stock—the sheer volume of that demand will inevitably push the price up. The market is a fluid system; a sudden wave of buying absorbs the available liquidity, forcing the price higher to entice more sellers. A broker who knows this order is coming has a crystal ball.

Consider the mechanics of a classic scheme. A broker receives a market order from a customer to buy that large block of 400,000 shares. Before the broker places the customer's order into the system, they quietly purchase 20,000 shares of the same stock for their own personal account at $100 per share. Then, they execute the customer's massive order. This influx of buying pressure drives the market price up to $102 per share. The broker immediately sells their 20,000 shares at $101.75, locking in a risk-free profit of roughly $35,000 in a matter of seconds.

That $35,000 profit is not magic; it is a tax on the customer. The customer, who intended to buy at $100, now has to pay an average price significantly higher due to the price distortion caused by the very trade they placed. The broker has prioritized their own financial interest over the client's, a clear breach of fiduciary duty. In a large scheme, the front-running orders may approach or even exceed the size of the victimized firm's orders, pushing the price so far away from the firm's target that the client's execution becomes disastrously expensive before they have even placed their first order.

This behavior is considered a form of market manipulation in virtually every regulated market. Cases typically involve individual brokers or brokerage firms trading stock in and out of undisclosed, unmonitored accounts of relatives or confederates. Institutional and individual investors may also commit a front running violation when they are privy to inside information. Unscrupulous employees with access to their firm's order management system may engage in front running after observing consistent stock price movements in response to the firm's largest trades. To hide the scheme, the employees typically feed information about the victimized firm's upcoming orders to a third-party who places earlier orders for the same securities with different brokers.

The Evolution from Paper to Pixels

The term "front running" originates from the era when stock market trades were executed via paper carried by hand between trading desks. The routine business of hand-carrying client orders between desks would normally proceed at a walking pace, but a broker could literally run in front of the walking traffic to reach the desk and execute his own personal account order immediately before a large client order. Likewise, a broker could tail behind the person carrying a large client order to be the first to execute immediately after.

Such actions amount to a type of insider trading, since they involve non-public knowledge of upcoming trades, and the broker privately exploits this information by controlling the sequence of those trades to favor a personal position. However, the landscape of finance has shifted dramatically since the days of paper runners. In practice, computer trading splits up large orders into many smaller ones, a technique known as "algorithmic slicing" or "iceberg orders," designed specifically to hide the true size of an order and make front-running more difficult to detect.

Yet, the digital age has not eradicated the practice; it has merely changed the weapons. The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission's (SEC) 2001 change to pricing stock in pennies, rather than fractions of no less than 1/8 of a dollar, inadvertently facilitated front running. By reducing the minimum price increment, it reduced the extra amount that must be offered to step in front of other orders. In a world where prices moved in eighths of a dollar, a front-runner had to pay a significant premium to jump the queue. In a world of pennies, the cost of jumping the line became negligible, allowing high-frequency traders and predatory algorithms to engage in the practice with greater efficiency.

Wall Street traders may have manipulated a key derivatives market by front running Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, exploiting the massive liquidity of government-sponsored enterprises. The sophistication of these schemes often relies on the fact that very large market orders, simply by being exposed to the market, may cause traders to front-run as they seek to close out positions that may soon become unprofitable. It is a self-fulfilling prophecy of market impact: the larger the order, the more visible the signal, and the more likely it is to be exploited.

The 2003 Late Trading Scandal

The scale of front running became undeniably clear during the massive scandals that rocked the mutual fund industry in the early 2000s. In 2003, several hedge fund and mutual fund companies became embroiled in an illegal late trading scandal made public by a complaint against Bank of America brought by New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer. While late trading and front running are distinct violations, they often traveled in the same pack of malfeasance, revealing a culture where the rules were bent for the powerful.

The scandal implicated some of the most prestigious names in American finance. A resulting US Securities and Exchange Commission investigation into allegations of front-running activity implicated Edward D. Jones & Co., Inc., Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Strong Mutual Funds, Putnam Investments, Invesco, and Prudential Securities. These were not rogue traders operating in the shadows; these were titans of the industry, trading with inside information that should have been walled off from their proprietary desks.

The fallout was swift and severe. Following interviews in 2012 and 2013, the FBI said front running had resulted in profits of $50 million to $100 million for the bank involved in specific schemes. The sheer magnitude of these sums highlighted the systemic nature of the problem. It was not an anomaly; it was a business model. The front-running firm either buys for its own account before filling customer buy orders that drive up the price, or sells for its own account before filling customer sell orders that drive down the price. Front running is prohibited since the front-runner profits come from nonpublic information, at the expense of its own customers, the block trade, or the public market.

The Digital Hunt: The SEC's New Eyes

As technology advanced, so did the methods of enforcement. The old days of relying on whistleblowers or manual audits are gone. In 2021 and 2022, the SEC charged three separate front running schemes discovered by its own data analysis. This marked a turning point in regulatory oversight. The regulator was no longer just watching the game; it was using the same data streams as the traders to catch them in the act.

The largest of the three schemes discovered using the Consolidated Audit Trail (CAT) was alleged to have generated ill-gotten gains of nearly $50 million. The CAT is a massive database that tracks every trade in the US equity and options markets, providing regulators with a granular, second-by-second view of market activity. By analyzing this data, the SEC could identify patterns where orders were consistently placed milliseconds before large client orders, a digital signature of front running that was impossible to hide in the noise of the market.

This capability to detect front running in real-time data streams has forced a new level of compliance. However, the cat-and-mouse game continues. A third-party trader may find out the content of another broker's order and buy or sell in front of it in the same way that a self-dealing broker might. The third-party trader might find out about the trade directly from the broker or an employee of the brokerage firm in return for splitting the profits, in which case the front-running would be illegal. But there is a gray area that remains difficult to police: the trader might find out about the order by reading the broker's habits or tics, much in the same way that poker players can guess other players' cards.

The Insurance Industry and the "Heard on the Street" Precedent

Front running is not limited to the stock market. In insurance sales, front running is a practice in which agents "leak" information (usually false) to consumers about a competitor insurance company that leads the consumer to believe that the company's products or services are inferior, or worthless. The agent subsequently obtains a sale at the consumer's expense, earns a commission, and the consumer may have given up a perfectly good product for an inferior one as the result of the subterfuge. Here, the "block trade" is the consumer's intent to buy, and the "front runner" is the agent manipulating the narrative to steer the sale.

In the realm of securities, the term is sometimes used informally for a broker's tactics related to trading on proprietary information before its clients have been given the information. Analysts and brokers who buy shares in a company just before the brokerage firm is about to recommend the stock as a strong buy, are practising this type of "front running." Brokers have been convicted of securities laws violations in the United States for such behavior.

The most famous legal precedent for this behavior involves R. Foster Winans, a writer for the Wall Street Journal. In 1985, Winans tipped off brokers about the content of his column, "Heard on the Street," which based upon publicly available information would be written in such a way as to give either good or bad news about various stocks. The tipped-off brokers traded on the information, buying stocks before the column was published and selling them immediately after the price moved. Winans and the brokers were prosecuted by the prosecutor Rudolph Giuliani, tried and convicted of securities fraud. Their convictions were upheld by the United States Supreme Court in 1986. This case established a critical legal principle: the misuse of confidential information, even when that information is derived from a journalist's column, constitutes fraud if it violates a duty of trust.

High-Frequency Trading and the Modern Debate

The debate over front running has reignited in the era of High-Frequency Trading (HFT). In May 2013, the legendary investor Charlie Munger went on record stating, "HFT is Legalized Front-Running." This provocative statement captures the tension between modern market structure and traditional notions of fairness.

HFT firms use ultra-fast computers and algorithms to detect large orders in the market milliseconds before they are fully executed. They may then "front run" these orders by buying the stock and selling it back to the large buyer at a slightly higher price. While HFT firms argue they provide liquidity and tighten spreads, critics like Munger argue that they are simply extracting rent from other market participants by exploiting speed advantages that amount to a form of legalized front running.

The regulatory response has been mixed. In July 2020, Ken Griffin's Citadel Securities was fined $700,000 for trading ahead of its clients from 2012-2014. This fine, while relatively small compared to the firm's revenues, signaled that the regulators were watching. Citadel Securities, a major market maker, was found to have engaged in a pattern of trading ahead of customer orders, violating its duty to act in the best interest of its clients.

The legal framework remains rooted in the concept of the "principal-agent problem." In economic theory, the stock broker is the agent, and the brokerage client is the principal. The agent is supposed to act in the best interest of the principal. By front running, the agent puts their own financial interest above the customer's interest and is thus committing fraud. In the United States, they might also be breaking laws on market manipulation or insider trading.

The Persistence of the Scheme

Despite the fines, the convictions, and the sophisticated surveillance systems, front running persists. The incentives are simply too high. The profit margins in front running can be astronomical, especially when the volume of trades is massive. A broker who successfully front runs a $100 million block trade can generate tens of thousands of dollars in risk-free profit with a single keystroke.

The methods have become more elusive. Computer trading splits up large orders into many smaller ones, making front-running more difficult to detect. Yet, the data trails are still there for those who know how to look. The Consolidated Audit Trail and other regulatory tools are becoming increasingly powerful, capable of reconstructing the sequence of events with millisecond precision.

The story of front running is a story of the eternal conflict between information asymmetry and market fairness. From the literal runners of the 19th century to the algorithms of the 21st, the goal remains the same: to get ahead of the crowd. The technology changes, but the human desire to exploit a secret for profit remains constant.

For the modern investor, understanding front running is crucial. It is not just a historical footnote or a legal technicality; it is a real risk that affects the price they pay for every share they buy. The $35,000 profit generated in the hypothetical example is not an anomaly; it is a microcosm of the billions of dollars that are lost to the market every year due to these practices.

The fight against front running is a fight for the integrity of the market itself. If the market is perceived as a rigged game where the house always wins by seeing the cards first, then the trust that underpins the entire financial system erodes. The SEC, the FBI, and the courts have done much to stem the tide, but the battle is far from over. As long as there are secrets to be traded and profits to be made, there will be those who try to run ahead of the pack.

The evolution of front running from a physical race to a digital contest highlights the adaptability of market participants. The "paper" of the past has been replaced by data streams, but the fundamental dynamic remains unchanged. The broker with the inside information will always try to exploit it. The challenge for regulators is to stay one step ahead, using the same tools and technologies that the front-runners use to build their empires.

In the end, the lesson of front running is clear: in finance, information is power, and the misuse of that power is the enemy of the market. Whether it is a broker sprinting down a hallway or an algorithm executing a trade in nanoseconds, the result is the same: a transfer of wealth from the many to the few, a violation of trust, and a distortion of the price discovery mechanism that is supposed to make markets efficient. The history of front running is a testament to the resilience of greed, but also to the resilience of the rule of law.

As we look to the future, the rise of blockchain and decentralized finance offers new frontiers. The question is whether these new systems will be immune to front running, or whether the same human impulses will simply find new ways to manifest. For now, the lessons of the past serve as a warning. The runners may have changed their shoes, but they are still running. And the market is still watching.
