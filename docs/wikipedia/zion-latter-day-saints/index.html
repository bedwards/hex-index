Zion (Latter Day Saints)

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Zion (Latter Day Saints)

In 1833, the people of Zion were expelled from Jackson County, Missouri, not by a foreign army, but by their neighbors. The violence was intimate and brutal. Mobs burned homes, tarred and feathered men, and drove women and children into the freezing winter of the American Midwest with nothing but the clothes on their backs. Among the displaced was a young mother named Jane Manning James, whose family lost everything in the chaos that swept through Independence. This was not a theoretical failure of a utopian experiment; it was a human catastrophe that forced a movement to confront the terrifying gap between its highest ideals and the reality of human nature. Yet, from the ashes of this expulsion, the concept of Zion did not die. It fractured, expanded, and evolved, becoming the central, pulsating heart of the Latter Day Saint movement—a word that refuses to settle into a single definition, vibrating instead between the physical and the spiritual, the specific and the universal.

To understand Zion, one must first strip away the modern tendency to view religious concepts as purely metaphorical. For the early Latter Day Saints, Zion was never just a state of mind; it was a place, a people, and a political economy, all bound together by a divine mandate. It began with a revelation given to Joseph Smith in 1831, which designated a specific plot of land in Jackson County, Missouri, as the "center place" of Zion. This was not a vague spiritual territory but a geography with county lines, a county seat in Independence, and a specific plot of ground where a temple was to be built. The vision was grand in its scope: a New Jerusalem, a millennial city to be constructed by a remnant of the house of Joseph, assisted by repentant Gentiles. It was to be the physical capital of Christ's coming kingdom on earth.

But a city requires more than just land; it requires a new way of living. The economic engine of Zion was the United Order, a form of communitarian economics that stood in stark opposition to the individualistic, competitive spirit of Jacksonian Democracy. In this system, class distinctions were to be minimized, if not erased entirely. Members would consecrate their property to the church, receiving back only what they needed to maintain an acceptable quality of life. The goal was absolute unity and the eradication of poverty. As the scripture of the time put it, there would be "no poor among them." This was not merely charity; it was a radical restructuring of social relations based on the pure love of Christ, known as charity. It demanded that the community function as a single organism, where the suffering of one was the suffering of all, and the prosperity of one was the prosperity of all.

The failure to establish this physical Zion in Missouri is often cited as the definitive tragedy of the early church. The expulsion of 1833 was a direct result of the friction between this radical communitarian vision and the existing social order. The "Babylon" of the world—the prevailing culture of greed, disunity, and self-interest—could not tolerate the "Zion" of the Saints. The mob violence that drove them out was the inevitable collision of two incompatible worldviews. One side saw a religious cult threatening property rights and social stability; the other saw the people of God being martyred for their attempt to build heaven on earth. The human cost was measured in burned barns, lost livelihoods, and the trauma of forced migration. These were not footnotes to a theological debate; they were the shattered lives of families who believed they were walking in the path of the Lord.

Yet, as the Saints fled Missouri, the concept of Zion proved remarkably resilient. It did not vanish; it transformed. The disappointment of being driven from the "center place" led to a theological evolution. If the physical city could not be maintained in the face of such hostility, then Zion must also be the people themselves. The definition shifted from a strictly geographical location to a spiritual condition. A revelation received during this tumultuous period crystallized this new understanding: "Therefore, verily, thus saith the Lord, let Zion rejoice, for this is Zion—THE PURE IN HEART." This was a pivotal moment. It suggested that the true Zion was not the soil of Missouri, but the state of the soul. It was an association of the righteous, a unified society where the pure in heart dwelt in righteousness.

This duality remains the core of the Latter Day Saint understanding of Zion today. It is a concept that refuses to be pinned down to a single meaning. In the lexicon of the movement, "Zion" can refer to the ancient City of Enoch, a biblical city so righteous that it was taken up into heaven, leaving no poor behind. It refers to the New Jerusalem, the future millennial city expected to rise in Independence, Missouri, a promise that remains central to the eschatology of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints and the Community of Christ. It refers to the physical gathering places where the saints have lived throughout history: Kirtland, Ohio; Nauvoo, Illinois; and the Salt Lake Valley, where Brigham Young led the pioneers to establish a new refuge in the Rocky Mountains.

The geographical history of Zion is a map of displacement and hope. The term has been applied to every major settlement of the early church, each one a temporary attempt to realize the ideal. In Kirtland, the focus was on the temple and the gathering. In Jackson County, the focus was on the land and the New Jerusalem. In Nauvoo, the focus was on building a city-state and preparing for the end times. In the Salt Lake Valley, the focus shifted to creating a self-sufficient society in isolation. Each location was a "stake of Zion," a metaphorical tent pole holding up the community of believers. Even today, the name is applied to the entire continent of the Americas. Joseph Smith himself stated that "the whole of America is Zion itself from north to south," expanding the concept from a single city to an entire hemisphere, a vast territory set apart for the gathering of the faithful.

The tension between the physical and the spiritual definitions of Zion is not a contradiction but a dialectic. The movement has always oscillated between these poles. On one hand, there is the literalist interpretation, particularly strong in the Mormon fundamentalist movement. For these groups, the failure to establish Zion in Missouri and Nauvoo was not due to external persecution alone, but to an internal failure to live the "fulness of the gospel." They argue that the Saints were expelled because they did not live the United Order or practice plural marriage, which they view as essential prerequisites for Zion. Ogden Kraut, a prominent fundamentalist writer, summarized this view by noting that the church had gone astray by abandoning these higher laws. In this interpretation, Zion is a specific, tangible reality that can only be achieved through strict adherence to commandments and a literal application of communitarian economics.

In contrast, the mainstream LDS Church has largely moved toward the metaphorical and the spiritual. While the doctrine of the New Jerusalem in Missouri remains, the emphasis in modern practice is on the "pure in heart" and the building of Zion in one's local community. A "stake" of the church is referred to as a stake of Zion. A ward, the basic congregational unit, is a gathering place where members strive to live the fulness of the gospel. The focus is on the unity and charity of the people, rather than the specific geography. This shift allowed the movement to survive and flourish globally. If Zion were only the soil of Missouri, the church would have been confined to a small, persecuted sect in the American Midwest. By redefining Zion as a condition of the heart and a global community of believers, the movement became a worldwide phenomenon.

The word "Zion" appears with striking frequency in the canon of the Latter Day Saint movement. It is found 53 times in the Book of Mormon and 268 times in the Doctrine and Covenants, the record of modern revelation. These numbers are not accidental; they signal the centrality of the concept to the faith. In the Book of Mormon, the story of the people of Nephi and the events of Fourth Nephi describe a society that briefly achieved the state of Zion, living in harmony with no poor among them. This narrative serves as both a model and a warning, showing what is possible when the law of consecration is lived, and the tragedy that ensues when it is abandoned.

The philosophical underpinnings of Zion are rooted in a deep critique of the modern world. In the 19th century, as the world became increasingly industrialized and competitive, the concept of Zion offered a radical alternative. It was a vision of a society based on mutual feelings of charity, where the individual's success was inextricably linked to the well-being of the community. This stood in direct contrast to the "Babylon" of the age, a world characterized by wickedness, disunity, and the prevalence of poverty. The call to build Zion was a call to reject the values of the world and to create a new social order based on divine principles.

The history of Zion is also a history of struggle. The attempts to establish the New Jerusalem in Jackson County were met with violent resistance, and the subsequent attempts in Kirtland and Nauvoo were marked by internal strife and external persecution. The failure of the United Order in Nauvoo, where the church leadership did not fully implement the communitarian economic system, is often seen as a missed opportunity. The Saints were driven out of Nauvoo in 1846, leaving behind a city that was never fully realized as a Zion community. The journey to the Salt Lake Valley was a desperate flight, a migration of a people who had lost their homes and their hope of establishing the physical city. Yet, in the valleys of the Rockies, they built a new society, a new Zion, even if it was not the one originally envisioned.

The legacy of Zion is complex. It is a story of high ideals and human failure, of divine promise and earthly disappointment. It is a story of a people who sought to live the law of consecration and were met with violence. It is a story of a concept that adapted and survived, moving from a specific plot of land in Missouri to a global community of believers. The human cost of this journey cannot be overstated. The women and children who walked through the snow, the families who lost their homes, the men who were tarred and feathered—these are the real people behind the theology. Their suffering was the price paid for the dream of Zion.

Today, the concept of Zion continues to shape the identity of the Latter Day Saint movement. For the mainstream church, it is a metaphor for unity and purity of heart, a goal to be pursued in every ward and stake around the world. For the fundamentalists, it remains a literal destination, a specific place where the laws of God must be lived in their entirety. For both, the vision of a society without poverty, where all are one heart and one mind, remains a powerful and compelling ideal.

The name "Zion" has even been applied to the natural world, with Zion National Park in Utah named by LDS settlers in the 1850s. The canyon, with its towering red cliffs and deep canyons, was seen as a reflection of the divine beauty and grandeur associated with the concept. It stands as a physical reminder of the movement's deep connection to the land, even as the theological definition of Zion has expanded beyond any single geography.

The evolution of Zion from a specific city to a universal ideal is a testament to the resilience of the Latter Day Saint faith. It shows how a religious movement can adapt to failure and persecution, transforming a tragedy into a new theology. The expulsion from Missouri was not the end of Zion; it was the beginning of a new chapter. The people of Zion did not die; they scattered, and in their scattering, the concept of Zion grew. It became a global movement, a worldwide gathering of the pure in heart.

In the end, the story of Zion is the story of the human desire for a better world. It is the story of a people who believed that a different way of living was possible, a society based on love, unity, and the common good. Whether that society is to be built on a specific plot of land in Missouri or in the hearts of believers around the world, the vision remains the same. It is a vision of a world where there are no poor, where everyone is one heart and one mind, and where the pure in heart dwell in righteousness. It is a vision that has inspired millions, driven them across deserts and mountains, and sustained them through centuries of struggle. And it is a vision that continues to shape the lives of Latter Day Saints today, a beacon of hope in a world that often seems to be moving in the opposite direction.

The journey of Zion is far from over. As long as there are people who seek to live the law of consecration, to build unity in their communities, and to work for the common good, the dream of Zion will continue to live. It is a dream that has survived expulsions, persecutions, and the failures of men. It is a dream that transcends geography and time, reaching from the ancient City of Enoch to the modern world, and beyond. It is the enduring hope of the Latter Day Saint movement, a hope that one day, the pure in heart will inherit the earth, and Zion will be fully realized, not just as a memory or a metaphor, but as a living, breathing reality.
