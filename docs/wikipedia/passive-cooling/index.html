Passive cooling

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Passive cooling

In the sweltering summer of 2024, as global temperatures shattered records and London's grid strained under the weight of millions of air conditioners roaring in unison, a quiet counter-movement was gaining traction in the architecture of the future. While the city's skyline seemed to be surrendering to the need for mechanical refrigeration, a different philosophy was being re-imagined in the design studios of the world's leading climate-adaptive firms. This approach does not fight the heat with energy-intensive machinery; instead, it negotiates with the environment, using the building itself as a shield and a filter. It is a return to first principles, a realization that the most powerful cooling system is not a compressor, but the architecture of the building envelope and the intelligent harnessing of natural forces. This is passive cooling, a design discipline that has moved from the fringes of sustainable theory to the center of climate change adaptation strategy.

Passive cooling is fundamentally a study in heat gain control and heat dissipation. Its goal is singular and urgent: to improve indoor thermal comfort with low, or ideally, zero energy consumption. The logic is deceptively simple yet requires profound architectural sophistication. The system works by doing two things simultaneously: preventing heat from entering the interior in the first place, and removing heat that has already accumulated through natural means. Unlike the mechanical systems that dominate modern construction, which pump heat from inside to outside using electricity, natural cooling utilizes on-site energy available from the natural environment. It relies on the architectural design of building components—specifically the building envelope—rather than motors and refrigerants to dissipate heat. Therefore, the success of a passive cooling strategy depends not only on the design of the building but on how the site's natural resources are used as heat sinks. A heat sink is simply anything that absorbs or dissipates heat. In the context of passive design, these sinks are the upper atmosphere (the night sky), the outdoor air (wind), and the earth or soil beneath the foundation.

The urgency of this approach cannot be overstated. As the planet warms, the dependency on energy-intensive air conditioning becomes a vicious cycle. Air conditioners cool the inside of a building while expelling hot air into the street, raising the ambient temperature of the urban environment, which in turn forces the air conditioners to work harder, consuming more electricity and generating more waste heat. Passive cooling breaks this cycle. It is an essential tool for the design of buildings intended for climate change adaptation, offering a way to reduce dependency on the very technologies that are accelerating the warming of our cities. Some purists argue that true passive cooling must exclude all mechanical intervention. However, a pragmatic evolution of the concept, often termed 'hybrid cooling systems,' allows for minor and simple mechanical systems, such as pumps and economizers, to be integrated. These are permitted only when they are used to enhance the effectiveness of the natural cooling process, acting as a boost to the natural forces rather than a replacement for them.

The techniques for passive cooling can be grouped into two distinct but complementary categories. The first involves preventive techniques, which aim to provide protection and/or prevention of external and internal heat gains. The second category comprises modulation and heat dissipation techniques, which allow the building to store and dissipate heat gain through the transfer of heat from heat sinks to the climate. This second technique can be the result of thermal mass or natural cooling strategies. The distinction is critical: prevention stops the problem before it starts, while modulation and dissipation manage the heat that inevitably finds its way in.

The Art of Prevention

Protection from or prevention of heat gains encompasses all the design techniques that minimize the impact of solar heat gains through the building's envelope and of internal heat gains generated inside the building due to occupancy and equipment. This is the first line of defense, and it begins long before the first brick is laid.

Microclimate and site design form the foundation of this prevention strategy. By taking into account the local climate and the site context, specific cooling strategies can be selected to apply which are the most appropriate for preventing overheating through the envelope of the building. The microclimate can play a huge role in determining the most favorable building location. An architect must analyze the combined availability of sun and wind, understanding that a building sited on a windward slope may enjoy natural ventilation that a building in a sheltered valley will never know. To navigate this complexity, designers rely on specific analysis tools: the bioclimatic chart, which maps temperature and humidity against time; the solar diagram, which traces the sun's path across the sky for every day of the year; and the wind rose, a graphical representation of wind speed and direction data. These are not mere aesthetic choices but survival calculations.

Solar control is the next critical layer. A properly designed shading system can effectively contribute to minimizing solar heat gains. Shading both transparent and opaque surfaces of the building envelope will minimize the amount of solar radiation that induces overheating in both indoor spaces and the building's structure. When the sun strikes a window, the glass may be transparent, but the heat energy is not. It is absorbed by the interior surfaces, turning the room into an oven. By shading the building structure, the heat gain captured through the windows and envelope is drastically reduced. This requires a deep understanding of the sun's angle; a horizontal overhang might block the high summer sun while allowing the low winter sun to penetrate, providing warmth when it is needed and rejecting it when it is not.

Building form and layout also play a pivotal role in the battle against heat. Building orientation and an optimized distribution of interior spaces can prevent overheating. Rooms can be zoned within the buildings in order to reject sources of internal heat gain and/or allocating heat gains where they can be useful, considering the different activities of the building. For example, creating a flat, horizontal plan will increase the effectiveness of cross-ventilation across the plan, allowing air to sweep through the space rather than getting trapped. Locating the zones vertically can take advantage of temperature stratification. Typically, building zones in the upper levels are warmer than the lower zones due to the physics of warm air rising. Vertical zoning of spaces and activities uses this temperature stratification to accommodate zone uses according to their temperature requirements. A library or server room, which generates significant heat, might be placed on an upper level where the heat can be vented easily, while a bedroom or reading room, which requires cooler air, is situated on the lower levels.

The form factor of the building—the ratio between its volume and its surface area—also plays a major role in the building's energy and thermal profile. This ratio can be used to shape the building form to the specific local climate. In cold climates, a compact form is desirable to minimize heat loss. However, in hot climates, a less compact form with a higher surface-area-to-volume ratio can be advantageous. More compact forms tend to preserve more heat than less compact forms because the ratio of the internal loads to envelope area is significant. In a cooling context, a building that is spread out has more surface area through which heat can be dissipated, provided the envelope is well-shaded.

Thermal insulation remains a non-negotiable component of passive cooling. Insulation in the building's envelope will decrease the amount of heat transferred by radiation through the facades. This principle applies both to the opaque surfaces, such as walls and roofs, and the transparent surfaces, such as windows. Since roofs could be a larger contributor to the interior heat load, especially in lighter constructions like warehouses or workshops with metal structures, providing thermal insulation can effectively decrease heat transfer from the roof. The sun beats down on a metal roof with immense intensity; without insulation, that heat radiates directly into the workspace below. Proper insulation acts as a barrier, keeping the interior climate stable regardless of the external temperature.

Beyond the physical structure, behavioral and occupancy patterns offer a human dimension to heat gain prevention. Some building management policies, such as limiting the number of people in a given area of the building, can also contribute effectively to the minimization of heat gains inside a building. Human bodies generate heat, and in a crowded space, this internal gain can be substantial. Building occupants can also contribute to indoor overheating prevention by adopting simple habits: shutting off the lights and equipment of unoccupied spaces, operating shading when necessary to reduce solar heat gains through windows, or dressing lighter in order to adapt better to the indoor environment by increasing their thermal comfort tolerance. This shift in behavior is as important as the technology itself.

Finally, internal gain control addresses the heat generated by the building's own systems. More energy-efficient lighting and electronic equipment tend to release less energy, thus contributing to less internal heat loads inside the space. An incandescent bulb is inefficient not just because it wastes electricity, but because the "waste" is heat. Replacing it with an LED reduces the light output's thermal footprint, easing the burden on the building's cooling systems.

The Physics of Dissipation

While prevention is the shield, modulation and heat dissipation techniques form the building's immune system. These techniques rely on natural heat sinks to store and remove the internal heat gains. Examples of natural sinks are the night sky, the earth soil, and the building mass itself. Therefore, passive cooling techniques that use heat sinks can act to either modulate heat gain with thermal mass or dissipate heat through natural cooling strategies.

Thermal mass is the ability of a material to absorb and store heat. Heat gain modulation of an indoor space can be achieved by the proper use of the building's thermal mass as a heat sink. Materials like concrete, stone, and brick have high thermal mass. During the day, as the sun heats the building, the thermal mass absorbs the heat, preventing the indoor air temperature from rising rapidly. The heat is stored within the material. At night, when the ambient temperature drops, the stored heat is released back into the space or dissipated to the cooler night air. This process smooths out the temperature curve, keeping the interior comfortable during the hottest part of the day. However, this strategy has a caveat: if the stored heat is not removed, it will eventually re-radiate into the room at night, causing discomfort. This is why thermal mass is often coupled with a night ventilation strategy. If the stored heat that will be delivered to the space during the evening or night is not desirable, it must be flushed out by the cooler night air, resetting the thermal mass for the next day.

Natural cooling refers to the use of ventilation or natural heat sinks for heat dissipation from indoor spaces. It is a diverse field, separated into five different categories: ventilation, night flushing, radiative cooling, evaporative cooling, and earth coupling. Each relies on the physical properties of the environment to move heat away from the building.

Ventilation as a natural cooling strategy uses the physical properties of air to remove heat or provide cooling to occupants. In select cases, ventilation can be used to cool the building structure, which subsequently may serve as a heat sink. The most common form is cross ventilation. The strategy of cross ventilation relies on wind to pass through the building for the purpose of cooling the occupants. This requires openings on two sides of the space, called the inlet and the outlet. The sizing and placement of the ventilation inlets and outlets will determine the direction and velocity of cross ventilation through the building. Generally, an equal or greater area of outlet openings must also be provided to provide adequate cross ventilation. If the outlet is too small, the air will struggle to escape, and the cooling effect will be minimal.

Cross ventilation is an effective cooling strategy, but it has a fundamental weakness: wind is an unreliable resource. There are days when the air is still, and the building becomes stagnant. Stack ventilation is an alternative design strategy that relies on the buoyancy of warm air to rise and exit through openings located at ceiling height. Cooler outside air replaces the rising warm air through carefully designed inlets placed near the floor. This creates a continuous flow driven by the temperature difference between the inside and outside air. The greater the temperature difference, the stronger the stack effect. These two strategies, cross ventilation and stack ventilation, are the pillars of ventilative cooling.

One specific application of natural ventilation is night flushing, also known as night ventilation, night cooling, night purging, or nocturnal convective cooling. This is a passive or semi-passive cooling strategy that requires increased air movement during the cooler night hours. By opening the building to the night sky, the cool air sweeps through the structure, removing the heat that was stored in the thermal mass during the day. This process is critical for the success of thermal mass strategies in hot climates. Without night flushing, the building would essentially cook in its own stored heat by the second day. The strategy turns the building into a sponge that soaks up heat during the day and wrings it out at night.

Radiative cooling takes advantage of the fact that the night sky is much colder than the Earth's surface. All objects radiate heat in the form of infrared radiation. If a building surface is exposed to the clear night sky, it can radiate its heat into the deep cold of the upper atmosphere, cooling itself to a temperature below the ambient air temperature. This phenomenon can be harnessed by designing roofs with high emissivity materials that radiate heat efficiently. In some advanced applications, these surfaces are used to condense moisture from the air, providing a dual benefit of cooling and water collection.

Evaporative cooling is another ancient technique that is finding new life in modern design. It relies on the principle that water absorbs heat as it changes from a liquid to a gas. By introducing water into the airflow, the temperature of the air drops. This can be achieved through simple means, such as water features, fountains, or wetted pads, or more complex systems like evaporative coolers. This method is highly effective in hot, dry climates but less so in humid environments where the air is already saturated with moisture.

Earth coupling, or ground cooling, utilizes the stable temperature of the earth below the frost line as a heat sink. The temperature of the soil a few meters underground remains relatively constant throughout the year, typically cooler than the summer air. By burying pipes or using earth tubes, air can be drawn through the ground, where it is cooled by the surrounding soil before entering the building. This pre-cooled air can then be used for ventilation or further conditioned with minimal energy input.

The integration of these techniques requires a holistic approach. A building cannot simply be a collection of passive features; it must be a cohesive system where the orientation, the form, the materials, and the ventilation strategies work in concert. The microclimate dictates the primary strategy, but the internal layout and the user behavior refine the outcome. In a world facing the existential threat of climate change, the shift toward passive cooling is not merely a design preference; it is a necessity. It represents a move away from the dominance of fossil-fuel-dependent technology toward a symbiotic relationship with the natural world. By understanding and utilizing the heat sinks of the upper atmosphere, the wind, and the earth, architects can create buildings that breathe, adapt, and cool themselves, ensuring that the interiors remain habitable even as the world outside grows warmer. The future of comfort lies not in fighting the climate, but in learning from it.
