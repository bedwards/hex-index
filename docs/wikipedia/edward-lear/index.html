Edward Lear

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Edward Lear

On a sweltering day in 1812, near the bustling fairgrounds of Highgate on the outskirts of London, a six-year-old boy named Edward Lear experienced the first violent tremor that would haunt his entire life. As he stood beside his father, the world suddenly lurched; his body seized in a tonic-clonic fit, a terrifying loss of control that left him shaking and humiliated in front of strangers. This was not merely a childhood illness but the onset of epilepsy, a condition that would force Lear to develop an acute, lifelong sensitivity to his own physical state, always sensing the warning tremors before they struck so he could retreat from public view into the shadows of silence. That boy, born the penultimate of twenty-one children and the youngest to survive infancy on May 12, 1812, would grow up to become one of the most beloved figures in English literature, a man who filled his days with the absurdity of limericks and the whimsical nonsense of "The Owl and the Pussycat," yet whose internal landscape was dominated by a profound melancholia he called "the Morbids."

Edward Lear's life was a study in contrasts, a tapestry woven from threads of brilliant artistic observation and deep personal isolation. He did not inherit a legacy of wealth or stability; rather, his childhood was upended when his father, Jeremiah Lear, a stockbroker who had previously worked for the family sugar refining business, defaulted on the London Stock Exchange during the economic upheaval following the Napoleonic Wars. The financial collapse was swift and devastating, stripping the family of their comfortable existence at Bowmans Lodge in Holloway. When Edward was just four years old, he and his eldest sister, Ann, were forced to leave the home they knew. Ann, who was twenty-one years older than her brother, stepped into a role far beyond that of a sibling; she became his mother, his guardian, and his emotional anchor. She doted on him with an intensity that would last until her death when Edward was nearly fifty. This early trauma, the sudden descent from middle-class security to financial precariousness, likely sowed the seeds for the severe depression that plagued Lear throughout his life.

Yet, even as a child grappling with seizures and the shadow of "the Morbids," Lear possessed a mind that saw the world with extraordinary clarity. By the age of sixteen, he was already drawing "for bread and cheese," turning his artistic talent into a means of survival. His trajectory shifted dramatically when he developed into a serious ornithological draughtsman, a role that would define his early career. In an era where naturalists typically studied birds from stuffed skins preserved in jars, Lear revolutionized the field by insisting on drawing them from life. From 1832 to 1836, he was employed by the Earl of Derby, who maintained a private menagerie at Knowsley Hall. Here, Lear produced sketches that captured the vitality and posture of living creatures, a skill so remarkable that he is often compared to the American naturalist John James Audubon. His first major publication appeared in 1830 when he was only nineteen: Illustrations of the Family of Psittacidae, or Parrots. The work was a masterpiece of precision and color.

Lear's influence on ornithology was so profound that his name was etched into the natural world itself. The blue-and-yellow macaw known scientifically as Anodorhynchus leari, or Lear's Macaw, bears his name in permanent tribute to his artistic genius. During this same period, he served as a mentor to Elizabeth Gould and contributed significantly to the works of John Gould, further cementing his reputation as one of the greatest bird artists of his era. However, the very precision that made his scientific illustrations so valuable would eventually become his undoing. As Lear aged, his eyesight began to deteriorate, a cruel irony for an artist whose livelihood depended on the ability to see minute details. The onset of partial blindness in his later years made it impossible for him to continue the fine etchings and lithographic plates required for ornithological work.

Faced with this physical limitation, Lear did not abandon art; he pivoted. He turned his gaze from the intricate feathers of birds to the vast horizons of the landscape. The artist who had once captured the stillness of a parrot in a cage began to chase the light across continents. Between 1848 and 1849, Lear embarked on journeys that would take him through regions then part of the Ottoman Empire: Albania, Vardar Macedonia, Egypt, and Greece. Later, in a grand tour from 1873 to 1875, he traveled extensively through India, including a brief detour to Ceylon (now Sri Lanka). During these travels, he produced large quantities of colored wash drawings in a distinctive style that emphasized strong sunlight and intense contrasts of color. These sketches were not merely souvenirs; they were the raw material for his later oil paintings and prints, which he would refine back in his studio.

One of Lear's most significant artistic expeditions took place between 1878 and 1883, during which he spent his summers on Monte Generoso, a mountain straddling the border between the Swiss canton of Ticino and the Italian region of Lombardy. The view from this peak inspired The Plains of Lombardy, an oil painting that now resides in the Ashmolean Museum in Oxford. But it was not only the grand vistas that captivated him; Lear had a profound connection to the rugged, often overlooked corners of Italy. In 1842, he began a journey into the Italian peninsula, traversing Lazio, Rome, Abruzzo, Molise, Apulia, Basilicata, Calabria, and Sicily. His journals from this period are filled with personal notes and drawings that capture not just the architecture but the soul of the places he visited.

Lear's affection for the Abruzzo region was particularly deep. In 1843, he traveled through the Marsica area—visiting Celano, Avezzano, Alba Fucens, and Trasacco—and crossed the plateau of Cinque Miglia via an ancient sheep track used by shepherds. He sketched the medieval village of Albe with Mount Sirente looming in the background and described the town of Celano, dominated by the Piccolomini castle overlooking the vast plain of Lago Fucino. This lake, which Lear documented before it was drained a few years later to promote agricultural development, served as a backdrop for his observations of the changing landscape. At Castel di Sangro, he wrote of the winter stillness of the mountains and the beauty of the basilica. His most adventurous travel writing emerged from a voyage in 1847 to southern Italy, chronicled in Journals of a Landscape Painter in Southern Calabria. The section on Calabria is widely considered among his finest work, filled with breathtaking descriptions of landscapes and encounters with surreal characters that would have felt right at home in one of his nonsense poems.

While Lear is celebrated today primarily for his literary whimsy, his artistic output was multifaceted, encompassing music as well. He played the piano with skill but also mastered the accordion, flute, and small guitar. His musical compositions were not mere hobbies; he published twelve musical settings of Alfred, Lord Tennyson's poetry, a task he had longed to accomplish since his youth. Tennyson, one of the most revered poets of the Victorian age, gave his stamp of approval exclusively to Lear's settings. Between 1853 and 1860, Lear published four settings in 1853, five in 1859, and three in 1860. These works were the only musical interpretations of Tennyson's verses that the poet himself endorsed.

Lear also composed music for his own nonsense songs, infusing them with a playful melody that matched their absurd lyrics. Although he never performed professionally, he delighted countless social gatherings with his renditions of "The Owl and the Pussycat" and other creations. He was known to improvise, sometimes adding his own lyrics to existing tunes—such as in "The Nervous Family"—or replacing serious poetry with nursery rhymes for comedic effect. Only two of his original musical scores have survived: the music for "The Courtship of the Yonghy-Bonghy-Bò" and "The Pelican Chorus." These surviving fragments offer a glimpse into a man who could seamlessly blend high art with low comedy, treating both with equal seriousness.

Behind the public persona of the cheerful nonsense poet lay a complex and often painful personal life. Lear's most fervent friendship was with Franklin Lushington, a young barrister he met in Malta in 1849. The two toured southern Greece together, but their relationship was marked by an imbalance of affection that tormented Lear for decades. He developed an infatuation with Lushington that the other man did not fully reciprocate. Despite this disparity, they remained friends for almost forty years until Lear's death in 1888. The intensity of Lear's affections often seemed to doom his relationships; he proposed marriage twice to Augusta Bethell, a writer he had known for many years, despite being twenty-six years her senior. These rejections, combined with his epilepsy and social anxiety, drove him further into a life of solitary travel and correspondence.

In his later years, Lear relied on a small circle of companions who became family by choice rather than blood. Among them was Giorgis, an Albanian Souliote chef who traveled with him for many years. Though Lear complained frequently about Giorgis's culinary skills—describing him as a "thoroughly unsatisfactory chef"—the man remained a faithful friend and confidant. In San Remo, where Lear eventually settled in the 1870s, his most trusted companion was not a human at all but a cat named Foss. When Foss died in 1887, Lear buried him with a ceremony that reflected the depth of their bond, interring the cat in a garden at Villa Tennyson.

Villa Tennyson, located on the beautiful Mediterranean coast of San Remo, became Lear's final home. He named it in honor of his beloved poet friend and his musical collaborations. Here, in the warm climate that suited his failing health, Lear continued to paint and write until the end. It was in this villa that he developed a habit of introducing himself with long, fabricated pseudonyms, reciting names like "Mr Abebika kratoponoko Prizzikalo Kattefello Ablegorabalus Ableborinto phashyph" or "Chakonoton the Cozovex Dossi Fossi Sini Tomentilla Coronilla Polentilla Battledore & Shuttlecock Derry down Derry Dumps." These names, based on the nonsensical title Aldiborontiphoskyphorniostikos, were a final performance of his trademark absurdity, a shield against the seriousness of his declining health.

The decline was gradual but inevitable. Lear had suffered from heart disease since at least 1870, and by 1888, his body could no longer sustain him. He died on January 29, 1888, at Villa Tennyson. His funeral was a somber affair, described as sad and lonely, a stark contrast to the laughter he had inspired in thousands of readers over his lifetime. Yet, the legacy of Edward Lear is not defined by his death or his suffering, but by the unique world he created for others. He popularized the limerick, a form of verse that he never actually called by name during his life, yet which remains synonymous with his work today. His nonsense collections, filled with poems, songs, short stories, botanical drawings, recipes, and alphabets, continue to delight children and adults alike.

Lear's life was a testament to the resilience of the human spirit in the face of physical and emotional adversity. He took his epilepsy, his blindness, his depression, and his loneliness and transformed them into art that celebrated the absurdity and beauty of existence. From the stuffed birds of Knowsley Hall to the sun-drenched plains of Lombardy, from the quiet grief of a man losing his vision to the raucous joy of "The Jumblies," Lear's journey was one of constant reinvention. He taught us that even in the darkest moments, there is room for a limerick; even when the world feels too heavy, there is value in making things up. His work reminds us that art is not just about capturing reality as it is, but about imagining how it could be—brighter, stranger, and infinitely more colorful.

The story of Edward Lear is not simply a biography of a 19th-century man; it is a narrative about the power of creativity to transcend suffering. He was an artist who saw the world with the precision of a scientist but interpreted it with the heart of a dreamer. When he drew the parrots, he saw more than feathers and beaks; he saw life itself. When he traveled through the mountains of Calabria or the valleys of Albania, he did not just record the scenery; he absorbed the spirit of the places and brought them home in his sketches and journals. And when he wrote nonsense verse, he was doing something far deeper than making people laugh; he was offering a refuge from the rigid structures of a Victorian society that often felt too confining for someone who felt so much pain.

In the end, Edward Lear remains a singular figure in literary history, a man who managed to be both a serious scientist and a master of the ridiculous. He proved that one could hold two opposing truths at once: that life is full of tragedy and that it is also full of wonder. His legacy lives on not only in the museums that display his paintings or the books that reprint his poems but in the very way we understand the power of play. Every time a child recites "The Owl and the Pussycat," every time an adult chuckles at a limerick, Edward Lear is present, bridging the gap between the pain he endured and the joy he created. He died over a century ago, but his nonsense remains timeless, a reminder that even in the face of "the Morbids," there is always room for a little bit of magic.
