Ben Franklin effect

17 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Ben Franklin effect

In 1736, Benjamin Franklin faced a political rival in the Pennsylvania Assembly who viewed him with such intense animosity that the two men refused to speak. The opposition was personal, sharp, and seemingly immovable. Rather than counter-argue, flatter, or ignore the man, Franklin executed a move of such counter-intuitive brilliance that it would eventually define a fundamental law of human psychology. He wrote a note to his enemy requesting a favor: he had heard the rival possessed a rare and curious volume in his library, and he wished to borrow it for a few days. The request was granted immediately. Franklin returned the book a week later with a note of profound gratitude. The next time they met in the House, the rival spoke to him with great civility. Over time, that hostility evaporated, replaced by a friendship that endured until the rival's death. Franklin later recorded this in his autobiography, citing an "old maxim": "He that has once done you a kindness will be more ready to do you another, than he whom you yourself have obliged."

This anecdote is not merely a charming story of 18th-century diplomacy; it is the genesis of a phenomenon known as the Ben Franklin Effect. It describes a psychological paradox where a person who performs a favor for someone they dislike or are indifferent toward ends up liking that person more afterward. This is not a simple transaction of social niceties. It is a deep-seated cognitive mechanism where the human mind, unable to reconcile conflicting actions and beliefs, rewrites its own history to ensure internal consistency. When we do something for another, our brain searches for a justification. If we do not like the person, the act feels irrational. To resolve this tension, we convince ourselves that we must have done the favor because we actually like them. We do not change our actions to match our feelings; we change our feelings to match our actions.

The Mechanics of Cognitive Dissonance

To understand why this happens, one must look at the engine driving it: cognitive dissonance. This theory, formalized in the mid-20th century, posits that humans have an innate drive to maintain consistency between their attitudes, beliefs, and behaviors. When these elements clash, it creates a state of psychological discomfort known as dissonance. The mind cannot easily tolerate the thought, "I am a reasonable person who does not help people I despise, yet I just helped this person I despise."

The brain has two ways to resolve this conflict. It can change the behavior, perhaps by refusing to help, or it can change the attitude. In the context of the Ben Franklin Effect, the behavior has already occurred. The favor is done. The money is lent. The book is borrowed. The action is a fixed point in reality. Therefore, the only variable left to shift is the internal attitude. The subject rationalizes the action by concluding, "I must have helped him because I find him likable or worthy." The mind essentially tricks itself into believing the affection was the cause of the action, rather than the result of it. This adjustment preserves the self-concept of being a consistent, logical individual. It is a subtle but powerful form of self-persuasion that operates largely outside our conscious awareness.

Franklin's insight was centuries ahead of the academic theory that would later codify it. He understood that asking for a favor was not an act of weakness, but a strategic maneuver to manipulate the psychological state of the requester. By placing the rival in the position of the benefactor, Franklin forced the rival to engage in a behavior that contradicted his hatred, thereby forcing a shift in his feelings.

The Science of the Favor

For decades, the Ben Franklin Effect remained an anecdotal observation, a clever trick of the Founding Fathers. It was not until the late 1960s that researchers attempted to isolate and measure this phenomenon in a controlled laboratory setting. In 1969, psychologists Jecker and Landy conducted a seminal study that would provide empirical weight to Franklin's claim.

The experiment involved students invited to participate in a question-and-answer competition run by the researcher. The students were promised monetary prizes. After the competition concluded, the researchers divided the "winners" into three distinct groups. The first group was approached by the researcher himself, who asked them to return the money. He explained that he had used his own personal funds to pay the winners and was now facing a shortage of his own. The second group was approached by a secretary, not the researcher, who asked them to return the money, citing that the funds came from the psychology department and were running low. The third group was not approached at all and kept their winnings.

The results were striking. When asked how much they liked the researcher, the first group—the ones who had returned the money directly to him—liked him the most. The second group, who had returned the money via an intermediary, liked him the least. The third group fell somewhere in the middle. The data suggested a direct causal link: the act of doing the researcher a personal favor (returning the money) increased the subjects' affection for him. Conversely, returning the money to a faceless departmental intermediary had no such effect, and in fact, the impersonal nature of the request seemed to breed resentment. The direct request created a personal bond; the bureaucratic one did not.

This experiment highlighted a crucial nuance: the favor must be personal. It must involve a direct interaction that the subject can attribute to their own voluntary choice. When the request is filtered through an intermediary, the psychological link is broken. The subject cannot easily rationalize, "I am helping the researcher," because the request came from a secretary. The dissonance is not triggered, and the attitude does not shift.

The Cost of Cruelty

If doing a favor makes us like someone more, logic dictates that doing harm should make us like them less. The human mind seeks consistency in both directions. If we are kind to someone, we must like them. If we are cruel, we must dislike them. This inverse relationship was explored in a 1971 study by University of North Carolina psychologists John Schopler and John Compere.

In their experiment, subjects were asked to administer learning tests to accomplices who pretended to be other students. The setup was elaborate: the "teachers" were told the "learners" would watch them tap out long patterns on wooden cubes, which the learners would then have to repeat. The teachers were instructed to use two different methods on two different people. In one scenario, they offered encouragement when the patterns were correct. In the other, they insulted and criticized the learner whenever an error was made.

After the session, the teachers filled out a questionnaire rating the attractiveness and likability of the "learners." The results were unequivocal. The subjects who had insulted and criticized the learners rated them as significantly less attractive and less likable than those who had received encouragement. The subjects' own conduct toward the accomplices directly shaped their perception of them. As the researchers noted, "You tend to like the people to whom you are kind and dislike the people to whom you are rude."

This finding has profound implications for how we understand human conflict and relationships. It suggests that our treatment of others is not merely a reflection of our feelings, but a generator of them. We do not just hate people because we are mean to them; we become mean to people because we are starting to hate them. But the causality runs both ways. Once we cross the line into cruelty, our brain works overtime to justify that cruelty by amplifying the dislike we feel. We de-humanize the victim to make the act of harming them feel acceptable.

This mechanism helps explain the escalation of violence in various contexts. In the context of war, soldiers who kill enemy combatants often report a deep-seated hatred for those they have killed. This is not just a reaction to the trauma of combat; it is a psychological necessity. To kill someone and then view them as a human being with a family, hopes, and fears creates an unbearable dissonance. The mind resolves this by stripping the enemy of their humanity. "I killed him," the soldier thinks, "so he must have been a monster." This psychological maneuver decreases the dissonance of killing, allowing the soldier to function.

This dynamic also sheds light on long-standing grudges and vendettas, such as the legendary Hatfield-McCoy feud. Once the cycle of retaliation begins, each act of violence necessitates a deeper devaluation of the opponent. "They did this to us, so they must be evil." The hatred grows not in spite of the violence, but because of it. The behavior forces the attitude to align, creating a feedback loop that can last for generations. Once we start, we may not be able to stop, engaging in behavior we would normally never allow ourselves to do.

Cross-Cultural Validation and Modern Applications

The universality of the Ben Franklin Effect was further tested in a more recent, albeit smaller, study by psychologist Yu Niiya, who examined the phenomenon with both Japanese and American subjects. The results were reproduced, suggesting that this cognitive mechanism is not a cultural quirk of the West but a fundamental aspect of human social cognition.

Niiya offers a compelling alternative explanation that complements the cognitive dissonance theory. She attributes the effect to the requestee reciprocating a perceived attempt by the requester to ignite friendly relations. When someone asks for a favor, it signals a desire for a connection. The act of granting the favor is a way of accepting that invitation. This theory elegantly explains why the effect fails when an intermediary is used. An intermediary request is not an invitation to a relationship; it is a bureaucratic transaction. The direct request, however, says, "I trust you," or "I value your help." This implicit flattery triggers a social response to nurture the budding bond.

This psychological principle has found fertile ground in the modern world of sales and business. In an era where consumers are bombarded with pitches, the traditional approach of "let me help you" often falls flat. The Ben Franklin Effect suggests a more subtle strategy: ask the client for help. A salesperson might ask a potential client, "What product benefits do you find most compelling?" or "Where do you think the market is headed in five years?"

This is not a trick to extract free consulting. It is a method of building rapport. By asking for the client's expertise, the salesperson signals that the client has something valuable—knowledge, insight, experience—that the salesperson lacks. This is a form of flattery, as Dale Carnegie famously interpreted it in his 1936 bestseller, How to Win Friends and Influence People. Carnegie wrote, "When we ask a colleague to do us a favor, we are signaling that we consider them to have something we don't, whether more intelligence, more knowledge, more skills, or whatever." This immediately raises the other person's opinion of us. They feel admired and respected. They begin to like us, not because we sold them something, but because we made them feel significant.

The application of this effect extends beyond sales into the realm of mentorship. Successful mentor-protege relationships are often defined by a fundamental imbalance of knowledge and influence. The mentor has the wisdom; the protege seeks it. A common mistake for eager proteges is to try to "reciprocate" favors immediately, perhaps by helping the mentor with a task they are struggling with. However, this can backfire. It disrupts the role dynamic and can make the mentor feel uncomfortable or indebted. The Ben Franklin Effect suggests that the protege should instead allow the mentor to do the favor of teaching them. By accepting the mentor's guidance and allowing them to invest time and effort, the protege strengthens the mentor's commitment to the relationship. The mentor, having invested in the protege, comes to like and value them more.

The Anatomy of Dependence and the Human-Animal Bond

Psychologist Yu Niiya has also drawn a fascinating parallel between the Ben Franklin Effect and amae, a concept described by Japanese psychoanalyst Takeo Doi in his book The Anatomy of Dependence. Amae refers to a desire to be loved and cared for, a dependent, childlike behavior that induces a parent-child bond. When one person acts dependent, the other feels a responsibility to care for them.

In this light, asking for a favor is a subtle invocation of amae. It is a way of saying, "I need you." This triggers a caretaker response in the other person. They step into the role of the provider, and in doing so, they strengthen the emotional bond. The requester is free to act slightly immaturely or make demands, and the requestor feels a sense of responsibility and affection for the one they are helping. This dynamic is not limited to human-to-human interactions.

Consider the world of dog training. Trainers often focus on the impact of positive and negative reinforcement on the dog's behavior. However, the Ben Franklin Effect suggests we should also consider the impact on the trainer. When a trainer uses positive reinforcement—treats, praise, play—they are doing nice things for the dog. According to the effect, this behavior should make the trainer like the dog more. The trainer rationalizes their kindness: "I am being so kind to this dog; I must really like it."

Conversely, if a trainer relies on harsh words, collar jerks, or physical punishment to change a dog's behavior, they are engaging in negative actions. To resolve the dissonance of hurting a living creature, the trainer may begin to like the dog less. They may start to view the dog as "difficult," "stubborn," or "ungrateful." The dog's behavior does not necessarily change first; the trainer's attitude shifts to justify the harsh methods. This insight challenges the very nature of how we train animals, suggesting that our treatment of them shapes our affection for them just as much as our affection shapes our treatment.

The Dark Side of the Effect

While the Ben Franklin Effect is often used to build bridges, it also explains the construction of walls. The same mechanism that makes us like those we help can make us hate those we harm. This is the dark mirror of the effect. When we commit an act of aggression, injustice, or betrayal against someone, our mind seeks to justify that action. We cannot easily hold the thought, "I am a good person who just hurt an innocent person." To resolve this, we dehumanize the victim. We convince ourselves that they deserved it, that they are evil, or that they are less than human.

This process is visible in the most extreme human conflicts. Soldiers in combat situations often develop intense hatred for the enemy they have killed. This hatred is a psychological defense mechanism. It decreases the dissonance of killing. If the enemy is a monster, then killing them is not a tragedy; it is a necessity. This phenomenon can also explain long-standing vendettas and feuds. Once a cycle of violence begins, each act of retribution deepens the hatred, making the next act of violence easier to justify. The behavior drives the attitude, and the attitude fuels more behavior.

One commentator noted that jailers can come to look down on inmates, not just because of the crimes they committed, but because of the way the jailers treat them. The act of imprisoning and degrading someone forces the jailer to view them as less than human to justify the daily cruelty of the system. The Ben Franklin Effect, in this context, becomes a tool of oppression. It allows the powerful to rationalize the subjugation of the powerless.

Conclusion: The Power of Asking

The story of Benjamin Franklin and his rival is more than a historical curiosity; it is a blueprint for human connection. It teaches us that we are not static beings with fixed opinions of others. Our feelings are fluid, shaped by our actions. We are not just the architects of our relationships; we are also the products of them.

When we find ourselves in conflict, the instinct is often to push harder, to prove our point, to demand respect. But Franklin's strategy offers a different path. By asking for a favor, we invite the other person into our world. We give them the opportunity to be the hero, the benefactor, the wise advisor. In doing so, we trigger a psychological shift that no amount of argumentation could achieve. We change their attitude by changing their behavior.

This principle is as relevant today as it was in 18th-century Pennsylvania. Whether in the boardroom, the classroom, the training yard, or the home, the power to influence others lies not in what we can do for them, but in what we can ask them to do for us. It is a subtle, counter-intuitive truth: the best way to make someone like you is to let them do something nice for you.

The next time you face a difficult colleague, a skeptical client, or even a stubborn pet, remember the Ben Franklin Effect. Do not assume that your feelings are the cause of your actions. Instead, realize that your actions are the cause of your feelings. Be the one who asks. Let them be the one who helps. And watch as the animosity dissolves, replaced by a bond forged not in words, but in the quiet, powerful act of a favor returned.

"He that has once done you a kindness will be more ready to do you another, than he whom you yourself have obliged."

This maxim, spoken by a man who helped found a nation, remains a timeless guide to the intricate dance of human psychology. It reminds us that we are not merely observers of our relationships, but active participants in their creation. By understanding the mechanics of the Ben Franklin Effect, we gain the power to shape our world, one favor at a time.

The implications of this are vast. In a world increasingly divided by ideology and conflict, the ability to bridge the gap between "us" and "them" is more critical than ever. The Ben Franklin Effect offers a simple, accessible tool for this task. It requires no grand gestures, no expensive gifts, no elaborate speeches. It requires only the humility to ask for help. And in that act of vulnerability, we find the strength to connect, to reconcile, and to build a future defined not by our differences, but by our shared humanity.

The next time you feel the urge to prove a point, to win an argument, or to assert your dominance, pause. Consider the alternative. Consider the power of the request. Consider the possibility that the person you are trying to convince is already capable of helping you, if only you would ask. In that moment of asking, the dynamic shifts. The barrier falls. The mind adjusts. And the path to friendship, understanding, and peace opens up before you.

This is the enduring legacy of Benjamin Franklin's insight. It is a reminder that we are all capable of change, not just in our actions, but in our hearts. And sometimes, the key to unlocking that change lies in the simplest of requests: "Could you do me a favor?"
