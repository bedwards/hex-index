Timeline of the 2026 Iran war

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Timeline of the 2026 Iran war

At 20:38 UTC on February 28, 2026, the order was given. In the Situation Room, President Donald Trump authorized Operation Epic Fury, a directive that would instantly shatter the fragile peace of the Middle East. This was not a gradual escalation or a series of calibrated diplomatic ultimatums; it was a sudden, violent collision of two military superpowers, executed with a precision that left the world holding its breath. By the time the sun rose over the Persian Gulf at 06:35 UTC the following morning, the skies over Iran were already burning. The United States Central Command (CENTCOM) confirmed what the sensors had already detected: a coordinated assault involving "partner forces" had commenced. The scale was staggering. US warships unleashed volleys of Tomahawk missiles across the water, while the Army deployed High Mobility Artillery Rocket Systems (HIMARS) from forward positions. But the true weight of the American hammer fell from the stratosphere. B-2 stealth bombers, B-1 Lancers, and the legendary B-52 Stratofortresses roared into the night, unleashing their payloads on fortified ballistic missile facilities deep within Iranian territory. These were not precision strikes on isolated radar arrays; they were a systematic dismantling of the regime's ability to project power.

While the Americans hammered the infrastructure, the Israelis struck the head. At 06:45 UTC, the Israeli Air Force launched an unprecedented wave of decapitation strikes that would redefine modern warfare. The target was not merely military; it was the very soul of the Iranian state. Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, the paramount authority of the Islamic Republic, was at his residential compound attending three simultaneous meetings. In a matter of minutes, he and several high-ranking officials were killed, along with members of his family. The message was clear: the leadership vacuum was not a possibility; it was a calculated outcome. The United States Navy, Air Force, and Marine Corps focused their fury on command-and-control infrastructure, missile silos, UAV sites, and radar installations, effectively blinding the Iranian military machine. The Fars News Agency and CNN reported a symphony of explosions echoing across the nation, from the holy city of Qom to the industrial hubs of Kermanshah, Isfahan, and Karaj. In Tehran, the capital itself was no longer a sanctuary. Missile strikes tore through University Street, the Jomhouri area, and the northern Seyed Khandan district, turning the city's arteries into arteries of fire.

But war, by its nature, is never clean. The collateral damage of this high-stakes geopolitical gambit was immediate and horrific. At 07:15 UTC, a missile struck the Shajareh Tayyebeh girls' elementary school in Minab. The Iranian media, struggling to process the scale of the tragedy, reported 180 dead. Moments later, a sports hall in Lamerd, where girls were in the middle of practice, was bombed. Witnesses and state media counted at least 18 civilians among the dead. The IRGC reported strikes on the port city of Bushehr, raising fears for the nuclear reactor located there. Yet, Rafael Mariano Grossi, the chief of the UN nuclear watchdog, would later state that no evidence suggested the nuclear facilities themselves had been hit. The attacks were surgical in intent but devastating in their reach. Israel issued a stark warning to Iranian civilians residing near military industries: "Your presence in these areas puts your life at risk." It was a rare acknowledgment that the front lines of this war were not just in the deserts, but in the cities, the schools, and the homes of ordinary people.

The technological landscape of the conflict shifted instantly. For the first time in combat history, low-cost one-way attack drones, reverse-engineered from the Iranian Shahed 136 by a group known as Task, were deployed. These were not the sophisticated, expensive platforms of the past, but cheap, expendable weapons of attrition. The Israeli effort was massive, involving about 200 fighter jets in the largest combat sortie in the nation's history. They utilized the newly developed Black Sparrow air-launched ballistic missile, fired from F-15s, a weapon system designed to punch through the thickest air defenses. The ripple effects of this opening salvo were felt as far away as Jurf al-Sakhar, south of Baghdad, where a spokesperson for the Iraqi Popular Mobilization Forces confirmed the death of two of their fighters and injuries to three others in the crossfire.

The retaliation was not long in coming. Beginning at 09:05 UTC, Iran launched a barrage of ballistic missiles towards Israel and several Gulf states. The response was a wall of fire, with the IDF estimating that approximately 170 ballistic missiles had been launched. At 09:26 UTC, a missile struck the US Navy Fifth Fleet service center in Bahrain, the nerve center of American naval power in the region. By 10:15 UTC, the strikes had reached Israel. Haifa and Tel Aviv were under assault. In a residential area of Tel Aviv, a civilian woman in her forties was killed, and 27 others were injured. The IRGC boasted that Iran had targeted four major US bases: Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar, Ali Al Salem Air Base in Kuwait (which hosted Italian soldiers), Al Dhafra Air Base in the UAE, and the US Navy Fifth Fleet headquarters in Bahrain. The air-raid sirens in Bahrain wailed, warning of attacks on the capital, Manama, where Arabic media reported explosions and smoke rising into the sky.

The geography of the war expanded rapidly. Explosions were reported at Kuwait International Airport and in Abu Dhabi in the United Arab Emirates. Saudi Arabia confirmed Iranian attacks on Riyadh and the Eastern Province, claiming to have successfully intercepted the threats while vowing to "take all necessary measures," including the option of responding to aggression. The strikes were relentless. Residential areas of Dubai, in the proximity of the iconic Burj Khalifa, Dubai Marina, and the Dubai Palm, were hit. The Fairmont The Palm hotel was set ablaze, causing four injuries. A residential building on the outskirts of Doha was also struck. Local sources identified the use of Shahed drones, the workhorse weapons of Iran and its proxies, including Hamas and Hezbollah. The UAE reported intercepting a "new wave" of missiles, but the cost of defense was high. Fragments from the interceptions fell in Abu Dhabi and Dubai, causing damage to the Burj Al Arab. In Kuwait, the airport strikes caused several undefined injuries. In Bahrain, a tower in a residential area was hit by an Iranian drone during the evening. The UAE noted that one Asian national had been killed by interceptor debris that had fallen on a residential area. Qatar claimed to have intercepted at least two waves of missile attacks, reporting no casualties or property damage, a rare silver lining in a sea of destruction.

The conflict spilled over into the Kurdistan Region of Iraq, where Iranian aerial attacks targeted infrastructure hosting US military and civilian personnel, including Erbil International Airport and the US Consulate General. According to local media, most missiles and drones were intercepted, but the threat was palpable. Jordan announced that its armed forces had shot down two Iranian ballistic missiles targeting its territory and had handled 54 reports of falling debris that caused material damage but no casualties. The war was no longer a bilateral conflict; it had engulfed the entire region, turning the Gulf into a kill zone.

Israel and the United States did not stop with kinetic strikes. They executed a coordinated cyberattack on Iranian infrastructure, media, and phone apps. The goal was psychological as much as it was physical: to incite a revolution from within. The popular BadeSaba Calendar prayer app, with over 5 million downloads, was compromised. Early that morning, the app broadcast push notifications in Persian, urging military personnel and citizens to defect, lay down their weapons, and join opposition forces. "Help has arrived," the messages read. "It's time for reckoning." It was a digital siren, calling on the Iranian people to rise up against their government. Israel declared a state of emergency, citing the expectation of further Iranian attacks. Sirens blared across the Jewish state, and the government warned citizens to remain in protected areas. The Israeli Ministry of Health moved its hospital operations underground, a grim preparation for the worst-case scenario. The IDF called up 20,000 reservists in addition to the 50,000 already on duty, mobilizing the entire nation for a total war.

Following the initial barrage, the foreign ministry of Kuwait summoned Mohammad Toutounchi, Iran's ambassador, in a diplomatic protest that felt hollow against the backdrop of the airstrikes. HRANA, a human rights organization, reported 72 strikes in 20 provinces, with at least 6 civilians killed and 4 injured. The targets were diverse: 15 military or security bases, 7 pieces of civilian infrastructure, 5 hospitals and emergency bases, and two residential or educational facilities. The war was indiscriminate. Israel executed another wave of strikes against Iran, this time aiming at central Tehran. The USAF flew B-2 bombers to attack hardened ballistic missile facilities with 2,000-pound bombs. USN F/A-18s and USMC F-35s, aided by Tomahawk missiles and Lucas drones, joined the assault. CENTCOM declared that it was instructed to "dismantle the Iranian regime's security apparatus." This was the new doctrine: not just to degrade, but to destroy the very fabric of the state's power.

Israel and the United States launched strikes against targets in Tehran, Sanandaj, and other military and strategic locations, including command centers and missile sites. The IDF reported that they had bombed an F-4 and an F-5 fighter jet while they were preparing to take off from a runway, and the HQ-9B air defense system around Tehran was inactivated. The Tehran Revolutionary Court was bombed and destroyed, erasing a symbol of the regime's judicial terror. Explosions were reported near the Azadi Stadium, Azadi Square, and the Milad Tower, landmarks of a city that had become the epicenter of a global conflict. The Iranian Red Crescent Society reported explosions near hospitals in Tehran, as well as near its Peace Building, a grim irony given the organization's mission. The IDF declared that it had levelled Iran's internal security service, a move that signaled the end of the regime's ability to maintain order from within.
