Harrison family of Virginia

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Harrison family of Virginia

In the sweltering heat of July 1745, a storm battered the windows of Berkeley Plantation on the James River. Benjamin Harrison IV, a man of significant wealth and standing in the Virginia Colony, rushed to close an upstairs window. As he did, lightning struck him directly. The bolt was so fierce it killed both him and his young daughter, Hannah, who stood beside him. This sudden, violent end to a life of political ambition and agricultural expansion serves as a grim prelude to a family saga that would span nearly three centuries, intertwining the destiny of a single Virginia bloodline with the very birth and evolution of the United States. The Harrison family of Virginia is not merely a genealogical curiosity; they are the architectural framework upon which much of American political history was built, a dynasty that produced a signer of the Declaration of Independence, two presidents, and a lineage so vast it eventually touched the life of the "King of Rock and Roll" himself.

To understand the Harrisons, one must first dismantle the modern notion of a political dynasty as a closed loop of privilege. Instead, the Virginia Harrisons represent a complex bifurcation of origins, two distinct branches that sprouted from the soil of northern England before taking root in the chaotic, fertile ground of the American colonies. These two lines—the James River Harrisons and the Shenandoah Valley Harrisons—would eventually converge in the halls of power, yet they began with different trajectories, different coats of arms, and different legacies. The story begins in the north of England, in the counties of Yorkshire and Durham, where the name Harrison likely emerged from the Norse or Celtic warrior cultures that swept through the region centuries prior. Some genealogical traditions even suggest a lineage tracing back to the Viking king Cnut the Great, though the family's documented history firmly anchors them in the counties of Yorkshire and Durham by the late Middle Ages.

The first great wave of this family arrived in the New World with Benjamin Harrison I, a man whose journey would define the James River branch. Before 1633, Benjamin left the familiar stone landscapes of Yorkshire, traversing the Atlantic and making a stop in Bermuda before landing in the Virginia Colony. He settled on the banks of the James River, establishing the family seat at what would become Berkeley Plantation. This was not a quiet migration; it was a conquest of sorts. Benjamin and his descendants, often called the "James River Harrisons," became the epitome of the Virginia planter class. They were not just farmers; they were legislators, magistrates, and architects of the colonial government. By 1642, Benjamin Harrison I had already secured a place in the Virginia House of Burgesses, the first of his line to do so. His son, Benjamin II, followed in his footsteps, serving as county sheriff and eventually joining the Governor's Council, the upper house of the colony's legislature.

This momentum was not lost on the next generation. Benjamin Harrison III (1673–1710) rose to become the colony's acting Attorney General, its Treasurer, and the Speaker of the Burgesses. It was under his stewardship that the family solidified its physical and political dominance, acquiring the vast Berkeley Hundred lands from his father. He married into the powerful Carter family, uniting his lineage with Robert "King" Carter, one of the wealthiest men in the colony. His son, Benjamin Harrison IV, built the grand Berkeley Plantation that still stands today, a testament to the family's opulence. But as the lightning strike of 1745 demonstrated, the life of a Virginia planter was precarious, balancing on the edge of nature's fury and the complexities of a slave-based economy. The early Harrisons were, by the standards of their time and place, abusive slaveholders, a fact that casts a long, dark shadow over their celebrated contributions to liberty.

The transition from colonial subject to American citizen occurred under the watch of Benjamin Harrison V (1726–1791), a man whose personality was as imposing as his family's estate. Known as "the Signer," Benjamin V was a central figure in the Continental Congress. He was a man of substantial girth and boisterous spirit, a stark contrast to the stern, ascetic John Adams. Adams, who found Harrison's love for storytelling, fine food, and wine distasteful, once referred to him as the Congress's "Falstaff," a Shakespearean character known for his gluttony and wit. Adams did not mince words, calling Harrison "obscene," "profane," and "impious" in his private letters. Yet, even his harshest critic acknowledged the depth of Harrison's influence. Adams admitted that Harrison's "contributions and many pleasantries steadied rough sessions" and recognized him as a scion of one of the "most ancient, wealthy, and respectable Families in the ancient dominion."

Benjamin V's role in history was pivotal. He served as the Chairman of the Committee of the Whole in the Continental Congress, a position that placed him at the helm of the final deliberations of the Declaration of Independence. When the moment came to sign the document, it was Harrison who put his name to the paper, cementing his family's place in the founding of the nation. His political career did not end there; he later served as the Governor of Virginia. However, the friction between Harrison and Adams was not merely personal; it was ideological. Adams, a radical federalist in many respects, found Harrison too conservative, while Harrison found Adams too extreme. This dynamic created opposing alliances within the Congress, a microcosm of the political divisions that would eventually fracture the nation.

The legacy of the James River branch did not stop with the Revolution. It extended directly into the presidency of the United States, a rare feat of dynastic continuity. William Henry Harrison, the son of Benjamin V, would become the ninth President of the United States, famously known for his "Log Cabin" campaign and his shortest tenure in office, dying just 31 days after his inauguration in 1841. Decades later, Benjamin Harrison, the grandson of William Henry, became the 23rd President, serving from 1889 to 1893. These two men, separated by generations but united by blood and the legacy of Berkeley Plantation, brought the Harrison name to the highest office in the land twice. The family's influence extended beyond the presidency; descendants of this branch would go on to serve as mayors of Chicago and members of the U.S. Congress, proving that the political instinct of the James River Harrisons was a genetic trait passed down through the centuries.

Yet, while the James River line dominated the eastern seaboard and the national stage, a second, quieter branch of the family was carving out a different destiny in the rugged terrain of the Shenandoah Valley. This was the line of Isaiah Harrison, who immigrated to Virginia in 1687 from Durham, England. Unlike Benjamin I, who arrived with the full force of the colonial establishment, Isaiah settled in the frontier of the Shenandoah Valley in 1737. He was likely the son of Reverend Thomas Harrison, a chaplain in the Jamestown Colony, adding a spiritual dimension to this branch's origins. The "Valley Harrisons" were founders of entire towns, establishing Harrisonburg and Dayton, and integrating themselves into the fabric of western Virginia society.

The most profound impact of the Valley branch, however, was not in the founding of towns but in the shaping of a presidency that would redefine the nation. Isaiah Harrison's granddaughter, Bathsheba, married Captain Abraham Lincoln. Together, they were the grandparents of the 16th President of the United States, Abraham Lincoln. This connection places the Harrison family at the very heart of the American narrative, linking the Virginia planter class to the man who would eventually dismantle the institution of slavery that the Harrisons had once profited from. It is a profound irony of history that the grandson of a Virginia planter family would be the instrument of that family's moral redemption. While the early Harrisons were complicit in the "iniquity" of slavery, Abraham Lincoln, a descendant of the same stock, would dedicate his life to eliminating it.

The Valley branch also produced a diverse array of leaders and innovators who operated outside the sphere of high politics. Gessner Harrison, a professor at the University of Virginia, became a renowned linguist, contributing to the academic rigor of the young republic. His daughter, Mary Stuart, was an early leader in the women's advocacy movement, fighting for rights in an era when such ideas were radical. The medical field also claimed several Harrisons from this branch, most notably Hartwell Harrison. In 1954, Hartwell played a crucial role in the world's first successful kidney transplant, serving as the donor's surgeon. This achievement marked a turning point in modern medicine, a testament to the family's adaptability and their capacity to excel in the sciences.

The reach of the Harrison bloodline is so extensive that it eventually touched the life of Elvis Presley, the "King of Rock and Roll." Elvis was a descendant of Isaiah's granddaughter Elizabeth, bridging the gap between the aristocratic Virginia planters and the cultural icon of the 20th century. This connection underscores the sheer breadth of the family's dispersal. From the opulent ballrooms of Berkeley Plantation to the stage lights of Graceland, the Harrisons have been present in every era of American life. The family also includes Sarah Embra Harrison of Danville, Virginia, who launched the "Pass-It-On Club," a decades-long church ministry that began in the Roaring Twenties. Her four siblings would go on to become chief executives in the tobacco and banking industries, illustrating the family's continued dominance in the commercial and social sectors of the nation.

The heraldry of the family provides a visual map of these two distinct branches. Both lines utilized similar coats of arms issued in English heraldry, featuring helmets and shields emblazoned with gold eagles on a dark field. However, the specific details reveal their separate origins. The arms of William Henry Harrison, the James River president, are sourced to Yorkshire and feature three eagles, known as the "Yorkshire arms." These were granted in England in 1574 and 1613, with a pedigree dating back to 1374. A crescent below the helmet denotes that he was the second eldest son, a subtle heraldic nod to the rules of primogeniture that governed the family's inheritance.

In contrast, the "Durham arms" were used by Daniel Harrison of the Shenandoah Valley branch. These featured a single eagle and were sourced to the Harrisons of Durham. The crest included a shoulder gules, or red, signaling strength or martyrdom. These arms were established by the pedigree of Robert Harrison in 1630, tracing his lineage back to Rowland Harrison of Barnard Castle in Durham. The existence of these two distinct sets of arms serves as a reminder that while the Harrisons shared a name and a broad origin, their paths in the New World were divergent. One branch rose with the tide of the established colonial order on the James River; the other pushed westward into the frontier, building communities and shaping the character of the Shenandoah Valley.

The genealogy of the family is not without its mysteries and debates. The parentage of the original brothers who immigrated—Benjamin, Thomas, Richard, and Nathaniel—is the subject of several different viewpoints. Some genealogists, like McConathy, argue that the father was Richard Harrison, a descendant of Rowland Harrison of Durham. Others suggest the father was Thomas Harrison, Lord of Gobion's Manor, and his wife Elizabeth Bernard. Still other sources point to a merchant named Robert Harrison of Yorkshire. What is certain is that the brothers dispersed widely. Richard settled in the Connecticut Colony, Nathaniel in Boston, and Thomas briefly in Virginia before returning to England. Benjamin, the patriarch of the James River line, arrived by 1633 and immediately began his ascent in the colonial hierarchy.

The story of the Harrisons is also a story of survival and adaptation. They navigated the treacherous waters of colonial politics, the upheaval of the Revolution, the expansion of the frontier, and the Civil War. They survived lightning strikes, political rivalries, and the moral reckoning of slavery. They adapted from being slaveholding planters to becoming advocates for medical advancement, education, and social reform. The family's history is a microcosm of the American experience itself—a journey from European roots to a complex, multifaceted identity that includes both the heights of achievement and the depths of moral failure.

In the end, the Harrison family of Virginia is more than a collection of names and dates. It is a living testament to the enduring power of family legacy. From the lightning-struck Benjamin IV to the "Signer" Benjamin V, from the frontier settlers of the Shenandoah to the presidents in the White House, the Harrisons have been woven into the very fabric of the United States. Their story reminds us that history is not just made by great events, but by the people who live through them, the choices they make, and the legacies they leave behind. Whether through the political acumen of the James River line or the pioneering spirit of the Valley branch, the Harrisons have left an indelible mark on the nation. And as the story of Elvis Presley reminds us, that mark extends far beyond the grave, resonating in the culture of the modern world.

The two Virginia lines, though distinct in their origins and heraldry, ultimately share a common destiny. They are part of the same great American narrative, a narrative that began in the northern counties of England and unfolded across the vast landscape of the United States. The Harrisons did not just witness history; they helped to write it. Their lives were a tapestry of triumph and tragedy, of power and service, of wealth and redemption. As we look back on their 250-year history, we see a family that was as flawed as it was formidable, as complex as it was consequential. They are a reminder that the American experiment is built on the shoulders of those who came before, individuals whose names may be forgotten by the masses but whose impacts are felt in every corner of the nation.

The legacy of the Harrisons is not confined to the past. It lives on in the descendants who continue to serve in public office, in the medical breakthroughs that save lives, in the educational institutions that shape minds, and in the cultural icons that define eras. The "Pass-It-On Club" started by Sarah Embra Harrison in the 1920s continues to inspire faith and community service. The medical achievements of Hartwell Harrison continue to save lives through organ transplantation. The political traditions of the family continue to influence the way the nation is governed. The Harrisons of Virginia are a testament to the enduring power of family, a power that transcends time, geography, and even the moral failings of the past. They are a family that has shared in the 250-year history of the United States, and their story is far from over.
