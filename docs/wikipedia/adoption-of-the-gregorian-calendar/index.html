Adoption of the Gregorian calendar

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Adoption of the Gregorian calendar

Imagine going to sleep on October 4th and waking up on October 15th. Not because you were in a coma, but because the Pope said so. That's exactly what happened across Catholic Europe in 1582, and it would take the rest of the world more than three centuries to catch up—during which time, the simple question "What's today's date?" had no single answer.

The story of how the world adopted the Gregorian calendar is really a story about power, religion, and the stubborn mathematics of planetary motion. It explains why the October Revolution happened in November, why some Christians celebrate Christmas on January 7th, and why your Russian grandmother and your Ukrainian cousin might have argued about the date of Easter for decades.

The Problem with Julius Caesar's Calendar

The Julian calendar, introduced by Julius Caesar in 45 BCE, was a marvel of its time. Caesar's astronomers calculated that a year lasted 365.25 days, which they elegantly handled by adding a leap day every four years. Simple. Elegant. And just slightly wrong.

The actual length of a solar year—the time it takes Earth to complete one orbit around the Sun—is approximately 365.2422 days. That missing 0.0078 of a day doesn't sound like much. But here's the thing about small errors: they accumulate.

Every 128 years, the Julian calendar drifted one full day away from astronomical reality. By the 16th century, this drift had become a serious problem for the Catholic Church, but not for the reason you might expect. They weren't worried about farmers planting at the wrong time or sailors navigating by incorrect stars. They were worried about Easter.

The First Council of Nicaea in 325 CE had established that Easter should be celebrated on the first Sunday after the first full moon following the spring equinox. But if your calendar says the equinox is on March 21st while the actual astronomical equinox has slipped back to March 11th, you're celebrating Easter at the wrong time. For a church that considered the resurrection of Christ the most important event in human history, getting the date wrong was theologically intolerable.

Pope Gregory's Radical Solution

In 1582, Pope Gregory XIII issued a papal bull—the most solemn form of decree available to the Catholic Church—called "Inter gravissimas," which translates roughly as "Among the most serious concerns." The document proposed two changes.

The first was surgical: skip ten days. Just remove them from existence. Thursday, October 4, 1582, would be followed immediately by Friday, October 15, 1582. Those ten days would never happen. This would realign the calendar with the astronomical reality that had existed at the Council of Nicaea.

The second change was preventive: modify the leap year rule to prevent future drift. Under the Julian system, every year divisible by four was a leap year. Under Gregory's reform, century years would only be leap years if they were also divisible by 400. So 1700, 1800, and 1900 would not be leap years, but 1600 and 2000 would be. This subtle adjustment reduced the average year length from 365.25 days to 365.2425 days—much closer to the true solar year of 365.2422 days.

The Gregorian calendar still drifts, but so slowly that it won't be off by a full day for about 3,236 years. Our distant descendants can worry about that.

The Pope's Authority Problem

Here's where things got complicated. Pope Gregory XIII was the spiritual leader of the Catholic Church, but he had no authority over civil governments. He could decree that Catholic feast days would be calculated using his new calendar, but he couldn't force France or Spain or any other country to change the dates on their contracts, tax records, and birth certificates.

That required each civil authority to adopt the calendar separately, through their own legal processes.

Catholic countries fell in line quickly. Spain, Portugal, the Italian principalities, Poland-Lithuania, and the Catholic states of the Holy Roman Empire all adopted the new calendar in October 1582, skipping those same ten days. France followed in December, jumping from Sunday the 9th to Monday the 20th. The Spanish and Portuguese colonies followed somewhat later—not out of resistance, but simply because news traveled slowly across oceans.

But Protestant Europe looked at this papal innovation with deep suspicion.

The Protestant Resistance

For Protestants, the Reformation was still a living memory. Martin Luther had posted his Ninety-Five Theses only 65 years earlier. Wars of religion had devastated the continent. And now the Pope wanted everyone to adopt his calendar?

The resistance wasn't about mathematics. Everyone agreed the Julian calendar was wrong. But accepting the Pope's fix felt like accepting the Pope's authority, and that was a theological and political bridge too far.

In England, Queen Elizabeth I and her advisors actually agreed that the calendar needed reform. A royal commission recommended dropping ten days, just as Gregory had prescribed. But the Anglican bishops objected violently. They argued that the Pope was "undoubtedly the fourth great beast of Daniel"—a reference to the Book of Daniel's prophecy about evil kingdoms. The Queen, ever the pragmatist, quietly let the matter drop.

In Ireland, the calendar became a weapon of rebellion. Catholic rebels, fighting against English Protestant rule in the Nine Years' War, made a point of celebrating Easter on the "new" Gregorian date as a mark of defiance. Later, after England had crushed the rebellion, Irish Catholics practicing their faith in secret petitioned Rome for permission to use the old calendar—because following the new one would mark them as disloyal to the Protestant crown.

The same calendar could mean loyalty or treason, depending on who was asking and who was watching.

Prussia's Quiet Adoption and Sweden's Bizarre Experiment

The first Protestant state to adopt the Gregorian calendar was an unlikely one: the Duchy of Prussia. Though Lutheran, Prussia was technically a fiefdom of Catholic Poland, and in 1611, under pressure from the Polish crown, it agreed to the switch. On August 22, 1612, Prussians went to sleep and woke up on September 2nd.

But this was just Prussia proper. The Hohenzollern family also ruled Brandenburg—which remained on the Julian calendar because it was part of the Holy Roman Empire, not Poland. The same dynasty, the same ruling family, operating on different calendars depending on which territory you were standing in.

Sweden's approach to calendar reform was even more chaotic. In 1700, the Swedish government decided to transition to the Gregorian calendar gradually—by simply not observing leap days for the next forty years. Every four years, Sweden would skip February 29th, slowly catching up to the Gregorian calendar one day at a time.

This was a terrible idea.

For four decades, Sweden would have been out of sync with both the Julian and Gregorian calendars. The difference wouldn't even be constant—it would change every leap year. Imagine trying to coordinate trade, diplomacy, or military campaigns with neighbors whose calendars didn't match yours and wouldn't match for another generation.

Making matters worse, the plan was poorly executed. Sweden correctly skipped the leap day in 1700, but then someone forgot to skip it in 1704. And again in 1708. By that point, Sweden was ten days behind the Gregorian calendar—the same as the Julian calendar—except they had also skipped one day in 1700, leaving them uniquely positioned one day ahead of the Julian calendar and nine days behind the Gregorian.

King Charles XII finally threw up his hands and ordered a return to the Julian calendar. To make up the extra day Sweden had dropped, the kingdom invented a date that has never existed anywhere else in history: February 30, 1712.

Sweden eventually adopted the Gregorian calendar properly in 1753, dropping eleven days all at once. Finland, then part of Sweden, did the same. When Russia conquered Finland in 1809, it allowed the Finns to keep using the Gregorian calendar for autonomy, though official documents were dated in both systems until Finnish independence in 1917.

Britain Finally Joins the Modern World

Great Britain held out until 1752—170 years after the Catholic countries had switched. By then, the discrepancy had grown to eleven days, since 1700 had been a leap year in the Julian calendar but not in the Gregorian.

The Calendar (New Style) Act of 1750 is a masterpiece of British diplomatic face-saving. The law established a new calendar that just happened to match the Gregorian calendar exactly, achieved the same Easter calculation through different mathematical means, and never once mentioned the Pope. Wednesday, September 2, 1752, was followed by Thursday, September 14, 1752.

The same act also changed the start of the legal year from March 25th to January 1st—a separate reform that England had been resisting since the Middle Ages. This is why the British tax year still begins on April 6th: it's March 25th plus the eleven days that were "lost" in 1752, with an additional day added when 1800 wasn't a leap year. Tax authorities have long memories.

There's a popular myth that mobs rioted in the streets chanting "Give us back our eleven days!" Historians have found little evidence this actually happened on any significant scale, though there was certainly some confusion, especially around rents, wages, and interest calculations that were measured in days.

The Long Holdouts of Eastern Europe

The last major European regions to adopt the Gregorian calendar were Eastern Orthodox countries, and their resistance lasted into the twentieth century.

For Orthodox Christians, the objection wasn't just about the Pope's authority—it was about breaking faith with tradition. The Orthodox churches traced their practices back to the early church councils, and the Council of Nicaea had used the Julian calendar. To abandon it felt like abandoning the authentic Christian heritage.

Russia didn't adopt the Gregorian calendar until after the October Revolution—which is why the October Revolution actually happened in November. On January 24, 1918, the Soviet government decreed that Wednesday, January 31st would be followed by Thursday, February 14th. The thirteen-day gap reflected the additional century years (1700, 1800, 1900) that had been leap years under the Julian system but not under the Gregorian.

Romania switched in 1919. Greece was the last Orthodox country in Europe to adopt the Gregorian calendar for civil purposes, doing so in 1923 after a military revolution. February 15th was followed by March 1st.

But here's the crucial distinction: these countries adopted the Gregorian calendar for civil purposes—government documents, business contracts, train schedules. Their Orthodox churches did not. Most Orthodox churches continue to use the Julian calendar for religious observances, which is why Orthodox Christmas falls on January 7th in the Gregorian calendar. It's not a different date; it's December 25th on a different calendar.

The Revised Julian Calendar: A Compromise That Almost Works

In 1923, a council of Orthodox churches met in Constantinople to address the calendar problem. They didn't want to adopt the Pope's calendar, but they recognized the astronomical arguments were sound. Their solution was the Revised Julian calendar.

The Revised Julian calendar uses a more complex leap year rule than either the Julian or Gregorian systems. Century years are leap years only if, when divided by 900, they leave a remainder of either 200 or 600. This produces an average year length of 365.242222 days—actually closer to the true solar year than the Gregorian calendar.

More importantly, for the next several centuries, the Revised Julian calendar produces exactly the same dates as the Gregorian calendar. The two won't diverge until the year 2800. This allowed Orthodox churches to claim they were using their own calendar, distinct from Rome's, while in practice matching the civil calendar.

Some Orthodox churches adopted the Revised Julian calendar. Others refused. The Russian, Serbian, Georgian, Polish, and Macedonian Orthodox churches still use the original Julian calendar. The Greek Orthodox Church of Jerusalem does too. These churches celebrate Christmas thirteen days after the civil calendar says December 25th—and they'll celebrate it fourteen days later starting in 2100, when the drift adds another day.

Ukraine: Where Calendars Became a Battlefield

The history of calendars in Ukraine perfectly encapsulates the country's complicated position between East and West.

When Pope Gregory XIII issued his reform in 1582, most of Ukraine was part of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, a Catholic state that immediately adopted the new calendar. The Roman Catholics in Ukraine switched right away. But the Orthodox Christians refused—not because they were loyal to Russia, but because they weren't loyal to Rome.

For centuries, multiple calendars coexisted in the same territory. The civil calendar followed whoever was in charge—Poland, Austria-Hungary, the Russian Empire, the Soviet Union, independent Ukraine. But the Orthodox churches maintained the Julian calendar for religious purposes.

This changed dramatically after Russia's full-scale invasion in 2022. Suddenly, using the same calendar as the Russian Orthodox Church felt like collaboration with the enemy. The Ukrainian Lutheran Church switched to the Revised Julian calendar in 2018. After the invasion, the Evangelical Christian Baptists and Pentecostals switched to the Gregorian calendar. In September 2023, both the Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church and the Orthodox Church of Ukraine announced they would abandon the Julian calendar.

Only the Ukrainian Orthodox Church affiliated with the Moscow Patriarchate declared it would continue celebrating Christmas on the old Julian date—a decision that, in the context of an ongoing war, carried profound political implications.

For Ukrainian families with members in Russia, or with relatives who belong to different church traditions, this means that Christmas might now fall on different days depending on which branch of the family you're celebrating with. A calendar disagreement that began with Pope Gregory XIII in 1582 has become, four and a half centuries later, another front in a war that has already split families in countless other ways.

Why Any of This Matters

The adoption of the Gregorian calendar might seem like a settled historical question. Almost every country in the world now uses it for civil purposes. International business, aviation, computing, and diplomacy all run on Gregorian time.

But the story of how we got here reveals something important about how human institutions actually work. A calendar isn't just a way of counting days. It's a statement about who has the authority to define time itself. When Protestant countries refused to adopt the Pope's calendar, they weren't arguing about astronomy—they were arguing about power. When Orthodox churches still use the Julian calendar for religious purposes, they're not making a mathematical claim—they're making a spiritual one about the continuity of tradition.

And when Ukrainian churches abandon the Julian calendar in the midst of a war, they're not suddenly convinced by arguments that have been available for 440 years. They're making a statement about identity, about which community they belong to, about who they refuse to share even the date of Christmas with anymore.

The Gregorian calendar won because it was more accurate. But accuracy was never really the point. The question was always: whose accuracy? Declared by whom? Adopted on whose terms?

Those questions, it turns out, can take centuries to answer. And for some families, they're being answered right now.
