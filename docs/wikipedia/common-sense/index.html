Common sense

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Common Sense

The Pamphlet That Lit the Fuse

In January 1776, a 47-page pamphlet appeared in Philadelphia bookshops with no author's name on the cover. Within three months, it had sold 120,000 copies. Adjusted for population, that's the equivalent of selling more than six and a half million books today—in just ninety days. The pamphlet was called Common Sense, and it didn't just argue for American independence. It made independence thinkable.

Before Thomas Paine put pen to paper, most colonists weren't dreaming of a new nation. They were angry at Parliament, yes. They were shooting at British soldiers, certainly—the Battles of Lexington and Concord had already happened. But separate from Britain entirely? That felt like madness.

Paine himself noticed this when he arrived in America in November 1774. "Their attachment to Britain was obstinate," he later wrote, "and it was, at that time, a kind of treason to speak against it."

He spoke against it anyway.

A Stranger With a Printing Press

Thomas Paine was not a founding father in the traditional sense. He wasn't a Virginia planter or a Boston lawyer. He was a recent immigrant, a failed corset-maker from England who had arrived in the colonies just months before the shooting started. He had a letter of introduction from Benjamin Franklin and not much else.

What he did have was a gift for plain language. While other political writers of his era wrapped their arguments in Latin phrases and classical allusions, Paine wrote like he was talking to you in a tavern. This was deliberate. He wanted common people—farmers, shopkeepers, tradesmen—to understand exactly what he was saying and why it mattered to them.

He started writing what would become Common Sense in late 1775, originally planning it as a series of newspaper letters under the working title Plain Truth. The project grew too big for that format. It wanted to be a pamphlet—a self-contained argument you could carry in your pocket, read aloud to your neighbors, pass from hand to hand.

The Publishing Wars

Getting Common Sense into print created its own drama. Benjamin Rush, the Philadelphia physician and reformer, recommended a printer named Robert Bell, assuring Paine that Bell wouldn't hesitate at the pamphlet's incendiary content. Rush was right about that—Bell published it on January 10, 1776, and immediately began promoting it with characteristic zeal.

The pamphlet sold out almost instantly. Bell ordered a second printing.

This is where things got complicated. Paine had planned to donate his profits to buy mittens for General Montgomery's troops, who were freezing in Quebec. When he sent intermediaries to collect his share from Bell, they discovered the accounts showed zero profit. Whether Bell was cooking the books or genuinely operating on thin margins, Paine was furious.

He told Bell not to proceed with a second edition—Paine wanted to add appendices and release an expanded version himself. Bell ignored him and advertised a "new edition" anyway.

What followed was a month-long public feud conducted in the pages of Philadelphia newspapers. Paine, still anonymous, accused Bell of fraud. Bell accused the unknown author of duplicity. Both men kept publishing competing editions. The scandal probably helped sales—nothing moves merchandise like controversy.

Paine never made money from Common Sense. He gave away the copyright, letting any printer who asked reproduce it freely. The Bradford brothers, who published his expanded edition, didn't generate profits either. The most influential political pamphlet in American history earned its author nothing but fame.

What Made It So Powerful

The genius of Common Sense wasn't just its timing, though the timing was perfect. It wasn't just its accessibility, though Paine's prose style was revolutionary in itself. The pamphlet worked because it reframed the entire debate.

Before Paine, colonial grievances focused on specific policies—taxes, quartering of troops, interference with local governance. The goal was to restore the rights of Englishmen, to get Parliament to back off, to return to some imagined golden age of benevolent imperial oversight.

Paine said that goal was absurd. The problem wasn't bad policies. The problem was monarchy itself.

He structured his argument in four parts, building from philosophical first principles to practical military strategy. The architecture was deliberate. Historian Gordon S. Wood called it "the most incendiary and popular pamphlet of the entire revolutionary era."

Part One: Why Government Exists

Paine began not with America or Britain but with human nature. Imagine a small group of people, he said, isolated from the rest of humanity. They would quickly discover that cooperation made life easier. They would form a society.

As that society grew, problems would emerge. Not everyone would cooperate voluntarily. Rules would become necessary. Someone would need to enforce them. Government, Paine argued, was a "necessary evil"—necessary because humans weren't angels, evil because it restricted freedom by its very nature.

The best government, then, was the one that secured order with the least restriction. And that brought him to the English constitution, which colonists had been taught to revere as the pinnacle of political wisdom.

Paine found two tyrannies embedded in it: the king, who ruled by hereditary right, and the House of Lords, filled with aristocrats who had done nothing to earn their power. Neither contributed anything useful. Both extracted enormous wealth from ordinary people. The whole system was rigged to benefit those who happened to be born into the right families.

Part Two: The Case Against Kings

This section reveals something often forgotten about Paine and his audience: both were steeped in Protestant Christianity. Paine made his case against monarchy not just from political philosophy but from scripture.

The Bible, he argued, clearly opposed the institution of kingship. He walked readers through the story of Gideon, who refused the people's demand that he rule over them. He reproduced nearly all of First Samuel, chapter eight, in which the prophet Samuel warns the Israelites that a king will take their sons for his armies, their daughters for his servants, and the best of their fields for his own use.

The Almighty hath here entered his protest against monarchical government.

This biblical framing was strategic. Paine knew his readers. Many were dissenting Protestants who had fled religious persecution in Europe. They already distrusted established churches and the monarchs who headed them. Paine gave them permission to see independence not as rebellion against God-ordained authority but as faithfulness to scriptural principles.

He then turned historical, cataloging the wars, usurpations, and oppressions that monarchies had inflicted throughout European history. The king of England, he noted acidly, had little to do "than to make war and give away places; which in plain terms, is to impoverish the nation and set it together by the ears."

Eight hundred thousand pounds a year—the king's income—for that.

Of more worth is one honest man to society and in the sight of God, than all the crowned ruffians that ever lived.

Part Three: What Independence Would Look Like

Having demolished the case for monarchy, Paine turned to what should replace it. He proposed what he called a "Continental Charter"—essentially a constitution—that would guarantee freedom, property, and religious liberty to all.

His proposed structure was remarkably detailed. Each colony would elect representatives to a Continental Conference, which would draft the charter. The new government would center on a Congress of at least 390 delegates, meeting annually. A president would be chosen through a lottery system designed to ensure every colony had its turn providing the chief executive.

Passing laws or electing a president would require three-fifths of Congress to agree. This supermajority requirement was meant to prevent any faction from dominating—though it would later prove controversial when similar thresholds were embedded in the actual Constitution.

One passage in this section stands out for modern readers. Paine accused Britain of having "stirred up the Indians and the Negroes to destroy us." This was a reference to British policies that offered freedom to enslaved people who escaped to British lines—policies that terrified slaveholding colonists. The irony of denouncing Britain for offering liberty to the enslaved while demanding liberty for the colonies themselves apparently escaped Paine. It would not escape later readers.

Part Four: We Can Win This

The final section addressed the practical question: could the colonies actually defeat the British Empire? Paine argued yes, spending pages describing how American shipyards, with their abundant lumber supplies, could quickly build a navy to rival Britain's.

This was optimistic—arguably wildly so. The Royal Navy was the most powerful military force on Earth. American shipbuilding capacity, while substantial, was nowhere near sufficient to challenge it directly. But Paine understood that revolutions require confidence. People don't risk their lives for causes they think will fail.

The Backlash

Not everyone was convinced. The Reverend Charles Inglis, an Anglican cleric in New York who remained loyal to the Crown, published a response called The True Interest of America Impartially Stated. "I find no common sense in this pamphlet," he wrote, "but much uncommon frenzy."

Inglis defended constitutional monarchy as "the form of government which is most favourable to liberty." He dismissed Paine as a "crack-brained zealot for democracy."

Another loyalist, James Chalmers, published his own rebuttal under the pen name "Candidus." His pamphlet was titled, pointedly, Plain Truth—the name Paine had originally planned for his own work. Chalmers warned that without the monarchy, the colonies would descend into chaos. "Independence And Slavery Are Synonymous Terms," he proclaimed.

Even some future American leaders had reservations. John Adams, who would become the second president, found Paine's vision too radical. The proposed government was "so democratical, without any restraint or even an attempt at any equilibrium or counter poise," Adams wrote, "that it must produce confusion and every evil work."

Adams wasn't wrong that Paine's sketch lacked the checks and balances that would later characterize the Constitution. But Adams was also a Massachusetts lawyer whose ideas about proper government came from classical education and colonial assemblies. Paine was writing for farmers who had never read Montesquieu.

Six Months That Changed Everything

The debate over Common Sense dominated American public discourse for the first half of 1776. Paine threw himself into it, writing under the pen name "The Forester" to answer critics in Philadelphia newspapers. He was running, essentially, a six-month publicity campaign for independence.

The reviews—or what passed for reviews in that era, mainly excerpts from letters printed in colonial papers—were overwhelmingly positive. "If you know the author of COMMON SENSE," one Maryland reader wrote to the Pennsylvania Evening Post, "tell him he has done wonders and worked miracles. His stile is plain and nervous; his facts are true; his reasoning, just and conclusive."

Another reviewer attributed the pamphlet's appeal to its invitation for ordinary people to participate: it gave "liberty to every individual to contribute materials for that great building, the grand charter of American Liberty."

The pamphlet didn't just sell. It spread through networks of reading and recitation that extended far beyond the literate population. Tavern keepers read it aloud to their customers. Preachers referenced it from their pulpits. Hand-copied summaries circulated in areas where printed copies hadn't yet arrived.

When the Continental Congress voted for independence on July 2, 1776—the Declaration itself was adopted two days later—many of the delegates had Common Sense ringing in their ears. The document Thomas Jefferson drafted echoed Paine's arguments, particularly the catalog of royal abuses.

The Afterlife of an Idea

Paine remained anonymous for nearly three months after publication. His name didn't become publicly connected to Common Sense until March 30, 1776. By then, the pamphlet had taken on a life of its own.

It crossed the Atlantic in both directions. In France, where revolution was still thirteen years away, a translated edition appeared in 1791—though the French publishers prudently cut the passages attacking monarchy. The British government, unsurprisingly, was not pleased by any edition.

The sales figures remain disputed. Paine claimed 120,000 copies in the first three months. Some historians accept estimates as high as 500,000 copies sold in the first year, including European editions. Others argue these numbers are implausibly high given colonial literacy rates and suggest 75,000 as an upper limit.

Even the conservative estimates make Common Sense one of the most successful publications in proportion to population in American history. As of 2006, it remained the all-time best-selling American title relative to population. It's still in print today, nearly 250 years later.

The Art of Rhetorical Reversal

Part of what made Common Sense so effective was a technique as old as rhetoric itself: turning your opponent's arguments against them. Paine took the language of British political thought—common sense, plain truth, the natural rights of Englishmen—and used it to demolish British authority.

The title itself was a provocation. "Common sense" in eighteenth-century usage meant the basic reasoning capacity shared by all people, not just the educated elite. By claiming that name, Paine implied that anyone who disagreed with him was denying obvious reality. His opponents weren't just wrong; they were unreasonable.

This technique of rhetorical reversal runs through American political history. The British had accused revolution-minded colonists of regressing to barbarism, of abandoning the civilized order that monarchy represented. Paine flipped the script: monarchy was the barbarism. Hereditary rule was the primitive holdover. Democracy was the enlightened future.

Every revolutionary movement since has borrowed from his playbook.

What Paine Got Wrong

Hero worship makes for bad history. Paine was a brilliant polemicist, but he was not infallible.

His confidence that America could quickly build a navy to rival Britain's was fantasy. The Continental Navy never came close to challenging British sea power; American victory in the Revolution depended heavily on the French fleet.

His proposed government, with its lottery for the presidency and its three-fifths voting requirements, was never seriously considered by the Continental Congress. The Articles of Confederation that the states eventually adopted had different problems, and the Constitution that replaced them borrowed almost nothing from Paine's sketch.

Most troublingly, his denunciation of British policies that offered freedom to enslaved people reveals the limits of his radicalism. Paine elsewhere wrote against slavery—his essay "African Slavery in America" appeared in 1775—but in Common Sense, he prioritized solidarity with slaveholding colonists over consistency on human liberty.

The revolution Paine championed created a nation that would take another ninety years to abolish slavery, and another century beyond that to begin dismantling legal segregation. The "common sense" of 1776 had significant blind spots.

Why It Still Matters

Two and a half centuries later, Common Sense remains worth reading—not as scripture but as a masterclass in political persuasion.

Paine understood something that many political writers forget: people don't change their minds because of superior logic alone. They change their minds when someone gives them permission to think what they were already half-thinking, when someone makes a new idea feel inevitable rather than radical.

Before January 1776, independence was unthinkable for most colonists. By July, it was common sense.

That transformation didn't happen because Paine invented new arguments. It happened because he made existing arguments feel different. He wrote with urgency and confidence. He addressed his readers as equals. He made monarchy seem not just wrong but absurd, and democracy seem not just possible but natural.

The pamphlet form helped. A 47-page argument you could read in an afternoon and discuss with your neighbors had different social dynamics than a treatise that sat on a shelf. Common Sense was designed to spread, and spread it did.

When you read it today, you're reading the moment when America stopped being an argument and started being a foregone conclusion. That moment was manufactured—by a recently arrived immigrant with a gift for plain language and an instinct for what people were ready to hear.
