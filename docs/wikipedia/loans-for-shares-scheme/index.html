Loans for shares scheme

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Loans for shares scheme

In the winter of 1995, the Russian state was hemorrhaging money. The economy had contracted by nearly 20 percent in the previous year, the ruble was in freefall, and the government of Boris Yeltsin faced a fiscal deficit so severe that it could not pay its own workers, let alone fund a presidential campaign. The political horizon was equally grim; in the December 1995 elections, the Communist Party had seized control of the State Duma, the lower house of parliament, signaling a potent resurgence of the very ideology Yeltsin had spent his political life dismantling. The specter of a 1996 Communist victory loomed large, threatening to reverse the decade of market reforms and return Russia to a command economy. In this crucible of economic desperation and political existentialism, a solution was forged in the backrooms of Moscow that would irrevocably alter the trajectory of the nation: the loans-for-shares scheme.

It began not with a grand legislative decree, but with a pragmatic, if cynical, handshake between the state and the emerging class of Russian bankers. The premise was deceptively simple on paper. The government, lacking the cash to pay its debts or fund its operations, would offer state-owned shares in the crown jewels of the Soviet industrial heritage—massive oil refineries, nickel smelters, and steel mills—as collateral for loans provided by commercial banks. If the government repaid the loans by a certain date, the shares would return to the state. If not, the banks would be free to auction off the shares to recover their money. It was a standard financial instrument in theory, but in the context of a collapsing state, it became a mechanism for the largest transfer of wealth in human history outside of a war.

The architects of this scheme were not faceless bureaucrats but specific individuals driven by specific motives. Anatoly Chubais, the deputy prime minister and the architect of Russia's privatization, was the central figure. He was a man of intense conviction, backed by a network of Western advisors, including those from the Harvard Institute for International Development (HIID) and funded in part by USAID programs coordinated by Richard Morningstar. Chubais believed that the only way to save Russian democracy was to create a class of private owners who had a vested interest in the survival of the market economy. He feared that without a robust private sector, the Communist resurgence would be inevitable. To him, the speed of privatization outweighed the price. As Professor of political science Russell Bova later noted, Chubais may have been motivated by the fear that the country would revert to communism if progress was not maintained, believing that long-term political goals of democratization were more important than short-term gains from asset sales. "If that meant undervaluing State assets then so be it," was the unspoken calculus.

However, the execution of this plan was far from a neutral economic exercise. The scheme was proposed by Vladimir Potanin, the head of Oneximbank, a man who understood the levers of power in the new Russia better than almost anyone. It was Potanin who realized that the auctions could be structured to ensure Yeltsin's victory. The logic was chillingly circular: the loans would be allocated before the 1996 election, but the actual auction of the shares would only take place after the election. This meant that the banks holding the collateral had a massive financial incentive to ensure Yeltsin won. If Yeltsin lost and the Communists took power, the new government would likely repudiate the loans or, worse, nationalize the assets without paying. Therefore, the survival of the banks' fortunes was inextricably linked to the survival of Yeltsin's presidency.

On August 31, 1995, Yeltsin held a pivotal meeting with ten of Russia's most powerful business moguls. The atmosphere was one of mutual recognition of necessity. In his remarks to the group, Yeltsin made a comment that would echo through the history of post-Soviet Russia: "Russian bankers take part in the country's political life. … The banks, like all of Russia, are learning democracy." It was an endorsement of a new political reality where capital and state power were merging. The banks agreed to provide the billions of dollars needed to keep the government solvent and fund the election campaign. In exchange, they were given the keys to the kingdom.

The auctions, held between November and December 1995, were a masterclass in rigging. They lacked competition, were controlled by favored insiders, and were designed to benefit the commercial banks themselves. The government, desperate for liquidity, set reserve prices at bargain-basement levels, often a fraction of the true market value of the assets. The "loan" amounts were frequently set so low that the banks knew they would never be repaid, effectively turning the transaction into a direct sale. The result was the rapid emergence of a new elite, a class of enterprise owners who would come to be known as the Russian oligarchs.

Vladimir Potanin was the first to strike gold. Through Oneximbank, he secured the rights to Norilsk Nickel, the world's number one smelter of palladium and nickel. The valuation was absurdly low, a fraction of the global value of the reserves. Potanin, along with Mikhail Khodorkovskii of Menatep and Boris Berezovskii, moved to acquire the oil giants Sidanco, Yukos, and Sibneft. These were not just companies; they were the lifeblood of the Russian economy. In a matter of months, the state lost control of its most profitable industrial assets to a handful of men who had previously been minor players in the Soviet shadow economy.

The mechanism was structured to eliminate the old guard. To make these companies profitable, the new investors needed to restructure them, installing a Western-style management approach and eliminating the communist bureaucracy that had paralyzed the Soviet industrial complex. This required pushing out entrenched managers with communist allegiances. This task had already become immensely more cumbersome once the communists took control of the legislature in the 1995 elections. If the communists were to take control of the executive government, the restructuring would be impossible. The auctions were, therefore, a political weapon as much as a financial one. By tying the fate of the banks to Yeltsin, the oligarchs created a financial imperative for a Yeltsin victory. They poured money into his campaign, used their media empires to broadcast his message, and mobilized their resources to ensure that the Communists did not win.

The 1996 presidential election became a referendum on the loans-for-shares scheme. The oligarchs, now captains of industry, threw their weight behind Yeltsin. The campaign was fierce, funded by the very money borrowed against the state's assets. When Yeltsin won, the scheme was vindicated in the eyes of its architects. The Communists were defeated, the market economy was secured, and a new class of owners was in place. The loans were never repaid, and the shares were transferred to the banks, effectively privatizing the Russian state's wealth at a fraction of its cost.

The aftermath of the loans-for-shares scheme was profound and largely negative for the Russian public. The scheme is widely perceived as unfair and fraudulent. It gave rise to a class of oligarchs who concentrated enormous assets in their hands, further increasing the wealth gap in Russia and contributing to political instability. The wealth gap that emerged was not just a statistical anomaly; it was a social fracture. A handful of men controlled the oil, the metals, and the media, while the average Russian citizen watched the value of their savings evaporate and their public services collapse.

In the medium term, the scheme impeded Russian growth. The oligarchs, realizing that their purchases could be seen as fraudulent by future governments, did not have the incentive to build up the enterprises they had acquired. Instead, they attempted to strip assets from the government enterprises. They extracted value, moved capital offshore, and prioritized short-term gains over long-term investment. The lack of legitimacy of their ownership hung over the entire economy, creating an environment of uncertainty that discouraged foreign investment and stifled domestic innovation. The companies that were supposed to be the engines of a new, dynamic Russian economy often became hollow shells, drained of their value by the very men who were supposed to revitalize them.

The legacy of loans-for-shares is a testament to the dangers of privatization without transparency or fairness. It demonstrated that the transfer of state assets to private hands, if done in the shadows and driven by political expediency, can lead to the consolidation of power and wealth in the hands of a few, at the expense of the many. The scheme was a victory for Yeltsin in the short term, securing his reelection and preventing a Communist resurgence. But it was a defeat for the Russian people, who lost the potential wealth of their national resources to a small, unaccountable elite.

The story of loans-for-shares is also a story of international involvement. The role of the United States and Western institutions in this process is a subject of intense debate. The scheme was overseen by Chubais, who was linked to the USAID program managed by the head of HIID. USAID provided targeted investments to assist Chubais, and the World Bank made a loan for a small percentage of the Sibneft oil company. Richard Morningstar, a U.S. aid coordinator for the former Soviet Union, acknowledged these targeted investments. The involvement of Western advisors and institutions lent a veneer of legitimacy to the process, but it also raised questions about the extent to which the West was complicit in the creation of the Russian oligarchy. The "Letter of thirteen," a document signed by prominent Russian bankers, highlighted the extent of the banking community's involvement, but it also underscored the lack of oversight and the concentration of power.

The "less cynical" interpretation proposed by Russell Bova suggests that Chubais may have been motivated by a genuine belief that the survival of democracy in Russia required a rapid and decisive break with the past. In the face of mid-1990s economic difficulty, the fear of a Communist resurgence was real and palpable. Chubais may have believed that the long-term political goals of democratization and asset distribution were more important than the short-term losses from undervaluing state assets. This perspective frames the loans-for-shares scheme not as a conspiracy, but as a desperate gamble to save Russian democracy from itself. However, even if this interpretation holds water, the outcome was the same: a massive transfer of wealth to a few, and a deepening of the social and economic divides that continue to plague Russia today.

The loans-for-shares scheme was a turning point in Russian history. It marked the end of the Soviet era and the beginning of the oligarchic era. It was a moment when the fate of a nation was decided not by the ballot box, but by the balance sheet. The scheme helped with fundraising for Yeltsin's 1996 reelection campaign and restructuring freshly-sold companies, but it did so at a cost that Russia is still paying. The emergence of the oligarchs, the concentration of wealth, the erosion of public trust in the state, and the impediment to economic growth are all direct consequences of this scheme.

The story of the loans-for-shares scheme is a cautionary tale about the power of capital and the fragility of democracy. It shows how, in times of crisis, the lines between public and private, between state and market, can become dangerously blurred. It demonstrates how the pursuit of short-term political goals can lead to long-term economic and social disasters. And it serves as a reminder that the way a nation distributes its wealth is as important as the wealth itself. In 1995, Russia chose a path that secured the presidency of Boris Yeltsin but forfeited the economic future of its people. The oligarchs emerged as the winners, but the country lost. The scars of that decision are still visible today, in the concentration of wealth, the political instability, and the deep-seated cynicism that pervades Russian society.

The events of 1995 and 1996 were not just a financial transaction; they were a political revolution. The oligarchs, once minor players, became the captains of industry, the masters of the media, and the kingmakers of Russian politics. They used their newfound wealth to shape the political landscape, ensuring that their interests were protected and their power was maintained. The loans-for-shares scheme was the catalyst for this transformation, a moment when the state sold its soul to the highest bidder. The result was a Russia that was rich in resources but poor in democracy, a nation where the wealth of the few was built on the poverty of the many. The story of the loans-for-shares scheme is a story of ambition, desperation, and the high cost of political survival. It is a story that continues to shape the destiny of Russia, and a warning to the world about the dangers of privatization without accountability. The lessons of 1995 are not just for Russia; they are for any nation that faces the difficult choices of economic reform and political transition. The loans-for-shares scheme was a moment when the future of a nation was decided in the shadows, and the consequences of that decision are still being felt today. The oligarchs may have won the auction, but the Russian people paid the price. The scheme was a victory for the few, but a defeat for the many. And in the end, the cost of that victory was too high to bear.
