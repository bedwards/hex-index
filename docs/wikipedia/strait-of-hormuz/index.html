Strait of Hormuz

9 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Strait of Hormuz

In the summer of 1988, a U.S. Navy guided missile cruiser mistakenly identified an Iranian civilian airliner as a hostile fighter jet and destroyed it over the Strait of Hormuz. Nearly three hundred people perished in the explosion—passengers en route to their lives, gone in an instant because of a warship's error in one of the world's most contested waterways. The tragedy was only one chapter in a decades-long saga of tension, confrontation, and strategic calculation that has made this narrow channel between Iran and Oman perhaps the most consequential strip of ocean on Earth.

The Strait of Hormuz stretches roughly 167 kilometers—about 104 miles—when measured from tip to tip across its most logical crossing points. It is long but not uniformly so; the width varies dramatically, contractinge compresses to as narrow as 39 kilometers in certain stretches while widening to nearly 97 kilometers in others. The northern coast belongs to Iran. The southern coast belongs to Oman, an exclave of its own territory called the Musandam Peninsula that juts into the Persian Gulf like a finger pointing toward the Arabian Sea. Together, these two nations control virtually every vessel that passes through.

It is the only sea passage from the Persian Gulf—the vast oil-laden inland sea that powers global energy markets—to the open ocean. Without this channel, tankers carrying millions of barrels of crude would be locked in place, unable to reach Asia, Europe, or anywhere else. The numbers are staggering: during an ordinary year between 2023 and 2025, twenty percent of the world's liquefied natural gas and twenty-five percent of seaborne oil trade moved through this strait. Every single day, millions of barrels flow toward Asian markets—Japan, India, South Korea, China—where energy-hungry economies hunger for more.

In 2018, twenty-one million barrels passed through the strait daily, worth approximately $1.2 billion at that year's prices. The United States Energy Information Administration documented that in 2011 alone, an average of fourteen tankers per day emerged from the Persian Gulf carrying about seventeen million barrels of crude oil. More than eighty-five percent of this crude traveled to Asian markets. The Strait of Hormuz is not merely a geographic feature—it is the jugular through which the world's petroleum flows.

Etymology and Ancient History

The name "Hormuz" carries with it layers of myth, empire, and linguistic confusion that stretch back centuries. Persian etymology derives the word from the Middle Persian pronunciation of Ahura Mazda—the great Zoroastrian god of wisdom and light. The resemblance between this sacred name and the geographic term has produced a durable belief among scholars that these words are related.

Yet other theories persist. Some linguists argue "Hormuz" comes from the local Persian word Hur-Mogh, meaning "Place of Dates," referencing the date palm groves that once lined the coast. Another theory suggests the strait may have been named after Ifra Hormizd, the mother of King Shapur II of Persia, who ruled between 309 and 379 AD—a figure whose memory somehow survived into the naming of a channel she certainly never saw.

A less likely but persistent hypothesis holds that the name derives from the Greek word ὅρμος—hormos—meaning "cove" or "bay," suggesting an ancient connection to Mediterranean navigational terminology. Scholars, historians, and linguists continue to debate these origins, though certainty remains elusive.

The earliest known description of the strait appears in the Periplus of the Erythraean Sea, a first-century mariner's guide written by anonymous navigators who described the opening to the Persian Gulf without giving it a name. The ancient text noted: "At the upper end of these Calaei islands is a range of mountains called Calon, and there follows not far beyond, the mouth of the Persian Gulf, where there is much diving for the pearl-mussel."

The guide continues with vivid detail: "To the left of the straits are great mountains called Asabon and to the right there rises in full view another round and high mountain called Semiramis; between them the passage across the strait is about six hundred stadia; beyond which that very great and broad sea, the Persian Gulf, reaches far into the interior."

From the tenth to the seventeenth centuries AD, the Kingdom of Ormus flourished in this region—a name that also gave rise to the strait's alternate designation as the Strait of Ormuz. Scholars derived "Ormuz" from the local Persian word هورمغ, Hur-mogh, meaning date palm. The resemblance between this Persian word and the Persian name of the Zoroastrian god هرمز Hormoz—a variant of Ahura Mazda—created a linguistic bridge that scholars continue to untangle.

Foreign Powers and Strategic Geography

From the fifteenth century onward, European powers arrived and fundamentally altered the strategic calculus of this waterway. The Portuguese maintained a presence between the sixteenth and eighteenth centuries, treating the region as their private commercial preserve until other emerging powers—England in particular—arrived to contest their dominance.

By the seventeenth century, the geography of the strait had become a focal point for international rivalry. The arrival of foreign powers transformed it from a local passage into a global crossroads where empires vied for control over the world's energy flows.

Legal Status and Territorial Claims

To reduce the risk of catastrophic collision between the tens of thousands of vessels that now traverse these waters annually, ships follow a traffic separation scheme—commonly called TSS. Inbound ships use one lane; outbound ships use another. Each lane is two miles wide, separated by a two-mile-wide median where no vessel should linger.

The passage through the strait involves navigating the territorial waters of both Iran and Oman under the transit passage provisions of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea—UNCLOS. Although not all nations have ratified this convention, most—including the United States—accept these customary navigation rules as codified in international law.

Yet legal status is not static; it shifts with political ambition. In 1959, Iran altered the legal status of the strait by expanding its territorial sea to twelve nautical miles and declaring it would recognize only transit through innocent passage within this newly expanded area. In 1972, Oman followed suit, also expanding its territorial sea to twelve nautical miles by decree.

By 1972, the Strait of Hormuz was completely enclosed—closed—by the combined territorial waters of both nations. During the 1970s, neither Iran nor Oman attempted to impede the passage of warships through these claimed waters. But in the 1980s, both countries asserted legal claims that diverged from customary international law.

Upon ratifying UNCLOS in 1989, Oman submitted declarations confirming its 1981 royal decree that only innocent passage was permitted through Omani territorial waters—a declaration requiring prior permission before foreign warships could pass. Iran entered a similar declaration in 1982, stating that only states parties to the Law of the Sea Convention shall be entitled to benefit from contractual rights created therein—including the right of transit passage through straits used for international navigation.

In 1993, Iran enacted a comprehensive law on maritime areas whose provisions conflict with UNCLOS conventions, including requirements that warships, submarines, and nuclear-powered ships obtain permission before exercising innocent passage through Iranian territorial waters. The United States does not recognize any of these claims by Oman or Iran and has contested each one.

Conflicts and Confrontations

The strait has never been closed for extended periods during Middle East conflicts—unlike the Straits of Tiran or Bab-el-Mandeb—though Iran has occasionally threatened to close it, and preparations to mine the channel have been undertaken. The threats alone are enough to send shockwaves through global energy markets.

During the Iran–Iraq War, Iraq attacked Iranian oil terminals and tankers at Kharg Island in early 1984, initiating what became known as the Tanker War. Saddam Hussein's aim was to provoke retaliation from Tehran so extreme that it would compel American intervention. Iran limited its retaliatory attacks to Iraqi shipping but left the strait open—refusing to give Baghdad the escalation it sought.

In April 1988, the United States launched Operation Praying Mantis—an attack within Iranian territorial waters in retaliation for the Iranian naval mining of international waters in the Persian Gulf and the subsequent damage to an American warship. The U.S. Navy attacked with several groups of surface warships plus aircraft from the carrier USS Enterprise and her cruiser escort, USS Truxtun.

The most infamous confrontation came on July 3, 1988: Iran Air Flight 290 was shot down by the United States Navy guided missile cruiser USS Vincennes (CG-49) when it was wrongly identified as a jet fighter. Nearly 290 people perished—the deadliest air incident in Iranian history and one of the most controversial naval engagements of the twentieth century.

In January 2007, the nuclear submarine USS Newport News collided with MV Mogamigawa—a 300,000-ton Japanese-flagged crude tanker—south of the strait. There were no injuries and no oil leaked, though the collision underscored the dangers of navigating such crowded waters.

On March 20, 2009, another incident: the Los Angeles-class submarine USS Hartford collided with the San Antonio-class amphibious transport dock USS New Orleans inside the strait itself. The collision ruptured a fuel tank.

The Choke Point

If shipping through the Strait of Hormuz were significantly disrupted for an extended period, it could trigger a major oil supply crisis affecting major Asian importers—India, China, Japan—and send shockwaves through every petroleum-dependent economy on Earth. This is precisely why the strait has been described as "critical" to Europe's energy security.

It is also the only maritime route for several Gulf regions including Qatar, Kuwait and Bahrain; disruption creates severe supply shortages across entire nations that have no alternative pathways to the sea.

The strait became a major focus of international attention during the 2026 Iran War—resulting in what journalists called the Strait of Hormuz crisis. What once was merely a shipping channel has become a geopolitical flashpoint where world powers meet, trade dollars are counted, and history continues to be written.

The numbers tell the story: twenty percent of all liquefied natural gas traded across the world's oceans flows through this place; twenty-five percent of global oil passes through its narrows. In a world increasingly dependent on fossil fuels—and increasingly vulnerable to their disruption—the Strait of Hormuz remains what it has always been: a narrow throat through which the world's energy flows, and perhaps the most dangerous choke point on Earth.
