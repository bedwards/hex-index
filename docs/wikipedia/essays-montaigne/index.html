Essays (Montaigne)

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Essays (Montaigne)

In 1580, a French nobleman named Michel de Montaigne walked into a printing shop in Bordeaux and handed over a manuscript that would fundamentally fracture the way the Western world understood itself. He did not present a treatise on theology, a manual on statecraft, or a compilation of heroic deeds. Instead, he offered a collection of "attempts"—the literal translation of the French word essais. He had spent the preceding decade, from roughly 1570 to 1580, retreating to the tower of his family estate to record "some traits of my character and of my humours." The result was a book of three volumes and 107 chapters, a sprawling, chaotic, and profoundly intimate record of a single mind grappling with the impossibility of certainty. Montaigne, writing at the height of the French Wars of Religion, a time when Catholics and Huguenots were tearing France apart with religious fervor, chose a different path. He chose skepticism. He chose to turn the lens inward, not to find God, but to find the man.

The Essays were not designed to educate, to prove a point, or to establish a dogmatic system of truth. The name itself is a declaration of humility. Montaigne understood that the human condition was defined by fallibility. He wrote in a conversational style that was revolutionary for its time, blending a highly literate vocabulary with popular sayings, local slang, and the rough texture of everyday speech. The earlier essays, written in the 1570s, often adhered to a more formal structure, sometimes quite short and focused on specific topics like "Of prognostications." But as the years wore on, and Montaigne revised his work through editions published in 1580, 1588, and posthumously in 1595, the structure dissolved. The later essays became longer, more complex, and far more associative. He would begin a thought on the nature of friendship, drift into a discussion of the smell of a dog, and land, unexpectedly, on the futility of human ambition. This was not disorganization; it was a method of inquiry. It was a search for truth that acknowledged the mind's tendency to wander, mimicking the very process of thinking itself.

"I am myself the matter of this book; you would be unreasonable to suspend your leisure on so frivolous and vain a subject."

This was Montaigne's warning to the reader in the preface. He believed he was offering something trivial, a vanity project of a retired judge. He did not suspect that he was inventing a new genre of literature, one that would dominate French philosophy until the Enlightenment and ripple outward to influence the English language, the theater of Shakespeare, and the very concept of individualism. He wrote to describe himself with utter frankness, a concept he called de bonne foi or "in good faith." He stripped away the masks of the Renaissance humanist. He spoke of his kidney stones, his love of smoking, his fear of death, and his doubts about the nature of the soul. The insight into universal human nature that readers have found for centuries was merely a by-product of his intense, unrelenting introspection. He sought to know himself, and in doing so, he inadvertently described everyone.

The topics Montaigne covered were as vast as the human experience itself, spanning the spectrum from the profound to the absurdly trivial. One chapter is dedicated to "Of Sadness and Sorrow," exploring the crushing weight of grief. Another is titled "Of Smells," a meditation on the olfactory senses. There is "Of Conscience," a deep dive into the moral compass, and "Of Posting," a practical discussion on the logistics of sending letters. This juxtaposition was intentional. By placing the highest philosophical questions alongside the most mundane details of daily life, Montaigne dismantled the hierarchy of knowledge. He suggested that the trivial was as worthy of examination as the divine. This was a radical act in the 16th century, a time when scholarship was often rigid and bound by authority. Montaigne's work was a testament to the diversity of received opinions and the folly of certitude. He never claimed to have the answers. He only claimed to be honest about the questions.

To understand the weight of Montaigne's skepticism, one must understand the world in which he wrote. He lived during the French Wars of Religion (1562–1598), a period of brutal civil war between Catholics and Protestant Huguenots. The violence was fueled by an absolute certainty on both sides. Each faction believed they possessed the divine truth and that the other was a heretic to be exterminated. In this climate, Protestant authors were using reason and scholarship to subvert Church doctrine, while Catholic scholars often embraced skepticism as a weapon to discredit reason entirely. They argued that since human reason was flawed, one should reject it in favor of faith alone. Montaigne walked a razor's edge between these extremes. He never found certainty in his inquiries into the nature of man. He mistrusted the certainty of human reason just as much as he mistrusted the certainty of experience.

"What do I know?"

This was his motto, famously inscribed in the middle of his longest and most significant essay, "An Apology for Raymond Sebond" (Book 2, Chapter 12). In this essay, Montaigne embraced the philosophy of Pyrrhonism, a form of radical skepticism that argued that for every argument, an equal and opposite argument could be found. He posits that we cannot trust our reasoning because thoughts simply occur to us; we do not truly control them. He argued that human beings are finite, while truth is infinite. Therefore, the human capacity is naturally inhibited in grasping reality in its fullness. Montaigne did not deny the existence of absolute truth; he believed it existed, but he argued that it could only be reached through divine revelation, leaving humanity in the dark on most matters. This was not a nihilistic rejection of truth, but a humble admission of human limitation.

In "An Apology for Raymond Sebond," Montaigne also dismantled the anthropocentric view of the world. He argued that we have no good reason to consider ourselves superior to animals. He observed the behavior of beasts with a scientific eye, noting their intelligence, their social structures, and their ability to survive without the pretensions of human philosophy. He cited the famous case of Martin Guerre, a man who successfully impersonated a peasant husband for years, deceiving the entire community, including the wife, until the real husband returned. This story illustrated Montaigne's point: if a human can deceive his own family and community so thoroughly, how can we trust our perceptions of reality? If our senses and our reasoning are so easily fooled, how can we claim to know the truth?

Montaigne's skepticism was not limited to the metaphysical; it bled into his social and political views. He considered marriage necessary for the raising of children, yet he harbored a deep suspicion of romantic love, viewing it as detrimental to freedom. He saw the institution as a cage, capturing the restless spirit of humanity.

"Marriage is like a cage; one sees the birds outside desperate to get in, and those inside desperate to get out."

This metaphor captures the tension he felt between the social obligations of his time and the desire for individual autonomy. In education, he was equally ahead of his time. He favored concrete examples and direct experience over the teaching of abstract knowledge that was expected to be accepted uncritically. His essay "On the Education of Children," dedicated to Diana of Foix, argued against the rote memorization and harsh discipline that characterized 16th-century schooling. He believed that a student should learn to judge, to think for themselves, and to engage with the world, rather than simply filling their mind with the opinions of others. This educational philosophy was a direct application of his skeptical method: truth is not a set of facts to be memorized, but a process of inquiry to be undertaken.

Perhaps his most radical social stance was his opposition to the European colonization of the Americas. While many of his contemporaries celebrated the expansion of Christendom and the acquisition of new lands, Montaigne saw the suffering it brought upon the native peoples. In his essay "Of Cannibals," he described the indigenous people of Brazil not as savages, but as a society that often possessed more virtue and honor than the "civilized" Europeans. He criticized the cruelty and hypocrisy of the colonizers, noting that the true "cannibals" were the Europeans who devoured the souls and bodies of the natives. This was a stunning reversal of the prevailing narrative, a moment where the "savage" was held up as a mirror to the "civilized," revealing the latter's moral bankruptcy.

The text of the Essays is not a static monument; it is a living organism that evolved over the course of Montaigne's life. He heavily edited his work, sometimes inserting a single word, other times adding entire passages. The evolution of his thought is visible in the different editions. The first edition, published in 1580, represents his thoughts from 1571 to 1580. The second edition, published in 1588, added a layer of reflection from 1580 to 1588. The third and final edition, published posthumously in 1595, included additions written between 1588 and 1592. Modern editors mark these layers with letters: A for the earliest passages, B for the middle period, and C for the final additions.

A unique and invaluable artifact of this process is the Bordeaux Copy, a copy of the fifth edition that contains Montaigne's own handwritten "C" additions. Preserved at the Municipal Library of Bordeaux, this manuscript gives modern editors a text that is dramatically indicative of Montaigne's final intentions. It allows us to see the mind of the writer in motion. Remarkably, Montaigne did not remove previous writings, even when they conflicted with his newer views. He kept the contradictions. He kept the doubts. He allowed the earlier, more confident Montaigne to stand alongside the later, more skeptical one. This refusal to erase his past self is a testament to his integrity. He did not want to present a polished, consistent persona; he wanted to present the messy, evolving reality of a human life.

The influence of the Essays on subsequent literature and thought cannot be overstated. In France, their impact on education and culture remains profound even today. The official portrait of former French president François Mitterrand shows him facing the camera, holding an open copy of the Essays in his hands, a visual testament to the book's enduring status as a cornerstone of French intellectual identity. The work remained the most prominent in French philosophy until the Enlightenment, bridging the gap between the Renaissance and the modern age.

Across the channel, the impact was equally significant. The Scottish journalist and politician J. M. Robertson argued that Montaigne's essays had a profound influence on the plays of William Shakespeare. The similarities in language, themes, and structures are striking. Shakespeare, who likely read Florio's English translation published in 1603, seems to have absorbed Montaigne's skepticism. The soliloquies of Hamlet, with their endless questioning of existence and the futility of action, echo the voice of Montaigne. The character of Hamlet is, in many ways, a dramatic realization of Montaigne's "What do I know?" The translation of the Essays into English by John Florio in 1603 brought these ideas to a wider audience, influencing not just Shakespeare but the entire trajectory of English essay writing. Francis Bacon, the father of the English essay, was writing in the shadow of Montaigne, though he often took a more empirical and scientific approach.

The legacy of the Essays is not just in the ideas they contain, but in the form they invented. Montaigne created a genre that allowed for the expression of the self in all its complexity. Before Montaigne, writing was largely about the external world: history, philosophy, theology, politics. After Montaigne, the internal world became a valid subject of serious inquiry. He gave permission for writers to write about themselves, to admit their doubts, to explore their quirks. This is the origin of the modern essay, the personal narrative, and the memoir. Every time a writer sits down to explore a thought, to wander from one idea to another, to admit that they don't have all the answers, they are walking in the footsteps of the man in the tower at Montaigne.

The remarkable modernity of thought apparent in Montaigne's essays is what continues to draw readers in. In an age of polarization, where certainty is often weaponized and nuance is discarded, Montaigne's voice is a soothing balm. He reminds us that the human condition is defined by fragility and volatility. He reminds us that we are finite beings trying to grasp an infinite truth, and that the only honest response is to acknowledge our limitations.

"I have never seen a greater monster or miracle than myself."

This famous quote encapsulates the essence of his project. He saw himself as a monster because of his contradictions, his flaws, his capacity for cruelty and love. He saw himself as a miracle because of the sheer complexity of his existence. He did not try to resolve the tension between the two. He simply observed it. He recorded it. He let the reader observe it with him.

The Essays are a testament to the power of the written word to preserve the human experience. They are a record of a man who refused to be defined by the certainties of his time, who chose instead to embrace the uncertainty of his own mind. They are a reminder that the search for truth is not a destination, but a journey. And they are a challenge to every reader to look at themselves with the same honesty, the same curiosity, and the same lack of pretension that Montaigne brought to his work.

The text itself has been preserved and translated through the centuries, ensuring that Montaigne's voice remains audible. From the early translations by John Florio and Charles Cotton to modern editions by Donald M. Frame, J. M. Cohen, and Michael Andrew Screech, the Essays have been continually reinterpreted. The complete, searchable text of the Villey-Saulnier edition from the ARTFL project at the University of Chicago allows scholars to trace the evolution of his words with unprecedented precision. Public domain audiobooks and online libraries have made the work accessible to a global audience, ensuring that the "attempts" of a 16th-century French nobleman continue to resonate in the 21st century.

Montaigne's work was not just a literary achievement; it was a philosophical intervention. He challenged the dogmatism of the Church, the brutality of the state, and the arrogance of human reason. He did not offer a new system to replace the old ones. He offered a method: the method of inquiry, the method of self-reflection, the method of doubt. He showed that the only way to navigate a world of conflicting truths is to acknowledge the conflict. He showed that the only way to know oneself is to be honest about one's own ignorance.

In the end, the Essays are a mirror. They reflect the reader back to themselves, with all their contradictions, their doubts, and their hopes. They are a reminder that we are all, in some way, Montaigne. We are all trying to make sense of a world that makes no sense, to find certainty in a universe that offers none. And in that shared struggle, we find a connection that transcends time, culture, and language. Montaigne's tower was not a place of isolation; it was a place of connection, a place where one man's introspection became the voice of humanity.

The Essays remain a vital part of our intellectual heritage. They are a warning against the dangers of certainty, a celebration of the diversity of human experience, and a call to live with honesty and integrity. They are a testament to the power of the individual mind to question, to doubt, and to seek. And they are a reminder that the greatest miracle of all is the human capacity to wonder, to question, and to know that we do not know.

Montaigne's legacy is not in the answers he provided, but in the questions he asked. He asked us to look at ourselves, to look at the world, and to ask: "What do I know?" It is a question that is as relevant today as it was in 1580. It is a question that we must ask again and again, in every generation, in every age. And it is a question that, perhaps, will never be fully answered. But that is the point. The journey is the destination. The attempt is the achievement. And in that attempt, we find the true essence of being human.
