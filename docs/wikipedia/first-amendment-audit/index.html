First Amendment audit

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: First Amendment audit

In a post office in West Texas, a man sets up his camera on a tripod and begins to record. He is not there to mail a package or inquire about a lost shipment. He is there to test the limits of the law. He walks toward the counter, filming the postal worker's face, asking pointed questions about their authority to stop him from recording. When the worker asks him to lower the camera, he refuses, citing his constitutional rights. The worker calls security. Security calls the police. Within minutes, armed officers arrive, weapons drawn, demanding the man identify himself and cease his activity. He stands his ground, filming every second of the encounter, waiting to see if they will uphold the Constitution or break it.

This is the essence of a First Amendment audit. It is a phenomenon that has swept across American public squares, government lobbies, and library aisles in the last decade, transforming the act of recording into a form of high-stakes activism. Auditors, as these practitioners call themselves, are not merely tourists with smartphones. They are self-styled constitutional testers who believe they are engaging in citizen journalism to expose government overreach. Their goal is to force a confrontation, to create a scenario where law enforcement or public employees must choose between respecting the First Amendment right to record in public spaces or violating it. If an employee attempts to stop the filming, the facility "fails" the audit. If no one objects, it passes. But the stakes are rarely that binary.

The movement is rooted in a specific legal reality: the United States Constitution protects the right to photograph and video record government officials and public spaces. There is no reasonable expectation of privacy for a police officer performing their duties on a sidewalk or a postal worker behind a counter. Yet, this right is not always understood by those hired to enforce the law, nor is it universally accepted by property managers who confuse public access with private control. Auditors exploit this gap in knowledge and practice. They travel to places considered public property—sidewalks, post offices, police stations, libraries—and openly record everything in their view. Their stated aim is transparency. They argue that by documenting these interactions, they are creating an unfiltered check on government conduct where formal accountability mechanisms are perceived as weak or inaccessible.

The Architecture of Confrontation

The mechanics of an audit are deceptively simple, yet the execution often relies on psychological pressure. An auditor selects a public facility and begins filming. They may ask employees why they are wearing certain uniforms, demand to see their badge numbers, or question their authority to be in specific areas. Sometimes, auditors will claim they are recording for a story; other times, they cite "personal use." Frequently, they refuse to answer any questions at all.

This refusal is often the catalyst for escalation. When local law enforcement is called—usually by an employee who feels threatened or confused—the auditor becomes the suspect. Officers arrive and request identification. In many states, there are no "stop and identify" statutes that apply when a person is simply standing in public, yet officers often insist on knowing who the camera operator is. If an auditor refuses to identify themselves, citing their Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination or simply choosing not to engage, the situation can deteriorate rapidly. Auditors have been arrested for obstruction of justice, disorderly conduct, and trespassing. In extreme cases, they have had their equipment confiscated, been assaulted by officers, or even had weapons drawn on them.

The tension is palpable because it is a test of power dynamics. The auditor enters a space where the authority figures—police, security guards, clerks—are accustomed to controlling access and behavior. By refusing to comply with informal requests to stop filming, the auditor challenges that control. They are not just recording; they are performing a legal experiment in real-time. As one auditor told Fox News in 2019, the goal is to "put yourself in places where you know chances are the cops are going to be called. Are they going to uphold the constitution, uphold the law ... or break the law?"

This approach has drawn a sharp divide between supporters and critics. Supporters view audits as a continuation of the civil liberties tradition of "test cases" and public protest. Just as activists in the 1960s intentionally sat at segregated lunch counters to challenge Jim Crow laws, modern auditors intentionally film police officers to challenge unconstitutional restrictions on recording. They argue that without these provocations, the true extent of government overreach would remain hidden behind closed doors or internal memos.

But critics see a darker motivation. They argue that many audits are not about civil liberties but about online notoriety and advertising revenue. The rise of YouTube monetization has turned confrontation into a commodity. A calm interaction where an auditor simply films a building yields few views. A shouting match with a police officer, complete with weapons drawn and arrests threatened, generates millions. According to a report by The Daily Beast, the growing popularity of these videos has led to "ruthless competition" among auditors, incentivizing more dramatic, confrontational, and abusive tactics.

The Human Cost of Testing Limits

The human cost of this movement is not measured in dollars or views, but in trauma, violence, and the erosion of trust. While the discourse often centers on legal theories and constitutional amendments, the reality involves real people standing at a breaking point. Auditors have been detained, arrested, and had their homes raided by SWAT teams. In one harrowing instance, an auditor was shot while video recording in a public place. The line between "testing rights" and inviting violence has become dangerously thin.

For law enforcement officers, the experience is often one of confusion and fear. Many officers are not trained to handle individuals who claim absolute constitutional immunity from all interaction. When an auditor refuses to identify themselves or yells insults and vulgarities at an officer trying to de-escalate a situation, it creates a volatile environment. The language used by some auditors has drawn comparisons to the anti-government sovereign citizen movement, a fringe group known for its belief that they are not subject to federal laws. This association has further polarized public opinion, leading many police departments to view all auditors with deep suspicion.

The violence is rarely one-sided. In several documented cases, auditors have been assaulted by police officers or private security personnel who felt threatened by the recording. The psychological toll on both sides is significant. For the auditor, the encounter can result in criminal charges, legal fees, and physical injury. For the public servant, it can mean a career-ending mistake made in the heat of the moment, where the pressure to maintain order clashes with an individual who refuses to acknowledge any authority beyond their own interpretation of the Constitution.

Critics argue that the tactics used by some auditors cross the line from activism into harassment and intimidation. Auditors have been known to enter public buildings asserting they have a legal right to openly carry firearms, a tactic that often triggers immediate and severe responses from law enforcement. Some auditors refuse to self-identify or explain their activities, creating an atmosphere of hostility that makes cooperative dialogue impossible. When the goal is to elicit a reaction rather than to foster understanding, the result is often chaos.

The Legal Landscape: Theory vs. Practice

The legal foundation for auditing rests on several key court rulings that have gradually clarified the rights of citizens in public spaces. The landmark case Glik v. Cunniffe (2011) in the United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit was a turning point. In this case, Simon Glik was arrested for recording police officers arresting an individual on the Boston Common. The court ruled that Glik's arrest violated his First and Fourth Amendment rights, confirming that citizens have a clear right to record police activity in public places as long as they do not interfere with law enforcement operations.

This ruling was reinforced by the Seventh Circuit Court of Appeals in ACLU v. Alvarez (2012), which stated that "the act of making an audio or audiovisual recording is necessarily included within the First Amendment's guarantee of speech and press rights." The court reasoned that the right to publish a recording would be ineffective if the antecedent act of making the recording were unprotected. This legal logic forms the bedrock of the auditor's argument: they are not merely observing; they are exercising their freedom of the press.

However, as of November 9, 2022, the Supreme Court of the United States had yet to explicitly affirm a universal right to record government employees in all contexts. While lower federal courts have established strong precedents, the lack of a definitive Supreme Court ruling leaves room for ambiguity at the state and local levels. This legal gray area is where auditors operate, testing the boundaries of what is protected and what is not.

The International Association of Chiefs of Police (IACP) has attempted to bridge this gap by releasing documents advising officers on how to handle auditing activities. Their guidance states that the use of a recording device alone is not grounds for arrest unless other laws are violated. Yet, in practice, these guidelines are often ignored or misunderstood. Local law enforcement agencies, under pressure from constituents and facing the complexities of modern policing, sometimes react with force rather than patience.

The conflict often arises over the definition of "public forum." In sidewalk audits, property owners or managers may claim that photography is prohibited on their premises. While the First Amendment protects recording in public spaces, private property rights complicate the issue. If a building is publicly funded but has rules against commercial filming or specific types of surveillance, auditors must navigate a legal minefield. The flashpoint is often when an auditor refuses to leave after being told that their presence or actions are violating house rules.

The Profit Motive and the Performance

A significant portion of the controversy surrounding First Amendment audits stems from the financial incentives involved. For many practitioners, auditing is not just a hobby; it is a career. The videos they produce are uploaded to platforms like YouTube, where views translate into advertising revenue. A video that features a calm, uneventful walk through a library may garner a few hundred views. A video featuring a shouting match with a police officer, complete with handcuffs and sirens, can generate hundreds of thousands of dollars in ad revenue.

This economic reality has led to accusations that the movement is less about civil liberties and more about content farming. Critics argue that auditors are not interested in genuine accountability but in creating dramatic narratives that drive engagement. The "ruthless competition" mentioned in The Daily Beast report suggests a market where the most aggressive tactics yield the highest returns. This dynamic creates a perverse incentive structure where escalation is rewarded and de-escalation is ignored.

Some auditors have been accused of staging scenes or provoking officers to elicit specific reactions that will make for compelling video content. They may yell insults, use vulgar language, or refuse to comply with reasonable requests not because they believe it is legally necessary, but because it serves the narrative of their video. This performative aspect undermines the credibility of the movement in the eyes of many observers.

The debate over whether auditing functions as constitutional activism, performance for profit, or a mix of both remains unresolved. Supporters insist that even if some auditors are motivated by fame or money, the overall effect is positive: it raises awareness of police misconduct and pressures government agencies to train their employees to respect First Amendment rights. They argue that the ends justify the means, and that the exposure gained through these videos forces a necessary conversation about civil liberties.

Critics counter that the performative nature of many audits actually harms the cause they claim to support. By associating the right to record with harassment and intimidation, auditors risk alienating the public and giving law enforcement agencies an excuse to impose stricter restrictions on filming. When an auditor is seen as a provocateur rather than a citizen journalist, it becomes easier for officials to dismiss their concerns and justify aggressive responses.

The Future of Public Recording

As First Amendment auditing continues to evolve, the tension between individual rights and public order shows no sign of abating. The movement has spread beyond the United States, with similar practices emerging in Australia, the United Kingdom, Canada, and Russia, though the legal frameworks in these countries differ significantly from the U.S. model. In nations without a robust First Amendment equivalent, the risks for those who attempt to audit government officials are even higher.

The future of this practice depends on how courts continue to interpret the boundaries of public recording and how law enforcement agencies adapt their training. As more officers become familiar with the rights established in cases like Glik and ACLU v. Alvarez, the likelihood of confrontations may decrease. However, as long as there is a financial incentive for creating dramatic content, the pressure to escalate will remain.

For the average citizen walking down a public street or entering a government building, the rise of auditing means that their interaction with authority figures may be recorded and broadcast to millions without their consent. This has profound implications for privacy and civil liberties, raising questions about the balance between transparency and dignity.

The human cost of this movement cannot be overstated. Every time an auditor pushes the envelope, there is a risk that someone will get hurt—physically, emotionally, or legally. The story of First Amendment auditing is not just a legal case study; it is a chronicle of human conflict in the digital age. It is a struggle over who controls the narrative, who has the right to speak, and what price we are willing to pay for the truth.

In the end, the question remains: Is this activism or entertainment? Is it a necessary check on power or a dangerous game of chicken with the law? The answer may depend on whom you ask. But one thing is certain: as long as cameras remain in our pockets and the internet remains open, the audits will continue. And with every encounter, the boundaries of our democracy are tested once more.
