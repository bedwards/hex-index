North American Confederacy

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: North American Confederacy

In 1794, the fate of the American experiment did not hinge on the outcome of a military battle or the signing of a treaty, but on the insertion of a single, seemingly innocuous word into the preamble of the Declaration of Independence. That word was "unanimous." By altering the phrase to state that governments derive their just power from the "unanimous consent of the governed," the trajectory of North American history fractured irrevocably. This textual shift, the catalyst for a century of radical transformation, turned the Whiskey Rebellion of 1794 not into a suppressed insurrection, but into the spark of a Second American Revolution. In this timeline, Albert Gallatin, the Swiss-American politician who in our history would have been a Treasury Secretary, interceded on the side of the farmers against the fledgling federal government. The result was the overthrow of the central authority, the nullification of the United States Constitution, and the most shocking event in the annals of this alternate world: the execution by firing squad of George Washington for treason.

This is the genesis of the North American Confederacy, a fictional nation and the setting for L. Neil Smith's acclaimed alternate history series. It is a world where the remnants of central government did not just shrink; they dissipated entirely. It is a history where the concept of a national currency was abandoned, replaced by a chaotic yet vibrant system where individuals backed their own money with gold, silver, wheat, corn, iron, and even whiskey. The United States, as we know it, never coalesced into a monolithic superstate. Instead, the continent evolved into a loose, hyper-individualistic confederation that, by 1893, merged with other North American nations to form the North American Confederacy (NAC). This is not merely a story of political divergence; it is a narrative of a society where the absence of government interference created a libertarian utopia, accelerating science, medicine, and human rights to levels that dwarf our own timeline.

The Death of the Federalist Dream

The divergence point is precise and brutal. In our history, the Whiskey Rebellion was a minor disturbance quelled by the presence of George Washington and a militia, reinforcing the power of the new federal government. In the NAC timeline, the "unanimous consent" clause provided the legal and philosophical ammunition for the farmers to reject federal authority entirely. When Gallatin sided with them, the rebellion metastasized. The Federalist Party, led by the iron-willed Alexander Hamilton, found its power base eroded. Hamilton, unable to salvage the central government, fled to Prussia. There, he lived in exile until his death in 1804, killed in a duel, a final, tragic footnote to his failed vision of a strong central state.

The political vacuum left by the collapse of the United States was filled by a new order. In 1795, a caretaker government was established, and in 1797, a revised version of the Articles of Confederation was ratified. This new charter placed an unprecedented emphasis on individual and economic freedom. The government lost the power to create money, a radical departure from the mercantilist and statist trends of the late 18th century. Instead, the monetary system became a decentralized marketplace of value. If you had gold, you printed gold-backed notes. If you were a farmer with a surplus of corn, you issued corn-backed currency. The economy became a living, breathing organism of direct exchange, unburdened by the inflationary policies or central banking monopolies that have plagued our history.

Gallatin, the architect of this new world, was proclaimed the second president, though the title carried a different weight than in our timeline. He was not a ruler in the traditional sense, but a figurehead for a system that actively resisted rulership. His leadership was defined by his commitment to the principle that the revolution of 1776 had been hijacked by the Federalists, an aberration that had to be corrected. This correction came with a heavy price: the execution of Washington. The act was not one of bloodlust, but of ideological necessity in this universe. The new government needed to sever the ties to the old order completely, and the execution of the "father of the country" served as the ultimate symbol of the death of centralized monarchy and the birth of absolute liberty.

The New Calendar and the End of Slavery

The cultural shift in the North American Confederacy was as profound as the political one. The very measurement of time was reimagined to honor the birth of liberty. Thomas Jefferson, who in our history struggled with the paradox of slavery, became the champion of a peaceful abolitionist movement in this timeline. By 1820, slavery was brought to a peaceful end, a feat that in our history would not be achieved without the devastation of the Civil War. Jefferson's role in this transformation was not merely moral but systemic. He understood that true freedom required a new framework for human interaction, including how humanity measured its own existence.

In 1796, Jefferson first proposed a new calendar system. The original proposal marked the year of Gallatin's ascension to the presidency as Year Zero. However, Gallatin, ever the pragmatist and guardian of the revolution's legacy, protested. He argued that the true revolution was 1776, and that the Federalist period should be regarded as a dark interlude. More poignantly, he insisted that commemorating the overthrow and execution of Washington might set a hideous precedent for future uprisings. Gallatin argued that historians should still count Washington as the first President, preserving the continuity of the struggle for liberty even while rejecting the methods of the Federalists.

The compromise reached was elegant and symbolic. The year 1776 was designated as Year Zero, Anno Liberati (Latin for Year of Liberation). This new calendar, with its metric inches and pounds, became a daily reminder of the NAC's distinct identity. It was a rejection of the old world's arbitrary measurements and a commitment to a rational, liberty-centered existence. The calendar served as a unifying thread for a continent that had rejected the idea of a single, powerful government. It was a declaration that time itself belonged to the people, not the state.

"The government can no longer create money, and only individual people can, it being backed by gold, silver, wheat, corn, iron, and even whiskey."

This decentralized monetary system, coupled with the new calendar, created a society where the individual was the primary unit of value. Without a central bank to manipulate the economy, the NAC experienced a surge in innovation and wealth. The absence of government interference meant that science and medicine advanced at a pace significantly greater than in our baseline history. The constraints of bureaucracy and regulation, which often stifle progress in our timeline, were nonexistent. The result was a civilization that looked toward the future with a confidence and speed that seemed almost alien to us.

The Technological Utopia

The technological achievements of the North American Confederacy are the most striking evidence of Smith's thesis: that libertarianism is a superior political order. Without the drag of state intervention, the NAC leaped ahead in nearly every field. Elisha Gray invented the telephone in 1867, a full decade ahead of our timeline. But it was Alexander Graham Bell's work that truly set the NAC apart. Instead of focusing solely on human communication, Bell developed voder technology that allowed chimpanzees, gorillas, orangutans, and other simians to communicate.

This was not a scientific curiosity; it was a paradigm shift in the definition of citizenship. In 1867, the greater primates were granted citizenship rights equivalent to all races of humans. The NAC recognized sentience not as a human monopoly, but as a trait that could be discovered and honored. Later, the discovery of sentience in dolphins, porpoises, and orcas led to the integration of oceanic civilizations with land-based societies. The NAC became a multi-species confederation, a true testament to the power of a society built on individual rights rather than anthropocentric dominance.

The pace of innovation continued unabated. In 1888 (112 A.L.), Thomas Edison invented electrically heated streets, transforming urban life and eliminating the dangers of open fires. In 1947 (171 A.L.), colored television was invented, bringing a new dimension to entertainment and information. By 1993 (217 A.L.), the NAC had achieved the seemingly impossible: the cloning of mastodons back to life using frozen tissue. This was not just a scientific feat; it was a philosophical statement about the relationship between the past, present, and future. The NAC did not just preserve history; it actively reshaped it, bringing extinct species back into the fold of a living, breathing world.

The wealth of the NAC was staggering. The absence of government interference created a fertile ground for entrepreneurship and innovation. The continent was much more advanced in science and technology and much wealthier than our Earth. This prosperity was not the result of state planning or subsidies, but of the unfettered creativity of individuals. The NAC proved that when people are free to pursue their own interests, the collective result is a society that thrives in ways that centralized planning could never achieve.

The North American Confederacy: A Mosaic of Liberty

The formation of the North American Confederacy in 1893 was a natural evolution of this decentralized society. The remnants of the United States, along with several other North American nations, merged to form a loose confederation that respected the sovereignty of its constituent parts. The individuals who led the NAC were not career politicians but prominent figures in the history of anarchism and libertarianism. Their names are listed in the timeline of the Probability Broach, a chronicle of the NAC's presidents.

The series of novels by L. Neil Smith, beginning with The Probability Broach in 1979, explores this world in depth. The series includes eight sequels: The Venus Belt (1980), Their Majesties' Bucketeers (1981), The Nagasaki Vector (1983), Tom Paine Maru (1984), The Gallatin Divergence (1985), Brightsuit MacBear (1988), Taflak Lysandra (1989), and The American Zone (2001). While Their Majesties' Bucketeers takes place in the same universe, it does not feature the characters from the main series, expanding the scope of the NAC's history and culture.

Smith's novels are not just entertainment; they are a deliberate promotion of libertarianism. The author has stated that his purpose is to illustrate the benefits of a society based on individual freedom and the rejection of state coercion. The Probability Broach won the 1982 Prometheus Award, an award created by Smith himself and given by the Libertarian Futurist Society. Other novels in the series, including The Nagasaki Vector, Tom Paine Maru, The Gallatin Divergence, and The American Zone, were all finalists for the award. This recognition underscores the series' impact on the libertarian movement and its ability to engage readers with complex political ideas through compelling storytelling.

The NAC is a world where the old fears of anarchy have been replaced by the reality of order through freedom. It is a society where the government is not a master, but a servant of the people, and where the people are the masters of their own destiny. The execution of Washington, the abandonment of the Constitution, and the establishment of a new calendar were not acts of chaos, but of creation. They were the foundation of a new world, a world that is richer, more advanced, and more just than our own.

The Legacy of the Probability Broach

The legacy of the North American Confederacy extends far beyond the pages of Smith's novels. It is a thought experiment that challenges our assumptions about the necessity of the state. It asks us to consider what might have happened if the American Revolution had been taken to its logical conclusion, if the principles of liberty had been applied with absolute consistency. The NAC is a mirror held up to our own history, reflecting the path not taken and the possibilities that were lost.

The series invites readers to explore the implications of a world without a central government. It is a world where the individual is the ultimate authority, where the marketplace of ideas and goods is unregulated, and where the pursuit of happiness is the only law. The NAC is not a perfect world, but it is a world where the potential for human achievement is limitless. It is a world where science and medicine have advanced to the point of cloning extinct species and communicating with other sentient beings. It is a world where the concept of slavery was abolished without a civil war, and where the calendar itself is a testament to the power of liberty.

The North American Confederacy is a testament to the power of ideas. It shows how a single word, a single decision, can change the course of history. It is a story of a continent that chose freedom over power, and in doing so, created a civilization that is a beacon of hope for a future where the individual is free to be all that they can be. The novels of L. Neil Smith are not just a series of adventures; they are a roadmap to a better world, a world that exists in the realm of possibility, waiting for us to seize it.

The history of the NAC is a reminder that the future is not written in stone. It is shaped by the choices we make today. The execution of Washington, the ratification of the Articles of Confederation, the invention of the telephone, the granting of citizenship to primates—these are all choices that were made in a different world. But they are choices that could be made in ours. The North American Confederacy is a vision of what could be, a vision of a world where the government is limited, and the people are free. It is a world that is worth striving for, a world that is worth imagining, and a world that is worth creating.

In the end, the North American Confederacy is more than a fictional nation. It is a challenge to our own complacency. It asks us to question the status quo, to imagine a different future, and to work towards a world where the principles of liberty are not just words on a page, but the foundation of our society. The NAC is a testament to the power of the human spirit, a spirit that is unbound by the constraints of the state and free to soar to new heights. It is a world where the impossible becomes possible, and where the future is limited only by our imagination.
