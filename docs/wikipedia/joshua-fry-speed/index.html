Joshua Fry Speed

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Joshua Fry Speed

On November 14, 1814, a child was born in Louisville, Kentucky, into a family whose wealth was measured not merely in acres of fertile black soil, but in the human beings forced to work it. Joshua Fry Speed was the fifth of eleven children born to Judge John Speed and Lucy Gilmer Speed. His father's lineage stretched back to the 17th century, rooted in the land through a great-grandfather who had emigrated from Virginia, fought in the American Revolution, and been awarded thousands of acres for his service. By the time Joshua came of age, the family estate, known as Farmington, was a sprawling operation that relied on the labor of 54 enslaved people, one of the largest holdings in central Kentucky at the time. Yet, behind the grandeur of the plantation and the legacy of a judge who had helped separate Kentucky from Virginia, there ran a quieter, more ominous current: a history of profound depression that would haunt Joshua Speed for his entire life.

The family's financial might was built on staples like hemp and tobacco, crops that demanded back-breaking labor and were cultivated by those held in chains. Joshua's father, John Speed, had amassed a fortune through salt works, land speculation, and a smithery, but he left behind a domestic landscape fractured by tension. While his wife Lucy came from a family of Virginia gentry closely tied to Thomas Jefferson, the relationship between father and son was strained. At fifteen, young Joshua received a stinging rebuke from his father, who complained of "all your abuse" directed at him. This emotional distance may have been an early symptom of a darker malady that seemed to plague the Speed men; two of Joshua's brothers would later show clear signs of clinical depression. The ailment was hereditary, a shadow that Lincoln himself would one day identify in his closest friend, noting that Joshua was "naturally of a nervous temperament."

Despite the turmoil at home and his own poor health, which had cut short his formal education at St. Joseph's College near Bardstown, Joshua was thrust into the world of commerce. His father, though lacking extensive schooling himself, insisted his children have the advantage of education, but when illness forced Joshua to return home, he argued persuasively that he was ready for a career. He spent a few years as a clerk in Louisville's largest wholesale establishment before making a fateful decision in 1835. At twenty-one, with nothing more than his family name and a desire to make his own fortune, Joshua Speed set out for the frontier town of Springfield, Illinois.

Springfield was then a place of fewer than 1,500 souls, a muddy, bustling hub where the state capital had recently been moved. It was here that the trajectories of two men would collide, altering the course of American history. Joshua immediately immersed himself in merchandising and even helped edit a local newspaper. In April 1837, a young, aspiring lawyer named Abraham Lincoln arrived in Springfield to seek his own fortune. The two met at the general store Speed ran. What followed was an intimacy that defied the rigid social norms of later decades but was common enough for its time: Lincoln sublet the apartment above Speed's store. For four years, they were roommates. They shared a bed.

This arrangement has sparked centuries of speculation regarding Lincoln's sexuality, with historians like Professor Thomas Balcerski examining the nuances of their bond. While men often shared beds in that era due to a lack of private rooms and heating costs, the depth of their emotional reliance suggests something more profound. They were inseparable, navigating the loneliness of a strange new city together. But life on the frontier was not just about camaraderie; it was also about the crushing weight of personal crisis.

In 1840, the patriarch Judge John Speed died. Joshua, now the head of his family's interests in Kentucky, made plans to sell his Springfield store and return to Farmington. His departure coincided with a personal tragedy for Lincoln. Lincoln had become engaged to Mary Todd, a spirited woman from Kentucky's gentry who moved in similar social circles to the Speeds. Yet, as the wedding date of January 1, 1841, approached, something within Lincoln fractured. He broke the engagement on the very day it was supposed to take place.

The timing was catastrophic. Joshua Speed left for Kentucky as planned, leaving a devastated Lincoln alone in Springfield. The young lawyer was mired in a depression so severe that his friends feared for his sanity. He withdrew from public life, haunted by guilt and an overwhelming sense of despair. It took seven months before he could summon the strength to travel back to Kentucky in July 1841, seeking refuge with Joshua.

Speed welcomed him without hesitation. For a month, Lincoln stayed at the family estate, Farmington. It was a sanctuary where the air was thick with the scent of hemp and the sounds of enslaved laborers working the fields, but for Lincoln, it was a place of healing. He rode into Louisville almost daily to discuss legal matters with James Speed, Joshua's older brother, who also lent him books from his extensive law library. It was during this stay that the true depth of their friendship—and their fundamental divergence on the moral issue of the age—became clear.

Joshua Speed was a man of the South, a slaveholder who believed in the economic necessity and social order of his world. He argued that Northerners should not interfere with Southern institutions. Lincoln, even at this early stage, found himself unable to reconcile his friendship with his moral repugnance toward slavery. The tension between them did not break their bond, but it defined its limits.

In 1855, nearly fifteen years after that pivotal stay in Kentucky, Lincoln wrote a letter to Speed that remains one of the most poignant documents of their relationship. He wrote:

You know I dislike slavery; and you fully admit the abstract wrong of it. ... I also acknowledge your rights and my obligations, under the constitution, in regard to your slaves. I confess I hate to see the poor creatures hunted down, and caught, and carried back to their stripes, and unrewarded toils; but I bite my lip and keep quiet.

Lincoln then recalled a specific memory from 1841, a journey they had taken together on a steamboat from Louisville to St. Louis. It was a low-water trip, tedious and slow. But the horror lay not in the travel time, but in what they saw on board:

You may remember, as I well do, that from Louisville to the mouth of the Ohio [River], there were, on board, ten or a dozen slaves, shackled together with irons. That sight was a continued torment to me; and I see something like it every time I touch the Ohio, or any other slave-border.

He challenged his friend's assumption that he could remain indifferent:

It is hardly fair for you to assume, that I have no interest in a thing which has, and continually exercises, the power of making me miserable. You ought rather to appreciate how much the great body of the Northern people do crucify their feelings...

This letter reveals a man who was not just politically opposed to slavery, but physically and emotionally tortured by its reality. The sight of human beings chained together on a riverboat was not an abstract policy debate for Lincoln; it was a daily torment that he bore in silence.

As the nation moved toward civil war, the divergence between the two men widened into the political chasm separating the North and South. Yet, their personal loyalty never wavered. When Abraham Lincoln became President in 1861, he offered Joshua Speed several government appointments. Speed refused them all. He did not want a position of power; he wanted to serve his country in a way that aligned with his complex loyalties as a Southern Unionist.

During the American Civil War, Kentucky was a border state, torn apart by conflicting allegiances. While the Speed family owned slaves and held Confederate sympathies, Joshua Speed remained loyal to the Union. He coordinated Union activities in Kentucky, working behind the scenes to support the cause of the United States government while navigating the dangerous social landscape of his home state. His brother James Speed took a more public role, serving as Lincoln's Attorney General starting in November 1864. When Lincoln nominated James to Congress for confirmation, he acknowledged that he did not know James as well as he knew Joshua, underscoring the depth of his personal trust in the elder Speed.

The war brought immense suffering to Kentucky and the nation. Families were divided, neighbors turned against neighbors, and the human cost was measured in thousands of lives lost on battlefields where the lines between friend and foe blurred. In the midst of this chaos, Joshua's friendship with Lincoln stood as a testament to the possibility of holding two opposing worlds together through personal bond.

When John Wilkes Booth assassinated President Lincoln on April 15, 1865, the shockwave was felt most deeply by those who had known him best. In Louisville, Joshua Speed organized a memorial service for the departed leader. It was a somber gathering in a city that had just witnessed the death of its own son. Sixty members of the Speed family contributed money to erect a monument honoring Lincoln in Springfield, a physical testament to their enduring love and respect.

Speed also dedicated himself to preserving Lincoln's legacy. He wrote lengthy, detailed letters to William Herndon, Lincoln's former law partner, who was attempting to write a biography of the late President. These letters provided crucial insights into Lincoln's character, his depression, his humor, and his moral evolution. They served as a bridge between the private man Joshua knew in Springfield and the public figure who had led the nation through its darkest hour.

Joshua Fry Speed died on May 29, 1882, in Louisville, Kentucky. He was interred in Cave Hill Cemetery, resting not far from the family estate that had once been a symbol of his wealth and his burden. The farm, Farmington, has since been reduced in size but remains a National Register of Historic Places. The house has been restored and now serves as a local event venue, hosting living history events that attempt to recreate the world in which these two men lived.

The story of Joshua Fry Speed is not just about a businessman or a politician; it is a story about the human capacity for friendship in the face of moral contradiction. For decades, the narrative of their relationship remained relatively straightforward: two friends who shared a bed and an office in a frontier town. But in 1999, author and gay activist Larry Kramer claimed to have uncovered new primary sources that would radically alter this understanding. He alleged the discovery of a hitherto-unknown Joshua Speed diary and letters hidden beneath the floorboards of the old store in Springfield.

These documents reportedly contained explicit descriptions of the nature of their relationship, shedding fresh light on Lincoln's sexuality and the depth of his emotional dependence on Speed. While the authenticity and full impact of these alleged sources have been subjects of debate among historians, they serve as a reminder that the past is never fully settled. The story of Lincoln and Speed continues to evolve, inviting new generations to ask difficult questions about love, loyalty, and the secrets men keep.

What remains undeniable is the power of their connection. In a time when the nation was tearing itself apart over the issue of slavery, Joshua Speed offered Abraham Lincoln a place of healing when he was at his lowest point. He listened to Lincoln's torments over the sight of chained slaves on a riverboat, even as he defended the system that kept them in chains. They disagreed on the most important question of their age, yet they remained friends until the end.

This is the legacy of Joshua Fry Speed: a man who lived at the intersection of privilege and pain, who loved a future President enough to let him heal in his home even as he refused to agree with his politics. He was a slaveholder who supported the Union; a depressive soul who found solace in another's company; a friend who remained loyal when the world burned around them. His life reminds us that history is not just made by grand policies and battlefield victories, but by the quiet, enduring bonds between individuals who choose to stand together despite their differences.

The house at Farmington still stands today, its walls holding the echoes of conversations that shaped a nation. Visitors walk through rooms where Lincoln once paced in despair and found relief. They see the spaces where two men debated the future of slavery while sitting by a fire, unaware that their words would one day be dissected by historians and activists alike. The story of Joshua Fry Speed is a reminder that the past is complex, often contradictory, and deeply human.

In the end, Joshua Fry Speed's life was a testament to the idea that friendship can transcend ideology. He did not try to change Lincoln's mind about slavery; he simply offered him the space to heal. And when Lincoln became the President who would eventually dismantle the institution that separated their worlds, Speed was there to honor his memory, to protect his legacy, and to ensure that the world remembered the man behind the monument.

The tragedy of their era—the suffering of enslaved people, the blood spilled in civil war—is not a footnote to their friendship. It is the backdrop against which their bond shines all the more brightly. They were men of their time, flawed and limited by the prejudices they inherited, yet capable of a connection that defied the very divisions that destroyed so many others. Joshua Fry Speed's story forces us to confront the uncomfortable truth that love and hatred can coexist in the same heart, that loyalty can exist alongside moral disagreement, and that history is written not just by the victors, but by those who loved them best.

Today, as we look back on the life of Joshua Fry Speed, we see a man who was more than a footnote in Lincoln's biography. He was a mirror reflecting the contradictions of America itself—a land built on the labor of enslaved people, divided by war, yet held together by the ties of human connection that refused to break. His legacy is not just in the monuments raised in his honor or the historic designation of his family home, but in the enduring question he poses to us: How do we remain friends when our worlds are at war?

The answer lies perhaps in the quiet months Lincoln spent at Farmington, recovering from a depression that threatened to consume him. It is found in the letters they wrote years later, filled with mutual respect and unspoken understanding. It is in the refusal of Joshua Speed to accept power, choosing instead to serve in his own way. And it is in the memory of two men who shared a bed in a small town, unaware that their friendship would one day be seen as a microcosm of the nation's struggle for its soul.

Joshua Fry Speed died in 1882, but his story continues to unfold. As new evidence emerges and as our understanding of history deepens, we are reminded that the past is not a static collection of facts, but a living narrative that demands our empathy and our attention. The human cost of the era in which he lived—the enslaved people shackled on riverboats, the families torn apart by war—is the heavy price paid for the ideals he and Lincoln debated. Yet, within that darkness, their friendship remains a beacon of light, a reminder that even in the most divided times, we are capable of seeing each other as human beings first.

In the restored rooms of Farmington, where living history events bring the past to life, visitors can almost hear the voices of Joshua and Abraham. They speak of law, of politics, of depression, and of a love that transcended the boundaries of race, class, and ideology. Their story is not just about the past; it is a challenge for our present, asking us to find common ground in a world that seems more divided than ever.

Joshua Fry Speed was a planter, a businessman, a politician, and a friend. He was a man of contradictions, like the nation he helped shape. And in his life, we see the complexity of human nature, the capacity for both great good and great harm, and the enduring power of friendship to bridge the widest of divides.
