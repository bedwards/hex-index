Come Back Alive

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Come Back Alive

On May 30, 2014, a single thermographic camera left a warehouse in Kyiv, carrying with it a weight far heavier than its physical mass. It was destined for the front lines in the east, where Ukrainian soldiers were fighting an undeclared war with outdated gear and a desperate need for eyes that could see through the smoke and darkness of the Donbas. That camera, sent by a newly formed group of volunteers, marked the first tangible delivery of a movement that would eventually raise over $100 million, license the purchase of lethal weapons, and become the logistical spine of the Ukrainian defense. This was the birth of "Come Back Alive" (Povernys' zhyvym), an organization that would evolve from a simple crowdfunding initiative into the most sophisticated non-governmental military support system in modern European history.

The story begins not with a grand declaration of war, but with a quiet realization among a group of IT specialists in Kyiv. In May 2014, Vitaliy Deynega, a software engineer, and his peers watched the early stages of the conflict in the east with growing alarm. The Ukrainian military, still reeling from years of post-Soviet neglect, was ill-equipped to face a hybrid adversary. The state apparatus was slow, bureaucratic, and often paralyzed by corruption. Deynega and his colleagues saw a gap that only civil society could fill. They did not set out to build a charity in the traditional sense; they set out to build a logistics company for war.

The foundation was legally registered in 2015, but its operational heartbeat began the moment it was conceived. The very first purchase was a shipment of bulletproof vests, a rudimentary but vital intervention. In a move that would define the organization's culture of radical transparency and personal connection, these vests bore the names of the sponsors and the organization itself. It was a psychological weapon as much as a physical one; a soldier wearing a vest knowing that a stranger in Kyiv, a family in Lviv, or a diaspora in Toronto had personally paid for the steel plates protecting their heart was a soldier fighting with a different kind of resolve.

"The first month of operation saw the purchase of thermographic cameras, devices that required advanced imaging technology to detect enemy heat signatures in the dead of night."

The speed of their initial response was staggering. Within weeks of their formation, the 80th and 95th Airmobile Brigades received their first aid. This was not a slow drip of resources; it was an immediate injection of capability. By the middle of May 2014, the organization had already initiated public reporting, a standard that would remain rigid throughout its decade of existence. Every dollar raised, every item purchased, and every shipment sent was logged and published. This transparency became their currency, allowing them to raise over $3.19 million by early 2022, a figure that would soon explode into the hundreds of millions as the scope of the conflict widened.

The evolution of "Come Back Alive" was not linear; it was an exponential curve driven by the changing nature of the war on the ground. In 2014, the focus was on optics and navigation. The foundation provided the 40th Tactical Aviation Brigade with aviation GPS navigators, a critical upgrade for pilots flying into contested airspace. By 2015, their reach had expanded to the naval domain, supplying radar equipment to the transport ship Horlivka and the frigate Hetman Sahaidachny, as well as a radar system for the Donbas border guard ship. They were not just filling holes in the supply chain; they were upgrading the entire technological baseline of the Ukrainian Armed Forces.

Simultaneously, the foundation recognized that equipment was useless without training. A drone is a piece of plastic and circuitry until a human understands how to fly it; a thermal imager is a black box without an operator who knows what to look for. In June 2014, just a month after sending that first camera, they began training soldiers in demining. By September, they were running courses for artillerists and tank crews. The curriculum was practical, urgent, and born directly from the feedback of soldiers in the trenches. In March 2017, a dedicated sniper training program launched, and by the summer of 2021, they had opened a specialized branch for drone operator instruction. This shift from pure logistics to a hybrid logistics-training model proved prescient, as the war of 2022 would become, in many ways, a war of drones.

The organizational structure of "Come Back Alive" also underwent a significant transformation, mirroring the maturation of the organization itself. From its inception in 2014 by three individuals, the foundation grew to a staff of 40 and a volunteer network of 100 by 2021. The leadership transitioned in 2020 when Vitaliy Deynega, the founder, handed over management to Oksana Koliada, a former minister for veterans' issues. Although Koliada's tenure was brief, it signaled a move toward professionalization. In November 2021, Taras Chmut, a veteran, former marine, and co-founder of the "Ukraine Military Center," took the helm. Chmut brought a different energy, a combat-hardened perspective that aligned the foundation's strategy even more closely with the tactical realities of the front line.

The true test of the organization's capabilities arrived with the full-scale Russian invasion on February 24, 2022. The scope of the conflict shifted overnight from a regional skirmish in the Donbas to a national existential struggle. The foundation's priority areas were immediately recalibrated. The needs were no longer just about optics; they were about survival. The demand for bulletproof vests, which had been a niche purchase in previous years, skyrocketed. In the first five months of the full-scale invasion, the foundation procured and delivered over 20,000 bulletproof vests and sets of armor plates. This was a logistical feat of immense proportion, requiring a supply chain that could move thousands of heavy, life-saving units daily.

The scale of procurement during the invasion years was unprecedented. Between February 24, 2022, and June 2022 alone, the foundation purchased 3,460 thermal imagers and thermal sights. They acquired 2,869 quadcopters and 139 unmanned aircraft systems, effectively creating a private drone army that operated in tandem with the state military. They provided more than 20,000 communication devices and 1,220 tablets pre-installed with the ARMOR program, a proprietary software for graphical estimation and artillery coordination. The foundation also supplied over 250 new automobiles, creating mobile groups that could respond to crises anywhere in the country. The numbers were not just statistics; they represented the difference between a unit holding a line and a unit being overrun.

"Come Back Alive became the first charity organization in Ukraine to obtain a license to purchase military and dual-use goods, including lethal weapons."

This license was a game-changer. It allowed the foundation to bypass intermediaries and purchase goods directly from manufacturers. The efficiency gains were massive, reducing costs and eliminating the risk of fraud or delay. This legal standing culminated in a landmark achievement in 2022: the purchase of a Bayraktar TB2 unmanned aerial vehicle complex. The cost was $16,502,450, a sum raised entirely through public donations. It was the first time a non-governmental organization in Ukraine had purchased such a sophisticated weapon system, blurring the lines between civil society and the state military apparatus. The Bayraktar, often called the "drone of the people," became a symbol of Ukrainian resistance, and its purchase by "Come Back Alive" demonstrated the power of decentralized funding in a total war scenario.

By 2024, the organization had reached a new level of maturity. As of April 2024, 118 people were working directly for the foundation, managing a complex web of procurement, logistics, training, and finance. On May 10, 2024, the foundation celebrated its tenth anniversary, a decade of continuous operation in a country that had been at war for most of that time. The organization's commitment to quality and accountability was further solidified in 2025, when it underwent a rigorous audit in accordance with the international standard ISO 9001:2015 for Quality Management Systems. This certification applied not only to the foundation but also to the CBA Initiatives Center, an entity established in 2018 to research Ukraine's security sector and prepare recommendations for reform. This dual focus on immediate military support and long-term strategic development set "Come Back Alive" apart from other charities.

The geopolitical consequences of the foundation's success were not limited to the battlefield. The organization's effectiveness and the sheer volume of international support it garnered made it a target for Russian aggression. In 2022, the Russian Federal Security Service (FSB) issued warnings to several Russian citizens for making donations to the foundation. The stakes were raised to life and death in 2024, when the FSB detained a resident of the Novgorod region for the same reason, charging him with high treason. In the spring of that same year, Russia officially declared "Come Back Alive" an "undesirable organization," a legal designation intended to cut off its funding and criminalize any interaction with it. These actions by the Kremlin were a tacit admission that the foundation was a significant threat to Russian military operations, a non-state actor that had become more effective at equipping the Ukrainian army than many state bureaucracies.

The operational model of "Come Back Alive" is a masterclass in feedback loops and adaptability. The military department works directly with the soldiers. Military logisticians and instructors maintain constant communication with unit commanders, creating a real-time list of needs. This is not a top-down distribution of generic supplies; it is a tailored response to specific tactical requirements. When a request is received, the foundation evaluates its expediency. If the equipment is already in stock, it is distributed immediately. If not, new equipment is purchased.

A critical innovation in their logistics was the introduction of a rental system in 2016. Equipment was handed out on a temporary basis, with every device engraved with a unique serial number. Upon receiving equipment, the military unit registered it in a central database. Before the full-scale invasion, the foundation would retrieve equipment after a brigade's rotation for technical maintenance, ensuring that the next unit received gear in optimal condition. This "circular economy" of military hardware maximized the utility of every dollar donated. However, the nature of the full-scale invasion forced a change in this model. Since February 24, 2022, equipment given out by the foundation is now put on the permanent balance of the receiving unit, reflecting the desperate need for immediate, permanent reinforcement of the front lines.

The foundation's product development arm has also been prolific. Since 2019, they have supplied their own mobile observation complexes, a proprietary product designed to enable soldiers to observe the front line 24/7 outside of shelter. These complexes detect the movement and actions of enemy units and diversion intelligence groups, providing a critical intelligence advantage. They have also developed individual and group kits for demining, the ARMOR graphical estimation complex, an inclinometer, and an artillery weapon model. These are not off-the-shelf items; they are custom-engineered solutions to problems that the Ukrainian military faces daily.

The financial transparency of the organization remains its bedrock. Funds collected for the army and the expenditures for the needs of the Armed Forces of Ukraine (AFU) are made public on the organization's website. Donors can trace their contribution from a bank transfer or a crypto wallet to a specific thermal imager or a specific drone. The foundation accepts support through PrivatBank, Oschadbank, SWIFT payments, and various crypto wallets, creating a global network of micro-donors. This decentralized funding model made the organization incredibly resilient to sanctions and blockades. Even when the banking sector was under attack, the crypto channels remained open, ensuring a continuous flow of resources.

The training programs have expanded to cover five key specialties, with instructors teaching soldiers the skills necessary to operate the sophisticated equipment they receive. The instructors are often veterans themselves, bringing real-world combat experience to the classroom. They teach marksmanship, first aid, artillery operation, and, most critically, drone operation. In the war of 2022 and beyond, the drone operator has become the most valuable soldier on the battlefield, and "Come Back Alive" has positioned itself as the primary academy for this new generation of warriors.

As of 2026, the foundation continues to be a pillar of Ukrainian defense. It has provided aid to almost all combat brigades and detachments of the Armed Forces and Territorial Defense Forces. The journey from a single thermographic camera in 2014 to a multi-million dollar logistics and training powerhouse is a testament to the power of civil society. It proves that in the face of a superior military machine, a motivated, well-organized, and transparent civilian network can level the playing field.

The story of "Come Back Alive" is not just about hardware; it is about the human connection between the home front and the front line. When a soldier in the Donbas or the Kharkiv region wears a vest with a sponsor's name, or uses a drone purchased by a donor in Germany, or drives a car funded by a teenager in Kyiv, they are reminded that they are not alone. They are part of a collective effort that spans continents. The foundation has turned the abstract concept of "national defense" into a tangible reality, where every citizen can participate in the defense of their country.

The legal and ethical challenges the organization has faced, from Russian treason charges to the complexities of purchasing lethal weapons, have only strengthened its resolve. By adhering to international standards like ISO 9001 and maintaining rigorous transparency, "Come Back Alive" has built a reputation that transcends borders. It is a model for how modern societies can organize for defense, blending the agility of a startup with the discipline of a military command.

In the end, the name of the foundation carries a dual meaning. It is a command: "Come Back Alive." It is a hope: that every soldier equipped by the foundation will survive the war. And perhaps most importantly, it is a promise: that as long as the foundation exists, the soldiers will not have to face the enemy alone. From the first bulletproof vest in 2014 to the Bayraktar TB2 and beyond, the mission remains unchanged. The equipment changes, the tactics evolve, and the enemy adapts, but the core principle of "Come Back Alive" endures: to ensure that the men and women fighting for Ukraine have the best chance possible to return home.

The foundation's impact extends far beyond the immediate battlefield. By researching the security sector and preparing recommendations for reform, they are helping to build a post-war Ukraine that is more resilient, more professional, and better prepared for future threats. The CBA Initiatives Center is laying the groundwork for a defense industry that can stand on its own, reducing the need for foreign aid in the long term. This long-term vision is what separates "Come Back Alive" from a simple relief organization. They are building the future of Ukrainian security while fighting for the present.

As the war drags on, the lessons learned by "Come Back Alive" will be studied by militaries and charities around the world. How does a small group of volunteers outmaneuver a state intelligence apparatus? How does an NGO purchase a multi-million dollar weapon system? How does an organization maintain transparency while operating in a high-risk war zone? The answers lie in their adaptability, their transparency, and their unwavering commitment to the soldiers they serve.

The story of "Come Back Alive" is a story of the 21st century. It is a story of how technology, social media, and global connectivity can be harnessed to defend a nation. It is a story of how the power of the individual, when aggregated and organized, can change the course of history. In a world where great powers often dictate the terms of conflict, "Come Back Alive" has shown that the will of a people, backed by the right tools and the right organization, is the ultimate force on the battlefield.

The foundation continues to operate, raising funds, purchasing equipment, and training soldiers. The war is not over, and the need for support is as great as ever. But the foundation is ready. With a staff of over 100, a network of volunteers, a global donor base, and a decade of experience, "Come Back Alive" is prepared to meet whatever challenges lie ahead. They have proven that they can adapt, they can scale, and they can deliver. And in doing so, they have given Ukrainian soldiers something that no amount of money can buy: the knowledge that their country is fighting for them, and that they will come back alive.
