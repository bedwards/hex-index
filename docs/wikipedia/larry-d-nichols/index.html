Larry D. Nichols

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Larry D. Nichols

In the quiet laboratories of Cambridge, Massachusetts, long before the world was captivated by the frantic twisting of a 3x3x3 cube, a chemist named Larry D. Nichols was already solving the geometry of chaos. On April 11, 1972, the United States Patent and Trademark Office granted patent number 3,655,201 to a man whose name would soon become a footnote in the history of toys, yet whose invention fundamentally altered the landscape of mechanical puzzles. This was the Nichols Cube, a 2x2x2 mechanism held together not by friction or complex interlocking plastic teeth, but by the invisible, silent power of magnets. It was a device that could be scrambled and solved, a precursor to the global phenomenon that would arrive two years later, yet it remained shrouded in the complex machinery of corporate law and intellectual property battles. Nichols, a man of science first and a designer second, spent his life navigating the intersection of chemistry, physics, and play, creating a legacy that extended far beyond the colorful plastic of a toy store shelf.

Born on December 27, 1939, in Xenia, Ohio, Larry D. Nichols was destined for a life of rigorous inquiry. His early years were spent in the American Midwest, a region that often breeds a practical, hardworking ethos, but Nichols possessed a curiosity that demanded more than just practicality. He moved to Greencastle, Indiana, to attend DePauw University, where he immersed himself in the study of chemistry. It was here, in the late 1950s, that his academic prowess began to shine; he was named a Rector Scholar, an honor reserved for the most distinguished students, and he earned his degree in 1958. But chemistry alone was not enough to satisfy the scope of his ambitions. He packed his bags and moved east, seeking the intellectual crucible of Harvard University.

At the Harvard Graduate School, Nichols did not merely study; he innovated. The university, with its storied history of fostering scientific breakthroughs, provided the perfect environment for a mind that saw puzzles where others saw only problems. It was during his tenure as a graduate student that he first began to conceptualize the twist cube puzzle. The idea was deceptively simple in its objective but revolutionary in its mechanism. While the standard puzzle of the era often involved sliding tiles or static shapes, Nichols envisioned a three-dimensional object that could rotate along three axes. He imagined a cube composed of eight unit cubes, a 2x2x2 configuration, where each of the six faces was a distinct color. The goal was to mix these colors into a chaotic state and then restore order, a task that required both spatial reasoning and an understanding of group theory.

The true genius of Nichols' design lay in its internal architecture. In the mid-20th century, creating a rotating cube that stayed together was a significant engineering hurdle. Most designs relied on complex internal cores or friction fits that often failed under the stress of rapid twisting. Nichols, drawing on his background in chemistry and materials science, chose a different path. He embedded magnets on the inside faces of the unit cubes. These magnets allowed the smaller cubes to attract one another strongly enough to hold the structure together while permitting them to rotate freely in groups of four. It was a marriage of magnetic force and mechanical movement that allowed the puzzle to function with a fluidity that had never been seen before. The object was not just a toy; it was a kinetic sculpture, a physical manifestation of magnetic attraction and rotational freedom.

Nichols did not rush to market. He understood that a working prototype was the difference between a theoretical concept and a commercial reality. For years, he refined his design, creating many preliminary models that likely failed, broke, or simply did not hold their shape. It was not until 1968 that he finally constructed a fully working prototype. This was a pivotal moment, occurring sixteen years before the Rubik's Cube would sweep the globe, and seventeen years before the world would know the name of the Hungarian architect who popularized the 3x3x3 version. Nichols had the patent in his pocket, but the world was not yet ready to see it. He continued his work, balancing his life as a scientist with his passion for puzzle design, all while living in Arlington, Massachusetts, with his wife, Karen. They had been together since 1959, a constant in his life as he navigated the academic and corporate worlds.

The journey from prototype to patent was a testament to Nichols' persistence. On April 11, 1972, the legal machinery of the United States turned in his favor. Patent 3,655,201 was officially issued, covering the Nichols Cube. The patent document was specific, focusing primarily on the 2x2x2 puzzle, though it astutely noted the possibility of larger versions. This was a critical distinction. The patent did not just claim the idea of a twisty cube; it claimed the specific mechanism of using magnets to hold the unit cubes together. This detail would become the fulcrum upon which a massive legal battle would later pivot. The patent was a shield, a legal declaration that the concept of a magnetically held, rotatable 2x2x2 cube belonged to Larry D. Nichols.

While the patent was secured, the commercialization of the puzzle was a different story. Nichols was not a toy manufacturer; he was a scientist working for a research corporation. He had become the chief scientist for the Moleculon Research Corporation, based in Cambridge, Massachusetts. The founder and owner of Moleculon was Arthur S. Obermayer, a man who recognized the potential in Nichols' inventions. Obermayer was not just a financier; he was an active participant in the innovation process. He encouraged Nichols to patent his ideas and helped navigate the complex waters of intellectual property. It was under Obermayer's stewardship that the Nichols Cube was brought to the attention of the toy industry. The Ideal Toy Company, a major player in the world of games and puzzles, became the subject of a court hearing regarding the rights to the puzzle.

The legal landscape of the 1970s and 1980s was a battleground for intellectual property, and the Nichols Cube became a central figure in one of the most significant cases in toy history. The dispute was not merely about who invented the cube first; it was about the definition of infringement and the scope of patent protection. When the Rubik's Cube, the 3x3x3 version invented by Ernő Rubik in 1974, exploded onto the global stage, it overshadowed the earlier, smaller 2x2x2 versions. However, the legal question remained: did the Rubik's Cube infringe on the Nichols patent? The answer was not immediate. It took years of litigation, expert testimony, and judicial interpretation to untangle the threads of the case.

In 1985, a U.S. District Court delivered a verdict that seemed to vindicate Nichols completely. The court ruled that the Rubik's Cube infringed on the Nichols patent. This was a monumental moment for the chemist from Ohio, a recognition that his work had preceded the Hungarian phenomenon and that his intellectual property rights had been violated. The ruling suggested that the entire Rubik's Cube family, from the 2x2x2 to the 3x3x3, was built on the foundation Nichols had laid. But the legal world is rarely final in a single sentence. The Ideal Toy Company, representing Rubik's interests, appealed the decision. The case moved to the Court of Appeals, where the narrative took a sharper, more nuanced turn.

In 1986, the Court of Appeals issued a ruling that refined the scope of the infringement. The court agreed that the 2x2x2 version, often called the Rubik's Pocket Cube, did indeed infringe on the Nichols patent. The mechanics of the smaller cube, with its specific arrangement and magnetic-like behavior (even if Rubik used a different internal mechanism), were deemed too similar to the protected 2x2x2 design. However, the court drew a line in the sand regarding the 3x3x3 Rubik's Cube. They ruled that the larger, more complex version did not infringe on the Nichols patent. This decision highlighted the complexity of patent law, where the specific details of a mechanism can determine the fate of an entire product line. The 3x3x3, with its internal core and different structural integrity, was deemed distinct enough from the magnetically held 2x2x2 to escape liability. It was a partial victory for Nichols, a recognition that his 2x2x2 was unique, but a limitation on his claim over the entire cube phenomenon.

The saga of the Nichols Cube was not just about legal victories and defeats; it was about the broader context of invention and the race to solve the puzzle of mechanical rotation. While Nichols was securing his patent in 1972, others were also exploring the boundaries of twisty puzzles. In 1960, William O. Gustafson had received a patent for a manipulative toy that hinted at the same geometric challenges. In 1974, Frank Fox secured a British patent for a spherical sliding puzzle, showing that the desire to manipulate shapes in three dimensions was a global obsession. In 1976, Terutoshi Ishige received a Japanese patent for a 3x3x3 cube, further illustrating that the idea of the twisty cube was in the air, a zeitgeist that multiple inventors were trying to capture. Yet, it was Nichols who had the specific combination of a working prototype, a patent, and a corporate backer that brought the concept to the forefront of the legal and commercial world.

Beyond the world of puzzles, Larry D. Nichols' contributions to science were significant and enduring. His work at Moleculon Research Corporation extended far beyond the Nichols Cube. In 1973, he invented Poroplastic, a material that would have a profound impact on medicine and environmental safety. Poroplastic was a film that possessed the mechanical properties of a typical plastic, yet it was capable of holding large quantities of almost any liquid within its tiny pores. This was a breakthrough in materials science, a substance that could be used for controlled drug delivery, allowing medications to be released slowly and steadily into the human body. It was also used in environmental health and safety products, where the ability to absorb and contain liquids was crucial. This invention showcased the versatility of Nichols' mind; he was not just a puzzle designer but a chemist who could create materials that solved real-world problems. The same mind that figured out how to make a cube twist with magnets also figured out how to make plastic hold liquid in a way that could save lives.

The story of Larry D. Nichols is a reminder that invention is often a solitary, quiet process, even when it leads to global phenomena. He lived in Arlington, Massachusetts, with his wife Karen, a place that became the backdrop for his life's work. He was a man who valued the precision of science and the creativity of design. His death on July 20, 2022, at the age of 82, marked the end of a remarkable journey. He was 82 years old, a testament to a life well-lived, a life that spanned the mid-20th century and into the modern era of technology and global connectivity. His legacy is not just in the patents he held or the court cases he won, but in the way he approached the world. He saw the potential in the mundane, the magic in the mechanical, and the solution in the problem.

The Nichols Cube remains a fascinating artifact of this history. It is a puzzle that predates the Rubik's Cube, a puzzle that was legally recognized as the precursor to the phenomenon, yet it never achieved the same level of fame. The 2x2x2 version is often seen as a simplified version of the 3x3x3, but in the context of the patent, it was the original. The magnets that held it together were a unique feature that distinguished it from later versions, which used friction and internal cores. This distinction is a crucial part of the puzzle's identity, a reminder that the path to innovation is rarely a straight line. It is a winding road filled with legal battles, corporate maneuvers, and the quiet persistence of a scientist who believed in the power of a good idea.

Arthur S. Obermayer, the founder of Moleculon, played a pivotal role in this story. He was the catalyst that allowed Nichols' ideas to reach the world. Without Obermayer's encouragement and support, the Nichols Cube might have remained a secret experiment in a Cambridge laboratory. Obermayer understood the value of intellectual property and the importance of protecting the innovations of his chief scientist. His involvement highlights the collaborative nature of invention, where the scientist and the businessman must work in tandem to bring a product to market. The partnership between Nichols and Obermayer was a successful one, resulting in a patent that would stand the test of time and a material invention that would change the field of drug delivery.

The legal battle over the Rubik's Cube is a complex chapter in the history of toys, one that is often simplified in popular culture. The idea that one man invented the cube and another man stole it is a narrative that appeals to the imagination, but the reality is far more nuanced. The 1986 ruling by the Court of Appeals was a testament to the complexity of patent law, where the specifics of the mechanism matter more than the general concept. The 2x2x2 was different from the 3x3x3, and the law recognized that difference. This ruling did not diminish the importance of Nichols' work; rather, it clarified the scope of his contribution. He had invented a specific type of puzzle, one that was unique in its design and function. The fact that the 3x3x3 did not infringe on his patent does not mean it was not influenced by the same geometric principles, but it does mean that the legal protection was limited to the specific design he had patented.

Larry D. Nichols' life was a blend of the academic and the practical, the theoretical and the tangible. He was a Rector Scholar at DePauw, a doctorate holder from Harvard, a chief scientist at a research corporation, and a puzzle inventor. These roles were not separate; they were interconnected parts of a single identity. His work in chemistry informed his puzzle design, and his puzzle design informed his materials science. The Poroplastic film, with its ability to hold liquids in tiny pores, was a reflection of the same spatial reasoning that allowed him to create the Nichols Cube. He understood the properties of matter and the forces that held them together, whether it was the magnetic attraction of a puzzle or the capillary action of a film.

The story of the Nichols Cube is also a story about timing. In 1972, the world was not ready for a twisty puzzle. The market was dominated by different types of games, and the concept of a mechanical puzzle that required spatial reasoning to solve was not yet mainstream. By the time the Rubik's Cube arrived in 1974 and exploded in popularity in the late 1970s, the world was ready. The timing was off for Nichols, but the invention was ahead of its time. The patent he secured in 1972 would later become the basis for a legal victory, proving that the idea was his, even if the world had to wait a few years to embrace it. This delay is a common theme in the history of invention, where the inventor is often ahead of the market, waiting for the world to catch up.

In the end, Larry D. Nichols was a man of many talents, a chemist who could see the puzzle in the molecule and the molecule in the puzzle. His life was a testament to the power of curiosity and the importance of protecting one's ideas. He lived in Arlington, Massachusetts, with his wife, a quiet life that belied the impact of his work. He died in 2022, leaving behind a legacy that includes a patent, a legal victory, a material invention, and a puzzle that changed the way we think about space and rotation. The Nichols Cube may not be as famous as the Rubik's Cube, but it is no less important. It was the first, the original, and the legal foundation upon which the puzzle phenomenon was built. It is a reminder that innovation is often a quiet process, a process that happens in laboratories and legal offices, far away from the spotlight, but with consequences that ripple through the world. The magnets inside the 2x2x2 cube were more than just a mechanism; they were a symbol of the invisible forces that hold our world together, and the invisible hands that guide the course of history.
