SQUID

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: SQUID

In the quiet hum of a laboratory cooled to near absolute zero, a device exists that can detect a magnetic field a trillion times weaker than the one holding your refrigerator door shut. This is not a fantasy of future technology, but a working reality developed in the 1960s: the SQUID, or Superconducting Quantum Interference Device. While the name might evoke the tentacled marine creature, the acronym stands for something far more precise and profound—a magnetometer so sensitive it can measure the magnetic whispers of a single neuron or the subtle spin of an electron. To understand the SQUID is to understand the intersection of quantum mechanics and macroscopic engineering, where the bizarre rules of the subatomic world are harnessed to perform measurements that were once thought impossible.

The scale of this sensitivity is difficult for the human mind to grasp without a frame of reference. A typical refrigerator magnet produces a field of roughly 10⁻² Tesla. In the biological world, the processes within living animals generate fields between 10⁻⁹ and 10⁻⁶ Tesla, signals so faint they are usually drowned out by the ambient noise of the Earth itself. Yet, a SQUID can detect magnetic fields as low as 5×10⁻¹⁸ Tesla. That is a number with seventeen zeros after the decimal point. With a few days of averaged measurements, the device can discern these infinitesimal fluctuations, operating with noise levels as low as 3 fT·Hz⁻¹/². It is the ultimate listening device for the magnetic universe, capable of hearing the heartbeat of a cell from across a room.

The technology relies on a phenomenon known as the Josephson effect, a quantum mechanical discovery that changed the trajectory of sensor technology. In 1962, physicist Brian Josephson postulated that a supercurrent could flow between two superconductors separated by a thin insulating barrier, a junction that would later bear his name. The race to realize this effect began immediately. By 1963, John Rowell and Philip Anderson at Bell Labs had constructed the first physical Josephson junction. But it was a team at Ford Research Labs who would turn this theoretical curiosity into the most sensitive magnetometer in existence. In 1964, Robert Jaklevic, John J. Lambe, James Mercereau, and Arnold Silver invented the Direct Current (DC) SQUID. Their creation utilized a superconducting loop containing two Josephson junctions in parallel, a design that would become the gold standard for sensitivity.

To understand how a SQUID works, one must first discard the intuition of classical electricity. In a normal circuit, current flows like water through a pipe, and resistance is a constant friction. In a superconductor, however, resistance vanishes entirely. Electrons pair up into what are known as Cooper pairs and move through the lattice without losing energy. When these pairs encounter the Josephson junction, they can tunnel through the insulating barrier. The behavior of the SQUID is governed by the quantum phase of these electron waves. In the absence of an external magnetic field, the input current splits equally between the two branches of the superconducting loop. The system is in perfect equilibrium.

The magic happens when an external magnetic field is introduced. As soon as a tiny magnetic flux threads the superconducting loop, a screening current begins to circulate. This current generates its own magnetic field, which acts to cancel the applied external flux, a quantum mechanical attempt to maintain the status quo. This screening current creates a phase shift in the electron waves, a shift that is directly proportional to the external magnetic flux. The result is a redistribution of the total current. In one branch of the loop, the screening current adds to the input current, while in the other, it subtracts. One branch carries I/2 + Is, and the other carries I/2 - Is.

This delicate balance is the key to the device's function. If the current in either branch exceeds the critical current (Ic) of the Josephson junction, the superconductivity breaks down locally, and a voltage appears across the junction. This is the signal. As the external magnetic flux increases, the SQUID responds in a periodic, rhythmic fashion. When the flux exceeds half of a magnetic flux quantum (Φ0/2), the system reaches a tipping point. Nature prefers to keep the flux inside a superconducting loop quantized in integer multiples of Φ0. Therefore, instead of continuing to screen the increasing external field, the SQUID energetically prefers to admit a full quantum of flux. The current reverses direction, opposing the difference between the admitted flux and the external field. As the external field increases further, the current decreases to zero when the flux is exactly Φ0, and then reverses again. This cycle repeats every time the flux increases by half an integer multiple of Φ0, creating a voltage output that oscillates in perfect synchronization with the magnetic field.

The DC SQUID is not without its complexities. Its current-voltage characteristic is hysteretic, meaning the state of the device depends on its history. To eliminate this hysteresis and ensure a clean, predictable signal, a shunt resistance is connected across the junction. In some modern high-temperature superconductors, the intrinsic resistance of the junction is sufficient to handle this task. The device effectively becomes a flux-to-voltage converter, where a minute change in magnetic flux (ΔΦ) results in a measurable change in voltage (ΔV).

While the DC SQUID reigns supreme in sensitivity, it is not the only player in the field. A second type, the Radio Frequency (RF) SQUID, was invented in 1967 by the same Ford Research Labs team, joined by James Edward Zimmerman. The RF SQUID is a simpler beast, utilizing only a single Josephson junction. It operates based on the AC Josephson effect and is inductively coupled to a resonant tank circuit. As the external magnetic field changes, the effective inductance of the SQUID changes, which in turn shifts the resonant frequency of the tank circuit. These frequency shifts are easy to measure and correlate directly with the applied magnetic flux. While the RF SQUID is less sensitive than its DC counterpart, it is cheaper to produce and easier to manufacture in small quantities. For decades, it has been the workhorse for fundamental measurements in biomagnetism, capable of detecting the extremely small signals emitted by living tissues.

The materials used to build these devices are as critical as the quantum mechanics that drive them. Traditionally, SQUIDs were crafted from pure niobium or a lead alloy containing 10% gold or indium. Pure lead was avoided because it is unstable when its temperature is repeatedly changed, leading to mechanical fatigue. For these classic devices to function, the entire assembly must be cooled to within a few degrees of absolute zero. This requires liquid helium, a cryogenic fluid that is expensive, difficult to handle, and requires complex infrastructure. The need for such extreme cooling was a significant barrier to the widespread adoption of SQUID technology for decades.

The landscape changed in the late 1980s with the advent of high-temperature superconductors. Scientists developed SQUID sensors using materials like YBCO (Yttrium Barium Copper Oxide). These materials can achieve superconductivity at temperatures above the boiling point of liquid nitrogen. Liquid nitrogen is far cheaper, more abundant, and easier to manage than liquid helium. While high-temperature SQUIDs are generally less sensitive than their low-temperature niobium cousins, they are "good enough" for a vast array of applications, democratizing access to ultra-sensitive magnetometry.

The quest for miniaturization and new operating principles continues to push the boundaries of the field. In 2006, researchers demonstrated a proof of concept for CNT-SQUID sensors, built with an aluminum loop and a single-walled carbon nanotube Josephson junction. These sensors, measuring only a few hundred nanometers in size, operate at temperatures of 1 Kelvin or below. Their tiny scale allows them to count individual spins, opening new avenues in quantum computing and nanotechnology. The evolution did not stop there. In 2022, a SQUID was constructed on magic-angle twisted bilayer graphene (MATBG), a material that has revolutionized the study of correlated electron systems. These developments suggest that the SQUID is not a static technology but a living field of research, constantly adapting to new materials and new questions.

The true power of the SQUID, however, lies not in the laboratory but in its ability to map the invisible activity of the human body. The field of biomagnetism has been transformed by this device. Consider Magnetoencephalography (MEG), a non-invasive imaging technique that uses an array of SQUIDs to map neural activity in the brain. Unlike EEG, which measures electrical potentials on the scalp and suffers from distortion as signals pass through the skull, MEG measures the magnetic fields produced by neuronal currents. These fields pass through the skull and scalp with almost no distortion. Because SQUIDs can operate at acquisition rates much higher than the highest temporal frequency of interest in brain signals (reaching into the kilohertz range), MEG achieves excellent temporal resolution. It allows neuroscientists to watch the brain think in real-time, tracking the millisecond-by-millisecond dance of cognition.

Beyond the brain, SQUIDs are used in magnetogastrography to record the weak magnetic fields of the stomach, providing insights into digestive disorders that are invisible to other diagnostic tools. In cardiology, the technology enables magnetic field imaging (MFI), which detects the magnetic signature of the heart. This is used for diagnosis and risk stratification, offering a clearer picture of cardiac health than traditional methods. Perhaps one of the most novel applications is the magnetic marker monitoring method, where SQUIDs are used to trace the path of orally applied drugs through the digestive tract, ensuring that medication reaches its target with precision.

The clinical environment is becoming increasingly dependent on these sensors. As the size of SQUID sensors has decreased over the last decade, they have begun to equip the tips of Atomic Force Microscopes (AFM), allowing for the mapping of magnetic fields at the nanoscale. This convergence of scanning probe microscopy and SQUID technology promises to reveal the magnetic structure of materials and biological specimens with unprecedented detail.

It is worth noting that while SERF (Spin Exchange Relaxation Free) atomic magnetometers, invented in the early 2000s, offer potential sensitivity that rivals or exceeds SQUIDs, they come with their own constraints. SERF sensors do not require cryogenic refrigeration, a massive advantage, but they are orders of magnitude larger in size, typically around 1 cm³, and must be operated in a near-zero magnetic field environment. This makes them difficult to deploy in scenarios where the background magnetic field cannot be perfectly shielded. The SQUID, despite its need for cryogenic cooling, remains the most versatile and sensitive tool for general-purpose magnetometry.

The history of the SQUID is a testament to the power of theoretical physics to drive practical innovation. From Josephson's postulation in 1962 to the sophisticated graphene-based sensors of 2022, the device has evolved from a laboratory curiosity to an indispensable tool in medicine and materials science. It stands as a bridge between the quantum world and the macroscopic world, proving that the most elusive phenomena can be tamed and measured. As we push deeper into the era of quantum sensing, the SQUID will likely remain at the forefront, listening to the faintest magnetic whispers of the universe, from the beating of a human heart to the spin of a single electron.

The journey of the SQUID also highlights the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration. It required the theoretical insight of Josephson, the experimental prowess of the Ford and Bell Labs teams, the materials science of high-temperature superconductors, and the medical vision of neurologists and cardiologists. Each of these fields contributed a piece to the puzzle, resulting in a device that is more than the sum of its parts. The SQUID is a reminder that the most profound scientific advances often occur at the intersection of disciplines, where the abstract meets the concrete, and where the impossible becomes the measurable.

In the end, the SQUID is a triumph of human ingenuity. It takes the chaotic, probabilistic nature of quantum mechanics and harnesses it to create a device of exquisite precision. It allows us to see the unseen, to feel the faintest touch of a magnetic field, and to understand the inner workings of the living brain. As we look to the future, with new materials like twisted bilayer graphene and carbon nanotubes, the SQUID is poised to become even more powerful, even more sensitive, and even more integral to our understanding of the world around us. It is a small device, yes, but its impact on science and medicine is immeasurable. It is the ultimate sensor, a testament to the fact that with enough cooling and enough cleverness, we can listen to the universe whisper.
