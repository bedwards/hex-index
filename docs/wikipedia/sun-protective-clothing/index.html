Based on Wikipedia: Sun protective clothing

Here's something that might surprise you: that white linen shirt you wear to the beach could be letting through more ultraviolet radiation than a window. Meanwhile, your old pair of dark denim jeans—the ones you'd never dream of wearing in summer heat—might be blocking ninety-seven percent of the sun's most damaging rays.

The science of sun-protective clothing turns most of our intuitions inside out.

The Problem with Regular Clothes

We tend to think of clothing as a natural barrier against the sun. And it is—sort of. But the protection varies wildly depending on what you're wearing, and not always in the ways you'd expect.

A typical summer fabric—the kind of lightweight, breathable material that feels so comfortable on a hot day—might have what's called a Ultraviolet Protection Factor, or UPF, of around six. What does that mean? If thirty units of UV radiation hit the fabric, roughly five of those units are passing straight through to your skin. That's not nothing, but it's also not the impenetrable shield we imagine.

Now compare that to specially designed sun-protective clothing, which typically carries a UPF of thirty or higher. At UPF 30, only one unit out of thirty gets through—blocking about ninety-seven percent of the ultraviolet radiation. The difference is enormous.

What Actually Blocks the Sun

Several factors determine how much UV protection a fabric provides, and understanding them reveals why some materials work so much better than others.

Weave density matters most. The tighter the weave, the fewer gaps for UV rays to slip through. Think of it like a net catching fish—a fine mesh catches more than a loose one. This is why that breezy, open-weave beach cover-up might look protective but offers surprisingly little actual defense.

Color plays a significant role too, and here's where things get counterintuitive. Darker colors generally provide better protection than lighter ones. Studies at the University of Alberta confirmed what textile scientists had long suspected: black and dark blue fabrics absorb more UV radiation before it can reach your skin. The elegant white summer outfit might look perfect for a sunny day, but it's letting more radiation through than you'd think.

Fiber type makes a difference as well. Polyester has a particular advantage—its molecular structure includes a benzene ring that naturally absorbs UV light. This is pure chemistry at work. One hundred percent polyester, especially in shiny finishes, ranks among the most protective everyday fabrics you can wear.

Weight follows logically from density. Heavier fabrics tend to be more tightly woven with more material per square inch. But this creates an obvious problem for summer clothing: the most protective fabrics are often the least comfortable in heat.

Stretch reduces protection. When you stretch a fabric, you're pulling the fibers apart and creating larger gaps. That fitted athletic shirt might look sleek, but every time you reach overhead, you're momentarily reducing its UV-blocking ability.

Wetness is perhaps the most surprising factor. Get a fabric wet, and its UV protection can drop by half. The water essentially makes the fabric more transparent to UV radiation. There are exceptions—silk and viscose actually become more protective when wet—but for most materials, a wet shirt is a less effective sunscreen.

The Australian Connection

Modern sun-protective clothing traces its origins to Australia, and this makes perfect sense when you consider the geography. Australia sits beneath an unusually thin section of the ozone layer, particularly during its spring months. Combined with a largely fair-skinned population of European descent, this created a public health crisis. Australia has some of the highest rates of skin cancer in the world.

This urgency drove innovation. Australian swimwear companies began developing fabrics specifically designed to block UV radiation, and in 1996, the Australian government established the first formal testing standard through ARPANSA—the Australian Radiation Protection and Nuclear Safety Agency. The slogan "slip, slop, slap"—slip on a shirt, slop on sunscreen, slap on a hat—became one of the most successful public health campaigns in history.

Britain followed in 1998 with its own standard, developed by the National Radiological Protection Board and the British Standards Institute. The United States came later, formally establishing its standard in 2001, but American regulations eventually became more stringent in some ways, incorporating requirements for fabric longevity, resistance to abrasion and wear, and performance after repeated washing.

UPF vs. SPF: Understanding the Numbers

You're probably familiar with SPF—Sun Protection Factor—from sunscreen bottles. UPF works similarly but measures something slightly different.

SPF, traditionally, was determined through human testing. Researchers would apply sunscreen to volunteers' skin and measure how long it took for the protected skin to burn compared to unprotected skin. An SPF of 30 meant you could theoretically stay in the sun thirty times longer before burning.

UPF takes a more mechanical approach. Scientists use a spectrophotometer—an instrument that measures light at specific wavelengths—to shine artificial UV light through a fabric and measure how much gets through. They then apply something called an erythemal action spectrum, which is a fancy way of saying they weight the measurements according to how likely each wavelength is to cause a sunburn.

The result? Both systems should produce comparable numbers. A UPF 30 fabric and an SPF 30 sunscreen should provide roughly equivalent protection. But there's a practical difference: you can't forget to reapply your shirt, and you won't miss spots when putting it on.

The Industry Standards

The current American testing protocol is called AATCC Test Method 183, developed in 1998 by Committee RA106 of the American Association of Textile Chemists and Colorists. It's based on those original Australian guidelines from 1994.

But here's what makes the American system more comprehensive: it requires testing fabrics not just when they're new, but simulating their entire life cycle. ASTM D 6544—another standard—specifies that manufacturers must test fabrics near the end of their projected lifespan, when protection is typically at its lowest. Your shirt doesn't just need to be protective when you buy it; it needs to maintain that protection through years of wear and washing.

ASTM D 6603 covers how this information gets communicated to consumers—the hang tags and care labels you see in stores. A manufacturer can claim a maximum rating of UPF 50+, meaning anything that blocks more than ninety-eight percent of UV radiation gets capped at the same label.

Interestingly, sun-protective clothing in North America is largely self-regulated. There's no government agency actively policing these claims the way the Food and Drug Administration oversees drug efficacy. The FDA briefly regulated sun-protective clothing as a medical device in the early 1990s—clearing only one brand, called Solumbra, under those regulations—but later transferred oversight to the Federal Trade Commission for general consumer protection.

What to Look For (and What to Avoid)

If you're shopping for sun protection, some fabrics consistently outperform others.

At the top of the list: polyester, especially shiny polyester blends. Tightly woven fabrics in any material. Black or dark blue denim. Wool, surprisingly—though you won't want to wear it in summer. Satin-finished silk. Unbleached cotton performs better than the bleached cotton that makes up most of what's sold, because the bleaching process affects the fiber structure.

Some newer materials deserve mention too. REPREVE fabric, made from recycled materials, offers excellent protection. Bamboo-lycra blends, when tightly woven, score well. In 2003, the chemical company BASF developed a nylon fabric embedded with nanoparticles of titanium dioxide—the same active ingredient in many mineral sunscreens—which maintains its protection even when wet.

What performs poorly? Bleached cotton. Viscose. Polyester crepe, despite polyester's general advantages. Knit fabrics, which tend to have more stretch and openness. Undyed or white denim. And fabric that's simply old and worn—the UV protection degrades as fibers break down.

The Heat Problem

Here's the fundamental tension in sun-protective clothing design: the same properties that block UV radiation tend to make fabric hot and uncomfortable.

Dark colors absorb more UV—but they also absorb more visible light and heat. Dense weaves block more radiation—but they also block more air circulation. Heavy fabrics provide more protection—but they trap more body heat.

This is why modern sun-protective clothing has become a specialized field. Manufacturers work to balance these competing demands through ventilated weaves that still maintain density where it matters, moisture-wicking properties to help sweat evaporate, and antibacterial treatments to manage the inevitable perspiration. Some fabrics achieve high UPF ratings through chemical treatments rather than weave density alone, allowing for lighter, more breathable designs.

A Counterintuitive Finding

One discovery has intrigued researchers: washing your clothes in detergents containing fabric brighteners might actually increase their UV protection. These optical brighteners are designed to absorb UV light and re-emit it as visible blue light, making whites appear whiter and brighter. As a side effect, they're absorbing some of the UV radiation that would otherwise pass through to your skin.

It's not a substitute for proper sun-protective clothing, but it suggests that your laundry habits might be having invisible effects on your sun protection.

The Visible Light Trap

Don't trust your eyes when evaluating fabric for sun protection. There's some correlation between visible light and UV transmission—fabric that looks opaque tends to block more UV—but it's not a strong relationship. Modern UV-blocking textiles can be designed to let through reasonable amounts of visible light while still blocking most ultraviolet radiation.

Holding a shirt up to the light won't tell you much. You need actual UPF testing to know for sure.

The Practical Takeaway

Sun-protective clothing offers something sunscreen can't: consistent, reliable, reapplication-free protection. You won't miss spots. You won't forget to reapply after swimming. You won't accidentally wipe it off.

The styles that work best are the obvious ones: long sleeves, full-length trousers, ankle-length or longer skirts, collars that protect the neck. Coverage matters. The best fabric in the world doesn't help if it's not covering the skin that needs protection.

And perhaps the most important insight from all this research? The clothes you already own might be doing more—or less—than you think. That dark denim you've avoided wearing in summer might be your most protective garment. That breezy white linen might be barely better than nothing at all.

In sun protection, as in so many things, intuition fails us. The science tells a different story—one where the uncomfortable choice is often the safe one, and the comfortable choice carries hidden costs.
