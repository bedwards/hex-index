Criterion Capital

11 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Criterion Capital

In the heart of London's most iconic entertainment district, a building stands as a paradox of modern urban development: the London Trocadero on Piccadilly Circus. Acquired in 2005 by Asif Aziz, a Malawi-born developer who would go on to helm Criterion Capital, the historic complex was transformed into a leisure destination that now houses the Zedwell Piccadilly, a hotel boasting 728 windowless rooms and a sprawling rooftop bar. This transformation is not merely a story of architectural repurposing; it is the defining narrative of a private British property empire that has become synonymous with both the revitalization and the aggressive commercialization of London's prime real estate. Criterion Capital, a privately held entity with its ultimate ownership resting in a trust linked to Aziz via the Isle of Man company ACT Property Holdings Ltd, operates at the intersection of high-stakes finance, historic preservation, and social controversy.

The company's footprint is concentrated in the most prestigious postcodes in the United Kingdom, with a particular stranglehold on the areas surrounding Leicester Square and Piccadilly Circus. Yet, to view Criterion Capital solely as a landlord of luxury is to miss the complex, often contentious reality of its operations. The firm's history is a tapestry woven from ambitious redevelopment projects, legal confrontations with tenants, and a growing pattern of public scrutiny that has turned community hubs into battlegrounds for the soul of the city.

The Zedwell Piccadilly, opened in 2020, represents the company's flagship achievement in reimagining the Trocadero. The decision to create a hotel with hundreds of rooms devoid of windows was a bold architectural gamble, capitalizing on the concept of deep sleep in the heart of a city that never sleeps. However, the building's evolution has not been without friction. In May 2020, amidst the global upheaval of the COVID-19 pandemic, the company submitted plans to convert parts of the Trocadero's basement into a mosque. The proposal ignited a firestorm of controversy in a neighborhood globally renowned for its nightlife, theaters, and commercial vibrancy. Critics argued that the introduction of a place of worship in such a high-traffic entertainment zone was fundamentally at odds with the area's identity, highlighting the tension between the diverse needs of a modern metropolis and the entrenched character of its historic districts.

The pandemic also exposed the raw power dynamics inherent in the landlord-tenant relationship. In April 2020, as the nation reeled from lockdowns and economic paralysis, Criterion Capital threatened tenants with legal action for unpaid rent. This stance was taken despite a government-imposed moratorium on evictions, a safeguard designed to protect vulnerable businesses during a national emergency. While the company did extend rent-free periods to certain tenants deemed to be "in need," the initial threats sent a chilling message about the company's priorities. It signaled a rigid adherence to contractual obligations even when the broader social contract was fraying, setting a precedent that would define their interactions with tenants in the years to come.

Beyond the glittering lights of Piccadilly, Criterion Capital has been quietly expanding its portfolio into other corners of London and beyond. Around 2015, the company purchased two office buildings in Croydon, signaling an interest in the regeneration of South London's commercial hubs. They also secured a riverside site at Enderby's Wharf in East Greenwich, a location with significant potential for residential and commercial development. In 2022, the company made a decisive move to look beyond the British Isles, investing €80 million into residential apartments in Miraflores, Portugal. This marked the firm's first major expansion outside of the United Kingdom, diversifying its asset base and demonstrating a willingness to capitalize on the Mediterranean property market.

However, it is the company's actions in 2025 that have thrust Criterion Capital into the center of a national conversation regarding the preservation of community spaces and the ethics of commercial development. In early 2025, the company acquired the world's oldest YMCA building on Great Russell Street in London. This acquisition was not merely a property transaction; it was the closure of a historic community hub that had provided affordable facilities, fitness classes, and educational programs to residents across the capital for decades. The building's closure sparked an immediate and fierce public backlash.

The "Save YMCA Club" campaign emerged rapidly, uniting protesters, community leaders, and former members of the YMCA in a call for a six-month pause on the closure. The goal was to explore sustainable solutions that could preserve the historic site while perhaps adapting its function. The petition gathered momentum, reflecting a deep-seated public anxiety about the loss of non-commercial spaces in central London. As of June 2025, the issue remained unresolved on the company's official channels; there was no mention of the YMCA building or any future plans on Criterion Capital's website. This silence, in the face of such a loud public outcry, only deepened the scrutiny. The incident has sparked broader discussions about the preservation of historic community spaces in central London, questioning whether the drive for commercial development is eroding the social fabric of the city.

The controversy surrounding the YMCA was not an isolated incident but part of a pattern that became even more visible later that year. In early 2025, the Prince Charles Cinema, a beloved independent cinema in Leicester Square, found itself in a bitter dispute with its landlord, Zedwell LSQ Ltd, a subsidiary of Criterion Capital. The cinema received demands for a significant rent increase and a six-month break clause in their new lease. The terms were viewed by the cinema's management as untenable, threatening the very existence of one of London's most cherished cultural institutions.

The response from the cinema was swift and decisive. A petition entitled "Save The Prince Charles Cinema" was launched, and the public response was overwhelming. Within just two days, the petition gathered 115,000 signatures, a testament to the cinema's status as a cultural icon and the public's fear of losing it to commercial pressures. Asif Aziz, the chief executive of Criterion Capital, addressed the controversy with a statement that revealed the company's perspective.

"We've not bought Prince Charles to close it down. We have a planning application to convert the upper parts into a hotel. Having the Prince Charles Cinema there adds value and panache to our building, to our hospitality offering."

Aziz further defended the rent demands, asserting that the cinema had been paying "nothing" and were simply objecting to having to pay market rent. The cinema, however, disputed these claims, arguing that the rent demanded was not fair and did not reflect the reality of their operational history. This clash highlights the fundamental disconnect between the financial logic of a property developer and the cultural value assigned to community institutions by the public. For Criterion Capital, the cinema is a revenue stream and a value-add for a hotel development; for the public, it is a vital piece of London's cultural heritage.

The scrutiny on Criterion Capital extended beyond high-profile disputes over rent and closures to the day-to-day management of their residential properties. In 2025, reports emerged detailing widespread maintenance issues and vermin infestations in properties managed under the company's "Dstrkt" housing brand. These reports stood in stark contrast to the rapidly rising rents charged to tenants, raising serious questions about the quality of service and the company's commitment to tenant welfare. The "Dstrkt" brand, intended to offer modern living solutions, was instead associated with neglect and unsanitary conditions, leading to a reputation that threatened to undermine the company's broader brand equity.

The controversies surrounding Asif Aziz personally also came to the forefront in 2025. Reports surfaced that Aziz had paid £150,000 to settle allegations that he had illegally operated an unlicensed Forrest Gump-themed shrimp restaurant at Piccadilly Circus. The restaurant, which had drawn attention for its lack of proper licensing, was closed, and the site was subsequently repurposed. However, the story did not end there. The location later reopened in December as a reportedly tax-avoiding, unofficial Harry Potter-themed shop. The transition from an unlicensed shrimp restaurant to a tax-avoiding themed shop in such a short period drew further media attention and public scrutiny, painting a picture of a business culture that operates on the fringes of regulation and ethical norms.

The narrative of Criterion Capital is one of aggressive expansion and high-stakes redevelopment. From the windowless rooms of the Zedwell to the contested future of the Prince Charles Cinema, the company's actions have consistently prioritized commercial value and asset optimization. Yet, this strategy has come at a cost. The company has found itself at odds with tenants, community groups, and the broader public, who view their actions as a threat to the social and cultural infrastructure of London.

The story of the YMCA building on Great Russell Street serves as a poignant example of this conflict. The building, once a beacon of community support, became a symbol of the struggle between private profit and public good. The "Save YMCA Club" campaign was not just about saving a building; it was a plea to preserve the idea that London's most central and valuable land should serve the needs of its residents, not just its investors. The silence of Criterion Capital on the issue, as of mid-2025, speaks volumes about their priorities. The lack of a public response suggests a belief that the market will ultimately dictate the outcome, regardless of public sentiment.

Similarly, the dispute with the Prince Charles Cinema illustrates the limitations of the "value-add" argument. Aziz's claim that the cinema adds "panache" to the hotel offering may be true from a marketing perspective, but it fails to address the ethical implications of driving a beloved institution out of business through aggressive rent hikes. The public's response, evidenced by the 115,000 signatures on the petition, indicates that the value of the cinema extends far beyond its ability to enhance a hotel's appeal. It is a cultural asset that cannot be easily replaced or monetized.

The issues surrounding the "Dstrkt" brand and the personal controversies of Asif Aziz further complicate the company's profile. The reports of vermin infestations and maintenance neglect suggest a disconnect between the high rents charged and the quality of life provided to tenants. This is a common challenge in the luxury rental market, but the scale and visibility of the issues at Dstrkt properties have drawn specific criticism. The settlement regarding the unlicensed shrimp restaurant and the subsequent reopening of the site as a tax-avoiding Harry Potter shop adds a layer of skepticism to the company's operations. It raises questions about the transparency and ethical standards that underpin the Criterion Capital empire.

As the company continues to expand, both within London and internationally with its investments in Portugal, the pressure to balance commercial success with social responsibility will only increase. The events of 2025 have marked a turning point. Criterion Capital is no longer just a developer; it is a focal point for debates about the future of London's urban landscape. The decisions made in the coming years will determine whether the company can adapt to the changing expectations of the public or whether it will continue to face resistance from a community that feels its history and culture are being commodified.

The story of Criterion Capital is not just about property; it is about power. It is about who gets to decide what London looks like, who gets to use its spaces, and what values are prioritized in the city's most valuable real estate. The windowless rooms of the Zedwell, the closed doors of the YMCA, and the contested lease of the Prince Charles Cinema are all chapters in a larger narrative about the soul of a city. As Asif Aziz and his team navigate this complex landscape, they will find that the value of a building is not just in its bricks and mortar, but in the lives it touches and the community it supports. The public has made it clear that they are watching, and they are not willing to let the story be written solely by the bottom line.

The future of Criterion Capital will likely be defined by how it responds to these challenges. Will it continue down the path of aggressive commercialization, risking further public backlash and regulatory scrutiny? Or will it seek a more collaborative approach, engaging with communities and finding ways to integrate its developments with the social fabric of London? The answer to this question will not only shape the fate of the company but also the future of the city it calls home. The events of 2025 have set the stage for a critical reckoning, one that will resonate far beyond the boardrooms of Piccadilly Circus.

The legacy of Criterion Capital is being written in real-time, with every rent notice, every closure, and every public protest. The company's ability to navigate these turbulent waters will determine whether it is remembered as a catalyst for regeneration or a symbol of corporate overreach. The story is far from over, and the next chapter will be written by the choices made in the face of a public that is increasingly demanding accountability and respect for the city's shared heritage.
