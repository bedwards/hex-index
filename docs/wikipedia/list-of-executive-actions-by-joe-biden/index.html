List of executive actions by Joe Biden

12 min read

Based on Wikipedia: List of executive actions by Joe Biden

On January 20, 2025, the corridors of the White House witnessed a rapid and systematic dismantling of the previous administration's legacy. In a single morning, newly inaugurated President Donald Trump signed Executive Order 14148, titled "Initial Rescissions of Harmful Executive Orders and Actions." This singular document did not merely tweak policy; it effectively erased 78 of the executive orders and presidential memoranda signed by Joe Biden. The scale of the reversal was immediate and absolute, marking the end of a four-year experiment in executive governance that had produced 162 executive orders, 99 presidential memoranda, and 719 proclamations. To understand the magnitude of this political pendulum swing, one must first understand the machinery of the American presidency itself, a system where a signature on a piece of paper can reshape the daily lives of millions, yet remains as fragile as the next election cycle.

The presidency of Joe Biden, spanning from January 2021 to January 2025, was defined by a prolific use of the executive branch's inherent powers. Unlike the legislative branch, which moves at the pace of compromise and debate, the executive branch operates with a singular voice. This voice is articulated through various instruments, each with its own weight, history, and legal standing. To the untrained eye, these documents might look like bureaucratic noise, but they are the levers of modern governance. They are the mechanism by which a president manages the vast federal apparatus without waiting for Congress to pass a law.

At the apex of these tools sit the executive orders. These are not mere suggestions; they are binding directives issued to help officers and agencies of the executive branch manage operations within the federal government. When a president signs an executive order, they are essentially commanding the machinery of the state to turn in a specific direction. During Biden's singular term, he signed 162 of these orders, numbered sequentially from 13985 to 14146. Each number represents a distinct policy shift, a reorientation of federal resources, or a new interpretation of existing law. These orders covered a vast terrain, from climate change and pandemic response to civil rights and national security.

Closely related to executive orders, yet often misunderstood, are presidential memoranda. While they carry the force of law within the Executive Branch, they are generally considered less prestigious than their numbered counterparts. There is no established, rigid process for their issuance, and unlike executive orders, they are not assigned a sequential number in the official register. Instead, they serve as flexible instruments for the president to communicate policy directives quickly. Biden signed 99 of these during his term. They are the workhorses of the administration, allowing for agile responses to emerging crises or the refinement of broader strategies outlined in executive orders. Yet, as the events of January 2025 demonstrated, their lack of formal numbering does not make them invulnerable. Of the 99 memoranda signed, 11 were specifically targeted and revoked by President Trump's rescission order, proving that in the eyes of the successor, even the less formal instruments of power were deemed "harmful" and in need of removal.

Then there are the presidential proclamations, a different breed of executive action entirely. These are statements issued on matters of public policy, typically granted to the president by specific authority from Congress. They are often ceremonial or symbolic, marking national days of observance, but they can also carry significant policy weight when tied to specific statutory authorities. The sheer volume of Biden's proclamations is staggering. As of November 14, 2024, he had signed 719 of them. These documents often address widespread interests, declaring days of prayer, national holidays, or moments of unity. While they may not always command the same immediate operational shift as an executive order, they shape the national narrative and signal the administration's priorities to the public.

Beyond these primary categories lie a host of other administrative tools. Administrative orders are the signed documents—notes, letters, and directives—that conduct the day-to-day administrative operations of the federal government. Presidential determinations establish official policy positions of the executive branch, often required by statute to trigger certain actions, such as admitting refugees or authorizing military aid. There are also presidential notices and sequestration orders, which manage budgetary cuts and administrative adjustments. Furthermore, the realm of national security operates in a shadowed lane. National security directives function much like executive orders but are confined to the sensitive sphere of national defense and intelligence. Historically issued under various names by different presidents, these directives allow the commander-in-chief to act with a level of secrecy and speed that public executive orders do not permit.

The timeline of Biden's executive activity reveals a presidency deeply engaged with the levers of power. From the moment he took office in January 2021, the administration moved to address the legacy of the previous era while simultaneously forging new paths. The first executive orders signed in 2021 were largely focused on reversing the policies of the Trump administration, particularly regarding climate change, immigration, and the pandemic. As the years progressed, the focus shifted toward domestic economic recovery, infrastructure, and the reassertion of American leadership on the global stage. By the time the term concluded in January 2025, the cumulative effect of 162 executive orders and nearly 100 memoranda had fundamentally altered the landscape of the federal government.

However, the true story of this list is not just in the actions taken, but in the actions undone. The political lifecycle of an executive order is often short, dictated by the whims of the electorate and the successor. The data from January 22, 2025, paints a stark picture of this impermanence. As of that date, 67 of Biden's 162 executive orders had been revoked by Donald Trump. That is a revocation rate of 41 percent. Nearly half of the formal executive directives issued over four years were wiped from the books in a matter of weeks. This is not merely a change in tone; it is a structural reset. It signifies that the executive power, while potent, is not cumulative in the way legislative precedents often are. It is a power that can be erased as easily as it was written, provided the next person in the Oval Office has the will to do so.

The revocation of 11 of the 99 presidential memoranda further illustrates this point. While the percentage is slightly lower than that of the executive orders, the impact is significant. These memoranda often contained the nuanced, day-to-day instructions that kept the administration's machinery running. Their removal suggests a deliberate effort to strip away the operational memory of the previous administration, forcing agencies to start from scratch or revert to older protocols. The 78 rescissions under Trump's EO 14148 were a targeted strike, designed to dismantle the Biden legacy piece by piece. The title of the order, "Initial Rescissions of Harmful Executive Orders and Actions," speaks volumes about the political framing of these documents. What one president views as a necessary correction, the other views as harmful overreach.

This cycle of action and reaction is a defining feature of the American presidency, but the scale of the Biden-Trump reversal is particularly notable. It highlights the fragility of policy that is established through executive action rather than legislation. Laws passed by Congress are difficult to overturn, requiring a new act of Congress and often a presidential signature or a veto-proof majority to repeal. Executive orders, by contrast, are the product of a single individual's authority. They can be signed on a Tuesday and rescinded on a Thursday. This volatility creates a governance environment where long-term planning is often hamstrung by the uncertainty of the next election. For the agencies tasked with implementing these orders, the shift can be disorienting. A department might spend years building a regulatory framework based on a specific executive order, only to have that foundation pulled out from under them by a new administration.

The context of these actions is also crucial. The list of executive actions by Biden does not exist in a vacuum. It is part of a continuous historical thread that stretches back to the early days of the republic. The executive orders signed by Barack Obama, ranging from 13489 to 13764 between 2009 and 2017, set precedents that Biden both followed and expanded upon. Similarly, the executive actions of Donald Trump's first presidency, numbered 13765 to 13984, created a template for the aggressive use of executive power that Biden initially sought to reverse, only to be reversed in turn. The current era, beginning with Trump's second term in 2025, continues this pendulum swing, with EO 14147 and subsequent orders promising to reshape the government once again.

Understanding the sheer volume of these actions requires a shift in perspective. The 719 proclamations signed by Biden are not just a list of dates and titles; they are a record of the administration's attempt to shape the national consciousness. They mark moments of crisis, celebration, and reflection. They are the voice of the presidency speaking to the people, often bypassing the political noise of Capitol Hill to deliver a direct message. Similarly, the 99 memoranda represent the internal dialogue of the administration, the strategic decisions made behind closed doors that would ripple out into the lives of citizens. And the 162 executive orders are the hard edges of policy, the specific commands that change how the government interacts with the economy, the environment, and the individual.

The revocation rate of 41 percent is a statistic that demands attention. It suggests that a significant portion of the Biden administration's executive agenda was viewed by the subsequent administration not just as a policy difference, but as an error that needed correction. This is the nature of the executive branch: it is a vehicle for the president's will, and when that will changes, the vehicle turns sharply. The fact that 67 orders were rescinded means that entire sectors of federal policy were effectively reset. Whether it was environmental regulations, immigration enforcement, or labor standards, the rules of the game changed overnight. This creates a unique challenge for the American public, who must navigate a regulatory environment that can shift with the changing winds of politics.

The list of executive actions also serves as a historical archive. It documents the priorities of a specific moment in time. The focus on climate change, the emphasis on racial equity, the response to the pandemic, and the re-engagement with international alliances are all etched into these documents. Even if they are rescinded, they remain part of the historical record, a testament to what the administration believed was necessary at the time. They are the fossilized remains of a political philosophy that once held sway. Future historians will study these documents to understand the tensions of the early 21st century, the battles over the role of government, and the struggle to define American values.

As we look at the list of executive actions by Joe Biden, we see more than just a catalog of paperwork. We see a story of power, limitation, and the relentless churn of democracy. We see a president who used the full weight of his office to try to steer the nation in a specific direction, only to have that direction challenged and reversed by his successor. It is a reminder that in the American system, no victory is permanent, and no policy is safe from the next election. The executive orders, memoranda, and proclamations are the tools of the trade, but the outcome is determined by the will of the people and the judgment of history.

The contrast between the two administrations is stark. Biden's term was characterized by a desire to restore norms and rebuild institutions, using executive power to fill the gaps left by a gridlocked Congress. Trump's second term, beginning with the sweeping rescissions of January 2025, is characterized by a desire to dismantle and rebuild from the ground up, viewing the previous administration's actions as harmful deviations. This ideological clash is played out in the numbers: 162 orders signed, 67 rescinded. 99 memoranda issued, 11 revoked. These are not just figures; they are the battle scars of a political war fought in the corridors of the White House.

The legacy of these actions will be debated for years. Will the rescinded orders be remembered as temporary setbacks, or will they be seen as the beginning of a new era of policy? Will the 719 proclamations be viewed as mere ceremony, or as a genuine attempt to heal a divided nation? The answers lie not in the documents themselves, but in the impact they had on the lives of Americans. Did the executive orders on climate change slow the pace of environmental degradation, even if they were later reversed? Did the memoranda on civil rights provide a blueprint for future advocacy? The durability of an executive action is not measured by its longevity in the Federal Register, but by its imprint on the culture and the institutions it touched.

In the end, the list of executive actions by Joe Biden is a testament to the power of the American presidency. It shows a system where one person can issue hundreds of directives, reshape the government, and leave a mark on history. But it also shows the limits of that power. It is a power that can be undone with the stroke of a pen, a reminder that in a democracy, the ultimate authority rests not with the president, but with the people who elect them. The pendulum swings, the orders are signed and rescinded, and the cycle continues. This is the rhythm of American governance, a constant dance of action and reaction, where the past is always being rewritten by the present.
