Infinite scrolling

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Infinite scrolling

In 2019, Aza Raskin, the man who claims to have invented the infinite scrolling technique, publicly expressed deep regret for his creation. He admitted that he "did not foresee the consequences" of a design pattern that has since become ubiquitous across the digital landscape. Raskin described the mechanism not merely as a user interface choice, but as "one of the first products designed to not simply help a user, but to deliberately keep them online for as long as possible." This confession strikes at the heart of the modern digital experience, a reality where the very act of reading this sentence might trigger a reflex to scroll down, seeking the next piece of content before the current thought has fully settled. The invention was born from a desire to streamline browsing, yet it evolved into a psychological engine that fundamentally altered how humans interact with information, time, and their own attention spans.

To understand the gravity of this shift, one must first grasp the mechanical difference between the old world and the new. For decades, the internet operated on a principle of pagination. This was the digital equivalent of a physical magazine or a book. Content was divided into discrete, finite pages. When you reached the bottom of a list of search results or a news archive, you were presented with a clear, undeniable stop cue: a number, a "Next" button, or a "Page 2 of 10" indicator. These were architectural boundaries. They provided a natural rhythm to consumption, a beginning, a middle, and an end. The user had to make a conscious decision to cross that threshold, to click and load the next batch of data. This friction, as it turns out, was a feature, not a bug. It allowed the brain to process what it had just seen, to decide if it was satisfied, and to choose whether to continue or stop.

Infinite scrolling, also known as endless scrolling, obliterated these boundaries. It is an approach to web design where new material is continually and dynamically added to the bottom of the page as the user scrolls downward. There is no footer, no final page number, and no "Next" button. The content simply flows, a digital river that never reaches a delta. The apparent ability to scroll forever creates a state of perpetual motion. The user does not need to click; the act of sliding a finger or moving a mouse wheel automatically pulls the next batch of images, videos, or text into view. This seamless integration of action and result creates a hypnotic feedback loop. The effort to consume is reduced to a reflex, and the reward—the next piece of content—is delivered instantaneously.

The origins of this design philosophy are often traced back to the desire for efficiency. In the early days of social media and search engines, the goal was to remove friction. Why force a user to click through ten pages of search results when you could show them the next ten results the moment they reached the bottom? Why make a reader wait for a page to load when the content could pre-load in the background? The logic seemed impeccable from a productivity standpoint. It maximized the amount of content a user could see in a single session. However, it failed to account for the human psyche's reaction to the absence of limits. The removal of the "stop cue" transformed browsing from a task of information retrieval into a state of continuous, undirected consumption.

Over time, variations of infinite scrolling emerged, attempting to reintroduce some semblance of user agency. One such variation is infinite scrolling with a button to load more content. This hybrid approach gives the user a degree of control, requiring a deliberate click to fetch the next batch of data. A prime example of this is Google Search, which, for many years, utilized a "Show more results" button rather than auto-loading every page. This design choice respected the user's cognitive load, allowing them to pause and decide if the current search had yielded sufficient results. It acknowledged that sometimes, the goal is to find the answer and stop, not to browse forever.

Another variation is infinite scrolling with integrated pagination. This method attempts to bridge the gap between the endless flow and the need for orientation. It indicates to the user how far they have scrolled, providing a subtle map of their journey through the content. The mobile website of Google Shopping serves as an example of this implementation. By offering a breadcrumb or a progress indicator, the design tries to mitigate the disorientation of the endless feed. Yet, even with these mitigations, the core mechanic remains the same: the barrier to entry for the next piece of content is lower than ever before. The friction is gone, and with it, the natural pauses that once governed our digital habits.

The implications of this design choice extend far beyond mere convenience. Usability research has increasingly pointed to the fact that infinite scrolling can present significant accessibility issues. For users with motor impairments, the constant loading of new content can make navigation difficult, as the page layout shifts unexpectedly. For those with cognitive disabilities, the lack of a clear endpoint can be disorienting, making it hard to track progress or determine when a task is complete. The interface, designed to be fluid and seamless, can become a labyrinth where the user loses their sense of place.

More profoundly, the lack of stopping cues has been described by psychologists and researchers as a primary pathway to smartphone addiction and social media addiction. The human brain is wired to seek closure. When we read a chapter, watch a movie, or walk through a room, we experience a natural conclusion. This closure triggers a release of dopamine and a sense of satisfaction. Infinite scrolling denies the brain this closure. It creates a "variable reward schedule," a concept borrowed from behavioral psychology and famously applied to slot machines. The user does not know what is coming next. It could be a hilarious video, a breaking news story, a friend's update, or nothing of interest at all. Because the outcome is uncertain, the brain remains in a state of high arousal, constantly scanning for the next hit of dopamine.

This phenomenon has given rise to the term doomscrolling. It describes the act of obsessively scrolling through negative news and social media posts, often late at night, despite the emotional distress it causes. The mechanism of infinite scrolling facilitates this behavior perfectly. There is no natural stopping point to say, "I have seen enough bad news; it is time to stop." The feed just keeps coming, pulling the user deeper into a spiral of anxiety and despair. The design is not neutral; it is engineered to capture and hold attention, often at the expense of the user's well-being.

The landscape of problematic smartphone use and problematic social media use is inextricably linked to this design pattern. When the barrier to consumption is removed, the time spent on platforms increases exponentially. Users report losing track of time, intending to check a notification for thirty seconds and emerging an hour later with no memory of the intervening period. The seamless flow of content creates a dissociative state where the user becomes passive, a vessel being filled with an endless stream of data. This is not a failure of individual willpower; it is the result of a system designed to exploit human psychology.

The rise of specific social media formats has accelerated this trend. The Story format, popularized by Instagram, introduced a vertical, auto-advancing feed that mimics the endless scroll but with even less friction. Users swipe up to move to the next story, a motion that is almost too easy to be conscious. TikTok took this to its logical extreme. The For You page is a vertical infinite scroll where the content is not just endless but algorithmically curated to be maximally engaging. The transition between videos is instantaneous. There are no loading bars, no buttons, no pages. Just a continuous stream of short-form video that adapts to the user's preferences in real-time. The result is a platform where the average user spends significantly more time than on any other app, driven by a design that removes every possible point of friction.

YouTube Shorts and Facebook Reels followed suit, creating their own versions of the endless vertical feed. These platforms are not merely competing for content; they are competing for the user's attention span. The winner is the platform that can keep the user scrolling the longest. This has led to an arms race in design, where every innovation is geared toward reducing the likelihood of the user stopping. The "pause" button has become the enemy of the platform. The "next" button is a relic. The only metric that matters is the time spent on the app, and infinite scrolling is the engine that drives that metric.

The consequences of this shift are cultural as well as psychological. Our relationship with information has changed. We no longer search for answers; we browse for stimulation. The depth of engagement is often sacrificed for the breadth of exposure. We skim headlines, watch thirty-second clips, and scroll past complex arguments in favor of quick, emotionally charged content. The infinite scroll encourages a shallow mode of thinking, where the next piece of content is always just a swipe away, making it easy to abandon a thought before it is fully formed.

This design also impacts how we consume news and current events. The endless feed of Instagram, TikTok, and Facebook often presents a distorted view of reality, where the most sensational or emotional content rises to the top. The algorithm, driven by the goal of keeping the user scrolling, prioritizes engagement over accuracy or nuance. This creates echo chambers and amplifies polarization, as users are constantly fed content that reinforces their existing beliefs or triggers their emotional responses. The lack of a stopping point means that users can spend hours in a loop of confirmation bias, unaware of the broader context or the diversity of opinion.

The invention of infinite scrolling was a moment of unintended consequence. Aza Raskin's regret in 2019 was a recognition of a fundamental truth: that a design choice made for efficiency can have profound and often negative effects on human behavior. He saw that the tool he had created was being used not to empower users, but to capture them. The "endless" nature of the scroll was not a feature that helped the user navigate the world; it was a mechanism that trapped them within a digital loop.

As we look at the current landscape, it becomes clear that the infinite scroll is more than just a web design trend. It is a structural element of the modern internet, one that shapes our habits, our emotions, and our society. From the Google Shopping mobile site to the viral videos on TikTok, the pattern is everywhere. It has redefined the concept of "browsing," turning it from a deliberate act of exploration into a passive state of consumption. The user is no longer the driver; they are the passenger, being carried along by a current that has no end in sight.

The challenge for the future is to find a balance. Can we design interfaces that offer the convenience of dynamic loading without sacrificing the human need for closure? Can we reintroduce the "stop cue" in a way that respects the user's time and attention? Some platforms are beginning to experiment with this, offering options to limit screen time or to pause the auto-loading of content. But the inertia of the industry is strong. The business model of the modern internet relies on attention, and infinite scrolling is the most efficient way to harvest it.

The story of infinite scrolling is a cautionary tale about the power of design. It reminds us that the tools we build to serve us can also serve to control us. It is a reminder that in the digital age, the most important interface is not the one on the screen, but the one between the screen and our own minds. As we navigate this endless landscape, we must remain vigilant, aware of the mechanisms that drive our behavior, and conscious of the moments when we need to stop scrolling and start thinking. The scroll may be endless, but our time is not. And in that finite resource, we must find the courage to hit the brakes, even if the design was never built to let us do so.

The legacy of Aza Raskin's invention is a double-edged sword. On one side, it democratized access to information, allowing us to explore vast oceans of content with a single gesture. On the other, it created a machine that feeds on our attention, exploiting our psychological vulnerabilities to keep us hooked. As we move forward into a future dominated by Reels, Shorts, and Stories, the question remains: will we continue to scroll endlessly, or will we find a way to reclaim the power to stop?

The answer lies not in the technology itself, but in our awareness of it. We must understand the mechanics of the infinite scroll, the psychology behind the variable rewards, and the impact of the missing stop cues. We must recognize that the friction we once found annoying was actually a safeguard. By stripping away that friction, we have stripped away our ability to control our own digital lives. The path forward requires a conscious effort to reintroduce that friction, to demand design that respects our humanity, and to remember that the goal of technology should be to serve us, not to keep us online for as long as possible.

The infinite scroll is a testament to human ingenuity and a warning about its potential misuse. It is a design that works too well, a mechanism that has reshaped the way we live, think, and feel. As we stand on the precipice of a new era of digital interaction, we must ask ourselves what kind of future we want to build. Do we want a world where the feed never ends, or do we want a world where we have the power to say "enough"? The choice, ultimately, is ours to make, even if the interface tries to tell us otherwise. The scroll may be endless, but our agency does not have to be.
