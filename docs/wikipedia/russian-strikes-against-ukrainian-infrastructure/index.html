Based on Wikipedia: Russian strikes against Ukrainian infrastructure

In the bitter cold of a Ukrainian winter, the lights go out. Heat dies. Water stops flowing. This is not the chaos of a battlefield but something more insidious: a deliberate campaign to freeze and starve a nation into submission by destroying the invisible systems that keep modern civilization alive.

Beginning in October 2022, Russia launched what experts have called the largest attack on a nation's health infrastructure since World War II. The targets were not military bases or ammunition depots. They were power plants, electrical substations, gas distribution networks, and district heating systems. The weapons were cruise missiles, ballistic missiles, and swarms of cheap Iranian-designed drones called Shaheds that buzz through the night sky like mechanical wasps before detonating on impact.

By mid-2024, Ukraine had lost two-thirds of its electricity generating capacity.

The Logic of Terror

Why attack power plants instead of tanks? The strategy, according to the United Kingdom's Defense Ministry, was to demoralize the Ukrainian population and force the government to capitulate. Make life so unbearable, the thinking went, that ordinary Ukrainians would demand their leaders negotiate on Russian terms.

This approach has a name in military doctrine: strategic bombardment of civilian infrastructure. It was the logic behind the German Blitz on London during World War II and the Allied firebombing of Dresden. The idea is that if you make the home front suffer enough, the will to fight collapses.

History suggests this rarely works. The Blitz famously stiffened British resolve rather than breaking it. The same pattern emerged in Ukraine. President Volodymyr Zelenskyy described the attacks as "absolute evil" and "terrorism," but there were no mass protests demanding surrender. The strategy, by most assessments, failed spectacularly at its stated goal.

What it did accomplish was suffering on an enormous scale.

October 2022: The Campaign Begins

The systematic targeting of infrastructure began on October 10, 2022, just two days after an explosion damaged the Crimean Bridge, a crucial supply line between Russia and occupied territories in southern Ukraine. The bridge was both a military asset and a potent symbol. Russian President Vladimir Putin had personally driven a truck across it at its 2018 opening ceremony. Its partial destruction was a humiliation, and Russia vowed retaliation.

That retaliation came in the form of eighty-four cruise missiles and twenty-four loitering munitions, also known as kamikaze drones or suicide drones, launched in a single day. These are not drones in the hobbyist sense. Loitering munitions are essentially guided bombs with wings and an engine, designed to circle over an area until a target is identified, then dive into it and explode.

The strikes hit fourteen regions across Ukraine. Explosions rocked cities from Lviv in the west, just fifty miles from the Polish border, to Kharkiv in the east, barely twenty miles from Russia. The capital, Kyiv, took the heaviest blows.

Missiles slammed into a playground. A university campus was damaged. The famous Glass Bridge, a pedestrian walkway that had become a popular Instagram backdrop, was struck. The Kyiv Metro stopped running, though its deep Soviet-era tunnels continued to serve as bomb shelters, as they had for months.

Energy Minister Herman Galushchenko announced that roughly thirty percent of Ukraine's energy infrastructure had been damaged in a single day.

The Weapons

Russia employed a varied arsenal. The workhorse of the campaign was the Kh-101, a cruise missile launched from Tupolev Tu-95 and Tu-160 bombers that can fly over a thousand miles while hugging the terrain to avoid radar detection. The older Kh-555 served a similar role. From the Black Sea, Russian ships fired 3M-54 Kalibr cruise missiles, which have become infamous for their use against Ukrainian cities.

But Russia also deployed more exotic weapons. The Kh-22 is a massive anti-ship missile from the Soviet era, originally designed to sink aircraft carriers. It weighs nearly six tons and travels at speeds exceeding Mach 3, which is three times the speed of sound. Ukraine has no way to intercept it. When a Kh-22 struck an apartment building in Dnipro on January 14, 2023, it killed forty-six people and caused part of the structure to collapse.

Most spectacular were the Kinzhal missiles. The name means "dagger" in Russian, and the weapon is one of the hypersonic missiles that Russia has boasted cannot be stopped by any defense system. On March 9, 2023, Russia launched an unprecedented six Kinzhals in a single attack. But on May 16, 2023, Ukraine claimed to have intercepted six Kinzhal missiles using American-supplied Patriot air defense systems, a claim that, if true, would represent a significant blow to Russian propaganda about their wonder weapons.

The cheapest weapons were often the most numerous: Shahed-136 drones, designed in Iran and manufactured in large quantities in Russia. These slow, propeller-driven munitions cost perhaps twenty thousand dollars each, compared to millions for a cruise missile. They are easy to shoot down individually, but when launched in swarms of dozens, some inevitably get through.

Wave After Wave

The attacks came relentlessly through the fall and winter of 2022-2023.

October 18: Missile strikes caused blackouts affecting 1,162 towns and villages. Zelenskyy announced that thirty percent of power stations were knocked offline.

October 31: A mass strike left eighty percent of Kyiv residents without running water.

November 15: One hundred missiles and drones targeted energy infrastructure. About seventy were intercepted.

November 23: A missile struck the maternity ward of a hospital in Vilniansk, killing a newborn baby. The attacks on Ukraine's power grid were so severe that neighboring Moldova, which shares electrical infrastructure with Ukraine, lost power across more than half its territory.

By November 19, according to Zelenskyy, nearly half of Ukraine's power grid was out of commission. Ten million Ukrainians had no electricity.

By mid-December, Russia had fired more than one thousand missiles and drones at Ukraine's energy grid alone.

The Human Cost

The statistics of missiles fired and megawatts lost obscure what this meant for ordinary people. Imagine your heat going out when the temperature is well below freezing. Imagine no light to see by, no refrigeration for food and medicine, no way to pump water into your apartment building. Imagine hospitals operating on backup generators, knowing the fuel will eventually run out.

Ukrainian engineers performed what officials called "titanic work" to restore power between attacks, often repairing substations only to see them targeted again days later. Every thermal and hydroelectric power station in Ukraine was damaged at some point during the campaign. The country developed rolling blackouts, scheduled power outages that cycled through neighborhoods to prevent total grid collapse.

People adapted. They bought generators and power banks. They stocked up on candles and thermal blankets. They huddled in subway stations and other public shelters. But adaptation has limits. The elderly, the sick, and the very young are vulnerable when heating fails. The economic damage compounded daily as businesses struggled to operate.

Legal Dimensions

Under international humanitarian law, specifically the Geneva Conventions, deliberately attacking civilian infrastructure is a war crime. The principle is straightforward: military operations should distinguish between combatants and civilians, and attacks on objects essential to civilian survival, such as power plants that heat homes and hospitals, are prohibited when the military benefit is disproportionate to civilian harm.

The International Criminal Court took notice. In June 2024, the court issued indictments against four Russian officials for alleged war crimes connected to the infrastructure attacks. Among those charged were Sergei Shoigu, who served as Russia's Minister of Defense from 2012 to 2024, and Valery Gerasimov, Chief of the General Staff of the Russian Armed Forces. These are the highest-ranking officials to face such charges since the war began.

Russia, which does not recognize the jurisdiction of the International Criminal Court, dismissed the indictments. The officials are unlikely to face trial unless they travel to a country willing to arrest them and transfer them to The Hague.

Western reactions were swift and predictable. The European Commission called the attacks "barbaric." Jens Stoltenberg, then Secretary General of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization, called them "horrific and indiscriminate."

Why It Failed

The campaign to bomb Ukraine into submission has not worked, but understanding why requires thinking about what "working" would mean.

The attacks certainly caused immense suffering and economic damage. They forced Ukraine to divert resources to air defense and infrastructure repair rather than offensive operations. They made daily life harder for millions of people.

But they did not break Ukrainian will to resist. If anything, like the Blitz before them, they may have strengthened it. There is something about being attacked in your home, in your daily life, that transforms abstract patriotism into visceral determination. Each strike on a playground or hospital, each newborn killed in a maternity ward, became a reason to keep fighting rather than a reason to surrender.

There may also be a strategic miscalculation at work. Russia's leadership, steeped in the Soviet tradition of centralized control, may have overestimated how dependent Ukrainian society is on centralized infrastructure. Ukrainians proved resourceful, decentralizing where possible, using services like Starlink satellite internet to maintain communications, and finding workarounds that a more rigid society might not have managed.

The Zaporizhzhia Factor

One target deserves special attention: the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant, the largest nuclear power station in Europe. Russian forces occupied the facility early in the war, and it has been a constant source of international anxiety ever since.

The plant requires electricity to cool its reactors. When grid power is cut, it must rely on backup diesel generators, and diesel fuel is finite. On March 9, 2023, a Russian attack damaged the electricity supplies to the plant, forcing it onto emergency generators yet again.

Rafael Grossi, Director General of the International Atomic Energy Agency, issued a stark warning: "Each time we are rolling a dice. And if we allow this to continue time after time then one day our luck will run out."

A nuclear accident at Zaporizhzhia would not be Chernobyl, which involved a far different type of reactor failure, but it would still be catastrophic, potentially spreading radioactive contamination across Ukraine and into Europe. The fact that the plant sits in an active war zone, subject to shelling and power disruptions, represents an ongoing nightmare for nuclear safety experts worldwide.

The Pattern of Escalation

Looking back at the timeline, a pattern emerges. Major Ukrainian successes on the battlefield were often followed by intensified attacks on civilian infrastructure.

In September 2022, Ukraine launched a stunning counteroffensive in Kharkiv Oblast, liberating thousands of square miles of territory. Days later, Russian missiles struck the Kharkiv TEC-5, the second largest power plant in Ukraine.

In October 2022, the Crimean Bridge was damaged. Days later came the massive October 10 attack.

In January 2023, the United States and Germany agreed to supply Ukraine with advanced battle tanks. The next day, Russia launched drone and missile strikes on Ukrainian infrastructure.

This suggests the infrastructure campaign served partly as punishment for Ukrainian success, a way to exact a cost even when Russia was losing on the battlefield. It may also have been intended to deter further Western support by demonstrating that Russia can always escalate the pain.

Adaptation and Recovery

By early April 2023, Ukraine had performed enough repairs to resume exporting electricity to European neighbors, a remarkable achievement given the scale of destruction. But this was a fragile recovery. Every repaired power station remained a target. The work of restoration never ended because the attacks never stopped.

The international response included stepped-up air defense supplies. Patriot missile batteries from the United States, NASAMS systems from Norway, IRIS-T from Germany, and other systems gradually improved Ukraine's ability to intercept incoming missiles and drones. The interception rate climbed over time, though no air defense is perfect, and Russia adapted by launching larger salvos and mixing fast ballistic missiles with slower cruise missiles to overwhelm the defenses.

The Broader Meaning

The Russian campaign against Ukrainian infrastructure may be remembered as a case study in the limits of strategic bombardment. For over a century, military theorists have debated whether attacking an enemy's civilian population and economy can win wars without the need to defeat their armies in the field. The evidence has generally been discouraging for proponents of such strategies, from the failure of the Blitz to the limited results of American bombing in Vietnam.

What makes the Ukrainian case distinctive is the precision of modern weapons combined with the transparency of the modern information environment. Every attack was documented, often in real time on social media. Every destroyed playground and hospital was photographed and shared. This generated international sympathy and support for Ukraine while isolating Russia diplomatically.

Russia may have calculated that the world would eventually grow tired of the war, that fatigue would erode support for Ukraine. That calculation may yet prove correct over a longer timeframe. But in the short term, the infrastructure attacks accelerated rather than slowed Western military assistance.

The campaign also demonstrated the vulnerability of modern societies to attacks on critical infrastructure. Ukraine is not unique in its dependence on centralized power grids, water systems, and heating networks. The relative ease with which these systems can be disrupted is a lesson that military planners around the world are surely studying.

Conclusion

As of mid-2024, Ukraine retained only a third of its pre-war electricity generating capacity. The attacks continued. The repairs continued. The suffering continued.

What began as an apparent attempt to shock Ukraine into surrender had become a grinding war of attrition against civilian infrastructure, a campaign of collective punishment that international courts have deemed criminal but that shows no sign of stopping.

In Kyiv, in Kharkiv, in Dnipro and Lviv and Odesa, people continue to live their lives in the intervals between sirens. They go to work when they have power, huddle in shelters when they do not, mourn their dead, and somehow maintain the defiance that has characterized the Ukrainian response from the beginning.

The lights go out. And then, eventually, someone repairs the damage, and the lights come back on. Until the next missile arrives.
