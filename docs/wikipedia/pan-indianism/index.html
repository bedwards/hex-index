Pan-Indianism

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Pan-Indianism

In 1811, at a meeting in Vincennes, a Shawnee leader named Tecumseh stood before William Henry Harrison, the governor of the Indiana Territory, and delivered a thunderous rebuke to the very concept of private land ownership. He argued that the earth was not a commodity to be parceled out in deeds and fences, but a common heritage belonging to everyone, a sentiment that united the disparate tribes of the Great Lakes region against the encroaching tide of American expansionism. Tecumseh's Confederacy was not merely a military alliance; it was the first great spark of a philosophical and political fire that would burn through two centuries of Indigenous history: Pan-Indianism. This approach, which prioritizes unity and a degree of cultural homogenization over strict tribal distinctions, has become the bedrock of political organizing for Native Americans seeking social justice and cultural revitalization in the Continental United States, eventually rippling out to Indigenous communities across Canada and the Americas.

To understand the magnitude of this movement, one must first dismantle the assumption that Indigenous peoples are a monolith. The Americas are home to thousands of distinct nations, each with its own language, governance structures, spiritual practices, and historical traumas. Pan-Indianism does not erase these differences; rather, it builds a bridge across them. It is a strategic recognition that while a Cherokee and a Navajo may have never traded or spoken the same language before European contact, they face a common set of existential threats from colonial powers, federal policies, and resource extraction. The philosophy suggests that survival and sovereignty are not just local struggles but collective ones. This is why the term has evolved. While "Pan-Indianism" is the dominant label in the United States, academics and activists in Canada often prefer "pan-Aboriginal" to encompass the Inuit and Métis peoples, who possess distinct identities yet share the broader struggle for Indigenous rights. Some scholars have even pushed for the term "pan-Amerindianism" to distinguish these peoples from the other groups globally also referred to as "Indians," sharpening the focus on the specific geopolitical reality of the Americas.

The roots of this movement run deep, stretching back long before the modern era of political activism. While Tecumseh's early 19th-century confederacy stands as a monumental early example, the machinery of Pan-Indian organizing truly began to turn in the early 20th century, driven by the urgent need to resist the federal government's assimilationist policies. By 1912, the pressure of the Allotment Act, which sought to break up communal tribal lands into individual parcels to be sold off, had created a crisis point. In response, members of the Creek, Choctaw, Cherokee, and Chickasaw tribes, despite their distinct histories, united to form the Four Mothers Society. This was not a cultural fusion but a political merger of necessity, a collective action to halt the disintegration of their land base. That same year, in the far north, the Alaskan Native Brotherhood and Sisterhood emerged, centering their mission on the protection of Native resources, proving that the impulse toward unity was not confined to the lower 48 states but was a continental phenomenon.

The federal government's relationship with this rising tide of unity was complex and often contradictory. In 1934, Congress passed the Indian Reorganization Act, a landmark legislation that attempted to reverse the damage of decades of assimilation and allotment. The act granted legal sanction to tribal landholdings, returned unsold allotted lands to tribes, and made provisions for the purchase of new lands. It encouraged the formation of tribal constitutions, systems of justice, and business corporations, while expanding educational opportunities through new facilities and loans. Perhaps most significantly, it advocated for the hiring of Indians by the Office of Indian Affairs, extended the Indian Trust Status, and finally granted Indians Religious Freedom. This legislative shift created a space where tribal governments could reorganize, but it also inadvertently fueled the growth of inter-tribal cooperation. As tribes rebuilt their internal structures, they began to look outward, realizing that their individual constitutions were stronger when supported by a unified front.

Before successful national organizations could take root, the groundwork was laid by several regional bodies that united multiple nations within the context of post-settlement politics. In Ontario, the Grand General Indian Council was organized with missionary assistance in the 1870s and persisted until 1938, serving as a vital platform for Indigenous voices in Canada. Similarly, the Allied Tribes of British Columbia were created in 1916 to tackle the unique land claims challenges of the Pacific Northwest. But the true explosion of national consciousness occurred in 1911 with the creation of the Society of American Indians, the first national Indian political organization in the US. This group, comprised largely of educated Indigenous elites, pursued better educational programs and improved living conditions, arguing that the path to progress lay in engagement with the American political system. Their influence was profound. They played a critical role in advocating for Indian citizenship, a goal finally realized with the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924, which granted US citizenship to all Native Americans born within the country.

This era of early activism was characterized by a spirit of cooperation and a belief in the power of diplomacy. Before World War II and throughout the 1940s and 50s, Native activism was less developed and for the most part non-violent. Many leaders made genuine efforts to work within the American government's framework to secure rights. In 1923, Deskaheh, a Cayuga chief, traveled to the League of Nations in Geneva in a symbolic gesture, hoping to obtain international recognition of his tribe's sovereignty. Although his request was denied, the act itself was a declaration that Indigenous nations saw themselves as independent entities on the world stage. In 1939, the Tonawanda Band of the Seneca tribe issued a "Declaration of Independence" to the state of New York. It was ignored, and natives who broke state law were arrested, but the document remains a testament to the early refusal to accept subjugation. In other cases, American Indian tribes struggled to maintain their sovereignty over tribal land that had been granted to them by treaties with the federal government, realizing that without a united voice, their individual treaties were easily ignored.

The mid-20th century saw a shift in the nature of this unity. Unrelated Native American groups, and Americans in general, began to notice and sympathize with their aims, but the internal dynamics of the movement were also changing. The 1960s brought a new energy, a sense of urgency that would culminate in the American Indian Chicago Conference. For one week in June 1961, 420 American Indians from 67 tribes convened at the University of Chicago. This was not a gathering of like-minded neighbors but a convergence of vastly different cultures, from the arid Southwest to the dense forests of the East. After exchanging opinions that covered many aspects of Indian affairs, the group drafted the Declaration of Indian Purpose. This document was a clarion call for self-determination, asserting that Indigenous peoples had the right to control their own destinies.

As the decade progressed, the tone of Pan-Indianism grew more radical. The American Indian Movement (AIM) was created in 1968 in Minneapolis by Dennis Banks, George Mitchell, and Clyde Bellecourt (all Ojibwe), and Russell Means (Lakota). Inspired by groups like The Black Panthers, AIM took a new, more radical approach than had previous Native groups. They rejected the slow, bureaucratic path of earlier organizations, arguing for liberation instead of assimilation. AIM became well known for its involvement in the Wounded Knee incident in 1973 and the seizure of the Bureau of Indian Affairs in 1972. They advocated for the abolition of the Office of Indian Affairs, which they viewed as an instrument of assimilation and control. AIM mobilized many people to become activists and quickly became the most visible of the pan-Indian organizations of the era, using direct action at protests to force the American public to confront the reality of Indigenous poverty and broken treaties.

Across the border, the movement took on a slightly different, yet equally potent, form. Founded in 1967, the Assembly of First Nations (AFN), formerly known as the National Indian Brotherhood, became a representative body of First Nations leaders in Canada. The aims of the organization were to protect the rights, treaty obligations, ceremonies, and claims of citizens of the First Nations in Canada. It represents the majority of all First Nations governments or "band councils" in Canada and is led by a National Chief, a position that gives the organization a unified voice in negotiations with the Canadian government. This parallel development in the north highlighted that while the specific legal landscapes of the US and Canada differed, the underlying drive for a collective Indigenous political identity was a continental force.

The scope of Pan-Indianism also expanded to include specialized organizations that tackled specific issues with a unified front. The Association on American Indian Affairs (AAIA) aims to improve Native American health, education, and economic and community development while maintaining tradition, culture, and language. Protecting Native American sovereignty, natural resources, and constitutional, legal, and human rights is also included in their mission, demonstrating how the broad philosophy translates into targeted action. The Black Hills Treaty Council was established in South Dakota in 1911 on the Cheyenne River Sioux reservation to prepare a suit in the U.S. Court of Claims, a legal battle that would continue for decades. The Columbia River Inter-Tribal Fish Commission (CRITFC) was created in 1977 by four tribes—the Nez Perce, Umatilla, Warm Springs, and Yakama—to "renew their authority in fisheries management." Their mission includes protecting treaty rights for fishing guaranteed by treaties with the federal government "through the exercise of the inherent sovereign powers of the tribes."

The work of CRITFC is a perfect example of how Pan-Indianism operates on the ground. "For generations, traditional fishing authorities governed tribal communities on the Columbia River," the commission notes. One such authority was the old Celilo Fish Committee. The authority exercised by the Celilo Fish Committee was derived from the sovereign powers of the people living and fishing in nearby tribal territories. The committee ordained fishing practices that were disciplined and designed to serve a high purpose: to ensure that the salmon resource was served first—even worshipped—so that it would flourish and always exist. When state and federal regulations threatened these practices, the four tribes did not fight alone; they formed a commission that pooled their resources, legal expertise, and political power. They argued that their right to fish was not a privilege granted by the state but an inherent right reserved by treaty, a right that applied to all the tribes of the river collectively.

Another powerful example of this unity in action is the All Indian Pueblo Council, founded in 1922. This council successfully opposed the proposed Bursum Bill, which legislated rights for squatters on Native grounds along the Rio Grande. The All Indian Pueblo Council declared that Pueblo Indians had lived in a "civilized condition" long before European Americans came over to America. They appealed to public morality by claiming to have pride in their past. The All Pueblo Council needed public support to help preserve lands, customs, and traditions and to turn their interest to the Pueblo tribes so they could gain assistance in court. By presenting a united front, the nineteen Pueblo tribes were able to defeat a bill that would have devastated their land base, proving that solidarity could overcome even the most entrenched political interests.

The international dimension of Pan-Indianism cannot be overstated. In 1989, the Indigenous and Tribal Peoples Convention, also known as the International Labour Organization's (ILO) Convention No. 169, occurred. To date, this has been the only formally binding international convention that specifically applies to indigenous peoples. The conference recognized the goal of native groups to maintain their position as entities independent of national governments. This international recognition provided a new layer of legitimacy to the Pan-Indian movement, allowing Indigenous leaders to bypass national governments and appeal directly to the global community for support. It validated the centuries-long struggle for sovereignty and provided a framework for future legal battles.

Today, the legacy of Pan-Indianism is woven into the fabric of Indigenous life in the Americas. It is present in the way tribes coordinate on water rights, in the way they share legal resources to fight for treaty rights, and in the way they celebrate cultural revitalization not just as a tribal affair but as a continental one. The movement has evolved from the early confederacies of the 19th century to the sophisticated legal and political organizations of the 21st century. It has weathered the storms of assimilation, the trauma of forced removal, and the indifference of the state. Yet, it has remained resilient, adapting to new challenges while holding fast to its core principle: that unity is strength.

The story of Pan-Indianism is not just a history of political organizations; it is a story of human resilience. It is the story of people who, despite being told they were separate, distinct, and inferior, chose to stand together. It is the story of Tecumseh's vision, of the Four Mothers Society's defiance, of the Society of American Indians' diplomacy, and of AIM's radicalism. It is the story of the Columbia River tribes protecting their salmon, the Pueblo tribes protecting their land, and the First Nations of Canada protecting their treaty rights. Through it all, the message has remained the same: we are many, but we are one. The struggle for justice, for land, and for culture continues, but it is no longer a lonely struggle. It is a collective one, powered by the enduring spirit of Pan-Indianism.

The impact of this philosophy extends far beyond the borders of North America. Some pan-Indian organizations seek to pool the resources of Native groups to protect the interests of indigenous peoples across the world, forging connections with Indigenous communities in Australia, Latin America, and Scandinavia. The term "Indigenous" itself has become a global identifier, a testament to the success of the Pan-Indian movement in creating a shared identity that transcends national boundaries. While the specific challenges faced by the Inuit in the Arctic are different from those faced by the Lakota in the Great Plains, the underlying desire for self-determination and the recognition of inherent sovereignty is a universal thread that binds them all together.

As we look to the future, the principles of Pan-Indianism will undoubtedly continue to evolve. New generations of Indigenous leaders are emerging, bringing fresh perspectives and new strategies to the movement. They are using social media to organize, international law to advocate, and cultural innovation to revitalize their traditions. But the core of the movement remains unchanged. It is a commitment to unity, to the belief that our shared history and shared struggles make us stronger together than we could ever be apart. From the Great Lakes to the Pacific Northwest, from the Rio Grande to the Arctic Circle, the voice of Pan-Indianism continues to rise, a testament to the enduring power of Indigenous resilience and the unbreakable bond of unity.

In the end, Pan-Indianism is more than a political strategy; it is a philosophy of survival and a vision of the future. It is a reminder that while the past may have been marked by division and conflict, the future can be defined by cooperation and strength. It is a call to action for all Indigenous peoples to stand together, to protect their lands, to honor their traditions, and to fight for their rights. And it is a promise that as long as there are Indigenous peoples in the Americas, the spirit of unity will never die. The legacy of Tecumseh, the Four Mothers, and the countless others who have walked this path is a beacon of hope for the future, a reminder that no matter how dark the times may seem, there is always the power of the collective to bring about change. The journey is far from over, but the path is clear, and the voice of the people is louder than ever before.
