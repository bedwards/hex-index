Semantic change

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Semantic change

In 1830, the word nice meant "kind" and "thoughtful," a sentiment of warmth we still cherish today. But rewind the clock to the 14th century, and that same word described someone who was "foolish, ignorant, frivolous, and senseless." It was an insult, a label for the simpleton, derived from the Latin nescius, literally meaning "not-knowing." This is not a minor shift in tone; it is a complete inversion of value, a linguistic journey from the depths of stupidity to the heights of virtue. It is the story of semantic change, the invisible engine that drives the evolution of language, turning "terrible" from something that inspires terror into something merely bad, and "awful" from something that commands awe into something that is just, well, annoying.

Language is not a static museum where words are preserved under glass, fixed in their original meanings. It is a living, breathing organism, constantly shedding skin and growing new ones. Semantic change, also known as semantic shift, drift, or progression, is the process by which the meaning of a word evolves, often to the point where the modern usage bears little resemblance to its ancestral root. In the field of diachronic, or historical, linguistics, this is the study of how words travel through time. Every word carries a baggage of senses and connotations that are added, removed, or altered by the people who speak them. Over centuries, these changes can be so radical that cognates—words that share a common origin across different languages and eras—end up speaking completely different languages of meaning.

Consider the pair awful and awesome. In their original construction, both words were built on the foundation of "awe." To be awful was to be "full of awe," inspiring a sense of wonder mixed with fear, the kind of feeling one might have standing before a towering mountain or a thunderstorm. It was a word of reverence, describing something impressive and formidable. Today, however, we use it to describe a bad day, a cold meal, or a dull movie. It has slid down the scale of intensity to mean "extremely bad." Conversely, awesome has ascended. It too once meant "inspiring wonder or fear," but it has shed the fear entirely, retaining only the wonder to become a synonym for "extremely good." We have taken a word of terror and made it a word of praise, while the word that once commanded respect has become a synonym for mediocrity.

The trajectory of terrible and terrific follows a similar, yet more complex, path. Both stem from the Latin terrere, meaning "to frighten." Originally, terrible meant "inspiring terror." It described a force of nature or a deity that struck fear into the heart. Over time, the meaning shifted to indicate anything spectacular or extraordinary, regardless of whether it was good or bad. Eventually, the scale tipped, and it settled into its modern meaning: something spectacularly bad. Terrific took the same journey but landed on the opposite shore. It began as "inspiring terror," moved to mean "anything spectacular," and finally settled into its current usage as "something spectacularly good." We now tell a friend their haircut is "terrific," completely unaware that we are essentially calling it "fear-inducing."

These shifts are not random accidents; they are the result of how human beings perceive, categorize, and interact with the world. The word nice provides perhaps the most extraordinary case study in the history of English. Its journey is a map of changing social values. Starting as "foolish" in the 12th century, it moved to "timid" or "faint-hearted" by the pre-1300s. By the late 14th century, it had shifted to "fussy" or "fastidious," describing someone overly concerned with details. Around 1400, it became "dainty" or "delicate," and by the 1500s, it meant "precise" or "careful," a sense still preserved in phrases like "a nice distinction." It was not until 1769 that it acquired the meaning of "agreeable" or "delightful," and finally, in 1830, it settled on "kind" and "thoughtful." This evolution from "ignorant" to "kind" is a testament to the fluidity of language and the way society redefines virtue over time.

"The sense development has been extraordinary, even for an adj." — Weekley

Sometimes, words shift because the world they describe changes. The word demagogue offers a stark example of how political reality reshapes vocabulary. Originally, it was a neutral term from the Greek dēmagōgós, meaning "leader of the people." It was composed of dēmos (people) and agōgós (leading or guiding). In the ancient world, a demagogue was simply a popular leader, a figure who spoke for the common citizen. Today, however, the word carries a heavy, negative connotation. It describes a politician who panders to emotions, prejudices, and irrational fears to gain power. The shift reflects a deep-seated cynicism in modern political discourse, where the act of leading the people is increasingly viewed with suspicion and associated with manipulation rather than guidance.

Similarly, the word egregious has undergone a complete reversal of moral valence. It comes from the Latin egregius, meaning "illustrious" or "select." Literally, it meant "standing out from the flock" (ex- meaning "out of" and greg- meaning "flock"). In the 16th and 17th centuries, to call something egregious was to call it remarkable in a positive sense, a standout achievement. The mathematician Carl Friedrich Gauss even titled a famous theorem Theorema Egregium, meaning "Remarkable Theorem." But over time, the meaning flipped. Today, egregious means something that is remarkably bad, flagrant, or shocking. We speak of "egregious errors" or "egregious violations," stripping the word of its original brilliance and replacing it with a sense of outrage.

The evolution of gay is perhaps the most complex and culturally significant shift in recent history. In the 13th century, it meant "lighthearted," "joyous," or "carefree." By the 14th century, it described something "bright and showy." It was a word of celebration. However, as early as 1637, it began to acquire connotations of immorality, often referring to sexual promiscuity. A "gay woman" was a prostitute, a "gay man" was a womanizer, and a "gay house" was a brothel. By the late 19th century, in the United States, the term "gay cat" referred to a hobo, specifically a younger one accompanying an older one. By 1935, it had entered prison slang to refer to a homosexual boy. By 1951, clipped to simply gay, it referred to homosexuals.

Historian George Chauncey, in his book Gay New York, suggests that this shift occurred even earlier, in the late 19th century, within a specific "in-crowd" familiar with gay nightlife. The word was reclaimed by the community itself, transforming from a slur or a descriptor of immorality into a neutral, and eventually proud, identifier of identity. Yet, the word still retains traces of its past; in some contexts, it can still be used as a derogatory synonym for "silly," "dumb," or "boring," a linguistic ghost of its earlier meanings. The story of gay is a reminder that words are not just labels; they are battlegrounds for identity, dignity, and social change.

Even the most mundane words carry the weight of history. The word guy is a perfect example of how a specific historical event can ripple through language for centuries. It comes from Guy Fawkes, the alleged leader of the Gunpowder Plot to blow up the English Houses of Parliament on November 5, 1605. The plot failed, and Fawkes was executed. To commemorate the event, the English established "Guy Fawkes Day," where they would parade and burn a ragged manikin of Fawkes, known as a "Guy." Over time, the word guy came to refer to any "person of grotesque appearance." By the late 1800s, particularly in the United States, it shed its negative connotation and became a general term for "any man." Today, it has replaced words like "fellow," "chap," and "bloke," becoming the default term for a man in American English. Under the influence of American popular culture, it is even used for mixed-gender groups, as in "Come on, you guys!" The man who tried to destroy a parliament became the generic name for the common man.

How do these changes happen? Linguists have spent centuries trying to categorize and understand the mechanisms behind semantic change. The study of this phenomenon is a cornerstone of etymology, onomasiology, semasiology, and semantics. While early discussions date back to ancient times, the first major systematic works emerged in the 19th century with scholars like Reisig (1839), Paul (1880), and Darmesteter (1887). Their work laid the foundation for modern linguistics, moving beyond the analysis of single words to the study of entire lexical fields.

One of the most influential figures in this field was Jost Trier, who in 1931 proposed that every semantic change of a word affects all other words in its lexical field. If the meaning of one word shifts, it creates a vacuum or a pressure that forces neighboring words to adjust their meanings to fill the gap. This approach was later refined by Eugenio Coseriu in 1964. Later, Fritz introduced "Generative semantics" in 1974, and more recent works by Warren (1992), Dirk Geeraerts, Traugott (1990), and Blank (1997) have integrated pragmatic and cognitive theories to explain how our brains process and shift meaning.

The most commonly used typologies today are those by Bloomfield (1933) and Blank (1999). Reisig's earlier classification, published posthumously, relied on classical rhetoric and distinguished between several key mechanisms. The first is Synecdoche, a shift between part and whole. If you say "all hands on deck," you are using a part (hands) to represent the whole (sailors). Metonymy involves a shift between cause and effect or between things that are closely associated, such as using "the crown" to refer to the monarchy. Metaphor is the most creative mechanism, where a word is applied to a new concept based on a perceived similarity.

Beyond these rhetorical devices, semantic change often follows patterns of Generalization and Specialization. Generalization, or widening, occurs when a word's meaning expands to include more concepts. For example, the word bird once referred only to young birds, but now it refers to all feathered creatures. Specialization, or narrowing, is the opposite. The word meat originally meant any kind of food, but now it refers specifically to the flesh of animals. These changes are not just about the word itself; they are about how our categorization of the world changes.

Another mechanism is Transfer, where a word shifts its meaning based on a spatial, temporal, or causal link. Metaphor can also lead to a "thickening" of sense, where an abstract concept becomes concrete. Substitution occurs when the object, the knowledge about the object, or the attitude toward the object changes. For instance, artillery originally meant "engines of war used to throw missiles," but now it specifically refers to "mounted guns." The word atom shifted from meaning an "inseparable smallest physical-chemical element" to a "physical-chemical element consisting of electrons, protons, and neutrons." The word scholasticism changed from a "philosophical system of the Middle Ages" to a derogatory term for "servile adherence to the methods and teaching of schools."

Analogy drives change when the shift in one word triggers a shift in an associated word. For example, the adjective fast originally meant "fixed and rapid," while the adverb meant "fixedly and rapidly." The adjective shifted its meaning to align with the adverb, creating the modern sense of "moving quickly." Shortening is another common process, where a longer phrase is clipped, such as periodical paper becoming simply periodical.

Sometimes, the change is intentional. Nomination is the deliberate naming of a referent with a new name, such as calling a brave man a lion. Regular transfer is a subconscious version of this, while Permutation is a non-intentional shift where a word is reinterpreted in a new situation, leading to a new meaning.

The study of semantic change is not merely an academic exercise; it is a window into the human mind. It reveals how we perceive the world, how we value things, and how our societies evolve. When a word like nice shifts from "foolish" to "kind," it reflects a change in what we value in human behavior. When demagogue becomes a term of abuse, it reflects a change in our political consciousness. When gay is reclaimed, it reflects a struggle for identity and dignity.

Every word we speak is a fossil of the past, carrying the sediment of centuries of usage, misinterpretation, and reimagining. We use them without thinking, assuming their meanings are fixed and permanent. But they are not. They are in a constant state of flux, shaped by the people who use them. The word semantic change itself is a testament to this process. It is a term that describes the very process of change, a word that evolves as we learn more about it.

As we look to the future, the process of semantic change will continue. New technologies, new social movements, and new cultural shifts will create new meanings for old words and new words for old concepts. The words we use today will be the fossils of tomorrow, studied by future linguists to understand the values and realities of our time. The journey of awful, awesome, terrible, terrific, nice, gay, and guy is just the beginning. There are countless other words waiting to shift, to expand, to narrow, and to transform.

The next time you use a word, pause for a moment. Consider its history. Think about the journey it has taken to get to your lips. Is it a word of terror that has become a word of praise? Is it a word of ignorance that has become a word of kindness? Is it a name for a traitor that has become a name for a man? The story of the word is the story of us. It is a story of our fears, our hopes, our mistakes, and our triumphs. It is a story that is still being written, one word at a time.

In the end, semantic change reminds us that language is not a set of rules to be followed, but a living conversation to be joined. It is a conversation that has been going on for thousands of years, and it will continue long after we are gone. The words we use today will be the seeds of the language of the future, growing and changing in ways we cannot yet imagine. And that is the beauty of it. That is the power of it. That is the magic of semantic change.
