Franz Kline

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Franz Kline

On May 13, 1962, ten days before his fifty-second birthday, Franz Kline died of rheumatic heart disease in New York City. He left behind a body of work that had irrevocably altered the landscape of American art, yet his death arrived just as he was beginning to dismantle the very style that made him famous. Kline was a man of stark contrasts: the son of a coal miner who killed himself when the boy was seven, he grew up to become the preeminent "black and white" painter of the New York School. His canvases, often spanning six feet or more, slammed against the viewer with the force of a locomotive or a falling timber, yet they were born from a lifetime of meticulous drafting and quiet observation. To understand Kline is to understand the tension between the spontaneous gesture and the calculated plan, between the industrial grit of Wilkes-Barre, Pennsylvania, and the high-stakes intellectual ferment of 1950s Greenwich Village. He was not merely an Abstract Expressionist; he was the architect of a visual language that turned the act of painting into a physical event, where the white of the canvas was as active and aggressive as the black paint itself.

Kline was born on May 23, 1910, in Wilkes-Barre, a small community nestled in the Coal Region of Northeastern Pennsylvania. The geography of his childhood was defined by the earth and the machines that dug it, a landscape of dark skies, towering smokestacks, and the rhythmic clatter of trains. This industrial backdrop would haunt his work for the rest of his life, even as he stripped it of all recognizable detail. The defining trauma of his youth arrived when he was only seven years old. His father, a local figure, committed suicide. The loss was absolute and sudden, leaving a void that Kline would spend decades trying to fill with ink and oil. His mother eventually remarried, sending the young Franz to Lehighton, Pennsylvania, where he graduated from high school. It was here, amidst the coal dust and the quiet desperation of a mining town, that he first found a voice. He served as the cartoonist for the school newspaper, a role that honed his ability to reduce complex scenes into bold, defining lines.

Following graduation, the path to artistry was neither linear nor guaranteed. His mother, seeking a structured environment for a fatherless boy, sent him to Girard College, an academy in Philadelphia. It was a place of discipline, but for Kline, it was a stepping stone toward a different kind of rigor. From 1931 to 1935, he studied at Boston University, immersing himself in the traditional techniques of illustrating and drafting. He was not yet the wild abstractionist of legend; he was a student of form, learning the rules so he could eventually break them. In 1936, he traveled to England, spending a year at the Heatherley School of Fine Art in London. It was in the foggy, historic streets of London that his personal life intersected with his artistic trajectory. There, he met Elizabeth V. Parsons, a British ballet dancer. Their relationship was immediate and profound. Elizabeth returned to the United States with Kline in 1938, a partnership that would anchor him as he navigated the treacherous waters of the New York art world.

Upon his return, Kline did not immediately achieve fame. He worked as a designer for a department store in New York State, a job that required a commercial eye but offered little creative freedom. In 1939, he moved to New York City, taking a position with a scenic designer. These years were a crucible. While his peers were beginning to experiment with the new idioms of Surrealism and abstraction, Kline was still working figuratively. He painted landscapes and cityscapes, executed commissioned portraits, and designed murals. His individual style began to emerge not in the galleries, but in the streets. In 1940, he painted a mural series titled Hot Jazz for the Bleecker Street Tavern in Greenwich Village. This was the first glimpse of the Kline that would become a legend. The series revealed a fascination with breaking down representative forms into quick, rudimentary brushstrokes. The figures were not static; they were in motion, collapsing into lines that suggested energy rather than form.

Many of the subjects he depicted during this transitional period were drawn from his native Pennsylvania. He painted locomotives, stark landscapes, and the large mechanical shapes of the coal-mining community. However, to look at these early works and expect a literal representation of a train or a mine is to misunderstand his intent. The connection was often only apparent because of the titles he gave the pieces, not because the images resembled their subjects. The "train" in his painting was not a train; it was the force of a train. The "landscape" was not a view; it was the weight of the earth. As he moved through the late 1940s, influenced by the seething energy of the contemporary New York art scene, Kline pushed further into abstraction. He began to generalize his figurative subjects, breaking them down into lines and planes that fit together with the structural logic of Cubism, yet with the emotional intensity of Expressionism.

The pivotal moment in Kline's career is shrouded in the mythology of the New York School, specifically the story of the Bell-Opticon projector. It is widely believed that his signature style—the massive, sweeping black strokes on a white ground—derived from a suggestion made by his friend and creative rival, Willem de Kooning. De Kooning's wife, Elaine, provided a romanticized account of the event that has become part of art history's folklore. She claimed that in 1948, a frustrated Kline was advised by de Kooning to project a sketch onto the wall of his studio using a projector. Kline later described the moment with a sense of awe:

"A four-by-five-inch black drawing of a rocking chair...loomed in gigantic black strokes which eradicated any image, the strokes expanding as entities in themselves, unrelated to any entity but that of their own existence."

According to Elaine de Kooning, it was in that instant, seeing a small drawing transformed into a monumental presence, that Kline dedicated himself to large-scale, abstract works. He began to develop a personal form of Abstract Expressionism that was distinct from the drip paintings of Jackson Pollock or the slashing colors of de Kooning. However, the reality was likely more complex and less instantaneous. Even Willem de Kooning, who recalled that Kline delved into abstraction "all of a sudden, he plunged into it," also conceded that the process took considerable time. He noted, "Franz had a vision of something and sometimes it takes quite a while to work it out." The projector story may be a myth, a convenient origin point for a style that was actually the result of years of struggle and evolution.

Over the next two years, Kline's brushstrokes became completely non-representative, fluid, and dynamic. It was also during this period that he began painting almost exclusively in black and white. This monochrome palette was not a limitation; it was a philosophical choice. Kline explained his approach by stating, "I paint the white as well as the black, and the white is just as important." For Kline, the white space was not a background; it was a positive element, a shape that fought with the black for dominance. This use of black and white drew comparisons to the works of de Kooning and Pollock from the 1940s, but Kline's application was unique in its architectural precision. There were also apparent references to Japanese calligraphy in his work, a connection that sparked debate. Through his exchange with the Japanese avant-garde calligraphy group Bokujinkai and its leader Morita Shiryu, Kline explored the idea of the brushstroke as a spiritual gesture. Yet, Kline later denied this direct connection, insisting that his work sprang from his own internal logic and the industrial rhythms of his youth.

The world finally took notice in October 1950. Kline's first one-man show took place between October 16 and November 4 at New York's Charles Egan Gallery. The exhibition consisted of eleven abstract paintings, and it was a revelation. Color was a rare element in these works, appearing only as brown underpainting near the bottom of Nijinsky or as fleeting hints of green in Leda. The paintings displayed a variety of compositions and moods, but they all shared one defining trait: Kline's signature style of black on white. In the early 1950s, his work appeared very much inspired by the French painter Pierre Soulages, who had exhibited in Betty Parsons' gallery in New York in 1949. However, Kline's work possessed a visceral, American energy that Soulages lacked. In the late 1930s, while in London, Kline had called himself a "black and white man," but it was not until the Egan Gallery show that the accuracy of this self-description became clear to the art world.

Because of his impact and his concrete style, Kline was dubbed the "black and white artist." The label stuck, becoming a brand that he would occasionally feel restricted by. The Egan show was a pivotal event in Kline's career because it marked the virtually simultaneous beginning and end of his major invention as an abstract artist. At the age of forty, Kline had secured a personal style that he had already mastered. The danger for any artist is the moment of perfection: when you have found your voice, how do you speak again? There were no real ways for him to further his investigation within the constraints of his black-and-white idiom; he had the potential only to replicate the style he had already mastered. To move on, there was only one logical direction for Kline to go: back to color. This was the direction he was headed at the time of his premature death.

In the late 1950s, Kline began to experiment with a more complex chiaroscuro, moving away from the strict monochromatic palette that had defined him. Paintings like Requiem (1958) showed a shift in tone, introducing a sense of depth and shadow that the stark black-and-white works had eschewed. Then, in 1958, he reintroduced the use of color in his work through colorful accents in his black-and-white paintings. This exploration was still in development when Kline died in 1962. He was on the verge of a new chapter, a synthesis of his structural rigor and a renewed palette, but the heart failure that claimed him cut the story short. In 2022, a digital catalogue raisonné on "Franz Kline Paintings, 1950–1962" was published by the Hauser & Wirth Institute using the Navigating.art platform, offering a final, comprehensive look at the trajectory of his late work.

Kline is recognized as one of the most important yet problematic artists of the Abstract Expressionist movement in New York. His style is difficult for critics to interpret in relation to his contemporaries because it defies the easy categorization of "action painting." While his paintings have a spontaneous and dramatic impact, Kline often closely referred to his compositional drawings. He carefully rendered many of his most complex pictures from extensive studies, commonly created on refuse telephone book pages. Unlike those of his fellow Abstract Expressionists, Kline's works were only meant to look like they were done in a moment of impulse. They were the result of a slow, agonizing process of refinement. The "spontaneity" was an illusion, a carefully constructed effect designed to convey the energy of the moment while maintaining the structural integrity of the composition.

This duality is perhaps the most human aspect of Kline's story. He was a man who carried the trauma of his father's suicide and the grit of the Pennsylvania coal mines into the sterile, intellectual world of the New York gallery. His black strokes were not just paint; they were the weight of memory, the force of machinery, and the silence of loss. His white spaces were not just empty canvas; they were the light of a new day, the void left by absence, and the possibility of something new. When he stood before a canvas, he was not just a painter; he was a survivor, using the brush to rebuild a world that had been broken for him as a child. The "black and white" label, while accurate in a technical sense, fails to capture the emotional complexity of his work. It was a monochrome of the soul, a dialogue between the darkness of his past and the light of his present.

The legacy of Franz Kline extends far beyond the canvas. He taught at a number of institutions, including Black Mountain College in North Carolina and the Pratt Institute in Brooklyn, passing on his rigorous approach to a new generation of artists. He spent his summers from 1956 to 1962 painting in Provincetown, Massachusetts, a place that offered him a respite from the city but not from the work. In Provincetown, the light was different, the rhythms of life were slower, but the drive to create remained constant. It was there, and in his New York studio, that he continued to push the boundaries of his art until the very end.

His death in 1962 was a shock to the art world. He was too young, too vital, and too full of potential. The "black and white artist" was only beginning to find his color. The fact that he died just as he was breaking free from his own signature style adds a layer of poignancy to his legacy. It suggests that Kline was always in motion, always seeking the next horizon, never content to rest on his laurels. His work remains a testament to the power of abstraction to convey the deepest human experiences. In a world that often demands clear answers and distinct categories, Kline's paintings offer a space of ambiguity and tension. They ask the viewer to participate, to fill in the white spaces with their own memories and emotions, to feel the weight of the black strokes and the lightness of the white.

The story of Franz Kline is not just a story of art; it is a story of resilience. It is the story of a boy who lost his father and found his voice in the lines of a newspaper cartoon. It is the story of a man who moved from the coal mines of Pennsylvania to the galleries of New York, carrying the weight of his history in every brushstroke. It is the story of an artist who mastered a style and then immediately began to dismantle it, driven by an insatiable need to explore the unknown. Kline's work reminds us that art is not about the destination; it is about the journey. It is about the struggle to find meaning in the chaos, to create order out of the void, and to express the inexpressible through the simple act of painting.

In the end, Kline's paintings are deceptively subtle. They may appear simple at first glance, a series of black lines on a white field. But look closer, and you will see the complexity, the tension, the history. You will see the locomotive, the mine, the suicide, the dancer, the projector, the telephone book, the color that was just beginning to emerge. You will see the life of Franz Kline, distilled into a language of black and white, a language that speaks to the very core of the human experience. His work is a reminder that even in the face of loss and tragedy, there is the possibility of creation, of beauty, and of meaning. And that is why, more than sixty years after his death, Franz Kline remains one of the most important artists of the twentieth century.

The impact of Kline's work continues to resonate in the contemporary art world. His influence can be seen in the work of countless artists who have followed in his footsteps, exploring the power of the gesture, the weight of the mark, and the relationship between figure and ground. But no one has quite replicated the unique combination of industrial grit and spiritual depth that Kline brought to his canvas. He was a singular figure, a man who forged a new path in the landscape of American art. His story is a testament to the power of the human spirit to overcome adversity, to create something beautiful out of the darkness, and to leave a legacy that endures long after the artist is gone.

As we look back on his life and work, we are reminded of the importance of perseverance, of the courage to take risks, and of the value of staying true to one's vision. Kline did not follow the crowd; he forged his own way, guided by his own internal compass. He did not fear the void; he embraced it, filling it with his own unique vision. And in doing so, he created a body of work that continues to inspire and challenge us today. The black and white of his paintings are not just colors; they are a state of being, a way of seeing the world, a testament to the enduring power of art to transform our lives.

The story of Franz Kline is a story of a man who found his voice in the silence, who found his color in the black and white, and who found his life in the art. It is a story that is as relevant today as it was in 1962, a story that speaks to the heart of what it means to be human. And it is a story that will continue to be told, as long as there are those who are willing to look at a canvas and see not just paint, but a life.
