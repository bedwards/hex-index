China–Russia relations

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: China–Russia relations

The Partnership That Wasn't Supposed to Happen

In the winter of 1969, Soviet and Chinese soldiers killed each other along the frozen Ussuri River. Nuclear war between the two communist giants seemed plausible. Moscow reportedly sounded out Washington about a preemptive strike against Chinese nuclear facilities.

Fast forward to February 2022. Chinese President Xi Jinping and Russian President Vladimir Putin released a joint statement declaring their relationship had "no limits." Within days, Russian tanks rolled into Ukraine.

How did two nations that nearly annihilated each other become, in their own carefully chosen words, "not allies, but better than allies"? The answer reveals how geopolitics can override ideology, history, and even common sense—and why this partnership may be the most consequential relationship shaping the twenty-first century.

Four Centuries of Collision

The story begins in the 1600s, when Russian fur traders and Cossack adventurers pushed eastward across Siberia and bumped into the northern edge of the Qing dynasty. The Qing emperors, ruling China at its greatest territorial extent, were not amused. They sent armies to drive these unwelcome visitors out of Manchuria.

The result was the Treaty of Nerchinsk in 1689—the first formal agreement between Russia and China, and remarkably, the only treaty the Qing ever signed with a European power as equals. The Chinese got their way. Russia retreated.

But two centuries later, the balance had shifted dramatically. A weakened Qing dynasty, reeling from the Opium Wars with Britain and internal rebellions, could not resist when Russia swooped in to claim vast territories in what is now the Russian Far East. Between 1858 and 1860, China lost lands the size of France, including the area where Vladivostok—whose name means "Ruler of the East"—would rise as Russia's Pacific stronghold.

The Chinese have not forgotten. When you hear Chinese officials speak of "century of humiliation" at Western hands, the Russian seizure of Manchurian territory is part of that memory, even if today's leaders diplomatically avoid mentioning it.

Comrades, Then Enemies

After Mao Zedong's Communist revolution triumphed in 1949, the new People's Republic of China turned to the Soviet Union as its natural ally. Both nations shared Marxist-Leninist ideology. Both opposed the capitalist West. Soviet engineers helped build Chinese factories. Soviet advisors trained Chinese military officers.

The honeymoon lasted barely a decade.

The Sino-Soviet split, which became public around 1961, was one of the Cold War's most consequential developments. On the surface, it appeared to be about ideology—Mao accused Soviet leader Nikita Khrushchev of betraying revolutionary principles by seeking peaceful coexistence with the West. Khrushchev thought Mao was a dangerous hothead willing to risk nuclear war.

But beneath the ideological rhetoric lay more prosaic concerns: national pride, competing claims to leadership of the global communist movement, and genuine strategic disagreements. The Soviets refused to help China develop nuclear weapons. China resented being treated as a junior partner.

By 1969, Soviet and Chinese troops were shooting at each other over Zhenbao Island—a frozen sandbar in the Ussuri River that neither country particularly needed but both claimed. Hundreds died. The incident terrified both sides into realizing how close they had come to full-scale war.

This fear had an unexpected beneficiary: the United States. President Richard Nixon and his national security advisor Henry Kissinger recognized an opportunity. If China and the Soviet Union were enemies, perhaps China could be pried away from the communist bloc entirely. Nixon's 1972 visit to Beijing—unthinkable just years earlier—exploited the Sino-Soviet split to America's advantage. For the rest of the Cold War, China tacitly aligned with the West against Soviet power.

The Awkward Thaw

When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, the Chinese Communist Party watched with horror. Here was their worst nightmare made real: a ruling communist party overthrown, a socialist state dissolved, a superpower humiliated. Chinese leaders studied the Soviet collapse obsessively, determined to avoid the same fate.

They also found themselves suddenly dealing with a very different Russia.

Boris Yeltsin, Russia's first post-Soviet president, initially looked westward. He wanted Russia to join the club of Western democracies, to integrate with European and American institutions. China was an afterthought—and when Russian officials did pay attention, they sometimes proved tone-deaf. Foreign Minister Andrei Kozyrev publicly criticized China's human rights record during a visit to Beijing. Russia even flirted with Taiwan, exploring unofficial ties with the island that China considers a breakaway province.

The Chinese leadership was privately furious. A leaked Communist Party Politburo meeting from January 1992 captured their attitude with brutal frankness: even if Yeltsin was "very reactionary," they could "internally curse him and pray for his downfall" while maintaining normal diplomatic relations. Pragmatism trumped pride.

Within months, Yeltsin started reconsidering. His romance with the West was yielding less than promised—economic advice that proved disastrous, a condescending attitude from Western leaders, and the sting of no longer being treated as a superpower. When China invited him to visit in late 1992, he accepted.

The visit produced twenty-five cooperation agreements and a joint declaration calling the two countries "friendly" and "good-neighborly." Former paramount leader Deng Xiaoping, architect of China's economic reforms, pointedly refused to meet with Yeltsin—a snub that signaled lingering distrust. But the foundation for a new relationship had been laid.

The Multi-Polar Dream

Throughout the 1990s, China and Russia discovered they shared a common frustration: the unipolar world dominated by the United States.

The Cold War's end had left America as the sole superpower. NATO—the North Atlantic Treaty Organization, the Western military alliance originally created to contain the Soviet Union—was expanding eastward toward Russia's borders. American military interventions in places like Iraq and the Balkans demonstrated a willingness to use force without seeking Russian or Chinese approval.

Neither Beijing nor Moscow could challenge American dominance alone. Together, they might at least limit it.

This convergence of interests drove increasingly warm rhetoric. In 1994, the relationship was upgraded from "friendly countries" to a "constructive partnership." By 1996, it had become a "strategic partnership of equality and mutual trust for the 21st century"—the kind of elaborate diplomatic formulation that signals serious intent behind the bureaucratic language.

In 1997, the two countries issued something remarkable: a joint foreign policy statement titled "Declaration on the Trend to a Multi-polar World and the Establishment of a New International Order." The clunky title captured a genuine shared vision. Both nations wanted a world where multiple great powers balanced each other, where no single country could impose its will, where the United Nations Security Council—where both held vetoes—remained the legitimate arbiter of international disputes.

When NATO bombed Yugoslavia in 1999 to stop Serbian ethnic cleansing in Kosovo, without UN authorization, China and Russia were unified in outrage. American B-2 bombers accidentally struck the Chinese embassy in Belgrade, killing three journalists. Whether truly accidental or not—Chinese public opinion assumed deliberate targeting—the incident inflamed anti-American sentiment and pushed Beijing closer to Moscow.

Putin Takes the Wheel

Vladimir Putin became Russia's president in 2000. A former intelligence officer in the KGB—the Soviet security agency—Putin had witnessed the Soviet collapse firsthand while stationed in East Germany. He famously called the Soviet Union's dissolution "the greatest geopolitical catastrophe of the twentieth century."

Unlike Yeltsin, Putin harbored no illusions about Russia joining the West. He wanted respect, not integration. And he recognized that a close relationship with China could amplify Russia's diminished influence.

In 2001, the two countries took a significant step: creating the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation, or SCO. This new body brought together China, Russia, and four Central Asian states—Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, and Uzbekistan. Officially focused on fighting terrorism and separatism, the SCO also served as a vehicle for Chinese and Russian influence in a region both considered their backyard, and from which both wanted to exclude American presence.

A month after the SCO's founding, China and Russia signed the Treaty of Good-Neighborliness and Friendly Cooperation. This twenty-year agreement formalized their strategic partnership, committing both sides to consultation on major international issues and expanded economic cooperation. The treaty was renewed in 2021 for another five years.

The Hu-Putin Era

When Hu Jintao became China's leader in 2002, he and Putin developed an effective working relationship. The two met five or six times annually—an extraordinary frequency for heads of state. Their prime ministers met so regularly that Chinese Premier Wen Jiabao joked in 2007 that they no longer bothered with prepared speeches.

The relationship deepened but remained carefully calibrated. China supported Russia during the Second Chechen War, when Moscow brutally suppressed an Islamist insurgency in the restive republic. Russia reciprocated by backing China on Taiwan, Tibet, and Xinjiang—the issues Beijing considers non-negotiable matters of sovereignty and territorial integrity.

Yet limits existed. During Russia's 2008 war with Georgia—when Russian forces invaded the small Caucasus nation after a Georgian military operation against a breakaway region—China grew uncomfortable. Beijing abstained from supporting Moscow, citing concerns about sovereignty and territorial integrity. Within the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation, China used its influence to prevent the bloc from endorsing Russian actions.

The message was subtle but clear: China valued the partnership with Russia, but not at any price. Principles that China applied to its own territorial claims—Tibet, Taiwan, Xinjiang—could not simply be discarded when Russia violated another country's borders.

Enter Xi Jinping

Xi Jinping became China's paramount leader in late 2012. Within months, he made a state visit to Moscow, signaling that the Russia relationship would be a priority. Putin gave Xi unprecedented access, including a tour of the Operational Command Headquarters of the Russian Armed Forces—the first time any foreign leader had entered the building.

The personal chemistry between Xi and Putin became a defining feature of the relationship. Between 2013 and 2025, the two leaders met more than forty times. Both are strongmen who have concentrated power, eliminated term limits, and crushed domestic opposition. Both believe their nations were humiliated in recent centuries and deserve restoration to greatness. Both view American-led liberal internationalism as a threat to their rule.

Yet in 2013, Russia still had reservations about drawing too close to China. The power imbalance was becoming awkward. China's economy was growing rapidly; Russia's depended heavily on oil and gas exports. China's population was ten times Russia's. In the long run, might China become a threat rather than a partner?

Events in 2014 resolved Russia's hesitation—but not in a way Moscow would have chosen.

Crimea Changes Everything

When Russia annexed Crimea from Ukraine in 2014, following a contested referendum held under military occupation, the West responded with economic sanctions unprecedented in their scope. The United States and European Union targeted Russian banks, energy companies, and individuals close to Putin. Russia's access to Western capital markets was restricted. Technology transfers were blocked.

Russia desperately needed an alternative. China offered one.

The two countries had been negotiating a natural gas deal for years, but price disagreements had stalled progress. With sanctions biting and Western energy markets closing, Russia suddenly had far less leverage. In May 2014, Putin flew to Shanghai and signed a thirty-year gas supply agreement worth four hundred billion dollars. A new pipeline, eventually named Power of Siberia, would carry Russian gas directly to China.

China drove a hard bargain. The exact pricing was never disclosed, but analysts concluded Beijing got a significant discount compared to what Russia charged European customers. Russia needed the deal more than China did—and China made sure the terms reflected that reality.

The 2014 crisis established a pattern that would intensify in subsequent years. International pressure on Russia pushed Moscow toward Beijing. Each new round of Western sanctions reduced Russia's options and increased its dependence on Chinese markets, Chinese investment, and Chinese diplomatic support.

China benefited enormously. It secured reliable energy supplies at favorable prices. It gained a partner willing to challenge American dominance in international institutions. And it watched as Russia, once at least an equal in the partnership, became increasingly the junior partner—though Beijing was too polite to say so publicly.

Not Quite Allies

What exactly is the China-Russia relationship? This question has occupied analysts, diplomats, and scholars for years.

The two countries insist they are not allies. They have no mutual defense treaty requiring one to come to the other's aid if attacked. They do not share military bases. Their armed forces do not operate under unified command.

Yet they describe themselves as "better than allies"—a formulation that sounds like diplomatic wordplay but actually captures something important. Traditional alliances often exist despite underlying mistrust, bound by formal obligations. China and Russia claim their partnership rests on genuinely shared interests and worldviews, making formal alliance structures unnecessary.

Critics argue this framing is convenient for both sides. An alliance would require each to potentially fight for the other—a commitment neither wants to make. Russia does not want to be dragged into a Chinese conflict over Taiwan. China does not want to formally support Russian aggression in Ukraine. By avoiding alliance language, both retain flexibility while still coordinating closely on most issues that matter to them.

The practical cooperation is extensive. Russian and Chinese naval vessels conduct joint exercises. Russian fighter jets patrol alongside Chinese aircraft in the Asia-Pacific. Intelligence is shared. Weapons technology flows from Russia to China—though increasingly, as Chinese capabilities advance, the flow may reverse.

In international forums, the two countries vote together with remarkable consistency. Both wield vetoes on the UN Security Council, and they use them to block Western-backed resolutions—on Syria, on human rights, on almost any issue where the United States takes the opposite position.

February 2022

On February 4, 2022, Xi Jinping flew to Beijing for the opening ceremony of the Winter Olympics. Vladimir Putin was there to meet him. The timing was notable: American intelligence agencies had been warning for weeks that Russia was massing troops on Ukraine's border and might invade.

The joint statement Xi and Putin released that day was remarkable in its ambition. They declared their partnership "superior to the political and military alliances of the Cold War era." They announced friendship with "no limits" and cooperation with "no forbidden areas."

Russia explicitly backed China's position on Taiwan, calling the island "an inalienable part of China" and opposing any movement toward Taiwan independence. Both countries condemned NATO expansion. Both criticized American plans for missile defense systems. Both complained about "certain states" seeking "global hegemony"—diplomatic code for the United States.

Twenty days later, Russian forces invaded Ukraine from multiple directions.

The Ukraine Test

Russia's war in Ukraine put the China-Russia partnership under its greatest stress.

China's official position was careful ambiguity. Beijing said it respected Ukraine's sovereignty—a principle China emphasizes regarding its own territorial claims. But China also said Russia's security concerns about NATO expansion deserved to be addressed. China abstained from UN votes condemning the invasion rather than joining Russia in voting against them. China refused to impose sanctions on Russia and criticized Western sanctions as illegitimate.

What China did not do was equally significant. Beijing did not recognize Russia's annexation of four Ukrainian provinces in September 2022. Chinese banks and companies remained cautious about transactions that might trigger American secondary sanctions—penalties that would cut them off from the far more valuable American financial system. China did not provide Russia with the weapons Moscow desperately wanted.

The partnership remained intact, but its terms had shifted further. Russia was now internationally isolated, its economy under siege, its military bleeding in Ukraine. China had become Russia's economic lifeline—buying Russian oil and gas at discounted prices, providing goods that Russia could no longer obtain from Europe.

Trade between the two countries surged. By 2024, annual bilateral trade reached two hundred forty-five billion dollars, making China by far Russia's largest trading partner. Russia was selling energy to survive. China was buying energy at bargain prices.

The power dynamic was now unmistakable. Russia needed China far more than China needed Russia.

The Limits of "No Limits"

For all the rhetoric about unlimited partnership, the China-Russia relationship has real constraints.

China has not recognized Russian territorial acquisitions in Ukraine, just as China did not recognize Russia's 2014 annexation of Crimea. This is not squeamishness about aggression—China's own territorial claims rest on principles that would be undermined by endorsing border changes through force. If Russia can simply annex Ukrainian territory, what stops other countries from questioning China's claims to Taiwan, or the South China Sea, or Tibet?

Russia, for its part, has carefully avoided taking China's side in its maritime disputes with Southeast Asian nations. When asked about Chinese claims in the South China Sea, Russian officials criticize interference by "non-regional powers"—meaning the United States—but decline to endorse Chinese positions explicitly. Russia has its own interests in Southeast Asia and does not want to alienate countries there.

The COVID-19 pandemic revealed other friction points. In late 2020, China sharply reduced imports of Russian seafood, citing coronavirus contamination concerns that Russian officials considered pretextual. Border restrictions caused logistical chaos, with Russian truck drivers staging protests.

And there remains the fundamental question of what happens if one partner faces a military conflict with the United States. Would China actually fight for Russia in Ukraine? Would Russia send soldiers to defend China in a Taiwan scenario? Neither side has made such commitments, and neither shows much interest in doing so.

The Border That Nearly Divided Them

One relationship success story deserves mention: the border.

The four-thousand-kilometer frontier between China and Russia—the world's sixth-longest international boundary—was once a source of deadly conflict. The 1969 clashes were just the most dramatic example of disputes that had festered for decades.

Starting in the 1990s, both sides worked methodically to resolve these issues. Agreements in 1994 and 1997 demarcated most of the boundary. The remaining disputes involved islands in the Amur and Ussuri rivers—including the very Ussuri islands where soldiers had died in 1969.

In 2004, Russia and China reached a comprehensive settlement. Russia transferred parts of several islands to China, including territory that had been disputed since Japan's invasion of Manchuria in 1931. Both countries' legislatures ratified the agreement. The transfer was presented as a gesture of reconciliation and partnership.

Today, the China-Russia border is quiet. Both sides have reduced military deployments. Trade flows across crossing points that once bristled with fortifications. It is a genuine achievement—proof that determined diplomacy can resolve even the most contentious territorial disputes.

What Comes Next

The China-Russia partnership is now the most significant geopolitical alignment challenging the American-led international order. Both nations oppose what they see as Western hegemony. Both want a multipolar world where great powers like themselves have recognized spheres of influence. Both are willing to support each other in confrontations with the United States—up to a point.

Yet the relationship is profoundly asymmetric. China's economy is roughly ten times larger than Russia's. China's population is ten times larger. China is a rising power; Russia, by most measures, is a declining one. The war in Ukraine has accelerated Russia's slide into junior partner status.

This imbalance creates its own risks. Russia may feel it has nothing to lose—a dangerous condition in a nuclear-armed state. China may find that its increasingly desperate partner drags it into conflicts Beijing would prefer to avoid. Both may discover that marriages of convenience, like "no limits" partnerships, have limits after all.

For now, though, the relationship serves both countries' interests. Russia gets an economic lifeline and diplomatic cover. China gets cheap energy, strategic coordination against America, and access to Russian weapons technology. Both get a partner in their campaign to reshape global institutions away from Western dominance.

Four centuries after Qing armies drove Russian settlers from Manchuria, and five decades after Soviet and Chinese soldiers killed each other on a frozen river, the two nations have found common ground. Their partnership was not inevitable. It was driven by shared grievances, complementary interests, and the pressure of American power. Whether it endures will depend on whether those conditions persist—and whether two proud nations can sustain a relationship where one is clearly the senior partner.

History suggests that such arrangements rarely remain stable forever. But for now, the partnership that wasn't supposed to happen has become one of the defining features of our era.
