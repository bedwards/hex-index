State-sponsored terrorism

15 min read

Based on Wikipedia: State-sponsored terrorism

In the shadow of the Cold War, a chilling doctrine emerged from the halls of the KGB, articulated by agent Alexander Sakharovsky with a clarity that still sends shivers through diplomatic corridors: "In today's world, when nuclear arms have made military force obsolete, terrorism should become our main weapon." This was not a metaphorical flourish but a strategic pivot, a realization that in a world teetering on the brink of mutual assured destruction, the most effective way to bleed an adversary was not through the clashing of massive armies, but through the covert, deniable, and terrifying violence of non-state actors. This is the essence of state-sponsored terrorism: the deliberate, active support of national governments to violent non-state actors, a practice that transforms terrorism from a chaotic act of desperation into a precise instrument of foreign policy.

The distinction is critical, yet often blurred in the heat of geopolitical rhetoric. State-sponsored terrorism is not state terrorism. The latter is when a government itself turns its machinery of violence against its own citizens or foreign populations directly. The former is a game of proxies, where the state pulls the strings while the puppet strikes the blow. States sponsor these groups through a myriad of channels: funding their operations, supplying the weapons that make them lethal, providing training camps where violence is honed into a craft, offering intelligence that turns blind ambushes into surgical strikes, and, perhaps most crucially, providing sanctuary within their borders where these groups can plot, rest, and regroup under the protection of national sovereignty.

Yet, identifying these acts is rarely a matter of simple fact-finding. The word "terrorism" itself is a battlefield, a pejorative label so laden with political weight that its application is almost always a subject of fierce dispute. There are at least 250 distinct definitions of "terrorism" floating through academic literature, government white papers, and intergovernmental agreements. Some definitions are broad enough to include state actions; others are narrowly tailored to exclude them. This ambiguity is not an accident; it is a feature of the system. It allows nations to condemn the actions of their rivals while shielding their own proxy networks from the same scrutiny. As scholars like Alex P. Schmid, former Officer-in-Charge of Terrorism Prevention at the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime, and others such as Daniel Byman and Richard Chasdi have pointed out, the definitions used by major powers, particularly the United States, are often criticized for being politicized, analytically unclear, and inherently self-serving.

The United States, a nation that has repeatedly engaged in the sponsorship of terrorism as a feature of its own foreign policy, maintains its own list of state sponsors of terrorism through the State Department. This list is a political instrument as much as a legal one. The Gilmore Commission of the U.S. Congress attempted to cut through the fog with a definition that focused on intent and mechanism: the active involvement of a foreign government in training, arming, and providing logistical and intelligence assistance, as well as sanctuary, to an otherwise autonomous terrorist group for the purpose of carrying out violent acts on behalf of that government against its enemies. This definition captures the nuance of the relationship—it is not merely about the existence of a terrorist group, but about the active, functional partnership between a state and a non-state actor.

The rise of this phenomenon in the mid-20th century was not a random occurrence; it was a logical response to the changing nature of warfare. The post-World War II era saw the cost of traditional conflict skyrocket and the risk of nuclear annihilation become a tangible reality. In this new landscape, using terrorist organizations as proxies became an attractive option for states seeking to project power without triggering a global conflagration. It allowed nations to wage war in the shadows, to destabilize regimes, and to strike at enemies without the political fallout of a formal declaration of war.

For decades, this strategy defined international conflict. During the 1970s and 1980s, state sponsorship of terrorism was a frequent, almost ubiquitous feature of the global order. From the Middle East to Latin America, from Africa to Asia, governments were arming and directing groups that operated with a terrifying freedom. The pattern was one of escalation, of violence becoming increasingly sophisticated and deadly as state resources flowed into these non-state veins.

But the landscape has shifted. From the height of the Cold War through to the 2010s, there was a steady, observable pattern of decline in the prevalence and magnitude of state support. The end of the Soviet Union removed a primary patron of global revolutionary violence. The rise of global jihadism, with its decentralized, transnational networks, seemed to some scholars to render the traditional model of state sponsorship less significant. Why would a state need to sponsor a group when a religious ideology could recruit and fund its own army? Yet, this view is contested. Daniel Byman, a leading scholar on the subject, argues that the importance of state sponsorship has actually increased in the modern era. The reality on the ground suggests he is right. Organizations like Hamas, Hezbollah, and Palestinian Islamic Jihad remain heavily dependent on state support. Without the funds, weapons, materials, and secure areas provided by state patrons, these groups would struggle to plan and conduct the complex operations that define their campaigns. The U.S. Counter-Terrorism Coordinator's Office has explicitly noted this dependency, highlighting that state support is not just a luxury but a necessity for these organizations' survival and operational capacity.

The legal framework governing this shadow war is woefully inadequate. The Max Planck Encyclopedia of Public International Law notes a gaping hole in international justice: there is currently no mechanism to prosecute terrorist leaders who "instruct, support or succour" terrorism. The law is designed to catch the killer, not the banker or the strategist who sent the killer. The Lockerbie trial, which concluded with the conviction of a Libyan intelligence officer for the 1988 bombing of Pan Am Flight 103, left many commentators with lingering doubts about the legitimacy of the verdict and the full extent of Libya's involvement. The domestic trial proved insufficient to identify those who had given the instructions, revealing the limits of legal proceedings in untangling the web of state sponsorship.

Nowhere is this web more tangled, or the consequences more deadly, than in the region surrounding Pakistan and Afghanistan. The United States and Pakistan have long accused Afghanistan's KHAD, the intelligence agency of the Soviet-backed regime in Kabul, of being responsible for a staggering number of terrorist attacks on Pakistani soil in the 1980s and early 1990s. A report by the U.S. Department of Defense estimated that approximately 90% of the 777 acts of international terrorism committed worldwide in 1987 took place in Pakistan. This was not a coincidence of geography; it was a campaign.

By 1988, KHAD and KGB agents had penetrated deep inside Pakistan, carrying out attacks on mujahideen sanctuaries and guerrary bases with a precision that suggested inside knowledge. There was strong circumstantial evidence implicating the Moscow-Kabul axis in the August 1988 assassination of Pakistani President Zia ul-Haq, a move perceived by the Soviets as a necessary step to disrupt the Geneva process that threatened their grip on Afghanistan. The accusations did not stop there. KHAD was also suspected in the assassination of Palestinian jihadist Abdullah Yusuf Azzam and his son in 1989, a blow to the global jihadist movement that many believed was orchestrated by Afghan security agencies.

The violence was indiscriminate and devastating. Afghanistan's KHAD was one of four secret service agencies accused of perpetrating terrorist bombings in multiple Pakistani cities, including Islamabad, Lahore, Karachi, and Rawalpindi, during the early 1980s, resulting in hundreds of civilian casualties. By the late 1980s, the U.S. State Department was explicitly blaming the WAD (later KHAD) for these bombings. The scope of the operations was vast. Between the late 1970s and the early 1990s, Afghan security agencies supported the terrorist organization Al-Zulfiqar, a group that hijacked a Pakistan International Airlines plane from Karachi to Kabul in 1981. Notable attacks included the Karachi car bombing and an attempted car bombing on the U.S. Consulate in Peshawar, which ended up killing over 30 people in 1987. KHAD was also accused of being behind the Hathora Murders, a two-year reign of terror in Karachi during the mid-1980s that left the city in a state of fear.

Decades later, the ghosts of that era still haunt the region. On June 24, 2017, Pakistani army chief Qamar Javed Bajwa chaired a high-level meeting in Rawalpindi, calling on Afghanistan to "do more" in the fight against terrorism. According to the Inter-Services Public Relations (ISPR), attacks in Quetta and Parachinar were linked to terrorist sanctuaries in Afghanistan which enjoyed the "patronage of Afghanistan's National Directorate of Security (NDS)." The cycle of accusation and retaliation, of state sponsorship and proxy violence, had simply taken a new form, but the underlying dynamic remained unchanged.

The map of state sponsorship is not limited to the Middle East and South Asia. In the complex geopolitics of Asia, accusations fly with equal ferocity. India has accused China of supporting the Naxalites in the Naxalite–Maoist insurgency, a long-standing internal conflict that has plagued the country for decades. In 2011, Indian police accused the Chinese government of providing sanctuary to the movement's leaders, while simultaneously accusing the Pakistani ISI of providing financial support. India has also reported Chinese support for militant groups in its Northeast states of Manipur, Nagaland, and Mizoram, regions that have seen decades of separatist violence.

The most visible manifestation of this dynamic, however, has been the diplomatic stalemate over Masood Azhar, the founder and leader of the Pakistan-based organization Jaish-e-Mohammed. The Chinese government has repeatedly blocked the UN Security Council Sanctions Committee from listing Azhar as a terrorist. Starting in 2009, there have been four attempts to put him on the UN Security Council's counter-terrorism sanctions list. All were vetoed by China, citing a "lack of evidence." In 2016, China blocked India's appeal to the United Nations to label Azhar as a terrorist. China also blocked a U.S. move to get Azhar banned by the UN in February 2017. The most recent attempt was on March 13, 2019. It was not until May 2019, under immense international pressure and a shifting geopolitical landscape, that China finally backed the listing of Masood Azhar as a global terrorist. This delay was not merely bureaucratic; it was a strategic calculation, a refusal to condemn a proxy until the political cost of doing so became too high.

The tentacles of state sponsorship extend even further. In mid-2020, Myanmar accused China of arming the Arakan Army, a group that was legally considered a terrorist organization by the Myanmar government from 2019 to 2021. The allegations were specific and alarming: China allegedly provided the Arakan Army with assault rifles, machine guns, and FN-6 Chinese Manpads, portable air-defense systems capable of shooting down helicopters, drones, and combat aircraft. This is not the shadowy support of the Cold War; this is the provision of cutting-edge military technology to a non-state actor in a active conflict zone, a direct escalation of the proxy warfare model.

The persistence of state-sponsored terrorism in the post-9/11 era challenges the notion that the world has moved beyond such tactics. While some scholars argue that the rise of global jihadism has diminished the role of state sponsors, the evidence suggests a more complex reality. The nature of the support may have evolved, becoming more subtle or more sophisticated, but the fundamental mechanism remains. States continue to use violent non-state actors as tools of their foreign policy, leveraging the ambiguity of international law and the difficulty of attribution to achieve their goals without the risks of direct confrontation.

The human cost of this strategy is measured in the thousands of lives lost in bombings, assassinations, and insurgencies. From the streets of Karachi to the hills of Nagaland, from the skies over the Atlantic to the borders of Myanmar, the legacy of state-sponsored terrorism is a world where violence is not just a crime, but a policy. It is a world where the line between the state and the terrorist is deliberately blurred, where the government of one nation can wage war on another through the hands of a proxy, and where the international community often lacks the tools, or the will, to hold the true architects of violence accountable.

As we look toward the future, the question is not whether state-sponsored terrorism will disappear, but how it will adapt. The rise of new technologies, the fragmentation of the global order, and the emergence of new geopolitical rivals suggest that the toolkit of state sponsorship is far from exhausted. The challenge for the international community is to develop a coherent legal and diplomatic framework that can pierce the veil of deniability and bring the sponsors of terror to justice. Until then, the shadow of Sakharovsky's doctrine will continue to loom, a reminder that in the age of nuclear weapons, the most dangerous weapon may not be the bomb, but the hand that points the finger of a terrorist at the enemy.

The complexity of these relationships cannot be overstated. It is a web of alliances, betrayals, and strategic calculations that defies simple categorization. To understand the current state of global security, one must look beyond the headlines of individual attacks and examine the underlying currents of state sponsorship. It is in the funding of a militia, the training of a sniper, the provision of a sanctuary, that the true nature of modern conflict is revealed. And it is in these shadows that the next great geopolitical battles will likely be fought.

The lesson of history is clear: when states choose to outsource their violence, they lose control of the narrative and the consequences. The violence they unleash often turns on them, creating a cycle of retaliation that is difficult to break. The assassinations, the bombings, the insurgencies—these are not isolated events. They are the symptoms of a deeper disease, a global system where the use of terror is legitimized as a tool of statecraft. And until that system is fundamentally reformed, the threat of state-sponsored terrorism will remain one of the most pressing issues of international security.

The path forward requires a rethinking of the very definition of terrorism and the mechanisms of accountability. It requires a commitment to transparency and a willingness to confront the uncomfortable truths about the alliances that underpin the modern world order. It demands that we look past the convenient narratives of self-defense and recognize the reality of state-sponsored violence for what it is: a calculated strategy that prioritizes political gain over human life. Only by confronting this reality can we hope to build a world where the weapons of the state are not turned against the innocent, and where the ghosts of the past do not continue to haunt the future.

In the end, the story of state-sponsored terrorism is a story of power. It is a story of how nations use the weak to strike at the strong, how they hide behind the masks of others to pursue their own interests. It is a story of the dark underbelly of international relations, where the rules of war are bent and the laws of nations are ignored. And it is a story that, as long as states continue to seek power through violence, will never truly end. The challenge for us all is to ensure that the next chapter is not written in blood, but in the ink of justice and the resolve of a world that refuses to be terrorized.

The numbers tell a grim story. From the 777 acts of terrorism in 1987 to the complex web of accusations in the 2010s and 2020s, the scale of the problem has only grown. The sophistication of the weapons, from simple bombs to MANPADS, reflects the increasing capabilities of these proxy groups. The resilience of the networks, despite decades of counter-terrorism efforts, speaks to the deep roots of state sponsorship in the global order. And the failure of international law to provide a mechanism for prosecution highlights the urgent need for reform.

As we move forward, the focus must shift from the symptoms to the source. We must address the motivations that drive states to sponsor terrorism, the strategic calculations that make it seem like a viable option, and the international norms that allow it to persist. We must build a world where the cost of sponsorship is higher than the benefit, where the risks of exposure are real, and where the victims of terror are not forgotten. Only then can we hope to break the cycle of violence and build a future that is free from the shadow of state-sponsored terrorism.

The journey will not be easy. It will require courage, diplomacy, and a willingness to confront the uncomfortable truths of the past and present. But it is a journey that must be taken if we are to secure a future where peace is not just a dream, but a reality. The stakes are too high, and the cost of inaction too great, to do otherwise. The time to act is now, before the next bomb is planted, before the next assassination is carried out, before the next generation is born into a world where terror is a tool of statecraft. The future depends on it.
