Brexit

10 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Brexit

At 11:00 p.m. GMT on January 31, 2020, the United Kingdom ceased to be a member of the European Union. It was a moment that had been decades in the making, a geopolitical rupture that transformed the political landscape of Europe and sent shockwaves through global markets. In that single hour, the UK became the first and only member state to formally withdraw from the bloc, ending a 47-year partnership that began when the nation joined the European Communities (EC) on January 1, 1973. While Algeria had left the EC following its independence from France in 1962, and Greenland had withdrawn as part of the Kingdom of Denmark in 1985, no other sovereign nation had ever attempted to dismantle its integration with the European project until Brexit.

The word itself is a portmanteau of "Britain" and "Exit," a linguistic coinage that would come to define a political era. Yet, the reality of Brexit was far more complex than a simple departure. As the clock struck midnight on February 1, 2020, European Union law and the jurisdiction of the Court of Justice of the European Union lost their primacy over British law. The UK was suddenly free to amend or repeal the vast body of EU legislation that had been transposed into domestic law by the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018. However, this newfound sovereignty did not exist in a vacuum; the nation remained bound by a web of treaties with countries around the world, including many EU member states and the EU itself. The legal architecture of the relationship had fundamentally shifted, but the economic and social threads remained tightly woven.

The Long Shadow of Euroscepticism

To understand the magnitude of the 2016 referendum, one must look back at the decades of tension that preceded it. The story of Brexit is not a sudden eruption but a slow-building pressure cooker. Throughout the entire period of British membership, Eurosceptic groups had existed, opposing various aspects of the EU and its predecessors. These were not fringe voices initially; they were part of the mainstream political discourse. In 1975, just two years after the UK joined the EC, the Labour prime minister Harold Wilson held a referendum on continued membership. The result was a decisive victory for the status quo, with 67.2% of the electorate voting to stay.

For the next four decades, the political consensus held firm, even as the nature of European integration deepened. From 1975 to 2016, a minority of UK politicians grew increasingly opposed to the concept of an "ever closer union." This opposition was particularly vocal within factions of the Conservative Party from the 1980s through the 2000s. Despite this growing dissent, no further referendums were held. The political establishment largely treated the European question as a settled matter, or at least one manageable within the parliamentary system.

The dynamic shifted dramatically in the mid-2010s. The UK Independence Party (UKIP) surged in popularity, capitalizing on anxieties regarding immigration, sovereignty, and national identity. Simultaneously, pressure mounted from Eurosceptic members within the Conservative Party itself. These internal and external forces converged on the prime minister, David Cameron. To placate his party and stem the bleeding of voters to UKIP, Cameron made a fateful promise: if re-elected, his government would hold a referendum on British membership in the EU.

The 2016 Vote and the Fracture

Following the 2015 general election, which produced a small but unexpected majority for the governing Conservative Party, the promise was kept. On June 23, 2016, the British electorate was asked to decide the nation's future. The campaign that ensued was one of the most polarized in modern British history. The "Remain" campaign was a broad coalition of the political establishment, including then-prime minister David Cameron, future prime ministers Theresa May, Liz Truss, and Sir Keir Starmer, as well as former prime ministers John Major, Tony Blair, and Gordon Brown. They argued for stability, economic security, and global influence.

Opposing them was the "Leave" campaign, a coalition that would eventually produce two more prime ministers: Boris Johnson and Rishi Sunak. They campaigned on the principles of sovereignty, control over borders, and the ability to strike independent trade deals. The result was a shock to the political class. The electorate voted to Leave by a slight but decisive margin: 51.9% in favor of leaving versus 48.1% to remain.

The geographical divide was stark. Every region of England and Wales, with the sole exception of London, voted in favor of Brexit. In contrast, Scotland and Northern Ireland voted to remain. This split highlighted deep fractures not just in policy, but in the national identity itself. The result triggered an immediate political earthquake. David Cameron, who had campaigned vigorously for Remain, resigned that very night. He was replaced by Theresa May, the former Home Secretary, who had to navigate the impossible task of delivering a vote that she herself had opposed.

The Four-Year Negotiation

What followed was four years of agonizing negotiation, a period defined by political paralysis and public division. The UK formally notified the EU of its intention to withdraw on March 29, 2017, invoking Article 50 of the Treaty on European Union. The process was originally scheduled to conclude two years later, on March 29, 2019. However, the path to departure was anything but linear. The UK government, led by May, sought to leave the EU customs union and single market, a position that created immediate friction with the EU's demand to prevent a hard border between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland.

The proposed withdrawal agreement, finalized in November 2018, became the casualty of a deeply divided Parliament. The Labour Party demanded a customs union, while many Conservatives opposed the financial settlement and the controversial "Irish backstop," a mechanism designed to avoid border controls but which critics feared could trap the UK in EU regulations indefinitely. The Liberal Democrats and the Scottish National Party (SNP) sought to reverse Brexit entirely through a second referendum.

The deadlock was absolute. On three separate occasions, the British Parliament voted against ratifying the withdrawal agreement. The uncertainty was paralyzing. The deadline was pushed back, then pushed back again. Three extensions of the Article 50 process were granted by the EU, stretching the timeline well beyond the original two-year window. The political instability was so severe that it led to two snap general elections, in 2017 and 2019, as politicians sought a mandate to break the logjam.

May's resignation in 2019 paved the way for Boris Johnson. Johnson, a leading figure in the Leave campaign, campaigned on a platform of "Get Brexit Done." In the December 2019 general election, the Conservatives, advocating for a "hard-brexit" withdrawal agreement, won an overwhelming majority of 80 seats. This mandate finally broke the deadlock. The Parliament, previously unable to agree on anything, ratified the Withdrawal Agreement with the European Union (Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020. The UK was set to leave at the end of January 31, 2020.

The Transition and the New Deal

Leaving the EU was only the first step. Upon exit, the UK entered an eleven-month transition period that ended on December 31, 2020. During this time, the UK remained subject to EU law and stayed within the European Union Customs Union and the single market. It was a limbo state; the UK was no longer part of EU political bodies or institutions, but the rules of the game had not yet changed. The hope was that this period would allow for the negotiation of a comprehensive post-Brexit relationship.

Time was of the essence. Trade deal negotiations continued with intense intensity right up to the scheduled end of the transition. It was only on December 30, 2020, just hours before the transition period expired, that the EU–UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement was signed. The deal provisionally applied from January 1, 2021, and formally came into force on May 1, 2021. This agreement would determine the economic effects of Brexit, governing everything from trade tariffs to fishing rights and security cooperation.

The withdrawal was a triumph for right-wing and conservative hard Eurosceptics who had long advocated for a clean break. Conversely, it was a defeat for pro-Europeanists from across the political spectrum who had warned of economic isolation and diminished global standing. The ideological battle lines that had been drawn in the 1970s, when the political left often championed withdrawal, had shifted dramatically by the 2010s, with the left largely supporting Remain and the right driving the Leave agenda.

The Unresolved Question

The journey from the 1973 accession to the 2020 exit was marked by a series of missed opportunities for democratic clarity. The 1992 Maastricht Treaty, which founded the modern European Union, was ratified by the British parliament in 1993 but was never put to a referendum. This decision fueled a sense of betrayal among many voters, particularly after the Treaty of Lisbon was ratified in 2008 without a vote, despite previous promises to hold a referendum on the abandoned European Constitution. These unfulfilled promises created a reservoir of distrust that the 2016 referendum tapped into.

The 2016 vote was not just a decision to leave; it was a rejection of the political elite's management of the European project. The electorate, frustrated by the lack of control over immigration and the perceived erosion of national sovereignty, voted to reclaim both. The subsequent years of chaos demonstrated just how difficult it is to unwind forty years of integration. The political system, designed for compromise, was pushed to its breaking point by a binary choice that offered no middle ground.

Theresa May's attempt to find a compromise that satisfied both the Remain and Leave factions ultimately failed. The "Irish backstop" became a symbol of the impossibility of leaving the single market and customs union without creating a border in the Irish Sea or on the island of Ireland. It was a geopolitical puzzle that seemed unsolvable until the political will shifted with the 2019 election. Boris Johnson's victory was less about a specific policy victory and more about a demand for closure. The electorate, weary of the uncertainty, voted for a definitive end to the debate, even if the terms were not perfect.

The legacy of Brexit is still being written. The Trade and Cooperation Agreement, while preventing a cliff-edge exit, established a relationship that is more distant and transactional than the one that existed prior to 2020. The UK is no longer a participant in the EU's political decision-making, yet its economy remains deeply intertwined with the bloc. The legal framework has changed, with the UK now able to diverge from EU regulations, but the practical costs of this divergence are only beginning to be understood.

In the end, Brexit stands as a unique chapter in the history of the European project. It proved that the integration of the continent was not irreversible. It demonstrated that the will of the electorate could override the calculations of the political establishment. But it also showed the immense complexity of disentangling a nation from a supranational union. The UK left the EU on January 31, 2020, but the political, economic, and social consequences of that decision will resonate for generations. The nation that once joined the European Communities in 1973 has now charted a new, uncertain course, defined not by the rules of Brussels, but by its own, self-imposed boundaries. The experiment of leaving continues, a testament to the enduring power of the national vote and the enduring challenges of sovereignty in a globalized world.
