National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2024

13 min read

Based on Wikipedia: National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2024

On December 22, 2023, President Joe Biden signed the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) for Fiscal Year 2024 into law, cementing a budget of $886 billion for the American military machine. This was not merely a line-item adjustment in a ledger; it was the annual ritual where Congress redefines the very architecture of American power, dictating everything from the pay of a junior sailor to the strategic posture of the United States against peer adversaries. While the headline figure—the largest defense budget in history—dominated the news cycle, the true significance of the 2024 NDAA lay in its quiet, seismic shifts regarding national security, the regulation of artificial intelligence, and the most controversial topic in the intelligence community: the disclosure of Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena (UAP). For a reader grappling with the question of why AI is not regulated like a weapon, this legislation offers a profound case study in how Congress attempts to catch up with technology that moves faster than the legislative process, often resulting in laws that are less about immediate prohibition and more about managing the unprecedented risks of the unknown.

The path to the final law was a masterclass in legislative friction. The process began in the House of Representatives with H.R. 2670 and in the Senate with S. 2226. On July 14, 2023, the House passed its version by a razor-thin margin of 219–210, revealing deep partisan fractures that would haunt the bill's journey. In contrast, the Senate, led by Majority Leader Chuck Schumer and Republican Mike Rounds, moved with surprising speed, passing their version on July 27 by a landslide vote of 86–11. This disparity set the stage for a months-long conference. The Senate effectively replaced the House text with their own, passing it by unanimous consent, while the House initially resisted. It was not until December 6, with the filing of the conference report H. Rept. 118-301, that a compromise was forged. The final bill was approved by both chambers and sent to the President, who signed it into law on December 22.

Yet, even as the ink dried, the President voiced reservations. In his signing statement, Biden expressed significant concern over provisions that restricted the executive branch's discretion regarding the transfer of detainees at Guantanamo Bay. He also raised constitutional questions about the extent of congressional oversight over foreign policy, hinting at the eternal tension between the legislative and executive branches when it comes to the sword of the state. But beyond these constitutional squabbles, the 2024 NDAA contained provisions that would ripple far beyond the Pentagon, touching on the nature of reality itself through its handling of UAP.

The UAP Disclosure Act and the War on Secrecy

Perhaps the most culturally resonant section of the 2024 NDAA was the inclusion of the UAP Disclosure Act of 2023. This was not a sudden whim but the culmination of a legislative arc that began years prior, with language appearing in the 2022 NDAA, the 2023 NDAA, and various Intelligence Authorization Acts. The core mission of these provisions, codified in Sections 1841 et seq., was to dismantle the decades-long culture of secrecy surrounding Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena.

The law directed the National Archives and Records Administration (NARA) to create a comprehensive collection of government records on UAP. The mandate was clear: assemble, preserve, and provide public access to these files. The legislation set a hard deadline, requiring disclosure no later than 25 years after enactment, with a powerful presumption of immediate disclosure. The logic was simple but radical: the American public had a right to know what their government knew about objects that defied the laws of physics.

However, the executive branch was granted a specific, albeit narrow, escape hatch. The President could certify that continued postponement was necessary if disclosure would cause identifiable harm to military defense, intelligence operations, law enforcement, or foreign relations. This clause was the fulcrum upon which the entire debate balanced. It acknowledged the reality that some secrets are vital to national survival, even as it demanded transparency.

The scope of the act extended far beyond just declassifying old documents. Sections 1687 and 7343 explicitly disallowed the use of funds or independent research and development (IRAD) indirect expenses for UFO or UAP research unless the material was made available to the appropriate congressional committees and leadership. This was a direct attempt to curb the "black budget" culture where defense contractors and intelligence agencies could pursue fringe technologies without oversight. It forced the disclosure of not just what the government found, but what they were spending money to find.

Yet, the version of the act that ultimately passed was a shadow of its original proposal. The initial draft included a provision granting the federal government the power of eminent domain over UAP-related material controlled by private persons or entities. This would have allowed the government to seize recovered technologies of unknown origin (TUO) and biological evidence of non-human intelligence (NHI) held by aerospace companies or private collectors. Journalists covering the subject described the final text as a "heavily watered down" version, stripped of the teeth necessary to force private entities to hand over their secrets. The removal of the eminent domain clause meant that while the government could demand records, it could not necessarily seize the physical evidence that might lie in a hangar in Nevada or a vault in a private facility.

The legislative history of this section is a testament to the changing winds of American politics. In 2026, President Trump, looking back on the legislative landscape, stated he would order agencies to begin the process of identifying and releasing files related to alien and extraterrestrial life. This future declaration underscores how the 2024 NDAA laid the groundwork for a future where the distinction between science fiction and national security policy would blur. The act forced a conversation about the "demand" side of information, mirroring the approach taken against corruption, but for the unknown.

The Foreign Extortion Prevention Act: A New Weapon Against Bribery

While the UAP provisions captured the public imagination, the 2024 NDAA also introduced a critical legal tool to combat global corruption: the Foreign Extortion Prevention Act (FEPA). For decades, American anti-bribery law had been asymmetrical, focusing almost exclusively on the "supply" side. The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) was designed to stop American companies and citizens from paying bribes to foreign officials. It was a powerful tool, but it left a gaping hole in the legal armor.

FEPA addressed this gap by enabling US authorities to prosecute foreign officials who demand or accept bribes from a US citizen, US company, or within a US jurisdiction. This was a paradigm shift. Previously, if a foreign official in a developing nation demanded a bribe to award a contract to an American firm, the US legal system had little recourse unless the American firm voluntarily reported the extortion. FEPA turned the tables, making the act of demanding a bribe a crime under US law, regardless of where the demand originated, as long as it had a nexus to the United States.

Analysts immediately recognized the significance of this move. By tackling the "demand" side of bribery, FEPA aimed to level the playing field for American businesses and reduce the systemic corruption that often plagues international infrastructure and defense contracts. It signaled that the United States was no longer willing to be a passive victim of foreign extortion, transforming the legal landscape of international commerce. This provision, like the AI regulations discussed in your previous reading, represents an attempt to use domestic law to regulate global behaviors that were previously outside the reach of the American judicial system.

The Battle Over Extremism and the Military's Soul

If the UAP and FEPA sections were about the future and the external world, the provisions regarding extremism were about the internal health of the military itself. The 2024 NDAA became a battleground over the role of diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) within the armed forces, a conflict that had been simmering since the January 6, 2021, attack on the US Capitol.

Secretary of Defense Lloyd Austin had established an anti-extremism working group following the revelation that active-duty service members and veterans had participated in the insurrection. The group released recommendations in late 2021, aiming to root out radicalization within the ranks. However, implementation stalled. Republicans in Congress vigorously opposed the working group, deriding its efforts as "wokeness" and arguing that DEI initiatives were distracting from the primary mission of national defense.

The irony of this political standoff was starkly illuminated by a recent inspector general report, which found dozens of troops advocating for the violent overthrow of the US government. The seriousness of the threat was not theoretical. The history of military extremism in America was long and violent. Timothy McVeigh, the Oklahoma City bomber who killed 168 people, was a decorated veteran of Operation Desert Storm. Yet, even as a sergeant, he had earned a reputation for assigning undesirable work to Black servicemen and using derogatory language. His radicalization was not an anomaly but a symptom of a deeper cultural rot that the military had struggled to address.

The 2024 NDAA's treatment of these issues highlighted a profound disconnect. While the intelligence community was preparing to release files on alien life, the military leadership found itself unable to effectively combat the domestic extremism that threatened the republic from within. The legislative battle over the anti-extremism working group was a proxy war over the identity of the American military, a question of whether the force would be a mirror of the diverse society it protects or a reflection of a monolithic, exclusionary ideology.

NATO, Space Force, and the Architecture of Power

Beyond the cultural wars and the mysteries of UAP, the 2024 NDAA fundamentally altered the structural framework of American alliances and military organization. One of the most significant provisions was Section 1250A, which inserted a new layer of constitutional gravity into the United States' relationship with NATO. The law prevented the President from withdrawing the United States from the North Atlantic Treaty Organization without the approval of a two-thirds Senate supermajority or an act of Congress.

This was a direct response to the political volatility of the previous years, where the prospect of a US withdrawal from NATO had been a talking point in the highest echelons of power. By requiring a supermajority, Congress effectively tied the hands of the executive branch, ensuring that the US commitment to the transatlantic alliance could not be undone by a single leader or a simple majority vote. It was a legislative firewall, designed to preserve the stability of the global order against the whims of populism.

Simultaneously, the act reorganized the Space Force, the newest branch of the armed services. The original House bill proposed the "Space Force Personnel Management Act," which would disestablish the regular Space Force and integrate both active duty and reserve personnel into a single entity. The goal was to introduce flexibility by replacing the traditional "active duty" and "reserve" distinctions with "sustained duty" (full-time) and "part-time" status. This change was unique to the Space Force, recognizing the hybrid nature of space operations where commercial and military lines are increasingly blurred.

The final legislation also permanently established the commissioned officer ranks of the Space Force to match those of the Army, Marine Corps, and Air Force, resolving an identity crisis that had plagued the new branch. It elevated the position of the Vice Chief of the National Guard Bureau to a statutory rank of general, acknowledging the growing strategic importance of the National Guard. These changes were not mere administrative tweaks; they were the growing pains of a military adapting to a new domain of warfare.

The Billion-Dollar Reality

At the heart of the 2024 NDAA lay the cold, hard reality of the budget. The act authorized a staggering $886 billion for national defense programs. This was not just a number; it was a promise of capability. Included in this massive sum was a 5.2% pay increase for service members, a necessary measure to retain talent in a tight labor market. It authorized the procurement of up to 13 Virginia-class submarines, the backbone of the US Navy's undersea dominance. It also authorized the sale and transfer of defense articles and services to implement the AUKUS partnership, the trilateral security pact between the US, UK, and Australia.

The AUKUS provisions were particularly complex. The act allowed for the sale of up to three Virginia-class submarines, one year after passage. However, the Navy made it clear that they did not intend to implement this until 2032, highlighting the long lead times involved in modern naval construction. This gap between legislative authorization and operational reality is a recurring theme in defense policy. The law can authorize a submarine today, but the shipyard, the supply chain, and the trained crew take years to materialize.

The 2024 NDAA was a document of contradictions. It sought to open the secrets of the cosmos while tightening the reins on executive power in foreign policy. It attempted to root out domestic extremism while facing fierce political opposition from the very party that controlled the purse strings. It authorized a historic budget increase while struggling to modernize the force structure for a new era of warfare.

For the reader interested in the regulation of AI as a weapon, the NDAA serves as a powerful analogy. Just as the UAP provisions tried to regulate the disclosure of unknown technologies through a framework of mandatory review and congressional oversight, AI regulation faces the same challenge: how to control something that evolves faster than the law can write it. The 2024 NDAA did not solve the problem of AI, nor did it solve the problem of extremism. But it laid out the boundaries of the debate, the mechanisms of oversight, and the political realities that will shape the next decade of American security. It was a law that tried to look into the future, even as it wrestled with the ghosts of the past.

The story of the 2024 NDAA is not just about what was passed, but what was stripped away. The removal of the eminent domain clause for UAP materials showed the limits of congressional power when faced with private interests. The stalling of the anti-extremism working group revealed the depth of the political polarization that infects the military. The inclusion of FEPA showed that the US is willing to extend its legal reach globally, even as it struggles to clean up its own house.

In the end, the National Defense Authorization Act is a mirror. It reflects the fears, hopes, and contradictions of the nation that writes it. In 2024, that mirror showed a country grappling with the unknown, from the depths of the ocean to the edges of the atmosphere, and from the corruption of foreign officials to the radicalization of its own soldiers. The $886 billion price tag was just the entry fee for a much larger conversation about what it means to be a superpower in an age of uncertainty. As the files on UAP are slowly opened and the submarines are slowly built, the legacy of this law will be written not in the text of the statute, but in the reality it creates. And in that reality, the question of how to regulate the unregulated—whether it be AI, alien technology, or the human capacity for violence—remains the defining challenge of the era.
