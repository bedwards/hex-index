Battle of Moshchun

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Battle of Moshchun

On March 21, 2022, the village of Moshchun was liberated, but the silence that followed was not one of peace; it was the hollow, ringing quiet of a place where the earth itself had been turned over by artillery. Just three weeks earlier, on March 5, Ukrainian defenders had urged the remaining civilians to flee, knowing that the small settlement on the outskirts of Kyiv was about to become the epicenter of a collision between two modern armies. What followed was not merely a skirmish but a grueling, eighteen-day meat grinder that would come to be described as one of the fiercest and most critical engagements in the defense of the Ukrainian capital. The battle for Moshchun was the hinge upon which the entire northern front turned, a desperate stand where the geography of a flooded river, the resolve of a single brigade, and the brutal calculus of modern warfare converged to halt a Russian juggernaut that had already swept through Chernobyl and threatened to swallow Kyiv whole.

To understand the strategic desperation of the moment, one must first understand the geography of the invasion's opening gambit. By late February 2022, the Russian Ground Forces had executed a rapid, terrifying advance. Following the capture of Hostomel Airport on February 25, the main group of Russian troops launched a massive offensive from Belarus, driving south through the eerie, radioactive silence of the Chernobyl exclusion zone. They did not stop at the zone's edge. They rolled through Chernobyl itself, then Ivankiv, Dymer, and Borodyanka, their armored columns kicking up dust as they reached the outskirts of Bucha. The Ukrainian command, realizing that the capital was under direct threat, faced a nightmare scenario: how do you stop a mechanized spearhead when your own forces are stretched thin?

The answer lay in the Irpin River. As Ukrainian troops retreated from the advancing Russians, they made a calculated, devastating decision to blow up the bridges crossing the Irpin. This act was not just a tactical delay; it was a transformation of the landscape itself. The goal was to create a natural moat, a barrier that would force the enemy into a kill zone. But the river was not a static obstacle. In a move that demonstrated the adaptability of the Ukrainian defense, part of the Kozarovichi dam was detonated. This allowed a massive surge of water from the Kyiv reservoir to flood the Irpin, raising its level by 1.5 to 2 meters. The banks overflowed, turning the surrounding lowlands into a swampy quagmire. For the Russian army, stationed at Hostomel and eyeing Moshchun as the gateway to Kyiv, this was a catastrophe. The flooding made vehicle crossings nearly impossible, channeled their movement, and turned the village of Moshchun into the only viable breach point in the entire defensive line.

Moshchun was, by all accounts, an unlikely fortress. It was a small village, a cluster of residential buildings and forests that sat precariously on the river's edge. Yet, in the eyes of the Ukrainian high command, it was the key to the kingdom. Oleksandr Syrskyi, the commander overseeing the defense of Kyiv, took a personal interest in the town's fate. He understood that if Moshchun fell, the Russian tanks would have a clear road into the capital. The responsibility for holding this line fell to the 72nd Mechanized Brigade, a unit that would become legendary for its tenacity. The defense was initially organized around the 5th Company, led by Captain Roman Kovalenko, operating under the overall command of Colonel Oleksandr Vdovychenko.

The first clash occurred on the morning of February 27. Russian forces attempted to cross the bridge over the Irpin at Moshchun, expecting a quick passage. Instead, they walked into a trap. Mechanized units of the 5th Company, supported by local territorial defense forces, engaged the Russian vehicles immediately. The Russian advance was not just slowed; it was forced into a retreat. But this was merely the prelude. From February 28 onward, Moshchun became a target for a relentless barrage. The village was subjected to regular mortar fire, heavy artillery, and airstrikes. The sky over Moshchun was a constant churn of smoke and explosions, a visual and auditory assault that never ceased.

By March 5, the situation had deteriorated to a point of no return for the civilians. The 5th Company, realizing the intensity of the coming storm, urged everyone remaining to evacuate. They were right to be afraid. That same day, a small contingent of Russian special forces, including elements of the 76th Guards Air Assault Division under the command of Sergey Chubarykin, managed to slip into the northwestern part of the village and occupy it. The battle had truly begun. The Russians were no longer probing; they were establishing a foothold.

The following morning, March 6, the Russians committed to a full-scale crossing. A significant number of troops began to wade and swim through the flooded Irpin, or attempt to cross on the few remaining viable paths. They were met by the full force of the Ukrainian defense. Colonel Vdovychenko's command was now under immense pressure. The Russian assault was not a single wave but a continuous, multi-vector attack. They brought everything they had: "Grads" multiple rocket launchers, heavy artillery, mortars, precision airstrikes, drones, and attack helicopters. The Russians also deployed sophisticated electronic warfare tactics, jamming Ukrainian communications and disabling their drones, attempting to blind the defenders in the very moment they needed visibility most.

The fighting was brutal and intimate. Ukrainian forces, pushed back by the sheer volume of fire, retreated to the center of the village to regroup. It was here that they received a critical reinforcement: Ukrainian special forces armed with anti-tank weapons. These units were the game-changers. In the narrow streets and ruined houses of Moshchun, the high-tech Russian armor found itself vulnerable to close-quarters ambushes. The battle devolved into a chaotic struggle for every house, every tree line, and every meter of ground.

As the days turned into a week, the Russian command realized that their attempts to break through Ukrainian defenses in Irpin and other areas west of the capital were failing. The flooding of the Irpin had worked; the main axis of advance was blocked. With no other options, the Russian army made the fateful decision to prioritize the advance through Moshchun. They threw more qualified troops, jet artillery, and aircraft into the fray. The village was now the primary target. The Russians understood that Moshchun was the lock, and they had the key, but the Ukrainian defenders had welded the door shut from the inside.

By mid-March, the toll was becoming evident. About 2,000 of the 2,800 buildings in Moshchun were destroyed. The village was no longer a place of residence; it was a graveyard of concrete and timber. Yet, the Ukrainian defense held. The density of shelling was so intense that it seemed impossible for any human to survive in the open. Colonel Vdovychenko, facing the exhaustion of his troops, made a desperate call to the Commander-in-Chief of the Armed Forces of Ukraine, Valerii Zaluzhnyi. Vdovychenko offered to retreat from Moshchun, arguing that the combination of heavy fire and the biting cold of early spring made it impossible for his men to stay any longer. He was asking for a tactical withdrawal to save the lives of his soldiers.

The response from General Zaluzhnyi was immediate and uncompromising. He replied with words that would echo through the history of the war: "If you move away, then Kyiv." He promised that they would "look for forces and means" to support the defense, but the order was clear: hold the line. There would be no retreat. This moment encapsulated the strategic reality of the battle. Moshchun was not just a village; it was the buffer between the Russian army and the heart of the nation. To lose it was to lose Kyiv.

The command decided to change tactics. Vdovychenko implemented a rotation system, swapping out troops every three days. The logic was simple but desperate: the density of shelling and the freezing temperatures meant that staying longer than 72 hours was a death sentence. He also introduced a new battalion into the battle, fresh eyes and fresh legs to take over the weary defenders. This rotation kept the defense dynamic, preventing the Russians from gaining momentum against a static, exhausted force.

The turning point came in the days following the decision to hold. The Ukrainian army, bolstered by reinforcements and a new tactical approach, began to push back. By around March 14, Ukrainian units had managed to surround Moshchun on two sides. They began to heavily shell the Russian troop concentrations and the river crossings, turning the tables on the aggressors. The Russians, who had expected a quick victory, found themselves pinned down in a kill zone of their own making. The flooding that had hindered their initial advance now worked against their retreat, trapping them in the swampy lowlands.

The final assault came on March 21. The Ukrainian forces, having regrouped and reorganized, launched a coordinated push that completely liberated the village from Russian occupation. The victory was total. The Russian army, having failed to break through the Moshchun line, was forced to pull back to the left bank of the Irpin River. The gateway to Kyiv was closed. The battle of Moshchun had been a decisive factor in the larger narrative of the war. The Russian losses in Moshchun, combined with the defeats in nearby Irpin and Makariv, contributed directly to their decision to completely withdraw from the Kyiv Oblast in the weeks that followed. The Russian dream of a swift capture of the capital had been shattered, not by a single grand battle, but by the grinding, bloody resistance of a single village.

The human cost of this victory was staggering. During the battle, 118 Ukrainian soldiers were confirmed killed. The Russian losses were even higher, though harder to verify with absolute precision. In the year following the battle, Ukrainian forces claimed to have identified 100 Russian soldiers killed in the fighting, a number that included 45 paratroopers from Russia's 331st Guards Airborne Regiment and 30 soldiers from the 155th Separate Marine Brigade. These were not just numbers; they were the elite units of the Russian military, the spearhead of the invasion, ground down in the mud of Moshchun.

The legacy of the battle is etched into the landscape and the memory of the nation. In 2023, the memorial "Angels of Victory" was created in Moshchun to commemorate the fallen soldiers. It stands as a silent witness to the ferocity of the fighting, a place where the community can honor the men and women who held the line when the odds were insurmountable. On September 6, 2024, the battlefield was officially recorded by Ukraine as a monument of national historical significance. This designation ensures that the story of Moshchun is not forgotten, that the sacrifice of the 72nd Mechanized Brigade and the territorial defense forces is preserved for future generations.

The battle of Moshchun offers a profound lesson in the nature of modern warfare. It was a conflict where technology and traditional tactics collided. The Russians brought drones, electronic warfare, and heavy artillery, expecting to overwhelm the defenders with superior firepower. But the Ukrainians used the terrain, the weather, and the sheer will of their soldiers to neutralize these advantages. The flooding of the Irpin River was a masterstroke of defensive engineering, turning a natural feature into a weapon. The rotation of troops was a logistical triumph, keeping the defense fresh in the face of relentless bombardment. And the refusal to retreat, the decision to hold the line at all costs, was a moral victory that rippled across the entire front.

For the reader seeking to understand the broader context of the war, Moshchun is essential. It was the moment the myth of Russian invincibility began to crack. It was the first time a major Russian offensive was not just stalled, but reversed. The battle demonstrated that a determined defense, supported by a willing population and a clear strategic vision, could defeat a much larger and better-equipped army. It was a microcosm of the entire war, where the defense of a single village became the defense of a nation.

Today, Moshchun is a place of reconstruction. The 2,000 destroyed buildings are being rebuilt, and life is slowly returning to the village. But the scars remain. The memorial stands as a reminder of what was lost and what was gained. The battle of Moshchun was not just a military engagement; it was a defining moment in the history of Ukraine. It proved that the will to survive could overcome the machinery of war. It showed the world that even in the face of overwhelming odds, a small group of people could change the course of history. The story of Moshchun is a testament to the resilience of the Ukrainian people, a story that will be told for generations to come. It is a story of courage, sacrifice, and the unbreakable spirit of a nation that refused to bow.

The events of March 2022 in Moshchun were not an anomaly; they were a blueprint. They showed that the defense of Kyiv was not a foregone conclusion, but a battle that had to be won, day by day, house by house. The Russian army, with its vast arsenal and superior numbers, found itself bogged down in a village that it could not take. The Ukrainian forces, with their limited resources and desperate situation, found a way to win. The battle of Moshchun was the moment the tide turned. It was the moment the world realized that the invasion of Ukraine would not be a quick victory for Russia, but a long, bloody struggle. And it was the moment that Ukraine began to write its own history, a history of resistance, of defiance, and of ultimate victory. The village of Moshchun, once a quiet settlement on the edge of the Irpin River, is now a symbol of that victory.
