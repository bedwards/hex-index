Panic buying

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Panic buying

In August 2024, Japanese consumers emptied supermarket shelves of rice, a staple that had never before faced such sudden, collective desperation. The trigger was not a drought or a war, but a convergence of warnings: a "megaquake" alert, approaching typhoons, and the lingering shadow of supply chain fragility. This was not an isolated incident of mass hysteria, but the latest chapter in a decades-long saga of human behavior where the fear of scarcity creates the very scarcity people dread. Panic buying is the economic manifestation of a primal survival instinct, a herd behavior where the collective anxiety of millions overrides individual logic, turning well-stocked aisles into barren landscapes and stable markets into chaotic fronts.

The phenomenon is deceptively simple in definition but complex in execution. It occurs when consumers purchase unusually large quantities of goods in anticipation of a disaster, a perceived disaster, or a looming price hike. It is not merely shopping; it is a frantic, often irrational race against a shadowy deadline. The drivers behind this behavior are a potent cocktail of psychological and social factors. First, there is the perception of threat and scarcity; the mind sees a gap in the supply chain and fills it with catastrophe. Second, there is the fear of the unknown, a heavy emotional pressure that thrives in uncertainty. Third, panic buying serves as a coping mechanism, a tangible action taken to relieve anxiety and regain a sense of control when the world feels like it is spinning out of orbit. Finally, it is deeply social; the behavior of one's network acts as a contagion, validating the fear and accelerating the rush.

The Architecture of Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

At its core, panic buying is a self-fulfilling prophecy. The risk of a shortage may be entirely real, or it may be a phantom born of rumor, yet the outcome remains the same: genuine shortages. When thousands of people act on the belief that a product will vanish, they ensure that it does vanish. This dynamic has long fascinated economists and psychologists, placing panic buying squarely within the study of consumer behavior and collective action. It is the same psychological engine that drives fads, stock market crashes, banking panics, and runs on nondurable goods.

The history of this behavior reads like a chronicle of human vulnerability in the face of crisis. During the First World War (1914–1918) and the Second World War (1939–1945), populations mobilized not just for soldiers, but for supplies, hoarding food and materials in anticipation of siege and blockade. The 1918–1919 global influenza pandemic, known as the Spanish flu, saw a similar frenzy. The fear was not just of the virus, but of the lack of remedy. Consumers flocked to pharmacists and doctors' surgeries, stripping shelves of quinine and other treatments. The commercial impact was staggering; sales of Vicks VapoRub skyrocketed from $900,000 to $2.9 million in a single year, a number that speaks to the desperate willingness to buy anything that promised safety.

Economic instability has proven just as potent a trigger as biological ones. In 1922, the First Austrian Republic was engulfed in hyperinflation. As the Austrian krone depreciated with terrifying speed, the currency became worthless by the hour. The rational response was to convert money into goods immediately. Panic buying and food hoarding swept the nation, threatening to collapse the economy entirely until a rescue backed by the League of Nations intervened. The lesson was clear: when the value of money evaporates, the instinct is to grab whatever physical value remains.

The Cold War and the Paper Crisis

As the 20th century progressed, the triggers for panic buying became more geopolitical and cultural. The 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis brought the world to the brink of nuclear annihilation, and the American response was to fill pantries with canned food. It was a defensive crouch, a literal attempt to prepare for a bunker life that never came. Yet, the behavior left a mark on the national psyche, embedding the habit of stockpiling in the face of existential threat.

Sometimes, the threat is entirely manufactured, born of policy rather than catastrophe. The 1973 toilet paper panic in the United States stands as a bizarre footnote in economic history. A rumor, or perhaps a misinterpreted supply chain issue, sparked a frenzy that left bathrooms empty across the nation. It was a moment where a basic, ubiquitous commodity became a symbol of security. A decade later, the 1979 oil crisis saw a similar dynamic, with Japan leading the charge in panic buying oil as global supplies tightened. The 1985 arrival of "New Coke" triggered a different kind of panic, one of cultural preservation, as consumers rushed to buy the original formula before it vanished forever, fearing a permanent loss of a familiar taste.

The turn of the millennium brought new anxieties. The Y2K bug, a computer glitch fear, led to widespread stockpiling of food, as people worried that digital failures would halt the supply chain. The September 11 attacks in 2001 shifted the focus from domestic goods to international commodities, with panic buying of metals, gold, and oil as the world reeled from the shock. In 2003, during the SARS outbreak, the panic buying phenomenon took on a distinct regional flavor. In the Chinese province of Guangdong and neighboring areas like Hainan and Hong Kong, consumers engaged in rounds of frenzied purchasing. The list of items was eclectic and specific: salt, rice, vinegar, vegetable oil, antibiotics, face masks, and traditional Chinese medicine. It was a defense against a virus that was poorly understood, a physical barrier built from household staples.

When Policy Fuels the Fire

Panic buying is not always a spontaneous reaction to nature; often, it is the direct result of government mismanagement or political maneuvering. The 2008–2016 United States ammunition shortage serves as a stark example of how political fear can drive market behavior. Gun owners, fearing that President Barack Obama would implement tougher gun control laws, engaged in massive panic buying of ammunition. The resulting shortage was not due to a lack of manufacturing capacity, but to a surge in demand driven by political anxiety.

In Venezuela, the phenomenon reached a level of surreal chaos. The September 2013 economic crisis saw the government take drastic measures to manage the shortage of basic goods. The state temporarily seized the Aragua-based Paper Manufacturing Company to control the production and distribution of toilet paper, a product that had vanished from shelves due to a mix of price controls, currency restrictions, and hoarding. The situation culminated in the "Dakazo," a bizarre event in November 2013 where President Nicolás Maduro, amid waning support before municipal elections, ordered the military to occupy stores. He proclaimed, "Leave nothing on the shelves!" The announcement of lowered prices did not calm the population; instead, it sparked looting. Stores across Venezuela were stripped bare, and by the end of the Dakazo, the shelves remained empty for months, a testament to how political theater can accelerate economic collapse.

The Digital Age and the Viral Panic

As we moved into the 21st century, the speed at which panic buying could spread accelerated exponentially, fueled by the internet and social media. The 2000s saw localized fuel protests in the UK and chemical plant explosions in Jilin that led to panic buying of water and food. But the true scale of the phenomenon was revealed in May 2021, during the Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack in the Southeastern United States. Panic buying exacerbated fuel shortages, turning a cyber incident into a physical crisis. A year later, in September 2021, the United Kingdom faced a similar scenario. A shortage of tanker drivers, widely attributed to Brexit, led to empty fuel stations across the country. The panic was immediate and total, driving consumers to fill their tanks regardless of immediate need.

The power of the social network was on full display in November 2021, when floods in the Pacific Northwest triggered panic buying of groceries in the British Columbia Interior and Fraser Valley. The physical disruption of the floodwaters was compounded by the digital spread of fear. In March 2022, the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine triggered a unique panic in Russia. When IKEA announced the closure of its 17 Russian stores, consumers rushed to buy kit furniture and home appliances. The scenes were surreal: extensive queues in Moscow and Saint Petersburg, with customers attempting to enter stores through exit doors because the entrances were closed. They were not buying furniture for immediate use; they were buying a piece of the old world before it disappeared.

The pattern repeated with terrifying regularity. In May 2023, a brief interruption in tap water supply in the Malaysian states of Penang and Kedah, lasting less than 24 hours, was enough to trigger a panic buying of bottled water. In August 2023, the discharge of radioactive water from the Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant sparked a transnational panic. In China, consumers bought salt and radiation detectors, driven by anxiety over the radioactive water. In South Korea, the hoarding extended to salt and seafood. By August 2024, the fear had shifted back to rice in Japan, driven by supply shortages and natural disaster warnings.

The 2024-2025 Global Spasms

The latter half of the 2020s proved to be a period of relentless, fragmented panic. In October 2024, American consumers purchased vast quantities of toilet paper and paper towels during a port strike, even though these products were not affected by the labor action. The fear was generalized; the specific threat did not matter. The same month, Spanish floods in Valencia emptied the shelves of Mercadona and Consum supermarkets of bottled water and other essentials.

Political instability continued to drive the behavior. In December 2024, after South Korean President Yoon Suk Yeol declared martial law, consumers began panic buying food, water, and essential goods. The declaration of a state of emergency acted as a green light for the herd to run. The following April, in 2025, a massive power outage across the Iberian Peninsula triggered a similar response in Spain. On April 28, 2025, Spaniards rushed to buy radio receivers, bottled water, and non-perishable food. Some supermarkets were forced to close to the public entirely, overwhelmed by the sheer volume of desperate shoppers.

The Pandemic: The Defining Moment

While these events were significant, none matched the sheer scale and global synchronization of the COVID-19 pandemic. Between February and March 2020, panic buying became a major international phenomenon. It was the first time in modern history that the behavior occurred simultaneously across the globe, from Australia to the United States, from the United Kingdom to Singapore, Hong Kong, and Japan.

Stores were depleted of items that had previously been taken for granted: face masks, food, bottled water, milk, toilet paper, hand sanitizer, rubbing alcohol, antibacterial wipes, and painkillers. The image of the empty toilet paper aisle became the universal symbol of the era. It was a bewildering sight to the public, who could not fathom why such a simple item was vanishing. In Australia, the tension boiled over into violence; two women were charged after a physical altercation over a pack of toilet paper at a supermarket. The severity of the situation drew sharp criticism from political leaders, with Australian Prime Minister Scott Morrison calling for the public to "stop it."

The mechanics of this panic were amplified by the digital sphere. Research suggested that social media played a crucial role in stimulating mass anxiety. Images of empty shelves were shared virally, creating a feedback loop of fear. Many posts were negative, filled with anger and frustration over the frantic situation. This high volume of negative viral content validated the fears of those who had not yet panicked, convincing them that the situation was dire and that immediate action was required. The "herd" was no longer just in the supermarket; it was online, feeding on each other's anxiety.

Retailers were forced to adapt. Many began rationing the sale of essential items. Online giants like eBay and Amazon pulled certain items listed by third-party sellers, including toilet paper, face masks, pasta, canned vegetables, and hand sanitizer, due to concerns over price gouging. Amazon went so far as to restrict the sale of these items, along with thermometers and ventilators, to healthcare professionals and government agencies. The panic even extended beyond physical goods; there was a surge in "panic renting" of self-storage units as people sought places to hide their hoards.

The Psychology of the Empty Shelf

What drives a person to buy fifty rolls of toilet paper when they need two? The answer lies in the intersection of fear and control. In a world that feels increasingly unpredictable, where pandemics, wars, and economic collapses seem to happen with little warning, the act of buying provides a tangible sense of agency. It is a way to say, "I am prepared. I am safe." The scarcity of the product, whether real or imagined, triggers a deep-seated fear of death or destitution.

The social psychological factors are equally potent. When we see others buying, we interpret it as a signal that there is a threat we cannot see. This is the "herd behavior" in action. If everyone is running, there must be a tiger. In the case of the 2020 pandemic, the tiger was invisible, a virus, and the herd's reaction was to buy everything in sight. The behavior is contagious, spreading from person to person, from screen to screen, until the entire population is acting in unison.

The consequences of this behavior are often as damaging as the crisis that triggered it. The shortages created by panic buying are frequently more severe than the actual supply chain disruptions would have caused on their own. The "self-fulfilling prophecy" ensures that the crisis is real, even if the initial trigger was a rumor or a misunderstanding. In Venezuela, the government's attempt to control the market only accelerated the looting and the empty shelves. In the US, the fear of gun control led to a shortage of ammunition that had nothing to do with production. In Japan and China, the fear of radiation or earthquakes led to the hoarding of salt and rice, leaving genuine needs unmet for those who could not afford to stockpile.

A Cycle of Fear

The history of panic buying is a history of human vulnerability. It is a reminder that our economic systems, for all their complexity, are built on the fragile foundation of human psychology. When the walls close in, when the future looks uncertain, we revert to our most primal instincts. We hoard. We run. We fight over the last roll of toilet paper.

From the hyperinflation of 1922 Austria to the martial law of 2024 South Korea, from the Spanish flu of 1918 to the COVID-19 pandemic of 2020, the pattern remains the same. The triggers change—wars, viruses, political declarations, natural disasters, cyberattacks—but the response is constant. We buy. We fear. We create the very shortage we are trying to avoid.

As we look toward the future, with the world facing new and complex challenges, the lesson of panic buying is clear. The only way to break the cycle is to understand the psychology behind it. We must recognize that the empty shelf is often a reflection of our own fears, not a lack of goods. We must learn to distinguish between a real threat and a perceived one, to resist the pull of the herd, and to trust in the resilience of our systems. Until we do, the shelves will continue to empty, and the cycle of panic will continue to repeat itself, a recurring nightmare in the supermarket of life.

The events of 2025 in Spain, the 2024 rice crisis in Japan, and the 2020 global toilet paper run are not anomalies. They are symptoms of a deeper condition. In a world of uncertainty, panic buying is the price we pay for the illusion of control. It is a testament to the power of the collective mind, capable of moving markets, toppling shelves, and reshaping economies with a single, shared thought: I must have it, now.

"Leave nothing on the shelves!"

These words, shouted by a leader in a time of crisis, capture the essence of the panic. It is a command to consume, to hoard, to prepare for a future that may never come. And in obeying that command, we ensure that the future we fear becomes the present we live in. The empty shelf is not a sign of the end of the world; it is a mirror reflecting our own anxiety. And until we learn to look away from the mirror, the shelves will remain empty.
