Royal Canadian Mounted Police

10 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Royal Canadian Mounted Police

In 1932, a man named Albert Johnson, known to history as the Mad Trapper of Rat River, turned the frozen wilderness of the Yukon into a stage for a deadly game of cat and mouse. After a dispute with Indigenous trappers over destroyed traps and verbal harassment escalated into a confrontation where Johnson pointed a firearm at his accusers, the Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP) arrived with a search warrant. Johnson opened fire, wounding an officer, and vanished into the blinding white expanse. What followed was not merely a manhunt but a myth-making event that cemented the image of the Mountie as an unstoppable force of nature, capable of enduring sub-zero temperatures and outlasting any fugitive. Johnson was eventually killed in a shootout, but the legend of the pursuit became the bedrock of the RCMP's public identity, even as the reality of the force shifted from a frontier cavalry to a modern, bureaucratic intelligence apparatus.

The Royal Canadian Mounted Police, or the Gendarmerie royale du Canada in French, stands as the national police service of Canada, a singular agency that has evolved into something far more complex than the romanticized image of men in red serge on horseback. It is an agency of the Government of Canada, yet its reach extends into the daily lives of citizens through a patchwork of contractual agreements. The RCMP provides police services to eleven provinces and territories, all except Ontario and Quebec, which maintain their own provincial forces. Beyond the provincial level, the Mounties police over 150 municipalities and 600 Indigenous communities. Their sworn members possess the unique status of peace officers with jurisdiction across all provinces and territories, a legal authority that allows them to enforce federal legislation, investigate crimes that cross provincial or international borders, and manage the integrity of Canada's vast boundaries.

To understand the RCMP is to understand a force that has been shaped by a tension between its paramilitary roots and its role as a community service provider. Established in 1920 through the amalgamation of two distinct entities, the Royal North-West Mounted Police (RNWMP) and the Dominion Police, the new service inherited a dual legacy. The RNWMP, originally created to police the Canadian West, was modeled after the Royal Irish Constabulary and carried a culture of frontline, paramilitary policing. By 1920, however, this force was becoming rapidly obsolete in the face of a changing nation. The Dominion Police, conversely, was the federal body responsible for intelligence, parliamentary security, and federal law enforcement in the east. The merger created a national service, but one that initially found its local policing role superseded by the rise of municipal and provincial police forces in the major cities and eastern provinces.

The trajectory of the RCMP changed dramatically in 1928 when the federal government authorized the force to enter into heavily subsidized contracts with provinces and municipalities. This was a strategic pivot that allowed the RCMP to return to its roots in local policing. Under the new arrangement, the federal government covered 60 per cent of the costs, leaving the remaining 40 per cent to the provinces and municipalities. The economic logic was irresistible. By 1950, eight of the ten Canadian provinces had disbanded their own provincial police services in favor of the subsidized RCMP model, effectively nationalizing local law enforcement across the vast majority of the country. This expansion transformed the RCMP from a federal investigative body into the primary police force for millions of Canadians, embedding it deeply into the fabric of local governance.

Yet, this expansion came with a dark shadow. As the RCMP grew in power and reach, its mandate to protect national security often morphed into a campaign of surveillance and suppression against groups deemed dangerous to the state. In the early decades of its existence, the service infiltrated ethnic and political organizations with a zeal that often crossed the line of civil liberties. The Communist Party of Canada, founded in 1921, was a primary target, but the net was cast much wider. The RCMP monitored Indigenous groups, minority cultural communities, and nationalist movements, viewing them through the lens of potential subversion.

The service's involvement in immigration matters was particularly severe. Responsible for deporting suspected radicals, the RCMP paid particular attention to Ukrainian nationalist and socialist groups, as well as the Chinese community. The latter was targeted under the guise of combating opium dens, with the RCMP disproportionately linking Chinese Canadians to narcotics. Historians estimate that between 1923 and 1932, Canada deported two per cent of its entire Chinese community, a massive demographic upheaval largely driven by the provisions of the Opium and Narcotics Drugs Act and the enforcement efforts of the Mounties. The first Mountie to go undercover in these operations was Frank Zaneth, operating under the code name Operative Number 1. Zaneth infiltrated not only radical political groups but also the Mafia, setting a precedent for the covert operations that would define the RCMP's intelligence wing for decades.

The violence of this era was not limited to the hunt for foreign radicals. In 1935, the RCMP found itself at the center of a domestic political crisis that would shock the nation. Acting as the provincial police service for Saskatchewan, and against the explicit wishes of the Saskatchewan government, the Mounties collaborated with the Regina Police Service to break up the On-to-Ottawa Trek. This trek was a protest movement organized by unemployed men in relief camps, who were marching to the capital to demand better conditions. In the Germantown neighbourhood's market square, the RCMP attempted to arrest the organizers by kettling, or trapping, approximately 300 rally-goers. The tactic sparked the Regina Riot, a violent confrontation that left one city police officer and one protester dead. The trek failed to reach Ottawa, but the political reverberations were profound, exposing the deep class tensions of the Great Depression and the heavy hand of the state.

During the interwar period, the RCMP's role in labor disputes was equally contentious. The force employed special constables to assist with strikebreaking, effectively acting as a private army for business interests against organized labor. In the late 1930s, the RCMP even affiliated with the Legion of Frontiersmen, a volunteer militia group prepared to serve as an auxiliary police service. Many Mounties belonged to this organization, blurring the lines between state authority and paramilitary volunteerism. It was a time when the force was less a protector of the law and more an enforcer of a specific social and economic order.

The Second World War brought new challenges and new frontiers for the RCMP. In 1940, the force achieved a historic milestone when the RCMP schooner St. Roch facilitated the first effective patrol of Canada's Arctic territory. The vessel became a symbol of Canadian sovereignty in the north, achieving a series of navigational firsts: it was the first to navigate the Northwest Passage from west to east, taking two years; the first to do so in a single season in 1944; and the first to sail either way through the passage in one season. In 1950, it completed the first circumnavigation of North America. These feats were not just nautical curiosities; they were assertions of control over a remote and strategic region.

However, the war also exposed the racial prejudices entrenched within the force. In 1941, two African-Canadian men from Nova Scotia applied to join the RCMP. The commissioner at the time, Stuart Wood, allegedly allowed them to sit for the entrance tests not in the spirit of inclusion, but in the hopes that they could be definitively refused entry. The rationale was that "their colour would raise the question of policy." Both men passed the rigorous tests, yet neither was offered employment. It would take nearly three more decades for the barrier to fall. In 1969, the RCMP finally hired its first black police officer, Hartley Gosline, marking a slow and painful shift toward a more representative force.

The post-war era saw the RCMP pivot once again, this time toward the Cold War and the complexities of international espionage. The defection of Soviet cipher clerk Igor Gouzenko in 1945 revealed that the Soviet Union was actively spying on Western nations, sending shockwaves through the Canadian government. In response, the RCMP separated its domestic intelligence and counter-espionage units from the Criminal Investigation Branch, creating the new Special Branch in 1950. This unit would change names twice, becoming the Directorate of Security and Intelligence in 1962 and finally the Security Service in 1970. This evolution reflected a growing recognition that the threat to national security had shifted from internal political radicals to sophisticated foreign intelligence networks.

The integration of the RCMP into the global policing community also accelerated. On April 1, 1949, Newfoundland and Labrador joined Confederation, and the Newfoundland Ranger Force amalgamated with the RCMP, further expanding the force's geographic reach. In June 1953, the RCMP became a full member of the International Criminal Police Organization (Interpol), cementing its role in international law enforcement. The 1960s brought technological innovation; an agreement with the Canadian Press allowed the RCMP to transmit images and fingerprints of criminals using the press organization's wirephoto equipment, a precursor to the digital databases of today.

The 1970s and 1980s were defined by a renewed focus on the symbolic identity of the Mountie and the urgent need for specialized tactical units. On July 4, 1973, during a visit to Regina, Saskatchewan, Queen Elizabeth II approved a new badge for the RCMP. The force subsequently presented the sovereign with a tapestry rendering of the new design, a gesture that highlighted the deep connection between the monarchy and the police service. Despite the name, the Royal Canadian Mounted Police are no longer an actual mounted police service in their daily operations. Horses are used only at ceremonial events and certain other occasions, and the iconic red serge, Stetson hat, and boots are reserved for these formal moments. The Government of Canada considers the RCMP to be an unofficial national symbol, and in 2013, 87 per cent of Canadians interviewed by Statistics Canada said that the RCMP was important to their national identity. This enduring cultural resonance persists even as the force operates as a modern, high-tech agency.

The reality of modern policing, however, required capabilities far beyond the ceremonial. In 1978, the RCMP formed 31 part-time emergency response teams across the country to handle serious incidents requiring a tactical police response. The need for a full-time, specialized unit became undeniable in the wake of the 1985 Turkish embassy attack in Ottawa and the bombing of Air India Flight 182. These tragedies, which claimed hundreds of lives, exposed gaps in Canada's counter-terrorism capabilities. In 1986, the Canadian government directed the RCMP to form the Special Emergency Response Team (SERT), a full-time counter-terrorism unit designed to respond to the most extreme threats to national security.

As the force moved into the 1990s, the shadows of its past began to surface in the public eye. In the early 1990s, journalists at the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation's The Fifth Estate opened an investigation into rumors of corruption and misconduct within the force. These inquiries would eventually lead to a broader reckoning with the culture of the RCMP, challenging the romanticized narrative of the unblemished Mountie and forcing the organization to confront the realities of its history. From the Mad Trapper of Rat River to the sophisticated counter-terrorism units of the 21st century, the RCMP has been a mirror of Canada itself—complex, contradictory, and constantly evolving. It is a force that has policed the Arctic ice and the urban streets, infiltrated political movements and protected the monarchy, and in doing so, has become an inseparable part of the Canadian story. The red serge may be retired to the museum, but the legacy of the Mountie remains a living, breathing, and sometimes controversial institution at the heart of the nation.
