Post-literate society

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Post-literate society

In 2009, Patrick Tucker wrote an article for THE FUTURIST Magazine titled 'The Dawn of the Post-literate Age,' marking a specific moment when the academic and speculative conversation about humanity's relationship with text shifted from a distant warning to an imminent reality. This was not a prediction of a sudden, catastrophic loss of the ability to read, but rather an observation of a gradual, seductive displacement. The concept of a post-literate society describes a hypothetical future where multimedia technology has advanced to such a degree that literacy—the mechanical ability to decode written symbols into meaning—is no longer necessary for full participation in society, nor is it common. It is a world where the written word has been usurped by a sensory overload of recorded sounds, broadcast spoken word, static images, and moving visuals. This is not the silence of illiteracy, but the roar of a Global Carnival where the screen has replaced the page.

The term itself carries a heavy intellectual lineage, appearing as early as 1962 in Marshall McLuhan's seminal work, The Gutenberg Galaxy. However, McLuhan's usage was distinct from the modern understanding; he applied the term to the society of his time, arguing that the ubiquity of literacy was already giving way to the electronic age, where the "global village" was being forged by the immediacy of television and radio. He saw the written word, which had organized human thought for centuries, as a technology that was becoming obsolete in the face of a new, all-encompassing media environment. Today, we stand on the precipice of what McLuhan only glimpsed. The post-literate society is not merely a literary device found in the dystopian visions of science fiction; it is a philosophical framework that helps us understand the seismic shift occurring in human cognition, communication, and culture.

To understand the magnitude of this shift, one must first distinguish a post-literate society from the oral cultures that preceded the invention of the printing press. Historical oral cultures, such as those of ancient Greece before the widespread adoption of writing or various indigenous societies, did not deploy writing systems. Their aesthetic traditions were the domain of oral literature, oral history, and memory, aided by the rhythmic structures of art, dance, and singing. In those societies, knowledge was ephemeral, living only as long as the human voice and the collective memory could sustain it. A post-literate society is fundamentally different. It does not lack the tools of writing; it simply chooses not to use them as the primary interface for reality. Instead, the written word is replaced by a vast, digital ecosystem of recorded sounds in the form of CDs and audiobooks, the broadcast spoken word of radio, the static visual power of JPEGs, and the overwhelming dominance of moving images found in television, film, video games, and virtual reality.

This transition is not a return to the primitive, but an evolution into a state of hyper-sophisticated media consumption. In a post-literate world, the population might still include individuals who are aliterate—people who possess the mechanical skill to read and write but consciously choose not to engage with text because the information is more efficiently or pleasurably consumed through other media. They are not incapable; they are indifferent. The barrier to entry for knowledge shifts from the cognitive labor of decoding syntax to the sensory engagement of interpreting visual and auditory data. Consequently, the definition of literacy itself expands and mutates. In this new epoch, most, if not all, people would be media literate, multimedia literate, visually literate, and transliterate. The ability to navigate a complex information environment becomes less about reading a manual and more about intuitively understanding the interface, the flow of data, and the visual language of the screen.

The Fiction of the Future

The concept of the post-literate society has long been a favorite playground for science fiction authors, who often use it to critique the trajectory of modern civilization. These narratives serve as cautionary tales, exploring the psychological and social costs of abandoning the written word. Ray Bradbury's 1953 masterpiece, Fahrenheit 451, remains the most iconic depiction of this phenomenon. In Bradbury's vision, a post-literate society is not one where technology has naturally evolved to make reading obsolete, but one where the state has actively suppressed it to ensure social stability. Books are banned, and "firemen" burn them to prevent the dissent and melancholy that deep reading can provoke. The population is immersed in a sea of interactive walls and "seashell" ear radios, consuming endless streams of shallow entertainment. Bradbury's society illustrates the danger of a culture that prioritizes speed and comfort over the slow, often uncomfortable process of critical engagement with text.

Dan Simmons takes the concept in a different direction in his 2009 novel Ilium. Here, the post-literate condition is the result of a merger between human consciousness and artificial intelligence, where the written word becomes an archaic relic, a dusty artifact of a primitive past. The characters navigate a world where communication is instantaneous and telepathic, rendering the linear progression of reading and writing a clumsy, inefficient method of data transfer. Simmons explores the loss of the individual narrative voice when the collective, instantaneous flow of information takes precedence. The written word, with its requirement for solitude and linear thought, is incompatible with the hive-mind efficiency of his futuristic world.

Gary Shteyngart's 2009 novel, Super Sad True Love Story, offers a more immediate and biting critique, set in a near-future America where the collapse of the economy and the rise of a Chinese-dominated global order have accelerated the decline of literacy. In Shteyngart's world, characters carry "a-phones" that display their social credit scores, credit ratings, and real-time emotional states to everyone around them. Reading is viewed as a sign of mental illness or a lack of social sophistication. The protagonist, Lenny, is one of the few who still reads books, and his obsession with literature marks him as an alien in his own culture. Shteyngart's work is particularly relevant because it bridges the gap between the speculative and the plausible, showing how the erosion of literacy can happen not through censorship, but through the sheer seduction of digital distraction and the commodification of attention.

These fictional worlds are not merely flights of fancy; they are philosophical laboratories. They allow us to test the limits of human cognition when the primary tool for organizing thought—the written word—is removed. They force us to ask what happens to our ability to engage in abstract reasoning, to construct complex arguments, or to maintain a sense of historical continuity when our primary mode of interaction is visual and immediate. The post-literate society, as depicted in these novels, is often a society of the present, where the written word is already losing its hegemony to the image.

The Philosophy of the Image

The philosophical relevance of the post-literate society extends far beyond the realm of fiction, grounding itself in the work of Marshall McLuhan and his "Global Carnival Theory." McLuhan argued that the medium is the message, suggesting that the form of the communication shapes the content of human association and thought more profoundly than the content itself. In a post-literate society, the medium shifts from the printed page to the electronic image. This shift fundamentally alters the structure of human perception. The printed page encourages linearity, logic, and individualism. It requires the reader to move their eyes across a static line of text, processing one symbol after another in a sequential order. This fosters a type of thinking that is analytical, detached, and focused on cause and effect.

In contrast, the electronic image is simultaneous, immersive, and holistic. A video, a video game, or a virtual reality environment does not require the user to decode symbols sequentially. Instead, it presents a flood of sensory data that must be processed all at once. The user is not a detached observer but an active participant in a swirling carnival of sound and light. McLuhan saw this as a return to the tribal, oral modes of thinking, but on a global scale. He called this the "Global Carnival," a state where the boundaries between the self and the world, between the observer and the observed, are dissolved by the immediacy of the electronic medium. In this carnival, the written word is just another prop, a static element in a dynamic, moving landscape.

This philosophical shift has profound implications for how we understand truth, history, and identity. In a literate society, truth is often found in the text, in the argument, in the evidence presented in writing. History is a linear narrative constructed from documents and records. In a post-literate society, truth becomes more fluid, more subjective, and more dependent on the emotional resonance of the image. History is no longer a story told in words but a montage of clips, memes, and viral moments that can be re-edited and re-contextualized in real-time. The stability of the written word is replaced by the volatility of the digital stream. This is not to say that truth disappears, but that it becomes a matter of perception and engagement rather than verification and documentation.

The rise of the post-literate culture is also tied to the concept of "transliteracy," which is the ability to read, write, and interact across a range of platforms, tools, and media from signing and orality through handwriting, print, TV, radio and film, to digital social networks. It is the skill of navigating the complex interplay between the old and the new, the text and the image. In a post-literate society, being literate in the traditional sense is insufficient. One must be able to interpret the visual language of a meme, the auditory cues of a podcast, and the spatial logic of a video game. The mind must be agile, capable of switching between different modes of communication and understanding the unique affordances of each.

The Rise of the Post-Literate Age

The transition to a post-literate society is not a future event; it is a current reality that is accelerating with every technological innovation. The nonfiction works of Neil Postman and Chris Hedges provide a critical lens through which to view this phenomenon. In his 1985 book Amusing Ourselves to Death, Neil Postman argued that the public discourse in America was shifting from the age of exposition, dominated by the printed word and logical argument, to the age of show business, dominated by the television and visual entertainment. He warned that the medium of television, with its requirement for visual appeal and emotional engagement, was making serious political, religious, and educational discourse impossible. The content of the message was being sacrificed to the format of the medium. Postman's prediction has proven to be prophetic. The television has been replaced by the smartphone, the computer, and the streaming service, but the underlying dynamic remains the same. The written word is increasingly marginalized in public life, relegated to the margins of a world that demands instant visual gratification.

Chris Hedges, in his 2009 book Empire of Illusion, takes this critique a step further. He observes a sudden rise of a post-literate culture where the line between fact and fiction, between reality and the spectacle, has completely collapsed. Hedges argues that we are living in an empire of illusions, where the media constructs a reality that is more appealing than the actual world. In this world, the written word, with its demand for nuance and complexity, is seen as a barrier to the immediate emotional experience. The post-literate culture, according to Hedges, is a culture of denial, where the population refuses to engage with the harsh realities of their lives because the media offers a more comfortable, more entertaining alternative. The rise of the post-literate age is not just a technological shift; it is a moral and psychological crisis.

The phenomenon of "pivoting to video" in the digital marketing world is a clear indicator of this shift. Content creators, educators, and businesses are increasingly abandoning text-based content in favor of video. The logic is simple: video gets more engagement, more clicks, and more shares. The written word is seen as slow, boring, and difficult. The "a-phones" of Shteyngart's novel are becoming a reality, with devices that prioritize video and audio over text. The "cyberculture" of the internet, once a text-heavy domain of forums and email, has become a visual and auditory landscape of TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube. The written word is not dead, but it is increasingly confined to the background, a tool for the few who still value it, while the majority of the population navigates the world through the lens of the screen.

The "a-phones" and the "seashell" radios of fiction are now our smartphones and our AirPods. We are surrounded by a post-literate environment where the written word is just one option among many, and often not the preferred one. The rise of audiobooks, podcasts, and streaming video has created a society where information can be consumed without ever looking at a page. This is not a regression; it is an adaptation. The human mind is remarkably plastic, capable of adjusting to new forms of communication. But the cost of this adaptation is the loss of the specific cognitive skills that literacy provides. The ability to engage in deep, sustained, critical thought is being eroded by the constant stream of visual and auditory stimuli.

The Asemic Frontier

As we move deeper into the post-literate age, we are also seeing the emergence of new forms of expression that challenge the very definition of writing. Asemic writing is a wordless, open semantic form of writing that uses the form of writing without any specific semantic content. It is a visual art form that mimics the look of text but contains no readable words. It is the ultimate expression of the post-literate condition: the form of writing without the function. Asemic writing suggests that the visual structure of language has become more important than its meaning. It is a bridge between the written word and the visual image, a reminder that the power of language lies not just in its ability to convey information, but in its ability to evoke emotion and create aesthetic experiences.

The post-literate society is not a utopia or a dystopia; it is a reality that is unfolding before our eyes. It is a society where the written word is no longer the primary mode of communication, but where the visual and the auditory have taken center stage. It is a society where the Global Carnival is in full swing, and where the individual is swept up in the flow of the media. The challenge for us is not to resist this shift, but to understand it, to navigate it, and to find a way to preserve the values of literacy in a world that no longer requires it. We must be media literate, visually literate, and transliterate. We must be able to read the image, to understand the sound, and to navigate the complex interplay of the digital world. The post-literate society is here, and it is up to us to decide what kind of world we want to live in.

The events and facts described in the historical record of this transition are documented. From McLuhan's early warnings in 1962 to Tucker's declaration in 2009, the trajectory has been clear. The rise of the post-literate culture is not a matter of speculation; it is a matter of observation. We are living in the dawn of a new age, an age where the written word is no longer the master, but the servant of a more powerful, more immediate, and more immersive medium. The question is no longer whether we will become a post-literate society, but how we will survive the transition and what we will lose and gain in the process. The Global Carnival is calling, and the written word is just one of the many voices in the choir. The future is visual, auditory, and immersive. The future is post-literate.
