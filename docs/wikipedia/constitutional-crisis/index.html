Constitutional crisis

14 min read

Based on Wikipedia: Constitutional crisis

In September 1960, the Congo was a nation tearing itself apart at the seams. President Joseph Kasavubu ordered the dismissal of Prime Minister Patrice Lumumba. Lumumba refused to go, declaring the President's order illegal. Two men, two titles, one country, and zero clear answers in the rulebook on who actually held the power to fire whom. Within weeks, General Mobutu Sese Seko stepped into the vacuum, deposed both men in a coup, and then paradoxically restored Kasavubu to a throne that no longer functioned. This was not merely a political squabble; it was a constitutional crisis, the precise moment when the machinery of government grinds to a halt because the manual no longer fits the machine.

In the realm of political science, a constitutional crisis is defined as a problem or conflict in the function of a government that the political constitution—or any fundamental governing law—is perceived to be unable to resolve. It is the breakdown of the social contract's legal architecture. But definitions are cold things. In reality, a crisis is the heat generated when the rubber meets the road of history and the tire blows out. There are variations to this definition, of course. One might describe it as the crisis arising from the failure, or at least the terrifying risk of failure, of a constitution to perform its central functions. The crisis may arise from a variety of possible causes, each a different flavor of legal despair.

Consider the government that wants to pass a law but knows it is contrary to its own constitution. Or the constitution that is silent, failing to provide a clear answer for a specific, unprecedented situation. Sometimes, the constitution is crystal clear, yet it is politically infeasible to follow it. Perhaps the government institutions themselves falter, failing to live up to the idealized role the law prescribes for them. Or, most dangerously, officials may justify avoiding a serious problem based on narrow, self-serving interpretations of the law. When the rules of the game are ignored by the players, the game ends.

The consequences of such a breakdown are rarely abstract. A constitutional crisis can lead to administrative paralysis, where the state simply stops moving. It can result in the eventual collapse of the government, the total loss of political legitimacy, or a slide into democratic backsliding. In the most extreme cases, it precipitates civil war. It is crucial, however, to distinguish a constitutional crisis from a rebellion. A rebellion occurs when political factions outside the government challenge the government's sovereignty, as in a coup d'état or a revolution led by the military or civilians. A constitutional crisis is different. It happens within the system. It results when one or more parties to a political dispute willfully choose to violate a law of the constitution, flout an unwritten constitutional convention, or dispute the judicial interpretation of a constitutional law. It is a war fought with legal briefs and executive orders, not just rifles.

The American Precedent: Ambiguity and Power

The United States has not been immune to these tremors. In fact, some of the most defining moments of American governance were born from the cracks in the constitutional edifice. Take the XYZ Affair. A contingent of American commissioners was sent to preserve peace between France and the United States, only to be met with demands for bribery. The incident, when published in the American press, created a foreign policy crisis that precipitated the passage of the Alien and Sedition Acts. These acts, designed to stifle dissent, were immediately challenged. The Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions cited that the acts violated freedom of speech and exhorted states to refuse their enforcement, arguing they violated the Constitution. The crisis was not resolved by a single decree but by a deepening of the national debate on the limits of federal power.

But ambiguity is often the true architect of crisis. When a crisis arises because the constitution is legally ambiguous, the ultimate resolution usually establishes the legal precedent that resolves future crises of constitutional administration. The classic example is the succession of John Tyler. When President William Henry Harrison died in 1841, the Constitution stated the powers and duties of the president would "devolve on the Vice President." It did not explicitly say the Vice President became President. Tyler, however, moved into the White House immediately, declaring himself the President without limitation. His rivals argued he was merely an "Acting President." Tyler's refusal to step down established the precedent that a successor assumes the full office, a rule that has held for every subsequent succession.

Yet, not all crises are resolved so peacefully. The secession of the southern U.S. states in 1860 and 1861 remains the most catastrophic failure of constitutional interpretation in American history. The constitution provided no clear mechanism for a state to leave the union, nor did it clearly define the federal government's power to prevent it. When the political dispute could not be contained within the legal framework, the result was not a court case, but a four-year war that reshaped the nation.

Global Tremors: From Rhodesia to the Gambia

The phenomenon is not unique to the West. In the mid-20th century, the British Empire faced its own constitutional reckoning in Rhodesia. Amid demands from British politicians to enfranchise the black majority population as a condition for independence, the white minority government of Ian Smith unilaterally declared independence in 1965. The UK rejected the declaration and continued to claim sovereignty, leaving the territory in a legal limbo that lasted until a framework for independence and black enfranchisement was negotiated in the 1979 Lancaster House Agreement. For fourteen years, the colony existed in a state of suspended constitutional animation, a lawless zone created by a refusal to accept the constitutional order of the empire.

In Africa, the pattern of crisis often involves the violent intersection of military power and constitutional succession. In 2012, Malawi experienced a crisis regarding the succession of President Bingu wa Mutharika. The President and Vice-President were from different parties, leading to fierce deliberations over who the rightful successor would be. The constitution was clear on the paper, but the political reality was a minefield. Vice-President Joyce Banda eventually succeeded wa Mutharika, but only after a period of intense uncertainty that threatened the stability of the young democracy.

The Gambia provided a more dramatic, albeit shorter, example. Following the victory of Adama Barrow in the 2016 presidential election, the incumbent president, Yahya Jammeh, rejected the results and refused to step down. On 17 January, Jammeh declared a 90-day state of emergency in a desperate attempt to extend his term of office. The constitutional crisis escalated until Senegalese, Ghanaian, and Nigerian forces entered the Gambia on 19 January to enforce the election results. On 21 January, Jammeh stepped down and left the country. Here, the crisis was resolved not by a court, but by the threat of regional military intervention, highlighting how constitutional failures often bleed into questions of sovereignty and force.

Egypt's recent history offers a harrowing case study in the collapse and rebuilding of constitutional order. The country experienced a constitutional crisis when President Hosni Mubarak was removed in the Egyptian Revolution. The country was left without a president until President Mohamed Morsi was elected. But the crisis was far from over. It resurfaced when Morsi was arrested by the Egyptian Armed Forces in a 2013 coup d'état, leaving the nation in a state of legal flux until President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi took office. In each instance, the constitutional framework was bent, broken, or entirely rewritten by the sheer weight of political upheaval.

The Modern Era: Crises in the 21st Century

As we move deeper into the 21st century, the nature of constitutional crises has evolved, often becoming more complex due to the interplay of established democracies and emerging authoritarian tendencies. In 2024, a crisis developed in Somalia when the Somali President, Hassan Sheikh Mohamud, changed the Constitution of Somalia on 30 April 2024. The change was immediately opposed by the President of Puntland, Said Abdullahi Deni, followed by the President of Jubaland. As a result, Puntland withdrew its recognition of the Federal Government of Somalia and declared itself an independent state based on Article 4 of the Puntland Constitution. Jubaland state meanwhile cut its relations with the Somali government. The central authority, attempting to solidify its power, inadvertently fractured the federation, proving that constitutional amendments can sometimes be the spark that ignites a larger explosion.

The year 2024 also brought a crisis to Bangladesh. Following the resignation and ouster of Sheikh Hasina, which ended her 16-year rule through a popular mass uprising, the country faced a period of political unrest marked by a disputed succession of power. The vacuum left by a long-standing autocrat created a situation where the existing constitutional mechanisms were insufficient to manage the transition, leading to a period of instability that required international attention and domestic negotiation.

Perhaps the most striking recent example occurred in Georgia. The 2024–2025 Georgian constitutional crisis began leading up to the 2024 Georgian parliamentary election. Oligarch Bidzina Ivanishvili had established de facto control over government institutions. The elections resulted in a majority for his party, Georgian Dream, amid widespread reports of irregularities and suppression. President Salome Zourabichvili announced that they would be re-run. Despite this, the new parliament convened and elected Mikheil Kavelashvili as president with opposition parties boycotting. Zourabichvili vacated her official residence on 29 December 2024 but maintained, along with opposition parties, that she was the legitimate president. The country found itself with two presidents, one elected by a disputed parliament and one claiming legitimacy from the people and the original constitution. It was a crisis of legitimacy, where the law and the political reality were speaking two different languages.

The Institutional Failure: When the Guard Dogs Turn

One of the most insidious forms of constitutional crisis occurs when the institutions themselves fail to live up to the law. This was demonstrated in the 1950s in South Africa during the Coloured vote constitutional crisis. The National Party government disputed a Supreme Court decision overturning its Separate Representation of Voters Act, which was designed to disenfranchise Coloured voters in the Cape Province. The court ruled the act illegal. The government's attempt to reverse the decision in an ad hoc court was also overturned. The government, faced with a clear legal barrier, did not back down. Instead, it used reforms to the Senate to pass the measure legally. By packing the Senate with supporters, they changed the rules to make their illegal goal legal. This was a constitutional crisis where the government manipulated the constitution itself to bypass the judiciary, effectively neutering the checks and balances designed to protect minority rights.

Similar institutional fractures occurred in Asia. The 1966 Sarawak constitutional crisis started by a group of politicians who were dissatisfied towards Stephen Kalong Ningkan's leadership as chief minister. Ningkan was later removed from the chief minister post by the Governor of Sarawak in June 1966. The crisis was a clash between the executive and the legislative branches, with the Governor stepping in to resolve a dispute that the local political machinery could not handle. In 1988, Malaysia faced a constitutional crisis that began with the United Malays National Organisation (UMNO) party elections in 1987 and ended with the suspension and the eventual removal of Lord President of the Supreme Court Tun Salleh Abas from his seat. This was not a dispute over policy, but a direct assault on the independence of the judiciary. The executive branch, led by Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad, successfully amended the constitution to make the monarchies more accountable to their actions in 1993, but the 1988 events marked a turning point where the rule of law was subordinated to political expediency.

The Distinction: Crisis vs. Rebellion

It is vital to understand that a constitutional crisis is distinct from a rebellion. A rebellion, such as a coup d'état or a revolution led by the military or by civilians, is an external challenge to the government's sovereignty. In a constitutional crisis, the players are often still inside the arena, playing by a set of rules that everyone agrees are broken. The crisis results when one or more parties to a political dispute willfully chooses to violate a law of the constitution or to flout an unwritten constitutional convention. They do not necessarily try to overthrow the government; they try to reshape it to their will, often using the constitution as a weapon against its own spirit.

This distinction is evident in the 1953 Iranian crisis. Mohammed Reza Pahlavi's dismissal of Prime Minister Mohammed Mossadegh and Mossadegh's subsequent refusal to quit the office, led to the 1953 Iranian coup d'état. The initial conflict was a constitutional crisis: the Shah and the Prime Minister were in a legal standoff over who had the authority to dismiss whom. Mossadegh argued his position was protected by the constitution; the Shah argued otherwise. When the legal battle could not be resolved, it escalated into a coup, moving the conflict from a constitutional crisis to a rebellion. The crisis was the precursor; the coup was the violent conclusion.

The Uncodified Conundrum

Not all nations have a single document to call their constitution. The United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland does not have a codified constitution. It is deemed to have an uncodified one, composed of statutes, common law, and conventions. Yet, issues and crises in the UK and its constituent countries are still described as constitutional crises. In a system without a single written text, the crisis often revolves around the violation of unwritten conventions. When a government acts in a way that breaks the spirit of the unwritten rules, even if it is technically legal, a crisis ensues. The flexibility of the UK system allows for adaptation, but it also means that the boundaries of power are less defined, making crises harder to predict and harder to resolve.

The Future of Constitutional Stability

The history of constitutional crises is a history of human failure and resilience. From the XYZ Affair to the 2024 Georgian crisis, the pattern remains consistent: a gap between the law and reality, a refusal to compromise, and a system pushed to its breaking point. A constitutional crisis can range from minor to requiring a new constitution. It can lead to administrative paralysis and eventual collapse of the government, the loss of political legitimacy, democratic backsliding, or to civil war. It is the moment when the social contract is tested, and the question is no longer about who wins the election, but whether the rules of the game still exist.

In the course of government, the crisis results when one or more of the parties to a political dispute willfully chooses to violate a law of the constitution or to flout an unwritten constitutional convention. The resolution of these crises often establishes the legal precedent for the future. John Tyler's succession, the South African Senate reforms, the Malaysian judicial suspension—each of these events carved a new path in the legal landscape. But the cost is high. Every crisis erodes the trust in institutions. Every time a constitution is bent or broken, the foundation of the state is weakened.

The 2020 Malaysian constitutional crisis, which began when Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad faced a challenge to his leadership, and the subsequent 2024 Bangladesh crisis, which followed the ouster of a long-standing ruler, serve as reminders that no system is immune. The world is full of constitutions, written and unwritten, codified and fragmented. But a constitution is only as strong as the will of the people and their leaders to abide by it. When that will fractures, the crisis begins. And when the crisis begins, the only question left is how the nation will survive the fallout.

"A constitutional crisis is the moment when the rules stop working and the real power plays begin."

The examples are countless, the patterns clear. From the dismissal of the Australian federal government in 1975 to the 2007 Ukrainian crisis, the world has seen it all. The crisis is a warning. It tells us that the law is not a magic shield. It is a tool, and like any tool, it can be used to build or to destroy. The responsibility lies not in the text of the constitution, but in the hands of those who interpret it. When they fail, the crisis arrives. And when the crisis arrives, everything is on the line.
