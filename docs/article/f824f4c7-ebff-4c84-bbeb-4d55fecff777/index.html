Real Lawyer Reacts to Suits

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Sep 11, 2018 · 26 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just watch legal dramas; he dissects them with the precision of a practicing attorney who knows exactly where the scriptwriters cut corners. In his breakdown of Suits, Stone argues that the show's most dramatic moments aren't just unrealistic—they are often criminal acts that would instantly end a real lawyer's career. For busy professionals who appreciate the gap between Hollywood fantasy and courtroom reality, this analysis reveals why the show's "genius" lawyers are actually walking violations of the bar code.

The Architecture of Fraud

Stone immediately dismantles the show's central premise regarding deal-making. He points out that the series frequently depicts money moving before terms are finalized, a scenario that would never survive scrutiny in the real world. "No transaction on the planet would have someone transfer a huge amount of money before the deal is consummated," Stone writes. He explains that legitimate transactions rely on escrow companies to hold funds until every condition is met, noting that any lawyer drafting a deal with premature wire transfers would be guilty of malpractice.

This distinction is crucial because it highlights how the show prioritizes plot momentum over procedural integrity. Stone goes further when analyzing a character who fabricates facts to close a deal. "When you induce someone based on fraud that is called fraud and the inducement and when you commit fraud in the inducement that deal is null and void," he explains. The commentary lands hard because it reframes a "clever" TV plot twist as a career-ending ethical violation. In reality, lying to a client to secure a deal isn't a sign of a shark; it's a sign of someone who should be disbarred.

Critics might argue that television requires high-stakes drama that real-life bureaucracy rarely provides, but Stone's point remains that the show ignores the fundamental duty of loyalty a lawyer owes their client.

The Ethics of Hiring and the "Rainmaker" Myth

The analysis shifts to the show's portrayal of recruitment and the infamous "Harvard only" hiring policy. Stone acknowledges that while firms are selective, the show exaggerates the exclusivity. "There probably aren't any law firms that hire only from Harvard," Stone notes, clarifying that top firms typically recruit from the top five to twenty schools. He validates the show's depiction of the "summer associate" program and the hotel-based interview process, calling it "spot on" regarding the logistics of on-campus interviews.

However, the commentary takes a sharp turn when addressing the protagonist's lack of a law degree. Stone identifies a massive ethical hole: knowingly hiring a non-lawyer to perform legal work. "If he is knowingly sort of conspiring to have someone who's not a member of the bar participate in these activities that is a huge problem both for Mike but also for Harvey," Stone argues. He emphasizes that this isn't just a plot device; it exposes the senior partner to losing their own license for the unauthorized practice of law.

"Good lawyers don't lie. Good lawyers use the facts as they exist in the real world to make sure that all the parties get their best deal."

Stone also touches on the "rainmaker" dynamic, explaining that partners who bring in clients are vital, but their behavior is still subject to strict ethical oversight. He notes that the partnership model creates "joint and several liability," meaning if one partner defrauds a client, the entire firm is at risk. This adds a layer of corporate realism that the show often glosses over in favor of individual heroics.

Sexual Harassment and Procedural Missteps

Perhaps the most sobering section of Stone's commentary addresses the show's handling of sexual harassment. When a character describes being told to sleep with a superior for a promotion, Stone identifies this as a textbook case of "quid pro quo sexual harassment." He explains that while this is "totally illegal," it is notoriously difficult to prove in court due to the "he said, she said" nature of the evidence. This adds necessary gravity to a plotline that the show often treats as a minor interpersonal conflict.

Finally, Stone critiques the show's casual approach to legal procedure, specifically the filling out of subpoenas. He points out that a summer associate filling out a subpoena without attorney supervision is effectively engaging in the unauthorized practice of law. "If he files a subpoena... and signs it he has effectively pretended to be an attorney," Stone writes. He argues that the show's characters should be terrified of this action, as it could lead to sanctions and disbarment for the entire team. The show treats it as a comedic beat; Stone treats it as a fireable offense.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's commentary succeeds because it refuses to let the audience off the hook for accepting Hollywood's version of the law. His strongest argument is that the show's "genius" is actually a catalog of ethical violations that would destroy a real career. The biggest vulnerability in the show's logic is its assumption that the law is a game of wits rather than a system of strict accountability. For the smart, busy reader, this breakdown offers a fascinating glimpse into the high-stakes reality that Suits pretends to be.
