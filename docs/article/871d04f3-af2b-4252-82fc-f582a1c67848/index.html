US Hits Russia Ahead of Peace Talks

Good Times Bad Times · Good Times Bad Times ·Jan 18, 2025 · 21 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a landscape often dominated by wishful thinking, Good Times Bad Times offers a stark, unvarnished assessment of the war's trajectory: the battlefield reality is dictating the terms of the upcoming peace talks, not the other way around. While Washington pushes for a "peace through escalation" strategy, the author argues that the incoming US administration is inadvertently accelerating a Russian victory by demanding Ukrainian concessions that the front line simply cannot support. This is not a story about diplomatic breakthroughs, but about the brutal arithmetic of attrition where Russian momentum meets Ukrainian structural decay.

The Illusion of Leverage

The author's central thesis challenges the prevailing Western narrative that military pressure alone will force Moscow to the negotiating table. Good Times Bad Times writes, "the outgoing US Administration... is inflicting further blows on the Russians to force them into submission before the upcoming talks." This framing suggests a dangerous disconnect between American strategy and the ground truth in Eastern Europe. The commentary argues that while the US hopes to strengthen its hand, it is actually pushing Kyiv into a corner where it must lower the conscription age to 18, a move the author deems counterproductive.

The piece highlights a grim irony: as the US tightens sanctions, Ukraine is actively dismantling the very infrastructure that could sustain a long war. Good Times Bad Times notes that Ukrainians are "closing Europe's largest gas artery effectively ending the era of Russian gas Triumph on the old continent," yet this victory comes at the cost of their own energy independence and leverage. The author points out that Gazprom has been forced to sack 40% of its management, but questions whether this administrative shakeup translates to battlefield success.

Critics might argue that ending Russian gas exports is a strategic necessity regardless of the cost, but the author insists this is a symptom of a deeper desperation rather than a calculated win. The text suggests that the "American peace through escalation strategy" is failing to account for the fact that Ukraine is running out of the resources needed to execute it.

"The subject of the mobilization and overall effectiveness of the Ukrainian Army is a much more complex topic than just the age of conscription."

The Frontline Reality

Moving from strategy to the map, the author provides a granular, often depressing, update on territorial changes. The narrative shifts from high-level geopolitics to the specific, grinding losses in the Donetsk and Kharkiv regions. Good Times Bad Times observes that "the extent of Ukrainian control has shrunk since early December particularly in the North near Novo Ivanovka." The author details how Russian forces, bolstered by North Korean troops, are exploiting Ukrainian weaknesses with "frontal attacks in groups of 20 to 100 in open terrain which end tragically for them."

The coverage of the Pokrovsk axis is particularly damning. The author explains that Pokrovsk is not just a logistics hub but the gateway to Ukraine's metallurgical industry, noting that a key coke plant there supplies "up to 90% of Ukraine's demand for Coke." As Russian forces close in, the plant has suspended operations, and the author warns that "tactically for Ukrainians it will important to remain in control of Udacha and Ktil." The loss of these high-ground positions would be catastrophic, yet the author suggests the defense is crumbling.

The text paints a picture of a Russian advance that is methodical and relentless. Good Times Bad Times writes, "the Russians are basically already defending the outskirts of the city with the Russians at the gates." This is not a stalemate; it is a slow-motion collapse. The author notes that in some areas, Russian forces are "less than 10 kilometers from the Dnipro region," signaling a potential strategic encirclement that could sever Ukraine's western supply lines entirely.

Internal Rot and Structural Failure

Perhaps the most striking part of the commentary is its deep dive into the internal failures of the Ukrainian military, drawing on analysis from Tatary Gam of Front Intelligence Insight. The author argues that blaming the West for a lack of weapons is a "simplistic and misleading" distraction from Ukraine's own systemic rot. Good Times Bad Times quotes the analyst: "when battalions desperately need food soldiers they start pulling people from crucial support roles... this creates a deadly domino effect."

The author highlights a culture of fear and incompetence within the command structure, where reporting problems is "condemned so it is better not to do it." This leads to a situation where units are "full only on paper" while the social fabric of the army erodes. The commentary points out that the 155th Brigade, despite French training and Western arms, suffers from high absent-without-leave rates and low effectiveness, proving that equipment cannot fix a broken culture.

Good Times Bad Times writes, "the system all the time allows wealthier and higher status individuals to avoid conscription compounding the sense of Injustice." This inequity is driving a wedge between the state and its citizens, making mobilization increasingly difficult. The author suggests that the army is suffering from a "Soviet element" in its structures, where senior officers promoted during peacetime lack combat experience and resist necessary reforms.

"Is Ukraine meant to be just a buffer State a bloody Frontier between the EU and Russia's authoritarian regime?"

The author also touches on the psychological toll of perceived abandonment. The text questions whether Ukraine is being used as a "bloody Frontier" while Europe hides behind American protection. This sentiment, the author argues, is fueling the perception of abandonment that is as damaging as any lack of ammunition. The commentary suggests that without a fundamental restructuring of the command hierarchy and a merit-based promotion system, no amount of Western aid will turn the tide.

The Diplomatic Clock

As the piece concludes, it turns to the political horizon, specifically the incoming Trump administration. The author notes that figures like Michael Waltz and Keith Kellogg are pushing for a deal within 100 days of inauguration, but the author remains skeptical. Good Times Bad Times writes, "you can't enter a deal if you don't have some type of relationship and dialogue with the other side." The author implies that the US is rushing toward a settlement that the battlefield does not yet support.

The commentary suggests that Washington is pressuring Kyiv to lower the conscription age to 18, a move the author sees as a desperate gamble. Good Times Bad Times notes that Kyiv has replied that "there is little point in mobilizing thousands of young men as they have nothing to arm them with." This stalemate between American expectations and Ukrainian reality is the central tension of the piece. The author warns that if the US forces a premature peace, it may simply be codifying a Russian victory.

Bottom Line

Good Times Bad Times delivers a necessary corrective to the optimism of Western media, grounding the discussion in the hard realities of territorial loss and internal military dysfunction. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to separate the battlefield from the bureaucracy, showing how command failures and social injustice are actively undermining the war effort. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its somewhat fatalistic tone, which risks underestimating the resilience of the Ukrainian population and the potential for rapid, unforeseen shifts in the conflict. Readers should watch closely to see if the incoming US administration can bridge the gap between its diplomatic timeline and the grim reality on the map.
