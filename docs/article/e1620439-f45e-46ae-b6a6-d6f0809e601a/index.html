The bus flying into the ditch

Various · Geopolitical Dispatch ·Apr 1, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In an era obsessed with algorithmic foresight, Damien of Geopolitical Dispatch delivers a jarring, necessary correction: the future is not a puzzle to be solved, but a chaotic force to be navigated. The piece's most striking claim is that the collapse of predictability is not a temporary glitch, but the new permanent operating system of global affairs, rendering our hunger for data-driven forecasts not just futile, but dangerous.

The End of the Illusion of Control

The author opens by dismantling the comforting narratives of 2025, describing a year where "threats were made that later proved empty while others — presumed to be bluff, bluster, or bravado — were carried out." This inversion of expectations sets the stage for a broader critique of how we process geopolitical risk. The text argues that the "rules-based international order" has been discarded for a brutal return to "spheres of influence" and "might makes right," a shift that has left institutions and analysts scrambling.

The commentary is particularly effective when it dissects the failure of military planning. The author notes that despite the obvious strategic risks, the United States Department of War failed to anticipate the closure of the Strait of Hormuz following an attack on Iran. To underscore this incompetence, the piece invokes a damning observation from former Secretary of Defense Robert Gates: "When it comes to predicting the nature and location of our next military engagements, since Vietnam, our record has been perfect. We have never once gotten it right." This historical anchor adds weight to the argument that institutional memory is often a myth. Critics might argue that the article leans too heavily on American failures, potentially overlooking similar blunders by other global powers, but the specific focus on the driver of the global system serves the central metaphor well.

The Hydra and the Staircase

The author then pivots to the limitations of historical analogy. We naturally reach for the "Concert of Europe" or the Cold War to make sense of today, but the text argues these comparisons fail because they assumed a baseline of stability that no longer exists. The nineteenth-century system was fundamentally about Western domination, whereas today, "the collective power of the West and the internal cohesion of its nations are both on the wane." Furthermore, the Cold War's "mutual assured destruction" created a perverse stability that is absent now.

To capture the visceral feeling of this instability, the piece leans heavily on the Georgian historian Boris Akunin. The author describes Akunin's metaphor of a "staircase that is not only descending, but becoming steeper with every step," creating a sensation of "growing madness." This is not just pessimism; it is a diagnosis of acceleration. The text expands this into a powerful image of a global bus driven by an erratic American figure: "He jerks the wheel this way and that. He shouts some strange songs. There is a complete feeling that all of this is about to fly into a ditch." This analogy reframes the crisis not as a policy failure, but as a systemic loss of control at the center.

The world in which we all live has simply flown apart into pieces. It has crumbled. And then this image, which I find almost impossible to improve upon: I imagine the world as a huge bus, in which sits a very motley, quarrelsome public, and at the wheel sits an American.

The author suggests that the mythological Lernaean Hydra serves as a grim reminder of modern conflict dynamics. Just as chopping off one head of the Hydra resulted in two growing back, the article notes that President Trump's attempt to "decapitate" Iran's leadership resulted in a resurgence of threats rather than a resolution. This historical allusion reinforces the idea that linear solutions are ineffective in a non-linear world.

Positioning Over Prediction

The core of the argument shifts from diagnosis to prescription. The author contends that the human instinct to seek "more data, better models, smarter algorithms" is a trap. Instead, the focus must move from asking "what will happen next" to "how do you position yourself for a range of outcomes." The distinction is subtle but vital: the first rewards confidence, while the second rewards humility. The text argues that in a world where the bus is careening toward a ditch, "knowing roughly where you are matters more than knowing exactly where you're going."

This philosophy is embodied in the collaboration with RANE to create the "World Humility Index of Predictions" (WHIP Index). Rather than listing successful forecasts, this index catalogs 194 events from 2025 that "nobody predicted," serving as a "training exercise" in accepting uncertainty. The author quotes Yogi Berra to seal the point: "it's tough to make predictions, especially about the future." The piece concludes that the goal is not better forecasting, but "better positioning, an openness to possibilities, a wider aperture, and a disposition to think in scenarios."

Critics might note that this approach could be interpreted as an abdication of responsibility for analysts who are paid to provide direction. However, the author effectively counters this by framing humility not as ignorance, but as a more rigorous form of intelligence that accounts for the "black swans" and lurches of history.

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this piece is its refusal to offer false comfort, successfully arguing that the era of predictable geopolitics is over and that resilience requires a fundamental shift in mindset. Its greatest vulnerability lies in the potential paralysis that can come from admitting total unpredictability, though the author mitigates this by emphasizing "orientation" as a practical alternative. Readers should watch for how organizations begin to operationalize this shift from forecasting to scenario-based positioning in the coming year.

Excerpt from the original article on Geopolitical Dispatch

Hello from Melbourne,

Even to a modern-day Nostradamus, 2025 was a year full of surprises.

The war in Ukraine ground on, despite repeated claims that resolution was imminent. The war in Gaza stopped, despite assumptions it would continue. The United States imposed tariffs on friends and rivals alike, as if they were going out of fashion. Threats were made that later proved empty while others — presumed to be bluff, bluster, or bravado — were carried out. Language that had come to define an era — “the rules-based international order” — was consigned to the dustbin of history and replaced by older notions of “spheres of influence” and “might makes right”. “International law” would have had a bad year on Google Ngram.

From our vantage point at Geopolitical Dispatch — where our unofficial analytical line is to describe the world as a wry Martian might do so while looking upon Earth — international politics often took on a vaudevillian, almost burlesque quality. It was frequently unclear what should be taken seriously, what should be taken literally, and what should be ignored altogether.

And then 2026 happened. While many thought an American intervention in Venezuela was possible, few envisaged how it would be carried out. Seemingly emboldened by this military success, President Trump then took on Iran — ‘decapitating’ its leadership only to find, like the mythological Hydra, two heads regrow for every one chopped off.

Anyone who had war-gamed an enormous aerial attack on Iran would have predicted the closure of the Strait of Hormuz — except, it seems, the US Department of War. Maybe its military planners had forgotten what one of its former Secretaries, Robert Gates, once observed: “When it comes to predicting the nature and location of our next military engagements, since Vietnam, our record has been perfect. We have never once gotten it right.”

The conceit of prediction

We live in an era obsessed with prediction. The zeitgeist favours Big Data, when often in international affairs it is small data — the thoughts and hopes and fears of just a few world leaders — that matters at least as much in shaping history. With AI bursting onto our screens and getting more powerful by the day, it is easy to be lulled into the conceit that with just a little bit more computing power we will be able to, finally, see into the future.
