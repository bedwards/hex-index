The Risk the War in Iran Poses to the Global Economy

Richard Coffin · The Plain Bagel ·Mar 6, 2026 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Richard Coffin delivers a jarring pivot from the immediate human tragedy of war to the cold, hard arithmetic of global supply chains, arguing that a conflict in Iran will not be contained by the country's isolation. While most headlines focus on regime change or geopolitical maneuvering, Coffin zeroes in on a single, terrifying variable: the Strait of Hormuz. He posits that the closure of this narrow waterway could trigger an economic shockwave far more damaging than the war itself, upending everything from fertilizer prices to the cost of a summer vacation.

The Myth of Isolation

Coffin begins by dismantling the assumption that Iran's economic insignificance protects the world. He notes that despite being "a fairly isolated country" and "the most heavily sanctioned country in the world until it was replaced by Russia," Iran remains a linchpin of global energy. The author writes, "Iran is, after all, a fairly isolated country... So most would probably anticipate pretty limited economic fallout from a war in the country. But that's actually far from the case." This framing is crucial because it corrects a common cognitive bias: we often equate political isolation with economic irrelevance. Coffin proves that even a hermit state can hold the world hostage if it controls the choke point for its neighbors' exports.

He contextualizes this by reminding readers that Iran was designated a state sponsor of terrorism as far back as 1984, yet it managed to maintain a sophisticated black market for its oil, sending "over 80% to China." This historical resilience suggests that the regime is prepared for long-term conflict, making the current escalation not a sudden anomaly but a calculated risk. However, Coffin wisely avoids getting bogged down in the moral debates of the conflict, focusing instead on the mechanics of the disruption. He writes, "not to make light of the other more horrific aspects to this development, but as an economics and finance channel, I think it's worth diving into the implications here." This pragmatic stance allows him to deliver hard numbers without getting lost in the fog of war, though critics might argue that ignoring the humanitarian cost of the strikes undermines the urgency of the economic warning.

"Most people have been pointing to another more far-reaching variable here, the straight of Hormuz."

The Choke Point Reality

The core of Coffin's argument rests on the geography of the Strait of Hormuz. He explains that this narrow passage, just 33 kilometers wide at its most constricted point, handles "about 31% of all seaborn crude and about 1/5 of global crude oil." The stakes are immediate and tangible. Coffin writes, "on Monday, we saw that risk become reality when the Islamic Revolution Guard Corp announced that they would be closing the straight, warning that it would set any vessel attempting to pass through ablaze."

This is not theoretical. The author details how shipping companies are already suspending transit, effectively "killing transit in the channel and leaving hundreds of ships stranded." The economic ripple effect is already visible in the markets. Coffin notes that West Texas Intermediate has "reached nearly $90 a barrel, up over 30% over the span of a week." The logic is sound: when supply is threatened, prices spike, but the impact goes beyond fuel. He points out that "about a third of the world's ura, a key nitrogen-based fertilizer, flow through the passageway," which could directly impact food prices globally.

Coffin attempts to provide balance by acknowledging that alternatives exist, such as Saudi Arabia's pipeline capacity to the Red Sea. Yet, he quickly undercuts this hope by noting that "the Red Sea, there's risk there as well, given that we've seen Houthi rebels based in Yemen strike freight vessels in the past." This connection to the broader regional instability is vital; it suggests that closing the Strait is just one move in a chess game where the board itself is on fire. The author estimates that even with alternatives, "crude oil supply could still drop by 8 to 10 million barrels per day, equivalent to nearly 10% of global supply."

"A disruption lasting over a month could send prices well into the triple digits, something we haven't seen since 2022."

The Global and Domestic Toll

Moving beyond oil, Coffin explores the cascading failures in other sectors. He highlights that natural gas futures have "jumped over 60% in European markets" and that marine insurance companies are canceling coverage for war risks, creating a gap that could paralyze trade. The impact on neighboring Gulf states is particularly acute. Coffin writes, "Many of the Gulf countries have nationalized their oil production, meaning that any impact to their ability to export oil directly decreases government revenue." This creates a vicious cycle where economic contraction fuels political instability, which in turn worsens the conflict.

For the United States, the narrative of war being "good for business" is challenged. Coffin writes, "US inflation tends to spike during geopolitical conflicts in part from this higher government spending." He warns that the Federal Reserve may be forced to hike rates, complicating an already fragile economic recovery. Furthermore, he notes that while Treasury bonds are traditionally safe havens, "we've so far actually seen yields increase slightly with fears over inflation seemingly offsetting this factor." This observation is a sharp critique of the assumption that the US is immune to global shocks.

Coffin concludes with a sobering projection from the Kiel Institute, suggesting a prolonged conflict could cost Iran nearly "$1 trillion in lost capital" and cause its economy to contract by over 30%. He cautions that while this is a "very crude estimate," the history of Middle Eastern conflicts suggests they often "stretch well beyond their initial scope." This historical context, referencing the long shadow of the 2015 nuclear deal and the 2018 US withdrawal, adds weight to the fear that a short-term strike could morph into a decade-long quagmire.

"It is not usually wise to trade off of short-term geopolitical headlines if you're investing for the long term, especially given that we just don't know how things will ultimately settle."

Bottom Line

Richard Coffin's most compelling contribution is his ability to translate the chaos of the Strait of Hormuz into a clear, quantifiable economic threat, moving the conversation from abstract geopolitics to concrete inflation and supply chain disruption. His argument is strongest when he connects the closure of the waterway to secondary effects like fertilizer and natural gas prices, revealing the fragility of the global system. However, the piece's primary vulnerability lies in its reliance on worst-case scenario modeling; while the risk is real, the timeline of a "four to six week" conflict versus a multi-year war remains highly speculative, and the market's ability to adapt to alternative routes may be underestimated. Readers should watch for whether the Strait remains closed for more than a month, as that threshold appears to be the tipping point for a global recession.
