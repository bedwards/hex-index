Cool your home with zero electricity costs!

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Oct 16, 2022 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace challenges a fundamental assumption of modern living: that cooling our world requires burning more energy. In a piece that cuts through the noise of climate doom, he introduces a counter-intuitive technology that uses the sun's heat to generate cold, potentially slashing the electricity demand of the world's fastest-growing energy sector.

The Paradox of Solar Cooling

Borlace begins by acknowledging the sweltering reality of rising global temperatures, noting that "we even broke some all-time records over here in Europe." However, he immediately pivots away from the debate on climate causation to focus on a radical solution: "using the sun's heat to cool us down." This framing is crucial. It moves the conversation from passive adaptation to active, physics-based innovation. The author distinguishes this from simply powering air conditioners with solar panels, describing a system that "collects heat directly from sunlight outside and apply[s] some clever physics to it to produce cool air inside." This distinction is the piece's intellectual anchor, separating incremental green energy adoption from a genuine paradigm shift in thermodynamics.

The urgency of this technology is underscored by staggering statistics. Borlace points out that while half of all air conditioners are in China and the United States, "of the nearly 3 billion people living in the hottest mid-latitude parts of the world only eight percent have access to their own air conditioning." The International Energy Agency projects that by 2050, two-thirds of global homes will have AC, a surge that threatens to overwhelm power grids and spike emissions. Borlace argues that current systems are fundamentally flawed because they "only transfer heat from one place to another" rather than removing it from the planetary system. This insight reframes air conditioning not just as a comfort issue, but as a planetary heat trap.

"The infrared radiation from this cooling system goes straight up through the atmosphere and out into space... it really is removing heat from the Earth's system."

The Triple-Layer Breakthrough

To solve the heat trap, Borlace details a new design from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) that merges three distinct physical principles. He explains that nature cools us through evaporation (sweat) and radiative cooling (heat escaping to space), but traditional attempts fail because evaporation adds humidity and radiative cooling struggles during the day. The MIT team, however, has engineered a "triple layer sandwich" that overcomes these limitations.

The device uses an aerogel top layer to insulate while letting infrared light pass, a hydrogel middle layer to provide water for evaporation, and a reflective bottom layer to bounce away direct sunlight. Borlace highlights the elegance of this design: "The novel insight that the team has shown here is simply to bring together the three principles of evaporation, radiation and insulation into a single design architecture." This synthesis allows the system to achieve significant cooling—over 9 degrees Celsius—even in direct sunlight, a feat previously thought impossible for passive systems.

Critics might note that the reliance on specific pore sizes in the aerogel introduces a manufacturing bottleneck. Borlace admits that "producing the aerogel is currently not a cheap process," requiring specialized equipment to reach a critical drying point without damaging the delicate structure. While the team is exploring cheaper alternatives like freeze-drying, the cost of materials remains a significant hurdle for widespread adoption in the developing world, where the technology is most needed.

Beyond the Rooftop

The implications of this technology extend far beyond residential comfort. Borlace suggests the design is "so slimline that it could theoretically be incorporated into the lid of a food container," offering a lifeline for food security in off-grid areas. Furthermore, he envisions larger panels cooling the condensers of conventional air conditioners, which would "greatly improve" their efficiency and reduce energy requirements. This dual application—direct cooling for the poor and efficiency boosting for the wealthy—makes the technology uniquely versatile.

The author concludes by emphasizing the low maintenance of the system, requiring water only "about once every four days in the hottest driest areas." This practicality is a stark contrast to the complex infrastructure required for traditional HVAC systems. While the path to commercialization is not without its wrinkles, the potential to decouple cooling from carbon emissions is a narrative worth following.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage effectively demystifies a complex thermodynamic breakthrough, grounding it in the urgent reality of global warming and energy inequality. The strongest part of his argument is the clear explanation of how this system actively removes heat from the Earth, unlike conventional AC. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability lies in the economic reality of aerogel production, which could delay the very applications needed most. Readers should watch for breakthroughs in low-cost aerogel manufacturing, as that will be the true gatekeeper to this technology's global impact.
