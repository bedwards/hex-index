When Non-Interference Is No Longer Enough: A Qualified Case for Chinese “Interventionism 2.0”

Various · Sinification ·Apr 5, 2026 · 18 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In a landscape dominated by calls for Chinese restraint, Zheng Yongnian makes a startling pivot: the era of strict non-interference is over, replaced by a doctrine he dubs "Interventionism 2.0." This is not a manifesto for global hegemony, but a pragmatic, conditional argument that China must actively defend its overseas interests when they are threatened by host nations or third-party powers. For busy strategists tracking the 2026 Middle East crisis, Zheng offers a rare theoretical framework that bridges the gap between Beijing's traditional diplomacy and the harsh realities of a "Hobbesian" world order.

The End of Passive Neutrality

Zheng Yongnian opens by dismantling the comforting illusion that China can remain a bystander while global supply chains convulse. He argues that the Strait of Hormuz crisis is no longer a regional skirmish but a systemic shock that renders the old doctrine of non-interference obsolete. "Today's world reflects a Hobbesian state of nature in which the strong prey on the weak," Zheng writes, invoking the 17th-century philosopher to describe a landscape where historical experience suggests a new order will take decades to emerge. This framing is effective because it strips away the moralizing often found in Western commentary, replacing it with a cold, realist assessment of power dynamics.

The core of his argument rests on the idea that China's massive overseas investments and energy needs have outgrown the protection offered by passive neutrality. He posits that the traditional policy should be updated to "Interventionism 2.0": active intervention is warranted when host countries infringe on China's overseas interests, third countries threaten them, or overseas factors profoundly affect China's domestic interests. This is a significant departure from the stance of Li Hongzhang, the late-Qing statesman who famously tried to balance great powers to preserve a crumbling empire; unlike Li, who sought to survive by yielding space, Zheng argues China must now project power to secure its lifelines.

Chinese traditional non-interference should be updated to "Interventionism 2.0": active intervention is warranted when host countries infringe on China's overseas interests, third countries threaten them, or overseas factors profoundly affect China's domestic interests.

Critics might note that defining "infringement" or "threat" is inherently subjective, leaving Beijing with a dangerous degree of discretion to intervene whenever it deems convenient. However, Zheng attempts to ground this by listing specific triggers, suggesting a move toward a rules-based approach to intervention rather than arbitrary aggression.

Weaponization and the Double Standard

Perhaps the most provocative section of the interview is Zheng's defense of Iran's "weaponization" of the Strait of Hormuz. While Western media condemns Tehran for blocking shipping lanes, Zheng points out the hypocrisy of ignoring how the United States has weaponized its own leverage. "Condemning Iran's 'weaponisation' of the Strait while ignoring Western weaponisation of semiconductors, supply chains and trade corridors applies a double standard that fails to withstand scrutiny," he asserts. This comparison is a powerful rhetorical tool, forcing the reader to acknowledge that in a multipolar world, every major power treats critical infrastructure as a strategic asset.

He draws a sharp parallel between Iran's blockade and Western sanctions on chip exports, asking, "Are US chip restrictions not a form of 'weaponisation' against China?" By reframing the issue as a universal tactic rather than a unique Iranian transgression, Zheng normalizes the concept of economic coercion as a standard tool of statecraft. He argues that under the logic of war, "if an enemy is coming to attack your home, are you supposed to leave the door wide open?" This logic holds up well when applied to the current crisis, where Iran is facing a decapitation strike and a hostile coalition.

However, the argument glosses over the potential long-term reputational costs for China if it openly validates the weaponization of global trade routes. If every nation begins to treat shipping lanes as tactical weapons, the very globalization China relies on could fracture entirely.

Strategic Maneuvering in a Fractured World

Zheng Yongnian concludes by analyzing the broader geopolitical chessboard, particularly the role of Russia and the potential for a wider war. He suggests that Russia's threat to cut gas supplies to Europe is an act of "opportunistic solidarity" with Iran, designed to push Europe into a corner. "Russia has obviously spotted its opportunity," Zheng notes, highlighting how Moscow hopes to leverage the chaos to advance its own interests while Europe is already reeling from the Ukraine conflict.

He envisions Iran adopting a strategy reminiscent of Li Hongzhang's late-Qing diplomacy: leveraging rivalries among the US, Europe, and Japan to create room for maneuver. "Iran may adopt a similar approach in dealing with the West—manoeuvring among the competing interests of the US, European countries and Japan," he explains. This historical echo adds depth to the analysis, suggesting that the tactics of the 19th century are being repurposed for the 21st. Yet, the situation is far more volatile now; as Zheng admits, "historical experience suggests that the emergence of a new order will take decades," implying that the transition will be messy and dangerous.

North-South antagonism stems from the West's practice of "ladder-pulling" after having reached development itself.

Zheng's critique of the West's "ladder-pulling"—where developed nations restrict access to technology and capital once they have secured their own position—resonates with many in the Global South. He frames China's Belt and Road Initiative as a countervailing model of "open-source, shared modernisation." This is a compelling narrative, but it remains to be seen if the "shared modernisation" model can survive the pressures of a world increasingly defined by security dilemmas rather than economic cooperation.

Bottom Line

Zheng Yongnian's "Interventionism 2.0" is a bold, necessary recalibration of Chinese foreign policy theory that acknowledges the limits of non-interference in a hyper-connected, volatile world. Its greatest strength lies in its unflinching realism about the weaponization of global infrastructure, but its biggest vulnerability is the lack of a clear mechanism for multilateral oversight, risking a descent into a purely transactional global order. Readers should watch closely to see if Beijing's actions in the coming months align with this theoretical shift or if the doctrine remains a rhetorical shield for continued caution.

Excerpt from the original article on Sinification

Zheng Yongnian (郑永年), one of China’s best-known public intellectuals, was among the very few voices in our Iran briefing to suggest that the US–Israeli strikes should prompt a more assertive Chinese foreign policy. In this interview with Greater Bay Area Review, he develops that line with greater theoretical precision.

He is not the only scholar calling for a recalibration of China’s foreign policy. Last month, we published Jin Canrong’s (金灿荣) argument that China will struggle to win true friends if it cannot offer support beyond economic engagement. We also observed similar calls in both our January and February digests.

These arguments remain heavily qualified, light on substance and notable precisely because they are unusual, but they do appear to be part of a nascent trend.

In the interview below, Zheng defends China’s non-alignment strategy, crediting it with helping to prevent both a new world war and a new Cold War. But he argues that the related doctrine of non-interference has not kept pace with the scale of China’s overseas interests.

He puts forward one of the more developed cases for recalibration that we have seen, providing both a name—“Interventionism 2.0”—and a set of conditions for more active intervention: namely, when host countries infringe on China’s overseas interests, when third countries threaten them, or when overseas factors profoundly affect China’s domestic interests.

— Jacob Mardell

Key Points

  1. The Strait of Hormuz crisis is no longer a regional conflict. It is reshaping global energy markets, supply chains and geopolitical alignments in ways that no major economy can insulate itself from.

  2. A 1970s-style oil crisis is unlikely, but short-term shocks—especially for economies with limited supplies—will be severe.

  3. Condemning Iran’s “weaponisation” [武器化] of the Strait while ignoring Western weaponisation of semiconductors, supply chains and trade corridors applies a double standard that fails to withstand scrutiny.

  4. Iran’s most viable course is not a total shutdown of Hormuz, but a calibrated strategy of selective, step-by-step closure, tightening pressure on adversaries while avoiding the self-inflicted costs of severing its own export lifeline.

  5. Diplomatically, Iran may seek to play on tensions among the US, Europe and Japan—a modern echo of Li Hongzhang’s late-Qing strategy of using great-power rivalries to preserve room for manoeuvre under pressure.
