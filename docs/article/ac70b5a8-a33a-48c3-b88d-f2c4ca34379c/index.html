Solar Scattered, Coal Still Standing

Various · Energy Bad Boys ·Mar 14, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In a landscape where renewable energy is often treated as an unassailable good, the authors of "Solar Scattered, Coal Still Standing" make a jarring, data-driven pivot: they argue that a single, relatively weak tornado has exposed a fatal flaw in the rush to replace dispatchable coal plants with fragile solar infrastructure. By juxtaposing the destruction of a billion-dollar solar facility against the untouched resilience of a nearby coal plant, the piece forces a uncomfortable question on ratepayers and regulators alike: are we trading reliability for ideology, and who is footing the bill when the weather turns extreme?

The Cost of Fragility

The authors anchor their argument in the stark reality of the March 10 tornado that devastated the Dunns Bridge solar facilities in Indiana. They note that while the storm was only an EF1—the second lowest classification on the tornado scale—it managed to destroy 2.4 million solar panels. "Just the path of the tornado that came through, we have several large solar fields to the east of the town here it went right through the solar field and just ripped a lot of them out," notes Chief Deputy Brandon Napier, a quote the authors use to illustrate the sheer physical vulnerability of the installation.

The financial implications of this destruction are where the authors' analysis becomes most biting. They calculate the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) not over the standard 25-year lifespan, but over the facility's actual, truncated two-year existence. The result is a staggering figure. "Because the LCOE is like calculating the cost of driving your car over the number of miles driven, if your car dies after two years when you expected to drive it for 10, the cost per mile obviously increases," the authors explain. This logic leads them to a sobering conclusion: the cost of electricity from these facilities skyrockets to an unsubsidized $405.09 per megawatt-hour.

Critics might argue that insurance should cover these losses and that rebuilding would reset the cost basis, but the authors rightly point out that ratepayers often absorb the deductible and the interim costs of replacement. The piece effectively highlights a risk that is rarely priced into the public discourse: the exposure of massive capital investments to increasingly frequent extreme weather events.

The Coal Counterpoint

Perhaps the most provocative element of the coverage is the comparison to the R.M. Schahfer Generating Station, a coal plant located nearby that was slated for retirement but kept running by a federal order. While the solar farm was shredded, the coal plant remained untouched. "The situation begs several questions: If climate change is going to make the weather more extreme, how does it make any sense to shut down coal plants and build energy generation facilities, like solar, that are destroyed by extreme weather?" the authors ask, challenging the very premise of the utility's decarbonization strategy.

The authors dig into the economics, noting that despite high fuel costs, the coal plant remains competitive. "At $70 per MWh, the Schahfer plant is competitive with subsidized solar over a 25-year lifespan, cheaper than the unsubsidized cost over 25 years, and a bargain compared with our admittedly uncharitable comparison to the facility's actual 2-year lifespan." This comparison is the article's strongest weapon, stripping away the subsidies to reveal the raw economic disparity between a destroyed asset and a functioning one.

The demise of the Dunns Bridge I & II solar facilities by one of the weakest classifications of tornado should be a nudge to Indiana policymakers and utility regulators that shutting down dispatchable thermal plants in favor of flimsier wind and solar facilities is not a prudent course of action.

However, the argument glosses over the long-term environmental costs of coal, which the authors briefly dismiss by focusing solely on immediate reliability and cost. While their point on weather resilience is valid, a balanced view must acknowledge that coal plants face their own existential threats from carbon regulations and water scarcity, issues the authors treat as secondary to the immediate storm damage.

The Ratepayer's Burden

The commentary shifts to the broader trend of rising electricity rates in Northern Indiana Public Service Company (NIPSCO) territory. The authors connect the dots between the utility's aggressive shift toward renewables and the doubling of its rate base since 2016. "This upward trend in rate base growth led to higher rates," they state, linking the capital expenditure on vulnerable infrastructure directly to the bills families pay. They highlight that while transmission costs have risen, the generation spending rebound in 2024 suggests the push for new renewables is driving prices up even further.

The authors express frustration with the utility's lack of transparency, noting that NIPSCO did not respond to inquiries about insurance deductibles or cleanup liabilities. "They can dodge our questions, but they had better come clean to the regulators," they write, calling for accountability at the upcoming Indiana Utility Regulatory Commission hearing. This demand for transparency is crucial, as it shifts the narrative from abstract energy policy to concrete financial responsibility for the consumer.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its ruthless application of basic accounting to a political debate, proving that a short-lived asset is economically disastrous regardless of its green credentials. Its biggest vulnerability lies in treating the coal plant as a permanent solution rather than a transitional one, ignoring the long-term regulatory risks that could render it obsolete. Readers should watch how regulators handle the insurance and liability questions, as the outcome will determine whether ratepayers are forced to subsidize the fragility of the green transition.

A quick note: On Tuesday, March 10th, an EF1 tornado touched down in Northwestern Indiana and Northeastern Illinois. Police and fire officials say that at least 10 to 15 homes have severe damage, and an elderly couple was tragically killed in the storm. We encourage anyone who is interested in helping to donate to the Indiana charity of their choice.

On Tuesday, March 10th, an EF-1 tornado destroyed the Dunns Bridge Solar I and II facilities owned by the Northern Indiana Public Service Company (NIPSCO). The facilities, located outside of Wheatfield, Indiana, had 2.4 million solar panels, totaling 700 megawatts (MW) of power capacity, and reportedly cost $1 billion to construct—a little over $1,400 per kilowatt (kW).

NIPSCO issued the following statement in the aftermath:

On the evening of March 10, while actively monitoring severe weather and responding to storm‑related outages across our service area, NIPSCO became aware of damage to its Dunns Bridge I and Dunns Bridge II solar facilities in Starke and Jasper counties. Our team was tracking the storm in real time and moved in to assess conditions and respond as soon as it was safe to do so. Debris from the damage could have been displaced, and we are working to safely secure the area, assess the damage and proactively communicate with the community.

We recognize there may be questions and concerns about potential environmental impacts related to the damage at the solar farm. Solar panel leaching concerns have been thoroughly evaluated in industry-leading research, which shows that the risk is extremely low. Overall, the available evidence demonstrates that both crystalline silicon and thin-film PV (i.e., photovoltaic) modules do not pose a meaningful risk of environmental or human exposure from leaching, even when damaged.

The Chief Deputy of Jasper County Sheriff’s Department, Brandon Napier, noted, “Just the path of the tornado that came through, we have several large solar fields to the east of the town here it went right through the solar field and just ripped a lot of them out.”

While the solar panels were damaged by the tornado, we are not aware of any reports of damage at the nearby R.M. Schahfer Generating Station, a 950 MW coal facility that NIPSCO was planning to retire at the end of 2025.
