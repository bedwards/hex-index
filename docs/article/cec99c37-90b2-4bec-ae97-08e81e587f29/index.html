14. Purgatory Xxiv, XXV, Xxvi

Yale University · Yale Courses ·Sep 8, 2009 · 34 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Professor David Lamas, substituting for a traveling colleague at Yale, delivers a lecture that reframes Dante's Purgatorio not merely as a journey of the soul, but as a radical manifesto on the evolution of poetry itself. He argues that Dante uses the terrace of gluttony to distinguish his "sweet new style" from the self-indulgent, worldly verse of his predecessors, turning a discussion of hunger into a profound meditation on literary inspiration and moral conversion.

The Poetry of Hunger

Lamas begins by contextualizing the encounter in Canto 23 between Dante and the poet Forese Donati. He notes that their relationship has shifted from the bitter, body-obsessed insults of their youth to a brotherly recognition in the afterlife. "In these sonnets they were rated each other's lives and their their vices and this canto they recognize each other they treat each other like brothers," Lamas explains, highlighting how the physical disfigurement of the gluttonous penitents serves as a mirror to their past literary excesses. This connection is crucial: the "goliardic" poetry of the past, rooted in the Latin gula (throat or gluttony), is portrayed as a literature that consumes without generating, focused entirely on the flesh rather than the spirit.

The professor suggests that Dante's transformation is marked by a move away from this circular, self-referential writing. He points to the imagery of birds in Canto 24, contrasting the sinners in Inferno who are buffeted in a tempest with the penitents in Purgatorio who move forward in a straight line. "There is a progress that that opposes the circularity of the of the storm of birds in in federal five," Lamas observes. This distinction is vital; it suggests that true poetry, like the soul in Purgatory, must move toward a destination rather than spinning in the endless loop of ego.

The Dictate of Love

The core of Lamas's argument centers on Canto 24, where Dante meets the critic Bonagiunta Orbicciani. Bonagiunta identifies Dante as the author of the "new rhymes" that began with Donne ch'avete intelletto d'amore (Ladies who have understanding of love). Lamas dissects Dante's famous response: "I am one who when loves love breathes in me take note and in that manner which he dictates within go on to set it forth." This is not just a stylistic claim; Lamas argues it is a theological one. He posits that Dante is equating poetic inspiration with the Holy Spirit, describing a process where the divine breathes into the poet, who then acts as a scribe.

The professor draws a parallel to Dante's political treatise De Monarchia, where God is described as the only true "dictator." By using the language of dictation, Dante claims his work is not a product of his own limited intellect but a transmission of divine truth. Bonagiunta, having achieved a new moral perspective in Purgatory, finally understands this shift. "Oh brother he said now I see the knot that held me held back the notary and wheat ona and me short of the sweet new style," Lamas quotes, illustrating how the old poets were bound by a lack of moral vision that prevented them from accessing this higher style.

Critics might note that Lamas's theological reading of poetic inspiration risks over-intellectualizing a text that is also deeply personal and emotional. While the connection to the Holy Spirit is compelling, it may downplay the role of Dante's own artistic agency and the sheer craft required to produce the Comedy. However, Lamas effectively uses this framework to explain why Dante felt the need to break so decisively with the Sicilian and Tuscan schools that preceded him.

The Uniqueness of the New Style

Lamas concludes by examining the nuance in Dante's self-description. When Dante says, "I am one who..." he is simultaneously humble and asserting a unique identity. Lamas connects this phrasing to Exodus 3:14, where God tells Moses, "I am Who I am." This is a bold claim for a poet to make, suggesting that his voice is a unique vessel for the divine, distinct from the collective "school" of the dolce stil novo.

He's pointing out that he has a different perspective on that time of his life... the motor I guess it is of his this change that Dante experiences are both Virgil and Beatrice.

The lecture ties this literary evolution back to the broader narrative of the Comedy. Just as the pilgrim is led by Virgil and Beatrice out of the dark wood, the poet is led by these figures to a new mode of expression. The "knot" that bound earlier poets was their inability to see beyond the self, a limitation that Purgatory cures. By framing the canto as a "reformation of a poetic community," Lamas provides a fresh lens through which to view Dante's rivalry with his contemporaries.

Bottom Line

Lamas's lecture succeeds in transforming a dense theological discussion into a clear argument about the moral necessity of artistic innovation. His strongest point is the identification of "gluttony" not just as a sin of the body, but as a metaphor for self-indulgent poetry that consumes without creating. The argument's vulnerability lies in its heavy reliance on theological parallels that may feel abstract to readers unfamiliar with Dante's political treatises, yet the core insight—that Dante's poetry is an act of divine dictation rather than human invention—remains a powerful and enduring interpretation of the text.
