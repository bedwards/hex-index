Digital socialism or extinction

Rezgar Akrawi · ·Jan 14, 2026 ·19 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Rezgar Akrawi presents a chilling, high-stakes thesis: the future of political liberation depends entirely on winning the algorithmic war, or facing total extinction. While mainstream coverage fixates on the political drama of a 2026 military intervention in Venezuela, Akrawi argues that the true story is a silent, pre-emptive digital decapitation executed by the executive branch using tools far more sophisticated than traditional espionage. This is not a recounting of a regime change; it is a forensic dissection of how artificial intelligence and big data have become the primary weapons of modern hegemony, rendering traditional leftist organizing obsolete unless it undergoes a radical technological transformation.

The Digital Decapitation

Akrawi frames the recent abduction of Venezuelan leadership not as a kinetic military victory, but as the culmination of a years-long digital siege. "The employment of Artificial Intelligence in analysing millions of phone calls, text messages, and emails was not just a traditional espionage process, but a complex operation to determine the locations of leaders with extreme accuracy, and to predict their intentions and next moves before they took them," the author writes. This reframing is crucial. It shifts the narrative from a story of boots on the ground to one of data on servers, suggesting that the physical arrest was merely the final step in a process of total informational isolation.

The author draws a direct line from this event to the infrastructure of the National Security Agency's PRISM program, revealed by Edward Snowden over a decade ago, and the data-mining capabilities of firms like Palantir Technologies. Akrawi notes that these systems, originally marketed under the banner of counter-terrorism, are now being deployed to "paralyse decision-making centres before direct confrontation." The argument here is that the executive branch has perfected a method of neutralizing opposition by mapping their entire digital footprint, effectively cutting them off from their base before a single shot is fired.

"The capitalist technological system today has become largely capable of total surveillance, accurate monitoring, and systematic tracking of political movements, organisations, and political actors."

This assertion carries significant weight, especially when contextualized by the historical trajectory of information warfare. Just as the internet was once a military project before becoming a public utility, Akrawi warns that the next generation of AI weapons is likely being developed in the shadows of closed intelligence circles before they are ever publicly acknowledged. The piece forces the reader to confront the reality that the battlefield has moved from the street to the server, and the rules of engagement have changed irrevocably.

The Algorithm as the New Overseer

Moving beyond statecraft, Akrawi argues that this same technological architecture is being used to enforce class exploitation on a global scale. The author contends that "algorithms in digital companies are used to exploit manual and intellectual workers in ways more accurate and cruel than any human manager in history." This is a powerful critique of the gig economy and automated management, where wage determination and disciplinary action are outsourced to "cold technical logic" that lacks human empathy or the capacity for negotiation.

The commentary suggests that the digital sphere is no longer a neutral ground for free expression but a curated environment designed to reinforce capitalist ideology. Akrawi points out that platforms systematically promote "the culture of individualism, competition, and the eternity of the capitalist system" while using "reach reduction" and "shadow banning" to make progressive content invisible. This creates a feedback loop where the very tools used for organization are rigged to suppress the organizers.

Critics might note that this view risks underestimating the resilience of human networks and the potential for decentralized, encrypted communication to bypass these controls. History shows that oppressed groups often find ways to adapt and organize even under heavy surveillance. However, Akrawi's warning remains potent: relying on unencrypted phones and traditional paper leaflets against an adversary armed with predictive AI is a strategy destined for failure.

"Artificial Intelligence has become the backbone of this digital war, providing unprecedented capabilities in predictive analysis, total surveillance, and accurately directed psychological warfare."

The Historical Wager

The piece concludes with a stark ultimatum for progressive movements: adapt or die. Akrawi writes that the technological factor is "no longer just a secondary or luxury addition in the Left's battle against capitalism... It has become a basic and vital condition for survival." The author argues that criticism alone is insufficient; the solution lies in the development of "open-source, transparent, neutrally-oriented systems, managed democratically with societal controls."

This call to action is both the piece's most ambitious and its most difficult challenge. It demands that political movements not only understand code but become architects of their own digital infrastructure. Akrawi posits that just as the left built independent media, it must now build independent AI. The argument is that without this shift, the asymmetry of power will only widen, leaving progressive forces unable to compete in a world where the rules are written by algorithms designed to maintain the status quo.

"The required and radical solution is the development of open-source, transparent, neutrally-oriented systems, managed democratically with societal controls."

Bottom Line

Rezgar Akrawi's most compelling contribution is the reframing of military intervention as a digital operation, exposing how the executive branch leverages AI to achieve strategic dominance with minimal physical footprint. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its prescription; while the diagnosis of the problem is sharp, the path to building a democratic, open-source alternative against the resources of global capital remains vague and incredibly difficult to execute. Readers should watch for how progressive movements attempt to bridge the gap between political theory and technical reality in the coming years, as this divide may well determine the future of dissent.

1. The Lesson of Venezuela

At the dawn of an ordinary day in early January of this year, 2026, the world woke up to shocking news whose repercussions were not clear at first glance: a brutal US military aggression and the abduction of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro and his wife, Cilia Flores, in a complex operation executed with supreme military and intelligence precision. Although the operation included a direct military attack, intensive shelling, and systematic destruction of weapon depots and defence platforms, it relied heavily on a massive employment of digital technology, and this is the axis we will focus on analysing here. Mainstream media coverage focused on the political and diplomatic aspects of the event, without real attention—whether spontaneous or intentional—to the pivotal role that advanced technology played in this operation. It was not merely a traditional military intervention as some Western media attempted to portray it; rather, it was a comprehensive and organised digital war that preceded the arrest by long months, if not years of planning, monitoring, and preparation.

Before continuing, I would like to mention here my reservation regarding the policies of the Maduro regime in suppressing dissent, restricting freedoms, and tightening the grip on leftists and labor unions. Our critique of American capitalist intervention, the violation of international law, and the use of technology as a weapon of hegemony does not, in any way, mean justifying the repressive practices of the Maduro regime against progressive forces and the labor movement in Venezuela, especially the Communist Party of Venezuela. Standing against the aggressive militarism of American capitalism does not contradict the critique of internal tyranny; rather, it is a position consistent with leftist values. What we are highlighting here is the technological and strategic lesson that this incident provides to all leftist and progressive movements, regardless of our assessment of the Maduro regime itself.

According to what has been circulated in the media, the most advanced US satellite surveillance systems in the world were used in this operation to track the movements of the Venezuelan leadership via US satellites orbiting the Earth around the clock. Big data analysis was not limited to monitoring physical movements only, but extended to include drawing accurate and detailed maps of the Venezuelan government communication networks with all their complexities and branches.
