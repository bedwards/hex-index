Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill deliver a dispatch that cuts through the diplomatic theater to reveal a terrifying reality: while the White House stages a "Board of Peace," the machinery of war is being assembled with unprecedented speed. This is not a standard news roundup; it is a forensic audit of how a new administration is simultaneously courting global leaders and preparing for a massive, potentially catastrophic conflict in the Middle East. The piece forces the listener to confront the dissonance between the rhetoric of reconciliation and the reality of a 350-acre military fortress being built in a war-torn enclave.

The Architecture of Conflict

The authors expose a stark contradiction at the heart of current policy. While President Trump hosts an inaugural "Board of Peace" meeting in Washington, the administration is quietly finalizing plans for a massive military footprint in Gaza. Grim and Scahill write, "The Trump administration is planning a 5,000-person military base in Gaza spanning more than 350 acres to serve as an operating hub for the International Stabilization Force (ISF) under the 'Board of Peace.'" This is not a humanitarian outpost; the plans detail "26 armored watchtowers, a small arms range, bunkers, equipment warehouses, and barbed-wire perimeter defenses." The sheer scale of this construction suggests a long-term occupation rather than a temporary stabilization effort.

Even more alarming is the proposal to populate a new Gaza police force with the very groups that have destabilized the region. The authors note that the White House is considering recruiting "anti-Hamas Palestinian gangs—armed by Israel since the onset of the genocide" into this new force. These are not peacekeepers; as Grim and Scahill report, some of these groups are "organized crime groups and drug-trafficking clans" that have been "looting aid amid famine conditions and carrying out murders and kidnappings." This strategy risks importing the chaos of the West Bank directly into Gaza, creating a security apparatus built on criminal enterprise rather than law.

The human cost of these strategic miscalculations is already being tallied with brutal precision. The authors highlight that a new study in The Lancet estimates "more than 75,000 people were killed in the first 16 months of Israel's war on the Gaza Strip," a figure that challenges official narratives by suggesting the death toll is "at least 25,000 more than figures released by local authorities." The study confirms that the vast majority of victims are civilians, noting that "about 56 percent of violent deaths... fell into" the categories of women, children, and the elderly. This data serves as a grim counterweight to the diplomatic optimism of the "Board of Peace," reminding us that the violence continues unabated even as the world watches the summit.

The Shadow of War

While the Board of Peace convenes, the executive branch is preparing for a direct confrontation with Iran. The authors describe a "massive concentration of air and naval power in the Middle East," including two carrier strike groups and over 30,000 personnel. Grim and Scahill write, "Current and former U.S. officials told Drop Site that the scale of the buildup would support sustained strikes targeting Iranian air defenses, missile forces, and senior leadership if diplomacy collapses." This is a posture of extreme aggression, one that leaves little room for error. The administration's stance is summarized by Vice President JD Vance, who warned that talks have "red lines Iran has not accepted," while Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei has rejected these demands as "ultimatums disguised as negotiations."

The geopolitical stakes are further complicated by the administration's willingness to bypass traditional alliances. Trump has threatened to launch attacks on Iran from British territory, including the strategic base on Diego Garcia, "with or without London's consent." This move signals a willingness to fracture Western unity to pursue military objectives. Critics might note that such brinkmanship could backfire, pushing regional actors closer together and destabilizing global energy markets. However, the authors present the evidence of a administration that is prioritizing military dominance over diplomatic consensus.

The article also touches on the disturbing intersection of intelligence and personal corruption, revealing that the Israeli government ran a security operation at a Manhattan apartment linked to Jeffrey Epstein. Grim and Scahill report that "officials from Israel's permanent mission to the United Nations coordinating regularly with Epstein's staff over access and arrangements." This revelation adds a layer of opacity to the region's security dynamics, suggesting that intelligence networks may be deeply entangled with illicit activities that extend far beyond the battlefield.

The Congressional Pushback

Amidst this executive overreach, a rare bipartisan effort is emerging in Congress to check the administration's war powers. Representatives Ro Khanna and Thomas Massie are forcing a vote on the War Powers Act, a move the authors describe as potentially "as politically consequential as the 2002 vote to authorize war in Iraq." This legislative maneuver highlights the growing unease among lawmakers about the trajectory of U.S. foreign policy. The fact that this resolution has been "assiduously ignored by the mainstream press" underscores the importance of independent reporting in holding power accountable.

The authors also draw attention to the plight of Palestinian-Americans caught in the crossfire. They detail the case of Mohammed Ibrahim, a teenager detained for nine months in Israel, who was "allegedly beaten, pepper-sprayed, and denied adequate food and medical care, losing roughly a third of his body weight." This personal story humanizes the broader geopolitical conflict, reminding the audience that behind the strategic maps and military bases are real people suffering under the weight of these policies.

Bottom Line

Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill have assembled a damning indictment of a foreign policy that masks aggression with the language of peace. The strongest part of their argument is the juxtaposition of the "Board of Peace" summit with the concrete reality of a 5,000-person military base and a massive naval buildup near Iran. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on anonymous officials for some of the most explosive claims, though the specificity of the details lends them credibility. Readers should watch closely for the upcoming House vote on Iran, as it may be the last line of defense against a conflict that the administration seems eager to ignite.
