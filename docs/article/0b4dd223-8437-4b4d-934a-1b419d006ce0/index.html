Economies have always and will always run on energy. For an economy to prosper, energy needs to be cheap. For an economy to be safe from geopolitical shocks, energy needs to be secure. And now, to meet new global commitments, energy eventually needs to be green in whatever form that may take.

Now, in reality, most economies are struggling to achieve just one of these objectives. And trying to balance all three of these, often opposing goals has, in a growing number of cases, led to tangible long-term economic consequences. The global price of energy has almost tripled since 2000. Trade shocks continue to spook national energy supplies, and alarm bells are ringing louder than ever over our impact on the climate.

The trouble is, they're conflicting interests. When push comes to shove, energy abundance and security come first, and nobody wants to make sacrifices while other economies benefit from going full steam ead since that won't fix the problem anyway. But what if we simply stopped caring about the environment at all? If we looked at global warming purely as a cold-hearted economist without care for the extinction of polar bears or habit wrecking wildfires, unless of course it affects our GDP, then how would we arrange our energy systems and economies to maximize longrun economic prosperity?

It's a pretty ruthless way to look at it, but it's pragmatic because reframing global warming from an environmental issue to an economic one is probably the only realistic way to solve it. Now, of course, little disclaimer time. Please don't take this too seriously. It's just that we may as well use our impending doom as a fun little case study because otherwise what's the point?
