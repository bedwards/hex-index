Agents will pay like locals, not tourists

Agents as Locals, Not Tourists

Sam Broner, a partner at a16z crypto, argues that the payments industry is thinking about AI agents all wrong. The default assumption — that agents will browse, compare, and pay like individual consumers — misses how economics actually work at scale. Broner's thesis is straightforward: agents will consolidate into business-like platforms with pre-negotiated vendor relationships, and the payment rails they need look nothing like a credit card swipe at checkout.

When we talk about how agents will pay for things, we default to thinking like tourists.

The bazaar metaphor anchors the piece. Tourists haggle and pay cash. Locals have relationships, credit lines, and standing orders. Broner contends that AI agents, despite being software, will behave far more like the locals — repeat players with deep vendor integrations rather than one-off shoppers fumbling through checkout flows.

Why Agents Consolidate

The argument rests on a key economic insight: agents are easy to build, but the advantages of scale are not easy to replicate. A travel agent platform booking a million flights per year negotiates better terms than a scrappy startup booking ten. Broner points to ChatGPT's partnerships with Shopify, Amazon, and Expedia as early evidence.

Only ChatGPT has the distribution to negotiate partnerships with Shopify, Amazon, Expedia, and more. Small startups are stuck with automated browsers or reverse-engineered APIs while paying retail fee structures.

This leads to a prediction that most agents will either consolidate into vertically dominant platforms or be built on top of them. Each vertical ends up with a handful of winners who have the vendor relationships and margins to reinvest in better experiences.

Agents are easy to build, but economics favor a small number of agents per vertical — each with deep vendor relationships and the margins to reinvest in better experiences.

It is a tidy story, though perhaps too tidy. The history of the internet is littered with predictions of inevitable consolidation that never fully materialized. Broner assumes that vendor relationships and volume pricing create insurmountable moats, but agents could just as easily commoditize those relationships by making switching costs negligible — the very property of "zero startup cost" he celebrates could cut both ways.

The Credit Card Problem

Broner spends considerable time on why credit cards, despite being a reasonable starting point, ultimately fail the agent use case. The technology stack assumes a human approver. The fee structure breaks down at the extremes.

Agent adoption is happening too quickly for the thousands of PSPs, POSs, merchants, and client endpoints to slowly upgrade their interface, programmability, and fraud detection for this new payment flow.

The unit economics argument is sharp. Visa does not support sub-cent payments. The 30-cent fixed fee makes micropayments impossible. An agent streaming fractions of a penny per second to a compute provider simply cannot use card rails. Neither can large B2B settlements where interchange fees eat margins.

Imagine an agent streaming funds to a compute provider or delivering micropayments for API access. Neither of these payments will work on card rails.

Broner frames this as a classic innovator's dilemma: card networks could theoretically build the technology, but the economics of doing so would cannibalize their existing revenue. The incumbents are stuck.

The Stablecoin Pitch

This is where the essay reveals its hand. Broner, writing from a16z crypto, argues that stablecoins are the natural payment rail for the agentic economy. They are programmable, global, and cheap. No 30-cent minimums. No interchange fees. An agent paying a thousandth of a cent per second and a manufacturer settling a fifty-thousand-dollar invoice can use the same rail.

Stablecoins are programmable, global, simple to reconcile with digital services, and trivial to integrate into APIs and agent checkouts. They'll work from day one even without negotiated merchant agreements or complicated B2B terms.

The vision is compelling in theory. Broner imagines a Bloomingdale's-style experience where an agent accumulates purchases across multiple vendors and presents a single approval to the user. Statements, arbitration, credit, batch approvals — all layered on top of programmable money.

The user sees "your agent wants to book a flight, reserve a hotel, and rent a car" — not three separate checkout flows.

There is an honest admission buried in the argument: the most common objection to stablecoins is that on- and off-ramping is expensive. Broner waves this away by saying agents themselves can smooth over the friction, acting as tour guides for the uninitiated. This is somewhat circular reasoning — the technology needs agents to overcome its own adoption barriers, while agents supposedly need the technology to function.

What Goes Unsaid

The essay is notably quiet on regulatory risk. Stablecoins operate in a patchwork of global regulatory frameworks, and the assumption that entrepreneurs can simply "reach for tools that work well today" glosses over compliance costs that could rival or exceed the card network fees Broner criticizes. A startup building on stablecoin rails in 2026 faces a different kind of uncertainty than one building on Visa.

Broner also concedes a crucial point almost in passing: pre-negotiated B2B terms, which he argues will carry the bulk of agent payment volume, do not actually need new payment rails at all.

Pre-negotiated B2B terms do not need new payment rails. The settlement layer can be anything — wires, ACH, boring batch transfers. Legacy payments work fine for established relationships.

This admission significantly narrows the addressable market for stablecoins in the agent economy. The opportunity is not the bulk of payments — it is the edges: first-time relationships, cross-border checkout, micropayments, and streaming payments. These are real use cases, but they are a far cry from replacing the entire payments stack.

Bottom Line

Broner makes a strong structural argument about how AI agents will consolidate into business-like platforms with long-term vendor relationships rather than acting as individual consumers. The bazaar metaphor is effective, and the analysis of credit card limitations at the extremes of payment size is genuinely insightful. The stablecoin pitch, while well-constructed, requires readers to accept that a technology currently burdened by on-ramp friction and regulatory uncertainty will mature fast enough to capture the narrow window before incumbents adapt. The best insight in the piece is not about crypto at all — it is that agents will behave like businesses, and the payments infrastructure they need already exists for most use cases. The opportunity for new rails is real but smaller than the essay implies.

Walk through a bazaar as a tourist, and you’ll witness a spectacle: people buzzing everywhere, gawking at merchandise, comparing wares, sampling items, haggling with every vendor, exchanging coins. It looks like one-off commerce — each interaction its own little negotiation, trust mediated by cash in hand, or value exchanged via card.

But that’s not how most business gets done in the bazaar. Look more closely: Most people are locals, moving purposefully to their favorite merchants. The restaurant owner visits his friends, the butcher, the fishmonger, and the farmer. The tailor goes to the mechanic, the weaver, and the craftsman. They both pay on credit.

When we talk about how agents will pay for things, we default to thinking like tourists.

But agents will behave like locals. The properties that make agents different from humans — infinite duplication, flexible resourcing, zero startup cost — mean that a small number of agents can win niches. And even as agents get easier to build, relationships, partnerships, and trust can help create winning experiences. Dominant agents don’t need tourists’ payment rails. They need vendor relationships, working capital, and credit. The agent can lead the tourist (that’s you).

What does this look like? As agents consolidate into business-like platforms, agent payments must shift from retail payment rails to pre-negotiated B2B terms and credit, an opportunity that current rails can’t fully meet. This is the opening for next-generation payment rails, like stablecoins, if entrepreneurs can build great solutions for next-generation payment scenarios, like agents, streaming payments, and high-volume, low-dollar global business.

This essay explores that idea in three parts: how agents differ from humans and how those differences shape which payment strategies win; why current approaches fall short; and what needs to be built for next-generation payment rails to win.

How agents are different than humans

To understand agents and payments, we have to consider two questions: Will agents act like people or businesses? And will agents play long-term games or short-term ones?

Agents will be more like businesses, with long-term relationships with their vendors and partners. Agents will be lightly customized instances on top of a larger business’s structure — the perfect tour guide from a well-connected travel agency, or a franchisee tuning a playbook for local tastes without renegotiating the supply chain.
