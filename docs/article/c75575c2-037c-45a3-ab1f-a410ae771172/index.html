Benn Jordan doesn't just review a game; he exposes a fundamental misunderstanding of what modular synthesis actually is, arguing that The Signal State is less a music tool and more a rigorous exercise in analog computing disguised as a video game. While most critics focus on the patch cables and oscillators, Jordan brings the rare perspective of a working musician who realizes the software's true power lies in its logic gates, not its sound engine. For the busy professional who needs to understand the intersection of gaming and high-level audio engineering, this stream offers a masterclass in how constraints can force a deeper understanding of signal flow.

The Physical Cost of Performance

Before diving into the software, Jordan sets a somber but necessary tone regarding his own physical limitations as a performer. He reveals that years of touring have taken a toll, describing a specific, nagging pain that prevents him from playing guitar with the same ease as before. "I've noticed that my piano playing has just... I've been better with... yeah," he admits, noting that he is now far more comfortable on the keys than on the fretboard. He offers a visceral metaphor for his condition: "Playing the guitar feels like I'm shaking my head a little bit... I have this like fatigue with it and it's an ongoing issue that's been getting worse and worse as I get to my elderly ages."

This personal confession grounds the technical review in human reality. Jordan weighs the option of surgery against the alternative of total inactivity, concluding that the mental toll of boredom outweighs the physical pain of playing. "I could have an operation but I would be out of commission for a while... so I feel like suffering from the pain is easier than suffering from the intense boredom I would feel from not being able to use my hands for weeks or months." This admission reframes his entire approach to music production; he isn't just tinkering with a new toy, he is adapting his craft to a changing body. Critics might argue that this focus on physical pain distracts from the software review, but it actually explains his intense focus on the "Sandbox Mode"—a space where he can experiment with logic and voltage without the physical strain of a guitar neck.

Redefining the Modular Experience

Once Jordan loads The Signal State, his commentary shifts from the personal to the philosophical. He immediately distinguishes this title from standard digital audio workstations or even other modular simulators like VCV Rack. The core of his argument is that the game forces the user to think in terms of analog computers rather than traditional synthesizers. "This is not a music making tool in the traditional sense like I wouldn't choose this over you know soft tube module or something like that," he states bluntly. "This is fun and the game is extremely fun... this is an actual video game."

Jordan's analysis of the "Sandbox Mode" reveals that the game's value isn't in its presets, but in its ability to simulate the raw logic of voltage control. He notes that the system has limitations that actually serve the educational purpose: "I feel like the wires do only carry voltages not audio signals so it is a little different than..." He struggles initially with the interface, even pausing the game to mute the background music, a moment that highlights the raw, unpolished nature of the simulation. "I just wonder how to get rid of the background music... synced playback that does it." This trial-and-error process is central to his thesis. The game isn't about making a perfect track instantly; it's about the struggle to make the machine work.

He draws a parallel to a specific pop culture moment to illustrate the satisfaction of solving these logic puzzles. "The story mode was nice like you're saving the world by patching it... for some reason it reminded me... of Eugene from the walking dead like like oh finally he has a purpose other than just like pissing everybody off on the show." This comparison is surprisingly apt. It suggests that the joy of The Signal State comes from the intellectual victory of making a chaotic system function, rather than the aesthetic pleasure of the resulting sound. A counterargument worth considering is that this "game-first" approach might alienate professional musicians who need reliable, immediate sound generation for production work. However, Jordan's point is that the process of building the signal path is the product itself.

The Logic of Sound

As Jordan dives deeper into the sandbox, he attempts to build complex sequences without relying on standard sequencers, pushing the limits of the game's logic gates. He admits his own frustration and the potential futility of his experiment for the audience: "I'm just probably being my normal very annoying self so let's just actually use a sequencer and grow up not have to do everything the super hard way all the time." Yet, this struggle is where the educational value lies. He realizes that the game is teaching him to think in terms of triggers, dividers, and adders rather than notes and chords.

The distinction he makes between a synthesizer and an analog computer is the piece's most significant insight. "It is a modular synth it looks like a modular synth but what it really is what you're really doing is uh analog computing actually a very powerful and useful thing." He demonstrates this by trying to create a melody without a sequencer, a task that proves nearly impossible without understanding the underlying voltage logic. "The goal of right here... is for me to make a melody without completely screwing it up and and literally me doing this is so stupid because I'm literally just trying to figure it out without using a sequencer which is like a waste of everyone's time."

Jordan's commentary effectively strips away the mystique of modular synthesis, revealing it as a problem-solving exercise. He acknowledges the game's limitations, noting that the drum voices are "really boring," but emphasizes that the fun comes from the system's flexibility. "This is a challenging game after all that's the whole point." He concludes that while the game may not replace professional software, it offers a unique, gamified pathway to understanding the complex logic that underpins electronic music.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's coverage succeeds because it refuses to treat The Signal State as a standard music app, instead framing it as a rigorous simulation of analog computing that demands intellectual engagement over instant gratification. The strongest part of his argument is the distinction between playing a synthesizer and programming a computer, a nuance often lost in general gaming reviews. The biggest vulnerability is the steep learning curve he describes, which may deter casual listeners looking for immediate musical results, but for those willing to engage with the logic, the payoff is a deeper understanding of sound itself.
