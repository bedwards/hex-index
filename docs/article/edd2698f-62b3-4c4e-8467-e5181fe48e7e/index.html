March 27, 1918: The Union of Bessarabia and Romania. Past and Present.

David Smith · Moldova Matters ·Mar 27, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

David Smith cuts through the fog of modern political posturing to reveal a startling truth: the 1918 Union of Bessarabia was not a foregone conclusion of ethnic destiny, but a desperate, chaotic gamble made in the shadow of collapsing empires. While today's discourse often treats the event as a mythic reunion, Smith's forensic look at the vote count and the linguistic reality of the time exposes a far messier, more human story of survival against Bolshevik anarchy. For a busy listener navigating the current war in Ukraine, this historical clarity is not just academic; it is a vital correction to the dangerous simplifications that often drive modern foreign policy.

The Illusion of a Unified Front

Smith immediately dismantles the romantic notion that the union was a smooth, pre-planned merger. He writes, "The Union of Bessarabia with Romania came as a surprise to the residents of the other regions on the right bank of the Prut river that make up modern-day Romania." This is a crucial distinction that many modern nationalists overlook. The Bessarabian peasantry was not marching toward Bucharest with flags of liberation; they were terrified of the "Bolshevik raiding parties" and "rampant violence" that had consumed the Russian Empire.

The author argues that the local parliament, Sfatul Țării, was a "quasi-representative body" born of necessity during the war, yet it held a legitimacy that the chaotic times demanded. Smith notes that "the Bolsheviks, Mensheviks and other Russian political parties as well as the province's main ethnic groups were represented," and they all recognized it as the supreme institution. This framing is effective because it grounds the union in democratic procedure rather than imperial ambition. However, critics might note that Smith glosses over the extent of Romanian military pressure, which some historians argue coerced the parliament's decision more than the author admits.

"The divided vote speaks to the fact there was some room for freedom of the ballot."

This observation is the piece's most compelling evidence against the idea of a monolithic pro-Romanian bloc. Smith highlights that 36 members abstained and only three voted against the union, including a Ukrainian named Ștefan Balamez. The fate of Balamez, who was later arrested by the Soviets and died in Siberia, serves as a grim reminder that the political lines of 1918 were not the same as today's. Smith uses this to prove that the vote "did not clearly map onto what we might now call pro-Romanian or pro-Russian lines." This nuance is vital; it suggests that identity in Bessarabia was fluid and pragmatic, not rigidly ideological.

The Paradox of Language and Identity

Perhaps the most striking revelation in Smith's coverage is the linguistic reality of the union. Despite the political goal of reuniting with Romania, "most of the discussions took place in Russian" during the historic meeting. Smith explains that this was because Russian remained the language of education, while Romanian was known only at a "vernacular level."

This creates a fascinating paradox that Smith explores with clarity: "The Union of Bessarabia with Romania made the century long process of Russification of the Moldavians of Bessarabia reversible." The author argues that the interwar period was not just a political union but a cultural renaissance where "speaking Romanian more and more prestigious and respectful." He points out that even the Gagauz, a Turkic minority, became "one of the most loyal ethnic groups to the Romanian state" because they were not seduced by Bolshevik propaganda.

"The language was lively and loved by the Moldavians / Bessarabians across ethnic groups, including by the Gagauz."

This section effectively counters the narrative that the union was purely an ethnic Romanian project imposed on others. By highlighting the integration of minorities like the Gagauz and Bulgarians, Smith shows a more inclusive vision of the interwar state. Yet, he does not shy away from the darker side of this era, acknowledging that "Romania bears responsibility for its role in the Holocaust during World War II," where hundreds of thousands of Jews were killed. This admission adds necessary moral weight to the argument, preventing the piece from becoming mere nationalist apologia.

1918 vs. The Modern Reality

Smith's most urgent contribution is his comparison of the 1918 context to the present day. He warns against drawing false parallels between the collapse of the Russian Empire and the current war in Ukraine. "The Union of Bessarabia with Romania was decided in the context of collapsing empires, limited state capacity, and a very different international system," he writes.

The author is blunt about the current political climate: "Any comparable decision today would have to follow true democratic procedures, reflect the will of the population, and take place within a complex European and international legal framework." He suggests that while the "legacy of 1918 continues to shape political imagination," a direct replication is "unlikely." Instead, the focus should be on the "unresolved status of the Left Bank, or Transnistria," which he describes as a "domestic and regional security problem."

"In this sense, while the legacy of 1918 continues to shape political imagination and identity, its direct replication under current conditions appears unlikely."

This is a sobering conclusion that challenges the "imminent union" narratives often heard in Bucharest and Chișinău. Smith argues that a deterioration in security would likely lead allies to prioritize "stability and deterrence" over complex political integration. This pragmatic stance is the piece's strongest asset, grounding high-minded historical ideals in the hard realities of geopolitics.

Bottom Line

David Smith's analysis is a masterclass in separating historical fact from modern myth, proving that the 1918 Union was a fragile, chaotic survival strategy rather than a pre-ordained destiny. Its greatest strength lies in its willingness to confront the linguistic and ethnic complexities of the era, but its biggest vulnerability is the difficulty of translating this nuanced history into a clear path forward for a region currently under siege. Listeners should watch for how this historical realism influences the next wave of policy debates regarding Moldova's European integration versus territorial reunification.

What happened and how was it possible? Was it planned in advance? Why is it still hotly debated in Romania and Moldova? And could it happen again?

On March 27, 1918, Bessarabia, a former Tsarist province dating back to 1812, when the Principality of Moldavia was divided in two, united with the Kingdom of Romania. Bessarabia, unlike modern Moldova, had clear cut natural borders, stretching from the Prut River in the West and the Dniester River in the East, Habsburg Bukovina in the north and the Danube mouth, and Black Sea in the south.

The decision was made by Sfatul Țării, the local parliament, a quasi-representative body like many others in the national peripheries of the former Tsarist Empire. In the later parts of WWI, similar indirectly representative bodies were created at a time where war prevented direct elections. Such parliaments decided the fates of other provinces and fragments of empire - creating national states (e.g. Poland and Estonia), or recreating lost independent states out of pieces of the Russian Empire (e.g. Lithuania).

This is to say Sfatul Țării was broadly representative and held democratic legitimacy according to the standards of the time. Moreover, it had legitimacy to decide the future of the province. The Bolsheviks, Mensheviks and other Russian political parties as well as the province’s main ethnic groups were represented. They all recognized the Sfatul Țării as the supreme and legitimate institution of the province.

Since then, the legitimacy of Sfatul Țării’s vote on March 27, 1918 has often been questioned or contested with the argument that the Romanian Army controlled the province since mid January 1918. At that time, the Sfatul Țării had requested Romanian support in a situation of deteriorating security and rampant violence. Much of this was driven by the disintegration of the Russian Army on the Romanian front and the transformation of these units into Bolshevik raiding parties in the chaotic context of the Russian civil war.

With this background, on the historical day of March 27, 19181, 3 members of the Sfatul Țării voted against the Union with Romania and 36 abstained. Only 86 voted for, while 12 were declared absentees.
