Every significant B2B company is becoming a security company

Ross Haleliuk · Venture in Security ·Mar 17, 2026 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Ross Haleliuk delivers a startling verdict on the future of enterprise software: the era of the standalone security vendor is ending, replaced by a landscape where every major B2B platform must become a security company. He backs this bold claim not with vague predictions, but with a staggering ledger of recent acquisitions, revealing that the largest deals for tech giants like Google, ServiceNow, and Cisco are no longer about new features, but about buying protection. For busy leaders watching their cloud bills and risk profiles, this is the definitive signal that security is no longer a department—it is the product.

The Acquisition Signal

Haleliuk cuts through the noise of market speculation by pointing to hard capital allocation. "If you have been following the news over the past several years, you know that cybersecurity has been exploding in growth," he observes, before dropping a statistic that redefines the industry: "Google's largest-ever acquisition is a security company (Wiz)." This is not an anomaly; it is a pattern. He notes that "ServiceNow's largest-ever acquisition is a security company (Armis)" and "Mastercard's second-largest ever acquisition is also a security company (Recorded Future)." The sheer volume of capital flowing into security from non-security giants suggests a fundamental shift in how value is created.

The author's analysis of Google's strategy is particularly sharp. He writes, "Out of more than 260 acquisitions, only 65 had disclosed prices, and across those deals, Google spent nearly as much on just three cybersecurity companies... as it did on the other 62 disclosed acquisitions combined." This data point is the piece's anchor. It proves that for a cloud provider, security is not a side project; it is the primary lever for enterprise trust. Haleliuk explains that "Google is no longer just an advertising company, it is an enterprise cloud company competing with AWS and Microsoft," and in this arena, "security is foundational." Without native, first-class security, a cloud platform cannot win the most critical workloads. This logic holds up under scrutiny, especially when contrasted with the history of Alphabet's mergers and acquisitions, where the company historically prioritized consumer growth over enterprise infrastructure until the cloud wars demanded a pivot.

Security is no longer a standalone category reserved for specialized vendors, it has become a foundational capability expected from every enterprise platform.

Critics might argue that platform vendors will never match the depth of specialized security firms, but Haleliuk counters this by noting that platforms are buying the best talent to bridge that gap instantly. "Acquiring companies like Mandiant and Wiz allows Google to instantly strengthen its credibility with CISOs and security teams, something that takes decades to build organically." The strategy is about speed and integration, not just capability.

The Gravity of Workflows and Data

The argument extends beyond cloud infrastructure into the very workflows that run modern businesses. Haleliuk introduces the concept of "workflow gravity," arguing that "once a company owns where work flows, it gets the ability to influence and eventually own how decisions are made." ServiceNow's $7.75 billion purchase of Armis is the prime example. The author posits that "every incident, vulnerability, asset, configuration change, and compliance process ultimately turns into a workflow," making security a natural extension of the platform's core value.

This is a compelling reframing of the market. Haleliuk suggests that "ServiceNow is betting on what I call the 'workflow gravity' effect," effectively turning the company into a cybersecurity powerhouse by default. The implication is that if you manage the IT ticket, you must also manage the security patch. However, the author acknowledges that not every player is making this move. He points out that "Atlassian... has largely missed the opportunity to dominate developer security," despite its dominance in developer workflows. This contrast highlights that the transition is a strategic choice, not an inevitability, though the pressure to make it is mounting.

Similarly, the financial sector is being pulled into this vortex. "Mastercard isn't just a payment processor, it operates one of the largest global transaction networks," Haleliuk writes. For them, security is existential. By acquiring Recorded Future, they are not just buying a tool; they are "turning cybersecurity into both a defensive capability and a new growth business." This mirrors the broader trend where data visibility becomes the moat. Just as zero trust architecture has evolved from a buzzword to a mandatory standard, companies like Mastercard are realizing that their data networks are only as strong as their ability to detect threats in real-time.

The End of the Point Solution?

The most provocative implication of Haleliuk's analysis is what it means for the future of cybersecurity startups. He warns that "if a platform vendor can ship a 'good enough' native security capability, a standalone product has to be substantially better to justify its existence." This raises the bar for innovation dramatically. The era of selling a single-point solution that plugs into a fragmented stack is fading. "The result is a world where cybersecurity is both its own massive market and a horizontal capability that every major B2B company must build, buy, or partner to deliver."

Haleliuk suggests that the path forward for founders is not to fight the platforms, but to become their innovation layer. "The future of cybersecurity startups is increasingly connected to the ecosystems of large B2B platforms, not just security products." This is a sobering reality check. The list of potential acquirers is expanding beyond the traditional security giants like Palo Alto Networks to include the likes of HP, Mitsubishi Electric, and Apple. He cites how "HP acquired Bromium to use security as a selling point for its devices," embedding protection directly into the hardware. This vertical integration makes it harder for standalone vendors to compete on convenience.

Yet, a counterargument worth considering is that platform-native security often lacks the neutrality required for complex, multi-cloud environments. If Google secures Google Cloud, does it have the incentive to protect workloads on AWS? Haleliuk touches on this by noting that Wiz gives Google "deep visibility into customer deployments across other clouds," suggesting that the best platforms are becoming agnostic enough to remain useful. Still, the tension between platform loyalty and open security remains a vulnerability in this new model.

Bottom Line

Ross Haleliuk's argument is a powerful corrective to the assumption that security will remain a siloed industry; the evidence of massive M&A activity proves that security is now the primary battleground for enterprise platform dominance. While the strategy of buying one's way to security maturity works for giants with deep pockets, it leaves a precarious gap for startups that cannot compete on scale or integration. The reader should watch not just for more acquisitions, but for how these platforms integrate security into their core pricing and contracts, effectively making it invisible and unavoidable.

The future of cybersecurity startups is increasingly connected to the ecosystems of large B2B platforms, not just security products.
