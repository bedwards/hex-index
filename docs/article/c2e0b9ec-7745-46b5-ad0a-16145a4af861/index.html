America’s Birth Rate “Crisis”

PolyMatter · PolyMatter ·Sep 28, 2024 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

PolyMatter exposes a chilling paradox at the heart of modern America: we are closing our colleges not because students don't want to learn, but because there are simply not enough young people left to fill the seats. While most headlines scream about tuition costs or political polarization, this piece identifies a silent, demographic time bomb that has been ticking since 2007, threatening to reshape the nation's economic and social fabric in ways that policy makers are only just beginning to grasp.

The Vanishing Cohort

The author anchors the argument in a stark, real-world tragedy: the sudden closure of Mount Ida College. "April 5th 2018 was just another Thursday... but when they woke up the next day their school no longer existed." This abrupt end serves as a microcosm for a national trend where "a college closes about once a week." The narrative effectively shifts from a specific institutional failure to a macro-demographic reality, noting that "the vast majority of college students are under 25 and America is running low on under 25y olds."

The data presented is sobering. Between 1990 and 2007, family sizes were stable, but then "it suddenly began to fall precipitously and it's been falling ever since." PolyMatter highlights the scale of the coming drop, citing Dr. Nathan Grawe's projection that university enrollments will fall by 11% in the next five years. "That's a decrease of nearly 300,000 people a year," the author notes, a figure that seems abstract until one considers that "a third of US universities have less than a thousand students." This framing is crucial; it moves the crisis from a regional issue to a nationwide structural collapse. Critics might argue that online education or international recruitment could fill these gaps, but the author correctly points out that these solutions are insufficient for the sheer volume of the demographic cliff.

The Economic and Cultural Trap

The commentary then dissects the "why," dismantling the idea that money alone is the culprit. While the 2007 financial crisis clearly played a role, the author observes that birth rates usually bounce back after a recession. "The only thing is it usually bounces back... that's exactly what we saw for example during and after the recent pandemic but that's not what happened after 2007." Instead, the article points to a perfect storm of rising costs in housing, childcare, and education, which have outpaced inflation dramatically.

However, PolyMatter makes a more nuanced claim: money isn't the whole story. The cultural shift in parenting has transformed child-rearing from a "passive state" into a "NeverEnding verb." Modern parents are trapped in a high-stakes game where "keeping up with the Joneses is no longer about boats or cars... it's about summer camps violin instructors honors programs and soccer leagues." This cultural pressure creates a barrier that cash bonuses alone cannot overcome. As the author puts it, "unlike the status games of yester year you can choose to opt out of these simply by not having children." This insight is powerful because it explains why government incentives in other countries have often failed to reverse the trend; the barrier isn't just the price tag, it's the exhausting, high-anxiety nature of modern parenting.

The gap between what we want and what we get, desired versus actual fertility, has tripled.

The Illusion of Choice

Perhaps the most surprising revelation in the piece is that Americans haven't changed their minds about family size. Citing Gallup data, PolyMatter notes that "the average American still thinks 2.7 kids is ideal," a number that has remained remarkably steady for decades. The decline in birth rates is not a rejection of family life, but a failure to achieve it. "What has changed is people's ability to fulfill those ideals." The author argues that we have created a "success sequence" where one must finish school, start a career, get married, and buy a house before having children. The problem is that achieving these prerequisites has become exponentially harder.

The interaction between economics and culture is where the argument shines. "As the bar for what a 'good parent' looks like has grown... it only makes sense that young people would take much longer to get there." This delay pushes many against the biological clock. The author notes that the median age for marriage and first birth has risen significantly, creating a situation where "progressing through it has become harder it's no wonder they're having fewer children." A counterargument worth considering is that this "success sequence" is a cultural construct that could be abandoned, yet the author suggests that the economic reality of housing and wages makes it a near-universal prerequisite for stability.

Bottom Line

PolyMatter's strongest contribution is reframing the birth rate decline not as a cultural rejection of family, but as a systemic failure to support the traditional path to parenthood. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its assumption that the "success sequence" is immutable; however, the piece effectively warns that without addressing the intersection of housing costs and parenting expectations, the demographic collapse will continue to ripple through every sector of American life, from higher education to the labor market.
