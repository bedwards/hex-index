Iran is Causing An Economic Panic In China

Chris Chappell · China Uncensored ·Mar 6, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Chris Chappell delivers a blistering assessment of China's strategic miscalculation in the Middle East, arguing that the regime's hollow alliance with Iran is backfiring with immediate economic consequences. Rather than offering a standard geopolitical recap, Chappell frames the narrative around a singular, ironic truth: Beijing's attempt to posture as a global power is crumbling under the weight of its own energy insecurity. The piece stands out by connecting the dots between a bizarre American political inside joke and the very real collapse of Chinese oil prices and military spending.

The Sword of Chang and the Illusion of Power

Chappell opens with a disarming look at the phrase "unleash Chang," a conservative code word for overwhelming force that traces its lineage to the anti-communist leader Chiang Kai-shek. He notes that while the origin story is murky, the intent is clear: "You're going to really begin to perceive a change in the scope and in the intensity of these attacks." By linking this rhetoric to the historical figure who fled to Taiwan, Chappell underscores the deep ideological divide that defines the current US-China standoff. This historical grounding is crucial; it reminds the listener that the "unleashing" of force is not a new concept but a recurring theme in the struggle between the two systems.

The author then pivots to the core of his argument: the economic panic gripping Beijing. As Chappell puts it, "China is now ordering its largest oil refineries to suspend exports of diesel and gasoline" because prices have skyrocketed to roughly $100 a barrel. This is not merely a market fluctuation; it is a structural crisis. He points out that China has set its lowest economic growth target since 1991, a stark admission of weakness from a regime that prides itself on stability. The irony is palpable: the very regime that condemns US actions in Iran is suffering the most because its "friend" in Tehran cannot guarantee the flow of cheap oil.

The CCP, folks, the world's most infamous fair weather friend.

Chappell's characterization of China's foreign policy as transactional rather than ideological is the piece's sharpest insight. He argues that Beijing's support for Iran evaporates the moment the cost of that support becomes too high. "Basically, we're not providing military support to Iran. We only offer moral backing," he summarizes the CCP's current stance. This aligns with historical patterns where authoritarian regimes prioritize regime survival over ideological solidarity. Critics might note that China's restraint could also be a strategic calculation to avoid direct conflict with the US, rather than simple cowardice. However, Chappell's evidence of domestic economic strain suggests the latter is the primary driver.

The Limits of Influence and the Cost of Friendship

The commentary deepens as Chappell examines the tangible failures of China's military aid. He highlights reports that Chinese-supplied air defenses failed to protect Iran's leadership, leaving Beijing in an awkward position. "If China spent half as much time defending their friends as they do defending the failure of their defenses, maybe they wouldn't have failed," he quips, exposing the gap between Beijing's propaganda and reality. This failure is particularly damaging given the recent loss of Venezuela as an oil partner, leaving China with few reliable energy sources.

In a striking comparison, Chappell contrasts China's inaction with Taiwan's humanitarian response. "So, the lesson here is that it pays to be friends with Taiwan and not China. Specifically, $200,000," he observes, using a specific dollar amount to underscore the absurdity of the situation. This juxtaposition serves to delegitimize the narrative of Chinese benevolence. The author further illustrates the reach of Chinese influence by detailing the protests against Palantir, a US defense contractor, orchestrated by Neville Roy Singham's network. He describes Singham as a "Marxist billionaire who resides in Shanghai and pushes CCP propaganda," linking domestic US dissent to foreign state interests.

The piece also touches on the bizarre case of three University of Michigan graduate students accused of smuggling worms into the US, suggesting Chinese diplomatic intervention may have led to the dismissal of charges. Chappell wryly notes, "The term having worms is almost as bad as saying something is moist," before highlighting the deeper concern: "the Chinese consulate getting involved moved the needle, which is deeply concerning because it shows that CCP influence in this country runs deeper than many of us suspected." This section serves as a warning that the conflict is not just about missiles and oil, but about the erosion of legal and institutional integrity within the US.

Bottom Line

Chappell's most compelling argument is that China's global ambitions are being strangled by its own economic fragility, a vulnerability that will only deepen as the conflict in the Middle East drags on. The piece's greatest strength lies in its ability to connect disparate events—from a Florida political joke to a biological smuggling case—into a coherent narrative of Chinese decline. However, the argument occasionally leans too heavily on satire, which may obscure the genuine strategic calculations Beijing is making to avoid a wider war. Readers should watch for whether China's economic pain forces a genuine shift in foreign policy or if the regime doubles down despite the costs.
