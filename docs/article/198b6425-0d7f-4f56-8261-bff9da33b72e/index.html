Australia Has Turned Into a Dystopian Police State

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Oct 7, 2021 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

The Hated One delivers a chilling indictment of Australia's rapid transformation from a liberal democracy into what they term a "dystopian police state." This is not a standard political critique; it is a forensic dismantling of how surveillance powers were quietly expanded during the pandemic and the East Timor oil dispute, arguing that the nation now possesses the least oversight of any democracy. For the busy listener concerned about the erosion of civil liberties globally, this piece serves as a stark warning that the mechanisms of control are often built in the shadows of crisis.

The East Timor Precedent

The author anchors their argument in a specific, shocking historical event: the 2004 bugging of East Timor's cabinet by the Australian Secret Intelligence Service to secure oil interests. The Hated One writes, "Australia prevented a witness from attending an international trial and destroyed an office of an attorney all for the sake of grabbing cheap oil from an impoverished nation." This incident is presented not as an anomaly, but as the foundational moment of an arrogant, abusive diplomacy that prioritized resource extraction over international law. The narrative details how the government raided the homes of a whistleblower and his lawyer without a terrorism warrant, using anti-terrorism laws as a procedural loophole. This framing is effective because it moves the conversation from abstract surveillance fears to concrete, documented acts of state overreach.

Critics might argue that intelligence agencies in all nations operate under similar secrecy to protect national security, but The Hated One counters this by highlighting the unique lack of external review in Australia. "Australian intelligence is one of the most powerful entities in the world, it has the least amount of oversight out of all democratic nations," they assert. The author points out that while committees in the US, UK, and Canada can review ongoing operations, Australian spies essentially review themselves. This distinction is crucial; it suggests a structural flaw rather than just a temporary emergency measure.

"No Edward Snowden or Julian Assange could ever come out of Australia because even reporting journalists would be charged with the same crimes as whistleblowers."

The Pandemic as a Catalyst

The commentary then shifts to the pandemic, arguing that the health crisis was merely an accelerator for powers that had been expanding since 9/11. The Hated One describes how Victoria, Australia, declared a state of disaster covering the entire state, granting police the power to "suspend any law that could contradict with the response to the disaster." The author notes that the government suspended parliament to avoid scrutiny, a move one scholar observed had never happened even during the World Wars or the Spanish Flu. This historical comparison lends weight to the claim that the response was disproportionate.

The piece details the introduction of facial recognition home quarantine software, which required citizens to take selfies with location data or face police intervention. The Hated One sarcastically notes, "Who needs KGB agents when you have people willingly spending their own money to buy advanced tracking devices?" This rhetorical flourish underscores the insidious nature of the surveillance: it is not just imposed, but normalized as a convenience. The argument is that the pandemic provided the perfect cover to test technologies that would have been rejected in normal times.

The Architecture of Mass Surveillance

Moving beyond the pandemic, the author dissects the legislative machinery that now allows for total digital intrusion. The Hated One explains the "Identify and Disrupt" laws, which grant federal police the power to modify, delete, or copy data and even take over accounts. The text highlights a 2014 amendment that allows a single warrant to wiretap the entire internet by defining a "network" so broadly that it includes every device connected to the web. "Australian spies can now legally access, modify and delete files, install malware, gain higher privileges and monitor or impersonate people on any device connected to the internet anywhere in the world," The Hated One writes. This is perhaps the most alarming section, as it suggests that Australian intelligence can legally weaponize the internet itself.

The commentary on the "anti-encryption law" is particularly biting. The author argues that the law forces technology companies to build backdoors under threat of imprisonment, effectively making Australia a "global security threat." The Hated One writes, "Any encryption backdoor intended for targeted individuals would be a backdoor on everyone else using that service." They point out that the law even bars employees from discussing these requests, meaning a tech company's own staff could be forced to compromise their employer's security without the bosses knowing. This creates a scenario where the government can hijack global infrastructure through local employees.

Critics might suggest that these powers are necessary to combat terrorism and foreign interference, but The Hated One dismisses this by noting the lack of definition for "systemic weakness" in the law, leaving the door open for universal backdoors. The argument is that the safeguards are illusory, designed to look like protections while enabling total access.

The Global Implications

The piece concludes by connecting Australia's domestic policies to the "Five Eyes" intelligence alliance, which includes the US, UK, Canada, and New Zealand. The Hated One warns that "any surveillance capability in one member state is shared with all the others," meaning that Australia's radical laws effectively lower the privacy bar for all its allies. The author notes that Australia bypasses European GDPR protections because the EU legislation doesn't restrict government data collection. "Nobody has more power with less oversight than Australian intelligence," they state, emphasizing the danger of a "elite class" that operates above the law.

The final blow to the reader is the realization that this is not just an Australian problem. The Hated One writes, "If you think none of this affects you because you don't live in Australia, remember that Australia is a member of the Five Eyes surveillance alliance." This reframes the article from a local critique to a global warning about the erosion of digital privacy and civil liberties.

"Nobody has more power with less oversight than Australian intelligence. It gives the phrase an elite class a whole new meaning."

Bottom Line

The strongest part of The Hated One's argument is the detailed tracing of how emergency powers and intelligence laws have been permanently embedded into the legal framework, transforming temporary measures into permanent surveillance tools. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a singular, highly critical perspective that offers little nuance regarding the genuine security threats Australia faces, though this does not diminish the factual weight of the legislative changes described. Readers should watch for how these Australian precedents influence legislation in other Five Eyes nations, as the normalization of such powers could quickly become a global standard.
