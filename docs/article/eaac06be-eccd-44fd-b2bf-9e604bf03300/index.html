Why Culture Wars Matter

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Jun 5, 2023 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now makes a startling claim that cuts through the noise of modern political punditry: the fiercest battles of our time are not fought in legislatures or courtrooms, but in the quiet, emotional response to a film, a song, or a novel. By tracing the lineage of these conflicts back to the German philosopher Friedrich Schiller and the Enlightenment, the author argues that we cannot understand the current polarization without acknowledging that our political values are downstream from our aesthetic experiences. This is not a dry history lesson; it is a urgent reminder that the stories we consume are actively shaping the laws we live under.

The Collision of Reason and Passion

The piece begins by dismantling the comfortable Enlightenment assumption that we can neatly separate the rational world of politics from the emotional world of art. Then & Now writes, "Culture, politics, and our own psychological lives are inseparably intertwined." This observation is crucial because it explains why debates over movies or textbooks often feel so visceral and personal compared to arguments about tax brackets. The author suggests that while we have built a modern state based on rules, schedules, and bureaucratic management, we remain driven by the "romantic tradition" of passion, myth, and national identity.

The argument gains traction when it highlights how these two realms constantly bleed into one another. As Then & Now puts it, "The rational politician relies on poetic rhetoric and impassioned speeches... but when it comes down to it... we get into a murkier area where national identity, passions, or cultural stories... collide." This framing is effective because it validates the intuition many feel: that political movements are fueled by something deeper than policy platforms. However, the piece could benefit from acknowledging that this "muddy" area is also where misinformation and demagoguery thrive, not just noble ideals.

Schiller and the Revolutionary Power of Art

To ground this abstract connection, the author turns to Friedrich Schiller, a figure often overlooked in Anglophone discourse but central to understanding the roots of culture wars. Then & Now notes that Schiller watched the French Revolution descend into bloodshed and asked a pivotal question: "How is art related to freedom?" The author explains that Schiller believed the revolution failed because it tried to impose political freedom on a society that lacked the moral education to sustain it. Instead of just changing laws, Schiller argued we must change the human character through beauty.

The core of Schiller's thesis, as presented by Then & Now, is that "it is through beauty that one progresses towards freedom." The author paraphrases Schiller's idea that modern life fractures us into specialized roles, but art reunites our senses and our reason. In a beautiful story or song, we experience "living form," where emotion and logic play together. This is a powerful insight for a modern audience feeling fragmented by specialization and digital noise. The author writes, "The play of art encourages us to play with all our faculties... this free play frees us up from the narrowness caused by specialization."

The Beautiful alone we enjoy both as individual and as group.

This quote captures the unique power of art to bridge the gap between personal feeling and collective identity. Then & Now argues that this is why culture wars matter: they are battles over who gets to define the "beautiful" and the "moral" for the whole society. Critics might note that Schiller's faith in the universalizing power of beauty can be naive; history shows that art can also be used to dehumanize and divide just as easily as it can unite. Yet, the author's point stands that the stakes are incredibly high because these aesthetic judgments form the bedrock of our political reality.

The Unacknowledged Legislators

The piece concludes by connecting Schiller's 18th-century philosophy to the modern reality of "imagined communities." Then & Now observes that we relate to strangers not just through laws, but through the shared cultural touchstones of television, music, and film. The author cites Percy Shelley's famous assertion that "poets are the unacknowledged legislators of the world," suggesting that artists are constantly molding new moral horizons. The argument posits that progressive wins in history, from civil rights to women's suffrage, were always accompanied by powerful artistic movements that shifted the cultural mood.

As Then & Now puts it, "Artists of all types are engaged in the practice of creating these new moral horizons... surround them with noble and great forms and symbols until illusion conquers reality." This is a compelling, if slightly idealistic, view of cultural change. It suggests that the path to political reform runs directly through the theater and the cinema. While the author leans heavily on the idea that liberals have historically won these wars through tolerance, a counterargument worth considering is that conservatives are currently winning by mastering the same emotional and aesthetic tools, proving that the battlefield is neutral ground.

Bottom Line

Then & Now delivers a sophisticated and necessary argument: the culture war is not a distraction from politics, but the very soil in which politics grows. The piece's greatest strength is its historical depth, using Schiller to show that the fight over art is as old as the fight for freedom itself. Its vulnerability lies in a somewhat optimistic view of art's ability to unify, potentially underestimating how easily aesthetic tools can be weaponized for division. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: ignore the cultural battles at your peril, for they are writing the laws of tomorrow.
