Muslim Schism: How Islam Split into the Sunni and Shia Branches

Kings and Generals · Kings and Generals ·Apr 20, 2021 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In an era where headlines reduce the Sunni-Shia divide to a monolithic sectarian war, Kings and Generals offers a crucial corrective: the schism was not a theological inevitability but a violent political fracture rooted in the specific, messy succession crisis following the death of Prophet Muhammad. This narrative distinguishes itself by treating the split not as a static dogma, but as a dynamic evolution of power struggles, where the definition of a single Arabic word—mawla—and the silence of a dying prophet altered the course of human history. For the busy listener, this is not just a history lesson; it is a masterclass in how political ambiguity can calcify into religious identity.

The Succession Crisis and the Weight of Words

The core of the argument rests on the immediate aftermath of Muhammad's death in 632, where the absence of a clear heir created a vacuum that different factions filled with their own interpretations. Kings and Generals writes, "But who was going to succeed aging Muhammad and this is the root of the split in Islam." This framing is effective because it grounds the entire theological divergence in a tangible, historical moment of uncertainty rather than abstract belief.

The author highlights the pivotal sermon at Gadir Khumm, where Muhammad reportedly took Ali's hand and declared, "Anyone who has me as his mawla has Ali as his mawla." The commentary here is sharp: the schism hinges entirely on the translation of mawla. Kings and Generals notes that "Shia theology believes that in Gadiaakum, the prophet designated Ali as his successor by calling him Mher... one of which is leader or master," while Sunnis interpret it as "a proclamation of affinity... interpret the word mher using its second definition, a friend." This linguistic pivot is the piece's strongest analytical move, demonstrating how a single ambiguous term can spawn two distinct civilizations.

"The split in Islam... is the root of the split in Islam."

The narrative then moves to the "pen and paper" incident, a moment of tragic irony where the dying prophet's wish to write a guiding statement was thwarted by his own companions. Kings and Generals writes, "The prophet is seriously ill, and we have got Allah's book with us, and that is sufficient for us." This quote captures the arrogance of the moment that Shia tradition views as a conspiracy, while Sunnis view as a pragmatic reliance on the Quran. The author effectively uses this to show that the split was not just about who should lead, but about the very nature of authority: is it divinely appointed or community-elected?

Critics might note that the video transcript glosses over the complexity of the early Medinan political landscape, where tribal alliances often outweighed religious loyalty. However, the focus on the personal betrayal felt by Ali's faction remains a compelling emotional anchor for the narrative.

The First Fitna and the Transformation of Martyrdom

As the story progresses from theological debate to armed conflict, the tone shifts from historical analysis to a tragedy of escalating violence. The author details the assassination of Caliph Uthman and the subsequent civil war, known as the First Fitna, where Ali's attempt to centralize power clashed with the regional ambitions of Muawia. Kings and Generals writes, "Neither side was keen to commit to a major battle. But after three months of occasional skirmishes, when serious fighting finally broke out, Muawia's followers called for an arbitration... riding out with copies of the Quran on their lances to bring the conflict to a stop."

This description of the Battle of Siffin is crucial. It illustrates how religious symbols were weaponized for political stalemates, leading to the emergence of the Kharijites, a third faction that rejected both Ali and Muawia. The author explains that "Ali was forced to agree, but some of his followers objected and abandoned him," a moment that fractured the unity of the early Muslim community beyond repair.

The narrative culminates in the tragedy of Karbala, where the political struggle transformed into a spiritual martyrdom that defines Shia identity to this day. Kings and Generals writes, "The Umeiad army had orders to prevent Hussein's men from accessing the Euphrates River in order to force them to concede due to lack of water." This detail underscores the brutality of the conflict, turning a political dispute into a massacre of the Prophet's family. The author argues that this event was the "final straw, completing the schism in the Islamic world," transforming the Shia from a political party into a distinct religious branch centered on the veneration of suffering.

"The killing of Ali, the massacre of Hussein, his family members and companions strengthened the sense of injustice against the prophet's family among the Shia Muslims and turned martyrdom into one of its main pillars."

This section effectively connects the historical event to modern practice, explaining why the month of Muharram and the day of Ashura remain the peak of Shia mourning. The author notes that "Millions of devoted Shia make the Arbain pilgrimage on foot to the holy shrine of Imam Hussein in Cabala every year," grounding the ancient history in a living, breathing tradition. A counterargument worth considering is whether the video overstates the immediacy of the theological split; many historians argue that the distinct religious identities took centuries to fully crystallize, rather than forming instantly at Karbala. Yet, for the purpose of understanding the emotional core of the divide, the narrative holds firm.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals succeeds in demystifying the Sunni-Shia split by tracing it back to a specific sequence of political failures and personal betrayals rather than abstract theological differences. The strongest part of the argument is its focus on the ambiguity of the Prophet's final days, which serves as the fertile ground for the schism. Its biggest vulnerability is the inevitable simplification of complex tribal and political dynamics into a binary narrative, but for a general audience, this clarity is a necessary trade-off. Readers should watch for how these historical grievances continue to shape modern geopolitics, proving that the past is never truly dead in the Middle East.
