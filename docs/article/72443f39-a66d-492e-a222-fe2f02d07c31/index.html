Top five climate science scandals 2025

Roger Pielke Jr. · The Honest Broker ·Dec 29, 2025 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In a field often paralyzed by partisan finger-pointing, Roger Pielke Jr. delivers a stinging indictment not of political opponents, but of the scientific community itself. He argues that the most dangerous threat to climate policy isn't denialism, but the community's own willingness to embrace flawed data and institutional designs that invite political interference. This is a rare, self-critical audit that suggests the path to credibility lies in admitting error, not doubling down on certainty.

The Integrity of Data and the Cost of Convenience

The most immediate and damning charge Pielke Jr. levels concerns the use of a fabricated economic dataset known as "ICAT" in peer-reviewed literature. He notes that "in any area of scientific publishing, you will not find a more open and shut case for the retraction of published studies than with those peer-reviewed papers that have used the so-called 'ICAT dataset' of economic losses from hurricanes." The core of his argument is that this fake data has not only polluted academic journals but has been elevated to influence major assessments by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and the U.S. National Climate Assessment.

Pielke Jr. frames this not as a conspiracy, but as a failure of basic scientific hygiene. He writes, "Papers that used the fake dataset should be retracted. The IPCC and USNCA should issue corrections. None of this should be difficult, it would be science working as it should and showing the world that when mistakes are made, science self-corrects because that is how science progresses towards truths and earns trust." The strength of this position is its appeal to the fundamental social contract of science: the ability to admit and fix errors. However, critics might argue that the sheer volume of papers relying on this data makes a rapid, coordinated retraction logistically difficult and potentially damaging to the careers of researchers who acted in good faith. Yet, Pielke Jr. insists that the alternative—letting bad science stand—is far more corrosive to public confidence.

"Developing effective policies related to energy, development, extreme events and disasters depends upon grappling effectively with these two truths: humans influence the climate system, and climate research is a deeply politicized endeavor."

Institutional Design Flaws in the National Climate Assessment

Moving from data to structure, Pielke Jr. identifies a 35-year-old legislative flaw that has turned the U.S. National Climate Assessment (USNCA) into a political football. He points out that the law establishing the assessment, P.L. 101-606, places the oversight bodies within the Executive Office of the President. "The 'Council' and 'Committee' referred to in the legislation are bodies established within the Executive Office of the President — that is, the White House," he explains. This design means the assessment has always been under the direct political control of the sitting administration, a fact that has allowed both Democratic and Republican leaders to weaponize the process.

He observes that "both Democratic and Republican administrations have used the assessment to advance their political goals, rather than produce a balanced assessment of the state of scientific understandings." This is a crucial distinction: the problem isn't just one party's interference, but a structural vulnerability that invites it. Pielke Jr. suggests that the scientific community has often been complicit, noting that "many in the media and scientific community welcome (and participate in) the politicization of the NCA when done by Democrats but oppose it when done by Republicans." The proposed solution is a legislative fix to remove the process from White House control, a move that would require Congress to amend a law Pielke Jr. has studied since his doctoral work in the 1990s.

The Weaponization of Science Policy and the "Woke" Narrative

The commentary then shifts to the aggressive dismantling of federal climate research capabilities by the incoming administration, a move Pielke Jr. describes as a "campaign of vengeance." He details how the administration's blueprint, Project 2025, targets specific agencies like NOAA and the EPA, labeling their work as part of a "climate change alarm industry." He cites a training video where a high-ranking appointee stated the administration "will have to eradicate climate change references from absolutely everywhere" in government.

Pielke Jr. argues that this approach throws the baby out with the bathwater, destroying useful scientific functions in a bid to eliminate what they deem "woke programs." He writes, "It is at once possible to critique the Biden Administration's approach to climate change as an 'existential threat,' and recognize that there are indeed risks that should be seriously considered." The author's recommendation here is less about stopping the cuts—which he admits are largely out of the scientific community's hands in the short term—and more about the long-term need to "reestablish our end of the social contract between science and society." He urges scientists to assess how the field came to be at "war" with a major political party, a reflection that is often absent from scientific discourse.

The Distortion of Risk and the Trap of Extreme Scenarios

Finally, Pielke Jr. turns his critique toward the financial sector and the modeling scenarios that drive policy. He challenges the narrative of a unique "climate risk" in global finance, arguing that "there is actually no unique risk to global financial stability that can be characterized as physical 'climate risk' apart from baseline risks of extreme weather events." He contends that the financial industry has created a "cottage industry of climate risk modeling" with questionable quality, often relying on the same groups that generate the most extreme climate scenarios.

This leads to his top-ranked scandal: the continued dominance of implausible emissions scenarios. He notes that despite a shift in academic consensus, "extreme scenarios continue to dominate climate research, media reporting, and policy discussions." He highlights that in 2025 alone, over 11,000 studies employed scenarios like RCP8.5, which he argues have "little ability to inform us reliably about future climate or its impacts." The danger, he posits, is that these scenarios have become a self-fulfilling prophecy that warps policy. "Climate change is indeed real and a threat, but it is not the end of the world," he asserts, calling for a decentralization of scenario creation to better serve policymakers rather than just researchers.

"The collective view of climate science and policy has been warped by the continuing misuse of scenarios."

Bottom Line

Roger Pielke Jr.'s most powerful contribution is his refusal to let the scientific community off the hook for its own institutional failures, arguing that credibility is lost not just by opponents, but by bad data and politicized structures. The argument's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the scientific community's willingness to self-correct in a polarized environment where admitting error is often viewed as weakness. The reader should watch closely to see if the proposed institutional reforms for the National Climate Assessment gain traction in Congress, as that will be the true test of whether the field can truly depoliticize itself.

Top five climate science scandals 2025

by Roger Pielke Jr. · The Honest Broker · Read full article

Two things are true at once — First, humans influence the climate system, presenting risks that merit policy attention. Second, climate research, broadly construed, is a deeply politicized endeavor, leaving much room for improvement.

One feature of the deep politicization of climate is that some people wish to only believe one of these two truths. Developing effective policies related to energy, development, extreme events and disasters depends upon grappling effectively with these two truths.

In 2024, I created a table of the top five climate science scandals in an effort to shine a bright light on areas where the climate community could readily address some of the most egregious failures within the field.

The scientific community can only control what it can control — Big P politics is not among those things, and there is no guarantee that efforts to uphold scientific integrity will make much difference in the political world. But it does seem clear that securing the confidence and trust of our fellow citizens and those empowered to lead will be much more likely if we act to uphold scientific integrity, rather than letting bad science stand.

In today’s post, I both identify fives scandal that I judge to be the most significant as 2025 comes to a close and recommend positive steps that the community might take to correct course.

With the throat clearing out of the way, let’s get to it...
