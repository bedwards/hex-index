Weekend Update #176: Ukraine Is Showing Its Value, Particularly For Europe

Phillips P. O'Brien · Phillips P. O'Brien ·Mar 15, 2026 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Phillips P. O'Brien delivers a jarring reality check: while the world fixates on US-Iran tensions, Ukraine has quietly transformed from a desperate beggar into a critical defense technology hub that Europe cannot afford to ignore. The author argues that the narrative of Ukrainian collapse is not just outdated, but actively dangerous, as the very capabilities born of that war are now the only things keeping US forces in the Middle East safe from drone swarms.

The Great Narrative Flip

O'Brien begins by dismantling the pessimistic consensus that dominated late 2025, a time when experts predicted Ukraine's imminent defeat due to manpower shortages and a hostile US administration. He contrasts this with the current reality where the US, under Donald Trump, finds itself reliant on Ukrainian innovation. "For Donald Trump, saying 'thank you' is the hardest word. Saying 'thank you' to Ukraine, therefore, is cosmically impossible for him," O'Brien writes, highlighting the cognitive dissonance of a president who denies receiving help from a nation he claims is doomed.

The author points to specific incidents where Trump lied about Ukrainian contributions to Gulf defense, claiming the US has "the best drones in the world" while ignoring the fact that American forces were burning through expensive interceptors at an unsustainable rate. O'Brien notes that estimates suggest over 800 Patriot missiles were fired to counter Iranian drones in a matter of days, a level of expenditure that would bankrupt any other defense system. "Ukraine is the only nation in the world who could have provided aid in this way so quickly, and aid that actually seems to work," he asserts. This evidence is compelling because it shifts the metric of value from moral obligation to hard-nosed strategic necessity.

Critics might argue that O'Brien overstates the scale of this aid or that relying on a war-torn nation for critical defense is a fragile strategy. However, the sheer speed with which Ukraine mobilized these systems suggests a maturity in their defense industrial base that rivals established powers.

The Startup Nation of War

The most distinctive claim in the piece is the reframing of Ukraine not as a victim, but as a "start-up hub" that Europe desperately needs to emulate. O'Brien observes that while European defense industries have struggled with stagnation, Ukraine has fostered a new generation of military innovators. "Ukraine has seen a whole new generation of start-ups blossom in military/high tech areas, start-ups which should also have significant civilian cross-over implications in the future," he writes. He cites companies like Magura, which values sea drones at nearly $1 billion, as proof that Ukraine has solved the entrepreneurial problems that plague the rest of the continent.

This argument lands because it connects the immediate tactical success of anti-drone systems to a broader economic vision. O'Brien explains that Ukraine is now capable of producing up to $50 billion in weapons annually, a capacity that far exceeds its own budget but offers a golden opportunity for European investment. "Europeans have the money to help grow this defense/technology hub and not outsource to American or other producers," he argues. This is a pivot from the old model of aid to a partnership model where Europe buys into a high-tech ecosystem that can secure its own future.

People need to stop talking about Ukraine just needing help. What Ukraine has done in the Gulf shows that whole intellectual framework being turned on its head.

A Lifeline for Putin and a Thief in the Ranks

The commentary takes a darker turn as O'Brien details how the US administration's actions in the Middle East are inadvertently fueling the Russian war machine. He describes a scenario where the Trump administration, in an effort to lower oil prices, suspended sanctions on Russian oil, effectively handing Putin a massive financial windfall. "Overall it is estimated that Russia is already making $150 million more a day from its oil sales," O'Brien notes, pointing out that this revenue is vital for sustaining the war effort.

Simultaneously, O'Brien exposes the complicity of European allies, specifically Hungary, in undermining Ukraine. He recounts the shocking seizure of $80 million in Ukrainian gold reserves by the Hungarian government, an act he describes as "another example of the kinds of things that must change if European states are going to take more responsibility for themselves." The contrast drawn between Ukraine's rapid, effective aid to the US and Hungary's theft of Ukrainian assets serves as a stark illustration of the diverging paths within the European alliance.

Bottom Line

O'Brien's strongest argument is the undeniable shift in leverage: Ukraine has evolved from a recipient of aid to a critical supplier of defense technology, a fact that even a hostile US administration cannot erase. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that European governments will recognize this value and invest accordingly, rather than continuing to be paralyzed by internal political fractures like the Hungarian incident. The reader must now watch to see if Europe can seize this technological asset before the political window closes.

Hi All,

I realize that I and others have been writing much more about the US bombing of Iran in the last two weeks. While that is not surprising (nor a bad thing as what is happening there is so important), it is also vital that the Russo-Ukraine War does not lose attention. Indeed, as we are still in the early phases of 2026, it is worth thinking about where things stood a year ago to understand the strategic value of Ukraine for Europe now and going forward. In a nutshell, a year ago many people were saying Ukraine was failing, that Europe had to take on Ukraine as a burden, etc. Ukraine was portrayed as facing a manpower crisis that would soon see unable to hold back the Russian steamroller and with the USA now under the leadership of Donald Trump, it was proclaimed that Ukraine faced a very bleak future.

The reality ended up being rather different. This year the focus is not so much on Ukrainian weakness but on Ukrainian attempts to fight back and whether they have a chance at success. That is remarkable with the US changing sides and the Russians hammering Ukrainian infrastructure all winter. This change in narrative is because certain key Ukrainian strengths and capabilities are now far more apparent to the media and analytical community than they were a year ago. Much of this strength is in war production and technology, strengths that have become glaringly apparent over the last two and a half weeks. They are so vital that Europe’s defense future now requires Ukraine. Ukraine is now achieving what Europe has struggled with for decades—creating new and innovative start-ups.

Two other stories stood out this week. The first builds on what started last week. The Trump administration has expanded how it was using the bombing of Iran to aid the Russian war effort against Ukraine. Its extraordinary and likely to continue for a while yet. Finally, the Hungarian government stole Ukrainian funds and kidnapped Ukrainian government employees. Just another week for Putin’s allies in Europe.
