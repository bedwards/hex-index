The ritualism of jury trials

Muzainy Shahiefisally · ·Jan 10, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In a moment when the UK's Labour government faces a political firestorm over plans to abolish jury trials for minor offenses, Muzainy Shahiefisally delivers a startling counter-narrative: the institution of the jury is not a democratic safeguard, but a coercive anomaly that violates the very liberties it claims to protect. While critics scream about tradition and ancient rights, Shahiefisally argues that the jury system is fundamentally incoherent, unrepresentative, and hostile to the rule of law. This is not a defense of the government's specific policy, but a dismantling of the sacred cow itself, forcing readers to question whether the 'right' to a jury trial is actually a right at all.

The Myth of Democratic Representation

Shahiefisally begins by dismantling the most common defense of juries: that they are a sacrament of democracy. The author writes, "Where a tradition persists only because it is a tradition, and not because there are good reasons buttressing it, then surely we shouldn't allow the dead hand of the past to continue to bind us in the present." This is a sharp, necessary rebuke to the reflexive appeal to history that dominates the current debate. The author points out that random selection does not equal representation. If a region is 50% Black, a random twelve-person jury is statistically unlikely to reflect that demographic reality. Furthermore, the mandatory nature of jury duty, enforced by fines, directly contradicts the democratic principle of free choice. As Shahiefisally notes, "It is constitutive of the freedom of choice that one also possess the freedom to abstain from choosing. Were jury trials truly reflective of democratic choice, then a potential juror would be at liberty to decline to submit to jury duty for any reason whatsoever."

This argument lands hard because it exposes a hypocrisy in the democratic justification. Critics might argue that voluntary juries would solve the coercion issue, but Shahiefisally counters that this would shrink the pool to only the wealthy or the civically obsessed, destroying any claim to representativeness. The author effectively strips the jury of its democratic halo, revealing it as a system where the state compels citizens to serve against their will under the guise of civic duty.

The Safety Valve Illusion

The piece then turns to the idea that juries act as a necessary check on state power, a view championed by groups like the Free Speech Union. Shahiefisally writes, "A jury which deliberates on the basis of the law's content is, quite literally, undermining the rule of law." This is the article's most provocative claim. The author argues that when juries acquit based on the perceived injustice of a law rather than the facts of the case—a practice known as jury nullification—they are not protecting liberty but usurping the legislative function. The reference to Henry Fonda in 12 Angry Men is acknowledged as a "starry-eyed depiction" that collapses under scrutiny. Instead of a romantic check on tyranny, the jury becomes an unaccountable body of twelve random individuals with the power to veto laws without explanation.

The jury's job is not to temper state overreach. To be sure, a jury which delivers an acquittal or guilty verdict as a means to condemn the law's injustice has exceeded its institutional mandate.

This framing is crucial for understanding the stakes of the current UK debate. If the goal is a consistent application of law, the jury system introduces a variable of unpredictability that can be just as dangerous as state overreach. However, one must consider that in an era of draconian legislation, the ability of a jury to refuse to convict under unjust laws is often the only remaining shield for the accused, a nuance the author minimizes in favor of procedural purity.

The Right to Reason and the Transparency Deficit

Perhaps the strongest philosophical blow comes from the argument that the "right" to a jury trial is not a natural right at all. Shahiefisally argues that rights usually require others to refrain from action, but a jury trial requires active, positive investment of time and labor from the community. "The 'right' to jury trials confers upon accused persons the power to wield the state's coercive machinery over those eligible to serve as jurors," the author writes. This reframes the accused's privilege as a burden imposed on the citizenry.

More damning is the lack of transparency. Unlike judges, who must explain their reasoning, juries deliver verdicts without justification. Shahiefisally cites Lord Bingham to note that "an offender is thus deprived of his liberties without knowing whether the reasons for their conviction were arbitrary or principled." This opacity is a fundamental violation of the rule of law, which demands that decisions be public and reasoned. The author contrasts this with the judicial system, where the obligation to give reasons "serves to constrain biases" and allows the legal community to hold judges accountable. While the author acknowledges that requiring juries to give reasons would be logistically difficult and potentially lead to internal domination, the current system's silence remains a glaring flaw. A counterargument worth considering is that forcing jurors to articulate reasons might chill honest deliberation, but the author's point stands: a system that convicts without explanation is inherently suspect.

Bottom Line

Muzainy Shahiefisally's analysis is a rigorous, if uncomfortable, deconstruction of a cherished institution, proving that the jury system is neither democratic nor a guaranteed protector of liberty. The argument's greatest strength is its exposure of the coercion and opacity inherent in mandatory, unreasoned verdicts. Its biggest vulnerability is the potential underestimation of the jury's role as a moral conscience in the face of unjust laws, a function that procedural consistency cannot always replicate. For busy observers of the UK legal landscape, the takeaway is clear: the debate should not be about saving a tradition, but about designing a system that truly serves justice without sacrificing the rights of the accused or the freedom of the citizen.

The ritualism of jury trials

by Muzainy Shahiefisally · · Read full article

I spent the holiday season watching with quiet bemusement as the UK’s Deputy Prime Minister, David Lammy, threw a wrench into Britons’ festive plans by announcing that the increasingly embattled Labour government will abolish jury trials in cases that carry a maximum term of imprisonment of three years. In place of juries, these cases will be heard solely by judges.

The announcement led to an immediate political firestorm with Lammy and his party drawing criticism from virtually every corner of British politics. Robert Jenrick of the Tories appealed to tradition and accused Lammy of “rip[ping] up centuries of jury trials.” Lammy faced internal criticism from within Labour as Bradford MP, Imran Hussain, excoriated him for seeking to eradicate “public duty” and eroding “a cornerstone of [British] democracy.” The Liberal Democrats pitched in by valorising jury trials as a “fundamental right” that was being stripped from accused persons.

We should pause momentarily to note that none of Lammy’s critics have offered principled justifications for retaining jury trials. What they have supplied instead are politically resonant formulas designed for rapid media circulation rather than considered reflection.

Perhaps the most detailed criticism of Lammy’s proposal came from the Free Speech Union (“FSU”), which framed it primarily in statistical terms, attacking it as “a direct assault on [Britons’] ancient liberties—and a profound threat to free speech.” The FSU invoked statistics which purported to demonstrate that, in June 2025, magistrates acquitted just 14 per cent of accused persons relying on a free defence, while juries in the Crown Courts did so 33 per cent of the time.

Although such figures are rhetorically powerful, they assume—without substantive argument—that higher acquittal rates are, in themselves, a proxy for justice without controlling for other important variables such as the doctrinal adequacy of invoking the defence, the facts of the individual case, or the quality of evidence adduced. Indeed, as I will subsequently argue, the invocation of acquittal rates conflates the purpose of a trial, that is, the impartial application of facts to law, with the preferred outcomes of various political factions.

Although spirited and undoubtedly earnest, much of the criticism of judge-led trials rests on the uncritical assumption that jury trials are either preconditions of a healthy democratic society, fundamental rights of the accused, or normatively superior to judge-led trials.
