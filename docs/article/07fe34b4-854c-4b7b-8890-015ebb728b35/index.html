Grid Scale Energy Storage 30x cheaper than Lithium-ion! How do they do that?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·May 15, 2022 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace presents a claim that sounds like science fiction until the engineering details reveal a startling economic reality: a grid-scale energy storage system that could cost thirty times less than lithium-ion batteries. By focusing on a breakthrough from MIT involving molten metal and thermal photovoltaics, Borlace moves beyond the usual hype cycle to offer a tangible pathway for decarbonizing the grid without the prohibitive price tags of current solutions. This is not just another incremental improvement; it is a fundamental reimagining of how we store renewable energy.

The Physics of Heat and Light

Borlace introduces the core concept with a striking image: plunging photovoltaic cells into a super-hot material at 2400 degrees Celsius. "Instead of simply being instantly vaporized as you might logically think, the cells would actually start producing electricity with an efficiency of at least 40 percent completely bonkers right?" he asks. The author's strength lies in his ability to acknowledge the apparent absurdity of the premise before dismantling it with rigorous technical explanation. He clarifies that this is not about standard solar panels but about capturing infrared light from a heat source to generate power.

The narrative pivots to the specific engineering hurdles that previously made this impossible. Borlace notes that the challenge was not just heating a material, but "how to avoid the whole tpv cell vaporization thing." He highlights the work of MIT professor Asegun Henry, who solved the paradox of keeping cells cool while they sit in a white-hot environment. "The water is continually drawing heat away from the cells and as long as it flows quickly enough it'll always stay liquid and not change phase into steam," Borlace explains. This fluid cooling mechanism is the linchpin that allows the system to function, turning a theoretical impossibility into a practical engineering solution.

Critics might note that maintaining such extreme temperatures over decades in a real-world grid environment presents material science challenges that lab prototypes have yet to fully prove. However, Borlace effectively grounds the argument by detailing the specific carbon-based sealing solutions that allow molten tin to be pumped at temperatures exceeding 2000 degrees Celsius.

The Economics of Scale

The most compelling part of Borlace's coverage is the shift from physics to economics. He describes a system where heat is stored in massive graphite blocks, leveraging the laws of thermodynamics where larger objects lose heat more slowly. "The graphite blocks in this system cover an area the size of half a football field which means they will literally take months to cool down," he writes. This observation reframes energy storage not as a chemical reaction in a battery, but as a simple, scalable thermal mass problem.

The rate at which heat is able to leak out of the blocks is proportional to their surface area whereas the amount of energy stored in the blocks is proportional to their volume.

Borlace uses this physical principle to justify the system's viability for long-duration storage, a critical gap in current renewable infrastructure. He contrasts this with the limitations of chemical batteries, noting that "the big advantage of tpv cells is that they can facilitate very large utility scale energy storage at very low cost compared to existing technologies." The argument is bolstered by the modularity of the design, where charge times and storage capacity can be adjusted independently by changing the size of the heater or the number of graphite blocks.

The Bottom Line

The economic projection Borlace cites is the piece's most disruptive element. He states that once all variables are factored in, the team reached an "overall cost of less than ten dollars per kilowatt hour" compared to the "fully installed cost of a typical utility scale lithium ion battery configuration which can be more than 300 per kilowatt hour." This staggering difference suggests that the barrier to 100% renewable grids may not be technology, but the cost of storage. While the timeline for commercial deployment—aiming for a gigawatt-hour system by 2028—remains ambitious, Borlace's coverage provides a clear, evidence-based roadmap for why this technology deserves serious attention from grid operators and policymakers alike.
