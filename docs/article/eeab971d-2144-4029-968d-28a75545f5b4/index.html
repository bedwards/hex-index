The College Enrollment Crisis

PolyMatter · PolyMatter ·Jan 11, 2025 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

PolyMatter delivers a chilling revelation that reframes the college crisis not as a failure of value, but as a collision of demographics and broken economics. While most analysts focus on student sentiment, the author exposes a silent, ticking clock: the demographic cliff created by the Great Recession is about to crush institutions that have become dangerously dependent on tuition. This is not a story about young people rejecting higher education; it is a story about a system running out of students just as it runs out of public funding.

The Demographic Time Bomb

The piece's most striking insight is that the crisis is structural, not cultural. PolyMatter writes, "you can convince skeptical 18-year-olds about the value of a college degree but you can't convince an 18-year-old that doesn't exist." This simple observation cuts through the noise of opinion polls. The author correctly identifies that the drop in enrollment is a lagging indicator of the birth rate collapse that began in 2008. With the US birth rate now half a child smaller than it was in 2007, the pool of potential freshmen is shrinking by the millions. As PolyMatter notes, "College hasn't gone out of fashion but having children has and that spells trouble for the institutions that depend on them." This argument is compelling because it shifts the blame from the institutions' marketing failures to an immutable demographic reality.

Critics might argue that international students or older adult learners could fill the gap, but the author effectively counters this by highlighting the specific fragility of small, regional schools that rely on local cohorts. The math is unforgiving: if a school enrolls fewer than 250 new students a year, losing just a few dozen can be fatal.

The Broken Funding Model

The commentary then pivots to the financial mechanics that made this demographic shift so dangerous. PolyMatter explains that the Great Recession triggered a permanent shift in how colleges are funded, moving the burden from the public to the individual. "The burden of paying for college has moved in Only One Direction each session permanently shifts more of the cost from the public at large to individual families." The author illustrates this with stark data, noting that state funding in Arizona was cut in half, and in many states, appropriations never recovered even a decade later.

This financial squeeze forced universities into a corner. Because universities are "notoriously inflexible" with tenured staff and massive fixed assets, they could not easily cut costs. Instead, they pulled the only lever available: tuition. PolyMatter writes, "it's much easier to update a number on the website then cancel a $20 million renovation or sell the 100-year-old Library." This observation is a devastating critique of institutional inertia. The result is that public universities have effectively become "hyper capitalist quasi corporate Enterprises," relying on out-of-state and international students to subsidize local education.

Schools didn't just become extremely dependent on tuition Revenue they did so at the exact moment birth rates fell to 2008 laying the foundations for a demographic Time Bomb 18 years later.

The Great Divergence

Perhaps the most urgent part of the analysis is the distinction between the "sellers" and the "buyers." PolyMatter defines the elite institutions as "sellers," noting that "these prestigious names draw students from around the country they receive far more qualified applications than they have seats in each class." These schools, with massive endowments, are insulated from the crisis. The vast majority, however, are "buyers" that "recruit almost exclusively from their surrounding region."

The author warns that for these 67% of schools, the margin for error has vanished. "A few dozen students here or there can make or break an entire University." This framing is effective because it personalizes the abstract concept of a budget shortfall. It explains why Birmingham Southern College, despite its history and competitive baseball team, simply closed its doors. The author estimates that 560 public and nonprofit four-year universities are at serious risk of potential closure. This is not a prediction of a slow decline but of a sudden, painful reckoning.

Critics might note that this analysis underestimates the potential for state governments to reverse course and reinvest in higher education. However, the historical trend cited by PolyMatter suggests that political will is unlikely to return to pre-recession levels, making the author's pessimistic outlook the more prudent assumption for students and families.

Bottom Line

PolyMatter's strongest contribution is the synthesis of demographic data with institutional finance, proving that the college crisis is a math problem waiting to happen rather than a cultural shift. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that schools will continue to raise tuition rather than downsize, but the historical evidence of institutional rigidity makes this a safe bet. The most critical takeaway for any prospective student is to look beyond the campus brochure and ask a single, uncomfortable question: will this school still be around by the time I graduate?
