Blue Hydrogen. The greatest fossil fuel scam in history?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Sep 5, 2021 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace delivers a scathing indictment of a policy that governments are rushing to embrace as a climate solution, arguing that "blue hydrogen" is not a bridge to net zero but a "greatest fossil fuel scam in history." By dissecting a new peer-reviewed study, Borlace exposes how the industry's favorite "clean" fuel actually generates more greenhouse gas than simply burning coal or oil, a revelation that threatens to derail the very climate targets it claims to support.

The Color of Deception

Borlace begins by dismantling the comforting narrative that hydrogen is a monolithic solution. He notes that while "green hydrogen" is produced cleanly via electrolysis, the industry is pushing "blue hydrogen," which is essentially "exactly the same as grey hydrogen except the production facility operators promise to capture all the carbon dioxide and do something jolly grown up and responsible with it." This framing is effective because it strips away the marketing veneer to reveal the underlying mechanics: the continued burning of natural gas.

The author's central critique hinges on a specific, often ignored variable: fugitive methane. Borlace explains that while carbon dioxide lingers for centuries, methane is short-lived but "mass for mass it's a hundred times more potent as a greenhouse gas than carbon dioxide." He argues that the industry conveniently uses a 100-year timeframe to downplay this threat, whereas a 20-year window reveals the true urgency. This distinction is crucial; as Borlace writes, "our climate emergency is playing out right now in front of our very eyes and not in 80 or 100 years from now."

Critics of the study might argue that the 3.5 percent methane leakage rate assumed in the paper is high, or that carbon capture technology will improve. However, Borlace counters this by noting that even at the lowest estimated leakage rates, the emissions footprint remains problematic. The argument holds weight because it relies on the physics of the atmosphere rather than the optimism of future engineering breakthroughs.

The Math Doesn't Add Up

When Borlace turns to the data, the case against blue hydrogen becomes stark. The study he cites found that "greenhouse gas emissions from blue hydrogen are actually quite high particularly due to those pesky fugitive methane emissions from the gas extraction process." The logic is sound: to capture the carbon, plants must burn more natural gas, which in turn releases more methane. Consequently, the total emissions are only "9 to 12 percent lower than for grey" hydrogen.

Perhaps the most shocking finding Borlace highlights is the comparison to direct fossil fuel use. He writes, "the greenhouse gas footprint of blue hydrogen is more than 20 greater than simply burning natural gas or coal for heat." This is a devastating conclusion for a fuel being marketed as a replacement for gas boilers. It suggests that the entire supply chain—extraction, processing, transport, and conversion—is so inefficient and leaky that it is worse than the status quo.

The author also points out the flaw in the assumption that captured carbon will stay underground. He notes that "most carbon dioxide that's currently captured is used for enhanced oil recovery which we've talked about a couple of times... that process liberates co2 that had been safely locked up underground for millions of years and spews it straight back out into the atmosphere." This exposes a cynical economic loop where climate policy is subsidizing the extraction of more oil.

A Political Distraction

Borlace shifts his focus to the political machinery driving this narrative, specifically the UK government's strategy. He observes that while the government talks of a "twin track approach," the reality on the ground favors fossil fuels. He points out that the proposed blue hydrogen facilities are massive, with "9 blue hydrogen facilities are all more than 100 megawatts each," whereas green hydrogen projects are tiny and fragmented.

The author suggests this is not an accident but a deliberate strategy. "A cynical person might suggest that blue hydrogen looks set to become yet another piece of governmental obfuscation by clever accountants with sharp pencils and a wealth of experience in making excel spreadsheets look acceptable rather than a genuine step towards real reductions." This critique is bolstered by the resignation of Chris Jackson, who told the author that blue hydrogen is "at best an expensive distraction and at worst a lock-in for continued fossil fuel use."

Borlace also questions the logic of using hydrogen for home heating at all. He argues that even if we used green hydrogen, the energy loss in creating it makes it inferior to direct electrification. "If you're installing all that renewable power to run electrolysis why not just use it to run electric ovens hobs and heat pumps for our domestic homes and cut out a very energy-hungry middleman?" This practical perspective cuts through the hype, suggesting that the push for hydrogen in homes is driven by the desire to keep gas infrastructure alive rather than by physics.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's commentary is a powerful corrective to the uncritical enthusiasm surrounding blue hydrogen, grounding the debate in hard data about methane leakage and energy efficiency. The strongest part of his argument is the demonstration that blue hydrogen is not just a marginal improvement, but potentially a net negative compared to burning fossil fuels directly. However, the piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its absolute dismissal of any transitional role for blue hydrogen, which some policymakers might argue is necessary while green hydrogen scales up. The reader should watch for how governments respond to these findings, as the economic incentives for blue hydrogen remain deeply entrenched despite the environmental costs.
