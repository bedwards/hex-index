188. Five questions about "extrajudicial killings"

Steve Vladeck · One First ·Nov 6, 2025 ·15 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Steve Vladeck delivers a chilling legal autopsy of a military campaign that has slipped past the public's radar, arguing that the administration's strikes on drug boats in the Caribbean and Pacific are not just controversial, but blatantly unlawful under U.S. domestic law. While the headlines focus on the number of vessels sunk, Vladeck forces the reader to confront the human cost of 67 dead individuals and the terrifying legal vacuum that allowed it to happen. This is not a debate about policy efficacy; it is a warning that the executive branch has severed the last threads of congressional authorization for war.

The Illusion of Authority

The core of Vladeck's argument rests on a stark absence: there is no statute that authorizes these strikes. He dismantles the administration's likely defenses one by one, noting that Congress has not declared war since 1942 and that the 2001 Authorization for the Use of Military Force (AUMF) applies only to the groups responsible for the September 11 attacks, not drug cartels. "There is no claim, nor could there be one, that the drug cartels to which these boats are allegedly connected fall within the aegis of that statute," Vladeck writes. This is a crucial distinction that separates these operations from the post-9/11 drone campaigns, grounding the analysis in hard statutory text rather than vague executive ambition.

Vladeck further argues that even the War Powers Resolution, often cited as a loophole for limited military action, has run its course. He points out that the 60-day clock triggered by the administration's own notification to Congress expired just days before the most recent strikes. "That argument necessarily collapsed earlier this week—when the 60-day clock triggered by President Trump's September 4 letter to Congress ran its course," he notes. The administration's refusal to share its internal legal rationale with the full Congress or the public only deepens the opacity. "Whatever the internal legal rationale for these strikes is... there's no publicly obvious reason why it would be limited to non-citizens and/or targets outside U.S. territory," Vladeck observes. This lack of transparency suggests a precedent that could expand far beyond the current theater of operations.

Critics might argue that the executive branch has inherent authority to protect national interests from evolving threats like transnational cartels. However, Vladeck counters that stretching the definition of "self-defense" to include intercepting drug boats on the high seas is a dangerous overreach. "Even if the boats are actively transporting drugs... there is no universe in which Article II of the Constitution could fairly be read to authorize unilateral military action by the President," he asserts. The comparison to World War II is telling; Vladeck reminds us that President Roosevelt could not have legally attacked weapons shipments between Germany and its allies in 1940 simply because they might be used against the U.S. one day.

"They're even worse than that, for they are, near as I can tell, blatantly unlawful as a matter of U.S. domestic law—and a quickly spreading stain on whatever is left of the executive branch's commitment to the rule of law."

The Slippery Slope of "Three Hops"

The most disturbing aspect of the coverage is the comparison Vladeck draws between these strikes and the targeted killings of the Obama and Bush eras, specifically the case of Anwar al-Awlaki. While the killing of al-Awlaki was deeply controversial, it operated under the 2001 AUMF and involved rigorous criteria for targeting. In contrast, the current strikes rely on a tenuous connection to designated terrorist organizations. Vladeck highlights a staggering revelation from Pentagon officials: they needed to prove only that the targeted people were connected to designated terrorist organizations, even if the connection is "as much as three hops away from a known member."

This "three hops" standard represents a fundamental shift from targeting specific combatants to targeting a broad, undefined network of individuals. "The difference between targeting a specific individual against whom force could lawfully be used and targeting unknown individuals who might be as much as three degrees of separation removed from a 'known member' of a designated FTO is … large," Vladeck explains. The human cost of this legal ambiguity is immediate and severe. The strikes have killed 67 people, yet the administration has offered no public evidence that these individuals posed an imminent threat to the United States.

Furthermore, the operational context differs significantly from the drone strikes in Yemen or Pakistan. Those operations often occurred in areas where no effective government control existed, making arrest and detention impossible. Here, in the Caribbean and Pacific, law enforcement alternatives were viable. "We know that at least one of those strikes resulted in the capture of two of the boat's occupants," Vladeck notes, pointing out that the administration chose to repatriate them rather than prosecute them in civilian courts. "That's not what you do when you believe you have a strong case on your legal authority," he concludes. The decision to kill rather than capture suggests a preference for lethal force over due process, raising profound questions about the administration's commitment to the rule of law.

The Remedy Gap

Vladeck concludes by addressing the grim reality that there is likely no judicial remedy for those harmed by these strikes. The legal system has historically relied on procedural doctrines to avoid reaching the merits of such cases, leaving victims without recourse. "Long before January 20 (or September 2), there has been a serious gap in the ability of those against whom the United States directs military force to obtain any judicial redress in U.S. courts," he writes. This legal void creates a dangerous environment where the executive branch can act with impunity, unchecked by the judiciary or the legislature.

The argument that these strikes are a necessary response to the "devastating consequences" of drug trafficking is not without merit in terms of policy goals. However, Vladeck's analysis suggests that the means chosen are legally indefensible. "Without an AUMF, without the WPR, without the ability to rely upon the FTO designation regime, and with statutes like the MDLEA focused exclusively on law enforcement, there's just no viable argument that Congress has provided the kind of authorization for these uses of force that the Constitution generally requires," he states. The stakes are high: if this precedent stands, the definition of war in the 21st century could be rewritten to allow the President to launch military attacks against anyone remotely connected to a designated group, anywhere in the world.

Bottom Line

Vladeck's strongest contribution is his rigorous dissection of the statutory void, proving that the administration's actions lack any credible legal foundation under current U.S. law. His argument's greatest vulnerability lies in the political reality that without a congressional declaration of war or a new AUMF, the courts may remain unwilling to intervene, leaving the executive branch's overreach unchallenged. Readers must watch for whether Congress will attempt to reclaim its war powers or if this "three hops" standard becomes the new normal for American military engagement.
