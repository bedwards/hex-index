In late 2008, the CEOs of General Motors, Ford, and Chrysler pulled up to the Russell Senate office building in DC following a 500-mile road trip from Detroit. Each drove one of their company's newest, most fuelefficient hybrid vehicles, a Chevrolet Malibu, Ford Escape, and Chrysler Aspen in an effort to repair their tarnished public image. The previous month, they'd traveled to DC by private jet. And well, the media didn't love the optics of CEOs burning tens of thousands of dollars of jet fuel in order to beg for a bailout.

This time though, the strategy was to be more consiliatory. Quote, "We're here today because we made mistakes." General Motors' Rick Wagner told the Senate Banking Committee, "We're sorry to be asking for this support." This trio of American auto manufacturers had long been referred to as the big three based on their historical dominance of not only the US but the world auto industry. And yet here in the United States, the country where Henry Ford invented the Model T and car-shaped postwar culture, the three companies that had built that legacy were failing. Though the decline occurred quickly, the circumstances leading up to it did not.

In the mid-century, the auto industry was relatively unregulated, and vehicles were big, loud, and dirty. But legislation in the early60s started to target air pollution and automobile safety through efforts like emission standards, which required automakers to cut hydrocarbon and oxides of nitrogen emissions by more than 50% by 1975, and essentially made the popular muscle cars of the ' 50s and60s extinct. This a of regulation also included the implementation of seat belts and bumpers, which while inarguably worthwhile, shook up the pecking order of the industry. Everyone had to change, which made it easier for newer competitors to step in.

Japan was the biggest threat. In 1958, Toyota introduced the first Japanese car into the US market, the Toyota Crown, and over the next decade, others would join like Honda, Nissan, Mishubichi, and Mazda. The timing couldn't have been better as Americans faced not one but two oil crises during the 1970s. The more fuel efficient Japanese vehicles were increasingly attractive and the country's exports to the United States more than tripled in 5 years to upward of 650,000 cars delivered to America by 1980.
