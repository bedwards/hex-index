Our year in review

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Jan 15, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider's year-in-review does something rare for a media retrospective: it uses hard data to dismantle the assumption that traditional institutions hold a monopoly on influence. While the establishment waits for funding cycles to dictate relevance, Schneider argues that agility and unrestricted capital have allowed a small independent operation to outpace the largest think tanks in the foreign policy ecosystem.

The Rise of the Independent Operator

Schneider frames 2025 as a definitive shift in how expertise is consumed, noting that "Brands matter in DC way less than they used to." He points to a staggering metric: ChinaTalk's subscriber base grew 60% to reach 65,000, surpassing the second-largest specialized Substack by nearly double. This growth isn't just vanity; it signals a market correction where audiences are bypassing the slow, report-heavy output of legacy organizations for immediate, high-fidelity analysis.

The author contrasts this with the structural inertia of major institutions. "In traditional think tanks, podcasts, newsletters and responses to news developments are afterthoughts to the long reports and small in-person events funders expect as outputs," Schneider writes. This observation cuts to the heart of the problem: the incentive structures of established bodies often punish the very speed required to analyze fast-moving fields like artificial intelligence and semiconductor policy. By relying on unrestricted funding, Schneider's team avoids the trap of writing "long reports that won't matter by the time they're finished."

"Writing a smart newsletter in some ways even gains you credibility vs working at a brand name think tank, university, or news organization."

Critics might argue that independent operations lack the institutional memory and peer-review rigor of a university or a century-old think tank. However, Schneider counters this by highlighting the quality of the output, noting that their worst-performing posts exceed the view counts of top-tier legacy journalism. The data suggests that for a specific, high-stakes niche, the audience values the immediacy of independent analysis over the perceived safety of a brand name.

The Human Cost of Geopolitics

The commentary takes a necessary turn toward the human and strategic realities of the US-China relationship, moving beyond abstract policy to the visceral nature of modern conflict. Schneider highlights an interview with Dan Wang, who describes China's "engineering state" as a system treating society "as liquid flows" where "all human activity can be directed with the same ease as turning valves." This metaphor is chilling not just for its description of efficiency, but for what it implies about the suppression of individual agency.

Schneider notes that this mindset enabled decades of rapid growth but also created "novel forms of political repression humanity has never seen." The piece does not shy away from the darker implications of this governance model, linking it to the PLA purges and the broader tightening of elite control. The coverage of the PLA purges is described as the "most interesting domestic elite political development in years," suggesting that the internal stability of the Chinese state is as volatile as its external posture.

This framing is crucial because it moves the conversation away from simple trade deficits to the fundamental nature of the opposing system. The discussion of the "Second ChinaTalk" deep dive on the People's Liberation Army adds necessary historical weight, reminding readers that the current military buildup is not an anomaly but a continuation of a long-term strategy to project power.

"If America doesn't use its allies, it will lose the 21st century."

Schneider amplifies this point through an interview with Rush Doshi, who critiques both the persuasion-heavy approach of the previous administration and the coercion-heavy tactics of the current one. The argument here is that the US must pivot to "capacity-centric statecraft," where allies help each other build economic and military strength. This is a pragmatic, if sobering, assessment of the geopolitical landscape.

The Machinery of War and Diplomacy

The year's coverage also grappled with the terrifying intersection of technology and warfare. Schneider reflects on the "AI and the Future of War" series, which explores how artificial intelligence is reshaping the battlefield. The tone here is grave, acknowledging that "war is changing" in ways that are difficult to predict.

A significant portion of the commentary focuses on the "Liberation Day" tariffs and the chaotic dynamics of the current administration's trade policy. Schneider describes the administration's approach as "disjointed," noting how internal factional conflict is used as a management tool. This unpredictability is presented not as a feature, but as a destabilizing force in global markets.

The piece also touches on the historical parallels of authoritarian overreach, citing Sergey Radchenko's work on the Soviet Union. Radchenko argues that authoritarian regimes pursue global influence to "compensate for internal weakness," a dynamic that resonates deeply with current observations of Beijing. The reference to the 1950 propaganda poster calling to "Annihilate the American aggressors" serves as a stark reminder that the rhetoric of total war has not disappeared, even in the nuclear age.

"What if boredom, not grand strategy, is what starts wars?"

This question, drawn from the discussion on Soviet history, forces the reader to consider the role of human error and ego in great power conflict. It is a sobering counterpoint to the high-tech focus of the AI coverage, grounding the discussion in the timeless, often tragic, realities of human leadership.

The Bottom Line

Schneider's strongest argument is that the monopoly on foreign policy analysis has been broken, and the most vital insights are now coming from agile, independent operators who are not beholden to the slow-moving machinery of traditional think tanks. However, the piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on a single voice to curate this vast landscape; while the team is growing, the depth of coverage inevitably depends on the bandwidth of a small group. As the US-China rivalry intensifies, the world needs more voices like this one, but it also needs to ensure that the "engineering state" model does not become the default for how we understand the future of global conflict.

Our year in review

by Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk · Read full article

In 2025, ChinaTalk’s eighth year of existence and my third doing it full time, we did the thing. We put out on the newsletter over 150 editions that centered on China AI lab, policy, and application coverage.

On the podcast we published a hundred shows about:

Chinese elite politics and US-China policy

US-China chips and AI

Economic statecraft around export controls and tariffs, which made up the majority of our ten emergency pods this year (double 2024’s emergencies!)

A growing focus on defense, with the launch of our weekly Second Breakfast show, a good bit of military history and our AI and the Future of War series

ChinaTalk’s substack grew 60% this year to 65k subscribers. This is a really big number. The second largest think tank substack is SCSP, which has 35k. Recent CFR, the Atlantic Council, and Brookings annual reports say that, after two decades of building lists, they each have around 200k total email subscribers. Not a bad showing for ChinaTalk’s much smaller budget only three years in the game.

The show gets 10-15k listens per show across the podcast and YouTube, and was downloaded a million times last year. These are also really big numbers. Across all of foreign policy think tank-dom, only one show (CFR’s The President’s Inbox) is bigger. And it’s not like Mass Ave isn’t trying. CSIS has 40 shows alone.

Why do so many people engage with our work?

US-China tech is an covering important, underserved niche. A year after DeepSeek, to my endless surprise there are still only a handful of analysts working in English in public on tech and China. While there is more out there on the defense side, most coverage tends toward SpecOps bro, Zeihan geopolitics bro, or lifeless industry coverage.

We make substantive, engaging content that resonates in today’s media landscape. In traditional think tanks, podcasts, newsletters and responses to news developments are afterthoughts to the long reports and small in-person events funders expect as outputs. Since podcasts and research with outputs under 10,000 words often aren’t directly funded and so happen on fellows’ personal time, talent in these areas isn’t hired for or developed.
