Scot McKnight delivers a startlingly honest assessment of American religious life, arguing that the narrative of mass apostasy is largely a myth while exposing a far more dangerous trend: the calcification of belief through sheer inertia. He pairs this sociological data with a searing critique of how theological power, specifically Theonomy, distorts the very nature of divine authority in the modern political sphere.

The Power of Inertia and the Myth of Switching

McKnight begins by dismantling the popular assumption that Americans are rapidly abandoning their faith. He points to data showing that despite decades of cultural upheaval, the majority of people remain in the religious tradition of their youth. "Inertia may be one of the most powerful forces in the social world," McKnight writes, noting that "it's just easier to stay the same than it is to change." This observation reframes the decline of religious institutions not as a failure of recruitment, but as a failure of retention.

The author highlights a specific divergence in these trends. While Evangelical retention has remained remarkably stable, hovering around 75 percent since the 1970s, Mainline Protestant retention has plummeted from 78 percent to 57 percent. McKnight notes, "Decline is about retention, not just recruitment." This distinction is crucial for understanding the current religious landscape; it suggests that the problem isn't that people are leaving the faith en masse, but that specific traditions are failing to keep their own children.

"Inertia still rules American religion. Despite decades of change, most Americans remain in the same broad religious group they were raised in."

Critics might argue that this focus on retention ignores the quality of belief, suggesting that staying in a tradition without genuine conviction is a hollow victory. However, McKnight's data on the rise of the "non-religious" counters this. He observes that "non-religion is no longer just an endpoint—it's increasingly a starting point," with retention rates for those raised without faith doubling. This indicates a fundamental shift where the default setting for a growing segment of the population is now secularism, a change as significant as any religious conversion.

The Cost of Sociological Ignorance

Transitioning from religious data to broader social analysis, McKnight leans heavily on the work of sociologist Megan Theile Strong to diagnose a deeper cultural malaise. He argues that the lack of sociological literacy in the American education system has left the public ill-equipped to understand systemic issues like racism, economic inequality, and extremism. "We are sociologically ignorant, and it shows," McKnight writes, channeling Strong's frustration that only 7 to 10 percent of college students take an introduction to sociology.

The author connects this educational gap directly to the rise of conspiracy theories and reactionary politics. He notes that "nearly one in five Americans believed in QAnon four years ago, an alt-right extremist fantasy that was as popular as some major religions." This comparison is jarring but effective; it places fringe extremism on the same statistical footing as organized religion, suggesting that the vacuum of sociological understanding has been filled by dangerous myths.

"The answers to the problems that plague our society need better-funded, more accessible and more sociological education."

This argument is strengthened by the context of religious switching. Just as people rarely switch religions due to inertia, they rarely switch their worldview without a framework to understand why their current one is flawed. Without the tools to analyze social structures, individuals are left to interpret complex societal shifts through the lens of personal grievance or conspiracy.

The Trap of Complementarian Silence

McKnight then turns his gaze inward to the church, specifically addressing the silence of women in complementarian spaces. He shares a poignant insight from a ministry colleague: "Just remember, many women have been trained not to rock the boat." McKnight expands on this, drawing from his own childhood in a "racist, misogynistic doomsday cult" where obedience was enforced through the fear of divine retribution. He recalls being taught that "one day, someone in our church would oppose his message, and the Spirit would strike."

This personal history gives weight to his critique of patriarchal structures. He argues that these systems do not just oppress women; they also imprison men. "Patriarchy imprisons us, too, with rigid expectations, broken promises, a false sense of security and superiority," McKnight writes. He calls for men to "cowboy up and stop letting women take the attacks alone," urging a shift from passive observation to active solidarity.

"It is time for all of us to be free."

The strength of this section lies in its refusal to treat the issue as a simple gender war. Instead, McKnight frames the restriction of women's voices as a theological error that damages the entire body of believers. By admitting his own privilege as a male who could "become preachers" and "be bold warriors," he disarms potential defensiveness and invites a more honest conversation about power dynamics within the church.

The Theological Error of Theonomy

Perhaps the most aggressive part of McKnight's commentary is his attack on Theonomy, a theological movement seeking to apply Old Testament civil laws to modern governance. He identifies Pete Hegseth, the Secretary of Defense, as the "most prominent and most powerful Theonomist today." McKnight argues that this political theology is fundamentally flawed because it "fails to ground its theology in the cross."

He contends that Theonomy isolates God's justice from His love, creating a vision where "God's Kingdom advances through bombing campaigns." McKnight writes, "Hegseth is convinced that God acts through the might of chariots and horses... and that he, Hegseth, is an instrument of God's wrath." This is a direct challenge to the idea that military might is a sign of divine favor.

"This is the triumphalism of a 'Christianity' that has laid down the cross and taken up the sword, believing it is living resurrection as it delivers death."

This argument echoes the historical tension found in the early church's relationship with the Roman Empire, where the cross was often seen as a symbol of weakness rather than power. Just as early Christians rejected the Theonomy-like impulse to use the sword to establish God's kingdom, McKnight suggests that modern Theonomy repeats the same error by conflating national power with divine blessing. The danger, he warns, is that this mindset "isolates the violence of God from the love of God," leading to a theology that justifies lethal force while ignoring mercy.

Trauma in the Classroom

Finally, McKnight addresses the prevalence of trauma in education, noting that "trauma is not defined simply by what happens to a child. It is defined by how the child's nervous system responds to overwhelming stress." He cites research showing that over 60 percent of adolescents have experienced at least one traumatic event, a number likely higher post-pandemic.

He illustrates this with the story of Pauline Naftel, a principal who was taught to suppress her grief after her brother's death. "God gives and takes away," she was told, leading her to carry on without processing her pain. McKnight contrasts this with a healthier approach where leaders "listened and prayed" and "space was made for tears."

"When stress becomes chronic and unbuffered, it can become toxic, altering brain development, affecting learning, and shaping behaviour."

This section serves as a practical application of the broader themes in the piece. Just as theological rigidity can silence women and justify violence, emotional rigidity can silence the trauma responses of children. The solution, as McKnight suggests, is presence and flexibility rather than protocol and suppression.

Bottom Line

McKnight's most compelling argument is that the greatest threat to American faith and society is not external opposition, but internal stagnation and theological distortion. His critique of Theonomy stands out as the piece's most urgent warning, exposing how a misunderstanding of the cross can lead to the justification of state violence. The article's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that better sociological education will naturally lead to better outcomes, a leap that ignores the deep emotional and political roots of resistance to such education. Readers should watch for how these themes of inertia and theological error play out in the next election cycle, where the line between faith and power will be tested again.

It was a full-circle moment for one soon-to-be medical school graduate.

Shay Taylor-Allen learned last Friday that she had matched with her first-choice residency pick, Yale School of Medicine, a hospital that has played an outsized role in her life.

Taylor-Allen wasn’t just born there, she also worked there as a janitor when she was 18, she told ABC News.

The 32-year-old shared a video clip of her joyous reaction to being matched on Instagram, where it has quickly gone viral with more than 3.7 million views and counting.

“I was jumping up and down to the point I think the concrete was going to break,” Taylor-Allen told ABC News of her excitement.

Taylor-Allen, who attends Howard University College of Medicine, said it feels “surreal” to be returning to her hometown of New Haven, Connecticut, for her upcoming residency.

“I am still just feeling like I’m in a dream, because I could have never imagined that I’ll be going back to the same hospital I was not only born at, but a janitor at, to be a doctor for my community,” she said.

Taylor-Allen said she didn’t always know she wanted to go to medical school and was motivated to become a doctor after her experience helping her mom when she was sick.
