Gravity is not a force. But what does that mean?

Sabine Hossenfelder · Sabine Hossenfelder ·Dec 23, 2023 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sabine Hossenfelder tackles the most persistent misconception in modern physics with a clarity that cuts through decades of classroom simplification: gravity is not a force. While standard curricula teach us that an invisible pull drags us toward the ground, Hossenfelder argues that the sensation of weight is actually the result of the Earth pushing us upward, accelerating us away from our natural path through curved spacetime. This is not just semantic quibbling; it is a fundamental shift in how we understand the mechanics of the universe, one that turns our intuitive experience of standing still on the surface of the planet upside down.

The Accelerometer Paradox

Hossenfelder begins by dismantling the Newtonian intuition that we are being pulled down. She points to a device most of us carry in our pockets—the accelerometer—to prove that when we stand still, we are in a state of constant upward acceleration. "If you hold an accelerometer steadily in your hand it'll tell you it's accelerated upwards," she writes, noting that this is not a theoretical abstraction but a measurable reality. The device registers a force because the ground is physically pushing against the atomic bonds of our feet, preventing us from following the straightest possible line through spacetime.

This framing is effective because it relies on empirical evidence rather than abstract geometry. Hossenfelder explains that the pressure within the Earth's crust generates a force that accelerates everything on the surface upward. "The entire Earth has internal pressure otherwise it collapse that pressure generates a force on everything on the surface and as Newton taught us a force creates acceleration." The brilliance here lies in the inversion: we feel weight not because gravity is pulling us down, but because the floor is pushing us up. Critics might note that this distinction feels counterintuitive to anyone who has ever felt the "pull" of gravity, but Hossenfelder insists that the sensation is a result of resistance, not attraction.

"Gravity is not a force. It's a consequence of moving in a spacetime that's been curved by the presence of masses."

The Equivalence Principle and the Spring

To bridge the gap between this strange new reality and our daily experience, Hossenfelder leans on Einstein's equivalence principle. She uses the analogy of a spring in a rocket to demonstrate that acceleration is absolute, not relative. If a spring stretches in a rocket accelerating through empty space, it stretches for the exact same reason it stretches when sitting on a planet: a force is acting upon it. "Einstein said that if you're in the Box you can't tell whether you're being accelerated or whether you're sitting still on the surface of a planet," she notes. This indistinguishability is the cornerstone of general relativity.

However, the argument requires a crucial pivot that Hossenfelder handles with precision. She clarifies that while the effects are indistinguishable, the cause is not a gravitational force. "The point where people usually go wrong is to conclude that because gravity is indistinguishable from acceleration that means gravity accelerates you but that isn't so because to accelerate something you need a force and gravity is not a force." This distinction is vital. In free fall, such as an astronaut orbiting Earth or a plane in a zero-g maneuver, the spring does not stretch. There is no force acting on the object; it is simply following the curvature of spacetime. "If you took away the surface of Earth you'd fall and if you fell there'd be again no force acting on you," she explains. This redefines "free fall" not as a state of weightlessness caused by a lack of gravity, but as the only true state of unaccelerated motion.

Critics might argue that for practical engineering on Earth, treating gravity as a force is a useful approximation that saves time and reduces error. Hossenfelder acknowledges this, admitting that the Newtonian picture is "useful so long as you are on Earth" but ultimately "strictly speaking it's wrong." The danger lies in the linguistic habit of physicists themselves, who often refer to the "four fundamental forces" including gravity, a misnomer she attributes to the fact that "it starts with an F."

The Verdict on Velocity

The most challenging aspect of Hossenfelder's coverage is the redefinition of velocity and acceleration. In our daily lives, we assume that standing still means zero acceleration. Hossenfelder dismantles this by pointing out that velocity is relative, but acceleration is not. "You can however return to talking about Newtonian Gravity by redefining what you mean by no acceleration in general relativity," she writes, but warns that this redefinition is arbitrary. When you jump off a building, you are not accelerating; you are moving inertially. It is only when you hit the ground that the floor exerts a force, causing a sudden, violent acceleration. "If you hit the floor you're very suddenly accelerated again which has well measurable consequences," she states.

This perspective forces the reader to confront the idea that the ground is the active agent in our experience of gravity. The Earth's surface is constantly accelerating upward, crushing us against the curvature of spacetime. Hossenfelder's explanation of why the Earth doesn't collapse into a black hole without internal pressure reinforces this: "Without internal pressure stuff would just freely fall in the curvature that it itself creates." The planet holds itself up not by resisting a downward pull, but by pushing outward against the natural tendency of matter to follow curved paths.

"If you are only exposed to the gravitational interaction you fall and that is measurably not an acceleration."

Bottom Line

Sabine Hossenfelder's coverage succeeds by stripping away the comforting fiction of a downward pull and replacing it with the rigorous, if counterintuitive, reality of upward acceleration. Her strongest argument is the reliance on the accelerometer as a tangible proof that our intuition is inverted; we are not falling, we are being pushed. The piece's only vulnerability is the sheer cognitive dissonance required to accept that the ground is the one accelerating, a concept that clashes violently with centuries of Newtonian teaching. Readers should watch for how this reframing impacts our understanding of black holes and the nature of time, where the distinction between force and geometry becomes a matter of life and death.
