The Godfather, and the Birth of the Modern Gangster Epic

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Aug 11, 2023 · 51 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden doesn't just rehash the plot of The Godfather; he dismantles the assumption that its enduring power comes from a traditional, linear narrative. Instead, he argues that the film's genius lies in its radical, almost baffling structural choices—choices that modern audiences have become so accustomed to that they no longer recognize them as revolutionary. For busy listeners who think they know this story, van der Linden offers a fresh lens: the movie isn't a masterclass in pacing, but a masterclass in unconventional rhythm.

The Myth of the Traditional Epic

Van der Linden challenges the perception of The Godfather as a "classical" Hollywood film. He points out that while we now view it as a cornerstone of traditional style, "at the time it was actually kind of or quite like subversive and new and part of this new wave that completely like subverted everything that had come before." This is a crucial distinction. The film didn't just tell a story well; it invented a new cinematic language that we have since taken for granted. By framing the movie as a product of the "New Hollywood" era rather than a throwback to the studio system, van der Linden elevates the discussion from plot summary to film history.

He notes that the genre itself has a unique staying power. "I think the gangster movie has been yeah there's just something special about it that's that felt like it has been more unchanged and it hasn't gone like with the whims of time." This observation is compelling because it suggests the genre taps into something fundamental about human ambition and the American psyche, rather than just reflecting the trends of a specific decade. The gangster story, he argues, is a "microcosmic story for um just wanting to achieve something in life and just building something of your own."

The gangster movie has been more unchanged and it hasn't gone like with the whims of time.

The Unconventional Pacing of Michael Corleone

The most striking part of van der Linden's analysis is his focus on the film's "odd choices" regarding pacing and character introduction. He highlights how the film spends the first twenty or thirty minutes on a wedding sequence before the plot truly kicks in. "There's like a lot of very odd choices there that you wouldn't see in a movie nowadays," he observes. This is not a flaw, but a deliberate strategy to build a world before introducing the protagonist.

Even more radical is the delay of Michael Corleone's central role. "He's kind of just this he's the main character of the movie like he his character Arc and his transformation is arguably like the main thing that this movie is about and he kind of floats in the background for a good part of that first hour." Van der Linden argues that this structural delay is what makes Michael's eventual transformation so potent. The audience has to wait to see him, making his descent into "ultimate Evil" feel more inevitable and tragic. He asks the lingering question that defines the genre: "is that a warning or is it a fantasy?" This duality is what keeps the genre alive, blending the "self-made man" fantasy with the grim reality of moral decay.

Critics might note that van der Linden's focus on structural novelty risks underplaying the film's emotional core—the family dynamics that resonate regardless of pacing. However, his point stands that the way the story is told is just as important as what is told. The film works because "on paper should not work and for some reason there's a kind of magic to it that does make the whole thing chilled together."

It's not a basically told story where every beat is clearly laid out; it's a magic trick that defies the rules of standard storytelling.

The Evolution of a Genre

Van der Linden connects The Godfather to a broader lineage, noting how it bridges the gap between the prohibition-era gangster films and the "New Hollywood" era of the 1970s. He suggests that the film's success was partly due to the craft, noting that "if instead of The Godfather it had been like a sports movie but it was just like made crafted as well as this movie is crafted maybe like sports movies would be like the big you know thing." This is a provocative thought experiment that shifts the focus from the mobster trope to the sheer quality of execution.

He also traces the genre's evolution into modern television, seeing echoes of the film in shows like Breaking Bad and Succession. "I see elements of like this movie The Godfather and the gangster movie in things like Breaking Bad and even succession and like all these stories that were still fascinated with about like people doing like playing power games." This connection validates the film's status as a foundational text for modern storytelling about power and family. The "lawlessness of crime violence" is not just for entertainment; it serves as a "power fantasy" that allows audiences to explore the limits of ambition in a safe, narrative space.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's analysis succeeds by stripping away the reverence that often clouds The Godfather and revealing its structural audacity. His strongest argument is that the film's "unconventional" pacing and delayed character introduction are the very reasons it remains a masterpiece, not despite them. The biggest vulnerability in his argument is a slight over-reliance on the idea that the genre is "unchanged," when in fact the tone and moral ambiguity have shifted significantly from the 1930s to today. Nevertheless, his insight that the film is a "revolutionary new take" rather than a traditional classic offers a vital perspective for anyone revisiting the film.
