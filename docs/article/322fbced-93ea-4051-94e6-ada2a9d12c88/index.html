How to capture 2 billion tonnes of CO2 AND fix our oceans

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Jul 31, 2022 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace introduces a climate solution that sounds almost too convenient: burying algae in the desert to lock away carbon while simultaneously healing the ocean. Unlike abstract models or expensive direct-air capture machines, this proposal relies on a simple, scalable biological process that turns the world's arid coastlines into carbon sinks. The claim is staggering—sequestering two billion tonnes of CO2 annually—but the mechanism is grounded in the specific, often overlooked chemistry of seawater and desert aridity.

The Mechanics of a Desert Bloom

Borlace explains that the company behind this, Brilliant Planet, does not try to replicate the ocean in a lab. Instead, they "scale down the ocean" by utilizing massive open-air ponds in coastal deserts. The process begins by pumping deep, nutrient-rich seawater into these facilities. "The carbon and nutrients in the sea water are used to feed local strains of algae as part of a greatly accelerated growth process," Borlace notes. This is the critical innovation: the algae double their biomass in less than a day, a rate far exceeding the 12 to 18 days typical of biofuel facilities.

The speed of this growth is not just a biological curiosity; it is the engine of the carbon capture. The algae cycle through laboratory troughs, greenhouses, and finally vast outdoor ponds over a 30-day period. Once the biomass fills the ponds, it is filtered, dried by the intense desert sun, and buried. Borlace highlights the simplicity of the final step: "the dried biomass is buried at a depth of between 1 and 4 meters below the desert surface." This shallow burial is sufficient because the environment itself acts as a preservative. The desert sun cross-links the proteins in the algae, making them resistant to decay, while the high salt content of the marine algae absorbs any stray moisture, preventing rot.

"Brilliant Planet's entire growth process only takes 30 days... 99 of the growth happens in that last 10 days."

This rapid turnover is what makes the economics viable. Critics might note that relying on open-air ponds invites contamination from other species, but Borlace points out that Brilliant Planet uses locally sourced strains isolated within kilometers of the site. These algae are already acclimatized, meaning they possess built-in resilience and pose no risk of becoming invasive species if the system leaks. This local adaptation is a clever workaround to the fragility that usually plagues large-scale biological engineering.

The Ocean's Second Chance

The argument extends beyond just removing carbon from the air; it addresses the acidification of the oceans. When the algae consume the CO2 and bicarbonate from the seawater, the water's alkalinity remains intact. Borlace writes, "the outgoing water is slightly warmed and because it's put out to sea in shallow waters at the beach the operators can be confident it'll stay at the surface long enough to reabsorb co2 from the atmosphere." This creates a feedback loop where the water leaving the facility is actually cleaner and less acidic, helping local marine ecosystems thrive.

This dual benefit—carbon removal and ocean de-acidification—is what Borlace identifies as the technology's "quadruple lock" against failure. The combination of extreme dryness, salt content, geological barriers, and protective liners ensures the carbon stays put. "The locations and processes that brilliant planet have chosen provide a kind of quadruple lock to avoid this ever happening," he asserts. This multi-layered defense mechanism is a strong counter to the skepticism often directed at carbon sequestration projects, which frequently fail due to leakage or decomposition.

The Economics of Verification

The financial viability of the project hinges on the voluntary carbon market, a sector Borlace acknowledges is fraught with controversy. He distinguishes between the low-quality, cheap credits that dominate the market and the high-quality credits that companies like Microsoft and Amazon are willing to pay a premium for. "Prices for projects in this sector tend to be between one and two hundred dollars per metric ton of carbon dioxide captured," he explains. The key differentiator for Brilliant Planet is transparency. Because the carbon is buried on land, buyers can physically verify the storage.

"Companies buying credits from them can physically come and see the space where their carbon credits are stored... they can literally stick probes in the ground and verify for themselves that the carbon content is there."

This physical verifiability addresses the biggest risk for corporate buyers: reputational damage from backing fraudulent projects. While the broader market suffers from a lack of trust, Borlace argues that the high-quality sector is growing rapidly as buyers become more discerning. The shift away from the cheapest option toward verified, high-integrity projects is a trend that favors this technology. However, the reliance on the voluntary market remains a vulnerability; if the demand for high-quality credits stalls or if regulations change, the economic model could face pressure. Yet, the global strategy to license the technology to local operators in suitable regions suggests a path toward widespread adoption that doesn't depend on a single market dynamic.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace presents a compelling case for a solution that leverages natural processes rather than fighting against them. The strongest part of the argument is the integration of ocean chemistry and desert geography to create a self-sustaining carbon sink that is both verifiable and ecologically beneficial. The biggest vulnerability lies in the scalability of the voluntary carbon market and the logistical challenges of managing such massive biological systems across diverse global locations. As the IPCC makes clear that emission reductions alone are insufficient, this technology offers a tangible, albeit complex, path toward the necessary physical removal of CO2.
