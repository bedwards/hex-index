Everyone IS Lying - Why you shouldn't believe the TikTok Ban

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Jan 28, 2025 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a landscape saturated with partisan panic over national security, The Hated One cuts through the noise with a radical, almost heretical claim: the TikTok ban is not a victory for American democracy, but a public relations stunt that exposes the fragility of free speech itself. This piece is notable not for its defense of a Chinese-owned app, but for its chilling assertion that the real threat isn't Beijing's surveillance, but the insatiable data hunger of every major tech platform, regardless of where they are headquartered.

The Illusion of National Security

The Hated One dismantles the government's primary argument—that TikTok is a unique vector for Chinese espionage—by pointing to the reality of modern cyber warfare. The author writes, "It is Trivial for any nation state sponsored hacking group to penetrate any network undetected anywhere in the world." This framing is effective because it shifts the debate from a specific app to a systemic vulnerability. By citing the "Salt Typhoon" hack into US telecoms, The Hated One argues that banning one application is a futile gesture against an adversary capable of stealing data from any source, including legitimate data brokers protected by US law.

Critics might note that while hacking is ubiquitous, a state-mandated app like TikTok offers a direct, unmediated pipeline to user data that a hacked third-party company does not. However, The Hated One's point stands: the panic over TikTok ignores the fact that "every major nation state us included is engaging in cyber offensive operations on their enemies and friends alike." The argument suggests that the ban is less about stopping spies and more about performing a political action that creates an illusion of safety.

Code as Speech and the End of Rights

Perhaps the most legally provocative section of the commentary addresses the Supreme Court's treatment of the algorithm. The Hated One notes that the defense hinges on the idea that "the algorithm is the speech here." The author traces this back to the 1995 Bernstein v. US Department of Justice case, where encryption code was ruled to be protected speech. The Hated One writes, "The Supreme Court eventually decided that a programming language is no different than English or German and must be protected equally."

This historical context is crucial, yet the author argues it is being discarded in real-time. "Now that the Supreme Court has officially approved the Tik Tock ban... code is no longer speech when the government can say National Security and all your constitutional rights go out the window." This is the piece's most crushing takeaway, suggesting that the precedent protecting digital expression is crumbling under the weight of geopolitical fear. The Hated One correctly identifies the hypocrisy in TikTok's position, noting that the company "doesn't have First Amendment rights" and that its owner must abide by Chinese laws that mandate censorship, making their free speech defense legally and morally complicated.

This is the end of free speech on the internet but no one cares.

The Technical Reality of Malware

Moving from law to technology, The Hated One challenges the assumption that TikTok functions as a sophisticated piece of malware capable of bypassing the security sandboxes of iOS and Android. The author argues that if TikTok were truly a virus designed to infect millions of phones, it would require "chains of multiple vulnerabilities to exploit each and every Safeguard put in place by multi-billion dollar security research by Apple and Google."

The logic here is sound: the difficulty of maintaining persistent, undetected access on millions of devices simultaneously makes the "malware" theory unlikely compared to the simpler reality of data collection. The Hated One writes, "TikTok is absolutely a privacy nightmare don't get me wrong it collects all of your usage and viewing habits... but we already know all of that from the permissions Tik Tok is asking for." The author's point is that singling out TikTok is a "shallow hypocritical stance" because US-based platforms engage in identical data harvesting practices. The distinction is political, not technical.

A Galactic Brain Solution

The commentary culminates in a proposal that the author admits is politically impossible but technically necessary. The Hated One suggests that the root problem is not TikTok or China, but the business model of data retention itself. "The most commonly stated solution was for Biden to divest from TiK ToK... this is the worst solution possible," the author argues, because it fails to limit the amount of data collected. Instead, the author proposes a "nationwide ban on all personal data collection and retention beyond the needs of a service delivery."

This "galactic brain" idea reframes the entire crisis. If companies did not hoard data, there would be nothing for foreign adversaries to steal. The Hated One writes, "If there is no personal data stored by the big Tech then there is no point hacking them." While this solution is unlikely to pass given the lobbying power of the tech industry, it exposes the fundamental flaw in the current debate: we are fighting over who owns the data rather than asking why we are collecting so much of it in the first place.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest contribution is the realization that the TikTok ban is a distraction from the universal privacy crisis affecting all social media users. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a "galactic brain" solution that ignores the economic incentives driving the data economy, yet it successfully forces the reader to question whether the ban is about security or simply about who gets to control the narrative. The reader should watch for the next political maneuver, knowing that the underlying issue of mass data collection remains entirely unresolved.
