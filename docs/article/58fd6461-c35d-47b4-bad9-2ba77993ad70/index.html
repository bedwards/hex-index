199. Four takeaways from the national guard ruling

Steve Vladeck · One First ·Dec 29, 2025 ·18 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Steve Vladeck delivers a crucial legal reality check on a Supreme Court ruling that quietly but decisively blocks the federalization of the National Guard for domestic law enforcement. While the administration sought to bypass traditional legal hurdles, the Court's refusal to validate the use of obscure statutes to militarize American cities restores a vital constitutional boundary. This is not just a procedural win; it is a structural defense against the erosion of civilian control over the military.

The Statutory Wall

The core of the analysis centers on the Court's interpretation of 10 U.S.C. § 12406, a statute the administration hoped would serve as a backdoor for deploying troops. Vladeck notes that the majority's two-and-a-half-page order effectively dismantled the government's argument by insisting on a strict sequence of events. "The Government has not carried its burden to show that §12406(3) permits the President to federalize the Guard in the exercise of inherent authority to protect federal personnel and property in Illinois," Vladeck writes, highlighting the Court's rejection of the administration's shortcut. This is a significant check because it forces the executive branch to prove it has already tried and failed with regular military forces before calling up the Guard—a standard the administration has not met.

The ruling's logic is rooted in the Posse Comitatus Act, which generally prohibits the military from executing domestic laws. Vladeck points out the administration's contradictory position: they claimed inherent authority to protect federal property but simultaneously admitted that such protection does not constitute "executing the laws." "If that is correct, it is hard to see how performing those functions could constitute 'execut[ing] the laws' under §12406(3)," Vladeck observes, exposing the legal incoherence in the government's filing. This exposes a critical flaw in the administration's strategy: they tried to have it both ways, claiming broad power while denying the legal trigger required to use it.

Critics might argue that this ruling creates a dangerous vacuum where federal property is left undefended, but Vladeck counters that the traditional chain of command—starting with state governors—is the intended safety valve. "The way our laws are set up... the first military forces to be used in a domestic crisis are supposed to be un-federalized National Guard troops, under the command and control of the relevant state governor," he explains. This historical precedent, dating back to the very structure of the militia system, ensures that local authorities remain the primary responders, preventing the federal government from easily overriding state sovereignty.

The Insurrection Act Trap

A major fear among legal observers was that this ruling would simply force the administration to invoke the Insurrection Act of 1807, a much more aggressive and historically loaded statute. Vladeck argues this is a false dichotomy, suggesting that the administration was avoiding the Insurrection Act precisely because its requirements are far stricter. "The Trump administration's novel interpretation of §12406(3) was an attempt to deploy federalized troops into our cities without running the legal or political gauntlet of invoking the Insurrection Act for the first time in 33 years," Vladeck writes. This reframes the administration's legal maneuvering not as a creative solution, but as an attempt to evade the rigorous standards of a statute designed for true insurrections.

The historical context is stark. The Insurrection Act has been used sparingly, often requiring evidence that local authorities have lost control or are complicit in violence. Vladeck cites a 1964 memorandum stating that federal intervention is historically reserved for when "those engaging in violence are either acting with the approval of state authorities or have... taken over effective control of the area involved." By sidestepping this high bar, the administration was attempting to militarize civil unrest without the necessary factual predicate. The Court's decision forces the administration to either meet that high bar or abandon the deployment, a constraint that protects civil liberties.

The administration's attempt to deploy federalized troops into our cities without running the legal or political gauntlet of invoking the Insurrection Act was a bug of these deployments, not a feature.

The Enigmatic Concurrence

The commentary also dissects Justice Kavanaugh's unusual concurrence, which Vladeck describes as "enigmatic" and potentially contradictory. Kavanaugh seemed to worry that the majority's ruling might limit the President's ability to protect federal property, suggesting it could "cause the President to use the U.S. military more than the National Guard." Vladeck pushes back, noting that this argument inverts the traditional hierarchy of crisis response. "In Justice Kavanaugh's mob-storming-the-Philadelphia-courthouse example, the whole point is that the first responders... would be the Pennsylvania National Guard under the command of Governor Shapiro, not the President," he argues. This highlights a fundamental misunderstanding of federalism in Kavanaugh's view; the state guard is the first line of defense, not the federal army.

Furthermore, Kavanaugh's footnote regarding immigration stops adds a layer of confusion. While he reiterates that immigration stops must not be based on race or ethnicity, Vladeck notes that this feels like a "mea culpa" that shouldn't need a "decoder ring." The concurrence attempts to signal that his previous opinions do not foreclose challenges to immigration enforcement, yet the damage of those earlier rulings remains. This creates a dissonance where a justice tries to walk back the implications of his own jurisprudence without explicitly doing so.

Procedural Battles and the Party Presentation Principle

The dissenting opinions, led by Justices Alito and Gorsuch, focused heavily on the "party presentation principle," arguing the majority violated procedure by relying on an argument raised by an amicus curiae rather than the parties themselves. Vladeck finds this criticism "remarkably overblown and/or hypocritical," pointing out that the Court often addresses legal questions that parties fail to frame perfectly. "The majority's interpretation of §12406(3) is necessarily going to have downstream effects not just in this case, but in the other places where the Trump administration has attempted to deploy federalized National Guard troops," Vladeck notes, suggesting that the procedural objection is a distraction from the substantive legal error the Court corrected.

This procedural focus by the dissenters seems designed to obscure the fact that the administration's legal theory was fundamentally flawed. By fixating on who raised the argument, the dissenters avoid engaging with the statutory text that clearly limits the President's power. Vladeck's analysis suggests that the Court prioritized getting the law right over adhering to a rigid procedural game, a choice that serves the long-term health of the judicial system.

Bottom Line

Steve Vladeck's analysis succeeds in demystifying a complex legal ruling, revealing it as a decisive check on executive overreach that prioritizes constitutional structure over political convenience. The strongest part of his argument is the exposure of the administration's attempt to use a minor statute to bypass the strict requirements of the Insurrection Act, a move that the Court rightly blocked. The biggest vulnerability in the dissenting view is its reliance on procedural technicalities to ignore the substantive danger of militarizing domestic law enforcement. Readers should watch for whether the administration attempts to invoke the Insurrection Act directly, a move that would face even steeper legal and political hurdles than the failed National Guard strategy.
