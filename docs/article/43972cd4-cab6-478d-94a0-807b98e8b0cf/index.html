This is La Paz, the capital of Bolivia. As far as capital cities go, it's fairly humble. Its population, well under a million, is not huge. Its industrial and economic output is not massive.

Outpaced within Bolivia by Santa Cruz Delier. And yet, Leaz is home to this, a robust, interconnected, reliable urban transit system that connects the core of the city to the far reaches of its sprawling outskirts. For a city of La Paza's stature, this is unique. Santa Cruz de Lasier has mass transit, but only in the form of bus lines.

The same goes for the similarly sized metro areas of Urgue's capital, Monte Vido, while Paraguay's capital city has planned then suspended its first bus rapid transit lines. Ecuador's capital, Kito, a much larger city, has a transit system expanding beyond just buses with the Kito metro. But this subway consists of just a single line. Not until you get to South American metro areas doubling the population of La Paz will you begin to see any sort of urban transit systems of a similarly robust network map as that of Bolivia's capital.

And making this system unique not just in South America but the world over is the fact that it is serviced exclusively by the sky. Each line being run by a technology more likely to be seen at a ski area. The gondola now a decade old with its first lines entering operation in 2014. Melopherico, as the network is called, has been framed as fantastical by travel vloggers and opulent and gimmicky by political rivals.

But so far, the unconventional system has worked out as it celebrated servicing some 520 million passengers in its first 10 years, expanded from three initial lines to 10, and grew from daily ridership of 90,000 to 200,000. The core of the logic behind La Paza's gondola system is that it solves the city's fundamental geography problem. The city is spread across steep, rugged terrain within a canyon where elevation ranges from 9,000 ft or 2,700 m to 12,000 ft or 3600 m. Generally, the wealthier and employment-rich areas sit lower and nearer to the river, while more working-class neighborhoods trend higher into the hills, punctuated by the sprawling city of Elto, sitting at top the plane above the canyon entirely.
