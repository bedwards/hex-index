This week's analysis cuts through the fog of war to reveal a startling reality: Ukraine has successfully weaponized its own vulnerability, turning Russia's energy infrastructure into a battlefield that now burns within sight of St. Petersburg. Mick Ryan does not merely report on drone strikes; he frames them as a calculated "40-day gambit" designed to break Moscow's economic will rather than just its front lines. For the busy strategist or policy observer, the critical insight here is the shift from defensive attrition to offensive coercion—a move that has forced fuel shortages and export bans deep inside Russian territory while exposing the fragility of the Kremlin's narrative of inevitable victory.

The Economics of Attrition

Ryan opens with a sobering assessment of the "40-day campaign," a unified operation targeting oil refineries, military facilities, and major cities to pressure the executive branch into ending the war. He writes, "This is attempted strategic coercion by attrition of the war economy." This framing is crucial because it moves beyond the spectacle of burning towers to the hard math of war finance. Ryan notes that while independent analysts estimate functional disruption closer to a third of capacity, the cumulative industry losses now stand at 13.5 billion dollars since August 2025.

The human cost of this economic warfare is immediate and visible. As Ryan observes, "Not only have we witnessed large clouds of smoke from burning refineries over Moscow and St Petersburg, but fuel shortages have also forced Putin to extend petrol export bans." This is a significant escalation; the conflict has physically entered the daily lives of Russian civilians in a way that previous years did not. However, one must remain cautious about overstating the strategic pivot. While Ryan correctly notes that "Putin... admitted that the strikes are painful," the core argument that this will alter Moscow's calculus remains unproven. Critics might note that authoritarian regimes often double down when faced with internal pain rather than capitulate, viewing such suffering as a necessary sacrifice for national survival.

Refineries burn spectacularly on video, but Putin has restated his maximalist strategic objectives in Ukraine.

The author draws a parallel to the 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis, suggesting that just as global energy flows were once threatened by geopolitical maneuvering in the Persian Gulf, Russia's domestic fuel grid is now the primary lever of conflict. This historical echo adds depth to the argument: energy security is no longer just about supply chains; it is a direct target of kinetic warfare.

The Ground War and the Illusion of Victory

On the ground, the narrative is far more grim. Ryan dissects the Kremlin's recent claims with surgical precision, contrasting official propaganda with the reality on the battlefield. He writes, "Putin told his commanders that Russian forces had fully captured Kostiantynivka... Of course, that is not true. It is just another Russian lie." This rejection of the "victory" narrative is vital for understanding the current stalemate. The General Staff confirmed that Ukrainian units continue defensive operations within the town, which serves as a critical anchor in the fortress belt of Donetsk.

The tactical reality described by Ryan is one of brutal, slow-motion grinding. He notes that Russian forces are advancing at "about fifty metres per day around Kostiantynivka... among the slowest rates of any major offensive since the Somme." This comparison to World War I underscores the catastrophic inefficiency of the current Russian strategy. The data supports this: new figures from the Center for Strategic and International Studies put Russian casualties at roughly 1.4 million, with an exchange ratio widening to nearly eight to one in favor of Ukraine due to drone dominance.

Yet, Ryan does not shy away from the tragedy of these "marginal gains." He points out that Russia lost more ground than it took in April and May, a net loss of roughly four hundred square kilometers. This is a stark contradiction to the image of an unstoppable juggernaut. The author suggests that Putin's appearance in military uniform is an attempt to sell a story of success when "the reality is quite different."

Russia has gained minimal ground over the past six months but paid a high cost to do so.

A counterargument worth considering is whether Ukraine's defensive success comes at an unsustainable human price. While the exchange ratio favors Kyiv, the sheer volume of casualties and the relentless missile strikes on civilian centers suggest that "victory" in this phase may simply mean survival rather than liberation. The State Emergency Service reported at least twenty-five killed and more than eighty injured in a single massive strike on Kyiv, a reminder that the war's brutality is indiscriminate.

Strategic Shifts: Belarus, Fighters, and Diplomacy

The geopolitical ripple effects of Ukraine's momentum are evident in its relations with neighbors. Ryan highlights a "useful success" where Ukrainian pressure forced Belarus to deactivate Russian-installed relay stations, effectively blinding Moscow's drone operations from the north. He writes, "Ukraine's working assumption is that Lukashenko will confine himself to threats... extracting concessions from Moscow while avoiding entanglement." This diplomatic maneuvering mirrors the high-stakes negotiations seen in other global flashpoints, such as the delicate balance maintained during the Nayara Energy crisis, where economic leverage was used to prevent broader regional escalation.

Looking forward, the acquisition of 16 Gripen E fighter aircraft represents a qualitative leap for Ukraine's air force. Ryan describes the Saab JAS 39 Gripen as capable of operating from "short runways, highways, and other dispersed locations with minimal logistic support." This capability is essential for survival in an environment where fixed airbases are under constant threat. However, the delivery timeline—end of the decade for the E variant, early 2027 for the C/D models—raises questions about immediate air defense gaps.

The diplomatic landscape is shifting toward a "NATO 3.0" model, where European nations carry more burden as the United States recedes to an enabler role. Ryan cites Liana Fox of the Council on Foreign Relations: "Regardless of the outcome in Ankara, Europeans are unlikely to get a structured process toward a 'NATO 3.0'." This suggests that while the rhetoric of alliance is strong, the practical planning for a European-led defense architecture remains fragmented and uncertain.

Kyiv has the momentum in this war. But it does not yet have all the resources needed to force Russia into suitable war termination negotiations.

The Pacific Parallel

Ryan briefly but significantly connects the Ukraine conflict to the broader strategic competition in the Indo-Pacific, noting that Taiwan's new Littoral Combat Command unifies its defenses against threats within twenty-four nautical miles of the coast. This mirrors the "daily dance" of coast guard cutters and carrier transits seen elsewhere, where the line between peace and war is increasingly blurred by continuous low-level aggression. The reference to China's near-continuous presence in waters east of Taiwan since June 1 serves as a sobering reminder that the tactics of attrition and blockade rehearsal are not unique to Eastern Europe.

Bottom Line

Mick Ryan's analysis provides a necessary corrective to the noise of daily headlines, arguing convincingly that Ukraine has achieved a strategic tipping point by hitting Russia where it hurts most: its economy and its sense of invulnerability. The strongest part of his argument is the evidence of systemic disruption within Russia itself, from fuel shortages in Crimea to smoke over St. Petersburg. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its assumption that economic pain will inevitably lead to political capitulation; history suggests that regimes often endure immense suffering rather than surrender their strategic ambitions. Readers should watch closely for the outcome of the upcoming NATO summit in Ankara, as the gap between Ukrainian momentum and the resources required to end the war remains the critical variable.
