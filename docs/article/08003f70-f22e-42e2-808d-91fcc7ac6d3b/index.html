Why We Won't Raise Our Kids in Suburbia

Jason Slaughter · Not Just Bikes ·Jun 6, 2022 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jason Slaughter doesn't just argue that suburbs are boring; he claims they are actively hostile to childhood development, forcing families to choose between safety and independence. By contrasting his upbringing in car-choked London, Ontario, with life in Amsterdam, Slaughter offers a stark, visual indictment of North American urban planning that goes beyond traffic complaints to question the very definition of a "good" childhood.

The Architecture of Fear

Slaughter begins by dismantling the primary justification for suburban isolation: the fear of abduction. He notes that while Canada is statistically one of the safest countries on earth, parents operate under the delusion that "somebody could come along with a white van scoop them up and nobody would even notice." This observation is powerful because it highlights a disconnect between statistical reality and cultural perception. The author correctly identifies that this fear is manufactured, yet he pivots to a more tangible danger that is often ignored: the design of the streets themselves.

He invokes Jane Jacobs' concept of "eyes on the street" to explain why Dutch neighborhoods feel secure. In Amsterdam, the presence of other people creates a natural surveillance network. By contrast, Slaughter describes North American suburbia as "desolate for people outside of a car," noting that he often feels like "the only one out walking" and that "if something happened to me hundreds of people would drive by without even noticing." This is a crucial distinction. The danger isn't necessarily a stranger in a van; it is the isolation of the built environment which removes the community safety net.

Critics might argue that the fear of abduction, however irrational statistically, is a rational response to a lack of community cohesion. If neighbors don't know each other, the "eyes on the street" are effectively blind. Slaughter acknowledges this cultural shift but places the primary blame on the physical layout that prevents those connections from forming in the first place.

"Suburbia may be a good place to shelter toddlers but as soon as a child is more than about six years old being trapped in a mcmansion on the edge of town seriously inhibits their growth and independence."

The Vicious Circle of Car Dependency

The commentary deepens when Slaughter addresses the physical lethality of car-centric design. He points out a paradox: while traffic fatalities have fallen in developed nations, this is largely because "putting almost everyone inside of a car" protected the occupants, while "pedestrian deaths have increased significantly." The author argues that this creates a feedback loop where parents drive children everywhere because it isn't safe, which in turn adds more cars to the road, making it even less safe for pedestrians.

He dismantles common excuses for why North American children don't walk or cycle, such as weather or topography. "Did Canada's weather get worse in the past 40 years? Did the hills get higher? No," Slaughter writes. "Of course not. But we did stop building family-friendly housing anywhere that wasn't car dependent." This rhetorical questioning is effective because it exposes the absurdity of blaming geography when the real culprit is policy. The infrastructure simply does not exist to support independent travel for children.

The author also touches on the legal and social consequences of this design. He recounts the story of Adrian Crook, a father in Vancouver who was reported to child welfare services for letting his children ride the bus alone. The ministry threatened to remove the children unless they were supervised at all times. Slaughter uses this to illustrate a chilling reality: "You're crazy if you don't think this has a chilling effect that ultimately limits the independence of children in north america." The system has become so risk-averse that normal childhood exploration is now treated as neglect.

"Nobody ever kept their kids from walking to school just because too many people took the tram."

The Myth of the Suburban Ideal

Slaughter concludes by challenging the entrenched North American belief that the suburbs are the ideal place to raise a family. He argues that this is a "myth" that persists despite evidence to the contrary. The reality, he suggests, is that "kids can't drive but they can walk or cycle though only if it's safe." Since the safety infrastructure is missing, the only option for parents is to become full-time chauffeurs, a role that Slaughter implies is detrimental to both the parent and the child.

He contrasts the "soul-crushingly boring" nature of the suburbs with the vibrant, independent life of Dutch children who cycle to school and sports alone. The author's personal decision to move to the Netherlands is presented not as a retreat, but as a strategic choice for his children's development. He notes that in the Netherlands, "almost all new neighborhoods are walkable and some of them are even car free," creating an environment where independence is the default, not the exception.

A counterargument worth considering is that the Dutch model is difficult to replicate in North America due to the sheer scale of existing infrastructure and the cultural inertia of car ownership. Slaughter admits that "walkability is nearly impossible to retrofit on top of car-dependent suburbia," suggesting that the solution requires starting over rather than fixing the old system. This is a sobering admission that limits the immediate applicability of his solution for most readers.

"What children really need is fewer cars because cars are dangerous especially for children plus news flash kids can't drive but they can walk or cycle though only if it's safe."

Bottom Line

Jason Slaughter's strongest argument is his ability to reframe the "safety" debate from a fear of crime to a fear of infrastructure, proving that the design of our streets is the primary driver of child dependency. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a total systemic overhaul, which offers little guidance for families currently trapped in un-reformable suburbs. Readers should watch for how cities attempt to retrofit walkability in car-dependent zones, as that will be the true test of whether this vision can ever be realized outside the Netherlands.
