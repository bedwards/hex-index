'Truth and the Budget Have the Last Word'

Various · Animation Obsessive ·Mar 29, 2026 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In a media landscape obsessed with the next big franchise, the author of this piece makes a startling claim: the most revolutionary animation of the 20th century wasn't a blockbuster, but a series of debt-ridden, hand-painted experiments made by a couple who refused to compromise. This isn't just a history lesson; it's a manifesto on the cost of artistic integrity, revealing how John and Faith Hubley turned the blacklist and financial ruin into a crucible for a new kind of humanist storytelling.

The Rebellion Against the "Sweet Chipmunk"

The author frames the Hubleys not merely as animators, but as ideological rebels who actively dismantled the prevailing aesthetic of their time. As Various writes, "John was a name at UPA, where he did socially engaged and graphically fresh cartoons. Some saw him as a 'genius.'" The text effectively contrasts John's modernist approach with the Disney standard, noting his rejection of "18th-century watercolor" aesthetics and "sweet sentimental chipmunks and bunnies." This framing is crucial because it positions the Hubleys' work as a direct response to the sanitized, corporate animation that dominated the mid-century. By highlighting their move from Hollywood to New York after John's blacklisting, the author suggests that their exile was actually a liberation. As Various puts it, "As a country, we paid a heavy cost [for the blacklist], but personally, it allowed us great freedom." This is a compelling, if slightly romanticized, angle. It overlooks the sheer terror of being blacklisted, but it correctly identifies that the Hubleys' unique voice could only have emerged outside the studio system.

"If your value in life is to be always doing what comes along, you are abandoning your responsibility as an artist."

The piece argues that their commercial work, specifically the Maypo cereal commercials, was a strategic Trojan horse. Rather than viewing these ads as a sell-out, the author presents them as a funding mechanism that bought the Hubleys the freedom to make their personal films. The text notes that the ads "derived from unscripted, improvised audio recordings, spliced into narratives," and that Faith said, "We just loved the idea of doing something natural and truthful with a non-professional actor." This evidence holds up well; the Maypo ads were indeed groundbreaking for their time, proving that commercial constraints could be subverted to create authentic art. However, one might argue that the author underplays the tension between the two goals. While Faith claimed the ads "supported our personal films," the constant scramble for money, as John noted, meant they spent "20, 30, 40 percent of your day dealing with matters that have nothing to do with the pure creative." The author acknowledges this debt but perhaps glosses over the toll it took on their mental health and relationships.

The Alchemy of Collaboration

A significant portion of the commentary focuses on the unique, often volatile, partnership between John and Faith. The author resists the urge to simply credit John as the visionary and Faith as the helper, instead presenting a nuanced picture of their dynamic. As Various writes, "Faith gave it a try: We collaborate on the story. John does most of the backgrounds and I do some. I do the character rendering. We both work on the soundtracks." The text highlights Faith's role as an editor who shaped improvised dialogue into narrative, a contribution the author notes "approaches being something like a writer for the films." This is a vital correction to the historical record, which often marginalizes women in animation production. The author also touches on the emotional friction, quoting Faith's admission that they "argued relentlessly over their work," yet insisting that "I don't believe there's anything wrong with fighting." This adds a layer of human realism that prevents the piece from becoming a hagiography. The inclusion of Tissa David, a legendary animator who worked on Everybody Rides the Carousel, further strengthens the argument that the Hubleys' success relied on a small, trusted circle of artists who shared their ethos. David's philosophy that a "low budget... does not inhibit me at all" reinforces the idea that their limitations were actually their greatest creative asset.

"We make one for them, and one for us."

The author's treatment of their failures is particularly striking. Rather than hiding behind their Oscar wins for Moonbird and The Hole, the piece details the financial disaster of Of Stars and Men, noting that "even some festival audiences were skeptical" and that Faith recalled "banging doors and loud hisses" at Annecy. This honesty about their struggles makes their eventual legacy more impressive. The text suggests that their commitment to truth and humanity, even when it meant financial ruin, is what allowed their work to resonate decades later. The author connects this to the broader context of independent animation, noting that in the 50s and 60s, "an indie animated film wasn't a simple proposition. It was often impossible to get one funded and distributed." By weaving in the context of their work with UPA and the specific techniques like wax resist used in The Adventures of (1957), the author provides a rich texture that goes beyond a simple biography.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its refusal to separate the Hubleys' commercial success from their artistic integrity, showing instead how the former was the necessary fuel for the latter. The piece's biggest vulnerability is a slight tendency to gloss over the sheer exhaustion of their constant financial scrambling, though it does mention the debt. Readers should watch for how this model of "art for art's sake" funded by commercial work translates to the modern streaming era, where the line between independent and corporate is increasingly blurred.

The author concludes that despite the pain and the debt, the Hubleys' legacy endures because they never stopped making the films they wanted to make. As John said, "you have to make what you want to make." This simple, stubborn principle is the heartbeat of the entire piece, reminding us that truth and the budget have the last word, but the artist decides which one wins.

1. The Hubley films

An animated sequence went very, very big on social media last year.

It’s about two people in a relationship. At one point, they sit in a rowboat. At another, they have an awkward conversation while wearing masks. Nothing major happens, but their interactions sound natural, unscripted — and they’re animated with artful drawings and a real sense of life. That was enough to hook millions.

The clips came from a little-known ‘70s movie called Everybody Rides the Carousel (watch). It was made for TV, but it doesn’t feel throwaway. In fact, handling this section of the film were two great animators: Barrie Nelson and Tissa David.1

Those artists had already worked a lot, on earlier projects, with the directors of Everybody Rides the Carousel. And the sequence turned out so well for a reason. The film was a Hubley production, after all.

John and Faith Hubley always brought something special to their work. It was never phoned in — even the ads they made on the side, to pay the bills, were left-field and human. Across the Hubleys’ decades together, their films won three Oscars. And they made those films outside America’s big studios.

Back then, from the ‘50s through the ‘70s, an indie animated film wasn’t a simple proposition. It was often impossible to get one funded and distributed. Even the Hubleys had to scramble, deep in debt. “[Y]ou spend a certain percentage — let’s say 20, 30, 40 percent of your day — dealing with matters that have nothing to do with the pure creative,” John said.2

But they kept doing one of these unusual films each year. John noted that “you have to make what you want to make.” For Faith, the less commercial of the pair, it was non-negotiable. “If you are going to grow, stay alive, remain sensitive and valid, you have to keep working seriously,” she said. “If your value in life is to be always doing what comes along, you are abandoning your responsibility as an artist.”
