Every now and then during my research for the Just Have a Think channel, a technology headline comes along that really grabs my attention. A perfect example is a recent claim from a group of Australian researchers that they've achieved a conversion efficiency of 30.6% from a single junction perovskite solar cell. Now, if you're not all that interested in the minutia of these things, you might be thinking, well, that sounds plausible. So, what's the big deal?

You might even be thinking, what's perovskite and why is it relevant? Which is a fair question and one we'll come back to a bit later on. The thing is though, 30.6% conversion efficiency for an apparently realworld production ready solar cell is significantly higher than anything we've seen on the market before. The very best perovskite solar cells available today are achieving conversion efficiencies of around 26%.

Which is already considered to be a cuttingedge breakthrough for this material. To jump from there up to more than 30% would be quite the technological leap. So is this apparent breakthrough the real deal or just another lab curiosity destined never to make it out of the press release? Hello and welcome to Just Have a Think.

So, here's the story. A small company called Halo Cell Energy working with researchers at Queensland University of Technology in Australia has been playing around with another well publicized technological gamecher, graphine. What they were searching for were ways to incorporate graphine into the conductive layers of a peroskite cell to improve performance and reduce cost. To do that, they teamed up with a material supplier called First Graphine.

Graphine, as I'm sure is a single atom thick sheet of carbon that, as well as being incredibly strong, also has the enviable quality of being extremely electrically conductive. So the idea here was to insert a very thin functionalized graphine layer into a perkite solar cell stack with the goal of moving electrons around more efficiently, reducing resistive losses, and crucially replacing the expensive conductors like gold and silver that are used in existing solar cells. So what do they mean by functionalize then? Well, the precise details of that are a trade secret for now.
