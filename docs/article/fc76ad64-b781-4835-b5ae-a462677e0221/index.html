How the Mongol Empire Fell - Medieval History Documentary

Kings and Generals · Kings and Generals ·Apr 26, 2022 · 56 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Kings and Generals dismantles a pervasive historical myth: that the Mongol Empire shattered immediately upon the death of Genghis Khan. Instead, they present a compelling case for a thirty-three-year period of unified, terrifying expansion that fundamentally reshaped the administrative landscape of Eurasia before fracturing into a complex web of rival states. This is not just a timeline correction; it is a re-evaluation of how empires actually function when they outgrow their founder's charisma.

The Myth of Immediate Collapse

The documentary opens by addressing a common misconception head-on. "It is often inaccurately stated that it collapsed upon the death of its founder Chingis Khan in 1227," Kings and Generals writes, noting that the empire remained "surprisingly unified" and "extraordinarily committed to expanding" under his successors. This distinction is crucial because it shifts the narrative from a sudden implosion to a gradual, structural failure. The author argues that under Ögedei Khan, the Mongols moved beyond simple raiding to establish a "proper administrative structure" with branch secretariats that managed taxation and civilian governance.

This administrative shift is the piece's most underrated insight. By creating a bureaucracy that answered to a central secretariat in Karakorum, the Mongols attempted to govern rather than just plunder. However, the commentary notes a critical tension: these secretariats often clashed with Mongol princes who viewed conquered lands solely as personal spoils. "These secretariats were often in competition with the Mongol princes who saw the lands of the Empire as existing solely to be exploited by the Chingisids," the author explains. This friction between state-building and tribal entitlement sowed the seeds of future disintegration long before the civil wars began.

The Succession Crisis and the Fracture

The narrative accelerates with the death of Möngke Khan in 1259, a turning point that the documentary frames as the moment the empire's unity became unsustainable. With Möngke dying in a siege bogged down by "mud and rain in Sichuan Province," the vacuum of power triggered a deadly race between his brothers, Kublai and Ariq Böke. Kings and Generals highlights the illegitimacy of Kublai's move: "his election was decidedly illegal; it lacked representation from the other descendants of Chingis Khan's children and was not done in Karakorum."

This section effectively illustrates how the very mechanisms of Mongol power—the Kurultai, or council of electors—became weapons of division. The author points out that Kublai declared himself Khan in a Chinese-style city, signaling a cultural pivot that alienated traditionalists. "For the first time the Mongol Empire had two declared Great Khans," the documentary states, marking the beginning of the end for a unified state. The consequences were immediate and catastrophic. Without a central authority to mediate, other branches of the family turned on each other. "In the far west of the Empire... Berke had gone to war with Kublai's other surviving brother Hulagu," Kings and Generals notes, explaining how the Golden Horde and the Ilkhanate became locked in conflict over territory in the Caucasus and Iran.

The Mongol Empire was irrevocably broken apart, not into the four khanates of popular knowledge, but into a chaotic array of six competing states ruled by rival descendants of Genghis Khan.

Critics might argue that the documentary overstates the role of succession disputes while underplaying the sheer logistical impossibility of governing such a vast, culturally diverse territory. Even without the civil war, the empire may have been too large to hold together. However, the author's focus on the specific political choices made by Kublai and Ariq Böke provides a necessary human element to the structural collapse.

The Ilkhanate: Reform and Decline

The commentary then zooms in on the Ilkhanate in the Middle East, using the chronicles of Rashid al-Din to paint a picture of instability followed by brief, brilliant reform. The author describes a period where Khans were "more interested in hunting, feasting and drinking rather than governing," allowing corruption to fester. This changed with the rise of Ghazan in 1295, who "oversaw economic revitalization" and converted to Islam to tie the dynasty's legitimacy to the region's dominant faith.

Ghazan's reforms were comprehensive, from standardizing weights and measures to building irrigation canals. "Ghazan oversaw economic revitalization... stamping out banditry with highway patrols," Kings and Generals writes, crediting him with restoring order. Yet, the documentary also highlights the fragility of these gains. Ghazan died young, producing no heirs, and his successors struggled to maintain the momentum. The final segment focuses on Abu Sa'id, the last significant ruler, whose reign was dominated by the powerful general Choban. The narrative takes a tragic turn when Abu Sa'id's desire for Choban's daughter leads to the execution of the general and his entire family. "After Choban's death, Abu Sa'id... killed almost all of Choban's family," the author notes, a move that ultimately destabilized the regime further.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals delivers a nuanced, evidence-rich correction to the popular understanding of the Mongol collapse, proving that the empire's end was a slow, multi-generational process of political fragmentation rather than a single event. The strongest part of this argument is the detailed breakdown of how administrative overreach and succession disputes interacted to shatter the unified state. The biggest vulnerability remains the reliance on Rashid al-Din's perspective for the Ilkhanate, which may gloss over the agency of non-Mongol populations in the empire's decline. Readers should watch for how these internal fractures set the stage for the rise of new powers like the Ottomans and the Ming Dynasty.
