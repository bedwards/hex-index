How Vail Destroyed Skiing

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Mar 7, 2025 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union delivers a scathing indictment of the modern ski industry, arguing that the very companies promising convenience have systematically dismantled the culture, affordability, and community that made skiing possible. The piece is notable not just for its data on skyrocketing costs, but for its on-the-ground reporting from Park City, where the human cost of corporate consolidation is measured in evictions, strikes, and the loss of local identity. For anyone who has ever stared at a $300 lift ticket and wondered where the sport went, this investigation offers a clear, if painful, answer.

The Rise of the Duopoly

The author traces the industry's transformation from a fragmented market of independent operators to a tightly controlled duopoly dominated by Vail Resorts and Altera. More Perfect Union writes, "We made a conscious decision to essentially decide you know we're going to increase our profits at the expense of people's happiness." This admission, attributed to industry insiders, frames the entire narrative not as an accident of market forces, but as a deliberate strategic choice. The coverage effectively illustrates how Vail's aggressive acquisition spree—purchasing 18 resorts in 2019 alone—eroded competition that once kept prices in check.

The piece highlights how the introduction of the Epic Pass and the Icon Pass created a financial trap for consumers. As More Perfect Union puts it, "The prices of the day tickets and the season passes are interconnected if you raise the price of the day tickets it starts to make the season package look affordable now." This pricing strategy, designed to lock in revenue before the snow even falls, has priced out casual skiers and families while flooding the mountains with passholders who face endless lift lines. The author argues that this model prioritizes shareholder returns over the actual experience of skiing, turning a recreational activity into a high-stakes financial gamble for the resorts.

Critics might note that the consolidation of resorts has brought investment in infrastructure and snowmaking that saved many mountains from bankruptcy. However, the article counters this by showing that these efficiencies have been passed to investors rather than customers or workers, leaving the core experience degraded.

The priority feels like it's on acquisition instead of maybe operation.

The Human Cost in the Mountains

The most compelling section of the coverage moves beyond economics to the social fabric of ski towns. More Perfect Union details the plight of locals in Park City, where the median home price hit $2.9 million, pushing out the very people who keep the resorts running. The author interviews ski patroller Lou Kiaa, who lives in employee dorms with seven roommates, noting, "I'm not proud of having slept on a couch in somebody's studio apartment for a whole winter just to like go skiing and be able to do something I'm passionate about." This personal testimony grounds the abstract numbers of housing inflation in a stark reality of displacement.

The article connects this housing crisis directly to the business model, suggesting that the influx of wealthy passholders and the conversion of housing into short-term rentals have made it impossible for workers to live near their jobs. As More Perfect Union writes, "There is a sense of not necessarily recognizing the town that they grew up in." The coverage effectively argues that the industry's growth has come at the cost of the community's soul, creating a destination that is increasingly hostile to the locals who built it.

The recent strike by Park City ski patrollers serves as a climax to this section, with the author noting that workers are "fed up with the low wages and difficult living conditions." The union's victory, which resulted in significant pay raises and a dip in Vail's stock price, suggests that the duopoly's grip is not unbreakable. However, the article also acknowledges that this is a symptom of a larger systemic issue that requires more than just wage hikes to solve.

A Path Forward?

In the final act, the piece pivots to potential solutions, spotlighting the Indie Pass as a viable alternative to the mega-corporate model. More Perfect Union explains that this pass allows skiers to access multiple independent resorts, providing "real geographical and terrain diversity that they may not have otherwise." The author interviews Eric Mogenson, CEO of Entabeni Systems, who argues that the solution lies in empowering small operators rather than letting them be swallowed by giants. The coverage highlights a co-op model for Black Mountain in New Hampshire as a beacon of hope, where the community reclaims ownership of their local hill.

This section offers a constructive counter-narrative to the despair of the first half, suggesting that the market can self-correct if consumers and workers organize. The author writes, "It's really easy to complain and it's another thing to do something about it," framing the Indie Pass not just as a product, but as a movement. While the scale of this alternative is currently small compared to Vail's empire, the article makes a persuasive case that it represents the future of sustainable, community-focused skiing.

Critics might argue that independent resorts lack the capital to compete with the marketing and infrastructure of the big players, making the Indie Pass a niche solution at best. Yet, the article's emphasis on the unique value of local ownership and the growing fatigue with overcrowding suggests a shifting consumer sentiment that could tip the scales.

There really is a sense of ownership and that's really what brought us here.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union's argument is strongest in its unflinching exposure of how corporate consolidation has sacrificed the skier's experience and the local community for profit, backed by compelling on-the-ground reporting. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the scalability of the proposed alternatives, which currently struggle to match the reach of the duopoly. Readers should watch for whether the momentum behind the Indie Pass and labor strikes can translate into a broader structural shift in the industry before the next ski season begins.
