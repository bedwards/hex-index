Mantic Monday: The monkey's paw curls

Scott Alexander · Astral Codex Ten ·Jan 12, 2026 ·27 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Scott Alexander tackles a paradox that defines our current moment: prediction markets have exploded in volume and accuracy, yet society remains stubbornly unchanged. The piece is notable not for celebrating a financial boom, but for diagnosing why a tool capable of forecasting global events with uncanny precision is failing to revolutionize decision-making. It forces a sobering question on the reader: if we can now bet billions on the future, why does the future still feel so chaotic?

The Trojan Horse of Gambling

Alexander begins by acknowledging the sheer scale of the growth, noting that markets like Polymarket and Kalshi have surged from millions to billions in monthly volume. Yet, he immediately undercuts the hype with a stark reality check. "The catch is, of course, that it's mostly degenerate gambling, especially sports betting," Alexander writes. He points out that while Kalshi is 81% sports volume, Polymarket is only slightly better at 37%, with the rest often devolving into trivialities like betting on how often a tech billionaire will tweet. This framing is crucial; it suggests that the "revolution" is being hijacked by the very regulatory loopholes that allowed it to exist. The executive branch and regulators have inadvertently created a sanctuary for high-stakes speculation on sports while remaining hostile to markets on sensitive geopolitical events.

The author's self-reflection adds a layer of intellectual honesty rarely seen in tech commentary. "Insofar as my advocacy helped make this possible, I am bad," he admits, acknowledging that his push for these markets may have primarily enabled gambling rather than enlightenment. This admission holds up because it aligns with the observable data: the infrastructure is there, but the incentives are misaligned. Critics might argue that gambling is a harmless byproduct, but Alexander's point is that it crowds out the serious work of forecasting. The noise of sports betting drowns out the signal of global crises.

The Missing Link in Decision Making

Despite the noise, the data itself is undeniably powerful. Alexander highlights a specific market regarding the potential removal of Iran's Supreme Leader, noting that thousands of investors have converged on a 20% probability. "The several thousand people who have invested $6 million in that question have probably converged upon the most accurate probability possible with existing knowledge," he argues. This is the strongest evidence the piece offers: the market is working exactly as theory predicts. Yet, the revolution hasn't happened. Why? Alexander suggests the failure lies not in the data, but in its integration. "The missing link seems to be between 'it's now possible to place global events in probabilistic context → society revolutionized'." He posits that news outlets ignore these markets, and policymakers may not know how to act on a 20% versus a 40% chance. The argument here is compelling because it shifts the blame from the tool to the user. We have the dashboard, but we haven't learned how to drive.

The several thousand people who have invested $6 million in that question have probably converged upon the most accurate probability possible with existing knowledge, just the way prediction markets should.

The Rulescuck and the Edge Cases

The piece takes a darker turn when examining the mechanics of resolution. Alexander introduces the term "rulescuck" to describe traders who lose bets due to technicalities in the fine print. He details a series of absurd scenarios, from whether a world leader wore a "suit" to the exact definition of an "invasion" in Venezuela. "With thousands of markets getting resolved per month, chances are high that at least one will hinge upon one of these edge cases," he writes. The analysis of these failures is sharp, particularly the example of the US military action in Venezuela, where a market resolved "No" to an invasion because the criteria required "establishing control," a nuance that ignored the reality of a Special Forces capture. This highlights a critical vulnerability: the world is too messy for binary rules. When the definition of an event becomes a game of semantic hair-splitting, the market's utility for serious policy analysis erodes. The human cost of these conflicts is reduced to a technicality in a smart contract.

Alexander also touches on the decentralized nature of these resolutions, noting how the system can be manipulated by coordinated groups. In the case of a mineral deal between the US and Ukraine, a "cabal" of traders exploited a timing loophole to force a resolution before the platform could intervene. "Then, with two minutes to spare, a Polymarket employee showed up and said that Polymarket's opinion was that it should be NO," Alexander recounts, only for the platform to capitulate to the vote. This exposes the fragility of relying on decentralized consensus for high-stakes geopolitical events. The system is prone to gaming, and the "truth" can be bought or bullied into existence.

The Illusion of Insider Trading

Finally, Alexander addresses the media's obsession with scandal, arguing that it distracts from the real story. He recounts a case where a trader seemingly won big on the Nobel Peace Prize by finding a draft on a WordPress site, and another where a false map update led to a massive payout. "The media likes engaging with prediction markets through dramatic stories about insider trading and market manipulation," he observes. "This is as useful as engaging with Waymo through stories about cats being run over." This analogy is devastatingly effective. It reframes the narrative from one of systemic corruption to one of statistical inevitability. In a system processing thousands of markets, outliers are guaranteed. The real story isn't the fraud; it's the base rate of accuracy that remains high despite the noise.

Critics might note that dismissing insider trading concerns entirely is risky, as it could erode trust in the system if high-profile frauds go unpunished. However, Alexander's focus on base rates is the more pragmatic approach for a busy reader trying to understand the tool's utility. The occasional cheat does not invalidate the aggregate wisdom of the crowd.

Bottom Line

Scott Alexander's analysis is a necessary corrective to the hype surrounding prediction markets: the technology works, but the ecosystem is broken by gambling incentives and rigid rules. The strongest part of the argument is the identification of the "missing link" between accurate data and societal action. The biggest vulnerability remains the inability of these markets to handle the ambiguity of real-world events without devolving into semantic games. Readers should watch for whether regulators and platforms can evolve beyond sports betting to handle the messy, high-stakes questions that actually matter.

The Monkey’s Paw Curls.

Isn’t “may you get exactly what you asked for” one of those ancient Chinese curses?

Since we last spoke, prediction markets have gone to the moon, rising from millions to billions in monthly volume.

For a few weeks in October, Polymarket founder Shayne Coplan was the world’s youngest self-made billionaire (now it’s some AI people). Kalshi is so accurate that it’s getting called a national security threat.

The catch is, of course, that it’s mostly degenerate gambling, especially sports betting. Kalshi is 81% sports by monthly volume. Polymarket does better - only 37% - but some of the remainder is things like this $686,000 market on how often Elon Musk will tweet this week - currently dominated by the “140 - 164 times” category.

(ironically, this seems to be a regulatory difference - US regulators don’t mind sports betting, but look unfavorably on potentially “insensitive” markets like bets about wars. Polymarket has historically been offshore, and so able to concentrate on geopolitics; Kalshi has been in the US, and so stuck mostly to sports. But Polymarket is in the process of moving onshore; I don’t know if this will affect their ability to offer geopolitical markets)

Degenerate gambling is bad. Insofar as prediction markets have acted as a Trojan Horse to enable it, this is bad. Insofar as my advocacy helped make this possible, I am bad. I can only plead that it didn’t really seem plausible, back in 2021, that a presidential administration would keep all normal restrictions on sports gambling but also let prediction markets do it as much as they wanted. If only there had been some kind of decentralized forecasting tool that could have given me a canonical probability on this outcome!

Still, it might seem that, whatever the degenerate gamblers are doing, we at least have some interesting data. There are now strong, minimally-regulated, high-volume prediction markets on important global events. In this column, I previously claimed this would revolutionize society. Has it?

I don’t feel revolutionized. Why not?

The problem isn’t that the prediction markets are bad. There’s been a lot of noise about insider trading and disputed resolutions. But insider trading should only increase accuracy - it’s bad for traders, but good for information-seekers - and my impression is that the disputed resolutions were handled as well as possible.
