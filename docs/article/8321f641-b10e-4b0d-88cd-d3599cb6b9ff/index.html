How College Broke the Labor Market

PolyMatter · PolyMatter ·Apr 13, 2024 · 21 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

PolyMatter delivers a jarring diagnosis for a crisis that has quietly eroded the American middle class: the very promise of "college for all" has become the primary engine of labor market dysfunction. By juxtaposing the staggering enrollment numbers at universities like the University of Central Florida against the critical shortage of skilled tradespeople, the author exposes a dangerous disconnect between educational policy and economic reality. This is not just a story about student debt; it is an indictment of a three-decade national experiment that convinced millions that a bachelor's degree was the only path to dignity, while systematically dismantling the alternatives that once guaranteed it.

The Great Mismatch

The piece opens with a stark statistical reality that immediately grounds the abstract debate in tangible numbers. PolyMatter writes, "at least 37% of us workers hold a 4-year degree yet only about 32% of jobs require one." This simple arithmetic reveals the core of the argument: we have engineered a massive oversupply of graduates for a market that simply cannot absorb them. The author illustrates the human cost of this imbalance by noting that "52 out of 100 [new college grads] are underemployed and nearly all will remain that way over the next 9 years."

The commentary here is particularly sharp because it rejects the common narrative that young people are simply unwilling to work hard. Instead, PolyMatter argues that the market is "consistently failing to clear," a technical economic term that effectively describes a system where supply and demand are artificially severed. The author points out that while entry-level white-collar jobs now demand years of experience, pushing graduates into service roles, "most jobs about 52% are what we would call middle skill meaning they require some specialized skills or training but not a bachelor's." This reframing is crucial; it shifts the blame from individual laziness to a structural failure in how we define "work."

The market is consistently failing to clear.

The Manufactured Panic

To explain how we arrived at this point, PolyMatter digs into the historical origins of the "college for all" ideology, tracing it back to a specific moment of national anxiety. The author highlights the 1983 report "A Nation at Risk," noting that it "cleverly embraced the militaristic language of the era" to frame education as a matter of national security. This historical context is vital because it shows that the push for universal higher education was not a natural evolution of market forces, but a top-down policy decision driven by Cold War fears.

PolyMatter writes, "suddenly it had America's attention far more than this little known Department ever imagined... education like an increasing number of things in America was now an issue of National Security." This shift had profound consequences, narrowing the definition of "human capital" from broad education to specifically a four-year degree. The author argues that this created a self-reinforcing cycle where "the days of technical skills and career Readiness were over in were academics general education standardization and testing." The result was a bipartisan consensus that ignored the value of vocational training, with politicians from George Bush to Barack Obama uniformly pushing the same message.

Critics might note that the 1983 report was a response to genuine concerns about global competitiveness and that the expansion of higher education did, in fact, lift millions out of poverty. However, PolyMatter's counterpoint is that the solution became a dogma that ignored the diminishing returns of a degree for non-selective institutions. The author observes that "by 2012 96% of Democrats and 99% of Republicans expected their kids to attend college," creating a social environment where opting out was seen as a failure rather than a valid choice.

The Status Trap

Perhaps the most insightful part of the coverage is the analysis of how college transformed from an economic investment into a status symbol. PolyMatter explains that as the middle class poured into universities, "money and Status once inextricably connected began to diverge." The author argues that the equation was simplified in the public mind to "college equals status," even as the financial rewards of a degree began to erode.

This cultural shift created a perverse incentive structure. PolyMatter writes, "today many high status jobs like journalists are so poorly paid that only the Rich can afford to take them." The author suggests that the stigma attached to trade schools has become so potent that even children of well-paid blue-collar workers are pushed away from the family trade. The piece cites the example of elevator installers, who earn a median of nearly $100,000, yet their children are "over a thousand times more likely to take up the same line of work" only if they don't go to college. This creates a generational knowledge gap where "we don't have anyone left to install elevators."

The commentary effectively highlights the psychological barrier preventing graduates from pivoting to skilled trades. PolyMatter notes that for a young adult who has spent years preparing for college, "taking up skilled Blue Collar work would mean accepting that the four to six years they spent laboring away at College were wasted." This emotional weight is often overlooked in economic analyses, yet it is the primary reason why "it's far easier for these young adults to accept low paid low skill work" than to retrain as a plumber or electrician.

The Illusion of the Wage Premium

Finally, the author dismantles the most common defense of the current system: the college wage premium graph. PolyMatter points out that while the graph shows a massive lifetime earnings gap between graduates and high school dropouts, it relies on two flawed assumptions. First, it ignores the cost of attendance. Second, and more critically, it assumes everyone graduates. PolyMatter writes, "about half of college students don't just 41% earn their bachelor's degree in 4 years only 59% doe in 6 years." The author emphasizes that "financially College really only becomes valuable the day you graduate," meaning that for the millions who leave with debt but no degree, the investment is a net loss.

This section is the piece's strongest critique of the data used to sell the idea of college for all. The author argues that the "shiny promotional brochure facade" of mass higher education hides the reality that for many, the path to the middle class has been blocked by the very system designed to open it. The piece concludes that while college serves a valuable social function, the "ugly side to this Mass Market popularization" is that it has created a generation of underemployed graduates and a labor market starved of essential skills.

College was no longer One path it was the path it wasn't a solution it was the solution.

Bottom Line

PolyMatter's strongest argument lies in its ability to connect historical policy decisions with the current psychological paralysis of young workers, revealing that the labor market is broken not by a lack of talent, but by a misalignment of incentives. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its relative silence on the specific policy mechanisms needed to reverse the stigma of trade schools, though it clearly identifies the cultural roots of the problem. Readers should watch for how this narrative shifts as the next generation of workers, burdened by debt and disillusionment, begins to reject the "college for all" mandate in favor of tangible skills.
