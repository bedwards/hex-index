Green Hydrogen : Can Australia lead the world?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·May 24, 2020 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through the green hype to ask a question most industry cheerleaders avoid: can Australia actually lead the global hydrogen race without becoming a haven for fossil fuel loopholes? While the narrative often focuses on the sheer abundance of Australian sun and wind, Borlace brings a necessary skepticism to the table, scrutinizing the carbon accounting methods that could allow dirty energy to masquerade as clean. This is not just a story about export potential; it is a critical examination of whether the world's largest fossil fuel exporter can genuinely pivot to a sustainable future or if it is merely polishing the same old coal with a new label.

The Promise of the Sun and Wind

Borlace anchors his optimism in the dramatic drop in renewable costs and Australia's geographic luck. He notes that the drivers behind the current surge in interest include "a 100 fold reduction in the price of solar electricity in the past four decades coupled with Japan's commitment to be a long-term large-scale customer." This framing is effective because it moves the conversation away from theoretical science to hard economics and geopolitical necessity. Japan, importing 94 percent of its energy as fossil fuels, needs a partner, and Australia has the resources to fill that void.

The author highlights the potential for regional economic revival, citing a report that suggests a new hydrogen industry could "provide a much needed lifeline to many regional communities" by locating facilities near remote solar farms. This is a compelling argument for domestic policy, turning a global energy transition into a local jobs engine. However, the scale of the ambition is staggering. Borlace points out that the CSIRO expects demand from Asian nations to reach 3.8 million tons by 2030, with Australia potentially meeting 10 to 20 percent of that need.

"If Australia can tap into our abundant wind and solar resources to produce hydrogen using renewable energy we could export hydrogen at large scale."

Critics might note that relying on a single market like Japan creates significant vulnerability; if Japan's demand shifts or if other competitors like Saudi Arabia undercut prices, the entire economic model could wobble. Borlace acknowledges the global race but perhaps underestimates how quickly geopolitical alliances can shift in a volatile energy market.

The Water Problem and the CCS Loophole

The commentary takes a sharper turn when addressing the hidden costs of green hydrogen. Borlace rightly identifies that electrolysis requires "enormous quantities of purified water to work properly," a critical issue in a continent already plagued by drought. He points to emerging innovations, such as Stanford University's work on overcoming anode corrosion in salt water, as a potential game-changer that could allow the industry to draw directly from the oceans.

Yet, the most contentious part of Borlace's analysis is his examination of "blue hydrogen"—hydrogen produced from fossil fuels with carbon capture and storage (CCS). He quotes Dr. Alan Finkel, Australia's chief scientist, who claims that with best-practice CCS, emissions are "very small less than naught point eight kilograms of carbon dioxide emitted for every kilogram of hydrogen produced." Borlace presents this as a viable bridge, noting that the high pressure of the gas mixture makes carbon extraction "more energy efficient."

However, the author does not shy away from the skepticism surrounding this technology. He highlights the danger that fossil fuel companies might use CCS as a "potential loophole" to continue business as usual. The reality in the United States, he notes, is that many CCS projects are economically viable only because they are used for "enhanced oil recovery," where the captured CO2 is injected underground to squeeze out more oil. This creates a grotesque paradox where a low-carbon technology actually fuels higher fossil fuel extraction.

"If creating that storage also creates secondary almost hidden carbon dioxide emissions then we'll have failed to move away from our collective delusion that we can stay on a business-as-usual trajectory and still have any hope of survival."

This is the piece's most vital warning. Borlace argues that without strict verification, the "certificate of origin scheme" Australia is developing could be gamed. The evidence suggests that while the technology exists, the economic incentives for fossil fuel giants to actually reduce emissions rather than maximize extraction remain a massive hurdle.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage succeeds by refusing to treat green hydrogen as a silver bullet, instead presenting it as a complex, high-stakes gamble that hinges on integrity and innovation. The strongest part of his argument is the exposure of the CCS loophole, which challenges the reader to look beyond the marketing of "clean" fuel to the actual carbon math. The biggest vulnerability remains the reliance on unproven large-scale carbon capture technology and the risk that economic pressures will prioritize profit over genuine decarbonization. Readers should watch closely to see if Australia's proposed certification schemes can withstand the pressure of a fossil fuel industry desperate to stay relevant.
