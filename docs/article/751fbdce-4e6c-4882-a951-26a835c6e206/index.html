It had always been my understanding that the movie No Country for Old Man was a pretty much perfect adaptation of the Cormack McCarthy novel. But then, not too long ago, I actually read the book and was surprised to see some key elements being significantly different. Now, of course, no adaptation is ever going to be 100% accurate, but we're talking about like major emissions here about significant plot elements and backstories that were completely removed from the movie. Despite really deepening my appreciation for the story when I encountered them in the book.

And yet, the strangest thing is that even after learning all this, I still wouldn't change anything about the movie. In fact, the whole experience has quietly challenged my conception of what the relation between an adaptation and its source can look like. But first, let's talk about these differences. The first has to do with a character who is entirely absent in the movie, and that is the young hitchhiker who Llewellyn picks up and travels with for a while.

This subplot begins after Luwellyn has called his wife Cara Jean to tell her he'll meet her at the motel in El Paso, which would be about 90 minutes into the movie, so pretty close to the end, which already gives you some idea as to why it was cut out. But anyways, he picks up this hitchhiking girl, mostly because he still has a long way to go and it would be easier with a second driver. The girl says she's 18, but Luwellen calls as she clearly is no older than 15 or 16. They make a couple of stops to grab a bite and Luwellyn learns that the girl is running off to California to make a fresh start and to escape from what seems like a troubled past.

Luwellyn, who has been on the run himself for some time now, sympathizes with her and wants to help her. He gives her some money and promises to drop her off at a bus stop once they reach El Paso.
