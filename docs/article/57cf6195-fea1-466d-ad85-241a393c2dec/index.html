Trump's "Revenge Tax" On Investors Outside the US

Richard Coffin · The Plain Bagel ·Jun 6, 2025 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Richard Coffin uncovers a legislative time bomb buried in a 10,000-page budget bill that could fundamentally alter the global investment landscape for non-US citizens. This isn't just a tax adjustment; it is a potential weaponization of the US market against nations the administration deems unfair. For the busy investor relying on US dividends, the stakes are immediate and severe.

The Anatomy of a Punitive Levy

Coffin cuts through the noise of the "big beautiful bill" to focus on Section 899, a provision titled "Enforcement of Remedies against unfair foreign taxes." He writes, "A new levy that if approved and utilized could end up costing Canadian and other foreign investors up to 20% or potentially even more of the income they generate from their US investments." This is the crux of the issue: a penalty that scales. The proposal starts at a 5% withholding tax and increases by 5 percentage points annually until it hits a cap of 20%. Coffin notes that this would kick in as early as January 1, 2026, depending on the legislative timeline.

The author's analysis is particularly sharp when he contextualizes this against existing tax treaties. Currently, countries like Canada benefit from reduced withholding rates, often dropping from a standard 30% to 15%. Coffin points out the danger of the new law overriding these agreements: "This new proposed section for the US tax law 899 would look to add a new withholding tax that would not only be on top of current taxes, but may even be applied where withholding taxes aren't currently being charged." The result? A foreign investor could face a combined tax burden of 50% on dividend income. This framing is effective because it moves the discussion from abstract policy to concrete portfolio erosion.

Critics might argue that the US has the sovereign right to set its own tax rates regardless of treaties, but the economic blowback of such a move is rarely discussed in Washington. Coffin highlights this by noting that the legislation is written to "circumvent certain treaty arrangements that currently reduce or eliminate withholding tax," creating a legal gray area that could strip protections from even tax-advantaged accounts like Canada's RRSP.

"Foreign investors could start facing a withholding tax of up to 50% of any income generated from their US investments."

Beyond the Individual: A Systemic Shock

The scope of Coffin's coverage extends far beyond the individual retail investor, which is where the piece gains its true gravity. He explains that the definition of "applicable persons" is broad, encompassing "governments, corporations, foundations, trusts, and partnerships that are considered foreignowned or controlled." This means sovereign wealth funds, pension plans, and mutual funds are all on the chopping block. Coffin illustrates the ripple effect with a sobering example: "In Canada, for example, the Canadian Pension Plan or CPP is a retirement income program currently exempt from any US withholding taxes... However, with the change explicitly stripping away the section 892A withholding tax exemption for discriminatory foreign countries, it appears that the CPP could soon see withholding taxes on its US-based income returns."

This is a crucial insight. By targeting the CPP and Norway's sovereign wealth fund, the US risks taxing the very mechanisms that fund social safety nets abroad. Coffin argues that the rationale for this tax is the US administration's belief that countries like Canada, France, and the UK are enacting "unfair taxes" against American tech giants, such as digital services taxes. He writes, "The primary point of contention here again is that it's primarily US firms that are going to be paying these newly introduced taxes." While the US frames this as a defensive measure, Coffin suggests it is likely a negotiating tactic: "It seems pretty clear that this new section... is being used as a negotiation tactic, whether that be to try and ultimately get countries to walk back the digital service tax or UTPRs or to provide the administration another bargaining chip in ongoing trade negotiations."

A counterargument worth considering is that these foreign taxes are necessary to modernize a global system where digital giants escape source-based taxation. Coffin acknowledges this nuance, noting that proponents argue these taxes "help to modernize tax programs to prevent the exploitation of varying tax programs around the world by large multinationals." However, his coverage effectively demonstrates that the US response is disproportionate, threatening to destabilize global capital flows rather than just correcting a tax imbalance.

The Economic Self-Harm

Perhaps the most compelling part of Coffin's argument is the admission that this policy could backfire on the United States itself. He cites the Joint Committee on Taxation's estimate that Section 899 could generate $116 billion in revenue over a decade, but immediately undercuts that figure by highlighting the behavioral response of investors. "An investor who is earning a 6% income return from a US investment who is hit with this fee would see their net return drop to 4.8%... which would make that US investment less competitive to other options," Coffin writes.

This is the piece's strongest logical pivot. By making US assets less attractive, the US risks reducing the foreign capital that currently funds its deficits. Coffin warns, "Something that would hurt the US government's ability to raise capital, likely increasing Treasury yields further and possibly even impacting the US dollar, which would see less foreign demand if these taxes ended up broadly targeting other countries." The irony is palpable: a tax designed to punish foreign nations could end up punishing the US economy by raising the cost of borrowing. Coffin's clean-up of the transcript's speech artifacts allows this economic logic to shine without the distraction of conversational filler.

Bottom Line

Richard Coffin's coverage is a masterclass in finding the signal in the noise, transforming a dry budget provision into a high-stakes geopolitical warning. The argument's greatest strength is its demonstration of how a trade negotiation tactic could inadvertently cripple the retirement savings of millions and raise the US cost of capital. The biggest vulnerability remains the uncertainty of implementation; if this is purely a bluff, the market panic may be premature, but if real, the damage is already done.

"An additional 20% fee on investment income is going to discourage US activity."

Readers should watch the Senate's reaction to Section 899 closely; if it passes without modification, the era of frictionless global investing may be over.
