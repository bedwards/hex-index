How to Get Involved in City Planning — City Beautiful Basics

Dave Amos · City Beautiful ·Nov 5, 2025 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Amos doesn't just tell you to care about your city; he hands you a literal ladder, rung by rung, to climb from passive observer to active shaper of your community. While most urbanist content stops at the frustration of bad design, Amos offers a distinct, actionable roadmap that validates the introvert's hesitation while challenging the activist's impatience. He argues that the gap between watching a video and changing a policy isn't a canyon—it's a series of manageable steps, starting with something as simple as signing a guest book.

The Ladder of Engagement

Amos structures his argument around a progression of effort, acknowledging that not everyone can or wants to run for office immediately. He begins by dismantling the assumption that local politics is too boring or complex to matter. "We tend to get really caught up in the national political discourse, but local elections are super important," he writes. This reframing is crucial; it shifts the reader's focus from the paralyzing scale of federal gridlock to the tangible impact of a city council vote. The author's insistence that readers "take the time to read their policy positions" and even "reach out with questions" transforms the voter from a consumer of platforms into a constituent with agency.

The second rung involves physical presence. Amos notes that local governments often host workshops where the setup varies, but they "almost always have a way for you to express your viewpoint." He suggests these meetings can be "fun and a great way to shake off some of your cynicism." This is a bold claim for anyone who has sat through a dry zoning hearing, yet Amos backs it by emphasizing the human element: "You can see other people that care about this stuff as much as you do. You might even make a new friend." Critics might argue that not all public meetings are welcoming or productive, and that cynicism is a rational response to bureaucratic inertia. However, Amos's point stands that isolation fuels cynicism, while community engagement often reveals a shared desire for improvement.

"I mean, you can do those things [leave spicy Reddit comments and create memes], but in this video, I'm talking about the tangible real world things you can do to have a more measurable impact on your community."

From Attendance to Authority

As the ladder ascends, the commitment deepens. Amos suggests joining advocacy organizations, noting that "there's only one way to find out, and that's to join." This moves the reader from individual action to collective power. He then introduces the concept of advisory bodies, a mechanism often overlooked by the public. "This is a great way to get involved in local government without switching careers or running for office," he explains. By sharing his own experience joining a transportation committee, Amos demystifies the process. He describes the application as a standard vacancy posting, followed by an interview, making the path to influence feel accessible rather than reserved for the politically connected.

The author is refreshingly honest about the difficulty of the highest rungs. Regarding starting a new organization, he admits, "We're getting pretty far up the ladder here, so I know this all starting a little bit scary." Yet, he provides a counter-narrative of success, citing a student who started a local chapter and became a "mover and shaker." When discussing running for office, Amos clarifies the nuances of different government structures, explaining that in a "council manager form of government," the mayor often has very little extra power compared to a typical council member. This distinction is vital for readers trying to understand where the actual leverage lies in their specific municipality.

The Introvert's Path and Historical Context

Amos wisely pauses to address the barriers for those who prefer not to be in the spotlight. He acknowledges that for introverts, the idea of "having to leave your house in the evenings and meet with people... may not sound appealing." He offers a viable alternative: digital and written engagement. "I know that whenever I have an upcoming active transportation committee meeting, I get an agenda packet... community members can email their comments on the packet," he writes. He validates this method by revealing his own workflow: "I then receive those emails and read them before the meeting. I find the emails to be really helpful." This admission from a committee member that written feedback is genuinely read and valued is a powerful incentive for the quiet citizen.

The piece culminates in a necessary historical grounding. Amos reminds readers that the current openness to public input is a hard-won victory, not a default state. He details the devastation of "Urban Renewal," a policy under the Housing Act of 1949 where "little to no outreach done with the people who lived in those neighborhoods" led to the erasure of communities. He connects this past failure to the present imperative: "The general public has not always had a voice in city planning, and we should take advantage of all the rights and responsibilities we have today." This historical context elevates the piece from a simple "how-to" guide to a civic duty. It suggests that participation is not just about better parks or bike lanes, but about correcting a legacy of exclusion.

"Cities are better when there are more involved citizens, and they're at their best when the involved citizens mirror the views and demographics of the city as a whole."

Bottom Line

Dave Amos's strongest asset is his ability to normalize civic engagement, making it feel like a series of small, logical choices rather than a heroic leap. The piece's only vulnerability is the assumption that local government structures are equally accessible in every jurisdiction, as some cities may have more opaque or hostile processes than the ones Amos describes. However, the core message remains unassailable: the gap between a video and a policy change is bridged not by grand gestures, but by showing up, signing in, and speaking up.

Transcript excerpt on City Beautiful

I like to think that watching YouTube videos about cities and city planning is just the first step towards making positive changes in your community. Good YouTube videos can give you an introduction to a topic and provide you with the information you need to form your own conclusions about policies, urban design, and more. YouTube videos are a lot like going to one of those cool science museums. You don't become an expert in physics overnight, but you learn a little and are maybe inspired to learn and do more.

This video is all about that more. What is there beyond watching a YouTube video? And no, my answer is not leaving spicy Reddit comments and creating memes. you can do those things, but in this video, I'm talking about the tangible real world things you can do to have a more measurable impact on your community.

I've organized this like a list, starting with the easiest and ending with the most time and effort. Everyone has a different stopping point on this ladder up, and that's okay. I'll even tell you where I'm at now, and it's not at the top. Let's climb the ladder after the bike bill.

>> >> Step one, vote in local elections. I consider this one to be the easiest because I'm assuming all of you are already voting, right? We tend to get really caught up in the national political discourse, but local elections are super important. Please do not skip voting for city council, county board of supervisors, and other local offices.

Take the time to read their policy positions. You can even reach out with questions. You may get a staff person response if it's a bigger city, but it's not a bad idea to ask about their positions on housing, ask for transportation, or whatever your issue is, particularly if they haven't outlined it in their policy positions. Figure out where they stand and vote accordingly.

Step two, attend a meeting. This is going to be a theme, but you really need to show up if you want your voice to be heard. Local governments often host workshops or meetings about planning issues. Maybe your city is doing an update of their comprehensive plan or refreshing their zoning code or redesigning a corridor to be safer for bikes and pedestrians.
