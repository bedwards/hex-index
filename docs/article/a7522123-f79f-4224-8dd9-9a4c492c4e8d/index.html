Who Were the Last Pagans of Europe?

Andrew Henry · Religion For Breakfast ·Mar 6, 2026 · 28 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Andrew Henry dismantles a comfortable historical myth: that Europe's conversion to Christianity was a clean, medieval event. Instead, he reveals a messy, centuries-long reality where pre-Christian animism persisted deep into the modern era, surviving alongside, and often within, Christian structures. This isn't just a history lesson; it's a correction of how we understand the very concept of religious change.

The Myth of the Clean Break

Henry opens with a jarring contradiction: while the Enlightenment reshaped Paris and Washington took office in 1789, Estonian peasants were still fencing off sacred groves and sacrificing black roosters to an earth goddess. "While revolution was sweeping through Europe and the Americas, some Estonian villagers were still honoring pre-Christian gods with live animal sacrifice," Henry writes. This specific, dated evidence shatters the assumption that paganism vanished with the Middle Ages.

The core of Henry's argument rests on the idea that Christianization was not a wave but a stalled process. He cites historian Francis Young, noting that "Christianization did not sweep across Europe like an unstoppable wave. In some regions, Christianization just stalled." This framing is crucial because it shifts the focus from top-down edicts to the stubborn resilience of local practice. It forces us to recognize that for many, the official religion was merely a veneer over a deeper, older worldview.

"Christianization is often presented as a series of decisive conversion moments... But for this topic, the classic example is the Christianization of Lithuania in 1387."

Henry uses the 1387 conversion of Lithuania as the perfect counter-example to the "decisive moment" narrative. While the Grand Duke accepted baptism, the reality on the ground was far different. As Henry notes, "Official adoption often blanketed over deep continuities in ritual life that persisted for generations." This parallels the Christianization of Lithuania's deep dive, where records show locals refusing Christian burials for generations after the state's conversion. The political shift was instant; the spiritual shift took centuries.

Redefining the Believers

The article tackles the semantic minefield of labeling these groups "pagans." Henry argues that the term is too vague, lumping together radically different traditions under a single negative label. "You worship Zeus, pagan. You offer milk to a sacred rock in 17th century Estonia, also pagan. But these two examples have basically nothing in common except that they're not Christian," he writes. This distinction is vital for intellectual honesty.

Instead, Henry leans into the concept of animism, redefining it not as primitive superstition but as a relational worldview. He quotes scholar Graham Harvey: "Animism is a worldview in which the world is full of persons, only some of whom are human." This reframing elevates the practices of the Sami and Estonians from "survivals" to coherent systems of meaning. It suggests that for these communities, the sacred wasn't confined to a church building but was woven into the landscape itself.

Critics might argue that applying the term "animism" to 17th-century Europeans risks romanticizing or over-systematizing what were likely fragmented, localized folk practices. However, Henry's reliance on Young's alternative term, "unchristianized peoples," offers a more neutral, descriptive path that focuses on what was missing (deep Christian integration) rather than what was present (a monolithic "pagan" religion).

The Creole of Religion

Perhaps the most sophisticated part of Henry's coverage is his application of "Creoleization" to religious history. He explains that what we often call "pagan survivals" might actually be new, hybrid forms born of contact. "Apparently, so-called pre-Christian beliefs and rights were sometimes a response to Christianity rather than something predating its introduction," Henry writes. This challenges the idea of a pure, untouched past.

He illustrates this with the story of the Latvian goddess Decla, who may have been a paganized version of a Christian saint, and the "Blue deity" honored on Christmas Eve. The inkeeper lighting four fires and offering bread wasn't necessarily preserving an ancient ritual; he was likely engaging in a "Creoleized religious practice" where Christian and pre-Christian elements fused. Henry points out that the famous Yule log tradition, often cited as pagan, has roots in German Christmas customs, suggesting that "rather than preserving a purely pre-Christian Baltic ritual... it is perhaps better understood as the wooden equivalent of a goose or turkey."

"Religions can do this too... Over generations, this produced religions like Haitian vodu and Santia."

By comparing these European practices to Haitian Vodou, Henry provides a powerful framework for understanding religious syncretism. Just as enslaved Africans blended West African deities with Catholic saints to create a distinct new religion, the peoples of the Baltic and Fennoscandia blended their animist roots with Christian forms to create something entirely new. This moves the narrative from "survival of the old" to "adaptation of the new."

Bottom Line

Andrew Henry's most compelling contribution is his rejection of the "decisive conversion" model, replacing it with a nuanced view of slow, uneven, and hybridized religious change. The argument's greatest strength lies in its use of "Creoleization" to explain the strange mix of Christian and pre-Christian practices found in 17th and 18th-century records. However, the piece occasionally risks over-correcting by downplaying the genuine continuity of pre-Christian elements, suggesting that almost every "pagan" practice was actually a Christian invention or adaptation. The truth likely lies in the messy middle Henry describes: a world where old gods and new saints danced together in the same sacred groves.
