Plastic Pollution : What are the sustainable alternatives?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Jan 5, 2020 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace doesn't just list eco-friendly swaps; he exposes a fundamental biological mismatch that has turned our most useful invention into an ecological nightmare. By tracing the molecular architecture of plastic back to its carbon-carbon bonds, he reveals why nature simply cannot digest what we have created, turning a material science lesson into an urgent call for a new industrial paradigm.

The Molecular Trap

Borlace begins by acknowledging the sheer utility of the material before dismantling its longevity. He notes that while plastic is "almost as vital to modern human survival as the food we eat and the air we breathe," this dependency is precisely what makes its persistence so catastrophic. The core of his argument rests on a chemical distinction: nature prefers nitrogen-to-carbon bonds found in proteins, which organisms have evolved to break down for fuel. In contrast, man-made plastics rely on "extremely strong carbon to carbon bonds" that no natural enzyme has ever been programmed to dismantle.

This framing is brilliant because it shifts the blame from human carelessness to a structural impossibility. We aren't just littering; we are introducing a substance that exists outside the planet's metabolic cycle. Borlace writes, "Nature does make polymer like materials they call peptides... but those organisms have never had to decompose carbon to carbon bonded polymers so they've got no metabolic way of doing it." This explains why, despite our best intentions, the 9% of plastic that gets recycled is the exception, not the rule. The vast majority of the eight billion metric tons produced since the mid-20th century remains a permanent fixture in our ecosystems.

Critics might argue that focusing on the chemical bond ignores the economic reality that breaking down these bonds would be energy-intensive, making recycling inherently difficult regardless of biology. However, Borlace's point stands: the solution isn't better recycling, but entirely different materials.

From Wood Pulp to Seaweed Bubbles

The commentary then pivots from the problem to a surprisingly diverse array of solutions, moving beyond the usual "use less" rhetoric to showcase actual industrial replacements. Borlace highlights "liquid wood," a biopolymer made from lignin, a byproduct of paper mills. He explains that this material is "strong, versatile, and non-toxic," and crucially, it can be recycled as wood, closing the loop in a way petroleum plastics never can.

He also spotlights innovations that turn waste into resources, such as a food wrapper derived from soybean pulp residue. "Using a fermentation process to liberate the cellulose from the residue, the team has created an alternative to plastic food wrap that utilizes otherwise discarded food waste," Borlace notes. This approach is compelling because it solves two problems at once: waste management and material production. Similarly, he discusses Poly Lactic Acid (PLA), derived from corn and wheat starch. While he admits PLA won't degrade in a standard landfill, he emphasizes that in industrial composting, it "completely degrades in two to three months." This distinction is vital; it suggests that the failure of current bioplastics often lies in our infrastructure, not the materials themselves.

"You can eat and drink the whole thing which means you will personally be doing the biodegrading if you get my drift."

Perhaps the most striking example Borlace offers is the edible water bubble made from seaweed. Described as a "double membrane made from sodium alginate and calcium chloride," it eliminates the container entirely. While this sounds like science fiction, Borlace presents it as a logical endpoint of the search for sustainable alternatives. A counterargument worth considering is whether these niche solutions can scale to meet the demands of global logistics, but the sheer creativity of the examples proves that the technological ceiling is far higher than we assume.

The Bamboo Paradox and the Cassava Revolution

Borlace does not shy away from the complexities of green alternatives. When discussing bamboo, he praises its rapid growth and soil-restoring properties but issues a necessary warning about the chemicals used to process it into textiles. "Some pretty nasty chemicals including sulfur and sodium hydroxide have to be used to extract the cellulose fibers," he writes, noting that without careful control, these processes can harm local ecosystems. This nuance prevents the piece from becoming a naive celebration of "natural" materials and acknowledges that sustainability is a spectrum, not a binary switch.

The most impactful section focuses on packaging, specifically the work of Kevin Kamala in Indonesia. Kamala developed a bag from cassava starch that dissolves instantly in hot water. Borlace describes the material as "100% bio-based" and capable of breaking down in months, even in the ocean. This is a direct response to the "one trillion plastic bags a consumed worldwide" statistic, offering a tangible solution that addresses the specific pain point of marine pollution.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest asset is his ability to explain complex polymer chemistry in plain language, revealing that the plastic crisis is a biological incompatibility rather than just a waste management failure. His biggest vulnerability is the reliance on industrial composting infrastructure for materials like PLA, which remains scarce in many parts of the world. Readers should watch for the scaling of these bio-based alternatives, as the technology is ready, but the supply chains are not yet built.
