China's AI Education Experiment

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Apr 3, 2026 · 26 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider doesn't just report on China's new AI education push; he exposes the stark disconnect between Beijing's techno-optimism and the gritty reality of rural classrooms. While the Ministry of Education proclaims a "post-equity era," Schneider argues that the state is actually deploying surveillance and automation to manage a system that remains fundamentally unequal. For busy listeners tracking the future of work and governance, this is essential context: China isn't just building smarter schools; it is building a digital panopticon to keep a restless population hopeful.

The Illusion of Equity

Schneider opens by dismantling the official narrative that China has solved its access problems. He writes, "In official discourse, China is said to have entered a 'post-equity era' since the MOE announced that all counties had met the baseline quality level for compulsory schooling in 2021." This claim is immediately undercut by the data he presents. The government has shifted focus from building schools to "improving the quality of that education," but the infrastructure gap remains a chasm.

The author's use of comparative data is particularly effective here. He notes that according to the 2020 census, "only 30.6% of the population has ever attended high school... which Stanford professor Scott Rozelle notes, 'is lower than South Africa, lower than Turkey and lower than Mexico.'" This statistic is a gut punch that reframes the entire AI initiative. It suggests that the push for "intelligent" education is not about catching up to global leaders, but about managing a massive structural deficit.

"Every locality, he explained, has designated 'elite' schools that talented students from surrounding areas compete to transfer into. The result is a system where 'educational resources are systematically sucked up to the center from the periphery, leaving rural areas incredibly depleted.'"

Schneider's argument here is that AI is being used as a political tool rather than a pedagogical one. The state knows it cannot give every rural child a path to an elite university, so it uses shiny new technology to create the feeling of progress. As Schneider puts it, "Infrastructure is pretty much the perfect tool for this. It makes schools feel luxurious on the ground without changing the fundamentals that make the system so unfair." This is a cynical but compelling read of the Party's incentives. Critics might argue that any investment in rural tech is better than nothing, but Schneider rightly points out that hardware cannot fix the "zero-sum system" where one student's gain is another's loss.

The Human Cost of Automation

The piece shifts to the practical application of AI, revealing a system designed to manage teacher shortages and student behavior. Schneider highlights the "super-size" classrooms where 56+ students are the norm, noting that "Beijing's most recent initiative to tackle this problem is the 'County-managed, school-hired' system... Teachers, unsurprisingly, seem to resent this system." The government hopes AI can fill the void left by burnt-out or poached staff, but the human element is being stripped away.

The most controversial aspect of Schneider's coverage is the discussion of biometric monitoring. He details how schools in Wuhan and Beijing are using AI to "monitor and analyze students' emotional states" and track facial expressions to gauge attention. He writes, "There's quite a bit of propagandizing going on in these descriptions. The hardware on the ground hasn't yet reached panopticon levels, but the fact that the white paper posits this kind of surveillance as a desired outcome is telling."

This is where the article's tension peaks. The state frames this as "early intervention" for mental health, but the underlying logic is control. Schneider connects this to the broader pressure of the Gaokao, the national college entrance exam. Just as the Gaokao determines a student's entire future trajectory, these AI tools are designed to optimize performance for that single, high-stakes moment. The author notes that "rural areas already lag behind developed areas in educational infrastructure and philosophy by more than 10 years," yet the solution offered is a high-tech surveillance state that demands "obedient" students.

"People who have gone to university won't work in factories... No matter how popular it would be, Beijing is not interested in building a system where a college education is available to anyone who wants one."

Schneider's insight here is that the AI experiment is a pressure valve. With youth unemployment hovering around 20% and a record number of graduates entering the workforce, the state cannot promise college degrees to everyone. Instead, they promise "hope." As anthropologist Andrew Kipnis is quoted saying, educational reform is a priority "because it's a way of keeping people happy. If they think there's some hope their child will attend university, that gives them some investment in the system."

The Bottom Line

Schneider's strongest contribution is his refusal to treat China's AI education push as a simple story of technological advancement; he frames it as a sophisticated political strategy to maintain legitimacy in a zero-sum game. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that the "shiny new facilities" will fail to deliver real mobility, a prediction that history shows can sometimes be upended by unexpected social shifts. However, the evidence of deep structural inequality suggests that without addressing the hukou system and university seat allocation, no amount of artificial intelligence will bridge the gap between the city and the countryside.

Excerpt from the original article on ChinaTalk

Pilot schools in China are already using AI to grade children’s artwork, monitor their facial expressions during lectures, and screen them for psychological problems — and the Ministry of Education (MOE) wants schools across the country to follow suit.1

Integrating AI into the education system has rapidly become a top priority of the Chinese central government, which is betting that AI tools can eliminate China’s vast educational inequities and make the next generation of workers more productive. The State Council highlighted education as a key area of focus in the “AI+” plan, it received a shout-out in the 15th Five-Year Plan, and in Summer 2025, the Ministry of Education (MOE) released a white paper on AI for education. This MOE document proclaims that 2025 marked the beginning of a system-wide effort to “intelligentize” 智能化 education using AI tools. But the gap between Beijing’s techno-optimism and rural China’s reality is enormous.

This report explores why the Party wants to integrate AI into education, what applications the MOE is most optimistic about, and where the barriers to successful rollout lie. We’ll limit our analysis to K-12 education today, but university AI initiatives will be the focus of our next report in this series!

Institutional History

In official discourse, China is said to have entered a “post-equity era” 后均衡时代 since the MOE announced that all counties had met the baseline quality level for compulsory schooling in 2021. Now, the focus is shifting from access to education to improving the quality of that education. The 14th 5-year plan (2021-2025) prioritized expanding infrastructure in rural schools through the “county-level high school revitalization initiative” (县中振兴), part of which involved equipping classrooms with ‘smart hardware’ such as digitized blackboards. During this period, the party spent significant resources to provide nearly every school with an internet connection.
