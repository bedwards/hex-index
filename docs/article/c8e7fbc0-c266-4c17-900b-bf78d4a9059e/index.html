Are EVs really better for the climate?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Aug 14, 2022 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In an era of climate anxiety and conflicting headlines, Dave Borlace cuts through the noise with a forensic analysis of vehicle emissions that challenges the most persistent myths about electric cars. Rather than relying on idealized test conditions, he leans on a rigorous 2021 report from the International Council on Clean Transportation (ICCT) to prove that even on today's dirtiest power grids, electric vehicles (EVs) are already superior to gas cars. This is not a theoretical plea for the future; it is a data-driven verdict on the present.

The Data That Silences the Skeptics

Borlace anchors his argument in the sheer scale of the problem, noting that "Global Transport emissions account for around 12 billion metric tons of CO2 equivalent going into the atmosphere each year." He correctly identifies that without a massive shift, these numbers are set to nearly double by 2050, making the choice of vehicle technology a matter of survival rather than preference. The author's strength lies in his refusal to ignore the manufacturing phase of EVs, a common talking point for critics. He acknowledges the "energy intensive nature of lithium ion battery production" but swiftly counters it with the reality of operational efficiency.

The ICCT data he presents is damning for internal combustion engines. Borlace writes, "In Europe they're 66 to 69% lower [in emissions] and in the states the difference is 60 to 80%." Even in coal-heavy markets like India and China, the gap remains significant, with EVs producing 19% to 45% fewer emissions over their lifetimes. This finding is crucial because it dismantles the "two wrongs make a right" argument often used to defend fossil fuel vehicles in developing nations. Borlace points out that as grids decarbonize, "the gulf between EVs and combustion engines will only get wider and wider."

Critics might argue that the study overlooks the full supply chain, including factory construction and road building. However, Borlace notes that the ICCT intentionally excluded these factors because they are "pretty similar whichever type of vehicle you use," meaning they do not skew the comparative analysis. This methodological choice strengthens the piece by focusing strictly on the variables that actually differ between a gas car and an EV.

Only battery electric and hydrogen fuel cell electric vehicles have the potential to achieve the reductions in life cycle emissions needed to meet the Paris agreement goals.

The Dead End of Hybrid Compromise

Perhaps the most valuable contribution of this piece is its definitive rejection of hybrids as a long-term solution. While many policymakers and consumers view hybrids as a sensible middle ground, Borlace presents evidence that they are a distraction. He explains that "self-charging hybrid vehicles only get to an average global reduction of about 20% over internal combustion engine vehicles at best." Even plug-in hybrids, which are often marketed as green alternatives, show a meager reduction of just 6% to 12% in China and roughly 25% in Europe.

The author's analysis of biofuels is equally sobering. He argues that "it's just not feasible to supply enough low carbon biofuels from residues and waste-based biodiesel ethanol or biomethane to substantially displace fossil fuels." This is a vital correction to the narrative that we can simply blend our way out of climate change. The high cost and limited supply of these fuels mean they cannot support the massive fleet of cars on the road today, let alone the growing number of vehicles in the future.

Borlace's conclusion is stark: "The registration of new combustion engine Vehicles needs to be phased out in the 20130 to 2035 time frame." He emphasizes that only a full transition to battery electric or green hydrogen fuel cell vehicles will align with the Paris Agreement. This framing is effective because it moves the debate from "are EVs perfect?" to "are there any other viable options?" The answer, according to the data he cites, is a resounding no.

Efficiency and the Break-Even Point

A recurring theme in climate denial is the idea that the emissions from making an EV battery take decades to pay off. Borlace dismantles this by comparing the fundamental physics of the machines. He notes that "Electric vehicle Motors are about 80% efficient," whereas combustion engines are "still only about 30% efficient at best." This massive difference in energy conversion means that even with a dirty grid, the EV wins.

He provides a concrete metric for the skeptical driver: "An average European EV would pay back after 12,000 Mi and in the USA it would take about 125,000 mil." This translates to roughly a year and a half of driving for the average motorist, a timeframe that renders the "manufacturing emissions" argument largely moot. The author also highlights the often-forgotten upstream emissions of fossil fuels, noting that for petrol and diesel, "those Upstream emissions add about 20% to the emissions of fuel combustion itself." By including the cost of refining and transporting oil, the comparison becomes even more lopsided in favor of electrification.

Beyond the Car: The Bigger Picture

While the bulk of the commentary focuses on cars, Borlace wisely broadens the scope to include all forms of transport. He cites data showing that "The worst offenders are short hall flights and driving alone with no passengers." This contextualizes the EV debate, reminding readers that while electrifying cars is necessary, it is not a silver bullet for all travel habits.

He suggests that for many, the most impactful change is not buying a new EV, but changing the mode of transport entirely. "Maybe think about ditching the Carl together and getting yourself a nice shiny new two-wheeler," he suggests, linking environmental impact to personal health. This shift in perspective prevents the piece from feeling like a sales pitch for the automotive industry and instead frames it as a guide to rational, low-carbon living.

Critics might note that the push for EVs assumes a rapid expansion of charging infrastructure and renewable energy generation that may not happen in all regions. Borlace acknowledges the current limitations of coal grids but emphasizes the trajectory: "as electricity grids continue to decarbonize rapidly through the rest of this decade." The argument relies on the reasonable expectation that policy will follow the scientific consensus, a gamble that is currently the only realistic path forward.

If you're looking for the answer to the question I posed at the start of the video which is what can I do to make a difference maybe think about ditching the Carl together and getting yourself a nice shiny new two-wheeler.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage is a masterclass in using data to cut through emotional noise, effectively proving that the debate over EVs is no longer about "if" they are better, but "how much" better they are compared to the alternatives. The strongest part of the argument is the rigorous lifecycle analysis that exposes the futility of hybrids and biofuels, while its biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that global power grids will decarbonize fast enough to maximize EV benefits. Readers should watch for the next wave of data on grid mix changes, as that will be the final nail in the coffin for the internal combustion engine.
