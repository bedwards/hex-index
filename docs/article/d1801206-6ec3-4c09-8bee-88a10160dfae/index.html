ALL of the Laws Broken in Christmas Vacation

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Dec 17, 2020 · 18 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just dissect a holiday classic; he dismantles the very premise that Clark Griswold is a lovable protagonist. By applying a strict legal lens to National Lampoon's Christmas Vacation, Stone argues that the film's hero is actually a "crime boss in the making" whose festive chaos constitutes a felony spree. This isn't a nostalgic look back at a favorite movie; it is a forensic audit of a man who, according to Stone, should be serving decades in prison rather than hosting a family reunion.

The Architecture of Chaos

Stone begins by reframing the neighborhood conflict not as a comedic rivalry but as a series of escalating criminal acts. He notes that while viewers empathize with Clark's eccentric relatives, they conveniently ignore that Clark "has absolutely no regard for the law whatsoever." The author points out that the film's slapstick violence quickly transitions into genuine property crimes, specifically when a gutter is launched into a neighbor's window. Stone writes, "Clark's mishap with the gutter is the first blow against law and order," grounding the comedy in the reality of criminal damage statutes.

The commentary is particularly effective when Stone breaks down the specific Illinois laws violated. He explains that under state law, a person is guilty of criminal damage if they "perform any of the following acts without the consent of the property owner," including damaging property, recklessly using fire, or intentionally placing offensive compounds on another's land. Stone argues that by the movie's end, Clark has committed "basically all five" of these offenses. This detailed statutory analysis transforms a funny scene into a prosecutable case, forcing the reader to reconsider the consequences of the Griswold family's actions.

Clark Griswold may even be a crime boss in the making, and you thought your family holidays were bad.

Critics might argue that applying real-world statutes to a cartoonish movie undermines the genre's intent, but Stone's point is precisely that the movie asks us to overlook the absurdity of the crimes. He highlights that the penalties for these actions are not trivial; a broken window could be a misdemeanor, but as the damage exceeds $500, the charge escalates to a felony punishable by years in prison.

The Environmental and Financial Fallout

The analysis deepens as Stone moves from property damage to environmental hazards and financial fraud. The arrival of Cousin Eddie introduces a new layer of illegality, specifically the dumping of chemical toilet waste into a storm sewer. Stone dryly observes, "All that's missing from the top of this RV is a Trump 2020 flag," before pivoting to the serious legal implications of the act. He cites the Illinois Environmental Protection Act, noting that open dumping of waste is a Class A misdemeanor or a Class 4 felony.

Stone goes further to suggest that Clark could face vicarious liability for Eddie's actions, arguing that Clark "knew darn well what Eddie was doing to that sewer." This connects the environmental crime back to Clark's earlier offenses, specifically the charge of intentionally placing an offensive smelling compound to interfere with the neighborhood's use. The author then shifts to Clark's financial crimes, pointing out that writing a check without sufficient funds is not just a social faux pas but "financial institution fraud." Stone writes, "Writing a check when you know you don't have the funds to cover it is considered financial institution fraud," which carries a potential sentence of up to five years in jail.

The piece effectively uses the escalation of crimes to build a case against Clark's character. As Stone puts it, "By now Clark Griswold is basically the Tony Soprano of Winnebago County." This comparison is striking because it elevates the stakes from a neighborhood dispute to organized crime, suggesting that Clark's leadership of the kidnapping plot is a natural progression of his disregard for legal boundaries.

The Ultimate Felony

The climax of Stone's argument centers on the kidnapping of Clark's boss. When Clark suggests bringing the boss to the house for a beating, he inadvertently orchestrates a Class X felony. Stone explains that aggravated kidnapping includes taking a person with the intent to collect a ransom or inflict bodily harm. Since Clark's motive was to secure a bonus to cover a swimming pool deposit, Stone argues this could be interpreted as a ransom demand.

Stone details the severity of the potential sentence: "First offenses are punishable by a prison term of 6 to 30 years with an additional 15 to 25 years depending on the severity of the crime." He notes that Clark admits to being the mastermind, stating, "It was my fault I lost my temper... but this was no simple kidnapping." The author also touches on the illegal tree cutting, citing the Illinois Wrongful Tree Cutting Act which requires paying three times the stumpage value for unauthorized timber. For the massive tree Clark steals, Stone calculates a potential fine of "fifty thousand dollars."

A counterargument worth considering is that the movie is a fantasy where consequences are suspended for the sake of comedy. Stone acknowledges this by noting that Clark is "supposed to be the good guy," yet he insists that the legal reality cannot be ignored. The juxtaposition of the holiday spirit with the harsh reality of felony charges creates a unique tension that defines the piece.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's strongest asset is his ability to map specific, real-world statutes onto the chaotic events of the film, transforming a holiday comedy into a case study for criminal liability. The argument's vulnerability lies in its refusal to engage with the film's satirical nature, treating a caricature as a real defendant, yet this is precisely what makes the commentary so compelling. Readers should watch for how this legal deconstruction changes their perception of the movie's "hero," turning a beloved icon into a cautionary tale of unchecked entitlement.
