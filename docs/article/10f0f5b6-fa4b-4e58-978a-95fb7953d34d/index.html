The Unfulfilled Potential of Minecraft – Assuming a Different Perspective

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Oct 10, 2019 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden does something rare in gaming criticism: he stops asking how to beat the game and starts asking what the game is teaching us about our relationship with reality. By applying a sociological framework usually reserved for environmental policy to a blocky sandbox, he reveals that Minecraft's greatest flaw isn't a lack of content, but a silent, dangerous assumption that nature is indifferent to human action.

The Architecture of Indifference

Van der Linden begins not with a critique of gameplay loops, but with a personal confession of his own playstyle. He describes a player who builds harmoniously, avoiding the "conquest-oriented gameplay" that defines the game's endgame. "I pretty much spend my time improving my base and exploring the immediate world around me," he notes, highlighting a disconnect between the game's mechanics and a more contemplative way of interacting with the world. This sets the stage for his central thesis: every game is built on "assumptions about the world and its people that are reflected in the game's mechanics."

The author dissects these assumptions using "frame analysis," a tool from his background in environmental sociology. He breaks the game's worldview into three layers: the strategic (what you do), the normative (what is good or bad), and the ontological (what is real). In Minecraft, the strategic layer encourages gathering and building, but the normative layer is curiously empty. "Unlike some other games Minecraft does not have an explicit morality system," Van der Linden writes, pointing out that the game allows players to "kill kidnap and even breed [villagers] with impunity."

This lack of consequence is the crux of the problem. The game operates on an ontological assumption that "the world is ultimately indifferent to the player." You can "Thera form the entire landscape and the worlds will remain silent." This silence, Van der Linden argues, is not neutral; it reinforces a specific worldview. "It exists solely for the players manipulation," he asserts, comparing the game to a "virtual box of Lego's where the only moral restrictions are the ones players and boson themselves."

The world isn't just a collection of raw materials waiting to be assemble it already has some life to it on its own.

Critics might argue that adding complex ecological consequences would turn a relaxing sandbox into a tedious simulation, alienating the very audience that enjoys the freedom to build without restriction. Van der Linden anticipates this, clarifying that he isn't asking for "political propaganda" but rather a design that "uses clever game design to make you think about it for yourself."

From Neoliberal Exploitation to Ecological Interdependence

Van der Linden connects Minecraft's design philosophy to a specific historical moment: the post-World War II neoliberal frame. He suggests the game mirrors a worldview where "nature is unaffected by human activity" and can be treated purely as an instrument for resource extraction. "By assuming this frame the world is only as interesting as the amount of things that there are to exploit," he observes, noting that this leads to an "everlasting hunger for more items more rewards more treasures."

This is a bold claim, linking a popular children's game to the "neoliberal zeitgeist" that prioritizes expansion over sustainability. He contrasts this with the "environmentalist frame" that emerged in the 1960s and 70s, which posits that "nature is strongly connected and reactive to human activity." Van der Linden argues that if Minecraft adopted this frame, the game would transform from a resource sink into a complex system of cause and effect.

Imagine, he suggests, a world where "biomes no thriving or deteriorating based on the available amount of water." In this hypothetical version, players would face real consequences: "keeping too many animals together increase the likelihood of diseases" or "excessive industrialization caused issues like sour rain or smog." The goal isn't to lecture players on climate change, but to "breathe more life into Minecrafts environments" so they can "better understand the ecological systems and our interactions with them."

To emphasize environmentalism as a frame and potential foundation for a video game is much broader than the hotly debated topic of climate change.

The strength of this argument lies in its refusal to be preachy. Van der Linden insists that the point is not to "raise awareness about the global environmental consequences" but to create a space where players can "find out for yourself how did different elements of the environment interact with each other." This shifts the burden of moral reasoning from the game's code to the player's curiosity.

The Joy of Unpredictable Consequences

Ultimately, Van der Linden's commentary is a plea for the return of genuine discovery. He recalls the "unmistakable charm" of his early days in the game, where he didn't know what he was doing. He wants mechanics that would "revitalize that feeling" of exploring a living world. "I want to feel the danger of new and unexpected environmental hazards the joy of surprising opportunities," he writes, envisioning a game where players must "find sustainable ways of modernisation" to survive.

This vision challenges the industry's obsession with "conquest" and "progression" as the only valid metrics of fun. By proposing a game where "even individual behavior for better or worse leaves a footprint," Van der Linden suggests that the most engaging gameplay might come from constraint and consequence, not infinite freedom. He concludes that if these mechanics could "invoke you to reflect on our natural world and your own ecological impact," it would be a "pretty great deal."

I want to learn about ecological cars and the fact and utilize them to improve my base and the world around it.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's analysis is a masterclass in reframing a familiar subject, using sociological theory to expose the hidden ideology of a beloved game. While his proposal for an ecologically reactive Minecraft risks alienating players who prefer the game's current "indifferent" sandbox nature, his argument that design choices shape our moral imagination is undeniable. The strongest takeaway is not that Minecraft needs to change, but that our most popular virtual worlds are missing a crucial layer of reality: the feedback loop between our actions and the living world.
