Qualcomm's ventana risc-v acquisition

Babbage · The Chip Letter ·Jan 13, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

While the industry fixated on Nvidia's acquisition of Groq, Babbage uncovers a quieter, more strategic maneuver that signals a potential tectonic shift in processor architecture. This piece is notable not for the price tag of the deal, which remains undisclosed, but for the stark market reaction it triggered: a 20% plunge in Arm's stock value as investors realized the true cost of dependency on a single licensing model. For anyone tracking the future of computing, this is the moment the open-source alternative moved from a niche experiment to a boardroom imperative.

The Quiet Exit

Babbage frames the acquisition of Ventana Micro Systems by Qualcomm as a classic "modified acquihire," where the primary assets are the engineering talent and the intellectual property rather than a thriving product line. The author notes that unlike the high-profile Nvidia deal, this transaction generated little fanfare, yet the implications are profound. "We are thrilled to join the Qualcomm team and contribute our RISC-V expertise in the development of Qualcomm's leading Oryon CPU technology," said Balaji Baktha, CEO of Ventana Micro Systems. Babbage highlights that even Intel CEO Lip-Bu Tan, a key investor in Ventana, focused his congratulations on the team's exit rather than the technology's commercial success, suggesting a pragmatic recognition that the startup's journey as an independent entity had reached its limit.

The core of Babbage's argument is that this deal is less about Ventana's current revenue and more about securing a backup plan for a giant that is increasingly wary of its suppliers. The author points out that while the market initially shrugged, the long-term signal is a warning shot to Arm. "Qualcomm moving from Arm to RISC-V with the consequent loss of its Qualcomm licensing revenues would be a big deal for Arm and a huge win for the RISC-V ecosystem." This observation is sharp; it reframes the acquisition not as a victory for Ventana, but as a defensive strike by Qualcomm to diversify its architectural portfolio away from a supplier it is currently suing.

Qualcomm still needs to chart a course for its Snapdragon cores after that date, either coming to a agreement with Arm or moving to another architecture.

Critics might argue that Babbage overstates the immediate threat to Arm, noting that Qualcomm's transition to RISC-V for high-performance computing is still years away. However, the author effectively counters this by detailing the legal friction that has already erupted. The piece reminds readers that despite a recent legal victory for Qualcomm, the relationship is fractured: "No signs of a rapprochement between the two firms here!" This context is crucial; it explains why a company would pay a premium for a startup that hasn't yet shipped a commercial product. The risk of being held hostage by a licensing agreement is simply too high.

The Architecture of Independence

Babbage meticulously traces Qualcomm's decade-long journey with RISC-V, revealing a strategy that has been quietly maturing since 2015. The author lists a series of milestones, from founding the RISC-V Foundation to shipping over one billion RISC-V cores hidden inside various System-on-Chips (SoCs) by the end of 2023. "RISC-V is an open standard Instruction Set Architecture enabling a new era of processor innovation through open collaboration," the piece quotes from RISC-V International, emphasizing the philosophy of shared technical investment. This background is essential because it dismantles the notion that this acquisition is a sudden pivot; rather, it is the culmination of a long-term bet on an open standard.

The analysis of Ventana's specific technology adds necessary nuance. Babbage acknowledges that Ventana's first product, the Veyron V1, had limitations, particularly a lack of vector capability which hampered performance in throughput-bound applications like video encoding. "Ventana will have a much harder time on throughput bound applications," Babbage writes, citing analysis from Chips and Cheese. Yet, the author argues that the acquisition allows Qualcomm to internalize this R&D, bypassing the slow pace of ecosystem development that plagues open standards. By bringing the team in-house, Qualcomm can accelerate the development of vector support and software compatibility, effectively fast-tracking the maturity of RISC-V for high-performance tasks.

The author also touches on the broader ecosystem implications, noting that Ventana's strategy of offering CPU chiplets rather than full SoCs was a unique approach to the market. "Ventana isn't only targeting the crowded bog-standard general-purpose CPU market," Babbage observes, highlighting the potential for heterogeneous computing. This strategic fit suggests that Qualcomm isn't just buying a CPU core; they are buying a flexible architecture that can be integrated into diverse computing environments, from datacenters to automotive systems. The lack of named hyperscaler customers for Ventana's V2 platform, as noted by the author, is a weakness in the startup's commercial track record, but for Qualcomm, it represents a blank slate to be filled with their own massive customer base.

The sale of Ventana looks like a decent exit for employees but doesn't seem to be a triumph for Ventana's investors.

This blunt assessment underscores the reality of the semiconductor industry: talent is the currency, and the market rewards those who can secure it. The author's focus on the human element—the "great exit for employees"—adds a layer of realism often missing from tech coverage, which tends to obsess over product roadmaps and market share. Babbage correctly identifies that for the engineers, this is a win, but for the investors, the return on a seven-year, $100 million-plus bet is likely modest compared to the strategic value it provides the acquirer.

Bottom Line

Babbage's most compelling insight is the reframing of this acquisition as a geopolitical and economic hedge against the fragility of the current licensing model. The strongest part of the argument is the detailed chronology of Qualcomm's RISC-V investments, which proves this is a calculated, long-term strategy rather than a reactive panic. The biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that RISC-V can mature fast enough to replace Arm in high-performance computing before the current licensing agreements expire. Readers should watch for the next phase of Qualcomm's Oryon development and whether they can successfully integrate Ventana's chiplet architecture into their flagship products without the software ecosystem lagging behind.

Whilst the world was focused on Nvidia’s acquisition of most of Groq’s team and a license for Groq’s technology just before Christmas last year, another December acquisition of a startup by a chip giant generated a lot less discussion.

On 10 December Qualcomm announced they were acquiring RISC-V CPU designer Ventana Micro Systems. Like Groq, Ventana was a venture capital backed startup, this time founded in 2018, and which raised over $100m in funding. Investors included Intel CEO Lip-Bu Tan, through his investment firm Walden International. LBT quickly congratulated the Ventana team:

It’s a small world! Aside from LBT’s personal financial interest, Intel is both a Qualcomm competitor and potential future (foundry) supplier so a degree of diplomacy is probably appropriate.

Unlike Nvidia / Groq this was a true acquisition of all of Ventana. Like the other transaction though it also seems to be, in essence, a modified ‘acquihire’ where the company is bought for its engineering talent and its intellectual property rather than its products.

“We are thrilled to join the Qualcomm team and contribute our RISC-V expertise in the development of Qualcomm’s leading Oryon CPU technology,” said Balaji Baktha, CEO of Ventana Micro Systems.

It was notable that Intel’s LBT mentioned the team first in his ‘great exit for employees and investors’ comment. The sale of Ventana looks like a decent exit for employees but doesn’t seem to be a triumph for Ventana’s investors. Unlike Qualcomm’s purchase of Arm CPU designer Nuvia in 2021 for $1.4bn, this deal wasn’t big enough to require disclosure of the purchase price.

Since the Ventana deal was announced the market has largely left Qualcomm’s stock price (in blue) unchanged, whilst the news looks like it started Arm’s stock (in yellow) on a downward path and it’s now down over 20%.

On the surface this makes sense. Arm has disclosed that Qualcomm is one of its biggest customers, accounting for 10% of Arm’s total revenue

Qualcomm moving from Arm to RISC-V with the consequent loss of its Qualcomm licensing revenues would be a big deal for Arm and a huge win for the RISC-V ecosystem. On the other hand fees paid to Arm make up a much smaller portion of Qualcomm’s expenses.
