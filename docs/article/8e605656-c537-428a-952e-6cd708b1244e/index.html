One of the greatest challenges of the historical biopic is having to mold a disjointed reality into a thematically and stylistically consistent narrative. It's a translative act that almost always results in some detriment to the real history, to the movie, or to both. Yet, it is also a revealing one, for it portrays the tension between what we want the past to be, what we want life in general to be, and what it actually is. Christopher Nolan's Oenheimer embodies this contradiction in intentional as well as unintentional ways.

More than a year after its release, the movie remains as enigmatic as the tormented character at its center. A tense historical account of science and politics injected with the scale and energy of an action blockbuster. An invitation to peer into my soul. As one of the first lines of the script reads, "Yet an experience that alienates more than it connects." As Nolan himself realized during production, the closer we got, the more obscure the picture became, like a newspaper photograph becoming incoherent on magnification.

It is no wonder then that the movie has proven to be difficult to engage with and that people have called into question various aspects of its story and its execution. But then again, it might also be the sign of a movie still waiting to be truly understood, to be truly appreciated on its own terms without being forced into some pre-existing mold, only to be judged for not fitting. Because what I think you need to understand first and foremost about Alenheimr is that this is not some journeyman's account of the creation of the nuclear bomb. No, this is a Nolan movie.

And today, more than anything perhaps, I want to really examine what that means, or rather what it has come to mean. Because I do believe Oenheimer doesn't fully open itself up for better and worse until viewed as the culmination of a cinematic vision that Nolan has been aspiring to for many years. A cinematic vision that cannot be fairly understood except in the context of his life and work. So, how long until Oenheimer?
