The Witcher 3 – Virtual Empathy & Moral Reflection In Video Games

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Jun 15, 2019 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden makes a startling claim: the guilt you feel for burning down a virtual forge isn't just a game glitch, but a genuine moral response that could reshape your real-world character. While most critics dissect The Witcher 3 for its graphics or combat, van der Linden argues the game functions as a virtual training ground for morality, forcing players to practice ethical decision-making in a space where consequences feel terrifyingly real.

The Architecture of Empathy

The author begins by dismantling the idea that video games exist in a moral vacuum. He posits that our virtual actions interact directly with our real-world sense of right and wrong. "The actions undertaken by players in a videogame do not occur in a moral vacuum," van der Linden writes, noting that studies confirm virtual behaviors can elicit genuine emotional reactions like guilt. This is a crucial distinction; it suggests that the medium is not merely a distraction but a mirror for our conscience.

However, for this reflection to work, the player must first be immersed. The author breaks down the mechanics of this immersion into three pillars: external presentation, world-building, and characterization. He argues that "filling the virtual space with a sense of history, culture, and identity" is what makes the world feel relatable rather than unbelievable. By giving even minor characters rich backstories, the game humanizes them, making the player care about their fate. This design choice is effective because it transforms NPCs from quest dispensers into individuals with their own lives, thereby raising the stakes of every interaction.

Critics might note that this reliance on high-fidelity graphics and voice acting limits the argument to AAA productions, potentially ignoring how simpler, text-based games can also evoke deep empathy through imagination alone. Yet, van der Linden's focus remains on how The Witcher 3 leverages these tools to maximum effect.

"Moral behavior is not a given but something that must be practiced continuously, something that one must get better at."

The Trap of Binary Morality

Once immersion is established, the conversation shifts to agency. Van der Linden identifies a common failure in game design: the reduction of morality to a simple binary. He critiques systems like Mass Effect, where players are rewarded for picking a side—Paragon or Renegade—rather than reflecting on individual choices. "Morality is simplified to a binary design of good versus bad," he observes, arguing that this encourages players to optimize their stats rather than engage in genuine ethical reasoning. When a game rewards consistency over nuance, it effectively removes the need for moral reflection.

The author points out that even games attempting complexity often fall into a trap of "morally gray option 1 versus morally gray option 2," where every choice leads to a cynical outcome. This design flaw can lead to player disheartenment, where the result is not reflection but resignation. "When the moral decisions you do make automatically lead to a rather extreme corruption... the end result is more likely to feel cynical," van der Linden warns. This is a sharp critique of the "lesser of two evils" trope that plagues modern narrative games.

Furthermore, he highlights the issue of obvious narrative forks. When a game signals too loudly that a decision is important, the player switches from intuitive empathy to analytical calculation. "The choice becomes too analytical and probably won't elicit strong effective moral response," he explains. True moral weight, he suggests, comes from quick, automatic evaluations, not deliberate reasoning in a vacuum.

The Witcher's Subtle Mastery

So, how does The Witcher 3 succeed where others fail? Van der Linden argues it does so by abandoning the "honor system" entirely. There is no meter telling you if you are being "good" or "bad." Instead, "Geralt's nobility is mostly left up to the subjective judgment of the player." This freedom forces players to rely on their own internal compass rather than chasing in-game rewards. The game trusts the player to find the moral weight in their own choices, not in a points system.

The author praises the game's quest design for its variation and lack of obvious patterns. "There are tests of conscience moments where your resolve might be put to the test," he writes, citing instances where players must choose between bribes, lies, and difficult truths. These aren't just plot points; they are designed to keep the player's sense of agency "alert at all times." The game creates situations where good intentions lead to unexpected negative outcomes, forcing a retroactive re-evaluation of earlier actions.

Perhaps the most powerful element is the permanence of these choices. Van der Linden notes that the game "deliberately eliminates the chance to reload and exercise your moral agency with an analytical mindset." Without a checkpoint to undo a mistake, the player is forced to live with the consequences, mirroring the irrevocable nature of real-life decisions. "There will be moments of mixed emotions... where your feeling of accomplishment at an earlier point is questioned by a later one," he describes. This design creates a feedback loop of reflection that is rare in entertainment.

"The game deliberately eliminates the chance to reload... instead it forces you to live with the consequences."

Bottom Line

Van der Linden's most compelling argument is that The Witcher 3 succeeds by refusing to tell players what is right, instead creating a space where they must discover it for themselves. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its assumption that this virtual practice translates seamlessly to the real world, a leap that psychology has yet to fully prove. However, the evidence presented suggests that for the first time, a game has moved beyond simple entertainment to become a genuine arena for moral rehearsal.
