Uvalde and Police "Duty"

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Jun 3, 2022 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone delivers a legal reality check that cuts through the moral outrage surrounding the Uvalde tragedy: the police have no constitutional duty to protect you. In a landscape dominated by emotional appeals and demands for accountability, Stone's analysis of Supreme Court precedent offers a chilling, unvarnished truth that most legal commentators gloss over. He argues that the gap between the police motto—"protect and serve"—and the actual law is not a bug, but a feature of American jurisprudence.

The Myth of the Protective Duty

Stone begins by dismantling the most common assumption held by the public and the victims' families alike. "Most people believe that the police have a legal duty to help and unfortunately according to the supreme court that is not the case," he writes. This assertion is the anchor of his entire argument, and it is as devastating as it is legally accurate. He clarifies that while the police may have a duty to the public at large, they have no specific obligation to any individual citizen in distress.

The author grounds this in the 1989 case DeShaney v. Winnebago County, a heartbreaking story of a four-year-old boy beaten into a coma by his father despite social workers documenting the abuse over a year. Stone notes that Chief Justice William Rehnquist, writing for the majority, established that "the due process clause only applies to states not to private citizens." The court reasoned that because the father, not the state, inflicted the injuries, the state was not liable. Stone explains that the Constitution is designed to protect people from the government, not to guarantee that the government protects people from each other. This framing is crucial; it shifts the blame from a failure of character to a failure of statutory design. Critics might argue that this interpretation renders the state's knowledge of danger irrelevant, effectively immunizing inaction even when the state is fully aware of a ticking time bomb.

From Social Workers to Active Shooters

Stone then bridges the gap between child welfare cases and active shooter scenarios through the 2005 ruling Town of Castle Rock v. Gonzales. In this case, a mother begged police to enforce a restraining order against her estranged husband, who had kidnapped their three children. The police delayed, and the children were murdered. Stone highlights Justice Antonin Scalia's opinion, which ruled that even mandatory state laws do not create an entitlement to police enforcement. "Law enforcement officers often consider themselves responsible for protecting the public and deterring criminal activity however the legal doctrine that specifies the legal duty of law enforcement officers holds that they have a duty to the public but not to individual citizens," Stone quotes. This distinction is the legal bedrock of the "public duty doctrine."

The author's analysis here is particularly sharp because he anticipates the emotional counter-argument: if the police have a duty to the public, why does that not translate to a duty to the specific child in the classroom? Stone answers this by explaining that the law views the police's role as general, not specific. "The doctrine means that the officers don't have a duty to protect citizens from harm by a third party," he states. This is a hard pill to swallow for a society that expects first responders to be saviors, but Stone's adherence to the text of the law leaves little room for ambiguity.

The due process clause was to protect people from the state not to ensure that the state protected them from each other.

The Custody Trap and the School Exception

Perhaps the most complex part of Stone's commentary addresses the unique status of schoolchildren. Since children are mandated to attend school, and schools are government-run, one might argue they are in "state custody." Stone dismantles this theory by pointing to the Parkland shooting litigation. In that case, the 11th Circuit Court of Appeals ruled that students are not in a custodial relationship with the state simply because they are required to be in school. Stone notes the court's finding that "a custodial relationship exists only if the government places limitations upon individuals ability to act on his own behalf." Since students can leave school (even if they shouldn't), they are not in custody in the legal sense required to trigger the state's duty of care.

Stone further explores the "state-created danger" exception, where the government might be liable if its actions made a situation worse. However, he concludes this likely wouldn't apply in Uvalde because the police did not create the shooter or the threat; they merely failed to stop it. "The police were doing something they were securing the parking lot and stopping parents from getting inside where they might get hurt and just because the effort failed and failed badly probably wouldn't change the fifth circuit's analysis," Stone argues. This is a sobering conclusion: even a catastrophic failure of execution does not necessarily constitute a constitutional violation if the initial duty to act never existed.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's analysis is a masterclass in separating legal liability from moral culpability, offering a clear-eyed view of why the Uvalde police may never face a successful lawsuit for their inaction. The strongest part of his argument is the rigorous application of the DeShaney and Castle Rock precedents, which strip away the comforting illusion of a constitutional safety net. However, the piece's greatest vulnerability is its potential to feel like a legal absolution for what many perceive as a moral abdication, leaving readers with the unsettling realization that the law is not always aligned with justice. Readers should watch for how future legislatures might attempt to codify a duty to protect, as the current constitutional framework appears permanently closed to such claims.
