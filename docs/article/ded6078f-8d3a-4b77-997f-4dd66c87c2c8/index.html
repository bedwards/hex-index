What is it Like to be a Bat? - the hard problem of consciousness

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·Sep 4, 2020 · 25 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan tackles the most stubborn puzzle in philosophy of mind: why no amount of physical data can ever explain what it feels like to be something. His commentary on Thomas Nagel's 1974 classic doesn't just summarize the argument; it dismantles the assumption that we can ever truly understand another creature's inner life, no matter how smart our science gets.

The Reductive Trap

Kaplan begins by exposing the limits of physicalism, the dominant view that everything in the universe, including the mind, is just physical stuff. He illustrates this with a simple analogy: lightning. "Science has discovered, I think, that lightning is nothing more than electric discharge. It's the movement of electrons," Kaplan notes. This reductive approach works perfectly for weather, but Kaplan argues it collapses when applied to consciousness. The core of his coverage is the distinction between knowing how something works and knowing what it is like to experience it.

He drives this home by contrasting a human with a rock. While we can imagine being 30 feet tall, we cannot imagine being a rock because "a rock doesn't experience darkness... It's not like anything." This distinction is crucial. Kaplan writes, "Conscious experience is that feature of mind whereby there's something that it is like to be that being at that time." The power here lies in the negative definition; he defines consciousness not by what it does, but by the undeniable fact that it feels like something. Critics might argue that this relies too heavily on subjective intuition rather than empirical data, but Kaplan's point is precisely that empirical data misses the subjective entirely.

Conscious experience is that feature of mind whereby there's something that it is like to be that being at that time.

The Bat's Perspective

The commentary then pivots to Nagel's famous thought experiment: the bat. Kaplan explains that bats navigate using echolocation, a sense humans lack. He rejects the common cinematic trope of imagining echolocation as seeing with sound. "Is that what it's like for a bat to echolocate? It's like being in a world where things don't have colors. There's just objects and they all have like a like a red outline around them. No, that's not what it's like," Kaplan asserts. This is a vital correction to a widespread misconception. We can imagine a human acting like a bat, but we cannot imagine the bat's actual experience.

Kaplan highlights the specific limitation of human cognition: "We have no idea what that feels like, what that conscious experience is like." He emphasizes that the bat's experience is not a modified version of our own; it is fundamentally alien. This leads to his most significant insight about the nature of facts themselves. As Kaplan puts it, "These appear to be facts that embody a particular point of view." He argues that some truths are inextricably tied to a specific subjective perspective, making them inaccessible to an objective, third-person observer.

The Unbridgeable Gap

The final section of Kaplan's analysis synthesizes these points into a direct attack on physicalism. If physicalism claims that the mind is nothing more than physical processes, it must be able to explain every aspect of the mind, including subjective experience. But if we cannot know what it is like to be a bat, then we cannot fully explain the bat's mind through physics alone. Kaplan notes that terms like "qualia," "phenomenal character," and "subjective experience" all point to this same elusive quality. "You get the pink experience, the pink consciousness whereas the color detector doesn't," he explains, distinguishing between a machine detecting a wavelength and a being experiencing the color pink.

This argument suggests that consciousness might be a fundamental feature of the universe, not just a byproduct of complex biology. While Kaplan stops short of fully endorsing dualism, he leaves the reader with a profound doubt about the completeness of the physicalist worldview. The gap between the objective description of neurons firing and the subjective feeling of hunger or seeing pink remains unbridgeable by current scientific methods.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's commentary succeeds by stripping away the jargon to reveal the raw intuition at the heart of the "hard problem" of consciousness: objective science can describe the mechanism, but it can never capture the feeling. The argument's greatest strength is its refusal to let the reader off the hook with easy analogies, while its vulnerability lies in the fact that it proves a negative—that we can't know something—rather than offering a new theory of what consciousness actually is.
