The Year of Zone Coverage

Ray Carpenter · The Spade ·Jan 22, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

Ray Carpenter argues that the modern NFL has shifted from a game of athletic execution to one of psychological warfare, where the primary battlefield is the quarterback's pre-snap read. He posits that the proliferation of analytics has not just raised the bar, but fundamentally changed the sport into a high-stakes game of bluffing, where defenses actively disguise their intentions to induce mental errors rather than just physical ones.

The Shift from Chess to Poker

Carpenter opens by dismantling the nostalgia for the "pure pocket passer," suggesting that the decline of this archetype is not a failure of quarterbacks but a triumph of defensive scheming. "The era of the pure pocket passer is gone in today's NFL," he writes, "but what if I told you it's not any quarterback's fault that this era is behind us? The blame lies on the other side of the ball." This reframing is crucial; it moves the narrative away from quarterback decline and toward defensive evolution. By comparing the sport to poker rather than chess, Carpenter highlights that while every team has access to the same data, the edge now belongs to those who can best manipulate the opponent's perception of that data.

The author illustrates this by explaining how traditional tells, such as a defender following a receiver in motion, are now being weaponized. "Maybe they send a man in motion with the wide receiver, but they're actually lined up entirely in zone," Carpenter notes. This layer of deception forces quarterbacks to process conflicting information in mere seconds. The argument holds significant weight when looking at the Seattle Seahawks, whose defense is described as "literally and figuratively causing headaches" for opposing offenses. The piece effectively uses the Seahawks' ability to disguise coverage as a case study for the league-wide trend.

"In a world where every team has the same access, how do you provide an edge to yours? This is where football veers more towards poker than chess."

Critics might argue that attributing quarterback struggles solely to defensive disguise ignores the impact of offensive line play and receiver separation, which remain physical constants in the sport. However, Carpenter's data suggests that even elite pass protectors are struggling against the mental load imposed by these schemes.

The Data Behind the Disguise

Moving beyond theory, Carpenter supports his claims with a granular look at league-wide statistics from 2021 through 2025. He points to a steady rise in quarterback scramble rates, which have climbed from 4.41% to 5.30% over this period. "Scrambling usually occurs when a quarterback bails on a passing play due to a lack of open receivers," he explains, linking the physical act of scrambling directly to the mental failure of identifying the correct coverage pre-snap. The data shows that even elite signal-callers like Patrick Mahomes and Josh Allen have seen their scramble rates jump significantly, suggesting this is a systemic issue rather than an individual one.

Perhaps the most compelling statistic is the shift in defensive alignment. Carpenter reveals that the league has moved to a near 70/30 split in favor of zone coverage, a massive departure from the past. "29 out of 32 teams are playing more zone defense now than they were three seasons ago," he writes. This trend is not just a statistical anomaly; it is a strategic pivot. By analyzing specific plays, such as a third-down sequence between the 49ers and Seahawks, Carpenter demonstrates how a single play can involve multiple layers of disguise, from motion to safety positioning, all designed to freeze the quarterback. The breakdown of this single play, where the offense had "fewer than 40 seconds to process all of this information," underscores the sheer cognitive overload now required to succeed.

The Playoff Implications

The commentary extends this analysis to the upcoming conference championships, using the data to frame the matchups. Carpenter suggests that the Seahawks' defensive mastery could be the deciding factor against the Los Angeles Rams, provided their quarterback can overcome his history of interceptions against that specific defense. "If Sam Darnold can limit the number of interceptions he throws, the Seahawks will win," he asserts, tying the outcome directly to the mental battle he has been describing throughout the piece. The prediction for the AFC Championship between the Patriots and Broncos is more straightforward, with Carpenter leaning toward a Patriots victory despite the harsh weather conditions, citing their recent offensive efficiency.

The piece concludes by emphasizing that the "Year of Zone Coverage" is defined by the inability of offenses to trust their pre-snap reads. "It's why you see college football quarterbacks stay silent pre-snap and just clap to hike the ball," Carpenter observes, noting that the complexity of the NFL game has forced even the most vocal leaders to simplify their communication to avoid giving away their reads. This observation serves as a powerful summary of the league's current state: silence is the new strategy.

Bottom Line

Carpenter's strongest argument is the compelling link between rising scramble rates and the increasing sophistication of defensive disguise, a connection often overlooked in favor of blaming offensive line play. The piece's main vulnerability is its heavy reliance on the Seahawks as the primary example, which may overstate the universality of this trend across all teams. Readers should watch whether this defensive dominance holds up against the most talented offenses in the playoffs, or if the league eventually finds a counter-scheme to the bluff.
