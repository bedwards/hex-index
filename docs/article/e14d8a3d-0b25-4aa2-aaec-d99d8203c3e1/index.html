The surprisingly long life of the vacuum tube

Brian Potter · Construction Physics ·Jan 15, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In an era obsessed with the next breakthrough in silicon, Brian Potter's deep dive into the vacuum tube delivers a startling correction: the technology we consider obsolete is not only still here, it is the invisible backbone of modern civilization. Potter argues that while we celebrate the microchip, we ignore the "enormous technological ecosystem" built decades earlier that still powers our hospitals, our fusion experiments, and our radar systems. This is not a nostalgia trip; it is a necessary reminder that technological progress is often an act of layering, not replacing.

The Dual Lineage of Electron Flow

Potter begins by dismantling the myth of a clean break between old and new. He notes that "decades before the invention of the transistor, another enormous technological ecosystem was built around a device that manipulated the flow of electrons — the vacuum tube." The author's framing is precise here, tracing two distinct but converging paths: the study of rarefied gases and the refinement of incandescent lighting.

He highlights the meticulous work of 19th-century scientists like Michael Faraday and Julius Plücker, who observed that "running an electric current through rarefied gasses could make them glow colorfully." Potter points out that Plücker, who "idolized Faraday," utilized glass tubes from instrument maker Heinrich Geissler to push these experiments further. This historical grounding is crucial; it shows that the vacuum tube was not a sudden eureka moment but the result of decades of incremental refinement in vacuum technology. Potter draws a sharp parallel to the incandescent bulb, noting that "Edison would use the same strategy of platinum lead-in wires" to solve thermal expansion issues, a detail that connects the history of lighting directly to the history of electronics.

The narrative builds to the discovery of the "Edison Effect," where current unexpectedly flowed from a hot filament to a metal plate. Potter writes, "It was eventually discovered that electric current was unexpectedly flowing from these metal plates, a phenomenon that Edison modestly dubbed 'the Edison Effect'." This moment is the pivot point. While Edison saw a nuisance, others saw a mechanism. The author effectively illustrates how scientific curiosity often outpaces practical application, as researchers like John Fleming and Lee de Forest turned this "effect" into the rectifier and the amplifier.

"De Forest had little idea how his device (which he dubbed 'the Audion') worked: he thought, incorrectly, that it relied on the flow of charged particles of gas within the tube."

This admission of ignorance is powerful. Potter uses it to underscore a broader point about innovation: the utility of a technology often precedes the full theoretical understanding of its mechanics. As Potter puts it, "AT&T studied the Audion, working out a theory for how it behaved, and turned it into the more useful and reliable triode vacuum tube device." The corporate machinery of AT&T is credited here not just for funding, but for the theoretical rigor that transformed a curiosity into the engine of the first transcontinental telephone lines.

Beyond Logic: The Physical Applications

The most compelling section of Potter's argument is his refusal to limit the vacuum tube's legacy to early computing. While the ENIAC computer is famous for its "18,000 vacuum tubes, mostly dual triodes," Potter insists that the device's true dominance lies in its ability to generate and manipulate high-energy radiation. He writes, "Vacuum tubes could also be used to generate other sorts of electromagnetic radiation," a claim that shifts the reader's perspective from logic gates to physical power.

Potter details the invention of the magnetron, originally an electronic switch, which the British later refined into the "cavity magnetron" that made aircraft-mounted microwave radar possible during the war. He notes that "following the war it was discovered that magnetron-emitted microwave radiation could be used to heat food, and magnetrons are still found in microwaves today." This is a striking example of dual-use technology, where a device critical to military survival became a household staple.

The author also touches on the medical and scientific frontiers, explaining that "modern X-ray machines still use vacuum tubes to generate X-rays" and that "Klystrons provide power for particle accelerators like Stanford's SLAC, are the heart of radiation therapy machines for cancer treatment." Potter's enumeration of these applications serves as a quiet rebuttal to the idea that solid-state electronics have achieved total supremacy. He notes that "four Nobel Prizes were the product of vacuum tube devices," a statistic that underscores the technology's profound impact on human knowledge.

Critics might note that Potter's focus on the continued utility of vacuum tubes risks overstating their prevalence in consumer electronics, where semiconductors have indeed won the day for efficiency and miniaturization. However, his argument is not that vacuum tubes are better everywhere, but that they remain irreplaceable in high-power, high-frequency, and high-radiation environments where silicon fails.

"And while the vacuum tube feels like an ancient technology that has long been superseded, much of the technological edifice still stands."

This sentence encapsulates the piece's central thesis. Potter is not arguing for a return to the past, but for a recognition that the past is still present. The "technological edifice" he describes is not a museum exhibit; it is the active infrastructure of the modern world. From the neon lights that trace the history of gas discharge tubes to the gyrotrons heating plasma in fusion experiments, the vacuum tube remains a vital component of our physical reality.

Bottom Line

Potter's strongest argument is his ability to recontextualize the vacuum tube not as a relic, but as a specialized tool that occupies a critical niche in the modern technological stack. His biggest vulnerability is a slight romanticism of the "glowing" aesthetic of these devices, which might distract from the harsh realities of their energy inefficiency compared to LEDs. The reader should watch for how this understanding of legacy technology influences future engineering, particularly in fields like fusion energy and high-power radar where the vacuum tube's unique physics remain unmatched.

The surprisingly long life of the vacuum tube

by Brian Potter · Construction Physics · Read full article

The last several decades of technological progress have, in large part, been about finding more and more things we can do with semiconductors and the technology for producing them. Microchips have found their way into virtually every car, aircraft, appliance, and electronic device. Light-emitting diodes are steadily replacing older, less efficient methods of generating light (such as incandescent bulbs). Solar photovoltaic panels have become the most rapidly deployed energy source in history. Semiconductor lasers have enabled fiber-optic communication. Semiconductor-based charge-coupled devices (CCDs) and CMOS sensors are used for digital imaging. The list goes on.

But decades before the invention of the transistor, another enormous technological ecosystem was built around a device that manipulated the flow of electrons — the vacuum tube. In the first half of the 20th century, vacuum tube technology found its way into all manner of devices, from radios to TVs to the earliest computers. And like semiconductors today, vacuum tubes had applications far beyond electronic logic — the phenomenon they leveraged could be applied to everything from lighting and displays to video cameras and radars. And while the vacuum tube feels like an ancient technology that has long been superseded, much of the technological edifice still stands.

Origins of the vacuum tube.

A vacuum tube is an evacuated tube (often, though not always, made of glass) containing electrodes, between which electrons flow. These tubes, along with various offshoots and technological cousins, were the product of two parallel strands of development.

The first line of descent was via what are known as “gas discharge tubes” — tubes where electricity is discharged through a highly rarefied gas (gas at very low concentration and pressure). Not long after the German scientist Otto von Guericke invented the first vacuum pump in 1650, early scientists began using such pumps to study highly rarefied gasses. It was observed that running an electric current through rarefied gasses could make them glow colorfully, but for many years this was mostly regarded as an interesting curiosity.

It wasn’t until the 1830s, with the experiments of the English chemist and physicist Michael Faraday, that the effects of electricity on rarefied gasses began to be studied more seriously. Faraday subjected a variety of rarefied gasses to electric current, observing their colorful glow, along with a curious “dark space” between the two electrodes.
