Why China is About to Start a Trade War

PolyMatter · PolyMatter ·Mar 22, 2024 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

PolyMatter reframes the global trade narrative by arguing that China's most alarming economic symptom isn't inflation or debt, but rather the unsettling reality that its infrastructure is too good and its goods are too cheap. The author suggests that this abundance is not a sign of strength, but a desperate signal of a domestic consumption crisis that Beijing is now poised to export to the rest of the world.

The Hidden Cost of Cheap Bikes

The piece begins by dismantling the intuitive assumption that high export volumes equal economic health. PolyMatter writes, "Exports are one of the four components of GDP... but China in ways both good and bad is not a normal country." This distinction is crucial because it forces the reader to look past the surface-level success of Chinese manufacturing. The author argues that the low cost of Chinese goods isn't just about efficient labor; it is the result of a systematic devaluation of the workforce and the environment.

The commentary highlights how the Chinese state acts as a direct market participant rather than a regulator. PolyMatter notes, "The party is unapologetic indeed proud of its role as director of the Chinese economy." This pride is manifested in a web of subsidies ranging from obvious tax credits to more insidious tactics like suppressing labor unions and ignoring environmental regulations. The author effectively illustrates this by comparing a Chinese bicycle to French wine. While the wine is produced due to natural comparative advantage, the bike is produced because of artificial cost-cutting.

"These subsidies aren't free... ultimately Chinese workers pay for all these shortcuts rebates credits and other pro-business policies first and most obviously they pay in the form of lower effective wages."

This argument lands powerfully because it shifts the blame from abstract market forces to specific policy choices. It suggests that the "cheapness" of Chinese goods is actually a transfer of wealth from Chinese workers to foreign consumers. A counterargument worth considering is whether these "subsidies" are truly unique to China or simply an extreme version of the industrial policy practiced by many developed nations, including the United States. However, PolyMatter's focus on the scale and the specific suppression of domestic demand makes the distinction feel significant.

The Consumption Trap

The core of the analysis shifts to the internal mechanics of the Chinese economy. The author explains that by keeping wages low and benefits minimal, the state forces citizens to save rather than spend. PolyMatter writes, "Chinese families do what anyone would do in their shoes knowing they're on their own save in case they get laid off sick or gasp retire."

This creates a structural imbalance where the economy relies on external buyers to absorb its surplus production. The author points out that this model worked beautifully for decades but is now hitting a wall. The pandemic provided a temporary boost as Western consumers spent stimulus checks, but that has faded. Now, with a shrinking population and a collapsing real estate market, the internal engine of growth has stalled. PolyMatter observes, "The surge of demand simply never arrived... consumers are buying so little that companies from Apple to Tesla have started discounting their prices."

The author's diagnosis is that Beijing faces a fork in the road. The "right way" involves a painful restructuring to boost domestic welfare and consumption. The "easy way," which the author suggests China is choosing, is to dump its excess capacity on the global market. PolyMatter states, "China could simply export its problem handing it off like a hot potato." This framing is compelling because it predicts a specific geopolitical flashpoint: the automotive industry. With a potential surplus of 15 to 20 million electric vehicles, China is effectively forcing its way into Western markets.

"Another trade war is brewing and as the EV competition heats up demand for engineering math programming and AI will only intensify."

Critics might argue that Western nations have the leverage to simply block these imports through tariffs, rendering the "export the problem" strategy ineffective. Yet, the sheer volume of surplus capacity described by PolyMatter suggests that even with tariffs, the pressure on global prices and domestic industries will be immense. The author's logic holds that China has no other viable option for growth given its demographic and investment constraints.

Bottom Line

PolyMatter's strongest contribution is connecting the dots between China's domestic social safety net failures and the coming global trade conflict, arguing that the trade war is not an act of aggression but a survival mechanism. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in assuming Beijing has no capacity for the difficult internal reforms required to fix the root cause, though the political reality makes this a reasonable bet. Readers should watch for how Western democracies respond to the inevitable flood of subsidized Chinese electric vehicles, as this will define the next decade of global economic relations.
