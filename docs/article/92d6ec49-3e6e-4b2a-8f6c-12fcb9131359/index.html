The Oppenheimer Discussion

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Aug 3, 2023 · 66 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden and Thomas Flight do not merely review Christopher Nolan's Oppenheimer; they dissect the film's unique ability to transform a historical biopic into a claustrophobic study of bureaucratic dread. In a landscape of film criticism often obsessed with plot mechanics or visual spectacle, their standout claim is that the true antagonist of the film is not the atomic bomb itself, but the "dread of banal American bureaucratic military politics" that drives the narrative. This perspective shifts the conversation from the physics of destruction to the psychology of the men who wield it, offering a crucial lens for listeners trying to process the film's dense, three-hour runtime.

The Architecture of Dread

The hosts immediately ground their discussion in the visceral experience of the film, noting its overwhelming density. Van der Linden admits that even hours after viewing, he is "still kind of just like trying to wrap my head around the movie," describing it as "Nolan's like most dense movie" where "wall-to-wall dialogue" creates a relentless pressure. This observation is vital because it validates the audience's feeling of being overwhelmed, reframing it not as a failure of the film, but as an intentional stylistic choice to mirror the protagonist's mental state.

The conversation quickly pivots to the film's central thematic conflict. Van der Linden identifies a chilling realization: "the real villain in the shadows of this movie is not the nuclear bomb but just like the Men Behind the Scenes who are like pulling the strings." This reframing is the piece's most potent insight. By focusing on the petty emotions and political maneuvering of figures like Lewis Strauss, the commentary argues that the film exposes how human frailty, rather than scientific genius, dictates the fate of the world.

"The real conflict that is in the movie is not so much the bomb but rather those in power who wield it and the kind of almost petty emotions that come into play with that."

This argument holds up well against the film's structure, which juxtaposes the scientific breakthrough with the subsequent political purge. However, a counterargument worth considering is whether this focus on "petty emotions" risks minimizing the sheer scale of the technological horror. While the bureaucracy is indeed the mechanism, the bomb remains the ultimate consequence, and some critics might argue that the film's obsession with the hearing room occasionally overshadows the moral weight of the destruction in Japan.

Synthesizing Politics and Psychology

Van der Linden and Flight contextualize Oppenheimer within a broader cinematic history of nuclear anxiety, positioning it as a synthesis of political thrillers like Dr. Strangelove and trauma narratives like the original Godzilla. They argue that Nolan's film is unique because it adopts a "very subjective perspective that tries to grapple with the consequences," specifically through the lens of the scientists' conscience rather than just the victims' suffering.

The hosts praise the film for avoiding a simplistic moral binary. Van der Linden notes that the movie is "not right shied away from either sanitizing Oppenheimer as a character... but it was also not condemning him entirely." Instead, it presents a paradox where Oppenheimer is "caught in this trap almost that ran out of his control." This nuanced approach allows the film to explore the "inevitability of this giant machine" without absolving the individuals of their agency. The commentary suggests that the film's power lies in showing how fear acts as a driving force, making the decision to build the bomb feel psychologically inevitable even if it wasn't morally required.

"It's to some extent sympathetic in the way that that's how human beings just work like that's how fear works as a driving force within us that's how you know decisions are made a lot of the times."

This psychological framing is effective, yet it leaves a gap regarding the global perspective. The hosts acknowledge that the film focuses on the American experience, from the Manhattan Project to the Cold War hearings, but they do not deeply explore how the film handles the Japanese victims. While they mention the "aftermath" could have been treated with more impact, the commentary largely accepts the film's choice to remain within the American political sphere, which may limit its scope as a universal meditation on nuclear dread.

The Limits of Mainstream Cinema

Despite their admiration, the hosts offer a sharp critique of the film's execution, suggesting that Nolan sometimes struggles to balance his blockbuster ambitions with the film's dark, existential themes. Van der Linden feels that "Nolan was kind of stuck between wanting to have this honest depiction of like deep dark humanity while also being... the mainstream blockbuster director." This tension, they argue, results in moments where the film "took the edge off," particularly in the depiction of Hiroshima and Nagasaki.

"I felt some moments that I had seen done better in different movies and that kind of took the edge off I think for me where that maybe should have been a little bit more edge."

This critique is essential for a balanced review. It suggests that while the film succeeds in portraying the internal paradox of Oppenheimer, it occasionally pulls its punches when facing the external reality of the bomb's use. The hosts imply that a truly unflinching exploration of nuclear dread might require a film that is less concerned with mainstream appeal and more willing to sit in the discomfort of the aftermath.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden and Thomas Flight provide a compelling framework for understanding Oppenheimer as a film about the banality of power rather than the spectacle of destruction. Their strongest contribution is the argument that the true horror lies in the "petty emotions" of the political elite, a perspective that elevates the film beyond a standard biopic. However, their admission that the film's mainstream constraints may have dulled its sharpest edges serves as a necessary reminder that even the most ambitious blockbusters have limits in confronting the full weight of historical trauma.
