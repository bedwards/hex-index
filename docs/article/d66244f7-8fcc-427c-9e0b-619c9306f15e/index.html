The bucks traded giannis too late

Nate Silver · ·Jun 25, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Nate Silver cuts through the noise of a chaotic NBA summer with a cold, calculating assessment: the Milwaukee Bucks didn't just miss a trade window; they watched it close while holding onto a depreciating asset too long. While headlines scream about star power moving to Miami, Silver argues that the real story is one of optimal portfolio management gone slightly awry, where the fear of regret cost a franchise its best chance at a total rebuild. This isn't just sports gossip; it's a masterclass in how advanced metrics and contract leverage collide with human hesitation in high-stakes business.

The Cost of Hesitation

Silver frames the Bucks' decision not as a failure to find a partner, but as a failure to act when their leverage was highest. He writes, "Trading players when they can fetch the most value is optimal portfolio management — though 'selling high' is difficult for any number of reasons." The core of his argument rests on the idea that Milwaukee's front office, led by Jon Horst, let emotional attachment and fear of missing out cloud their judgment. They traded Giannis Antetokounmpo and Bobby Portis for Tyler Herro, Kel'el Ware, Jaime Jaquez Jr., Kasparas Jakucionis, three first-round picks, a pick swap, and a second-rounder. While this looks like a haul on paper, Silver suggests it pales in comparison to the packages available just months prior.

The author points out that "Giannis's star has dimmed from consensus top-3 player to a guy who might need to be paired with another star or an outstanding core of complementary players." This decline is subtle but critical. As Silver notes, "He won Defensive Player of the Year in 2020... But he hasn't made an All-Defensive team since 2022." He attributes this to a classic aging pattern where players hide their decline by maintaining box score stats while reducing effort on the defensive end. This is where the context of Adjusted Plus Minus becomes vital; the data suggests the Bucks were holding onto a player whose on-court impact was already eroding, even if his reputation remained stellar.

"Giannis's star has dimmed from consensus top-3 player to a guy who might need to be paired with another star or an outstanding core of complementary players to compete for a title."

Critics might argue that the Bucks had no choice but to trade now, given Giannis's impending free agency and injury history. However, Silver counters this by noting that "the absence of asset-rich teams like the Spurs, Rockets and Thunder from the conversation should be noted." If those teams were truly out of the picture a year ago, then Milwaukee missed a window where they could have secured a true cornerstone rather than a collection of role players and picks. The author suggests that a deal involving young talent like Stephon Castle would have been superior to what was ultimately accepted.

The Miami Gamble and the CBA Reality

The analysis shifts to Miami, where Silver sees a calculated risk that defies traditional wisdom. He writes, "Something I've learned to not underrate is the value of short-term championship equity." The Heat are betting that acquiring a superstar like Giannis clears the highest hurdle in the league: finding a centerpiece around which to build. As Silver puts it, "It's more or less impossible to sign your way to a top-five player, while rotation pieces, by contrast, are a renewable resource."

This argument is bolstered by the NBA salary cap context. The Collective Bargaining Agreement makes it nearly impossible for teams to sign multiple max-contract stars, but once you have one, filling out the roster with minimum-salary veterans becomes feasible. Silver draws a parallel to history: "The trade reminds me a bit of the Kevin Garnett sweepstakes in 2007... If the 'Big Three' hadn't won a ring together, would the trade be considered as much of a win?" The implication is clear: Miami doesn't need Giannis to be healthy for five years; they just need him to carry them through one or two playoff runs.

"It's more or less impossible to sign your way to a top-five player, while rotation pieces, by contrast, are a renewable resource."

However, the risk is palpable. Silver notes that Giannis "hasn't finished a season truly healthy since 2022." The Heat are betting on a player whose durability is questionable, trading away future draft capital in a league where the new lottery system has made those picks even more valuable. While Miami's market and coaching staff under Erik Spoelstra offer an edge, the clock is ticking loudly.

The Jaylen Brown Aftermath and the Wolves' Pivot

The ripple effects of the Giannis saga extend far beyond Milwaukee and Miami. Silver highlights a bizarre twist for Boston: "Had the trade gone through [with Giannis], the Eastern Conference landscape would have shifted drastically in favor of the Celtics." Instead, they are left with Jaylen Brown, who is now reportedly unhappy. Silver observes that "swapping him and Giannis for only two additional picks would have been highway robbery," suggesting Boston's hesitation to part with their star was actually a strategic blunder.

The author predicts Brown's departure: "I'd be surprised if he's still on Boston's roster to start next season." The reasoning is grounded in advanced metrics which show Brown as a high-volume scorer but one whose passing and turnover rates limit his ceiling. "Even with his scoring getting directionally better, there are clear ceilings on what he can do in a lead role," Silver writes. This creates a unique market dynamic where a player with All-NBA honors might be valued lower by analytics but higher by teams desperate for shot creators.

Meanwhile, the Minnesota Timberwolves made a move that flew under the radar but carries massive implications. In trading Julius Randle and acquiring LaMelo Ball (plus Josh Green) for Naz Reid and draft assets, they engaged in what Silver calls "a bet against Minnesota's stability." The logic here is counterintuitive: by trading for a volatile star like Ball, the Wolves are hedging their bets on Anthony Edwards' potential unhappiness. If Edwards demands a trade, the Wolves can flip Ball or use the swaps to acquire lottery picks.

"The bet is mostly that either LaMelo will be injured or Anthony Edwards demands a trade, which would let one of those swaps actually land in the lottery."

This strategy reflects a modern NBA trend where teams are willing to embrace volatility if it means securing future assets. Silver notes that this move pays off "given the high bar established by the Spurs and Thunder in the West," suggesting that only a chaotic, high-upside gamble can compete with the asset-rich contenders.

Bottom Line

Nate Silver's commentary excels at reframing emotional sports narratives into cold financial realities, exposing how hesitation cost the Bucks their best rebuild opportunity while Miami makes a high-variance bet on short-term equity. The argument is strongest when dissecting the intersection of advanced metrics and contract leverage, though it arguably underestimates the intangible value of retaining a franchise icon in Milwaukee. Readers should watch closely to see if Miami's "championship equity" translates to a title or if the clock runs out on an aging superstar before the roster can be filled.

The trade reminds me a bit of the Kevin Garnett sweepstakes in 2007 — the Celtics traded a lot of their middling depth for the aging superstar and ended up winning a championship... If the 'Big Three' hadn't won a ring together, would the trade be considered as much of a win?"
