Strikes, Summits and the Possibility of Boots on the Ground in Iran: The Week That Shaped Two Wars. The Big Five, 5 April Edition

Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Mick Ryan delivers a rare, granular look at how two simultaneous wars are reshaping global power dynamics, not through abstract geopolitics, but through the hard data of drone ranges, refinery output, and the terrifying efficiency of robotic ground troops. While most coverage treats the Middle East and Eastern Europe as separate theaters, Ryan argues they are inextricably linked by a single, escalating logic of attrition that threatens to boil over into direct great-power confrontation.

The Long Arm of the Strike

Ryan's most striking contribution is his detailed mapping of Ukraine's ability to project power deep into Russia's industrial heartland, a capability that has shifted from experimental to industrial scale. He writes, "Strikes are penetrating over 1,000 kilometres into Russian territory on a near-nightly basis," a fact that fundamentally alters the strategic calculus for Moscow. The author doesn't just list targets; he connects the dots between a fire at the Bashneft-Novoil refinery in Ufa and the subsequent ban on Russian gasoline exports, showing how tactical strikes are strangling the Russian economy's blood supply.

This narrative is bolstered by the introduction of Ukraine's new FP-5 Flamingo cruise missile. Ryan notes that with a 3,000-kilometer range and a massive 1,150-kilogram warhead, the weapon is "bringing all of Russia's European industrial and defence complex within reach." This is a critical escalation. Just as the FP-5 Flamingo deep dive revealed the missile's technical specifications, Ryan's coverage shows how its deployment is already forcing Moscow to ban gasoline exports, a move that has left gas stations across multiple regions running dry. The evidence suggests Ukraine is no longer just defending; it is actively dismantling the Russian war machine's logistical backbone.

The cumulative effect on Russia's Baltic export capacity is significant, with at least 40% of oil shipping capacity disrupted by late March.

However, the author also highlights a fascinating, shadowy expansion of this campaign. Ryan details a French investigation confirming that Ukraine is running a Mediterranean drone campaign from bases in Libya, a move that mirrors the strategic ingenuity seen in the Operation Odyssey Dawn era but with a distinctly Ukrainian twist. He writes, "It might be described as Ukraine's Mediterranean drone line!" This infrastructure allows Kyiv to strike Russian shadow fleet tankers, such as the Arctic Metagaz, which sank in international waters. Critics might argue that stretching operations to Libya overextends Ukrainian resources, yet Ryan's data on the sinking of the Qendil and the potential elimination of a senior GRU general suggests this is a high-value, low-risk asymmetry that Moscow cannot easily counter.

The Air War and the Ground Grind

The commentary shifts to the brutal reality of the aerial bombardment, where Ryan presents a chilling statistic: Russia fired at least 6,462 long-range drones against Ukraine in March 2026 alone. He observes that "Russia's method, exhausting Ukrainian air defences overnight then following with daytime swarms, exploits the finite capacity of any layered air defence system." This is not just a numbers game; it is a strategy of systematic exhaustion. Ryan points out that despite a near-90% interception rate, the sheer volume of attacks is designed to wear down the defender's will and infrastructure, a tactic that recently targeted water supplies and logistics.

On the ground, the narrative is one of stubborn resilience rather than breakthrough. Ryan writes, "Thus far, the Russians have failed to generate any operational momentum with their spring offensive." He contrasts the meager 17 square miles gained by Russia in late March with the 47 square miles gained in the same period last year, suggesting a war of attrition that is bleeding the Russian army dry. The author notes that Russian training cycles have been slashed from one month to one week, a "desperate measure that degrades the quality of those being fed into the assault." This detail underscores the fragility of the Russian advance, even as they claim to have "liberated" the last 0.2% of Luhansk.

The Robot Revolution

Perhaps the most forward-looking section of Ryan's piece is his analysis of Unmanned Ground Vehicles (UGVs). He describes a decisive shift from "experimental phase to industrial deployment," citing the K2 Brigade as commanding "the world's first dedicated UGV battalion." The author illustrates this with a haunting image: "Three Russian soldiers surrendered to a Ukrainian UGV near Lyman in January. This was possibly the first recorded case in warfare of troops capitulating to a remotely piloted ground vehicle."

Ryan argues that these machines are not just toys but force multipliers that could "reduce frontline infantry requirements by up to 30% by year's end." With Ukraine facing a manpower deficit of 300,000, this technological pivot is existential. He notes that in the 3rd Assault Brigade, UGVs now handle 80% of logistics, a figure that rises to 90% in contested cities. This is a profound transformation in the nature of warfare, where the average age of a frontline soldier is 43 to 45, and robots are taking over the most dangerous tasks. A counterargument worth considering is whether this reliance on technology creates new vulnerabilities to electronic warfare, but Ryan's data on the sheer volume of production—15,000 units delivered in 2025—suggests a scale that is difficult to disrupt.

The Global Flashpoint

Finally, Ryan weaves the Ukraine conflict into the broader geopolitical tapestry, noting the precarious situation in the Middle East. He writes, "How, and if, he follows through on this threat to 'unleash hell' on Iran if it does not open the Strait of Hormuz in 48 hours will be interesting to watch." The author warns that while America postures for boots on the ground, Iran continues to lob missiles at civil infrastructure, creating a volatile mix with no clear pathway to de-escalation. This tension threatens to delay the Xi-Trump summit and destabilize the Pacific, where Japan is already deploying long-range strike weapons. The interplay between the drone strikes in the Mediterranean, the ground war in Ukraine, and the threats in the Strait of Hormuz creates a global pressure cooker that demands immediate attention.

The war in Iran continues with no clear pathway to de-escalation, and a theory of victory beyond 'keep smashing until they surrender' has not yet emerged.

Bottom Line

Mick Ryan's strongest argument is his ability to connect the dots between Ukraine's technological innovation in UGVs and long-range strikes with the broader strategic stagnation of the Russian offensive. His biggest vulnerability is the inherent uncertainty of the Middle East theater, where a single miscalculation could derail the entire global balance he describes. Readers should watch closely for the next wave of UGV deployments and any signs of direct US military intervention in the Strait of Hormuz, as these will likely define the next phase of the conflict.

As I hit ‘send’ on this weekly update, initial reports are appearing in social media about the rescue of the second crew member of the U.S. Air Force F-15E that was shot down over Iran 48 hours ago. While there is no definitive confirmation just yet, I hope that this downed crew member is rescued safe and well soon.

In the last few hours, the American president issued yet another social media ultimatum to the Iranian regime. How, and if, he follows through on this threat to “unleash hell” on Iran if it does not open the Strait of Hormuz in 48 hours will be interesting to watch.

In Ukraine this week, Ukrainian forces struck deeper into Russia’s industrial heartland than at any point in the war and revealed that its Mediterranean drone campaign is being run from bases in Libya. Ukraine was also on the receiving end of a 579 drone and missile Russian aerial attack, which was partly answered by a Ukrainian Easter ceasefire proposal - answered by the Russians with continued bombardment. On the ground, Ukrainian forces have continued to hold the line in Donetsk against a Russian spring offensive that has – so far – failed to generate operational momentum.

The war in Iran continues with no clear pathway to de-escalation. Despite the speech by President Trump during the week, a theory of victory beyond “keep smashing until they surrender” has not yet emerged. America is posturing and threatening to put boots on the ground, while Iran continues to lob missiles and drones at military bases, and civil infrastructure, in countries across the Middle East.

In the Pacific, preparations continue for the forthcoming Xi-Trump summit, although given how the war in Iran is progressing, watch for another delay proposed by Trump. The defence budget impasse in Taiwan continues, and Japan deploys more long-range strike weapons to protect its territory and deter expanded Chinese military aggression in the western Pacific.
