Super Bowl Ad Announces 4th Amendment Nightmare

by Devin Stone · LegalEagle · Read full article

During the Super Bowl, millions of Americans watched a cute commercial about neighbors teaming up to find a lost dog. A But they also saw cameras everywhere. Cameras that track and talk to one another through AI. They saw a crowdsourced surveillance state.

The company behind that ad was Ring. Ring is owned by Jeff Bezos. And behind Ring was a planned partnership with Flock Safety. Flock Safety is Ring, but for cops and feds, etc.

And that's where the constitutional questions begin. Questions that are being litigated in court right now. Because as Ben Franklin famously warned, those who would give up essential liberty to purchase a little temporary safety for their puppies deserve neither liberty nor puppies or something like that. In October 2025, Ring announced a planned partnership with Flock Safety, a surveillance technology firm known for its automated license plate reader systems and other law enforcement tools.

The apparent goal was to integrate Flock technology with Ring's existing community request feature, which lets Ring camera owners voluntarily share video footage with law enforcement in active investigations. Under the partnership, Ring users would have had the option to allow video requested by police to be handled via Flock's platform, potentially giving authorities streamlined access to camera footage from private homes and neighborhoods as part of public safety inquiries. At the time, the announcement barely registered, but that changed after the Super Bowl. Ring's Super Bowl ad introduced a feature called Search Party, an AIdriven tool designed to help find lost pets by scanning footage from Ring cameras across a neighborhood.

Even Matt Nelson, the guy behind We Rate Dogs, posted a short video that sums up what pretty much all of us were thinking. Another report found that a police department in Texas did a warrantless nationwide search of over 83,000 flock cameras with the reason for the search logged as had an abortion, search for female. In its ad, Ring claims that the search party feature finds one dog a day. This would equate to roughly 03% of the over 1 million lost or found pet reports posted to the Ring app annually.

Ring and Flock stressed that search party wasn't connected to the partnership. But the commercial premiered right as immigration enforcement is ramping up and local police departments are arguing over whether to assist ICE, which made the timing, let's just say less than ...
