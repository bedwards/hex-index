How Singapore Plans To Pipe Electricity From Australia

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Apr 16, 2021 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Asianometry tackles a proposition that sounds like science fiction but is rapidly becoming engineering reality: piping solar energy from the Australian outback to Singapore across 3,700 kilometers of ocean. The piece stands out not just for its ambition, but for its granular dissection of why High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) is the only viable physics for this distance, moving beyond the usual "green energy is good" rhetoric to explain the brutal economics of the seabed. For a busy professional tracking energy security, the argument that this project could supply 20% of Singapore's daily needs while unlocking Australia's vast, underutilized solar potential offers a concrete glimpse into the future of Southeast Asian infrastructure.

The Physics of Distance

Asianometry begins by grounding the reader in Singapore's precarious energy reality, noting that "ninety-five percent of Singapore's electricity comes from burning natural gas" with virtually no room for domestic renewables. The author correctly identifies that the solution cannot be local; it must be imported. The core of the argument rests on a technical distinction that often gets glossed over in policy debates: the difference between Alternating Current (AC) and Direct Current (DC). As Asianometry explains, "AC transformers do all this more efficiently and affordably than equivalent DC equipment," which is why AC dominates our grids. However, this efficiency collapses over long distances due to the "skin effect," where AC current crowds the surface of the cable, increasing resistance.

This is where the project's engineering logic becomes compelling. The author notes that while DC terminal costs are high, "the cost of the line rises faster with AC than DC the further out you get." By the time you reach the break-even distance of roughly 100 kilometers, DC becomes the only economic choice. For a 3,700-kilometer undersea link, the math is undeniable. Asianometry writes, "assuming that electrical losses for hvdc cable are about three percent per 1000 kilometers the sun cables 3 200 kilometers is estimated to will have about 10 percent electrical losses in total. An equivalent hvac line is likely to have over twice that." This quantitative comparison effectively shuts down the idea that this could be done with traditional grid technology.

Critics might note that the reliance on HVDC introduces a single point of failure; if the cable snaps, the entire import line goes dark. However, the author mitigates this by pointing to the Three Gorges Dam as a precedent, proving that HVDC is already the standard for moving massive power over vast distances.

"Without hvdc a lot of renewable energy sources like offshore wind or hydro would not be so economically tenable."

The Seabed Gauntlet

Moving from theory to practice, the commentary shifts to the immense logistical nightmare of laying a cable across some of the world's most treacherous and busy waters. Asianometry does not shy away from the geography, identifying the Timor Trough as a "major challenge" where the cable must traverse waters over a mile deep. The author draws a sharp parallel to the Sapei cable, noting that deep-water installations require specialized materials like aluminum to reduce weight, even if it means trading some conductivity for feasibility. "Since aluminum cable is also lighter than copper you can carry and lay more of it from your cable laying vessel," the author observes, highlighting the delicate balance between material science and maritime logistics.

The analysis becomes even more critical when addressing the human element of the seabed. The Java Sea and Singapore Strait are described as "some of the busiest waterways in the world," crisscrossed by fishing trawlers and shipping lanes. Asianometry writes, "engineers will likely have to trench or bury the cable some two to five feet 0.6 to 1.5 meters deep to protect the cable from being dredged up by fishing nets." This detail is crucial; it transforms the project from a theoretical power line into a complex diplomatic and legal undertaking involving multiple nations and thousands of maritime actors. The risk of damage is not just technical but operational, potentially triggering liability clauses that could bankrupt the project.

The Economic Reality Check

Perhaps the most sobering section of the coverage is the financial breakdown. The project is not just expensive; it is unprecedented in scale. Asianometry points out that the subsea cable alone is projected to cost "about 5.6 billion usd," with the majority going toward the cable itself. This dwarfs existing projects like the Basslink, which cost $670 million. The author astutely notes that the manufacturing capacity for such a massive cable simply does not exist today. "The sun cable as currently proposed uses two cables of 3200 kilometers each more than the total annual output of all the hvdc cable manufacturers in europe," Asianometry writes. This implies a lead time of three to five years just to build the supply chain, a detail that often gets lost in optimistic press releases.

The funding model is another area where the author provides necessary skepticism. While billionaire seed funding exists, the project will likely require a multi-decade service agreement to attract institutional capital. Asianometry draws a cautionary tale from the Basslink project, which suffered a brutal outage and subsequent arbitration that "forced the company into a technical default." The author warns, "it makes me wonder about the feasibility of doing infrastructure like this using these public-private type partnerships." This is a vital counterpoint to the techno-optimism; the engineering might work, but the contract law could be the breaking point.

The Strategic Horizon

Despite the hurdles, the strategic implications are profound. The project offers Australia a way to diversify its economy beyond mining and fossil fuels, leveraging its Northern Territory's solar irradiation. Asianometry writes, "Australia's current economy is heavily dependent on mining petroleum and exports of platypuses," a witty but pointed observation on the need for economic evolution. The project could also serve as a blueprint for the region, with Indonesia potentially signing on to receive power before Singapore. "From 2015 to 2040 energy demand from the southeast asian countries is estimated to expand 65 almost all of it from fossil fuels unless otherwise there's ample demand for clean energy," the author argues, framing the project as a necessary intervention in the region's carbon trajectory.

The timeline is tight, with a scheduled online date of 2027. Asianometry concludes with a sense of cautious wonder: "that year feels some like something right out of science fiction. It lasts less than a decade away." The piece succeeds by balancing the sheer audacity of the vision with a rigorous accounting of the physics, geography, and economics that must align for it to succeed.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's strongest contribution is demystifying the physics of HVDC, proving that this is an engineering inevitability rather than a political whim. However, the project's biggest vulnerability lies not in the ocean depths, but in the supply chain bottlenecks and the complex legal frameworks required to manage a multi-national, multi-decade infrastructure megaproject. Readers should watch for the next milestone: the finalization of the route and the securing of long-term power purchase agreements, which will determine if this science fiction becomes reality.
