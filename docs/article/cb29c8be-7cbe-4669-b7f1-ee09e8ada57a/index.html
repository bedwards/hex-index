Brad DeLong resurrects a 2022 debate to expose a profound psychological fracture within the American intellectual left, arguing that the current war on "woke" language is less about policy and more about the personal resentments of a specific generation of academics. The piece is notable not for its critique of buzzwords, but for its forensic dissection of why a former New Leftist like Russell Jacoby would pivot to the Paleolithic Right, suggesting that the "snowflake" narrative is actually a defense mechanism for those who never received the institutional validation they believed they deserved.

The Psychology of Resentment

DeLong begins by framing Jacoby's recent arguments as a "horrible atrocity of defending good causes badly," a phrase that captures the tragedy of the situation. He notes that Jacoby, once a sympathetic voice against the "theory silliness" of the 1980s and 90s, has now updated his script to claim that leftists without academic power are actually the most dangerous. As DeLong writes, "Their weakness is their terrible strength!" This observation lands because it flips the standard conservative narrative on its head, suggesting that the fear of the "barbarians at the gates" is a projection of the author's own insecurity rather than a reflection of reality.

The commentary then pivots to the specific targets of Jacoby's ire: the "buzz words" of modern discourse. DeLong paraphrases Jacoby's claim that terms like "microaggression" and "white privilege" have become "noxious off campus," but he immediately counters this by grounding the concepts in lived experience. DeLong writes, "Time spent reflecting upon how to make the social world less of a zero-sum dominance and more of a positive-sum cooperation game is rarely wasted." This reframing is crucial; it suggests that the vocabulary Jacoby dismisses is actually a tool for empathy, not a weapon of exclusion.

"If you are an elderly or middle-aged straight white male in America who does not know that you have been playing the videogame of life on the 'easy' setting, I judge you to be a pathetic loser."

DeLong's use of the "easy mode" metaphor is striking because it strips away the abstract nature of privilege and makes it personal. He argues that acknowledging one's luck is a form of self-knowledge, citing Milton and Rose Friedman as examples of people who recognized their own "supremely lucky" status. This contrasts sharply with Jacoby's apparent inability to see his own advantages. A counterargument worth considering is that for many, the "easy mode" narrative can feel dismissive of genuine structural barriers they face, even if they are white; however, DeLong's point remains that denying the baseline advantages of one's demographic is a failure of historical awareness.

The Ghost of the Ivory Tower

The piece takes a sharp turn into biography, where DeLong reconstructs Jacoby's career path to explain his current bitterness. He notes that Jacoby spent decades bouncing between short-term, non-renewable positions at institutions like UCLA, UC Irvine, and Simon Fraser University before finally landing a semi-permanent role. DeLong writes, "He always saw himself as failing to run the course in the expected time, as hanging onto his academic post by his fingernails." This biographical detail is the engine of the entire argument; it suggests that Jacoby's hostility toward "snowflakes" is actually a displacement of his own fear of obsolescence.

DeLong connects this to the broader history of Western Marxism, noting that Jacoby's work cast a long shadow on the field, yet he remained an outsider. He references the "Long March from New Left" to the "Paleolithic Right," a journey that mirrors the trajectory of many intellectuals who felt the academy betrayed them. DeLong writes, "Max Weber would have understood. Weber strongly cautioned those seeking academic careers thus: 'Do you in all conscience believe that you can stand seeing mediocrity after mediocrity, year after year, climb beyond you, without becoming embittered and without coming to grief?'" This quote serves as a grim warning that the academic system is designed to produce resentment, and Jacoby is merely a victim of its machinery.

The commentary also touches on the specific incident involving the New York Times and the Tom Cotton op-ed, which Jacoby uses as a prime example of "snowflake" behavior. DeLong paraphrases Jacoby's view that the editor's resignation was a sign of weakness, but he reframes it by highlighting the gravity of the op-ed itself. "Cotton explicitly called for the extrajudicial execution of American citizens, en masse, by the US military," DeLong notes, arguing that the staff's reaction was a rational response to a threat, not a fragile overreaction. This distinction is vital, as it separates legitimate safety concerns from the "barbarism" Jacoby claims to fear.

The Failure of Empathy

DeLong concludes by examining Jacoby's personal history, including his time in a Boston bookstore collective and his rejection of psychoanalysis. He suggests that Jacoby's inability to accept the concept of "white privilege" stems from a deep-seated belief that his own ancestors—the Jacobys—were the true strivers, while others were "entitled parasites." DeLong writes, "Maybe what Jacoby really wants to say, but cannot quite bring himself to, is that his ancestors' American white-ethnic experience was not all that easy." This insight is powerful because it humanizes the political opponent, showing that the "culture war" is often just a proxy for unresolved personal trauma.

The piece also draws a parallel to the historical figure of the Count of St. Germain, a mysterious 18th-century adventurer who claimed exotic pasts and moved among elites. DeLong uses this reference to illustrate the importance of respecting how people present themselves: "If a man is introduced to you as M. le Comte de St.-Germain, you refer to him as M. le Comte or St. Germain." This anecdote serves as a metaphor for the broader argument: civilization is built on empathy and the willingness to take others' self-presentation at face value, whereas barbarism is the refusal to do so.

Critics might note that DeLong's psychological profiling of Jacoby risks ad hominem attacks, shifting focus from the validity of the arguments to the character of the arguer. However, the strength of the piece lies in its ability to show how personal history shapes public discourse, making the abstract debate about "buzzwords" feel immediate and human.

Bottom Line

DeLong's most compelling argument is that the backlash against progressive language is often a symptom of institutional insecurity rather than a genuine defense of Western civilization. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its heavy reliance on biographical speculation, which, while insightful, cannot fully account for the broader cultural shifts Jacoby describes. Readers should watch for how this dynamic of "resentment vs. empathy" plays out in future debates over academic freedom and social justice.
