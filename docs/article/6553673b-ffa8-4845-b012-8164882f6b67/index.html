Your Phone Is Listening - Ultrasonic Cross-Device Tracking

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Oct 25, 2020 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

The Hated One delivers a chilling revelation that cuts through the noise of standard privacy warnings: your devices are not just listening for your voice commands, they are actively broadcasting and receiving inaudible signals to link your digital identities without your knowledge. This piece distinguishes itself by exposing how ultrasonic tracking bypasses every traditional defense, from airplane mode to Tor networks, turning your own hardware into a clandestine surveillance node. In an era where we obsess over cookie blockers and ad trackers, this is the invisible layer of monitoring that renders those tools obsolete.

The Silent Beacon

The core of the argument rests on the seamless, permissionless nature of this technology. The Hated One explains that the mechanism is deceptively simple yet terrifyingly effective: "everywhere you go and whatever you might be doing as long as you have an electronic device with a microphone speakers or gyroscope that device might be listening." This is not a hypothetical future threat; it is a current reality where your television acts as a beacon and your phone as a receiver. The author illustrates this with a visceral example, noting that "your tv will emit ultrasounds that you won't able to hear but your phone and your dog will." The comparison to a dog's reaction serves as a stark reminder that the signal is physically present, even if our human senses are blind to it.

What makes this coverage particularly urgent is the realization that software toggles are insufficient. The Hated One writes, "you won't be able to stop it even if you disable location services or take your phone completely offline." This is a critical distinction from standard tracking; ultrasonic signals travel through the air, independent of cellular or Wi-Fi networks. The author argues that this method is "completely permissionless and secret," meaning users are never notified when their device is participating in this data exchange. Critics might note that the practical utility of this tracking is limited by bandwidth, but as the author points out, "it is just enough to transfer identifiers from your phone and nearby beacons to communicate your proximity location and fingerprinting information." That small packet of data is all an advertiser needs to stitch your life together.

Ultrasonic tracking has proven to be so effective it can even de-anonymize tor users.

The Hardware Trap

The piece takes a darker turn when it reveals that the vulnerability extends beyond microphones to sensors we rarely consider. The Hated One details how researchers found that "gyroscope sensors... are designed to have a resonance frequency between 19 to 29 kilohertz which is well within the ultrasonic range." This means the very sensor used to rotate your screen can be weaponized to transmit data. The author emphasizes the severity of this by stating, "using gyroscopes doesn't usually require special permissions which is why this is a zero permission access." This effectively closes the door on the most common user defense: revoking microphone access.

The coverage also explores the terrifying potential of "Dolphin attacks," where ultrasonic commands can hijack voice assistants. The Hated One writes, "they were able to send ultrasonic commands to iphones and amazon echo devices essentially taking full control over their virtual assistants." This moves the threat from passive tracking to active exploitation. Furthermore, the author notes that even if you physically remove the microphone, "ultrasonic tracking will still be doable via its speakers." The implication is clear: the hardware itself is compromised. The author suggests that "the best defense mechanism would be with microphone hardware switches that completely cut off electric current," yet admits these are currently absent from mainstream devices. A counterargument worth considering is that the complexity of this attack vector might limit its widespread adoption compared to simpler tracking methods, but the author rightly argues that the potential for a "mesh network of audio beacons" makes the risk unacceptable.

The Corporate Ecosystem

The final section of the commentary shifts to the economic engine driving this surveillance. The Hated One argues that advertisers are obsessed with this technology because it solves the "attribution problem" across devices. "If you saw an ad on your phone and went on to check that product on your laptop audiobeacons can link this activity," the author explains. This creates a deterministic identity that is far more accurate than probabilistic guessing. The piece reveals that major brands like McDonald's and Krispy Kreme have been found using these SDKs, embedding the tracking in apps downloaded millions of times.

The author highlights the lack of transparency, noting that "finding out which apps and which media companies are utilizing ultrasonic cross device tracking is no easy task." Even Google's requirement for disclosure in privacy policies is rendered ineffective because the technology is often embedded in ad scripts rather than the app code itself. The Hated One concludes that "corporations researchers hackers and are going to continue investing into this technology and are bound to find ways to make ultrasonic tracking more and more effective." The only viable path forward, according to the text, is a fundamental shift in hardware design, as software solutions have already been outmaneuvered.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest contribution is the dismantling of the illusion that standard privacy settings offer meaningful protection against modern surveillance; the argument that hardware sensors like gyroscopes can bypass software permissions is a game-changer for privacy advocates. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its lack of concrete, accessible solutions for the average user, leaving them with the daunting task of switching to niche operating systems like Graphene OS. Readers should watch for upcoming regulatory battles over sensor permissions, as the industry is currently racing to standardize these ultrasonic features for legitimate device pairing, potentially locking in this invasive capability forever.
