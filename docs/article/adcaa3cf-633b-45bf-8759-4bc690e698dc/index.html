Gravity Energy Storage. Who's right and who's wrong?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Jul 10, 2022 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through the hype of futuristic energy solutions by exposing a stark divide between flashy concepts and engineering pragmatism. While the world chases the promise of massive, free-standing towers, Borlace argues that the real breakthrough in gravity storage lies not in building new structures, but in repurposing the earth's own scars—decommissioned mine shafts. This is a crucial pivot for anyone trying to understand the actual trajectory of grid-scale storage, moving the conversation from science fiction renderings to the gritty reality of geology and mechanics.

The Tower That Never Was

Borlace begins by revisiting Energy Vault, a company that initially proposed a towering crane system to lift and drop 30-ton blocks. The concept was visually arresting but physically dubious. "Suffice to say there were some very significant questions of physics that appeared not to have been addressed," Borlace notes, highlighting the absurdity of blocks at the bottom of a pile having zero potential energy and the structural nightmare of keeping 30-ton weights vertical in high winds. The company has since pivoted to a massive, stadium-sized warehouse design, yet Borlace remains skeptical of the infrastructure required to support it.

He points out that the current proposal is merely a "computer-generated rendering" featuring "little white blobby trees," with no physical construction to show for it. "I must admit it does look like an awful lot of expensive infrastructure to support the loads involved," he writes, suggesting that the maintenance costs for such a complex system could be prohibitive. The argument here is compelling because it forces the reader to look past the marketing gloss. Critics might note that Energy Vault's pivot shows adaptability, but Borlace's insistence on physical evidence over digital renderings remains a necessary check on over-optimism.

"It's bloody hard work to get from design concept to a real world commercial operation."

The Pragmatic Alternative

In sharp contrast, Borlace turns his attention to Gravitricity, an Edinburgh-based firm taking a far more grounded approach. Their strategy relies on the planet itself to support the weight, utilizing existing mine shafts rather than constructing new towers. "Arguably the most fundamental difference between the two concepts is that Gravitricity believed that... the only serious way to achieve safe reliable dispatchable grid-scale gravity energy storage is to use the geology of the earth," Borlace explains. This approach mirrors pumped hydro but without the need for specific topographical features like mountains and valleys.

The company recently validated their concept with a small-scale test rig, proving that the system could handle the dynamic forces of stopping and starting massive weights. "Engineering simulations showed that the system would operate within acceptable parameters," Borlace reports, confirming that the mechanics are sound. The system uses winches similar to regenerative braking in electric vehicles, converting the potential energy of falling weights directly into electricity. With projected operational lifetimes exceeding 50 years and cables capable of 75,000 cycles, the durability argument is strong.

The financial case is equally robust. Borlace highlights a study by Imperial College London which suggests the Levelized Cost of Storage (LCOS) for Gravitricity stacks up favorably against lithium-ion batteries. "For a storage system with a discharge time of two to six hours... the Gravitricity system stacks up very favorably indeed," he writes. This is a critical data point for grid operators looking for long-duration storage that doesn't degrade like chemical batteries. A counterargument worth considering is the geographic limitation; while there are many closed mines, not every grid needs storage in a mining region, potentially limiting the scale of deployment compared to a modular tower system.

"There's no amount of slick marketing will make the process move any faster."

The Path Forward

The ultimate test for Gravitricity will be execution. Borlace outlines a realistic timeline, noting that the company is currently in an 18-month engineering design phase, with the first full-scale facility potentially operational by 2025 or 2026. The company is already engaging with mine owners in the Czech Republic, Poland, and beyond, turning the European Union's push to close coal mines into an opportunity for energy infrastructure.

Borlace praises the team for their "pragmatic and realistic" approach, acknowledging that they understand the slow, unglamorous nature of heavy engineering. "Their system does look like it could serve a very useful purpose in a very large number of otherwise useless and redundant locations all over the world," he concludes. This framing shifts the narrative from a race to build the biggest tower to a strategic integration of energy storage with industrial legacy sites. The strength of this argument lies in its alignment with existing assets, reducing both capital expenditure and environmental impact.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage effectively demystifies gravity energy storage by separating the physically impossible from the merely difficult. His strongest move is contrasting Energy Vault's unproven, infrastructure-heavy vision with Gravitricity's geology-dependent pragmatism, backed by concrete engineering data. The biggest vulnerability remains the timeline; while the physics checks out, the delay until 2025 means the technology may miss the immediate urgency of current grid instability, leaving readers to watch and wait for the first real-world proof of concept.
