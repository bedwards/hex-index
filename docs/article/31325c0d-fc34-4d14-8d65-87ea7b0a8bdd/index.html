Weekend update #191: It is not just oil refineries

Phillips P. O'Brien · Phillips P. O'Brien ·Jun 28, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

This piece cuts through the noise of daily headlines to reveal a quiet but devastating shift in the war: Ukraine is no longer just striking fuel depots; it is surgically dismantling Russia's ability to manufacture the very weapons used to bomb Ukrainian cities. Phillips P. O'Brien brings a level of granular detail on specific industrial complexes that transforms abstract "long-range strikes" into a tangible narrative of supply chain collapse. For the busy reader, this is not just a list of targets; it is an analysis of how a nation's war machine is being starved at its source.

The Industrial Heartland Under Siege

O'Brien opens by reframing the conversation away from oil and toward the factories that build missiles. He writes, "More and more the Ukrainians are going after the Russian ability to create the components and finished munitions needed to wage their war." This is a critical pivot in understanding the conflict's trajectory. The author argues that while refinery attacks grab headlines for their immediate economic pain, the strikes on military-industrial targets strike at the long-term viability of Russia's offensive capabilities.

The evidence provided is specific and unsettling. O'Brien details an attack on the Titan-Barrikady weapons complex in Volgograd, a city steeped in history as Stalingrad during the 1942-1943 battle that broke the back of the German advance. He notes that President Zelensky's commentary on the strike revealed a deeper strategic intent: "The Ukrainians... are trying to reduce Russia's ability to maintain its long-range campaign against Ukraine by limiting the ability of the Russians to make their most effective missiles in the first place."

If that can be reduced by even 25%, that will do the job of a large number of Patriot missiles and save a lot of Ukrainian lives.

This calculation is the core of O'Brien's argument: attrition works both ways, but targeting production lines yields a multiplier effect on defense. He lists a cascade of targeted facilities, from the Progress Plant in Michurinsk, which makes navigation systems for missiles, to the Azot Chemical Plant, a major producer of ammonia and nitrogen fertilizers that are essential precursors for explosives. The author points out that these are not random targets but critical nodes: "This is an attempt to damage Russia's own long-range strike campaign."

Critics might argue that industrial complexes can be repaired or production moved underground, but O'Brien suggests the Russian system is fragile, noting that Moscow is "living pretty much 'hand-to-mouth' when it comes to the production of some of their most effective systems." The sheer geographic spread of these attacks—from Cheboksary in the east to Volgograd in the south—forces Russia to stretch its air defenses thin. As O'Brien observes, "Russia will have to do a better job protecting its missile building capacity. That will take more systems and more ammunition spread around the vastness of Russia."

The Human Cost of Fuel Shortages

While the industrial strikes target military output, the campaign against refineries is hitting Russian civilians with immediate, visceral force. O'Brien shifts focus to the Lukoil-Nizhegorodorgsintez refinery in Kstovo, where a strike damaged a primary unit capable of processing 12,000 metric tons of oil daily. The consequence is not just a statistic but a tangible shortage: "Fuel shortages are appearing across Russia."

The author highlights the cascading failure this causes for civilian life and infrastructure. He writes, "These attacks are hitting civilians not just with long-lines at the pump or the inability to drive." The situation has deteriorated to the point where commercial aviation is struggling, with reports of high-grade fuel supplies down by a third. O'Brien points out a particularly dangerous development: "Things are reaching such a state that the Russians are reportedly on the verge of using automobile fuel to power their smaller aircraft—which is incredibly dangerous (think explosions)."

This section underscores the dual nature of modern warfare: the strategic goal of degrading military logistics inevitably bleeds into the daily survival of the population. O'Brien notes that Russia may soon be forced to buy fuel on the open market from India, a move that would drain state coffers just as oil prices fall. The human cost is implicit in every line about rationing and flight cancellations, yet it remains the driving force behind the economic pressure on the Kremlin.

The Diplomatic Disconnect

The final section of O'Brien's commentary addresses a jarring contradiction: the gap between public rhetoric and policy action regarding Russia's economy. He critiques the current administration for quietly removing sanctions that protect Russian oligarchs and their enablers. "Very quietly, the US government removed sanctions on important Russian businessmen, Russian ships and even some Turkish companies that do business with Russia," O'Brien writes.

He details how seven Russian nationals, including the son of a metals giant's owner, were cleared to operate freely, alongside ship owners linked to state leasing companies. The author finds it particularly telling that a Turkish elevator maker was also de-sanctioned, despite being originally targeted for trading in high-quality electronics components vital to the war effort. O'Brien argues this is part of an effort "to get back to some business 'normality' with Putin."

The most stinging critique comes when addressing the administration's stance on Iran and Russia's role there. O'Brien writes, "Trump made one of his most astonishing public lies recently about how Putin did not help Iran in the recent war," quoting the claim that Putin was "very neutral." The author dismantles this by listing the concrete support Russia provided to Iran, from drone components to targeting intelligence. He concludes with a sharp observation on the disconnect between words and deeds: "So Trump has recently gone to great lengths to lie and protect Putin and to loosen sanctions on the Russian economy. But hey, he did not insult Ukraine. People are such rubes."

The reality, as always, is that the administration... is bending over backwards, doing large things and small, to help Russia.

This framing forces the reader to look past the soundbites of diplomatic niceties and examine the material reality of policy decisions. It suggests that while public statements may offer fleeting praise for Ukraine's resilience, the underlying economic policies continue to lubricate the very machinery Russia uses to wage war.

Bottom Line

Phillips P. O'Brien delivers a compelling case that the war has entered a new phase where targeting production capacity is as vital as striking fuel reserves, potentially saving thousands of Ukrainian lives by slowing the flow of munitions to the front. The piece's greatest strength lies in its granular evidence of industrial disruption, though it risks oversimplifying the resilience of Russia's supply chains. Readers should watch closely whether these strikes can sustain their momentum or if the administration's contradictory economic policies will ultimately blunt Ukraine's strategic gains.
