Software Abundance for Government

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Mar 10, 2026 · 39 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider and Russell Kaplan don't just predict that artificial intelligence will write code faster; they argue it will finally break the $100 billion annual stranglehold of broken government software. While most tech commentary focuses on the thrill of new capabilities, this conversation zeroes in on the grim reality of the public sector: critical infrastructure running on languages few living humans understand, trapped by procurement rules that make switching vendors impossible. For busy leaders watching the federal budget bleed into legacy debt, this is the first serious case for why AI agents, not just chatbots, are the only viable path to modernization.

The $100 Billion Trap

The conversation opens with a stark assessment of the current state of affairs. Kaplan, co-founder of Cognition, points out that despite massive spending, the government is failing to modernize. "More than $100 billion a year is spent on IT for the US government," Kaplan notes, "but a lot of these systems are ancient." He cites a Government Accountability Office study revealing that out of ten critical legacy systems identified in the 2010s needing updates, only three have even begun the process. This isn't just a technical glitch; it's a systemic failure of value delivery. The author effectively uses these numbers to highlight a massive inefficiency that private sector competitors would have long ago exploited.

The root cause, Kaplan argues, is not a lack of talent but a broken purchasing mechanism. In the private sector, companies buy the best tool for the job. In government, the process is bogged down by compliance hurdles like FedRAMP, which Kaplan describes as a "journey" that creates "enormously slow" timelines for software adoption. The result is a market where vendors lock customers in, knowing the government cannot easily leave. "You can't hold your customers hostage with switching costs when AI is going to do the switching and work on it 24 hours a day without getting bored of a really tedious process," Kaplan predicts. This is the core of the "software abundance" thesis: AI agents collapse the friction of migration, forcing vendors to compete on actual value rather than bureaucratic inertia.

Critics might note that government procurement rules exist for a reason—to prevent corruption and ensure fair competition—and that removing friction could introduce new risks. However, the argument holds that the current system has created a different kind of corruption: the stagnation of essential services.

The COBOL Crisis and the End of Coding

The discussion then pivots to the specific technical debt haunting agencies like the Social Security Administration and the Treasury. These systems run on COBOL, a language from the 1960s. Kaplan explains the danger not as the language's inefficiency, but as the disappearance of the people who speak it. "The problem is that nobody knows how to write COBOL anymore," he states. "The people who wrote these systems are often no longer there when changes need to be made."

This creates a terrifying bottleneck where a tiny cohort of aging specialists holds the keys to national infrastructure, and they are increasingly reluctant to touch systems that are "really hard to move with those requirements." Kaplan frames the evolution of programming as a ladder of abstraction, moving from punch cards to assembly, to COBOL, and now to natural language. "AI is actually the next logical rung on the ladder," he argues. "It's not some fundamentally structurally different thing... it's telling your computer what you want it to do, but in English, in a way that's natural for everyone."

The ability to move from whatever you have to the best tool for your problem is going to lead to a lot of changes.

This framing is compelling because it demystifies the technology. It suggests that the barrier to entry for fixing government software isn't the complexity of the code, but the scarcity of human translators. By automating the translation, AI agents can handle the "painful migration and modernization work engineers tend to avoid," turning a multi-year nightmare into a three-week sprint.

Beyond the Tool: The Human Bottleneck

Perhaps the most insightful part of the commentary is Kaplan's rejection of the idea that a better AI model alone will solve everything. He uses a powerful analogy involving inflation across different sectors. "Have you seen the chart of inflation by time where plasma screen TVs are massively deflationary, but healthcare and tuition keep going up?" he asks. His mental model for the post-AGI future is that intelligence-soluble problems will become cheap, but the "rest of the complexity of the real world" will remain expensive.

Kaplan argues that the bottleneck is shifting from writing code to understanding problems and navigating organizational politics. "How are you even allowed to deploy in the environments you need? How do you work with the people who are ultimately in charge of these systems to drive the outcomes they want?" he asks. This is a crucial distinction. While many tech optimists assume the model will do the rest, Kaplan insists that "the real skill of the future is knowing which problems matter." He suggests that the future of procurement and implementation will require forward-deployed engineers who can partner with government entities to restructure processes, not just drop in a software tool.

A counterargument worth considering is whether the government has the internal capacity to manage these AI-driven transformations. If the bottleneck is organizational complexity, simply introducing AI agents without changing the culture of risk-aversion might lead to new failures. Yet, Kaplan's emphasis on partnership over pure tooling suggests he is aware of this hurdle.

Bottom Line

Schneider and Kaplan offer a pragmatic, non-hype vision of AI in government: it is not about replacing human judgment, but about removing the friction that keeps critical systems in the stone age. The strongest part of their argument is the identification of "switching costs" as the primary enemy of progress, and AI agents as the only force capable of breaking that lock-in. The biggest vulnerability remains the human element; even with perfect code generation, the government's bureaucratic inertia may still outpace the speed of technological change. Readers should watch for how agencies adapt their procurement rules to leverage these new tools, as that will be the true test of "software abundance."
