Fed up with the fed fight

Peter Rothpletz · Zeteo ·Jan 13, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Peter Rothpletz delivers a jarring diagnosis of a political system where the weaponization of justice has finally backfired on its architects, not out of moral awakening, but because the financial elite have been threatened. The piece's most striking claim is that the administration's attempt to intimidate the Federal Reserve has fractured the Republican coalition, revealing that the donor class values institutional stability over blind loyalty. This is not a story about personality clashes; it is a structural crisis where the executive branch's overreach is colliding with the very economic machinery it seeks to control.

The Sledgehammer and the Walnut

Rothpletz frames the administration's investigation into Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell as a catastrophic miscalculation. He writes, "Launching a criminal investigation sounds like taking a sledgehammer to a walnut… A criminal investigation? Well, that undermines the credibility of America's central bank." The author argues that while the administration has previously targeted political opponents, this specific move crosses a red line for Wall Street. The evidence suggests that the threat of criminal indictment against the Fed chair is not merely a legal maneuver but a coercive tool to force lower interest rates.

The commentary highlights a rare moment of rebellion from within the GOP ranks. Rothpletz notes that Republican officials, who typically ignore the plight of blue America, are suddenly alarmed because their wallets are at risk. He quotes Louisiana Senator John Kennedy, who told reporters, "My thought was, we need this like we need a hole in the head… I know Chairman Powell pretty well. I would be stunned if he had done anything wrong." This reaction underscores a cynical reality: the party's loyalty is conditional on the preservation of their economic interests. Rothpletz observes that anonymous House Republicans are now whispering that they might prefer to lose their majority rather than continue "doubling down on stupid."

Investors do not like this. Stocks down across the board.

Critics might argue that this backlash is purely transactional and lacks any genuine commitment to the rule of law. However, Rothpletz's point stands: when the machinery of justice is used to settle personal scores against the central bank, the market reacts with panic, and the political fallout becomes immediate. The administration's attempt to menaced the Fed into submission is being met with a wall of resistance from the very people who usually enable the executive branch.

The Human Cost of Escalation

Shifting from the corridors of power to the streets, Rothpletz addresses the administration's aggressive expansion of immigration enforcement and the disturbing trend of monetizing violence. The author details how Secretary Kristi Noem announced the deployment of hundreds more federal officers to Minneapolis, a move that has sparked legal challenges from local governments. But the most harrowing section focuses on the financial rewards being funneled to law enforcement officers involved in lethal force.

Rothpletz writes, "Billionaire Bill Ackman, millionaire MAGA dolt Megyn Kelly, and their ideological peers are helping funnel HUGE sums to the ICE agent who murdered Renee Nicole Good." The piece exposes a chilling dynamic where right-wing fundraisers are turning acts of violence into profitable ventures. He notes that over $500,000 has been pledged to an ICE agent involved in a deadly confrontation, sending a clear message that "escalation and (potentially lethal) violence pay." This is not just about policy; it is about the incentivization of brutality.

The author connects this domestic phenomenon to a broader pattern of dehumanization. Rothpletz points out that scammers and bad actors are not acting in a vacuum; they are buying data and exploiting vulnerabilities. He states, "Once your data is out there, it's not just calls. It's phishing, impersonation, and identity theft." While this section touches on data privacy, the core argument remains focused on how the administration's rhetoric and actions create an environment where violence is rewarded and accountability is eroded. The human cost is stark: a five-year-old targeted with racial slurs, a protester killed, and a society where the wealthy are encouraged to fund retribution.

Global Shadows and Institutional Decay

The piece does not stop at domestic borders. Rothpletz weaves in a grim picture of global instability, from the rising civilian death toll in Ukraine to the collapse of hospitals in Sudan. He notes that civilian deaths in Ukraine reached at least 2,514 in 2025, a 70% increase from the previous year, driven by "expanded use of long-range weapons." This context serves to illustrate a world where the US administration's focus on internal retribution is happening alongside a broader unraveling of international norms.

Rothpletz also touches on the administration's bizarre admission regarding the 2020 election, where the president suggested he should have seized voting machines. He writes, "The possibility of Trump attempting to overturn future elections is more likely than you may think." This is not hyperbole; it is a direct challenge to the democratic process. The author's framing suggests that the administration's actions are not isolated incidents but part of a coordinated effort to consolidate power, regardless of the legal or humanitarian consequences.

WHAT ARE WE DOING HERE?!?

This rhetorical question captures the essence of the piece's frustration. Rothpletz is not just reporting news; he is issuing a warning. The combination of economic coercion, state-sanctioned violence, and electoral subversion creates a perfect storm. The author's choice to juxtapose the Fed investigation with the funding of violent actors is deliberate: it shows a system where the rules are being rewritten to serve a specific agenda, and the cost is being paid by ordinary citizens and the stability of the nation itself.

Bottom Line

Rothpletz's strongest argument is the revelation that the administration's authoritarian overreach is finally hitting a wall of self-interest within its own coalition. The piece effectively demonstrates that when the executive branch weaponizes the justice system against the Federal Reserve, even the most loyal allies begin to panic. However, the biggest vulnerability in the narrative is the assumption that this backlash will lead to meaningful restraint; history suggests that the administration may simply double down, risking further economic and social instability. The reader must watch for whether the Republican donor class can sustain this resistance or if the allure of power will once again silence their concerns.
