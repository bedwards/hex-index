Real Lawyer Reacts to Ace Attorney

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Apr 15, 2024 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just watch a video game; he dissects a legal fantasy with a Japanese criminal defense attorney to reveal how absurdly the Ace Attorney franchise treats justice. While most commentary focuses on the fun of the gameplay, Stone and his guest Kichiro Matsui expose a courtroom where the rules of evidence, procedure, and human decency are not just bent, but shattered for the sake of drama.

The Myth of the Solo Defender

The most immediate red flag in the game's narrative is the premise of a rookie attorney representing a client in a capital murder case without any prior investigation or indictment. Stone highlights the sheer procedural impossibility of this setup, noting, "you have to start a new trial first of all if you're an accused from some other person... you can't just like you know say he's a new murderer in in the same trial." This isn't just a minor plot hole; it is a fundamental misunderstanding of how due process works in both the United States and Japan. In reality, the idea that a defendant could be thrown into a murder trial immediately after a previous case concludes is legally nonsensical.

Stone and Matsui also dismantle the game's portrayal of self-representation. While the game allows Phoenix Wright to represent himself, Stone points out the grim reality: "in the US we have a saying a lawyer who represents himself has a fool for a client." Matsui confirms that in Japan, while civil cases might allow self-representation, criminal cases—especially murder—require a hired attorney, with the government providing one if the defendant cannot afford it. The game's premise of a lone hero fighting the system ignores the very real protections designed to prevent exactly this kind of chaotic, unrepresented defense.

The Theater of the Courtroom

The visual and behavioral theatrics of Ace Attorney are perhaps the most entertaining, yet legally disastrous, elements of the series. Stone and Matsui agree that the layout of the courtroom, the audience positioning, and the dramatic posturing are pure fiction. Stone observes, "the audience they're looking from like you know from the top we don't have audience looking from the top that's ridiculous." The idea that a prosecutor would wear a frilly, flamboyant outfit is also dismissed as impossible, with Matsui noting he has "never seen one of these like prosecutors wearing the fluffy S."

The game's signature mechanic—the loud, dramatic objection—is another point of contention. Stone explains that in real life, attorneys do not shout objections to witnesses; they address the judge. "We don't really say we don't say that we just say politely that's how we do it," Stone notes, emphasizing that the game's "Hold it!" and "Objection!" are theatrical devices that would result in an attorney being removed from the court. The constant shouting and the prosecutor's ability to interject during cross-examination are also flagged as violations of basic courtroom decorum. Stone remarks, "often in Ace Attorney they are questioning at the same time... that shouldn't happen that go they they show that in American legal shows all the time and it it drives me crazy."

Evidence, Hearsay, and the Brady Rule

Perhaps the most critical legal failure in the game is the handling of evidence. The prosecutor in Ace Attorney frequently withholds evidence until the last possible moment, forcing the defense to scramble. Stone contrasts this with the American "Brady rule," which mandates that "any evidence that would be helpful to the defense must be turned over to the defense long before the trial starts." Matsui confirms that while Japanese prosecutors have some discretion, they must disclose evidence before the trial begins, especially in murder cases. The game's reliance on surprise evidence is a fantasy that would be thrown out of court immediately.

Furthermore, the game's treatment of hearsay and witness knowledge is laughably inaccurate. Stone points out that questioning a witness about a document they have no knowledge of would be grounds for an immediate objection: "there would have been an objection that there's no basis to question this witness about this receipt because he has no knowledge about this." The game also features a witness channeling the spirit of the victim, a concept Stone admits has no analog in American culture and is "completely insane" in a legal context. The idea that a judge would opine on the quality of testimony mid-trial, as seen in the game, is another violation of judicial neutrality. Stone notes, "it's completely improper for the judge to opine From the Bench... you have to wait until the end."

Critics might note that Ace Attorney is a work of fiction designed for entertainment, not a legal textbook, and that its dramatic liberties are part of its charm. However, Stone's commentary serves as a necessary reminder that even in a fantasy setting, the stakes of a murder trial are too high to ignore the fundamental rules that protect the accused. The game's portrayal of justice is not just unrealistic; it is dangerously misleading about the actual safeguards in place.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone and Kichiro Matsui deliver a masterclass in legal literacy by exposing the absurdity of Ace Attorney's courtroom drama. Their strongest argument is that the game's reliance on surprise evidence and dramatic outbursts ignores the very real procedural rules that ensure fair trials. The biggest vulnerability in the game's logic is its assumption that a judge would allow such chaos to proceed, a flaw that Stone and Matsui dismantle with precision. For anyone interested in the law, this commentary is a must-watch reminder that real justice is far less dramatic, but far more important, than any video game could ever portray.
