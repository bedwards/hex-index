What we're missing about the social media verdict

Jacqueline Nesi · Techno Sapiens ·Mar 30, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jacqueline Nesi cuts through the noise of a landmark verdict by refusing to treat social media as a singular villain, instead arguing that the real breakthrough lies in shifting liability from content to product design. While headlines scream about a "Big Tobacco moment," Nesi offers a far more nuanced, psychologically grounded reality: social media is not a cigarette, but a complex tool where harm is idiosyncratic, not inevitable. For busy parents and policymakers, this distinction is the difference between panic and actionable strategy.

The Design of Harm

Nesi anchors her analysis in a recent jury verdict where Meta and YouTube were found liable for a teenager's mental health crisis, marking a pivotal shift in how the law views tech giants. She explains that the jury determined the companies were negligent because they "had a duty of care to protect kids from harm related to their products" and "failed to do so by designing features they knew were harmful." This is a critical evolution from previous legal battles. As Nesi notes, "In the past, they have been shielded by Section 230, a law that protects online platforms from liability for the content people post." By successfully arguing that the design itself caused the injury, the plaintiff bypassed the traditional shield that has protected these companies for decades.

This legal strategy mirrors the early days of tobacco litigation, yet Nesi is quick to dismantle the oversimplified comparison. She writes, "Cigarettes have clear negative impacts on physical health for the vast majority of people who smoke them... There is no way to safely smoke cigarettes, for anyone. This is not the case for social media." The argument holds water because it respects the complexity of human behavior. Unlike smoking, where the dose-response relationship is linear and universally toxic, social media's impact varies wildly based on individual vulnerability.

"It is one thing to say that for some kids, social media is one factor contributing to mental health symptoms. It is another entirely to say that social media is causing mental health problems on a large scale."

Critics might argue that this nuance lets the platforms off the hook too easily, suggesting that even if harm isn't universal, the sheer scale of usage makes the aggregate damage undeniable. However, Nesi's point is that without this precision, we cannot craft effective regulations. Blaming the tool for every tragedy risks ignoring the specific mechanisms—like infinite scroll and autoplay—that are actually driving the harm.

The Psychology of "Addiction"

The piece shines brightest when Nesi applies her background as a child psychologist to the popular narrative of "addiction." She challenges the blanket labeling of heavy usage, stating, "For the vast majority of people (and kids) using social media, though, I do not think addiction is the right word to use." She warns that using this label risks "removing agency by suggesting that they are incapable of managing social media use." This is a vital correction to the current discourse, which often pathologizes normal developmental behaviors.

Nesi introduces two psychological concepts to explain why outcomes differ so drastically: multifinality (same cause, different outcomes) and equifinality (different causes, same outcome). She illustrates this by noting that "two children can experience the exact same circumstances, and one might develop depression, while one might be totally fine." This framework forces us to look at the whole picture, acknowledging that while features like infinite scroll are designed to maximize engagement, they are not the sole architects of mental illness.

The author draws a compelling parallel to another regulated industry to illustrate the point. She suggests a better analogy than tobacco is alcohol or cars: "Cars, like social media, are something we use regularly, but which can be very dangerous, especially if used in unsafe ways." Just as we don't ban cars but enforce seatbelt laws and age limits, we can regulate social media features without banning the technology entirely. This pragmatic approach avoids the binary trap of "all good" or "all bad."

A Path Forward for Parents

Ultimately, Nesi's commentary serves as a grounding wire for anxious families. She asserts that despite the legal drama, "The outcome of this trial does not change the research. It does not change the facts." The core advice remains consistent: "If you know your teen is vulnerable, you may want to be extra careful." The verdict validates parental concerns, confirming that "it can't all be on them: the companies themselves need to take responsibility for kids' safety, too."

However, the path to safety is not as simple as removing a button. Nesi points out the complexity of distinguishing between features and content: "If every video on TikTok was just me, wide-eyed and reading the statistical analysis portion of my dissertation, kids would not exactly be 'hooked,' even if these videos auto-played." This suggests that solutions must be multifaceted, addressing both the algorithmic design and the nature of the content being served.

"Social media may be one piece of the puzzle, but it's certainly not the whole thing."

Bottom Line

Jacqueline Nesi's strongest contribution is her refusal to let the "Big Tobacco" analogy obscure the unique, non-linear nature of digital harm, effectively arguing that liability must target specific, harmful design features rather than the platforms as a monolith. Her biggest vulnerability lies in the difficulty of translating this psychological nuance into clear, enforceable legislation that can keep pace with rapidly evolving algorithms. Readers should watch for how courts interpret "defective product" in the digital age, as this verdict could force a fundamental redesign of the attention economy.

Welcome back! I’m Jacqueline Nesi, a psychology professor and mom of three young kids who has spent many more hours learning about personal injury liability than I would have predicted when I decided to become a child psychologist 15 years ago.

8 min read (sorry! I tried to make it shorter!)

Last week, a jury found Meta and YouTube liable for harm caused to a teenager from using their products.

Here are answers to some of the most common questions I’ve been getting about it.

What happened?

Last Wednesday, the jury delivered their verdict in a landmark trial. A now 20-year-old woman (K.G.M.) sued Meta (including Instagram), TikTok, Snap, and YouTube,1 claiming that she became addicted to the products as a child, and developed mental health problems as a result.

This lawsuit involved a new legal strategy in cases directed at Big Tech companies: arguing that the companies caused personal injury through defective products.2

What exactly did the jury decide?

In a personal injury case like this one, the jury needed to determine that it is “more likely than not” that the defendant (i.e., the social media companies) were negligent. This means the jury agreed that:

  1. The companies had a duty of care to protect kids from harm related to their products

  2. The companies failed to do so by designing features they knew were harmful (e.g., infinite scroll, autoplay, beauty filters) and neglecting to warn people about them

  3. This failure was a substantial factor in causing harm to K.G.M.

Why is this important?

This is the first time we’ve seen social media companies held liable for harms caused by their products. In the past, they have been shielded by Section 230, a law that protects online platforms from liability for the content people post. In this case, the plaintiff successfully argued that the design of the products themselves (i.e., the features) caused harm.

The companies will appeal the verdict, but if it holds, this will have major implications. There are thousands of other lawsuits following this same playbook slated to go to trial this year. As the financial penalties accumulate, the companies may be forced to make substantial changes to their products to protect young users.
