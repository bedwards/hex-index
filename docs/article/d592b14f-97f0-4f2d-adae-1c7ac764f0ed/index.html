How to make a BobbyBroccoli video

BobbyBroccoli · BobbyBroccoli ·May 11, 2024 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

BobbyBroccoli doesn't just teach animation; he dismantles the myth that high-end 3D storytelling requires a Hollywood budget or a formal degree. In a field often gatekept by expensive software and steep learning curves, he argues that the barrier to entry isn't money—it's a willingness to embrace a chaotic, non-linear workflow that defies traditional editing logic. This is a rare, transparent look at the messy reality of digital creation, proving that the most compelling visual narratives often come from self-taught experimentation rather than institutional training.

The Non-Linear Trap

The most striking aspect of BobbyBroccoli's methodology is his insistence on locking in the script before a single frame is animated. He acknowledges that this approach feels counterintuitive to most creators. "Everything is animated in chronological order as opposed to traditional editing that can be done in pretty much any order," he explains. This creates a rigid structure where changing a single line of dialogue mid-production can trigger a cascade of continuity errors. While this sounds restrictive, it forces a level of discipline that traditional editing often lacks. By committing to the narrative early, the creator ensures the visual journey is perfectly synchronized with the audio, a feat that is "really hard to make changes to" once the 3D space is populated.

Critics might argue that this rigidity stifles the organic evolution of a story, where the best ideas often emerge during the editing phase. However, in the context of 3D space where objects exist in a fixed timeline, the trade-off is necessary to maintain coherence. BobbyBroccoli admits, "It is not intuitive by any means but it is the best way I found to do what I do," a candid admission that validates the struggle many beginners face when transitioning from 2D to 3D workflows.

"The part that really throws people off is that when you make a video like this you have to lock in your script really early."

The Blender Ecosystem

Once the script is set, the technical execution relies heavily on Blender, a free, open-source tool that BobbyBroccoli credits for democratizing 3D animation. He is quick to clarify that he is not the inventor of the style, acknowledging the work of John Boyce, but he has refined the process using tools that are accessible to everyone. "All of my blender knowledge was self-taught through YouTube advice from friends and experimentation," he notes. This highlights a crucial shift in the creative landscape: expertise is no longer defined by formal education but by the ability to curate and synthesize community knowledge.

He details a specific workflow where the camera acts as the backbone of the video, moving through a scene like a character in a video game. "You basically get to control the camera like you're playing Minecraft," he describes, using a feature called walk mode to navigate the 3D space. This gamified approach to professional software lowers the psychological barrier for new users. He further simplifies the process by advocating for the use of pre-made assets and procedural generation, noting that "there was no way I'd ever have been able to [create thousands of objects] manually." By leveraging Python scripting and add-ons, he turns what would be days of manual labor into a matter of minutes.

The Power of Community and Automation

The true strength of BobbyBroccoli's argument lies in his reliance on the broader creator community. He treats his own work as a collage of other people's tutorials, a strategy that accelerates learning and innovation. "I cannot stress this enough: all of my videos are a collage of other people's tutorials," he writes. This collaborative ethos challenges the notion of the solitary genius, suggesting that modern creativity is inherently networked. He points to specific examples, such as using a script to generate a graph from a CSV file, a task that would be impossible to do by hand.

He also touches on the technical trade-offs between Blender's two main render engines, Eevee and Cycles. While Eevee is fast and accessible, Cycles offers realistic lighting at the cost of immense render times. "The trade-off is render times are immense," he warns, a practical tip that saves beginners from hardware frustration. This transparency about the limitations of the tools adds credibility to his advice. He encourages viewers to embrace the learning curve, stating, "once you get past the initial hump I think blender is a super powerful tool for making 3D story videos."

"I often have to track down really old clips or articles behind pay walls and this class has great advice for navigating academic databases."

Bottom Line

BobbyBroccoli's guide is a masterclass in demystifying complex technology, proving that the most powerful creative tools are those that empower the individual to tell their own story. While his rigid scripting requirement may deter those who prefer a more fluid editing process, the resulting precision is what gives his videos their unique, cinematic quality. The biggest takeaway is not the software itself, but the mindset: creativity in the digital age is about leveraging community resources and automation to overcome technical limitations, turning a steep learning curve into a manageable path for anyone willing to start.
