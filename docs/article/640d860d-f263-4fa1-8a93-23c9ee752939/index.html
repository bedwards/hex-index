A History of the Heart in 4 Chapters

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Jul 25, 2021 · 46 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis doesn't just recount medical history; he dismantles the myth that scientific progress is a straight line, revealing instead a jagged path paved by unlikely collaborators and centuries of hesitation. In a talk aimed at medical students but accessible to anyone, he argues that our modern understanding of the heart is less about a sudden explosion of genius and more about the slow, often accidental convergence of art, engineering, and sheer stubbornness. This is not a dry recitation of dates, but a narrative that exposes how deeply cultural beliefs—specifically the idea of the heart as a magical, untouchable soul—delayed life-saving surgery for nearly a millennium.

The Myth of the Untouchable Organ

Francis begins by challenging the assumption that early medicine was simply ignorant. Instead, he suggests that the heart was treated with a reverence that bordered on paralysis. "Doctors felt that the heart was so delicate that... almost magical really that even touching it might cause it to stop irreversibly," he explains. This cultural barrier meant that while humanity was performing amputations and trepanations for thousands of years, the heart remained a forbidden zone until the late 19th century. The first successful operation on a beating heart didn't occur until 1896, and true cardiac surgery only emerged in the 1940s. This delay highlights a crucial point: scientific capability often outpaces cultural permission. We knew how to cut, but we were too afraid to cut here.

Renaissance Minds and the Art of Observation

When Francis turns to the Renaissance, he bypasses the usual list of names to focus on three figures who redefined the organ through observation rather than dogma. He highlights William Harvey and Stephen Hales, noting that they "contributed more to the early understanding of the heart than anyone else." Harvey's De Motu Cordis finally proposed the heart as a pump, overturning the 1,400-year-old Galenic theory that the heart was merely a heat producer. Hales, a clergyman and polymath, took this further by measuring blood pressure invasively using glass tubes in animal arteries, effectively describing hemorrhagic shock and arterial elasticity.

"Hales was a clergyman and polymath really in those wonderful days gone by where you could just do a bit of everything."

Francis uses Hales to illustrate a lost era of interdisciplinary curiosity, but the true star of this section is Leonardo da Vinci. Francis reveals that da Vinci wasn't just sketching anatomy; he was modeling fluid dynamics centuries before the math existed. Da Vinci observed vortices in water flowing through dikes and correctly predicted that similar eddies in the aorta were essential for closing the aortic valve. "He postulated really that the same vortices are created as blood goes through the aortic valve," Francis writes. It took 450 years for engineers in the 1960s and modern 4D flow MRI scans to prove that a man without a microscope had solved a complex hemodynamic problem. This story serves as a powerful reminder that deep observation can sometimes outpace the tools available to verify it.

The Human Cost of "Hopeless" Cases

The narrative shifts from the drawing room to the operating theater as Francis enters the "right ventricle" of his story: the 20th-century battle against congenital heart disease. He focuses on the "blue babies" born with tetralogy of Fallot, a condition that was once a guaranteed death sentence. "Tetralogy of Fallot really to put it bluntly was a death sentence back then," he states, noting that half the children died before age three. The medical consensus was to let nature take its course, advising parents to keep their children calm and avoid excitement.

Francis then introduces the unlikely trio behind the breakthrough: Alfred Blalock, Helen Taussig, and Vivian Thomas. He paints a vivid picture of their individual struggles. Taussig, a woman told she'd never make it as a doctor by Harvard, went deaf and had to learn to diagnose heart murmurs by feeling the chest with her hands. Thomas, a Black man whose medical dreams were crushed by the Great Depression, was forced to work as a janitor despite being the surgical genius behind the procedure. "He had his own dreams of becoming a doctor which were dashed when the bank holding all his money went bust," Francis recounts. Thomas taught himself surgery on animals but was legally barred from touching patients, paid only as a janitor while guiding Blalock's every move during the first successful operation.

"He had practiced this hundreds of time... despite his huge contribution to developing the procedure which we'll hear about in a second because that was all that a black man could be paid."

The story of the Blalock-Thomas-Taussig shunt is framed not just as a medical triumph, but as a testament to the power of collaboration across rigid social barriers. The procedure worked by redirecting blood flow to the lungs, saving thousands of lives. However, Francis acknowledges the grim reality that the shunt was not a cure, but a palliative measure that allowed children to live long enough for modern corrective surgery to evolve. Critics might note that while this story celebrates diversity in the face of adversity, it also underscores how long the medical establishment ignored the potential of women and people of color, delaying progress for decades.

Bottom Line

Francis's strongest argument is that medical history is driven as much by human character and cultural context as it is by data. The narrative effectively humanizes the cold mechanics of cardiology, showing how a deaf woman, a janitor, and a surgeon overcame systemic prejudice to save thousands of lives. The piece's vulnerability lies in its brevity; it touches on centuries of history in a single talk, occasionally glossing over the complex political and social forces that maintained the status quo for so long. Yet, for a busy reader, it offers a compelling verdict: the heart's history is a mirror of our own, reflecting our fears, our biases, and our capacity for unexpected brilliance.

"Leonardo is the polymath to end all polymaths... a genius the likes of which we we've rarely ever seen."

This is a reminder that the most profound breakthroughs often come from those who look at the world differently, whether it's a Renaissance artist seeing vortices in a dam or a deaf doctor feeling a murmur with her hands.
