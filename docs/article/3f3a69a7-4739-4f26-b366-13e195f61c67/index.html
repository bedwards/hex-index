Australian Wild Fires Explained

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Jan 19, 2020 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace doesn't just explain the Australian wildfires; he dismantles the convenient narrative that these disasters were merely a result of bad luck or criminal arson. By weaving together meteorological data, ecological studies, and climate modeling, he reveals a terrifyingly precise recipe for catastrophe where human emissions have inadvertently engineered the perfect storm. This is not a story about a single bad summer, but a systemic shift in how the planet's climate engine operates.

The Four Ingredients of Disaster

Borlace anchors his analysis in a 2009 research paper by Sally Archibald, which identifies the four non-negotiable requirements for a wildfire: the right weather, available fuel, dry fuel, and a spark. The author's genius lies in showing how climate change has aggressively manipulated each of these variables simultaneously. He notes that while bushfires are a natural occurrence, the scale of this event is unprecedented. "The overwhelming scientific evidence is that human emissions of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases are by far the main driver of increased average global temperatures since the beginning of the industrial revolution." This framing is crucial because it moves the conversation from reactive disaster management to proactive climate accountability.

The argument gains traction when Borlace addresses the counterintuitive idea that rising carbon dioxide levels actually increase fire fuel. Citing Professor Andy Pitman, he explains that CO2 acts as a fertilizer, causing plants to grow more vigorously. However, in the nutrient-poor Australian bush, this growth is unsustainable. "More carbon fixed or locked up in woody matter means more fuel for fire, and as the green vegetation also uses the additional carbon to produce more leaves, when those leaves drop, you've got even more potential fire fuel lying around on the ground." This is a sophisticated point often missed in mainstream reporting: the very thing we think of as helping plants (CO2) is creating a massive, dry tinderbox.

Critics might argue that natural climate variability has always driven fire cycles, but Borlace effectively counters this by highlighting the unprecedented dryness. He points out that 2019 was the driest year on record, with rainfall 40% lower than average. The combination of record heat and record drought creates a feedback loop where the ground becomes so dry that even minor sparks ignite uncontrollable infernos.

The Atmospheric Mechanics of Heat

The commentary deepens as Borlace explores complex atmospheric phenomena that are being altered by global warming. He breaks down the Indian Ocean Dipole and the Southern Annular Mode (SAM) into accessible concepts, showing how human emissions are shifting their behavior. A positive phase of the Indian Ocean Dipole draws moisture away from Australia, and Borlace cites a 2018 study in Nature Communications suggesting that extreme positive phases could double if the planet warms by 1.5 degrees Celsius. "If our planet does reach an average global atmospheric temperature of 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels... the frequency of extreme positive phase dipoles in the Indian Ocean looks set to double." This transforms abstract climate models into a concrete threat multiplier.

Similarly, he explains how the Southern Annular Mode, which typically brings rain to southern Australia, was pushed into a negative phase by stratospheric warming. This caused winds to track further north, fanning the flames. Borlace describes this as a "macabre metaphorical icing on the combustible cake." The evidence here is compelling because it links specific, short-term weather events to long-term climate trends, proving that the fires were not an anomaly but a symptom of a changing system.

Debunking the Arson Myth

Perhaps the most vital part of Borlace's coverage is his rigorous debunking of the conspiracy theory that arson was the primary cause of these fires. He directly addresses the noise generated by political figures who claimed a "wave of arson" was to blame. "New South Wales police say they've actually only charged 24 people with deliberately lighting bush fires this season." This statistic is devastating to the arson narrative. He clarifies that while some arsonists were active, the main ignition source was dry lightning—thunderstorms that produce lightning but no rain.

By citing Professor David Bowman, a hazard and disaster scientist, Borlace shifts the blame from malicious individuals to the environment itself. "Whatever the source of ignition maybe though, it does appear that our rapidly changing climate is playing a significant role in producing the perfect conditions for Australian wildfires to become progressively longer lasting and more severe." This distinction is critical; it prevents the public from fixating on a few bad actors while ignoring the systemic driver. It also highlights the danger of misinformation, which Borlace notes was amplified by high-profile figures retweeting false claims.

The New Normal

The piece concludes with a sobering look at the future. Borlace quotes Professor John Caney of the Australian Academy of Sciences, who warns that current pledges under the Paris Agreement are insufficient. "We're looking more like 3 degrees Celsius." This is not a prediction of a distant future but a trajectory the world is already on. Borlace emphasizes that even at 1.5 degrees of warming, the impacts are severe, and at 3 degrees, they become catastrophic. He notes that the fires are generating their own weather systems, known as pyrocumulonimbus storms, which create their own lightning and violent winds, making them impossible to fight with traditional methods.

We're looking more like 3 degrees Celsius. That's it for this week, but the reality is we are on an upward trajectory.

The author's reliance on peer-reviewed studies from the Bureau of Meteorology and the National Environmental Science Program gives his argument immense weight. He doesn't just offer opinions; he presents a consensus view that human-caused climate change has resulted in more dangerous weather conditions for bushfires. The only vulnerability in his argument is the sheer scale of the challenge he presents; while the science is clear, the political will to act remains elusive, a gap he acknowledges but cannot bridge.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage is a masterclass in connecting the dots between carbon emissions and immediate, devastating reality, successfully reframing the Australian fires as a climate signal rather than a natural disaster. His strongest asset is the use of specific, peer-reviewed data to dismantle the arson myth and explain the complex atmospheric shifts driving the fires. The biggest vulnerability remains the gap between this scientific clarity and the political inaction required to prevent the predicted 3-degree warming scenario.
