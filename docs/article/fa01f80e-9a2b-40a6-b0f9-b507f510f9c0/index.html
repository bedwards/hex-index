The Secret to Japan's Great Cities

Jason Slaughter · Not Just Bikes ·Jul 14, 2024 · 33 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jason Slaughter challenges a comfortable urbanist assumption: that the secret to Japan's world-class cities isn't just their trains or culture, but the specific, narrow geometry of their streets. While many observers fixate on Tokyo's density or Amsterdam's bike lanes, Slaughter argues that the true engine of livability is a zoning code that permits mixed-use development everywhere, coupled with streets designed so narrowly that walking down the middle feels natural, not dangerous.

The Geometry of Walkability

Slaughter begins by dismantling the idea that Japanese urbanism is a monolith of high-rises. Instead, he points to the "typical Japanese Street," which averages just five meters wide from building to building. "These kind of narrow Japanese streets might be synonymous with busy urban areas in Tokyo or traditional cities bustling with tourists but streets like these are found all over Japan in cities towns and suburbs of all sizes," he writes. This observation is crucial because it shifts the focus from the spectacular to the mundane; the magic isn't in a single landmark, but in the consistent, repetitive design of the neighborhood block.

The author's most compelling insight is how this physical constraint creates a psychological shift for drivers and pedestrians alike. "On a street like this it feels Perfectly Natural to walk right down the middle of it which is so much better than being constrained to a tiny sidewalk," Slaughter notes. This design forces a hierarchy where the car is the guest, not the master. Critics might argue that this relies heavily on cultural norms of politeness that don't translate to other societies, but Slaughter counters that the physics of the street do the heavy lifting: "drivers even Mercedes and BMW drivers immediately understand that they they do not own the street which means that they're less likely to bully everybody else out of the way."

"How easy and comfortable it is to walk there should ultimately be the single most important aspect in the design of any City."

Zoning as the Invisible Architect

The physical narrowness of the streets is only possible because of a regulatory framework that most Western cities lack. Slaughter explains that Japan's zoning is specified at a national level and is remarkably "permissive compared to most other countries." He details how the most restrictive zone, "lowrise residential," still allows for apartments, schools, and home-based businesses. This stands in stark contrast to the "highly restrictive single family residential zoning common in American Suburbia."

The consequence of this permissiveness is a dynamic housing market that adapts to human life stages. Slaughter argues that in Japan, "houses and apartments of all different sizes can be built just about anywhere which is important because people's housing needs change throughout their lives." He highlights a growing crisis in North America where seniors are "trapped in their Suburban mcmansions" because they can no longer drive and cannot find smaller housing within their own neighborhoods. By allowing density to organically increase where needed, Japanese zoning prevents the isolation of the elderly and maintains community vitality.

This approach also solves the transit problem. Slaughter observes that "you can't have good public transit without a good land use plan," and points to the staggering difference between a Japanese train station, surrounded by hundreds of shops and homes, and a Canadian one surrounded by parking lots. "The land around a rapid transit stop is some of the most valuable land in the whole city so it should be where you find dozens of shops restaurants and houses not parking lots," he asserts. This is the epitome of Transit Oriented Development, yet it is often the first thing sacrificed in Western planning.

The Economics of Compactness

Beyond livability, Slaughter touches on the fiscal sustainability of this model. He notes that car-dependent cities are "bankrupting themselves by spreading everything out so thin that the tax base cannot support the replacement cost of the infrastructure." In Japan, the compact nature of the streets means there are "fewer water pipes sewage lines electrical wires and meters of asphalt per person which makes Japanese cities more financially viable."

Even the aesthetic choice of overhead utility poles, which Slaughter admits he finds "really ugly," serves a functional purpose: it makes upgrading infrastructure "extremely quick and inexpensive." This pragmatic approach allowed Japan to rapidly adopt fiber optic internet, a feat that would be far more costly in cities where utilities are buried. "Keeping streets more compact means means that there are fewer water pipes... which makes Japanese cities more financially viable," he writes, linking the physical design directly to the long-term solvency of the municipality.

"The absolute worst case scenario for inducing traffic ingestion is the ukian zoning common throughout the US and Canada which enforces a strict separation between where people live and where people shop or work."

Bottom Line

Slaughter's strongest argument is that the narrow Japanese street is not a constraint but a catalyst, forcing a density and mix of uses that make walking the default mode of transport. His analysis of the zoning code provides the necessary policy context to explain why these streets can exist everywhere, not just in historic districts. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on a specific cultural context of low car ownership and high social trust, which may be difficult to replicate in car-centric cultures without a radical, and politically difficult, shift in zoning laws. However, the core lesson remains undeniable: if you want a city that works for people, you must design the street first, and the zoning second.
