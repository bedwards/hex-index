How Tyson Captured All The Pork You Eat

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Jun 21, 2024 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union doesn't just report on the meat industry; it exposes a quiet corporate coup that has turned American agriculture into a monopoly designed to enrich three giants while bankrupting the communities that feed us. The author's most startling claim is that the industrial model didn't emerge from market efficiency alone, but was a deliberate strategy pioneered by Tyson Foods to transfer all risk to farmers while capturing all the profit. This is not a story about better burgers; it is a forensic accounting of how a single company's DNA reshaped the ecology and economy of the Midwest.

The Architecture of Risk

The piece begins by contrasting the vanished landscape of independent farming with the rise of Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations, or CAFOs. More Perfect Union writes, "In those same 40 years the number of hogs that have been raised slaughtered and sold has exploded and the profits made in the hog industry in the state of Iowa has increased by 300%." Yet, as the author notes, "you don't see that growth in the communities anymore so where did that money go?" The answer lies in the consolidation of power. The article argues that the industry shifted from a system where farmers owned their means of production to one where they are merely contractors for massive corporations.

The author traces this shift to the "chicken-ization" of hog farming, a term coined to describe how Tyson applied its poultry model to pigs. "The core DNA of Tyson's business model is that it can go out to massive industrial scale customers like Walmart and it can promise them consistent high volume products." To achieve this, Tyson required farmers to take on hundreds of thousands of dollars in debt to build facilities to the company's exact specifications. "The farmer is the one that takes out a mortgage for the land who buys and builds the barns and who cares for the animals during their short life," the author explains. This structure ensures that if anything goes wrong—a disease outbreak, a storm, a market dip—the farmer loses their home, while the corporation remains insulated.

It's profits off the back of farmers. It's pitted neighbor against neighbor and Tyson has destroyed not just the economy of Iowa but the ecology as well.

Critics might argue that contract farming provides stability for farmers who would otherwise be unable to access capital, but the text suggests this stability is an illusion. The author points out that "with all that debt and with structures meant for hogs it makes it difficult for them to leave the business or to convert to a different type of operation." This creates a "true corporate takeover" where the open market has been replaced by a closed, controlled system.

The Environmental Bill

The commentary then pivots to the physical consequences of this economic model, linking corporate consolidation directly to environmental degradation. The author presents a stark calculation: "Iowa alone there's 25 million hogs... a hog defecates about three times more than a human being so you're talking about the manure of 75 million people." This waste, the article argues, has turned waterways into open sewers. "Every year as the summer heats up lakes and rivers in Iowa shut down because of health advisories," More Perfect Union writes, noting that the pollution is so severe it creates toxic algae blooms that kill fish and contaminate drinking water.

The piece highlights the disconnect between the corporate beneficiaries and the local residents. "The people who have to live with their creations have to live next to their CAFOs on a daily basis," the author observes, while the executives "drive away" to their homes miles away. The argument is bolstered by the observation that regulatory oversight is being dismantled; "just last year Governor Kim Reynolds cut funding for a program that monitors nitrates in Iowa's waterways." This suggests a political landscape where corporate power actively suppresses the data needed to protect public health.

The Death of the Community

Finally, the author connects the economic and environmental collapse to the social fabric of rural America. The loss of independent farmers is framed not just as a business failure, but as a community extinction event. "When a farmer leaves a community or he has no longer a purpose within that community he not only goes himself but he also takes the future and the potential future when he moves his family," the text states. The result is a hollowed-out landscape where local businesses, churches, and schools cannot survive without the economic engine of the independent farm.

The author contrasts the fate of farmers like Ron Martensson, who survived by finding a niche market, with the majority who were forced into the industrial model. "As farmers enter contracts the value of pork fell but even as farmers lost income pork prices in the grocery stores stayed about the same," the article notes. This divergence proves that the efficiency gains of the industrial model were not passed on to consumers or producers, but captured entirely by the middlemen. "Tyson uses that monopoly power to charge you more and pay farmers less," the author concludes, framing the issue as a direct transfer of wealth from the rural working class to corporate shareholders.

For every farmer we keep on the land we have another reason for a local grocery store to stay open, we have another reason for a local church to stay open, we have another reason for a local school to stay open.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union delivers a devastating indictment of vertical integration in agriculture, successfully linking corporate strategy to the decay of rural communities and the poisoning of waterways. Its strongest asset is the clear narrative arc connecting the "chicken-ization" of hogs to the specific mechanism of risk transfer that bankrupts farmers. However, the piece leans heavily on the emotional weight of the community loss, potentially underplaying the complex global supply chain pressures that drive consolidation. Readers should watch for how this narrative influences upcoming antitrust enforcement in the food sector, as the evidence presented here suggests the current market structure is fundamentally broken.
