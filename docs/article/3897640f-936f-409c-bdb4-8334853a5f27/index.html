a toast to breastfeeding

Caroline Chambers · What to Cook ·Mar 11, 2026 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Caroline Chambers does not offer a sentimental eulogy for breastfeeding; she offers a raw, unvarnished inventory of its physical toll and its quiet, stolen joys. In a cultural landscape often polarized between glorification and shame, Chambers argues that the true value of the experience lies in its paradoxical nature: a biological superpower that simultaneously felt like a prison sentence. This piece is essential listening because it refuses to sanitize the reality of the postpartum body, replacing abstract ideals with the specific, messy details of a mother's life.

The Anatomy of a Paradox

Chambers immediately dismantles the expectation of pure sentimentality by cataloging the physical degradation breastfeeding can entail. She writes, "Breastfeeding: You made my boobs resemble empty wind socks, you made me question every single food or drink I had consumed in a 48-hour period whenever my baby wiggled slightly too much in his sleep." This blunt enumeration of discomfort serves as a necessary counterweight to the polished imagery often seen in parenting media. By listing the bleeding, the leaking, and the odor, she validates the frustration many mothers feel but rarely articulate.

The author's argument is not that breastfeeding is difficult, but that it is a totalizing force that consumes time and autonomy. She calculates that the practice "consumed roughly 2,400 hours, aka 99 entire days of my life over the past seven years." This statistical framing is powerful because it translates the abstract concept of "sacrifice" into a tangible loss of time, a metric that resonates deeply with busy professionals. However, she pivots quickly to acknowledge that this time was also a sanctuary. "You created quiet pockets of time in the middle of loud, busy life," she notes, suggesting that the very constraint of the act created a unique form of freedom.

I have loved you, I have hated you, and I have felt pretty whatever about you, sometimes all in the same minute.

This emotional ambivalence is the piece's strongest asset. It rejects the binary of "good mother" versus "bad mother" in favor of a more complex human reality. Critics might argue that focusing on the difficulty of breastfeeding could inadvertently discourage new mothers, but Chambers explicitly states, "Any way you feed your baby is absolutely perfect. Formula rules." This disclaimer is crucial; it ensures her personal narrative does not become a prescriptive standard, but rather a shared story of a specific experience.

The Biology of Intimacy

Chambers weaves the biological mechanics of nursing into her narrative of emotional connection, highlighting the chemical and physical responses that define the experience. She recalls "the newly postpartum oxytocin blasts that felt like drugs," a reference that aligns with the scientific understanding of the hormone's role in bonding and stress reduction. This biological high, however, is juxtaposed with the physical reality of the "Dysphoric milk ejection reflex," a condition where the release of milk triggers feelings of panic or revulsion rather than comfort. While she does not name the condition directly, her description of the "frantic 'I NEED WATER! SERIOUSLY, I'M DYING, PLEASE HELP ME RIGHT NOW!' shouts" captures the visceral urgency that can accompany these biological events.

The author also touches on the historical weight of the lactational amenorrhea method, the natural birth control effect of breastfeeding, though she frames it through the lens of personal exhaustion rather than family planning strategy. She describes the body's hyper-vigilance: "my body waking up when the baby's breathing changed ever so slightly because they'd just woken up." This instinctual alertness, a survival mechanism honed over millennia, is presented not as a romanticized trait but as a relentless demand on the mother's nervous system.

She finds beauty in the absurdity of the situation, from "flipping the pages of so many books with my chin because I didn't have any free hands" to "tasting the sugariness of my own milk and being kind of grossed out but not really." These details ground the experience in the mundane, making the extraordinary intimacy of nursing feel accessible and real. The argument here is that the value of breastfeeding is found in the accumulation of these small, weird, and often uncomfortable moments, not in some idealized vision of maternal perfection.

The End of an Era

As the piece moves toward its conclusion, Chambers frames the cessation of breastfeeding not as a loss, but as a liberation that allows for a new kind of connection. "No more nursing bras. No more leaky boobs. No more pumping!" she declares, listing the removal of these burdens with the relief of someone shedding a heavy coat. Yet, she does not discard the memory of the practice. Instead, she reframes it as a chapter that allowed her to "get to know my babies" in a way that was uniquely possible during those seven years.

The transition from the physical act of nursing to the broader experience of motherhood is marked by a shift in focus from the body to the mind. She reflects on the "thousands of hours of anxiety, joy, pain, and overwhelming contentment," acknowledging that the end of breastfeeding is the end of a specific mode of interaction, but not the end of the bond. The piece concludes with a respectful goodbye, honoring the complexity of the relationship between mother and child that was mediated through milk.

Thank you for allowing me to feed another human with my body, how cool.

This final sentiment encapsulates the piece's core message: a recognition of the biological miracle without the burden of moral judgment. It is a celebration of the body's capabilities, even when those capabilities are exhausting and inconvenient.

Bottom Line

Chambers succeeds by refusing to choose between the pain and the pleasure of breastfeeding, presenting them as inextricably linked parts of a single, transformative experience. The piece's greatest strength is its honesty about the physical toll, which lends credibility to its celebration of the emotional rewards. Readers should watch for how this nuanced perspective shifts the conversation from "how long should you breastfeed" to "what did you actually feel while you were doing it."
