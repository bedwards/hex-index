Iran as the "Bridgehead" for Securing China’s Western Frontier | by Zhang Wenmu

Various · Sinification ·Mar 17, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In an era where Washington and Beijing are often framed as the only players on the board, Zhang Wenmu offers a startlingly different map: one where Iran is not a rogue state to be contained, but the essential "bridgehead" holding the line for China's western security. This 2013 argument, penned by a strategist from Beihang University, flips the standard script on South Asian geopolitics by positing that India is a vulnerable lowland, while Iran is the high-altitude shield that has historically absorbed the shock of Western expansion. For busy listeners tracking the fallout of recent US-Israeli strikes, this piece is vital because it suggests that the stability of the Iranian Plateau is not just a Middle Eastern concern, but a direct determinant of Beijing's own strategic depth.

The Geography of Defense

Zhang Wenmu, a researcher with a background in national security strategy, builds his case on a single, imposing geographical fact: the continuity of the mountain ranges. He writes, "The most historically significant phenomenon in South-west Asian geopolitics is that the Zagros Mountains run east into the Hindu Kush and then into the Himalayas... which, from west to east, safeguards the 'pivot area' of the Asian continent." This is not merely a description of topography; it is a strategic thesis. Zhang argues that this "Zagros–Hindu Kush–Himalaya" barrier has functioned as a massive buffer, preventing outside powers from ever reaching China's western frontier directly.

The author's logic is that the states sitting on this plateau have historically done the heavy lifting for China's security. "Iranian Plateau states, not India, historically absorbed and blunted west-to-east pressure from outside powers before it could reach China's western approaches," Zhang asserts. This perspective reframes the entire history of the region's conflicts. When Alexander the Great or the British Empire pushed east, they were stopped or worn down by the terrain and the resistance of these plateau states, not by the armies of India. As Zhang notes, "India, by contrast, lay outside this strategic barrier and in modern times was subjected to prolonged British colonial rule, ultimately becoming a kind of 'London orphan' abandoned in South Asia by its imperial metropole."

"Iran, the state at the barrier's westernmost end, has in effect become the first 'firewall' for the security of China's western frontier."

This framing is compelling because it aligns with the "Great Game" history, where the British Empire spent decades trying to secure its Indian flank against Russian expansion, only to find the Hindu Kush an insurmountable obstacle for projecting power further north. Zhang uses this historical precedent to argue that the terrain itself dictates the outcome of great power competition. He points out that even when the British managed to capture Lhasa in 1904, they were forced to withdraw by winter, having learned that "Tibet's inaccessibility makes it impracticable to do anything to stiffen military resistance to China." The geography is the ultimate strategist.

The Parthian Precedent and Modern Implications

Zhang Wenmu does not rely solely on modern history; he reaches back to the Han Dynasty to illustrate a long-standing strategic dependency. He recounts how Zhang Qian, an envoy sent by Emperor Wu, specifically dispatched a deputy to the Parthian Empire (modern-day Iran) in 119 BCE. "The Parthian king, in evident excitement, dispatched 20,000 cavalry to welcome the Chinese envoy from afar," Zhang writes, interpreting this as evidence of "mutually dependent strategic needs at the time." The argument is that China and the Iranian Plateau have always been natural allies against western incursions, a relationship that predates the modern nation-state by two millennia.

The author contrasts this with the vulnerability of India. "India, lying outside this highland barrier and dominated by lower terrain, is more vulnerable to invasion and less strategically consequential for China's security," he argues. This is a bold claim for a Chinese strategist, given the current focus on the India-China border. Zhang suggests that India's low-lying plains make it easy to traverse and conquer, whereas the Iranian Plateau's elevation and arid basins create a natural fortress. He writes, "Without exception, these invasions by outside great powers were either blocked outside the Iranian Plateau or heavily worn down within it."

Critics might note that this historical determinism overlooks the complexity of modern statecraft, where ideology and economic interdependence often override geographical advantages. The fact that Iran has frequently been fragmented or internally divided challenges the idea of it being a monolithic "firewall." However, Zhang's point remains potent: the potential of the terrain to absorb shock is what matters. He concludes that "today it is they [Iranian Plateau states], not India, that are resisting and wearing down 'NATO's eastward expansion'."

The Strategic Verdict

The core of Zhang's argument is that China's security calculus must prioritize the stability of the Iranian Plateau over its relationship with India. He writes, "The anti-hegemonic struggle of the Iranian Plateau states, and not merely that of India, carries major and far-reaching significance for China's national security." This is a significant departure from the typical narrative that views India as a counterweight to China. Instead, Zhang sees India as a secondary player, while Iran is the primary gatekeeper.

He ties this geopolitical reality to economic necessity, noting that "China's heavy dependence on crude oil imports from the Iranian Plateau states" reinforces the strategic bond. "The security of the Iranian Plateau states has greater strategic value for China than India's, in a true lips-and-teeth sense," he concludes. The metaphor of "lips and teeth" suggests that if the lips (Iran) are lost, the teeth (China) are exposed. This is a stark warning that the collapse of the Iranian regime would not just be a regional crisis, but a direct threat to China's western frontier.

"If Iran were crushed, then powerful Western forces advancing eastwards by sea or by land... would bear down on China and pose a major threat to China's western frontier."

This argument gains renewed urgency when viewed through the lens of the "Forward policy" debates in Beijing. If the West is indeed trying to expand its influence eastward, as Zhang suggests, then the resilience of the Iranian Plateau is the single most important variable in maintaining the status quo. The author's analysis suggests that Beijing's patience with the Iranian regime is not born of ideological affinity, but of cold, hard strategic necessity.

Bottom Line

Zhang Wenmu's most powerful contribution is the reframing of Iran from a pariah state into the indispensable "bridgehead" for China's western security, a role that geography has assigned for millennia. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on historical geography to predict modern political outcomes, potentially underestimating the impact of internal Iranian instability or the shifting dynamics of US-China relations. However, for any analyst tracking the strategic implications of the Zagros Mountains, this piece provides an essential, if unsettling, perspective on why Beijing cannot afford to let the Iranian firewall fall.

With Iran’s future in question, we found this 2013 article by Zhang Wenmu, an old-school strategist from Beihang University, worth revisiting. One conclusion from our briefing on Chinese reactions to the recent US-Israeli strikes is that many analysts appear relatively unworried about the Iranian regime’s immediate prospects. Zhang offers a contrasting perspective—albeit one from over a decade ago—casting Iran as a strategic barrier to NATO’s eastward expansion and the westernmost link in the Zagros–Hindu Kush–Himalaya chain that has historically shielded China from outside powers’ eastward advance. That barrier has not yet collapsed, but it appears more vulnerable now than it has in years. Read in that light, this piece lands rather differently today.

Key Points

  2. Iranian Plateau states, not India, historically absorbed and blunted west-to-east pressure from outside powers before it could reach China’s western approaches.

  3. Afghan resistance and the Himalayan barrier are proof that even British India could not effectively project land power into China’s western frontier.
