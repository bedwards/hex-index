Last year, a vacant Lowe's home improvement store in Aliso Viejo, California was refreshed and converted into new smaller scale stores, including Daiso and some restaurants. Developers are also removing some of the parking to build 343 apartments, 10% of which will be affordable to lowincome households. This is just one of the many big box store reuse projects that have been built over the last 20 years. I could have picked from any number of projects.

These projects are called adaptive reuse. And big box stores have been adaptively reused into libraries, schools, offices, healthcare facilities, smaller shops, and more. There are a lot of these projects because, frankly, there are a lot of big box stores out there. There are currently over 4,600 Walmart stores open in the US right now and nearly 2,000 Targets.

But not all stores survive. Bed Bath and Beyond, a HomeGoods store, had over 1,000 locations as its peak in 2009, and now all are closed. Here in my town, Party City closed its doors last year along with 800 other stores nationwide. Kohl's in My Town is closing as the brand shutters some of its locations to cut costs.

We're currently in a time where some big box store companies are thriving and growing, while others are on the decline, battered after competing with online retail and dealing with CO. How should cities regulate the construction of new big box stores? And what should we do with existing empty big box stores? Let's figure it all out after the bike bell.

What is a big box store exactly? Maybe you haven't heard the term, so let's define it now. It's a retailer that has a large store, typically more than 50,000 ft of space. They look like, well, big boxes, hence the name.

They can be general retailers like Walmart and Target or specialty retailers like Home Depot or REI. They might stand alone or be linked together with each other forming something called a power center. Think of a power center as like a swollen strip mall. Big box stores have become a fact of life, a fixture on the landscape of the suburbs since seemingly forever.
