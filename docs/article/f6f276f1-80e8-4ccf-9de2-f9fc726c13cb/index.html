Heather Cox Richardson delivers a harrowing account of how the executive branch weaponized misinformation in real-time, transforming a fatal police shooting into a litmus test for American reality. The piece's most disturbing claim is not just that a legal observer was killed by federal agents, but that the administration immediately deployed a sophisticated propaganda machine to rewrite the event, demanding citizens choose loyalty over evidence.

The Death of a Legal Observer

Richardson opens with the visceral details of the shooting of Renee Nicole Good, a 37-year-old mother and legal observer in Minneapolis. She writes, "Three videos taken at the scene show a maroon SUV perpendicular on a snowy street... The driver backs up the vehicle, and straightens the wheel as if making a three-point turn. Then she starts slowly to accelerate along the street." This visual evidence, Richardson argues, directly contradicts the official narrative spun by the Department of Homeland Security (DHS). The core of her argument is that the agency's claim of "domestic terrorism" collapses under the weight of four separate video recordings showing the officer firing three times as the vehicle turned away, with the wheels fully pointed in the opposite direction.

The human cost is front and center. Richardson notes the chilling indifference of the agents on the scene: "Although there is no one tending the clearly visible woman in the car, an agent says: 'We have medics on scene. We have our own medics.'" When a bystander screams, "Where are they? WHERE ARE THEY?!" the agent's response—"Relax"—highlights a profound disconnect between the federal presence and the community they claim to protect. This moment is not just about a failed rescue; it is a symbol of the administration's disregard for civilian life. The administration's immediate pivot to blaming "sanctuary politicians" for fueling violence, despite the lack of evidence of a riot, suggests a strategy of pre-emptive deflection rather than accountability.

"If this works, it would be a demonstration of totalitarian power, the ability to control how people think. Accepting that lie is a loyalty test."

Richardson draws a sharp parallel to the shooting of Marimar Martinez in Chicago, where the DHS initially claimed the victim had ambushed agents, only for video evidence to prove the agents had rammed her car first. The Department of Justice eventually dropped charges against Martinez, yet the pattern of false narratives remains. Critics might argue that federal agents operate in high-stress environments where split-second decisions are inevitable, but Richardson's emphasis on the available video evidence challenges the notion that the officer's fear was genuine or that the use of lethal force was proportional.

The Architecture of Disinformation

The commentary shifts to the administration's response, which Richardson characterizes as a deliberate assault on shared truth. She highlights the absurdity of the official statement: "ICE officers in Minneapolis were conducting targeted operations when rioters began blocking ICE officers and one of these violent rioters weaponized her vehicle... an act of domestic terrorism." Richardson points out that this narrative was constructed even as videos circulated proving the shooter made no effort to move away from the car, suggesting he did not feel in danger.

The administration's strategy, as Richardson frames it, is to create a reality where facts are secondary to political loyalty. She notes that the White House's response to the shooting was not an investigation, but a demand for Americans to "abandon reality in favor of whatever the administration says." This approach is reinforced by the deployment of 2,000 federal agents to Minneapolis, an operation Governor Tim Walz described as "a war that's being waged against Minnesota." Richardson writes, "You're seeing that we have a ridiculous surge of apparently 2,000 people not coordinating with us, that are for a show of cameras." The sheer scale of the deployment, without state coordination, underscores the executive branch's willingness to bypass local governance to project power.

The historical context of the Insurrection Act of 1807 looms over the piece. Richardson notes that Governor Walz prepared to call out the Minnesota National Guard, reminding citizens that "the Minnesota National Guard does not wear masks and that it is theirs, not Trump's." This distinction is crucial: it highlights the tension between federal overreach and state sovereignty. By invoking the National Guard, local leaders are effectively blocking the administration's potential move to invoke the Insurrection Act, a tool historically used to suppress domestic unrest but rarely deployed in such a brazen manner against a state's own citizens.

Financial and Geopolitical Distractions

While the nation grappled with the shooting in Minneapolis, Richardson reveals a parallel strategy of financial maneuvering and geopolitical aggression. The administration quietly sought a $6.2 million payout in attorney's fees from Fulton County, Georgia, after charges related to election interference were dismissed. Richardson notes, "Trump had been indicted for trying to overturn the 2020 presidential election in Georgia... In November 2025 a new special prosecutor dropped the charges, citing the difficulty of prosecuting a case against a sitting president." This move, she argues, transforms a legal victory into a financial windfall, further blurring the lines between public office and personal gain.

The piece also exposes the administration's entanglement in cryptocurrency and foreign oil. Richardson details how the Trump family's crypto venture, World Liberty Financial, applied for a national banking license, a move that critics say reveals a "major conflict of interest." The company's leadership includes Zach Witkoff, whose father was handpicked by Vladimir Putin as an envoy to Russia. Simultaneously, Energy Secretary Chris Wright announced that the U.S. would seize all oil from Venezuela, with proceeds funneled into "off-shore" accounts. Richardson writes, "Yesterday, Trump announced that, as president of the United States, he would control the money from the sale of Venezuelan oil." This concentration of power over foreign resources and domestic finance raises profound questions about the integrity of the executive branch.

The military budget expansion is another focal point. Richardson quotes the administration's claim that tariffs would fund a $1.5 trillion military budget, a figure that defies economic logic. She notes, "If it weren't for the tremendous numbers being produced by Tariffs from other Countries... I would stay at the $1 Trillion Dollar number." This rhetoric, she argues, is designed to distract from the reality of the shooting and the administration's authoritarian tendencies. Simon Rosenberg of The Hopium Chronicles is quoted as saying, "Trump has gone completely mad," a sentiment that Richardson's evidence seems to support.

"To the family, I'm so deeply sorry," Frey said. "There's nothing that I can say right now that's going to make you or your relatives, friends of the victim feel any better."

Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey's blunt assessment of the federal presence—"Get the f*ck out of Minneapolis"—captures the local anger and the failure of the administration's strategy. Richardson uses this moment to underscore the disconnect between the federal narrative and the lived experience of the community. The administration's attempt to frame the shooting as a victory for law enforcement is not just false; it is an insult to the victims and their families.

Bottom Line

Richardson's strongest argument is her meticulous dissection of the administration's disinformation campaign, proving that the official narrative is not just wrong, but deliberately constructed to suppress dissent. Her biggest vulnerability is the sheer volume of concurrent crises she covers, which risks overwhelming the reader with the scale of the administration's overreach. However, the piece succeeds in connecting the dots between the shooting in Minneapolis, the financial maneuvers in Washington, and the geopolitical aggression abroad, revealing a coherent strategy of power consolidation. The reader must watch for the administration's next move: whether it will double down on the lie or face the consequences of a reality that refuses to be rewritten.

by Heather Cox Richardson · Letters from an American · Read full article

This morning, a federal agent from Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) shot and killed 37-year-old Renee Nicole Good as she was driving away from ICE agents on a residential street in Minneapolis, Minnesota. According to Minneapolis leaders, Good was a legal observer: a volunteer trained to observe police conduct in case of future legal action.

Three videos taken at the scene show a maroon SUV perpendicular on a snowy street. A silver SUV driving up the street stops. Two officers wearing badges that say “police” and body armor get out of the vehicle and walk toward the maroon car.

One of them says, “Get out of the f*cking car,” and the other reaches through the open driver’s side window while trying to open the door. The driver backs up the vehicle, and straightens the wheel as if making a three-point turn. Then she starts slowly to accelerate along the street.

A third officer who has been standing on the side of the road pulls out a gun as the car is turning away. He shoots three times. The maroon car does not hit anyone as it rolls up the street, hitting another vehicle and then a utility pole. The shooter walks briskly away, apparently uninjured.

Seen in slow motion, a video shows the wheels of the maroon vehicle were fully turned away from the shooting officer, who made no effort to jump away, clearly suggesting he did not feel as if he were in danger. His first shot went through the windshield; the next two went through the driver’s side window as the car moved past him. An onlooker shouted “What the f*ck?!”

Video taken by another eyewitness shows ICE agents refusing to allow a self-identified physician to tend to the victim and telling him to back up. Although there is no one tending the clearly visible woman in the car, an agent says: “We have medics on scene. We have our own medics.” When another bystander screams: “Where are they? WHERE ARE THEY?!” an agent tells her, “Relax.” “How can I relax?” she shouts. “You just killed my f*cking neighbor.”

Yesterday the Trump administration deployed federal agents and officers to Minneapolis for what they called the largest federal immigration operation ever carried out, eventually planning to deploy 2,000 agents.
