We Chased Driverless Trucks In Texas. What We Saw Will Scare You

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·May 28, 2025 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union doesn't just report on the arrival of driverless trucks; they frame it as an immediate, visceral threat to the American middle class, arguing that the "driver shortage" is a manufactured excuse for wage suppression. By chasing a test vehicle down Interstate 45 in Texas, the author exposes a regulatory vacuum where Silicon Valley engineers are rewriting the rules of labor without public oversight. This is not a distant future scenario; it is a race happening right now on the most dangerous freight corridor in America, and the stakes are the livelihoods of millions of high school-educated men.

The Myth of the Shortage

The piece cuts through the industry's standard narrative with surgical precision. More Perfect Union writes, "The trucking industry has faced and continues to face a structural challenge with not having access to enough drivers... They're not addressing a shortage because there isn't one. There's never been one." The author argues that the industry is suffering from a crisis of retention, not a lack of qualified workers, driven by decades of deregulation and deteriorating working conditions. This evidence is compelling because it reframes the automation push not as a necessary response to labor scarcity, but as a strategic move to eliminate human costs entirely.

Critics might argue that even if a shortage doesn't exist today, the aging workforce and the physical toll of the job will inevitably create one tomorrow. However, the author's point stands that companies are using the fear of a shortage to justify replacing humans with machines rather than fixing the broken business model that drives workers away in the first place. As the text notes, "There's a shortage of willingness to invest in people and pay them better."

The Safety Promise vs. The Job Reality

The tension between public safety and private profit forms the emotional core of the coverage. Aurora Innovation, led by former Google and Tesla executives, promises a future with zero fatalities. More Perfect Union captures this stark trade-off: "The Aurora driver can drive that number to zero... But is there a way to make it work for us instead of against us?" The author highlights that while eliminating accidents is a noble goal, the path to get there involves removing the human element from the most stable, well-paying trucking jobs: long-haul linehaul routes.

This framing is effective because it refuses to accept the false dichotomy that we must choose between safety and employment. The piece suggests that the technology itself is neutral, but the corporate strategy is not. "Self-driving trucks aren't going to do anything to truck drivers. Their employers are going to do something to truck drivers," the author asserts. This distinction is crucial; it shifts the blame from the invention to the implementation, forcing readers to question who benefits from the "efficiency" of a driverless cab.

"The advent of self-driving trucks, in my view, is actually the end point of a long series of technologies that have caused a decline in wages and working conditions."

The John Henry Parallel

Perhaps the most striking element of the commentary is the historical parallel drawn between today's truckers and the folk hero John Henry, who famously raced a steam drill to prove human value. More Perfect Union writes, "I saw this YouTube of a Budweiser truck being driven by a driverless truck and it was like this visceral fear that gripped me... I really felt like okay this must be what John Henry felt like when the steam driven hammering machines came out." This analogy elevates the story from a policy debate to a cultural moment, connecting the modern struggle of the working class to a timeless narrative of man versus machine.

The author notes that while some propose "platooning"—where one human leads a convoy of autonomous trucks—as a compromise, the reality is likely to be a net loss for workers. "Those jobs that go along a particular lane... are going to be the ones that get automated next," leaving drivers with the lower-paid, more dangerous "first and last mile" work. This observation underscores the fragility of the current labor market and the speed at which automation can erode job security.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union delivers a powerful indictment of the unregulated rollout of autonomous vehicles, successfully arguing that the "driver shortage" is a convenient fiction used to mask a desire for cheaper, disposable labor. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to treat technology as an inevitable force of nature, instead highlighting the policy choices that will determine whether this innovation serves society or destroys it. The biggest vulnerability remains the sheer momentum of capital; without aggressive federal intervention, state-level bills like Texas's HB 4402 may be too little, too late to stop the tide of automation. Readers should watch closely for how the federal government responds to these state-level experiments, as the outcome will define the future of American freight and the families that depend on it.
