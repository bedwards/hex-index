The Indian Genius Nobody Understands

Derek Muller · Veritasium ·Aug 10, 2025 · 29 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

One of history's most extraordinary mathematicians came from nowhere. He had no university education. No formal training. Yet the formulas he produced baffled even the world's brightest minds — equations so complex that top mathematicians spent pages trying to unpack them. This is the story of Srinivasa Ramanujan: a genius whose religious devotion to mathematics produced work that still puzzles scholars today.

The Caste and the Child

Ramanujan was born into a respected family in colonial India. His parents belonged to the Brahmin caste — the highest rank in the Hindu social hierarchy. But being Brahmin meant more about spiritual status than wealth. His family was poor. To survive, they took in subtenants for extra income.

He was a quiet child who disliked school. He resisted the rigid system that told him what to do. After repeatedly skipping classes, his family hired a local police officer to escort him to school.

Eventually, his family settled in Kumbaakarnam. At last, Ramanujan began to excel — especially in mathematics. His classmates came to him for help. He breezed through exams in half the time and soon challenged his teachers themselves.

Around age 13, his math teacher explained a basic concept: divide anything by itself and the result is always one. Whether you divide three fruits among three people or a thousand fruits among a thousand, each person gets one fruit. Then young Ramanujan spoke up. But what about zero divided by zero? If no fruits are divided among no one, will each still get one?

It was the first sign of an unusual mind.

The Book That Changed Everything

By age 16, Ramanujan had independently rediscovered solutions to quadratic equations — a major mathematical problem from Renaissance Italy. He dove into advanced topics like infinite series, far beyond standard curriculum. Most classmates and even teachers couldn't understand what he was talking about.

Then he discovered a book. Likely passed along by a friend from the local college library, it was essentially a list of 5,000 equations and theorems with almost no explanations — meant to help math students cram for exams. By the time Ramanujan found it, it was already more than a decade old.

He became completely absorbed. In mathematics, proof is everything. Mathematicians must show step by step why equations are always true. But this book rarely provided any proof. It left readers to figure things out on their own.

For Ramanujan, every equation was a puzzle begging to be solved. He tackled them using his own methods — far from anything in academia.

But he didn't just study the numbers. He developed religious devotion to them.

The Devotion

Ramanujan was a devout Hindu with deep faith. He regularly prayed to the family goddess Amagiri and followed what he believed to be her will. He credits all his extraordinary mathematical abilities to the gods.

He didn't see math as a human concept. He saw it as a window into deeper structure of the universe.

"An equation for me has no meaning unless it expresses a thought of God."

After discovering that book, he finished school with top marks — not just in math, but across subjects. The school's director said he deserved more than an A+. Ramanujan was off the scale.

He earned a scholarship to college in his hometown. But this is where things fell apart.

Academic Ruin

For the first time in his life, Ramanujan faced real failures. The college expected him to attend multiple lectures, but he lost interest in anything besides math. While professors lectured on Roman history, he scribbled formulas — completely absorbed in algebra, trigonometry, and calculus.

This came with major consequences. Eventually, he lost his scholarship. His parents were pouring what little money they had into his education. He felt guilty and miserable. The pressure to perform in every subject mounted, but he was terrified it would take him away from the one subject he lived for.

The strain became too much. One day, he walked away — dropping out of college.

At 18, he tried studying at a different college but failed again. By his early twenties, he desperately searched for work. Everyone close to him knew he was a genius, but there seemed to be no place for him in academia.

Around this time, his mother arranged a marriage for him. His bride was just nine years old.

The pressure to earn a living grew heavier. Day after day, he worked in solitude, driven by ideas no one around him fully understood. He carried personal notebooks worn from use — packed cover to cover with equations and theorems.

Three of those notebooks survive, though he likely had more. The first one was written in green ink, packed with hypergeometric series and continued fractions. It was organized into chapters with theorems neatly numbered. But over time, that order began to fray. When he ran out of space, he scribbled calculations on the backs of pages.

For Ramanujan, these notebooks were his ticket to a job related to mathematics.

The Letter

He presented them to influential people who might help. Eventually, someone from the newly founded Indian Mathematical Society in Pune saw his potential. They agreed to support him, allowing him to focus entirely on mathematics.

It didn't take long for Ramanujan to publish his first paper in the society's journal — a piece on Bernoulli numbers that revealed his talent with infinite sums. But still no one truly understood his brilliance. The modest salary wasn't enough. He had to work as a clerk at the port in Madras just to make ends meet.

Those close to him urged him repeatedly to contact mathematicians in the west. If no one in India could support him, maybe they would.

He started writing letters but nothing came of them. Then finally he wrote to Godfrey Harold Hardy — a rising mathematician and fellow at Trinity College Cambridge. At this time, Cambridge was regarded as the center of mathematics in England.

Hardy was just 35 but already highly respected. He shared deep passion for mathematics but didn't believe in God. He was outspoken and never shy about sharing opinions, especially regarding other mathematicians' work. Hardy was full of contradictions — strict and serious, but also kind and generous. He claimed not to care about untalented students, yet he never let any of them fail.

Ramanujan's letter arrived in 1913. Hardy would later call it the most remarkable letter he ever received.

The first sentences were brutally honest:

"Dear sir, I beg to introduce myself to you as a clerk in the accounts department of the port trust office at Madras on a salary of only twenty pounds. I am now about twenty-three years of age. I have had no university education."

Ramanujan wrote that he'd been doing mathematics in his spare time and didn't shy away from being called a genius.

"I've not trodden through the conventional regular course which is followed in a university course, but I am striking out a new path for myself. I've made a special investigation of divergent series theories in general, and the results I get are termed by the local mathematicians as startling."

To catch Hardy's attention, Ramanujan referred to a paper Hardy had published. In it, Hardy asserted that a specific term's precise order has not been determined. In the letter, Ramanujan disagreed — and even provided a solution.

This sounded insane. A poor clerk without a college degree claimed he knew better than some of the most famous mathematicians in history.

But Ramanujan didn't stop there. He'd attached at least nine pages filled with equations, hoping they might be published. And of course, he wanted Hardy's feedback.

Hardy studied the pages — neatly arranged equations laid out in the style of that old book. But the content was unlike anything he'd ever seen. The numbers and symbols were familiar, but their arrangement felt alien — as if they came from another world.

Some equations were crisp and clear but seemed outright impossible. One where Ramanujan claimed that rather than infinity, the sum of all natural numbers can be written as minus twelve. A statement that turned out to be true — although not in the way we usually add numbers. It relied on highly advanced number theory.

In Ramanujan's notes, there were hardly any explanations for what he'd come up with.

Hardy's first reaction was suspicion. This had to be some kind of hoax. He set the letter aside and picked up the newspaper just like any other ordinary day.

But the letter continued to linger on his mind. What if this poor Indian clerk was actually brilliant?

Hardy showed Ramanujan's letter to almost everyone at Cambridge. He sent parts of it to experts in specific fields. And he reached out to a colleague, John Littlewood — just two years older than Ramanujan but unlike him, Littlewood had received the finest education. Like Hardy, he was already regarded as one of the leading mathematicians of his generation.

The two spent hours pouring over the formulas. After a while, they made a discovery. Not every formula was new. Even so, only a mathematician of the highest caliber could have produced work like this.

The more they analyzed the work, the more dazzled they became. Hardy and Littlewood were convinced that even the toughest exams wouldn't cover the kinds of theorems Ramanujan was creating — just too advanced.

Just before midnight, they reached their conclusion: these formulas must be true because if they were not, no one would have the imagination to invent them.

This wasn't merely the work of an exceptional mathematician. This was the work of a genius.

Hardy decided to write back. He was thrilled by Ramanujan's ideas but insisted on something crucial — proof.

Critics might note that Ramanujan's lack of formal proofs made many mathematicians skeptical of his work during his lifetime. Some of his results were later proven correct, but without documentation, they remained unverifiable. His religious framing — that equations expressed God's thoughts — could be seen as unconventional and even mystical rather than rational. But it was precisely this devotion that produced mathematics unlike anything academia had ever seen.

Bottom Line

Ramanujan's story is extraordinary not just because of his mathematical genius but because he proved that brilliance can emerge from anywhere — including poverty, exclusion, and systems that dismissed him entirely. His biggest vulnerability: the academic establishment required proof to validate his work, yet his lack of formal training meant he never learned to provide it in ways that satisfied traditional standards. That tension between raw talent and institutional validation is what makes his story so compelling — and unresolved.
