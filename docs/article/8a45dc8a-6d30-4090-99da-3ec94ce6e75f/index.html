The Film That Should Be Nominated

Various · Animation Obsessive ·Mar 8, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In a year dominated by box-office titans and streaming giants, the author of this piece makes a startling claim: the true future of animation isn't found in the Oscar-nominated heavyweights, but in a low-budget, 2D Chinese film that barely made it to American screens. Various argues that the exclusion of Nobody from the Academy Awards isn't just an administrative oversight, but a symptom of a "provincial" industry blind to the most resonant storytelling of the year. For the busy listener seeking substance over spectacle, this is a necessary correction to the cultural narrative.

The Provincial Blind Spot

The article opens by dismantling the assumption that the current Oscar slate represents the pinnacle of the medium. While acknowledging the massive success of Zootopia 2 and the Netflix hit KPop Demon Hunters, the author points out a glaring gap: the absence of China's cinematic output. "The largest animated feature of 2025 won't be there, nor will (arguably) the finest," Various writes, highlighting the irony that Nezha 2 broke every box-office record yet was never even submitted, while Nobody slipped through the cracks due to a lack of strategic theatrical distribution.

This framing is effective because it shifts the focus from popularity to artistic merit. The author suggests that the Academy's criteria favor films with aggressive marketing campaigns over those with genuine cultural depth. "It's a shame — because Nobody, especially, feels like a sign of animation's future," they note. This is a bold assertion, but the evidence provided regarding the film's emotional impact in its home country lends it weight. Critics might argue that the Oscars are inherently bound by submission rules and cannot simply nominate films that weren't entered, but the piece successfully argues that the rules themselves create a barrier to global art.

The Myth of the "Nobody"

The heart of the commentary lies in its exploration of director Yu Shui's journey. The author paints a portrait of a creator who spent two decades as an academic before finally getting his chance. "At the beginning, I had nothing and worked alone," Yu is quoted as saying, a sentiment that mirrors the film's own themes of underdog resilience. The article details how Yu's team, rather than relying on flash or high-budget 3D rendering, turned to "mythological realism," grounding their fantasy in the tangible textures of Shanxi Province's Buddhist statues and traditional ink-wash painting.

The author's analysis of the film's aesthetic choices is particularly sharp. They explain that the team deliberately avoided the "elastic, smoothly flowing deformation" typical of American animation in favor of characters that felt "lived-in" and "a little bent and clumsy." "We're portraying the most ordinary people from everyday life," explains producer Chen Liaoyu, as cited by the author. This approach challenges the notion that animation must be polished to be powerful. The piece argues that Nobody's success proves that "it's not that you can't be cool, but don't be cool for the sake of being cool... above all, you need to tell a good story."

Most people, myself included, are just ordinary individuals. But how do ordinary people establish themselves and navigate life? So many things are beyond our control — whether it's the demands of our career, family or society.

This quote, attributed to Yu Shui, serves as the emotional anchor of the article. The author uses it to illustrate how the film transcends its specific cultural setting to address universal human anxieties. The commentary suggests that the film's "frauds"—a boar, a toad, a weasel, and a gorilla—resonate because they are trying to find their own path in a world shaped by external forces. This is a compelling argument for why the film deserves recognition, even if the Academy ignores it.

A Global Conversation

The piece concludes by broadening its scope, noting that Nobody is part of a larger trend of small, thoughtful animation rising globally, citing the French film Flow as another example of a low-resource film achieving massive success. "The untold stories are coming to light," the author writes, suggesting that the industry is slowly shifting away from the "lavish, over-the-top" style that has dominated recent years. The inclusion of news about other international projects, from Ireland's Cartoon Saloon to Cuba's Animaluz Academy, reinforces the idea that animation is a global conversation, not a Hollywood monologue.

However, the author's optimism about this shift might be slightly premature. While Nobody and Flow are successes, the market still overwhelmingly favors big-budget franchises. The piece acknowledges this tension but ultimately champions the idea that "tiny movies, wherever they're made, continue to have a shot against giant ones." This is an inspiring, if perhaps idealistic, conclusion.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its rigorous defense of "mythological realism" as a viable and superior alternative to the glossy, effects-heavy style of modern blockbusters. Its biggest vulnerability is the assumption that the Oscar snub is purely a failure of the Academy, rather than a complex issue of international distribution and qualification rules. Readers should watch for whether the industry can sustain this momentum for small-scale, culturally specific films as the next wave of animation releases hits theaters.

1. The underdogs

Next Sunday, the Oscars happen. The nominees have been public for a while. And, in the list of animated features, there are conspicuous holes.

Major and highly regarded projects got nominated, no doubt. Among them: the biggest box-office smash in Hollywood animation history (Zootopia 2), and the most-watched original film from Netflix (KPop Demon Hunters). The two contenders from Europe, Arco and Little Amélie, have won awards worldwide.

If you follow animation closely, though, you might wonder: “what about China?”

Although Zootopia 2 broke records, Nezha 2 broke more of them. It out-earned every movie everywhere during 2025 — and it’s widely loved. Yet it isn’t present here. Reports suggest it wasn’t even submitted to the Oscars.1

There’s a second Chinese absentee, too. It’s a wonderful film watched by too few outside China. In its home country, it was a “dark horse” phenomenon last year — almost $250 million in revenue, on a budget below $10 million. People fell in love with it.2

It’s called Nobody, and its stateside distribution has been pretty thin. You can find subtitled copies floating around unofficially on YouTube — if you know where to look. It’s our favorite mainstream movie we’ve seen from 2025, and it belongs on any list of last year’s best animation.3 But it won’t be competing next Sunday.

Like with Nezha 2, there are reasons for Nobody’s omission. It didn’t make the longlist, possibly because its brief release in American theaters wasn’t tuned to get it qualified.4 This isn’t simply an oversight by the Academy.

Yet it still makes this year’s Oscars feel a bit provincial. The largest animated feature of 2025 won’t be there, nor will (arguably) the finest. And it’s a shame — because Nobody, especially, feels like a sign of animation’s future.

Before Nobody, Yu Shui wasn’t exactly a nobody himself. Years ago, his student film About Life (2004) won prizes, and his Flash series FOF and His Story (2013) had its fans.
