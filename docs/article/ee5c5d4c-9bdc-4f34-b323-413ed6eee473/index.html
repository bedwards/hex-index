Why Essential Workers Are Going Extinct

We're living through an age of job anxiety. Every week there's a new headline warning that artificial intelligence is coming for our livelihoods. We're told it could reshape everything from office jobs to creative work and even professions that once seemed untouchable. And that fear isn't irrational.

Big technological shifts have always reshaped labor markets. But while we're fixated on jobs that might disappear someday, something far more concrete is already happening. Countries around the developed world are quietly running out of teachers and doctors. These are some of the most stable jobs in the economy.

They don't vanish in recessions and societies quite literally cannot function without them. Yet across the OECD, the teaching workforce is aging fast just as fewer people are coming in behind them. Shortages have existed in education for a long time, but they were usually manageable. Lately, they haven't been.

According to UNESCO, maintaining today's education systems alone will require millions of new teachers by 2030. In healthcare, the picture is even starker. The World Health Organization warns of a global shortfall of around 11 million health workers by 2030, driven by aging populations and medical staff retiring faster than they're being replaced. Everyone agrees this is a problem.

And in practice, many developed countries have already leaned on the same solution. Instead of training more teachers and doctors, they've increasingly relied on immigration to keep schools and hospitals running. At first glance, that seems like a win-win. But this fix only works for as long as people stay.

And increasingly, they don't. So, as always, we've got some important questions to answer. Why are essential workers disappearing in the first place? Why can't migration continue to be the solution for this issue?
