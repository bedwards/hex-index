Megiddo 1457 BC - Oldest Battle in History - Bronze Age Documentary

Commentary by Brian Edwards

Kings and Generals makes a bold claim that resonates far beyond the battlefield: the Battle of Megiddo in 1457 BC is not just a skirmish, but the first war in human history for which we possess a detailed, contemporary record. This distinction transforms a dusty archaeological footnote into a living narrative of strategy, deception, and the birth of imperial ambition. The author's coverage is notable because it relies on the rare, first-hand journal of the scribe Tjaneni, offering a level of tactical granularity that most ancient history simply cannot provide.

The Geopolitical Stakes

The author frames the conflict not as an isolated incident, but as the inevitable result of a shifting power dynamic in the Bronze Age. Kings and Generals writes, "The Hyksos's takeover had taught the Egyptians that they were vulnerable to attack especially from western Asia." This observation is crucial; it explains why the New Kingdom pharaohs abandoned isolationism for aggressive expansion. The narrative effectively argues that the Egyptian policy of creating a buffer zone in Canaan was a direct response to the rise of massive northern empires like the Mitanni and the Hittites.

The text highlights a fascinating transition in Egyptian leadership. While Hatshepsut's reign was marked by prosperity and monumental building, her lack of military focus allowed vassal states to drift away. Kings and Generals notes that "her apparent lack of attention to military matters has led to many of Egypt's vassals... to gradually obtain more autonomy for themselves." This sets the stage for Thutmose III's ascension. The author presents the young pharaoh not merely as a successor, but as a corrective force, a warrior-king tasked with reversing the tide of rebellion that had crept up during his regency.

Critics might note that the sources regarding the Hyksos are heavily biased by later Egyptian propaganda, which paints them as cruel invaders rather than potential settlers. However, the author acknowledges this ambiguity, stating, "Regardless of the truth of their arrival..." which lends the narrative a necessary historical nuance.

The Gamble at Aruna

The core of the article's drama lies in the tactical decision-making at the town of Yehem. Here, the author showcases the brilliance of Thutmose III by contrasting his boldness with the caution of his generals. The tension is palpable as the pharaoh is presented with three routes: two safe but long, and one direct but perilous. Kings and Generals quotes the terrified advice of the council: "How will it be to go on this road which becomes narrow when it is reported that the enemies are waiting out there and they are numerous?"

This moment is the pivot point of the entire campaign. The author argues that Thutmose's genius was psychological; he understood that his enemies would expect the safe routes. By choosing the dangerous Aruna pass, he achieved total surprise. The text describes the march vividly, noting that the convoy stretched nearly 20 kilometers through a pass only 30 feet wide. Kings and Generals writes, "Thutmose had been right and had taken the king of Kadesh by surprise. He had not expected the Egyptians to come through the central pass."

The payoff of this gamble is described with visceral clarity. As the Egyptian forces emerged, the rebel army, expecting a frontal assault from a different direction, was caught off guard. The author captures the chaos of the retreat: "The Canaanites saw his majesty prevailing over them and they fled head-long to Megiddo with faces of fear." This quote underscores the psychological collapse of the enemy, a victory as much about morale as it was about military might.

The surprise attack had not only caught the Canaanite rebels off guard but also left them in a poor position to face the Egyptian chariots and infantrymen that were racing towards them from the south and west.

The Cost of Victory

The commentary takes a sharp turn to critique the aftermath, highlighting a critical failure in the Egyptian execution of the victory. Despite routing the enemy and watching them abandon their "chariots of gold and silver," the Egyptian army failed to capitalize immediately. Kings and Generals writes, "The battle would have ended there and then in a decisive victory for the Egyptians had Thutmose's usually disciplined troops not made a crucial mistake instead of..." The sentence trails off, but the implication is clear: the soldiers stopped to loot the abandoned enemy gear.

This detail is vital. It humanizes the ancient soldiers, showing that even in the presence of a god-king, the temptation of wealth can override military discipline. It also explains why the city of Megiddo was not taken immediately, prolonging the siege and the campaign. The author uses this moment to illustrate that even the greatest strategic minds are at the mercy of human nature.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals delivers a compelling reconstruction of history's first recorded battle, successfully blending archaeological evidence with narrative flair. The strongest part of the argument is the psychological profiling of Thutmose III, whose decision to take the Aruna pass is presented as a masterclass in military deception. The biggest vulnerability, however, lies in the reliance on a single source—the annals of Tjaneni—which, while detailed, is inherently a piece of state propaganda designed to glorify the pharaoh. Readers should watch for how future archaeological discoveries might challenge or confirm the specific troop numbers and tactical movements described in this account.
