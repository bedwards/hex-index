Lies of Heroism – Redefining the Anti-War Film

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Aug 31, 2020 · 41 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden tackles the most stubborn paradox in cinema: why do films intended to expose the horror of war often end up glorifying it? He argues that the ambiguity isn't a failure of filmmaking, but a reflection of a deeper human psychological trap where the desire to escape death fuels the very heroism that drives conflict. This is not a simple film review; it is a philosophical excavation of why we watch violence and how our need for "symbolic immortality" corrupts the anti-war message.

The Illusion of the Anti-War Film

The piece begins by dismantling the binary assumption that a war film is either pro-war or anti-war. Van der Linden notes that even legendary directors disagree on the medium's inherent nature, citing the contradictory claims of Stanley Kubrick and Steven Spielberg. He writes, "Kubrick saying that every film about war ends up being pro-war and Spielberg claiming that every war film good or bad is anti-war." This tension sets the stage for a more nuanced definition. The author suggests that true anti-war cinema requires more than just showing blood; it demands a specific psychological framing. As Van der Linden puts it, "Anti-war films must at least portray a nuanced view of deadly combat one that depicts the infliction of lethal violence as a norm of behavior and as a force striking down randomly."

However, he points out that even when films attempt this, the audience often misses the point. The spectacle of action can override the intended tragedy. He illustrates this by contrasting the chaotic, terrifying perspective of Dunkirk with the cinematic grandeur of Michael Bay's Pearl Harbor. Van der Linden writes, "Michael Bay filmed the devastating attack on Pearl Harbor with the same cinematic spectacle that is also seen in fictional Blockbuster adventures." This observation is crucial: the camera's language matters as much as the script. When the camera treats a real-life massacre with the same visual grammar as a superhero movie, the "anti-war" intent evaporates.

"The goal of this discussion however is not to end up with another dichotomy to label films as either Pro or anti-war but to construct a deeper understanding of a complicated subject that does not let itself be captured in such simplified terms."

Critics might argue that this focus on directorial intent ignores the viewer's agency, suggesting that audiences are capable of distinguishing between a thrill ride and a tragedy. Yet, Van der Linden's evidence regarding the "simulated" nature of the experience suggests otherwise. He reminds us that no matter how realistic the sound design, "the audience is and will always be observing it from a safe and comfortable distance." This safety creates a buffer that allows the horror to be consumed as entertainment rather than felt as a warning.

The Psychology of the Hero System

To explain why we are so easily seduced by war, Van der Linden pivots from film theory to existential philosophy, drawing heavily on the work of Ernest Becker. The core of his argument is that human culture is built on a "hero system" designed to help us deny our inevitable death. He writes, "Our primary driving force is the desire to escape death... unlike animals we are constantly aware of this inevitable fate." In this framework, war becomes a stage for symbolic immortality. The soldier who sacrifices their life is not just dying; they are achieving a form of eternal life through the memory of their heroism.

This psychological mechanism explains why films like American Sniper are so difficult to categorize. Van der Linden argues that the film captures the paradox of a man trapped in a "self-destructive hero system" while simultaneously indulging in his legendary status. He notes, "While the film does portray its main character in all his human fallibilities... it also indulges rather uncritically in his legendary hero status within that system." This is a sharp critique of how modern cinema often validates the very mindset it claims to question. The film offers the catharsis of the hero without the cost of the hero's delusion.

The argument deepens when Van der Linden introduces the concept of the "shadow"—the parts of ourselves we refuse to acknowledge. He explains that hero systems work by projecting this shadow onto an external enemy. "The shadow which is in conflict with the acknowledged values cannot be accepted as a negative part of One's Own psyche and is therefore projected," he writes. This projection is the root of scapegoating and, ultimately, genocide. By framing the enemy as the embodiment of evil, the hero system absolves the individual of their own capacity for darkness.

"Hero systems lead to scapegoating... it absolves individuals of their deepest struggles and unites them in the face of an external evil."

This section is the piece's intellectual heavy lifting, connecting the dots between personal psychology and global conflict. Van der Linden uses the rise of Nazi Germany to illustrate this, quoting Becker on how Hitler promised "heroic victory over evil" by identifying specific groups as "devils." While the historical application is clear, some might argue that reducing complex geopolitical conflicts solely to psychological projection oversimplifies the material causes of war, such as resource scarcity or territorial disputes. However, Van der Linden's focus remains on the human element that makes those conflicts possible.

The Banality of the Hero

The commentary culminates in a discussion of Hannah Arendt's "banality of evil," linking it back to the hero system. Van der Linden suggests that the true horror of war is not the presence of monsters, but the presence of "terrifyingly normal" people following orders within a system that demands heroism. He writes, "The banality of evil then was coined to point out this thoughtlessness with which evil acts can be committed." In Becker's terms, this is the ultimate failure of the hero system: the pursuit of symbolic immortality blinds the individual to the reality of their actions.

The film The Bridge on the River Kwai serves as the perfect case study here. The British officer becomes so obsessed with building a bridge as a monument to his own dignity that he inadvertently aids the enemy. Van der Linden writes, "He becomes so attached to his heroic goal that when a group of soldiers sneak into the camp to blow up the bridge he has become too deluded to recognize them as His allies." The tragedy is not just the destruction of the bridge, but the total loss of perspective that the hero system demands.

"The great paradox of human existence is that our consciousness seemingly transcends our animal nature in an almost Godlike fashion while our body does not."

This final synthesis is powerful. It suggests that the only way to make a truly anti-war film is to expose the mechanism of the hero system itself, rather than just the violence of the battlefield. It requires showing the audience that their desire for a hero is the very thing that fuels the war machine.

Bottom Line

Van der Linden's strongest contribution is reframing the debate from "is this film pro- or anti-war?" to "how does this film interact with our psychological need for heroism?" His argument holds up because it explains why even well-intentioned films often fail to stop war; they feed the very hunger they seek to starve. The piece's vulnerability lies in its heavy reliance on existential philosophy, which may feel abstract to readers looking for concrete film criticism, but this theoretical depth is exactly what makes the analysis so necessary. To truly understand war cinema, we must first understand the death-denying machinery of the human mind.
