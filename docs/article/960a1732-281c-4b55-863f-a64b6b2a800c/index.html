NEW POLL: Trump's approval stuck at record low as approval on prices sinks to -39

G. Elliott Morris · G. Elliott Morris ·Mar 24, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

G. Elliott Morris delivers a stark reality check for the political class: President Donald Trump is not just struggling; he is facing a historic collapse in public trust, driven by a perfect storm of economic pain and foreign policy overreach. What makes this March 2026 polling data distinctive is not just the record-low numbers, but the specific convergence of a new war in Iran and surging gas prices that has erased the President's last remaining political advantage. This is not a standard midterm slump; it is a fundamental unraveling of the voter coalition that returned Trump to office.

The Economy and the War Machine

The core of the argument rests on a brutal correlation: the administration's foreign policy decisions are directly tanking its domestic approval. Morris writes, "Trump's approval on prices and inflation has cratered to net -39, the worst rating on any issue in the history of our poll." This is a devastating metric. When a president's strongest issue becomes their weakest, the political foundation cracks. The author connects this directly to the national average price of gas, which has jumped over a dollar since the conflict with Iran began in late February.

The data suggests voters are punishing the administration with a clarity that transcends partisan loyalty. As Morris notes, "58% of Americans say the war is a bad use of taxpayer dollars... 61% would oppose it if gas prices were to rise by $1 or more per gallon." This is a pragmatic, almost transactional rejection of military escalation. It echoes the public sentiment during the 1973 oil crisis, where a sudden spike in fuel costs triggered a profound shift in how Americans viewed government competence and foreign entanglements. Just as the 1973 crisis exposed the fragility of the American economic model, this 2026 data exposes the fragility of the current administration's "America First" rhetoric when it collides with the reality of global supply chains.

"Trump enters April without a single issue where more Americans approve than disapprove of his performance."

Critics might argue that the margin of error in polling makes the shift on border security statistically insignificant, but the trend line is undeniable. Morris points out that "border security, his last remaining bright spot, slipped from net 0 to net -2." This is the moment the "law and order" narrative loses its shield. The author effectively argues that the administration has no safe harbor left. Every pillar of support—economy, foreign policy, border security—has been compromised.

The Erosion of the Base

Perhaps the most alarming finding for the President's allies is not the opposition's strength, but the defection of his own. The polling reveals a fissure in the Republican base that goes beyond typical election fatigue. Morris highlights that "13% of Trump voters say they regret how they voted in 2024 — twice the rate of Harris voters." This asymmetry is critical. It suggests that the coalition is not just shrinking; it is actively disillusioned.

The author contextualizes this by noting the specific preferences of these regretful voters. While not all will switch sides, the fact that "28% said they'd switch to Harris" indicates a vulnerability that is far more dangerous than simple apathy. This mirrors the psychological phenomenon often studied in the Hawthorne effect, where the mere act of observation or the realization of consequences can alter behavior. Here, the "observation" is the daily reality of $3.96 gas and a war with no clear end, forcing voters to re-evaluate their initial choices. The data shows that the "buyer's remorse" is real, and it is growing faster among the President's supporters than among his opponents.

A Trust Deficit on Democracy

The poll also introduces a new metric that has immediate, explosive implications: trust in elections and democracy. Morris writes, "Trump's net approval on elections and democracy is -21... That immediately makes it one of his worst-rated issues." This is a significant development because it places the integrity of the political system itself as a top-tier concern for voters, ranking fourth in importance behind prices, jobs, and health care.

The author argues that this issue is not just a niche concern for political junkies but a mainstream priority. "24% named elections and democracy as one of their top three most important issues," Morris states. This suggests that the administration's rhetoric on the electoral process is backfiring, creating a trust deficit that Democrats are successfully capitalizing on. The generic ballot reflects this shift, with Democrats leading by six points. Morris observes that "Democrats have led in every poll since we launched the monthly partnership in May 2025," suggesting a structural advantage that is hard to overcome.

"Voters trust the party on the issues they rate as most important to them."

A counterargument worth considering is that the "generic ballot" is often a poor predictor of actual midterm outcomes, as the out-party typically gains ground regardless of specific issue performance. However, the depth of the dissatisfaction here—where 52% of Americans say "things are going poorly and major changes are needed"—suggests this is more than a standard cycle. The desire for "major, disruptive changes" is a powerful force that often overrides historical trends.

Bottom Line

G. Elliott Morris has assembled a compelling case that the Trump presidency in 2026 is defined by a total loss of issue ownership, driven by a self-inflicted economic crisis and an unpopular war. The strongest part of this argument is the data showing the complete erosion of the President's "strongman" brand on border security and the economy simultaneously. The biggest vulnerability for the administration is the rising rate of regret among its own voters, a sentiment that is historically difficult to reverse once it takes hold. The reader should watch for whether the "major changes" demanded by the majority translate into a midterm wave or if the political system can absorb this level of dissatisfaction without fracturing further.
