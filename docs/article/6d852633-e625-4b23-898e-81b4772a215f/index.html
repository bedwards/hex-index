I Think Faster Than Light Travel is Possible. Here's Why

Sabine Hossenfelder · Sabine Hossenfelder ·Apr 8, 2023 · 20 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sabine Hossenfelder, a theoretical physicist known for her skepticism of hype, makes a startling claim: the universal speed limit isn't a law of nature, but a mathematical artifact of our current understanding. While most physicists treat the speed of light as an unbreakable barrier, Hossenfelder argues that the evidence for this limit is far less solid than we've been taught, especially when considering the origins of mass itself.

The Illusion of the Barrier

Hossenfelder begins by dismantling the common intuition that faster-than-light travel is strictly forbidden. She writes, "I believe there's intelligent life on other planets and the most plausible reason why they haven't contacted us is that we're too boring... we haven't even figured out how to send information faster than light pathetic but wait let me guess you've heard that it's impossible to send information faster than the speed of light because physics yes I've heard that too but I think it's wrong." This opening is effective because it immediately reframes the Fermi Paradox—the question of why we haven't seen aliens—not as a sign of their absence, but as a sign of our technological immaturity. By admitting she previously made a video on this topic that she herself didn't understand, she disarms the reader's skepticism, inviting them to reconsider a fundamental assumption alongside her.

The core of her argument rests on the distinction between a "limit" and a "barrier." She explains that while you cannot accelerate a massive object to the speed of light because it would require infinite energy, Einstein's theory does not actually forbid objects that are already moving faster than light. "The problem seems to be instead that you can't Accelerate from below the speed of light to above the speed of light it's more like a barrier than a limit," she notes. This is a crucial nuance often lost in pop-science explanations. Critics might note that while mathematically permissible in relativity, such "tachyons" (hypothetical faster-than-light particles) introduce severe causality issues, but Hossenfelder is careful to separate the mathematical possibility from the physical mechanism of acceleration.

The idea that the speed of light is a limit comes from Albert Einstein's theory of special relativity... but this argument has some issues.

Where Mass Really Comes From

To explain why the "infinite energy" argument fails, Hossenfelder dives into the origin of mass, revealing that most of what we think of as matter is actually energy. She writes, "most of the mass of objects around you isn't really Mass it's binding energy... most of the mass of neutrons and protons is The Binding energy from the strong nuclear force that holds them together." This is a profound simplification of quantum chromodynamics, stripping away the jargon to reveal that humans are "almost entirely made of pure energy." By grounding the abstract concept of mass in the familiar context of atomic structure, she makes the subsequent argument about the Higgs field much more accessible.

She then pivots to the Higgs field, describing it not as a mystical force, but as a phase transition similar to water vapor condensing into dew. "The hick field has done a very similar thing not in the early morning but in the early Universe... in the early Universe it was really hot there was a hick field but it wasn't condensed." Her analogy of the early universe cooling down and the field condensing to give particles mass is a masterclass in explanation. It allows her to argue that if mass is a result of this field, then the equation requiring infinite energy to reach light speed breaks down when mass approaches zero. "Mathematically it's pretty obvious what goes wrong with the earlier argument if you look at this equation again you see that if this Factor goes to zero but the mass also goes to zero then the ratio can well remain finite," she asserts. This logical pivot is the piece's strongest intellectual move, suggesting that the "barrier" is an artifact of assuming mass is constant, rather than a fundamental law of the universe.

The Time Travel Paradox

The final hurdle Hossenfelder addresses is the fear of time travel paradoxes. She acknowledges the standard argument: if you can send a message faster than light, you can send it back in time. "Imagine that this video greatly disturbs you and you send a message to your younger self to not watch it then you'd never have sent the message in the first place so did you or didn't you watch it," she illustrates. This is the classic grandfather paradox, and it is the primary reason physicists dismiss faster-than-light communication.

However, Hossenfelder treats this with a healthy dose of pragmatism. She points out that the paradox relies on a specific interpretation of causality that might not hold up under closer scrutiny. While she doesn't fully resolve the paradox in this text, her willingness to engage with it without immediately retreating to "it's impossible" is refreshing. She suggests that the paradox is a sign that our current models of time and causality are incomplete, rather than proof that faster-than-light travel is impossible. A counterargument worth considering is that even if the math allows it, the energy requirements to manipulate the Higgs field or create a warp drive remain astronomically beyond our reach, making the theoretical possibility practically irrelevant for the foreseeable future.

Bottom Line

Hossenfelder's argument is compelling because it attacks the "infinite energy" barrier not with new physics, but by exposing a flaw in how we apply old physics to the origin of mass. Her strongest point is the realization that mass is not an intrinsic property but a dynamic result of the Higgs field, which changes the energy equation entirely. The biggest vulnerability remains the causality paradox; while she acknowledges it, she doesn't offer a definitive solution to how faster-than-light travel could coexist with a consistent timeline. Readers should watch for how this theoretical framework holds up against future experimental data on the Higgs field and the nature of dark energy.
