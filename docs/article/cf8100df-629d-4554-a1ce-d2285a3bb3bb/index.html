Hollywood's Diversity Crisis Isn't a Market Problem—It's a Political One

A new UCLA report shows representation behind the camera has hit its lowest point since 2018. But the data tells a different story than what studios want to tell us.

The Numbers

The 2026 Hollywood Diversity Report documents grim results across nearly every major category. Women directors, BIPOC leads, and writers all lost ground or stalled. The report's authors identified the cause: as the country experiences blowback against diversity, studios have relapsed into what they call "colorblind complacency."

But the data challenges the story studios tell.

The Market Excuse

Someone at a studio, on cable news, or in social media mentions will say audiences rejected diverse films. They will claim moviegoers got "fatigued." Diverse films "underperformed." It was never political—just business.

The evidence says otherwise.

Films with casts between 41 and 50 percent BIPOC—which roughly mirrors the actual U.S. population—generated the highest median global box office of any cast diversity interval in 2025. BIPOC moviegoers bought the majority of opening weekend tickets for 11 of the top 20 films. Horror, the most profitable genre by return on investment, is disproportionately driven by Black and Latino audiences. Ryan Coogler's "Sinners" made history as the most Oscar-nominated film of all time and grossed $368 million.

The market is not rejecting diversity. The market keeps paying for it. Studios are choosing to ignore the receipt.

So if this isn't about profit, what is it about?

The Original Alliance

In 1944, Hollywood's most powerful figures formed an organization called the Motion Picture Alliance for the Preservation of American Ideals. Founding members included Walt Disney, director King Vidor, Sam Wood, and Leo McCarein. John Wayne and Gary Cooper were among its prominent supporters.

Their statement pledged "to fight, with every means at our organised command, any effort of any group or individual, to divert the loyalty of the screen from the free America that gave it birth." They dedicated their work to "the presentation of the American scene, its standards and its freedoms, its beliefs and its ideals."

Underneath the patriotic language was a precise enforcement mechanism.

The Alliance defined what counted as un-American in Hollywood, and the list should sound familiar: leftist ideas, union organizing, any critique of law enforcement or nationalist mythology, anything that challenged white, Christian, patriarchal norms. What they called "subversion," we might recognize today as early stirrings of what their successors would eventually brand as "woke."

Walt Disney was not just a name on the letterhead. According to Neal Gabler's biography, Disney brought armed guards to his studio when cartoonists attempted to form a union. He fired organizers, cut wages, and testified enthusiastically before the House Un-American Activities Committee about what he described as communist infiltration of the industry. He viewed the Screen Actors Guild itself as a communist front. His anti-union ferocity was matched only by his willingness to name names.

The Alliance was performing a crucial political service: linking in the public imagination the words "Hollywood" and "Red." Producer Walter Wanger noted in 1944 that the Alliance had accomplished this "without proof." The evidence was never really the point. The point was the association.

How the Blacklist Worked

The pipeline from the Alliance to the Hollywood Blacklist was short and well-mainished.

By 1947, the House Un-American Activities Committee subpoenaed writers, directors, and actors, demanding they answer for their political beliefs and deliver up the names of colleagues. Those who cooperated became "friendly witnesses." Those who refused became the Hollywood Ten—primarily screenwriters who refused to answer on First Amendment grounds, were cited for contempt of Congress, and were subsequently jailed for sentences of up to a year. Five of them were under studio contract at the time and were fired within weeks.

The blacklist grew from there. According to research by Stanford professor Hayagreeva Rao, artists not directly named by HUAC saw their chances of employment drop by 13 percent simply for having previously worked with someone who was. Actors faced a 20 percent employment drop if they had collaborated with blacklisted writers. Even Oscar winners were not protected.

The chilling effect was the actual product. Studios did not need to jail every dissident. They only needed to make the industry understand which topics were off-limits: class struggle, police brutality, labor power, anti-fascism, racial critique. Studios responded by turning toward what they called patriotic, "American" fare. Most of it was poor art and box-office poison. They made it anyway, because the goal was never to make money. The goal was to demonstrate loyalty.

Screenwriter Dalton Trumbo kept writing under a pseudonym. His script for "The Brave One" won the Academy Award in 1956, credited to a man named Rich. The award went unclaimed for nearly two decades. The industry understood what it was doing and did it regardless.

The New Version Has Better Branding

Eighty years later, the language has been updated. The Motion Picture Alliance pledged to protect "American ideals." Today's version talks about "merit," "audience fatigue," and the threat of "DEI propaganda."

The mechanism is functionally the same: create a political threat environment, attach that threat to specific kinds of stories and storytellers, and let executives do the rest.

Under Trump's second term, federal agencies cut funding for diversity-related research, banned the word "diversity" in numerous government contexts, and positioned anti-DEI politics as a mainstream governing philosophy. Right-wing media cycles have reliably targeted films with queer characters, Black-led stories, and anything that could be branded as ideologically suspect. Congressional figures raised the specter of hearings over "ideological indoctrination" in entertainment, consciously echoing HUAC's posture while updating the vocabulary.

Studios have not needed a formal blacklist. They have something more efficient: the reasonable fear of becoming the next culture-war target.

The result is a soft blacklist, operating through greenlights that don't happen, budgets that don't materialize, and diversity departments that get quietly gutted.

Darnell Hunt, co-founder of the Hollywood Diversity Report, put it plainly: "as the country experiences a powerful blowback against diversity, studios have relapsed into colorblind complacency." He warned that more downward trends are almost guaranteed—not just for diversity and representation, but for the industry's financial survival. Only 10.1 percent of top theatrical films in 2025 were directed by women, the lowest share since 2018. BIPOC directors stood at 22 percent, still less than half what proportional representation would require. White men alone directed nearly three-quarters of all top films. White men alone wrote nearly 72 percent of all scripts.

The Receipts

The UCLA report is not a lament. It is documentation. It shows with thirteen years of consistent data that the industry knows exactly what it is doing and keeps doing it anyway.

Diverse casts travel better internationally. They generate stronger opening weekends. They anchor the horror and animation genres that deliver the highest returns on investment. The audience is not white, male, and suburban. BIPOC 18-to-34-year-olds made up the largest audience share for 18 of the top 20 films of 2025.

Hollywood is not confused about who its audience is. It has chosen under political pressure to keep producing for an imagined one.

Studios understood what they were doing and did it regardless.

The original Alliance's members described themselves as defenders of freedom. They were in practice building an infrastructure for ideological surveillance and professional punishment inside the most powerful storytelling industry in the world. What they called "the American scene" was always a selective edit: certain people, certain stories, certain futures were permissible. The rest were threats.

The new version is less dramatic. There are no congressional hearings yet, no contempt charges, no jailed screenwriters. There are just fewer greenlights. Fewer slots at wide release. Fewer names in the credits. Progress that looks like a rocking horse—moving constantly, going nowhere.

Counterpoints

Critics might note that framing this as purely political ignores legitimate creative concerns about audience appetite and franchise storytelling. Some executives genuinely believe certain stories resonate more than others, and pointing to market success for diverse films doesn't automatically invalidate those beliefs. Additionally, the parallel to HUAC is powerful but imperfect—today's pressure operates through cultural conversation rather than formal investigation, making it harder to identify when a project dies from politics versus ordinary business decisions.

Bottom Line

Greene's core argument is strong: the data proves diversity drives profits, and studios are deliberately choosing not to pursue it. The historical parallel to 1944 is provocative but useful—it shows how Hollywood has always used political fear to narrow what stories get told. Greene's biggest vulnerability is that the "soft blacklist" is harder to document than the old one; there's no House Un-American Activities Committee to point at, just absent greenlights and quiet cancellations. The evidence is in what's not made rather than what's made—and that's the hardest kind of case to prove.
