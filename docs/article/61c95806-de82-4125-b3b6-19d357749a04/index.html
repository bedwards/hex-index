How Socrates Beat Addictions

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Jul 24, 2023 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now makes a startling claim that cuts through the noise of modern self-help: addiction isn't a failure of willpower, but a failure of measurement. By reframing the ancient Greek concept of akrasia—acting against one's better judgment—not as a moral weakness but as a cognitive error in weighing immediate pleasure against distant pain, the author offers a radical, non-judgmental path to recovery that feels surprisingly modern despite its ancient roots.

The Chariot and the Single Soul

The piece opens with a vivid metaphor from Plato's Phaedrus, describing the soul as a chariot pulled by two horses: one rational and upward-looking, the other unruly and earth-bound. Then & Now writes, "The soul can lose its wings though and wings have something Divine about them... troubled Souls Socrates says cannot break through into heaven." This imagery effectively sets the stage for the internal conflict we all feel, yet the author quickly pivots to a more nuanced Socratic insight. Rather than accepting the dual-horse model as the final word, the commentary highlights Socrates' alternative view that the soul is actually an "organic whole." As Then & Now puts it, "we're not really driven by two horses there's only one of us and we act for reasons." This is a crucial distinction. It suggests that when we succumb to a bad habit, we aren't being overpowered by a separate, evil part of ourselves; we are making a unified, albeit flawed, calculation.

This reframing is powerful because it removes the shame that often paralyzes those trying to quit. If the problem is not a broken will but a miscalculation, the solution becomes intellectual rather than purely disciplinary. The author notes that Socrates believed the cause of choosing wrong is simply "lack of knowledge." But this isn't just book smarts; it is a deep, experiential understanding of consequences. Then & Now writes, "no one he States who either knows or believes that there is another possible course of action better than the one he is following will ever continue on his present course." The commentary here is spot on: we don't do things we genuinely believe are worse for us in the total sum of our lives. We do them because, in that specific moment, the immediate reward feels larger than the future cost.

The Art of Measurement

The core of the argument rests on the idea that human error stems from the deceptive nature of appearances. We are bad at measuring value over time. Then & Now writes, "do not the same magnitudes appear larger to your sight when near and smaller when at a distance." This observation transforms the struggle with addiction from a battle of character into a problem of perspective. The author argues that the "saving principle of human life" is the "art of measuring," which allows the soul to see the truth and find rest. This is a compelling synthesis of Socratic ethics and behavioral psychology. It suggests that willpower is not about resisting a monster, but about correcting a visual distortion.

The piece effectively bridges this ancient wisdom with the modern experience of smoking, noting how even famous writers who loved cigarettes were often blind to the long-term cost. Then & Now writes, "what we're really doing is balancing what feels pleasurable as good with what we think will be painful as Evil And if what we think will give us pleasure in the moment is greater than what we perceive the consequences to be then it's the right action it's the correct thing to do." This logic is sound, yet it invites a counterpoint: critics might argue that addiction physically alters the brain's reward system, making the "knowledge" of consequences inaccessible regardless of how well one measures. The author acknowledges the "madness of lust" described by Augustine but leans heavily on the rationalist view that knowledge, once truly acquired, is a "noble and commanding thing that cannot be overcome." While this is an optimistic view of human agency, it ignores the biological reality that sometimes the "unruly horse" has hijacked the chariot's controls entirely.

Knowledge is a noble and commanding thing that cannot be overcome and will not allow a person if only he knows the difference between good and evil to do anything which is contrary to knowledge.

The author extends this argument to Baruch Spinoza, noting that an impulse can only be restrained by a stronger, opposite impulse. Then & Now writes, "if we have adequate knowledge of a choice it will always beat the Lesser impulse to do otherwise." This provides a practical framework for change: we must not just know the facts, but feel the weight of the future consequences so deeply that they outweigh the present craving. The author's personal reflection on their own smoking habit illustrates this perfectly, admitting that while they intellectually know smoking is bad, the "inimitably unidentifiably satisfying" nature of the act in the moment creates a temporary imbalance in the scale. The solution, then, is not to fight the urge with brute force, but to acquire a deeper, more visceral knowledge of the alternative.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's strongest contribution is the shift from a moral framework of addiction to an epistemological one, arguing that we act against our interests only because we misjudge the value of the present versus the future. The argument's vulnerability lies in its heavy reliance on rationality, potentially underestimating the physiological grip of chemical dependency that can render even the clearest knowledge powerless. However, for the busy reader seeking a new angle on self-mastery, the call to practice the "art of measurement" offers a practical, dignified tool for navigating the human condition.
