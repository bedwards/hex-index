Khamenei Joins Saddam in Hell, but Iran 2026 Is Not Iraq 2003

Niall Ferguson · Niall Ferguson's Time Machine ·Mar 6, 2026 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Niall Ferguson opens with a jarring cultural reference to a satirical puppet movie to dismantle the immediate, knee-jerk comparisons between the 2026 strikes on Iran and the 2003 invasion of Iraq. His most provocative claim is that the Trump administration has not reverted to the failed "regime change" playbook of the Bush era, but has instead adopted a surgical strategy of "regime alteration" that avoids the manpower and fiscal traps of the past. For a busy reader trying to navigate the fog of breaking news, Ferguson offers a crucial distinction: this is not a war of occupation, but a decapitation strike designed to leave the Iranian people to seize their own freedom.

The Ghost of Team America

Ferguson begins by invoking the 2004 film Team America: World Police, noting its ambivalent satire of both liberal isolationists and neoconservative interventionists. He writes, "The South Park team understood before many commentators that the United States has a track record of coming to save the day and leaving a trail of devastation." This sets the stage for his critique of the immediate reaction to the U.S.-Israeli strikes. He acknowledges that critics like Simon Tisdall of The Guardian are quick to draw parallels, with Tisdall ranting that "a bellicose U.S. president has unleashed overwhelming military firepower to force a sovereign nation to its knees." Ferguson admits this analogy is "too obvious to be resisted" for habitual critics, yet he argues it is fundamentally flawed because the structural conditions of 2003 no longer apply.

The author's argument here is effective because it forces the reader to look past the surface-level similarities of "American aggression" and examine the specific mechanics of the operation. Ferguson reminds us that in 2003, the U.S. suffered from a "manpower deficit, a fiscal deficit, and an attention deficit" that doomed the nation-building project. By contrasting the chaotic aftermath of Iraq with the current strategy, he suggests that the lesson of history has actually been learned, albeit in a cynical way.

"Iran 2026 is not Iraq 2003."

The Doctrine of Regime Alteration

The core of Ferguson's analysis rests on a new term he claims to have coined in conversation with a senior administration official: "regime alteration." He explains that unlike the total overthrow of Saddam Hussein, the goal here is to replace the leadership while leaving the state structure intact, but with a new loyalty to Washington. He notes, "The alteration was that Delcy would now report to Washington, not to Havana or Beijing." This concept is the piece's most distinctive contribution, offering a framework to understand why the U.S. is not sending ground troops.

Ferguson writes, "The strategy rules out the deployment of American ground forces other than special forces. It requires a short time frame for military operations." This distinction is vital because it addresses the primary fear of a "forever war." He argues that Trump has internalized the lesson that "the principal beneficiary of Hussein's downfall was none other than Iran," and thus is avoiding a scenario where the U.S. occupies a vacuum that empowers the very adversary they are fighting.

Critics might note that "regime alteration" is a euphemism that risks underestimating the chaos of a power vacuum. Even without a full occupation, the removal of a supreme leader could still trigger the very civil war Ferguson hopes to avoid. However, Ferguson counters this by pointing to the massive concentration of force preceding the strike, including thirteen U.S. Navy ships and stealth fighters, which he compares to the buildup before the 1991 Gulf War rather than the 2003 invasion.

The Geopolitical Chessboard

Ferguson then pivots to the broader geopolitical context, arguing that the isolation of the Iranian regime makes this operation more viable than previous interventions. He observes that unlike in 1973, "Russia has seen its influence in the Middle East significantly degraded" due to the war in Ukraine, and China is focused on the Indo-Pacific. He writes, "The other members of the Axis of Authoritarians can do little to assist the regime in Tehran." This assessment suggests that the "grand coalition" feared by Zbigniew Brzezinski has failed to materialize in a way that could save the Islamic Republic.

The author also highlights the diplomatic maneuvering that preceded the strikes, describing it as maskirovka—a Russian term for military deception. He notes that while talks were ongoing in Oman, the U.S. was explicit about its red lines, with Trump telling Iran it had "10 to 15 days to avert potential military action." Ferguson argues that the Iranian refusal to yield on uranium enrichment and missile programs left them with no choice but the "unfortunate option" of war.

"It is to decapitate the Islamic Republic's political structure and leave the Iranians to take their freedom from the mullahs and their murderous henchmen."

The Uncertain Successors

The most speculative part of Ferguson's commentary concerns the aftermath: who will replace Khamenei? He dismisses the idea of a clear successor like the "Delcy" of Venezuela, suggesting instead that power may lie with Khamenei's sons or the Office of the Supreme Leader. He asks, "Is this the cast of characters for a full-blown Iranian civil war, as opposed to regime alteration?" Ferguson admits that no one knows the answer, but he warns of the risk that Iran could implode and create a vacuum filled by an ascendant Turkey.

This uncertainty is the piece's greatest vulnerability. While Ferguson's analysis of the intent is sharp, the outcome relies heavily on the assumption that the Iranian military and security forces will simply lay down their weapons, as Trump promised they could with immunity. A counterargument worth considering is that the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) has a deep ideological commitment that might not dissolve simply because their leader is gone, potentially leading to a prolonged insurgency even without U.S. boots on the ground.

Bottom Line

Ferguson's strongest argument is his reframing of the conflict as a surgical decapitation rather than a nation-building occupation, a distinction that fundamentally changes the risk profile of the intervention. His biggest vulnerability lies in assuming that the removal of the supreme leader will automatically lead to a stable "regime alteration" rather than a chaotic power struggle that could destabilize the entire region. Readers should watch closely to see if the promised immunity for Iranian security forces holds, as that will be the true test of whether this is a new strategy or just a variation of an old failure.
