Is San Francisco's New Transit Center a Waste?

Dave Amos · City Beautiful ·Sep 19, 2024 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Amos confronts a painful irony at the heart of San Francisco's urban renewal: a $2.3 billion architectural marvel that currently functions as the world's most expensive bus station. While the Salesforce Transit Center was marketed as the "Grand Central Station of the West," Amos reveals a facility that feels eerily hollow, designed for a future of high-speed rail that may not arrive for another decade. For busy readers tracking the fate of American infrastructure, this piece offers a rare, ground-level audit of a project that risks becoming a monument to timing rather than transit.

The Architecture of Anticipation

Amos begins his tour by acknowledging the sheer scale of the building, noting that "this is San Francisco's $2.3 billion Grand Central Station of the West." Yet, the visual reality contradicts the grandeur. He describes the structure as "440 M long but only 50 m wide," creating a space that feels "inserted into the city separate from the normal building grain." This architectural isolation mirrors its functional isolation. The author's observation that the building "sort of looks like it's inserted into the city" is a sharp critique of how modern mega-projects often ignore the existing urban fabric in favor of self-contained grandiosity.

Inside, the main hall is bright but lacks the kinetic energy of true train hubs. Amos points out a massive screen that serves as a "modern take on the departure Boards of old," but notes with biting irony that "most of the board is used up by ads and announcements as there aren't many departures shown on the bottom half." The emptiness is not just a lack of passengers; it is a lack of purpose. The author captures this dissonance perfectly: "it pretty much feels like a train station without any trains because that's literally what it is."

This is a beautiful new bus terminal that is Miles better than the competition but it just needs a few more buses now.

The comparison to New York's Port Authority Bus Terminal highlights the paradox of the Transit Center. While Port Authority is "cramped crowded a little smelly," the Salesforce center is pristine precisely because it is underutilized. Amos argues that the station is "Miles better than the competition" in design, yet this very quality makes its current state feel like a waste. Critics might argue that high-quality infrastructure must be built before demand materializes, but the gap between completion and utility here is so vast it risks losing public support entirely.

A Park for the Few, A Station for the Future

The tour ascends to the rooftop, a verdant space Amos calls "Salesforce Park" despite its public funding. He describes it as giving off "Highline Vibes" with an "episodic effect" as one walks through its length. The park is undeniably a success for the few who work nearby, offering a "verdant and peaceful atmosphere" with an amphitheater and bamboo garden. However, Amos notes that these schemes are "tailor made for downtown office workers," raising the question of who the station serves when the office workers are no longer downtown.

The real story, however, lies beneath the surface. Amos details the "train box," a subterranean structure already built and waiting for platforms that won't exist for years. He explains that the station is meant for long-distance rail, with high-speed rail infrastructure currently being built in the Central Valley. The timeline is staggering: a $6.7 billion extension to bring Caltrain and high-speed rail to the center is not expected to be complete until 2032. "I remain a high-speed rail Optimist," Amos writes, suggesting that the terminal could eventually "Elevate the station and make it feel almost like a bustling airport."

Yet, the path to that future is fraught with political and technical hurdles. Amos discusses "Link 21," a proposal for a second Trans Bay tube to connect San Francisco and Oakland. He notes a critical flaw: the current plans for the new tube do not connect to the Transit Center. "The obvious answer is to build two tunnels," he argues, one for BART and one for commuter rail, but the cost is prohibitive. The disconnect between the station's design and the actual rail network is a major vulnerability. As Amos puts it, "the transit center exists very close to a whole lot of train service but no direct entrances and exits."

The Naming Rights Dilemma

Amos also touches on the corporate branding that defines the facility. He expresses a personal aversion to the naming rights deal, where the Joint Powers Authority sold the name to Salesforce for $110 million over 25 years. "I have a bit of a problem of selling naming rights to places like train stations," he admits, imagining the absurdity of "Grand Central Station" bearing a corporate sponsor. This corporate overlay adds another layer of tension to a building that already struggles to justify its public cost.

The station is meant for long distance and commuter rail and that could be coming in the future with High-Speed Rail infrastructure being built right now in the Central Valley.

The author's optimism is tempered by the reality of the post-pandemic city. He questions whether the building was "designed before downtown San Francisco emptied out after covid." If the workforce does not return to the density of 2018, the "crown jewel of Bay Area Transportation" may remain a quiet, expensive shell. The argument is compelling because it does not dismiss the architecture but rather highlights the misalignment between the building's potential and the city's current trajectory.

Bottom Line

Dave Amos delivers a nuanced verdict: the Salesforce Transit Center is an architectural triumph trapped in a logistical limbo. Its strongest asset is its future potential as a high-speed rail hub, but its greatest vulnerability is the decade-long wait to realize that vision in a city that has already shifted. The piece succeeds by refusing to simply dismiss the building as a white elephant, instead asking whether the city can wait long enough for the trains to arrive.

It will be hard to know the impact highspeed rail will have on the station but I remain a high-speed rail Optimist.

The ultimate test for this project will not be its design or its rooftop park, but whether the political will exists to fund the connecting tunnels and rail lines by 2032. Until then, it remains a beautiful, empty promise.
