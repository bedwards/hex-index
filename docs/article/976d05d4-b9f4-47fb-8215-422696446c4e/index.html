Real Ghost of Tsushima - Mongol Invasion of Japan Documentary

Kings and Generals · Kings and Generals ·Jul 17, 2020 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Kings and Generals strips away the myth of the 'kamikaze' wind to reveal the brutal, tactical reality of the first Mongol invasion of Japan, focusing on the overlooked slaughter of the Tsushima and Iki islands. While popular memory fixates on the storm that saved Hakata Bay, this account argues that the true story lies in the desperate, doomed resistance of local samurai against a superpower that had already conquered half the world.

The Diplomatic Failure and the Gathering Storm

The narrative begins not with swords, but with a catastrophic breakdown in diplomacy. Kings and Generals writes, "The continuous diplomatic offenses were not forgotten and Kublai's pretensions only increased with the declaration of the Yuan dynasty in 1271." This framing is crucial; it establishes the invasion not as a random act of aggression, but as a calculated response to perceived slights by the Kamakura shogunate. The author details how Kublai Khan, frustrated by the refusal of Japanese envoys to show proper respect, escalated from envoys to a massive armada.

The scale of the threat is emphasized through the sheer numbers involved. As Kings and Generals puts it, "On the 2nd of November 1274 an armada of perhaps 20,000 Mongols, Northern Chinese, Jurchen, Kitens and Korean soldiers and sailors aboard nearly 1,000 ships set out from Hapo on Korea's southern coast." This detail underscores the impossibility of the task facing the Japanese defenders. The author effectively contrasts the grandeur of the Mongol empire with the isolation of Tsushima, a mountainous island with little arable land, populated by a clan of samurai who were more accustomed to raiding Korea than defending against a continental superpower.

The Clash at Komoda Beach

The heart of the piece is the tactical breakdown of the first engagement. Kings and Generals notes that the samurai, despite their legendary status, were ill-equipped for this specific type of warfare. "The primary weapon of the samurai was the bow... though their maneuvers on horseback could not compare to step nomads born into the saddle." This admission is vital; it humanizes the samurai and dismantles the myth of their invincibility. The author describes how the Mongols used a combined-arms approach, sending expendable Chinese and Korean levies in the first waves to soak up arrows before the elite Mongol and Turkic horse archers even disembarked.

The narrative captures the tragedy of the samurai code in the face of total war. "One of Sukikuni's closest comrades, Saito Sukisada, brought down several of the Yuan soldiers... the Mongols responded with arrows, three of which pierced the armor of Sukisada's chest and ended him." Kings and Generals highlights the futility of the samurai's traditional challenge to single combat, which went unanswered by an enemy that viewed battle as a collective, industrial effort. The author's description of the aftermath is particularly harrowing: "A worse fate awaited the prisoners... wire was threaded through the palms of the prisoners, mainly women, who were strung along the prows of the Yuan ships as a gruesome necklace." This graphic detail serves as a stark reminder of the brutality that awaited the Japanese, far removed from the romanticized duels of later folklore.

The Fall of Iki and the Regent's Resolve

The coverage shifts to the island of Iki, where the defense was slightly more organized but ultimately just as futile. Kings and Generals writes, "Kagitaka watched his family commit suicide before he too joined them in his final act of defiance." This moment encapsulates the cultural stakes of the invasion. The author notes that even the attempt to send a messenger, Kagitaka's daughter Katsurime, ended in failure, with her boat falling victim to Mongol arrows. The narrative effectively conveys the isolation of these defenders, cut off from the mainland and facing an enemy that used their own captured countrymen as human shields.

The piece concludes with the legendary reaction of the regent, Hojo Tokimune. "When asked by a nearby priest how he intended to reply to the Mongol invasion, Tokimune shrieked 'Katsu'—victory." Kings and Generals uses this moment to pivot from the tactical defeat of the islands to the strategic resilience of the Japanese nation. While the author acknowledges that the local defenses on Tsushima and Iki were quickly overrun, the focus shifts to the psychological impact of these losses on the mainland. Critics might note that the account glosses over the internal political fractures within the Kamakura shogunate that nearly led to a different outcome, but the focus remains firmly on the external threat.

The local defenses on Tsushima and the Japanese islands continued to be maintained during the following decades for the possibility of a Mongol return which was threatened but never materialized.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals succeeds in reframing the Mongol invasion of Japan by shifting the spotlight from the divine wind to the human cost of the initial landings. The strongest part of this argument is the detailed tactical analysis that reveals the samurai's vulnerability against Mongol combined-arms tactics. Its biggest vulnerability is the reliance on a single Japanese source for the emotional beats of the battle, which may romanticize the samurai's final stand. Readers should watch for how this narrative of resilience sets the stage for the second, even larger invasion attempt in 1281, where the stakes were raised to a national level.
