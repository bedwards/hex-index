Top Africa stories of the year 2025

Jeffrey Paller · This Week in Africa ·Dec 31, 2025 ·15 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Jeffrey Paller's year-in-review for This Week in Africa does something rare: it treats 2025 not as a collection of isolated crises, but as a single, accelerating fracture in the global order where African agency is both the primary victim and the emerging architect of a new reality. While other outlets might focus on the noise of Washington or the latest headline from a capital city, Paller directs the lens toward the human cost of policy shifts and the quiet, desperate resilience of civilians who are no longer waiting for saviors. This is a necessary listen for anyone who wants to understand how the world is actually changing, rather than how it is being reported.

The Human Cost of Geopolitics

The piece opens with a grim accounting of Sudan, where the author refuses to let the conflict fade into background noise. Paller writes, "Two years on, Sudan's civil war has created the largest displacement crisis in the world. The country faces erasure, again." This framing is crucial; it moves beyond the standard "civil war" label to suggest an existential threat to a nation's very history. The coverage details how foreign powers, particularly Gulf monarchies, have turned the conflict into a proxy war, fueling a "new regional conflict economy." The human toll is starkly illustrated by the fall of El Fasher, where the Rapid Support Forces were accused of killing hundreds in a hospital. Paller notes that "the world has failed," quoting Mo Ibrahim's assertion that this failure is rooted in the fact that "the world ignores the conflict because of the skin colour of its people."

This is a devastating indictment of international apathy. The evidence presented—satellite imagery of mass burials, reports of brazen attacks on medical facilities—leaves no room for the diplomatic obfuscation that often plagues coverage of African conflicts. Critics might argue that focusing solely on external interference overlooks internal power dynamics, but Paller balances this by highlighting the "united civilian front" and groups like Somoud who are fighting to end the war. The argument holds up because it centers the agency of those on the ground rather than just the machinations of distant capitals.

The End of the Aid Era and the Rise of Transactionalism

Perhaps the most consequential section of the review addresses the seismic shift in US foreign policy. Paller describes a "Trump 2.0" approach that is "very transactional, a 'carrot and stick' approach to the continent." The administration's decision to effectively shut down USAID is framed not just as a budget cut, but as a catalyst for a "man-made hunger crisis." Paller writes, "The cost of the executive order measured in lives." This is a bold claim, but the piece supports it by linking the aid freeze directly to the opening of a vacuum that China is eager to fill.

The commentary suggests that this isn't merely a policy change but a fundamental restructuring of the relationship between the West and Africa. As Paller puts it, "Africa seeks a post-West plan." The review highlights how the US has pivoted to chasing minerals deals and implementing tariffs, signaling an end to development-focused trade. While the administration frames this as a move toward mutual benefit, the text argues it is actually a retreat from responsibility. A counterargument worth considering is that African nations have long sought to reduce aid dependency, as noted by Ken Opalo's work on ending aid reliance. However, the sudden, unilateral nature of these cuts, which imperil millions, suggests a lack of strategic planning rather than a genuine partnership.

Resistance, Repression, and the Youth Wave

The review then pivots to the internal political landscapes of Tanzania, Kenya, and Togo, where the theme of resistance is paramount. In Tanzania, despite the ruling party's efforts to "deepen their control," Paller notes that "the public is getting fed up with the government's increasing opposition crackdowns." The coverage of the "sham elections" and the subsequent youth protests paints a picture of a continent where the old guard is losing its grip. The author writes, "The protests illustrated years of bottled-up anger finally being let out."

This is not just about elections; it is about a generational rebellion. From the streets of Lomé to the protests in Nairobi, the text identifies a "wave of demonstrations" driven by a generation that is "no not content sitting on the sidelines." The narrative connects these disparate events to a broader disillusionment with democracy that has failed to deliver jobs or security. Paller argues that "Africans are growing disillusioned with democracy," a sentiment that is echoed in the rise of military leaders like Ibrahim Traoré in Burkina Faso, who is celebrated for his attempts to "securitize and development spaces beyond the capital."

Critics might note that celebrating a coup leader, even one with popular support, risks normalizing authoritarianism. Yet, the piece contextualizes this popularity as a reaction to the failure of democratic institutions to provide basic services. The tension here is palpable: the desire for stability versus the cost of freedom. The coverage wisely avoids simplistic judgments, instead presenting the complex reality of a continent in flux.

A New World Order

Finally, the review looks outward to the shifting global balance. Paller argues that "the changing world order offers new opportunities for African nations," moving away from Western centrism toward a multipolar reality. The text highlights how African countries are leveraging their position in BRICS and engaging with "middle powers" to reshape global power relations. As the author notes, "Africa is no longer looking from the sidelines as part of BRICS." This section reframes the narrative from one of victimhood to one of strategic opportunity, suggesting that the chaos of the current era is also a chance for African nations to redefine their place in the world.

The piece concludes by touching on the critical minerals race, warning that it is "recreating colonial models." This serves as a sobering reminder that while the players may be changing, the extractive logic of the past remains potent. The review ends on a note of cautious optimism, suggesting that the future of Africa will be written by its own people, not by foreign powers.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Paller's review is a masterclass in connecting the dots between local suffering and global strategy, offering a clear-eyed view of a continent that is neither helpless nor passive. Its greatest strength lies in its refusal to let the human cost of geopolitical games be ignored, while its biggest vulnerability is the sheer scale of the crises it documents, which can feel overwhelming. The reader should watch for how African nations navigate the vacuum left by the US retreat, as this will define the next decade of global politics.
