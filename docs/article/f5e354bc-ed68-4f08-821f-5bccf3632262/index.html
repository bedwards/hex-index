Live IN Hong Kong 10: Un-Independence Day

Chris Chappell · China Uncensored ·Jul 1, 2019 · 25 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Chris Chappell does not merely report on the July 1st anniversary in Hong Kong; he captures the suffocating tension of a city where the government's attempt to suppress dissent is visibly failing against the sheer weight of public will. While official narratives suggest the crisis is fading, Chappell's on-the-ground account reveals a movement that has evolved from demanding specific policy reversals to questioning the very legitimacy of the "One Country, Two Systems" framework. For the busy professional tracking the geopolitical fault lines of Asia, this is not just a protest update—it is a real-time case study in how a regime's "ham-fisted tactics" can inadvertently radicalize a moderate populace.

The Theater of Control

Chappell's coverage begins by dismantling the official story that the protests are waning. He notes that while the crowd is smaller than the historic two million seen weeks prior, the atmosphere remains electric and the stakes higher. "This isn't as big as last Sunday when there were two million people but it's definitely pretty big," Chappell observes, immediately contextualizing the scale. The core of his argument here is that the government's strategy of containment is backfiring. He describes how police attempted to funnel thousands of citizens into a confined park, creating dangerous conditions where people were "fainting having heatstroke" and required wheelchairs and stretchers.

The author highlights the absurdity of the police response, noting they were "trying to limit the size of the protests as much as possible make it kind of aggravating for people to march." This framing is crucial because it shifts the blame for the unrest from the protesters to the authorities' inability to manage a peaceful assembly. Chappell points out that the police were "trying to get them back into the main road" even as the crowd naturally spilled over, a tactical error that only prolonged the demonstration and increased public frustration. Critics might argue that the police were simply maintaining order in a volatile situation, but Chappell's evidence of heatstroke and the forced confinement suggests a deliberate strategy of attrition rather than safety.

"The police and the government... are using ham-fisted tactics to try and limit and suppress everything."

The Collapse of the Political Middle

Perhaps the most significant shift Chappell documents is the evolution of the protesters' demands. Initially focused on the extradition bill, the movement has broadened its scope to include systemic political reform. Chappell reports that organizers began speaking in English, signaling a shift toward international attention, and the crowd began chanting for "free Hong Kong democracy now." He notes that while traditional demands like the resignation of Chief Executive Carrie Lam persist, the rhetoric has sharpened: "The only way to fix the system is to have greater democracy."

Chappell contrasts this grassroots energy with the hollow performance of the government. He describes Carrie Lam's flag-raising ceremony, which was moved indoors due to weather, where she promised to "listen to the youth of the city" before leaving immediately. Chappell cuts through the diplomatic language, pointing out the irony that "the youth of the city is right outside actually not right outside because they put so many police barricades... preventing the youth from actually getting anywhere near the flag-raising ceremony." This juxtaposition effectively exposes the disconnect between the administration's rhetoric and the reality on the streets. The argument lands because it relies on the visual and logistical impossibility of the government's claim to be listening while physically blocking the very people they claim to hear.

The Taiwan Connection and the Global Stakes

Chappell also weaves in the broader regional implications, specifically the solidarity between Hong Kong and Taiwan. He addresses the question of why celebrity Jackie Chan is absent, noting that he has become "very Pro CCP" and is viewed by protesters as a "sell out." In contrast, the movement looks to Bruce Lee as a model. This cultural nuance is vital for understanding the local sentiment. More importantly, Chappell connects the dots to Taiwan, suggesting that the Hong Kong protests are a warning signal for the "One Country, Two Systems" policy. "Taiwan seeing what happens with one country two systems and then being like oh oh no you know no no any hope that Beijing could convince Taiwan to why with China under one country two systems policy like it's clear that's not going to work," he explains.

This section elevates the piece from local news to international strategy. Chappell argues that the protests are demonstrating that the promise of autonomy is a broken contract. He mentions the collaboration between Hong Kong and Taiwanese pop stars releasing a joint song of support, describing it as their version of "We Are the World." This cultural solidarity reinforces the political argument that the two regions are increasingly aligned against Beijing's encroachment. A counterargument worth considering is that Beijing may view these cultural exchanges as manageable noise rather than a strategic threat, but Chappell's reporting suggests the psychological impact on the Taiwanese public is profound.

The Verdict on Non-Violence

Finally, Chappell addresses the question of tactics, specifically the efficacy of non-violent resistance. He cites research indicating that "nonviolent resistance movements are far more successful at regime change and establishing democracies than violent revolution," with violent revolutions succeeding only about 4% of the time. This data-driven approach grounds the emotional energy of the protests in strategic reality. He observes that despite the chaos, the protests remain largely peaceful, with families and children even picking up litter after the march. "Protesters are picking up the trash... that's the thing," Chappell notes, highlighting the discipline of the movement.

However, he acknowledges the limits of this approach, quoting a viewer who noted that "peaceful protests only do so much." Chappell counters this by arguing that the sheer volume of participants—potentially 500,000 people—is the primary weapon. The goal is not to topple the government through force but to demonstrate that the government has lost its mandate. "If enough people show up... they'll show that... the whole is still not satisfied with what the government is doing," he argues. This reframes the protest not as a failure to achieve immediate legislative change, but as a successful assertion of public will that the government cannot ignore.

"If you don't come out today you can't come out tomorrow."

Bottom Line

Chris Chappell's coverage succeeds by stripping away the diplomatic fog to reveal a city in the throes of a fundamental identity crisis. His strongest argument is that the government's attempt to contain the protests through physical barriers and bureaucratic delays has only accelerated the radicalization of the movement and solidified its cross-generational support. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that non-violent mass mobilization will inevitably lead to political concessions, a strategy that has historically met with mixed results against authoritarian regimes. Readers should watch whether the government's refusal to withdraw the extradition bill forces the movement to abandon its non-violent stance, a shift that would fundamentally alter the trajectory of the crisis.
