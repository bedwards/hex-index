Passivhaus . A low carbon future for our homes?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Dec 27, 2020 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through the noise of the climate crisis by targeting the most stubborn culprit: our own homes. While most discussions focus on electric vehicles or renewable grids, he reveals a startling reality where the building sector alone generates nearly 40% of global greenhouse gas emissions. This isn't just about saving money on utility bills; it is a fundamental redesign of how we inhabit the planet, proving that the most effective carbon reduction tool might already exist in a 1991 German prototype.

The Architecture of Waste

Borlace begins by dismantling the logic of modern construction, describing it as a cycle of "appalling inefficiencies." He paints a vivid picture of a system where we "spend oodles of cash putting heat back into our living spaces just so it can leak straight back outside again." This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from the homeowner's habits to the structural design itself. The author argues that current building codes are merely a baseline for mediocrity, allowing heat to escape through "badly sealed windows and poorly insulated walls."

The solution, he posits, lies in the work of Dr. Wolfgang Feist, whose 1991 home sparked a movement that uses "about 90 less energy than a traditional house." This statistic is the piece's anchor, transforming Passive House from a niche eco-obsession into a practical necessity. Borlace notes that the consequence of such efficiency is that "there's virtually no requirement for expensive electrical heating and cooling systems." This is a powerful economic argument wrapped in an environmental one: by building better, we stop paying for the energy we don't need.

"The more time and care they give to the design details before starting the build the less time and money they'll need to spend later on expensive remedials and retrofits."

Critics might argue that the upfront costs and specialized labor required for such precision remain a barrier for average homeowners, despite Borlace's claim that the market has matured. However, the author's emphasis on the "six Ps" mantra—replacing "Proper Planning" with "Passivhaus"—successfully reframes the conversation from cost to long-term value.

The Five Pillars of Efficiency

The commentary then dissects the technical principles that make this possible, starting with the "super insulated envelope." Borlace explains that this involves doubling or tripling the thermal insulation found in standard regulations, using materials that "don't rot won't compact don't get attacked by insects and are completely fireproof." This detail is crucial; it addresses durability concerns that often plague green building initiatives.

The second principle, the minimization of thermal bridging, is where the engineering gets clever. Borlace identifies steel joists as "the worst offenders" due to their conductivity, forcing builders to "over insulating connections and protrusions." This level of detail highlights that Passive House is not just about adding more insulation, but about strategic interruption of heat flow.

"Ideally you want a completely continuous unbroken wrap of materials around the structure."

The third pillar, airtight construction, introduces a strict metric: fewer than 0.6 air changes per hour. Borlace acknowledges the obvious counterpoint—that sealing a building hermetically could be dangerous—by pivoting immediately to the fourth principle: heat recovery ventilation. He describes systems that "extract heat from the exhaust air and puts it into the incoming air without directly mixing the air streams together." This mechanism ensures that fresh air doesn't come at the cost of thermal comfort, a common fear among skeptics of airtight homes.

The final principle involves high-performance glazing, which Borlace admits is the "weakest link in the barrier continuity chain." He details the use of triple-glazed units filled with inert gases like argon and coated with low-emissivity layers. The strategic orientation of these windows allows for "free passive heating from the sun," turning the building's skin into an active energy generator rather than a passive barrier.

"Smart wall systems and ultra slim insulation panels... give the same insulation value as traditional products but with only a tenth of the thickness."

Borlace also touches on the integration of smart technology, such as DC solar-powered lighting systems that eliminate the need for battery replacements. This suggests that the Passive House standard is evolving into a holistic ecosystem of energy management, not just a static building method.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest asset is his ability to translate complex engineering concepts into a compelling narrative of waste reduction and economic sense. His argument holds up because it relies on thirty years of empirical data rather than theoretical promises. The biggest vulnerability remains the perception of cost and complexity, which, while diminishing, still requires a significant leap of faith from traditional builders and homeowners. The reader should watch for how rapidly the supply chain adapts to these standards, as the technology is ready, but the industry's inertia is the final hurdle.
