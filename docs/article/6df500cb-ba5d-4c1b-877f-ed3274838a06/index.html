How America and China are fighting for African minerals

Shirvan Neftchi · CaspianReport ·Apr 25, 2024 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Shirvan Neftchi delivers a stark warning: the race for African minerals isn't just about economics; it is a geopolitical chess match where the next move could trigger a train wreck. While much of the discourse focuses on trade balances, Neftchi zeroes in on the physical reality of the ground beneath the tracks, arguing that "neither the Americans nor the Europeans are leaders in EV technology" and that the West is waking up to a decade-long Chinese head start. This piece matters now because the infrastructure being built today will lock in supply chains for the next forty years, determining who powers the green transition and who gets left in the dark.

The Geography of Desperation

Neftchi begins by dismantling the assumption that resources alone guarantee power. He notes that while Africa holds "30% of the world's known critical minerals," the real bottleneck is not the dirt, but the lack of roads and rails to move it. The author paints a grim picture of the Democratic Republic of Congo, where "between the DRC Capital Kinshasa and the resourcer katanga region sits nearly 1,600 km of rainforest this terrain is largely impassible." This geographical isolation has allowed local power brokers to operate independently of the federal government, creating a vacuum that external powers are desperate to fill.

The commentary here is sharp because it moves beyond abstract trade wars to the gritty reality of logistics. Neftchi writes, "if treated recklessly either one of the corridors could be the last stop before a train wreck." This metaphor effectively captures the fragility of the situation; a failed railway isn't just a financial loss, it's a catalyst for instability. Critics might note that the author slightly underplays the agency of African leaders, who are often adept at playing rivals against one another rather than being passive victims of a "scramble."

The scramble for mines and resources could just as easily turn into a scramble for territory and eventually a scramble for Nations.

The American Gamble: The Lobito Corridor

In response to Chinese dominance, the United States has pivoted to a massive infrastructure play: the revival of the Lobito Corridor. Neftchi details how President Biden has pledged "$360 million toward Reviving the libito corridor," aiming to connect the mineral-rich Katanga region to the Atlantic port of Lobito in Angola. The strategy is ambitious, seeking to transform a simple railway into a full "value chain that will not only export critical minerals but also Foster Business Development and Commercial activities."

The author argues that this is more than a transport project; it is an attempt to create new urban centers and economic ecosystems. "It could take decades to develop but that's how urban life evolves by turning the libito corridor into the libito value chain." This long-term vision contrasts sharply with the immediate extraction models often criticized in the past. However, the sheer scale of the challenge cannot be overstated. The region is plagued by conflict, disease, and a history of civil war that destroyed the original line built in 1905. Neftchi acknowledges this, noting the area is "rich in Copper gold and Cobalt but also conflict disease and turmoil not exactly a stable environment to set up shop."

The Chinese Counter-Move: TAZARA

While the West focuses on the Atlantic, China is securing the Indian Ocean route. Neftchi highlights Beijing's negotiations to take over the "Taza Railway which runs in the opposite direction from Africa to Asia Pacific." This line, originally built by China in 1975, connects Zambia to the port of Dar es Salaam. Despite China's recent economic slowdown and a shift away from massive new projects, they have offered "$1 billion to refurbish the infrastructure and take over its operations."

The author's analysis of this move is particularly insightful. He suggests that even a reduced investment signals a "long-term geoeconomic blueprint looking ahead to the next decade." By linking the line to "Lubumbashi the capital of cobalt Rich katanga," China aims to secure its own supply of critical materials for its dominant electric vehicle industry. Neftchi points out that "almost 90% of cell component manufacturing takes place in Asia with China having impressive EV capabilities." This creates a self-reinforcing cycle where Chinese infrastructure feeds Chinese manufacturing, which in turn demands more African resources. A counterargument worth considering is whether African nations will accept this dependency, or if they will leverage the American offer to extract better terms from Beijing.

The African Balancing Act

The most compelling part of Neftchi's coverage is his assessment of how African nations are navigating this rivalry. He argues that countries like Angola and Zambia are not choosing sides but are "looking to leverage American interests against those of the Chinese and vice versa." Angola, with its navigable river and hydrocarbon wealth, is uniquely positioned to keep its options open. "Angola is not looking to Wed itself to China it may prefer to have its options open."

However, the Democratic Republic of Congo presents a different reality. Neftchi describes it as a "failed State since 2006 with no fix in sight," where the US focuses on humanitarian aid while China holds the leverage of deep business ties. "China has an edge in Risk tolerance it has deep running business ties in the DRC and holds more sway over the country." This distinction is crucial; it suggests that while some nations can play the field, others are locked into Chinese influence due to a lack of alternatives and the sheer scale of Chinese investment, which Neftchi estimates at "roughly 170 billion dollar in massive infrastructure projects."

Bottom Line

Neftchi's strongest argument is that the infrastructure being built today will dictate the geopolitical landscape of the 21st century, turning the mineral belt into a potential flashpoint for great power conflict. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that African nations will successfully balance these powers; in a state like the DRC, the lack of central authority may render such balancing acts impossible. Readers should watch whether the American-led Lobito Corridor can overcome the security and logistical hurdles that have plagued it for decades, or if it remains a symbolic gesture against a more entrenched Chinese reality.
