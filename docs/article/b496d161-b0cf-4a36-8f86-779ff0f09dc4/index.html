Real Lawyer Reacts to She-Hulk

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Oct 20, 2022 · 24 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just review She-Hulk; he dissects the show's legal logic with the precision of a closing argument that never happened. While most pop-culture critics focus on special effects or character arcs, Stone identifies a fundamental flaw in the series' premise: the show treats the legal system as a flexible narrative device rather than a rigid procedural framework. For busy professionals who know that one missed deadline can sink a case, Stone's observation that the show gets the basics of trial procedure wrong is the hook that demands attention.

The Procedural Breakdown

Stone immediately flags the show's opening courtroom scene as a disaster for legal realism. He notes that the defense delivers its closing argument before the prosecution, a sequence that violates the most basic rules of criminal procedure. "This is a terrible closing argument," Stone writes, "it's basically just a word solid of legal Concepts thrown together in a way that is not persuasive at all." He points out that in the real world, the prosecution bears the burden of proof and always speaks last to rebut the defense. This isn't just a nitpick; it signals to Stone that the writers prioritized dramatic tension over logical consistency.

The commentary deepens when Stone addresses the show's portrayal of mistrials. The series suggests that a mistrial means the defendant walks free, but Stone clarifies that a mistrial simply means the current jury is dismissed and the trial starts over. "The idea that her district attorney boss is upset is preposterous," he argues, noting that saving the jury from a falling desk would actually bolster the lawyer's reputation, not ruin her career. This highlights a significant disconnect between the show's dramatic needs and the actual incentives within the legal profession.

This is an employment lawyer's worst nightmare.

Stone's critique of the firing scene is particularly sharp. The show posits that Jen Walters is fired for transforming into She-Hulk and saving the jury. Stone dismantles this by invoking the Americans with Disabilities Act and the Civil Rights Act of 1964. He argues that firing someone for a genetic abnormality that makes them a superhero would be illegal discrimination. "The manner in which she was fired impossible absolutely possible ridiculous," he states, emphasizing that government employees have due process protections and union grievance procedures that the show conveniently ignores. Critics might note that television often bends reality for plot progression, but Stone's point stands: the show fails to even acknowledge the legal hurdles it creates for itself.

The Business of Superheroes

Moving to the corporate side of the story, Stone analyzes the law firm's attempt to capitalize on Jen's new identity. The firm offers her a job as the face of a new "superhuman law division," but Stone questions the business acumen behind the deal. He notes that while a firm might want her notoriety, they would demand more than just a title. "I would want to know if I was a capital or Equity partner in the law firm," Stone paraphrases, suggesting that a savvy lawyer would negotiate for a percentage of the profits, not just a job description. He finds it unrealistic that a senior partner would accept her condition to hire her own paralegal without questioning the financial implications.

The commentary takes a darker turn when Stone examines the firm's demand that Jen must appear in court as She-Hulk. He identifies this as a potential violation of employment law, comparing it to the sexualized uniforms required at places like Hooters. "Some old white guy is demanding that Jen Walters turn into She-Hulk as a condition of her employment absolutely not," Stone asserts. He argues that a law firm has no legitimate business interest in requiring an employee to transform, especially given the potential for harassment and discrimination claims. This section effectively bridges the gap between superhero fantasy and real-world workplace dynamics.

Parole, Magic, and Standing

Stone also tackles the subplot involving the Abomination's parole hearing. He praises the show for exploring the concept of reintegration for former super-villains, noting that prisons shouldn't confine people indefinitely unless the crime warrants it. However, he tears apart the legal strategy used by the character Wong. The show suggests that Wong's mere presence as a witness is the key to the case, but Stone explains that a written declaration under penalty of perjury would suffice. "The whole thing about Wong well they show up or not is totally contrived," he says, pointing out that the stakes are artificially inflated for dramatic effect.

Finally, Stone addresses the cease-and-desist letter sent to a magician using magic. He explains that the letter lacks legal standing because the plaintiff, Wong, is a third party who hasn't suffered a direct injury. "Negligence is a cause of action that requires you to be injured by the act of negligence," Stone clarifies. He concludes that the case should be dismissed on a motion to dismiss, as there is no valid cause of action. This final point underscores his broader argument: the show treats legal concepts as plot devices rather than functional systems.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's commentary is a masterclass in applying real-world expertise to fictional narratives, revealing how She-Hulk sacrifices legal integrity for plot convenience. His strongest argument is that the show's procedural errors aren't just minor mistakes but fundamental misunderstandings of how the legal system actually works. The biggest vulnerability in the show's writing, as Stone highlights, is its failure to grapple with the real-world consequences of its own premise, leaving viewers with a fantasy that collapses under the weight of even basic legal scrutiny.
