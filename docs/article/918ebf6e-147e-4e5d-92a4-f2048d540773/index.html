On the Ground in China: Sentiment is Improving

Kaiser Kuo · Sinica ·Apr 1, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Kaiser Kuo cuts through the prevailing gloom with a counter-intuitive thesis: the Chinese economy isn't just stabilizing, it is hitting an inflection point where consumer confidence is finally returning. Drawing on six recent trips and conversations across ten cities, he offers a ground-level reality check that contradicts the "uninvestable" narrative dominating Western headlines. For busy investors and strategists, the value here isn't just the optimism, but the specific mechanism Kuo identifies—a massive, pent-up reservoir of household savings waiting to be unlocked by a policy pivot.

The Policy Pendulum Swings Back

Kuo anchors his argument in a specific turning point: the Politburo meeting of September 26, 2024. He argues that this was the moment leadership admitted that four years of regulatory crackdowns had gone too far. "Since that date, the most important new policy was what hasn't happened: no new regulatory storms," Kuo writes. This observation is crucial because it shifts the focus from what the government is doing to what it is not doing. By simply stopping the bleeding of confidence, the administration has allowed sentiment to recover organically.

The author supports this with hard data on the stock market, noting that the CSI 300 index is up 27% since that meeting, outperforming the S&P 500 even as the Iran conflict rattled global markets. "Domestic equity investors have been very optimistic," he notes, contrasting this with the lingering pessimism in Western analysis. This evidence holds up well against the narrative of total stagnation, though critics might point out that stock market gains don't always translate to broad-based wage growth or small business health.

"The challenge for Xi is not creating a brand-new consumption focused economic growth model, it is restoring confidence among entrepreneurs and consumers so that the economy returns to the pre-2020 model."

This framing is effective because it demystifies the economic strategy. It suggests China isn't trying to reinvent the wheel, but rather repair a broken engine. The core of the argument rests on the sheer volume of liquidity sitting idle. Kuo points out that household savings have more than doubled since 2020, a net increase equal to US$13 trillion. Once confidence returns, this capital is poised to flow into consumption and small business investment.

Tech, Green Energy, and the 1970s Parallel

Kuo expands his scope to the technology sector, arguing that the new five-year plan is a direct response to the need for self-reliance. He highlights a shift in financing, where state-controlled banks are now actively funding privately-owned tech firms. "Small tech firms accounted for 48% of IPO funds raised in Chinese equity markets last year," he writes. This detail challenges the assumption that China's tech sector is entirely state-directed; the private sector remains the primary engine for innovation in robotics and AI.

He draws a compelling historical parallel to the 1973 oil crisis to explain the current surge in green tech demand. Just as the oil shocks of the 1970s boosted American appetite for fuel-efficient Japanese cars, Kuo suggests the current geopolitical tension over oil will accelerate foreign demand for Chinese batteries and electric vehicles. "The 1970s oil shocks led American consumers to change their car preferences, which boosted Japanese makers of smaller, more fuel-efficient autos," he observes. This comparison adds necessary depth, suggesting that China's dominance in renewables is not just a policy choice but a structural advantage in a volatile energy market.

Critics might argue that relying on foreign demand for green tech is risky if Western nations impose tariffs, but Kuo's point about China's energy independence mitigates this. He notes that oil and gas account for only 27% of China's energy consumption, compared to 74% in the US. Furthermore, China has filled its strategic petroleum reserves, a move that mirrors the defensive posture taken after the 1973 crisis. "China filled its strategic petroleum reserve when oil prices were low," Kuo states, highlighting a level of preparedness that insulates the economy from external shocks.

Geopolitics and the Taiwan Question

The piece takes a sober turn when addressing the relationship with the United States and the Taiwan issue. Kuo suggests that while Beijing will offer Donald Trump the "pomp and circumstance he craves," there is no expectation of deep strategic concessions. "Beijing will give Trump the pomp and circumstance he craves but does not trust him enough to engage in serious negotiations," he writes. This assessment of the bilateral relationship is pragmatic, viewing stability as the primary goal rather than a breakthrough in policy.

On the sensitive topic of Taiwan, Kuo aligns with recent US intelligence assessments to debunk the "2027 invasion" narrative. He argues that the economic and military risks of an amphibious invasion are simply too high for the leadership. "Using force would raise unacceptable military risks for Xi, as highlighted by the stalemate in Ukraine," he explains. This is a strong, evidence-based rebuttal to the hawkish speculation often found in Washington.

"There is no domestic political pressure on Xi to use force. The vast majority of people in China are satisfied with the current situation."

This section is particularly valuable because it separates domestic political posturing from strategic reality. Kuo notes that the US intelligence community has confirmed that Chinese leaders "do not currently plan to execute an invasion of Taiwan in 2027." While some might argue that intelligence assessments can be wrong or that leadership priorities can shift overnight, the lack of visible military buildup and the high cost of conflict make Kuo's conclusion difficult to refute.

Bottom Line

Kuo's strongest asset is his refusal to rely on macro-aggregates alone, instead grounding his optimism in the specific behaviors of consumers and entrepreneurs on the ground. His argument is most vulnerable to the assumption that policy stability will automatically translate into sustained consumer spending, ignoring potential structural headwinds like demographic decline. However, for an investor looking for the first credible signs of a Chinese recovery, this piece provides the most grounded roadmap available.

Excerpt from the original article on Sinica

I would like to share my thoughts after spending two weeks in China in mid-March:

Sentiment is improving. Almost everyone I spoke with in Shanghai and Beijing said they are more optimistic about the economy than they were a year ago. This was my sixth trip to China since September 2024, when the political leadership announced a change in policy direction, and confidence among entrepreneurs, consumers and equity investors has been steadily, albeit slowly, improving.

The Chinese consumer is likely to bounce back. Since September 2024, Xi Jinping has switched his policy focus from regulatory control to supporting the return of consumption as the main driver of economic growth. Household savings have more than doubled since the start of 2020, a net increase equal to US$ 13 trillion, and as confidence returns I expect consumers and entrepreneurs to begin drawing on this to spend on goods and services and invest in small businesses.

Deeper government support to further accelerate tech advances. The new five-year plan, published during my visit, provides high-level guidance on an ecosystem to support tech self-reliance and innovation for industrial development. The government is pushing banks and equity markets to finance privately-owned tech firms. If successful, this would boost hiring and sentiment, which would stimulate consumption.

Rising oil prices re-enforce Beijing’s belief in green tech. China is already the world leader in batteries, renewables and electric vehicles, and I heard expectations that the war in Iran will accelerate foreign demand for these goods as the 1970s oil shocks boosted American appetite for fuel-efficient autos made in Japan.

Iran conflict less damaging to China than to the US and its allies. China is better prepared: it reduced its dependence on oil and gas, filled its strategic petroleum reserve, expanded renewable generation, and has lots of coal. Beijing is also able to limit the impact of higher oil prices on retail gasoline prices.

Xi will welcome Trump to Beijing but doesn’t trust him. I heard that China is prepared to receive Trump whenever the war is cooled down to the point where he feels he can travel. Beijing will give Trump the pomp and circumstance he craves but does not trust him enough to engage in serious negotiations. China’s objective is to maintain stability in the bilateral relationship. My contacts do not expect Xi to push Trump to make a significant change to long-standing US policy on Taiwan.
