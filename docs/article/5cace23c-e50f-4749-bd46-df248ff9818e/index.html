Doctor Asks: Are We Doing The Right Thing?

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Mar 22, 2020 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis doesn't just argue for social distancing; he dismantles the seductive logic of "doing nothing" by revealing how a year's worth of mortality risk, when compressed into weeks, collapses the very systems meant to save us. While many commentators debate the economy versus health, Francis brings a clinician's cold clarity to the math, showing that the "background risk" of death is not a static number but a curve that spikes catastrophically when hospitals are overwhelmed. This is essential listening for anyone tired of the noise, offering a bridge between the panic of the anxious and the skepticism of the doubters.

The Illusion of Background Risk

Francis begins by addressing the growing fatigue with lockdowns, acknowledging the validity of concerns about economic ruin and mental health. He admits that his own previous attempts to reassure the anxious may have missed the mark for those who feel strong-armed by inconsistent government messaging. "I sympathise with these views because governments have not been clear or consistent in their message and the Internet is a mess liable to confuse anybody," he writes. This honesty disarms the reader, establishing Francis not as a partisan alarmist, but as a pragmatic observer trying to navigate a chaotic information landscape.

However, he quickly pivots to the core statistical fallacy driving the opposition: the idea that because many people die every year anyway, the current surge in deaths is negligible. He challenges the notion that 500,000 projected deaths is acceptable because it matches the annual death toll. "Unchecked COVID-19 is like exposing the health system to a year's worth of risk but condensed into a few weeks," Francis explains. This distinction is crucial. It reframes the crisis not as a simple death count, but as a timing problem that breaks the machinery of care.

Critics might argue that this framing ignores the long-term economic devastation that could also lead to preventable deaths. Yet, Francis counters this by noting that inaction is itself a choice with evidence-backed consequences. "Not taking action in the face of such a rapidly changing immediate threat is also a choice which requires evidence," he asserts. The argument holds weight because it shifts the burden of proof onto those advocating for a "wait and see" approach, demanding they justify why they would gamble with a system already on the brink.

Unchecked COVID-19 is like exposing the health system to a year's worth of risk but condensed into a few weeks.

The Failure of Targeted Quarantine

The piece then tackles the popular "protect the vulnerable" strategy, often championed by experts like John Ioannidis and David Katz. Francis treats these arguments with respect, refusing to strawman them, before systematically dismantling their feasibility. He points out that even the most optimistic models of isolating only the elderly and infirm result in hundreds of thousands of deaths, far exceeding the targets set by health officials. "Every twenty-five-year-old with cystic fibrosis, every thirty-one-year-old with congenital heart condition... and everyone over the age of 60 gets locked up in quarantine," he illustrates. The sheer logistical impossibility of identifying and isolating every at-risk individual without error makes this a dangerous fantasy.

Furthermore, Francis highlights a grim reality often overlooked in these debates: the virus does not respect age boundaries. He cites data showing that a significant portion of intensive care admissions are young, fit, and previously healthy. "87% of whom were fit and healthy beforehand and 50% of those admissions are not making it out of ITU at the moment," he notes. This statistic serves as a brutal correction to the assumption that the young are immune to severe outcomes. The argument is effective because it uses the data to show that even a "perfect" targeted quarantine would fail to stop the virus from overwhelming the system through the very people it was supposed to spare.

The Urgency of the Hammer

Francis concludes by addressing the delay in policy response, using visual analogies to explain why waiting for more data is a fatal error. He compares the situation to a weather forecast: if a storm is predicted, you take the umbrella even if the forecast might be slightly off. "If the weather forecast predicts torrential rain, wouldn't you rather take an umbrella and be wrong than vice versa?" he asks. This simple analogy cuts through the academic debate, emphasizing that the cost of being wrong about a lockdown is far lower than the cost of being wrong about inaction.

He draws a sharp contrast between the evidence-based modeling of the Imperial College report and the opinion-driven arguments of skeptics. "Only one side of this debate has backed up their hypotheses with evidence and statistics; the other... is comprised of opinion," Francis states. He urges listeners to look at the devastation in Italy, where even world-class medical systems have been reduced to triage units, making life-and-death decisions like army doctors. "They are pleading with the rest of the world to bring the hammer down now," he says, using the testimony of a colleague on the front lines to drive home the urgency.

Critics might suggest that the economic collapse from a total shutdown could cause more long-term suffering than the virus itself. Francis acknowledges this trade-off but argues that the debate on societal sacrifice must wait until the immediate threat is contained. "There is a time to have discussions about how much of our way of life should be sacrificed to save lives... but now is not that time," he concludes. This prioritization of immediate survival over philosophical debate is the piece's most compelling call to action.

Bottom Line

Francis's strongest asset is his ability to translate complex epidemiological modeling into a visceral, human reality, stripping away the abstraction of "background risk" to reveal the immediate danger of a collapsing healthcare system. His biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that the public can sustain the psychological and economic toll of a total shutdown, a challenge he acknowledges but does not fully solve. The reader should watch for how governments balance this urgent need for a "hammer" with the inevitable long-term costs of such drastic measures.

There is a time to have discussions about how much of our way of life should be sacrificed to save lives... but now is not that time.
