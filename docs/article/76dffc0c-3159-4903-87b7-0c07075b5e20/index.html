Cooking for groups

Michael Ruhlman · Ruhlman's Newsletter ·Oct 4, 2025 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Michael Ruhlman transforms the mundane logistics of feeding a crowd into a masterclass on efficiency, arguing that the secret to hosting isn't perfection, but strategic imperfection. He challenges the modern obsession with dietary restrictions and rigid recipes, suggesting instead that the most satisfying meals for groups emerge from technique, timing, and a willingness to embrace the unexpected.

The Architecture of the Crowd Meal

Ruhlman opens by recounting a writers' workshop where he and his wife, Ann, fed a dozen people over three nights. The premise seems simple, but his execution reveals a sophisticated philosophy of labor management. He notes the rarity of the situation: "To our great good fortune, there were no vegans or vegetarians and zero food allergies, all but unheard of these days!" This observation serves as a pivot point; rather than lamenting the modern complexity of dietary needs, he uses the absence of them to highlight how much mental energy is usually wasted on accommodation. By stripping away the constraints, he can focus on the core mechanics of cooking.

His approach to the Beef Bourguignon exemplifies his rejection of traditional dogma. "I often say that I don't use recipes, but that's not true. I don't usually follow recipes. I look at recipes from trusted sources for ideas and quantities." This distinction is crucial. He treats recipes as data points rather than commandments. For the stew, he bypasses the tedious forty-minute searing process, opting instead to roast the beef at 425˚F. He explains that this method "sets the protein and squeezes out water that would otherwise become foam on the top of the stew." This is a practical, physics-based solution to a common culinary frustration, prioritizing the quality of the final liquid over the ritual of browning.

I don't like carrots in stews because by the time the stew is cooked, the carrots are dull, mushy, and no longer have any flavor.

Here, Ruhlman makes a bold, sensory claim that defies standard practice. He argues that the ingredient often used to add sweetness is actually a liability in long-cooked dishes. His solution is a two-stage process: cook whole carrots to infuse flavor, discard them, and then add fresh, obliquely cut carrots at the end. This ensures the vegetable retains "vivid" texture and flavor. It is a small tweak that fundamentally changes the eating experience, proving that his "time-efficient" meals are not shortcuts, but optimizations.

The Psychology of the Host

The piece shifts from the kitchen to the social dynamics of hosting, where Ruhlman's voice becomes even more distinctive. He recounts a bizarre interaction involving a can of mixed vegetables, Veg All, which sparked an unexpected online frenzy. After a friend gifted him a case, he posted a photo that garnered 200 comments, including accusations of snobbery. "What is with this Veg All? Mom, where did you go wrong? Why did you hide Veg All from me for all these years?!" This anecdote serves as a humorous but sharp critique of how food memory and class intersect. The anger directed at him for not knowing a 1970s product reveals a deep, almost tribal connection people have to the food of their childhood, regardless of its culinary merit.

Ruhlman also addresses the modern anxiety of the host, noting that the pressure to cater to every dietary niche often paralyzes the cook. By focusing on "time-efficient but satisfying meals," he reframes the goal of entertaining. It is not about impressing guests with novelty, but about creating a space where the food supports the conversation. His menu planning—ribs smoked then slow-cooked, corn pudding made ahead—demonstrates a strategy where the host is not chained to the stove during the event. "All 3 components ready ahead to bring to Hester and Michael's house, 30 minutes to finish." This allows the host to be present, a luxury often denied by the demands of traditional dinner party preparation.

Critics might argue that Ruhlman's advice is privileged, relying on the ability to buy specific ingredients or the luxury of a kitchen with a grill and oven. However, the underlying principle—that preparation and technique matter more than rigid adherence to a recipe—is universally applicable. The focus on method over menu allows any cook to adapt these strategies to their own constraints.

The Rhythm of Routine

Beyond the food, the newsletter offers a meditation on the rhythm of a creative life. Ruhlman describes a schedule packed with travel, teaching, and writing, yet finds peace in the "five weeks of mainly empty space" following a busy autumn. He contrasts the chaos of the travel season with the simple pleasure of an evening cocktail and a game of cribbage. "Heavenly," he writes, describing the prospect of a simple dinner and a television show. This section humanizes the author, moving him from the role of culinary expert to a relatable figure seeking balance.

His cocktail recipes are presented with the same precision as his cooking, yet they serve a different purpose: they are anchors for routine. He details the exact ratios for a Manhattan and a Martini, noting the "cosmic apt" connection between the word "rationale" and "ratio." This linguistic observation underscores his broader theme: that there is a logic and reason to the things that bring us joy. Whether it is the ratio of gin to vermouth or the ratio of work to rest, finding the right balance is the key to a satisfying life.

The exact recipes I make on such a night. Ann's Exact Manhattan... Michael's Exact Martini...

By sharing these specific recipes, Ruhlman invites the reader into his private ritual. He is not just teaching how to make a drink; he is teaching how to create a moment of pause. The inclusion of "Pepperidge Farm Flavor Blasted Xtra Cheddar goldfish" alongside a classic cocktail reinforces this blend of the refined and the mundane. It is a reminder that the best moments often come from the intersection of high and low culture.

Cultural Consumption and the Human Cost of Art

The newsletter concludes with a survey of recent cultural consumption, ranging from books to films. Ruhlman's review of Napoleon is particularly sharp, describing it as a "meaningless film that actually does a disservice to historical facts." He notes the criticism from the French, who "scoff at the film" made by a British director starring an American actor. This critique extends beyond mere taste; it touches on the ethics of historical representation. When a film prioritizes spectacle over truth, it risks erasing the complexity of the past.

In contrast, he praises Maestro as a "gorgeous work of filmmaking" that focuses on the marriage of two artists rather than just the genius of one. He highlights the film's refusal to hide the aging of its stars, noting that Annette Bening and Jodie Foster "do nothing to attempt to hide or 'fix' their wrinkled necks and wizened faces." This appreciation for authenticity in art mirrors his approach to cooking: both require a willingness to embrace the reality of the subject, whether it is the texture of a carrot or the lines on an actor's face.

These women are stunning.

This simple declaration carries significant weight in an industry obsessed with youth and perfection. Ruhlman's commentary suggests that true beauty lies in the unvarnished truth, a theme that runs through his entire piece. From the discarded carrots in the stew to the wrinkled faces on screen, he champions the authentic over the artificial.

Bottom Line

Michael Ruhlman's piece is a triumph of practical wisdom, proving that the best way to feed a crowd is to stop worrying about the crowd and start focusing on the food. His argument that technique and preparation outweigh rigid recipes offers a liberating perspective for anyone who feels overwhelmed by the demands of modern entertaining. While his reliance on specific ingredients and tools may not be universally accessible, the core philosophy—that cooking should be an act of joy, not a chore—is a lesson for everyone. The strongest part of his argument is the seamless integration of culinary technique with a broader philosophy of life, reminding us that the right ratio of effort to enjoyment is the secret ingredient in both the kitchen and the living room.

by Michael Ruhlman · Ruhlman's Newsletter · Read full article

(Originally published 12/2023.)

The weekend before we traveled to Guatemala, Ann and her colleague, the writer Hester Kaplan, held a writers’ workshop in Providence, RI, wherein a dozen or so writers engaged in a weekend of prompts, writing, and discussion.

Ann enlisted me and Hester’s physician-writer husband, Michael Stein, to tend bar and cook. Once again it got me thinking about time-efficient but satisfying meals for groups that can be made in advance. Here are the menus for the three nights. To our great good fortune, there were no vegans or vegetarians and zero food allergies, all but unheard of these days!

Opening night cocktail:

The Negroni (gin, sweet vermouth, Campari), because it’s one of the best cocktails there is, and it lends itself to a lighter cocktail for those who want less alcohol, the Americano (seltzer replaces the gin, recipes in the last Saturday newsletter). If you’re entertaining and you want to offer more, put a bottle of decent bourbon on the table, for those who might, from these four bottles, want a Boulvardier or a Manhattan.

Meal 1, Beef Bourguignon over egg noodles, and salad, because it’s frigging cold, and what better meal is there on a dark cold November night than beef braised in wine?

Here are a couple of great tips. I often say that I don’t use recipes, but that’s not true. I don’t usually follow recipes. I look at recipes from trusted sources for ideas and quantities. Beef cooked in wine is a no brainer technique-wise (I looked to this excellent recipe from my friend Ina for quantities.) But a couple of things made the cooking easier. I did not spend forty minutes searing 5 pounds of cubed beef. I roasted it in a 425˚F oven (which sets the protein and squeezes out water that would otherwise become foam on the top of the stew).

Also, I don’t like carrots in stews because by the time the stew is cooked, the carrots are dull, mushy, and no longer have any flavor. So I cook whole carrots in the stew. When the stew is nearly done, I discard the now-tasteless carrots, which have given their sweetness to the stew. During the final 20 minutes or so simmering on the stove, I added fresh carrots, cut obliquely, so that they are vivid and have texture and flavor (along with roasted shallots and cremini mushrooms).
