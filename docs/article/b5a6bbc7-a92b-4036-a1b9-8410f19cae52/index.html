January Q&A w/ Novara Co-Founder Aaron Bastani

Novara Media · Novara Media ·Jan 24, 2024 · 47 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a landscape of performative outrage and fleeting hot takes, Aaron Bastani offers a rare moment of grounded reflection, admitting that his biggest regret is not a policy failure or a controversial tweet, but the sheer slowness of his own ambition. While many media founders preach urgency, Bastani candidly confesses he wishes he had abandoned his PhD in 2010 to build Novara Media "guns blazing" a decade earlier, a confession that reframes the entire conversation about political media from one of strategy to one of personal sacrifice and timing.

The Cost of Delayed Action

Bastani's reflection on his career trajectory is surprisingly vulnerable for a public intellectual. He notes, "I wish we had gone in guns blazing sooner... I wish that in 2010 I had focused on starting something like this with James with other friends." This admission is striking because it challenges the narrative that academic rigor is always the necessary precursor to effective activism. Bastani suggests that his time in academia, while valuable for building his network, may have been a delay tactic rather than a foundation.

The core of his argument here is that the urgency of the political moment often demands immediate action over perfect preparation. He acknowledges the irony that he wouldn't have met his co-founders without the PhD, yet maintains that the trade-off was still a loss of time. This lands with particular weight for young activists who feel paralyzed by the need to have all the answers before they begin. Bastani's advice to a 24-year-old asking how to get involved is blunt: "Please don't rush yourselves. Go easy on yourselves." He reminds listeners that he didn't write his first public article until he was 26, offering a counter-narrative to the pressure of instant relevance that plagues modern political discourse.

Critics might note that this "go easy" advice could be interpreted as complacency in the face of accelerating climate and geopolitical crises. However, Bastani frames it as a strategic necessity to avoid burnout, suggesting that the long game of political change requires stamina rather than a sprint.

The Mechanics of Engagement and Activism

When addressing how to practically engage with politics, Bastani moves away from abstract theory toward tangible, low-barrier actions. He points out that "direct debits" to organizations like the National Trust or Novara Media itself are a form of "low hanging fruit" activism that is often overlooked. He argues that joining a union is insufficient unless one actively participates, stating, "if you join a union and don't do anything, doesn't matter does it?"

This pragmatic approach extends to his view on political parties. He encourages involvement in local politics, noting that community activists "make the world go round." The argument is effective because it democratizes political engagement, stripping away the requirement for high-level office or massive resources. Instead, he emphasizes the cumulative power of small, consistent actions.

"Don't try to cancel people or ratio people. Do good work. People will be interested."

Bastani's stance on online culture wars is particularly refreshing. He admits he enjoys "ratioing" people but warns against the culture of cancellation, arguing that the best feedback comes from those who disagree with him. He recalls meeting center-right individuals who, despite disliking his values, admitted his work was "really good." This suggests a media strategy focused on persuasion and integrity rather than tribal signaling. He posits that if his work can soften the views of opponents by even 10% over several years, the mission is accomplished.

Geopolitical Realities: Palestine, Iran, and the Risk of State Collapse

The conversation shifts to the most volatile geopolitical flashpoints, where Bastani's analysis becomes notably nuanced. On the International Court of Justice (ICJ) case regarding Israel, he admits uncertainty about the outcome but asserts that the evidence of genocide is strong, citing the UN definition. He provocatively expands the scope, suggesting that "Britain with regards to the great hunger in Ireland in the 19th century... would be a genocide now."

This broad application of the term is a double-edged sword. While it highlights historical injustices often minimized by mainstream narratives, it risks diluting the specific legal weight of the term in current conflicts. Bastani acknowledges this tension, noting that while the left often treats support for Palestine as "left coded," this framing is "deeply erroneous" and actually drives the right to support Israel in opposition.

On the topic of Iran, Bastani dismantles the binary choice between supporting the Islamic regime or advocating for Western-led regime change. He shares a deeply personal reason for his position: his family's losses after the 1979 revolution and his inability to visit his dying grandmother due to fears of arrest. "The idea that because I don't want a failed state in Iran... I must support the ayatollahs is so dumb," he argues. He warns that external regime change could turn Iran into "Afghanistan," a scenario he views as catastrophic.

His analysis of the region's stability is sobering. He rejects the notion of a simple regional war, instead describing a "region at war" involving multiple state and non-state actors across six or seven countries. His primary fear is not a traditional war, but the collapse of major states like Egypt or Iran. "If you think about what Iraq did when it became a failed State... times that by a factor of a few with regards to Iran or Egypt, that's my biggest worry," he says. He argues that the instability from such a collapse would dwarf current conflicts, creating millions of refugees and decades of volatility.

Critics might argue that Bastani's focus on the dangers of state collapse underestimates the moral imperative to oppose authoritarian regimes, regardless of the chaos that might follow. However, his argument is rooted in a realist assessment of the humanitarian costs of failed states, suggesting that the current Western policy of sanctions and regime change rhetoric is counterproductive.

Bottom Line

Bastani's most compelling contribution is his rejection of the "regime change" fantasy in favor of a long-term, domestic-led evolution in Iran, coupled with a call for the left to decouple support for Palestine from partisan identity politics. His biggest vulnerability lies in the tension between his desire for diplomatic normalization and the immediate, brutal reality of the current Iranian regime. Ultimately, the piece succeeds by replacing ideological certainty with a complex, human-centered realism that acknowledges the high stakes of state collapse without offering easy answers.
