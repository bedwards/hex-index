Your product won’t sell itself

a16z crypto · web3 with a16z ·Mar 14, 2026 · 18 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In an industry where technical brilliance is often mistaken for market inevitability, a16z crypto delivers a jarring reality check: the best product does not sell itself. This is not a generic sales pep talk; it is a specific, high-stakes playbook for the enterprise adoption era of crypto, arguing that a world-class sales organization is as critical to survival as the engineering team that built the protocol. For founders who have spent years perfecting code, the author's insistence that "success here is tangible and metrics-driven" offers a compelling, if intimidating, new metric for success.

The Myth of the Self-Selling Product

The core of the argument is a direct assault on the technical founder's aversion to go-to-market strategy. a16z crypto writes, "Technical founders sometimes have an aversion to investing in go-to-market and sales, believing that the best products sell themselves — but that's not the case." This framing is particularly potent because it targets the specific ego of the crypto builder, who often views sales as a necessary evil rather than a strategic lever. The author correctly identifies that in the current market, where institutional players are entering the space, the complexity of the sale has outpaced the ability of a product to speak for itself.

The piece argues that building a sales team is not just about revenue; it is about operational excellence that competitors cannot replicate. As a16z crypto puts it, "A world-class sales team can be just as important and effective as an outstanding engineering team, and it can unlock new levels of growth and operational excellence that competitors can't replicate." This is a bold claim in a sector obsessed with code, but it holds up when considering the high-trust, high-compliance nature of enterprise deals. The author suggests that the excitement of sales comes from its clarity: "You can literally count the number of marquee account wins and losses, see how this translates into increased revenue, and quickly separate the 100x performers from the also-rans."

Critics might argue that this metrics-driven approach risks commoditizing the relationship-building aspect of sales, reducing it to a numbers game. However, the author anticipates this by emphasizing the need for "missionary leaders" who attract "like-minded, hardcore enterprise sellers," suggesting that culture and conviction remain central to the process.

The Architecture of a Sales Machine

The most actionable part of the commentary is the specific sequencing of hires, which challenges the common instinct to hire junior talent first. a16z crypto writes, "The first and most critical hire is your sales leader... They lead all revenue efforts across the company and design the sales playbook." The author is adamant that founders should not start by hiring junior staff to "explore the market," noting that "any enterprise sales leader who's worth their salt will want to hire and shape their own team." This is a crucial distinction; a leader hired to execute a pre-existing plan is different from one hired to design the plan itself.

The text warns against the founder's reluctance to step back from the sales floor. While acknowledging that a CEO often has "the divine flame" that resonates with early customers, a16z crypto argues that "if the CEO remains the primary salesperson for too long, it prevents the company from moving quickly enough to capture the market opportunity." The timing is precise: the first sales leader should be hired "once a founder believes that they've achieved product-market fit or are on the cusp of doing so." Waiting longer risks competitors defining the market terms, effectively boxing the founder out.

Success here is tangible and metrics-driven. You can literally count the number of marquee account wins and losses, see how this translates into increased revenue, and quickly separate the 100x performers from the also-rans.

The author also debunks the myth that a salesperson's "Rolodex" is the primary asset. Instead, the focus shifts to "demonstrated ability to close large deals, navigate complex organizations, and win against strong competition." This is a vital correction for founders who might hire based on who a candidate knows rather than what they can do. The advice extends to the Solutions Architect, described as the bridge between technical capability and business outcome. a16z crypto notes that "a strong Solution Architect paired with a great AE and a compelling product is a lethal combination," emphasizing that this role must translate technical innovation into enterprise outcomes like ROI and compliance.

Process Over Intuition

The final pillar of the argument is the necessity of a repeatable process, treating sales with the same rigor as software development. The author writes, "Sales organizations, like software development processes and factories, perform best when they are built on a clear, repeatable process." This analogy is effective because it speaks the language of the technical founder. The goal is to ensure the company is "setting the agenda, defining the requirements, and boxing out the competition" rather than reacting to them.

However, the piece includes a stern warning about the limits of optimization. a16z crypto states, "Sales ops will not fix a broken sales motion or a weak product. If deals are consistently lost due to weak differentiation, late entry, or product gaps, the root cause lies elsewhere." This is a necessary counterpoint to the enthusiasm for hiring a Head of Sales Operations. The author makes it clear that this role is for scaling a working machine, not for building one from scratch. The sequence matters: hire the head of sales, the account executives, and the solutions architects first. Only then, once the engine is running, does the sales operations role make sense to "scale and tune the machine."

Bottom Line

a16z crypto's strongest contribution is the dismantling of the "product sells itself" myth with a concrete, sequential hiring strategy that prioritizes leadership and process over junior execution. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its heavy reliance on the assumption that product-market fit has already been achieved; without that foundation, even the best sales team cannot manufacture demand. Readers should watch for how these principles apply to non-enterprise crypto markets, where the sales cycle and buyer psychology may differ significantly. "

Technical founders sometimes have an aversion to investing in go-to-market and sales, believing that the best products sell themselves — but that’s not the case. And it’s especially true now that we’ve entered the enterprise adoption era of crypto. So building an effective enterprise sales organization is one of the most powerful motions for scaling a company after finding product-market fit. A world-class sales team can be just as important and effective as an outstanding engineering team, and it can unlock new levels of growth and operational excellence that competitors can’t replicate.

It can also be surprisingly exciting and satisfying — even for deeply technical, product-oriented founders — to build a sales organization. The reason is simple: success here is tangible and metrics-driven. You can literally count the number of marquee account wins and losses, see how this translates into increased revenue, and quickly separate the 100x performers from the also-rans.

Here’s a step-by-step playbook to help you build a world-class enterprise sales organization.

Step one: Build the right team

Sales Leader

The first and most critical hire is your sales leader. They lead all revenue efforts across the company and design the sales playbook: how the organization goes to market, how it operates day to day, and how deals are won. This role shapes the processes by which campaigns are fought and victories are secured.

You must give this leader a seat at the table. They should report directly to the CEO and participate in executive staff meetings alongside other senior leaders so they are fully integrated into company strategy and decision-making. Otherwise you’re not signaling that sales is a priority in the organization, or are siloing it too much as a function.
