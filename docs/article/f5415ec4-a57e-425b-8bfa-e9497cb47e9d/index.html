The Real Harm of Social Media

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Mar 7, 2024 · 20 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now cuts through the noise of general screen-time debates to pinpoint a specific, terrifying mechanism: social media isn't just a distraction, but an active vector for self-harm and depression, particularly among teenage girls. The piece's most arresting claim is that the correlation between social media use and mental health decline is statistically stronger than the link between childhood lead exposure and brain damage, a comparison that forces a re-evaluation of how we regulate digital spaces.

The Gendered Crisis and the Algorithm

The author anchors the argument in a stark timeline: the surge in teenage depression, especially among girls, aligns almost perfectly with the 2010 release of the iPhone 4 and the ubiquity of front-facing cameras. Then & Now writes, "Instagram was found in 2010 just before that uptick... the first phone with a front facing camera." This temporal coincidence is bolstered by internal research from Facebook itself, leaked by whistleblower Frances Haugen, which revealed that the company knew its platform was damaging. The author notes that teens themselves "blame Instagram for increases in the rate of anxiety and depression," a finding that was "unprompted and consistent across all groups."

This evidence is compelling because it shifts the blame from the user to the design. The platform quantifies popularity, turning the schoolyard into a public scoreboard where "how many people don't like you" is visible to everyone. Then & Now observes that "social media is popularity Quantified for everyone in the schoolyard to see." The argument gains weight when contrasting social media with passive consumption; the author points out that "watching television or Netflix is not the problem it's specifically social media."

Critics might argue that correlation does not equal causation, noting that the same period saw rising economic anxiety and climate fears. However, the author effectively counters this by asking why these macro-threats would disproportionately affect teenage girls in the same way social media does. The specificity of the demographic impact suggests a unique digital mechanism rather than a general societal malaise.

The Mechanism of Harm: From Algorithms to Real-World Tragedy

The commentary moves from statistical correlation to the specific mechanics of algorithmic recommendation. The piece highlights the tragic case of Molly Russell, a 14-year-old whose death was ruled to be influenced by online content. Then & Now writes, "in a grind breaking verdict the currer ruled that the negative effects of online content contributed to Molly's death." The report revealed that out of 16,300 pieces of content she viewed, 2,100 were related to self-harm and depression, often served to her without any search query.

This is where the argument becomes most urgent. The author explains that "83% of them had been recommended the content on Instagram and Tik Tok without searching for it." The algorithm does not wait for a user to seek out harm; it actively pushes it. Then & Now describes the ease of access: "one researcher said that she jumped on Instagram to see how fast she could get to an image which contained the problems and it took her about a minute and a half."

The piece also touches on the frustrating reality of content moderation, noting that the author had to replace references to specific tragic issues with vague phrases because "YouTube have decided that discussing the issues in the following section are against their guidelines." This meta-commentary underscores the very problem being discussed: platforms are so sensitive to their own reputations that they obscure the severity of the harm they facilitate.

The Harm Principle and the Edge of Speech

The author pivots to a philosophical framework, invoking John Stuart Mill's "harm principle" to argue for intervention. The core logic is that we regulate other technologies to prevent harm, so why not social media? Then & Now writes, "The only purpose for which power can rightly exercised over any member of a civilized Community against his will is to prevent harm to others."

The piece draws a parallel between social media content and other forms of "edge speech" that society already regulates, such as incitement to violence or false advertising. The author cites the example of ISIS's social media campaign, which "engaged in a 1 billion social media campaign to try and raise 1 billion Fighters." This historical precedent shows that we are willing to curtail speech when it directly leads to physical harm. Similarly, the author points to gang violence in the UK, where "Snapchat is the root of a lot of problems," facilitating a cycle of provocation and retaliation that ends in stabbings.

However, the author acknowledges the difficulty in drawing the line. "First is there even any such thing as a harmful post?" they ask, noting that words are not bullets. Yet, they argue that we do intervene before harm is done in other contexts, such as with "false advertising or leaving ingredients that might be harmful off labels." The argument suggests that the current lack of regulation is an anomaly, not a principle.

Critics might note that defining "harmful speech" in a liberal democracy is fraught with risk, potentially opening the door to censorship of legitimate dissent. The author admits this tension, referencing the difficulty of defining hate speech laws, but maintains that the "threat of harm from things like genocide was so great" that intervention was necessary.

"We regulate and have safety codes to make sure technology transport and buildings and medicine safe so to make sense of all of this I want to start from cases where social media has actually harmed in some way and then work backwards from there."

Bottom Line

Then & Now makes a devastatingly clear case that social media is not a neutral tool but an active agent in the mental health crisis, with evidence that rivals the severity of lead poisoning. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to accept "correlation is not causation" as a shield for inaction, instead pointing to internal corporate knowledge and specific algorithmic mechanisms that drive harm. The biggest vulnerability remains the practical challenge of regulation without stifling free speech, but the author successfully frames this not as a philosophical debate, but as a public safety imperative.
