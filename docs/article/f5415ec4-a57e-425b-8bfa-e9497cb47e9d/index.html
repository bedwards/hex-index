The real harm of social media

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Mar 7, 2024 ·20 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now presents a chilling correlation that demands immediate attention: the explosion of teenage depression, particularly among girls, aligns precisely with the rise of social media platforms, not just general screen time. This is not a vague worry about technology; it is a data-driven argument linking specific platform mechanics to a tripling of self-harm and suicide attempts, framed through the lens of the "harm principle" to argue for urgent intervention.

The Data Behind the Despair

The piece opens with a stark visual: a graph showing a sharp uptick in teenage depression rates starting around 2010. Then & Now writes, "findings like this have been replicated in many countries that in a worrying number of studies reports of mental health problems depression anxiety self harm suicide and so on have almost tripled." The author immediately pivots to the timing, noting that while other factors like the 2008 financial crash or climate anxiety exist, they fail to explain why teenage girls are disproportionately affected.

The commentary leans heavily on internal research leaked by whistleblower Frances Haugen to bolster the claim that the platforms themselves know the damage they are causing. Then & Now highlights that "teens themselves blame Instagram for increases in the rate of anxiety and depression," a finding that was unprompted and consistent. This is a crucial distinction; the harm is not just an external observation by psychologists but an internal admission by the users themselves. The author notes that in 2011, one in three teenage girls reported persistent sadness, a figure that has now climbed to 57% according to CDC data.

"Instagram was found it in 2010 just before that uptick and the iPhone 4 was released at the same time the first phone with a front facing camera I mean this will have a lasting impact on the way that we actually connect with each other."

Then & Now argues that the correlation here is stronger than the link between childhood lead exposure and brain development, or even binge drinking and overall health. This comparison is powerful because it reframes social media from a "lifestyle choice" to a public health crisis comparable to toxic environmental hazards. Critics might note that correlation does not equal causation, and the article acknowledges that existential threats like global politics play a role. However, the author effectively counters this by pointing out that the specific design of social media—quantifying popularity and forcing constant comparison—is the unique variable that separates it from passive consumption like watching Netflix.

The Mechanics of Harm

The argument deepens by dissecting how these platforms inflict damage. Then & Now describes social media as "popularity Quantified for everyone in the schoolyard to see," exposing children to a relentless stream of idealized body images and the visible metrics of social rejection. The author cites studies showing that high-speed internet access leads to increased addictive use, decreased sleep, and reduced face-to-face socialization, with girls bearing the brunt of these effects.

The piece then shifts to the most disturbing mechanism: algorithmic amplification of self-harm content. Then & Now recounts the tragic case of Molly Russell, a 14-year-old whose death was ruled to be contributed to by online content. The author states, "out of the 16,300 pieces of content that Molly interacted with on Instagram in the 6 months before she died 2,100 were related to Su self harm and depression." This is not a case of a user actively searching for harm; it is a case of the system pushing it. A Swansea University study found that 83% of respondents with a history of self-harm had been recommended such content on Instagram and TikTok without ever searching for it.

"I cannot describe how frustrating I find this and as a result I've replaced references to a very serious specific tragic issue with the phrase very serious mental health issues which just doesn't fully capture what I'm referring to at all."

The author's personal frustration with platform censorship, which forced them to obscure the very topic they are discussing, serves as a meta-commentary on the opacity of these systems. If the platforms are so sensitive to the discussion of harm that they censor the analysis of it, the danger is arguably even greater than the data suggests.

The Case for Intervention

Finally, Then & Now tackles the philosophical hurdle: why should we regulate speech that isn't explicitly violent? The author invokes John Stuart Mill's "harm principle," arguing that we already intervene when speech signals imminent physical harm, such as in cases of terrorism recruitment or gang violence. Then & Now points to the Islamic State's billion-dollar social media campaign and the rise of gang violence in the UK, where Snapchat has become a stage for escalating feuds.

The author writes, "we usually tolerate Intervention when there's a clear risk of harm... we regulate and have safety codes to make sure technology transport and buildings and medicine safe." The piece argues that social media posts encouraging self-harm or violence are the digital equivalent of leaving a dangerous ingredient off a medicine label. The argument is that we do not need to wait for the bullet to be fired or the jump to be made to intervene; we must address the "edge speech" that signals the intent to harm.

"The only purpose for which power can be rightly exercised over any member of a civilized Community against his will is to prevent harm to others."

While the comparison to hate speech laws and terrorism recruitment is compelling, critics might argue that applying these standards to mental health content is far more complex, as the line between "harmful" and "supportive" communities is often blurry. Yet, the sheer volume of algorithmically promoted content encouraging self-harm, as detailed in the article, suggests that the current "do nothing" approach is failing.

Bottom Line

Then & Now delivers a compelling, evidence-rich case that social media is not merely a mirror of societal issues but an active driver of a mental health crisis among young people. The argument's greatest strength is its refusal to accept "correlation is not causation" as an excuse for inaction, instead drawing parallels to established public health interventions. The biggest vulnerability remains the difficulty of defining and regulating "harmful" content without infringing on free speech, but the piece makes a persuasive case that the cost of inaction is already being paid in lives.

"Social media is popularity Quantified for everyone in the schoolyard to see, and for many, that metric is a death sentence."

The reader should watch for how the executive branch and regulatory bodies respond to this mounting evidence, as the window for voluntary reform appears to be closing.
