The harder it is to find the truth, the easier it is to lie to ourselves

Dan Williams · Conspicuous Cognition ·Jan 12, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Dan Williams challenges a comforting assumption in modern psychology: that the rise of false beliefs is solely due to individual irrationality or tribal motivation. Instead, he argues that the very difficulty of finding the truth in a complex world acts as a lubricant for self-deception, making it easier for us to lie to ourselves when the facts are murky. This is a crucial distinction for busy leaders and policymakers who often dismiss opposing views as mere stupidity, missing the structural incentives that make those views feel rational to the believer.

The Illusion of Naïve Realism

Williams begins by dismantling the idea that people are simply bad at thinking. He points out that throughout history, from Adam Smith to Freud, scholars have identified "motivated cognition" as a destructive force where we prioritize our desires over accuracy. We seek evidence that confirms our views and ignore what contradicts them. However, Williams notes that recent skepticism suggests people are actually quite rational, updating their beliefs when presented with facts. He rejects this optimism, arguing that skeptics miss the bigger picture.

"When properly understood, motivated cognition is consistent with the finding that people update their beliefs when presented with corrective information," Williams writes. He contends that these cognitive biases are not just individual quirks but a "team project" involving social norms and coordinated systems that protect favored narratives. This reframing is vital; it moves the blame from the lone thinker to the ecosystem of information they inhabit. It suggests that correcting misinformation requires more than just handing someone a fact sheet; it requires dismantling the social scaffolding that supports the lie.

The Trap of Epistemic Complexity

The core of Williams' argument rests on the concept of "epistemic complexity." He explains that in domains like politics or climate change, reality is rarely self-evident. We rely on intermediaries—journalists, experts, algorithms—to interpret the world for us. This echoes Walter Lippmann's observation from the 1920s that the modern world is "out of reach, out of sight, and out of mind," forcing us to rely on "low-resolution compressions" of reality. We are instinctive "naïve realists," believing our internal map is the territory itself.

Williams argues that when the truth is hard to pin down, our ability to justify our preferred conclusions skyrockets. He uses the example of climate change, noting that unless you are an expert, your opinion is likely a "socially-learned slogan" rather than a deep understanding of geophysics. The problem isn't that people are irrational; it's that the complexity of the world allows them to rationalize almost anything they want to believe.

"As it becomes increasingly difficult to determine what's true, it becomes correspondingly easier to convince ourselves of desirable untruths," he writes. This is a powerful insight that flips the script on recent academic trends. While some scholars argue that epistemic complexity explains away motivated cognition (i.e., people are just confused, not biased), Williams insists the opposite is true. The fog of complexity doesn't excuse bias; it enables it. It provides the cover needed for "rationalization constraints" to be satisfied without actual truth-seeking.

Critics might argue that this view risks overestimating the agency of bad actors and underestimating the genuine confusion of the public. If the world is truly that complex, perhaps many people are simply doing their best with limited tools, rather than actively engaging in self-deception. However, Williams' point remains that the opportunity for self-deception is structurally built into the complexity itself.

The Incentives of Elites

The practical implications of this dynamic are stark. Williams suggests that elites, particularly in politics, have a vested interest in maintaining this complexity. By "flooding the zone with shit" or manufacturing uncertainty, leaders can ensure their supporters remain in a state where motivated cognition thrives. When the truth is opaque, tribal loyalty becomes the primary metric for belief.

"Politicians who spread self-serving lies benefit when their supporters prioritise political tribalism over accuracy," Williams observes. This creates a feedback loop where the difficulty of verification is weaponized. If a leader claims an election was stolen, the sheer complexity of election administration makes it difficult for the average citizen to disprove it, allowing the motivated belief to take root and spread. This aligns with historical patterns of propaganda, where the goal is not necessarily to convince people of a specific lie, but to make the truth so inaccessible that any lie becomes plausible.

The article also touches on how scientific studies often fail to capture this dynamic. By presenting participants with clear, corrective evidence in a lab setting, researchers artificially reduce epistemic complexity. In the real world, that clarity rarely exists. "One way to understand such experiments is that they artificially and temporarily reduce epistemic complexity," Williams notes, suggesting that lab results may vastly underestimate the power of motivated cognition in the wild.

Bottom Line

Williams delivers a sobering verdict: the complexity of the modern information environment is not just a barrier to knowledge, but an active enabler of self-deception. The strongest part of his argument is the synthesis of psychological bias with structural complexity, showing how the two reinforce each other to make lies feel like truths. The biggest vulnerability lies in the difficulty of solving this; if the problem is systemic complexity, the solution requires more than just individual critical thinking skills. Readers should watch for how institutions respond to this dynamic, specifically whether they attempt to reduce complexity or exploit it for political gain.

The harder it is to find the truth, the easier it is to lie to ourselves

by Dan Williams · Conspicuous Cognition · Read full article

If you look at humanity, both today and throughout history, you can’t help but notice that people believe a lot of things that seem stupid and irrational. Pick your favourite example: conspiracy theories, religion, prejudice, ideology, pseudoscience, ancestor myths, people who hold different political opinions from your own, and so on.

This observation provokes a central question for the social sciences. Why do broadly rational people, people who often seem intelligent and competent in most aspects of their lives, sometimes believe highly irrational things?

One classic answer is that people are not disinterested truth seekers. In some contexts, our practical interests conflict with the aim of acquiring accurate, evidence-based beliefs. For example, we might want to believe things that make us feel good, that impose a satisfying order and certainty on a complex world, that help us persuade others that we’re noble and impressive, or that win us status and approval from our friends and allies.

Famously, when our goals come into conflict with the pursuit of truth in this way, the truth often loses out. We lie to ourselves, bury our heads in the sand, and engage in elaborate mental gymnastics. Less colloquially, we engage in what psychologists call “motivated cognition”: we—or our minds, at least—direct cognitive processes toward favoured conclusions, not true ones. For example, we instinctively seek out evidence that confirms those conclusions (confirmation bias), shield ourselves from evidence against them (motivated ignorance), insist on higher standards for arguments we dislike than for those we like (biased evaluation), and remember and forget information in convenient patterns (selective forgetting).

Throughout most of history, scholars had little doubt that this tendency was a central and destructive feature of the human condition.

For Adam Smith, for example, it “is the fatal weakness of mankind” and “the source of half the disorders of human life.” For Socrates in the Cratylus, “the worst of all deceptions is self-deception.” And of course, thinkers such as Freud and Nietzsche placed motivated cognition at the centre of their understanding of human psychology.
