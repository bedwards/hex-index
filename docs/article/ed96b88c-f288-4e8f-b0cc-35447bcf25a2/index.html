Why Geothermal Failed in China

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Mar 31, 2026 · 18 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider, writing through a guest contributor, delivers a counterintuitive diagnosis for China's clean energy gap: the nation's geothermal potential isn't failing due to a lack of state power or technical know-how, but because wind and solar have become so overwhelmingly dominant that they are actively crowding out the competition. In a landscape where the Chinese state can usually force any project into existence, Schneider reveals a scenario where market dynamics and entrenched industrial interests have created a "winner's curse" for renewables, leaving geothermal stranded despite its theoretical superiority as a baseload power source.

The Crowding-Out Effect

The core of Schneider's argument challenges the assumption that China's renewable boom is a monolithic success story. Instead, he posits that the very success of wind and solar has become a barrier to diversification. "The short version of the story is that solar and wind are so dominant (and their supply chains so involuted) that they are crowding out investment at basically every level," Schneider writes. This is a crucial distinction. While the 14th Five-Year Plan ambitiously pledged to increase renewable consumption to 25% by 2035, the mechanism to get there has narrowed rather than broadened.

The author effectively highlights how logistical realities favor the incumbents. Wind turbines and solar panels are commodities; they are "easier to mass produce and transport logistically, unlike geothermal which requires site-specific engineering and custom-made equipment." This creates a self-reinforcing cycle where the supply chain for wind and solar becomes cheaper and more efficient, while geothermal remains a bespoke, high-risk engineering challenge. Critics might note that this ignores the potential for state-directed industrial policy to force a geothermal supply chain into existence, as China has done for EVs. However, Schneider's point holds weight: without a clear profit motive or unified policy, the inertia of the existing wind and solar machine is immense.

"Realistically, wind turbines and solar panels are easier to mass produce and transport logistically, unlike geothermal which requires site-specific engineering and custom-made equipment."

This framing is particularly striking because it suggests that China's climate strategy is currently trapped by its own efficiency. The country has built a manufacturing juggernaut for intermittent power, but as Schneider notes, "Energy storage can help, but there's another obvious way to add green, non-intermittent power to the grid: geothermal." Yet, that obvious solution is being sidelined.

The Policy Vacuum and Fiscal Penalties

Schneider digs into the bureaucratic machinery to show how policy has actively worked against geothermal. The argument here is that the lack of a clear target is a form of policy failure. "Since 2021, China has stopped setting clear targets for geothermal development," he observes, noting that the 14th Five-Year Plan merely stated to "promote geothermal energy development in an orderly manner." This vague language, contrasted with the aggressive targets for wind and solar, signals to investors that geothermal is a low priority.

The financial disincentives are even starker. Schneider points out that "subsidies of geothermal plants are negotiated on a case-by-case basis, which increases the financial risks for private developers." This stands in sharp contrast to the "generous feed-in tariffs" that fueled the wind and solar boom. Furthermore, a 2020 revision to the Resource Tax Law reclassified geothermal energy, subjecting it to higher taxation. This is a critical detail: the state is not just ignoring geothermal; it is financially penalizing it relative to its competitors.

The author also touches on the historical context of China's energy choices, noting that "geothermal was left out of this development" during the 2000s push to combat pollution. While the 2007 NDRC report identified Yunnan and Tibet as ideal for geothermal, the state chose to double down on hydropower and, later, wind and solar. This decision has long-term consequences, especially as "hydropower, which is now severely at risk because of extreme droughts," struggles to meet demand. Schneider implies that the failure to diversify into geothermal has left the grid more vulnerable to climate shocks than it needed to be.

"Apart from higher costs, geothermal power development lacks unified policy support compared to wind and solar."

This evidence holds up well against the backdrop of China's recent energy crises. The reliance on a few dominant technologies, while efficient in the short term, creates systemic fragility. The argument that "entrenched interests" shape Beijing's decision-making is persuasive here; the wind and solar sectors have grown so large that they now dictate the terms of the energy transition.

The Technical and Economic Hurdles

Beyond policy, Schneider addresses the inherent difficulties of geothermal technology, which make it a harder sell for investors. The process involves "site exploration, drilling underground to create a geothermal well, establishing the power plant, and finally, electrical transmission." The exploration phase is the killer. "Over 80% of the Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE) is driven by capital costs, and exploration accounts for around 5%," Schneider cites from the Colorado School of Mines, but notes that the risk of drilling in unproductive sites makes the initial capital outlay a massive gamble.

The article provides a fascinating look at Hot Dry Rock (HDR) technology, which could theoretically unlock vast resources. "HDR geothermal systems employ similar technology to oil and gas fracking, where a geothermal power plant is built by creating a geothermal reservoir by drilling deep wells into hot rocks." Despite China possessing "856 trillion tonnes of standard coal" worth of HDR resources, the technology remains in the experimental stage. The author notes that "obtaining accurate drilling data was also difficult because the latest geological data was published six years prior, in 2017," highlighting a data gap that hampers investment.

The case of the Yangyi Geothermal Power Station in Tibet serves as a microcosm of these struggles. Development stalled for 20 years because "project funding was 'designated for national use and would not have passed through local government channels'." Local governments, unable to profit directly, had no incentive to push the project. Even when a private developer stepped in, the uncertainty of electricity tariffs—"the National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC) insisted that electricity tariffs could only be confirmed upon the project's completion"—scared them off. It was only after a state-owned enterprise stepped in that the plant finally came online in 2018, and even then, "profitability still remains an issue because feed-in tariffs in Tibet are still much lower than in the mainland."

"The reality that geothermal power generation is significantly riskier and more expensive to develop makes it an even less compelling option."

This section effectively illustrates that the problem isn't just a lack of will, but a misalignment of incentives. The state can force a dam or a solar farm, but it cannot easily force a market to accept a high-risk, high-cost technology when cheaper, lower-risk alternatives are available.

Bottom Line

Schneider's most compelling contribution is the revelation that China's renewable dominance is a double-edged sword, creating a "winner's curse" where the success of wind and solar actively suppresses the development of geothermal. The argument is strongest in its detailed breakdown of how fiscal policy and supply chain dynamics have conspired to make geothermal unviable, despite its potential to solve the intermittency problem that plagues the current grid. However, the piece leaves unresolved the question of whether the state will eventually intervene to break this cycle, especially as droughts threaten hydropower and the limits of solar and wind become apparent. The biggest vulnerability in the current strategy is the lack of a diversified baseload, leaving China's climate goals hanging on a single, fragile thread of technological success.
