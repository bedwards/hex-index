Did Austerity Ruin Britain's Economy?

Joeri Schasfoort · Money & Macro ·Jul 14, 2023 · 71 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Joeri Schasfoort does not merely ask if austerity failed; he dismantles the very narrative that justified it, revealing a thirteen-year experiment that left Britain with crumbling schools, malnourished children, and stagnant wages. The most striking element of this analysis is not the economic data itself, but the exposure of a deliberate political choice disguised as economic necessity. Schasfoort argues that the UK's severe contraction was not a reaction to a genuine fiscal crisis, but a specific ideological pivot that ignored the automatic stabilizers designed to protect the economy during a recession.

The Myth of the Empty Coffer

The conversation begins by confronting the foundational myth of the post-2008 era: the idea that the government literally ran out of money. Schasfoort traces this back to a handwritten note allegedly left by the outgoing Labor government, which the incoming Conservative administration weaponized to justify a sharp turn from fiscal expansion to contraction. "The headline story was this one about about debt about the labor government having spent too much," Schasfoort notes, highlighting how a simple household analogy replaced complex macroeconomic reality. This framing was effective because it resonated with voters, yet it ignored the technical reality that a sovereign currency issuer cannot simply "run out" of money in the way a household can.

The author points out that the debt-to-GDP ratio had actually fallen under the previous administration before the 2008 crisis, and the subsequent rise was a natural result of automatic stabilizers—unemployment benefits rising and tax revenues falling during a recession. "They were very good at blaming labor for that," Schasfoort observes, noting the irony that the financial crisis itself was exacerbated by the very deregulation the Conservatives championed. By treating the debt ratio as a moral failing rather than a cyclical metric, policymakers created a self-fulfilling prophecy where the cure (austerity) became more damaging than the disease.

Critics might argue that high debt levels eventually constrain policy options, but Schasfoort counters that the UK faced no immediate threat of insolvency. "There was no real indicator of... unwillingness of bondholders to finance the government," he states, pointing out that interest rates remained low and bond yields did not spiral. The comparison to Greece or Zimbabwe was, in his view, a rhetorical device rather than an economic forecast. This suggests the austerity drive was not a defensive maneuver against a market crash, but an offensive strategy to reshape the state.

Defining the Policy and the Choice

A crucial distinction emerges when Schasfoort defines austerity not just as tight fiscal policy, but as a specific mix of spending cuts and tax changes. He clarifies that while economists view fiscal contraction as a single tool, the public perceives it through the lens of service cuts. "The mix was about 80 cut 20 tax Rises," Schasfoort explains, emphasizing that this was a "very clear political choice." The government could have balanced the budget by raising taxes on capital gains or wealth, but they chose to cut services instead.

This distinction matters because it reveals that the outcome was not an inevitable economic law, but a policy decision. Schasfoort writes, "if the labor government were to... put an extra 50 billion 75 billion a year and we're going to do it by taxing capital gains... I don't think most people in in the British economy would think okay that's more austerity." This highlights the slippery nature of the term; it is often used to describe any fiscal tightening, but in practice, it has become synonymous with regressive spending reductions. The author argues that this specific mix—prioritizing cuts over revenue increases—was what drove the long-lasting social damage.

"They said they were going to fix it 13 years ago... and here we are 13 years later and it's worse."

The consequences of this choice are stark. Schasfoort notes that while the UK was once a top performer, it has now dropped down international rankings, with wages failing to recover to 2008 levels. "We're living with the legacy of austerity in the sense of crumbling schools malnourished children," he asserts. The narrative that "there's no money left" has become a permanent constraint on government action, even as the economy struggles. This framing effectively shifts the blame from the policy itself to the perceived lack of resources, making it politically difficult to reverse course.

The Legacy of a Structural Break

The discussion concludes by examining the long-term trajectory of the UK economy. Schasfoort acknowledges that Brexit complicates comparative studies, noting that the 2016 referendum acts as a "structural break" that makes direct comparisons with the US or Germany difficult. However, even controlling for this, the UK's performance lags significantly behind peers who did not pursue such aggressive austerity measures. "Britain was I think one of the best in class... with one of the strongest austerity programs and it was now the first to get into trouble," he remarks, underscoring the irony of the policy's failure.

The author suggests that the financial sector's dominance, often cited as a reason for the crisis, was not a unique vulnerability but a feature of the economy that the Conservatives actively supported. "The conservatives were absolutely... fully behind Financial deregulation," Schasfoort points out, challenging the idea that the financial sector's collapse was an unavoidable external shock. Instead, the austerity response exacerbated the downturn, turning a recession into a prolonged stagnation. The argument holds weight because it connects specific policy choices to tangible outcomes, rather than attributing failure to abstract market forces.

Critics might suggest that the UK's unique structural issues, such as its heavy reliance on finance, made it uniquely vulnerable regardless of policy. However, Schasfoort's evidence suggests that the severity of the downturn was a direct result of the policy response, not just the initial shock. The failure to recover suggests that the austerity measures themselves created a drag on growth that persisted for over a decade.

Bottom Line

Schasfoort's most compelling contribution is the dissection of the "no money left" narrative, exposing it as a political construct rather than an economic reality. His argument is strongest in linking the specific 80/20 split of cuts to taxes to the long-term social decay, but it faces the challenge of disentangling austerity's effects from the subsequent shock of Brexit. The reader should watch for how current policymakers continue to use the "broken country" narrative to justify further cuts, even as the data suggests the original diagnosis was flawed.
