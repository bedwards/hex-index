Zoning's War on Cuddly Animals, Cute Kids, and Christian Charity

Various · Reason ·Apr 7, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Most policy analysis treats zoning as a dry technicality of land use, but this piece argues it is actually a frontline battleground for religious liberty, animal welfare, and the very survival of the American family unit. Various reframes the conversation by showing how local codes are not merely about traffic or aesthetics, but are actively dismantling the infrastructure of community care. The evidence presented suggests that when cities prioritize rigid land-use categories over actual neighborhood impacts, they create a system where good intentions become illegal acts.

The Cost of Compliance

The author opens with a stark example from Bryon, Ohio, where a fire chief sued a church to stop its overnight ministry for the homeless. The dispute centered on a demand for a sprinkler system that the church pastor, Chris Avell, argued was cost-prohibitive and effectively a ban on their religious practice. Various writes, "The requirement to install one was thus an effective demand to shut down their nighttime ministry, which the church argued violated their Free Exercise rights." This framing is crucial because it shifts the debate from fire safety to constitutional rights. The court agreed, dismissing the lawsuit because the city had waived similar requirements for nearby hotels, failing the "strict scrutiny" standard required for religious restrictions.

The commentary highlights a critical inconsistency in how regulations are applied. As Various notes, the judge ruled that the city "had failed that strict scrutiny standard" by treating a religious shelter differently than a commercial hotel. This exposes a flaw in administrative enforcement where the intent of the regulation matters more than the impact. Critics might argue that fire safety is a non-negotiable public good, regardless of the building's use. However, the article effectively demonstrates that when regulations are applied selectively, they cease to be about safety and become tools of exclusion. The connection to the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA) is implicit here; this law was designed precisely to prevent such arbitrary burdens on religious exercise, a principle that has been tested since its enactment in 2000.

"Zoning codes don't directly regulate the negative externalities and neighborhood impacts. They instead play word games about what counts as 'commercial' or 'residential' uses."

The Definition of "Commercial"

Moving to North Carolina, the piece examines the case of Fairytale Farm, an animal sanctuary in Winston-Salem forced to cease operations because it hosted ticketed fundraising events. The city argued these activities made the property a "commercial enterprise" in a residential zone, even though the sanctuary caused no noise, traffic, or odor issues. Various writes, "Plaintiffs' attempts to analyze the discrete segments of a commercial use overlooks the fact Plaintiffs are still using the property in a commercial manner even if some of the negative impacts are not present." This rigid categorization ignores the reality of the situation: the sanctuary was helping the community, not harming it.

The author's critique of this legal logic is sharp. By focusing on the label of the activity rather than its consequences, the court validated a system where bureaucracy trumps common sense. The article points out that the sanctuary could host social parties but not charitable events, a distinction that seems entirely arbitrary to the average observer. This aligns with broader historical trends in single-family zoning, which has long been used to enforce social homogeneity rather than manage physical land use. The refusal of the appeals court to consider the lack of negative externalities suggests that the legal framework is fundamentally broken, prioritizing the purity of zoning categories over the actual well-being of the neighborhood.

The Flight of Families

The final section tackles a demographic shift that is reshaping American cities: the exodus of young families. Citing a new Manhattan Institute report, the author notes that the population of children under five in large urban counties declined by 7% between early 2020 and mid-2024. As Various puts it, "Families are uniquely sensitive to issues like public safety and school quality and are the backbone of functional, tightly knit communities. Losing families in large numbers is a sign that a city is failing to provide the fundamental building blocks of urban life." This statistic is alarming, particularly given that dense urban areas once had higher fertility rates than medium-density areas in the early 2000s.

The article identifies three root causes: rising childcare costs, declining school quality, and the lack of family-sized housing units. The proposed solution involves repealing zoning restrictions to allow for more three-bedroom units and expanding school choice programs. This argument is compelling because it links housing policy directly to demographic survival. However, a counterargument worth considering is that density itself may not be the enemy; rather, it is the type of density that matters. The piece acknowledges this, noting that while the densest areas globally have low fertility, American cities could likely permit more apartments without becoming "childless megacities." The real bottleneck, the author suggests, is often the hostility of local jurisdictions toward school choice, which prevents cities from leveraging their agglomeration advantages to offer diverse educational options.

"Families are uniquely sensitive to issues like public safety and school quality and are the backbone of functional, tightly knit communities. Losing families in large numbers is a sign that a city is failing to provide the fundamental building blocks of urban life."

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its ability to connect disparate issues—religious freedom, animal welfare, and demographic decline—under the single umbrella of restrictive zoning. It effectively demonstrates that land-use policy is not a neutral technicality but an active force shaping the moral and social fabric of communities. The biggest vulnerability lies in the political feasibility of the proposed reforms, as changing entrenched zoning laws often faces fierce local opposition. Readers should watch for how courts continue to apply strict scrutiny to religious land-use cases and whether state legislatures begin to preempt local zoning bans on family-sized housing.

Ohio Town Loses Civil Case Against Church's Overnight Ministry.

This past week, an Ohio judge dismissed a civil lawsuit brought by the fire chief of Bryon, Ohio, against a local church that had been letting people stay on its property during its overnight ministry.

Fire Chief Douglas Pool's suit argued that local church Dad's Place had converted its property to a residential use by allowing nightly stays without getting the proper zoning approvals or adopting all the fire safety measures required of residential properties.

His lawsuit demanded that Dad's Place stop its nighttime ministry until it installed a sprinkler system.

Dad's Place, and its pastor, Chris Avell, contended that the expense of installing a sprinkler system was cost-prohibitive for the church. The requirement to install one was thus an effective demand to shut down their nighttime ministry, which the church argued violated their Free Exercise rights.

Williams County Judge James D. Bates ruled that Pool's sprinkler-or-shutter demand was an unconstitutional restriction on Dad's Place's First Amendment rights to Free Exercise of religion.
