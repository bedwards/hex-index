Roundup #80: All AI, all the time

Noah Smith · Noahpinion ·Apr 5, 2026 ·15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Noah Smith cuts through the hype to reveal a startling disconnect: while experts agree AI capabilities will skyrocket by 2030, economists remain skeptical that this will translate into rapid economic growth. This roundup is essential listening because it moves beyond the binary of "AI will save us" or "AI will destroy us" to examine the specific, gritty frictions—energy constraints, demographic decline, and the nature of human desire—that could stall a technological revolution.

The Growth Paradox

Smith begins by dissecting a new study from the Forecasting Research Institute, which surveyed economists, AI experts, superforecasters, and the general public. The data reveals a consensus on capability but a fracture on consequence. "All the groups have about the same forecasts for AI capabilities by 2030," Smith notes, describing a future where AI can write high-quality novels and manage semi-autonomous labs. Yet, when asked about the economic impact, the groups diverge sharply.

The core of the argument rests on why economists predict modest growth despite "near-godlike AI." Smith highlights their reasoning: "Some economists argued that AI productivity gains would not be evenly distributed across all sectors, particularly where human labor is a bottleneck." This echoes the historical "productivity paradox" seen with electrification and personal computers, where multi-decade lags occurred between invention and widespread economic impact. Smith suggests the delay isn't just about adoption speed but a fundamental shift in capital allocation. "Part of this delay is attributed to a shift in capital away from labor and toward compute, data centers, APIs, and so on, which would not manifest as an increase in GDP until productivity improvements set in."

"Basically, none of these groups thinks that any amount of AI capabilities will enable economic take-off."

Smith pushes further, suggesting the hesitation might stem from something more philosophical than logistical. He posits that "people suspect that humanity is getting satisfied, at least in the developed countries, and that the amount of new valuable things that even a godlike AI could create for us is limited by our inability to desire more goods and services." This is a provocative angle, implying that the bottleneck isn't supply but demand. Critics might note that this underestimates the potential for AI to create entirely new categories of value we cannot currently conceive, much like the internet did in the 1990s.

The Biosecurity Gamble

Shifting to existential risk, Smith confronts the terrifying possibility of AI-enabled bioterrorism. He expresses deep concern that "a nihilistic, depressed teenager could tell a jailbroken version of Claude Code to make him a doomsday virus, and that the AI would actually go and do it for him." He references recent capabilities where researchers used AI to design functional viruses, noting, "That's an empowered world, but a terrifying one as well."

Smith engages with a counter-perspective from biosecurity expert Abhishaike Mahajan, who argues that creating an effective bioweapon is inherently difficult due to biological unknowns. Smith is unconvinced by the idea that trial and error is too slow. "Instead of just making one doomsday virus you can make 100 candidates and release them all," he writes. "Doomsday itself is the field experiment, and you can run a lot of experiments at once."

The author's fear is compounded by the likely lag in defensive measures. While vaccines and antivirals may improve, Smith argues, "I think it'll take a lot of time to deploy these countermeasures. A truly well-engineered doomsday virus will kill us long before we can distribute the cure." He also points out a critical policy failure: "It's likely that the U.S. will not proactively prepare for future pandemic threats, but merely react to them when they occur." This reactive stance leaves the executive branch and public health infrastructure dangerously exposed to asymmetric threats.

The Cybersecurity Cliff

The discussion then turns to the fragility of the financial system. Smith asks a chilling question: "How does the world know how much money you have?" He describes the modern financial infrastructure as a fortress of electronic records, now threatened by AI's accelerating offensive capabilities. Citing Lyptus Research, he notes that "offensive cyber capability has been doubling every 9.8 months since 2019," a pace that is now accelerating.

The stakes are existential for the digital economy. "If all records of personal wealth were erased in a cyberattack, what could banks or the government even do?" Smith wonders. The threat is compounded by breakthroughs in quantum computing, which threaten to break the encryption protecting these systems. Smith draws a historical parallel to the Manhattan Project, noting that recent quantum announcements remind him of "Frisch and Peierls, calculating how much U-235 was needed for a chain reaction in 1940."

"The entire modern world runs on cybersecurity — if there's a general failure in the methods we now use to keep information secure, all of society is in deep trouble."

While some experts argue that publishing these vulnerabilities forces necessary upgrades, Smith remains wary. The speed of AI-driven hacking suggests that the window to secure systems before they are compromised may be closing faster than institutions can adapt.

The End of Anonymity and the Rent-Seeking Trap

Smith also explores the social and economic distortions AI could cause. He predicts the "impending end of pseudonymity," arguing that large language models can now deanonymize users with high precision. "Soon, anyone who disagrees with your pseudonymous alt account... will be able to sic an LLM on your account and dox it," he warns. This loss of privacy could stifle honest discourse and eliminate a crucial "social and psychological safety valve" for people in restrictive societies.

Finally, Smith turns to the financial sector, questioning whether AI will be used for productive innovation or "rent-seeking." He references Charles Stross's Accelerando, where AI quants consume the solar system's resources for trading, and connects it to the economic theory of Hirshleifer (1971). "A lot of society's resources — compute, electricity, and so on — will be going to waste," Smith argues, if AI agents simply compete to beat each other to market by nanoseconds. This mirrors the "socialist economics" debate on resource allocation, where immense computational power is diverted from solving human problems to zero-sum financial games.

Bottom Line

Smith's most compelling insight is the disconnect between technological capability and economic reality; the world may build godlike AI only to find it stalled by energy limits, human satisfaction, or bureaucratic inertia. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on expert consensus, which may underestimate the disruptive potential of a paradigm shift that defies historical analogies. Readers should watch closely for how the administration and private sector balance the rush for AI supremacy against the escalating risks of biosecurity and cyber collapse.
