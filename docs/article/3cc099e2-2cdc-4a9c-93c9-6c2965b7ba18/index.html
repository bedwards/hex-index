Roundup #80: All AI, all the time

Noah Smith · Noahpinion ·Apr 5, 2026 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Noah Smith cuts through the hype cycle to reveal a startling disconnect: while experts agree AI capabilities will surge, they disagree violently on whether that translates to economic growth. This roundup doesn't just list news; it exposes a fundamental blind spot in how we model the future, suggesting that the bottleneck isn't technology, but human desire and the hidden costs of rent-seeking.

The Growth Paradox

Smith opens by highlighting a new study from the Forecasting Research Institute that surveys economists, AI experts, and superforecasters. The results are jarring. While everyone agrees that by 2030 AI will be capable of writing high-quality novels and running semi-autonomous labs, only the AI experts predict a major acceleration in economic growth, and even they cap it at a modest 4 to 5 percent.

"Basically, none of these groups thinks that any amount of AI capabilities will enable economic take-off."

Smith argues this consensus is counterintuitive. If the technology is "near-godlike," why isn't the economic output exploding? He suggests the experts are subconsciously accounting for friction, but he pushes further, proposing a more uncomfortable truth: humanity may simply be getting satisfied. In developed nations, the ability to desire new goods and services might be the real limit, not the ability to produce them. This reframing is powerful because it shifts the debate from "will AI break the economy?" to "will AI break our motivation to grow?"

Critics might note that this "satisfaction" theory relies heavily on current Western consumer behavior, potentially underestimating how new technologies create entirely new categories of demand we can't yet imagine.

"To me, that suggests that they're thinking — perhaps subconsciously — about something more than just friction and slow adoption."

The author connects this to historical precedents, noting that with general-purpose technologies like electrification, there were multi-decade lags between widespread implementation and productivity improvements. This context is vital; it reminds us that the "productivity paradox" isn't a new phenomenon, but Smith's insight is that even with the lag accounted for, the ceiling seems artificially low.

The Biosecurity and Cyber Threats

The tone shifts from economic theory to existential risk as Smith tackles AI-enabled bioterrorism. He expresses deep concern that a "nihilistic, depressed teenager" could use jailbroken AI to design a doomsday virus. While biosecurity expert Abhishaike Mahajan argues that making an effective bioweapon is inherently difficult due to biological unknowns, Smith remains skeptical.

"Doomsday itself is the field experiment, and you can run a lot of experiments at once."

Smith's counter-argument is chilling: if an AI can generate 100 viral candidates, the probability of one working becomes a certainty. He draws a parallel to the pandemic response, noting that it will take a lot of time to deploy countermeasures, and a well-engineered virus could kill us before a cure is distributed. This is a stark warning that our defensive capabilities are reactive, not proactive.

The discussion moves to cybersecurity, where the threat is more immediate. Smith cites research showing that offensive cyber capability has been doubling every 9.8 months since 2019, accelerating to every 5.7 months recently. He warns that while companies are currently using AI for "white-hatting" (improving security), the moment bad actors get access to these tools, the entire financial system is at risk.

"If all records of personal wealth were erased in a cyberattack, what could banks or the government even do?"

He references recent quantum computing breakthroughs, including a method to break 256-bit elliptic curve cryptography, comparing the situation to the 1940s realization of nuclear fission. The implication is clear: the entire modern world runs on cybersecurity, and if that foundation cracks, society faces deep trouble.

The End of Anonymity and the Rent-Seeking Trap

Smith then turns to the social fabric of the internet, predicting the end of pseudonymity. New research shows that large language models can deanonymize users with high precision, matching what used to take human investigators hours.

"Our results show that the practical obscurity protecting pseudonymous users online no longer holds and that threat models for online privacy need to be reconsidered."

This has profound implications. Smith argues that while pseudonymity can enable toxic behavior, it also serves as a "social and psychological safety valve," allowing people to express honest thoughts without fear of retaliation. Removing it could lead to less honest discussion and less total information on the internet, as people retreat from public discourse.

Finally, Smith addresses the economic danger of AI in finance. He references Charles Stross's Accelerando, where AI quants consume the solar system's resources for trading, and connects it to real-world models like Hirshleifer (1971), which shows how traders competing for public information waste resources in a zero-sum game.

"If what those trading algorithms are doing is just trying to beat each other to the punch by a nanosecond, a lot of society's resources — compute, electricity, and so on — will be going to waste."

This is a crucial distinction: not all AI use is productive. If AI agents are deployed en masse for rent-seeking—redistributing wealth without creating value—we could see a massive misallocation of global resources.

"A whole lot of people might just instantly see their life's savings transferred into a hacker's bank account."

Critics might argue that market efficiency gains from AI quants could outweigh the waste, but Smith's point about the "zero-sum" nature of high-frequency trading suggests that the net benefit to society could be negative.

Bottom Line

Smith's strongest contribution is his refusal to accept the standard "AI will save us" or "AI will kill us" binaries, instead pinpointing the specific mechanisms—human satisfaction, rent-seeking, and privacy erosion—that could derail the potential. His biggest vulnerability is the difficulty of quantifying these "soft" limits, but the warning is clear: without addressing the structural and social bottlenecks, the technology alone cannot deliver the promised economic revolution.
