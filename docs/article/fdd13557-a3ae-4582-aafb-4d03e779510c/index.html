Looking back at 2025

In a media landscape obsessed with viral metrics and algorithmic churn, Sarah Bessey offers a startling counter-narrative: that slow, earnest, text-heavy writing can not only survive but thrive without compromising its soul. While most digital strategists preach the gospel of constant novelty, Bessey presents evidence that deep loyalty is built on consistency, vulnerability, and a refusal to chase the 'hot take.' This is not merely a year-end recap; it is a case study in how to build a sustainable community in an era of digital exhaustion.

The Quiet Rebellion of Steady Growth

Bessey opens by dismantling the conventional wisdom of audience building. She notes that her newsletter, Field Notes, has grown to nearly four million views over eleven years, yet she rarely runs sales or chases viral moments. "Everything about Field Notes defies conventional wisdom for building a community or audience as a writer," she writes, pointing out that her growth comes almost entirely from word-of-mouth referrals rather than aggressive marketing. This is a powerful rebuttal to the idea that modern attention spans are too short for long-form thought.

The author attributes this success to a specific kind of trust. "I never take it for granted when you trust me with your people," she admits, highlighting a retention rate that far outpaces industry averages. By framing her readership not as a metric to be harvested but as a community to be nurtured, Bessey shifts the power dynamic. She argues that the "quiet space" she cultivates is a feature, not a bug. "Apparently not everyone loves text-heavy, wordy, earnest essays with footnotes? imagine!" she quips, turning a potential weakness into a badge of honor.

Critics might argue that this model is unsustainable for writers without other income streams or that it relies too heavily on the author's personal brand. However, Bessey's data suggests that the market for thoughtful, non-sensational content is larger and more loyal than the algorithms suggest.

The Economics of Integrity

Moving beyond content, Bessey addresses the financial model of her work with a transparency that is rare in the subscription economy. She reveals that the newsletter has given away over 2,000 paid subscriptions to those who cannot afford them, a practice that has paradoxically strengthened the community rather than weakened it. "Not one paid subscriber complains about the fact that we do give away subscriptions to every one who asks," she observes. Instead, many offer to pay more to support the initiative.

This approach challenges the standard subscription playbook, which often relies on scarcity and exclusivity. Bessey's strategy is rooted in a belief that access to spiritual and intellectual resources should not be gated by wealth. She promises to keep her rates affordable, noting that her yearly fee is "basically more than half the year free" compared to typical rates. This is not charity; it is a strategic investment in the health of the ecosystem she has built. By refusing to raise prices in 2026, she signals that the relationship with her readers is more valuable than short-term revenue maximization.

The Content That Resonates

When Bessey analyzes her most popular pieces, a clear pattern emerges: her audience craves honesty about the friction between faith and modern life. The top-performing post, "Are We Still Calling Ourselves Christians? Or Are We Done Here?" attracted nearly a million visits. Bessey explains that this piece resonated because it addressed the growing disillusionment with the label "Christian" when it has become "synonymous with harm." She validates the reader's struggle, writing, "Basically, if you are wondering how we can still claim the name of Christian when it has become synonymous with harm, well, suffice to say, you're not alone."

Similarly, her piece on aging, titled "Get bangs or get Botox, girl," sparked a robust conversation by rejecting the pressure to curate a perfect image. She writes about the response to her natural face and grey hair, turning a personal moment into a collective reckoning with the culture of youth. Another favorite, "Or, The Narrow Road," helped her articulate her own "work and witness at this moment in time." These posts succeed because they do not offer easy answers; they offer companionship in the confusion.

Anger is our holy starting point, but it is Love who sustains the passion and directs it into life-giving transformation.

Bessey also highlights quieter pieces that held deep personal meaning, such as her reflections on anger and the idea that "God is staying put with us, too." These selections reinforce her thesis that the most profound connections are often found in the mundane and the messy, not just in the triumphant.

Bottom Line

Sarah Bessey's year-end reflection is a masterclass in sustainable community building, proving that integrity and engagement are not mutually exclusive. The strongest part of her argument is the data: a loyal, growing audience that values depth over speed. Her biggest vulnerability lies in the scalability of her model, which relies heavily on her personal capacity and the unique trust she has cultivated over a decade. For readers seeking a refuge from the noise of the digital age, this piece offers a compelling blueprint for how to stay human in a machine-driven world.
