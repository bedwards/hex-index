The great US - China battery battle of 2023

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Apr 16, 2023 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace doesn't just track battery news; he tracks the moment hype collides with reality, and in early 2023, that collision happened with a deafening crash of three simultaneous breakthroughs. While the industry often drowns in promises of "game-changing" tech that never materializes, Borlace argues that this time, the shift from lithium dependence is already happening on the road, not just in the lab. He frames the narrative not as a distant future of electric vehicles, but as an immediate disruption driven by Chinese manufacturing scale and a surprising American contender in the aerospace sector.

The Sodium Surprise

Borlace begins by admitting a past miscalculation, noting that his prediction of sodium-ion batteries arriving "within the next few years" was technically correct, just premature. "It turns out that EVs using sodium ion batteries won't start hitting the markets in a few years time after all because they've already arrived right now in early 2023," he writes. This admission serves as a powerful hook, grounding his analysis in humility before pivoting to the hard data. The core of his argument rests on the sheer abundance of sodium compared to lithium. He explains that while lithium reserves are concentrated in a few volatile regions, sodium is a constituent of salt and is "pretty easy to get hold of," being one of the six most abundant elements on the planet.

The economic implications are stark. Borlace highlights that the president of Hina Battery Technologies believes sodium-ion cells could "slash the overall production cost of an entire electric vehicle by about 10 percent." This is not a marginal gain; it is a structural change that could make electric mobility accessible to the mass market, particularly for small urban vehicles where weight and energy density are less critical than in long-haul transport. He points to the JAC group's collaboration with Volkswagen and BYD's new Seagull model as proof of concept. These vehicles offer ranges of 150 to 190 miles, which Borlace argues is perfectly adequate for daily commutes, noting that "not everyone wants a huge electric SUV" that blocks views and requires two parking spaces.

Replacing lithium-ion with sodium could free up the more energy dense lithium technologies for applications where their high density is really necessary.

Critics might note that the lower energy density of sodium-ion batteries (140 watt-hours per kilogram versus the 255+ of advanced lithium cells) remains a significant barrier for long-distance travel and heavy-duty transport. However, Borlace effectively counters this by reframing the problem: for stationary grid storage or city scooters, weight is irrelevant, and the cost savings are the primary metric. He suggests that the industry is finally segmenting its battery needs rather than forcing a single technology to do everything.

The Thermal Breakthrough

Moving beyond chemistry, Borlace examines how engineering architecture can squeeze more performance out of existing lithium-ion technology. He focuses on CATL's "Chilin" battery, which utilizes a vertical stacking method with integrated cooling plates. "They reckon they can get zero to eighty percent charge into one of these things in just 10 minutes," Borlace writes, emphasizing the practical impact of thermal management. This is a crucial distinction; the breakthrough isn't necessarily a new chemical element, but a new way of managing heat that allows for faster charging without the risk of thermal runaway.

The efficiency gains are substantial. Borlace notes that this architecture has increased volume efficiency by 72 percent, translating to a gravimetric energy density of 255 watt-hours per kilogram for standard cells. This development addresses the "range anxiety" that still plagues many potential EV buyers. By stacking cells vertically and separating them with coolant, CATL has solved a problem that has long limited charging speeds. The result is a battery that can support a thousand-kilometer range in a vehicle like the Zeekr 001, effectively neutralizing one of the last major arguments against electric adoption.

The Silicon Anode Gambit

While the first two developments are rooted in Chinese manufacturing dominance, Borlace pivots to a distinctly American innovation from Amprius Technologies. He describes their lithium-ion pouch cell, which replaces the traditional graphite anode with a 100 percent silicon anode. Silicon has the highest capacity for storing lithium, but it historically suffers from swelling and cracking as it expands. "After more than a decade of development Amprius say they've overcome this challenge via a unique anode configuration," Borlace explains. Their solution involves a web of pure silicon nanowires that allow lithium to enter without wrecking the structure.

The performance metrics are staggering. Borlace cites an independently verified cell density of "no less than 500 watt hours per kilogram," which is more than twice the density of a Tesla 4680 cell. While he acknowledges that this technology is currently targeted at the aeronautics industry, with clients like Airbus and the US Army, the implications for the automotive sector are undeniable. He notes that Amprius is building a massive facility in Colorado with a five-gigawatt-hour capacity coming online in 2025, signaling a serious commitment to scaling this high-density tech.

These are precisely the kinds of scientific and engineering developments that are going on all over the world right now and which should be giving us average citizens a little bit of hope.

A counterargument worth considering is the cost of such advanced silicon technology. While the density is unmatched, the manufacturing complexity of nanowire arrays suggests these batteries will remain prohibitively expensive for mass-market cars for some time. Borlace hints at this by noting the "big" price tag and the current focus on aviation, but he correctly identifies that once the technology matures, the spillover effect into consumer vehicles could be transformative.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage succeeds because it moves past the binary of "lithium is dead" versus "lithium is forever," instead presenting a nuanced landscape where multiple battery chemistries will coexist to serve different needs. His strongest argument is the immediate reality of sodium-ion vehicles hitting the market, proving that the transition to affordable electric transport is already underway. The biggest vulnerability in his optimistic outlook is the timeline for scaling high-cost, high-performance technologies like silicon anodes, which may remain niche for years. Readers should watch for how quickly the cost of these advanced cells drops, as that will determine whether the "game-changing" potential becomes a daily reality or remains an aviation luxury.
