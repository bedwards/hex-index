America's Most Infamous Filmmaker

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Mar 5, 2026 · 53 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Tom van der Linden reframes Harmony Korine not as a provocateur of shock, but as a "last foot soldier" in cinema's infancy, arguing that his boredom with traditional storytelling was actually a radical catalyst for expanding the medium. This piece distinguishes itself by moving past the easy dismissal of Korine's work as mere "freak show" material to reveal a profound, if unsettling, humanism hidden within the absurdity. For the busy listener, the value lies in understanding how an artist's rejection of plot became the very mechanism for capturing a truth that Hollywood's polished narratives consistently miss.

The Rejection of the Traditional Script

The article opens with a pivotal moment from 1997, when a young Korine debuted his first feature at the Telluride Film Festival to critical disdain, yet earned the admiration of Werner Herzog. van der Linden captures the gravity of this endorsement through Herzog's own words: "I've just seen your movie. It is the most audacious debut." This early validation set the stage for a career defined by a deliberate refusal to follow the rules. The author notes that Korine viewed the film industry with a distinct weariness, stating, "I get nothing from them. I'm just bored. There's nothing. It's like the film. You see the screen and it's dead."

This boredom is the central thesis of the commentary: it was not apathy, but a driving force. van der Linden argues that Korine's dismissal of conventional cinema forced him to invent new forms of expression. The author writes, "An artist whose boredom with the medium was actually a catalyst to push the boundaries of storytelling, to find radical images and to expand cinematic language itself." This is a compelling reframing. It suggests that the "bad" filmmaking critics often cite is actually a calculated deconstruction of a language that had become stagnant.

An artist whose boredom with the medium was actually a catalyst to push the boundaries of storytelling, to find radical images and to expand cinematic language itself.

The piece then traces Korine's origins, noting his collaboration with photographer Larry Clark on the 1995 film Kids. While Kids was a commercial success and a cultural touchstone, van der Linden points out that Korine viewed it as a "commissioned work" rather than his true artistic voice. He writes, "Kids was never a story that I was destined to write or I was born to write. For me, kids is more of a commissioned work because kids is very traditional and that's what he wanted." This distinction is crucial. It separates Korine from the "grindhouse" label often slapped on his early associates, positioning him instead as a director who quickly outgrew the traditional narrative structures of his debut.

The Radical Honesty of Gummo

The commentary shifts to Korine's 1997 debut feature, Gummo, describing it as a "disjointed series of vignettes" that rejects linear plots in favor of raw imagery. van der Linden highlights Korine's own philosophy on this approach: "I never remember plots. I can't stand plots. I don't feel like there's life has any plots. What I remember from films is are scenes and characters." This rejection of plot is the film's greatest strength and its most divisive element. By stripping away the safety net of a three-act structure, Korine forces the audience to confront the "ragged reality" of his subjects without moral guidance.

The author addresses the criticism that Gummo is exploitative, citing film critic Natalie Keegan's observation that the film serves as a "disruptive reminder that human beings are often not so noble." van der Linden argues that the critics' discomfort stems from their own prejudices, noting that they dismissed the film as a "glorified freak show" while failing to see the humanity within the chaos. This is a strong defense, though critics might note that the line between humanizing the marginalized and exploiting their misery is perilously thin, and Korine's methods often walk right that edge.

The piece also draws a fascinating parallel between Gummo and the reality TV show Cops. Korine admired Cops not for its moral judgment, but for its "naked exposition" of private lives. van der Linden writes, "It felt really honest to me." This connection adds necessary context to Korine's aesthetic, grounding his "avant-garde" style in a very specific, American pop-culture obsession with the unvarnished truth of the lower class. The author suggests that the film acts as an invitation to "take a huff of their ragged reality," a metaphor that perfectly encapsulates the film's immersive, almost suffocating atmosphere.

You can really tell critics have come around on this movie. It asks us not to offer sanctimonious disdain or pity, but rather to glimpse the turmoil of their rich interiority.

The commentary emphasizes that the "truth" in Gummo comes from its form as much as its content. Many of the eccentric characters were real people Korine knew from Nashville, blurring the line between actor and subject. The author notes that Korine would ask parents for permission to film their children, only to be met with a casual "Fuck, dude. I don't give a [__]." This anecdote reinforces the film's authenticity; the chaos on screen was a reflection of the world Korine inhabited, not a constructed fiction.

The Distorted Mirror of Julien Donkey-Boy

The article moves to Korine's 1999 film Julien Donkey-Boy, describing it as a film that "does for mental illness what Gummo did for class." The author highlights the technical innovation of the film, which was shot on video and blown up to 35mm to create a "heavily grained image" that mirrors the protagonist's distorted inner life. van der Linden explains that this choice was less about aesthetics and more about the freedom it afforded the camera: "The thing that excites me about video isn't really the aesthetic of it. It's more what it allows for, the freedom."

This section underscores Korine's commitment to "radical honesty." The character of Julien was based on Korine's uncle, and the director wanted to avoid the "cute, lovable, eccentric" stereotype often assigned to the mentally ill in cinema. Instead, he sought to show "a guy with blood in his underwear hitting himself in the head." The author argues that this unromanticized portrayal is a form of deep respect, forcing the audience to confront the horror of the condition without the buffer of sentimentality.

The result is a movie that definitely isn't for everyone. In fact, as the renowned film critic Roger Ebert said in his review, Julien Donkey-Boy seems hardly made for anybody.

The commentary concludes this section by noting that despite the difficulty of the viewing experience, the film succeeds in its goal of revealing a hidden humanity. The author suggests that Korine's work is a testament to the idea that cinema is still in its infancy, capable of articulating experiences that traditional storytelling cannot reach. This is a powerful closing argument, positioning Korine not as a fringe figure, but as a pioneer at the very frontier of the art form.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden successfully argues that Harmony Korine's "boredom" with cinema is a sophisticated artistic strategy designed to strip away the artificiality of traditional storytelling. The piece's greatest strength is its ability to reframe the "exploitative" label as a necessary byproduct of a radical commitment to truth. However, the argument occasionally glosses over the ethical complexities of Korine's methods, leaving the reader to wonder if the "humanity" found in the chaos is genuine or merely a byproduct of the director's gaze. Ultimately, this commentary provides a vital lens for understanding an artist who challenges the very definition of what a movie can be.
