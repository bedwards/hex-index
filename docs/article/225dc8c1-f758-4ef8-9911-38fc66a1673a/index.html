China's Geography Problem

Sam Denby · Wendover Productions ·Oct 10, 2017 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sam Denby reframes China not as an unstoppable economic juggernaut, but as a nation held hostage by its own topography. While most analysis focuses on GDP growth or trade deficits, Denby argues that the very geography that allowed China to feed 1.4 billion people now leaves it strategically exposed to blockades and border conflicts. This is a crucial pivot for anyone trying to understand the limits of Beijing's power: the country is so large it can no longer be self-sufficient, yet its borders are so porous that its survival depends on maintaining a delicate, often hostile, regional balance.

The Agricultural Paradox

The piece opens by dismantling the myth of Chinese invincibility, grounding the argument in the agricultural reality of the Yellow River. Denby writes, "The flood plain of the Yellow River is some of the best agricultural land in the world," explaining how the region's ability to support double cropping of rice allows for a population density that wheat-dependent Europe simply cannot match. This is a compelling foundation because it shifts the conversation from abstract military might to the hard math of calories per acre. Without this specific agricultural advantage, the demographic explosion that defines modern China would have been impossible.

However, this abundance creates a paradox. The same land that supports the population also makes the country a target. Denby notes that "Europe mostly relies on wheat to feed its population which only outputs 4 million calories of food per acre of farmland," whereas rice produces nearly three times that. This efficiency is a double-edged sword; it sustains a massive population but creates a dependency on a specific, vulnerable geography. Critics might argue that modern logistics and global trade have rendered local agricultural yields less critical, but Denby's point stands: in a prolonged conflict or blockade, the ability to grow food locally becomes the ultimate strategic asset.

The Southern Jungle and the Tibetan Buffer

Moving to the borders, Denby highlights a often-overlooked vulnerability in the south. He points out that the borders with Vietnam, Laos, and Myanmar "seem almost arbitrarily set because they sort of were," established through wars rather than natural barriers. This lack of geographical protection is dangerous because, as Denby puts it, "Vietnam, Laos and Myanmar are all jungle countries, one of the most difficult environments for warfare." While China possesses technological superiority, the terrain neutralizes much of that advantage, making any invasion or counter-invasion a logistical nightmare.

This leads to the strategic necessity of Tibet. Denby argues that Tibet is not culturally or ethnically Chinese, but "it serves a purpose" as a massive, uninhabited buffer zone. He writes, "If China didn't rule Tibet then India would maybe not formally but there's little chance that an independent Tibet would not be economically and culturally dominated by either India or China." The logic here is stark: Tibet acts as a shield, pushing the border to the Himalayas and denying India the infrastructure needed to project power into the Chinese heartland. Yet, Denby acknowledges the fragility of this control, noting that the government is trying to "change who lives in Tibet" by moving in Han Chinese to ensure loyalty, a move that risks turning the population against Beijing if a foreign power ever arrives.

China is a country both blessed and cursed by geography: on one hand its land has allowed for the country to grow to almost 1.4 billion people, but on the other hand it really doesn't have great geographical protection.

The Northern Shield and the Eastern Trap

In contrast to the southern jungles, Denby describes the northern border with Mongolia and Russia as a natural fortress. He notes that Mongolia is "an enormous sparsely populated friendly country with the Gobi Desert," making it nearly impossible for a modern army to cross without massive supply lines. This stability, however, is contingent on the alliance with Russia, which Denby warns could sour. "China need not worry about its northern border until relations with Russia sour," he writes, a reminder that even the safest borders rely on political whims.

The real danger, according to Denby, lies to the east. Despite the ocean appearing to be a natural moat, it is actually a trap. The United States maintains a significant military presence in the Pacific, with bases in Japan, South Korea, and Guam. Denby warns that in a serious dispute, the US could "blockade China and cutting off its maritime access." This is the crux of China's modern dilemma: its economy relies on exports, and its food security relies on imports, both of which require open sea lanes. "Without jobs or food there's a good chance the population would rise up against the government and end the current regime," Denby asserts, highlighting the existential threat of a naval blockade.

The Neocolonial Pivot

To mitigate this vulnerability, China has turned its gaze toward Africa, a move Denby describes with a mix of economic analysis and geopolitical caution. He observes that "Africa has emerged almost as China's China," serving as a source for minerals, oil, and food to bypass the Pacific blockade. However, this strategy introduces new risks. "China now imports more food and oil than it exports," creating a dependency on foreign powers that may not be friendly. This reliance on distant resources, while a clever workaround for the Pacific blockade, essentially trades one vulnerability for another, stretching China's logistical reach across the globe.

Critics might note that Denby's focus on military geography underestimates the resilience of China's domestic political system and its ability to adapt economically. The assumption that a blockade would inevitably lead to regime collapse ignores the potential for rationing, internal substitution, or diplomatic maneuvering. Nevertheless, the geographical constraints he outlines are undeniable physical realities that no amount of political will can easily erase.

Bottom Line

Denby's strongest contribution is the reframing of China's rise not as a linear path to dominance, but as a precarious balancing act dictated by topography. His argument that China's greatest weakness is its inability to be self-sufficient while remaining surrounded by potential adversaries is both logical and unsettling. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its deterministic view of geography; while terrain sets the stage, it does not write the script, and China's recent infrastructure projects in Tibet and the South China Sea are direct attempts to rewrite those geographical rules. Readers should watch to see if China can successfully engineer its way out of these natural constraints or if the map will ultimately dictate the limits of its power.
