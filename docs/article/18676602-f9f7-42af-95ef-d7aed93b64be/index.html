A Fragmenting Order, Seen from Beijing

The February 2026 edition of Sinification's monthly digest for Sinocism lands at a moment of extraordinary geopolitical rupture. The joint U.S.-Israeli strikes that killed Iran's Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei on February 28 sent shockwaves through Chinese policy circles, but as editor Jacob Mardell notes, the digest was largely compiled before those events unfolded. What emerges is a portrait of China's strategic establishment wrestling with a question that refuses to resolve: what kind of global order is being born, and where does Beijing fit in it?

The range of voices is striking. Hawks and moderates, heterodox economists and establishment scholars, all jostle within the same pages. The consensus, such as it is, holds that American hegemony is in terminal decline. The disagreement is over everything else.

The Post-Free-Trade Architecture

Former MOFTEC official Ma Xiaoye offers one of the most concrete proposals in the entire digest, arguing that the coming era will be defined by "managed trade" rather than free trade. In Ma's vision, framework agreements will set target volumes and values by product category, replacing the liberal order with something more explicitly negotiated. The logic is blunt: the United States, now a thirty-nine-trillion-dollar debtor nation pursuing reindustrialization, has reversed the economic logic that built the postwar order.

Middle powers like Canada and the EU are racing to strike managed trade deals with China ahead of a US-China framework agreement -- securing a seat at the table before the two largest trading partners set the rules without them.

The three-way discussion among Ding Yifan, Jin Canrong, and Di Dongsheng pushes further. They characterize the emerging system not as "China + 1" but as "World - 1," with the United States retreating from its leadership role. Their framing of China as having been tempered into an "indestructible body" by years of American pressure veers into self-congratulation, but their acknowledgment of China's shortcomings is more interesting.

China cannot simply take the baton from America or it will repeat the same structural mistakes; it must build a fairer system of "common finance" in which both surplus and deficit countries bear adjustment responsibility, addressing international concerns -- acknowledged as legitimate -- that China is "walking its own road yet leaving no road for others."

That last phrase is a rare admission from establishment voices that China's trade surpluses generate legitimate grievances abroad. Whether Beijing's policy apparatus is prepared to act on such admissions remains an open question.

The Japan Alarm

Sanae Takaichi's landslide re-election has clearly unnerved Chinese analysts. Li Yongjing frames Japan not as a country with a right wing but as a "right-wing nation-state," arguing that right-wing thought, anchored in Emperor-system nationalism, has constituted Japan's defining political character since the Meiji era. Zheng Yongnian goes further, claiming that the three historical constraints on Japanese militarization -- constitutional limits, American restraint, and domestic pacifism -- have all collapsed simultaneously.

Japanese society's deep conformism makes the far right's capture of the state apparatus far more likely than in Europe, where citizens openly resist.

The most provocative proposals come from Zheng and Shen Yi, both of whom suggest China should reopen the question of Ryukyu's sovereignty status. Shen Yi additionally calls for activating the United Nations Charter's "enemy state clauses" against Japan. These suggestions read less like serious policy proposals than rhetorical escalation designed to signal that Beijing has options beyond diplomatic protest.

A genuine point of tension lies in the Taiwan-Japan nexus. Jin Canrong argues explicitly that China should resolve the Taiwan issue before Japan completes its military modernization. Zhang Wenmu agrees the window is closing but adds a dialectical twist:

The conditions for reunification with Taiwan are at their most favourable and most mature -- but by dialectical logic, peak maturity also means peak fragility, and the window is fleeting.

Liu Guoshen offers the lone dissenting voice on urgency, counseling patience and arguing that the sovereignty debate is already in "garbage time" -- that structural gravity will do the work if Beijing maintains composure. It is a strikingly different posture from Jin and Zhang's alarm, and the digest offers no resolution between these incompatible assessments.

Khamenei's Killing and Religious War 2.0

The Iran commentary is the most immediately charged section. Jin Liangxiang describes the U.S.-Israeli nuclear negotiations as a calculated "trap" designed to provide pretext for military action, while noting pragmatically that China's strategic oil reserves are sufficient to weather short-term disruption. Niu Xinchun frames the broader U.S.-Iran standoff as structurally irresolvable:

For the Iranian regime, softening its ideological stance to accommodate Washington poses a greater existential threat than confrontation itself, rendering the US-Iran diplomatic impasse "irresolvable" except through regime change.

Zheng Yongnian's post-strike analysis is the most sweeping, casting Khamenei's assassination as the opening of a "Religious War 2.0" and warning that the rules-based international order is being replaced by a "fear-based jungle world" governed by raw power. His observation that Chinese analysts, as products of a secular civilization, have underestimated the religious dimension of Trump's foreign policy is unusually self-critical for this corpus.

If Japan similarly leverages US power to pursue its own objectives, how is China to respond?

That question -- drawing a direct line from Israel's role as America's Middle Eastern pivot to Japan's potential role in East Asia -- is the sharpest strategic insight in the entire digest.

Europe Adrift, Latin America Under Pressure

The Europe section is the least surprising. Zhou Bo observes that European leaders are visiting Beijing en masse with a pragmatic trade focus, having abandoned their campaign to pressure China over the Ukraine war. Zhang Jian calls for Europe to abandon NATO outright. These are familiar Chinese talking points, though Jiang Feng's observation that at Munich, America was "no longer setting the agenda but was itself discussed as a problem" captures a genuine shift in transatlantic dynamics.

On Latin America, the tone is markedly more aggressive. Sun Yanfeng threatens to inflict "serious damage" on Panama through trade restrictions over the CK Hutchison port license cancellations. Tian Feilong goes further still:

China must maintain an "escalatory" posture over Panama's voiding of CK Hutchison's canal contract -- including secondary sanctions on American overseas interests, and potentially postponing Trump's planned visit to China.

This is notable for its willingness to recommend that Beijing weaponize a head-of-state visit as leverage in a commercial dispute. Whether such counsel influences actual policy or simply reflects academic posturing is, as always with these digests, difficult to determine.

The Economic Heterodoxies

The economic sections contain the most genuinely provocative arguments. Li Xunlei challenges the consensus that capital account liberalization would crash the renminbi, arguing the currency is undervalued by roughly half. His metaphor is vivid: the RMB remains a "local grain coupon" rather than a "national grain coupon," with 76.5 percent of offshore payments staying in Hong Kong. Miao Yanliang of CICC supports this view, noting that foreign holdings of Chinese government bonds account for just 5.9 percent of the market, compared with 31.5 percent for U.S. Treasuries.

Against these liberalizers, Xia Bin insists China remains a "financially weak nation" that must prioritize macroeconomic control. Zhang Chun offers the most pragmatic forecast: full onshore opening will not occur for at least twenty years, and the goal should not be to replace the dollar but to become the leading second-tier currency at roughly 20 percent global share within five to ten years.

On property, Zhao Yanjing's argument is the most provocative in the entire digest:

"Without these bubbles there would have been no success of reform and opening up, still less any so-called China miracle."

Zhao frames land revenue not as fiscal revenue but as financial capital -- equivalent to a city issuing equity in itself. Suppressing house prices, in this telling, is equivalent to crashing a company's share price to reduce reliance on IPOs. It is a clever reframing, though one that conveniently absolves local governments of responsibility for speculative excess. Sheng Songcheng's more orthodox response -- stabilize real estate within one to two years -- suggests the establishment view remains considerably more cautious than Zhao's heterodoxy.

AI and the Education Pressure Cooker

Cai Fang warns of a deepening "reverse-Kuznets process" in which AI pushes labor from high-productivity to lower-productivity sectors, widening income gaps in the near term. Zhang Dandan and Li Jia call for a data-driven early-warning system, which sounds sensible but raises the question of whether China's bureaucratic apparatus can actually respond with the speed such a system would demand.

The debate over abolishing the High School Entrance Exam exposes a fundamental tension in Chinese education policy. Yao Yang frames the exam as a relic of a utilitarian development model rooted in Legalist tradition, arguing that the current system "ruins rather than cultivates students." Chen Zhiwen counters that these exams function primarily as instruments of social governance:

Transitioning towards project-based evaluations or more subjective assessments would likely create additional competitive "tracks" more susceptible to elite manipulation, undermining the institutional foundations of equity and social justice without meaningfully reducing competition.

Chen's argument -- that imperfect but transparent metrics may be preferable to subjective alternatives in a society where connections matter enormously -- is compelling and underappreciated in Western discussions of Chinese education reform.

Bottom Line

This Sinification digest captures a Chinese policy establishment that is simultaneously confident and anxious. Confident that American hegemony is collapsing, that China's industrial strength is unmatched, and that history is bending in Beijing's direction. Anxious that the windows for action -- on Taiwan, on trade architecture, on financial reform -- may close faster than Chinese institutions can move. The killing of Khamenei adds a new variable that few of these analysts had fully reckoned with: a world in which the United States demonstrates its willingness to assassinate a head of state and face no meaningful consequences. Whether Beijing reads this as a cautionary tale about the costs of confrontation or as evidence that rules-based order is already dead will shape Chinese foreign policy for years to come. The most honest line in the entire compilation belongs to Ding Yifan, Jin Canrong, and Di Dongsheng, who concede that China's "psychological readiness for leadership has not yet caught up with its material strength." That gap -- between capability and confidence, between ambition and institutional capacity -- is the central tension running through every section of this remarkable document.
