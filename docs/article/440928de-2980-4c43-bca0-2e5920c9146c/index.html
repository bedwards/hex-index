Judicial notice (01.11.26): Who’s your lawyer?

David Lat · Original Jurisdiction ·Jan 11, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

David Lat turns a bizarre procedural squabble over who represents a foreign head of state into a revealing lens on the fragility of legal representation and the human cost of geopolitical conflict. While the article opens with the spectacle of two high-profile lawyers vying for a client who claims to be a "prisoner of war," the underlying narrative exposes how the machinery of justice grinds against the reality of a man facing decades of incarceration for alleged drug trafficking. The stakes are not merely professional reputations; they involve the potential life sentence of a head of state and the broader implications for U.S. foreign policy operations that have already resulted in significant civilian casualties in the region.

The Battle for Counsel

The core of Lat's reporting focuses on the chaotic entry of Bruce Fein into the defense team of Nicolás Maduro, a move that was swiftly challenged by the incumbent counsel, Barry Pollack. Lat writes, "Pollack wrote that he spoke to Maduro, who told him that 'he does not know Mr. Fein and has not communicated with Mr. Fein, much less retained him.'" This direct contradiction highlights a fundamental breakdown in the attorney-client relationship, a scenario that would be scandalous in any domestic criminal case but takes on surreal dimensions when the defendant is a foreign leader captured in a contested operation. Lat notes that Fein claimed to act "in good faith," relying on "information received from individuals credibly situated within President Maduro's inner circle or family."

This framing by Lat is effective because it strips away the political theater to reveal a procedural crisis. However, it also sidesteps the more uncomfortable question of whether the capture itself, which Lat mentions as a topic of discussion on the Advisory Opinions podcast, adhered to international norms. If the capture was unlawful, as Maduro claims, the entire legal proceeding rests on shaky ground. Lat observes that Judge Alvin Hellerstein has not yet ruled on whether to strike Fein's appearance, leaving the defense team in limbo. "As of this writing, Judge Hellerstein hasn't ruled on Barry Pollack's motion to boot Bruce Fein, so we'll have to wait a little longer to find out the exact composition of Nicolás Maduro's legal team," Lat notes. This uncertainty underscores the unique difficulty of defending a client who is effectively a prisoner of the executive branch's foreign policy decisions.

The question isn't just who is on the team, but whether the team can function when the client's voice is mediated by conflicting reports from an inner circle that may no longer exist in the same form.

Critics might argue that Lat's focus on the lawyers' credentials distracts from the humanitarian reality of the situation. The article mentions the "capture of President Nicolás Maduro" as a legal news item, yet it does not delve into the potential loss of life or the destabilization of Venezuela that such an operation entails. The legal maneuvering is real, but it occurs against a backdrop of violence that Lat treats as context rather than a central moral imperative.

The Aging Judiciary and Institutional Strain

Lat shifts the focus to the presiding judge, Alvin Hellerstein, a 92-year-old jurist appointed by President Clinton in 1998. The author highlights a troubling pattern of behavior, noting that "Judge Hellerstein did not acquit himself particularly well during the Javice trial, reportedly nodding off at various points during the proceedings." Lat cites Jeffrey Toobin's argument that "the best way for [the judge] to honor the system to which he has devoted decades of his life would be to withdraw from the case because, at 92, he is simply too old to preside over a matter of this magnitude." Lat agrees with this assessment, admitting to his own "negative experience in Judge Hellerstein's courtroom back in 2015."

This section of the commentary is particularly sharp because it connects the specific case to a broader institutional problem: the tension between judicial independence and the physical realities of aging. Lat writes, "Life tenure for federal judges has its advantages, especially for judicial independence—and putting up with superannuated judges is part of the price we have to pay for them." This is a sobering admission that the system prioritizes stability over competence in individual cases. The article also touches on the recent confirmation of Alexander Van Hook and the administration's new nominees, suggesting a shifting landscape in the judiciary that may eventually address these gaps.

Lat also weaves in a reference to the broader context of judicial conduct, mentioning the complaint against Judge Amy Zanelli for maintaining a "Book of Grudges" and engaging in abusive conduct. This juxtaposition serves to illustrate that the judiciary is not immune to human failings, whether they stem from age or temperament. "Judicial diva is as judicial diva does?" Lat asks, questioning the standards of behavior expected of those who hold the power of life and death over defendants.

The Distraction of the Executive Branch

The article concludes with a look at the broader pressures on the Department of Justice, noting that "nearly two-thirds of federal prosecutors in the Southern District of New York are spending all or most of their time reviewing around two million documents related to the Justice Department's investigation of Jeffrey Epstein." Lat reports that these distractions have raised fears among prosecutors that "a denigrated, distracted and depleted work force hurts the government's ability to identify and stop terrorist plots, cyberattacks, mass violence and fraud."

This observation is crucial because it contextualizes the Maduro case within a system that is already stretched thin. The administration's focus on high-profile investigations and political appointments, such as the nomination of John Shepherd and Anna St. John, may be contributing to the very inefficiencies that complicate cases like Maduro's. Lat notes that the Republican majority on the Senate Judiciary Committee held a hearing focused on "rogue judges," further polarizing the environment in which these legal battles are fought.

The system is creaking under the weight of its own distractions, from Epstein documents to impeachment resolutions, leaving little room for the careful deliberation that a case of this magnitude demands.

Lat's reporting here is a reminder that the legal system is not an abstract machine but a collection of overworked individuals navigating a chaotic political landscape. The mention of George T. Conway III running for Congress as a Democrat adds another layer of complexity, showing how legal professionals are increasingly drawn into the political fray.

Bottom Line

David Lat's piece succeeds in exposing the procedural absurdities and institutional frailties at play in the Maduro case, using the lawyers' dispute as a microcosm for a larger crisis of confidence in the legal system. The strongest part of the argument is the unflinching look at Judge Hellerstein's age and the potential consequences for a high-stakes trial. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability is its relative silence on the human cost of the operation that led to Maduro's capture, treating the conflict as a legal puzzle rather than a humanitarian tragedy. Readers should watch for Judge Hellerstein's ruling on Fein's appearance, as it will likely set a precedent for how the judiciary handles cases involving foreign heads of state and the limits of legal representation in politically charged environments.
