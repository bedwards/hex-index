Micro Hydropower : Turbulent Turbines

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Mar 7, 2021 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through the noise of massive infrastructure projects to spotlight a solution that feels almost too simple to be revolutionary: harnessing the gentle spin of a river vortex to power remote villages. While the world obsesses over billion-dollar dams and intermittent solar arrays, Borlace argues that the most effective path to decarbonization for the next billion people lies in micro-hydropower systems that are fish-friendly, flood-resistant, and economically superior to diesel. This is not just a technical update; it is a compelling case for rethinking how we deliver energy to the places the grid will never reach.

The Grid Gap and the Solar Limit

Borlace begins by dismantling the assumption that solar photovoltaic panels are the universal answer for off-grid communities. He notes that while solar has improved lives, it suffers from an "inherent and inconvenient intermittency factor which even with the battery storage can never be completely overcome." This is a crucial distinction often glossed over in renewable energy discussions. The author correctly identifies that for remote locations without grid backup, the sun's daily absence creates a reliability crisis that batteries struggle to solve cost-effectively.

He contrasts this with the historical reliance on "horrible dangerous kerosene lamps" and "noisy generators running on diesel," which dump carbon into the atmosphere. The core of his argument is that nature offers a more consistent alternative: "in most cases the glaringly obvious answer was the water flowing nearby in rivers and canals." This framing is effective because it shifts the focus from high-tech novelty to a fundamental resource that has been overlooked in favor of industrial-scale engineering.

Critics might note that not every remote community has access to flowing water with sufficient gradient, limiting the universal applicability of this solution. However, Borlace acknowledges that for the hundreds of millions living near rivers, the potential is immense.

"The trouble is though that most governments are fixated on extremely large centralized infrastructure projects that can supply enormous quantities of electricity."

This observation lands hard because it exposes a political and logistical bias rather than a technical one. Borlace explains that large dams, like the Three Gorges Dam, are favored because they are logistically easier to build in one location and offer a "nice neat and tidy monopoly" for state regulators. Yet, he meticulously details the environmental cost: blocking fish migration, reducing nutrient flow, and releasing methane from decaying organic matter in reservoirs.

"Building a dam on a river means blocking diverting or completely changing the natural course of the water that often screws up the migration routes for species of fish."

The author's critique of large-scale hydro is well-supported, citing the displacement of 1.3 million people by the Three Gorges Dam and the massive ecological disruption. By highlighting these failures, he sets the stage for a technology that avoids these pitfalls entirely.

The Vortex Solution

Enter Turbulent, a Belgian startup that Borlace describes as having a "visionary" approach. The technology is a vortex turbine designed to work with a water head of less than five meters. Unlike traditional turbines that are lethal to aquatic life, this system creates a low-pressure vortex where "soft rounded edges allowing aquatic life to pass straight through the entire system."

"All the water and fish are then returned straight back into the main flow of the river system to continue on their journey entirely unaffected."

This is the piece's most distinctive claim: energy generation that does not require sacrificing the ecosystem. Borlace emphasizes that the system is autonomous, monitored via an app, and capable of running 24/7. The reliability here is the key differentiator. He argues that for a system to be viable in developing nations, it must meet a strict shopping list: "small scale locally controlled power generation reliable affordable electricity available 24 hours a day seven days a week minimal or even zero environmental impact."

The economic argument is equally compelling. Borlace breaks down the costs, noting that a 15-kilowatt off-grid system costs around 90,000 euros. While the upfront price seems high, the lifetime cost per kilowatt-hour is "less than 2.3 euro cents," compared to "more like 50 euro cents per unit" for diesel.

"That's a pretty compelling business model."

This financial comparison is the strongest evidence in the piece. It moves the conversation from idealism to pragmatism, showing that micro-hydro is not just an environmental good but a financial necessity for communities currently burning expensive, dirty fuel.

Real-World Resilience

To prove the concept works in harsh conditions, Borlace points to the Green School in Bali, Indonesia. The school had previously failed with its own attempts at micro-hydro, with one installation destroyed by flood debris. Turbulent's system, however, was built to withstand "severe flooding" and "continuous heavy duty harsh environments."

"The whole system sits within a nice small neat footprint just outside the school a solar installation generating a similar output would have taken up the space of four tennis courts."

This comparison highlights the spatial efficiency of the technology, a critical factor for dense or land-constrained areas. The installation took only one day and required no heavy machinery, contrasting sharply with the decade-long timelines for large dams. Borlace suggests that these projects can be built in phases, reducing risk and allowing local communities to see immediate benefits.

"Developing and constructing a large-scale hydropower plant can take up to 10 years while multiple linked up micro hydropower installations can be completed within months."

This speed of deployment is a strategic advantage that governments and NGOs should consider. It allows for rapid electrification of rural areas, aligning with United Nations sustainable development goals without the bureaucratic drag of mega-projects.

"I for one would love to see many more of these brilliant devices installed in remote areas around the world."

While Borlace's enthusiasm is palpable, a counterargument worth considering is the scalability of the supply chain. Can a startup like Turbulent manufacture and deploy thousands of these bespoke systems globally without a significant increase in cost or a drop in quality control? The piece hints at this by noting the systems are "bespoke," which implies a labor-intensive process that could be a bottleneck.

Bottom Line

Borlace makes a persuasive case that the future of rural electrification lies not in bigger dams or better batteries, but in smarter, smaller turbines that work with nature rather than against it. The strongest part of his argument is the rigorous economic comparison showing that micro-hydro undercuts diesel on a lifetime cost basis while eliminating carbon emissions. The biggest vulnerability remains the site-specific nature of the technology; it is a solution for the water-rich, not a universal fix for all off-grid communities. However, for the billions living near flowing water, this approach offers a path to energy independence that is both environmentally sound and financially viable.

"The whole thing is controlled autonomously by electronic wizardry in the electrical cabinets that turbulence engineers install on site and of course there's an app for it so that all the performance parameters can be monitored remotely."
