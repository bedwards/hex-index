Why Americans come for treatment in a warzone

Tim Mak · The Counteroffensive ·Mar 28, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Tim Mak's reporting on Americans seeking surrogacy in war-torn Ukraine exposes a brutal paradox: families are risking missile strikes to escape the crushing financial reality of the American healthcare system. The piece is notable not for the medical details, but for its unflinching look at how desperation drives people into active conflict zones, turning a warzone into a destination for hope. This is a story where the cost of a child in the United States literally outweighs the risk of death in Europe.

The Calculus of Desperation

Mak anchors the narrative in the personal tragedy of Lucy and Brad, a Colorado couple whose journey illustrates the systemic failure of U.S. reproductive care. After four years of infertility treatment and three failed in vitro fertilization cycles, the couple faced a financial wall. "We always wanted a child, but first we had one miscarriage, then another. We spent a lot of money on treatment and IVF, but nothing worked out," Mak writes, quoting Lucy. The author uses this personal history to pivot to the broader economic argument: the United States has become too expensive for its own citizens to build families.

The core of the argument rests on a stark comparison of costs. Mak notes that in 2026, surrogacy in the U.S. ranges from $150,000 to $220,000, a figure that excludes the additional $25,000 to $45,000 often needed for donor gametes. In contrast, Ukraine offered a solution at half the price. This economic disparity is the engine of the story. As Mak puts it, "For Americans, the biggest reason for medical tourism is the U.S. healthcare system itself." The evidence here is compelling, supported by data showing that 41 percent of respondents carry medical debt and 36 percent forego necessary care due to cost. This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from individual choice to systemic failure.

Critics might argue that framing a decision to travel to a warzone as a rational economic choice glosses over the ethical implications of medical tourism in unstable regions. However, Mak does not shy away from the danger; he highlights that the World Health Organization has documented at least 2,881 attacks on Ukraine's healthcare system, grounding the risk in hard data rather than abstract fear.

The Legal and Ethical Labyrinth

Beyond the economics, Mak explores why Ukraine specifically became a hub for surrogacy, even as other nations closed their doors. The author explains that following bans in Thailand, India, and Nepal due to exploitation concerns, Ukraine emerged as a leading center. The legal framework is a key differentiator. "Surrogacy in Ukraine is so popular, in particular because of the Family Code of Ukraine, which leaves the surrogate mother with no possibility of claiming rights to the newborn, and the child belongs entirely to the intended parents from the moment of conception," Mak writes. This legal certainty is a powerful draw for American parents who often face complex pre-birth orders or adoption processes in their own states.

This section of the reporting is particularly sharp because it connects current events to a decade-long global trend. It references the historical context of surrogacy bans, noting how the vacuum left by other countries inadvertently funnels demand toward Ukraine. The author's decision to weave in the specific legal mechanism—the Family Code—adds necessary depth, showing that this is not just about price, but about legal security. Yet, the piece also acknowledges the moral weight of this arrangement, with Lucy admitting, "I was really worried that these women were being exploited. The price seemed very suspicious."

"It was like a fresh start for my husband and me. It was as if we were planning our wedding again, but this time for the baby."

Living in the Shadow of War

The most visceral part of Mak's coverage is the juxtaposition of intimate medical procedures with the chaos of war. The author does not sanitize the experience. He recounts how a maternity hospital in western Ukraine was damaged by Russian strikes, forcing evacuations during active labor. He references the iconic 2022 World Press Photo of the Year, taken by Evgeniy Maloletka, which captured the bombing of a Mariupol maternity ward, to remind readers that the threat is real and documented. "The photo... won the World Press Photo award," Mak notes, using this historical anchor to validate the ongoing danger.

The narrative tension peaks when Mak describes Lucy and Brad's time in Ukraine. Despite the risks, they stayed for three weeks, even utilizing the country's high-quality dental care—a nod to the broader medical tourism trend where refugees themselves return for treatment. The danger was not theoretical. "Just two months after their daughter was born, missile debris struck one of the offices at the clinic where they underwent treatment. Seven people were killed," Mak writes. This specific detail, citing the ADONIS network attack in Kyiv, transforms the story from a travelogue into a testament to survival. The author's inclusion of the couple's relief at not encountering explosions themselves, while acknowledging the deaths of others, creates a complex emotional landscape.

Critics might suggest that the story risks romanticizing the couple's journey by focusing on their "luck" rather than the systemic issues that forced them there. However, Mak counters this by including Lucy's own reflection: "It's not an easy journey, it's very difficult, and I'm not sure I'd recommend it to anyone."

Bottom Line

Tim Mak's strongest move is his refusal to treat the war as mere background noise; instead, he makes the conflict the central character that the parents must negotiate. The piece's greatest vulnerability is the inherent tragedy of the situation: it validates a system where safety is a luxury only the wealthy can afford in their own country, forcing others to gamble with their lives abroad. Readers should watch for how international surrogacy laws evolve as more nations face similar economic pressures, potentially turning other conflict zones into the next destinations for desperate families.

Lucy and Brad lived in Colorado. They had been dreaming of having a child ever since they met 14 years ago, but when they finally decided to try for a baby, they discovered that the journey was much more difficult and longer than they had imagined.

After four years of infertility treatment and three unsuccessful IVF (in vitro fertilization) attempts, they decided to explore surrogacy; however, upon learning the cost of the process in the United States, they had to consider more affordable international options. So, they chose Ukraine.

Even before the war, Ukraine was one of Europe’s leading destinations for medical tourism - primarily for dentistry, fertility treatments, and plastic surgery, these figures dropped significantly since the start of the full-scale invasion (the World Health Organization has documented at least 2,881 attacks on Ukraine’s healthcare system as of February 2026) but the healthcare sector is adapting and continues to treat patients. Clinics are purchasing generators, setting up shelters, and providing services though risking disruption.

These risks remain a daily reality. In late March, Russian strikes damaged a maternity hospital in western Ukraine, forcing patients and doctors to evacuate to shelters while they were providing medical care. A father and his daughter, who were visiting the maternity hospital in Ivano-Frankivsk to see the mother after the birth of a baby brother, were killed.

And perhaps the most famous image of the war is from in 2022, when the Russians bombed a maternity ward in Mariupol.
