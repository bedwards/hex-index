All you need to know to understand 5G

Sabine Hossenfelder · Sabine Hossenfelder ·Dec 19, 2020 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sabine Hossenfelder begins with a confession that immediately disarms the typical physics skeptic: she entered the 5G debate convinced the health fears were nonsense, but a deeper dive left her uncertain. This is not the usual dismissal of conspiracy theories; it is a physicist admitting that the lack of long-term data on millimeter waves creates a genuine, if small, gap in our safety knowledge. For busy listeners navigating a world of conflicting headlines, Hossenfelder offers a rare middle ground between blind panic and corporate reassurance.

The Physics of Fear

Hossenfelder grounds the controversy in fundamental physics, distinguishing between the energy of individual particles and the total power of the radiation. She explains that wireless technology relies on electromagnetic radiation, where the frequency determines the energy per photon. "The energy of the photons is proportional to the frequency of the radiation... so a high frequency means a short wavelength and a large energy per photon." This distinction is crucial because it dictates whether radiation can break molecular bonds, the primary mechanism for DNA damage.

She notes that the new millimeter waves used in 5G operate at frequencies far too low to cause this type of ionizing damage. "The energy per photon in the radiation is so useful because if you want to damage a molecule you need a certain minimum amount of energy to break the bonds between the atoms." Since 5G frequencies fall well below the threshold required to break these bonds, the direct link to cancer is scientifically tenuous. This argument holds up against the most common fear-mongering, which conflates all radiation with nuclear fallout.

However, Hossenfelder pivots to the nuance that often gets lost in the noise: heat. Even if the photons can't break DNA, they can still warm tissue. "How much warming you can get from electromagnetic is not determined by the energy per photon it is determined by the total energy per time that is transferred by all the photons." She contrasts a microwave oven, which uses high power to heat food, with a wireless router, which uses significantly less power and radiates in all directions, causing the signal to dissipate rapidly.

"The frequency tells you the energy per particle and determines what type of damage is possible the power tells you the number of particles and it drops very quickly with distance to the source."

This is the piece's most effective clarification. It explains why standing next to a cell tower is different from standing next to a microwave, yet it also acknowledges that the new 5G architecture changes the game. The shift to "small cells" means antennas are closer to people, potentially increasing local exposure even if the total power remains low.

The Data Gap

The controversy intensifies when Hossenfelder addresses the specific high-band frequencies unique to 5G. These millimeter waves are a new frontier for telecommunications, and the historical safety record of cell phones does not apply to them. "The people who are citing the lack of correlation between cell phone news and cancer incidents in the past 20 years missed the point these studies don't tell you anything about the 5g high band because that wasn't previously in use." This is a devastating critique of the standard "it's been safe for decades" defense used by industry lobbyists.

She highlights that while we know millimeter waves don't penetrate deeply, they do interact with the skin and eyes. "We know that at high power they warm the skin and irritate eyes exactly what power is too much in the long run no one knows because there just hasn't been enough research." Citing a review that found insufficient data for a meaningful safety assessment, she admits that the scientific community's confidence is based on an absence of evidence, not evidence of absence.

Critics might argue that this admission fuels unnecessary panic, especially given the decades of safe use of similar frequencies in radar and astronomy. Yet, Hossenfelder counters that the application context—continuous, close-range exposure to the human body—is fundamentally different. "It would be unscientific to flat out say there are no reasons to worry that's not very reassuring." She validates the public's unease without endorsing the arson and vandalism that have plagued the rollout in Europe.

"The protests that you see against 5g i am afraid to say are not entirely unjustified."

This statement is the article's emotional core. It acknowledges that while the physics suggests low risk, the political and scientific process has failed to provide the long-term certainty that the public deserves. The World Health Organization's stance that no adverse effects have been causally linked is technically true but, as Hossenfelder notes, relies on a scant number of studies at the specific frequencies in question.

Bottom Line

Hossenfelder's strongest contribution is her refusal to treat the 5G debate as a binary choice between science and superstition; she identifies a legitimate, data-driven uncertainty that neither side fully addresses. Her biggest vulnerability is the difficulty of translating "low probability risk" into actionable policy, a gap she wisely leaves for politicians to fill. Listeners should watch for the next decade of epidemiological studies, as the current consensus rests on a foundation of theoretical physics rather than long-term human data.
