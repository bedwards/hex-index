Electric or hydrogen trucks - which one will be the winner?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Nov 22, 2020 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through the noise of pandemic-era environmental optimism to expose a stubborn, growing carbon blind spot: the global freight industry. While headlines celebrated cleaner skies from grounded planes, Borlace argues that the surge in online shopping has quietly supercharged road freight emissions, making the race between electric and hydrogen trucks not just a technological debate, but a climate imperative. He brings a rare clarity to the logistics of decarbonization, moving beyond simple fuel swaps to dissect the infrastructure, grid capacity, and economic realities that will determine which technology actually wins.

The Hidden Cost of Convenience

Borlace begins by dismantling the illusion that the pandemic was a net positive for the planet's carbon footprint. He points out that while aviation stalled, the "ever expanding Global Freight industry" surged to meet the demands of homebound shoppers. The scale of the problem is staggering. "Just under 30% of All Transport emissions in 2018 came from Road Freight," Borlace writes, noting that these vehicles consume more than 17 million barrels of oil every day. This is not a niche issue; it is a massive, systemic driver of climate change that has historically escaped the rigorous regulations applied to passenger cars.

The author effectively traces how governments are finally waking up to this gap. He details how the United States, the European Union, and China have all introduced stricter fuel economy standards and reporting tools like the EU's VECTO. These policies are a necessary first step, but Borlace is quick to temper expectations about incremental improvements. "Ultimately there's only so much you can do with an internal combustion engine," he argues, suggesting that efficiency gains alone cannot bridge the gap to net zero. This framing is crucial because it prevents readers from falling into the trap of thinking minor tweaks to diesel engines are a sufficient solution.

Critics might note that Borlace briefly acknowledges the argument for slashing consumption entirely—stopping the shipment of luxury goods and choosing local products—but dismisses it as politically unrealistic given global demand. While logically sound, this concession risks underselling the potential of a circular economy to reduce the sheer volume of freight needed.

The Limits of Transition Fuels

Before diving into the electric versus hydrogen showdown, Borlace rigorously dissects the alternatives that often get a free pass in public discourse: biofuels and natural gas. He exposes the "shaky" claims of carbon neutrality surrounding biofuels, citing studies where biodiesel from palm oil produced three times the emissions of fossil fuels. "When a full analysis is carried out those claims can look pretty shaky," he writes, highlighting the catastrophic biodiversity loss involved in creating monocrop plantations.

Similarly, he challenges the narrative that natural gas is a clean bridge fuel. He notes that when leakage and lower efficiencies are factored in, the net benefit "varies from 15% at best to no net benefit at all." This skepticism is a vital corrective to industry marketing. By stripping away the greenwashing, Borlace clears the deck for the real contenders: battery electric and hydrogen fuel cells. He acknowledges the valid criticism that electric trucks charged on a coal-heavy grid or hydrogen produced from methane are not truly zero-emission, but he pivots quickly to the solution: "we need to continue accelerating the integration of Renewable Power and energy storage onto our National Grids."

The Great Race: Batteries vs. Hydrogen

With the dead ends of biofuels and natural gas identified, Borlace turns his attention to the two technologies vying for dominance. He paints a picture of a global sprint, with China leading the charge in electric commercial vehicles and South Korea and Japan betting big on hydrogen. He highlights the ambition of manufacturers like Hyundai, which has already begun exporting hydrogen trucks to Switzerland, and Daimler, which has committed to selling only zero-emission vehicles by 2039.

The Tesla Semi gets special attention for its compelling economics. Borlace notes the truck's "claimed fully laden 0 to 60 time of 20 seconds" and a lifetime fuel cost saving of around $200,000. He emphasizes that major logistics players like Amazon, FedEx, and UPS are already pledging to electrify their fleets, driven by both climate goals and the bottom line. "In this Modern Age of e-commerce these zero emissions trucks have a ready-made customer base," he observes, suggesting that market forces may drive adoption faster than regulation alone ever could.

"The cost of implementing new infrastructure will be pretty enormous but so will be the investment opportunities for the commercial Enterprises who choose to take the plunge into this Brave New World."

However, the article doesn't shy away from the logistical nightmares. Borlace explores the concept of "Electric Road Systems" in Europe, where trucks hook into overhead cables like trams. While this offers high efficiency, he contrasts it with the need for a robust charging network for long-haul routes. He introduces the Megawatt Charging System (MCS) being developed by ABB, which promises ultra-rapid charging to keep trucks moving. The challenge, he notes, is the strain on the electrical grid. Yet, he offers a clever counter-intuitive solution: the trucks themselves could act as mobile batteries, using vehicle-to-grid software to stabilize the network rather than just draining it.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest contribution is his refusal to treat the transition as a simple technology swap; he forces the reader to confront the massive infrastructure overhaul required to make it work. His argument is most vulnerable where it assumes that renewable energy integration will keep pace with the explosive demand of a fully electrified freight network, a timeline that remains uncertain. The reader should watch closely how the economics of the Megawatt Charging System evolve, as that infrastructure will likely determine whether electric trucks can truly conquer long-haul routes or if hydrogen will claim that territory. Ultimately, the piece makes a compelling case that the winner isn't just about the engine, but about the entire energy ecosystem supporting it.
