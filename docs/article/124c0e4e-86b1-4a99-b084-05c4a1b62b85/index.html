The Image Boards of Hayao Miyazaki

Various · Animation Obsessive ·Apr 5, 2026 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

The authors of "The Image Boards of Hayao Miyazaki" make a startling claim: the magic of Studio Ghibli's most beloved films did not begin in the script or the storyboard, but in the frantic, messy, and often discarded pencil sketches of a young man searching for a direction. This piece is notable because it bypasses the polished final product to argue that Miyazaki's entire career is actually a forty-year conversation with his own rough drafts. For the busy professional, this offers a rare glimpse into the mechanics of creativity, suggesting that the "genius" of a master is less about perfection and more about the relentless velocity of idea generation.

The Definition of a Mental Image

The article opens by dismantling a common misconception about Miyazaki's process. The authors correctly identify that his "image boards" are not storyboards in the traditional sense. "An image board is something drawn to prepare for a work," Miyazaki once explained, "They aren't storyboards — they're for loose ideas, not strict continuity." This distinction is crucial. While a storyboard maps the camera and the timing, an image board captures the feeling. The authors note that the English term "image" drifts from the Japanese imēji, which refers to a mental impression or an idea rather than a static picture.

This framing is effective because it recontextualizes the rough, almost frantic energy of Miyazaki's early sketches. The authors point out that for his work on Horus: Prince of the Sun (1968), Miyazaki drew quickly with a pencil and a single color wash. "Because they decided the overall feel of the film, and were the material that determined the direction of the story, it was necessary to draw as many as possible," Miyazaki recalled. The authors use this to argue that the volume of work was a feature, not a bug; the sheer quantity allowed the team to find the film's soul.

"I used to draw larger, but it got more and more troublesome, so they became smaller and smaller."

This detail about the shrinking size of the sketches is a brilliant piece of evidence. It shows a director prioritizing the flow of ideas over the aesthetics of the drawing itself. The authors weave in the context of Isao Takahata, Miyazaki's mentor and rival, noting that Miyazaki was one of several artists on Horus, but his sketches possessed a unique "cinematic eye." As the authors paraphrase, Yasuo Otsuka, a fellow artist, admitted that "no matter how much I drew, my drawing skill couldn't match Miyazaki-san's." This comparison highlights that Miyazaki's value wasn't technical perfection, but the raw energy that could sell a vision to a room full of skeptics.

The Grind and the Pivot

The commentary shifts to a more somber tone as it explores Miyazaki's mid-career crisis. The authors argue that Miyazaki's frustration with the naturalism of Takahata's later projects, like Heidi: Girl of the Alps, forced him to retreat into his own imagination. "After Heidi, I didn't have my whole heart in my work," Miyazaki said. The authors suggest this period of disillusionment was actually a incubator for his future style. While he was stuck drawing layouts for others, he was secretly sketching the bus stop scene that would eventually become My Neighbor Totoro.

This is a compelling narrative arc: the "lost" years were actually the most productive for his internal creative library. The authors note that he openly reused ideas, recycling visual concepts from the unmade Pippi Longstocking project into Panda! Go, Panda!. "Panda is a very big-hearted, easygoing character," Miyazaki later wrote. "In that respect Totoro and Panda are similar for me." The authors use this to show that Miyazaki's themes are not linear but cyclical, constantly refining the same core emotions through different visual lenses.

Critics might note that the article glosses over the collaborative friction this "recycling" might have caused with Takahata, who was moving toward a more objective, documentary-style realism. However, the authors' focus remains on Miyazaki's internal evolution, which is the story's primary strength.

"The drawings Miyazaki accumulated between 1980 and 1982 formed the basis of all his work, from Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind to Princess Mononoke."

This quote serves as the article's pivot point. The authors argue that the "gloom" of 1980, when Miyazaki felt his work on The Castle of Cagliostro was a "clearance sale" of old ideas, was actually the moment his true style was born. The evidence provided is the influence of Western underground comics, specifically artists like Moebius, which seeped into his image boards. The authors effectively demonstrate how Miyazaki's "manga eiga" (cartoon film) aesthetic evolved into something richer and more complex, driven by the accumulation of these rough sketches.

The Master's Hand

In the final section, the authors connect the dots from the early, frantic sketches to the confident, fluid lines of Miyazaki's later masterpieces. They argue that the "looseness" of his early work became a "purposeful looseness" in his maturity. "Every Miyazaki movie originates from this jotting habit: his collection, reuse and exploration of ideas on paper," the authors write. They point out that a U-shaped arrowhead sketched in the early 1980s for a proto-Nausicaä idea reappeared in The Boy and the Heron over forty years later.

This is the article's most profound insight: the image board is a time machine. It allows the artist to revisit and refine ideas across decades. The authors describe the sketches for The Boy and the Heron as showing "a master's touch, an absolute clarity of imagination." They contrast this with the "phantasmagoria" of Spirited Away, suggesting that Miyazaki's imagination had become more focused, not less wild.

"Forty years of nothing but this!" yells his pig character. Then a caterpillar mocks him: this method is "all he can do."

The authors use this self-deprecating joke from Miyazaki's own watercolor guide to underscore the humility of the process. Despite the mockery within his own work, the authors argue that the "energy" of these sketches—the splashes of watercolor and the lack of erasing—is what gives the films their life. The article concludes by noting that even at age 82, Miyazaki's ability to mesmerize with a sketch remained undiminished.

The page was now an open conduit for Miyazaki's imagination.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is the reframing of Miyazaki's "rough" sketches not as unfinished products, but as the primary engine of his storytelling. The article's biggest vulnerability is its heavy reliance on Miyazaki's own retrospective quotes, which may romanticize the chaos of his early career. However, the evidence of visual continuity across forty years of work makes a convincing case that the image board is the true blueprint of the Ghibli universe.

1. Ideas on paper

Watching The Boy and the Heron, back in 2023, wasn’t a theater experience like any we’ve had. We were a little speechless when we stood up to go — the credits rolling, white letters on blue. A stranger seated toward the front row had clapped at the ending. Mostly, people were quiet.

At age 82, Hayao Miyazaki had reinvented himself again. It was hard to find the director of My Neighbor Totoro in The Boy and the Heron, just as the link between Totoro and The Castle of Cagliostro (1979) had been faint. Miyazaki’s changed and adapted since his career began at Toei Doga in 1963, more than six decades ago.

What’s stayed consistent is his habit of sketching ideas. His “image boards.”

“An image board is something drawn to prepare for a work,” Miyazaki once explained. They aren’t storyboards — they’re for loose ideas, not strict continuity. He did his first image boards at Toei: “I myself started naturally [drawing them] with Horus.”1

Horus: Prince of the Sun (1968) was the debut film by the late Isao Takahata — Miyazaki’s close friend, rival, sounding board, foil and in some ways mentor. Miyazaki was a major artist on Horus, and one of several who drew its image boards. As he said:

Because they decided the overall feel of the film, and were the material that determined the direction of the story, it was necessary to draw as many as possible. I drew quickly and simply with a pencil and glossed it over with a single color — because it was the process of searching for a direction, I didn’t want to expend a lot of effort on each piece. I used to draw larger, but it got more and more troublesome, so they became smaller and smaller.

Loan words from English make up the Japanese term “image board” (imēji bōdo) — but the meaning of “image” drifted in translation. In Japanese, imēji refers to something more like a mental image, an impression, an idea.

Which is to say that an image board is concept art.
