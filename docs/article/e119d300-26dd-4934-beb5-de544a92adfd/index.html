Will China will grow old before it gets rich?

Shirvan Neftchi · CaspianReport ·May 21, 2025 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Shirvan Neftchi delivers a stark warning that cuts through the usual noise of economic forecasting: China's greatest threat isn't external sanctions or a trade war, but the very demographic clock it has ignored for decades. The piece is notable not for predicting a collapse, but for exposing a fundamental flaw in the math of "getting rich" when the population is simultaneously shrinking. Neftchi argues that the old playbook of building infrastructure to force GDP numbers is not just obsolete, but actively destructive, leaving Beijing with a terrifyingly narrow window to pivot before the math becomes impossible.

The Illusion of Growth

Neftchi opens with a visceral image that dismantles the credibility of China's reported growth figures. He describes a 2021 incident in Kunming where a dozen high-rise buildings were demolished not because they were unsafe, but because they were "in the way of a 5% GDP target." This anecdote serves as the foundation for his critique of how China has chased growth for decades: "Build fast, build big, and worry about purpose later." The author correctly identifies that this model is running out of steam, yet the reliance on construction remains a crutch.

The core of the argument rests on a technical but devastating truth about how Gross Domestic Product is calculated. Neftchi writes, "GDP growth is not always productive growth. You can build a highway, tear it down, rebuild it exactly as before, tear it down again, and do this over and over, and it will all register as economic growth." This is a crucial distinction often missed by casual observers who equate GDP with national wealth. Neftchi points out that this "loophole isn't unique to China, but local officials there have used it to hit their GDP targets." The commentary here is sharp: it exposes an economy that has been gaming its own metrics rather than building genuine value.

GDP doesn't care what the activity is. It just counts the money.

Critics might argue that infrastructure investment, even if redundant, creates jobs and stimulates short-term activity, which is a legitimate tool in a recession. However, Neftchi's point holds weight when viewed through the lens of long-term sustainability; "none of this is productive growth. It's not real. It doesn't benefit the public in the long run." The author effectively argues that the era of easy growth via concrete and steel is over, forcing a transition to a model that relies on the Chinese consumer.

The Consumption Trap

The pivot to a consumption-based economy is described by Neftchi as "perhaps the most complicated phase in becoming a high-income country." He notes the difficulty of the shift: "It's easier to build a machine than it is to convince people to use it." This metaphor perfectly captures the structural inertia China faces. The country has spent forty years optimizing for production, not for the psychology of spending.

Neftchi provides a sobering comparison of household consumption rates to illustrate the scale of the challenge. While the United States derives 68% of its GDP from consumption, China sits at a mere 39%, the lowest of any major economy. He attributes this to a deep-seated cultural and historical caution, noting that "saving isn't just a habit, it's almost instinctive." The author connects this frugality to recent traumas, observing that "consumer confidence plummeted and people started saving at a higher rate than before the pandemic."

The consequence of this behavior is a dangerous economic phenomenon: deflation. Neftchi explains that "when people expect prices to keep dropping, they often delay spending, hoping to get a better deal later." This creates a vicious cycle where businesses cut prices, make less money, and eventually lay off workers, further crushing demand. He warns that "persistent deflation can be more harmful than inflation," a counterintuitive point that challenges the common assumption that falling prices are always good for the consumer. The government's response, a fiscal stimulus worth 2% of GDP aimed at increasing wages and social safety nets, is presented as a necessary but perhaps insufficient attempt to break this psychological barrier.

The Demographic Cliff

The most alarming section of the piece addresses the demographic reality that makes the economic pivot even harder. Neftchi states bluntly, "China may be too old to get rich." The data supports this grim outlook: with birth rates falling for three straight years, the population is projected to shrink to around 760 million by 2100. This is not a slow drift but a precipitous drop that fundamentally alters the labor supply.

Neftchi draws a direct line between population dynamics and economic performance, noting that "countries with younger populations, such as Japan in the 1950s and South Korea in the 1960s, experienced rapid economic growth thanks to large working age populations." He points out that China's median age now mirrors Japan's in 1995 and Germany's in 2000, periods that preceded decades of stagnation or modest growth. The author highlights a critical threshold: "after the 15% elderly milestone, major economies have consistently struggled to grow more than 4% of GDP for more than a decade."

Beijing's proposed solution is a heavy reliance on technology. Neftchi writes, "Beijing's solution to its aging population is innovation. It is investing heavily in AI and robotics to offset the economic drag of its elderly demographic." However, he immediately undercuts the optimism of this strategy with a sobering reality check: "AI and robots don't consume like people do. They consume electricity, but that doesn't drive demand in the economy the way human spending does." This is the piece's most profound insight—technology can replace workers, but it cannot replace the customers who buy the products.

It's never been done before. Sure, innovation may buy time, but it won't replace demand.

A counterargument worth considering is that automation could drastically lower production costs, making Chinese goods so cheap that they spur global demand, effectively exporting the growth. Neftchi acknowledges the geopolitical headwinds, noting that "globalization is taking a step back," which limits the export route and forces China to rely on domestic demand. This makes his conclusion that the shift is "improbable" yet necessary all the more compelling.

Bottom Line

Shirvan Neftchi's strongest contribution is his dismantling of the myth that GDP growth equals national prosperity, revealing instead a fragile economy built on demolition and demographic denial. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in the assumption that cultural habits of saving cannot be rapidly altered by policy, yet the sheer scale of the demographic shift suggests that even perfect policy may not be enough. The reader should watch for whether China's massive investment in AI can truly decouple economic growth from population size, a feat no other nation has ever achieved.
