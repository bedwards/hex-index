Jeffrey Kaplan tackles a 19th-century German logic puzzle that still dictates how we understand human communication today, arguing that the gap between what a word points to and how we conceive of it is the only way to explain how we learn new things. While the subject—Gottlob Frege's 1892 paper "On Sense and Reference"—sounds impenetrable, Kaplan's distinctive move is to anchor high-level philosophy in the mundane reality of a subway ride with Jay-Z, proving that cognitive value isn't just for logicians but for anyone who has ever realized two names describe the same person. This is not merely a history lesson; it is a necessary framework for understanding why language can surprise us.

The Subway Puzzle

Kaplan begins by dismantling the intuitive assumption that names are just labels for objects. He illustrates the problem with a viral video where a woman, Ellen, recognizes the name "Jay-Z" but is startled to learn the man sitting next to her is him. "The noteworthy thing about this interaction is that the woman her name is Ellen she already knows the meaning of the name Jay-Z so it's not like what happens is that the guy tells her oh there's this name Jay-Z and the name refers to me it's my name that's not what happens," Kaplan explains. The crucial insight here is that Ellen learns an empirical fact about the world, not a linguistic rule. She already knew the definition of the name; the surprise was the identity.

This sets up the central conflict with John Stuart Mill's earlier theory. Mill argued that the meaning of a proper name is nothing more than the object it names. If this were true, Kaplan notes, "the name Shan Carter and the name Jay-Z mean exactly the same thing and so sentence one and sentence two are no different." Under Mill's view, the statement "Shawn Carter is Jay-Z" would be as trivial as "Shawn Carter is Shawn Carter." But as Kaplan points out, the former is genuinely informative while the latter is "totally banale and uninformative." This discrepancy creates a crisis for any theory of language that cannot distinguish between the two.

The name Shawn Carter and the name Jay-Z denote the same person, but the information that you get from sentence 2 can be in some cases not about language not about the meanings of the words but it can be something that has to be discovered through going out into the world.

Critics of Mill might argue that his theory works fine for simple identification, but Kaplan shows that it collapses when faced with the cognitive value of identity statements. The failure of Mill's model is the engine that drives Frege's entire project.

The Astronomical and Mathematical Evidence

To prove that this isn't just a quirk of celebrity culture, Kaplan pivots to Frege's classic astronomical example: the Morning Star and the Evening Star. For centuries, these were thought to be two different celestial bodies. "If you look at the Sunrise Just Before Sunrise right near where the sun is about to rise you can see a very bright star sometimes and the name of that star is the morning star," Kaplan describes, contrasting it with the evening star seen after sunset. The discovery that they are both the planet Venus transformed a trivial tautology into a massive scientific breakthrough.

Kaplan argues that this confirms the need for a third layer of meaning beyond the name and the object. He introduces Frege's solution: the "sense." "It is natural now to think of there being connected with a sign a name combination of words letter besides that to which the sign refers which may be called the reference of the sign also what I would like to call the sense of the sign wherein the mode of presentation is contained," Kaplan quotes. The "sense" is the specific way the object is presented to the mind. One might know the Morning Star as "the bright object at dawn" and the Evening Star as "the bright object at dusk." These are different modes of presentation, even though the reference (Venus) is identical.

To make this abstract concept concrete, Kaplan employs a geometric analogy involving a triangle. He describes three lines connecting the vertices to the midpoints of opposite sides. "The point of intersection of A and B is then the same as the point of intersection of B and C," he notes, highlighting that while the point is the same, the descriptions leading to it are distinct. "These two different names for that one point the point of intersection of A and B and the point of intersection of B and C these two names they pick out the same point but they present that point in different ways." This mathematical precision allows Frege to explain how a statement can be both true and informative.

The Mode of Presentation

Kaplan's most effective contribution here is translating Frege's "mode of presentation" into the modern concept of a description. He suggests that a sense is essentially a specific characterization of an object. "You could describe this guy as a billionaire music mogul or you could describe him as a middle aged man who was born in Brooklyn and still lives most of the time in the greater New York area those are two different ways of presenting or characterizing this guy Shan Carter or Jay-Z," Kaplan writes. This distinction is vital because it explains why we can hold two different mental images of the same person without contradiction.

The argument holds up remarkably well because it accounts for the difference between knowing that something is true and knowing what something is. "Fraga's point is this a name or for that matter any other linguistic term a name has not only this level of meaning which is the object named but also the way that that object is characterized or presented by the sign by the name," Kaplan summarizes. Without this distinction, we cannot explain how science progresses or how we learn new facts about people we think we already know.

These two names they refer to the same guy they have the same reference but they have different senses they present him in different ways and it's the same way with the morning star and the evening star.

A counterargument worth considering is whether "sense" is truly objective or if it varies wildly from person to person. If my "sense" of Jay-Z is "a rapper I like" and yours is "a wealthy businessman," does the statement "Shawn Carter is Jay-Z" mean different things to us? Kaplan implies a shared cultural understanding of these modes of presentation, but the subjective nature of description remains a potential friction point in Frege's otherwise robust system.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's commentary succeeds by stripping away the dense academic jargon of Frege's original text to reveal a powerful, intuitive logic: meaning is not just about what words point to, but how they point there. The strongest part of his argument is the seamless integration of pop culture and mathematics to demonstrate that the "sense" of a term is the key to cognitive value. The biggest vulnerability remains the potential subjectivity of these "modes of presentation," but for a busy reader, Kaplan provides the essential toolkit to understand why language can teach us about the world rather than just describing it.
