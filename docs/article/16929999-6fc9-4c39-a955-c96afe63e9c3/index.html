This newsletter issue cuts through the hype of "general intelligence" to reveal a quieter, more profound shift: AI is no longer just generating text; it is rewriting the very operating systems that power the digital world. Jack Clark's analysis suggests we are witnessing the birth of self-optimizing infrastructure, where the tools used to build AI are now being built by AI, fundamentally altering the economics and politics of compute.

The Self-Optimizing Infrastructure

Clark begins with a startling development from Meta: a system called KernelEvolve that uses large language models to write the low-level code—kernels—that make AI chips run efficiently. This isn't just automation; it is a recursive loop where AI designs the software that trains the next generation of AI. Clark writes, "The software is 'designed to take kernel specifications as input and automate the process of kernel generation and optimization for recommendation model across heterogeneous hardware architectures'."

The implications are immediate and financial. By automating what used to take teams of engineers weeks to perfect, the system has slashed development time to hours. Clark notes the staggering efficiency gains: "By using this software, Facebook says it has cut the development time of new kernels 'from weeks to hours'." In some specific tasks, the AI-generated code delivered performance improvements of up to 17 times over existing human-written baselines.

This mirrors the historical evolution of the operating system kernel itself, which decades ago moved from manual, assembly-level coding to higher-level abstractions, yet here we are returning to the metal, but with a new architect. The system doesn't just guess; it validates. Clark points out that the software achieved a "100% pass rate on all 250 problems across three difficulty levels" on the public KernelBench suite, a feat that top frontier models struggled to match just months prior.

"Marginal kernel-level performance improvements translate to multi-million dollar reductions in infrastructure operating costs while simultaneously enhancing user engagement metrics that correlate directly with advertising revenue."

The commentary here is sobering. This efficiency isn't abstract; it directly fuels the capacity of the administration's largest data-hungry platforms to monitor and monetize user behavior more effectively. Clark frames this as a "continuously running self-refining system" that will iteratively improve how platforms study human behavior. Critics might argue that focusing on efficiency overlooks the potential for these systems to be repurposed for public good, but the current trajectory is undeniably commercial. The vision, as Clark quotes the researchers, is a future where "LLM agents serve as the universal compilation layer for heterogeneous AI systems," effectively removing the need for human engineers to port code to new hardware.

The Politics of Decentralized Compute

The second major thread explores a counter-trend to the centralization of AI power. While the biggest models are trained by a handful of massive corporations, a parallel ecosystem of decentralized training is growing at an explosive rate. Clark synthesizes data from Epoch AI to show that while decentralized runs are currently tiny compared to frontier models, their growth rate is terrifyingly fast.

"The largest decentralized runs to date have spanned the 6e22-6e23 FLOP range, which they estimate to be 1000x less compute than what was used for Grok 4," Clark writes. Yet, the growth rate is the real story. He notes, "Since 2020, we have seen a 600,000x increase in the computational scale of decentralized training projects, for an implied growth rate of about 20x/year." This dwarfs the 5x annual growth of centralized frontier training.

Clark draws a compelling parallel to the Folding@home project, a decentralized network that once harnessed massive computing power for protein folding, suggesting that today's decentralized AI networks could expand 30 to 3,000 times their current scale. This isn't just about raw numbers; it's about the democratization of the frontier. Clark argues that decentralized training is a "political technology" that challenges the monopoly of the five or six American companies currently defining what AI is possible.

"Decentralized training could still be a very important part of AI. To the extent that decentralized networks remain associated with open weights, they could lead to larger open models to exist trailing the frontier."

The argument here is that while a decentralized network may never match the sheer brute force of a state-backed or corporate data center, it creates a viable path for independent researchers, academics, and non-tech actors to contribute to the frontier. This shifts the geopolitical landscape, potentially allowing entities outside the traditional US-China tech rivalry to punch above their weight. However, a counterargument worth considering is the fragility of these networks; without the capital reserves of a Meta or Google, can they sustain the hardware costs when the chips inevitably wear out?

The Convergence of Intelligence

Perhaps the most philosophical insight comes from the final section, which examines whether different AI systems "think" in the same way. Clark highlights research from MIT suggesting that as models become more capable, their internal representations of the world converge.

Clark explains that researchers analyzed nearly sixty scientific models and found that "representations learned by nearly sixty scientific models, spanning string-, graph-, 3D atomistic, and protein-based modalities, are highly aligned across a wide range of chemical systems." This challenges the idea that AI models are idiosyncratic black boxes. Instead, they seem to be discovering a universal language of physics and matter.

"Models trained on different datasets have highly similar representations of small molecules, and machine learning interatomic potentials converge in representation space as they improve in performance, suggesting that foundation models learn a common underlying representation of physical reality."

This finding supports the "bitter lesson" of AI history: that scale and data, rather than clever human-designed constraints, lead to the most general and powerful models. Clark suggests this could lead to a "democratized representation" of the world, where AI systems hold a shared, rich library of concepts—from elephants to quasars—that humans can access.

"Therefore, in a few years, AI systems may let us 'democratize the building blocks of imagination' - giving all of us one-on-one access to a tool that has the ability to summon within itself a highly descriptive, useful, 'universal representation' of anything we might imagine."

The implication is profound: if all smart systems converge on the same truth, then the "truth" of the physical world becomes a shared, verifiable resource, accessible to anyone with a model. This stands in stark contrast to the current fragmentation of information ecosystems.

Bottom Line

Jack Clark's analysis delivers a dual warning and promise: the infrastructure of the digital age is becoming self-repairing and self-optimizing, driven by the very intelligence it seeks to amplify. The strongest part of the argument is the evidence that decentralized compute is not a fringe experiment but a rapidly scaling political force that could fracture the oligopoly of AI development. The biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that efficiency gains will be distributed; without intervention, the "universal representation" of the world may become the exclusive property of those who own the most efficient kernels. Watch for the moment when a decentralized network first trains a model that rivals a frontier corporate effort—that will be the true inflection point.
