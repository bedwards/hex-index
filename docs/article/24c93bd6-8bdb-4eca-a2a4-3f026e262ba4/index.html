WeeklyStream43: Superliminal Multiplayer Debut!

Benn Jordan · Benn Jordan ·Nov 4, 2021 · 36 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Benn Jordan doesn't just play a video game; he conducts a live, high-stakes stress test on the intersection of perceptual psychology and chaotic multiplayer dynamics. In a world where game updates are often polished marketing stunts, Jordan presents a raw, unfiltered look at a beta build created specifically for a single stream, arguing that the resulting glitches and chaos are not bugs, but features of a genuinely innovative design philosophy. He frames the experience not as a competition to win, but as a "group therapy" session where the real victory is surviving the absurdity of shared reality distortion.

The Architecture of Chaos

Jordan opens by acknowledging the sheer improbability of the event. "We are about to play super liminal multiplayer for the first time publicly," he declares, immediately grounding the audience in the historical weight of the moment. He is transparent about the fragility of the build, warning viewers that "if something goes wrong... it's not the final version" and that the current state is "the result of developers having a lot of headaches." This honesty disarms the typical skepticism reserved for rushed game launches. Instead of hiding the rough edges, he highlights them as evidence of the team's dedication to making a complex mechanic work in real-time.

The core of his commentary revolves around the disorienting nature of the "Group Therapy" mode. He notes that for fans of the original single-player game, the concept of multiplayer initially seems impossible: "When most people who are familiar with Superliminal hear that there's a multiplayer update... their first response is, 'Wait, what? How? How does that work?'" Jordan's coverage is valuable because he doesn't just explain the mechanics; he embodies the confusion. He describes the visual dissonance of seeing other players loading on different hardware and the physical comedy of the gameplay, where "everybody else is apparently loading this on a Super Nintendo" while he struggles with the controls.

"This is like my house... Oh, here comes one. Oh, we have health bars now or I think interesting. This is all new to me."

His observation that the game introduces health bars and competitive elements to a genre defined by solitary puzzle-solving is a crucial insight. He recognizes that the shift changes the fundamental social contract of the game. "Basically it seems like everybody can sabotage one another, which is nice," he admits, capturing the shift from cooperative problem-solving to competitive chaos. This framing is effective because it validates the player's instinct to be helpful while acknowledging the game's design intent to create conflict.

Critics might argue that a beta stream is an unreliable metric for a game's final quality, especially when the developer explicitly warns of instability. However, Jordan's willingness to fail publicly—admitting, "I'm clearly losing like even Cinebrad" and "I'm so dumb"—turns a potential liability into a strength. It humanizes the development process and proves the game's mechanics are robust enough to handle a novice player without breaking entirely.

The Psychology of Sabotage

As the session progresses, Jordan's commentary shifts from technical observation to a philosophical musing on human behavior within the game's logic. He grapples with the ethics of the gameplay loop, noting the in-game instruction to "courteously reposition all obstacles for the next group of patients after you pass through them." He immediately subverts this by realizing the futility of such politeness in a race: "I should have just stuck with the group, I think." This highlights the central tension of the multiplayer mode: the conflict between the game's therapeutic framing and the competitive reality of the players.

Jordan's self-deprecating humor serves as a lens to examine the game's difficulty curve. "I feel like the other players are like watching me play maybe and and they they I'm so bad that they're just like, 'Let's let Ben go because otherwise he's bound to hurt himself in real life or something.'" This moment of empathy from other players underscores the game's ability to foster community even through sabotage. It suggests that the "Group Therapy" title is not just a joke, but a genuine reflection of the emotional rollercoaster the game induces.

He also touches on the unique visual language of the game, marveling at the "minimal arena-like aesthetics" and the way the game manipulates scale. "Oh, I'm tiny now... This is so oddly chaotic," he observes, capturing the disorienting joy of the mechanics. The game forces players to constantly re-evaluate their position in space, a mechanic that Jordan finds both frustrating and exhilarating. "I like how I'm having my own individual experience right now that like nobody else seems to be sharing," he notes, pointing out a fascinating emergent behavior where the shared server creates unique, isolated realities for each participant.

"If this was a competition and not a medical experiment, it would have been tremendously victorious. Not for me."

This line perfectly encapsulates the game's tone. It is a competition that feels like a medical trial, where failure is reframed as data collection. Jordan's acceptance of this premise—"Please look forward to reinterpretations of your numerous mistakes as evidence of hidden brilliance"—shows how well the game's narrative voice permeates the player's experience. He doesn't fight the game's logic; he leans into it, finding humor in the absurdity of being told his failures are actually signs of genius.

The Verdict on a Beta Build

Despite the chaos, Jordan concludes with a strong endorsement of the technical achievement. "I gotta say, man, this runs this runs pretty well for a uh you know, first multiplayer experience like beta that was made just for this," he asserts. He notes that he isn't seeing enough bugs to warrant criticism, calling it "actually pretty pretty incredible work." This is a significant endorsement coming from a streamer who is known for his critical ear and high standards. He acknowledges the difficulty of the task: "this build was made just for this stream," yet it holds up under the pressure of a live audience.

His final thoughts on the gameplay loop are particularly insightful. He realizes that the puzzles are "challenging enough to where this doesn't just become like a sabotage the multiplayer thing." This distinction is vital; it means the game retains its intellectual core even when the social dynamics turn toxic. The puzzles require genuine thought, preventing the mode from devolving into a simple brawl. "I'm stuck. Can I grab this?" he asks, illustrating the moment where the player must choose between brute force and clever manipulation.

"The degree to which you can succeed in life by copying the person in front of you there."

This observation ties the game mechanics back to a broader commentary on human behavior. Jordan sees the game as a microcosm of social learning and competition. Whether one chooses to help or hinder, the game forces a reaction. His conclusion that the game is "fun" despite his lack of success is the ultimate testament to its design. It works even when you lose.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's coverage succeeds because it treats a chaotic beta stream as a serious cultural moment, using his own struggles to illuminate the game's unique blend of psychological trickery and social friction. The strongest part of his argument is the realization that the game's "Group Therapy" framing is not just a gimmick, but a necessary lens to understand the absurdity of competitive puzzle-solving. The biggest vulnerability of the piece is its reliance on a single, unstable build, but Jordan's transparency about this limitation actually strengthens his credibility. Readers should watch for how this multiplayer mode evolves once the "headaches" of development are resolved, as the core mechanic of shared reality distortion has the potential to redefine the genre.
