Best Books Q1 2026

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Apr 5, 2026 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider doesn't just list books; he curates a masterclass in how the past explains the terrifying friction of the present. By weaving together naval history, military innovation, and modern tech leadership, he argues that the most vital lessons for today's founders and strategists are found not in Silicon Valley trend reports, but in the mud of World War I trenches and the barnacle-encrusted hulls of 18th-century frigates.

The Weight of History and the Cost of Vision

Schneider opens his literary survey with a striking observation on the physical reality of early naval power. He highlights Ian Toll's Six Frigates, noting how the author captures the "smell of a frigate slowed by its bottom covered with 'enormous colonies of barnacles, mussels, oysters, and seaweed.'" This sensory detail does more than set the scene; it grounds the grand strategy of the early U.S. Navy in the gritty, biological constraints that actually dictated outcomes. Schneider writes, "Toll is the only historian I've read who is so good with his material that you're not compelled to read more about the subjects he raises because you're that confident that's he done his homework and surfaced the most interesting angles."

The author then pivots to the psychological profile of the explorer, drawing a sharp, provocative parallel between 15th-century captains and modern tech titans. He asks, "What's Bill Gurley trying to fire you from Uber compared to your lieutenant plotting a munity to kill you in your sleep?" This comparison is jarring but effective, forcing the reader to reconsider the "risk" in "risk-taking." Schneider argues that while modern founders face career volatility, historical explorers faced literal death for their hypotheses.

Tech founders today have nothing on the explorer captains from back in the day. These guys had to raise money from monarchs, make split-second life-or-death calls handling their boats, and to rally a team together to do something half the world thought was crazy.

However, Schneider's treatment of Samuel Eliot Morison's Admiral of the Ocean Sea reveals a nuanced tension between historical empathy and moral clarity. He admits that Morison's 1942 biography initially "sells you on him as reasonably inspiring man with an impossible vision," but the narrative quickly curdles as the reality of the second voyage sets in. Schneider notes that "once you get to the second voyage, it's clear that this guy was the worst!" He points out that even a "pretty down-the-middle historian in the 1940s portrays Columbus as a horrendous human being" who "discovers paradise and then realizes the only thing it's good for is gold, so enslaves everyone and wipes out entire populations." This section serves as a reminder that the "vibes" of an era, as Schneider puts it, can be dangerously misleading; the late 1400s offered a "real shot in the arm" for Christendom, but the cost was catastrophic.

The Limits of Tactical Genius

The commentary shifts to military theory with Bruce I. Gudmundsson's WWI Stormtroop Tactics, where Schneider identifies a critical failure of the German Army: the inability to scale tactical innovation into operational victory. He quotes the book's central thesis: "A self-educating officer corps with the freedom to train their units as they saw fit gave the German Army a capacity for self-reform that no other military organization of the time could approach." This capacity for self-reform allowed them to develop infiltration tactics that broke the stalemate of trench warfare, yet Schneider argues it was ultimately insufficient.

He explains that while the Germans could breach the lines, they lacked the mobility to exploit those breaches. "The weapon that kept the German Army from winning a war of maneuver on the western front was not the machine gun but the railroad," Schneider asserts. This is a crucial distinction for any leader focused on "best practices." The author illustrates the futility of the old guard with a chilling anecdote: "One German machine gun crew reported having fired 12,500 rounds a the battle around Loos [where 8000 British died]. When the British started to retreat, the Germans stopped firing out of pity."

No tactical system, however, could solve the fundamental operational problem that the German Army faced in the west — the fact that the enemy's railroads and motor transport columns could always bring up more fresh troops.

Schneider contrasts this with the British officer class, who "worked hard to maintain an air of detached amateurism and snubbed the 'mug' who neglected hunting and polo in favor of maps and military history." This cultural rigidity left them vulnerable to the very innovations the Germans were perfecting. A counterargument worth considering is whether the German failure was truly just about logistics, or if their political leadership's inability to sustain a total war effort was the deeper rot. Schneider hints at this but focuses primarily on the mechanical failure of the "stormtrooper and his tactics" remaining "Germany's forlorn hope" due to a lack of motorized transport.

Command, Control, and the Human Element

The final section tackles the terrifying intersection of technology and human error in the nuclear age and modern corporate leadership. Schneider describes Command and Control as a "horrifying book about the prospect of nuclear accidents," citing the incident near JFK's inauguration where the Air Force was "this close to accidentally dropping a bomb over North Carolina which would have lead to an evacuation of the eastern seaboard." He uses this to frame a "new mental model: the titanic effect but for nukes (and runaway AI)."

He draws a fascinating line from these near-misses to the birth of the computer revolution, noting that "trying to solve command and control issues if the Soviets decided to nuke America is what gave us the computer revolution!" This historical context adds weight to his critique of modern tech leadership. When discussing Bill Gurley's biography, Schneider is less impressed, noting that the book feels like "Gurley giving his friends love" rather than a deep dive into leadership. He critiques the advice as "be a nice good person thank everyone give out good vibes work hard and things will work out," arguing that this glosses over the brutal reality of firing a founder like Travis Kalanick.

It was probably the right thing for Uber and Benchmark's investors to fire Travis but that was a hard decision which isn't just like being nice to everyone!

Schneider's review of Wagner's Tristan und Isolde at the Met offers a final reflection on the value of engaging with difficult, contemporary culture versus the comfort of the canon. He admits the music was "off-putting" and that he felt "stuck on a plane," yet he argues that engaging with it provides a unique connection to the artist's context. He contrasts this with the "worshipful relationship some have with their artistic idols today," noting that "Jewish fans of Kanye probably felt similarly to his Nazi turn as French Wagner stans after he trolled them for s." This comparison suggests that the intensity of modern fandom mirrors the historical fervor for figures like Wagner, regardless of the moral or aesthetic cost.

Bottom Line

Jordan Schneider's curation succeeds because it refuses to treat history as a static backdrop, instead presenting it as a dynamic, often brutal mirror for modern strategic dilemmas. His strongest argument lies in the distinction between tactical brilliance and operational failure, a lesson that resonates far beyond the trenches of World War I. The piece's only vulnerability is its occasional reliance on "vibes" to bridge centuries of cultural context, but the sheer density of insight makes this a rare, high-value read for anyone navigating complex systems today.
