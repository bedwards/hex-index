It's Not Just Shein: Why Are ALL Your Clothes Worse Now?

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Jul 11, 2024 · 17 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union delivers a damning diagnosis of the modern closet, arguing that the decline in clothing quality isn't a personal failure of taste or budget, but the inevitable result of a fifty-year economic shift toward disposability. The piece stands out by juxtaposing the tangible weight of vintage denim against the synthetic lightness of today's bestsellers, proving that "nothing fits properly anymore" is not a body issue, but a design failure. For the busy professional wondering why their $200 suit feels like a $20 one, this analysis connects the dots between trade policy, financial crises, and the fabric in your hand.

The Death of the Seasonal Cycle

The author anchors the argument in a stark statistical reality: Americans now buy five times more clothing than in 1980 while spending half the percentage of their income on it. This inversion forces a trade-off that More Perfect Union exposes with surgical precision. "In the '80s and '90s, stores only had new clothes a couple times a year," the author notes, explaining that the long lead times required designers to "think of hundreds of unique designs and whittle them down to the dozen or so best ones." This scarcity created a natural pressure for quality; if a collection flopped, the financial loss was catastrophic, so every stitch mattered.

The commentary here is compelling because it reframes the old model not as inefficient, but as a risk-management system that inadvertently protected the consumer. The author illustrates this by comparing a 1980s JC Penney suit, which cost the equivalent of $600 today, to modern counterparts that are cheaper but lack the "expertly tailored" craftsmanship. The shift wasn't just about cheaper materials; it was about the elimination of the "game of chance" that forced brands to bet on quality.

"The risk is why so much care and thoughtfulness went into making each piece of clothing, and why you could expect quality at every price point."

Critics might argue that the old system excluded the vast majority of people from trendy or well-made fashion, reserving quality for the wealthy. However, the author effectively counters this by showing how the current "democratization" of fashion has resulted in a race to the bottom where even mid-tier brands like Abercromie have degraded to the point where "everything is just a little bit shittier than you would want it to be."

The Fast Fashion Engine

The narrative then pivots to the disruptor: Zara. More Perfect Union details how this Spanish retailer revolutionized the industry by compressing the design-to-store timeline from nine months to fifteen days. "Zara can send those gray goods to their network of small shops in nearby regions where they're transformed into finished dresses, trousers, and tops," the author explains, highlighting a supply chain that responds to trends in real-time rather than predicting them years in advance.

This section is particularly astute in its distinction between "fast fashion" and "instant fashion." While Zara pioneered the model, the author argues that Shein has taken it to a chaotic extreme, functioning less like a manufacturer and more like an algorithmic marketplace. "Shein will release [35,000 items] in just a couple weeks," the text states, noting that their software treats factories like gig-economy drivers, ramping up production only after real-time sales data confirms demand. This removes the risk of unsold inventory entirely, but at the cost of any regard for durability.

The author's observation that "social media helps shorten our attention spans which extends to fashion trends too" provides a crucial cultural context. The pressure on influencers to wear "a totally unique never-before-seen outfit every single day" creates a feedback loop that makes high-quality, long-lasting garments economically unviable. The piece suggests that the problem isn't just corporate greed, but a fundamental mismatch between the speed of digital culture and the physical reality of textile production.

The Material Consequences

Perhaps the most visceral part of the coverage is the physical comparison of garments. More Perfect Union brings in Amanda McCarty, a former buyer, to validate the sensory experience of the decline. "The longevity of these clothes, even how you feel when you put them on, has degraded so much," McCarty is quoted saying. The author supports this with a side-by-side analysis of vintage versus modern jeans, noting that the old pair weighed 761 grams of pure cotton, while the new pair is a lighter, plastic-heavy blend with a zipper that feels like it will fail soon.

The commentary here effectively highlights the hidden costs of cost-cutting. By swapping natural fibers for synthetics like polyester and elastane, brands have created clothes that "break and the more you wash them, the sooner they break." The author points out the irony that adding stretch—a feature consumers demand—actually shortens the lifespan of the garment, trapping buyers in a cycle of replacement. "It's a pennies game to get the pricing to work with the targets you're given," the author writes, exposing how margin pressures from private equity firms dictate the very texture of our clothing.

"Five years of fast fashion and ultra-fast fashion has completely changed our relationship with clothes. Instead of being something to cherish and care for, they're now just another cheap and disposable plastic consumer good."

A counterargument worth considering is that the affordability of modern clothing allows low-income families to dress their children, a necessity in a world where wages have stagnated. The author acknowledges the financial crisis of 2008 as a catalyst for this shift but focuses heavily on the degradation of quality rather than the accessibility of price. While the emotional toll of disposability is clear, the economic necessity for some consumers remains a complex variable the piece touches on but does not fully resolve.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union's strongest asset is its ability to translate abstract supply chain economics into the tangible feeling of a fraying seam, proving that the decline in quality is a deliberate business strategy, not an accident. The argument's vulnerability lies in its limited exploration of how consumers might opt out of this system without sacrificing affordability, leaving the reader with a clear diagnosis but a difficult prescription. The piece serves as a necessary wake-up call: the clothes we wear are no longer made to last, because the system no longer rewards longevity.
