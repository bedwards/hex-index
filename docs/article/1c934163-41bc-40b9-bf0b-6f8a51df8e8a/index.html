How to punish bad drivers

Dave Amos · City Beautiful ·Feb 28, 2025 ·21 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Amos dismantles the comforting myth that car crashes are mere "accidents" and reveals a justice system that often protects dangerous drivers while punishing the poor. This piece is essential listening because it exposes how our legal framework treats vehicular homicide as a minor infraction while using license suspensions as a blunt instrument against low-income communities, effectively trapping people in a cycle of poverty and danger.

The Accident Narrative

Amos begins by challenging the language we use to describe violence on our roads. He points out that the media and the public often frame crashes as inevitable acts of nature rather than preventable outcomes of human error. "We in the United States at least tend to think of crashes as acts of God rather than consequences of poor driving or poor infrastructure," Amos writes. This framing is critical because it absolves the driver of moral responsibility. By calling it an "accident," society implicitly suggests that no one is to blame.

The author argues that this narrative is actively perpetuated by news outlets that focus on whether a victim was wearing a helmet rather than the driver's actions. "The media perpetuates the accident narrative it often goes so far as to victim blame further taking the heat off the driver," he notes. This is a powerful observation. It shifts the burden of safety entirely onto the vulnerable road user, ignoring the fact that a vehicle is a lethal weapon controlled by a human. The evidence here is strong: the language we use directly correlates with the leniency of our legal responses.

"There's an old dark joke in transportation planning that says that if you want to get away with murder you should hit someone with your car."

The Justice System's Blind Spot

When bad drivers are caught, the path to punishment is fraught with obstacles. Amos explains that juries, composed of fellow drivers, often sympathize with defendants because they recognize their own capacity for error. "Prosecutors need an ironclad case to convince 12 jurors jurors who were likely drivers that a murder or vehicular manslaughter charge should stick," he writes. This psychological reality leads to a system where plea deals are the norm, even for egregious offenses. He cites a New York case where a driver who killed a pedestrian and fled the scene received probation and community service, only serving jail time after being arrested for an unrelated sex crime.

The core of the argument is that our laws reflect a societal bias toward drivers. "Our laws are lenient because we believe drivers deserve leniency," Amos asserts. This is a difficult truth to swallow, but the data supports it. In California, negligent vehicular manslaughter can be a misdemeanor with a maximum sentence of only one year. A counterargument worth considering is that the criminal justice system is designed to require high burdens of proof to prevent wrongful convictions, but Amos suggests the threshold for vehicular crimes is set impossibly high compared to other violent acts. The result is that the punishment rarely fits the crime.

The Punishment Paradox

The piece takes a sharp turn into the unintended consequences of license suspensions. Amos contrasts the experience of losing a license in Manhattan versus rural Wisconsin. In a city with robust transit, a suspension is a manageable inconvenience. In car-dependent areas, it is a life-ruining event. "One punishment a license suspension has now turned into a real life ruiner," he writes. This highlights a massive disconnect between criminal justice policy and the reality of the built environment.

He reveals a shocking statistic: up to 91% of people who lose their licenses do so for non-driving related offenses, such as failure to pay fines or appear in court. "The most common reason for losing a license in the US is failure to pay a fine," Amos states. This transforms license suspension from a safety measure into a revenue collection tool that disproportionately impacts low-income individuals and communities of color. The author notes that wealthy people can simply pay their way out of these penalties, while others are trapped.

"People still need to get to work drop kids off at school and do all the other things people drive for so they risk getting caught and drive anyway."

Critics might argue that strict enforcement is necessary to maintain order, but Amos points out that this approach creates a paradox where people drive illegally just to survive. Interestingly, he cites a study finding that people driving with suspended licenses often drive more cautiously than the average driver, simply because they are terrified of getting caught. This suggests that suspension does not necessarily remove dangerous drivers from the road; it just removes their legal status.

Targeted Solutions vs. Blunt Instruments

Amos concludes by arguing that a one-size-fits-all approach to punishment fails. Instead, strategies must be tailored to the specific type of bad behavior. He focuses on drunk driving as a clear example where current policies are failing. He uses his home state of Wisconsin as a case study, noting that the state's DUI laws are "extremely lenient" due to the influence of the Tavern League. "Wisconsin's DUI arrest rate is 43.37 per 100,000 people the rest of the United States averages 28.7 per 100,000," he writes, highlighting a direct correlation between weak laws and high arrest rates.

The argument here is that we need to stop treating all bad driving as the same. While suspending licenses for unpaid fines hurts the poor and keeps dangerous drivers on the road, failing to punish drunk drivers severely allows a known, high-risk behavior to continue unchecked. The solution lies in precision: harsh penalties for those who choose to drive impaired, and reform for those who are penalized for poverty.

Bottom Line

Amos's strongest contribution is exposing the hypocrisy of a system that treats vehicular homicide as a minor traffic violation while using license suspensions to punish poverty. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on anecdotal examples for the severity of leniency, though the statistical trends he cites regarding suspension rates are compelling. The reader should watch for how states begin to decouple license suspensions from non-driving offenses, a reform that is gaining traction but faces significant political hurdles. This is a necessary correction to the way we think about road safety and justice.
