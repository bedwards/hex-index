What Does it Really Mean to "Find Your Voice?"

Jeannine Ouellette · Writing in the Dark ·Mar 31, 2026 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jeannine Ouellette dismantles the most persistent myth in creative writing: that 'finding your voice' is a moment of sudden discovery, like walking into a room where the lights are already on. Instead, she argues that voice is a construction project built from wreckage, secrecy, and the slow, often painful accumulation of lived experience. For the busy professional or the aspiring writer who feels stuck, this reframing is not just comforting; it is a strategic roadmap that validates the messy, non-linear work of becoming a writer.

The Architecture of a Chorus

Ouellette rejects the idea of a singular, fixed identity on the page. She writes, "The voice we are searching for on the page is not simply 'our voice,' as though such a thing exists whole and waiting." This is a crucial distinction. It shifts the writer's goal from excavation to assembly. She suggests that a writer possesses a "chorus" of voices gathered over decades, and the true skill lies in discerning which one belongs to which story.

This argument gains unexpected depth when Ouellette anchors it in the physical spaces of her youth. She recalls her twelfth summer on a "widow's walk," a railed rooftop platform common on 19th-century New England coastal homes where wives once watched for returning husbands. In her hands, this historical structure of waiting and longing transforms into a site of creative permission. "The rooftop was a kind of permission: that language could be important, that our voices counted," she notes. By invoking the widow's walk, she subtly connects the isolation of the writer to a historical lineage of women waiting and watching, suggesting that the act of writing has always been an act of endurance.

Voice is not a single thing. A writer does not have a voice the way a person has a fingerprint, fixed and unchanging.

Critics might argue that this 'chorus' approach risks diluting the concept of authorship, making it seem like a mere collage of influences rather than a unique perspective. However, Ouellette counters this by emphasizing the rigor required to manage these voices. It is not about having many voices, but about the "hard-won" skill of knowing which one to deploy.

The Necessity of the Basement

Perhaps the most striking part of Ouellette's coverage is her insistence that survival often precedes craft. She describes moving into her father's basement during junior high, a place where her voice "grew small" but where the act of writing became a lifeline. "To write in secret is to write in a space that belongs entirely to you," she observes. This is a profound insight for anyone who has ever felt their creativity stifled by external expectations or the need to be "acceptable."

She contrasts this with the "pretty" essays she wrote during early motherhood, which she admits were a form of circling around the truth rather than facing it. "I was reaching for beauty the way you reach for a railing on a dark staircase," she writes, "not because the railing is the destination, but because you need it to keep moving." This metaphor perfectly captures the transitional phase many writers experience: the use of style as a shield before the courage to be raw emerges. The argument here is that the 'bad' writing, the secret notebooks, and the circling are not detours; they are the necessary foundation.

The secret notebooks in the basement were no detours. They were the work.

This perspective challenges the modern obsession with immediate productivity and polished outputs. It suggests that the "dry stretches" and "lifeless words" mentioned earlier are not failures, but part of the "accumulation" process. The author's framing is effective because it removes the shame from the early, clumsy stages of writing.

The Paradox of the Child Narrator

Ouellette illustrates the power of finding the right voice with a specific technical breakthrough: the arrival of a child narrator. She explains that for years she tried to write about her childhood through the lens of an adult who had "processed" the trauma. But the adult voice was "too knowing." It had "gotten ahead of the material."

The solution came when she allowed a child narrator to speak with "deliberate unknowings." This voice could report on the terrible things of the world without the "mediation of retrospect." She writes, "A child narrator can say, 'I don't believe in jackalopes. But people have to make up their own minds about these things.'" This single sentence, she argues, holds the paradox of childhood—living in a world that is both magical and brutal—without resolving the tension. This is a masterclass in how voice serves the story, not the ego of the writer.

The argument here is that the 'true' voice is often the one that feels most vulnerable or unpolished, not the one that sounds the most sophisticated. It is a reminder that technical precision must eventually serve emotional honesty. As she puts it, "Sometimes what takes us our voice away... is the very thing that eventually drive us back to the page to reclaim the voice we lost."

Bottom Line

Jeannine Ouellette's most powerful contribution is her redefinition of 'voice' from a static trait to a dynamic, cumulative practice built on survival and specific historical moments. While the essay leans heavily on personal anecdote, which may feel abstract to those seeking a technical formula, its strength lies in validating the messy, non-linear reality of the writing life. The reader's takeaway should be this: your voice is not waiting to be found; it is waiting to be built, brick by brick, from the very things you thought were holding you back.

The Voices I Have Lived In

A craft essay

The writing life does not proceed linearly—and this is both its great frustration and its great mercy. It accumulates gradually—long dry stretches, sudden storms, seasons when the light is strange and everything you write seems to shimmer, seasons when the words land lifeless and you bury them in drawers. The voice we are searching for on the page is not simply “our voice,” as though such a thing exists whole and waiting, like a room with the lights already on. The voice is something we construct from wreckage and from wonder, from the places we have written and the places we have been too afraid to write ...
