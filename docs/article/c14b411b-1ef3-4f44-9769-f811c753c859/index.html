Jeffrey Kaplan tackles one of the most dense chapters in legal philosophy not by simplifying the jargon, but by exposing the radical, almost absurd simplicity at the heart of John Austin's theory of law. The most striking claim here isn't that law is complex, but that Austin attempts to explain it entirely through the lens of a bank robber with a gun. This is a bold move that forces the reader to confront whether legal obligation is truly just a matter of observable behavior and threats, or if it requires something more internal and psychological.

The Gunman as Legal Theory

Kaplan frames the entire chapter around a thought experiment that starts in a bank and ends in a parliament. He writes, "The gunman scenario is described purely behavioristically. It's described purely in terms of things that would be acceptable to a certain kind of naturalist." This framing is brilliant because it strips away the mystique of law immediately. By grounding the discussion in a scenario where a man says, "Put the money in the bag or I'm going to shoot you," Kaplan forces us to see the raw mechanics of coercion before we layer on the complexity of statutes.

The author explains that this approach is rooted in a specific philosophical commitment: naturalism. Kaplan notes, "Naturalism is the view that the only things that exist in the world are the things that natural science tells us exist." He extends this to behaviorism, where even private mental states like "hopes and desires" are considered "spooky" and excluded from serious scientific theory. This is a crucial context often missed in standard summaries; Austin isn't just describing law, he is trying to make law fit the rigid constraints of early 20th-century psychology. As Kaplan puts it, "The theory of law that we're going to get from Austin is fundamentally a behaviorist theory of law."

Critics might argue that reducing law to behavior ignores the very thing that makes it law: the belief in its legitimacy. However, Kaplan's commentary effectively shows that Austin's goal was to build a system that looks like law using only observable bricks and mortar.

The theory of law that we're going to get from Austin is fundamentally a behaviorist theory of law.

From Specific Threats to General Laws

The core of Kaplan's analysis lies in how he dissects the five modifications Hart makes to the gunman scenario to transform it into a legal system. The first modification shifts the focus from a specific demand to a general rule. Kaplan highlights Hart's observation: "The standard form, even of a criminal statute, is general in two ways. It indicates a general type of conduct... and it applies to a general class of persons."

This distinction is vital. A gunman demands money now from you. A law demands taxes every April 15th from anyone over eighteen. Kaplan paraphrases this shift effectively, noting that the content of the order changes from a specific instance to a "general type of behavior." This move allows the theory to scale from a single interaction to a societal system without invoking any new, unobservable concepts. It remains purely about sounds coming out of mouths and patterns of movement.

The second modification addresses the persistence of the threat. Kaplan writes, "Disobedience is likely to be followed by the execution of the threat not only on the first promulgation of the order but continuously until the order is withdrawn or cancelled." He contrasts this with a bar fight threat, which is fleeting. A legal system, under this view, requires a threat that sticks around indefinitely. This is a clever way to explain the concept of enduring legal authority without using the word "authority" at all.

The Illusion of the Sovereign

As the scenario evolves, Kaplan introduces the concept of efficacy. He quotes Hart: "We must suppose that whatever the motive, most of the orders are more often obeyed by most of those affected." This is the point where the gunman scenario starts to feel less like a crime and more like a state. The theory relies on the statistical reality that people follow orders, not on their moral agreement with them.

The final modifications, which Kaplan admits are "harder to understand," deal with the nature of the person giving the orders. He explains that these changes turn the gunman into a "sovereign" through the concepts of supremacy and internal supremacy. Kaplan notes, "Within the territory of each country, there may be many different persons or bodies of persons giving general orders backed by threats and receiving habitual obedience." The theory culminates in a system where the sovereign is the one who is habitually obeyed and obeys no one else.

This is where the behaviorist approach hits its limit, a point Kaplan hints at but saves for later chapters. The system is built entirely on external observation: who speaks, who listens, and who gets punished. As Kaplan summarizes, "We never introduce anything like a private uh psychological mental state... we never introduce any lofty normative notions like duty or authority." The result is a legal system that functions like a machine, driven by habits and threats, entirely devoid of the internal sense of "ought" that most people feel when they obey the law.

We never introduce anything like a private uh psychological mental state... we never introduce any lofty normative notions like duty or authority.

Critics might note that this description feels incomplete because it misses the reason people obey. Is it fear, or is it a sense of duty? Kaplan's commentary suggests that Austin's theory deliberately ignores this distinction, which is precisely why Hart will later spend chapters dismantling it. The behaviorist model is elegant in its simplicity but perhaps too simple to capture the reality of legal obligation.

Bottom Line

Kaplan's commentary succeeds in making a notoriously dry chapter accessible by treating the "gunman on the bank" scenario as a serious philosophical tool rather than a mere illustration. The strongest part of his argument is the clear exposition of how Austin attempts to build a legal system out of nothing but observable behavior and persistent threats. The biggest vulnerability, which Kaplan foreshadows but does not resolve, is that this model may explain why people do obey, but fails to explain why they feel they should obey. Readers should watch for how Hart eventually uses these very gaps to introduce the internal point of view in later chapters.
