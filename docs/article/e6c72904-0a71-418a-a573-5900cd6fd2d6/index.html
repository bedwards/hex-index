The String Theory Wars and What Happened Next

Sabine Hossenfelder · Sabine Hossenfelder ·Mar 9, 2024 · 20 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sabine Hossenfelder delivers a rare, unvarnished autopsy of modern physics' most ambitious failure: the decades-long pursuit of String Theory as a 'Theory of Everything.' Unlike standard scientific retrospectives that soften the blow of failed hypotheses, Hossenfelder argues that the field's collapse wasn't just a lack of data, but a systemic failure of scientific integrity driven by careerism and mathematical vanity. For the busy professional tracking the frontiers of knowledge, this is a crucial case study in how a community can convince itself it is on the verge of a breakthrough while actually drifting into a 'landscape' of untestable speculation.

The Accidental Contender

Hossenfelder begins by dismantling the romanticized origin story of String Theory. She notes that the theory was not born from a desire to unify gravity and quantum mechanics, but rather as a clumsy attempt to model the strong nuclear force inside protons. "String Theory it was supposed to be physicist's crowning achievement a theory that explains no less than everything with just one simple and elegant idea," she writes, immediately undercutting the premise by revealing its accidental nature. The theory only became a candidate for a 'Theory of Everything' when researchers noticed its mathematical strings could behave like gravitons—the theoretical particles of gravity.

The core of her argument here is that the theory's rise was fueled by its mathematical versatility rather than empirical success. She explains that once the theory was adopted, it required a series of increasingly desperate fixes to avoid contradicting reality. "String theorists needed a new symmetry super symmetry super symmetry fixed the problem just fine but it required that each of the particles in the stard model had a partner particle which couldn't be one of the already observed ones," Hossenfelder states. This is a critical point: the theory didn't predict new particles; it invented them to save itself from being wrong.

Critics might argue that the lack of observed super-symmetric particles could simply mean the energy scales are higher than our current colliders can reach, a standard defense in high-energy physics. However, Hossenfelder's framing suggests this is a moving target designed to indefinitely delay falsification.

The Landscape of Ambiguity

As the narrative moves to the mathematical hurdles, Hossenfelder exposes the sheer scale of the theory's failure to be unique. The requirement for ten dimensions of space, with six of them "rolled up," created a problem of infinite possibilities. "At this point the idea that strength theory was UN unique and which just spit out the standard model went out of the window," she observes. Instead of a single, elegant solution, the math produced an estimated 10 to the 500 different versions of reality.

I've always found that to be a particularly idiotic move just because you can't figure out which Theory describes reality doesn't mean all of them are real and in any case it didn't solve any problem because they did as a matter of fact not have the theory that they were looking for.

This is the piece's most biting critique. Hossenfelder argues that the 'String Theory landscape'—the postulation that all 10^500 versions exist simultaneously—was a retreat from the scientific method. She contends that the community stayed alive not because of evidence, but because the mathematics was so rich and unexplored that it provided endless material for academic papers. "It's simply because it was just mathematically so rich," she writes, suggesting that the field became a self-sustaining ecosystem of publication rather than a path to truth.

The String Wars and the Failure of Prediction

The commentary shifts to the 'String Wars' of the late 1990s and 2000s, a period Hossenfelder describes as a desperate, and ultimately damaging, attempt to make the theory testable. She details how prominent physicists, including Lisa Randall and Nima Arkani-Hamed, proposed that extra dimensions might be large enough to be detected by the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). "The key for getting this nonsense past peer review was to invent reasons for why this supposed evidence for string theory had not been seen so far but would show up in the next experiment," Hossenfelder writes, using strong language to describe the pressure to produce results.

She reveals her own involvement in this era, noting that she wrote her PhD thesis on testing these large extra dimensions before abandoning the field. "I myself wrote my PhD thesis on how to test those large extra dimensions of string through at the LHC then I unceremoniously dropped off the bandwagon," she admits. This personal anecdote lends significant weight to her critique; she is not an outsider looking in, but a former insider who watched the bubble burst.

When the LHC turned on and found nothing—no super-symmetry, no extra dimensions, no string balls—the theoretical foundation crumbled. "The result of the string Wass and the following failure of the LHC to see even a shred of evidence in support of it was that the branch of string theory which contained the last remains of the original idea to find Theory of Everything basically died off," she concludes. The fallout was ugly, with prominent theorists resorting to ad hominem attacks against critics like Lee Smolin and Peter Woit rather than addressing their arguments.

The Mathematical Pivot: AdS/CFT

In the final section, Hossenfelder addresses the current state of the field: a pivot toward the AdS/CFT correspondence, a mathematical duality discovered by Juan Maldacena. This correspondence links string theory in a specific type of space (anti-de Sitter space) to a quantum field theory without gravity in a lower dimension. While she acknowledges the mathematical beauty of this discovery, she remains skeptical of its utility for understanding our actual universe.

"His idea became known as the ads CFT correspondence... it's sometimes said to be a sort of Hol graphic principle because it expresses this three-dimensional space with information on a two-dimensional slice kind of like Holograms," Hossenfelder explains. However, she quickly points out the disconnect: our universe has a positive cosmological constant, while the AdS/CFT correspondence works best in a universe with a negative one. "It's not that it's a space which petitions against disit it just means that the sign of that constant is negative," she clarifies, highlighting that the theory's most successful application is in a mathematical model that does not resemble our physical reality.

Critics might note that AdS/CFT has proven incredibly useful as a tool for solving complex problems in other areas of physics, such as condensed matter physics, regardless of its direct application to quantum gravity. Hossenfelder acknowledges the mathematical richness but implies that this utility is a distraction from the original goal of unifying the forces of nature.

Bottom Line

Sabine Hossenfelder's strongest argument is her unflinching diagnosis of a field that prioritized mathematical elegance and career preservation over empirical falsifiability. Her biggest vulnerability lies in her dismissal of the AdS/CFT correspondence as a dead end for cosmology, potentially overlooking its profound utility as a theoretical tool in other domains. For the reader, the takeaway is clear: when a theory requires 10^500 fixes to match reality, it may be time to stop looking for the theory of everything and start looking for the theory of something else.
