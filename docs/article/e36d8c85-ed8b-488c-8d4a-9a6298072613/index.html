Alexander Sorondo delivers a searing indictment not of the bootleggers, but of the very institution tasked with stopping them, arguing that Prohibition didn't create crime—it created a vacuum where the state itself became the most violent actor on the street. By focusing on the mundane, brutal reality of 1921 Nashport, Sorondo reframes the era's defining policy not as a moral crusade, but as a systemic failure that incentivized police corruption and empowered the very criminals the law claimed to fight.

The Architecture of Impunity

Sorondo opens by dismantling the romanticized image of the gangster, replacing it with a gritty portrait of a city where the line between law enforcement and criminal enterprise has not just blurred, but vanished. He writes, "If in this modern world of 1921 they've gotta decide whether to raise their families in a world of cops or Carmellos, the choice isn't looking so obvious as maybe six months prior." This is the piece's most provocative claim: that the administration's enforcement strategy has made the criminal underworld a more predictable, and perhaps even preferable, alternative to the police.

The author's analysis of the economic incentives driving this shift is particularly sharp. He notes that with a salary of roughly $1,800 a year, an officer is underpaid for dangerous work, while the public is simultaneously criminalized for a universal desire: alcohol. "Because contrary to what the buttoned-up pols would have you believe, here's the truth: while all these godless drinkers are sitting at home, sipping tasteless hooch over a jigsaw, people are still getting murdered," Sorondo observes. The argument lands with force because it exposes the absurdity of a policy that targets the consumer rather than the supplier, effectively clogging the courts with petty offenders while leaving the major operators untouched.

"If your plan, as a '20s cop of the Nashport or New York or any other PD in a metro area, is to arrest every person you see buying alcohol, it'll be the only thing you do, ever, and you still won't make a dent."

This framing connects directly to the historical context of the era. Just as the temperance movement of 1921 marched with picket signs declaring "Lips That Touch Liquor Shall Not Touch Ours," the enforcement machinery they demanded ended up punishing the most vulnerable. Sorondo illustrates this by describing how the policy disproportionately impacts families who are already struggling, turning a single mother's kitchen into a crime scene while the "big-timers" operate with impunity. Critics might argue that the author downplays the genuine public health concerns regarding bathtub gin, but the evidence he presents regarding the sheer volume of arrests suggests the policy was never about safety—it was about political theater.

The Human Cost of Enforcement

The narrative takes a darker turn as Sorondo details the physical violence inflicted upon children, the most vulnerable demographic in this ecosystem. He describes the introduction of "Carney Company handcuffs," a new tool that removed the physical limitation of children being too small to shackle. "The Carneys ignite a new tension with cops and newsboys," he writes, noting that these "proper bracelets" meant no more slipping free. This detail is crucial; it signals a shift from a culture of rough policing to one of systematic containment.

The piece culminates in a harrowing account of the beating of Martin Hapcomb, a newsboy who resisted a cop's attempt to steal his papers. Sorondo describes the violence with unflinching clarity: "The cop's hand isn't going that way, outward. It's flashing upward... In a swift liquid move like a dancer he cross-drew the truncheon off his belt and arched it up, high up, then whipped it back down, straight... bending at the waist and hurling the weight of his back and legs and shoulder into a swing that brought this truncheon down on Marty Hapcomb's crown like to cleave it." The image of the officer then casually picking up the stolen paper from the child's chest and walking away serves as a devastating metaphor for the entire system: the law is not about justice, but about the theft of dignity.

This section resonates deeply when viewed through the lens of the "Newsboys" history, where the struggle for autonomy was often met with state violence. Sorondo's choice to focus on the aftermath—Marty's coma, the permanent disability, the community's resignation—grounds the abstract policy debate in a visceral human tragedy. It forces the reader to confront the reality that the "war on crime" was, in practice, a war on the poor.

The Paradox of Prohibition

The core of Sorondo's argument is that Prohibition created a paradox where the state's attempt to enforce the law actually fueled the rise of organized crime. He explains that by arresting small-timers and filling the jails, the police inadvertently cleared the market for figures like Donte Carmello. "What the dimebooks and the pols and the papers never mention about speakeasies, is the price," Sorondo writes, pointing out that the average worker could not afford the inflated costs of black-market alcohol, meaning the "speakeasies" were largely a myth for the working class.

Instead, the policy drove the market underground, where violence became the only mechanism for dispute resolution. "Violence means pressure on cops to crack down on organized crime," he notes, but then immediately undercuts this logic: "OK but you're still prosecuting the small-timers, cuz those're easy numbers, so now you need more cops." This cycle of escalation leads to a degradation of the police force itself. As the demand for officers grows, vetting is abandoned, and "people start getting hired to the police force who, any other time in history, are supposed to be running from the police force."

"The supply of alcohol, confiscated and ('hey where'd it go!') chronically misplaced by officers of the law, falls below the demand for alcohol. This means the price of booze goes way high, which in turn means there's all these households on hard times where, guess what, the teenage boys are getting propositioned by some local fat cat..."

This analysis effectively dismantles the official narrative that the executive branch's crackdown was a necessary evil. Instead, Sorondo presents a picture of a system that is self-perpetuating and self-defeating, where the only winners are the criminals and the corrupt officials who profit from the chaos.

Bottom Line

Sorondo's strongest asset is his ability to weave the macro-level policy failures of Prohibition into the micro-level tragedies of individual lives, making the abstract concrete and the historical immediate. His biggest vulnerability is the lack of counter-narratives from the law enforcement perspective, though the overwhelming evidence of systemic corruption makes such a defense difficult. The piece serves as a stark warning: when the law becomes an instrument of political pandering rather than justice, it does not protect the community—it destroys it.

Candycane casket

by Alexander Sorondo · · Read full article

Everybody knows Donte Carmello’s a criminal. But the crime’s not interesting. The reason he makes papers is he’s mysterious. Gray fedora with a black or white velvet band. Big scar up his cheek and over the brow. Driven day and night on these long slow laps around the city. Sitting in the back with a skinny brown cigar, misted by smoke, and his car’s got this dull bloated glide to it on account of there’s armor plating in the doors. Two thousand pounds. And so folks’ll pause on sidewalks or kick back on a tenement’s porch or set their chins on a third-story windowsill to catch sight of that jungle-green Cadillac purring past.

Fedora’d silhouette inside. Paintjob fresh and grabbing sun like a candycane casket.

People see it and think, Must be some kinda guy.

But then the car’s gone and they see a buncha kids craning their necks as it goes.

“Hey!” Some geezer shouting from a barbershop stoop, “Don’t look up to a guy like that.” Sweeping a sidewalk as he says it. Kids walk away like, Yeah. Sure thing.

Cuz the other thing they like about Carmello apart from the mystery and the glamor is he’s generous. Like there’s a story that he’s getting driven around through the rain one day and sees a buncha paperboys huddling under a storefront, curled over the bundles of newspaper, and he sees they need shelter. Can’t say no to it. See, those’re Krohly Diary papers they’re selling. The only penny-daily in the city. Printers advise reading it with your face averted so’s your breath won’t soak it. These newsboys, in a downpour like this one, even just the moisture in the air is enough to set the pages’ edges dissolving into wormy gray flecks.

Which means they have to protect those papers long enough to get them sold. Not because it’s their “job” to sell papers, or they’ll get in “trouble” with anybody; only consequence is they’ll lose the investment.

The thing nobody mentions about paperboys is they gotta buy those papers.

Go look at the Krohly Diary offices every morning at daybreak. The couple dozen kids lined up in the cold, hands in their coat pockets, paddy caps pulled snug on their heads. Chewing licorice with small steaming bites. Passing around a thermos with coffee and maybe a splash of some golden thing they pilfered from the fire extinguisher ...
