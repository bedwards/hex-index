CNN's "100% MAGA Trump approval" segment omits key nuance for virality

G. Elliott Morris · G. Elliott Morris ·Mar 22, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

G. Elliott Morris exposes a dangerous alchemy in modern political journalism: the transformation of a statistical tautology into a viral weapon. While the White House celebrates a CNN segment claiming Donald Trump holds 100% approval among "MAGA Republicans," Morris argues this number is not a sign of unity, but a mathematical illusion that hides a crumbling coalition. For the busy observer trying to gauge the true state of the Republican party, this distinction is the difference between reading a headline and understanding the political reality.

The Illusion of 100%

The core of Morris's argument dismantles the logic behind Harry Enten's viral claim. He writes, "Asking whether they approve of Trump is like polling Cubs fans on whether they like the Cubs, and then reporting that the Cubs are popular." This analogy cuts through the noise, revealing that the 100% figure is a result of self-selection bias rather than genuine consensus. By defining the group as those who already identify with the movement, the poll guarantees the outcome, rendering the data useless for predicting actual political shifts.

Morris points out that this approach actively obscures the "erosion happening in Trump's coalition." He notes, "Anyone disillusioned enough with Trump to stop calling themselves MAGA would drop out of the MAGA self-identifier cohort entirely, and then not show up in those numbers." This is a critical insight. It suggests that the metric isn't measuring support; it is measuring the shrinking pool of the most ardent loyalists. A counterargument worth considering is that political branding is fluid, and perhaps the "MAGA" label is simply too narrow to capture the full spectrum of Trump's base. However, Morris's data suggests this narrowing is a vulnerability, not a strength.

The 100% figure doesn't mean nobody is leaving. It means the people who leave stop being counted.

The comparison to the 1972 Miami Dolphins—a team that went undefeated but is often cited in discussions of perfection and anomaly—highlights the statistical absurdity of the claim. Just as the Dolphins' perfect season was a unique historical outlier, a 100% approval rating in a polarized political environment is a statistical impossibility that signals a methodological flaw rather than a political miracle.

The Real Fracture Line

Once the "100%" myth is dispelled, Morris shifts to the actual battleground: the non-MAGA Republicans. He writes, "If there is a 'MAGA civil war,' this is where it is happening — not among the 100% who selected into the label, but among the rest of the party beyond the MAGA core." This reframing is essential. It moves the story from a false narrative of total unity to a more complex, and dangerous, reality of internal division.

The evidence presented shows that while 59% of Republicans identify as MAGA, the remaining 40% are where the "slippage is." Morris cites data indicating that net approval of Trump among non-MAGA Republicans has dropped significantly, with nearly 27% holding an unfavorable view. This is not a minor fluctuation; it represents a massive segment of the party that is no longer reliably in the tank.

Furthermore, Morris challenges the assumption that overall approval equates to agreement on specific policies. He writes, "Approval is not the same as agreement." Even among the loyal MAGA base, support for the war in Iran is not unanimous, with only 87% approving of Trump's handling of the conflict and a mere 35% favoring ground troops. This nuance is vital for understanding the limits of Trump's power.

The groups that will decide November are independents and non-MAGA Republicans, not the 100% who selected into the label.

Critics might argue that in a polarized era, "soft partisans" are less relevant than the hard-core base that drives turnout. Yet, Morris's data on the generational crack—where support for the war among young Trump voters is barely positive—suggests that even the base is not monolithic. The erosion of support among younger voters and non-MAGA Republicans could prove fatal in a general election where margins matter.

The Economics of Attention

Finally, Morris critiques the media ecosystem that rewards this kind of misleading coverage. He writes, "In a media environment where attention is the only currency, unfortunately, we can expect this to continue." The cycle is self-perpetuating: a shareable number is generated, the White House amplifies it, and conservative media validates it, creating a feedback loop that prioritizes virality over accuracy.

This dynamic disincentivizes the kind of nuanced reporting that would reveal the true state of the party. Morris notes that "Enten has few incentives to dwell on the nuances and caveats in the data he's presenting." The result is a political discourse where the most optimistic, and often most misleading, numbers are the ones that dominate the conversation.

Spinning the numbers in this way just gives the White House ammo to keep fighting its public relations battle against what the broader public wants.

The piece serves as a reminder that in the age of algorithmic news, the most viral story is rarely the most true. The 100% approval rating is a perfect storm of self-selection, media incentives, and political spin, designed to obscure the very real fractures that could define the next election cycle.

Bottom Line

Morris's strongest argument is his deconstruction of the "100%" figure as a self-fulfilling prophecy that masks the erosion of Trump's broader coalition. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that non-MAGA Republicans are a cohesive voting bloc that can be easily swayed, which may underestimate the power of partisan loyalty. Readers should watch for how the "soft partisan" vote shifts as the Iran war continues, as this is the true bellwether for the Republican party's future.
