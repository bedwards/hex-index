Described derisively, the National Football League acts as a structure through which young men can sell the entertainment value derived from the destruction of their brains. Detractors and fans would debate that statement endlessly, but to the league itself, what's true hardly matters. What matters is what people believe. One can hardly overstate just how colossal the NFL is.

Whether by revenue per team, revenue per game, or just revenue outright, it is by far the largest sports league in the world. Its annual revenue is similar to that of McDonald's. Just one team, the Dallas Cowboys, is about as big financially as Planet Fitness, the largest gym chain in the world. The only live broadcast to have ever been viewed by more Americans than a Super Bowl was the Apollo 11 moon landing.

Well, each week about one in every 16 Americans tunes in to their Sunday Night Football broadcast, making it the most successful TV program to have ever existed. But this supremacy is facing a reckoning because more and more people are coming to believe that American football is bad. More and more people are being convinced by the scientific evidence that American football frequently causes permanent damage to the brain, leading to debilitating cognitive dysfunction later in life. The immediate risk to the league's financial interests is not that people are going to tune out on mass out of discomfort with the way their dollars incentivize neural destruction.

Football has always been perceived as a dangerous sport. It's something different, but something that the NFL is taking seriously as one of the biggest long-term threats to America's most popular contact sport. But as a point of comparison, take ice hockey, America's second most popular contact sport. While the National Hockey League has admitted the danger of brain injuries in its sport and implemented changes to mitigate their impact, the league appears far less concerned than the NFL in the threat to its financials.

But on the surface level, it looks like they should be. According to Harvard research into the matter, the NFL has a per game per player concussion rate that's 3.8 times higher than that of the NHL. In a given game, a professional football player is far likelier to get a concussion than a professional hockey player.
