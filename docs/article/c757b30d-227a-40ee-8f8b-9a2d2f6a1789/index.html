Deep dives· Original article →

Dan Williams offers a rare, unsentimental audit of the modern information ecosystem, arguing that the crisis of expertise stems not from algorithmic manipulation alone, but from the raw, unfiltered democratization of bad ideas. In a landscape obsessed with "fixing" social media, Williams suggests the real problem is that the platform has finally given a megaphone to the ugly, illiberal, and misinformed views that were always there, just previously silenced by gatekeepers.

The Democratization of Bad Ideas

Williams opens his review by dismantling the comforting narrative that technology is the primary villain in our political decay. He writes, "Much of the discourse about how social media is terrible blames engagement-maximising algorithms... I argue that a bigger factor is simply that social media has radically democratised media." This is a provocative pivot. By shifting the blame from the code to the user base, Williams forces a confrontation with the uncomfortable reality that many people hold "ugly, illiberal, misinformed, and generally bad views."

The argument gains traction when he applies this logic to the "crisis of expertise." He posits that hostility toward knowledge-producing institutions is driven by "feelings of humiliation and resentment among conservative voters with low levels of education, who view experts as a condescending and hostile social class." This framing echoes the work of Thorstein Veblen, whose concept of conspicuous consumption inspired the blog's title, suggesting that status anxiety is a more potent political force than policy disagreement.

"The truth is not self-evident, even though we are often disposed to think that it is."

This observation is the bedrock of Williams' entire year's output. He suggests that we are not dealing with a simple lack of information, but a complex failure of how we process testimony and trust. He notes that "Reality is vast and complex, much more complex than we can even imagine, and we access it not directly but through messy, often-opaque chains of testimony." This is a crucial corrective to the "conspiracy theory of ignorance," a concept he revisits from Karl Popper. Williams argues against the assumption that popular false beliefs must result from a deliberate conspiracy, noting that "the truth is so self-evident" is a dangerous fallacy.

Critics might argue that this defense of human rationality lets bad actors off the hook too easily. If we assume people are "far more sophisticated, critical, and intelligent than they seem," do we underestimate the power of coordinated disinformation campaigns? Williams anticipates this, but his focus remains on the individual's struggle to navigate complexity rather than the machinations of state actors.

The Blind Spot of the Elite

Perhaps the most stinging section of the review is Williams' critique of the "highbrow misinformation" circulating within prestigious institutions. He writes, "There is a tendency to think that 'misinformation' is entirely something that right-wing elites, sinister corporations, and uneducated hoi polloi engage in. But in fact, there is a considerable amount of left-coded 'highbrow misinformation' that circulates within the prestigious knowledge-producing institutions."

This argument challenges the "epistemic elites" directly. Williams contends that "experts are necessary but human," and that their advantages are often used to "indulge such biases rather than correct them." He draws a parallel to the Walter Lippmann–Dewey debate, reminding readers that Lippmann viewed modern politics as dealing with "indirect, unseen, and puzzling facts, and there is nothing obvious about them." By invoking Lippmann, Williams grounds his critique in a century-old realization: the public cannot possibly know everything, and the experts tasked with interpreting the world are just as prone to tribalism as the rest of us.

"The critical theorist who 'unmasks' ideology doesn't escape ideology."

This line cuts through the self-righteousness of modern political discourse. Williams suggests that the "reputation management" of the powerful is not just about persuading the oppressed, but about distorting the belief systems of those who think they are "unmasking" power. This is a sophisticated take on how "system-justifying ideologies" function, moving beyond the idea that they simply keep the poor quiet to the idea that they corrupt the intellectual integrity of the critics themselves.

Beyond Technopanics

Williams is equally skeptical of the moral panic surrounding artificial intelligence, a topic he is returning to after a hiatus. He warns against the "vibes, anecdotes, and moral panic" that drive so much discourse on technology. He writes, "People aren't passive, credulous victims of algorithms." Instead, he argues that the effects of social media are "mediated by long-standing pathologies of democracy, public opinion, polarisation, the growing diploma divide, and the politicisation of institutions."

His approach to AI is similarly nuanced. Rather than fearing the technology itself, he is focusing on its "social and political significance," including its interaction with "misinformation, institutional trust, expertise, and public opinion." He admits that "AI will be utterly transformative," but insists that the conversation must move beyond the "superintelligent alien species" narrative to the mundane, yet critical, ways it alters education and human connection.

"Humans are kinda sorta rational."

This simple, almost colloquial phrase encapsulates Williams' charitable view of the public. He rejects the image of humans as "credulous fools riddled with cognitive biases," suggesting instead that the perception of stupidity is a result of the "third-person effect" and a misunderstanding of people's real goals. This is a refreshing departure from the condescension often found in political analysis.

Bottom Line

Williams' strongest contribution is his refusal to scapegoat technology for the deep-seated human and institutional failures that drive polarization. His argument that "highbrow misinformation" is a critical, under-discussed problem is a vital correction to the current media landscape. However, his reliance on the assumption that most people are "kinda sorta rational" may underestimate the speed at which algorithmic feedback loops can radicalize even sophisticated users. As the executive branch and other institutions grapple with the future of information, Williams' insistence on the complexity of human belief systems offers a necessary, if uncomfortable, roadmap for understanding the road ahead.

by Dan Williams · Conspicuous Cognition · Read full article

I started this blog on January 1st, 2024, so I’ve now been publishing weekly essays here for over two years. It was one of the best decisions I’ve ever made. I’m grateful to everyone who reads and engages. Even the haters and losers (of which, happily, there aren’t many) often provide interesting and informative critiques.

I’m especially thankful to those who have paid subscriptions. I’m aware that many of you subscribe not simply to access paywalled articles but to support my writing. I’m truly moved by this. It’s also a helpful corrective to my broadly cynical views about human nature.

As of 5th January 2026, the blog has roughly 19,800 subscribers. It averages approximately 120,000 views per month, though with substantial variance.

In this post, I will review the blog’s output from 2025, recommend the best things I read last year (as well as other favourites), and then briefly outline how I will approach this newsletter in 2026.

Year in Review.

Let’s Not Bring Back The Gatekeepers — This argues that the media transformations of the digital age have created new pressures and responsibilities for small “l” liberals like me.
