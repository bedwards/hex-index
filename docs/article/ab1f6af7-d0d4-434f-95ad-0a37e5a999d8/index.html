On Good Friday in 1962, the scholar of religion Houston Smith said he encountered God for the first time. Smith joined an experiment held in the basement of Boston University's Marsh Chapel along with a group of fellow seminarians and religious studies scholars. He ingested some psilocybin, the psychedelic compound found in so-called magic mushrooms. The group then listened to the Good Friday service going on in the church upstairs, and many had a profound religious experience.

Smith said he had already believed in God for as long as I can remember. But until the Good Friday experiment, I had no direct personal encounter with God. But his experience on psilocybin was the most powerful cosmic homecoming I have ever experienced. Houston Smith's experience was not unique.

For thousands of years, humans have been taking psychedelic substances to induce so-called mystical experiences. Many people report feeling a sense of sacredness, transcendence, and awe. Others describe traveling to other domains of experience or encountering supernatural beings like gods or spirits. Phenomena classically considered to be religious or mystical.

When psychedelics are used in religious and spiritual contexts, they're often referred to as enthogens. The term enthogen derives from Greek, literally meaning something like to become divine within or generating God within. More simply, it could just mean inspired or full of God. It was coined in the 1970s as a way to avoid the negative associations with terms like hallucinogen or psychedelic.

In other words, enthogens are psychedelics by another name, one that emphasizes their religious properties. The Marsh Chapel experiment was a product of its time. It was designed as part of a larger initiative at Harvard University as research on psychedelics was ramping up in the 1960s. This ended with the Controlled Substances Act of 1970, which placed heavy restrictions on psychedelics and made them more difficult to study.

However, in the past couple of decades, psychedelic research has been making a comeback, shifting the conversation from the counterculture of the 1960s to mainstream discussions about mental health, therapy, and especially religion. This research gives us a better understanding of how psychedelics are believed to be a way to access spiritual or supernatural realms, agents, or experiences. Humans all over the world have been using psychedelics for rituals for thousands of years.
