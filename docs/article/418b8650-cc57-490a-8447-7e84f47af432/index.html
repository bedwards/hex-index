What voters in every state think about and prices

G. Elliott Morris · G. Elliott Morris ·Dec 29, 2025 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

G. Elliott Morris delivers a stark warning to the political establishment: a national approval rating of minus sixteen is not just a bad number, it is the statistical signature of a landslide defeat in the making. By deploying sophisticated modeling to map national polling down to the state level, Morris reveals a political landscape where the administration's unpopularity is not confined to opposition strongholds but has permeated the very red states that delivered victory in 2024. This is not a story about personality; it is a data-driven autopsy of a record that has failed to deliver on its core economic promise, leaving the executive branch exposed in every corner of the country.

The Geography of Discontent

The piece's most arresting claim is that the current political climate resembles the 1984 election more than the recent past. Morris writes, "The charts below show estimated Trump approval (a) overall and (b) for his handling of inflation, for each state, combining all the interviews we conducted in 2025 (from May through November)." He notes that while the overall approval is "pretty abysmal," the rating on inflation is "downright catastrophic." This comparison to 1984, when Walter Mondale lost by 18 points to Ronald Reagan, serves as a powerful historical anchor, suggesting that the current administration is facing a level of rejection that transcends typical mid-term slumps.

What makes this analysis particularly potent is the breakdown by state. Morris observes that the administration's approval is "not only weak in Democratic-leaning states; it's also underwater across many Republican-leaning states." This finding challenges the conventional wisdom that the base remains solid. As Morris puts it, "Trump's approval rating is not just underwater because of Democratic #Resistance; lots of independents and Republicans disapprove of how he's running the country, too." The data suggests a fracture within the coalition, with the decline in support being "steeper in redder parts of the country." This is a critical insight for strategists who might assume that a strong base can insulate the party from broader economic dissatisfaction.

In every state in the country except for Oklahoma, more adults disapprove than approve of how Trump is handling prices and inflation.

The Toxicity of Prices

The central thesis of the article rests on the idea that inflation has become a uniquely toxic issue for the administration, severing the link between economic voting and political loyalty. Morris argues that "whatever residual goodwill [the administration] retains among right-leaning parts of the electorate in general does not extend to his handling of prices and inflation." This is a devastating assessment for a campaign that ran on the promise of affordability. The author highlights the specific numbers to drive the point home: "The president is at -10 in Nebraska, -15 in Pennsylvania, and -20 in Iowa."

Even in states where the administration's overall standing is close to parity, the economic numbers remain deeply negative. Morris notes, "Even Wyoming!" is a place where voters disapprove of the handling of prices. This uniformity of disapproval suggests that the issue is not merely a matter of partisan polarization but a fundamental failure of policy delivery. Critics might argue that national polling aggregates can mask local nuances or that the specific timing of the polls (May through November 2025) captures a temporary dip rather than a structural shift. However, the breadth of the data across 50 states makes it difficult to dismiss as an anomaly. The sheer consistency of the negative ratings implies a deep-seated voter sentiment that is unlikely to be reversed by rhetoric alone.

The Mechanics of Measurement

Beyond the political implications, Morris offers a fascinating look into the methodological evolution required to capture these trends. He explains that standard polling models often fail to account for "non-ignorable partisan non-response," a problem that plagued predictions in 2016 and 2020. To solve this, he is implementing a new technique called "MRPW — MRP with weights." This approach allows the model to "first calibrate the sample to 'known' marginal benchmarks of various political characteristics of the population — past vote, turnout, party affiliation, volunteering, etc — and then include those weights in the last step."

This technical deep dive is not just academic; it is essential for understanding the reliability of the political forecast. Morris acknowledges the trade-offs, noting that "MRPW is cool, but it adds a lot of computational time." He is also experimenting with adjusting predictions based on real-world election outcomes to create a more accurate "augmented Census data" frame. As he writes, "This means our population data is properly calibrated at both the demographic and political levels, and via MRP we preserve information about joint population distributions." This level of rigor suggests that the negative trends identified are not artifacts of bad sampling but are robust findings derived from a sophisticated correction for bias.

When it comes to polling, there is no free lunch.

Bottom Line

G. Elliott Morris has constructed a compelling case that the administration's political vulnerability is structural, rooted in a universal rejection of its economic management that cuts across all fifty states. The strongest part of the argument is the use of granular, state-level modeling to demonstrate that the base is eroding, not just the opposition. The biggest vulnerability remains the inherent uncertainty of any model predicting future behavior based on current sentiment, but the sheer weight of the data makes the warning impossible to ignore. For down-ballot candidates, especially in swing states like Iowa and North Carolina, the message is clear: the economic tide has turned, and the political cost of the current administration's policies is being paid in full at the ballot box.
