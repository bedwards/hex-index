Bio-Energy with Carbon Capture and Storage

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Jan 27, 2019 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace delivers a sobering reality check on the climate community's most seductive technological promise: that we can grow our way out of the carbon crisis. While many reports treat Bio-Energy with Carbon Capture and Storage (BECCS) as a necessary safety net, Borlace exposes the staggering land, water, and ecological costs that the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) seems willing to overlook in its projections. This is not just a technical critique; it is a warning that our reliance on unproven negative emissions technology may be accelerating the very disasters we hope to avoid.

The Scale of the Gamble

Borlace begins by acknowledging the grim consensus: to keep global warming below 2°C, some form of carbon capture is likely unavoidable. However, he immediately pivots to the specific mechanism the IPCC is banking on. "In the IPCC projections that keep below 2° of warming, BECCS is assumed to remove up to 3.8 billion tons of CO2 per year up to 2050 and then up to 14 billion tons of CO2 per year by 2100." These numbers are not merely ambitious; they are astronomical. The author argues that while the theory is straightforward—grow crops, burn them, capture the emissions, and bury the carbon—the execution requires a transformation of the global landscape that borders on the impossible.

The core of the argument rests on land use. Borlace highlights a study suggesting that the scale required would consume a massive portion of the planet's arable land. "According to the IPCC, the average amount of BECCS in the RCP pathways requires 25 to 46% of arable and permanent crop area in 2100." This statistic forces a confrontation with reality: where does this land come from? Borlace points out that existing forests would likely be cleared to make way for monoculture plantations, destroying complex ecosystems to fuel a carbon cycle that might not even work as intended.

"Overall we found that in a majority of the areas where forests would be replaced, more carbon was stored by keeping the forests than with employing BECCS."

This quote from Dr. Anna Harper, cited by Borlace, serves as a devastating rebuttal to the technology's premise. The logic is sound: if the act of clearing land to grow biofuel releases more carbon than the fuel captures, the entire exercise becomes counterproductive. Borlace notes that this approach would also trigger a spike in food prices, pushing small-scale farmers out of business. Critics might argue that technological advancements could improve crop yields or that marginal lands could be used, but Borlace's reliance on current data suggests the trade-offs are too severe to ignore.

The Resource Trap and the Fossil Fuel Loophole

Beyond land, the resource requirements for BECCS present a second, equally critical bottleneck. Borlace explains that growing these massive crops requires immense amounts of water and fertilizer. "Estimates indicate that that could double the global consumption of both those commodities." When juxtaposed with UN projections that 5 billion people could face water shortages by 2050, the viability of BECCS crumbles. The author effectively frames this not just as an energy problem, but as a resource competition that humanity cannot win.

Furthermore, the logistics of the operation introduce their own carbon costs. Plowing, tilling, and transporting biomass across vast distances all generate emissions. A study by the Royal Society of Chemistry, referenced by Borlace, concludes that "depending on the conditions of its deployment, BECCS could lead to both carbon positive and carbon negative results." This ambiguity is dangerous for a technology being touted as a silver bullet. If the net result is positive emissions, the technology is not a solution; it is a distraction.

Perhaps the most cynical insight Borlace offers is the alignment of BECCS with the fossil fuel industry's interests. He reveals that the oil sector is not opposing carbon capture; they are championing it for a different reason. "The promotion of carbon capture technologies like BECCS for climate change mitigation and geoengineering coincides with the oil industry's fast growing demand for cheap continuous supplies of CO2."

The mechanism is "enhanced oil recovery," where CO2 is injected into depleted reservoirs to squeeze out more oil. Borlace points out the irony: "Despite the fact that enhanced oil recovery leads to the recovery and burning of potentially vast quantities of fossil fuel which would otherwise have remained under the ground, use of CO2 for this purpose is classed as a form of carbon capture and storage." This creates a perverse incentive where the "solution" to climate change becomes a subsidy for extracting more of the problem.

"Raising the world's forests would release more than 3 trillion tons of carbon dioxide, more than the amount locked up in identified global reserves of oil, coal and gas."

Borlace contrasts the risky, industrial approach of BECCS with the proven efficacy of protecting existing natural carbon sinks. He cites a group of 40 scientists who argue that protecting forests could achieve 18% of the emissions mitigation needed by 2030. This comparison highlights a strategic failure in current climate policy: we are chasing high-tech, high-risk fixes while neglecting the low-tech, high-reward preservation of the ecosystems we already have.

Bottom Line

Borlace's most compelling contribution is his dismantling of the "silver bullet" narrative, exposing BECCS as a technology that may exacerbate resource scarcity and extend the life of the fossil fuel industry rather than ending it. The argument's greatest strength lies in its synthesis of land-use data, resource constraints, and industrial incentives to show a system that is fundamentally flawed. However, the piece leaves the reader with a difficult question: if we cannot rely on BECCS, what is the political will required to achieve the rapid decarbonization Borlace advocates? The path forward is clear, but the political machinery to execute it remains dangerously slow.
