Midweek Update #3: For Now, This Looks Like Obama-Minus (And Trump Minus-Minus)

Phillips P. O'Brien · Phillips P. O'Brien ·Mar 25, 2026 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Phillips P. O'Brien delivers a sobering reality check on a conflict that the Trump administration desperately wants to call over, arguing that the United States has accidentally handed the strategic initiative to Tehran. While the President claims victory, O'Brien contends that the US is now trapped in a "Obama-Minus" scenario, facing a worse deal than the 2015 nuclear accord while Iran controls the flow of global oil. This is not just a diplomatic stumble; it is a strategic reversal where military force has backfired, leaving Washington with fewer options and a more dangerous adversary.

The Illusion of Victory

The core of O'Brien's argument rests on the dissonance between Trump's public bravado and the grim tactical reality on the ground. He observes that the President's frantic narrative of "amazing concessions" is actually a symptom of deep anxiety. O'Brien writes, "Trump seems to understand this, which is why he is desperately and publicly trying to create the narrative that he has already won." This observation is crucial because it reframes the President's rhetoric not as confidence, but as a cover for a deteriorating position.

The author highlights how Trump has convinced himself that regime change has already occurred, despite evidence to the contrary. "We have, really, a regime change," Trump claimed, "because the leaders are all very different than the ones we started off with that created all these problems." O'Brien dismantles this by pointing out that Iran has not only rejected every single demand from Trump's 2018 policy list but has accelerated its nuclear and missile programs in response. The argument lands hard because it contrasts the President's fantasy with the specific, unmet goals he once set: ending support for proxies like Hezbollah and stopping the threat to Israel.

"The reality is that the US is now, after using military force, set to get a deal from Iran that is far less satisfactory than the JCPOA, let alone the ambitious policy goals laid out by Trump himself in 2018."

Critics might argue that the administration is simply playing a long game, using maximum pressure to force a negotiation that looks weak now but will yield results later. However, O'Brien suggests the leverage has already flipped. The US is no longer the one dictating terms; it is the one needing the Straits of Hormuz to open. As O'Brien notes, "Iran possesses much of the power to determine when and how any negotiations conclude."

The Kharg Gambit

The commentary shifts to the most dangerous potential escalation: the seizure of Kharg Island. O'Brien treats this not as a viable military solution, but as a high-stakes bluff that could collapse catastrophically. He explains that while Kharg is Iran's primary oil export hub, attacking it creates a "wild-west" scenario where Iran has no incentive to keep the Gulf open for anyone else.

The author draws a sharp parallel to historical military blunders to illustrate the vulnerability of US forces. "Think of a Persian Gulf version of Dienbienphu," O'Brien warns, suggesting that US troops on the island would be isolated and cut off from supply lines. This historical reference adds necessary depth, reminding readers that holding territory near an enemy coast without secure supply lines is a recipe for disaster. The argument is strengthened by the observation that Iranian drone accuracy has improved significantly, turning the island into a "very vulnerable position."

"Seizing Kharg can be likened to taking a hostage that the US really will not want to hurt. It arguably gives the US far less leverage than it understands."

The piece also touches on the economic fallout, noting that destroying the island's facilities would spike oil prices globally, further alienating the American public. This connects to the broader political context O'Brien mentions: polling shows a majority of Americans believe the nation will suffer from this war. The author suggests that rather than de-escalating, the administration might double down to secure a "great victory," a move that could be politically motivated but strategically disastrous.

The Threat That Backfired

Perhaps the most damning section of the update focuses on Trump's specific threat to destroy Iranian power plants. O'Brien labels this "the worst threat in US military history," arguing it was both a potential war crime and a strategic blunder. The author points out that threatening blanket destruction of civilian infrastructure is not a calculated military move but a sign of panic.

"As Phillips P. O'Brien puts it, 'It was a textbook example of how not to run a country in the middle of a war.'" The analysis suggests that this threat did not intimidate Iran; instead, it signaled weakness. The administration's subsequent pivot to claiming talks were ongoing was, in O'Brien's view, a face-saving maneuver to retreat from an untenable position. This aligns with the broader theme of the piece: the US is reacting to Iranian moves rather than shaping the conflict.

The author also weaves in the context of the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) to show how far the US has fallen. The original deal, which Trump withdrew from in 2018, offered safeguards without military force. Now, after four weeks of conflict, the US is trying to negotiate a deal "very similar to that Iran accepted in 2015," but with Iran holding the cards. The irony is palpable: the administration's attempt to be tougher than Obama has resulted in a weaker position.

Bottom Line

Phillips P. O'Brien's analysis is a masterclass in separating political theater from strategic reality, exposing how the US has traded leverage for escalation. The strongest part of the argument is the clear demonstration that military force has not broken Iran's resolve but has instead unified it and strengthened its bargaining position. The biggest vulnerability in the administration's strategy is the assumption that Iran will blink first, a miscalculation that risks a much wider and more destructive conflict. Readers should watch for whether the US attempts to seize Kharg Island, as that move would likely confirm O'Brien's prediction of a strategic cascade the US cannot control.
