SpaceX IPO Scandal

Patrick Boyle · Patrick Boyle ·Mar 14, 2026 · 23 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Patrick Boyle cuts through the hype of a potential $1.75 trillion IPO by exposing a bizarre collision of hard physics, corporate desperation, and what he calls "astrology." He argues that the proposed June 2026 listing date isn't driven by market readiness, but by a celestial alignment and a frantic race to secure capital before the private market dries up. This is a rare, unvarnished look at how a man known for Mars ambitions is pivoting to the Moon to justify a valuation that defies current engineering realities.

The Celestial Calendar and the Capital Crunch

Boyle immediately dismantles the idea that SpaceX's timing is purely strategic in a financial sense, suggesting instead that it is steeped in numerology. He notes that while rocket science relies on "math to calculate orbital mechanics," the IPO timing seems to rely on "the belief that you should stay home from work next week because Jupiter's feeling a bit spicy." The author points out that the target date of June 9th aligns with a rare planetary conjunction and the number 69, a favorite of Elon Musk.

This framing is effective because it highlights the absurdity of the situation without dismissing the financial urgency. Boyle suggests that the planetary alignment is likely a "nice bit of branding," but the real driver is a "race for limited capital." He writes, "Venture capital funds that have spent the last few years aggressively funding the AI boom are now facing a liquidity plateau and are under pressure to finally return some cash to their own investors." The core of his argument is that Musk is rushing to the public markets to beat rivals like OpenAI and Anthropic to the punch, fearing there won't be enough "spare change" for three massive AI listings simultaneously.

"The first mover might get the biggest slice of the pie."

Critics might argue that Musk has always operated on a timeline that defies conventional market logic, and that his ability to attract capital is a testament to his unique brand power rather than a sign of desperation. However, Boyle's point about the "liquidity plateau" in private markets is a tangible economic constraint that even a visionary cannot ignore.

The Great Pivot: From Mars to Moon

Perhaps the most striking element of Boyle's coverage is his observation of Musk's sudden shift in mission. For over a decade, Musk insisted that going public would distract from the goal of making life "multilanetary" on Mars. Now, with the Mars transport system still a "work in progress," the narrative has shifted to the Moon as a "stepping stone." Boyle quips that Musk has been "sweeping those red planet ambitions under the rug like an embarrassing teenage phase."

He connects this pivot to a visual irony: Musk has worn "Occupy Mars" t-shirts since 2019 that feature a red-tinted image of the moon during a lunar eclipse. Boyle writes, "Maybe he just took a close look at his own chest and realized that he's been accidentally advertising for the moon all along." This observation underscores a recurring theme in Musk's career: the willingness to reframe goals to match current realities. The author notes that this shift is "a lot easier when you've been foreshadowing it for years, even if accidentally."

The $250 Billion Merger and the "Sentient Sun"

The commentary then turns to the controversial merger of SpaceX with XAI, the AI company that owns the social media platform X. Boyle describes this as a move where "SpaceX investors are now, by extension, the proud owners of the number 17 ranked social media website." He highlights the financial disparity: SpaceX generated $8 billion in earnings, while XAI is a "money furnace" burning a billion dollars a month with a market share of only 3.4% compared to the dominance of ChatGPT and Gemini.

Boyle is particularly skeptical of the justification for the $250 billion valuation, which relies on the concept of "orbital data centers." He writes, "Musk argues that space-based AI is the only way to scale because space is called space for a reason." The author dismantles this by explaining the physics of cooling in a vacuum, noting that the only way to eject heat is through massive radiators. He points out that a test satellite carrying a single chip had to shut down frequently due to overheating.

"To run just a handful of AI chips in orbit, you need hardware that rivals the most expensive structure ever built by humanity, the International Space Station."

The author calculates that a 1-gigawatt orbital data center would cost over $50 billion, more than three times the $16 billion cost of an equivalent terrestrial facility. Boyle concludes that the plan to build factories on the moon and launch them with a "space rail gun" is a technology that the US Navy failed to perfect after spending $500 million over 16 years. He suggests that Musk's approach is based on the idea that "the hardest way of doing something is actually the most efficient."

A counterargument worth considering is that Musk's history of overcoming "impossible" engineering challenges, such as reusable rockets, suggests he might succeed where others have failed. Yet, Boyle's reliance on basic thermodynamics and cost-benefit analysis provides a necessary grounding against the "sci-fi sales pitch."

Bottom Line

Boyle's strongest asset is his ability to juxtapose Musk's grandiose vision with the cold, hard constraints of physics and accounting, revealing a valuation that relies on "sentient suns" and "lunar catapults" rather than current profitability. The piece's biggest vulnerability is that it assumes market logic will prevail, potentially underestimating the irrational exuberance that could drive the IPO price regardless of the fundamentals. Readers should watch whether the "planetary alignment" of June 2026 actually delivers a functional business model or just a spectacularly expensive firework.
