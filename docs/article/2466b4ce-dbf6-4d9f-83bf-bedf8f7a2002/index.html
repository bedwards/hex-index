How To Protect Your Online Privacy With A Threat Model

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Aug 1, 2022 · 22 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a landscape where privacy tools are constantly debunked or compromised, The Hated One makes a radical claim: stop chasing the next best app and start building a consistent, personal defense strategy. The author argues that relying on specific software is a losing battle because "on the long enough timeline you can't trust any single product," and instead proposes a methodology that empowers users to evaluate their own exposure. This is not just another list of encryption tools; it is a shift from passive consumption to active threat modeling, a concept borrowed from security engineering and adapted for the everyday user.

The Flaw in the Tool-Centric Approach

The Hated One opens by dismantling the industry's habit of recommending specific countermeasures that inevitably fail. "Dug.go was recently exposed for allowing microsoft records in their mobile browser," the author notes, pointing out that a search engine can remain recommended for privacy while its browser becomes untrustworthy overnight. This inconsistency creates a false sense of security for users who think installing a tool is the end of the job. The core of the argument is that privacy is not a product you buy, but a process you manage. "Every decision would have a reason," The Hated One writes, suggesting that a methodology-based approach prevents users from being misled by marketing or temporary security flaws. This framing is effective because it acknowledges the fatigue of the privacy-conscious user who feels constantly behind the curve of new vulnerabilities.

Critics might note that asking busy individuals to build their own threat models is a high barrier to entry, potentially leaving the most vulnerable populations without protection. However, the author counters this by offering a structured, step-by-step framework designed to simplify the complexity.

"You wouldn't be using privacy tools improperly and you wouldn't get a false sense of security because every decision would have a reason."

Mapping the Invisible Data Flow

The guide introduces the "Linden" privacy threat model, a mnemonic for seven specific categories of privacy violations: linkability, identifiability, non-repudiation, detectability, disclosure of information, awareness, and non-compliance. The Hated One insists that before applying these categories, one must first create a data inventory. "Transactional data refers to the content of the communication while contextual data mostly refers to the metadata," the author explains, emphasizing that both are equally sensitive. The argument here is crucial: users often protect the message but ignore the envelope. "All of this metadata is unique open and traceable it is rarely protected and we kill people based on metadata," a stark warning that elevates the stakes beyond mere inconvenience. By forcing users to document where their data travels and who holds it, the methodology exposes the hidden economy of personal information.

The Seven Threats and the Human Element

Once the inventory is set, The Hated One guides the reader through the Linden categories, asking specific questions to uncover risks. For instance, regarding linkability, the author asks, "Are you reusing the same credentials and is the service using them to track you?" This moves the conversation from abstract fears to concrete behaviors. The discussion on identifiability highlights how modern AI can recognize individuals through behavioral patterns even if they are anonymous. "Your behavioral patterns are often so unique you can be identified even if you're anonymous browsing," The Hated One writes, a sobering reminder that anonymity is harder to achieve than it seems.

The piece also tackles the often-overlooked threat of "unawareness." The author argues that "users tend to submit way too much information to service providers without a second thought," and that this lack of understanding is a primary vulnerability. "A large portion of the blame lies on the backs of service providers," the text admits, but places the onus on the user to demand better controls and informed consent. This dual focus on corporate responsibility and user agency is a balanced approach, though it risks underestimating how opaque corporate data practices truly are for the average person.

"We kill people based on metadata."

The final category, non-compliance, challenges users to look beyond legal minimums. "Non-compliance shouldn't just be about what's in the legislation it should also be about upholding the best data protection principles," The Hated One asserts. This pushes the reader to evaluate whether a service is merely legal or actually ethical, asking if they collect more data than required or make automated decisions without human verification. This section transforms the user from a passive consumer into an auditor of their own digital life.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest contribution is reframing privacy as a dynamic, user-driven methodology rather than a static set of tools, offering a sustainable way to navigate a shifting digital landscape. The argument's biggest vulnerability is the sheer cognitive load required to maintain such a rigorous model, which may alienate the very people who need it most. Readers should watch for how this framework evolves as AI-driven surveillance becomes even more sophisticated, testing the limits of this proactive approach.
