Where Do Medical Words Come From?

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Jul 8, 2019 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis doesn't just decode medical jargon; he reframes it as a living archive of human myth, error, and poetry. In a field often criticized for obscurity, Francis argues that the "riddles" doctors speak are actually "the poetry of Medicine," weaving a narrative where every term tells a story of gods, monsters, and historical mistakes. This approach transforms a dry vocabulary lesson into a compelling defense of the past, suggesting that understanding the etymology of words like vaccine or hysteria is essential to understanding the evolution of care itself.

The Mythology of the Body

Francis begins by dismantling the assumption that medical language is merely a barrier to communication. He posits that these terms are not "business neologisms or marketing word salad" but rather a rich tapestry of history. "These aren't business neologisms or marketing word salad they're the poetry of Medicine and enthralling ballad now Gods myths and monsters," he writes, immediately setting a tone that elevates the subject from clinical utility to cultural artifact. By tracing vaccine back to vacca (cow) and linking it to the first smallpox inoculation, he shows how language preserves the literal origins of medical breakthroughs. This framing is effective because it humanizes the cold science, reminding the listener that medicine is a human endeavor rooted in observation and storytelling.

The author excels at revealing the absurdity hidden within standard terminology. He points out that orthopedics literally means "making straight children," a nod to a time when surgeons corrected clubfoot and rickets rather than fixing broken hips for traffic accidents. "Orthopedic surgeons spent their time correcting clubfoot scoliosis and rickets so they were literally making straight children," Francis notes. This historical context forces a re-evaluation of modern specializations, suggesting that our current definitions are often fossilized versions of past realities. A counterargument worth considering is that while this history is fascinating, the primary goal of modern medicine is clarity for the patient, not the preservation of historical accuracy. However, Francis's point stands: knowing the origin helps demystify the term, making it less intimidating.

"In our language also lies evidence of our mistakes for hundreds of years we believe disease stemmed for an imbalance of the four humors and personalities became associated to this day one may be described as melancholic choleric sanguine and phlegmatic."

Gods, Monsters, and Medical Errors

Francis dives deep into the intersection of mythology and anatomy, using the story of the Sphinx to explain the sphincter muscle. He recounts the Greek riddle where the creature has one voice but changes from four legs to two to three, noting that "incorrect answers led to death by strangulation and thus we derive sphincter a circular muscle that tightens like a band." This etymological leap from a mythological punishment to a physiological function is jarring yet memorable. It illustrates how ancient fears and stories have been physically encoded into our understanding of the body.

The commentary becomes even more provocative when addressing gender bias in medical history. Francis highlights how the Greek belief that the womb wandered the body led to the term hysteria, which he explains meant "only women could suffer from hysteria." This is a powerful critique of how language has historically pathologized women's experiences based on flawed anatomy. "The Greek histories gives us uterus the womb but also is as Greeks believed the womb wandered around the body causing disease meaning only women could suffer from hysteria," he states. While the piece is celebratory of etymology, this section serves as a necessary reminder that the "poetry" of medicine is sometimes written in the ink of prejudice. Critics might argue that focusing on these errors distracts from the scientific progress made since, but Francis uses them to show why we must remain vigilant about the stories we tell in medicine.

The author also explores the irony of naming life-saving drugs after figures associated with death. He describes atropine, derived from the deadly nightshade plant, named after Atropos, one of the three Fates who "ended mortals lives with one snip of their thread." "With her horde she ended mortals lives with one snip of their thread and yet lends her name to a life-saving drug," Francis observes. This juxtaposition of death and salvation encapsulates the dual nature of medical intervention. It suggests that the language of medicine is not just descriptive but deeply symbolic, reflecting the high stakes of the profession.

The Language of Learning

Francis concludes by emphasizing the power of active engagement over passive listening. He argues that "answering questions and solving puzzles reinforces learning better than just hearing information ever could," a principle he applies to his own storytelling. By turning the etymology lesson into a puzzle—asking the audience to decode endocarditis or allograft—he demonstrates that the "riddles" of medicine are solvable if one knows the rules. This shifts the dynamic from one of exclusion to one of empowerment. The reader is no longer a passive victim of jargon but an active decoder of history.

The piece also touches on the modern challenge of AI in medicine, with Francis admitting his own knowledge extends only as far as "Terminator and Short Circuit." He uses this vulnerability to pivot to the importance of continuous learning, framing the acquisition of knowledge as a defense against obsolescence. "To arm myself against the robots gearing up to take my job... I have found brilliant my one-stop shop for learning about algorithms," he says. While this section feels slightly disconnected from the historical focus of the rest, it reinforces the central theme: that understanding the roots of our tools, whether ancient words or modern algorithms, is crucial for navigating the future.

"Next time you visit the doctor your real off latin with ease but if said doctor doesn't communicate sanely ask them please explain these words plainly if their bedside manner remains an incomprehensible atrocity they might not know their electron from their issue tuberosity."

Bottom Line

Rohin Francis's greatest strength is his ability to transform the intimidating wall of medical jargon into a bridge of historical curiosity, proving that etymology is a tool for both education and empathy. However, the piece occasionally risks romanticizing the errors of the past, such as the gender bias in hysteria, without fully grappling with the harm those terms caused. The reader should take away not just a list of word origins, but a renewed appreciation for the stories embedded in the language of healing, and a healthy skepticism toward any doctor who cannot explain their terms plainly.
