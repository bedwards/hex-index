The rorschach regime change test

Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, Bill Kristol · The Bulwark ·Jan 6, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol expose a jarring dissonance at the heart of the administration's latest foreign intervention: the United States is simultaneously claiming a routine arrest and threatening a full-scale war, leaving Congress and the public to guess which reality is true. This piece is notable not for the spectacle of a captured leader, but for its forensic dismantling of the legal and constitutional contradictions that the executive branch is trying to sweep under the rug. For the busy reader, the takeaway is stark: the rules of engagement are being rewritten in real-time, and the cost of that ambiguity may be measured in civilian lives and legislative chaos.

The Legal Fiction

The authors immediately zero in on the administration's contradictory messaging regarding the capture of Nicolás Maduro. While Secretary of State Marco Rubio insists this was a domestic law enforcement action, the President's own rhetoric suggests a much more aggressive military posture. Longwell, Miller, and Kristol highlight the absurdity of this duality, quoting Rubio's claim that the Department of War is involved only because they "have to go to court," a statement that blurs the line between judicial process and martial force.

The commentary points out that if this were truly a Justice Department-led operation, the legal implications are narrow. Yet, the President's threat of a "second and much larger attack" and the declaration that they are "not afraid of boots on the ground" shatters that narrative. As the authors put it, "If the goal was merely to arrest Maduro, why is Trump threatening further attacks against Venezuela?" This is a critical question that the administration has failed to answer, creating a dangerous precedent where the executive branch can toggle between legal and military frameworks to suit its immediate political needs.

Critics might argue that the administration is simply using ambiguity as a strategic deterrent, but the authors correctly identify that this approach erodes the rule of law. The confusion extends to Capitol Hill, where Republican senators are desperate to have it both ways. They want to claim the moral high ground of a "law enforcement mission" while enjoying the political benefits of a military victory. The authors capture this cognitive dissonance perfectly: "The Maduro episode has provided yet another occasion for elected Republicans to twist themselves into knots to support the administration's shifting, contradictory messaging."

The administration's refusal to acknowledge our Committee's indisputable jurisdiction in this matter is unacceptable.

The Oversight Vacuum

The piece takes a sharp turn into the mechanics of congressional oversight, revealing a disturbing gap in accountability. If the operation was a law enforcement action, the Senate Judiciary Committee should have been briefed immediately. Instead, the authors note that the administration excluded the committee, prompting a rare joint rebuke from Senators Chuck Grassley and Dick Durbin. The authors write, "There is no legitimate basis for excluding the Senate Judiciary Committee from this briefing," a statement that underscores the severity of the executive overreach.

The authors' analysis of Senator John Kennedy's response is particularly damning. When pressed on how to investigate civilian casualties, Kennedy dismisses the idea as "ridiculous," arguing that the arrest of a criminal supersedes the need for an inquiry into collateral damage. Longwell, Miller, and Kristol dismantle this logic, pointing out that Congress has broad investigative powers that were utilized during the Special Inspector General for Afghanistan Reconstruction to track failures in nation-building. By ignoring these precedents, the current leadership is allowing a potential cover-up of civilian deaths to go unchecked. The human cost of the operation is treated as a footnote, a detail the authors insist must be brought to the forefront.

The historical parallel to the War Powers Resolution is implicit but powerful. Just as past administrations have struggled to define the scope of military engagement without congressional approval, this administration is attempting to bypass the very checks and balances designed to prevent unilateral war. The authors note that if this is a war effort, the lack of authorization is a constitutional crisis; if it is a police action, the military involvement and lack of briefing are illegal. There is no middle ground, yet the administration is trying to occupy it.

The Legislative Gridlock

Beyond the foreign policy implications, the authors pivot to the domestic fallout, describing a congressional calendar that is already buckling under the weight of its own contradictions. The capture of Maduro and the plan to install a pro-American advisory board to manage Venezuela's oil reserves require new appropriations and long-term oversight. The authors argue that these requirements are being dumped onto a legislature that is already paralyzed by debates over health insurance and government funding deadlines.

The authors paint a grim picture of the House of Representatives, noting that the Republican majority is a "two-vote margin for error." With members resigning, dying, or hospitalized, the logistical challenge of passing any legislation is immense. The piece quotes the reality of the situation: "The legislative agenda isn't going to get any lighter out of respect for that fact." This section serves as a sobering reminder that high-stakes foreign interventions cannot exist in a vacuum; they require a functioning government to sustain them.

The authors also touch on the bizarre cultural phenomenon surrounding Maduro's attire, noting the irony of the "online menswear world" celebrating the dictator's "fits" in Nike Tech Fleece and Carhartt jackets. While this seems like a trivial detail, the authors use it to highlight the surreal nature of the moment. "Maduro's new U.S. government–issued style sharply contrasts to his prior wardrobe," they write, noting the shift from revolutionary socialist attire to a "workaday style" that is "very much in vogue in 2026." This cultural dissonance mirrors the political one: a serious geopolitical event being trivialized by the very people who should be holding the administration accountable.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its relentless exposure of the legal and constitutional void the administration is creating by refusing to define its own actions. The authors effectively demonstrate that the "law enforcement" and "military operation" narratives are mutually exclusive, and the attempt to merge them is a recipe for disaster. The biggest vulnerability of the administration's position is its reliance on a compliant Congress that is too fractured to demand answers. Readers should watch for the upcoming Senate briefing and whether the Judiciary Committee can force a genuine investigation into the civilian casualties that the administration seems intent on ignoring.

    The article discusses whether Trump needs congressional approval for military action in Venezuela. This 1973 law defines exactly when presidents can use military force without Congress - central to understanding the legal debate between Rubio's and Trump's framings.

  • Special Inspector General for Afghanistan Reconstruction

    Mentioned explicitly in the article as precedent for congressional oversight of foreign operations. SIGAR's work exposing failures in Afghanistan nation-building directly parallels what oversight of Venezuela might look like - and the reader likely doesn't know how these inspector general offices actually function.

Whose line is it anyway?.

The American government has billed the dramatic capture and extradition of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro over the weekend as a law enforcement operation carried out with the support of the U.S. military. Now that Maduro is in custody, the United States intends to exert control of the country through (what the administration assumes will be) Maduro’s pliant vice president, whose primary task is to open up Venezuela’s nationalized oil production to American corporations.

That’s the administration’s high-level story, at least. But as more context comes out, it gets less clear whether Maduro’s capture was a law enforcement action, a military operation, or some secret third thing.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio went with the law enforcement framing in comments he made over the weekend. “The Department of War plays a very important role here along with the Department of Justice, for example, because they’ve got—they’re the ones that have to go to court,” he said on NBC’s Meet the Press.

“And I assure you the people left behind in Venezuela now that are in charge of the police and everything else, I assure you they’re going to probably be a lot more compliant than Maduro was as a result of this,” he added later, suggesting the show of force in apprehending Maduro would do much to convince the regime to follow America’s lead going forward without requiring an invading and occupying force.

President Donald Trump apparently hasn’t gotten that memo.

“We are ready to stage a second and much larger attack if we need to do so,” he said. “So we were prepared to do a second wave if we need to do so.”

“We’re not afraid of boots on the ground,” the president added.

If you’re not sure how exactly to square these statements, you’re in good company. Much of Capitol Hill is confused as well.

Accepting the law enforcement framing raises the question of what jurisdiction the United States has beyond its borders. (Among the charges in the indictment are allegations that Maduro and co. violated American firearms laws.) Further, if the goal was merely to arrest Maduro, why is Trump threatening further attacks against Venezuela?
