The two homelessness problems

Matt Yglesias · Slow Boring ·Apr 3, 2026 ·16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Matt Yglesias cuts through the semantic fog surrounding urban crises by insisting that society is conflating two entirely distinct problems: the economics of housing and the management of public disorder. While much of the political discourse treats homelessness as a monolith, Yglesias argues that the solution requires a bifurcated approach that is simultaneously more generous and more strict, a nuance often lost in polarized debates.

The Economics of Displacement

Yglesias begins by dismantling the assumption that homelessness is primarily a failure of individual morality or a lack of shelter beds. He points out that a significant portion of the unhoused population is actually gainfully employed, suggesting that the root cause is structural. "Homelessness, as a problem, is mostly driven by the economics of housing," he writes, noting that the sheer cost of living forces people into cars and shelters when they cannot find affordable units. This is a crucial distinction because it shifts the policy focus from social services to land-use reform.

The author argues that the United States has made low-end housing illegal through restrictive zoning, creating a shortage that disproportionately hurts those with financial difficulties. He suggests that a country more open to new construction and diverse housing typologies would see a dramatic reduction in these numbers. This argument gains historical weight when considering the decline of single-room occupancy (SRO) units, which were once a vital safety net for the working poor before being largely zoned out of existence in the mid-20th century. By framing the issue as a supply-side failure, Yglesias avoids the trap of blaming the victim.

"The solutions to this problem are fundamentally in the housing policy space... not the specifics of subsidies or policies targeting homelessness."

Critics might argue that simply building more market-rate housing is insufficient without direct subsidies for the most vulnerable, but Yglesias's point stands that without increasing the overall stock of affordable units, subsidies alone cannot solve the math of scarcity.

The Crisis of Public Order

The commentary then pivots to the second, often more visceral, problem: the nuisance behavior associated with severe mental illness and addiction. Yglesias observes that while the term "homeless" is used to describe these individuals, their housing status is often unknown to the public. "I think it is clearly net negative for the business climate on that street to have them out there, and it's the kind of thing that discourages people from living in and (especially) raising kids in the city," he writes. Here, he validates the concerns of residents who fear for their safety without conflating that fear with the economic plight of the housed poor.

Drawing on his experience growing up in New York during the 1980s, Yglesias acknowledges that while he is personally desensitized to the presence of disordered individuals, the broader societal cost is real. He advocates for a policy shift that is both stricter and more generous, suggesting that coercive treatment and stricter policing of public behavior are necessary, albeit expensive, components of a humane solution. This is a stark departure from the prevailing narrative that policing is inherently oppressive; instead, he frames it as a prerequisite for restoring public order.

"The solution is to be both stricter and also more generous. And to acknowledge that the problem of people behaving inappropriately in public space is not actually the same problem as homelessness."

This distinction is vital. A counterargument worth considering is whether the state has the capacity to fund the "generous" side of this equation—high-quality, involuntary treatment facilities—while simultaneously increasing enforcement. Without that funding, stricter policing risks becoming merely punitive.

The Political Taxonomy

Beyond the specific policy of homelessness, Yglesias uses the mailbag format to critique the current state of Democratic politics, particularly the tension between the party establishment and its left flank. He addresses the rise of figures like Zohran Mamdani, noting that his appeal to moderates stems from a willingness to compromise on key issues like crime and school admissions. "He was also vulnerable on the basis of his record of soft-on-crime statements," Yglesias notes, explaining how Mamdani's disavowal of those views and subsequent actions helped him bridge the gap between factions.

The author also tackles the foreign policy debate regarding Israel, arguing that while antisemitism is rising, the political upside for Democrats to adopt hardline anti-Israel stances is nonexistent. He suggests that the party should avoid "insane conspiracy theories" while also refusing to subsidize what he views as irresponsible military adventurism. This balanced stance attempts to navigate a minefield where both extremes offer political toxicity.

"The party needs fresh faces and it needs change, and the case for Emanuel isn't that he represents that kind of renewal, but that he offers a different ideological direction. I don't think that's enough."

Yglesias's refusal to simply rally behind a moderate figure like Rahm Emanuel or a progressive firebrand like A.O.C. highlights his frustration with the party's tendency to oscillate between extremes rather than finding a sustainable center.

Bottom Line

Yglesias's most compelling contribution is his insistence on separating the economic crisis of housing from the humanitarian crisis of public disorder, a distinction that allows for more precise and effective policy interventions. His argument is strongest when it challenges the semantic shortcuts that prevent meaningful debate, though it relies heavily on the political will to fund the expensive, dual-track solution he proposes. Readers should watch to see if this nuanced framing can gain traction in a political environment that thrives on simplification.
