The two homelessness problems

Matt Yglesias · Slow Boring ·Apr 3, 2026 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Matt Yglesias cuts through the semantic fog of the homelessness debate by making a distinction that is as politically inconvenient as it is analytically vital: the problem of housing affordability and the problem of public nuisance are not the same issue. While most political discourse conflates the two, Yglesias argues that solving one requires a different toolkit than solving the other, a nuance often lost in the heat of urban culture wars.

The Economics of Shelter

Yglesias begins by dismantling the assumption that homelessness is primarily a failure of individual morality or a lack of shelter beds. He points out that the "biggest driver of homelessness is the cost of housing," a reality that manifests when low-end housing is made illegal and average costs rise, leaving no spare capacity for friends or family to crash on a couch.

"A large share of the homeless population, as measured in various surveys, is gainfully employed."

This observation forces a reevaluation of the crisis. It is not just a matter of the "hard-core" homeless; it is a systemic failure where financial difficulties have nowhere to land because the market has been stripped of affordable options. Yglesias suggests that the solution lies not in targeted homelessness subsidies, but in generalized housing policy that allows for both upscale market-rate construction and the legalization of low-end housing typologies. This approach echoes the historical lessons found in the companion deep dive on single-room occupancy, where the deliberate removal of small-unit housing in the mid-20th century created a scarcity that still plagues cities today.

Critics might argue that simply building more housing ignores the immediate crisis of people currently on the streets. However, Yglesias maintains that without expanding the supply of cheap housing, any other intervention is merely treating a symptom while the disease spreads.

"In a country that was both more open to new, upscale market-rate construction and also more open to low-end housing typologies... we would have dramatically less homelessness."

The Nuisance of Public Disorder

The commentary shifts sharply when Yglesias addresses what the public actually fears: not the act of sleeping outside, but the behavior associated with severe mental illness and addiction. He notes that while people call these individuals "homeless," their housing status is often unknown to the observer. The fear is real, and the economic impact on a neighborhood is tangible.

"I think it is clearly net negative for the business climate on that street to have them out there, and it's the kind of thing that discourages people from living in and (especially) raising kids in the city."

Yglesias refuses to shy away from the uncomfortable reality that a "humane" approach must sometimes include coercion. He argues for stricter policing of public behavior and the option to force treatment for those who are routinely misbehaving. This is a departure from the standard progressive playbook, which often views enforcement as inherently punitive. Yglesias frames it as a necessary component of a "corrupt bargain" that currently exists, where moderate jurisdictions dump troubled individuals onto progressive ones.

"The solution is to be both stricter and also more generous. And to acknowledge that the problem of people behaving inappropriately in public space is not actually the same problem as homelessness."

This dual approach requires admitting that the "soft on crime" era has failed to address the specific needs of the chronically mentally ill. It draws a parallel to the historical context of sweat equity and inclusionary zoning, where the focus was often on the supply of housing rather than the management of the public sphere. Yglesias suggests that we cannot build our way out of the nuisance problem alone; we must also be willing to manage the behavior of those who cannot manage themselves.

"We should be real, though, that this would be expensive; it's not like a 'fix everything switch' where a little minor application of willpower would make the problem go away."

Bottom Line

Yglesias's strongest contribution is his refusal to let the conversation collapse into a single, unresolvable debate; he correctly identifies that housing policy and public safety policy require distinct, albeit simultaneous, interventions. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in the political feasibility of "coercive treatment," a concept that remains deeply polarizing and difficult to implement at scale without significant civil liberty concerns. Readers should watch for how this nuanced separation of issues plays out in upcoming local elections, where candidates are often forced to choose between the two rather than address both.
