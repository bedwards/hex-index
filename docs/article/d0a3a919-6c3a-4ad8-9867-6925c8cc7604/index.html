Ozempic and wegovy: All you need to know

Sabine Hossenfelder cuts through the celebrity hype surrounding the new generation of weight-loss drugs to reveal a stark reality: these are not magic bullets, but powerful biological tools with a catch that most headlines ignore. While the world fixates on Hollywood endorsements and Elon Musk's anecdotes, Hossenfelder argues that the true story lies in the brutal economics of access and the physiological trap of dependency.

The Mechanism and the Myth

Hossenfelder begins by dismantling the notion that these drugs are a sudden miracle, grounding the discussion in the biology of semaglutide. She explains that the active ingredient "resembles a glucagon like peptide known as gp1 which the body produces naturally," a hormone that regulates insulin and hunger. The distinction between the diabetes treatment Ozempic and the obesity treatment Wegovy is merely dosage, yet the market reaction has been explosive. "According to Komodo Health the number of prescriptions for this and similar drugs for weight loss purposes has increased over 2,000% from 2019 to 2022 in the USA," she notes, highlighting a demand so intense it has triggered shortages globally.

The efficacy is undeniable, but Hossenfelder is careful to contextualize the data. In a major trial, participants on the drug lost an average of nearly 15% of their body weight compared to just 2.4% for the placebo group. "That's really impressive," she admits, yet she immediately pivots to the limitations. The drug works by slowing stomach emptying and reducing hunger signals in the brain, effectively forcing the body to consume less. However, this biological intervention does not distinguish between fat and muscle with surgical precision. Hossenfelder points out a critical flaw in the weight loss composition: "the weight that people lost on stite was only 60% fat and that's despite them also doing exercise." For older adults or women with naturally lower muscle mass, losing lean tissue is a significant health risk that the marketing glosses over.

"The brief answer is that the weight comes back... those who had received stite had regained 2third of the weight they'd lost the ones who had received placebos regained all of it."

This observation strikes at the heart of the drug's long-term viability. The treatment is not a cure but a maintenance therapy; stop the injection, and the body reverts to its previous set point. Critics might argue that this makes the drug a lifelong financial and medical commitment rather than a solution, a point Hossenfelder underscores by noting that blood pressure improvements also fade once the medication stops.

The Economics of Exclusivity

The commentary shifts sharply from biology to the stark reality of cost and access. Hossenfelder exposes the absurdity of the current US healthcare landscape, where the monthly cost of treatment "exceeds $1,000 us a month." This price tag creates a two-tiered system where the drug is accessible to the wealthy and the healthy, while those with genuine medical needs like type 2 diabetes face shortages. "The majority of those taking it more than 34 are women," she observes, noting that many are using the drug for cosmetic reasons rather than medical necessity.

This dynamic has forced governments to intervene. Hossenfelder cites Australia's decision to limit the drug's use strictly to diabetes treatment to protect supply for those who need it most. In the US, the situation is more fragmented, with the national Medicare program legally prohibited from covering weight loss prescriptions. "The Biden Administration however has vowed to do something against obesity so maybe that law is going to change," she writes, but warns that the fiscal implications would be staggering given that 42% of American adults are obese. The cost disparity is equally jarring: a monthly supply costs $242 in France and just $95 in Turkey, highlighting how the US system uniquely penalizes its citizens for these innovations.

The Moral and Social Paradox

Perhaps the most provocative section of Hossenfelder's analysis addresses the moral implications of appetite suppression. She argues that the success of these drugs reveals a uncomfortable truth about human biology: "being overweight is in many cases a consequence of how our brains are W for some people." The drugs offer a way out of a biological trap, yet society often views the users with suspicion. "Handing out these drugs on prescription only to those who chose being overweight as the Lesser evil and leaving those who permanently starve themselves to their suffering strikes me as more questionable," she contends.

This tension has created friction between different groups. Hossenfelder notes that the body positivity movement, which encourages self-acceptance, is now at odds with the reality that "given a choice many obese people prefer losing weight over accepting it." Simultaneously, those who have struggled with diet and exercise feel "cheated" that others can achieve similar results with less pain. The result is a culture where the drug is viewed as a cheat code, fueling accusations of laziness against users, even as the science suggests their struggle was never a failure of willpower.

"I suspect that as it becomes easier to lose weight for people who don't need to lose weight it'll lose its appeal now."

Hossenfelder concludes with a wry observation on the future of these treatments. As the drugs become more accessible, the exclusivity that drives their current cultural cachet may vanish. She jokes that the next innovation needed is a drug to "reduce nonsense intake," suggesting that the root problem may not be the ability to lose weight, but the societal obsession with thinness itself.

Bottom Line

Hossenfelder's analysis is a necessary corrective to the breathless hype surrounding GLP-1 agonists, effectively framing them as powerful but imperfect tools with significant societal costs. Her strongest argument lies in exposing the dependency trap and the inequitable access that turns a medical breakthrough into a luxury good. The piece's vulnerability is its reliance on current trial data for long-term side effects, which remain unknown, but her call to view these drugs through a lens of biological reality rather than moral judgment is essential reading for anyone navigating this new landscape.
