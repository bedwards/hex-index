The case for clean energy abundance

Matt Yglesias · Slow Boring ·May 14, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Matt Yglesias delivers a provocative critique of the modern environmental movement, arguing that its obsession with conservation and efficiency is actually a barrier to solving the climate crisis. He contends that true progress requires a radical shift toward "clean energy abundance," a concept most current advocates pay lip service to but fundamentally reject in their policy proposals. For busy leaders watching energy markets and policy shifts, this piece is essential because it exposes a dangerous disconnect between the slogans of "green abundance" and the reality of restrictive land-use politics.

The Efficiency Trap

Yglesias opens by dismantling the "fanboy attitude" many institutions have toward specific technologies like solar panels and electric cars. He writes, "many of the people and institutions who believe that they have embraced an 'abundance' approach to clean energy have not, in fact, done any such thing." Instead, he argues, these groups remain trapped in an old mindset where energy is scarce and must be conserved at all costs. This is a sharp distinction that forces readers to question whether their preferred advocacy groups are actually aligned with their goals.

The author contrasts this with the historical vision of energy abundance, referencing a 1954 aspiration by Atomic Energy Commission Chair Lewis Strauss to generate electricity that was "too cheap to meter." Yglesias uses this historical touchstone to define what true abundance looks like: a world where we stop worrying about conservation because supply is so vast. He points out that while the organization Rewiring America claims to be "forging a path to limitless clean energy for everyone," their recent policy reports are dominated by discussions of "efficiency" and "virtual power plants."

"In a world of limitless clean energy for everyone, we would not be fussing around with 'aggregating household-based energy resources' or bragging that 'efficiency avoids unnecessary capital investment.'"

Yglesias argues that virtual power plants, which use software to manage demand rather than generating new power, are fundamentally at odds with an abundance mindset. He suggests that if we truly had limitless energy, we wouldn't need to be so meticulous about squeezing every watt out of existing infrastructure. Critics might note that efficiency remains a critical, low-cost tool for decarbonization, but Yglesias's point holds weight: treating efficiency as the primary solution limits our ability to electrify hard-to-decarbonize sectors like aviation and shipping.

The Land Use Conflict

The piece takes a harder turn when addressing the physical reality of building new energy infrastructure. Yglesias highlights a proposal to assemble 10,000 acres of land in Massachusetts for utility-scale solar farms, a move he describes as a "hot take" in a state with a powerful environmental movement. He notes that while the environmental community often claims to support the energy transition, they are often "relentlessly hostile" to the land-use changes required to make it happen.

He observes that major green groups prefer strategies like "colocating wind and solar" or "agrivoltaics" to minimize land impact, rather than embracing the scale needed for true abundance. Yglesias writes, "Rather than seeking maximally abundant electricity, they want to squeeze as much electricity as possible out of the existing human footprint." This framing is effective because it exposes a contradiction: you cannot solve a global crisis with local constraints if the solution requires massive scale.

The argument extends to the potential of geothermal and nuclear power, which are appealing to abundance advocates because they are compact. As Yglesias puts it, "ideas like geothermal and nuclear power are appealing to abundance advocates in part because they are compact." He contrasts this with the inefficiency of current biofuel production, which consumes 30 million acres of farmland. He suggests that with abundant, cheap electricity, we could pivot to vertical farming and lab-grown protein, potentially freeing up vast amounts of land for rewilding.

"If we had the energy for vertical farming, we could easily quadruple national parkland while opening up tons of new space for housing development by relying on electricity rather than sunshine for food production."

This vision is compelling, though it relies on the assumption that the technology for vertical farming and synthetic fuels will become economically viable at scale. A counterargument worth considering is that the energy density and intermittency of renewables still pose significant challenges that efficiency and storage can address more immediately than waiting for a breakthrough in synthetic hydrocarbons.

Beyond the Slogans

Yglesias concludes by criticizing the political strategy of simply "talking about climate change" without changing the underlying policy approach. He references the debate between Bill McKibben and Senator Sheldon Whitehouse, noting that Whitehouse's plan to block fossil-fuel infrastructure is a form of "supply-constraint" that mirrors the very restrictions the administration often imposes. He argues that sporadically blocking domestic extraction will not meaningfully curb global emissions.

Instead, he calls for a focus on breaking down barriers to deployment. "Clean energy abundance is identifying the barriers to deploying wind and solar and nuclear and geothermal power and breaking them down," he writes. This includes reforming permitting, updating interregional transmission, and changing Nuclear Regulatory Commission rules. The author insists that we must move beyond "posting levelized cost of energy charts" and start building the infrastructure that makes abundance a reality.

"Just recognize that if you embrace this vision, you're breaking in a real way with what major green groups are advocating for."

This final warning is the piece's most potent takeaway. It challenges readers to decide whether they want to support the status quo of environmental politics or push for a more aggressive, supply-side revolution. The argument is strong because it moves the debate from moral posturing to practical engineering and policy constraints.

Bottom Line

Yglesias's strongest argument is the exposure of the hypocrisy in current "abundance" rhetoric, which often masks a deep-seated fear of large-scale infrastructure projects. His biggest vulnerability is the assumption that cheap energy alone will solve complex technological hurdles like synthetic fuel production without significant innovation. Readers should watch for how this "abundance" framing influences the next wave of permitting reform and whether major environmental groups will adapt or fracture over this divide.

The case for clean energy abundance

by Matt Yglesias · Slow Boring · Read full article

I believe contentiousness and disagreeability can play a productive role in the discourse, so I’ve been meaning to say for a while that many of the people and institutions who believe that they have embraced an “abundance” approach to clean energy have not, in fact, done any such thing.

What they’ve done is adopt a kind of fanboy attitude toward a few booming technologies, notably solar panels and electric cars, and post charts showing their rapid adoption in some jurisdictions.

That’s great! I think it’s good for people to be excited about things. And the progress that’s been made in driving down the cost of batteries in particular is wonderful for the world.

It’s even possible that this kind of solar cheerleading could be a gateway that leads to a genuine clean-energy-abundance mindset.

Traditionally, solar power has been expensive, so advocacy for it was, almost by definition, not advocacy for abundance; solarheads were instead obsessed with conservation and efficiency. The idea was to get total energy use low enough that humans could conceivably meet their electricity needs with the generation that was possible via expensive solar installations on rooftops. The fact that you can now build cheap utility-scale solar projects genuinely does change the game and means that solar is actually a possible path to abundance.

But it’s still the case that an abundance mindset is a hard conceptual break from the old-time vision of humanity eking out just enough to get by through rinky-dink small-scale solar installations.

In 1954, Atomic Energy Commission Chair Lewis Strauss1 spoke of his aspiration to use nuclear reactors to generate electricity that was “too cheap to meter.”

And that’s abundance: a desire to create so much electricity that nobody’s bothering to conserve electricity.

The organization Rewiring America says that it’s “forging a path to limitless clean energy for everyone.” That’s a great slogan for an advocacy organization whose mission centers clean energy abundance. But they recently put out a report with a bunch of policy proposals that in turn are downstream of some efforts to model around incredible levels of uncertainty.
