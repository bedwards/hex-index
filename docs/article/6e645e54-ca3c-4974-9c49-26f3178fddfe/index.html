Busting the Luckin Coffee Fraud

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Sep 13, 2020 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a landscape where financial fraud is often dismissed as routine, Asianometry elevates the Luckin Coffee scandal from a mere corporate collapse to a masterclass in forensic detective work. The author argues that the true story isn't just about inflated numbers, but about the unprecedented, physical labor required to expose them—a 89-page report built on thousands of hours of video surveillance and tens of thousands of collected receipts. For the busy investor trying to navigate the opacity of cross-border listings, this breakdown reveals why the Luckin case remains a defining moment for Sino-American market relations.

The Architecture of the Lie

Asianometry sets the stage by contrasting the frantic pace of Luckin's rise with the methodical slowness of the investigation. The company, founded in 2017, moved with "uber and i guess the wework route" aggression, securing massive funding and listing on the US stock exchange in less than two years. The author notes that investors were seduced by metrics that seemed "eye popping," specifically the claim that the company was opening a store "once every 15 hours." This speed created a narrative of inevitability, where the sheer velocity of growth masked the lack of underlying substance.

The core of the author's argument rests on the sheer audacity of the fraud. Luckin management claimed their stores sold 495 items per day and that 63% of customers paid more than half the retail price. Asianometry writes, "Management can't keep track of their own data or they were lying through their teeth." This binary choice is stark, but the evidence provided suggests the latter. The author highlights that the company's own CFO predicted a net selling price of 11.2 RMB, while the reality was closer to 9.87 RMB. The discrepancy wasn't a minor accounting error; it was a fundamental fabrication of the business model.

Critics might argue that high-growth startups often struggle with accurate reporting due to rapid scaling, but the scale of Luckin's deception went beyond simple estimation errors. The author emphasizes that the fraud was systemic, affecting revenue, customer counts, and pricing simultaneously.

The Detective Work

What makes this piece truly compelling is the detailed reconstruction of how the fraud was uncovered. The short-seller Muddy Waters, often associated with broad allegations, executed a physical investigation of staggering proportions. Asianometry describes the effort: "They hired 92 full-time staff and 1,418 part-time staff to literally sit at 620 stores and run video surveillance on each of them for literally the whole day."

This was not a digital scrape or a statistical model; it was people sitting in coffee shops for 11 hours a day, counting bags and recording footage. The author notes the rigor of the method: "If over 10 minutes of footage was found to be missing then they would discard footage from the entire day." This level of diligence is rare in financial analysis. The team also collected 25,843 customer receipts to verify pricing and order frequency, achieving a "99 statistical confidence interval." The data revealed that items per order were actually declining, contradicting the company's narrative of growing customer engagement.

The investigation took a lot of work and money estimates were something like two to three million dollars and the people who did it were not just ordinary dudes on the internet.

The author points out the irony that despite the massive effort, the identity of the investigators remained hidden for a long time, with the Wall Street Journal eventually linking the work to Snow Lake Capital. This anonymity underscores the risks involved in challenging powerful Chinese entities. The author wonders, "How long Luckin Coffee its fraud could have gone undetected" without such an exhaustive, ground-level probe.

The Geopolitical Fallout

The narrative shifts from corporate fraud to international tension. The collapse of Luckin Coffee did not just destroy shareholder value; it fueled a political firestorm. Asianometry explains that the scandal gave US politicians a "pretty good reason" to demand greater transparency, leading to threats of delisting major Chinese companies like Alibaba and Baidu. The author notes the structural deadlock: "US watchdogs cannot see into Chinese companies books and thus rely on Chinese audit firms to confirm their numbers but China by law does not allow the US audit oversight committee the PCAOB to examine Chinese audits."

This section effectively connects a specific corporate scandal to the broader friction between the two superpowers. The author suggests that Chinese founders list in the US not just for capital, but to monetize stakes in dollars, a currency they cannot easily repatriate. The fraud exposed the fragility of this arrangement. As Asianometry puts it, the scandal "stains the corporate reputation of China," a sentiment echoed by friends in China who noted the lasting reputational damage.

Critics might note that focusing solely on Luckin ignores the many legitimate Chinese companies that have successfully listed in the US, potentially creating an unfair generalization. However, the author's focus remains on the specific failure of oversight mechanisms that allowed such a massive fraud to occur.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's coverage succeeds by shifting the focus from the numbers themselves to the human effort required to verify them, framing the Luckin scandal as a triumph of physical detective work over digital obfuscation. The strongest part of the argument is the vivid description of the surveillance operation, which makes the abstract concept of financial fraud tangible and undeniable. Its biggest vulnerability is the lack of a clear roadmap for how US regulators can solve the audit deadlock without escalating geopolitical tensions, leaving the reader with a sobering realization that the system remains broken. The piece serves as a stark reminder that in the absence of transparency, the only truth is the one you can count yourself.
