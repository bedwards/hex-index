The knicks might actually be great?

Nate Silver · ·May 31, 2026 ·14 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Nate Silver does something rare for a data analyst: he abandons his usual skepticism to argue that a team with a "limited ceiling" has suddenly become the most dangerous in the league. While conventional wisdom dismisses playoff hot streaks as statistical noise, Silver leverages his proprietary rating systems to make a startling claim about the 2025-26 New York Knicks: they aren't just lucky; they are fundamentally different.

The Data Behind the Hype

Silver begins by dismantling the idea that this is merely a fluke. He points to a staggering statistic that defies historical precedent. "The 2025-26 New York Knicks currently have the best playoff scoring margin in NBA history at +19.4 points per game." This isn't just a good run; it places them in elite company alongside dynasties like Jordan's Bulls and the Shaq-Kobe Lakers.

The author argues that in basketball, recent performance carries more weight than in other sports due to the high volume of possessions. He writes, "In the NBA, 14 games is not a small sample and especially not in the playoffs." This framing is crucial because it challenges readers who are still calibrating their expectations based on the team's regular-season record or midseason slumps.

If you're calibrating your expectations for the Knicks based on their 53-win regular season, you're doing it wrong.

This insight lands hard. It forces a re-evaluation of the entire postseason narrative. Silver notes that unlike typical breakout teams, the Knicks are not young and unproven, nor are they veteran squads coasting through the regular season. They occupy a unique middle ground: experienced but hungry. The team's defensive evolution is central to this shift.

The Defensive Pivot

The core of Silver's argument rests on a dramatic improvement in defense that began after a January slump. He details how the Knicks' defensive rating jumped from 14th in the league last year to 7th this year, eventually becoming the best in the postseason.

"Very good offense, average defense is a plausible formula to win the Eastern Conference," Silver writes. "Great offense, good defense is what they'll probably need to beat San Antonio." This distinction highlights why the Knicks are now a title contender rather than just a playoff participant. The author draws a historical parallel to the 1970s and 1999 championship teams, noting that those squads also relied on defensive grit over offensive fireworks.

The Knicks have always risen and fallen with their defense; the Patrick Ewing teams actually had a below-average offense even in the years they reached the Finals, but a defense that would beat you into submission.

Critics might note that Silver's optimism relies heavily on opponent shooting percentages that are likely to regress. He acknowledges this, writing, "There's no doubt that the Knicks have also benefited from some shooting luck in the playoffs." However, he counters by adjusting the math: even if opponents shot league-average numbers, the Knicks would still hold a dominant +13.8 point margin per game.

The author also addresses the perceived defensive liabilities of Jalen Brunson and Karl-Anthony Towns. While Brunson is indeed a defensive liability, Silver argues that Towns has had "the best defensive season of his career" due to elite rebounding. He reframes the narrative around Towns, stating, "Rebounding has become underrated among analytical types: it doesn't count as a defensive stop until you grab the board." This nuanced take on advanced metrics separates this analysis from standard sports commentary.

The Offensive Evolution

On the offensive end, Silver identifies a specific tactical shift that elevates the team. It isn't just about scoring; it's about playmaking. He observes that Karl-Anthony Towns has transformed his role, with his assist rate doubling to become a "full-on point forward."

They've let the KAT out of the bag.

This shift creates a dual-fulcrum offense involving Brunson and Towns, which solves previous issues of predictability. Silver notes that while Towns' usage rate has fallen, the team's overall optionality has increased significantly. The author also points to the impact of Mike Brown replacing Tom Thibodeau, suggesting that reducing the heavy minutes load on key players like OG Anunoby and Mikal Bridges has unlocked a "higher gear" for the roster.

It's easier to find it more often when you don't run your players ragged with such a heavy minutes load as Thibs did.

This connects back to Silver's earlier point about sustainability. A team that relies on exhaustion is prone to collapse; a team that manages its energy profile can maintain dominance deeper into June. The author also briefly touches on the team's health, noting they are "more or less fully intact," which is a significant advantage over previous years where injuries derailed their momentum.

Bottom Line

Silver's strongest move is his refusal to dismiss the Knicks' run as a statistical anomaly, instead using deep historical context and advanced metrics to prove this team has fundamentally changed its identity. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that Towns can sustain elite defensive rebounding against the physical intensity of a San Antonio Spurs series led by Victor Wembanyama. If the Knicks win their first title since 1973, it will be a testament to Silver's thesis: that high-floor teams with elite defense and dual-fulcrum offenses can indeed break through.
