World Premiere: The Power of Standards

Fred Mills · The B1M ·May 8, 2019 · 47 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Fred Mills, founder of The B1M, doesn't just argue that construction needs better marketing; he posits that the industry's survival depends on a radical shift from shouting about its own greatness to letting standardized technology tell the story. In a sector plagued by stagnant productivity and a reputation for mud and manual labor, Mills and his partner Bluebeam present a startling thesis: the key to modernizing the world's most important industry isn't flashy new hardware, but the boring, unglamorous work of agreeing on how we share PDFs.

The Perception Gap

Mills opens with a candid admission that the industry suffers from a severe image crisis. He notes that when the public thinks of construction, they imagine "white men working on muddy construction sites wolf whistling women in the street." This vivid, self-deprecating description effectively dismantles the industry's own complacency. The core of his argument is that this misperception is a talent drain; as he writes, "we cannot attract the best talents to this industry if our society does not understand or know about our sector." This lands because it connects a PR problem directly to a workforce crisis, a link often overlooked in trade publications.

However, Mills goes further than just asking for sympathy. He frames The B1M's mission as a bridge between the "dry and dull" reality of trade magazines and the mass audience that needs to be engaged. "My mission in life is a simple one," he states, "I love construction and I want the whole world to love it too." This emotional appeal is the engine of his argument, suggesting that passion, not just policy, is required to change the narrative. Critics might note that relying on emotional storytelling risks glossing over the hard, unsexy economic realities of construction, but Mills is betting that engagement is the prerequisite for solving those realities.

The Boring Revolution

The piece takes a sharp turn when James Chambers of Bluebeam Software enters the conversation, shifting the focus from perception to practical utility. The argument here is counter-intuitive: in an era obsessed with drones, virtual reality, and artificial intelligence, the most transformative tool is a standardized PDF workflow. Chambers illustrates this with a powerful anecdote about project assistants who spent "eight to twelve hours splitting apart pdfs" before discovering automation that reduced the task to "25 minutes."

"It's not just about technology; it's the technology that supports human interaction."

This quote captures the essence of the argument. The authors are not selling a new gadget; they are selling a common language. Mills and Chambers argue that the industry is fragmented, with designers, contractors, and subcontractors speaking different technical dialects. By standardizing how documents are marked up and shared, the industry can stop competing on who has the best file format and start competing on how well they build. "This is what happens when you stop telling and start compelling," Mills asserts, suggesting that the act of standardization itself is the most compelling story the industry has to tell.

The evidence presented is compelling because it is grounded in the daily struggles of the workforce. The story of the "epiphany moment" where a customer realized they could no longer stay in the "paper world" is a microcosm of the entire industry's transition. Yet, a counterargument worth considering is whether standardization can truly keep pace with the rapid evolution of technology. If the industry standardizes today's PDF workflows, will they be obsolete in five years? The authors seem to assume that interoperability is a timeless goal, which is a bold but necessary gamble.

The Productivity Paradox

Perhaps the most significant claim in the text is the link between standardization and productivity. Mills points out that "productivity UK construction hasn't really shifted" in over twenty years. He argues that this stagnation is not due to a lack of innovation, but a lack of collaboration on the basics. "If we can collaborate around standards that help us improve that productivity by taking out the inefficiency then that's going to help us be a more successful industry," he concludes.

This reframing of the productivity problem is the piece's strongest intellectual move. Instead of blaming workers or management, the authors blame the lack of a shared framework. The argument is that technology cannot work at its "best capacity" without a consensus on how data is structured. This is a subtle but profound shift: it suggests that the next great leap in construction won't come from a new machine, but from a new agreement on how we talk to each other.

"This is the sector that doesn't shout about itself... this is what happens when you stop boring and start engaging people how great construction really is."

Bottom Line

Mills and Chambers make a persuasive case that the construction industry's future lies not in chasing the next shiny object, but in mastering the unglamorous art of standardization. Their strongest argument is that a shared language is the prerequisite for any meaningful technological advancement. However, the biggest vulnerability remains execution: convincing a notoriously fragmented industry to agree on a single standard is a monumental challenge that no amount of cinematic storytelling can fully solve. Readers should watch to see if this "Power of Standards" can move from a compelling narrative to a universally adopted reality.
