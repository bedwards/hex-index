The Chinese Student Crisis

PolyMatter · PolyMatter ·Mar 12, 2022 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

PolyMatter exposes a disturbing reality: the very system American universities rely on to survive financially is built on a foundation of manipulated metrics and exploited international students. The author argues that college rankings are not merely flawed guides but active engines of deception, turning education into a commodity where prestige is manufactured rather than earned. For anyone paying attention to the future of higher education, this is not just an academic critique—it is an economic warning shot.

The Architecture of Deceit

The piece begins by identifying the perfect storm of information asymmetry. PolyMatter writes, "an 18 year old is more informed about the brand of shoes they wear than the institution at which they'll spend 200 weeks of their lives." This observation is sharp because it highlights a fundamental market failure. Students are making high-stakes financial decisions with almost no data, relying entirely on the seller's narrative. When you add the layer of international distance, the problem becomes critical. The author notes that for the 340,000 Chinese students in the U.S., the decision is made "seven thousand miles away, having never stepped foot in the country."

This vacuum of information is where rankings step in, not as neutral arbiters, but as predators. PolyMatter argues that these lists are "complete and utter garbage" that prey on anxiety. The author points out a specific, perverse incentive: rankings often include the proportion of international students as a metric for quality. This creates a feedback loop where schools are rewarded for recruiting more foreign students, regardless of their fit or success. "At some point to reject international students is to willfully forgo prestige and therefore revenue," PolyMatter writes. This is a devastating critique of how universities prioritize their balance sheets over student outcomes. Critics might argue that international students do bring cultural diversity, but the author's focus is on the financial motivation driving admissions, which often leads to social bubbles and inadequate support.

Rankings measure reputation and reputations are determined by ranking, creating a psychological pyramid scheme where everyone plays along even if they know the game is rigged.

The Commodification of Education

The commentary shifts to the economic origins of this crisis. PolyMatter traces the explosion of international enrollment to the 2008 recession, when state funding evaporated and universities needed new revenue streams. The author describes how schools began "exporting the product slapping their logos on deep deep pocketed modular architecture everywhere and importing customers." This framing is effective because it strips away the romanticism of the "global campus" and reveals a cold business transaction. International students, particularly from China, became the "lifeboat on the Titanic" for universities facing budget cuts.

The author details how rankings are gamed to attract these students. Metrics like class size are manipulated; schools cap classes at 19 students to game the "20 or fewer" rule in U.S. News rankings, even if it means restricting access to top professors. Similarly, research citations are boosted not for academic rigor, but to satisfy ranking formulas. PolyMatter writes, "the number of academic publications a school produces has almost nothing to do with the undergraduate experience." This distinction is crucial. It exposes the gap between what rankings claim to measure (quality) and what they actually measure (marketing and volume). The author notes that "few Americans pay the price of tuition shown on google," meaning international students are effectively subsidizing the entire system while being sold a product based on fake data.

The Volatility of Prestige

Perhaps the most damning evidence PolyMatter presents is the sheer instability of the ranking systems themselves. The author points out that rankings are not precise enough to distinguish between the first and tenth place, yet they are treated as absolute truth. In one egregious example, administrators at the University of Malaya were fired after their school dropped 80 places due to a mere change in definition. "This volatility is a core part of the ranking's business model," the author argues, producing annual drama that keeps the industry relevant. The system relies on a "threat either hand over the data we demand or your rankings will suffer," forcing compliance from institutions that would otherwise reject the methodology.

Even the reputation component, which makes up half of some ranking formulas, is flawed. It relies on surveys where respondents are rewarded with free swag, leading to absurd results like a business school being ranked in the top 10 despite not existing. PolyMatter writes, "Professor Emmanuel Orterica calls rankings Harvardometers suggesting they merely measure how close a school is to what we already assumed to be the best." This circular logic means that rankings reinforce existing biases rather than revealing true quality. The author concludes that "everyone suffers" in this system: professors farm citations, students are deceived, and administrators are encouraged to cheat.

Bottom Line

PolyMatter's strongest asset is the unflinching connection between financial desperation and academic fraud, showing how the 2008 recession permanently altered the incentives of higher education. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its focus on the supply side; while it brilliantly explains why universities cheat, it offers less on how students can practically navigate this broken market beyond avoiding rankings entirely. The reader should watch for the inevitable backlash as domestic funding pressures rise again, potentially leading to even more aggressive exploitation of international markets.

The real problem is much much deeper: almost everything wrong with higher education today is a symptom of one giant interconnected web of deceit.
