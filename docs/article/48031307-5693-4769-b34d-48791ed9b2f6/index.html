Why Iran is so hard to defeat

Shirvan Neftchi · CaspianReport ·Aug 25, 2024 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Shirvan Neftchi challenges the conventional Western military playbook by arguing that the most effective way to defeat Iran is not through invasion, but through a strategy that has historically failed: exploiting its internal ethnic fractures. While many analysts focus on Tehran's missile arsenal or its proxy network, Neftchi posits that the true source of Iran's resilience lies in a 500-year-old geopolitical reality where geography and religion have forged a fortress that is nearly impossible to breach from the outside.

The Geography of Survival

Neftchi's central thesis rests on the idea that Iran's topography is not merely a backdrop but an active participant in its survival. He writes, "Geography reveals the soul of a Nation," and proceeds to map out how the Zagros and Alborz mountain ranges act as natural walls that have repelled empires from the Romans to the British. The author effectively illustrates that these mountains do more than just protect; they confine the population into a highland belt, making the logistics of a foreign invasion a "Herculean task." This framing is compelling because it shifts the discussion from military hardware to the immutable laws of terrain, suggesting that no amount of technological superiority can easily overcome the physical reality of the Iranian plateau.

"Simply put, fighting Iran head-on is a terrible idea. It could backfire disproportionately."

The argument gains further weight when Neftchi connects this physical isolation to the country's strategic leverage. He notes that the Strait of Hormuz, guarded by the port of Bandar Abbas, allows Iran to threaten a "fifth of the world's petroleum," giving the state immense bargaining power even when its conventional military is outmatched. However, this geographic strength is a double-edged sword. Neftchi points out that while the mountains shield Iran, they also "contain it," preventing easy expansion and forcing the state to manage a complex web of internal diversity rather than a homogenous nation.

The Myth of the Homogenous State

A critical pivot in Neftchi's analysis is the dismantling of the idea that Iran is a monolith. He details how the country is a patchwork of Azeris, Kurds, Baloch, Arabs, and Turkmen, with ethnic Persians comprising only about 55 to 60 percent of the population. "Iran's mountains make fully assimilating its many diverse cultures and peoples exceedingly tricky," he observes, noting that these groups often maintain deep ties to kin across the border. This is where the author's analysis becomes particularly sharp for policymakers: the very borders that protect Iran also harbor the seeds of its potential fragmentation.

Critics might note that Neftchi leans heavily on the assumption that ethnic solidarity automatically translates to political separatism, overlooking the fact that many minority groups in Iran have historically prioritized national stability over cross-border ethnic unity. Yet, the author supports his caution by citing historical precedents, such as the failed Iraqi attempt to incite Arab separatism in the oil-rich Khuzestan province during the Iran-Iraq War. Neftchi writes, "Saddam hoped that Iran's Arab minority... would take up arms and support the Iraqi invasion. They did not." This historical evidence suggests that while ethnic tension is a vulnerability, it is not a guaranteed weakness.

"The fabric of society is based on shared values... sometimes Nations can achieve the same result by creating the 'us versus them' narrative."

Neftchi argues that the Safavid Empire's decision to declare Shia Islam the state religion in 1501 was the masterstroke that unified these disparate groups under a single identity, effectively overriding ethnic loyalty with religious cohesion. This religious unity, he contends, is the "two candles with one flame" that allows Iran to project power regionally while maintaining internal order. However, he acknowledges a growing crack in this foundation: the secularization of Iranian society. As he puts it, "Shia Islam is steadily losing its cohesive power," leaving the regime to rely increasingly on "good old-fashioned brute force" to suppress dissent.

The Authoritarian Dilemma

The commentary takes a darker turn as Neftchi examines the regime's reaction to these internal pressures. He describes a state that views liberal democracy not as a path to prosperity, but as an existential threat that would accelerate disintegration. "Iran believes that by embracing liberal ideas its cohesion would drift apart within a few generations," he explains, suggesting that the leadership sees authoritarianism as the only glue holding the country together. This perspective offers a chilling explanation for the regime's brutal crackdowns on protests, framing them not just as power grabs, but as desperate measures to prevent the "pain of regret" that would come from national fragmentation.

"Iran must choose one of two pains: the pain of discipline or the pain of regret."

The author's conclusion is stark: the West's desire to promote democracy in Iran may be counterproductive if the goal is to destabilize the regime, because the regime's survival depends on suppressing the very freedoms that democracy entails. Neftchi suggests that foreign-backed separatist movements would face an "uphill battle" against a security apparatus that has perfected the art of labeling dissenters as terrorists.

Bottom Line

Neftchi's most valuable contribution is his reframing of Iran not as a failing state on the brink of collapse, but as a resilient entity whose survival is engineered by its geography and a hard-won religious identity. The argument's greatest vulnerability, however, lies in its somewhat static view of religious cohesion, potentially underestimating how rapidly the "us versus them" narrative can erode in the face of economic collapse and generational change. Readers should watch for how the Iranian state balances its reliance on religious unity against the rising tide of secular nationalism, as this tension will likely define the next decade of Middle Eastern geopolitics.
