Excerpt from the original article on The Baseball Nerd

Some of you know exactly what wRC+ is. You’ve been using it for years. You pull it up on FanGraphs before I finish the sentence.

There are readers here who are just starting to get into advanced stats. People who love baseball deeply, who watch every game, who argue lineup construction and rotation decisions with genuine conviction, but who haven’t yet had a reason to dig into what wRC+ actually means or why it shows up in every serious hitting breakdown. When they land on one of my deep dives and hit a string of letters they don’t recognize, I don’t want them to close the tab. I want them to understand what the number is saying and why it matters, because the whole point of this publication is that the stats don’t flatten the game. They add to it. They reveal things. They tell stories that the box score alone never could.

That’s what I’m trying to build here. A community of people who love baseball for its intricacies, who find the numbers as compelling as the narratives, and who want both. To do that, everyone needs to be working with the same toolkit.

wRC+ is going to show up constantly in my analysis this season. Every player evaluation, every deep dive, every argument about whether a hitter is actually producing or just putting up counting stats in a favorable lineup. It’s the foundation of most of the hitting work I do. So before the first series is over, here’s the piece that explains it properly.

**wRC+ is the single most useful number for evaluating a hitter.** Not batting average. Not RBI. Not OPS, though OPS is at least asking the right questions. wRC+, Weighted Runs Created Plus, measures how much offensive value a hitter produces per plate appearance compared to the rest of the league, after adjusting for the ballpark he plays in and the run environment of the current era.

The number on the back of a baseball card lies to you. A .285 batting average sounds impressive. It might be. Or the hitter might play half his games in Coors Field in a season where the entire league was hitting .290. The raw number doesn’t know that. It doesn’t care.

wRC+ does. It is one number. It is ballpark adjusted, era adjusted, and every offensive action is accounted for. That’s why it’s the anchor stat ...
