China’s Authoritarianism Is Freedom!

Chris Chappell · China Uncensored ·Jan 5, 2021 · 29 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Chris Chappell exposes a disturbing convergence in modern journalism: the New York Times is increasingly echoing the propaganda lines of the Chinese Communist Party, framing authoritarian surveillance as a viable, even superior, form of freedom. In a sharp critique of a recent business section article, Chappell argues that Western media is not just failing to challenge Beijing's narrative but is actively amplifying it to regain access to the Chinese market. This matters now because the definition of "freedom" is being quietly rewritten by major American institutions, potentially reshaping global policy and public perception of human rights.

The Illusion of Choice

Chappell begins by dismantling the premise that surveillance and personal liberty can coexist. He highlights the absurdity of the New York Times claiming that "surveillance and censorship bolster Beijing's uncompromising grip on power" while simultaneously asserting that citizens have "resumed normal lives." The author points out the logical fallacy in suggesting that a population can be truly free while living under a regime that "harvests the organs of its citizens" and maintains "concentration camps," simply because they are not currently wearing masks. This framing is dangerous because it equates the absence of a pandemic lockdown with the presence of civil liberty, ignoring the systemic oppression that defines the Chinese state.

"The whole gist of this article is... surveillance and censorship is bad but it's also good because you can lead a normal life now under surveillance and censorship."

Chappell notes that the article in question relies entirely on Chinese government data, which he describes as a "lie" that has been debunked by independent observers. He argues that the New York Times accepts these numbers as fact to construct a narrative where China has "conquered the coronavirus" while the West fails. This is not journalism; it is a strategic pivot. The author suggests that the newspaper is desperate to return to China after being blocked, leading them to publish stories that flatter the regime. "The New York Times desperately wants to get back into China," Chappell asserts, noting that the piece was published in the business section, targeting investors rather than truth-seekers.

Critics might argue that the New York Times is simply reporting on the economic reality that China has reopened while the West struggles, but Chappell contends that the omission of the human cost renders the reporting dishonest. The article interviews wealthy businessmen who feel "safe, energized, and free" in China, ignoring the fact that their freedom is a privilege of class that does not extend to the general populace. One interviewee, Mr. Dong, claims he cannot find freedom in Hong Kong due to lockdowns, not the "draconian national security law." Chappell finds this inversion of reality particularly insidious, as it blames public health measures for the loss of liberty while ignoring the actual authoritarian crackdowns.

The Propaganda Feedback Loop

The commentary shifts to the broader implications of this media overlap. Chappell draws a parallel between the current situation and the Bolshevik Revolution coverage, suggesting a long-term trend of Western media softening its stance on communism to maintain access. He recalls how the New York Times was blocked in China until a "fawning interview" with a Chinese leader led to their re-entry. Now, the newspaper is publishing lines that "could have come straight from the Global Times," China's state-run tabloid.

"There's no denying that China's success in containing [the virus] has burnished Beijing's image especially when compared with the failures of the United States."

Chappell argues that this is not accidental but a calculated effort to normalize the idea that "freedom is slavery" and "slavery is freedom," a direct reference to George Orwell's 1984. The piece suggests that the West must "work harder to sell its vision of freedom" because China has made its model seem attractive. This framing creates a false equivalence where the "freedom to move around" is weighed against the "freedom of speech," implying they are interchangeable values. Chappell warns that this ideological twisting allows the Chinese Communist Party to claim victory in human rights by pointing to poverty reduction, even as they suppress basic civil liberties.

The author also touches on the geopolitical fallout, noting how Chinese propaganda influenced the global lockdown response, particularly in Italy, by promoting their authoritarian model as the only solution. Now, as the West grapples with the consequences, the New York Times is essentially validating that model. "The Venn diagram of Chinese state-run media and American media... the circles were pretty separate. However these days there's a lot of overlap," Chappell observes. This overlap is not a coincidence but a result of economic pressure and a willingness to compromise journalistic integrity for market access.

"We have freedom in the US but we're slaves to being locked in our house but in China where you might be a slave if you're really free so slavery is freedom."

Bottom Line

Chappell's critique is a necessary correction to a narrative that risks normalizing authoritarianism under the guise of pandemic recovery. The strongest part of his argument is the exposure of the economic motivations driving the New York Times' coverage, revealing a clear conflict of interest between their business goals and their journalistic mission. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that all Western media is equally compromised, which may overlook the nuanced efforts of other outlets to maintain independence. Readers should watch for how this "freedom" narrative evolves as the pandemic subsides and the true cost of China's model becomes undeniable to the global community.
