China's Overcapacity Trap and the Limits of Escalation Dominance

Multiple sources: PolyMatter PolyMatterand 1 other
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

Two recent video essays arrive at the same crossroads from opposite directions. PolyMatter argues that China is about to start a trade war — not out of strength, but out of a domestic deflationary spiral it cannot otherwise escape. Joeri Schasfoort of Money & Macro, by contrast, examines the trade war already underway and asks whether Beijing can actually win it. Read together, the two pieces sketch a more uncomfortable picture than either offers alone: a confrontation in which both sides believe the other is the desperate party.

The Paradox of Cheap Everything

PolyMatter opens with what sounds, to an American ear, like utopia. "China has a problem — its groceries are too cheap," he notes, before cataloguing the symptoms: falling prices on milk, televisions, vacations and clothing; record exports of increasingly sophisticated goods; sturdy bridges, fast trains, spotless airports. "To a New Yorker, San Franciscan or Seattleite with our crumbling infrastructure, diminished manufacturing and sky-high inflation, all this may sound downright absurd." The punchline is that these are not signs of health. They are symptoms of overcapacity and deflation — too many factories chasing too few domestic buyers, with the surplus dumped onto world markets.

"All of these trends, appealing as they may sound, are actually symptoms of a much larger economic problem — one China may soon attempt to solve by launching a trade war."

In PolyMatter's telling, the bicycle assembled in Guangdong and sold in Columbus, Ohio is not the virtuous circle of Economics 101. It is a pressure valve. When household consumption stays stuck near 38 percent of GDP and investment has exhausted its productive uses, exports become the only remaining outlet — and trading partners, eventually, push back.

Escalation Dominance, or a Losing Hand?

Schasfoort takes up the story from the other side of the Pacific. He shows a map of the world's largest trading partners shifting from American blue to Chinese red over two decades, and concedes the obvious: "No wonder the US escalated an unprecedented trade war against China." He then lays out the prevailing view among macro commentators. Ray Dalio, he notes, is "convinced that what we are seeing today is a classic pattern of how a rising power displaces an old hegemon — first through trade, and then by replacing its currency as the global reserve currency."

But Schasfoort refuses to concede the point. He quotes Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent bluntly: "If you look at the history of the trade negotiations, we are the deficit country. What do we lose by the Chinese raising tariffs on us? We export one-fifth to them of what they export to us. That is a losing hand for them." The asymmetry cuts in Washington's favor on the goods ledger, even if it cuts the other way on rare earths, pharmaceutical precursors, and the industrial commons.

"We are the deficit country. What do we lose by the Chinese raising tariffs on us?"

Where the Two Accounts Collide

The tension between the two videos is productive. PolyMatter frames Beijing as the aggressor-in-waiting, pushed by its own deflationary dynamics into confrontation. Schasfoort frames Beijing as the structurally weaker party in a contest it did not choose to escalate and cannot easily win. Both can be true. A country can be simultaneously desperate enough to lash out and too dependent on foreign demand to prevail.

Neither essay fully grapples with a third possibility worth raising: that the framing of "winning" a trade war is itself a category error. The 1930s precedent, which Dalio invokes selectively, suggests that protracted tariff spirals tend to impoverish both sides while accelerating the political realignments that produce shooting wars. The deficit country does not "win" simply by having less to lose in dollar terms; it loses more slowly.

The Counterpoints Neither Source Raises

PolyMatter's overcapacity thesis is strong but leaves out that American industrial policy — the CHIPS Act, Inflation Reduction Act subsidies, and tariff walls maintained by successive administrations of both parties — is itself a form of state-directed overcapacity building. If Beijing is guilty of subsidizing factories into a deflationary glut, Washington is now attempting the same trick in semiconductors and batteries. The two systems are converging on industrial policy even as they denounce each other for practicing it.

Schasfoort, for his part, accepts the Treasury Department's goods-trade framing a little too readily. Services, capital flows, and the dollar's reserve status complicate the "deficit country has less to lose" argument considerably. If Beijing were to accelerate yuan-denominated commodity settlement with Gulf producers — a process already quietly underway — the leverage calculation shifts.

Bottom Line

The honest read of these two videos together is that neither Beijing nor Washington is in a position of comfort. China faces a domestic demand problem it has postponed for fifteen years and can postpone no longer. The United States faces a manufacturing base hollowed out by the very trade relationship it now wants to unwind. Both governments are betting that the other will blink first, and both are probably wrong about how much pain their own populations will tolerate before demanding a settlement. The trade war is not a contest one side wins. It is a shared deleveraging — of supply chains, of assumptions, of the post-1989 bargain — and the question is only which political system metabolizes the adjustment with less damage.
