Waiting is a revelation

L. M. Sacasas · ·Jan 1, 2026 ·17 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In an era defined by the collapse of delay, L. M. Sacasas makes a counter-intuitive claim: the virtues we are losing are not found in efficiency, but in the friction of waiting. While most cultural criticism focuses on the speed of technology, Sacasas reframes the pause not as a failure of logistics, but as a necessary condition for human depth, arguing that our rush to eliminate the gap between desire and fulfillment is actually eroding our capacity for attention and freedom.

The Architecture of Impatience

Sacasas begins by observing the modern erosion of the "space between desire and fulfillment." He contrasts the ancient, patient rhythm of Advent with the "freneticism, exhaustion, and, too often, emptiness" of our current commercial season. The author notes that while the standard delivery window has shrunk from weeks to minutes, we have not gained time; we have lost the virtue of patience. "The space between desire and fulfillment or impulse and satisfaction has been effectively collapsed," Sacasas writes, suggesting that this collapse abets vices like rashness and profligacy.

This framing is powerful because it shifts the blame from individual willpower to the structural design of our economy. Sacasas argues that we are not merely choosing to be impatient; we are being conditioned by a "just-in-time economy" that thrives on our deprivation of time. However, critics might note that this analysis risks romanticizing waiting, ignoring that for many, waiting is not a spiritual exercise but a symptom of systemic failure—waiting for justice, for a cure, or for basic necessities.

"Waiting is not a passage of time to be traversed but a condition of our being … an opportunity to encounter those aspects of life deeply, perhaps neurotically, hidden in our busyness."

The Fear of Silence

To explain why we flee from waiting, Sacasas turns to the seventeenth-century philosopher Blaise Pascal. The author reminds readers that Pascal's famous observation—that "all of humanity's problems stem from man's inability to sit quietly in a room alone"—predates the smartphone by centuries. This historical context is crucial; it suggests that our anxiety is not a new technological defect but a fundamental human condition. Sacasas explains that we distract ourselves not because we are bored, but because solitude forces us to confront the "natural poverty of our feeble and mortal condition."

By invoking Pascal, Sacasas elevates the discussion from mere time management to existential philosophy. The argument holds up well: our digital diversions are indeed a shield against the uncomfortable reality of our own mortality and limitations. Yet, the piece could go further in addressing how the sheer volume of modern distraction makes this "quiet room" nearly impossible to find, even for those with the resources to avoid waiting.

Waiting as Resistance

The essay pivots from the psychological to the political, drawing on Henri Bergson's concept of time as "duration." Sacasas paraphrases literary scholar Harold Schweizer to explain that we only truly experience time when it resists our will. "In waiting, the waiter thus feels—impatiently—his own being: it is a feeling of the un-measurable, perhaps immeasurable, that which cannot be protracted or contracted," Sacasas writes. This is the crux of the argument: waiting strips away the illusion of control.

Sacasas then makes a bold move, redefining waiting not as a passive state but as an active form of resistance. In a world that demands immediate gratification, the choice to wait becomes a way to "relinquish the desire to exert power" and to honor the integrity of others. "If we claim our experience of waiting rather than being merely subjected to it, we resist the commercialization of time, we own our time, we make time matter—we matter," Sacasas asserts. This is the piece's most compelling insight: that in a techno-economic order designed to commodify every second, the act of tarrying is a radical assertion of human agency.

"To wait is to relinquish the desire to exert power, to achieve mastery, or to seek control in cases where such efforts would destroy the very goods that we desire."

The Virtue of Attention

Finally, Sacasas weaves in the thought of Iris Murdoch and Simone Weil to connect waiting with the capacity to love. The author argues that freedom is not the exercise of will, but the "liberation from fantasy" achieved through accurate vision. This vision requires what Weil called "a just and loving gaze directed upon an individual reality," a gaze that can only be sustained through patience. Sacasas concludes that waiting is essentially a "meditative temporal space" where we can cultivate the virtues necessary for a free society.

The synthesis of these philosophical traditions is elegant, but it leaves a practical question unanswered: how does one practice this "civic waiting" in a society where the infrastructure of daily life is designed to punish delay? The argument is philosophically robust but perhaps strategically fragile in the face of a world that rewards speed above all else.

Bottom Line

L. M. Sacasas offers a profound corrective to the cult of efficiency, successfully arguing that waiting is a vital, life-giving condition rather than a mere inconvenience. The piece's greatest strength is its reframing of waiting as a form of resistance against the commercialization of time, though its vulnerability lies in the difficulty of practicing this virtue in an economy that systematically eliminates it. Readers should watch for how this philosophy translates into concrete actions as the year unfolds.

Welcome to the Convivial Society, a newsletter about technology, culture and the moral life. And happy New Year. This installment took shape in the week between Christmas and New Year’s Eve, each associated with waiting, although the seconds we count down to midnight are rather different from the days some of us might count down to Christmas. In any case, this piece is about waiting. It is an attempt to reframe waiting as something other than tedious and wasteful, indeed, as something potentially life-giving. As always, I hope these reflections are valuable to you. Thank you for reading. And may this new year, inevitably laden with its frustrations and sorrows, also bring you joy and peace.

“Waiting is not a passage of time to be traversed but a condition of our being … an opportunity to encounter those aspects of life deeply, perhaps neurotically, hidden in our busyness. In waiting, in listening to the inward melody of duration, we become attuned to our being.”

— Harold Schweizer, “On Waiting”

I’m writing a couple of days after Christmas, and thus on the other side of the season of Advent. For those unfamiliar with the rhythms of the Christian liturgical calendar, Advent spans the four Sundays leading up to Christmas Day. Chiefly, it is a season of waiting, recalling and re-enacting an ancient anticipation of a long-expected Savior. The affective register of the season is characterized by patient longing, sober reflection, and resilient hope. Today, of course, this ancient tradition competes and mostly loses out to an alternative liturgical season that tends to be marked freneticism, exhaustion, and, too often, emptiness.1 Yet despite this, the spirit of ardent and even enchanted expectation seems to linger in the childhood experience of Christmas, even when it is observed in strictly secular contexts.2

Maybe it is because my own children have been especially eager for the arrival of Christmas this year. Maybe it’s because I recently learned that Amazon announced it would be piloting 30-minute deliveries in Philadelphia and Seattle, and I’m old enough to remember when the standard window for delivery of goods ordered by mail was six to eight weeks, which was occasionally long enough to forget that had you ordered anything at all! Whatever the case, I’ve been thinking about that practice of waiting and how unusual periods of sustained waiting have become.
