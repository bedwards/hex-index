Is the Gulf Stream collapsing?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Apr 19, 2020 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through Hollywood hype to reveal a far more insidious reality: the Atlantic's great ocean conveyor belt isn't just slowing; it is flirting with a collapse that could trigger regional cooling amidst global warming. While the 2004 film The Day After Tomorrow offered a thrilling, if scientifically bankrupt, narrative of instant ice, Borlace presents evidence that the real threat is a gradual, decades-long unraveling driven by fresh water from melting ice sheets. This distinction matters because the consequences of a sluggish Gulf Stream are not a sudden freeze, but a chaotic reshuffling of weather patterns that threatens agriculture, coastal cities, and global stability.

The Scale of the Stakes

Borlace begins by dismantling the cinematic myth, noting that the film's depiction of the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) collapsing in days "bought very little resemblance to real-world events or possibilities." Instead, he grounds the discussion in the sheer physical magnitude of the system. The Gulf Stream, he explains, moves "more than the amount of water carried by all the rivers of the world combined," a flow that acts as the planet's primary heat distributor. By stripping away the sensationalism, Borlace forces the reader to confront the quiet, massive machinery of the ocean that keeps Northern Europe temperate and the tropics habitable.

The author's use of high-definition modeling to visualize the problem is particularly effective. He describes how researchers at the Potsdam Institute used a simulation so precise it could outline the city of New York to track temperature shifts. The results were stark: a "pronounced cooling of the area of ocean just below Greenland" paired with warming along the U.S. East Coast. This visual evidence supports the core argument that the engine of the global conveyor belt is sputtering. As Borlace puts it, "The co2 doubling simulation predicted a very pronounced cooling... strongly indicating a big slowdown in heat being transferred across the Atlantic." This isn't just a temperature anomaly; it is a mechanical failure of the climate system.

The Fresh Water Paradox

The mechanism behind this slowdown is counterintuitive: the very heat that is melting the ice is the cause of the circulation's failure. Borlace explains that as the Greenland ice sheet melts, it releases "huge quantities of pure water into the salty ocean," which has the effect of freshening the water. Since fresh water is less dense than salty water, it refuses to sink, stalling the deep-ocean currents that drive the entire system. He cites a 2018 study showing the AMOC has declined by about 15% since the mid-20th century, reaching its weakest point in 1,600 years.

This freshening effect creates a dangerous feedback loop. The author notes that "less of it sinks and that slows down the engine of the great global thermohaline circulation." The irony is palpable: global warming is causing a regional cooling effect in the North Atlantic. Borlace highlights the complexity of this outcome, stating that while the net result is still warming, the "cold blob under Greenland" is already channeling warm air northwards over Europe, making heat waves more likely. Critics might note that regional cooling models can be difficult to isolate from natural variability, but the consistency of the data across multiple studies lends significant weight to Borlace's claims.

The entire world would experience major socio-economic consequences with catastrophic impacts on agriculture, wildlife, transport, energy demand, and coastal infrastructure, resulting in the likely loss of hundreds of millions of lives.

The Tipping Point and the Path Forward

Borlace does not shy away from the worst-case scenario, referencing a 1961 paper by Henry Stommel that identified a tipping point where the system could "create a very rapid collapse of the a mock." Modern models project that under high-emission scenarios, a shutdown could occur by 2300, bringing widespread cooling that might outweigh global warming effects. However, Borlace offers a crucial caveat: "Thankfully all the existing scientific evidence suggests we're not approaching that point anytime soon." This nuance is vital; it prevents the narrative from sliding into fatalism while maintaining urgency.

The author points to recent data, including a sharp dip in Arctic sea ice in early 2020, as a warning sign that the trend is accelerating. He connects this to the potential for "abrupt global warming" if atmospheric particles that reflect sunlight decrease, further stressing the system. The solution, he argues, is blunt and obvious: "The easiest and most blindingly obvious solution to this future calamity is to rapidly reduce our use of fossil fuels." While this may seem like a generic conclusion, Borlace frames it as the only way to prevent the system from losing its momentum entirely. He emphasizes that scientists are now racing to develop early warning systems to detect salinity changes decades in advance, buying time for humanity to act.

Bottom Line

Borlace's strongest asset is his ability to translate complex oceanographic data into a narrative of immediate, tangible risk without resorting to alarmist fiction. His argument is most vulnerable where it relies on long-term model projections that remain subject to uncertainty, yet the convergence of observational data and modeling makes the case for a weakening AMOC undeniable. The reader should watch for the next decade of Arctic sea ice records, as they will likely determine whether the conveyor belt stabilizes or crosses the threshold into irreversible collapse.
