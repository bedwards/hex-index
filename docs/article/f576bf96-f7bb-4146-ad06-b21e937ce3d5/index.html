Is the gulf stream collapsing?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Apr 19, 2020 ·12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Hollywood's The Day After Tomorrow promised an instant ice age, but the reality Dave Borlace uncovers is far more insidious: a slow-motion engine failure of the planet's heat distribution system that is already reshaping weather patterns across the globe. While the movie depicted a dramatic freeze, Borlace argues that the actual threat is a 15% slowdown of the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) driven by melting ice, a shift that is simultaneously making European summers hotter and American coastlines more vulnerable to hurricanes.

The Engine of the Climate

Borlace begins by dismantling the cinematic myth to reveal the sheer scale of the ocean's power. He notes that the Gulf Stream alone moves "more than the amount of water carried by all the rivers of the world combined," a staggering volume that acts as the primary heater for the Northern Hemisphere. This framing is crucial because it shifts the reader's perspective from a passive observer of weather to an understanding of the ocean as a massive, active climate control system. The author explains that this system, known as the AMOC, relies on a delicate balance of temperature and salinity; as warm water moves north, it cools, becomes denser, and sinks, driving a global conveyor belt that can take a millennium to complete a single circuit.

The core of Borlace's argument rests on the disruption of this density mechanism. He points to recent research from the Potsdam Institute which utilized high-definition modeling to show that "the co2 doubling simulation predicted a very pronounced cooling of the area of ocean just below Greenland." This is not a contradiction of global warming but a specific regional consequence of it. As the Arctic warms faster than the rest of the planet, the Greenland ice sheet melts, dumping fresh water into the salty ocean. Borlace clarifies the physics simply: "fresh water is less dense than salty water so less of it sinks and that slows down the engine of the great global thermohaline circulation." This explanation effectively bridges the gap between abstract climate data and tangible physical processes, making the mechanism of the slowdown accessible without oversimplifying the science.

The entire world would experience major socio-economic consequences with catastrophic impacts on agriculture, wildlife, transport, energy demand, and coastal infrastructure.

The Paradox of Cooling and Warming

What makes Borlace's coverage particularly compelling is his emphasis on the counterintuitive results of this slowdown. He highlights that while the North Atlantic region faces a "cooling effect," the net result for the planet remains warming, yet the local weather patterns become more extreme. He writes that the slowing conveyor belt "tends to cause an air pressure distribution that channels warm air northwards over Europe and that's making Europeans summertime heat waves more likely." This nuance is vital; it counters the simplistic narrative that a slowing Gulf Stream means a return to an ice age. Instead, Borlace argues it creates a chaotic mix of intensified storms, worsening droughts in the Sahel, and higher sea levels along the US East Coast.

Critics might note that the timeline for a total collapse remains uncertain, with some models suggesting a shutdown is centuries away. However, Borlace counters this complacency by pointing to the 2020 data, where Arctic sea ice levels dipped below previous records, coinciding with a reduction in atmospheric particles that usually reflect sunlight. He warns that "if that trend line continues all the way to the annual sea ice minimum in September then we may well be heading for a new record low that would most likely exert a noticeable influence on amock slowdown." This focus on immediate, observable trends rather than distant theoretical models grounds the urgency of the issue in current events.

The Tipping Point and the Path Forward

The stakes, as Borlace outlines, are existential. Citing research from the UK Met Office, he notes that under high-emission scenarios, models project a potential "effective amock shutdown by 2300," which would trigger widespread cooling in the north that could outweigh global warming effects. The author stresses that this is not a scenario to be welcomed, as it would bring "major shifts in rainfall patterns causing huge storms over Europe and a sea-level rise of about half a metre around the North Atlantic Basin." The argument here is that the system is approaching a tipping point where it could lose its momentum entirely, a threshold that scientists are now racing to monitor through salinity levels in subtropical waters.

Borlace concludes by stripping away the complexity to offer the only viable solution: "the easiest and most blindingly obvious solution to this future calamity is to rapidly reduce our use of fossil fuels in transport industry embrace renewable energy and regenerate our land and forests." This directness serves as a powerful call to action, framing the reduction of emissions not just as a climate goal but as a necessary intervention to keep the planet's heat engine running. While the scientific community continues to refine early warning systems, the author's message is clear that technology alone cannot fix a broken engine; the fuel source must change.

Bottom Line

Borlace's strongest asset is his ability to translate complex oceanographic data into a narrative of immediate, tangible risk, effectively debunking the "instant ice age" myth while presenting a more dangerous reality of climate chaos. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in the inherent uncertainty of predicting exactly when the tipping point will be crossed, though the trend data he presents suggests the window for prevention is narrowing. Readers should watch for the next annual Arctic sea ice minimum, as it will serve as a critical indicator of whether the AMOC is accelerating toward a collapse or stabilizing.

The entire world would experience major socio-economic consequences with catastrophic impacts on agriculture, wildlife, transport, energy demand, and coastal infrastructure.
