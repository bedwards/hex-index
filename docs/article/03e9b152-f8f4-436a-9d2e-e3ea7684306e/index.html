How To Measure The Tiniest Forces In The Universe

Commentary by Brian Edwards

The Kilogram Problem

The piece opens with a visual only a video can provide: tiny weights, some as small as an eyelash. Muller explains how the traditional kilogram works — a platinum-iridium cylinder stored in a vault on the outskirts of Paris. "The most precise standard weight people ever created was the kilogram of platinum iridium cylinder," he says, describing what scientists have used for over a century to calibrate scales worldwide.

But here's where it gets interesting. The uncertainty of that standard is about tens of micrograms — which Muller describes as "tens of parts per billion or about 0.00001%." That's incredibly precise. But there's a catch: if you want to measure something lighter than a kilogram, the uncertainty balloons. The smallest conventional masses are milligram weights made of stainless steel, and even handling them requires meticulous care — lint brushes, dust-free cloths, everything cleaned before each weigh.

A Physical Problem

Muller makes a crucial observation that reveals why metrology matters: "if you're working in a laboratory or something like that you could take one of these little weights and put it on here and you can look at the scale." The problem is that as you subdivide from kilogram to milligram, each step adds uncertainty. A 500-milligram mass might compare fine against two 200s plus a 100 — but go smaller and the errors compound.

The piece's most compelling section comes when Muller explains why this matters: "the replica kilograms were brought back to Paris a few times to be weighed with each other and from those measurements it became clear that their weights were diverging by up to 75 microgram." No one could tell if the replicas were getting heavier or the original lighter — but the definition was becoming unstable. This is why scientists redefined the kilogram based on Planck's constant, a universal constant of nature rather than a physical artifact.

The New Machines

The electrostatic force balance (or EFB) represents the cutting edge Muller showcases. Located underground with air temperature held at exactly 20°C to avoid thermal expansion, this lab creates vacuum conditions so no air currents or buoyant forces interfere. They even carefully measured the local gravitational acceleration — 9.81 m/s² — under the floor.

The Kibble balance compares gravitational force against electromagnetic force using a capacitor system. The electrical quantities can be read with extraordinary precision and traced directly to Planck's constant, giving what Muller calls "direct traceability." This is metrology's holy grail: measurements that connect all the way back to universal constants.

But Muller goes further — into atomic force microscopy. These tiny cantilever beams with sharp tips apply nano- to pico-newton forces. A pico-newton, as he notes, is about enough to stretch a DNA molecule. The laser sensor measures just seven pico-newtons of force — sufficient to pull apart single molecules.

Why It Matters

The piece's strongest move comes from answering its own question: "why does anyone need to measure forces this small?" Muller lists concrete applications: automotive manufacturers needing 50 micrograms of particulate measurement for emissions standards; pharmaceutical companies requiring milligram and microgram doses; laser power calibration in industrial processes. These aren't abstract concerns — they're practical problems driving real innovation.

But the deeper answer is more interesting: "the scientific new scientific discoveries benefit from the new measurement capabilities which then feed into new Precision Metrology capabilities." This circular relationship — better measurement enabling discovery, which creates even finer instruments — is what Muller identifies as the heart of metrology's value. It's not just about precision for precision's sake; it's about pushing science forward.

Counterpoints

A critic might note that this piece focuses heavily on demonstration rather than explaining why these measurements are actually used in everyday products. The applications mentioned (ex emissions, pharmaceuticals) are named but not explored in detail. Similarly, the explanation of Planck's constant and its relationship to mass is brief — viewers familiar with physics may want more mathematical depth.

Bottom Line

Muller delivers what he promises: a tour through how we measure the tiniest forces, from kilogram standards down to pico-newton sensors. The strongest element is his narrative arc — showing that measurement precision isn't abstract but drives real scientific discovery. The vulnerability is that the piece sometimes prioritizes demonstration over explaining practical applications; viewers may wonder after watching what these measurements actually enable. For anyone curious about how science gets more precise, this serves as a strong introduction to metrology's deepest questions.
