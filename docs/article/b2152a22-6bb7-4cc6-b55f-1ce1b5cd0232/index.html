Amsterdam Closed This Bridge to Cars

Jason Slaughter · Not Just Bikes ·Mar 31, 2024 · 17 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jason Slaughter doesn't just document a bridge repair; he dismantles the myth that Amsterdam's bike-friendly culture is a relic of a pre-car past. By focusing on the Bara Bridge—a structure built decades after the Model T—and the surrounding neighborhoods constructed in the 1920s and 30s, Slaughter exposes a dangerous narrative used by critics of modern urban design.

The Myth of the Pre-Car City

The most striking element of Slaughter's coverage is his historical correction. He points out that the area surrounding the bridge is not ancient history but a product of the early automotive era. "If you take the release of the Model T in 1908 as the date that cars became common then this is the only part of Amsterdam that existed at that time," Slaughter writes. "And this is everything constructed afterwards."

This is a devastating rebuttal to the common excuse that cities like Amsterdam are bike-friendly simply because they were built before cars dominated. Slaughter argues that the vast majority of the city's iconic infrastructure was built after the automobile arrived, yet it was designed with people in mind, not vehicles. "Heck about half the city was built after the 1960s which makes this even more ridiculous," he notes. The author's evidence is irrefutable: the Bara Bridge itself, opened in 1932, was a massive engineering feat designed for a mixed-traffic future that never fully materialized until mid-century car culture took over.

Critics might argue that the density of Amsterdam makes this model impossible to replicate in sprawling North American cities. However, Slaughter suggests the issue isn't density but design intent. The original plan for the area, known as Plan Zuid, was created by architect Hendrik Petrus Berlage to be a cohesive neighborhood, not a car corridor.

"Cities aren't loud. Cars are loud."

From Boulevard to Car Sewer

Slaughter traces the degradation of the space around the bridge, specifically the avenue leading to it. Originally a grand boulevard, the space was gradually eroded by the demand for car throughput. He describes how the avenue was widened and repurposed, noting, "This entire area built in the 1920s and 30s was known as Plan Zuid and was designed by Baraka himself... well except the parts that were bulldozed to build car infrastructure of course."

The author highlights a specific design flaw that plagues many cities: the confusion between a "road" and a "street." He explains that the original design allowed for efficient car travel without sacrificing the livability of the neighborhood, a balance that was lost when the space was converted into a six-lane road infested with cars by the 1970s. "This is a great example of something I've talked about many times on this channel the difference between the design of a road compared to a street," Slaughter observes. The shift from a destination-friendly street to a dangerous through-road required decades of protest from cycling advocates to reverse.

The commentary on the 1980s struggle is particularly vivid. Slaughter recounts how the city attempted to reclaim bike lanes for cars in 1984, only to be stopped by the Cycling Union. "The painted bicycle lane was eventually replaced with a curb protected bicycle path," he writes, marking a turning point where the city finally recognized that safety required physical separation, not just paint.

The Construction as a Case Study

The core of the piece focuses on the recent four-month closure of the bridge for renovation. Slaughter uses this temporary disruption to prove a larger point about urban resilience. When cars were banned, the city didn't collapse; it quieted down. "Once construction began there was almost no car traffic here and it was so nice for the road outside of my office to be quiet for a change," he shares.

The temporary bridge, designed exclusively for pedestrians and cyclists, revealed the inefficiency of the permanent car-centric infrastructure. Slaughter notes that without cars, the need for traffic lights vanished. "When the traffic lights are out the road markings take precedence," he explains. This observation challenges the assumption that complex traffic signals are necessary for safety, suggesting instead that they are often artifacts of car prioritization.

The renovation also allowed for the installation of innovative cycling infrastructure, such as the "banana" and "freak cone" designs at intersections. Slaughter describes these as critical for managing high volumes of cyclists: "The freak cone is here the bicycle path is wider at the start than at the end like the shape of a freak cone. This allows more people to queue up to wait and when the light goes green people cycling can easily negotiate who goes first."

"Transportation engineers in other countries should probably take note."

While the new design is largely praised, Slaughter identifies a lingering flaw: the shared lane for public transit and cars on the bridge deck. He points out that "a single car turning left can block a public transit vehicle with dozens of people on it." This is a valid counterpoint; even in a bike-centric city, car infrastructure can still bottleneck mass transit, suggesting that the transition away from car dominance is incomplete.

Bottom Line

Jason Slaughter's coverage is a masterclass in using local history to debunk global myths about urban design. His strongest argument is that Amsterdam's success is a result of intentional, people-first engineering, not historical accident. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a specific cultural context that may be difficult to export without significant political will. However, the visual evidence of a quieter, safer city during the bridge closure offers a compelling preview of what is possible when cars are deprioritized.
