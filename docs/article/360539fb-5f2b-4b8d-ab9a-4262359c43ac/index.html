The Oligarchical Corridor

Timothy Snyder · Thinking about... ·Mar 5, 2026 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Timothy Snyder drops a bombshell that reframes the current conflict not as a clash of civilizations, but as a private transaction between wealthy elites. He argues that the war against Iran lacks any traditional American origin story, emerging instead from a "closed domain of international oligarchs" who are using the U.S. military to settle regional scores while the American state atrophies. For a reader trying to make sense of why a war started with no public debate and no clear threat, Snyder offers a chillingly specific map of the decision-making process that bypasses the Constitution entirely.

The Missing American Origin

Snyder begins by dismantling the assumption that this is an American war in any meaningful sense. He writes, "If we examine the origins, or what we can discern of them, we get an inkling of something rather different: a closed domain of international oligarchs, exploiting the state's power and patriotic sentiment, while creating a world order in which the American state is much weaker, or simply ceases to function." This is a bold claim, suggesting that the machinery of state has been hijacked by a private channel of communication. The author's logic here is compelling because it explains the sudden, inexplicable timing of the conflict. Why Iran now? Why not Cuba, or a different target entirely? Snyder notes that while Americans have wanted war with Iran for decades, the specific trigger is missing from the domestic record.

He points out that in every previous major conflict, there was an "American component, some American process," whether it was a genuine threat, a secession crisis, or a government propaganda campaign. Here, that input is absent. "There was no threat, abroad or at home, nor any subjective public sense of such a threat," Snyder observes. This absence is the piece's most striking evidence. It forces the reader to look elsewhere for the cause. Critics might argue that presidential authority is inherently unilateral and that a formal debate is not always required for military action. However, Snyder counters that even when presidents act alone, they usually justify it with a narrative that resonates with the public or the government; Trump's silence on the "why" is the anomaly that demands explanation.

The pathway to Trump's mind seems to be an oligarchical corridor.

The Corridor and Its Emissaries

Once the traditional American inputs are ruled out, Snyder identifies the alternative channel: the "oligarchical corridor." He traces this directly to the private emissaries who have replaced formal diplomats. "Decades from now historians will be debating why this war started... because there will be little written evidence. What passes for American foreign policy is largely carried out by private emissaries who are often unaccompanied by note-takers." This is a stark departure from the institutional competence of the State Department. Snyder highlights the specific roles of Jared Kushner and Steve Witkoff, noting that they have been involved in negotiations with Iran, Israel, and Russia. Their positions, he argues, consistently tilt toward the interests of foreign powers like Israel, Saudi Arabia, and the Kremlin, rather than American strategic interests.

The author connects this to the broader historical context of the Project for the New American Century, which once sought to reshape the Middle East through American power, but notes that the current dynamic is different. It is not about American hegemony, but about private alignment with foreign oligarchies. Snyder writes, "We see three individual Americans -- Witkoff, Kushner, and Trump -- who share that cozy passage, an informational and emotional environment with interested and often extravagantly wealthy individuals from other countries." This framing is effective because it personalizes the geopolitical shift. It moves the blame from an abstract "deep state" or a vague "public will" to specific individuals who are acting as conduits for foreign interests.

The Strategic Cost to Ukraine and the U.S.

The commentary then shifts to the tangible outputs of this corridor: the strategic consequences. Snyder argues that the United States is not the beneficiary. Instead, the war serves the interests of Israel, Saudi Arabia, and, paradoxically, Russia. He points out a critical tactical error: the U.S. is expending expensive Patriot missile batteries against cheap Iranian drones. "According to Kyiv, the Americans went through more Patriots in the first three days of the war with Iran than the Ukrainians have used in four years of defending themselves from a full-scale invasion by Russia." This detail is devastating. It suggests that the war is not only strategically unsound but is actively degrading the defense of a key ally.

Snyder connects this to the Russian hydrocarbon economy. By driving up oil prices through the conflict, the war inadvertently benefits Russia, which relies on energy sales to fund its invasion of Ukraine. "The war exposes a Ukrainian vulnerability, and addresses a Russian one," he concludes. This is a powerful synthesis of the immediate military reality and the broader economic fallout. A counterargument might suggest that weakening Iran's drone program is a necessary long-term gain, but Snyder's evidence regarding the rapid depletion of U.S. stockpiles makes that short-term cost look catastrophic. He notes that the American propaganda now echoing Russian talking points—blaming Ukraine for the conflict's escalation—further illustrates how the "corridor" has distorted the national narrative.

It is the world of Trump's Board of Peace, in which peoples are understood as objects of deals, not as citizens of states.

Bottom Line

Snyder's most potent contribution is his ability to link the abstract concept of an "oligarchical corridor" to concrete military failures, such as the wasteful use of Patriot missiles and the strategic benefit to Russia. The argument's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the absence of evidence; while the lack of a public debate is telling, proving the exact contents of private conversations remains speculative. However, the pattern of private emissaries overriding state institutions is a documented phenomenon that makes the "corridor" theory a necessary lens for understanding this conflict. Readers should watch for whether the U.S. military can replenish its missile stockpiles before the next crisis, as that will be the true test of whether the state is still functioning or merely a tool for private deals.

Is this an American war? It surely is, in the sense that Americans will bear some of its consequences. And it surely is, in that American voters are among its causes, having brought Donald Trump to power. But in its causes and in its purposes, one sees very little that connects directly with the people or the institutions of the United States of America.

If we examine the origins, or what we can discern of them, we get an inkling of something rather different: a closed domain of international oligarchs, exploiting the state’s power and patriotic sentiment, while creating a world order in which the American state is much weaker, or simply ceases to function. An oligarchical corridor.

Is the war American in its origins? Can we find the genesis of Trump’s war against Iran in the United States? There is of course a domestic politics to the war. Trump has already telegraphed that he wants to exploit this war to try to fix (”federalize”) the elections of November 2026, and preserve his power with artificial majorities of supine Republicans. But that would work with any war. And, for all we know, by the summer or autumn Trump may well have moved on to another war in Cuba, or yet another one after that.

The question is: why this particular war? Why Iran? There are certainly Americans who have wanted a war with Iran for a very long time. Years, decades, in 2003, longer. But that does not explain the timing. Why now? There does not seem to be an American answer to that.

No doubt the decision was Donald Trump’s, in the narrow sense that no one else enjoyed the practical authority to order the American armed forces into battle. But this begs the question rather than answer it -- and Trump himself lacks answers. Why did he make the decision that he did? He has been unable to explain why he started the war, to a degree that defies any explanation from deliberate ambiguity or even mental decline. He does not even seem very interested in the question, as though it had been answered by others for him. He clearly wanted a war, after the pleasure of Venezuela. But why this one?
