A manipulative masterpiece

Naomi Kanakia · ·Jan 6, 2026 ·17 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Naomi Kanakia delivers a startling defense of a novel widely accused of being a manipulative trap, arguing that its brutal extremity is not a flaw but the very engine of its literary success. She challenges the critical consensus that A Little Life is gratuitous, suggesting instead that the book's power lies in its refusal to offer the moral safety net typical of the sentimental tradition. For the busy reader seeking to understand why a story of unrelenting trauma became a cultural phenomenon, Kanakia offers a framework that moves beyond simple condemnation to examine the mechanics of emotional endurance.

The Architecture of Pain

Kanakia begins by dismantling the assumption that a story about a man subjected to decades of abuse, self-harm, and spinal injury could ever be "good." She notes that the novel starts deceptively, "cozily describ[ing] a group of four college friends and their petty tribulations," before spiraling into a "Gothic saga of mental and physical abuse." This structural bait-and-switch is central to her argument. She posits that the book's genius is its ability to make the reader feel the weight of the trauma without retreating into the comfort of a redemptive arc. As Kanakia writes, "The book forces you to endure the pain in order to get back to the pleasure. And maybe the pleasure would be dull without the pain."

This perspective reframes the criticism of excess. While critics like Christian Lorentzen and Daniel Mendelsohn questioned why an Asian woman writer would spend hundreds of pages torturing gay men, Kanakia suggests the author, Hanya Yanagihara, was making a deliberate choice to strip away the reader's defenses. The narrative does not allow for the detachment one might find in standard literary fiction; instead, it demands an immersive, almost physical reaction. Kanakia observes that the writing is so effective at creating immersion that "you genuinely don't notice the writing," a rarity in a genre often defined by its stylistic flourish.

"It is so gothic and baroque and sentimental, how could it be good? But…those three adjectives are just pejorative terms that posterity has assigned to styles that people used to genuinely enjoy."

Kanakia's defense of the "baroque" and "sentimental" is particularly astute. She argues that modern critics have unfairly stigmatized these styles, forgetting that they once drove massive literary movements. By linking the novel to the Gothic tradition, she draws a parallel to the historical use of extreme imagery to evoke visceral responses, much like the self-harm themes explored in 19th-century Gothic fiction where the body becomes a site of moral and psychological conflict. The argument holds weight because it acknowledges the reader's discomfort while insisting that this discomfort is the point, not an accident of poor editing.

The Sentimental Trap

The commentary shifts to a deeper literary analysis, placing A Little Life within the lineage of the sentimental novel. Kanakia notes that while the book resembles "hurt-comfort" fan fiction, it is more accurately a descendant of 19th-century works like Uncle Tom's Cabin or Little Women. In those traditions, the protagonist's suffering was often a moral test, with the implicit promise that their saintliness would be rewarded or that their death would have a clear redemptive purpose. Kanakia writes, "In a typical sentimental novel, you know that the characters are saved. You know, even if they die in the end, that their life had some meaning."

Here, she identifies the novel's most radical and terrifying departure from tradition. Unlike the orphan in Susan Warner's A Wide, Wide World, whose struggles are minor and ultimately resolved, Jude's suffering is absolute and unredeemed. Kanakia argues that this lack of a "net" is what makes the novel so difficult yet so compelling. "It is fascinating that Yanagihara could've sustained for 700 pages that basic uncertainty about whether this man's suffering has any meaning at all," she writes. This refusal to provide a moral solution forces the reader to confront the raw reality of trauma without the buffer of a happy ending or a clear lesson.

Critics might argue that this lack of resolution renders the book nihilistic rather than profound, suggesting that the sheer volume of suffering becomes numbing rather than illuminating. However, Kanakia counters that the book's emotional impact relies on this very ambiguity. The juxtaposition of Jude's brilliant legal career and his deep friendships against his internal devastation creates a tension that standard realism cannot achieve. As she puts it, "The central relationship is between Jude and his friend Willem... He remains quite attached to Jude and eventually they start a romance and come out as gay partners, at considerable risk to Willem's career." This high-stakes emotional landscape, set against a backdrop of "glamorous, aspirational New York lifestyle," amplifies the tragedy rather than diminishing it.

The Mechanics of Immersion

Kanakia concludes by addressing the technical execution of the novel. She rejects the common critical fallback of praising "the writing" as a standalone virtue, arguing instead that the prose is designed to disappear. "This is not a book where any sentences will stand out in particular, but to me that is good writing," she asserts. The flatness of the narrative distance serves to make the horror more immediate, preventing the reader from finding safety in lyrical description. This approach mirrors the way trauma often feels: not as a dramatic, stylized event, but as a relentless, unvarnished reality.

The author's choice to include graphic details of Jude's abuse, such as the scene where a former monk "pressing him against the wall with his weight," is defended as necessary for the novel's emotional truth. Kanakia suggests that without these "gruesome" details, the character's present-day despair would feel unearned. The book demands that the reader witness the origin of the pain to understand the depth of the loss. This is a bold claim, one that challenges the notion that literature should always offer a path to healing. Instead, Kanakia argues that A Little Life succeeds because it refuses to look away.

"The book undeniably succeeds, and I have to think all this torture has something to do with that success."

Bottom Line

Naomi Kanakia's commentary is a compelling defense of a controversial text, successfully arguing that A Little Life's extremity is a deliberate artistic strategy rather than a failure of restraint. Her strongest point is the reclamation of the sentimental tradition, showing how the novel subverts the expectation of redemption to create a more profound, albeit painful, emotional experience. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the reader's willingness to endure such unrelenting darkness, a threshold that many may find too high to cross. Ultimately, this piece is essential for understanding why a book of such heavy content became a modern classic: it forces a confrontation with the limits of empathy and the nature of suffering that few other contemporary novels attempt.

A manipulative masterpiece

by Naomi Kanakia · · Read full article

A Little Life is one of literature’s greatest traps.

For the first hundred pages, this novel cozily describes a group of four college friends and their petty tribulations as they attempt to become successful members of New York’s creative class. But as the book progresses, it devolves into a Gothic saga of mental and physical abuse, self-harm, rape, and torture.

As we eventually learn, one of these four friends, Jude, is an orphan who as a child was beaten and sexually exploited by several groups of men, the last of whom hit him with a car and gave him severe spinal injuries that leave him in constant pain, which he can only alleviate by cutting himself.

But there’s another side to Jude. He is also a genius. He goes to college at sixteen. He gets a graduate degree in mathematics, and he is a brilliant litigator and a decent singer and baker. Jude is deeply insecure, childishly attached to his friends, and terrified of being rejected by them, even though they all adore him.

Over the course of this seven-hundred page novel, Jude’s story spirals out of control, becoming a bigger and bigger part of the novel. The central relationship is between Jude and his friend Willem. This guy, Willem, becomes a famous actor! He’s in all kinds of movies, has his face on billboards, becomes a bankable star. But he remains quite attached to Jude and eventually they start a romance and come out as gay partners, at considerable risk to Willem’s career.

A surprise hit.

The story I’m describing seems like it’s probably bad, no? If presented with the description above, surely nobody would be willing to slog through this novel.

Two things about this book are astonishing. The first is that when it was released in 2015, the book became a hit! I don’t think it got a lot of publicity from its publisher, Doubleday, but word of its merits must’ve spread pretty quickly, because that year it got shortlisted for the Booker and the National Book Award. However, this book’s success went way beyond award nominations. People talked about it. I only read this book because my coolest writer friend sent me a text saying I had to read it. And then I convinced my friend Courtney to read it, and we still talk about the book to this day.
