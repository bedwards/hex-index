“It’s not something i’m squeamish about”: Heated rivalry author on writing explicit sex scenes

The Walrus · ·Jan 9, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

This piece from The Walrus does something rare in literary journalism: it treats the mechanics of writing explicit romance not as a scandal to be managed, but as a rigorous craft requiring the same narrative discipline as any high-stakes drama. While the surface story tracks author Rachel Reid's sudden ascent from obscurity to bestseller status, the real value lies in her dismantling of the stigma surrounding the genre and her specific philosophy that intimacy must drive plot, not just titillate. For a busy reader, this is a masterclass in how cultural legitimacy is won—not by apologizing for one's work, but by defending its structural integrity.

The Architecture of Intimacy

The Walrus frames the conversation around a central tension: the fear of exposure versus the necessity of authenticity. Reid admits that when she first submitted her debut manuscript, she did so in secret, telling her partner only after the fact. "I told him after I submitted it, over dinner... I was just like, 'Look, I gotta tell you something. It probably isn't gonna go anywhere, but I did this thing,'" she recalls. This confession highlights a pervasive anxiety among genre writers, particularly those tackling sexually explicit material, who often feel their work is too vulnerable for public consumption.

The coverage effectively argues that Reid's journey from hiding her work to becoming a public figure mirrors a broader shift in how society consumes romance. The Walrus notes that the stigma has eroded, especially post-pandemic, as readers stopped treating the genre as a guilty pleasure. This is a crucial observation. It suggests that the market has matured enough to demand quality over shame, forcing the literary establishment to reconsider its snobbery. As Reid puts it, "I guess romance, since the pandemic, has become much less of a guilty pleasure... there's so much community around it now."

"It's not something I'm squeamish about. I do enjoy writing them, probably more than the hockey scenes."

This quote is the pivot point of the entire interview. Reid refuses to treat the explicit content as a hurdle to clear or a concession to the audience. Instead, she positions it as a creative joy. The Walrus uses this to challenge the reader's potential preconceptions: if the author enjoys the craft, why should the audience feel embarrassed? It's a bold stance that reclaims agency for the writer.

Narrative Discipline Over Gratuitous Detail

The most compelling argument in the piece is Reid's assertion that sex scenes must function as character development, not filler. The Walrus draws a parallel to film theory, noting that just as fight scenes in The Matrix were about revealing character, Reid's intimate scenes are about plot progression. "If the sex scenes are there for that reason [to be hot or titillating], they are the ones that I cut from the book," Reid states. "If it doesn't move the plot forward, there's no reason for it."

This is a sophisticated take on craft that elevates the discussion above mere content warnings. It aligns with a historical shift in romance literature, where the "cozy" or "formulaic" label is increasingly being replaced by a demand for emotional and narrative complexity. The Walrus points out that Reid approaches these scenes with the same rigor as her hockey scenes, refusing to write a play-by-play of a game without dialogue or stakes. "I would not write a hockey scene that's just, like, a play by play of a game that's imaginary," she explains. "It has to have some dialogue and storytelling and plot development that moves things forward."

Critics might note that this rigid adherence to plot utility could potentially sanitize the raw, chaotic nature of human intimacy, which doesn't always serve the narrative arc. However, Reid's argument holds up because she is writing fiction, where every element must earn its place on the page. The discipline she describes is what separates a memorable novel from a disposable one.

The Burden of Sudden Visibility

The piece also tackles the psychological toll of rapid success. Reid describes the surreal shift from paying for her own convention tables to having travel expenses covered by publishers. "I was put in the Four Seasons in Toronto for the premiere, and I felt like a hick, like I don't belong here," she admits, adding that she was "dumping maple toffees" into her purse every day. This anecdote humanizes the celebrity experience, stripping away the glamour to reveal the imposter syndrome that often accompanies it.

The Walrus highlights the pressure Reid now faces to produce a follow-up that justifies the hype. "Knowing that it might be very important for my next book to be a really good one is definitely putting new pressure on me," she says. This is a universal struggle for artists, but it is amplified here by the sheer scale of the adaptation's success. The executive branch of the publishing industry, represented by her new publicist and agents, is now managing a brand rather than just a writer. Reid notes the irony of her situation: "It's not like a one-person job anymore, I guess, being me."

The adaptation itself, directed by Jacob Tierney, is praised for its integrity. Reid trusted Tierney to treat the source material with respect, a gamble that paid off. "He wanted to take it very seriously and be quite faithful to it, which I thought was impossible to film," she says. This trust is rare in Hollywood, where adaptations often strip away the nuance of the original text to fit a perceived mainstream mold. The fact that the show succeeded because it didn't compromise is a testament to the power of faithful storytelling.

Bottom Line

The Walrus delivers a nuanced portrait of an author who refuses to apologize for her craft, using the lens of a sudden hit to explore the mechanics of writing and the shifting tides of cultural acceptance. The strongest part of the argument is Reid's insistence that explicit content is a narrative tool, not a marketing gimmick. The biggest vulnerability lies in the future: can the pressure of a massive audience sustain the quiet, intimate creativity that made the work special in the first place? Readers should watch to see if Reid can maintain her creative freedom as the machinery of fame continues to grind.

Rachel Reid is the bestselling author of the Game Changers hockey romance series that includes Heated Rivalry, the TV adaptation of which (by director Jacob Tierney) has become a massive hit since it premiered on Crave in November. Rachel and I talk about how she, as someone who submitted the manuscript of her first novel without even telling her partner or her family, is handling the sudden explosion of attention, about the pressure she feels to make her next book worthy of this attention, and about her rules when it comes to writing explicit sex scenes.

The interview has been edited here for length and clarity.

How have these few weeks been for you? Because I’m assuming this is not your usual pace. This is not your usual level of visibility.

No, no, usually my level of visibility is almost none. It’s been very weird and overwhelming and amazing but kind of still really hard to believe. I can’t believe it’s such a short amount of time, but things have changed so much for me. I mean, just professionally, I’ve definitely kind of levelled up, as an author, to another category, doing things I never thought I would achieve, like the New York Times Best Sellers list. Being invited to a lot of events that usually bigger authors might get invited to. And also getting offered all of my travel expenses paid for—that kind of thing. Before, it’d be more like a “Would you like to come to this convention? It costs $500 for a table” kind of thing. And now it’s “We would like to fly you here and put you up in a hotel and pay you.” And so, it’s just in the last couple of weeks that that’s changed a lot, and obviously, my book sales have gone up a lot, and just generally, everybody is very familiar with my characters and my story, which is a very unusual and amazing feeling.
