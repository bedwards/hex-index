Is Rhythm in Our Genes...Or Our Chest?

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Jun 26, 2020 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis challenges the modern medical obsession with high-tech diagnostics by proposing a radical, almost poetic thesis: that the rhythm we crave in music is not just cultural, but biological, echoing the very beat of the heart we first heard in the womb. He argues that the stethoscope is not merely a tool for data collection, but a bridge to the "art of medicine," a connection that is rapidly eroding in an era of digital efficiency. For the busy professional who has forgotten the tactile nature of human care, this piece offers a startling reminder that the most advanced technology cannot replicate the profound intimacy of listening to a human chest.

The Lost Art of Listening

Francis begins by grounding the reader in the practical reality of medical training, where the ability to listen is the ultimate discriminator. He notes that while modern doctors rely on screens, the core skill remains "listening to heart sounds," a discipline that separates the masters from the novices. "The biggest discriminator, the hardest skill that often makes or breaks a candidate is listening to heart sounds," he writes, emphasizing that this is the cardiologist's scalpel. This framing is effective because it elevates auscultation from a routine check to a high-stakes art form, forcing the reader to reconsider what they value in a doctor. He traces the history from ancient physicians placing their ears directly against patients to René Laennec's invention of the stethoscope, a device born from the need to hear a patient without violating social norms. The narrative arc suggests that while the tools have changed, the fundamental human act of listening remains the heartbeat of the profession.

Critics might argue that relying on the human ear is inherently subjective and prone to error compared to the precision of an echocardiogram. Francis acknowledges this, admitting that ultrasound probes are often more accurate, but he insists that accuracy is not the sole metric of care. "We could give every patient a head-to-head scan and never touch them once, but that wouldn't be providing care; it would be converting us into technicians," he argues. This distinction is crucial; it shifts the debate from diagnostic precision to the human experience of being treated. The argument holds up because it addresses the dehumanizing trend of modern healthcare without rejecting the utility of technology.

The Biological Metronome

The piece then pivots from medical history to evolutionary biology, suggesting that our love for rhythm is hardwired. Francis posits that the first sound a fetus hears is the mother's heartbeat, creating a lifelong association between rhythm and safety. "Our first metronome is our mother's heart," he writes, linking the soothing effect of a heartbeat to the primal urge for music. He illustrates this by describing how the heart's sounds—lub-dub, split sounds, and gallops—mirror the complex time signatures found in global music traditions. The author uses a clever analogy, comparing the rhythm of Shakespeare's iambic pentameter to a heartbeat, suggesting that great art resonates because it mimics our internal biology. "Shakespeare's writing still feels alive today... the ion pairs of notes with the accent on the second syllable as a heartbeat at the very center of it," he observes. This connection between literature, music, and physiology is a bold synthesis that explains why rhythm feels so universal.

Embodied musical cognition postulates that certain processes have musical correlates around the frequency of our heartbeats or how our muscles move when we speak.

However, Francis is careful not to overstate the science, admitting that musical tastes change and that the correlation is not a perfect map. He notes that while some research suggests an imprinting effect, the diversity of global music—from simple four-beat rhythms to complex Indian classical time signatures—complicates the picture. "Getting upset about every tenuous overreaching conclusion in psychology research would make about as much sense as shouting at clouds," he quips, showing a healthy skepticism that prevents the argument from becoming pseudoscience. This balance between wonder and rigor is what makes the piece compelling; it invites the reader to feel the magic without demanding they believe in a conspiracy of biology.

The Erosion of the Consultation

The final act of the commentary brings the focus back to the modern crisis in healthcare. Francis laments that the "erosion of the consultation" has stripped away the physical exam, replacing it with administrative burdens and digital screens. He recounts a poignant story of a retiring professor teaching him a physical sign that is now forgotten, highlighting the loss of intergenerational knowledge. "I'm not saying we ignore advances in our field... but just as a rocket engineer can derive immense enjoyment from hand building a wooden chair using ancient carpentry tools... doctors should treasure and maintain their traditions," he writes. This analogy is powerful, reframing the physical exam not as a primitive relic, but as a craft worth preserving for its own sake. The argument resonates deeply because it speaks to a universal longing for mastery and connection in a world increasingly mediated by algorithms.

Critics might note that the romanticization of the past ignores the life-saving benefits of modern imaging and the necessity of efficiency in overburdened systems. Francis anticipates this, stating that he is not ignorant of how medicine has changed, but he insists that the "art of auscultation" is a vital part of the doctor's identity. The piece concludes by suggesting that the physical exam is the "very essence of medicine," a sentiment that challenges the reader to value the human touch as much as the data.

Bottom Line

Francis's strongest argument is his reclamation of the stethoscope as a symbol of human connection rather than just a diagnostic tool, successfully blending medical history with evolutionary theory. However, the piece's vulnerability lies in its potential to oversimplify the tension between tradition and the undeniable efficiency of modern technology. The reader should watch for how this philosophy translates into practice: can the "art of medicine" survive in a system designed for speed, or is it a luxury we can no longer afford?
