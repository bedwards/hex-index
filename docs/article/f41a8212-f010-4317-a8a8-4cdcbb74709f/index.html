How to use Tor on your phone

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Nov 11, 2020 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In an era where over sixty apps on the average smartphone silently beam location data to hundreds of servers, The Hated One cuts through the noise of "surveillance capitalism" with a stark, actionable truth: anonymity is no longer optional, it is a survival skill. While many guides treat Tor as a niche tool for activists, this piece argues it is the "most essential line of defense" for any phone user who values their privacy, framing the choice not as paranoia but as a necessary digital hygiene step.

The Architecture of Anonymity

The Hated One begins by dismantling the illusion of privacy on modern mobile devices, noting that "your location data is beamed to hundreds of servers and whoever is running those servers or intercepting traffic between them and your phone can track where you are what you do and even who you are." This is a sobering reality check that sets the stage for the solution: the Onion Router. The author correctly identifies that Tor does more than just hide a user's IP address; it fundamentally alters the relationship between the device and the network. "It hides what apps or services you're using from wifi networks your internet service provider and the government," The Hated One writes, emphasizing that the network obfuscates traffic to make targeted attacks based on IP addresses impossible.

However, the author is careful to manage expectations, clarifying that "Tor does not make you unhackable but sort of living off the grid it's the next best thing for your anonymity privacy and security." This distinction is crucial. It prevents the reader from developing a false sense of invincibility while still validating the tool's immense utility. The commentary here is particularly sharp because it acknowledges the trade-off: you gain a layer of protection, but you lose the convenience of seamless, unencrypted connectivity.

The Android Advantage and the iOS Limitation

One of the most distinctive claims in the piece is the unapologetic preference for Android over iOS for privacy-conscious users. The Hated One argues that "the freedom to reliably use without any restrictions is why android is a much more superior choice for online privacy and anonymity than an iphone." The reasoning is technical and specific: iPhones lack an official Tor application maintained by the Tor Project, and no app can route system-wide traffic on iOS. "Unfortunately iphones don't have any official tor application available," the author notes, pointing out that while alternatives like the Onion Browser exist, they are not officially sanctioned.

This is a strong, evidence-based stance that challenges the common perception of Apple as the default for security. Critics might note that iOS's "walled garden" approach often prevents the very malware and tracking scripts that Tor aims to block, potentially offering a different kind of safety. Yet, for those who specifically need to route traffic through the Tor network to hide their identity, The Hated One's argument holds water: the inability to run Tor system-wide on an iPhone is a fatal flaw for high-stakes anonymity.

Navigating the Security Settings

The guide moves from hardware philosophy to practical configuration, offering a nuanced take on the Tor Browser's security levels. The Hated One warns that "every time you visit a website your browser is rendering javascript code that can be attempting to exploit vulnerabilities on your phone." To combat this, the author recommends the "safest mode," which disables all non-encrypted JavaScript and media. "The safest mode is what i would eventually recommend to everyone because it will disable all javascript and media even the legitimate ones," The Hated One asserts.

This advice is bold. It prioritizes anonymity over usability, a choice that many casual users might find frustrating. The author explains that even benign content like ads or videos can be used to "fingerprint track and de-anonymize you." By stripping away these features, the browser becomes a blunt instrument, but a highly effective one. The Hated One also addresses the myth that Tor users stand out, arguing that "targeting tor users is much more demanding on time resources and scale than targeting regular people so don't fool for the myth that using tor makes you stand out." This is a reassuring counter-narrative to the idea that using privacy tools paints a target on one's back.

System-Wide vs. App-Specific Routing

The piece concludes with a strategic breakdown of how to deploy Tor on a device: system-wide or app-specific. The Hated One outlines the risks of mixing Tor with real-world identities, warning that "signing into accounts tied to your real identity can also correlate your tor activity with those accounts and that can de-anonymize you." For users who cannot afford a second device, the author suggests a hybrid approach: "if you only have one phone it's probably a good idea to run tour for specific apps only and route the rest of your device over the clear net."

The author introduces Orbot, an official Android application, as the key to this flexibility. "Orbot is an official tour application for android that can route any app on your phone to go through a tour," The Hated One explains. The guide details how to configure Orbot to either route all traffic or select specific apps, and how to use "always on vpn" settings to prevent leaks. This section is dense with technical instruction but remains accessible, guiding the reader through the "cog icon" settings and proxy configurations with clarity. The final piece of advice is a reminder of the human element: "Tor is not all you need to remain anonymous it is essential but you need to be smart about what you do and disclose on the internet."

Bottom Line

The Hated One delivers a definitive, technically rigorous guide that successfully demystifies Tor for mobile users, particularly those on Android. The strongest part of the argument is the clear-eyed assessment of the trade-offs between anonymity and convenience, as well as the frank admission that iOS is currently ill-suited for serious Tor usage. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its assumption that users will diligently maintain operational security; without that discipline, even the best tools can fail. For the busy professional or the concerned citizen, this is a necessary roadmap to reclaiming digital sovereignty.
