Commentary by Brian Edwards

In a sea of superficial trade show recaps, Benn Jordan offers something radically different: a candid, stream-of-consciousness dissection of the music industry's most pervasive myths, delivered not from a podium but from the chaotic floor of the National Association of Music Merchants (NAMM) show. While most coverage focuses on new synthesizers or celebrity sightings, Jordan uses the event as a backdrop to interrogate the very value of musical labor, arguing that the industry's obsession with "grind culture" and free exposure is actively destroying the careers of emerging artists.

The Metronome of Chaos

Jordan's coverage begins not with a press release, but with a surreal observation of the trade show's absurdity. He describes a vendor displaying two hundred mechanical metronomes, noting, "I was like this is some asmr stuff... I was really wondering if there was like the Steve Reich moment where it would all like... slowly dying off." This anecdote serves as a perfect metaphor for the event itself: a massive, synchronized machine that is slowly losing its rhythm. He captures the whimsical yet disorienting nature of the show, admitting, "I didn't even think that I was thinking hotel room... I want 10 metronomes in my hotel room tonight like I'm willing to throw a thousand dollars into this project."

This willingness to embrace the strange over the commercial sets the tone for his entire piece. He bypasses the standard "what's new" narrative to focus on the human experience of the event, from the lack of vegetables to the strange behavior of a man hitting a box with a contact sensor. He even contemplates a video concept that defies all logic: "I was gonna just like give the guy 20 bucks and be like can I just sit next to you and eat pizza in silence and that's just going to be my video." This rejection of conventional content creation mirrors his broader critique of the music industry's demand for constant, often meaningless, output.

The Five-Word Verdict

The core of Jordan's argument emerges when he interviews fellow creators, distilling complex career advice into five-word maxims. When asked for advice for a beginning musician, he delivers a blunt truth: "Don't work without getting paid." He elaborates on this by dismantling the competitive mindset that drives musicians to devalue their own labor. "I think that your value you set your own value yourself and I think a lot of people see other musicians and they get competitive... where they value each other or they value themselves less thinking that that's how to compete." This is a crucial intervention in a field where "exposure" is often treated as currency.

Jordan connects this devaluation to broader economic shifts, drawing a sharp parallel between the music industry and fast food. He points out that "Spotify dropped the payment per stream by about 60% and if that happened at McDonald's you'd walk out of McDonald's so it's time to just demand to be paid." This comparison is effective because it strips away the romanticism of art and forces a confrontation with basic economic reality. Critics might argue that early-career musicians often need to trade labor for visibility, but Jordan's stance is that accepting free work sets a precedent that is nearly impossible to break later. He insists, "You're worth more than nothing and you know it whether people listen your music or not."

Another creator, Venus Theory, offers a complementary perspective, advising musicians to "Fear nothing, try everything." Jordan expands on this, noting that the modern availability of presets and sample packs has created a culture of fear where artists are terrified of failure. "Sample packs and presets have conditioned you that like if something is wrong it's you and that's not really the case." He argues that success often comes from "failing upwards," a concept that challenges the perfectionist culture fostered by digital tools.

The Myth of the Grind

Perhaps the most significant part of Jordan's commentary is his rejection of the "hustle culture" narrative. When asked about the worst musical advice he ever received, he identifies the belief that "working hard makes you like just that mere act of working hard makes you a better musician and human." He argues that this mindset ignores the necessity of smart practice and the dangers of burnout. "You don't want to burn out you want to practice smart you don't want to be practicing or working hard for others and then exploiting that."

He also takes aim at the "linear path" of success often sold by industry gatekeepers, specifically calling out services like TAXI as a "massive business scheme that rips off musicians." He recalls his own early career decisions, noting that he turned down record labels because he realized, "I'll probably make more money like Napster's around now and people are hearing my stuff just because they want to and I don't need this structure anymore." This reflects a growing sentiment among independent artists who are bypassing traditional structures to build direct relationships with their audiences.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's coverage succeeds because it refuses to play the game of the trade show, instead using the chaos of NAMM to highlight the structural failures of the music industry. His strongest argument is the economic imperative for musicians to stop devaluing their work, a point that lands with force in an era of streaming devaluation. The piece's vulnerability lies in its idealism; while the advice to "don't work without getting paid" is morally sound, it offers little practical guidance for artists who have no leverage and no audience. Nevertheless, Jordan's refusal to offer a standard "what's new" recap makes this a vital listen for anyone trying to navigate the modern music landscape without losing their soul or their savings.
