Dr. Strangelove or: How the Bomb Became Relevant Again

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Jul 20, 2023 · 53 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden argues that Stanley Kubrick's Dr. Strangelove is not merely a relic of Cold War anxiety, but a prophetic mirror for our current age of artificial intelligence and climate dread. By juxtaposing the film's absurd bureaucracy with real-world near-misses like the 1983 Petrov incident, he suggests that the mechanism of our potential destruction has shifted from human error to automated systems we no longer fully control.

The Revival of Nuclear Dread

Van der Linden opens by contextualizing the film within the release of Christopher Nolan's Oppenheimer, noting that while the immediate fear of nuclear annihilation has faded for many, it has been "revitalized a little bit here in Europe with the new conflict between Ukraine and Russia." He posits that for a generation that believed the "end of History" had arrived, the breach of European security has made old Cold War questions "as relevant as ever again." This framing is effective because it refuses to treat the nuclear threat as a closed chapter of history, instead presenting it as a recurring loop that adapts to new geopolitical realities.

The author's most compelling insight lies in the comparison between the film's fictional protocols and actual historical near-catastrophes. He recounts the story of Stanislav Petrov, the Russian officer who, in 1983, correctly identified a false alarm of incoming American missiles. Van der Linden writes, "he thought it was a fluke... and so he didn't go on to report it and set thereby preventing that the whole protocol was at emotion." This anecdote serves as the emotional anchor of the piece, illustrating that the "Fail-Safe" systems we trust are often held together by the fragile intuition of a single human being. The argument lands because it exposes the terrifying gap between our technological confidence and the reality of human fallibility.

The nature of accidents, mistakes like this or flaws in a system that you think is a Fail-Safe, happened in real life too.

Critics might note that relying on the "humility" of a single human operator is a dangerous strategy for modern defense, especially as automation accelerates. Van der Linden acknowledges this tension, questioning "do we let questions this big be decided by Humanity or do we have to set into motion these almost like external... constructions that make decisions for us." This is the central paradox of the nuclear age: we built machines to protect us, only to realize they might destroy us faster than we can intervene.

The Absurdity of the Machine

Moving to the film itself, Van der Linden praises Kubrick's choice to use satire to expose the "natural absurdity to how some of those government structures and Military Protocols are enacted." He highlights the dramatic irony of the film, where the audience is conditioned to root for the pilots to complete their mission, only to realize that their success means "bringing about the end of the world." This structural choice transforms a standard war movie into a "very effective anti-war statement" that feels "so much one that was relevant to me in the here and now."

The author draws a sharp parallel between the film's fictional opening disclaimer—"the Pentagon assured us that this cannot happen"—and the real-world complacency that led to the 1983 false alarm. He notes that the movie was so accurate in its depiction of bureaucratic failure that "there was supposedly some alterations that were made in government protocols based on this movie." This evidence strengthens his claim that art can sometimes diagnose systemic flaws better than the institutions themselves. The satire works because it does not mock the characters for being evil, but for being trapped in a logic that is "too big for us."

As Tom van der Linden puts it, "maybe it's good that we have someone... one of our own like not a computer not a protocol to... some agent that has that kind of humility in the back of his mind." This observation is the piece's strongest philosophical contribution. It suggests that in an era of AI and algorithmic warfare, the very human qualities of doubt and hesitation—often seen as weaknesses in military doctrine—are actually our only safeguard against total annihilation.

We have meddled with some aspect of reality that's too big for us in some way.

A counterargument worth considering is that the film's reliance on human error as a plot device might be outdated in a world of autonomous weapons systems. If the next conflict is fought by algorithms, the "humility" of a Stanislav Petrov may not be enough to stop a digital cascade. Van der Linden hints at this by connecting the nuclear dread to the "hot big topic right now is AI," but he stops short of fully exploring how the satire would translate to a non-human adversary.

Bottom Line

Van der Linden's coverage succeeds in reanimating Dr. Strangelove not as a period piece, but as a urgent warning about the fragility of human agency in the face of automated destruction. His strongest asset is the seamless weaving of historical fact with cinematic analysis, proving that the "dread" of the Cold War is not a ghost of the past, but a living threat. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on human intuition as a solution; as we delegate more power to algorithms, the "humility" of the human operator may soon become a luxury we can no longer afford.
