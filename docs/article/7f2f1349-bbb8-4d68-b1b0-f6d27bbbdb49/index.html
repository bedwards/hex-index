The Radical Act of Giving a Damn

Jeannine Ouellette · Writing in the Dark ·Mar 26, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jeannine Ouellette challenges a pervasive cultural script that equates emotional guardedness with maturity, arguing instead that the "radical act" of visible effort is the only path to genuine creativity. She does not merely offer platitudes about passion; she dissects the structural mechanics of how cynicism functions as a protective amputation that severs the artist from their own work. For the busy professional who has learned to hedge bets and hide their ambition, this piece offers a startling reframing: your fear of looking "too eager" is not sophistication, but the very thing killing your potential.

The Architecture of Indifference

Ouellette begins by tracing the lineage of our fear of caring back to the brutal social hierarchies of adolescence. She anchors this in a specific cultural moment, referencing David Sedaris's essay "Full House," where the author identifies the primal terror of being seen to care. Ouellette writes, "there is nothing worse than for other people to see that you actually care." She observes that this instinct does not vanish with age; rather, it calcifies into a more sophisticated armor. In middle school, the goal is "total invulnerability," but in adulthood, it disguises itself as pragmatism or modesty.

This distinction is crucial. The author argues that we have collectively confused "guardedness with depth and irony with intelligence." She notes that while a child's cool is blunt, "adult cynicism slides in quietly." This subtle shift is dangerous because it allows us to believe we are being wise when we are actually being small. As Ouellette puts it, "We carry a kind of been-there wisdom that tells us to hedge our bets before we've even placed them."

"Cynicism is the death of creativity."

She bolsters this claim by citing the advertising executive John Hegarty, who defined the relationship between cynicism and creation with brutal clarity. The logic follows that to create anything—a novel, a business, a life—one must be willing to be seen wanting it to work. Ouellette connects this to the research of Brené Brown, who documents how we armor up using "cynicism, perfectionism—needing to be the knower and be right versus the learner and get it right." The commentary here is sharp: the armor feels like protection, but it functions as "amputation."

Critics might argue that in a high-stakes professional environment, emotional exposure is a liability, not a virtue. However, Ouellette counters that the cost of the alternative is far greater. She suggests that the "protective layer" we wear is actually a barrier to the very success we seek, noting that "brilliance can rarely emerge from a work suffocated by relentless premature demands of greatness," but neither does it surface in a "swamp of cynical self-protection."

The Cost of the Cheap Seats

The argument deepens as Ouellette shifts from the psychology of the individual to the sociology of the creative arena. She invokes the image of the "cheap seats," a metaphor for the spectators who never enter the fray. She quotes Brené Brown again to describe these observers as those who "will never be brave with their lives but who will spend every ounce of energy they have hurling advice and judgment at those who dare greatly."

This is where the piece connects to the broader theme of impression management. The author suggests that the "philosopher in the cheap seat" knows, as Oscar Wilde noted, "the price of everything and the value of nothing." In contrast, the person in the arena is the only one learning what anything is worth. Ouellette writes, "There is no art in the cheap seats. There is no love there, no discovery, no surprise."

She supports this by looking at historical figures who refused to hide their efforts. She highlights Georgia O'Keeffe, who insisted that "Your life is your art, as well as the thing you call your art." This aligns with the companion deep dive on "Creativity (religion)," where the act of creation is viewed not as a hobby but as a spiritual imperative. Ouellette also draws on Franz Kafka's directive: "Don't bend; don't water it down; don't try to make it logical; don't edit your own soul according to the fashion."

The author's personal admission of being "constitutionally incapable of hidden effort" serves as a powerful counter-narrative to the modern ideal of the cool, detached professional. She admits that her "armor never fit," and that she eventually stopped fighting her temperament. "I am okay with that," she concludes regarding the potential for public failure. "Because the thing that Sedaris named as the worst thing for his pubescent self—being seen to care—turns out, on the other side of adolescence, to be the only way through."

"Cynicism is just its own distinct version of loss, a slower and quieter version than the big and small public losses we all fear."

Bottom Line

Ouellette's most compelling argument is that cynicism is not a shield against failure, but a slower, quieter form of loss that leaves the individual "safe, unwounded, and completely alone." The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to romanticize the struggle, instead framing visible effort as a necessary structural requirement for any meaningful output. The only vulnerability lies in the assumption that all audiences are ready to reward such vulnerability; in some corporate or political cultures, "trying too hard" may still be punished, regardless of the artistic merit. However, for the creator seeking to produce work that matters, the verdict is clear: the risk of exposure is the only price of admission.

I’ve been trying so hard my whole life.

Openly striving. Nakedly reaching after various hopes and dreams—whether to get through high school, get a job, make a family, grow a garden. Most recently, it’s been trying in all kinds of different ways: to make a Substack, create a giant camp for writers in the middle of Wisconsin, get an agent, write a book. And why not try to make that book one that will sell, maybe even be a bestseller? Why not try to make a book that makes a difference?

Obviously, that’s a long shot. But why not? Why not at least try?

Well, lots of reasons, if you listen to contemporary culture.

In his essay, “Full House,” in Dress Your Family in Corduroy and Denim, David Sedaris describes a moment where he is stripped down, literally and figuratively, in the middle of a childhood sleepover, playing strip poker with boys he is terrified to be attracted to—and he names, in more or less the words I’m about to paraphrase, one of the most primal fears of adolescent life: there is nothing worse than for other people to see that you actually care.

I’ve taught middle school. I know the truth of that sentence like I know my own handwriting. I watched eleven- and twelve-year-olds perform elaborate choreographies of indifference day after day—too cool for the assignment, too cool for the field trip, too cool for everything, including, often, their own best friends. The goal was total invulnerability. The goal was to need nothing and reveal nothing and want nothing that anyone else could see. Caring was the thing most likely to get you marked, socially speaking.

What we don’t talk about enough is how little this changes, even after puberty. At least in contemporary American culture, the need to not be observed caring doesn’t dissipate, it just gets subtler, which is worse.

A twelve-year-old’s cool is obvious—it has the blunt force of a hockey puck. But adult cynicism slides in quietly. Even the non-cynical among us tend to have a kind of protective layer against “trying too hard,” a layer that’s disguised as sophistication or pragmatism or even modesty or humbleness.
