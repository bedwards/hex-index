"Seasons clear, and awe" - chapter 10

PILCROW · ·Jan 9, 2026 ·29 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

This week's serialization of Matthew Gasda's novel Seasons Clear, and Awe offers something rare in contemporary fiction: a profound meditation on the paralysis of the intellectual class, framed not as a tragedy of circumstance, but as a deliberate, self-inflicted evasion. PILCROW, the author of this editorial commentary, argues that the novel's true subject is the "post-industrial, late 20th century America" that produced a generation "too intelligent for ordinary happiness, too self-aware for decisive action." The piece does not merely summarize the plot; it dissects the psychological machinery of characters who are "capable of everything except building lives," using the text to explore why high intellect often leads to inaction rather than achievement.

The Architecture of Evasion

PILCROW identifies the core conflict in the Gazda family saga as the inheritance of "unlived ambitions" and a mother's "gift for psychological dissection." The commentary highlights how the protagonist, Stephen, and his sister, Elizabeth, are trapped between the "working-class pragmatism of their fathers and the creative and spiritual aspirations of their mothers." This framing is crucial because it shifts the blame from external economic forces to an internal, almost genetic, inability to commit. PILCROW writes, "Matthew Gasda reveals how post-industrial, late 20th century America created a generation too intelligent for ordinary happiness, too self-aware for decisive action."

The piece draws a sharp distinction between the characters' desire for "existential permanence" and the reality of their stagnation. Through the discovery of letters from a friend named Dan Boettner, the narrative explores the tension between the "vision" of artistic greatness and the "practical stance" adopted to survive. PILCROW notes that the characters have "forced our outrageous egoistic/artistic aims back into our sub-consciousness and adopted, and convinced ourselves of a more practical stance." This is described not as genuine maturity, but as a "literary evasion."

"The only option, as you pointed out, was to become more 'Realistic.' But—here is the main idea—that shift was and is not genuine...merely a literary evasion."

This argument lands with particular force because it challenges the romantic notion that the artist must suffer to create. Instead, PILCROW suggests that the suffering of these characters stems from their refusal to fully embrace the "realistic" life, while simultaneously lacking the courage to fully embrace the "artistic" one. They are suspended in a limbo of self-consciousness. As PILCROW puts it, "The sign of the artist, I suspect, is merely this sort of self-consciousness."

The Shadow of Literary Giants

To deepen the analysis, PILCROW weaves in specific historical and literary contexts that resonate with the novel's themes. The commentary explicitly references the "Shakespeare authorship question," noting that the letters within the novel debate whether "the greatest works in Western Lit could have been written by a country bumpkin." PILCROW argues that the novel uses this debate to suggest that "anything more than the slightest touch of literary self-consciousness would have obliterated" a masterpiece. This connects the characters' paralysis to a broader literary history where "distraction" and a lack of "literary aims" are paradoxically essential to creation.

The commentary also invokes Jorge Luis Borges, a figure central to the companion deep dives mentioned in the background. PILCROW writes, "Borges too said he had but one character—himself," using this to illustrate the characters' tendency to create "worlds reflected in the mirror of the self." This reference adds a layer of meta-fictional depth, suggesting that the characters' inability to act in the real world is compensated by their ability to construct elaborate internal worlds.

Furthermore, the piece touches upon the "Prelude" by William Wordsworth, which appears physically in the novel as a hiding place for letters. PILCROW notes that the text is "bulging unnaturally," symbolizing the weight of the past and the "trove" of unspoken ambitions. This physical detail serves as a metaphor for the characters' internal states: "Writing and receiving letters is, or rather has become, an indispensable part of my intellectual development, on par, I think, with my constant desire for conversation."

Critics might note that this heavy reliance on literary allusion risks alienating readers who are not well-versed in the canon of Western literature. However, PILCROW effectively uses these references not as gatekeeping devices, but as tools to explain the characters' specific brand of neurosis. The argument is that these characters are defined by their relationship to these texts, making the references essential to understanding their psychology.

The Silence of the Artist

The commentary culminates in a discussion of the "silence between Acts 4 and 5" of Hamlet, interpreting it as a "mythic silence" that represents the artist's ultimate triumph. PILCROW writes, "It is indeed in death, in silence where waking and dreaming meet—for the only power over reality the waking mind has is to end." This interpretation reframes the characters' inaction not as a failure, but as a form of "fixity" achieved through the "dream of death."

The piece argues that the characters' obsession with "immortality in existential terms" is a "worthless ideal" compared to the goal of "finding meaning." PILCROW states, "What I really want is to change myself through art—to use writing... as a path and an end." This distinction is vital: the characters are not seeking fame, but a transformation of the self that they are ultimately unable to achieve.

"The silence between Acts 4 and 5 is a mythic silence—the silence of the artist who at once grasps his object. Silence. Not death precisely—but the muting of consciousness, of what Bloom would call 'cognitive music.'"

This section of the commentary is particularly strong because it connects the abstract philosophical debates to the concrete emotional reality of the characters. It suggests that the "triumph of the imagination" is a double-edged sword, offering a "salvation of the mind" while preventing any real engagement with the world.

Bottom Line

PILCROW's commentary on Seasons Clear, and Awe succeeds in framing the novel as a critique of the intellectual class's inability to translate self-awareness into action. The strongest part of the argument is the identification of "literary evasion" as a coping mechanism for the gap between ambition and reality. However, the piece's heavy reliance on literary theory and its focus on the internal lives of the characters may leave readers wondering about the broader social implications of this paralysis. The reader should watch for how the novel resolves this tension: will the characters break free from their "mythic silence," or will they remain trapped in the "mirror of the self"?

“Seasons Clear, and Awe” chronicles three decades in the life of the Gazda family, whose children inherit not wealth but something more dangerous: their parents’ unlived ambitions and their mother’s gift for psychological dissection. As Stephen and Elizabeth grow from precocious children into neurotic artists in their thirties, Matthew Gasda reveals how post-industrial, late 20th century America created a generation too intelligent for ordinary happiness, too self-aware for decisive action: suspended between the working-class pragmatism of their fathers and the creative and spiritual aspirations of their mothers, capable of everything except building lives.

Matthew Gasda is the founder of the Brooklyn Center for Theater Research and the author of many books, including the recent novel The Sleepers and Writer’s Diary.

Stephen had decided that before he left Boston and had to drive five or six hours that he wanted to take a run. Or rather, he ran every day when it was time to run. And it was a gorgeous fall day. He had to get out of the apartment. Elizabeth, who considered it her right, took the opportunity to look through her brother’s things. His text messages weren’t very interesting. No girls, as far as she could tell. She couldn’t find the journal that she knew he kept (he had learned to hide it).

But inside this copy of Wordsworth’s The Prelude, which she noticed was bulging unnaturally, were a trove of letters from their mutual friend from Bethlehem, Dan Boettner:
