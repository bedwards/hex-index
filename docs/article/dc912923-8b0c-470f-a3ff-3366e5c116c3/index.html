You can root for Iran at the world cup without rooting for the Iranian regime

Various · Reason ·Jun 23, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In a sports landscape often polarized by geopolitics, this piece from Reason makes a counterintuitive claim: you can cheer for Iran's national soccer team while vehemently opposing its government. The argument cuts through the usual binary of "enemy nation vs. American values" to explore how diaspora communities and global fans are decoupling athletic identity from state ideology—a nuance that feels especially urgent as the 2026 World Cup unfolds on U.S. soil.

Decoupling the Team from the State

The core of the argument rests on a historical precedent that many casual observers miss. Reason reports, "In the Islamic Republic, sports are too serious to be left to the athletes," citing Kambiz Foroohar's observation that security-military organizations have co-opted most sports bodies over the last two decades. The piece acknowledges the grim reality: dissent is repressed, and women face severe restrictions on attending matches. Yet, it argues that the regime's attempt to use World Cup victories for propaganda often backfires.

The editors draw a sharp parallel to history, noting that major Iranian soccer successes have frequently sparked anti-regime demonstrations rather than nationalistic unity. They point to the 1998 World Cup qualification, where thousands of young women flooded Tehran stadiums despite media orders to stay home, celebrating with "American rock music" and removing headscarves in acts of defiance. This historical context is vital; it reminds us that the tension between the people and their government predates current sanctions or diplomatic freezes.

"A lot of people want an excuse to love their country, even if they don't agree with everything that its government does."

Reason highlights the experience of freelance journalist Natalie Fertig at a recent qualifier in Los Angeles, where fans booed the national anthem and waved pre-revolutionary flags blended with American stars and stripes. The coverage suggests that for Iranian-Americans, supporting the team is an act of cultural preservation, not political endorsement. This framing effectively humanizes a population often reduced to headlines about nuclear programs or regional conflicts.

Critics might argue that any victory inevitably boosts the regime's morale regardless of fan sentiment, potentially aiding their legitimacy abroad. However, the piece counters this by noting that travel restrictions prevent current Iranian residents from traveling in large numbers, meaning the team on the field is often a proxy for the diaspora rather than the state apparatus.

The Rules of Engagement and Sports Governance

Shifting gears to the mechanics of the tournament itself, the article critiques FIFA's new strictures on player conduct, specifically the ban on covering one's mouth during confrontations. Reason argues that while cracking down on racism is noble, the rule assumes nefarious intent in every instance. The piece compares the ban to "banning guns or banning VPNs," suggesting it punishes legitimate tactical communication alongside potential slurs.

The editors note that a straight red card for this infraction seems disproportionate, especially when the rule relies on lip-reading technology that may misinterpret innocent gestures like wiping sweat or face-palming. This is a pragmatic critique of bureaucratic overreach in sports regulation, questioning whether the penalty fits the crime before any actual hate speech has been confirmed.

The U.S. Open and Access to Sport

The commentary also turns its gaze toward American golf, specifically the United States Golf Association's (USGA) choice of venues for the U.S. Open. Reason contends that the tournament should be restricted to public courses accessible to the average golfer, rather than exclusive country clubs. The editors argue, "The point of the U.S. Open's site selection should be 'You could play here too!'" This stance challenges the elitism often associated with the sport's premier event.

While the piece expresses a personal dislike for player Wyndham Clark and celebrates the human-interest story of Miles Russell's father caddying for him, the broader point remains about institutional inclusivity. The argument suggests that if an organization governs golf for everyone from "weekend hackers" to professionals, its flagship event should reflect that broad base.

"That's the World Cup, right? It's people finding ways to separate what governments do from who people are."

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this coverage is its refusal to let geopolitical hostility erase cultural connection; it successfully argues that supporting an athlete does not require endorsing a regime. However, the piece glosses over the potential economic reality that World Cup revenue still flows through state-controlled channels in Iran, which could indirectly bolster the very government fans are protesting. As the tournament progresses, watch to see if this nuanced separation of fan and state holds up under the pressure of high-stakes matches.
