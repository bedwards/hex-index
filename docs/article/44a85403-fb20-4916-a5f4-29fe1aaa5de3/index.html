Space Doctor Analyses Medicine In THE Expanse

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Jul 25, 2018 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis does not merely ask what happens to a human body in space; he asks what happens to a human life when gravity is removed from the very moment of conception. While most analyses of The Expanse focus on the physics of the Epstein drive or the politics of the OPA, Francis brings a degree in space medicine to bear on a terrifying biological reality: we are not built to become space-faring species without fundamentally breaking first.

The Myth of the Tall Belter

Francis immediately dismantles the show's visual shorthand for the Belters, the humans born in the asteroid belt. He notes that while the show depicts them as tall and lanky, the biological reality of growing up without gravity would be far more grotesque. "The creators can be very difficult and the activity of osteoclasts that destroyers outstrips them and bone can be very rapidly broken down," Francis writes, explaining that without the constant impact of gravity, the cells that build bone cannot keep up with the cells that eat it away.

This is not a case of simple stretching; it is a catastrophic failure of skeletal development. Francis points out that while astronauts on the International Space Station grow a few centimeters taller temporarily, their bones are fully formed before they leave Earth. For a child, the lack of gravity means a condition akin to "space rickets," where bones grow misshapen, brittle, and prone to collapse under even the slightest gravitational pull. The author argues that a Belter trying to walk on Earth would likely suffer immediate multi-organ failure, their heart too weak to pump blood against gravity and their skeleton too fragile to support their own weight.

Critics might argue that the show's producers simply lacked the budget to cast actors with Marfanoid features, but Francis suggests the biological inaccuracy goes deeper than casting choices. The show treats the Belter physique as an aesthetic choice, whereas Francis frames it as a medical impossibility without radical, untested intervention.

The Heart That Cannot Pump

The commentary shifts to the cardiovascular system, where Francis delivers some of his most chilling analysis. He explains that the heart, a muscle that works against gravity on Earth, would atrophy in microgravity. "The heart becomes rounder and smaller in space as it no longer has to do any work against gravity," he writes. This leads to a scenario where a Belter's cardiovascular system is simply incapable of generating the pressure needed to sustain life on a planet.

Francis highlights the irony of the show's depiction of Belters being tortured by hanging them in Earth's gravity. In reality, the scene is likely an understatement of the horror. A Belter would not just feel heavy; they would likely go into cardiogenic shock, their blood volume redistributed and their heart unable to circulate it effectively. "They'd probably just stumble around like a drunk just came home," Francis notes regarding the neurological effects, adding that the central nervous system would be equally compromised by the lack of vestibular input.

This section is particularly effective because it moves beyond abstract biology to visceral, immediate consequences. Francis forces the reader to visualize the physical trauma of a Belter stepping onto Earth, transforming a sci-fi trope into a medical emergency.

Reproduction and the Limits of Evolution

Perhaps the most profound argument Francis makes concerns the future of the species. He tackles the question of whether humans can reproduce in space, citing studies where sperm moved too fast to fertilize an egg and rats that miscarried in orbit. "Unless we find a way around these problems we won't be able to reproduce in a weightless environment," Francis writes, casting doubt on the feasibility of a self-sustaining off-world civilization.

The author also addresses the show's handling of radiation and immunity, praising the concept of "premature immuno senescence" as a realistic plot device. He explains that chronic stress and radiation exposure would shorten telomeres and age the immune system rapidly, leading to conditions where the body attacks itself. "Chronic stress and inflammation also negatively impacts the thymus which is a key organ in the immune system," he notes, linking the fictional suffering of the Belters to real-world biological mechanisms.

A counterargument worth considering is Francis's own admission that we do not know if genetic expression changes in space could eventually lead to a new, adapted human subspecies. While he leans on the twin study of the Kelly brothers to suggest dramatic changes are possible, the timeline for such evolution remains speculative. However, this uncertainty is precisely what makes the argument compelling; it acknowledges the limits of current science while highlighting the immense risks.

Unless we find a way around these problems we won't be able to reproduce in a weightless environment.

Bottom Line

Francis's strongest contribution is his refusal to romanticize space colonization, grounding the high-concept sci-fi of The Expanse in the brutal, unyielding laws of human physiology. His biggest vulnerability lies in the speculative nature of long-term evolution, but this does not weaken his core thesis: becoming a multi-planet species is not just an engineering challenge, but a biological one we are currently ill-equipped to solve. Readers should watch for how future scientific breakthroughs in gene editing or artificial gravity might bridge the gap Francis so vividly exposes.
