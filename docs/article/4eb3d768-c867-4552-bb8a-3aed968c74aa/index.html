Why China Can't Keep The Lights On

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Oct 14, 2021 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Asianometry cuts through the noise of "green transition" headlines to reveal a stubborn, expensive truth: China's massive renewable energy boom has not displaced coal; it has merely added to a grid that is still overwhelmingly dependent on fossil fuels. The author's most distinctive claim is that the sheer scale of China's economic growth and the specific physics of its geography have created a "coal trap" that policy alone cannot easily break. For busy observers tracking global supply chains, this is not just an environmental story; it is a critical explanation for why the world's factory floor is increasingly vulnerable to blackouts.

The Coal-Laden Path

The core of the argument is historical. Asianometry explains that China's energy infrastructure was not built for efficiency, but for survival. "During the socialist development era, the country saw electricity as an entitlement, a simple means towards an end," the author notes, highlighting how low prices led to waste while demand remained suppressed by political turmoil. When the economy finally opened up, the government needed power fast. They broke the state monopoly in 1985 to encourage investment, offering guaranteed returns. The result was a rapid expansion of capacity, but with a fatal flaw: "The subsidies did not give thought to the power mix and over 70% of these newly built units relied on coal."

This historical pivot locked China into a carbon-intensive trajectory. The author argues that while the percentage of coal in the energy mix has dropped, the absolute volume has surged. "Coal's share in the Chinese power mix declined from 76% in 2010 to 62% in 2019... but the absolute amount of total generation capacity from coal has actually gone up over that same time period, up by 41%." This distinction is crucial; it dismantles the narrative that China is simply swapping coal for solar. The grid is getting greener in relative terms, but dirtier in absolute terms. Critics might note that this framing underestimates the speed of recent battery storage deployments, but the sheer inertia of existing infrastructure remains a massive hurdle.

The Economics of Inertia

Why does coal persist despite the climate crisis? Asianometry identifies four structural barriers, with the most significant being simple economics and local inertia. The author points out that renewable energy faces a "triple penalty": lower technical efficiency, intermittency, and distance from demand centers. "Renewable energy sources are rarely close to where they are used," Asianometry writes, noting the billions required to build high-voltage direct current transmission lines to move power from the windy north to the industrial south.

In contrast, the economics of coal remain surprisingly robust. "Estimates from 2015 find that internal rates of return... for a 600 megawatt coal fired power plant are far above financial benchmarks reaching nearly 18% in Guandong." That is a return rate that would make any investor envious. Furthermore, the local nature of China's energy providers creates a political lock-in. "Energy providers in China are very local... and right now the incentives are still not compelling enough to change how they've been doing things for 30 plus years now." The author effectively argues that this is not a failure of will, but a failure of market design where the true costs of renewables are obscured by the need for massive new infrastructure.

The average Chinese coal fired plant is just 12 years old. By 2040, many of them will still not have fully depreciated.

This point about "stranded assets" is the piece's most sobering insight. The government cannot simply shut down these plants without incurring massive financial losses and political backlash. The transition is not just about building new tech; it is about managing the death of a young, profitable industry.

Geography, Imports, and the 2021 Crisis

The commentary then shifts to the physical reality of China's coal supply. The resource is concentrated in the unpopulated north and west, while the factories are in the east. "They ship it on a train to a port, usually Chinuang Dao. Then, it gets put onto a boat for its final destination." This logistical chain is expensive, accounting for up to 60% of the delivered price. This cost structure opened the door for imports, particularly from Australia, which offered higher quality coal at a lower price.

However, geopolitical tensions and domestic safety reforms have complicated this reliance. The author details how stricter safety regulations, while necessary to reduce the historically high death rate of miners, have increased domestic production costs. "The undeniable impact from this... is that domestic coal now costs more." This squeeze led to a precarious situation in 2021. When demand spiked and imports were restricted due to trade disputes, utilities found themselves unable to pass on rising costs. "As coal prices rose, utilities... fell into the red. Thus, they found themselves less willing to scale up their utilization hours, reducing the overall energy supply."

The human cost of this rigidity is becoming harder to ignore. The author highlights the exponential rise in cooling demand. "In 1990, the country used 7 terowatt hours for cooling. By 2016, that is now 450 terowatt hours." This is not just about comfort; it is a public health imperative. "Air conditioning is a critical measure in keeping the vulnerable protected, mostly seniors, but others too." As urbanization and heat waves intensify, the margin for error in the power grid shrinks to zero.

Bottom Line

Asianometry delivers a compelling, data-rich verdict: China's energy transition is a marathon, not a sprint, and the finish line keeps moving because the economy keeps growing. The strongest part of the argument is the rigorous debunking of the idea that renewable capacity growth equals coal displacement; the absolute numbers tell a different, more troubling story. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the assumption that market forces will eventually force the shift, given the heavy hand of state planning that has historically prioritized stability over efficiency. The reader should watch for how Beijing resolves the tension between protecting the value of young coal assets and the urgent, non-negotiable demand for reliable power in an increasingly hot world.
