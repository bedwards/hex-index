Last week, as part of a NATO innovation event in Turkey, two teams of officers squared up around the table to war game out a fictional invasion scenario. The defending blue team at the helm of a small fictional nation would have to see off an invasion attempt by their much larger red team neighbors and their significant stockpile of mostly Russian or old Soviet equipment. Given this was the cross-domain command or CDC war game, the teams brought together experts not just in the traditional domains, air, land, and sea, but also experts in cyber warfare, the space domain, a team of experienced umpires, directors, and observers, and me. Because thanks to an invitation from NATO's allied command transformation and a bit of negotiation, while I wasn't allowed to slap any fire prisms or battle mechs down on the board, I did get to spend most of last week with teams drawn from NATO countries as they slugged it out on the wargaming table.

And so today we're going to talk professional wargaming and NATO's cross domain command concept war game in particular. Asking questions like why militaries hold these things, what this war game specifically was meant to explore, how it played out, and a few observations both about potential lessons from the exercise and ways it might be potentially built or iterated on for future events. Okay, so before we get into the war game itself, a few quick notes about this event, what it was, and how on earth someone from the Asia Pacific ended up with an event for the North Atlantic Treaty Organization. While in a sense, you could call this a single innovation focused event under the opaces of allied command transformation within the NATO structure.

Realistically, it felt more like two events in one to the extent that I was wearing two different security passes for the different components. The first part was really technology and innovation focused with a lot of cool tech being shown off with a significant presence by NATO organizations like JKE for example which has a major focus on lessons learned out of Ukraine and integrates both Ukrainian and NATO personnel.
