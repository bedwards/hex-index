The AI Cultural Slop Apocalypse is Here

Then & Now · Then & Now ·May 29, 2025 · 22 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now confronts a terrifying reality that many prefer to ignore: the internet is not just being flooded with bad content, but with a new kind of "cultural slop" that is indistinguishable from reality and weaponized by algorithms. The author's most disturbing claim is not that AI will replace human creativity, but that it will render the very concept of "human effort" economically worthless by saturating our attention economy with infinite, high-quality, yet hollow simulations.

The End of the Signal-to-Noise Ratio

The piece opens with a visceral demonstration of the problem, citing Google's new Flow AI update, VO3, which generates photorealistic clips that "look like nothing CGI, let alone AI has produced before." Then & Now illustrates the immediate consequence: a digital environment where truth and fiction have collapsed into a single, unrecognizable mass. The author describes searching for commentary on this technology only to find their feed "flooded with AI data," making it impossible to distinguish between a real person discussing the tech and an AI avatar mimicking that discussion.

This observation strikes a nerve because it moves the debate from theoretical future risks to a present-day crisis of epistemology. The author notes, "I am already going slightly mad in that I often cannot tell the difference in my search feed between an AI generated video and a real video." This isn't just a technical glitch; it is the erosion of the shared reality necessary for public discourse.

The argument gains weight when the author points to the sheer volume of this content. Citing a study that found "something like 70% of long posts were AI generated on LinkedIn," Then & Now suggests that the internet is no longer a place of human connection but a "slop machine" designed to cater to "lower impulses and clicks." The author argues that algorithms, indifferent to quality, are pushing this content to the top, drowning out genuine journalism and human storytelling.

"The fiction here is indistinguishable from reality."

Critics might argue that human curiosity will eventually filter out the noise, or that platforms will develop better detection tools. However, the author's experience suggests that the speed of generation outpaces the speed of detection, creating a "flood" that is unignorable.

The Economics of Abundance

The commentary shifts to the economic implications, challenging the romantic notion that human skill is inherently valuable. Then & Now reflects on their own craft, mourning a skill that was rare until recently: the ability to sit for hours, read, take notes, and synthesize complex ideas. The author posits that "value in the world is determined... by how rare something is," and that "abundance is the opposite of value."

This is a stark, almost nihilistic economic argument. If AI can produce a "reasonably high quality hourlong video on anything they want" at a fraction of the cost, the market value of human-created content plummets. The author illustrates this with a hypothetical scenario where a corporation like Coca-Cola no longer needs a large marketing team but instead uses AI to "produce 1,000 movies" that subtly demonstrate product appeal.

The author warns that this leads to a massive inequality in the attention economy. "He who controls the memes of production" will dominate the landscape, just as the steam press centralized media in the 19th century. Then & Now observes that "huge corporations... will be able to out compete everyone else," leaving smaller creators and artisans unable to get heard.

"I've recently had the strange experience of mourning a skill that was rare until 2 minutes ago."

This section is particularly powerful because it personalizes the macro-economic shift. The author admits to feeling "blind" to this change just a few years ago, believing their hard-won skills would sustain them. Now, they fear that "if everyone can do something... then what you have is no longer valuable."

A counterargument worth considering is that human connection remains a premium good; people may crave "real" imperfections and authentic voices even if they can't always distinguish them from AI. Yet, the author counters this by noting that if the AI is good enough to mimic "vocal hiccups and mistakes," the distinction becomes moot to the average consumer.

The Future of Truth and Trust

The piece concludes by addressing the dual concerns of "hallucinations" and "theft." While the author acknowledges that AI still mixes truth and illusion—citing examples of non-existent books generated by chatbots—they predict this will be "ironed out" quickly. The more pressing issue, they argue, is the ethical dimension of "stolen artists, stolen writers, stolen musicians."

Then & Now describes a future where the internet is "inundated" with content so vast and personalized that users will "always find one that appeals to your own biases." This creates a feedback loop where corporations can "flood the zone with the sloppiest sloppiest [ __ ] you can imagine," ensuring that no single truth can emerge.

The author's fear is not just for their own job, but for the "social future" and the "public sphere." They warn that we are moving toward a world where "thousands of workers" are replaced because their jobs can be done by AI, and where the "flood metaphor" becomes a reality of our daily lives.

"The question is not what AI can do but who it can do it for."

This final insight reframes the entire debate. It is not about the technology's capability, but about the concentration of power. The author suggests that the "memes of production" are being centralized in the hands of a few tech giants and media conglomerates, leaving the rest of us to navigate a sea of synthetic content.

Bottom Line

Then & Now delivers a sobering verdict: the "AI cultural slop apocalypse" is not a distant sci-fi scenario but a current event that is already devaluing human effort and fracturing our shared reality. The argument's greatest strength is its refusal to rely on abstract fears, instead grounding the threat in the immediate, tangible experience of a flooded information ecosystem. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that human discernment will remain passive; the piece underestimates the potential for a cultural backlash that could re-elevate "human-made" as a premium label. Readers should watch for how quickly the cost of high-fidelity AI drops, as that will be the true tipping point for this new era.
