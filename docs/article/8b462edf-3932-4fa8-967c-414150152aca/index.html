Meanwhile, for our fifth of six installments of the Infinite Small intensive on micro prose, I want to talk about imagery as the heart of this form.

The writer Sean Glatch in his craft essay1 actually identifies four specific elements needed to make flash work:

  1. Concise Word Choice

  2. Imagery

  3. Theme

  4. Experimentation and Hybridity

What good flash is not driven by is explanation.

And, to my mind, image must provide the foundation (usually) needed for two of the other three components Glatch names—theme and experimentation/hybridity—to work their hardest. As for concise word choice, that’s inherent in this form! Doesn’t mean it’s easy or that it is just a matter of adhering to a lower word count, though. Concise word choice means weighing and measuring and considering each and every word and being willing and able to cut it or swap it as necessary to amplify the power of the micro prose.

All this said, I can readily acknowledge that for me, the best flash lives and breathes through image. In such a small space, the writer leans on sensory detail the way a body leans toward warmth: instinctively, urgently. An image can hold what there isn’t room to say outright—memory, metaphor, ache, astonishment.

And imagery isn’t only what we see. It includes the salt sting of language on the tongue, the shift of gravity when a feeling moves through the chest, the ghost of a smell that calls back a whole life. Motion counts. Temperature counts. The pulse beneath the surface counts.

The best flash leaves something lingering—an aftertaste, a shiver, a flicker in the mind’s dark. Long after the last sentence, the reader isn’t just remembering the piece. They’re carrying it.
