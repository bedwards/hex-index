Lecture #11: Taking Notes Effectively - which words should you write down?

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·Oct 15, 2021 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan doesn't just offer study tips; he dismantles the very premise of how most students approach learning. His most provocative claim isn't about what to write, but that writing down what you hear is actually counterproductive to memory. By marshaling a decade of cognitive science, he argues that the efficiency of modern technology is the primary enemy of deep understanding.

The Efficiency Trap

Kaplan begins by reframing note-taking not as a transcription service, but as a tool for cognitive efficiency. He asserts that the goal is to "squeeze out of [the same] hours... the most retention, the most understanding." This is a compelling pivot. Most advice focuses on working harder or longer; Kaplan insists on working smarter within the existing constraints of a lecture hall. He argues that the common habit of trying to capture every word results in a "transcript of the words" that the student never actually processes.

"If you just write down the words without understanding them, well then you'll have a transcript of the words... but you won't have understood any of it, and you certainly will not retain it."

This distinction is crucial. Kaplan posits that human memory retains meaning, not symbols. When students attempt to write down disconnected phrases or whole sentences, they are essentially acting as stenographers, a role that prevents them from engaging with the material. The author's logic here is sound: if your brain is busy decoding the mechanics of writing, it has no bandwidth left to decode the concepts being spoken. A counterargument worth considering is that some learners, particularly those with specific processing differences, may genuinely benefit from verbatim records to review later. However, Kaplan's evidence suggests that for the vast majority, the act of transcription creates a false sense of security.

The Skeleton and the Flesh

The most distinctive part of Kaplan's methodology is his "flesh" metaphor. He describes lecture notes as merely the "bones" of a creature, incomplete until they are fleshed out within 24 hours. He urges students to spend just ten minutes after class to "add in the other parts of understanding that you didn't have time to get in during class." This is a strategic move to combat the rapid decay of short-term memory.

"Because you're doing this within 24 hours, there'll be all these memories that will be that will be pulled up in your mind... Those memories will be gone after 24 hours. So, you need to get them while they're still there."

Kaplan admits with a touch of sadness that "many students won't do this part" because it requires extra time. Yet, he frames this ten-minute investment as the difference between a useless jumble of notes and a powerful resource for exam preparation. This advice is particularly potent for busy professionals or students who feel they have no time to spare. By compressing the review process into a single, focused window, he makes high-impact learning seem achievable without extending the workday. The strength of this argument lies in its timing; it leverages the brain's natural consolidation processes rather than fighting against them.

The Digital Distraction

Perhaps the most aggressive part of Kaplan's commentary is his stance on technology. He cites multiple studies to argue that typing notes is inferior to writing by hand, not because of distraction alone, but because of "shallower processing." He references a 2014 study showing that laptop users tend to "transcribe lectures verbatim rather than processing information and reframing it in their own words."

"Even when laptops are used solely to take notes, they may still be impairing learning because their use results in shallower processing."

Kaplan dismantles the common defense that one can use a laptop for "class-related" research. He cites a 2016 paper stating that "class related internet use was not associated with a benefit to classroom performance." He also highlights the social cost of laptops, noting that even those who don't use them suffer when sitting near multitaskers. The evidence he presents is robust, drawing from journals like Psychological Science and Computers and Education. Critics might argue that this advice ignores the reality of modern classrooms where digital tools are ubiquitous, or that the "handwriting" advantage might diminish as digital note-taking apps become more sophisticated. Yet, Kaplan's core point remains: the speed of typing allows the brain to disengage, while the slowness of handwriting forces the mind to synthesize.

"The problem with typing out your notes is that you can type too fast... your processing will be shallower. You won't think through the information."

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's argument is a powerful reminder that the medium of note-taking fundamentally shapes the depth of learning. His strongest point is the insistence that understanding must precede recording; if you don't get it, you ask, you don't write. The biggest vulnerability in his approach is the assumption that all students have the discipline to spend ten minutes reviewing notes within 24 hours, a habit that many find difficult to sustain. However, for any listener willing to trade the illusion of productivity for actual retention, his prescription is clear: put down the device, pick up the pen, and think before you write.
