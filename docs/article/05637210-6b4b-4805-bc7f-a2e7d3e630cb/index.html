The manifold mind of saul bellow

The Literarian Gazette · ·Dec 26, 2025 ·19 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In an era where literary criticism often defaults to political litmus tests, The Literarian Gazette makes a daring, necessary claim: Saul Bellow was never a reactionary, but a complex mystic whose work exposes the fragility of civilization itself. This piece refuses to let the author's later political associations or personal flaws obscure the radical humanity of his fiction, arguing instead that his true genius lies in his refusal to offer easy answers to the human condition.

The Ghost of the Left

The article opens by dismantling the comfortable caricature of Bellow as a neoconservative icon. The Literarian Gazette writes, "Over the decades he's come to be categorized by critics as a hundred different kinds of 'too much': too much of an intellectual, too European, too comic, too academic... too Jewish, un-American." This catalog of contradictions is not a weakness but the very texture of his work. The author reminds us that before he was the establishment darling, Bellow was a Marxist and a Trotskyist in the 1930s and 40s, a dangerous position that made him a target of both mainstream and Stalinist violence. The piece notes a chilling historical detail: when Leon Trotsky was assassinated in Mexico, Bellow was there and saw his corpse just hours after he died. This biographical anchor grounds the argument that Bellow's early work, including The Adventures of Augie March, was forged in the fires of anti-capitalist critique, not right-wing nostalgia.

The core of the argument is that we have forgotten this history because Bellow's novels are too dense to be reduced to a political slogan. As The Literarian Gazette puts it, "His novels do not admit of a political program. They do not promote candidates or political worldviews. Instead they attempt to examine, in the manner of the writers of the Russian Golden Age, certain social realities as they stand." This reframing is essential. It suggests that the modern tendency to cancel or dismiss authors based on their perceived politics is a failure of literary imagination. The author argues that Bellow's work is not a manifesto but a mirror, holding up both the "pearlescent beauty of the skies and the muck of the mud puddles below."

Critics might note that acknowledging Bellow's early radicalism doesn't fully absolve the later accusations of chauvinism or the violence he inflicted on his wife, which the article mentions but does not fully grapple with. However, the piece effectively argues that separating the art from the artist's later persona allows us to see the full scope of his moral inquiry.

The Moral Net

The commentary then shifts to Bellow's most controversial work, Mr. Sammler's Planet, defending it against charges of bigotry by revealing its true target: the universal capacity for brutality. The Literarian Gazette writes, "For the observant reader, however, it's clear that Bellow catches everyone — black, white, Jewish, young, old, family members, professionals, ordinary men and women — in his net of moral philosophy, revealing their potential for baseness, criminality, selfishness, and violence." This is a crucial distinction. The article points out that the protagonist, Artur Sammler, is not a mouthpiece for the author but a flawed survivor who himself committed murder to ensure his own survival during the Holocaust.

The piece argues that the book is a treatise on the "grave undercurrent of man, the amoral rivers that flow contiguous to our inner Great Lakes of generosity." By showing that a Holocaust survivor and a pickpocket are both capable of extreme violence, Bellow dismantles the idea of moral superiority based on identity or intellect. The author suggests that this perspective is terrifyingly relevant today, noting that "many of us, with unexpressed reference to the pre-World War Two rise of fascism, have indeed lately found ourselves wondering, like old Sammler, whether Western society is collapsing once again upon its haunches."

This analysis holds up because it moves beyond the surface-level offense of the novel's most infamous scene to the deeper philosophical question it raises: can liberal civilization defend itself against its own internal decay? The Literarian Gazette writes, "This eventuality is not hidden from us, it is within view, something we know very well to be possible." The article concludes this section with Bellow's own haunting conclusion: "For that is the truth of it... that we all know, God, that we know, that we know, we know, we know."

The Mystic Poet

Ultimately, the piece posits that Bellow is best understood not as a politician or a moralist, but as a mystic. The Literarian Gazette writes, "My own pet theory has always been that Bellow is less a moralist or political writer than a mystic poet, explorer of the human soul, one who combined intellectual play with transcendental yearning." To prove this, the author highlights a bizarre, lyrical passage from Augie March involving "Dagon" and "Neapolitans," asking, "Is it left-wing or right-wing? An absurd question. There is nothing to say here, nothing to do but read it again."

This is the piece's most successful move. It liberates the reader from the exhausting task of categorizing Bellow's politics and invites them instead to experience the sheer linguistic and spiritual power of his prose. The argument suggests that the "manifold mind" of the title is not a contradiction but a synthesis of the material and the spiritual. As The Literarian Gazette puts it, "His mind was turning toward the fundamentals of the human spirit, attempting to use the extant American scene to render ideas eternal."

Bottom Line

The Literarian Gazette offers a compelling, necessary rehabilitation of Saul Bellow, successfully arguing that his work is a vital diagnostic tool for a society on the brink of self-cannibalization. The strongest part of this argument is its refusal to let political labels obscure the universal moral questions Bellow raises about violence, survival, and the fragility of civilization. Its biggest vulnerability is a slight underestimation of the genuine harm caused by the author's personal actions, which the article acknowledges but does not fully integrate into the literary analysis. For the busy reader, this piece is a reminder that great literature does not offer comfort, but rather a terrifyingly clear mirror in which we must recognize our own capacity for both greatness and ruin.

Twenty years ago, the year of Saul Bellow’s death, I chanced upon an unusually clean copy of Ravelstein in a ramshackle bookstall in New Delhi. India is an unexpected place to wind up reading, for the first time, this midcentury urban intellectual, comic dissector of America’s moronic inferno. But, as it happens, amidst the street chaos and the tiresome pressures of train travel and seedy hostels, I found that my irked, overstimulated soul was peculiarly receptive to the master’s comforting genius. I consumed the book in a couple of hours at a restaurant counter. I remember thinking that I would not soon forget the experience. But, hardly in my 20s at the time, I’ve wondered since what I detected in it. What did the man have to offer me, so green, so goyish? This was Bellow’s very last novel, written in his 80s. Riffing on topics of Jewishness, sickness, aging, dying, marriage, politics, history, friendship; he was a very adult sort of thinker, a mature man’s writer. Yet I was so thrilled by this that upon my return home I read all of his work, and later his essays and letters, then the multitudinous biographies, memoirs, and studies that came out in the decades following his death. Today I am that rare creature: the millennial Bellovian.

The 2000s was an era with a certain showy yet wan style in literature. David Mitchell had bumbled onto the world stage with his egregious Cloud Atlas. The Big Writers with Big Initials — George R.R. Martin, J.K. Rowling — were pumping out their wearisome commercial blockbusters. David Sedaris was eking out a drearily comic thing seemingly every year. James Wood had coined the phrase “hysterical realism,” which correctly diagnosed a certain quality of prolific and anxious postmodern melodrama in then-popular novels by Zadie Smith, Jonathan Safran Foer, Junot Diaz and David Foster Wallace. The barely beating heart of American poetry, gluttonized upon the empty calories of flarf, was being bashed to death by carnival-barking anti-poets Dorothea Lasky and Kenneth Goldsmith. In nonfiction, the period was profusely Gladwellian, chockablock with lightly researched volumes that claimed to easily explain world phenomena. Every other person seemed to be carrying around a copy of Freakonomics.
