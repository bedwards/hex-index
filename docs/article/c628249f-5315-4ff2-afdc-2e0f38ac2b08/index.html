Live IN Hong Kong 9: Now the Eggs Are Flying

Chris Chappell · China Uncensored ·Jun 30, 2019 · 21 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Chris Chappell doesn't just report from the front lines of Hong Kong's July 1st protests; he documents the chaotic, rain-soaked reality of a city where the official narrative of unity is actively being dismantled by the very people it claims to represent. In a raw, unpolished livestream that reads more like a war correspondent's field notes than a polished news segment, Chappell captures the absurdity and tension of a day where the government's patriotic flag-raising ceremony is overshadowed by thousands of riot police and a sky full of thrown eggs. This isn't a retrospective analysis; it is a visceral, real-time account of the friction between a state desperate for a clean image and a populace determined to be heard.

The Chaos Behind the Ceremony

Chappell's coverage begins not with a thesis, but with the immediate, disorienting struggle to maintain a signal in a city that seems intent on jamming it. He notes the technical failures as a metaphor for the broader communication breakdown: "The phone we were using just would not connect to the internet... we've switched phones." This opening sets a tone of improvisation that defines the entire piece. He is not in a studio; he is navigating a shifting landscape where the police lines are moving, the weather is turning, and the equipment is failing.

As the team moves behind police lines, Chappell highlights the sheer scale of the security apparatus deployed to protect a single event. He observes, "There are way more police over here on this road as well," noting the presence of officers arresting protesters and the debris of abandoned umbrellas littering the streets. The visual evidence he provides—broken umbrellas, discarded eggs, and the ominous presence of riot gear—serves as a counter-narrative to the government's attempt to stage a peaceful handover anniversary. He points out the irony of the situation: "It's supposed to be the sort of patriotic thing of like how happy Hong Kong is to be reunited with the motherland except because people are all terrified of Beijing and have been protesting all week."

"It's supposed to be the sort of patriotic thing of like how happy Hong Kong is to be reunited with the motherland except because people are all terrified of Beijing and have been protesting all week."

This observation cuts to the heart of the conflict. Chappell argues that the government's reliance on massive police presence and helicopter arrivals for the Chief Executive reveals a deep insecurity about public sentiment. The ceremony was not a celebration of unity but a fortress built to prevent disruption. Critics might argue that Chappell's focus on the chaos overlooks the legitimate security concerns of the government, but the sheer volume of riot police and the need for a helicopter extraction suggest that the state anticipated, and perhaps provoked, the unrest it sought to contain.

The Symbolism of the Storm and the Egg

The narrative takes a surreal turn as the weather deteriorates, with Chappell noting, "It's raining wontons... I guess this might be the start of Noah's Flood." The storm becomes a character in the story, washing over the scene and adding a layer of biblical drama to the political standoff. Yet, amidst the deluge, the most striking image is not the rain, but the projectiles. Chappell describes a moment where the tension breaks into absurdity: "They just threw an egg somebody threw an egg at police and that please Ben used his shield to expertly block it."

He later discovers the aftermath of this skirmish: "I see a bunch of like a dozen broken eggs further down... actually looks more like 36 eggs than a dozen." This detail is crucial. It transforms the protest from a monolithic political movement into a series of individual, spontaneous acts of defiance. The eggs are not just trash; they are a symbol of the fragility of the police line and the determination of the protesters to disrupt the official narrative, even if only with breakfast food. Chappell's commentary on this is sharp: "Who's gonna pick up all the trash?" It's a rhetorical question that underscores the futility of the state's attempt to sweep the dissent under the rug.

The demands of the protesters, as Chappell explains, are not abstract ideals but specific, actionable grievances. He clarifies, "They've been asking for the extradition bill that would let the Hong Kong government send people to mainland China for trial... and not just paused." He also notes the call for an independent inquiry into police violence, specifically referencing the "150 tear gas canisters and pepper spray" used on June 12. By grounding the protest in these concrete demands, Chappell moves the story beyond the spectacle of the eggs and umbrellas to the substantive political crisis at its core.

"They've been asking for the extradition bill that would let the Hong Kong government send people to mainland China for trial... and not just paused."

This framing is effective because it strips away the government's propaganda about "radical elements" and returns the focus to the rule of law and civil liberties. The protesters are not asking for the overthrow of the government; they are asking for the protection of their legal rights and an investigation into excessive force. A counterargument worth considering is that the government views the extradition bill as a necessary tool for judicial cooperation, but Chappell's coverage highlights the lack of trust in the mainland legal system that makes this a non-starter for many Hong Kong residents.

The Role of the Press in a Lull

As the day progresses and the rain eases, Chappell reflects on the unique position of the press in this environment. He suggests that their presence serves a stabilizing function: "The whole point of doing this is because not just to let you guys know what's happening but by having a press presence here it also sorted we found it de-escalates any tensions between police and protesters because you know the world is watching." This is a bold claim, one that suggests that transparency itself is a form of conflict resolution. While the police and protesters remain in a tense standoff, the camera acts as a neutral third party, forcing both sides to moderate their behavior.

However, the coverage also reveals the limitations of this role. Despite the presence of the media, the police continue to arrest protesters, and the tension remains palpable. Chappell notes the presence of ambulances and injured protesters, reminding the audience that the "lull" is fragile. The fact that the police are using rubber bullets and pepper spray even in the rain, as Chappell confirms, suggests that the de-escalation is superficial. The underlying conflict remains unresolved, and the press is merely a witness to the ongoing struggle.

Bottom Line

Chris Chappell's coverage succeeds because it refuses to sanitize the reality of the Hong Kong protests, presenting a raw, unfiltered view of a city in turmoil where the government's attempt to project unity is met with the chaotic reality of thousands of dissenters. The strongest part of his argument is the juxtaposition of the government's staged patriotism with the visceral, messy reality of the streets, where eggs and umbrellas become symbols of resistance. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the inherent instability of a livestream format; while it captures the immediacy of the moment, it can struggle to provide the deep historical context needed to fully understand the long-term implications of the extradition bill and the erosion of autonomy. Readers should watch for how the government responds to the continued pressure, as the current "lull" is unlikely to last once the cameras stop rolling.
