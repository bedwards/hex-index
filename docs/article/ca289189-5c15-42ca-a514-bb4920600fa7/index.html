The man who faked human cloning

BobbyBroccoli · BobbyBroccoli ·Apr 21, 2023 · 56 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

BobbyBroccoli uncovers a startling paradox: the most significant moment in the history of human cloning wasn't a scientific breakthrough, but a political decision made in a Texas ranch that inadvertently pushed the world's most ambitious biotech fraud into the shadows. By tracing the arc from President George W. Bush's 2001 stem cell address to the economic desperation of post-1997 South Korea, the author reveals how national pride and regulatory gaps created the perfect storm for a notorious deception. This is not just a history of science; it is a case study in how the fear of "organ farms" and "spare body parts" can drive innovation underground, where oversight cannot follow.

The Political Catalyst

The piece opens with a vivid reconstruction of August 8, 2001, where President Bush addresses the nation not about terrorism or the economy, but about the moral weight of stem cell research. BobbyBroccoli writes, "My Administration must decide whether to allow federal funds, your tax dollars, to be used for scientific research on stem cells derived from human embryos." This framing is crucial because it immediately personalizes the policy, turning a complex biological debate into a question of taxpayer morality. The author argues that Bush's decision to ban federal funding for new embryonic stem cells didn't just slow progress; it "almost immediately kill[ed] all US momentum in the field."

The commentary effectively highlights the unintended consequences of this ban. While Bush evoked "worst case scenarios" of cloning humans for spare parts, the author notes that critics argued the opposite: "The best way to control the research is to fund it by the federal government because then you create rules." This is a powerful counterpoint that suggests regulation, not prohibition, is the only way to maintain ethical oversight. By opting for a ban, the administration ensured that the research would not stop, but rather migrate to jurisdictions with fewer constraints.

"By opting for an outright ban instead of regulating it, Bush was ensuring one thing: people are still going to do it. They're just going to go to different countries."

This insight reframes the entire narrative of the cloning era. It suggests that the subsequent scandals were not merely the result of rogue scientists, but a direct reaction to a policy vacuum in the world's leading superpower. Critics might note that the Bush administration's stance was also driven by a genuine, widespread public unease about the ethics of destroying embryos, a sentiment that went beyond simple political maneuvering. However, the author's point stands: the ban created a gravitational pull for research toward less regulated environments.

The Economic Desperation of South Korea

The narrative then pivots to the economic devastation of the 1997 Asian financial crisis, a context often overlooked in discussions of biotechnology. BobbyBroccoli explains that "South Korea's economy was by and large dominated by massive corporations known as chaebols," which collapsed under the weight of risky foreign loans. The author paints a grim picture where "1.5 million South Koreans lost their jobs," creating a desperate need for a new national identity.

The government's response was to identify a "cutting-edge technological niche" that could restore national pride. As BobbyBroccoli puts it, "The government looked at Dolly and said we can do that." The author argues that South Korea needed more than just funding; they needed a "celebrity scientist" to personify their resurgence. This strategy mirrors the way the West framed the Human Genome Project as a competition between the "bold American entrepreneur" and the "reserved British intellectual."

The selection of Hwang Woo-suk is presented as a calculated move to create a national icon. The author details Hwang's humble origins, noting that he "resorted to eating tree bark" as a child and was "ranked just 400th out of 480 students" in high school. This backstory is used to illustrate how the government crafted a narrative of the "rags-to-riches" genius. The author suggests that this narrative was so compelling that it blinded the public and the scientific community to the reality of the research.

"Koreans needed someone to latch on to who research would make headlines in the newspapers, whose face they would come to recognize on TV. They needed a celebrity."

This observation is the piece's most critical insight. It suggests that the fraud was enabled not just by scientific ambition, but by a national psychological need for a hero. The author implies that the pressure to succeed was so immense that it distorted the scientific process. A counterargument worth considering is that Hwang's success in cloning dogs and other animals was genuine, and that the fraud was a later, desperate attempt to maintain the momentum of his career rather than a premeditated deception from the start.

The Anatomy of a Fraud

The commentary then delves into the mechanics of cloning, using the story of Dolly the sheep to explain the difference between reproductive and therapeutic cloning. BobbyBroccoli writes, "Dolly's existence proved that any adult cell has all the necessary genetic information stored in their little nucleus to revert back to an embryonic state." This scientific explanation is essential for understanding why the promise of therapeutic cloning was so tantalizing. The author compares stem cells to "the Pokémon Eevee, a generic base template that can be molded into any type," a vivid analogy that makes the complex biology accessible to a general audience.

However, the author also highlights the moral and ethical dilemmas that arose from this potential. The distinction between reproductive cloning (making a full organism) and therapeutic cloning (making tissues) is blurred in the public eye, leading to the "deeply politicized topic" that Bush addressed. The author argues that the "looming question" of how to do this responsibly was never fully answered, leaving a vacuum that Hwang Woo-suk would eventually exploit.

"The majority of scientists agreed it was too risky, too morally dubious to try and clone a human child... Therapeutic cloning on the other hand was much harder to draw a line for."

This section underscores the fragility of the ethical framework surrounding cloning. The author suggests that the line between saving lives and creating "spare body parts" is thinner than policymakers admitted. The commentary effectively uses the history of Dolly to set the stage for the fraud, showing how the promise of a miracle cure created a fertile ground for deception.

Bottom Line

BobbyBroccoli's most compelling argument is that the cloning scandal was not an isolated incident of scientific malpractice, but a systemic failure driven by political policy and national desperation. The strongest part of the piece is its ability to connect the dots between a presidential address in Texas and a biotech fraud in Seoul, showing how global politics shape the trajectory of science. The biggest vulnerability is the lack of deep dive into the specific scientific methods Hwang used to deceive his peers, which leaves the technical details of the fraud somewhat abstract. Readers should watch for how modern biotech ethics are being shaped by the lessons of this era, particularly as new technologies like CRISPR emerge.
