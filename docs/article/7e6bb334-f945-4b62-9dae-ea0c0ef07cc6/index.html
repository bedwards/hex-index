There’s only one kind of tool security teams should be building with AI

Ross Haleliuk · Venture in Security ·Mar 10, 2026 · 18 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Ross Haleliuk cuts through the current AI hype cycle with a sobering reality check: while artificial intelligence makes coding faster, it does not magically conjure the deep, accumulated expertise required to build secure enterprise products. In a landscape where every LinkedIn feed seems to promise that "anyone can now be a product developer," Haleliuk argues that the gap between writing code and building functional, resilient security tools remains a chasm that AI cannot bridge. This is a vital intervention for security leaders tempted to trade vendor reliability for the allure of "vibecoding" their own defenses.

The Expertise Gap

Haleliuk's central thesis rests on a distinction that is often blurred in the rush to adopt new technology: the difference between generating code and engineering a product. He writes, "Software that can withstand real-world enterprise environments isn't some random CRUD (create - read - update - delete) apps." This observation is crucial because it dismantles the assumption that Large Language Models (LLMs) can replace the architectural rigor needed for security. The author correctly identifies that while AI accelerates the syntax of coding, it cannot replicate the "years of accumulated understanding about enterprise networks, identity models, logging pipelines, compliance nuances, or operational workflows."

The argument gains significant weight when Haleliuk contextualizes the scarcity of senior talent. He notes that security vendors essentially "industrialize very limited expertise, turning scarce security talent into software." This is a profound reframing of the vendor model; it is not merely a sales pitch but a mechanism for distributing rare, high-level knowledge to organizations that could never afford to hire a team of elite threat hunters. If every company attempted to build its own tools, we would face a scenario where "tens (or hundreds?) of thousands of enterprises all trying to recruit, retain, and manage their own in-house detection engineers." The math simply does not work.

Critics might argue that AI agents will eventually automate the maintenance and architectural oversight that currently requires human experts, but Haleliuk's point about the current state of the industry holds firm. The "cybersecurity talent shortage" is not a lack of entry-level coders but a deficit of senior professionals who understand how to find an attack path by hand. As Haleliuk puts it, "AI makes writing code super quick, but it makes skills like systems thinking and architecture more critical than ever."

"AI doesn't operate in isolation. It connects to the same data, permissions, and identities your security strategy already struggles to govern."

The Hidden Costs of Ownership

Beyond the initial build, Haleliuk tackles the often-overlooked reality of total cost of ownership. The excitement around AI lowering development costs is real, but it is a distraction from the long tail of maintenance. He writes, "The cost of building is going down, but the cost of ownership is not so much." This is a pragmatic reminder that software is a living entity requiring constant updates, integration refactoring, and on-call support.

This argument resonates strongly when viewed through the lens of technical debt. Just as the concept of "Shadow IT" emerged because departments built their own unmanaged solutions that later became unmanageable liabilities, internal security tools risk becoming the ultimate technical debt if they lack vendor-grade support. Haleliuk warns that "every software vendor has to deal with on-call rotations, documentation, knowledge transfer, feature requests, bug fixes, scaling infrastructure, compliance, audits, and retraining models." When a company builds in-house, they absorb all these risks without the economies of scale that vendors enjoy.

Furthermore, the liability implications are severe. Haleliuk points out that "auditors may not accept 'we built it ourselves' as proof of control." In a regulated industry, the risk of a breach combined with the inability to demonstrate rigorous change management could be catastrophic. The author notes that cyber insurance underwriters are increasingly scrutinizing whether tools are "industry-recognized" and "maintained." If a breach occurs and critical controls were replaced with internal "vibecoding" efforts, coverage could be invalidated.

The Intelligence Deficit

Perhaps the most compelling part of Haleliuk's analysis is the discussion on network effects. Security vendors benefit from seeing patterns across thousands of environments, a breadth of visibility that a single enterprise cannot replicate. Haleliuk writes, "An internal tool only sees one environment, so even though at some companies it can be super tailored to what they think they need, it is always going to lack breadth compared to something built by a vendor servicing many customers."

This lack of shared intelligence is a critical vulnerability. Detection logic improves when it is exposed to a wide variety of "badness" from across the industry. As Haleliuk notes, "The detection know-how of CrowdStrike or Palo Alto can only really be replicated if a company can see that many environments at the same time." This echoes the principles of Goodhart's law, where a measure becomes a target and ceases to be a good measure; an internal tool might optimize for a specific company's known threats while missing the novel attack vectors that vendors are already tracking globally.

"Exposure to many examples of 'badness' is a requirement for truly comprehensive coverage."

Haleliuk also highlights the operational drag of custom tooling on hiring and onboarding. New analysts arrive expecting to use industry-standard tools; forcing them to learn a proprietary, undocumented internal stack slows down their ability to contribute. He argues that "new hires will often already know the tools they'll be using or can rely on existing documentation and vendor support," whereas custom tools rely on "tribal knowledge" that creates bottlenecks.

Bottom Line

Ross Haleliuk delivers a necessary corrective to the "build everything" narrative, proving that AI is a force multiplier for engineering teams, not a replacement for the deep domain expertise required in cybersecurity. The argument's greatest strength is its focus on the hidden costs of ownership and the irreplaceable value of industry-wide threat intelligence. However, the piece leaves open the question of how mid-sized companies with moderate engineering talent can leverage AI for internal workflows without crossing the line into building core security controls, a nuance that warrants further exploration.
