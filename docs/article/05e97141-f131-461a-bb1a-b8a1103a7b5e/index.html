Co-packaged optics (cpo) book – scaling with light for the next wave of interconnect

Dylan Patel · SemiAnalysis ·Jan 1, 2026 ·112 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Dylan Patel delivers a rare, high-stakes verdict on the future of artificial intelligence infrastructure: the industry is hitting a hard wall with copper, and the only way through is light. While the hype cycle often focuses on chip speed, Patel's analysis at SemiAnalysis reveals that the real bottleneck is the energy and physical limits of moving data between chips. He argues that the long-promised shift to co-packaged optics is no longer a theoretical exercise but an urgent necessity for the next decade of AI scaling.

The Copper Ceiling

Patel's central thesis challenges the assumption that traditional pluggable transceivers can keep up with the explosive demands of AI workloads. He writes, "The AI networking bandwidth roadmap is such that interconnect speed, range, density and reliability requirements, will soon outpace what transceivers can provide." This is not a minor optimization; it is a fundamental shift in how data centers are built. The author contrasts the current copper-based solutions, like Nvidia's NVLink, which are limited to a two-meter range, with the potential of optical engines placed directly next to processors.

The argument gains weight when Patel breaks down the physics of the problem. He notes that with copper, "doubling bandwidth on copper by developing ever-faster SerDes is a highly challenging vector of scaling that is a slow grind." Here, the author draws on the history of electrical signaling, where the limits of speed over distance have long been known—a constraint that engineers have fought against since the days of early high-speed serial links. By moving the optical conversion closer to the source, the system eliminates the need for power-hungry digital signal processors (DSPs) that currently sit in every transceiver cage.

"CPO will be the main driver of bandwidth increases in scale-up networking for the latter part of this decade and beyond."

Patel's framing is compelling because it moves beyond the usual vendor marketing. He points out that while scale-out networks (connecting different servers) have been the focus of optical innovation, the real revolution lies in scale-up networking (connecting chips within a single system). He estimates that co-packaged optics can reduce the energy required to transmit data by more than 50%, with some targets aiming for an 80% reduction. This is a staggering efficiency gain for an industry where power consumption is becoming a primary constraint. Critics might note that the supply chain for these new optical components is not yet mature, and the transition from copper to light involves significant manufacturing risks. However, Patel suggests that the physics simply leaves no other viable path forward.

The Economics of Light

The analysis shifts from physics to economics, examining the total cost of ownership (TCO) for massive AI clusters. Patel highlights a counterintuitive finding: while co-packaged optics save massive amounts of power, the impact on the total cluster power budget is surprisingly diluted. He explains that "networking is just 9% of total cluster power to begin with for a three-layer network. So, at the end of the day the impact of switching to CPO is diluted considerably at least for scale-out networks."

This nuance is crucial. It suggests that the initial adoption of this technology will not be driven by a sudden drop in overall electricity bills, but by the ability to flatten network architectures. By using high-radix switches that integrate optics, data centers can reduce the number of network layers from three to two. Patel writes, "The magic comes from the fact that the number of ports k is exponentiated by the number of layers." This architectural change allows for connecting vastly more GPUs without the complexity of patch panels and external cabling.

As Patel puts it, "CPO for scale-up is now not a matter of if and why, but when and how – how to bring these systems to volume production, and when key component supply chain companies like laser manufacturers can ramp up sufficient production." The author identifies specific players like Celestial AI, estimating they could generate a $1 billion revenue run rate by 2028, primarily driven by partnerships with major cloud providers. This moves the conversation from academic papers to hard market commitments.

"Moving to a two-layer network delivers 48% lower networking cost, but only 4% total cluster power savings."

The author's distinction between scale-out and scale-up is particularly sharp. While Nvidia continues to push copper for scale-up connections through 2027 and 2028, Patel argues that the limits of copper will eventually force a hand. He notes that even with bi-directional SerDes, the energy cost of electrical signaling over distance is unsustainable. The historical context here is subtle but important; just as wavelength-division multiplexing (WDM) revolutionized long-haul telecommunications by sending multiple colors of light down a single fiber, co-packaged optics will bring that same density to the micro-scale of the data center rack.

The Path to Market

Patel concludes by outlining the complex supply chain required to make this vision a reality. The article details the specific components needed, from laser sources to fiber attach units, and names the key suppliers in Nvidia's ecosystem. He acknowledges that the technology is still in its infancy regarding mass production. "CPO focused companies are now well beyond papers, pilot projects and demonstrations and are making key product decisions such as optical port architecture to solve for high volume manufacturing."

The author's confidence in the timeline is grounded in the sheer scale of the problem. With AI clusters consuming hundreds of megawatts, the inefficiency of current transceivers is no longer an acceptable trade-off. Patel writes, "A 200,000 GB300 NVL72 GPU cluster on a three-layer network would consume 435 MW of Critical IT Power of which 17 MW would be consumed by optical transceivers alone." That 17 megawatt figure represents a massive opportunity for savings, even if it is a small fraction of the total.

Critics might argue that the industry's reliance on copper for scale-up is a testament to its reliability and cost-effectiveness, and that optical solutions may struggle with yield and thermal management in such a dense environment. Patel addresses this by emphasizing that the industry is already committing to suppliers, suggesting that the technical hurdles are being actively solved. The transition is not a question of possibility, but of execution.

Bottom Line

Patel's analysis is a masterclass in separating hype from engineering reality, proving that the shift to co-packaged optics is driven by the hard limits of physics rather than marketing cycles. The argument's greatest strength is its granular breakdown of power and cost, which reveals that the true value of this technology lies in architectural simplification rather than just raw efficiency. The biggest vulnerability remains the supply chain's ability to scale, but the momentum is clearly building toward a light-based future for AI infrastructure.

"CPO for scale-up is now not a matter of if and why, but when and how.""

Co-packaged optics (cpo) book – scaling with light for the next wave of interconnect

by Dylan Patel · SemiAnalysis · Read full article

Co-Packaged Optics (CPO) has long promised to transform datacenter connectivity, but it has taken a long time for the technology to come to market, with tangible deployment-ready products only arriving in 2025. In the meantime, pluggable transceivers have kept pace with networking requirements and remain the default path thanks to their relative cost-effectiveness, familiarity in deployment, and standards-based interoperability.

However the heavy networking demands that come with AI workloads mean that this time is different. The AI networking bandwidth roadmap is such that interconnect speed, range, density and reliability requirements, will soon outpace what transceivers can provide. CPO will provide some benefit and bring more options to scale-out networking, but it will be central to scale-up networking. CPO will be the main driver of bandwidth increases in scale-up networking for the latter part of this decade and beyond.

Today’s copper-based scale-up solutions, such as NVLink, provide tremendous bandwidth of 7.2 Tbit/s per GPU – soon to be 14.4 Tbit/s per GPU in the Rubin generation, yet copper-based links are limited in range to two meters at most, meaning the scale-up domain world size is limited to one or two racks at most. It is also increasingly difficult to scale bandwidth over copper. In Rubin, Nvidia will deliver another doubling of bandwidth per copper lane through bi-directional SerDes, but doubling bandwidth on copper by developing ever-faster SerDes is a highly challenging vector of scaling that is a slow grind. CPO can deliver the same or better bandwidth density and can provide additional vectors for scaling bandwidth, all while enabling larger scale-up domains.

A starting point for understanding the impetus for CPO is to consider the many inefficiencies and trade-offs when using a transceiver for optical communication. Transceivers can be used to achieve greater link range, but the cage on the front panel of a networking switch or compute tray that transceivers plug into is typically situated 15-30cm from an XPU or switch ASIC. This means that signals must first be transmitted electrically using an LR SerDes over that 15-30cm distance, with the electrical signal recovered and conditioned by a Digital Signal Processor (DSP) within the transceiver before being converted into an optical signal. With CPO, optical engines are instead placed next to XPUs or Switch ASICs, meaning that the DSP can be eliminated and that lower power SerDes can be used to move data from the XPU to the Optical Engine. ...
