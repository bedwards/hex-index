"Seasons clear, and awe" - chapter 9

PILCROW · ·Jan 1, 2026 ·17 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

PILCROW does not merely serialize a novel; they dissect the specific psychological architecture of a generation suspended between working-class pragmatism and unattainable creative ambition. This chapter offers a rare, unflinching look at how post-industrial America produced a class of people who are "too intelligent for ordinary happiness, too self-aware for decisive action." The narrative moves beyond standard family drama to explore a terrifying form of inherited trauma where children inherit not wealth, but "their parents' unlived ambitions and their mother's gift for psychological dissection."

The Architecture of Disconnection

The story centers on Elizabeth, a character whose internal landscape is defined by a profound inability to integrate her experiences. PILCROW writes, "Elizabeth's disparate experiences remained entirely disassociated from one another. The sensation of being with John, of fighting with him, never accumulated into a felt meaning." This is not just a character flaw; it is a clinical portrait of dissociation, a psychological defense mechanism where a person disconnects from their thoughts, feelings, memories, or sense of identity. Historically, this mirrors the diagnostic criteria for dissociative disorders that were rigorously defined in the mid-20th century, often emerging in response to overwhelming emotional stress that the psyche cannot process in real-time.

The tension in the "German house" scene is palpable, driven by Elizabeth's erratic emotional shifts and her boyfriend John's desperate attempt to impose rationality on chaos. When Elizabeth shatters a wine glass, the interaction becomes a metaphor for their entire dynamic. "If you don't express, if you don't... let any emotion you force my hand," she tells him, blurring the line between her internal state and external reality. John's response is equally telling: "You're literally talking circles," he asserts, highlighting the communication breakdown between those who feel everything and those who must justify every feeling.

"You just want to justify and justify and justify and you don't want to actually look another human being in the face and admit that you're causing them to suffer."

PILCROW's framing here is sharp. The author suggests that the inability to sit with another person's pain is a form of violence. Elizabeth's accusation that John is "afraid of the emotion inside of me in particular" cuts to the core of the conflict. It is a critique of a specific type of masculine rationality that views emotional volatility as a problem to be solved rather than a reality to be witnessed. Critics might argue that this portrayal risks romanticizing emotional instability, but the text undercuts this by showing the exhaustion it breeds. "I don't want to spend our last year of college doing this bullshit," John says, revealing the toll of constant psychic flux.

The Inheritance of Ambition

The novel's broader scope examines the Gazda family's legacy. PILCROW notes that the children are "capable of everything except building lives." This is a devastating observation on the failure of late 20th-century meritocracy. The characters are trapped between the "working-class pragmatism of their fathers" and the "creative and spiritual aspirations of their mothers." They possess the intellect to deconstruct their world but lack the grounding to inhabit it.

This dynamic is further illuminated by the concept of transgenerational trauma. The text hints at this when Elizabeth notes, "My mom keeps telling me when I come home for holidays that I remind her of her mother." This echoes the psychological understanding that trauma and behavioral patterns are often passed down through generations, not just through genetics but through the emotional environment of the home. The "bitch" label becomes a hereditary trait, a role the daughter is forced to play because the mother cannot process her own history.

Stephen, Elizabeth's brother, embodies the paralysis of this generation. He is reading Virginia Woolf's The Waves, a text obsessed with the fluidity of consciousness and time, mirroring his own struggle. "I keep getting high personal," he trails off, unable to finish the thought, a perfect example of the "suspended" state the author describes. His plan to "blow my brains out" or "become a troubadour" is not a genuine threat but a manifestation of his inability to choose a concrete path. He is "too self-aware for decisive action," a state that leaves him paralyzed in a world that demands productivity.

"They are too intelligent for ordinary happiness, too self-aware for decisive action: suspended between the working-class pragmatism of their fathers and the creative and spiritual aspirations of their mothers."

The narrative suggests that this suspension is a direct result of the era's cultural shifts. The characters are products of a time that promised infinite possibility but delivered only fragmentation. Elizabeth's inability to feel time as a continuum—"flying from past into future"—is a symptom of this dislocation. She feels herself to be a "pluralistic conglomeration of persons: mobile, nomadic," unable to anchor herself in a single identity or a single moment.

The Cost of Unlived Lives

The most haunting aspect of PILCROW's work is how it connects these personal fractures to a broader societal failure. The characters are not just struggling with individual mental health issues; they are struggling with the weight of "unlived ambitions" passed down from parents who could not fulfill their own dreams. Elizabeth's hope that John could be a "structuring male figure" is crushed because he is "woefully inadequate," just as her father and grandfather were insufficient in their own ways.

The text does not offer a neat resolution. Elizabeth and Stephen remain in their state of flux, moving from one pseudo-paradise to the next. "Every day of her life she encountered pseudo-paradises, pseudo-infernos, magic and gnosis and nothingness," PILCROW writes, capturing the hallucinatory cycle of a life lived in the abstract rather than the concrete. The ending of the chapter leaves the reader with a sense of unresolved tension, a reminder that for this generation, the struggle is not to find a solution, but to simply endure the weight of their own awareness.

Bottom Line

PILCROW's serialization offers a profound, if painful, exploration of a generation paralyzed by its own intelligence and the weight of inherited trauma. The strongest element is the seamless integration of psychological concepts like dissociation and transgenerational trauma into the narrative fabric, making the abstract deeply personal. The biggest vulnerability is the potential for the characters' paralysis to feel insurmountable, leaving the reader with little hope for resolution. However, this lack of easy answers is precisely what makes the piece so resonant for anyone navigating the complexities of modern identity. Watch for how the author handles the transition from personal fragmentation to a potential, albeit unlikely, reintegration of self in future chapters.

“Seasons Clear, and Awe” chronicles three decades in the life of the Gazda family, whose children inherit not wealth but something more dangerous: their parents’ unlived ambitions and their mother’s gift for psychological dissection. As Stephen and Elizabeth grow from precocious children into neurotic artists in their thirties, Matthew Gasda reveals how post-industrial, late 20th century America created a generation too intelligent for ordinary happiness, too self-aware for decisive action: suspended between the working-class pragmatism of their fathers and the creative and spiritual aspirations of their mothers, capable of everything except building lives.

Matthew Gasda is the founder of the Brooklyn Center for Theater Research and the author of many books, including the recent novel The Sleepers and Writer’s Diary.
