After the episode on the constellation class, I was sure we were done with US military ship building as a topic for the year. And after taking an existing European frig, redesigning it, partly to add more missile cells, only to eventually cancel and replace it with a converted coast guard cutter that doesn't have any missile cells, you might have expected the US Navy was now going to buckle down and focus on the basics. Maybe find a way to get more proven holes in the water to field more VLS cells and double down on the effort to design a next generation replacement for the venerable Arley Burke destroyers that are starting to show their age. Probably less common on everyone's bingo cards was the announcement of a new class of rail gun toading battleship.

In the 19th and early 20th century, the big gun battleship had been the accepted barometer of international naval power. Then amidst a flurry of technological changes spurred on by the Second World War, they started to disappear. Carriers and missile systems represented both new threats to the conventional battleship, but also new military alternatives. And by the early 1990s, the battleships as a class passed into history.

Now though, it seems like there might be a plan of sorts in Washington to bring the battleships back with the US Secretary of the Navy showing off plans for the new USS Defiant, first of the so-called Trump class guided missile battleships, toting everything from VLS cells, hypersonics, and lasers to a forward-mounted 32 megajel rail gun. Because apparently if your shipyards are already struggling to turn out enough basic destroyers to keep up in a building race with a peer competitor, the clear answer is to ask them to start turning out 35,000 ton super combatants packed with technology that strictly speaking doesn't exist yet. And so today, we're going to look at what we know so far about what might be the largest US Navy surface combatant constructed since the Second World War, including its announced features, some of the more interesting elements of the apparent proposed design, as well as some of the most obvious and pressing questions, like will this potentially work, what are the risks, how does the ship compare on paper to some of the possible alternatives, and what the return of the battleship could actually mean for ...
