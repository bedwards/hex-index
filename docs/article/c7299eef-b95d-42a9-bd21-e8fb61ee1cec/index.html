Donbas, not Istanbul. Russia Chooses War. [Mapped]

Commentary by Brian Edwards

While diplomatic channels buzz with rumors of backroom deals, Good Times Bad Times cuts through the noise with a stark, map-driven reality check: the war in Ukraine is not pausing for negotiations; it is accelerating into a new, terrifying phase. The author's most jarring claim is that the Kremlin has abandoned any pretense of seeking a negotiated settlement, instead flooding the Donetsk front with a fourfold increase in daily attacks to force a collapse. This is not a stalemate; it is a deliberate, grinding offensive designed to exploit a specific window of opportunity before the West can react.

The Illusion of Diplomacy

The commentary begins by dismantling the hope that high-level talks are close to fruition. Good Times Bad Times writes, "While the world anxiously awaits a diplomatic breakthrough, Russian forces are pushing forward with growing determination in the Donetsk region." The author points out the stark contrast between the Ukrainian delegation's high-stakes flight to Turkey and Moscow's response: sending low-level officials who immediately escalated demands. The piece argues that the Russian strategy is to use the talks as a delay tactic while their military machine grinds forward. "The goal is war, not peace," the author states bluntly, noting that Moscow's demands shifted from four annexed regions to five the moment Ukraine refused. This framing is effective because it aligns the diplomatic failures directly with the kinetic reality on the ground, suggesting that the West's policy of concessions is not just failing but actively encouraging Russian aggression. Critics might argue that the author oversimplifies the complexity of the Turkish mediation, but the evidence of the Russian delegation's behavior supports the conclusion that they have no intention of compromising.

A New Era of Warfare

Perhaps the most significant contribution of this piece is its analysis of how the nature of combat is fundamentally shifting. The author moves beyond standard troop movements to describe a technological and tactical revolution. "Artillery is giving way to FPV drones responsible for up to 80% of losses and traditional armored columns have been replaced by motorcycle groups reminiscent of Middle Eastern terrorist tactics." This observation is crucial. It suggests that the old rules of trench warfare and armored maneuver are obsolete. The author notes that the intensification of attacks, jumping from 50 to 200 per day, is not just about numbers but about a change in the very fabric of the battlefield. "The world is entering a new era of conflicts where preparing fortifications against drones than traditional trenches," they argue. This is a profound insight for busy readers trying to understand the strategic implications: the defense is no longer about holding ground with heavy steel, but about surviving a sky filled with autonomous killers. The shift to motorcycle squads allows for speed and fluidity that tanks cannot match, creating a threat that is harder to predict and counter.

The face of war is changing before our eyes, and the old maps of defense are no longer valid.

The Breakthrough at Ocheretino

The core of the military analysis focuses on the critical sector north of Ocheretino, where the author details a catastrophic breach in Ukrainian lines. Good Times Bad Times describes how Russian forces, utilizing the new motorcycle tactics, achieved a breakthrough of over 10 kilometers. "An assault group on motorcycles moving at high speed created a bridge threatening Pokrovsk, Kostiantynivka, and the grouping near Toretsk." The author meticulously traces the collapse of the Ukrainian defense, noting that the 109th Territorial Defense Brigade and 157th Mechanized Brigade were overwhelmed. The narrative highlights the speed of the Russian advance, capturing the village of Tarasa and pushing to Novo Kifka "almost without a fight." This rapid exploitation of the gap is presented as the most dangerous development in the war so far. The author writes, "The bridge fragmented Ukrainian defense and led to the creation of a gap between defending units," emphasizing that the structural integrity of the front line has been compromised. While the author notes that Ukraine threw in strategic reserves, including the 1st Tank Brigade, the outcome remains uncertain. "Reports from this area did not yet indicate stabilization," they conclude, leaving the reader with a sense of immediate, unresolved crisis. A counterargument worth considering is whether the Russian advance is sustainable given their own logistical strains, but the immediate tactical success is undeniable.

The Broader Front and the Kursk Reversal

The commentary also addresses the shifting dynamics in the Kursk and Belgorod regions, where the narrative of Ukrainian success has been reversed. The author details how, by the end of March, Ukrainians had lost almost all the territory they had gained the previous August. "By April 10th, as a result of flanking maneuvers, Ukrainian soldiers withdrew from Guvo and the village returned under Russian control." The piece describes a methodical Russian counter-offensive that has pushed Ukrainian forces back across the border, culminating in the capture of the last Ukrainian-controlled village in the Kursk region. "This allowed Chief of General Staff, General Gerasimov, to announce the completion of the operation to expel Ukrainian forces from Russian territory." The author notes that subsequent Ukrainian attempts to launch new offensives in the area have collapsed quickly, losing vehicles and failing to achieve any surprise. This section serves as a sobering reminder that the war is not static; gains made in one theater can be erased in another. The author's detailed account of the fighting around the monastery on Mount Fangor, which was destroyed by aerial bombardment after being recaptured, illustrates the brutal attrition that defines these battles.

Bottom Line

Good Times Bad Times delivers a sobering, evidence-rich assessment that the war has entered a dangerous new phase defined by drone dominance and rapid, fluid offensives. The strongest part of the argument is the clear linkage between the failure of diplomacy and the escalation of military tactics, proving that the Kremlin is betting everything on a battlefield victory. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on rapidly evolving battlefield reports that may shift by the hour, but the strategic trend it identifies—the collapse of static defense and the rise of drone-led maneuver warfare—is undeniable. Readers must watch the Pokrovsk sector closely, as the gap created by the Russian motorcycle assault could determine the fate of the entire Donetsk front.
