Inside the stunning collapse of the Quebec liberal party

The Walrus · ·Jan 14, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

This is not a story about a single politician's fall; it is a forensic map of a political ecosystem in freefall. The Walrus presents a startling thesis: Quebec is hurtling toward a constitutional crisis not because the public demands independence, but because the federalist anchor has shattered, leaving a vacuum that a referendum-seeking party is poised to fill. In a landscape where the governing Coalition Avenir Québec has collapsed in popularity and the Parti Québécois leads the polls despite a public that largely rejects a vote on sovereignty, the stakes have never been higher for the stability of the federation.

The Anatomy of a Collapse

The Walrus meticulously details how the Quebec Liberal Party, once the unshakeable guardian of federalism, has been reduced to a shadow of its former self. The narrative traces a long, slow bleed starting with the 2012 tuition hikes and the Charbonneau corruption scandal, accelerating through austerity measures, and culminating in the recent resignation of leader Pablo Rodriguez. The author argues that this is not merely a leadership issue but a structural one. "Those were not shining years for them," notes Daniel Béland, director of the McGill Institute for the Study of Canada, a sentiment that frames the party's current 20 percent polling not as a temporary dip but as a deep-seated rejection. The piece effectively illustrates that the party's base has eroded so thoroughly that even a federal comeback story cannot be replicated provincially.

The coverage highlights the absurdity of the current timeline: a new leader must be chosen by March, just six months before an election, while the party hemorrhages support among francophones. The Walrus writes, "Today, the provincial party has a much smaller base than the federal one did, even at the end of Trudeau's leadership. They also have a ceiling that's really low." This distinction is crucial; it dismantles the hope that a fresh face alone can reverse decades of mistrust. The author points out that the students who protested in 2012 are now in their mid-30s, a demographic that "will never, ever vote Liberal." This generational scar tissue suggests that the party's recovery may be mathematically impossible before the next election.

Those students who protested Liberal tuition hikes in 2012 are now in their mid-30s. And I can tell you that they'll never, ever vote Liberal.

Critics might argue that political landscapes can shift overnight and that a charismatic candidate could still galvanize a coalition. However, the evidence presented regarding the party's isolation to anglophone and allophone voters in Montreal suggests a structural ceiling that charisma alone cannot breach.

The Sovereignty Paradox

The most compelling section of the analysis explores the bizarre duality of the upcoming election. The Parti Québécois is surging in the polls, yet they are campaigning on a platform of holding a sovereignty referendum that most Quebecers do not want right now. The Walrus captures this tension perfectly, noting that while the party leads, "few support having a referendum now—just one out of four Quebecers want a vote on independence in the next four years." This creates a paradox where a party can win a majority government on a promise to trigger a vote that the majority of the electorate opposes.

The commentary weaves in the broader geopolitical context to explain why this might still happen. With global instability and a volatile international climate, the article suggests that the calculus for voters is shifting. The Walrus cites David Coletto, founder of Abacus Data, who observed that in a world of uncertainty, "Canada looks less like a constraint and more like a shield." This insight reframes the referendum not just as a provincial issue but as a question of national security and risk management. The author argues that while the committed sovereigntist base remains steady, the "cautious middle" is the key battleground, and their decision will hinge on whether they view the union as a safety net or a shackle.

The piece posits that the election could become a de facto referendum, even if the Parti Québécois does not win a majority on the issue alone. "Realignment and vote-splitting could still deliver the PQ a majority government, even without majority support for sovereignty," the author warns. This is a chilling prospect for federalists: a government elected on a mandate to hold a vote that the public hasn't explicitly endorsed, simply because the opposition is too fractured to stop them.

A Fragile Future

As the Quebec Liberal Party scrambles to find a new leader in Charles Milliard or Mario Roy, the window for rebuilding trust is closing. The Walrus notes that Milliard's strategy of focusing on "bread and butter" issues like health care and education might be enough to secure forty seats, but it is unlikely to restore the party to its former dominance. The article concludes with a sobering assessment of the coming months: "It's going to be a strange one," says poll analyst Philippe J. Fournier. The unpredictability of the race, combined with the potential for a constitutional rupture, makes this election one of the most consequential in recent Canadian history.

If the Liberals can't rebuild by October, voters will be left with a tricky choice: a vote on sovereignty most don't want—at least not now—a governing party they've soured on, or a smaller party that hasn't been tested.

The analysis suggests that the failure of the federalist anchor has created a dangerous vacuum. Even if the Parti Québécois wins, the lack of a strong federalist alternative could force a referendum under unfavorable conditions for the "no" side. The article leaves the reader with the unsettling realization that the collapse of one party could inadvertently trigger a constitutional crisis that the public did not ask for.

Bottom Line

The Walrus delivers a masterful dissection of how institutional decay and leadership failure can create a political vacuum with existential consequences. The strongest part of this argument is the clear linkage between the Liberal Party's long-term structural failures and the immediate risk of a sovereignty referendum that the public does not support. The piece's biggest vulnerability is the assumption that the "cautious middle" will remain passive; history shows that voters can be surprisingly volatile when faced with the threat of constitutional rupture. Watch closely for how the federalist vote consolidates in the final months—if it fails to coalesce, the path to a referendum becomes dangerously clear.

By Caitlin Walsh Miller

After two-and-a-half years without a permanent leader, the Quebec Liberal Party finally elected one last summer. Now, they’re leaderless again. A week before Christmas, Pablo Rodriguez resigned amid allegations of vote buying, plunging a once-dominant political machine back into crisis. The Liberals are now polling around 20 percent and scrambling to rebuild trust with a base that’s fed up with the disarray and fleeing. Meanwhile, support for the governing Coalition Avenir Québec (CAQ) has collapsed; according to recent polls, François Legault is Canada’s least popular premier.

In this vacuum, the Parti Québécois (PQ) has surged ahead, dominating the polls for the last two years, even as it pledges a sovereignty referendum that most Quebecers say they don’t want. All this with a provincial election scheduled for October. “I say this without hyperbole,” says poll analyst Philippe J. Fournier, “but this election has no precedent in Quebec history, at least in the last fifty years.”

At stake is more than one party’s fortunes. The Liberals have been Quebec’s federalist anchor for generations, but their collapse comes precisely as the PQ is positioned to win a majority and trigger that referendum. If the Liberals can’t rebuild by October, voters will be left with a tricky choice: a vote on sovereignty most don’t want—at least not now—a governing party they’ve soured on, or a smaller party that hasn’t been tested. When the referendum comes, it’s not clear who will be left to make the case for Canada.
