America's millennial tolkien

Some Guy · ·Jan 4, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In an era where the literary landscape is often dismissed as fragmented or diminished, Some Guy makes a startling claim: a thirty-two-year-old author has already produced a seven-volume masterpiece that rivals the greatest works of the twentieth century. This is not merely a book review; it is a defense of the human capacity for depth, arguing that Christopher Ruocchio's Sun Eater series proves our generation has not lost the ability to make fire. The piece forces busy readers to reconsider what constitutes "greatness" in science fiction, moving beyond plot mechanics to examine moral philosophy in the age of artificial intelligence.

The Architecture of a Masterpiece

Some Guy anchors the argument in the visceral reality of the author's development, noting that the series allows readers to witness "the slow birth of a literary genius" in real-time. The author argues that the first volume, Empire of Silence, is merely "serviceable," a necessary growing pain that makes the subsequent evolution of the text more profound. This framing is effective because it treats the series not as a static product, but as a living document of artistic maturation. By comparing the experience to reading The Lord of the Rings before it was canonized, Some Guy suggests that true innovation often arrives unrecognized.

"We all aren't on our cell phones. We're not all writing slop. Our generation still has the focus and ingenuity to achieve artistic depth."

The commentary highlights how Ruocchio navigates the tension between the "Roman Empire among the stars" and the ancient fear of technological singularity. The series posits that humanity, having learned that "infinite unconstrained power is infinitely destabilizing," built a rigid, fascistic empire to ensure unending sameness. This mirrors historical anxieties about the collapse of complex societies, echoing the fall of the Roman Empire, yet it adds a unique sci-fi twist: the ruling elite are genetically "cryptographically locked" to prevent natural reproduction, effectively holding billions of young royals hostage to maintain control. Some Guy notes that this setup allows the story to explore the "heat death of the universe" not as a physical event, but as a moral stagnation.

Critics might argue that drawing such direct parallels to historical empires risks oversimplifying the complexities of governance and human nature. However, the text acknowledges that no society presented is without flaw, suggesting that the dividing line between good and evil "lives nowhere but the individual human heart."

The Divine and the Flawed

The most distinctive element of Some Guy's analysis is the treatment of the divine within a hard science fiction setting. The author describes how the protagonist, Hadrian Marlowe, receives a vision of a future where he destroys a solar system, accompanied by a voice stating, "this must be." Some Guy writes that the series handles this revelation with a "solemn dignity" that avoids the heavy-handedness often found in religious allegory.

"If God is real, then He is the God of stars and time and reason, and on matters of the survival of entire sentient species, God is not silent."

The piece draws a powerful parallel between Hadrian and the biblical figure of Moses. Much like Moses, who was denied entrance to the Promised Land despite leading his people, Hadrian is a "flawed man doing his best" to follow a divine will he does not fully understand. The author emphasizes that the hero is not a cinematic savior but a prophet burdened by blood on his hands. This reframing elevates the narrative from a standard space opera to a "speculative continuation of the Biblical narrative itself," where the stakes involve the very definition of humanity.

Some Guy also touches on the influence of Gene Wolfe, a master of unreliable narration and theological complexity, noting that Ruocchio's work requires a familiarity with classical literature and theology to be fully appreciated. Yet, the author reassures readers that context is always provided, allowing the story to stand on its own. The argument is that Ruocchio does not steal from his predecessors but engages in a "deft conversation" with them, using references as signposts to "look over there."

"I would change the Lord of the Rings to remove razing of the Shire, for that matter, and I would have told Tolkien he was making several large mistakes in the way he was wrapping things up. And I would be wrong in both cases. Art is art, in part because it is a sort of proof that minds exist other than our own."

The Struggle Against the Machine

A significant portion of the commentary is dedicated to the real-world struggles behind the books. Some Guy reveals that the series was nearly canceled by its publisher, Penguin, after the fifth volume, only to be saved by the "heroic intervention of the BookTube community." The author argues that the series could not have been accomplished in the same way if it had been fully supported by traditional publishing structures, suggesting that the struggle itself shaped the work's authenticity.

"I say that as someone who is genuinely annoyed when artists have to struggle. Even its publisher, Penguin, went so far as to force Ruocchio to quickly split the fourth book into two volumes and subsequently canceled the series after the fifth book."

This section serves as a critique of the modern publishing industry, which often prioritizes marketability over artistic risk. The author's frustration is palpable, yet the ultimate message is one of triumph: the "curse of Rothfuss and Martin has been broken," and the "spring of science fiction and fantasy is upon us all." The piece suggests that the resilience of the author and the community is as compelling as the story itself.

Bottom Line

Some Guy's argument is strongest in its assertion that Sun Eater is a rare example of a contemporary work that successfully merges high-concept science fiction with deep moral philosophy. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the reader's prior knowledge of classical literature and theology, which may alienate those unfamiliar with the genre's touchstones. However, the core verdict remains clear: this is a work that demands attention, offering a profound meditation on the nature of humanity, divinity, and the cost of survival in a universe that has forgotten how to be human.

"The work I am about to describe makes the mere thought of indulging in some kind of cheap joke something akin to standing before a council of devil-worshippers and spitting on a picture of Jesus."

Readers should watch for how the final volume resolves the tension between Hadrian's flawed humanity and the divine mandate he carries, a conclusion that will likely redefine the genre for years to come.

I’m writing this piece to tell you about one of the greatest literary accomplishments of the present generation. We shall start this journey together with the explicit absence of a joke. It was going to be quite a long joke having to do with Space Romans and a made up language called Old High Italian. I can’t bring myself to write it.

I am a naturally crooked and profane man, from a naturally crooked and profane culture. My favorite uncle is Italian. I have developed such a powerful Italian joke-making reflex over the years that suppressing it feels like forcing my leg to stay still after a doctor strikes my patellar tendon. My only explanation is that certain rules are transcendent. One does not belch in the company of a lady, or utter obscenities in front of a child, or produce flatulence at a funeral. The work I am about to describe makes the mere thought of indulging in some kind of cheap joke something akin to standing before a council of devil-worshippers and spitting on a picture of Jesus.

With solemn dignity, I now arrive on your screen to tell you about the Sun Eater series by Christopher Ruocchio. These books are not only a masterwork of science-fiction but what can only be described as good. Good as in reading them will not only entertain you and demonstrate mastery of craft, or provide you with meaningful moral instruction and scaffolding, but also that they will reignite in you the belief that our present generation is capable of creating greatness. In the seven volume series, Ruocchio has crafted a story that in hindsight will be understood as one of the first fully realized and worthy narrative considerations of moral philosophy in the age of artificial intelligence.

Christopher Ruocchio, the Italian American Author of the Sun Eater, is a young man. At the time of this writing, I believe him to be thirty two. In other words, he is a Millennial. He began writing the Sun Eater when he was only twenty-one. This is apparent in the series. The first book of the series, Empire of Silence, is merely serviceable. It’s not a terrible book by any means and many much older authors would view it as their finest work, but it’s only great if you remind yourself that it was written by a young college student.
