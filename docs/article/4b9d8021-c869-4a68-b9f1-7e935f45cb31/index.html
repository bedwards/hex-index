I Went to Cannes to See 2025’s Most Promising Movies

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·May 27, 2025 · 17 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden does something rare in festival coverage: he admits that the most profound cinematic moments often arrive without trailers, reviews, or familiar names, framing the 2025 Khan Film Festival not as a showcase of stars, but as a crucible for the unknown. While most critics rush to rank films by plot mechanics, van der Linden argues that the true value of the festival lies in "going into a film that you know next to nothing about" to find works that "challenge my perception of what cinema can be."

The Architecture of Fragmented Souls

The author's most compelling insight emerges early when discussing Sound of Falling, a German drama that he suggests operates on a metaphysical level rather than a narrative one. Van der Linden writes, "Maybe all men got one big soul who everybody's a part of... but it is, as sound of falling seems to suggest, tragically fragmented into disperate points of consciousness." This observation shifts the critical lens from character development to existential philosophy, suggesting that the film's power comes from the "phantom pain of its absence" rather than traditional storytelling.

He describes the film's aesthetic as a "disorienting" blend of David Lynch, Béla Tarr, and Andrei Tarkovsky, noting that it is "a challenging first encounter, occasionally slow and meandering, but it is also an undeniably beautiful and haunting one." This framing is effective because it prepares the audience for difficulty, positioning the film's slowness not as a flaw, but as a deliberate demand for "attention and vulnerability." However, a counterargument worth considering is whether such dense, abstract filmmaking alienates the very "new generation of filmmakers" the author hopes it will inspire, potentially limiting its impact to a niche intellectual circle.

It dares to ask its audience for attention and vulnerability, and perhaps even more boldly, for a second viewing.

The House as a Character and the Descent into Madness

Moving from the abstract to the visceral, van der Linden turns his attention to films where the setting itself becomes the protagonist. He highlights Sentimental Value, the latest from Joachim Trier, arguing that it "expands its canvas to an entire family" in a way that feels "almost literary." The author posits that the film's strength lies in its exploration of "communication between family members," capturing both the "potential means by which we can reach out to each other" and the "limitations and obstacles that might get in the way."

This analysis is particularly sharp because it elevates a domestic drama into a study of human connection, suggesting Trier has "built on" his previous work to create "what might just be a future classic." Similarly, when discussing Want to Get Married? Die, my Love, van der Linden praises Jennifer Lawrence's performance as a "visceral descend into madness" that treads a "fine line between dark comedy and genuine tragedy." He notes that while the ending initially seemed cynical, the directors offered a "more hopeful interpretation" that changed his perspective. This willingness to revisit his own judgment in light of new context adds a layer of intellectual honesty to his review, though critics might argue that relying on a director's press conference to clarify an ambiguous ending undermines the film's own artistic integrity.

The Joy of Shared Laughter and the Vibe of History

In a festival dominated by heavy dramas, van der Linden finds unexpected relief in Splitzville, a comedy that he claims is "the kind of comedy movie that made me realize just how much I'd been missing this genre." He emphasizes the communal aspect of the experience, noting that "the whole audience was just completely electrified" and that the director spoke about "the importance of laughing together." This serves as a crucial reminder that cinema is a collective art form, a point often lost in the solitary consumption of streaming content.

He also tackles Richard Linklater's Nouvelv, a film about the making of Breathless, defending it against accusations of being a mere "cosplaying exercise." Van der Linden writes that Linklater "wasn't trying to recapture the revolutionary brilliance" of the original, but rather wanted to tell the story "as if it was being captured in the actual moment." This distinction is vital; it reframes the film from a nostalgic imitation to an immersive "hangout movie" that relies on the audience's prior knowledge of the French New Wave to fully appreciate the "vibe and atmosphere."

We need to keep making cinematic comedies for cinema, plain and simple.

The Wildcard and the Bridge of the Soul

The commentary culminates with a discussion of Sirat, a film the author describes as operating on an "entirely different level." Van der Linden explains that while the plot involves a father and son searching for a daughter in a desert rave scene, the story is ultimately "one of the soul" set in a "mythological realm." He grapples with the film's theological underpinnings, noting that the "Sirat is a bridge that can be crossed only by the most religiously righteous," yet the characters seem driven by "random chance" rather than moral acts.

This confusion, he argues, may be the point: "upon further reflection, I began to feel like this may have been exactly the point." He concludes that the film "defies an all-encompassing analysis," functioning more like a poem where "an explanation... never fully captures the complete revelation." This is a bold defense of ambiguity, suggesting that some films are meant to be felt rather than solved. Critics might note that such a defense can sometimes excuse incoherence, but van der Linden's insistence that the film "stirred something within me on that more foundational level" validates the emotional truth of the experience over logical consistency.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's coverage succeeds because it prioritizes the emotional and philosophical resonance of cinema over plot summaries, effectively arguing that the most important films are those that demand a second viewing to be fully understood. While his reliance on director commentary to resolve ambiguity in some films is a slight vulnerability, his overarching thesis—that the festival is a place to be "struck" by the unknown—remains a powerful and necessary reminder of why we watch movies.
