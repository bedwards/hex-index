Salarymen, specialists, and small businesses

Noah Smith · Noahpinion ·Apr 3, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Noah Smith cuts through the noise of AI doomerism with a counterintuitive thesis: the immediate future of work isn't about mass unemployment, but a radical restructuring of how we work. While headlines scream about robots stealing jobs, Smith points to data showing employment near all-time highs, arguing that we are witnessing a shift from task replacement to task reorganization. This is a vital distinction for anyone trying to navigate the next decade of their career, as it suggests the danger lies not in obsolescence, but in the inability to adapt to a "jagged" new reality where human and machine capabilities overlap unpredictably.

The Illusion of Immediate Displacement

Smith begins by dismantling the most common fear: that AI will instantly wipe out entire professions. He notes that "employment rates for prime-age workers in the U.S. are hovering near all-time highs," and cites a survey of corporate CFOs finding "little evidence of near-term aggregate employment declines due to AI." Even in fields predicted to be decimated, like radiology, demand is actually rising. The author argues that while the long-term trajectory might lead to total displacement, the short-term reality is far more nuanced.

The core of his analysis relies on the concept of "jagged" capabilities. As Smith puts it, "Observers of AI consistently find that its capabilities are 'jagged' — it's much better at some tasks than others." This unevenness creates a buffer. A machine might write code faster than a human, but it cannot yet navigate the specific, messy context of a legacy system or explain the ethical implications of a bug to a non-technical stakeholder. This aligns with historical shifts in the Capability approach, where the definition of human labor has always expanded to include the oversight and integration of new tools rather than their total replacement.

"In other words, so far, AI is replacing tasks, not jobs."

This framing is effective because it moves the conversation from a binary "job or no job" to a spectrum of task allocation. However, critics might note that this optimism relies on the assumption that demand for services will always expand to absorb the productivity gains. If AI becomes so cheap that the marginal value of human oversight drops to zero, the "jagged" protection could vanish overnight.

The Three Futures: Specialists, Generalists, and the Return of the Salaryman

Smith proposes a tripartite future for the labor market: specialists, small business owners, and a resurrected version of the Japanese "salaryman." He leans heavily on a theory by Garicano, Li, and Wu regarding "bundled" tasks. In "strongly bundled" jobs, tasks are so interconnected that they must be performed by a single human to maintain quality. Smith illustrates this with the example of a blogger: "AI, so far, is very good at doing background research, proofreading, and a number of other tasks... But even though it can generate infinite amounts of text, AI is not yet good at writing." The unique human perspective remains the bottleneck.

Conversely, "weakly bundled" jobs—where tasks can be easily separated—are the first to be automated. The challenge for companies is that AI's strengths change rapidly. Smith highlights the software engineering sector: "In 2025, companies hiring software engineers could judge their merit based on how good they were at writing code. In 2026, companies have to judge the merit of software engineers based on how good they are at checking and maintaining code." This volatility demands a new kind of worker: the generalist.

Smith cites Cedric Savarese to describe the new role of the generalist, who must develop a "mental model of the AI mind" to spot errors. "Curiosity becomes essential," Smith writes, noting that the new job is "to understand the AI mind enough to catch when something is off, and to defer to a true specialist when the stakes are high." This is a compelling reframing of the generalist role, moving it from "jack of all trades, master of none" to "master of integration."

"The first stage of 'vibe freedom' is...[t]he dreaded report that would have taken all night looks better than anything you could have done yourself and only took a few minutes... The next stage comes almost by surprise — there's something that's not quite right."

The most provocative part of Smith's argument is his suggestion that the West might adopt the Japanese "salaryman" model. Historically, this system involved rotating employees through various departments to build firm-specific knowledge. Smith argues this makes sense in an AI era where technical skills depreciate rapidly, but institutional knowledge and network effects become the primary assets. "If I'm a highly specialized engineer, I can take my talents and move to a different company... But if I'm a generalist who does a little bit of everything, what becomes more important to my value as a worker are my human networks within a company," he explains. This explains the current "no-hire, no fire" economy, where workers stay put to protect their firm-specific capital.

Critics might argue that the Japanese salaryman model was inefficient and contributed to stagnation, and that forcing American workers into long-term, non-specialized roles could stifle the very innovation AI is supposed to drive. Furthermore, the model assumes a cultural willingness to accept lifetime employment in a system that is increasingly precarious.

The Rise of the AI-Enabled Solopreneur

The third pillar of Smith's vision is the explosion of small businesses. AI provides leverage, allowing a single individual to manage tasks that previously required a team. "AI creates leverage; it allows you to do more with a smaller team," Smith observes. He points to Japan's high prevalence of small and medium-sized enterprises as a potential blueprint for the future, suggesting that the optimal team size for many ventures will shrink to one or two people.

This shift implies a move away from the traditional corporate ladder toward a landscape of independent contractors and micro-enterprises. "I expect to see a lot of small companies sprout up, as people use AI agents to increase their productivity to the point where they only need a few employees (or even zero)," he writes. This creates a bifurcated future: those who can leverage AI to build their own empires, and those who become the "cogs" in the new generalist machine.

Bottom Line

Smith's strongest contribution is his rejection of the binary "AI will kill jobs" narrative in favor of a complex, task-based restructuring that favors adaptability over deep specialization. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that human "judgment" and "networks" will remain economically valuable indefinitely; if AI eventually masters the nuance of human interaction, the "salaryman" and "specialist" roles could collapse just as quickly as the coder's did. Readers should watch for how quickly the "jagged" edges of AI smooth out, as that will determine whether the generalist is a permanent fixture or a temporary bridge to full automation.

Geoffrey Hinton, one of the pioneers of modern AI, famously predicted the imminent displacement of all radiologists by AI algorithms; in fact, radiologists are in greater demand than ever.

So even though AI may displace human beings en masse in the future, it’s not doing that today. But it is likely to change the nature of work. Software engineers, for whom “writing code” was a big part of the job description just a few months ago, are now mainly checkers and maintainers of code written by AIs. But this hasn’t eliminated the need for software engineers — at least, not yet. It has just shifted their job descriptions.

Yet…we estimate precise null effects on earnings and recorded hours at both the worker and workplace levels, ruling out effects larger than 2% two years after the launch of ChatGPT. What moves is the structure of work: employers absorb AI through task reorganization—including new tasks in content generation, AI oversight, and AI integration—and adopters transition into higher-paying occupations where AI chatbots are more relevant, though still too few to move average earnings.
