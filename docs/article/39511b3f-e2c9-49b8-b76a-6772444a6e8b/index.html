Salarymen, specialists, and small businesses

Noah Smith · Noahpinion ·Apr 3, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Noah Smith cuts through the noise of AI doom-mongering with a counterintuitive thesis: we aren't heading toward mass unemployment, but rather a radical restructuring of how work gets done. While headlines scream about radiologists and coders being replaced, Smith points to hard data showing employment rates near all-time highs, arguing that the real shift is from "doing" tasks to "managing" the jagged, unpredictable capabilities of artificial intelligence.

The Illusion of Displacement

Smith immediately dismantles the fear that AI is currently wiping out jobs. He notes that a recent survey of corporate chief financial officers found "little evidence of near-term aggregate employment declines due to AI." Even more striking, he highlights the case of Geoffrey Hinton, a pioneer of the field who predicted the imminent end of radiology, only to see those professionals remain in "greater demand than ever."

The core of Smith's argument rests on the distinction between replacing tasks and replacing roles. He cites research by Humlum and Vestergaard (2026) regarding the Danish labor market, observing that while AI adoption is widespread and boosts productivity, it has resulted in "precise null effects on earnings and recorded hours." The technology isn't firing people; it is forcing them to reorganize their daily routines.

"So far, AI is replacing tasks, not jobs."

This framing is crucial because it shifts the anxiety from job security to skill relevance. Smith argues that the "jagged" nature of AI—where it excels at some things and fails at others—creates a buffer for human workers. However, this creates a paradox for the next generation: if the advice to "learn to code" was sound a decade ago, what is the equivalent today? The rapid obsolescence of specific technical skills means that the very definition of a "job" is becoming fluid.

The Three Futures of Work

Smith proposes a tripartite model for the future workforce: specialists, salarymen, and small businesspeople. He leans heavily on a theory by economists Garicano, Li, and Wu regarding "weakly bundled" versus "strongly bundled" tasks. In weakly bundled jobs, tasks can be easily separated and assigned to different agents (human or AI). In strongly bundled jobs, the tasks must be performed by the same person to create value.

"AI tends to replace weakly bundled jobs a lot more quickly than it replaces strongly bundled ones."

This explains why radiologists (strongly bundled) are safe for now, while generic content creators or junior coders (weakly bundled) face disruption. Smith suggests that the "specialist" will survive because their work requires a unique human perspective that AI cannot replicate, such as the specific voice of a blogger or the nuanced judgment of a doctor.

Critics might note that this distinction is fragile; as AI models improve, the line between "weakly" and "strongly" bundled tasks will blur, potentially turning many specialists into generalists sooner than expected.

The Return of the Salaryman

Perhaps the most provocative part of Smith's analysis is his prediction that the corporate world will return to the Japanese "salaryman" model. He describes a system where employees are not hired for specific technical skills but are rotated through divisions to become "AI wranglers."

"Instead of hiring people who are good accountants or good HR specialists or whatever, companies might start hiring people who are just good AI wranglers."

Smith draws a parallel to the historical Japanese system where workers were "almost interchangeable labor," moving from HR to accounting to product design. While this system was once criticized for low productivity, Smith argues it is perfectly suited for an era where AI handles the specialized execution, and humans must provide the flexibility to patch AI's errors.

"Curiosity becomes essential. So does the willingness to learn quickly, think critically, spot inconsistencies, and to rely on judgment rather than treating AI as infallible."

This generalist role requires "firm-specific human capital." Because the skills needed are about navigating a specific company's unique AI integration, workers become less portable. Smith points to the current "no-hire, no fire" economy in the U.S. as a precursor to this, where workers stay put because their value is tied to internal networks rather than transferable technical skills.

"If I'm a highly specialized engineer, I can take my talents and move to a different company with my human capital intact. But if I'm a generalist who does a little bit of everything, what becomes more important to my value as a worker are my human networks within a company."

This aligns with the historical trajectory of the Capability Approach, which emphasizes that human development is about expanding the freedom to achieve various functionings. In Smith's view, the "functioning" of the future worker is adaptability, not static expertise.

The Rise of the One-Person Army

The third pillar of Smith's vision is the explosion of small business ownership. He argues that AI provides such immense leverage that the optimal team size for many ventures will shrink to one or two people.

"AI creates leverage; it allows you to do more with a smaller team."

Smith suggests that just as Japan historically had a high prevalence of small and medium-sized enterprises, the U.S. may see a similar shift. Individuals will use AI agents to manage entire workflows, effectively becoming "small businesspeople" who strike out on their own. This creates a bifurcated future: a few lucky specialists, a large class of corporate generalists, and a surge of solo entrepreneurs.

"If you're not one of the lucky few in the latter category, your choices will be to become a cog in an ever-changing corporate machine, or to strike out on your own and manage an AI 'team' to sell some good or service directly to the consumer."

This is a sobering outlook for those who have built careers on the assumption that deep, narrow expertise is the ultimate shield against automation. It suggests that the "middle class" of the future may not be defined by specialized roles, but by the ability to pivot constantly.

"It's probably better than humans becoming economically obsolete."

Bottom Line

Smith's strongest contribution is his rejection of binary thinking; he convincingly argues that AI will not simply erase jobs but will fundamentally alter the "bundle" of tasks that constitute a role. The most vulnerable part of his argument is the assumption that the "salaryman" model of long-term, low-mobility employment will be culturally palatable to a workforce that has historically prized flexibility and portability. As AI capabilities continue to evolve, the window for this "generalist" transition may be narrower than he predicts.

Excerpt from the original article on Noahpinion

In the medium to long term, AI may replace all human jobs (or maybe not). But in the short term, AI doesn’t seem to be doing this yet. Employment rates for prime-age workers in the U.S. are hovering near all-time highs:

A recent survey of corporate CFOs found “little evidence of near-term aggregate employment declines due to AI.” A survey of European firms found no evidence of job reductions so far, despite rising productivity due to AI. Geoffrey Hinton, one of the pioneers of modern AI, famously predicted the imminent displacement of all radiologists by AI algorithms; in fact, radiologists are in greater demand than ever.

So even though AI may displace human beings en masse in the future, it’s not doing that today. But it is likely to change the nature of work. Software engineers, for whom “writing code” was a big part of the job description just a few months ago, are now mainly checkers and maintainers of code written by AIs. But this hasn’t eliminated the need for software engineers — at least, not yet. It has just shifted their job descriptions.

Humlum and Vestergaard (2026) find that so far, this pattern — workers shifting to new tasks without losing their jobs — is the norm, at least in Denmark:

[M]ost employers in [AI] exposed occupations have adopted chatbot initiatives, workers report productivity benefits, and new AI-related tasks are widespread. Yet…we estimate precise null effects on earnings and recorded hours at both the worker and workplace levels, ruling out effects larger than 2% two years after the launch of ChatGPT. What moves is the structure of work: employers absorb AI through task reorganization—including new tasks in content generation, AI oversight, and AI integration—and adopters transition into higher-paying occupations where AI chatbots are more relevant, though still too few to move average earnings. [emphasis mine]

In other words, so far, AI is replacing tasks, not jobs. Alex Imas and Soumitra Shukla have written that as long as there are a few things that only humans can do, this pattern can be expected to hold. Observers of AI consistently find that its capabilities are “jagged” — it’s much better at some tasks than others.

That’s good news for people who are worried about losing their jobs (at least in the next decade). But it’s still very troubling for people trying to decide what to study. ...
