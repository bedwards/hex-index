The exilic style

Jonah Howell · ·Jan 14, 2026 ·14 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Jonah Howell uncovers a literary weapon in Ruyan Meng's The Morgue Keeper, arguing that the novel's true power lies not in its harrowing depiction of Maoist brutality, but in its "exilic style"—a linguistic rebellion forged in the gap between two languages. While many readers expect a straightforward indictment of totalitarianism, Howell reveals how Meng uses the very texture of English to dismantle the regime's claim to absolute truth, turning etymology into a form of quiet resistance.

The Architecture of Exile

Howell frames the novel's setting not merely as a historical backdrop but as a continuation of a specific lineage of Soviet-era literary exile. He notes that Meng's protagonist, Qing Yuan, operates in a world where the Chinese regime viewed the Soviet Union as a "beacon of Communism" and a "big brother," sending officials to Moscow to learn in what was termed "qujing," or "in search of enlightenment." Yet, Howell points out the irony that the very Soviet literature Meng channels, such as Andrei Platonov's Foundation Pit, was itself an exile, published abroad as tamizdat because it could not survive in its homeland.

The author argues that Meng's own biography mirrors this trajectory. Having lived through the Cultural Revolution in a worker village before fleeing to the United States after the 1989 Tiananmen Square Massacre, Meng writes in a language she mastered as an adult. Howell writes, "The exilic nature of her English — the studious obsessiveness, the aggressive curiosity required of one who would write in a language acquired and mastered in adulthood — grants The Morgue Keeper a linguistic idiosyncrasy and richness of wordplay that allows it to transcend the polemical vitriol to which it sometimes threatens to succumb." This framing is crucial; it suggests that the novel's value isn't just in what it says about China, but in how the act of writing in a second language creates a unique, destabilizing clarity.

Weaponized Wordplay and the Norman Shadow

Howell's most distinctive contribution is his analysis of Meng's specific word choices, which he describes as "weaponized wordplay." He draws a surprising parallel between the linguistic class divisions in post-Norman Conquest England and the social stratification in Meng's China. Just as the Norman Invasion created a divide where the French-speaking elite ate "beef" and "pork" while the Germanic-speaking laborers tended to "cows" and "pigs," Meng uses the word "pail" to describe the containers for Qing Yuan's food.

Howell writes, "It's jarring at first: 'pail' is a subtly old-fashioned word in any case, almost totally supplanted now by 'bucket,' and I've not once in my life heard someone talk of putting food for a person in a pail—maybe slop for pigs, chicken feed, water for plants." He then traces the etymology of "pail" back to the Latin patella, the word for a knee-cap, suggesting that Meng implies those who eat from pails are forced to kneel. This is a bold interpretive leap, but Howell defends it by noting that Meng is "foreignizing our relationship to her prose" to expose the hidden violence in language itself.

Critics might note that such deep etymological reading risks over-interpreting an author's subconscious choices, yet Howell's evidence that Meng consistently subverts standard English usage supports his claim that this is a deliberate stylistic strategy. He argues that by denying English its "phony simplicity of one word = one meaning lexicality," Meng exposes the "tenuous chaos of ambivalences and approximations that supposedly clear, solid language tries to hide."

Truth as Policy, Not Observation

The commentary shifts to the novel's treatment of truth, where Howell argues that Meng avoids the trap of simple propaganda by refusing to explain why her characters are persecuted. He contrasts this with the works of Solzhenitsyn or Orwell, noting that in Meng's world, "truth is a matter of policy, not observation, much less justification." The protagonist, Qing Yuan, is denounced not for a specific crime, but because the state decides he is not fully mobilized in its favor.

Howell writes, "His only crime is that someone with more party cred than he — he, who has labored faithfully in the position the party assigned him for almost two decades and has not protested even slightly — has decided that he is not totally mobilized in favor of the party, so he can't be trusted." This refusal to provide a rational explanation for the persecution is, according to Howell, the novel's greatest strength. It forces the reader to confront the absurdity of a system where the "dossier itself is the truth," regardless of reality.

Whatever appears in state-sanctioned print is truth, so the novel abandons truth as a serious (or, say, as an interrogatable) subject.

The Final Coup

The piece culminates in an analysis of the novel's climax, where the protagonist's devotion to a kitten named Xi'er becomes an act of political defiance. Howell highlights a brilliant phonetic pun: in Mandarin, the word for cat is "mao," which sounds like the name of the dictator, Mao Zedong. When Qing Yuan calls out for his cat, he is shouting "mao," but Howell notes that Meng describes this not as a shout, but as a "coo."

"Devotion to his own mao, voiced as 'meow, meow,' is his resistance against Mao, is Qing Yuan's personal coo/coup, after many long years of faithful, soul-sucking service in the morgue," Howell writes. This linguistic duality transforms a moment of personal grief into a subversive act of revolution. The state claims to embody every citizen, but by focusing on the cat, Qing Yuan reclaims his own agency. Howell concludes that this is a "triumphant climax, in its oblique, sad way," proving that even in a system of total mobilization, a small denial can be a profound rebellion.

Bottom Line

Howell's analysis successfully reframes The Morgue Keeper from a historical tragedy into a linguistic triumph, demonstrating how the constraints of exile can forge a unique and powerful literary voice. While the heavy reliance on etymological decoding may feel dense to some, the core argument—that language itself can be a site of resistance against totalitarianism—is both compelling and necessary. Readers should watch for how this "exilic style" influences the next generation of writers navigating the space between cultures and regimes.

The exilic style

by Jonah Howell · · Read full article

Ruyan Meng’s second novel, The Morgue Keeper, follows the exhausted, ultraresilient Qing Yuan, a morgue attendant in Mao’s China who runs afoul of the vengeful state while investigating the mysterious death of an unidentified woman known only as “number 19.” The book is harrowing in the strict etymological sense, not in the cloudy uprooted sense with which the word is often deployed: it left me feeling like I’d been scraped over by a plough’s tines. Cut into, yes, and a bit sore, yes, but also more open, more fertile of mind. Last thing I read that left me so earnestly non-metaphorically nauseous (and happy for it) was Andrei Platonov’s Foundation Pit (in Robert Chandler, Elizabeth Chandler, and Olga Meerson’s fabulous 2009 translation for New York Review Books).

But my stomach is not the two novels’ only overlap.

The Stalinist brutality whose foundations Platonov digs out is, in Meng’s own conception, not just the precursor but the mentor of the regime that mobs her morgue keeper. Meng wrote, in an essay on Maoist “worker villages” for Red Hen Press’ Medium site, that

the Soviet Union was considered the “beacon of Communism” and the “big brother” of the Chinese. The regime would often send high officials on trips to Moscow to learn from the Soviets. The Communist party termed such a trip “qujing” (取经 “in search of enlightenment”—a phrase from the Ming Dynasty novel Journey to the West).

But Platonov’s novel was itself an exile, like so many other classics of Soviet dystopian fiction (Yevgeny Zamyatin’s We, Andrei Sinyavsky’s Pkhentz, and so on and on), in the sense that it was first published abroad and in translation as tamizdat (literally “published over there”). Meng’s Morgue Keeper, however, is a work of exile.

Having lived, like her novel’s protagonist, in a Maoist worker village through the ’60s and ’70s, Ruyan Meng fled to the U.S. after the Tiananmen Square Massacre in ’89. She set up shop(s) as a business founder then real estate investor in Dallas — Mao is not just turning, but doing wacky somersaults in his grave. She published her first novel, Only the Cat Knows (Red Hen Press, 2022), in non-translated English.
