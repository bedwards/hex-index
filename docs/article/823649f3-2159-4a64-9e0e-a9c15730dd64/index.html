We Just Lost Earth’s “Parasol” – And Warming Is About to Accelerate

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Mar 8, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through the comforting noise of incremental climate policy to deliver a startling premise: the Earth's accidental cooling mechanism has vanished, and the planet is now heating up faster than our models predicted. By synthesizing fresh satellite data with the rigorous risk frameworks of financial actuaries, he argues that we have effectively removed a global "parasol," exposing a hidden acceleration in warming that threatens to breach critical thresholds within decades, not centuries.

The Vanishing Parasol

Borlace anchors his argument in a disturbing shift in the Earth's energy imbalance (EEI)—the difference between solar energy absorbed and heat radiated back into space. He writes, "The imbalance has effectively doubled in recent decades and is now equivalent to every one of the 8.3 billion people on Earth today, constantly boiling about 60 kettles around the clock, 24/7, 365." This vivid analogy transforms abstract physics into a tangible crisis, illustrating that the planet is accumulating heat at a rate that defies previous expectations.

The core of the argument rests on the sudden disappearance of sulfur aerosols, tiny particles from burning coal and shipping that have historically reflected sunlight and masked the full force of greenhouse warming. Borlace notes that stringent regulations since 2020, which cut sulfur content in marine fuels by over 80%, have caused a visible drop in Earth's reflectivity. "We've effectively removed a global cooling mechanism or a parasol as they put it that had been masking some of the warming we humans were causing," he explains. This is a crucial insight: the very regulations that improved air quality have inadvertently stripped away a shield, revealing the true velocity of climate change. Critics might note that attributing the entire acceleration to aerosol reduction oversimplifies a complex system involving ocean heat uptake and natural variability, yet the correlation with ship track data makes the case difficult to dismiss.

We've been running a gigantic accidental geoengineering experiment for the last century without even realizing it.

Rethinking Climate Sensitivity

The implications of this lost parasol extend far beyond immediate temperature spikes; they force a re-evaluation of how sensitive the climate system is to carbon dioxide. Borlace points out that mainstream models, often calibrated to a central estimate of 3°C warming for a doubling of CO2, are failing to match observed reality. "To match what we're seeing happening today, you need the models to be tracking closer to higher ECS values, possibly around 4° Celsius or more," he argues. This aligns with historical data from the Institute and Faculty of Actuaries (IFOA), which suggests that during past warm periods, the Earth's climate sensitivity was significantly higher than current policy models assume.

This shift in perspective is vital because it challenges the "low probability" categorization of catastrophic tipping points used by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). Borlace highlights that financial risk managers, unlike policy planners, must prepare for worst-case scenarios. "The IPCC categorization of these tipping points as low probability is looking increasingly outdated," he asserts, citing early signs of destabilization in the Greenland ice sheet and the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation. The argument gains weight from the actuarial tradition of stress-testing for extreme events, a discipline that climate science has only recently begun to fully embrace.

A Planetary Solvency Recovery

Faced with this accelerated trajectory, Borlace outlines a "planetary solvency recovery plan" that demands a radical departure from business-as-usual. The proposal moves beyond simple emissions cuts to include aggressive methane reduction, halting deforestation, and, controversially, preparing emergency geoengineering tools. "They're not in any way suggesting geoengineering as a replacement for emissions cuts, but more as a sort of break glass in emergency toolkit," he clarifies. This nuanced stance acknowledges the terrifying risks of "termination shock"—where stopping a cooling intervention causes temperatures to spike instantly—while arguing that ignoring the option is irresponsible risk management.

The inclusion of geoengineering as a potential, albeit dangerous, lever is the piece's most contentious element. Borlace admits that "discussions risk bias toward the status quo, underestimating the dangers of inaction" if we refuse to analyze these tools. While this pragmatic approach resonates with the actuarial mindset of weighing "risk against risk," it opens the door to moral hazard concerns where the mere existence of a technological fix could weaken political will for decarbonization.

Bottom Line

Borlace's most compelling contribution is the reframing of climate change from a slow-moving policy challenge to an immediate systemic risk driven by the sudden loss of natural cooling. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in the uncertainty of how quickly the climate system will respond to this new energy imbalance, but the data suggests that waiting for consensus is a luxury we can no longer afford. The reader must watch for how quickly global sulfur emissions continue to fall and whether climate models can be rapidly updated to reflect this higher sensitivity.
