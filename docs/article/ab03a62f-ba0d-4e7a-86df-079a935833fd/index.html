Bits In, Bits Out

Erik Hoel · ·Mar 5, 2026 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Erik Hoel cuts through the fever dream of artificial intelligence by asking a question no one in the hype cycle wants to answer: if these tools are so smart, why does the world's writing feel dumber? While the industry chases the specter of superintelligence, Hoel offers a sobering counter-narrative grounded in the tangible reality of six years of Large Language Model deployment.

The Tool, Not the Master

Hoel begins by grounding the debate in human history, reminding us that our species is defined not by raw processing power but by our ability to craft tools. He invokes the philosopher Henri Bergson to argue that "we should say not Homo sapiens, but Homo faber," meaning "man the maker." This framing is crucial because it shifts the lens from AI as a replacement for human thought to AI as an extension of human utility. Hoel notes that unlike other animals, "tools are our evolutionary niche," and we have co-evolved with them for millions of years.

The author suggests that language and mathematics are themselves abstract tools, and that our consciousness is a "protean mental firmament" shaped by their use. He connects this to Martin Heidegger's concept of Zuhandenheit, or "readiness to hand," describing how we interact with tools that simply work. However, the current AI landscape disrupts this relationship. Hoel observes that "we live in an age of tools that can talk back to us," creating a confusion between the tool and the user.

"If LLMs were a true source of intelligence to rival humans, then discovering them should be like discovering oil."

This analogy is the piece's intellectual anchor. If AI were a new source of surplus intellect, the output of civilization should have exploded in quality, just as oil production exploded in volume. Instead, Hoel argues, we are seeing a "dearth" of good writing. He points out that despite the hype, "beyond mass-producing stilted emails and stilted social media posts and stilted essays, the impact of LLMs on writing itself has not really been to improve or accelerate good writing overall."

Critics might argue that Hoel is measuring the wrong metric, suggesting that AI's value lies in speed and accessibility rather than the "quality" of a single text. Yet, Hoel's evidence that the average book on Amazon has actually gotten worse in the post-LLM era suggests that efficiency without quality control is a net negative for the cultural ecosystem.

The Illusion of the Singularity

The article dismantles the "Singularity" narrative by examining the lack of a "Move 37" moment for writing. Hoel references the legendary 2016 match between AlphaGo and Lee Sedol, where the AI made a creative, unexpected move that stunned human experts. He asks, "Would it be 'Wow, everything is more intelligent now!' Or would it be 'Why is everyone writing like a pod person now?'"

Hoel argues that the upper bound of AI writing capability has barely moved since the early days of GPT-3. He illustrates this with a stark example: when asked to write a children's book independently, even the most advanced models produce a "blurry jpeg" of a story. He describes the output as "exactly what a smiling alien would write if it had never interacted with a child before."

"The entire life of the artist, indeed, the life of the mind in general, is defined by resistance to slop."

This distinction between "approximation" and "automation" is the article's most potent insight. Hoel notes that while older models could write passable short texts, they fail when asked to generate independent ideas. He contrasts this with classic children's literature, where The Giving Tree explores unrequited sacrifice and Where the Wild Things Are deals with the threat of being eaten. These books have depth because they are born of human struggle, not statistical probability.

Hoel writes that "LLMs are 'views from nowhere,'" meaning they lack the specific, resistant perspective that makes art resonate. He challenges the reader to consider that "if you actually interact with them, and ask them to do things they aren't directly trained on, these models remain spectacularly intellectually shallow."

The Data Behind the Hype

Hoel moves from anecdote to data, citing a study on Amazon ratings that reveals a troubling trend: in the post-LLM era, the average book got worse. While the top 1,000 books improved slightly due to "more shots on goal," the top 100 books saw no quality improvement, and the overall volume of low-quality content swelled.

"The actual effects of LLMs on publishing were that: (a) the average book got worse, (b) the top 1,000 books in each category improved somewhat, and (c) the top 100 books in each category didn't change in quality."

This data undermines the narrative of an "intelligence explosion." If AI were truly augmenting human intelligence, the best works should have transcended their previous limits. Instead, Hoel suggests we are entering a "world of immense, overwhelming, scientific and philosophical and mathematical slop." He warns that the same pattern will likely repeat in science and math, as LLMs can "write a scientific paper" in the same mediocre way they write a story.

"If LLMs are tools, then we should expect their impacts to be a mirror of us, and concern efficiency and scale, rather than quality, and depend strongly on how people use them."

Hoel concludes by positioning himself not as a pessimist, but as a realist. He acknowledges that AI helps with efficiency, research, and editing, but insists that "engineering is changing and great engineers are more important than ever." The technology amplifies the user; it does not replace the need for human judgment.

"We are not in a glut of good writing. We are in a dearth of it."

Bottom Line

Hoel's argument is a necessary corrective to the "Singularity" fever, grounded in the observable reality that AI-generated text has not elevated the quality of human culture. His strongest evidence lies in the disconnect between the hype of "superintelligence" and the "blurry jpeg" results of independent creative tasks. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on subjective judgments of "quality" in literature, though the Amazon rating data provides a compelling, if imperfect, quantitative backup. Readers should watch for whether the "slop" Hoel predicts in science and math materializes, or if human oversight will successfully curate the noise.

When I was ten years old I visited the ruins in Cornwall where King Arthur had been conceived and born, at least in legend. There at Tintagel Castle, surrounded by the ocean air and the jagged rocks, I separated from my mother and sister and made my way down to the beach. And on the beach of Tintagel, right near Merlin’s cave, I spotted it in the sand. A stone. But not just any stone—a stone ax head. It could have been nothing else. It was shaped just like an ax, being unnaturally thick at the head, which was a smoothed blunt blade, and with near right angles it tapered to a point at the back. There was a notched cleft down the middle, to tie it to a shaft. As a 10-year-old boy in a place already dreamy with legend, I pocketed it, for I felt the Neolithic ax had come to me specifically, as if a Lady of the Lake had tossed it ashore. I am looking at it on my desk now.

Later I learned that such Neolithic axes are not so rare—much like ancient Roman coins, they were mass-produced, and you can buy them on eBay cheaply due to finds like mine. This one from Tintagel beach is a perfect specimen, although the ocean likely washed away its provenance. But on that day, even amid all the other sandy stones, it stood out to me immediately. I knew it was a tool instinctively, the way a baby knows the nipple.

The philosopher Henri Bergson wrote that:

We should say not Homo sapiens, but Homo faber.

Homo faber means “man the maker.” For if anything defines humans, it is tool use. I know it is now standard, in our rush to dethrone humanity, to play up that other animals also sometimes use tools. But unlike other animals, tools are our evolutionary niche. We have been making stone tools for at least 3.3 million years. We co-evolved with tools. At first, we made them from wood and bone and stone; later we began to craft abstract tools too. Language is a tool. Math is a tool. All of our vaunted cognition is, in some sense, a tool for a more protean mental firmament, which probably is consciousness itself. Heidegger’s term for this aspect of our consciousness was Zuhandenheit: “readiness to hand.”
