Movie Monologues That Changed My Entire Worldview

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Mar 26, 2024 · 22 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden makes a provocative claim that cuts through the noise of modern film criticism: the most transformative moments in cinema are not the visual spectacles we obsess over, but the spoken words that force us to confront our own avoidance of reality. In an era where we often mistake digital consumption for living, van der Linden argues that specific movie monologues serve as necessary interruptions, stripping away our pretenses and demanding we engage with the raw, uncurated texture of human experience.

The Cost of Vicarious Living

The piece opens by revisiting the iconic park scene in Good Will Hunting, where the character Sean challenges Will's intellectual arrogance. Van der Linden notes that while the scene is timeless, it hits with a different, sharper poignancy today because "we've only moved further away from this kind of meaningful living." He contrasts Will's pre-internet existence—flawed but physically present—with the modern condition of being "isolated, having everything within reach yet still experiencing it all indirectly."

This observation lands hard because it diagnoses a specific modern malaise: the confusion of information with wisdom. Van der Linden admits his own journey, noting that it took years to realize "no matter how informative or constructive the vicariously experienced life can be, it can never fully replace the directly experienced one." The author's point is that we often fool ourselves into thinking we know the world because we have read about it or watched it, when in reality, we are merely collecting "random bits of experiential trivia" that do nothing to deepen our character.

Critics might argue that this romanticizes physical hardship or ignores the genuine value of digital connection, but van der Linden's focus remains on the balance rather than a total rejection of technology. The core of his argument is that wisdom requires the friction of real-world consequence, something a screen cannot simulate.

"It's about deepening your own inner being. It's about forming your character, getting to know yourself. It's about becoming a person."

The Power of the Spoken Word

Shifting gears, van der Linden tackles a common cinematic assumption: that film is purely a visual medium where dialogue is secondary. He cites director Denis Villeneuve, who claims to remember "movies because of a strong image," and Hungarian master Béla Tarr, who believes stories are just "covering something." Yet, van der Linden pushes back, arguing that for the monologues that truly stick with us, it is the "performing of language" that matters most.

He illustrates this with a scene from Pig, where Nicolas Cage's character delivers a piercing critique of a chef living a lie. Van der Linden writes, "Every day you'll wake up and there'll be less of you. You live your life for them and they don't even see you. You don't even see yourself." What makes this moment transformative, he suggests, is not just the text but the delivery: Cage is "not trying to get a win... he genuinely wants to make him see." This distinction is crucial; the monologue works because it offers a straight, unavoidable truth rather than a performative win.

Similarly, he analyzes the ending of Call Me by Your Name, where a father tells his heartbroken son, "Right now you may not want to feel anything... but feel something." Van der Linden interprets this as a reframing of pain not as a defect, but as a "profound quality to be cherished." The argument here is that these spoken moments provide a vocabulary for emotions we often lack the courage to name, acting as mantras that guide us through our own vulnerabilities.

The Trap of Modern Cynicism

The commentary then pivots to a fascinating comparison between Terrence Malick's To the Wonder and the more famous Fight Club. Van der Linden acknowledges that Fight Club offers a sharp diagnosis of modern alienation, quoting the line: "The middle children of history, man. No purpose or place. We have no Great War. No Great Depression. Our Great War is a spiritual war. Our Great Depression is our lives."

However, he argues that while Fight Club identifies the sickness, it offers no cure. The anarchistic solution of "Space Monkeys" is, by the movie's own admission, not the answer. In contrast, Malick's work, though often criticized for being obscure, offers a more constructive path. Van der Linden highlights a priest's speech in To the Wonder: "We wish to live inside the safety of the laws... We fear to choose. Jesus insists on Choice." He emphasizes the warning that for the man who hesitates and "does nothing," "with him he can do nothing."

"The one thing he condemns utterly is avoiding the choice. To choose is to commit yourself and to commit yourself is to run the risk... of failure, the risk of sin, the risk of betrayal."

This is the piece's most compelling turn. While Fight Club leaves the audience feeling edgy but directionless, van der Linden suggests Malick's monologue provides a "real antidote" by framing the struggle as a timeless conflict between the "way of the world" and the "way of grace." Even for a non-religious reader, the author argues, the language serves as a powerful metaphor for the necessity of commitment over the safety of hesitation.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's strongest contribution is reframing movie monologues not as plot devices, but as essential tools for self-confrontation in a distracted age. His argument is most vulnerable when it risks dismissing the valid emotional resonance of purely visual storytelling, yet his insistence on the unique power of spoken truth remains a necessary counter-narrative to our image-saturated culture. Readers should watch for how this perspective shifts their own viewing habits, moving them from passive consumption to active engagement with the words that challenge them to live more fully.
