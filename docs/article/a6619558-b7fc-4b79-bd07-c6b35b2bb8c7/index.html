The silver bulletin year in review

Nate Silver · ·Dec 31, 2025 ·20 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Nate Silver offers a rare, unvarnished autopsy of the post-election media landscape, stripping away the usual hype to reveal how political drama directly fuels—and then starves—news consumption. While most commentators celebrate the end of a cycle, Silver presents a stark economic reality: the moment the election concludes, the audience evaporates, leaving publishers to navigate a "receding tide" with their business models intact but their momentum gone.

The Economics of Political Fatigue

Silver's most striking observation is the sheer volatility of attention in a democracy. He notes that "Presidential election years are insanely stressful but also very good for business," a sentiment that underscores the transactional nature of modern political coverage. The data supports this grim efficiency: citing Google Trends, he reveals that search interest in his former outlet, FiveThirtyEight, plummeted by "72 percent to 88 percent" in the year following an election compared to the election year itself. This isn't just a dip; it's a cliff.

Yet, Silver's current venture, Silver Bulletin, managed to weather this storm with surprising resilience. He reports that while paid subscriptions dropped 27 percent, overall subscriptions actually rose 12 percent. The key, he argues, was a structural shift in the audience: "Realized subscription revenues are down less than that, 17 percent, because our election-year numbers had a high ratio of short-term monthly subscribers, whereas this year, we're more weighted toward annual subs." This distinction is crucial. It suggests that while the casual observer leaves when the drama ends, the core audience remains, valuing depth over the daily adrenaline rush.

"You can feel like you're hurtling along an exponential curve as Election Day approaches, but unless there's some sort of extended recount, interest drops off quite quickly."

Critics might argue that this reliance on cyclical traffic is a fatal flaw for any independent media outlet, but Silver's pivot to a broader scope—covering sports, AI, and policy analysis—appears to be a deliberate hedge against this volatility. He acknowledges the difficulty of this transition, admitting that "it can sometimes be hard to find a routine" when the news cycle shifts from the high-stakes intensity of a campaign to the slower burn of governance.

The Trap of the "Norm-Violating" Narrative

Silver's retrospective on the first year of the new administration reveals a tension between predictive accuracy and the chaotic reality of governance. He highlights his January post, "113 predictions for Trump's second term," as a high point, noting that the piece was "full of testable predictions, something I wish (other) Trump critics did more of." The core of his argument is that while the administration has been "even more norm-violating than I was expecting," the fundamental trajectory he predicted—a backlash and a Democratic resurgence—remains valid.

However, Silver is quick to admit where his models stumbled. In April, he wrote a post "discounting the idea that the stock market could serve as a check" on the administration's trade policies. He concedes this was a miss: "Instead, Trump sometimes did TACO out, and stocks had a great year." Here, the market's indifference to policy chaos served as a counterweight to the conventional wisdom that economic pain would force a course correction. This highlights a recurring theme in Silver's work: the danger of assuming that rational actors will always respond to incentives in predictable ways.

"The point of the post was that even at the 10th percentile of reasonable expectations for AI, it's going to be far more impactful on society than merely being the 'chatbot' that it's sometimes dismissed as."

His analysis of Artificial Intelligence further demonstrates his willingness to adjust his timeline based on empirical evidence. While he initially projected a rapid takeoff, his experience with poker-playing AI led him to conclude that "AI-based intelligence is likely to be spiky in the immediate term rather than broad or necessarily general." This nuance is vital; it moves the conversation away from sci-fi doomsday scenarios toward a more grounded assessment of current technological limitations.

The Limits of Prediction Markets

One of the most compelling sections of the review concerns the reliability of prediction markets, a topic Silver has championed for years. He uses the election of an "American pope"—a scenario that never materialized but was discussed in prediction markets—to illustrate their flaws. Despite the candidate trading at only a 1 percent chance, the market failed to capture the reality of the conclave, reflecting "feedback loops around what was obviously not particularly well-informed conventional wisdom."

This serves as a cautionary tale for readers who treat these markets as crystal balls. Silver writes, "Oftentimes, like in sorting through incomplete returns on election nights, they're uncannily accurate. But in other cases... they can reflect feedback loops." The distinction is subtle but profound: prediction markets are excellent at aggregating information when the information is available, but they can be dangerously misleading when they merely amplify popular myths.

"Defending democracy is easier when you listen to voters... a nuanced critique of Democrats' emphasis on the Kilmar Abrego Garcia deportation case, but really a broader take on how democracy requires paying attention to public opinion."

Silver's critique of the Democratic Party's strategy in this area is sharp. He argues that the party's failure to connect with voters on issues like immigration was not just a messaging error, but a fundamental misunderstanding of how democracy functions. This aligns with his broader philosophy that data must be paired with an understanding of human behavior, not just abstract models.

Bottom Line

Silver's year-in-review is a masterclass in intellectual honesty, admitting where his models failed while doubling down on the value of rigorous, testable analysis. The strongest part of his argument is the demonstration that sustainable media requires moving beyond the "endorphin rush" of election cycles to build a loyal, long-term audience. However, his biggest vulnerability remains the inherent unpredictability of the political landscape he seeks to model; as he admits, even the best predictions can be upended by a "norm-violating" reality that defies historical precedent.

"The basics look about right, including the predictable backlash to Trump and resurgence for Democrats. You can argue that Trump has been even more norm-violating than I was expecting, but the initial predictions didn't present a particularly optimistic portrayal to begin with."

For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: trust the data, but never trust the narrative. The future of political analysis lies not in predicting the next scandal, but in understanding the structural forces that drive it.

It’s a good time to submit questions for SBSQ #28 in the comments to the previous edition. I’ve got some halfway-finished thoughts about tanking in the NBA, taxes in California and a recent media controversy or two that might make their way into the SBSQ depending on what questions people ask. But otherwise it’s open season.

We’re hiring a part-time editor! The original deadline passed and we have some promising candidates. But since I haven’t had a chance to review the applications in detail yet, I’ll go ahead and extend the deadline through next week (Jan. 7). I will give you a bonus point or two1 if you applied by the original deadline, however.

We have some big plans in store for 2026. Our generic ballot polling average will begin publishing soon, then our new NCAA basketball model, COOPER. Later in the year, of course, we’ll have the midterm forecast, and I’m really, really hoping to find time for some sort of World Cup model, too. (I’m honestly less certain about the timing of NBA stuff.)

But since this newsletter is headlined “year in review”, I’m going to take that mandate fairly literally and mostly look back on 2025, highlighting some of my favorite posts along with a couple that didn’t quite work. I’ll also give you some insight into our business metrics, as I always find it interesting when other writers do this.

Riding the election wave up and gently down.

When you run a newsletter that’s (partly) about politics, it can be hard to disaggregate how your business is going from how your life is going from how it’s going in the broader world. Presidential election years are insanely stressful but also very good for business (and the more drama there is in politics, the better it is from that standpoint).

It can also make for an abrupt transition when the election is over, however. You can feel like you’re hurtling along an exponential curve as Election Day approaches, but unless there’s some sort of extended recount, interest drops off quite quickly.
