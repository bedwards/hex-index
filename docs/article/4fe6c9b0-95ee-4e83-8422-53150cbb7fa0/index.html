Can History Decline?

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Sep 22, 2023 · 31 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now tackles a question that haunts every generation: does history actually move forward, or do we just get better at justifying our atrocities? The piece stands out not for cataloging horror, but for exposing the chilling logic that turns genocide, famine, and slavery into "necessary evils" through the very tools we celebrate as progress—reason, bureaucracy, and technology. In an era obsessed with metrics of safety and wealth, this is a vital reminder that our capacity for darkness has not declined; it has merely evolved.

The Architecture of Atrocity

The author begins by dismantling the romanticized view of historical conflict, starting with the Crusades. Then & Now writes, "Urban II told a French crowd in 1095 were a foreign race a race absolutely alien to God that have invaded the land of those Christians." This framing was not merely religious rhetoric; it was a calculated dehumanization that paved the way for unimaginable violence. The text details how soldiers, driven by a promise of eternal reward, engaged in acts of such savagery that they waded through pools of blood up to their ankles. The author notes that even the Pope expressed doubts about the motives of these soldiers, yet the machinery of "Just War Theory"—rooted in Augustinian thought—continued to justify the slaughter.

What makes this section particularly striking is the comparison to other historical catastrophes where death was not the primary intent but the inevitable result of systemic collapse. The author points out that during the An Lushan Rebellion in China, the vast majority of the 10 to 30 million deaths came not from the civil war itself, but from the "resulting collapse of canals and irrigation systems that peasants relied on for food." This distinction is crucial. It forces the reader to confront the idea that "evil" is not always a mustache-twirling villain; often, it is a bureaucratic oversight or a structural failure. As Then & Now observes regarding the Holocaust, "it was often banal bureaucrats doing their job checking off lists running the trains ordering gas following orders." The argument here is that the modern world has not eliminated cruelty; it has industrialized it.

Critics might argue that equating the chaotic famines of ancient empires with the industrialized murder of the 20th century dilutes the specific moral horror of the latter. However, the author's point is not to equate the intent, but to highlight the shared human capacity to rationalize mass suffering as a "necessary evil" for a greater good.

The idea that history improves over time is a narrative wrapped in a constant triumphalist linear march across history towards a better less violent future.

The Illusion of the Civilizing Thesis

The piece then pivots to challenge the "Whiggish" interpretation of history—the belief that we are on a straight, upward trajectory from savagery to civilization. Then & Now writes, "we've evolved biologically socially culturally psychologically from Savages to the Civilized from grunting cavemen to polite ladies and gentlemen." The author identifies this as a comforting but potentially dangerous myth, citing historian Herbert Butterfield's critique that such narratives merely "praise revolutions provided they have been successful" and glorify the present.

The commentary here is sharp: the belief in linear progress blinds us to the "Frankenstein moments" where our greatest innovations become our greatest threats. The author illustrates this with the example of fire, noting that while it gave us warmth and metalwork, it also "led to violence and death and the destruction of ecosystems." This duality is central to the argument. Then & Now writes, "each Innovation from agriculture to nuclear energy contains both the seeds of Liberation and the possibility of Destruction." The text suggests that our faith in technology as a savior is misplaced when we ignore that the same logic that builds a hospital can design a gas chamber.

A counterargument worth considering is that while technology can be misused, the rate of violent death has statistically declined, as argued by thinkers like Steven Pinker. The author acknowledges this but pushes back, suggesting that the nature of the threat has changed. We may be safer from swords, but we are more vulnerable to the systemic, calculated destruction that only modern reason can engineer.

Reason as a Slave to Passion

Perhaps the most profound section of the piece is its critique of the Enlightenment's faith in pure reason. The author challenges the notion that rationality is the antidote to darkness, citing David Hume's argument that "reason could only ever be the slave of the Passions." Then & Now explains that thinking logically can tell us how to achieve a goal, but it cannot tell us what goal to pursue. "Reason might Aid Us in designing a new car but then I jump in that car and use it to run someone over," the text illustrates. This is a devastating critique of the idea that education and logic alone will save us from our baser instincts.

The author further explores how the Enlightenment's drive to mathematize the world replaced older ethical guidelines with a new, insatiable pursuit of profit. The text notes that while the Bible warned against greed, philosophers like Thomas Hobbes and Adam Smith argued that the "pursuit of power" and "private vices" were natural and even beneficial. Then & Now writes, "profit systematized this was new human desire could become superhuman." This shift transformed human appetite from a biological need into an economic engine, fueling the Industrial Revolution but also enabling the brutal exploitation of colonies.

The piece concludes by suggesting that we must stop viewing irrationality as the sole source of evil. As the author puts it, "emotion is at the core of our lives," and it can be a force for both good and evil. The danger lies not in the absence of reason, but in the absence of wisdom to guide it. The author warns that when we treat humans as "calculating machines acting on their own interests," we risk unleashing a force that no amount of rational planning can contain.

Bottom Line

Then & Now delivers a sobering verdict: history does not march toward goodness; it merely changes the tools of its destruction. The strongest part of this argument is its refusal to let modernity off the hook for the atrocities it enables, effectively dismantling the comfort of the "civilizing thesis." Its biggest vulnerability is the risk of fatalism, as the text offers little guidance on how to break the cycle of "Frankenstein moments" other than a general call for wisdom. Readers should watch for how this framework applies to current technological booms, where the line between liberation and domination is becoming increasingly thin.
