A new, old type of lawlessness as attacks Venezuela — and an opening for a better America

Chris Geidner · Law Dork ·Jan 3, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Chris Geidner delivers a chilling diagnosis of a new era of American foreign policy, arguing that the recent military abduction of a sitting head of state is not a singular anomaly but a dangerous synthesis of historical precedents rolled into one night. He forces readers to confront the uncomfortable reality that the legal justifications for this escalation are not invented from thin air, but are the logical endpoint of decades of unchecked executive branch opinions. This is not just a story about a raid; it is a warning about the structural collapse of the constraints meant to prevent the United States from acting as a global bully.

The Synthesis of Lawlessness

Geidner's most striking observation is that the operation against Venezuela combines disparate historical actions—invasion, regime change, and the trial of a foreign leader—into a single, unprecedented event. He writes, "The unique nature of Trump's invasion and decapitation of the government in Venezuela masks what makes it less exceptional: It combines many aspects of things that the United States has done in its history — but all in one night." By anchoring the event to the 1989 invasion of Panama, Geidner provides a crucial historical mirror, noting the eerie parallel in dates: "The United States apprehended Panamanian General Manuel Noriega on January 3, 1990... This time around, the United States apprehended Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro on January 3, 2026."

This framing is effective because it strips away the novelty of the event to reveal its legal rot. Geidner highlights that while the Noriega operation had a formal declaration of war and a killed U.S. Marine, the current operation lacks even those thin veneers of legitimacy. As he notes, legal scholars like Steve Vladeck argue that "the only real precedent for what happened Friday night doesn't provide any legal support for the United States' actions." The administration's claim of imminent threat rings hollow when one considers the geography of the drug trade, a point Geidner underscores by noting the administration's own contradictions regarding fentanyl sources and past pardons.

In other words, the only real precedent for what happened Friday night doesn't provide any legal support for the United States' actions.

Critics might argue that the executive branch has always operated in gray areas during crises, but Geidner suggests this is not a gray area—it is a deliberate erosion of the rule of law. The administration is not breaking new ground; it is simply removing the guardrails that previously kept such actions in check.

The Architecture of Executive Power

The piece deepens its analysis by examining the specific legal memos that serve as the blueprint for this aggression. Geidner points to a 1989 Office of Legal Counsel opinion by then-Assistant Attorney General Bill Barr, which justified FBI arrests abroad even if they violated international law. He observes that this opinion is the "life" of the current operation, allowing the administration to argue that domestic law supersedes foreign sovereignty. Geidner writes, "Yes, that Bill Barr," emphasizing the continuity of this legal philosophy across decades and administrations.

The argument extends to the justification for the bombing that accompanied the abduction. Geidner explains how the administration is using the concept of "unit self-defense" to escalate a targeted arrest into a military conflict. He cites Harvard Law professor Jack Goldsmith, who notes the absurdity of the logic: "Yes, it seems like bootstrapping, or worse, to say that the United States can arrest a foreign dictator on foreign soil in violation of foreign sovereignty and then invoke the self-defense of the arresting forces to bomb the country." This "bootstrapping" allows the executive branch to create its own casus belli the moment forces arrive on the ground.

The human cost of this legal gymnastics is stark. When the Joint Chiefs of Staff chairman Dan Caine admitted that helicopters came under fire and were met with "overwhelming force," he was describing a scenario where a law enforcement action was instantly militarized. Geidner warns that this is not a one-off event, quoting Caine's ominous prediction: "There is always a chance that we'll be tasked to do this type of mission again." The danger here is that the legal precedents being set today will normalize the use of overwhelming military force for extrajudicial arrests, turning the world into a place where American sovereignty is absolute and foreign sovereignty is irrelevant.

A Path Forward

Despite the grim legal analysis, Geidner finds a sliver of hope in the very clarity of the crisis. He argues that the blatant nature of the administration's actions creates a moral imperative for reform. He writes, "Trump's actions over this weekend... create a global, moral imperative to do so." The piece suggests that the current system, where Congress is "AWOL" and courts refuse to intervene, is a failure of the constitutional design. As Goldsmith is quoted, "This is not the system the framers had in mind, and it is a dangerous system for all the reasons the framers worried about."

The argument here is that the extreme expansion of power has finally revealed the cracks in the foundation. Geidner posits that the future presidency cannot look like the one being constructed now, built on "promiscuously generous executive branch legal opinions." The challenge, he concludes, is identifying the leadership capable of dismantling this architecture. He asks, "Who are the leaders up to that task?" implying that the legal and political machinery required to rein in the executive branch is currently dormant, waiting for a catalyst that this event may finally provide.

This is not the system the framers had in mind, and it is a dangerous system for all the reasons the framers worried about.

Bottom Line

Geidner's strongest contribution is his refusal to treat this event as an isolated incident, instead tracing a direct line from the 1989 Panama invasion and decades of Office of Legal Counsel memos to the current abduction in Venezuela. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on a political awakening that may not materialize, given the historical inertia of executive power expansion. Readers should watch for how Congress responds to the "unit self-defense" precedent, as this could become the new normal for future military interventions. The human cost of this legal framework is already being paid in the streets of Venezuela, and the next step is to ensure the legal justification does not become permanent policy.

A new, old type of lawlessness as attacks Venezuela — and an opening for a better America

by Chris Geidner · Law Dork · Read full article

Overnight, the United States entered the sovereign territory of a foreign nation; bombed several areas; invaded its president’s compound; took that man and his wife from their house; and brought them to New York, where they face longstanding drug trafficking charges.

In a news conference from Mar-a-Lago on Saturday, President Donald Trump expanded the consequences and scope of the operation even further, saying of Venezuela, “We’re gonna run it … until such time as a proper transition can take place.”

Then, the chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, Dan Caine, warned: “There is always a chance that we’ll be tasked to do this type of mission again.“

This all happened under Trump’s command, and it represents something that is both unfamiliar — and all too familiar — to American history. The unique nature of Trump’s invasion and decapitation of the government in Venezuela masks what makes it less exceptional: It combines many aspects of things that the United States has done in its history — but all in one night.

The United States has invaded foreign countries before when it faced no imminent threats. The United States has taken out foreign leaders before. The United States has even put foreign leaders on trial before. And, more familiar, the United States has definitely engaged in “nation-building” before.

The closest the United States has come to doing what happened on Saturday was exactly 36 years ago — when the United States apprehended Panamanian General Manuel Noriega on January 3, 1990 and brought him to America to face drug charges in Florida.

This time around, the United States apprehended Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro on January 3, 2026 and brought him to America to face drug charges in New York.

The complicated nature of this moment centers on two aspects — a legal discussion, which this post will describe at length, as well as a normative discussion, on which I will end.

Where we are is an extremely difficult moment, and that is reality, but it need not be where we go. In this moment, then, there is a chance to create a better America, as hard as that might be to see this weekend.
