The quantum world is certainly strange. Objects in multiple places and states at once, random transitions between states and places, weird instantaneous communication over large distances, all that good quantum weirdness. But perhaps the strangest thing about quantum mechanics is that its rules seem so different from the classical large scale world. And yet the latter comes from the former.

But how? And at what size does that happen? A related mystery is the connection between gravity and the quantum. Gravity seems more in line with the classical world.

It is crisply defined and very non-random. But gravity is the fabric of spaceime which is knit together on scales far smaller than the quantum. So at what point does spacetime itself become quantum? The question of the quantum classical transition and the related quantum gravity connection are notoriously difficult to probe directly due to the challenge of accessing the minuscule quantum world and the minuscular quantum gravity world.

But maybe the answer is not to build tidy experiments or giant colliders, but to make the quantum world big enough to meet gravity and the classical world halfway, perhaps inside a regular laboratory. The known laws of physics can be pretty clearly broken up into these two realms, those describing the large scale classical world and the tiny quantum world. Classical physics works great above about a micrometer where objects are made of many atoms. Everything larger is as we say macroscopic.
