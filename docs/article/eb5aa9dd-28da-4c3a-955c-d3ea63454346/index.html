Original article →

In a year defined by funding cliffs and bureaucratic paralysis, Ben Reinhardt offers a stark diagnosis: the traditional model of government-funded science is broken, not because the ideas are bad, but because the political incentives are misaligned. This isn't just a recap of 2025's volatility; it is a strategic blueprint for why the next generation of breakthroughs must be built outside the walls of the university and the federal grant cycle.

The Political Reality of Ambition

Reinhardt opens by dismantling the romantic notion that the state is the ideal patron for high-risk research. He argues that democratic officials face an impossible math problem when funding science that may not yield results for decades. "The officials in democratic governments need to justify spending to voters," Reinhardt writes, noting that "a lot of the work that goes into ambitious research is hard to justify until long after the fact, if ever." This observation cuts through the usual policy chatter, exposing the structural fragility of relying on public funds for moonshots. When the political cycle demands immediate, visible wins, the slow, messy work of materials science or biological discovery becomes a liability.

The piece details how this dynamic played out in 2025, with grants suspended and personnel frozen at agencies like the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA). Reinhardt suggests that the administration's hesitation wasn't merely political posturing but a rational response to a system where the "upside" for the voter is invisible. "People regularly ask the (reasonable!) question 'isn't this the sort of work the government should fund?'" he notes, only to answer that the timeline mismatch makes it impossible for the government to be the primary engine. Critics might argue that this cedes too much ground to private interests, potentially prioritizing profitable applications over public goods, but Reinhardt's evidence of midstream funding cuts suggests the current model is already failing to deliver.

"Universities need to be unbundled. Putting so many societal functions in one basket makes them all mutually vulnerable."

This call to unbundle universities is perhaps the most provocative thesis in the piece. Reinhardt points out that when the government used scientific grants as leverage for unrelated political concessions, the entire academic ecosystem wobbled. By concentrating research, education, and international student visas in a single institution, the system created a single point of failure. The argument is compelling because it reframes the university not as a fortress of knowledge, but as a fragile conglomerate that is ill-suited for the agility required in modern science.

The Missing Middle of Capital

Perhaps the most insightful section of the commentary addresses the "missing middle" of funding. Reinhardt identifies a structural gap where transformative science falls between the cracks of traditional venture capital and philanthropy. "A lot of technology work makes sense neither for venture funding nor philanthropy," he writes. Venture capital demands high returns on short timelines, while philanthropy expects no financial return at all. Yet, the work that actually changes the world—like new manufacturing paradigms or drug discovery—often sits in a gray area where it might eventually generate value but takes too long for a VC fund and is too commercial for a charity.

The author illustrates this with the rise of "AI for science" initiatives, where massive sums are being poured into drug and materials discovery. However, he warns that simply throwing money at the problem isn't enough if the institutional model doesn't fit. "This misalignment creates a structural funding gap—one that leaves potentially world-changing ideas without the catalytic support they need to move from lab to real-world impact." The analysis here is sharp: it's not a lack of ideas or capital, but a lack of vehicles to deploy that capital effectively. The solution, Reinhardt suggests, isn't to force these projects into existing boxes, but to build new ones.

The Power of Doing and Deadlines

Reinhardt shifts from structural critique to tactical advice, arguing that the best way to de-risk ambitious research is to simply start doing it. "It is so much easier to make progress on ambitious research when you are already doing things," he asserts. This counters the common hesitation to wait for perfect funding or the ideal team. He introduces the concept of "right-sizing upside-adjusted risk," suggesting that successful projects don't start with massive, all-or-nothing bets. Instead, they begin with small, tangible outputs that prove execution capability.

He also challenges the academic dogma that research should be free of constraints. Citing the success of programs with strict timelines, he argues that "deadlines are incredibly powerful" in preventing work from expanding to fill the time available. "The reality we've found is that external deadlines help even focused, ambitious researchers," Reinhardt writes. This is a counterintuitive stance in a field that often prizes open-ended exploration, but the evidence from his own organization suggests that constraints force clarity and momentum. A counterargument worth considering is that excessive pressure could stifle the very creativity needed for breakthroughs, but Reinhardt frames deadlines as a tool for focus rather than a source of stress.

"Successful efforts to do ambitious things in a new way find ways to start doing work with a relatively small amount of resources."

The piece concludes with a call to build a "physical home for misfits and ambitious researchers," moving away from the model of exclusively working with external performers. Reinhardt notes that when his organization built its own lab, it "immediately started learning far more and executing far faster." This internalization of R&D, often dismissed as inefficient, is presented as a necessary evolution for the 21st century. The argument is that in an era of volatility, having a dedicated space and team allows for a speed and depth of learning that contracting out simply cannot match.

Bottom Line

Ben Reinhardt's analysis is a necessary corrective to the assumption that more government money or more university partnerships will solve the innovation crisis. His strongest argument is that the structural incentives of the current system are fundamentally at odds with the timeline of ambitious science. The biggest vulnerability in his thesis is the reliance on private capital to fill the gap left by the state, which risks narrowing the scope of research to only what is commercially viable. However, his call to "unbundle" institutions and embrace small, tangible starts offers a pragmatic path forward for those willing to build outside the traditional boxes.
