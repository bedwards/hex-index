Tohu and Tikkun

Various · Wayfare ·Apr 7, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In an era where self-worth is increasingly outsourced to algorithms and external validation, this essay offers a startlingly ancient framework for understanding modern anxiety: the distinction between a fragile, chaotic self and a resilient, integrated one. The author, writing under the collective byline of Various for Wayfare, does not merely diagnose the pain of envy; they reframe it as a structural failure of the soul, arguing that our inability to celebrate others stems from living in a state of spiritual "Tohu" rather than "Tikkun." This is not a platitude about being nicer; it is a rigorous psychological and theological claim that rigidity masquerading as strength is the root of our insecurity.

The Architecture of Fragility

The piece opens by dissecting a universal human experience: the pang of envy when a friend succeeds. "Have you ever received that call from a friend, brimming with joy over a great date, a dream job offer... and even as you congratulate them, you feel a quiet pang of envy?" The author identifies this not as a moral failing, but as a symptom of a deeper issue where "external validation becomes a barometer of self-worth, anchoring identity to other people's perceptions." This observation lands with particular force because it bypasses the usual self-help advice to "just be happy for them" and instead exposes the mechanism of the insecurity itself.

The argument pivots on a powerful anecdote about the author's cousin, a man whose "greatness was undeniable, but his need for validation made him small." The transformation described is profound: "Years later, I saw him again—calmer, more grounded. The same gifts were there, but now they flowed effortlessly, no longer begging for applause." The author uses this shift to introduce the core thesis: "Envy and insecurity aren't just moral failures; they're signs of a shallow grounding."

Critics might argue that this framing risks spiritualizing very real socioeconomic disparities that make success feel zero-sum. However, the essay sidesteps material competition to focus entirely on the internal landscape of the observer, a distinction that keeps the argument applicable regardless of one's external circumstances.

Tohu, Tikkun, and the Breaking of Vessels

To explain the mechanics of this fragility, the author draws deeply from Kabbalistic tradition, specifically the concepts of Tohu (chaos) and Tikkun (repair). "Kabbalah describes two modes of being mirrored in the first chapter of Genesis, 'the earth was without form and void'... The Kabbalists read this not only as chaos in matter but as chaos in spirit: the world of Tohu." The author notes that in this state, "The energies of this world were brilliant, intense, and unstable. The vessels meant to hold them shattered under the strain." This historical reference to Shevirat haKeilim (the breaking of the vessels) provides a mythic depth to the psychological concept of burnout or ego collapse.

In contrast, Tikkun is described as "balance, integration, proportion." The author writes, "Kindness informed by restraint. Strength softened by compassion." This is where the essay connects the cosmic to the personal: "So Tohu and Tikkun aren't only cosmic events, they are states of the heart." The author argues that what we often admire as "strength"—a refusal to change or admit error—is actually the rigidity of Tohu. "What looks like conviction is frequently fear, the fear of what change might mean, the terror of letting the self bend."

"Rigidity under pressure breaks."

To illustrate this, the author invokes the fable of the oak and the reed, noting that the oak's refusal to bend led to its destruction, while the reed survived by adapting. "Tohu holds fast to its intensity, unwilling to bend, mistaking rigidity for strength. Tikkun adapts. It integrates." This analogy is effective because it reframes flexibility not as weakness, but as a superior form of endurance. The argument suggests that true authenticity is "not the freedom to remain unchanged, but the obligation to see who we truly are, and adjust accordingly."

The Labor of Essence

The essay moves from diagnosis to prescription, arguing that reaching this state of Tikkun requires active labor. The author points out that "Authenticity doesn't come from blind certainty; it comes from the willingness to explore until what is real takes root." This is a crucial correction to the modern obsession with "finding oneself" as a static destination. Instead, the author posits that "The test of essence is humility."

Citing Rabbi Meshulam Zusha of Hanipol (1718–1800), the author writes, "In heaven, they won't ask why I wasn't Moses; they'll ask why I wasn't Zusha." This quote serves as the essay's moral anchor, shifting the focus from comparison to individual purpose. The author expands on this, stating, "When you can truly see the beauty of the other, when you can recognize the divinity within them, that's when you know you've touched your own core."

The piece also challenges the notion that being true to oneself means rejecting obligation. "Being true to oneself does not mean abdicating responsibility... On the contrary, it requires stepping outside of ourselves and into a relationship." The author suggests that service is the ultimate refining fire: "As one gives, one forges character." This is a compelling counter-narrative to the idea of self-care as self-indulgence, proposing instead that "service refines us more precisely than any self-improvement plan ever could."

The Turn from Envy

The conclusion offers a practical method for dealing with the initial pang of envy. The author advises treating the feeling as a diagnostic signal: "When you feel envy rise at a friend's success, treat it as a signal: Am I living from Tohu or from Tikkun?" The proposed solution is a return to the body and the breath, a grounding technique that allows one to "release the thoughts and identities that are gripping you."

The ultimate goal, as described, is a state where "other people's successes don't diminish ours at all—rather, we magnify each other's successes." This is the hallmark of Tikkun: a world where the vessels are strong enough to hold the light without shattering. The author concludes by returning to the image of the cousin, now "rooted differently—grown from insecurity to inner strength," proving that the shift from chaos to repair is not only possible but transformative.

"Real authenticity is not the freedom to remain unchanged, but the obligation to see who we truly are, and adjust accordingly."

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this commentary is its refusal to treat envy as a simple moral lapse, instead rooting it in a sophisticated metaphysical framework that explains why rigidity fails. The integration of Kabbalistic concepts like Shevirat haKeilim adds a layer of historical gravity that elevates the piece above standard self-help fare. However, the argument's reliance on spiritual grounding may feel abstract to readers seeking concrete policy or structural solutions to the pressures of modern competition. Ultimately, this is a vital reminder that resilience is not about hardness, but about the capacity to bend without breaking.

Have you ever received that call from a friend, brimming with joy over a great date, a dream job offer, a prestigious speaking engagement—and even as you congratulate them, you feel a quiet pang of envy? Maybe it’s the role you longed for, the person you hoped to date. Suddenly, you wonder: Is life unfair, or am I simply unworthy? You question the poets who claim that true friendship is pure joy in another’s success; you wish you could feel that joy, but instead, your worth becomes measured against theirs.

These moments expose how easily insecurity seeps into daily life: the anxious refreshing of social media feeds, the existential sadness after a rejection, the endless weighing of small choices—what to wear, what to post, what to say—all center on self-image. External validation becomes a barometer of self-worth, anchoring identity to other people’s perceptions.

And yet, I have noticed there are parts of the self that are more whole, grounded, and almost impervious to judgment. From that center, feedback feels like when a stranger compliments your child: pleasant, but not the source of your joy and love. When we are grounded in such a way, we can receive feedback without fear or genuinely celebrate another person’s success.

I think of my cousin—more like a brother, really. He had a presence that filled every room: booming voice, sharp wit, immense talent. He could make you feel like the only person in the world or cut you down in a moment. But beneath it all, his ego was fragile. If he wasn’t asked to sing, he felt slighted. If he wasn’t invited to speak, he brooded. His greatness was undeniable, but his need for validation made him small. Years later, I saw him again—calmer, more grounded. The same gifts were there, but now they flowed effortlessly, no longer begging for applause. It was the same person, but rooted differently—grown from insecurity to inner strength.
