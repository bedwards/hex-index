In the book of Revelation chapter 13, the author describes a monstrous figure rising from the sea, the beast. It speaks blasphemies, wages war on the saints, demands worship, and rules over the nations with terrifying power. It's the ultimate end times villain. Then, Revelation hits us with a math problem.

Let the one with understanding calculate the number of the beast, for it is the number of a man. Its number is 666. In both Greek and Hebrew, letters doubled as numbers. So, for example, take the name Nero Caesar, write it the way you would have transliterated it into Hebrew letters, then add up the numerical value of each letter, and you get exactly 666.

The Roman Emperor Nero makes perfect sense as a villain in Christian apocalyptic literature. According to the Roman historian, Tacitus, he was the first Roman ruler we know of to persecute Christians, blaming them for the great fire of Rome and subjecting them to brutal executions. If you were writing an apocalypse in that world, Nero was a natural figure to associate with cosmic evil. But hold on, Revelation was probably written late in the first century, maybe around the year 90 or 95.

Nero died in 68 CE. So why is this supposed vision of the future focusing so much on a Roman emperor who'd been dead for 20 or 30 years? Unless, what if Nero wasn't really dead? Almost immediately after his death, rumors spread that Nero had escaped, fled east, and was planning a return.

Some people even claimed to have seen him alive. And over the next few decades, several imposters appeared. Real individuals pretending to be Nero. So when Revelation describes the return of an evil ruler, it might not just be a metaphor.

Revelation's focus on Nero as an end times villain could be tapping into a real political myth already circulating throughout the Roman world. one that said Nero never really died and he'll be back. Nero was the last emperor of the Julio Claudian dynasty, the same family line that gave Rome Julius Caesar and Caesar Augustus. But by the end of his reign in 68 CE, Nero had managed to burn through his support in the Senate, the army, and the Roman elite.
