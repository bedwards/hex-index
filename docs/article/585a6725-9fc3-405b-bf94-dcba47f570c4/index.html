What's next for Venezuela?

Maduro Is Gone, but Chavismo Is Not

Caspian Report's latest analysis of Venezuela opens with a deceptively simple premise: the United States got what it wanted. Maduro is out. But as host Shirvan methodically lays out, the removal of a strongman is the easy part. What follows is a three-way power struggle between Washington's ambitions, Caracas's entrenched military-political class, and a desperate Venezuelan public that has watched its country lose three-quarters of its GDP in a single decade.

The Iraq parallel is the sharpest framing in the piece, and it deserves serious attention. When the United States toppled Saddam Hussein in 2003, the assumption was that regime change would unlock a democratic transition. Instead, it produced a power vacuum that sucked in insurgents, militias, and neighboring powers for years. Caspian Report draws the line directly:

When the United States toppled Saddam in 2003, the mission quickly collapsed into insurgency, fractured authority, and years of costly involvement. Venezuela now sits at a similar inflection point.

The comparison is instructive but imperfect. Venezuela in 2026 is not Iraq in 2003. There is no sectarian fault line comparable to the Sunni-Shia divide. There is no neighboring Iran with both the motivation and the capability to sponsor proxy warfare at scale. What Venezuela does have, however, is a deeply entrenched patronage network, a military that doubles as an economic actor, and paramilitary forces that answer to no one in particular. Those ingredients are sufficient for prolonged instability without requiring an external accelerant.

The Delsi Rodriguez Trap

The analysis of Vice President Delsi Rodriguez's position is among the most compelling sections. She is, in Caspian Report's telling, caught between two forces that will destroy her if she leans too far in either direction. Comply fully with Trump's demands and the Chavista hardliners will brand her a traitor. Resist Washington and she risks the same fate as Maduro.

If she complies fully with Trump's demands, she risks being toppled as a traitor by hardliners within the ruling party. At the same time, if she goes against Trump, she could face a fate worse than that of Maduro.

This framing captures something that much Western coverage misses: the internal dynamics of Chavismo matter far more than the external pressure Washington can apply. Rodriguez can plausibly deliver on oil concessions because she already knows the contracts. But dismantling the illicit networks, expelling Colombian guerrillas, and severing ties with Russia and China would mean going to war with the very people who control Venezuela's guns. Defense Minister Vladimir Padrino runs the armed forces. Interior Minister Diosdado Cabello commands the collectivos. Neither has any reason to cooperate with a leader who threatens their survival.

The Oil Mirage

Perhaps the most underappreciated section of the analysis concerns Venezuela's oil reserves. The headline number of 300 billion barrels has been repeated so often that it has acquired the weight of established fact. Caspian Report punctures this assumption with a detail that deserves wider circulation:

Venezuela's state-owned oil and gas company, the PDVSA, tripled its reserve estimate in the late 2000s without the appropriate exploration. At the time, Hugo Chavez needed a win to bolster his international leverage and thus coerced the PDVSA into inflating the numbers.

This matters enormously for any reconstruction scenario. If the true recoverable reserves are closer to 100 billion barrels concentrated in technically demanding heavy crude fields, Venezuela's margin for error in a post-Maduro transition shrinks dramatically. Heavy crude requires specialized refining infrastructure, commands steep discounts relative to light sweet crude, and demands capital investment that sanctioned or politically unstable countries struggle to attract. The fantasy that Venezuela can simply "turn on the taps" and fund a democratic transition deserves far more skepticism than it typically receives.

A counterpoint worth raising: even at reduced estimates, Venezuela's reserves remain among the largest in the Western Hemisphere. The issue is not the absence of oil but the absence of functioning institutions capable of managing it. Norway's sovereign wealth fund demonstrates what disciplined resource management looks like. Venezuela's trajectory under Chavismo demonstrates the opposite. The resource is not the variable. Governance is.

Three Futures, One Likely Default

Caspian Report outlines three possible outcomes: a managed democratic transition, cosmetic change without structural reform, or violent escalation among competing armed factions. The analysis correctly identifies the second option as the most probable near-term outcome:

The government's coercive and illicit machinery survives the leadership change. Armed groups continue to operate. Political change becomes cosmetic and all the while socioeconomic instability persists.

This is the path of least resistance, and history favors it. Post-authoritarian transitions that preserve the security apparatus intact while swapping out the figurehead are far more common than genuine democratic openings. The Soviet Union's collapse produced a handful of democracies and a larger number of states where the old security services simply rebranded. Latin America's own history of military governments transitioning to civilian rule often followed this pattern, with the armed forces retaining veto power behind a democratic facade.

The third scenario, violent fragmentation, cannot be dismissed. The analysis notes that military officers, collectivos, and criminal networks could carve out new territories if central authority collapses. This would not look like a conventional civil war. It would resemble the pattern seen in parts of Mexico or post-Gaddafi Libya, where armed groups control economic chokepoints and the state exists in name only across significant portions of the territory.

What the Analysis Understates

One dimension that receives insufficient attention is the regional migration crisis. Venezuela has already produced the largest displacement event in Latin American history, with more than seven million people leaving the country. A botched transition could reverse the tentative stabilization of migration flows or, worse, trigger a new wave. Colombia, Brazil, and Peru have absorbed enormous numbers of Venezuelan migrants, often at significant political cost to their own governments. The stakes of getting Venezuela wrong extend far beyond its borders.

The role of China and Russia also deserves deeper examination. Both have invested billions in Venezuela through loans-for-oil arrangements. Beijing in particular holds substantial leverage through its position as a major buyer of Venezuelan crude. Any transition government will have to negotiate with these creditors, and their willingness to cooperate with a Washington-aligned successor is far from guaranteed. The assumption that the United States can simply dictate terms ignores the multipolar reality of Venezuela's economic dependencies.

Bottom Line

Caspian Report delivers a clear-eyed assessment of a situation that most coverage treats as a resolved story. Maduro's removal is not an ending but a beginning, and almost certainly the beginning of the easier chapter. The harder questions, who controls the military, who controls the oil, and whether Washington has the patience for a transition measured in years rather than news cycles, remain entirely unanswered. The most likely outcome is a reshuffled Chavismo that changes its face without changing its nature, leaving Venezuela's people exactly where they have been for the past decade: trapped between an ideology that failed them and a superpower that may lose interest before the job is done.

Transcript excerpt on CaspianReport

America's raid on Venezuela has paid off. Or so it seems. Trump is now signaling that he can force Caracus back into line, open the door to new energy deals on US terms, and curb the influence of rival powers in the hemisphere. And while the optics suggest closure, reality points to the start of a far more difficult and dangerous phase.

For two decades, Venezuela has been the single largest driver of regional instability, fueling mass migration, crossber crime, and economic distress across the continent. And so from Washington's perspective, stabilizing Venezuela is not just about oil or geopolitics. It is about tackling the sources of those problems before they reach the US border. But history warns against easy victories.

When the United States toppled Saddam in 2003, the mission quickly collapsed into insurgency, fractured authority, and years of costly involvement. Venezuela now sits at a similar inflection point. Removing Maduro may clear the path for a genuine political transition, but it could just as easily pull America into open-ended commitments. Sometimes even superpowers trip over their own shadow.

Maduro is unlikely to see daylight anytime soon, but his removal does not mean the ruin of Venezuela's government or the end of Chavismo as an ideology. Power in Karakas has never rested in a single institution. It functions as a coalition stitched together by patronage, access to oil revenues, and control over hard power. With the arbitrator of that system gone, the coalition is now at risk of splintering.

The decisive variable is the military. Some commanders will move quickly to bargain with whatever authority emerges. Others will hold out, betting that uncertainty works in their favor. Civilian power brokers such as governors, party officials, and business tycoons will move the same way, hedging rather than committing.

Trump, however, has other plans. He prefers Vice President Deli Rodriguez as interim leader on the condition that she delivers on US demands. If she fails, the White House has threatened to use its naval task force in the Caribbean to cut off oil exports. Venezuela's economic lifeline.

But it's not that black and white. Rodriguez sits in a trap of her own. If she complies fully with Trump's demands, she risks being toppled as a traitor by hardliners within the ruling party. At the same time, if she goes against Trump, she could face a fate worse than that of Maduro....
