David Perlmutter constructs a startling allegory where the existential crisis of animated side characters mirrors the precariousness of human identity in a world dictated by external narratives. This is not merely a story about cartoons; it is a sharp, unsettling examination of what happens when the "creators" abandon their creations, leaving them to navigate a reality they were never designed to survive. The piece forces a re-evaluation of agency, suggesting that the most profound struggle for existence occurs not in the spotlight, but in the shadows of someone else's script.

The Architecture of Abandonment

Perlmutter's narrator, a red-haired woman exiled from a cancelled show, articulates a profound sense of displacement that resonates far beyond the realm of fiction. "You see, when an animated television program is created, a whole small universe is developed along with it," Perlmutter writes, establishing the high stakes of this fictional ontology. The author's choice to ground this metaphysical crisis in the mundane details of a New Year's Eve party—a bottle of whiskey, a checkerboard shirt, the smell of cheap alcohol—creates a jarring but effective contrast between the absurdity of their origin and the very real pain of their rejection.

The narrative posits that these characters are not just forgotten; they are actively discarded. "When a show ends, and a universe basically gets rubbed out of existence, you have a lot of people who are out of not just a job, but a whole way of life," the narrator explains. This framing elevates the story from a simple fantasy to a critique of how institutions treat expendable populations. It is a reminder that when systems are shut down, the human (or non-human) cost is often ignored. As Perlmutter notes, "Bad enough trying to be a lead character living without a full existence, but what about if you're just a supporting character?" This question strikes at the heart of the piece: the systemic undervaluing of those who are not the primary focus of power.

"We were both really the creations of people from other worlds, and thus didn't have complete control over our destinies."

The author draws a parallel between these fictional exiles and historical movements born of desperation, subtly invoking the spirit of groups like the Irish Republican Army, where marginalized factions turned to radical action after feeling abandoned by the political mainstream. Just as the IRA emerged from a sense of political exclusion, Perlmutter's characters form the "Cartoon Republican Army," a group fighting for recognition in a world that denies their reality. The parallel is not accidental; it underscores how quickly the oppressed can become the aggressors when their very existence is threatened.

The Violence of Erasure

The story does not shy away from the darker implications of this existential limbo. The characters, realizing they have no legal standing—"no off-stage lives, property, votes and dependents"—resort to extreme measures to be heard. Perlmutter writes, "From social media campaigns, to class-action lawsuits against the big studios that had 'created' us, to acts of 'terrorism' (at least according to the government, because we've really gotten on their bad side)." This progression from legal recourse to violence highlights the failure of the established order to accommodate these displaced beings. The government's labeling of their resistance as "terrorism" serves as a critique of how power structures delegitimize dissent, regardless of the justice of the cause.

The narrative also tackles the objectification of these characters, particularly the women. The narrator describes being treated as "eye candy" and facing harassment that mirrors real-world issues of sexual violence. "I mean, just as I was coming up the staircases in the lobby to get to the elevator, there were a few drunk guys getting fresh. Like #MeToo in embryo," she observes. Perlmutter uses this moment to bridge the gap between the fantastical and the contemporary, suggesting that the dynamics of power and exploitation are universal, transcending the boundary between the "real" and the "animated."

Critics might argue that equating fictional characters with real-world victims of systemic violence risks trivializing the actual suffering of human beings. However, Perlmutter's intent seems to be the opposite: to use the absurdity of the premise to strip away the distractions of reality and expose the raw mechanics of oppression. The story asks us to consider how we treat those we deem "less real" or "less important," whether they are animated figures or marginalized communities.

"We were only 'supporting' characters because the narratives of the shows chose to define us as 'supporting characters,' Wendy reminded me. In our own minds, on our own terms, in our own lives, especially in the parts that the camera didn't see, we were leads."

This assertion of self-definition is the emotional core of the piece. It challenges the notion that identity is solely conferred by external validation. The characters' refusal to accept their assigned roles is a powerful act of resistance, echoing the resilience found in historical movements where the oppressed reclaimed their narratives against overwhelming odds.

The Uncertain Horizon

As the New Year approaches, the characters are left with a mix of hope and terror. The flare that lights up the sky is a symbol of both celebration and warning. "It's 11:59 now. Somebody probably just decided to launch the New Year early," Wendy says, her voice a mix of cynicism and resolve. The story ends not with a resolution, but with a lingering sense of the unknown. "With a strong sense of faith who we were, but with a still strong and lingering sense of fear of the unknown world and life ahead of us," Perlmutter writes, capturing the precariousness of their new existence.

The piece leaves the reader with a haunting question: If we are all, in some sense, created by forces beyond our control, how do we find meaning in the chaos? The answer, Perlmutter suggests, lies in the courage to define oneself, even when the world insists on your irrelevance. The story's strength is its ability to make the reader feel the weight of this existential burden, transforming a tale of cartoon characters into a profound meditation on the human condition.

Bottom Line

David Perlmutter's piece is a masterful blend of surrealism and social commentary, using the plight of animated side characters to expose the harsh realities of systemic abandonment and the struggle for self-definition. While the premise is fantastical, the emotional truth it conveys is undeniable, forcing readers to confront the ways in which society discards those it deems expendable. The story's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the reader's suspension of disbelief, but its payoff is a powerful reminder that identity is not granted by others—it is claimed by the self.
