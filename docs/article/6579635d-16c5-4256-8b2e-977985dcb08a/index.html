The Five Freedoms of Aviation

Sam Denby · Wendover Productions ·Nov 22, 2016 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sam Denby reveals a hidden architecture governing the skies: the invisible treaties that determine whether a plane can fly over a nation, land for fuel, or even pick up passengers in a third country. While most travelers view flight paths as purely logistical choices, Denby argues they are actually the result of "more than a century of political negotiation and a court that dictate who how and where airlines can fly." This distinction transforms a mundane commute into a lesson in global sovereignty, proving that the air above us is not a free-for-all but a carefully managed grid of rights and fees.

The Sovereignty of the Sky

Denby begins by dismantling the assumption that the sky is a public commons. He writes, "the airspace above a country is sovereign territory of that country more or less." This is a crucial correction to the popular imagination. While we often think of space as starting where the clouds end, the boundary is legally murky. Denby notes that while some argue the limit is 19 miles, others claim it is 99 miles, and the U.S. classifies astronauts at 50 miles. Yet, this ambiguity does not grant airlines free rein. Denby points out a fascinating contradiction: "there are instances in which the u.s. hasn't respected sovereignty below two miles when Space Shuttle missions return to earth." The U.S. shuttle's descent paths over Canada were so long that they technically violated Canadian airspace, yet Washington never requested permission. This highlights a hierarchy where military and state assets often supersede the commercial rules that bind airlines.

For commercial carriers, the cost of this sovereignty is tangible. Denby explains that airlines must pay "over flight fees" to use air traffic control services, a charge that applies even when a plane never touches the ground. He illustrates the global reach of this system with a striking example: "flights from Australia New Zealand Papua New Guinea and other Melanesian countries pay over flight fees to the US when flying to Northeast Asian destinations." The U.S. manages a massive swath of oceanic airspace, turning a trans-Pacific journey into a revenue stream for American authorities. This creates a complex calculus for route planning. Denby observes that "complicated route planning software takes all the variables wait weather over flight fees etc and figures out which route is most financially efficient even if it wastes time or fuel." The most direct path is rarely the most profitable one, forcing airlines to navigate a maze of economic and political constraints.

"The most efficient route between San Francisco and Frankfurt goes north through Montana... however airlines don't always use this route... so that they'll pay less in Canadian over flight fees."

Critics might argue that this system prioritizes corporate margins over environmental efficiency, as planes burn extra fuel to avoid expensive fees. However, Denby's point remains that these fees are a necessary mechanism for maintaining the global air traffic infrastructure, even if the pricing models vary wildly between nations.

The Evolution of Technical Stops

The second freedom of the air—the right to land for technical reasons like refueling—has largely faded from necessity but remains a historical curiosity. Denby writes, "modern airplanes have ranges of up to 9000 miles so there's a little need anymore for refueling stops." This technological leap has reshaped global geography. He recalls how, during the Cold War, "almost no airline had permission to overfly the USSR so airlines had to literally go the other way around the earth," forcing flights between Europe and Asia to stop in Anchorage, Alaska. These stops added hours to travel times that are now halved. Yet, the third and fourth freedoms—flying from one's home country to another, and vice versa—remain the bedrock of international travel. Denby simplifies this: "if an airline is allowed to fly somewhere they're also allowed to fly back with passengers." It is a reciprocal arrangement that ensures the global network remains two-way.

The Hidden Complexity of Connecting Flights

The most intriguing part of Denby's analysis is the distinction between the official five freedoms and the unofficial ones that drive modern hub-and-spoke models. While the fifth freedom allows an airline to carry passengers between two foreign countries on a flight originating at home, the sixth freedom is the real engine of global connectivity. Denby describes it as "the right for an airline to transport passengers from one foreign country to another via their own country." He notes that "having six freedom rights is basically a fancy way to say airlines are allowed to connect passengers." This is the business model of carriers like Emirates and Turkish Airlines, which leverage their geographic position to funnel traffic through hubs like Dubai and Istanbul. "Whole airlines operate almost exclusively using six freedom rights," Denby writes, highlighting how geography and treaty rights combine to create economic powerhouses.

The most radical exception to these rules exists within the European Union. Denby explains that the EU's "single aviation market" allows any airline registered in an EU country to fly domestic routes in any other EU nation. This enables budget carriers like Ryanair to operate flights like "Edinburgh to London" despite being an Irish airline. The system is so porous that Norwegian Airlines, though based in Norway (which is not in the EU), registered subsidiaries in Ireland and the UK to access these rights. Denby points out a fascinating loophole: "Martinique and Guadeloupe are overseas territory of France they're also part of the EU single aviation market," allowing Norwegian to fly standalone short-haul routes from the U.S. to these islands. This demonstrates how regulatory arbitrage can create markets that would otherwise be impossible.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's coverage succeeds in demystifying the invisible legal framework that makes modern aviation possible, proving that flight paths are as much a product of diplomacy as they are of aerodynamics. The strongest element of his argument is the exposure of the "sixth freedom" as the silent giant of the industry, explaining why certain hubs dominate global travel despite having small domestic populations. The piece's only vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that current geopolitical tensions will not further fragment these open skies, a risk that could render these complex treaties obsolete. For the busy traveler, the takeaway is clear: every mile flown is a negotiation, and every landing is a treaty in action.
