The Complete Philosophy of The Lord of the Rings

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Nov 18, 2022 · 31 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden makes a provocative claim that cuts through decades of pop-culture analysis: The Lord of the Rings is not a Christian allegory, but a mythological vessel designed to make us feel that the world we left behind is less real than the one we entered. While many critics fixate on the surface-level religious imagery, van der Linden argues that J.R.R. Tolkien's true genius lies in "sanctifying" pagan virtues—courage, honor, and the will to fight—by stripping them of their desire for death and recontextualizing them within a framework of moral choice. For a busy reader seeking depth without the slog, this distinction is the key that unlocks why the story still resonates with such visceral power.

The Myth of Allegory

Van der Linden immediately dismantles the most common misconception about Tolkien's work. He notes that while the story contains angels, demons, and resurrection, "this does not mean the story is a Christian allegory." In fact, the author points out that Tolkien "famously disliked allegories stating he rather prefers history be it real or fictional." This is a crucial pivot. By rejecting the idea that every character is a direct stand-in for a theological concept, van der Linden allows the narrative to breathe as a genuine history rather than a coded lesson. The argument gains strength when he quotes Tolkien's own preference for fairy tales because "they open the door on other time and if we pass through though only for a moment we stand outside our own time outside time itself."

This framing is effective because it explains the universal appeal of Middle Earth. It isn't about converting the reader to a specific dogma; it's about accessing a timeless truth. As van der Linden puts it, "maybe when written successfully such Journeys beyond our own world allow us to Glimpse at deeper meanings that are not bound by any time or place but that are Universal to all." The author's reliance on this distinction separates his analysis from superficial readings that try to map Frodo directly onto Christ. Instead, the focus shifts to the feeling of the journey, which van der Linden describes as "sheer joy for we knew that here we had touched the truth."

Sanctifying the Pagan Spirit

The commentary then moves to the cultural heart of the story: the people of Rohan. Van der Linden identifies them as the embodiment of Anglo-Saxon mythology, a world defined by a "bleakness that was at the heart of many Pagan mythologies" where doom is inevitable. He argues that Tolkien's project was not to reject these pagan roots but to elevate them. The core of the argument is that the "best qualities of the Pagan World shouldn't be merely adopted they should be appropriated and recontextualized into Christianity and in that process be Sanctified."

This is where the analysis of King Théoden becomes particularly sharp. Van der Linden observes that Théoden's initial fear is not of death, but of the shame of inaction, yet he is only restored when he chooses to ride out not for glory, but because it is right. "Theoden achieves that Victory when he shows courage not only for his own people but for those of Gondor as well," van der Linden writes, noting that "the outcome of the battle no longer matters for him. It was his choice to give himself to this greater cause that granted him his moral Victory." This reframes the battle of Helm's Deep from a military triumph to a spiritual one, a nuance often lost in film adaptations that prioritize the spectacle of the fight.

The restoration of his spirit is not yet complete, but before we continue his character arc, I think it's useful to consider that of Éowyn.

The Cage of Honor

Perhaps the most compelling section of van der Linden's piece is his analysis of Éowyn. He argues that her arc is not simply about breaking gender roles, but about breaking a specific type of spiritual cage: the belief that honor is found only in death. "While her uncle is so afraid of death that he has become shameful she is so afraid of shame that she seeks death," van der Linden writes, capturing the tragic symmetry of their fears. The author highlights how Aragorn rejects her not because she is a woman, but because the ideal she projects onto him is a "shadow on a thought that you love."

Critics might note that van der Linden's interpretation of Éowyn's ending—choosing to be a "Healer" over a warrior—risks reinforcing traditional gender roles, suggesting women find fulfillment only in domestic care. However, the author counters this by drawing a parallel to Faramir, who is also spared a pointless death for glory. The point, van der Linden suggests, transcends gender: "Tolkien was making a point that transcends gender especially considering that Faramir and Éowyn go on to a level life that is not unlike his dearest characters." The moral victory here is the realization that "there is honor outside of battle," a radical idea for a culture obsessed with martial prowess.

The Ring as the Enemy of Choice

The final and most philosophical pillar of van der Linden's commentary addresses the nature of evil itself. He argues that the One Ring is not evil because it is a weapon, but because it is an instrument of domination that destroys the very possibility of moral choice. "Achieving a moral Victory requires choice and choice requires freedom and this is exactly what the ring takes away," he asserts. This is a profound insight that elevates the conflict from a battle of good versus evil to a battle for the human will.

Van der Linden explains that the Ring's power lies in its ability to isolate. "As Peter craved explains the ring cuts herself from community and contact we are alone with the eye there's no room for two eyes no room for another in the one ring." This isolation is the antithesis of the community and connection that define the heroes' victories. The author notes that the Ring operates through "illusions of grandeur" and secrecy, promising power but delivering slavery. "Ultimate power is singular it does not desire friends or Allies it does not work towards a greater good it's an end in itself like a black hole consuming everything it touches."

This analysis holds up remarkably well against the text, offering a clear definition of why the Ring is so dangerous: it removes the agency required for heroism. A counterargument worth considering is whether this view of evil as purely "domination" overlooks the more subtle, seductive nature of power that can exist even in "good" institutions. Yet, van der Linden's focus on the Ring as a singular, consuming force remains the most compelling explanation for its corrupting influence.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's analysis succeeds by shifting the focus from what The Lord of the Rings says about Christianity to what it does to the human spirit. The strongest part of the argument is the redefinition of heroism as a moral victory achieved through choice rather than a physical victory achieved through violence. The biggest vulnerability lies in the potential oversimplification of gender dynamics in Éowyn's arc, though the author's broader point about the universality of the struggle remains intact. Readers should watch for how this framework of "sanctified virtue" applies to other modern myths that seek to reclaim ancient values without their fatal flaws.
