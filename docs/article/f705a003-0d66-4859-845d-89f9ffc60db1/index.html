Russia's Geography Problem

Sam Denby · Wendover Productions ·Mar 28, 2017 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sam Denby delivers a startling thesis: Russia's relentless expansion and modern aggression are not merely ideological quirks, but desperate, rational responses to a fundamentally broken map. While most analyses focus on the Kremlin's psychology or the chaos of the post-Soviet transition, Denby argues that the country's geography is the silent architect of its foreign policy, forcing it into a perpetual state of insecurity that no amount of oil wealth can fully fix.

The Illusion of Safety

The core of Denby's argument rests on a stark contrast between Russia and its Western counterparts. He points out that while the United States enjoys the luxury of two vast oceans and France is ringed by mountains and seas, Russia is exposed. "The majority of Russians live in the western quarter of the country," Denby notes, creating a dangerous concentration of population and power in a region with almost no natural barriers to the west. This vulnerability, he explains, turned Russia's historical expansion from a quest for glory into a survival strategy: "There was no geographic protection to keep foreigners from migrating into Russian lands. The only natural resource the fledgling nation had at their disposal was pure manpower."

This framing is compelling because it reframes centuries of conquest as a defensive corkscrew. Denby writes, "Russia's first territorial expansion was entirely a quest for power... But over time, this expanse for glory transformed into an effort to protect the core of the country." The logic holds up: by pushing borders to the Urals and the Carpathians, Moscow created a buffer zone so vast that any invading army would face impossible supply lines. As Denby puts it, "Siberia is large enough that no army could invade through it and make it to Moscow. The supply lines would have to be thousands of miles long through inhospitable conditions."

Russia's geography is flawed, and that flaw explains at least a portion of almost every political decision the country has ever made.

However, this geographic determinism has a blind spot. Critics might note that while geography sets the stage, it does not write the script; the Soviet Union's expansion into Eastern Europe was driven as much by ideological ambition as by defensive necessity. Denby acknowledges this tension, admitting that while Stalin sought a buffer, he also aimed to "spread the Socialist Revolution," suggesting that the map is a constraint, not a sole cause.

The Maritime Trap

The piece takes a sharper turn when addressing Russia's economic stagnation. Despite being the largest country on Earth, it lacks the one thing that historically built global wealth: accessible, warm-water ports. Denby observes that "none of the 18 largest economies in the world are landlocked countries," and Russia, despite its massive coastline, is functionally landlocked by ice and geopolitics. He details how even Russia's major ports are strangled by choke points controlled by rivals: "To get to the ocean from Novorossiysk, you need to pass through the Bosphorus Strait, which is controlled by Turkey, a NATO country."

This analysis is particularly potent in explaining the annexation of Crimea. Denby argues that the invasion was not just about ethnic Russian populations, but about securing a strategic imperative. "The annexation of Crimea was in reality a strategic imperative to keep the warm water port of Sevastopol," he writes, noting that a pro-Western Ukraine would have likely evicted Russia from its only reliable deep-water naval base. This connects the dots between a 19th-century geographic disadvantage and a 21st-century military conflict with chilling clarity.

Yet, the argument that geography is destiny risks oversimplifying the role of policy. While Russia's ports are problematic, its energy reserves have allowed it to bypass some maritime limitations by selling gas via pipeline. Denby admits this, stating, "Russia has managed to overcome many of its geographic challenges, partially because of two things: oil and natural gas." This reliance on energy exports, however, creates a new vulnerability: it ties Russia's economy to the whims of its neighbors, a dynamic that is currently fracturing as Europe seeks alternatives.

The Flaw in the Northern European Plain

Perhaps the most critical insight Denby offers is the identification of the "Northern European Plain" as Russia's fatal weakness. While mountains and oceans protect most borders, this flat corridor acts as a funnel for invasion. "This completely flat plane just acts as a funnel, easily bringing an army from Western Europe right up to Moscow," Denby explains. This geographic reality explains why Russia has historically been so aggressive in maintaining a buffer zone in Belarus and Ukraine.

The author's synthesis of history and geography here is masterful. He traces the line from the Grand Duchy of Moscow to the Soviet Union's buffer states, showing a consistent pattern of behavior driven by the fear of the flat plain. "With his influence over all of Eastern Europe, the USSR had both manpower and political power to keep the West far from Moscow," he writes. The argument suggests that the current conflict in Ukraine is not an anomaly, but the latest iteration of a centuries-old struggle to secure that flat corridor.

Geography does have an enormous influence on human development, but it doesn't determine it. Much of history is defined by chance, not circumstance.

Denby wisely tempers his geographic determinism in the conclusion, acknowledging that while the map is a heavy burden, it is not an absolute sentence. He notes that "reality is just the confluence of chance and circumstance," leaving room for human agency and policy choices to alter outcomes. This nuance prevents the piece from becoming a fatalistic tract and instead presents geography as a powerful, but not insurmountable, variable.

Bottom Line

Denby's strongest contribution is his ability to link the abstract concept of "geography" to concrete, modern geopolitical decisions, particularly the annexation of Crimea and the ongoing war in Ukraine. His argument is most vulnerable when it risks over-attributing complex political motivations to physical terrain, but his core thesis—that Russia's insecurity is baked into its map—remains a powerful lens for understanding its behavior. Readers should watch for how Russia attempts to navigate these geographic constraints as its traditional energy leverage wanes and its neighbors seek deeper integration with the West.
