EP18 Iranian Interconnections

Dan Carlin · Dan Carlin ·Jan 28, 2022 · 83 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dan Carlin's latest episode, "EP18 Iranian Interconnections," does more than recount history; it dismantles the monolithic narrative that defines the United States and Iran as eternal enemies. By weaving together the personal nostalgia of a host who remembers a friendly Iran with the rigorous scholarship of historian John Gazvinian, the piece argues that the last forty years of hostility are a historical anomaly, not an inevitability. For listeners trying to make sense of a fractured geopolitical landscape, the revelation that the two nations spent nearly two centuries cultivating a "mutual admiration" offers a startling, necessary perspective shift.

The Myth of Eternal Antagonism

Carlin begins by anchoring the discussion in a shared cultural memory that many younger Americans lack entirely. He notes that for his generation, "Iran was a nation friendly to the United States in fact one of our best friends." This sets the stage for Gazvinian's central thesis: the current adversarial relationship is a recent rupture in a much longer story of fascination. The author highlights how the 1970s represented a "heyday of us iran relations," where Iran was viewed as a "glamorous uh kingdom to the east" amidst a region defined by conflict and oil embargoes.

This framing is effective because it humanizes the geopolitical shift. Rather than starting with the 1979 revolution, the commentary forces the audience to confront the depth of the prior bond. Gazvinian explains that while the state-to-state relationship was positive, a "huge current of underground resentment" was building against the Shah, who was seen as too closely tied to American interests. The argument here is nuanced; it acknowledges that the backlash against the Shah inevitably reflected on the United States, but it refuses to let that singular moment erase the preceding history.

Critics might argue that focusing on the "glamorous" era of the Shah glosses over the authoritarian realities of the Pahlavi dynasty, which fueled the very resentment that led to the revolution. However, the piece wisely distinguishes between the popularity of the regime and the historical trajectory of the relationship, suggesting that the public's anger was directed at foreign interference rather than an inherent cultural incompatibility.

The "Third Force" Strategy

The most compelling historical insight comes from the 19th-century strategy where Iran sought a "third force" to counterbalance British and Russian imperial pressure. Gazvinian details how Iran, realizing it was being "pulled the strings in many ways in the background" by European empires, looked to the United States as a "hands-off and even some ways anti-imperialist kind of viewpoint." This was not a random diplomatic choice; it was a calculated move by a declining power to find a partner that did not seek to colonize.

Carlin illustrates this with a fascinating anecdote about the first treaty negotiations in the 1850s. The sticking point was not religious or ideological, but rather a disagreement over military involvement. The Iranians wanted American warships to project power in the Persian Gulf, but the Americans refused. "The iranians saying to the u.s we want you more involved in our business the u.s say no no we don't want to get involved," Carlin notes. This reversal of the modern dynamic—where the U.S. is the aggressor and Iran the resister—serves as a powerful rhetorical device to underscore how far the relationship has drifted.

"History is not about laying blame or looking for who started it it's not a courtroom uh it's about understanding kind of how we got to where we are."

This quote encapsulates the episode's intellectual generosity. By refusing to assign fault for the current stalemate, the authors create space to analyze the structural forces at play. The argument suggests that the "mutual idealization" that characterized the 19th and early 20th centuries was built on a genuine, if sometimes naive, belief in American non-interventionism.

The Paradox of Revolution

The commentary takes a sharp turn when examining the nature of Iranian revolutions. Gazvinian points out a striking continuity: the 1906 Constitutional Revolution and the 1979 Islamic Revolution were both driven by a coalition of clerics, merchants, and intellectuals united by a single grievance. They were "forces of conservatism in many ways but they also are forces of radical" change, all motivated by the fear that the government was "giving away too much to foreign governments."

This observation is crucial because it challenges the Western tendency to view the 1979 revolution as a purely religious phenomenon. Instead, the piece frames it as the latest iteration of a long-standing Iranian political tradition: a deep-seated national pride that rejects foreign domination. Whether the threat is British, Russian, or American, the public reaction remains consistent. The authors suggest that the "weird anomaly" of the last forty years is not the existence of anti-American sentiment, but rather the duration of the preceding friendship.

A counterargument worth considering is whether the "third force" strategy was ever truly viable, or if it merely delayed inevitable conflicts with great powers. While the piece celebrates the idealism of the 19th-century diplomacy, it does not fully explore why the U.S. eventually abandoned its hands-off approach in the 20th century, a shift that arguably made the later conflicts more bitter.

Bottom Line

Carlin and Gazvinian succeed in reframing a decades-long conflict as a historical blip, grounded in a rich history of mutual respect and strategic alliance. The strongest element of their coverage is the identification of a recurring theme in Iranian politics: the fierce resistance to foreign interference, regardless of the foreign power's identity. The biggest vulnerability in the argument is the potential to romanticize the pre-revolutionary era, but the authors largely avoid this by acknowledging the underlying resentments that existed even during the "glamorous" years. For the busy listener, the takeaway is clear: understanding the present requires recognizing that the past was not a straight line toward hostility, but a complex tapestry of friendship and friction that could yet be rewoven.
