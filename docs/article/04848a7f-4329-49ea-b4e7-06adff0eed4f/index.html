The danger and foolishness of foreign policy

Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, Bill Kristol · The Bulwark ·Jan 5, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

This piece delivers a stinging critique of a foreign policy defined not by strategic calculation, but by theatrical boasting and a dangerous lack of accountability. The authors argue that the administration's recent maneuvers in Venezuela and its erratic threats against allies like Denmark are not signs of strength, but symptoms of a leadership style that treats national security as a reality show. For a reader trying to cut through the noise of daily headlines, this analysis offers a crucial framework: the real danger isn't just the policy itself, but the erosion of the institutional norms that prevent chaos.

The Cost of Frantic Boasting

Bill Kristol opens with a literary hook that feels strangely apt for a modern political moment, invoking Rudyard Kipling's 1897 warning against imperial hubris. He writes, "Trump has always been drunk on fantasies of power. His entire rhetorical repertoire consists of frantic boasts and foolish words." This framing is effective because it shifts the focus from the specific policy errors to the psychological driver behind them: a vanity that demands constant validation rather than results. The authors argue that when a leader claims "we are in charge" of a complex nation like Venezuela without a plan, or demands the acquisition of Greenland from a NATO ally, the result is a loss of credibility that weakens the United States globally.

The commentary highlights a terrifying possibility: that these empty threats could escalate into real conflict simply because the administration lacks the discipline to back down. Kristol notes, "The danger that Trump's vanity and grandiosity will lead him to try to seize Greenland is real." He even cites a colleague's dark prediction that the U.S. might "shoot at or seriously threaten to shoot at Danish troops." While some might argue this is hyperbolic, the historical context of the administration's impulsive decision-making suggests that such a miscalculation is not impossible. The authors contend that this behavior creates a binary trap: either the U.S. engages in reckless military action that weakens its standing, or it retreats from its own boasts, looking weak and unreliable.

If we permit Trump to go down his chosen path—if we permit him to lead us down this path—the legacy will be one not just of policy failure but of national dishonor. That will be Trump's legacy. But it will be ours too—unless we stop it.

The piece is particularly sharp in its distinction between "hubris" and mere narcissism. The authors argue that hubris implies a grand, albeit flawed, ambition to shape the world, whereas the current administration is driven by "greed and narcissism and vanity" with no great purpose. This is a devastating critique because it suggests the administration lacks even the internal logic of traditional imperialism.

The Belly-Button Rule and the Failure of Command

Mark Hertling brings a military perspective to the chaos, focusing on the operational failure of the regime change operation in Venezuela. He introduces the "belly-button rule," a principle of leadership that demands a single point of accountability for complex missions. Hertling writes, "When a task is truly hard... a senior leader in government, business, or on the battlefield must be able to point to one person and say, 'You're in charge.' One person. One belly button." This argument resonates deeply when applied to the administration's announcement that a collective group of advisers would oversee the post-Maduro transition.

The authors contrast this diffuse responsibility with the catastrophic lack of clear command in post-invasion Iraq, where fragmented authority led to strategic incoherence. Hertling observes, "Regime change fails less often from a lack of resources than from a lack of both planning and responsibility." The commentary suggests that by refusing to name a single accountable official, the administration has guaranteed that the mission will fail when reality inevitably intrudes. This is not just a bureaucratic complaint; it is a warning that without clear lines of authority, diplomatic messaging, economic pressure, and security operations will work at cross-purposes.

Critics might note that in a fast-moving crisis, flexibility is sometimes more valuable than rigid hierarchy. However, Hertling counters that "hope, as every commander learns, is not a method or a plan." The administration's approach of "shoot-ready-aim" regarding the oil infrastructure, where executives are expected to rebuild a country without a clear contract or plan, exemplifies this dangerous lack of preparation. The authors point out that oil companies are responding with caution, noting that administration outreach feels "very much like a shoot-ready-aim exercise."

Complex operations do not fail primarily because they are hard. They fail because responsibility is blurred. If the strategic leader—in this case the president—doesn't put his finger into the belly button of the person he is placing in charge, he's not leading. He's just hoping things magically work out.

The piece also touches on the human cost of this approach, implicitly. By treating regime change as a "decapitation" strike without a plan for the aftermath, the administration risks leaving a power vacuum that could lead to prolonged violence and instability for the Venezuelan people. The dismissal of opposition leader María Corina Machado, who won a significant portion of the popular vote, further underscores a disconnect between the administration's narrative and the reality on the ground.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its synthesis of moral critique and operational reality, showing how the administration's narcissism directly translates into strategic incompetence. The biggest vulnerability is the assumption that the political coalition to "stop it" exists, given the current polarization. Readers should watch for whether the administration's vague promises regarding Venezuela's oil and governance can survive the inevitable friction of implementation, or if the "belly-button rule" will be ignored until the mission collapses.

A Foreign Policy of Failure and Dishonor.

by William Kristol

The British author and poet Rudyard Kipling was a conservative, even a reactionary. He was certainly no anti-imperialist. Yet in his 1897 poem “Recessional,” written at the height of the British empire, Kipling warns his countrymen against becoming “drunk with sight of power” and indulging in “frantic boast and foolish word.”

Needless to say, there’s no chance that Donald Trump will attend to this warning. Trump has always been drunk on fantasies of power. His entire rhetorical repertoire consists of frantic boasts and foolish words. He’s not suddenly going to heed Kipling’s advice.

He won’t, but we should. Trump is our president. He’s acting in our name. Unfortunately, we won’t be able to undo all the dangerous mistakes he’s already made. And we won’t be able to stop him from continuing to indulge in frantic boasts. But an awful lot depends on whether we can limit the damage that he seeks to bring about over the next three years, and whether we can at least hold out hope of a responsible road ahead.
