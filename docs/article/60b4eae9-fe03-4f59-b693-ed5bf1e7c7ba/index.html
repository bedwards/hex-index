After the Raid: Venezuela's Hard Chapter Begins

Multiple sources: Shirvan Neftchi CaspianReportand 1 other
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

Two analysts looking at the same event — a U.S. special forces raid that lifted Nicolás Maduro out of Caracas in under two hours — have reached strikingly different conclusions about what it means. Perun, writing from a defense-analytics perspective, treats the operation as a demonstration of overwhelming military asymmetry and the opening move of a broader geopolitical realignment. Shirvan Neftchi at Caspian Report treats it as the easy part of a problem that is about to get much harder. Read together, the two pieces map the distance between a tactical success and a strategic outcome.

The Raid as Spectacle

Perun's account is, above all, a study in capability. The author spends considerable time dismantling any notion that the raid was improvised or lucky, tracing months of force concentration in the Caribbean and the steady arrival of assets like the USS Iwo Jima and the Gerald R. Ford carrier group. As Perun puts it, "The US relationship with the government in Caracas going into 2026 was absolutely dismal and what we saw through late 2025 was the United States steadily concentrate more and more military assets in the region."

The argument hinges on a brutal asymmetry. Venezuelan air defenses, on paper formidable, were in practice a museum. "Readiness rates though are a massive question mark. A lot of those F-16s probably aren't operational," Perun writes, before noting that radar transfer records going back two decades show nothing modern enough to threaten a stealth-enabled incursion. The most provocative line in the piece reframes the entire encounter:

The qualitative gulf between the Venezuelans and the Americans would have been wider than it was between the Russians and Ukrainians back in 2022.

That comparison is meant to shock, and it does. It also quietly does the work of making the raid feel inevitable rather than risky — a framing that matters, because inevitability is the language regimes use when they want the next phase to feel equally preordained.

The Day After

This is precisely where Caspian Report picks up, and where the two analyses diverge. Shirvan is less interested in the choreography of the extraction than in the morning after. His central claim is that removing a strongman is the easy part of regime change, and that the Iraq comparison — overused in some contexts — is genuinely instructive here.

When the United States toppled Saddam in 2003, the mission quickly collapsed into insurgency, fractured authority, and years of costly involvement. Venezuela now sits at a similar inflection point.

Shirvan is careful not to push the analogy too far. Venezuela has no sectarian fault line comparable to the Sunni–Shia divide, and no neighboring state with both the means and motive to sponsor a proxy insurgency. What it does have is a deeply entrenched patronage network, a military that functions as an economic actor in its own right, and paramilitary collectivos that answer to no centralized chain of command. Those ingredients do not require an external accelerant to produce prolonged instability.

The most compelling section of the Caspian Report analysis concerns Vice President Delcy Rodríguez, whom Shirvan describes as caught in an impossible vise. She can deliver on oil concessions because she knows the contracts. She cannot deliver on dismantling the illicit networks without going to war with Defense Minister Vladimir Padrino, who controls the armed forces, and Interior Minister Diosdado Cabello, who controls the collectivos. Neither has any reason to cooperate with a successor whose first task would be to neutralize them.

Where the Two Voices Meet — and Don't

Perun's strength is in showing what American power can do in a window of hours. Caspian Report's strength is in showing what it cannot do in a window of years. Perun gestures at the second question with the introduction of the "Shadow Fleet" — the network of sanctioned tankers the United States is now systematically targeting — and frames it as an extension of the kinetic operation by economic means. But Perun stops short of asking whether economic strangulation is a transition strategy or merely a continuation of pressure by other tools.

Shirvan's analysis answers that question, and the answer is uncomfortable. The most likely outcome, in his telling, is the path of least resistance:

The government's coercive and illicit machinery survives the leadership change. Armed groups continue to operate. Political change becomes cosmetic and all the while socioeconomic instability persists.

Post-authoritarian transitions that preserve the security apparatus while swapping out the figurehead are far more common than genuine democratic openings. The Soviet collapse produced a handful of democracies and a much larger number of states where the old security services simply rebranded. Latin America's own transitions from military to civilian rule frequently followed the same pattern, with the armed forces retaining a quiet veto behind a democratic facade.

The Oil Mirage

One detail from Caspian Report deserves wider circulation, because it complicates the entire economic premise of any reconstruction scenario. The headline figure of 300 billion barrels of Venezuelan reserves has been repeated so often that it has acquired the weight of established fact. Shirvan punctures it:

Venezuela's state-owned oil and gas company, the PDVSA, tripled its reserve estimate in the late 2000s without the appropriate exploration. At the time, Hugo Chávez needed a win to bolster his international leverage and thus coerced the PDVSA into inflating the numbers.

If the true recoverable reserves are closer to 100 billion barrels, concentrated in technically demanding heavy crude fields, the margin for error in any post-Maduro transition shrinks dramatically. Heavy crude requires specialized refining, trades at steep discounts to light sweet crude, and demands the kind of long-horizon capital investment that politically unstable countries struggle to attract. The fantasy that Caracas can simply turn on the taps and fund its own democratic transition deserves far more skepticism than it typically receives.

What Both Analyses Understate

Neither piece spends enough time on the regional migration crisis. Venezuela has already produced the largest displacement event in Latin American history, with more than seven million people having left the country. A botched transition could reverse the tentative stabilization of those flows, or trigger a fresh wave that lands hardest on Colombia, Brazil, and Peru — countries that have absorbed enormous numbers of Venezuelan migrants at significant political cost to their own governments. The stakes of getting this wrong extend well beyond Venezuela's borders, and well beyond the calculations of the State Department or the Pentagon.

The role of China and Russia also deserves harder examination than either author provides. Both have invested billions in Venezuela through loans-for-oil arrangements. Beijing in particular holds substantial leverage as a major buyer of Venezuelan crude, and any successor government will inherit those obligations. The assumption that the executive branch can simply dictate terms to a transition government ignores the multipolar reality of Venezuela's economic dependencies. A creditor in Beijing with billions outstanding does not need to fire a shot to shape the outcome.

Bottom Line

Perun is right that the raid was a demonstration of capability so overwhelming that resistance was effectively impossible. Caspian Report is right that capability and outcome are not the same thing, and that the harder questions — who controls the military, who controls the oil, and whether Washington has the patience for a transition measured in years rather than news cycles — remain entirely unanswered. The most likely near-term result is a reshuffled Chavismo that changes its face without changing its nature, leaving Venezuelans roughly where they have been for a decade: trapped between an ideology that failed them and a foreign patron whose attention span may not outlast the news cycle that brought it here. The raid was the spectacle. The aftermath is the test.

And while the optics suggest closure, reality points to the start of a far more difficult and dangerous phase.

For two decades, Venezuela has been the single largest driver of regional instability, fueling mass migration, crossber crime, and economic distress across the continent. And so from Washington's perspective, stabilizing Venezuela is not just about oil or geopolitics. It is about tackling the sources of those problems before they reach the US border. But history warns against easy victories.
