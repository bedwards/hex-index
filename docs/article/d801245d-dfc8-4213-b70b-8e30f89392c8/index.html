MAGA trumpism is failing. Here are ten examples

Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Jay Kuo delivers a startling counter-narrative to the prevailing doom-scroll: the administration's most aggressive authoritarian overtures in 2025 are not just being resisted, they are collapsing under their own legal and logistical weight. While the initial blitz of executive orders and military posturing seemed unstoppable, Kuo argues that the machinery of the state has been jammed by a coalition of courts, whistleblowers, and a public that refused to be cowed. This is not a story of inevitable defeat, but a forensic accounting of how a regime's own incompetence and lawlessness became its undoing.

The Collapse of Efficiency and Due Process

Kuo begins by dismantling the myth of the "Department of Government Efficiency," a project led by Elon Musk that promised to slash waste but instead delivered chaos. He writes, "Musk's DOGE team then infiltrated and seized control of computer systems at U.S. agencies and departments. They used that access to slash grant funding for anything they didn't agree with—meaning anything that sounded 'woke' or 'diverse.'" The argument here is that the initiative was less about fiscal responsibility and more about ideological purging, a distinction that matters when evaluating the long-term health of the federal bureaucracy. The evidence Kuo marshals is damning: the program failed to reduce spending, with federal outlays actually rising despite thousands of claimed cuts.

DOGE did massive damage to our government, stole our private data, will likely kill millions of the most vulnerable people around the world, all while saving nothing for us in the process.

This framing is effective because it shifts the critique from political disagreement to operational failure. The administration's attempt to weaponize efficiency against the civil service backfired when investigations revealed that the data driving these cuts was fundamentally flawed. Critics might argue that the chaos was a necessary growing pain of a necessary overhaul, but Kuo's point stands: you cannot claim to save money if the contracts you cancel were never actually terminated in the first place. The fallout included the public sparring between Musk and the White House, leading to Musk's departure and a shattered reputation for the project.

The narrative then pivots to the human cost of the administration's immigration crackdown, specifically the case of Kilmar Abrego Garcia. Kuo notes that the government, rather than admitting a clerical error, "tried to force its lawyers to attest in federal court that Abrego Garcia was a 'terrorist'—something DOJ whistleblower Erez Reuveni refused to do." This refusal highlights a critical fracture within the executive branch: career officials are increasingly unwilling to sign off on legally dubious orders. The administration's attempt to criminalize a man they had already wrongfully deported to a torture prison in El Salvador has resulted in his release and exposed the vindictive nature of the prosecution.

The White House very much wants to make an example of Abrego Garcia, but instead it is revealing precisely how lawlessly and contemptibly it is behaving.

This section is particularly potent because it connects the specific legal battle to broader historical concerns about due process. The administration's reliance on the Alien Enemies Act to bypass standard judicial review is reminiscent of the controversial Alien and Sedition Acts of 1798, which similarly sought to silence dissent and bypass normal legal channels. However, unlike the early republic, the modern judiciary has pushed back. The Supreme Court's recent rulings, including a unanimous decision affirming migrants' right to due process, signal that the courts are not merely rubber stamps for executive overreach.

The Limits of Military Force and Censorship

Perhaps the most significant development Kuo highlights is the judicial check on the administration's desire to deploy federal troops domestically. When the executive branch attempted to federalize the National Guard to quell unrest in Los Angeles and Washington, D.C., it ran headlong into the Posse Comitatus Act, a federal law from 1878 that generally prohibits the use of the military for domestic law enforcement. Kuo explains that the Supreme Court "knocked this claim back hard," ruling that the President likely needed to conclude that regular Army forces were inadequate before calling up the Guard.

The majority of justices, to our surprise, knocked this claim back hard just eight days ago.

This is a crucial pivot point. The administration's vision of a "police state" was legally stymied not by protests alone, but by the very legal frameworks they sought to ignore. The Court's skepticism regarding the use of the military to suppress dissent serves as a powerful reminder that institutional guardrails, while strained, have not broken. The administration's threats to turn cities like Portland and Chicago into war zones have been rendered moot by this legal reality.

On the front of media censorship, Kuo argues that the administration's attempts to punish critics have backfired spectacularly. The effort to force networks to fire outspoken hosts like Jimmy Kimmel resulted in a consumer revolt, with millions canceling subscriptions. Kuo writes, "It turns out, political persecution requires institutional buy-in, and that is far from guaranteed." This observation is astute; it suggests that the media landscape, while fragmented, still possesses a degree of resilience against direct political coercion. The "Streisand Effect"—where attempts to suppress information only amplify it—proved true when a censored report on prison abuses aired in Canada and immediately circulated globally.

Political persecution requires institutional buy-in, and that is far from guaranteed.

A counterargument worth considering is that the administration's tactics, while currently failing, may still succeed in creating a chilling effect that discourages future dissent. However, Kuo's evidence of high-profile reversals and judicial pushback suggests that the initial fear of total capitulation was premature. The courts, juries, and even corporate boards are acting as backstops, refusing to fully align with the administration's political agenda.

Economic Reality and Political Consequences

Finally, Kuo addresses the economic fallout of the administration's policies, noting that the promised "lower prices" have instead resulted in higher inflation and soaring healthcare costs. The administration's tariffs have sent markets reeling, and the lapse of healthcare subsidies has hit working families hard. Kuo points to polling data showing a dramatic shift in public perception: "Democrats have re-emerged with the advantage, 47% to 43%" on the question of which party can better keep the country prosperous. This is a significant reversal from previous years and suggests that the administration's economic narrative is losing its grip on the electorate.

The administration's attempt to rig the political playing field through mid-decade redistricting has also met with fierce resistance. Kuo describes how state Democratic legislators "broke quorum and fled the state" to prevent the redrawing of maps, a tactic that bought time for counter-measures like California's Proposition 50. This grassroots political maneuvering demonstrates that the fight for democratic norms is being waged at the state level as vigorously as in the courts.

The voters have taken notice. As polling analyst G. Elliott Morris noted, Gallup has been asking adults the following question since 1985: Looking ahead for the next few years, which political party do you think will do a better job of keeping the country prosperous — the Republican Party or the Democratic Party?

This data-driven approach grounds the commentary in tangible political reality. The argument is not just that the administration is unpopular, but that their core promise of economic competence has been demonstrably broken. The failure of the "One Big Beautiful Bill" to deliver relief, and its potential to close rural hospitals, threatens to alienate the very base the administration claims to represent.

Bottom Line

Jay Kuo's analysis offers a compelling, evidence-based rebuttal to the narrative of inevitable authoritarianism, demonstrating that the administration's most extreme actions are being systematically dismantled by the rule of law and public resistance. The strongest part of this argument is its reliance on specific legal victories and operational failures rather than abstract political theory, proving that the institutions of democracy are still functional, if fragile. However, the biggest vulnerability remains the administration's willingness to ignore these defeats and continue escalating, meaning the struggle is far from over. The reader should watch closely to see if the courts can maintain this momentum against a regime that has shown little regard for precedent or process.

Brutal ICE raids began on immigrant communities. Trump’s tariffs on our major trading partners, and later all the world, sent markets reeling and manufacturers and importers scrambling. There were mass layoffs of federal workers. He leaned on the TV networks to cancel his biggest critics. He caused America to turn its back on our allies, even threatening some with invasion, while rolling out the carpet for Russia. The White House even declared birthright citizenship invalid.

That’s an incomplete list, but you get the picture.

Today, on this last day of 2025, that picture is vastly changed. Opponents have struck back at every level. Protesters filled the streets and town squares in No Kings rallies. Ordinary people became citizen journalists, documenting and stymying the Department of Homeland Security in its ethnic cleansing program. And voters turned out in special and general elections to punish the President’s party for abandoning democratic values and its own constituents.
