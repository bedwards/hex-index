The image you can't submit to journals anymore

BobbyBroccoli · BobbyBroccoli ·Jul 22, 2022 · 30 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

BobbyBroccoli uncovers a bizarre artifact of academic history: a single, cropped photograph of a Playboy centerfold that became the de facto standard for testing image processing algorithms for decades, only to be officially banned by major journals today. This isn't just a story about copyright or outdated tech; it is a forensic look at how a casual, sexist decision in 1973 calcified into a global scientific tradition that persisted long after the ethical and technical justifications evaporated.

The Accidental Standard

The narrative begins with the mundane frustration of a researcher trying to submit a paper, only to hit a wall of obscure style guides. BobbyBroccoli writes, "use of the lena image in osa journals authors are encouraged to avoid use of the lena image authors who submit manuscripts to osa journals that include the lena image will be asked to justify the scientific necessity of using the image and why no reasonable substitute can be made." This sudden, specific prohibition serves as the entry point to a deeper mystery. The author traces the image back to a 1973 meeting at the University of Southern California, where researchers needed a glossy test image with a human face. They found it by tearing the top third out of a November 1972 Playboy magazine.

The sheer casualness of the origin story is striking. BobbyBroccoli notes, "a grad student walked in with the november 1972 edition of playboy the centerfold that month was of a swedish model named lena sutterberg... they tore off the top one third of the centerfold and gave it a scan." This highlights a critical flaw in early scientific standardization: convenience often trumped ethics or long-term viability. The image was chosen because glossy surfaces offered good dynamic range and human faces are sensitive to algorithmic errors. However, the fact that the "standard" was literally a ripped-out page from a men's magazine underscores how unexamined the culture of the field was at the time.

The reuse of lena's image goes far beyond the years where digital scanning was hard to come by.

The Viral Phenomenon

Once digitized, the image didn't just stick; it exploded. BobbyBroccoli charts the trajectory, noting that usage was negligible in the 70s and 80s until a tipping point in 1991 when a journal cover featured the image. "in june 1991 the journal optical engineering went a step further and put lena and peppers on their cover this led to an explosion of uses for the lena image compared to the last peak the uses tripled going over 60." The author points out the irony that a copyright threat from Playboy in the mid-90s didn't stop the trend; instead, it seemed to fuel it, with usage peaking in 1995 with over 280 recorded instances.

This section effectively demonstrates the power of academic inertia. Even when the source material was legally dubious and ethically questionable, the momentum of tradition kept the image in circulation. Critics might note that the author's reliance on digitized archives likely undercounts the 1970s and 80s, potentially obscuring how early the problem took root. Yet, the data clearly shows a cultural shift where the image became a shared language among researchers, regardless of its origin.

The Ethical Reckoning

The commentary shifts to the inevitable confrontation with the image's problematic nature. BobbyBroccoli highlights a 1996 editorial by David Munson, then editor-in-chief of IEEE Transactions on Image Processing, who candidly admitted the field's bias. "second the lena image is a picture of an attractive woman it is not surprising that the mostly male image processing research community gravitated towards an image that they found attractive." Munson's attempt to address the issue was tepid, suggesting a compromise rather than a ban, and even inserting a bizarre, irrelevant factoid about a Woody Allen movie to lighten the mood.

The author's critique of this response is sharp and necessary. BobbyBroccoli argues, "have you considered that you mostly got feedback from men because you work in a field predominantly filled with men and that the few women who do work in the field with you are only still there because they've learned not to rock the boat on issues like this." This reframes the debate from "political correctness" to a structural issue of who gets to speak up in the lab. The image wasn't just a test case; it was a barometer for the culture of the field, one that made female researchers uncomfortable and signaled that their presence was secondary to the comfort of the majority.

Demonstrating that something works on lena isn't really demonstrating that the technology works.

The Technical Obsolescence

Beyond the ethics, BobbyBroccoli makes a compelling case that the image is technically useless for modern science. The resolution is abysmal by today's standards, and the scanning process introduced artifacts that don't exist in real-world photography. "lennox contains 250 000 pixels some 32 times smaller than a picture snapped with a iphone 6." The author explains that relying on this single, low-resolution image creates a false sense of security; an algorithm might work perfectly on Lena but fail miserably on a modern, high-resolution photograph.

This argument is the strongest part of the piece because it removes the moral ambiguity and focuses on pure utility. If the goal is to build robust AI, training it on a 50-year-old, cropped, printed photograph is akin to teaching a driver only on a model car track. The author concludes that the image's persistence is a "blatant example of tradition for the sake of tradition," a habit that no longer serves the science it was meant to support.

Bottom Line

BobbyBroccoli's coverage succeeds by peeling back the layers of a seemingly trivial academic rule to reveal a history of sexism, inertia, and technological stagnation. The strongest argument is the dual realization that the image is both ethically toxic and scientifically obsolete, making its continued use indefensible on any front. The biggest vulnerability in the historical record remains the difficulty of quantifying the full extent of the image's use before digital archives existed, but the trajectory is undeniable: the era of Lena is finally, and rightly, over.
