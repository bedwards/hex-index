A16z: The power brokers

Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Packy McCormick doesn't just analyze a venture capital firm; he dissects a cultural phenomenon that has redefined how Silicon Valley operates, arguing that Andreessen Horowitz (a16z) isn't merely investing in companies but actively engineering the future through sheer scale and narrative power. While critics have long dismissed the firm's massive fund sizes as mathematically doomed, McCormick presents a compelling, data-driven rebuttal: the firm has consistently turned skepticism into outsized returns by betting on architectural shifts rather than incremental improvements. This deep dive is essential listening because it moves beyond the noise of recent political controversies to reveal the mechanical engine driving one of the most influential financial institutions of our time.

The Elephant Hunt

McCormick opens by confronting the most persistent criticism of a16z: that their funds have become too large to generate meaningful returns. He illustrates this with a vivid anecdote from 2015, when a rival venture capitalist questioned the math of a16z's massive ambitions. "When I started to check the math with Andreessen," McCormick writes, "He made a jerking-off motion and said 'Blah-blah-blah. We have all the models—we're elephant hunting, going after big game!'" This dismissal of conventional wisdom is not just arrogance; it is a calculated strategy that McCormick argues has been vindicated by history.

The evidence is staggering. In a year where the broader venture market struggled, a16z raised $15 billion, accounting for over 18% of all US venture capital raised. "In a year in which it took the average VC fund 16 months to close their fund, a16z took just over three months from start to finish," McCormick notes. The firm's ability to deploy capital at this scale is backed by a portfolio that includes ten of the top fifteen private companies by valuation, including giants like OpenAI, SpaceX, and Stripe. Critics might argue that concentrating so much capital in a few bets increases systemic risk, but McCormick points out that a16z's track record suggests they are simply identifying the only companies capable of moving the needle at this magnitude.

"It doesn't feel like venture capital is supposed to, in part, I think, because I don't think a16z cares if it's doing 'venture capital.' It just wants to BUILD the future and eat the world."

This framing is crucial. McCormick suggests that a16z has transcended traditional venture capital to become a platform for nation-building and industrial transformation. The firm's approach is not about finding the next great startup; it is about backing the infrastructure that will define the next century.

The Platform Advantage

The core of McCormick's argument rests on a16z's unique operating model, which he describes as a "platform" designed to support founders at scale. He traces this back to the firm's origins, noting that when they raised their first fund in 2009, "We visited a lot of our VC friends and many of them said it was a really dumbass idea and we should definitely not pursue it and it's been tried before and it didn't work," Ben recalls. Yet, today, nearly every significant VC firm has adopted some version of this platform team.

McCormick highlights how this model allowed a16z to navigate skepticism and deliver results. When the firm invested in Skype during a period of intense legal uncertainty, "everyone said it was an undoable deal due to IP risk," yet Microsoft later acquired it for $8.5 billion. Similarly, when they made large late-stage investments in Facebook, Groupon, and Twitter, rivals dismissed the move as outside the bounds of venture capital. "There's also money to be made in pork bellies and oil futures, but that's not what we do," quipped a rival partner at the time. History, McCormick argues, has proven that a16z's willingness to break the rules was the key to their success.

The firm's ability to identify and back category winners is perhaps best exemplified by their approach to big data. While competitors poured money into Hadoop, a16z's co-founder Ben Horowitz recognized its architectural limitations. "Ben didn't think Hadoop was going to be the winning architecture," McCormick explains, noting that the technology was "painfully slow for iterative workloads like machine learning." This contrarian view led them to bet heavily on Databricks, a company built on the Spark framework.

The Databricks Case Study

McCormick uses Databricks as the definitive case study for the "a16z formula." The firm didn't just invest; they doubled down. "Once a16z believes it has identified the category winner, the classic a16z move is to give it more money than it thought it needed," McCormick writes. This strategy, often mocked by critics, has resulted in Databricks becoming a16z's largest position, representing 23% of the firm's net asset value across all funds.

The story of Databricks is one of academic innovation meeting commercial scale. The company was founded by researchers from UC Berkeley who had developed Spark, an open-source engine for big data processing. "The idea was to 'replicate what the big tech companies did with neural networks without the complex interface,'" McCormick notes. Despite the technical brilliance, the founders initially struggled to find traction. "True to academic form, they released the code for free, and barely anyone used it," he writes. It was only after a16z stepped in with significant capital and strategic support that the company began to scale.

This section underscores the firm's engineering-first mindset. "a16z is founded and run by engineers. Not just founders, engineer-founders," McCormick emphasizes. This background influences how they design the firm and how they select markets. They are not looking for the next trendy app; they are looking for the next architectural shift that will reshape the global economy.

The Returns Reality Check

Despite the noise and the skepticism, the numbers speak for themselves. McCormick points out that a16z's third fund, which was criticized in 2016 for underperforming, is now sitting at an 11.3x net return. "AH III includes Coinbase, which resulted in distributions of $7 billion gross to a16z LPs across the funds it's in, Databricks, Pinterest, GitHub, and Lyft," he writes. The firm has already distributed $7 billion net to limited partners, with nearly as much in unrealized value still on the table.

The article also addresses the criticism that a16z's political activism and loud media presence might alienate investors or harm returns. McCormick acknowledges the noise but argues that the firm's internal culture is consistent with its public persona. "The things that a16z says about itself to the public are the same things it trains its team on internally," he states. This alignment, he suggests, is a source of strength, not weakness.

"It will take a decade to know whether they made the right decision, and nothing either I or any hypothetical critic could say will change that outcome, just as it hasn't in the past."

This is a sobering reminder that the true test of a venture capital firm is not short-term market sentiment but long-term value creation. McCormick's analysis suggests that a16z is playing a different game than its peers, one where the stakes are higher but the potential rewards are exponentially greater.

Bottom Line

Packy McCormick's deep dive successfully reframes the narrative around a16z, shifting the focus from political controversy to the mechanical realities of scale, engineering, and long-term value creation. The strongest part of the argument is the data-driven demonstration that the firm's "elephant hunting" strategy has consistently delivered outsized returns, even in the face of intense skepticism. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on past performance to predict future success; the sheer size of a16z's current funds may eventually make it impossible to find enough "elephants" to justify the investment. Readers should watch for how the firm navigates the next decade of market consolidation and whether their platform model can continue to scale without diluting returns.
