In a media landscape increasingly defined by algorithmic churn and disposable content, Kevin Alexander offers a rare, grounded meditation on the elasticity of time and the deliberate construction of human connection. Rather than chasing the next viral trend, Alexander anchors his 2026 outlook in the quiet, accumulating weight of personal history and the tangible value of community, arguing that the antidote to digital exhaustion is not more speed, but deeper focus.

The Squishiness of Time

Alexander opens with a personal narrative that transcends simple nostalgia, using the countdowns of retirement to illustrate a profound psychological shift. He writes, "The strangest thing about being middle-aged is how squishy time has become. I used to roll my eyes when people said it only gets faster. Turns out they were right." This observation resonates because it reframes the modern experience of time not as a metric of productivity, but as a subjective distortion that accelerates as we age. It is a reminder that while the calendar remains rigid, our perception of it is fluid, a concept that mirrors the way the band The Church explored the disorienting nature of memory in their 1988 album The Seance, where time seemed to stretch and warp under the weight of experience.

He contrasts the abstract nature of future goals with the visceral reality of the past, noting, "Eighteen months? Twenty-four? Those will be here next weekend—or feel like it anyway. Eighteen months ago, on the other hand, might as well have been 1992 in my mind's eye." By juxtaposing the rapid approach of his own retirement with the distant feel of recent years, Alexander highlights the dissonance between chronological time and lived experience. This framing is effective because it validates the reader's own feeling of time slipping away, turning a common complaint into a shared, almost poetic realization.

"2025 felt like it would take forever, and as hard as it fought to prove us right, it was gone before we knew it."

Reclaiming the Attention Economy

Moving from the personal to the structural, Alexander tackles the erosion of trust in media caused by the attention economy. He aligns with Substack executive Hamish Mackenzie's view that "the traditional media system once thrived on direct relationships, with big brands building trust with audiences over decades." Alexander argues that the social media era fractured these bonds, allowing tech companies to monopolize attention and ad dollars. This analysis is crucial because it identifies the root cause of the current media malaise: the shift from relationship-building to engagement-hacking.

He critiques the current trajectory of digital platforms, quoting Cal Newport to describe how content is "devolving toward that paragon of low-quality forgettability, commonly referred to as slop." Alexander's commitment to resisting this trend is clear when he states, "There will be some short videos here and there, but no one's 'pivoting to video.' You'll never think you accidentally landed on TikTok." This is a bold stance in an era where video dominance is often seen as inevitable. Critics might note that avoiding short-form video entirely could limit reach in a market saturated with visual content, but Alexander's argument is that quality and intentionality are more valuable than algorithmic reach. He positions his project not as a competitor to the attention economy, but as an "antidote to the cocaine-like pull" of distraction.

The Architecture of Community

The core of Alexander's 2026 plan is the intentional cultivation of community, which he describes as the project's "superpower." He rejects the notion of "toxic positivity," aiming instead for an "oasis of fun in a digital landscape that often isn't." This distinction is vital; it suggests a community that is authentic and resilient rather than merely cheerful. He emphasizes the value of conversation, noting that "Good conversations have lots of doorknobs," a metaphor that suggests depth and the possibility of entering new ideas.

Alexander also outlines a tangible commitment to "Artistic Tithing," where revenue is reinvested into the musician community, from filling gas tanks for touring artists to funding crowdfunding campaigns. He writes, "The only real barometer is that it helps make the world a little brighter." This approach moves beyond abstract support to concrete action, creating a feedback loop where the community supports the art that supports the community. He acknowledges that this model requires effort, stating, "Talking about building meaningful connections is one thing. Putting it into practice is another."

"Who will listen to a podcast or read a long essay, many now fret, when AI can generate endless novelty videos and platforms can serve a nonstop stream of distraction? Who indeed? I will. And I hope you will too."

Bottom Line

Kevin Alexander's piece succeeds by grounding high-level media criticism in the intimate, relatable experience of aging and time perception, making the case for slow, intentional media feel both urgent and human. The strongest element is his refusal to accept the inevitability of digital slop, offering a concrete alternative rooted in direct relationships and artistic support. The biggest vulnerability lies in the scalability of this model; while deeply effective for a niche community, the challenge remains whether such a human-centric approach can thrive without the massive reach of the very platforms it critiques. Readers should watch to see if this philosophy can sustain itself as the project evolves, proving that depth is still a viable currency in the attention economy.

For the record- 03. January. 2026

by Kevin Alexander · · Read full article

The first time I transferred to Madison was in July of ’99. As the secretary gave me the dime tour, I met a bunch of guys I’d never see again, whose pending retirements made this move possible in the first place. As I extended a hand to one guy, he warned me not to get to know him too much. He was a nice guy, he said, but he wouldn’t be there much longer. He had retirement in sight and even kept a handwritten countdown taped inside his locker.

That countdown became a running inside joke between us, with me betting him a gift card that I’d retire first. Ten years later, he and his wife enjoyed a trip to Texas Roadhouse on my dime. Life’s funny that way.

I’m now in the spot he was in (sorta). If all goes to plan, I have a little over two years left—689 days as of this writing. That’s aspirational more than anything, and just as hard to fathom as the idea that that July day was almost 27 years ago. This date also lines up with a few other life events: a milestone service anniversary (God, I’m old) and my youngest graduating (how did we get here?!). His countdown is 523 days, or roughly a year and a half. It might as well be 2057, as abstract as it seems. It all feels right around the corner and a lifetime away. Both are true.

My calendar tells me that a year and a half ago, I was lucky enough to finally see The Church and Afghan Whigs play. My mind tells me it’s been a decade. Around that same time, that same kid was playing in one of his first rugby tournaments.

The strangest thing about being middle-aged is how squishy time has become. I used to roll my eyes when people said it only gets faster. Turns out they were right. Why that guy told me he’d be gone soon makes sense now, too. Eighteen months? Twenty-four? Those will be here next weekend—or feel like it anyway. Eighteen months ago, on the other hand, might as well have been 1992 in my mind’s eye.

My point here is this: 2025 felt like it would take forever, and as hard as it fought to prove us right, it was gone before we knew it.

I have no idea what will happen ...
