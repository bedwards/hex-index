Making sense of the information ecosystem

Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Roger Pielke Jr. reframes the modern crisis of misinformation not as a failure of facts, but as an inevitable feature of how democracy functions. In a landscape where experts are often as confused as the public, he argues that the real challenge isn't finding a single truth, but managing the symbols that drive collective action.

The Architecture of Belief

The piece kicks off with a provocative admission: "In 2026, it is a question that all of us struggle with — Even experts are ignorant about most things." Pielke Jr. suggests that the search for a simple formula to distinguish truth from falsehood is a dead end. Instead, he directs us to the structural mechanics of the information ecosystem, utilizing a framework introduced by political scientist Harold Lasswell in 1948. This historical anchor grounds the argument, reminding us that the questions of "Who gets to speak credibly" and "What messages survive amplification" are as old as modern mass communication itself.

The author's central thesis is that facts are merely the starting point for negotiation, not the conclusion. "Truths are continually negotiated through the institutions of democracy," Pielke Jr. writes, citing contemporary scholar Dan Sarewitz. This is a crucial distinction. It implies that policy outcomes—whether on vaccines or energy—are not determined solely by scientific consensus but by the complex interplay of judges, voters, and advocates. This lands effectively because it removes the frustration of trying to "win" arguments with data alone, shifting the focus to the political machinery that converts data into action.

The real external environment is too big, too complex and too fleeting for direct acquaintance by citizens.

Symbols Over Facts

Pielke Jr. leans heavily on Walter Lippmann's 1922 classic, Public Opinion, to explain why we rely on mental shortcuts. Lippmann noted that the world is too vast to understand directly, so we live in "the pictures in our heads." Pielke Jr. expands this to argue that political communication is fundamentally about "significant symbols" rather than referential data. He distinguishes between simple signs, like a stop sign, and condensational symbols, which carry deep emotional weight.

"The manipulation of significant symbols in an effort to shape collective attitudes is propaganda," he asserts. This is perhaps the most controversial yet necessary part of his argument. By stripping the word "propaganda" of its purely negative connotation, he redefines it as a functional tool for democracy. He argues that propaganda is "fundamental to securing collective agreement in support of shared action." This reframing is bold; it suggests that the emotional mobilization of the public is not a bug in the system, but a feature required to get things done.

Critics might note that equating democratic persuasion with propaganda risks normalizing manipulation, potentially blurring the line between legitimate advocacy and deceitful coercion. However, Pielke Jr. anticipates this, arguing that the only way to counter bad propaganda is with "better propaganda," defined as communication that is more effective at shaping attitudes toward shared interests.

The Mechanics of Collective Attitude

The article delves into the specific functions of propaganda as defined by Lasswell: mobilizing hatred against enemies, preserving alliances, and demoralizing opposition. Pielke Jr. uses this framework to analyze political communication without focusing on the personality of any single leader. He notes that successful leaders have always understood that "no successful leader has ever been too busy to cultivate the symbols which organize his following."

He highlights the danger inherent in this system, quoting Lippmann: "The symbol is both a mechanism of solidarity, and a mechanism of exploitation." This duality is the core tension of the piece. Symbols allow people to work for a common end, but they also allow a few strategically placed individuals to "fatten on many, deflect criticism, and seduce men into facing agony for objects they do not understand." This is a sobering reminder that the very tools that enable democracy to function can also be used to hijack it.

Democracy means people acting together in pursuit of shared interests; People act together based on collective attitudes; Collective attitudes are typically created and shaped not through direct experience, but through communication.

Bottom Line

Pielke Jr.'s strongest contribution is his pragmatic rejection of the idea that we can simply "fact-check" our way out of political polarization; he correctly identifies that collective action relies on emotional alignment, not just intellectual agreement. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its potential to understate the moral hazards of normalizing propaganda as a democratic necessity. Readers should watch for how the series applies this framework to specific policy debates, particularly where the line between "shared interests" and "manipulated sentiment" becomes dangerously thin.

Making sense of the information ecosystem

by Roger Pielke Jr. · The Honest Broker · Read full article

One of the most common questions I get is, “How do I know what is true and what is not?”. In 2026, it is a question that all of us struggle with — Even experts are ignorant about most things.

I respond to this question by saying that I don’t have a simple answer or formula, but that there are better and worse ways to arrive at beliefs more likely to be accurate. In my courses, I explain to students that a very good starting point is to understand the roles of information in democratic politics, and in particular, why “facts” are inevitably contested and how “truths” are emergent and manufactured.

Today’s post kicks off a new THB series that I expect to run well into 2026 — Making Sense of the Information Ecosystem — as I begin pulling together a sequel to The Honest Broker: Making Sense of Science in Policy and Politics, my 2007 book that gives THB its name.1

I start the series by introducing some concepts that will help us to together make sense of the information ecosystem which we can characterize using a well-known framework introduced by political scientist Harold Lasswell in 1948:2

Who gets to speak credibly;

What messages survive amplification;

Which channels dominate attention;

To whom the messages are targeted;

What effects result.

More generally, an information ecosystem is the interconnected system of actors, institutions, technologies, norms, and incentives through which information is produced, selected, transmitted, amplified, interpreted, and acted upon within society.

As Walter Lippmann observed in his classic book, Public Opinion, in 1922, the goal of democracy is not to get everyone to think alike, it is to get people who think differently to act alike.

In his 1992 introduction to Lippmann’s Public Opinion, Michael Curtis explained the significance of Lippmann’s distinction between “the world outside” and the “pictures in our heads”:3

The real external environment is too big, too complex and too fleeting for direct acquaintance by citizens. The public can never fully understand political reality, “the buzzing, blooming confusion” of the world, partly because individuals could only devote a short amount of time to public affairs and partly because events have to be compressed into very short messages. In a letter dated May 18, 1922, Lippmann wrote that the bulk of public questions “deal with matters that are out of sight, and have therefore to be imagined.”
