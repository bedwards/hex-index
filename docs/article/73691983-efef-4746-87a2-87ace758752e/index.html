AI Literacy - Lecture 6.1: Using LLMs Effectively

Commentary by Brian Edwards

Kenny Easwaran cuts through the noise of constant AI updates to deliver a counter-intuitive truth: the most powerful tool in your conversation with a large language model isn't a complex prompt, but your own ability to forget. In a landscape obsessed with technical parameters, Easwaran argues that human cognitive limitations are actually the bottleneck, not the software.

The Human Bottleneck

Easwaran begins by dismantling the assumption that the user should simply react to the machine's output. He posits a hierarchy of control that places the human at the absolute center. "In any conversation with a large language model you are the most important conversation you are the one whose interests matter," he writes. This is a crucial reframe. Most guides treat the user as a passive recipient of the model's capabilities, but Easwaran insists that the user's strategy must be structured to protect their own creative process from being overwritten by the AI's suggestions.

The author draws a sharp parallel between professional writers and AI users. Just as a mathematician might step away from a problem for weeks to regain fresh eyes, a user must recognize that once an AI suggests an idea, that idea becomes part of the context and can crowd out the user's original thoughts. "If you haven't already come up with your ideas then its ideas will replace yours," Easwaran warns. "You need to be the boss not it." This advice is particularly vital for busy professionals who might use AI as a crutch for brainstorming. By outsourcing the initial ideation, they risk accepting the model's generic median rather than their own unique insight.

Critics might argue that this approach slows down the workflow, suggesting that the speed of AI generation is its primary value. However, Easwaran's point holds up: speed without direction leads to wasted time correcting the model's drift. The most effective use of AI requires a human to do the heavy lifting of definition before the machine does the heavy lifting of generation.

If the conversation gets off on the wrong foot and goes in the wrong direction it's easy enough to reset the llm so that it forgets the whole thing and gets started better the second time from a blank slate but you can't do that yourself.

Tuning the Engine

Moving from strategy to mechanics, Easwaran demystifies the technical settings that often intimidate non-engineers. He explains the concept of "temperature" not as a measure of heat, but as a control for chaos. In thermodynamics, higher temperature means more disorder; in AI, it means the model is more likely to pick words with lower probability scores, leading to more creative but less predictable outputs. "The simplest idea would be for the llm to always pick the answer with the highest score... but we don't always want the most generic text to come out," he explains.

He notes that while most users cannot adjust the raw numerical temperature, they can influence it through interface choices like "creative" versus "precise." This distinction is practical. If you are debugging code or extracting data, you want the "hard max" function of low temperature. If you are brainstorming marketing slogans, you need the probabilistic spread of high temperature. "This applies if you're trying to use it for computer programming or math or whatever too sometimes you want the more out there and brainstorming idea sometimes you want the more standard one," Easwaran writes.

The author also touches on the hierarchy of models, from GPT-4o to Claude's Opus and Gemini's Ultra. He argues that higher-end models, while more expensive and energy-intensive, generally offer superior reasoning and nuance. "In general you'll probably want to use the highest power model that you can unless you are limited by budget or if you're trying to use the large language model in some sort of app," he advises. This is a pragmatic take on the "best tool for the job" debate. For a one-off query, the premium model is worth the wait; for an automated app, the speed of a smaller model might be preferable.

The Context Trap

Perhaps the most insightful section of the lecture addresses the double-edged sword of context windows. Modern models can remember tens of thousands of words—enough to recall the beginning of a conversation from hours ago. Easwaran suggests this is a feature for continuity but a bug for creativity. "Once you mention an idea in the conversation that idea will remain and will keep influencing what it's thinking and what it's saying in future rounds," he notes.

This creates a feedback loop where the AI reinforces its own earlier mistakes or generic suggestions. The solution, he argues, is not to fight the context but to manage it by starting new conversations. "Sometimes it's better valuable to have multiple separate conversations going tracking different versions of the context," he suggests. This is a strategic move that many users overlook, often trying to force a single chat thread to do too much work.

Furthermore, Easwaran emphasizes that the user is also a "context machine." We remember what the AI said, and it changes our thinking. "Unlike the large language model you can't just click a button to forget everything that you've heard and brainstorm again from a blank slate," he writes. The only way to reset the human mind is to physically step away from the project. This biological constraint is the ultimate limit on AI productivity. You cannot simply prompt your way out of cognitive bias; you must manage your own attention.

You are also a context machine and you will keep coming back to ideas that have already been suggested in the conversation and unlike the large language model you can't just click a button to forget everything that you've heard and brainstorm again from a blank slate.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's strongest argument is that AI literacy is less about mastering prompts and more about mastering one's own cognitive workflow. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its assumption that users have the discipline to brainstorm before engaging the tool, a habit that runs counter to the immediate gratification AI promises. The reader should watch for the shift from treating AI as an oracle to treating it as a partner that requires strict boundaries to remain useful.
