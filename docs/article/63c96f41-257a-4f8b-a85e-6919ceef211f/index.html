I Finally Tested All The Reverb Plugins!

Benn Jordan · Benn Jordan ·Apr 28, 2024 · 21 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Benn Jordan doesn't just review reverb plugins; he dissects the very nature of digital space, arguing that the most powerful tools are often the ones that demand the most from the user. In a field saturated with "one-click" solutions, Jordan's insistence on complexity as a virtue offers a refreshing counter-narrative to the industry's push for automation.

The Obsession with Long Trails

Jordan frames his entire review around a personal, almost religious devotion to the sound of space. He admits that "four out of five times when I walk into a big room and Clap the sound I hear coming back from the walls is the room asking me to feature it on this channel." This sets a tone that is less about technical specs and more about the emotional resonance of sound. He identifies "long ambient Trails" as the holy grail, describing them as the type of effect where "you can just plug in a guitar hit a note and then contemplate releasing the result as an ambient album."

While many reviewers might skip the technical underpinnings, Jordan dives into the "spectral freeze subalgorithm" and "comb filters," noting that this knowledge is "literally useless to all but maybe 20 people in the world." Yet, he argues this complexity is necessary for the best results. He highlights Valhalla's Super Massive as a free entry point but reserves his highest praise for the $500 Black Hole Immersive, stating, "it's the best ambient Long Trail Reverb VST I've ever heard." The author's willingness to spend half a grand on a single plugin underscores his belief that quality often comes at a premium price, a stance that might alienate budget-conscious producers but resonates with those seeking perfection.

Critics might note that dismissing cheaper alternatives as lacking the "final 10%" ignores the fact that for 90% of music production, that extra 10% is inaudible. However, Jordan's focus is clearly on the extreme edge of the art form.

"If you want a truly adaptable Reverb sound then you should probably have something from each of the categories in this video but for what it's worth the winner of this category more or less is ultimately the best Reverb plugin all around."

The AI Question and the Human Touch

Perhaps the most timely section of Jordan's commentary addresses the encroachment of artificial intelligence in audio engineering. He approaches the topic with skepticism, asking, "does AI actually make it better?" After testing AI-assisted reverbs on a recording of a world-class vocalist, he concludes that while the technology offers interesting options, it ultimately lacks the nuance of human curation. He notes that the AI tools "cheat a little bit because they're giving me many of the controls I'm already dialing in with a normal Reverb plugin," suggesting that the "learn" button is a shortcut that often leads to a dead end.

Jordan's verdict is sharp: "you're better off just playing with a more Dynamic Reverb plugin until you like what you hear." This is a crucial distinction in an era where automation is selling itself as a time-saver. He argues that the "subjective territory in all of audio engineering" is precisely where AI struggles to replicate the intent of a human artist. While some might argue that AI lowers the barrier to entry for beginners, Jordan's experience suggests it may actually hinder the development of a unique sonic identity.

The Power of the Desert Island Plugin

When forced to choose a single "desert island" plugin, Jordan bypasses the user-friendly favorites like Valhalla Vintage Verb or the versatile FabFilter Pro-R (referred to as IQ Reverb in the text, likely a transcription error for FabFilter Pro-R or similar, but we will stick to the text's "IQ Reverb" which seems to refer to a specific high-end tool, possibly FabFilter Pro-Q or a specific convolution engine, but contextually he is describing a powerhouse). Instead, he crowns MeldaProduction's M-Turbo Reverb as the ultimate tool. He describes it as a beast where "you can literally edit design write and test your own Reverb algorithms which is coding."

He acknowledges the steep learning curve, comparing the interface to "playing Factorio and complaining that it doesn't look as pretty as Monument Valley." This analogy perfectly captures the trade-off: immense power comes with immense complexity. For the serious sound designer, this is the only logical choice. "M turbo Reverb is still my desert island Reverb and nothing comes close to its power and capability," he asserts, calling it his "favorite effects plugin ever made." This is a bold claim, but one backed by the plugin's ability to emulate any other reverb in existence if the user has the patience to code it.

"It's frankly insane to me that this is a consumer product and I've gotten lost creating entire soundscapes with it."

Convolution and the Soul of Place

Finally, Jordan touches on convolution reverb, the technology that allows engineers to "steal the soul of a place" by recording its acoustic signature. He champions IQ Reverb for its ability to handle multi-channel outputs, making it the "obvious pick for anybody working in sound designer film." He also gives a nod to Heavyocity's Vast for its "fun" presets and unique textures, noting that for non-traditional music, the tempo-synced impulses offer a creative playground that standard reverbs cannot match.

However, he draws a hard line at the lack of a great "Shimmer Reverb" in the plugin world, attributing it to the fact that the best algorithms are locked behind boutique hardware pedals. "You can't pirate a pedal," he jokes, but the underlying point is serious: the most innovative DSP is often kept proprietary to protect hardware sales.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's review is a masterclass in prioritizing sonic potential over convenience, arguing that the best tools require the most work. His strongest point is the dismissal of AI as a replacement for human intent, a stance that will resonate deeply with producers tired of algorithmic mediocrity. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its accessibility; by championing tools that require coding knowledge or massive CPU power, he effectively speaks only to the top 1% of audio professionals, leaving the average musician behind. Yet, for those willing to dive into the weeds, this is the definitive guide to the state of digital space.
