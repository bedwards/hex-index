I finally tested all the reverb plugins!

Benn Jordan · Benn Jordan ·Apr 28, 2024 ·21 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a field often dominated by marketing hype and vague sonic promises, Benn Jordan delivers a rare, granular autopsy of the digital reverb landscape. Rather than simply listing features, he dissects the computational cost of creativity, arguing that the most powerful tools often demand a steep price in processing power and user patience. This is not a buyer's guide for the casual listener; it is a technical manifesto for those who understand that sound design is as much about engineering as it is about art.

The Architecture of Ambience

Jordan begins by dismantling the myth of the "one-size-fits-all" plugin, categorizing reverbs by their specific sonic DNA. He identifies a distinct class of tools designed for "long ambient trails," describing them as complex engines where "a lot of delays and echoes with randomized delay times... layers through all pass filters comb filters and eventually Blends into a spectral freeze subalgorithm." This technical depth is crucial; it explains why some plugins sound organic while others feel sterile. He points to Valhalla's Super Massive as a free entry point, but reserves his highest praise for the $500 Black Hole Immersive, noting that it "actually slows down the sampling after about 2 seconds and then branches it to New delays." The result, he argues, is an "incredibly organic musical Reverb" that avoids the monotony of standard algorithms.

Critics might note that the price point of the top contenders places them out of reach for independent creators, yet Jordan's insistence on quality over cost reflects a professional reality where time is the ultimate currency. He acknowledges the trade-off, admitting he is "a little bummed out by the high price" but conceding that "aside from this, this is about as good as it gets."

The AI Illusion

The piece takes a sharp turn when addressing the industry's latest buzzword: artificial intelligence. Jordan approaches the concept with skepticism, asking, "anything safe from companies claiming that they're going to make it better with AI?" He tests several AI-assisted plugins against high-quality vocal recordings, concluding that while the technology offers interesting starting points, it often fails to replace human intuition. He finds that "both of these plugins do cheat a little bit because they're giving me many of the controls I'm already dialing in with a normal Reverb plugin." His verdict is stark: "I don't think that this is a task AI is a good solution for you're better off just playing with a more Dynamic Reverb plugin until you like what you hear."

Whether that's worth the money is up to you, but the worst that an AI Reverb can do is offer you some options that you may have not tried otherwise.

This section serves as a necessary corrective to the current tech narrative. Jordan suggests that AI in audio is currently a crutch rather than a catalyst, capable of generating options but lacking the contextual understanding to make the final artistic decision. He notes that even the most advanced AI options would be "quite underwhelming" if the user simply pressed a "learn button" and walked away.

The Power vs. Usability Paradox

When selecting a "desert island" plugin, Jordan confronts the eternal struggle between user interface simplicity and raw computational power. He dismisses the sleek, user-friendly Valhalla Vintage Verb as a "steal" but notes it lacks the depth for extreme soundscapes. In contrast, he champions MeldaProduction's M-Turbo Reverb as the ultimate tool, despite its steep learning curve. He describes the experience of using it as "like playing Factorio and complaining that it doesn't look as pretty as Monument Valley." The plugin allows users to "literally edit design write and test your own Reverb algorithms which is coding," a feature that elevates it from a simple effect to a sound design laboratory.

He also highlights FabFilter's Pro-R (referred to as IQ Reverb in the transcript context of convolution) as a juggernaut for convolution, capable of running "four powerful deeply customizable impulse response engines running simultaneously." This capability makes it an obvious choice for film and surround sound work, where spatial accuracy is non-negotiable. However, he warns that for beginners, the sheer volume of data can be overwhelming, noting that users could "easily spend hours lost in the details."

The Hardware Barrier

Finally, Jordan addresses a frustrating limitation in the digital realm: the absence of high-end "shimmer" effects found in boutique hardware pedals. He argues that "algorithm design takes years of research and work and you can't pirate a pedal," creating a barrier where the best sonic textures remain locked behind physical hardware. This is a significant gap in the software market, as the code for these unique effects is often "gatekept behind hardware." While convolution plugins like Heavyocity's Vast offer fun, textured alternatives with "odd and clever textures," they do not fully replicate the specific magic of dedicated shimmer pedals.

It's frankly insane to me that this is a consumer product and I've gotten lost creating entire soundscapes with it.

Bottom Line

Jordan's analysis succeeds because it prioritizes the physics of sound over the marketing of software, offering a clear-eyed view of where digital audio technology excels and where it still falls short of hardware. The argument's greatest strength is its rejection of AI as a silver bullet, forcing readers to engage with the actual mechanics of sound design. However, the piece's reliance on high-end, expensive tools may alienate the very beginners it claims to help, leaving a gap for affordable, high-performance alternatives. For the serious audio professional, this is an essential roadmap; for the casual user, it is a reminder that the best sound often requires the most work.
