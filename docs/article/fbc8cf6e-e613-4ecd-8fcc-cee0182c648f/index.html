By the end of 2024, many believed we had seen it all. Global politics had reached a state of peak structural chaos. But then came 2025, and with it, Donald Trump. Virtually every major geopolitical development of the past year has borne the unmistakable imprint of Donald John Trump.

successive rounds of trade wars, repeated and failed Russian Ukrainian peace talks, Iran's humiliation at the hands of Israel, the shaping of a deal in Gaza, and much more. Trump himself boasts that he has brought an end to eight wars, even though he for a moment recently started one. Yet, while each of these episodes carries significant geopolitical weight on its own, the most consequential shift may lie elsewhere. The unraveling of the so-called ironclad partnership between Europe and the United States.

The new US national security strategy, the single most important document guiding American foreign policy, states this openly. In the section devoted to Europe, the Trump administration adopts its most overtly confrontational tone, directly questioning Europe's political legitimacy by pointing to what it describes as anti-democratic processes. The NSS is only one of many signs of friction, if not outright hostility along the US Europe axis over the past year. Trump declares that he has enough of Europe's free riding and demands change while simultaneously expecting a quasi tribute to Washington.

Yet, this shift in trajectory and the end of the ironclad partnership may set a precedent that strikes at the very foundations of American hegemony. For decades, the transatlantic relationship was symbiotic, serving both sides. It breakdown threatens not only Europe. suddenly waking up exposed in a world of predators, but also the very system that underpins American global leadership.
