There is a temptation, as we observe the opening weeks of Operation Epic Fury, to see a single war taking place. This oversimplifies what is occurring. In fact, there are two wars occurring concurrently.

The United States and Iran have each entered a conflict defined by fundamentally different theories of what victory for them means, different methods for pursuing victory, and different audiences they are performing for. China and Russia, as they watch from a careful distance and offering minimal diplomatic and intelligence assistance, are however learning from both simultaneously. So are America’s Pacific allies, though what they are learning may not be particularly reassuring for them.

This assessment examines the two wars in and around Iran: the military campaign that Washington is fighting, and the economic campaign that Tehran is waging. It then asks the following question: what are the respective theories of victory, and how does that theory play out differently for each belligerent?

Finally, my assessment draws out the strategic insights that China, Russia, and America’s Pacific partners are absorbing — and what the authoritarian learning bloc might be able to accomplish with what it has learned.

Two Different Wars

The United States went to war against Iran believing it was fighting a regional military campaign with its Israeli partner. The strategic logic, at least as it can be reconstructed from the public statements and videos from the Trump administration, was that the precise application of intelligence and western weaponry would destroy Iran’s nuclear infrastructure, eliminate its ballistic missile production, degrade its naval capacity, decapitate its senior leadership, and create the conditions for the Iranians people to rise up and force regime change. The means was overwhelming air power enabled by AI-assisted intelligence from Israel and various U.S. agencies. The alliance structure was narrow: Israel and a small number of permissive basing partners. European and Asian allies were informed (if at all) rather than consulted.
