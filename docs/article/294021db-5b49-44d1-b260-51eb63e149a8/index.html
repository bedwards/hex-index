Are We in a Cold War with China?

PolyMatter · PolyMatter ·Aug 24, 2024 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

PolyMatter cuts through the noise of headline-grabbing hugs to reveal a startling truth: the emerging rivalry with China lacks the ideological soul of the Cold War, driven instead by a transactional marriage of convenience between two regimes with fundamentally different stakes. While the world fixates on the visual spectacle of Xi Jinping and Vladimir Putin embracing, the author argues that this alliance is not a repeat of history but a fragile, asymmetrical partnership where one side has everything to lose and the other has nothing left to burn. For busy leaders trying to navigate a shifting global order, understanding this distinction is not academic—it is the key to predicting whether this bloc will fracture or solidify.

The Illusion of a New Bloc

The piece opens by dismantling the emotional narrative surrounding the recent diplomatic interactions between Beijing and Moscow. PolyMatter writes, "The last time a Russian and Chinese leader embraced so effusively the two countries were in a full-blown Alliance... their Alliance would not last." This historical framing is crucial because it reminds us that personal chemistry between dictators does not equate to strategic alignment. The author traces the lineage of the current tension back to the Sino-Soviet split, noting that the ideological and power imbalances that tore the old alliance apart are re-emerging, albeit with the roles reversed.

The core of the argument rests on the timing of the rapprochement. PolyMatter explains that Russia, isolated after the 2014 annexation of Crimea, found a willing partner in a China that had emerged from the 2008 financial crisis with its global reputation soaring while America's waned. The author notes, "Putin began shopping for new partners someone ideally with an insatiable appetite for oil and a willingness to overlook certain uncomfortable topics China checked all the boxes and then some." This observation is sharp; it highlights that the alliance was born not from shared dreams, but from shared grievances against the US-led order. Critics might note that this analysis underplays the deep-seated historical mistrust between the two nations, which has existed for centuries, but the focus on current economic necessity remains a compelling lens.

China seeks to change the global order yet maintain its stability Russia seeks to destroy it.

The Limits of Friendship

Where the commentary becomes most valuable is in its dissection of the so-called "no limits" friendship. PolyMatter points out the contradiction in China's behavior: while it has provided a critical economic lifeline to Russia, it has stopped short of direct military involvement. The author writes, "Unlike North Korea China has not contributed unambiguous military equipment like artillery shells or missiles to the war in Ukraine instead it tries to present itself as a partner to Russia and a neutral responsible mediator to the rest of the world." This nuance is often lost in Western media, which tends to paint China as a monolithic aggressor. The author correctly identifies that China is walking a tightrope, trying to appease Moscow without triggering the full wrath of the West.

The distinction lies in what each leader has to lose. PolyMatter argues that "Moscow is firmly outside the us-led order... things can only get better this affords the Kremlin a certain degree of Freedom with nothing at stake." In contrast, China remains deeply embedded in the global economy. The author states, "China on the other hand simply can't afford such recklessness... it remains and will remain for the foreseeable future deeply embedded in the global order." This is the piece's strongest insight: the alliance is held together by the enemy of my enemy, not by a shared vision for the future. The author suggests that China's support is "pro-Russian neutrality," a phrase that perfectly captures the ambiguity of Beijing's position.

Divergent Visions and Future Fractures

The commentary concludes by exploring why this alliance is unlikely to evolve into a true ideological bloc. PolyMatter writes, "China's vision for the world is unlikely to spread as widely as the Soviet unions never mind that this isn't really an ideology in the way communism was for the USSR." Instead of a universal ideology, the author identifies "Han exceptionalism" as China's driving force—a belief in the unique superiority of Chinese civilization that leaves little room for exporting a model to others. This is a profound departure from the Soviet Union's active efforts to fund guerrillas and spread communism globally.

The author further notes that the power dynamic has shifted dramatically since the Cold War. "Today the tables have turned and China is now unmistakably the more powerful country its economy is almost nine times larger," PolyMatter writes. This imbalance creates inevitable friction, particularly in Central Asia where both nations vie for influence, and over energy deals where China resists dependency. The author suggests that history indicates these alliances often drift apart when the power balance shifts, and the current tension over the "Power of Siberia 2" pipeline is a harbinger of future cracks. A counterargument worth considering is that shared existential threats from the US could force these two powers to suppress their differences longer than historical precedents suggest, but the author's emphasis on divergent long-term goals remains persuasive.

Bottom Line

PolyMatter delivers a necessary corrective to the simplistic "Cold War 2.0" narrative, demonstrating that while the US faces a united front of adversaries, the alliance is held together by negative reaction to American dominance rather than a positive shared vision. The argument's greatest strength is its identification of the fundamental asymmetry in what China and Russia stand to lose, which serves as a built-in brake on their cooperation. The biggest vulnerability in the current geopolitical landscape is not the strength of the China-Russia bond, but the West's failure to recognize that this partnership is transactional and potentially fleeting, offering a narrow window to drive a wedge between them before their interests fully diverge.
