Beneath Canada's forests, rivers, and cities lies an unseen network of steel. One that fuels its economy and gives it political weight. You see, Canada is one of the world's largest energy producers, and crude oil is its money maker. Billions of dollars flow through its pipelines every year, but almost all of it, 98% in fact, goes straight to the United States.

And while Americans are very progressive when gas is a $1.50, they rediscover patriotism when it's higher. Now that Trump is abandoning free trade and leaning into protectionism, Canada's economic vulnerabilities have been exposed. Canada is so reliant on the US for its oil exports that even if a 10% tariff were applied, it would still have no choice but to continue selling to the US. So having alternative buyers for energy exports is key.

This 1150 km long pipeline dubbed the Trans Mountain expansion does precisely that. It will bring Canadian oil to overseas markets. Opened in 2024, the project was cluttered in controversy, costing roughly $25 billion and lasting for over 12 years in development. But now it's also more than just a pipeline.

It's an opportunity to have a different conversation, reduce interdependency, and strengthen Canada's sovereignty. Amid renewed discourse sparked by Trump's rhetoric, there is a growing push to expand Canada's pipeline network with Energy East and Northern Gateway emerging as key proposals. Projects such as these are needed to protect Canada's economic and political interests. But as Canada undergoes a crash course in geopolitics, it's about to find out that innocence is no shield and ignorance is no excuse.
