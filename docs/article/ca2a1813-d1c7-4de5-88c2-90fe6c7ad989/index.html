Saudi Arabia vs UAE: The New Middle East Rivalry

Transcript excerpt on Good Times Bad Times

As global attention turns to yet another round of confrontation between Washington and Tehran and to the American Armada gathering off Iran's coast, an equally high stakes contest is unfolding across the Persian Gulf. A contest between Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates. At the center of this struggle stands Crown Prince Muhammad bin Salman, the controversial heir to the Saudi throne, who rose to power amid a series of major scandals. In 2015, as a 30-year-old defense minister, he drew the kingdom into Yemen's civil war, a decision that would become a strategic disaster.

In 2017, he launched a sweeping purge within the royal family, detaining more than 200 princes and businessmen in Riyad's Ritz Carlton. A year later, inside the Saudi consulate in Istanbul, his agents murdered journalist Jamal Kashoji, a prominent critic of the crown prince. And yet, Muhammad bin Salman is a man of many faces. Alongside the image of a ruthless strongman stands that of an ambitious reformer.

One determined to transform not only the Saudi economy but Saudi society itself. For years in pursuing this transformation, MBS had a powerful ally, Muhammad bin Zed, the ruler of the United Arab Emirates and a political mentor to the young Saudi prince. Together they intervened in Yemen. Together they imposed a blockade on Qatar.

Together they backed the campaign of maximum pressure against Iran. It is no exaggeration to say that Muhammad bin Zed played a pivotal role in MBS's rise to Saudi throne. But their paths have since diverged and today the former mentor and his protetéé are locked in an increasingly intense rivalry for influence. The clearest symbol of that rivalry came last December when Southern Yemeni separatists backed by the UAE launched a large scale offensive against government forces supported by Saudi Arabia.

In response, Saudi aircraft struck the port of Mala, a key transit point for Emirati weapons shipments destined for the separatists. And yet the Saudi Airati competition extends far beyond Yemen. From Sudan to Libya, from Somalia to the corridors of power in Washington and Beijing, the interests of Riyad and Abu Dhabi are colliding. They are competing for influence, for resources, and for leadership in emerging sectors.
