The Toymaker vs. the Tariffs

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Mar 19, 2026 · 24 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider's "The Toymaker vs. the Tariffs" delivers a rare, ground-level view of how a century-old family business outmaneuvered the White House, proving that the most potent legal challenges often come from the smallest players. While the narrative of "David vs. Goliath" is a cliché, Schneider's coverage of Rick Woldenberg's lawsuit against the Trump administration's tariffs offers something the usual policy debates lack: a visceral accounting of the human cost when constitutional guardrails are ignored. This isn't just about trade; it's about the survival of a mission-driven enterprise and the integrity of the tax system itself.

The Courage of the Small

Schneider frames the story not as a dry legal victory, but as a moral imperative driven by existential math. He highlights how Woldenberg, CEO of Learning Resources, faced a choice between accepting a tariff hike that would have jumped costs from $2 million to $100 million or fighting back. "The risks of doing nothing were greater than the risks of doing something," Woldenberg tells Schneider, a sentiment that anchors the entire piece. This is a powerful framing because it strips away the abstraction of "trade policy" and replaces it with the concrete reality of 500 families facing unemployment.

The author effectively contrasts Woldenberg's willingness to sue with the silence of industry giants like Mattel or Apple. Schneider notes that while larger corporations hesitated, fearing political retaliation or legal costs, Woldenberg saw the tariff scheme as a direct threat to his family's legacy dating back to 1916. "I really was not prepared to allow a politician to ruin this," Woldenberg asserts. This highlights a critical gap in corporate America's response to overreach: the incentives for mid-sized, mission-driven firms to protect their communities are often stronger than the risk-averse calculus of conglomerates.

Critics might argue that this "lone wolf" approach is not scalable for the broader economy, as most companies lack the specific legal expertise or the financial runway to litigate against the federal government. Yet, Schneider's reporting suggests that the barrier was not capability, but courage, a distinction that makes the victory all the more significant.

"The stated intent of this government was to have me pay these taxes forever. If you look at what that really means in the long run, it was not a hard decision to make."

The Illusion of Easy Alternatives

A significant portion of Schneider's commentary is dedicated to dismantling the popular narrative that manufacturers can simply "move to Vietnam" or "bring it back to America." Woldenberg provides a masterclass in supply chain economics, explaining that China's dominance isn't just about cheap labor, but about a "giant fluid market" where every component, from molding machines to inspectors, is available locally. "They have everything," Woldenberg explains, contrasting this with the logistical nightmares of India, where monsoons can delay shipments for six weeks, or the fragmented ecosystems of Mexico and Vietnam.

Schneider uses this to illustrate the "hard economics" of toy production, noting that the industry's concentration in China is a result of decades of optimization, not just a temporary arbitrage. "If it were economically viable to manufacture here, everyone would be doing it," Woldenberg says, pointing out that the labor required for hand-finishing toys simply cannot be supported by American wages. This section is crucial because it counters the political rhetoric of "onshoring" with the messy reality of global logistics.

The author also weaves in a subtle but important historical nod to the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), the specific statute the Supreme Court found the tariffs violated. By grounding the legal victory in the specific mechanics of the law rather than just political ideology, Schneider strengthens the argument that the administration overstepped its constitutional authority. The case, Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump, serves as a reminder that even in times of emergency, the rule of law must hold.

The Rule of Law and the Refund

Perhaps the most compelling part of the piece is the discussion on what happens next: the refund. Schneider captures Woldenberg's unshakeable confidence that the government must return the illegally collected funds. "The federal government overcollected its taxes," Woldenberg states, dismissing the idea that logistics would prevent a refund. "They took money that isn't theirs. They're not entitled to it."

This argument is bolstered by Peter Harrell, a trade lawyer who co-hosted the conversation and submitted an amicus curiae brief in the case. Harrell draws a parallel to income tax, noting that if the Treasury Department arbitrarily doubled income tax rates and was later ruled out of bounds, no one would question the need for a refund. "This is really conceptually no different," Harrell argues. "It's just another kind of tax."

Schneider's inclusion of this legal perspective adds necessary weight to the moral argument. It shifts the conversation from "should they get their money back?" to "how could they possibly keep it?" The reference to the amicus brief underscores that this wasn't a rogue legal theory but a consensus view among trade experts.

"The DOJ is not a foreign party. The DOJ is us. We sent our neighbors to Washington, we pay them with our money to do those jobs... They took too much. They need to give it back."

Bottom Line

Schneider's coverage succeeds because it humanizes a complex legal and economic victory, showing that the rule of law is best defended by those with the most to lose. The strongest part of the argument is the unassailable logic that illegal taxation must be refunded, a point made irrefutable by the sheer scale of the overcollection. However, the piece leaves a lingering vulnerability: while the Supreme Court has spoken, the political machinery of the executive branch may still drag out the refund process, testing the limits of judicial enforcement in the face of bureaucratic inertia.
