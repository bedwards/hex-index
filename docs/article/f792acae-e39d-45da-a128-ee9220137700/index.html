How China Hopes to Build AGI Through Self-Improvement

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Mar 30, 2026 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider dismantles the comforting assumption that China is merely playing catch-up in the AI race, revealing a fundamentally different, and perhaps more dangerous, path to Artificial General Intelligence. While Washington fixates on the American dream of a software god that rewrites its own code, Schneider exposes a Beijing strategy that treats the physical world as the necessary training ground for true intelligence. This is not a story of lagging behind; it is a story of racing toward a different finish line entirely.

The Illusion of the Software God

The prevailing narrative in Silicon Valley and Washington relies on a specific theory: Recursive Self-Improvement, where AI agents automate their own research and coding to trigger an intelligence explosion. Schneider writes, "While in 2017 the term '通用人工智能' used by Beijing could safely be interpreted as general-purpose AI rather than AGI, the same cannot be asserted now that the term has resurfaced in 2026." This observation is crucial because it signals a shift from economic pragmatism to existential ambition. The author notes that the U.S. consensus, championed by figures like Dario Amodei and Sam Altman, assumes that automating the R&D process is the decisive lever. Schneider argues that this focus creates a blind spot: "If automating AI research is the decisive lever, then whoever initiates RSI first wins. China, on current assessments, is not close."

This framing is seductive because it simplifies a complex geopolitical contest into a single metric of software efficiency. However, Schneider points out that this metric ignores the broader ecosystem. He writes, "So even as China appears to lead the AI diffusion race that yields more immediate economic benefits, with the prospect of RSI, which promises rapid self-compounding gains through automated AI research, the US is still ahead, and the gap will soon increase rapidly." The author suggests this is a reasonable prediction only if one accepts the American definition of AGI as the sole valid endpoint. Critics might note that assuming the American path is the only path to AGI is a form of technological hubris, but Schneider's data suggests the U.S. is betting the farm on a single horse.

The differences in perceiving AGI result in two distortions: Beijing may not rush to build the software machine god the U.S. has in mind, and Chinese labs may be doing AGI work without framing it as such.

The Embodied Alternative

Schneider pivots to the Chinese counter-narrative, which rejects the idea that intelligence can be purely digital. Instead, the Chinese approach, described by Z.ai CEO Zhang Peng, insists on a "closed loop" where a brain is equipped with hands and feet to interact with the physical world. Schneider quotes Zhang: "Then you equip it with hands and feet so it can call upon the world model to solve problems... The results of that interaction are fed back as a reinforcement signal. I immediately receive this signal, learn again, and modify my model."

This is not merely a technical preference; it is a philosophical divergence rooted in the history of embodied cognition. Schneider highlights that this view is shared by top-tier academics, including Turing Award winner Andrew Yao and Tsinghua's Zhang Bo, who pioneered embodied AI studies in China in the 1980s. The author explains that for these researchers, "genuine intelligence only emerges when an agent can perceive the world, act upon it, and integrate the results back into its own cognition." This mirrors the logic of swarm intelligence, where complex behaviors emerge from simple physical interactions rather than top-down programming. By grounding AI in physical reality, Chinese labs aim to solve the "grounding problem" that plagues purely text-based models.

The evidence Schneider marshals is compelling. He notes that Alibaba CEO Wu Yongming argues that "AI's self-improvement loop cannot close on static data alone, which, however vast, is ultimately bounded by what humans have already expressed." Instead, the Chinese strategy relies on generating new data through physical interaction. Schneider writes, "Each physical interaction becomes a fine-tuning, each feedback a parameter optimization — and through enough cycles of that loop, Wu argues, AI will iterate itself toward intelligence that surpasses its own training."

This approach has tangible momentum. The author details how local governments are funding "robot boot camps" where hundreds of robots practice real-world tasks, generating the kind of physical interaction data that no static corpus can provide. While the U.S. focuses on agentic coding, Schneider observes that "the Chinese ecosystem has no single consensus path," but the convergence on multimodality and world models is undeniable. ByteDance and Tencent are investing heavily in world models to "predict the next state of the world," moving beyond the simple "predict the next word" paradigm.

Rather than a superbrain built from code as perceived by many in Silicon Valley, Chinese AI actors increasingly narrate a different endpoint: intelligence that requires a body to exist.

The Strategic Danger of Misalignment

The most chilling part of Schneider's analysis is the potential for a strategic mismatch. If the U.S. is racing to build a software entity that can rewrite its own code, and China is building a physical entity that learns through trial and error, the two powers may be racing toward different definitions of victory. Schneider warns, "When Beijing decides to 'race' towards AGI rather than 'explore' it, it will not rush to build the software machine god that the U.S. frontier labs have in mind." This creates a dangerous asymmetry. The U.S. might declare victory based on software benchmarks while China quietly achieves a form of embodied general intelligence that the U.S. models cannot replicate or even perceive as a threat.

Schneider writes, "Even if Chinese labs are already doing things that Silicon Valley would recognize as precursors to AGI, they may not frame the activities as AGI, as they understand the word differently." This semantic gap could lead to a failure of deterrence or safety coordination. The author suggests that the American obsession with Recursive Self-Improvement has led to a dismissal of China's efforts as mere "iteration" rather than "innovation." Yet, as Schneider notes, the components of the Chinese closed-loop are being assembled at speed, with startups like Spirit AI and X Square Robot developing models that learn through physical reinforcement learning.

The historical context here is vital. Just as the Manhattan Project was a top-down, centralized effort, the Chinese path to AGI appears to be a "bottom-up movement driven by constraint in compute," gradually gaining influence in Beijing's top policy circle. This organic, distributed approach might prove more resilient than the fragile, compute-hungry models favored in the West. Schneider concludes that the convergence of company founders, academic researchers, and state-adjacent scientists suggests "something more than coordinated messaging." It is a genuine, alternative theory of mind.

Bottom Line

Jordan Schneider's strongest argument is that the U.S. is losing the AI race not by falling behind, but by running the wrong race entirely. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that the Chinese state can successfully coordinate such a fragmented, bottom-up ecosystem into a coherent AGI strategy. Readers should watch for the next phase of this competition: whether the physical data generated by China's robot boot camps can actually outpace the software self-improvement cycles of American labs. The stakes are not just economic; they are definitional. Who gets to decide what intelligence looks like? The answer may determine the future of the species.
