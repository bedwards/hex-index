Welcome back to the office. You won’t get anything done

The Walrus · ·Jan 5, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Most coverage of the return-to-office mandate treats the commute as a logistical hurdle, but The Walrus reframes the office itself as a mechanism of psychological containment. This piece argues that the modern workplace was never designed for efficiency, but rather to domesticate worker autonomy and enforce a specific type of capitalist subjectivity. For busy professionals tired of the performative grind, this historical excavation offers a startling reason why the office feels so soul-crushing: it was built that way.

The Myth of Productivity

The Walrus begins by dismantling the primary justification for return-to-office (RTO) mandates: the claim that physical presence drives output. The author notes that "several studies have shown that hybrid work does not impact productivity," yet companies like Amazon and Goldman Sachs are insisting on four-day weeks anyway. Instead of collaboration, the author observes that employees often find themselves "on back-to-back virtual calls, irritating other people nearby who are also on back-to-back calls." This creates a paradox where the office becomes a space of isolation rather than connection.

The argument gains weight when The Walrus points out that these mandates often serve as a covert layoff strategy. "Some members of the C-suite have admitted that they implemented RTO mandates to encourage people to quit," the text reveals. This reframes the policy not as a management tool for culture, but as a financial maneuver to avoid severance costs. Critics might argue that some industries genuinely require physical presence for safety or collaboration, but the article effectively highlights that the blanket mandates ignore the nuance of actual work requirements.

The office is, first and foremost, a space geared toward organizing people to become a certain type of subject: a cog in the capitalist machine.

The Architecture of Control

The piece takes a sharp turn into history, tracing the modern office back to the 1960s and the "Action Office" by Herman Miller. While often celebrated as the precursor to the cubicle, The Walrus explains that the original design was intended to offer flexibility. However, it was quickly co-opted to serve a different purpose: "The Action Office allowed workers to stand up, move around, and customize their workspaces, all for the cause of working more productively." This historical pivot mirrors the shift from scientific management, where Frederick Taylor sought to optimize every motion of the factory worker, to the modern open-plan office where every motion is visible.

The Walrus connects this design evolution to a deeper ideological root, citing scholar Kathryn Lofton to suggest that the office has "religious roots." The text notes that the Dutch Reformed tradition's emphasis on asceticism influenced the clean, stark aesthetics of Herman Miller furniture. This framing suggests that the discomfort of the open office is not an accident of design, but a feature of a system that values "bodily asceticism" over well-being. The author writes, "The walls may be lower, the colors may be brighter, and the chairs ever more ergonomic, but the territory is still organized for your physical submission."

The Commodification of Time

Perhaps the most profound insight in the article is its analysis of time itself. The Walrus draws on anthropologist David Graeber to explain that the concept of "buying time" is a relatively recent invention. "In premodern times, you could buy a pot from a potter, or you could buy the potter and enslave him, but you cannot buy the potter's time," the author writes. This distinction is crucial because it reveals that the modern employment relationship is predicated on a form of "temporary enslavement" that was once considered degrading.

The article argues that employers are terrified of employees reclaiming their time because it threatens the illusion of control. When workers went remote, they didn't stop working; they often worked more, logging "10 percent more weekly hours" because they felt the need to prove their worth without surveillance. The Walrus suggests that the real fear for executives is not a drop in productivity, but the realization that people might have time to "take care of themselves" and "reflect" on their lives outside of labor. This is the ultimate threat to the system: a workforce that is no longer psychologically contained.

The notion of buying someone's time is an ideological invention that traces back to the dawn of the industrial period.

Bottom Line

The Walrus delivers a compelling, if unsettling, diagnosis of the return-to-office trend: it is less about business needs and more about the reassertion of control over the human body and mind. While the historical link to religious asceticism is a bold interpretive leap, the core argument—that the office is a tool for psychological containment rather than productivity—resonates with the lived experience of millions. The biggest vulnerability in the piece is its near-total dismissal of any potential benefit to in-person work, but its strength lies in exposing the hidden costs of the "culture" employers claim to cherish. Watch for how this growing resistance to RTO mandates reshapes the definition of work in the coming decade.

By Kathy Chow

My first office job was an internship at a law firm in Washington, DC. I was twenty years old and a college student, which meant that I was quite useless. I found out that it was one kind of torture to do pointless work for two or three hours a day—usually, producing research memos that no one read—and then another kind of torture to figure out how to do nothing until it was acceptable to leave the office at 5 p.m. I spent a lot of time texting two friends from high school, who were also newly stuck in office jobs of their own. I perfected my technique for napping while sitting upright.

My second office job was an internship at a management consulting firm in Manhattan when I was twenty-one. At work, I was either gossiping with my fellow interns, trying to figure out how to optimize my per diem for lunch, or messaging different people on dating apps while waiting for one particular person to text me back.

But my lackadaisical workdays as a management consultant weren’t entirely my fault. The office was full of distractions, and I found it difficult to focus. People were frequently pulling me into unnecessary meetings or taking calls around me. Also, I function best when I have a snack every two hours or so. At the office, I was too self-conscious to eat, so I spent hours trying to distract myself from my hunger instead of working. I inevitably did most of my “work”—making PowerPoints and fiddling with spreadsheets—in the evenings and over the weekends. After that summer, I absconded to graduate school and vowed to avoid any job that would require me to be in an office from nine to five for as long as possible.

During the pandemic, the glass high rises that struck terror into my young, impressionable heart stood empty, and for a while, people wondered whether offices were relics of the past. But over the past two years, companies have begun to call employees back into the office. Ontario public servants are expected to return to office full-time this month.
