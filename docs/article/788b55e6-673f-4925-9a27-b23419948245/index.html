How Mexico is creating its own Panama Canal

Shirvan Neftchi · CaspianReport ·Jan 6, 2025 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Shirvan Neftchi reframes Mexico not as a passive neighbor to the United States, but as an active geopolitical player constructing a rival to the Panama Canal. The piece is notable for its specific focus on the Tehuantepec Corridor as a strategic lever in the US-China trade war, arguing that geography is finally trumping history. This is not just a story about infrastructure; it is a high-stakes bet that Mexico can solve its own deep inequality by becoming the world's most efficient logistics hub.

The Geography of Inequality

Neftchi begins by grounding the reader in Mexico's stark reality: a nation of immense potential fractured by severe disparity. He notes that "in the Yucatan Peninsula, people have living standards comparable to France while nearby Chiapas has a GDP per capita similar to Bangladesh's." This comparison is jarring, yet it sets the stage for why the government is desperate for a new economic engine. The author argues that wealth has "never" trickled down in Mexico, a historical failure that has fueled revolutions and instability.

The proposed solution is the Tehuantepec Corridor, a massive infrastructure project linking the Pacific port of Salina Cruz to the Gulf Coast port of Coatzacoalcos. Neftchi writes, "Imagine an ocean-to-ocean shortcut so powerful it can rival the Panama Canal." The logic here is compelling: by moving cargo across 300 kilometers of rail rather than navigating the entire isthmus by sea, Mexico can offer a viable alternative when the Panama Canal faces bottlenecks.

"While it takes 8 to 10 hours to cross the Panama Canal, moving through the Tehuantepec Corridor by rail would take about 6 to 7 hours. However, when factoring in the time to load and unload thousands of containers between ships and trains, the trip could stretch to 15 hours."

Neftchi is refreshingly honest about the physics of the situation. He admits the corridor is not faster in pure transit time, but he pivots to the real value proposition: reliability. With water levels in the Panama Canal's Lake Gatun at record lows, ships are waiting up to two weeks. In that context, a 15-hour rail transfer beats a two-week delay. This pragmatic assessment elevates the piece above standard boosterism.

Critics might note that the "two-week wait" scenario is episodic, driven by specific drought conditions, and may not represent the long-term average reliability of the Panama Canal. However, the trend of climate volatility makes Neftchi's point about diversification increasingly valid.

The Nearshoring Boom

The commentary shifts from logistics to the broader economic shift known as nearshoring. Neftchi explains that the United States is actively "decoupling its economy from China," encouraging companies to move operations closer to home. This is where Mexico's geography becomes its greatest asset. "Mexico stands to gain significantly from this shift," Neftchi asserts, pointing to the 3,145-kilometer border shared with the US.

The author highlights a crucial nuance: this isn't just about moving factories; it's about moving high-value manufacturing. He notes that "companies producing spare parts for smartphones, computers, and cars could relocate to Mexico's industrial parks." The potential is staggering. Neftchi writes, "If even a fraction of the multi-trillion dollar electronics, automotive, pharmaceutical or semiconductor industries shift to Mexico, it could greatly boost the economy."

The evidence of success is already visible. Neftchi points out that in February 2024, "Mexico overtook China to become the top supplier of goods to the United States." This is a historic milestone, driven by the very policies the author describes. The argument is that Mexico is the "big winner in the US-China trade war," a position that aligns with the interests of American lawmakers seeking to reduce reliance on Beijing.

"China would still retain key advantages in engineering but its GDP per capita is similar to Mexico's, meaning labor costs would be roughly the same in Mexico as they are in China."

This observation is critical. It dismantles the assumption that Mexico is a "cheap labor" destination in the traditional sense. Instead, it positions Mexico as a cost-competitive alternative that offers proximity and trade agreement benefits.

The Risks of Success

Despite the optimism, Neftchi does not shy away from the formidable obstacles. He identifies a "major problem" that has plagued Mexico for centuries: wealth disparity. The corridor passes through some of the country's poorest regions, and the success of the project hinges on whether it can actually lift these communities or simply enrich a few.

The author warns that the project's success "hinges on the Mexican government's coordination across federal, state, and local administrations." This is a significant hurdle. Neftchi notes that "historically, Mexico has always struggled with such centralization of authority." Furthermore, security remains a paramount concern. "If organized crime or political instability threatens the corridor's infrastructure, investor confidence could plummet."

To illustrate the stakes, Neftchi mentions that in 2023, the government deployed "as many as 1,000 soldiers at key locations to secure a stretch of old railway." This militarization underscores the fragility of the endeavor. Additionally, there is the risk of inflation. He cites the Mexican Chamber of the Construction Industry, noting that when Tesla announced a factory, "local prices for cement and reinforced steel rose by 25%."

"If these trends continue, Mexico could risk pricing itself out and deterring companies from moving in."

This counterpoint is vital. The very success of the nearshoring boom could undermine it by driving up costs. Neftchi acknowledges this tension, suggesting that the government must balance growth with cost control to maintain Mexico's competitive edge.

Bottom Line

Shirvan Neftchi's analysis is strongest in its refusal to treat the Tehuantepec Corridor as a silver bullet, instead presenting it as a calculated gamble on geography and timing. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in the assumption that Mexico can overcome its historical struggles with security and administrative fragmentation quickly enough to capitalize on the current nearshoring window. Readers should watch whether the promised industrial parks can deliver jobs without triggering the inflationary spiral that could price Mexico out of the market.
