How to choose the most secure messaging app

The Hated One · The Hated One ·May 19, 2020 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In an era where digital privacy feels like a relic, The Hated One cuts through the noise with a stark, uncompromising reality: trusting a corporation with your private data is a gamble you are destined to lose. This isn't just another list of app recommendations; it is a rigorous "purity test" that challenges the very architecture of modern communication, arguing that true security requires abandoning the convenience of centralized platforms entirely.

The Illusion of Trust

The Hated One begins by dismantling the foundational assumption that tech giants are benevolent guardians. "Companies care more about their bottom line than about fixing bugs in their software or poorly under security dissenters," the author asserts, framing the issue not as a technical glitch but as a fundamental business model failure. This perspective is vital because it shifts the burden of safety from the user to the provider, a shift that most consumers are ill-equipped to handle. The argument holds water when one considers the history of data breaches, yet it overlooks the practical reality that for the average user, the friction of self-hosted security is often too high to sustain.

The core of the author's methodology is a series of non-negotiable questions designed to filter out "snake-oil" encryption. The first and most critical filter is end-to-end encryption, but with a specific caveat: it must be the default, not an optional toggle. "The best app for security is the one that only sends encrypted messages and doesn't fall back to plain text mode under any circumstances," The Hated One writes. This is a powerful distinction. Many apps claim encryption but allow users to accidentally send unencrypted data, creating a single point of failure. The author correctly identifies that security features must be invisible to the user to be effective.

"The most secure designs are the ones you don't have to trust."

The Open Source Imperative

Moving beyond the surface of encryption claims, the commentary pivots to the necessity of transparency. The Hated One argues that proprietary code is inherently suspicious, stating, "When developers of secure systems hide their source code we can't verify whether they didn't build a backdoor into their implementation." This is the crux of the security community's philosophy: security through obscurity is a fallacy. The author emphasizes that even if an app uses open-source cryptography, if the implementation is hidden, the promise is hollow. "Most still keep the rest of the source code including implementation of open source encryption hidden especially commercial apps like iMessage or what an offer too many features that make their source codes too complex for a single company to patch all the bugs."

Critics might argue that requiring full open-source status eliminates viable, user-friendly options that have undergone rigorous third-party auditing. However, The Hated One counters this by noting that "the more eyes on the code the more secure it becomes," suggesting that community scrutiny is superior to corporate security teams. The argument is compelling, though it assumes a level of technical literacy in the community that may not always exist.

The Economics of Privacy

The piece then tackles the often-ignored question of funding: "Where is the money?" The Hated One posits that if an app is free and not open source, "you're the product," but even in the open-source world, the funding model matters. The author warns against apps backed by advertising businesses or those hiding behind vague corporate speak. "If the team behind an app is publicly facing and you can verify who they are and how they earn a living they most likely take security seriously." This focus on the human element behind the software is a refreshing departure from purely technical analysis. It suggests that accountability is a feature, not a bug.

The discussion extends to jurisdiction and metadata, two areas where legal frameworks often outpace technical safeguards. The Hated One advises avoiding providers within the "five eyes surveillance Alliance" and scrutinizing privacy policies for length. "The general rule of thumb is the shorter the policy the better," the author notes, arguing that a policy spanning pages is a sign of excessive data collection. This is a pragmatic heuristic for busy readers who cannot parse legal jargon, though it risks oversimplifying complex legal requirements that necessitate detailed disclosures.

Anonymity and the Weakest Link

Perhaps the most demanding part of the purity test involves anonymity. The Hated One distinguishes between privacy and anonymity, noting that "if you are targeted by someone who knows your identity like the government or the Italian mob then you want a full anonymity." The author describes the gold standard: downloading over Tor, avoiding app stores, and creating an identity without a phone number or email. "The most anonymous account creation is one where we can download the app over tor connection outside of Google Play Store or Apple App Store." This is a high bar, and the author admits it is necessary only for those with specific, high-level threat models.

The commentary also addresses the human element of security, specifically contact verification. "Your communication can only be as secure as its weakest link," The Hated One writes, highlighting that a hacker can compromise a conversation by impersonating a contact. Features like fingerprint code comparison or QR scanning are presented as essential tools to prevent this. Furthermore, the concept of "forward secrecy" is introduced as a safeguard against future key compromises, ensuring that "even if the keys from the last message are compromised they can be used to decrypt past conversations."

"In the long run encryption is nice and all but without freedom your security will have a single point of failure."

The Decentralized Future

The piece concludes by looking toward a decentralized future, where users are not dependent on a single provider. The Hated One argues that "if your app is a peer-to-peer then there are no central servers to compromise and there is no single point of failure." The vision is of a federated internet where different apps can communicate securely, breaking the walled gardens of current tech giants. "Federation is the future of the Internet or at least that future that doesn't turn into a tech dystopian nightmare." While this is an idealistic goal, the current reality is that most users prioritize ease of use over federation, creating a tension between security and adoption that the author acknowledges but does not fully resolve.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's "purity test" is a robust, if demanding, framework that successfully reframes messaging app selection from a feature checklist to a philosophical stance on trust and power. Its greatest strength is the insistence that security must be default, transparent, and independent of corporate profit motives. However, the argument's biggest vulnerability is its high barrier to entry; the rigorous standards for anonymity and decentralization may alienate the very users who need protection the most, leaving them with a choice between total insecurity and total inconvenience.
