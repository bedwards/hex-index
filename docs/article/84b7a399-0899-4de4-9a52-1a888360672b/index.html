Asml: Tsmc's Critical Supplier, Explained

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Feb 16, 2021 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Asianometry cuts through the nationalist noise surrounding semiconductor dominance to reveal a startling truth: the battle between TSMC and ASML isn't a rivalry, but a symbiotic marriage where neither can survive without the other. While internet forums obsess over who holds the crown, the author argues that the real story lies in a decades-long, billion-dollar gamble on a technology that almost never happened. This isn't just a history lesson; it's a masterclass in how the world's most critical supply chains are actually built on shared risk and American intellectual property, not just Dutch engineering or Taiwanese execution.

The High-Stakes Gamble on EUV

The narrative begins by dismantling the idea that ASML was always the undisputed king. Asianometry notes that for its first six years, the company "lost money" and struggled to compete in a niche dominated by Japanese firms Nikon and Canon. The turning point wasn't a sudden breakthrough, but a strategic pivot to Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) lithography, a technology so complex it was widely considered a "money pit." Asianometry writes, "Some argued that current techniques like immersion lithography and another thing called multi-patterning could carry the industry forward after all."

This hesitation was rational. The industry faced a genuine fork in the road: stick with proven methods or bet the farm on a theoretical leap. Asianometry highlights the sheer scale of the risk, noting that "the light source would have to be entirely redesigned from the ground up" and customers would have to "retool their fabs." The author's framing is effective here because it humanizes the corporate decision-making process, showing that even giants like Intel and TSMC were terrified of backing the wrong horse. Without this context, ASML's current monopoly looks like inevitable destiny rather than a precarious victory.

"ASML could not have finished EUV without intimate participation and financing from its customers."

The solution to this existential threat was a radical collaboration. Asianometry details how ASML launched a co-investment scheme in 2012, where Intel, Samsung, and TSMC poured billions into the company in exchange for equity. This move was crucial. As the author puts it, "Without this buy-in, ASML would have ended up like Canon, unable to sustain this decades-long odyssey without going bankrupt." This argument holds up well against the common misconception that ASML simply out-innovated its rivals in a vacuum. It underscores that the technology was a collective effort, a "harmonious" alliance between fierce competitors who recognized that the alternative was mutual obsolescence.

The American Shadow in Dutch Machinery

Perhaps the most surprising revelation in the piece is the American DNA embedded in Dutch machinery. Asianometry addresses the geopolitical friction directly, asking why the U.S. government restricts EUV exports from a Dutch company. The answer, according to the author, is that "EUV is literally an American founded technology." The piece traces the lineage back to 1997, when U.S. firms Intel and Micron partnered with the Department of Energy to form EUV LLC.

Asianometry writes, "Think of EUV as a startup and the US as its controlling shareholder." This metaphor is powerful because it reframes the geopolitical tension not as a trade dispute, but as a shareholder dispute. The author points out that ASML only secured the license to use this technology in 1999, leapfrogging Japanese competitors who never received the same access. "The EUV LLC participation allowed ASML to leapfrog the Japanese's progress in EUV and get that critical first buy-in from major customers," Asianometry explains. This evidence is compelling and often overlooked in favor of nationalistic narratives that ignore the transnational nature of the tech stack.

Critics might note that this interpretation downplays the immense engineering prowess of the Dutch team who actually turned the theoretical concept into a working machine. While the U.S. provided the foundation, ASML's ability to execute the vision over thirty years is a feat of European industrial policy that deserves its own credit. However, the author's point remains valid: without the American IP foundation, the Dutch machine would have been a very different, and likely less successful, project.

The Baker and the Oven

To resolve the endless debate over who matters more—TSMC or ASML—Asianometry employs a simple but devastatingly effective metaphor: baking a pound cake. In this analogy, TSMC is the baker, and ASML is the high-tech oven. "You can liken TSMC to the baker in the kitchen," the author suggests, while ASML provides the "Middleby" oven. The logic follows that a great oven cannot bake a cake without a skilled baker, and a skilled baker cannot produce a cake without an oven.

Asianometry argues that focusing solely on the machine ignores the "skill of the cook," which explains why Intel and Samsung, despite having access to the same EUV machines, have struggled to match TSMC's yield and output. "Cooking skills and experience matter," the author concludes. This analogy successfully neutralizes the "fanboy" arguments that dominate online discourse. It shifts the focus from ownership to execution, suggesting that the value chain is a continuous loop of dependency rather than a hierarchy.

"You can try [to attribute credit] but it all ends up in a ridiculous exercise of numbers pulled out of your butt because the reality is they all came together in a single harmonious moment."

This final point serves as a strong counterweight to the reductionist thinking that plagues tech journalism. It acknowledges that while the oven is critical, the final product is a result of the entire ecosystem working in concert. The author's refusal to assign a specific percentage of credit to any single player is a refreshing admission of complexity in an industry often oversimplified for headlines.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's strongest contribution is the dismantling of the zero-sum game narrative, replacing it with a model of deep, interdependent collaboration that spans decades and borders. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a somewhat idealized view of this "harmonious" partnership, which occasionally glosses over the intense commercial maneuvering and IP litigation that also defined this era. Readers should watch for how this delicate balance holds up as geopolitical tensions continue to fracture the global supply chain, testing whether these "harmonious" alliances can survive in a world increasingly driven by national security rather than shared innovation.
