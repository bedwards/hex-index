Origins of the Muslim Brotherhood

Shirvan Neftchi · CaspianReport ·Jun 14, 2020 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Shirvan Neftchi reframes the Muslim Brotherhood not as a sudden explosion of religious extremism, but as a calculated, decades-long reaction to the visceral humiliation of colonial segregation. While many histories focus solely on the group's later militancy, Neftchi meticulously traces how the physical architecture of the Suez Canal created a "segregated state of affairs" that made the Brotherhood's rise inevitable. This is essential listening for anyone trying to understand why political Islam remains a potent force today; it reveals that the movement's roots are less about abstract theology and more about a desperate struggle for dignity in a world designed to exclude it.

The Architecture of Humiliation

Neftchi sets the stage by describing the 19th-century Middle East as a region where "centuries-old distribution of power... was turned upside down." He argues that the European presence was not merely political but cultural, creating a environment where "contradictory practices and customs struggled for ideological dominance." The author's most compelling evidence lies in the specific geography of the Suez Canal construction. He describes a stark reality: "one side of the canal had a European culture with luxurious housing... the other side meanwhile retained its Islamic character with bazaars tea shops barber houses etc."

This spatial segregation is the engine of Neftchi's entire narrative. He posits that the resulting poverty and unemployment in the Egyptian towns were not accidental but structural, forcing locals to choose between destitution and assimilation. "The prize for assimilating into a European culture was to become a second-tier subject," Neftchi writes, a line that perfectly captures the psychological trap of colonialism. The author effectively argues that Hassan al-Banna's early work was a direct response to this specific trauma, transforming a primary school into a movement to teach "self-respect and dignity" to a generation told they were inferior.

Critics might note that Neftchi's focus on economic and social humiliation risks oversimplifying the theological motivations of the Brotherhood, potentially downplaying the genuine religious revivalism that also fueled the movement. However, his emphasis on the material conditions provides a necessary counterweight to narratives that treat the group as purely ideological.

"It was a confusing time where contradictory practices and customs struggled for ideological dominance on the one hand there were European concepts such as liberalism humanism modernity etc on the other side were Islamic intellectuals who argued for reform without surrendering their religious virtues."

From Social Reform to Armed Resistance

The commentary shifts as Neftchi details the Brotherhood's evolution from a social service provider to a political powerhouse. He notes that by the 1940s, the group had grown to "over 2,000 local branches and roughly half a million members," building a parallel society of schools and hospitals. This grassroots success, Neftchi suggests, made the Brotherhood a threat not just to the British, but to the secular Egyptian elite. The author highlights the group's motto, "preparer," inscribed on their coat of arms, to illustrate their long-term strategic mindset.

Neftchi's account of the Brotherhood's entry into the political arena is particularly sharp. He describes how the group viewed "secularism and nationalism" as incompatible with Islam, instead preaching "pan-islamism where they called for Islamic solidarity and unity." This framing helps explain the Brotherhood's appeal across national borders, positioning them as the inheritors of a collapsing Ottoman legacy. The author writes, "the brotherhood viewed the space extending from Libya to Syria as a singular entity," a bold claim that underscores their ambition to transcend the artificial borders drawn by European powers.

The narrative takes a darker turn with the assassination of al-Banna and the subsequent crackdown by the government. Neftchi describes the tension vividly: "the Cairo government would indulge no second guessing and retaliated by assassinating the founder al-Banna." This event, he argues, was the catalyst that pushed the Brotherhood from political maneuvering into open rebellion and conspiracy with the military.

The Cycle of Alliance and Betrayal

The final section of the piece traces the Brotherhood's turbulent relationship with the Egyptian state, moving from the 1952 revolution to the modern era. Neftchi details the irony of the Brotherhood's role in overthrowing King Farouq, only to be betrayed by the very military officers they helped install. He recounts the dramatic 1954 assassination attempt on Gamal Abdel Nasser, noting how "Nasser maintained his posture and reassured the public of his commitment and resolve" while the broadcast was still live. This moment, Neftchi argues, was a turning point that allowed Nasser to crush the Brotherhood and imprison thousands.

The author's analysis of the group's subsequent reinvention is crucial. He explains how the Brotherhood adapted to survive, shifting toward a "policy of non-violence as a means to achieve its goals" to regain public trust. This strategic pivot allowed them to become an "umbrella institution for political opposition" under Hosni Mubarak, eventually leading to their electoral victory in 2012. Neftchi concludes by noting that despite being outlawed again, the "pan-Islamic message of the Muslim Brotherhood continues to resonate far beyond its indigenous base of operations."

A counterargument worth considering is whether Neftchi's focus on the Brotherhood's adaptability underestimates the internal fractures that led to the rise of more radical splinter groups like Hamas. While he mentions the split, the narrative remains heavily focused on the Brotherhood's institutional resilience rather than the ideological extremism that also grew from its ashes.

"The Muslim Brotherhood was by no means just a militant group; its growth represented a cultural battle for the soul of Egypt."

Bottom Line

Neftchi's strongest contribution is his refusal to treat the Muslim Brotherhood as a monolith of violence, instead grounding its history in the specific, humiliating realities of colonial segregation and economic exclusion. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its tendency to view the group's history almost entirely through the lens of Egyptian statecraft, potentially missing the broader global currents of Islamic thought that also shaped its ideology. Readers should watch for how this historical framing influences current debates on whether the Brotherhood can ever truly reintegrate into modern democratic systems or if the cycle of betrayal is inevitable.
