In an industry where innovation often feels like a slow crawl, Dylan Patel identifies a paradox: we are simultaneously in the "biggest supercycle ever seen" while hitting a "Moore's Wall" where massive research yields only incremental gains. This report from IEDM 2025 doesn't just list new specs; it exposes the desperate, high-stakes engineering gymnastics required to keep memory and logic scaling alive when traditional physics says stop. For the busy observer, the takeaway is clear: the era of easy density gains is over, replaced by a brutal competition of material science and architectural hacks that will define the next decade of computing power.

The Vertical Arms Race

Patel frames the current memory crisis not as a lack of demand, but as a physical constraint on factory space. "With memory prices exploding, 3D NAND technology is suddenly relevant again," he writes, noting that producers are now limited to upgrading existing lines rather than building new ones. The core of his argument rests on the shift from lateral scaling to vertical stacking. As Patel explains, "NAND is just about cramming as many storage cells onto the wafer as possible," a process that now manifests as vertical cylinders packed "like trees in a forest."

The data from SK Hynix illustrates the intensity of this vertical push. Patel highlights their new 321-layer process, which delivers "44% more memory per wafer than the previous generation." This isn't just a minor tweak; it's a structural overhaul involving a third "deck" of layers. However, Patel is quick to temper the excitement with a reality check on the manufacturing toll. He notes that while the layer count jumped 35%, the complexity skyrocketed, with "30% more process steps overall and 20% more etch steps."

Cheapest does not mean easiest. There are many, many challenges to increasing NAND layer count.

This framing is effective because it moves beyond the headline numbers to the operational friction. Critics might argue that focusing on the difficulty of etching ignores the sheer economic incentive driving these risks, but Patel's point stands: the cost of failure in these high-aspect-ratio holes is astronomical. The industry is betting that the 44% density gain justifies the 30% increase in process complexity, a gamble that requires perfect alignment as wafers begin to warp under the stress of nearly 100 additional layers.

Material Science as the New Frontier

When vertical stacking hits a wall, the industry turns to chemistry. Patel dedicates significant space to Samsung's controversial pivot from tungsten to molybdenum for their wordline metals. "Mo is more difficult in pretty much every way – chemically, mechanically, electrically – versus manufacturing with W," Patel writes, acknowledging the steep learning curve. Yet, the payoff is undeniable: Samsung claims a "40% reduction in contact resistance" and a "94% reduction in failure rate in lifetime testing."

The author details a clever workaround for molybdenum's tendency to oxidize: growing a seed layer of MoN before converting it to pure Mo. This allows for a "linerless" process, which is crucial because traditional liners are non-conductive and eat up valuable space. "A linerless process can enable both better performance and further shrink in future nodes," Patel observes. This is a pivotal moment for the industry, as it suggests that material substitution, rather than just geometric shrinking, is the next lever for growth.

However, the path is not without friction. The report notes that Lam Research is dominating the deposition tools for this new metal, taking share from competitors. This shift in the equipment supply chain is a subtle but critical signal of where the industry's capital is flowing. As Patel puts it, "The benefits are worth the trouble," but the implication is that only the most agile manufacturers will survive the transition.

Reading 32 different states from a traditional NAND cell is nearly impossible, the sensing margin is too narrow.

Patel also explores a more radical architectural change from SK Hynix: the "multi-site cell." By splitting a single channel into two half-cylinders, they aim to store 5 bits per cell. This is a clever solution to the physics problem of distinguishing between 32 voltage levels in a single cell. Instead, the system reads 6 levels from each of the two sites. While Patel admits this is "unlikely this technique is cost effective for now," the demonstration proves that the industry is willing to break traditional architectures to squeeze out more data. A counterargument worth considering is that the yield challenges of etching and depositing films into these asymmetric, split holes might render the technology commercially unviable before it ever reaches mass production.

The Interconnect Bottleneck

As logic chips shrink below 10 nanometers, the wires connecting transistors are becoming the new bottleneck. Patel points to Samsung's work with ruthenium (Ru) as the successor to copper. "Traditional copper (Cu) interconnects encounter a critical bottleneck caused by the 'size effect,' where resistivity rises sharply," he writes. Samsung's solution involves "Grain Orientation Engineering" to align the metal crystals, resulting in "46% lower resistance" in ultra-fine lines.

This section underscores a broader trend: the end of Moore's Law as we knew it. The industry is no longer just shrinking transistors; it is fundamentally re-engineering the materials that connect them. Imec's roadmap, cited by Patel, suggests a transition to ruthenium starting at the A14 node, with a shift to 16nm pitches representing a "practical limit" for current lithography. The move to fully self-aligned vias and new metal alloys isn't optional anymore; it is the only path forward.

It sometimes feels like Moore's Law has become Moore's Wall.

Patel's analysis here is particularly sharp because it connects the microscopic material changes to the macroscopic supply chain. The shift to ruthenium and the need for high-precision etching tools mean that the equipment manufacturers are just as critical as the chip designers. The "super-cycle" Patel mentions is driven not just by AI demand, but by the sheer necessity of these new technologies to keep performance growing.

Bottom Line

Patel's strongest argument is that the semiconductor industry is pivoting from geometric scaling to a complex mix of material science and architectural innovation to bypass physical limits. The biggest vulnerability in this narrative is the assumption that these increasingly expensive and complex processes—like 3-deck stacks and multi-site cells—can be made cost-effective at scale. The reader should watch for the commercial viability of these R&D breakthroughs, as the gap between a working lab device and a profitable product has never been wider.

It’s an odd time in the chipmaking industry. On one hand, we are ramping into the biggest supercycle ever seen. Advanced logic, DRAM, NAND, customers can’t get enough, chipmakers can’t build capacity fast enough, and we may soon be limited by fab equipment supply. On the other hand, the rapid technology improvement in scaling, power consumption, chip cost, etc. have slowed to a crawl. Massive research efforts yield small incremental gains. As costs increase but improvements asymptote and scaling runs out of room at the bottom, it sometimes feels like Moore’s Law has become Moore’s Wall.

The good news is the semiconductor industry has a long history of making naysayers look stupid in the long run. There are plenty of innovations on the bench that are promising for the next decade and beyond. In this report, as with 2022, 2023, and 2024, we’ll cover highlights from IEDM 2025 to find out what the future of chipmaking might look like.

With memory prices exploding, 3D NAND technology is suddenly relevant again. We’ll look at the technology and competitiveness of SK Hynix’s latest V9 NAND, Samsung’s use of Mo to improve its existing V9 products, and a big innovation that we may see in future production from SK Hynix. For advanced logic, we’ll look at interconnect metals beyond Cu, the potential of 2D materials to replace Si in transistors, and the progress in CFET, the next big inflection after gate-all-around.

3D NAND.

NAND scaling is critically relevant right now, as demand is spiking but there is no cleanroom space for building out capacity. Memory producers are limited to upgrading existing lines, so their supply is constrained by how dense their upgraded process is. For leading fabs, this is a 3xx-layer 3D NAND process, which yields on the order of 20-30 Gb/mm2 of memory. That pencils out to 30+ TB of memory on a single 12” wafer (note the difference between (b)its and (B)ytes in these abbreviations).

3D NAND: Hynix 321 Layer.

In the case of SK Hynix, the 321-layer process has 44% more memory per wafer than the previous generation 238-layer technology. If you are constrained by cleanroom space and by extension the number of wafers you can produce, 44% more memory on each of those wafers makes upgrades an obvious choice.
