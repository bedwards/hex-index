Rethinking Climate Change. The path to a 90% emissions reduction by 2035

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Aug 29, 2021 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through the paralysis of climate despair with a startling claim: we don't need to wait for political miracles or draconian regulations to solve the climate crisis. Instead, he argues that the market forces already driving down the cost of solar, electric vehicles, and lab-grown meat will trigger a cascade of disruptions so profound that they could slash global emissions by 90% by 2035. This is not a plea for hope; it is a data-driven forecast that the technology to save us is already cheaper than the fossil fuels we are trying to replace.

The Non-Linear Reality

Borlace begins by addressing the elephant in the room: the overwhelming sense that the situation is too entrenched to fix. He acknowledges that the "environmental breakdowns and their interconnected consequences appear to be so entrenched and advanced that it'd be easy to become quite despondent." However, he quickly pivots to the real question, which is not whether climate change is real, but "what exactly can we do to wrestle back some semblance of equilibrium in our earth systems."

The core of his argument rests on a fundamental misunderstanding of how change happens. We are conditioned to view problems as linear processes, like setting up an Excel spreadsheet, but the world operates on exponential curves. Borlace illustrates this by contrasting a 2014 IPCC prediction that renewables would reach only 4% of global capacity by 2100 against the reality of their explosive growth. "Technologies like wind and solar have seen exponential growth and are precipitous falling costs which have put them on course to hit that number by 2030 at 70 years sooner than the ipcc predicted," he notes. This framing is powerful because it shifts the burden of proof from the skeptics to the complacent; the delay isn't in the technology, but in our ability to recognize the speed of its adoption.

We're conditioned to tackle problems as linear processes that we can set up nicely in an excel spreadsheet but when it comes to technological disruptions history is littered with examples of long periods of technological stability punctuated by abrupt systemic transformations.

Critics might argue that market forces alone cannot solve a crisis that requires global coordination, pointing to the fact that cheaper technology doesn't automatically mean equitable distribution or immediate political will. Borlace anticipates this by emphasizing that these market transformations create "virtuous and vicious cycles" that force the issue, making the old technology economically unviable regardless of policy.

The Three Pillars of Disruption

The report Borlace analyzes, "Rethinking Climate Change" by the think tank RethinkX, identifies three specific sectors where this disruption is already underway: energy, transport, and food. In the energy sector, the combination of solar, wind, and batteries (SWB) is creating what the authors call a "clean energy superpower." The argument here is counter-intuitive: we don't need to store weeks of energy to solve intermittency. Instead, we will build so much capacity that we will have massive surpluses for most of the year.

"Future solar and wind generating capacity will greatly exceed the total electricity generating capacity installed today," Borlace explains. This surplus, available at near-zero marginal cost, will be used to power everything from desalination to industrial heating, effectively electrifying sectors that have been hard to decarbonize. The beauty of this approach is its decentralization; unlike massive fossil fuel plants, these systems can be installed anywhere, potentially leapfrogging energy poverty in developing nations.

In the transport sector, the disruption goes beyond simply swapping gas cars for electric ones. Borlace highlights the second phase of this shift: the move to "Transportation as a Service" (TaaS) via autonomous vehicles. The economics here are undeniable. "Once autonomous technology removes the labor cost of the ride services the cost per mile for tas will be 10 times cheaper than privately owned vehicles today." This isn't science fiction; it's basic arithmetic driven by the fact that electric drive trains can last over a million miles. The implication is a collapse in the demand for personal car ownership, which in turn reduces the need for roads, parking, and the manufacturing of billions of vehicles.

The operating costs of evs are already lower than internal combustion engine vehicles and even their initial costs are now rapidly approaching parity.

A counterargument worth considering is the social cost of this transition. If TaaS becomes dominant, what happens to the millions of livelihoods dependent on driving? Borlace touches on this, noting it is an "extremely important question," but the economic inevitability of the shift suggests that society will have to adapt its social safety nets rather than stop the technology.

The Food Revolution and the Land Bonus

Perhaps the most contentious yet transformative part of the analysis is the food sector. Borlace details how precision fermentation and cellular agriculture will make lab-grown proteins five times cheaper than animal protein by 2030 and ten times cheaper by 2035. The efficiency gains are staggering: "Precision fermentation foods will be as much as a hundred times more land efficient... 10 to 25 times more feedstock efficient... and 10 times more water efficient."

This economic disruption will trigger a "death spiral" for the livestock industry, not because of moral arguments, but because of simple cost. The most profound consequence of this shift is not just the reduction in methane emissions, but the liberation of land. "Today animal agriculture consumes 3.3 billion hectares of land... The disruption of these industries will free up 80 percent of that land," Borlace writes. This freed-up land, an area the size of the US, China, and Australia combined, becomes the key to the report's most optimistic projection.

Governments communities and interest groups must therefore begin planning today to take advantage of the enormous opportunities for conservation rewilding and ecological restoration that will emerge in the 2020s and 2030s.

The argument here is that we don't need to actively reforest the world with expensive, labor-intensive projects. Instead, if we stop using the land for farming, nature will do the work for us. Passive reforestation becomes the single biggest factor in decarbonization, accounting for a 20% reduction in emissions. This reframes the solution from a battle of engineering to a process of letting nature heal itself, provided we stop the pressure of industrial agriculture.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage of the RethinkX report offers a compelling, data-rich antidote to climate fatalism, arguing that the convergence of three technological disruptions will make a 90% emissions reduction by 2035 not just possible, but economically inevitable. The strongest part of this argument is its reliance on historical patterns of disruptive technology rather than wishful policy scenarios. However, its biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that the social and political fallout from such rapid economic shifts—particularly the loss of jobs in transport and agriculture—can be managed without significant friction or backlash. Readers should watch for how quickly these cost curves materialize in the next five years, as that will be the true test of this exponential forecast.

The technologies we need to achieve that goal all exist today and are either ready to deploy to market or are already deployed and can be scaled up immediately.
