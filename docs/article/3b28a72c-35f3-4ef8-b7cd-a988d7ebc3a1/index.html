Peter Singer - ordinary people are evil

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·Jan 23, 2020 · 28 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan doesn't just summarize a famous philosophy paper; he weaponizes it against the comfortable assumptions of modern life. His coverage of Peter Singer's 1972 work, "Famine, Affluence, and Morality," strips away the polite fiction that charity is optional, arguing instead that our daily spending habits on luxuries are acts of moral failure. For the busy professional who views their career success as a neutral baseline, Kaplan's framing is a jarring reminder that the status quo is not just inefficient, but actively evil.

The Supererogatory Trap

Kaplan begins by dismantling the distinction most of us rely on to sleep at night: the difference between what we must do and what is nice to do. He introduces the technical terms "supererogatory" (extra good deeds) and "obligatory" (moral duties) with a coffee-and-danish analogy that feels deceptively simple. "The act, that action is super derogatory. It's extra. It's super. You didn't have to do it, but it was a really nice thing to do," Kaplan explains, using the scenario of a colleague bringing treats to a meeting. This distinction is the bedrock of our moral comfort zone; it allows us to feel good when we donate, but absolves us of guilt when we don't.

However, Kaplan quickly pivots to show how Singer collapses this distinction entirely. He notes that in our society, "People do not feel in any way ashamed or guilty about spending money on new clothes or a new car instead of giving it to famine relief." The author's coverage is striking here because he points out that the alternative—wearing old clothes to save a life—simply "does not occur to them." This is the crux of the problem: our moral blindness isn't a lack of information, but a structural failure of imagination. By framing the purchase of a new car as a moral choice rather than a lifestyle preference, Kaplan forces the reader to confront the radical nature of Singer's thesis.

The only thing that can be justified is the way of thinking where you think that giving to famine relief instead of buying new clothes or a new car or whatever, giving to famine relief is obligatory.

The Argument from Necessity

Kaplan then reconstructs Singer's logic, breaking it down into four premises that are difficult to refute individually but devastating when combined. He highlights the first premise as the most controversial yet crucial: "If it is in our power to prevent something very bad from happening without thereby sacrificing anything of moral significance, then we must morally do it." The author's commentary is effective because he isolates this claim, noting that while it sounds abstract, it applies directly to the mundane choices of the 21st century.

He proceeds to validate the other premises with brutal efficiency. Hunger and disease are "very bad"; our luxuries are "not of moral significance"; and organizations like Oxfam and UNICEF effectively use our money to prevent suffering. Kaplan writes, "By donating money to relief agencies like Oxfam, we could prevent hunger, disease, and other sources of suffering, disability, and death." This empirical claim is unassailable, yet it leads to a conclusion that feels impossible to accept. The argument is valid, and as Kaplan puts it, "The upshot or the punchline is this. If you don't give to famine relief, Singer thinks you've done something wrong. You've done something evil, bad."

Critics might note that this logic ignores the psychological necessity of small luxuries for mental health or the systemic barriers that prevent effective aid distribution. However, Kaplan's coverage suggests that Singer anticipates these objections and dismisses them as rationalizations for moral cowardice. The author emphasizes that "Every time you buy new clothes that you don't absolutely need... you're doing something wrong." This relentless application of the principle leaves no room for the "good enough" morality that most of us practice.

The Radical Conclusion

The power of Kaplan's commentary lies in his refusal to soften the blow. He explicitly states that this argument requires us to "completely rework your life if your life is going to be a moral one." It is not a call for minor adjustments or occasional donations; it is a demand for a total overhaul of how we view our relationship to wealth. Kaplan notes that "Singer thinks that your whole life needs to be reworked if you're going to live in a morally acceptable way," and he does so without knowing anything specific about the reader's life. This universality is what makes the argument so terrifyingly persuasive.

He concludes by reiterating that the standard view of charity is unjustifiable. "This way of looking at the matter cannot be justified," Kaplan asserts, referring to the societal norm where buying a cup of coffee is seen as a neutral act of convenience. By linking the purchase of a coffee to the death of a starving person, Kaplan forces a collision between our daily habits and our moral ideals. The argument holds up because it relies on a premise that is hard to deny: preventing death is more important than comfort.

Every time you buy new clothes that you don't absolutely need, or you buy a new car when you could just drive your old car... you're doing something wrong. You're doing something bad.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's coverage succeeds by refusing to let the reader off the hook with vague platitudes about "doing good." The strongest part of his argument is the rigorous logical construction that makes Singer's radical conclusion inescapable. Its biggest vulnerability is the practical impossibility of living by such a standard without total societal collapse, a tension Kaplan acknowledges but does not fully resolve. The reader must decide if the logic is sound even if the lifestyle is unlivable.
