Artificial starch from CO2. Ground breaking new tech could reduce land and water use by 90%

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Dec 12, 2021 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace delivers a rare glimpse into a scientific breakthrough that could fundamentally decouple human food production from the planet's finite land and water resources. While the narrative of climate change often feels like a slow-motion disaster, Borlace highlights a specific, tangible intervention: the artificial synthesis of starch from carbon dioxide, a process that claims to slash agricultural land use by 90%. This is not merely incremental improvement; it is a potential paradigm shift in how we feed a growing global population without devouring the rainforests that remain.

The Problem with Natural Photosynthesis

Borlace anchors his argument in the staggering inefficiency of our current food systems. He points out that "fifty percent of all habitable land on earth is now dedicated to agriculture," with the vast majority serving livestock rather than direct human consumption. As our population races toward 10 billion, the traditional model of farming is becoming increasingly precarious. "As climate change causes arctic blasts, unprecedented flooding, historic heat waves and disastrous droughts that are making farming increasingly unviable in many parts of the world," the author argues, the need for a controlled, weather-independent alternative has never been more urgent.

The author does not shy away from the sheer scale of the challenge, noting that waiting for nature to evolve a faster way to produce starch is "bonkers." He correctly identifies that while photosynthesis is a miracle of evolution, it is a slow, 60-step process that struggles to keep pace with human industrial demand. By framing the current reliance on open-field agriculture as a vulnerability rather than a tradition, Borlace sets the stage for why a laboratory solution is not just a scientific curiosity but a strategic necessity.

The Engineering of Artificial Starch

The core of Borlace's coverage focuses on a team at the Chinese Academy of Sciences who have developed an "artificial starch anabolic pathway," or ASAP. This is where the piece moves from problem identification to solution architecture. Borlace explains that the team reduced the natural 60-step biological process down to a sleek 11-step "chemoenzymatic" system. He walks the reader through the complex chemistry with surprising clarity, describing how carbon dioxide is reacted with hydrogen to produce methanol, which is then converted through a series of enzyme-assisted reactions into starch.

"The starch synthesis rate of the ASAP 3.0 chemoenzymatic pathway was 22 nanomoles per minute per milligram of total catalyst and proteins... which equate to a production rate of starch that's eight and a half times the rate of starch synthesis via that natural Calvin cycle in maize."

This statistic is the piece's strongest evidence. It transforms the abstract concept of "lab-grown food" into a measurable efficiency gain. Borlace's ability to translate these nanomole-per-minute metrics into a comparison with maize synthesis makes the breakthrough feel concrete. However, he wisely acknowledges the complexity, admitting that the process required multiple iterations (ASAP 1.0 through 3.1) to overcome "unpredictable and undesired interactions" that initially inhibited the reaction. This transparency adds credibility; it suggests the science is rigorous, not just theoretical.

Efficiency and the Energy Question

A critical component of any synthetic biology claim is the energy cost. Borlace addresses this head-on, dismantling the assumption that artificial production is inherently energy-prohibitive. He breaks down the theoretical efficiency of the process, noting that while the solar-to-starch efficiency is around 7%, this is "about three and a half times the efficiency of solar to starch conversion that plants manage in a natural environment."

"According to the current technical parameters, the annual production of starch in a one cubic meter bioreactor theoretically equates with the starch annual yield from growing one third of a hectare of maize."

This comparison is the piece's most compelling argument for scalability. It suggests that the physical footprint of production could shrink from hectares of farmland to a single cubic meter of bioreactor space. Borlace argues that if costs can be brought down to match agricultural planting practices, the potential to "save more than 90 percent of the cultivated land and water currently used in the industry" is real. Critics might note that the energy inputs for hydrogen production and the heat/pressure required for chemical steps remain significant hurdles, and the 7% efficiency figure, while better than plants, still implies a massive energy infrastructure is needed. Yet, Borlace's framing that this is "comparable to the theoretical photosynthetic efficiency" effectively neutralizes the most common counter-argument that synthetic biology is too energy-intensive to be viable.

"Waiting for photosynthesis of starch to just sort of happen in crops planted in millions upon millions of hectares of open fields that used to be rain forests is bonkers."

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage is a masterclass in making complex biochemistry accessible and urgent. His strongest move is reframing the inefficiency of natural photosynthesis not as a biological wonder, but as a bottleneck for human survival. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in the gap between theoretical efficiency in a lab and the economic reality of industrial scaling, a transition that has tripped up many green technologies. However, the sheer magnitude of the potential land and water savings makes this a development that demands close attention as it moves from the laboratory to the bioreactor.
