America must embrace the electric age, or fall behind

Noah Smith · Noahpinion ·Jan 9, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Noah Smith makes a provocative claim that cuts through the usual climate-versus-economy debate: America's failure to dominate the electric age isn't a moral or environmental shortfall, but a catastrophic industrial blind spot. He argues that by treating electric vehicles and batteries as political symbols rather than superior technologies, the United States is ceding the foundational stack of future manufacturing to its primary geopolitical rival. For a busy reader, this reframing is essential—it shifts the conversation from ideology to raw national power.

The Musk Paradox and the Electric Stack

Smith begins with a jarring comparison, noting that "Elon Musk is America's China." He isn't praising the individual but highlighting a structural gap: "what the entire economy of China is set up to do — scale up high-tech manufacturing businesses — is something that only one man in America knows how to do." This observation forces the reader to confront the fragility of relying on a single outlier to maintain industrial parity. Smith suggests that without Musk's specific track record with SpaceX and Tesla, the United States would be losing the space race to Beijing.

The core of Smith's argument rests on the concept of the "Electric Tech Stack." He posits that the world is undergoing a "wholesale shift away from combustion, toward technologies that harness electricity directly." This isn't just about cars; it's about the physics of control. As Smith explains, "pushing electrons through a wire simply offers you much finer control over where the energy goes than blowing up hot gases to turn some gears." He traces this capability to three late-20th-century breakthroughs: the lithium-ion battery, the rare-earth electric motor, and power electronics. These inventions solved the historical limitations of energy density and torque that once made combustion superior.

"We no longer live among truly distinct technological paradigms, but within a world of variations on one single idea: the smartphone, endlessly turned inside and out and scaled across every domain."

Smith's framing here is potent. He draws on the "network effect" of consumer electronics to explain why Chinese firms like BYD and Xiaomi can outcompete legacy American automakers. Because an electric vehicle is essentially "a smartphone with wheels," the same supply chains, components, and manufacturing efficiencies apply to everything from drones to data centers. This creates a massive economies-of-scale advantage that the U.S. is currently ignoring. Critics might argue that legacy automakers are slow to pivot due to genuine market signals rather than political ideology, but Smith counters that the market is shifting globally while American policy actively resists it.

The Political Blind Spot

The article takes a sharp turn into policy analysis, arguing that the United States is missing this revolution because it has "insisted on thinking of EVs in terms of climate — as a 'green' technology whose purpose is to save the environment, rather than a superior technology whose purpose is to save you time and money." Smith points out that when the executive branch ties these technologies to the environmental movement, it alienates a broad swath of the population and invites political backlash. He notes that subsidies were canceled and efficiency standards modified, leading major automakers like Ford to write off billions and pivot back to gas-powered trucks.

This political framing has tangible consequences. Smith observes that "the Biden administration promised to build a vast network of EV charging stations, but managed to build almost zero," largely due to bureaucratic hurdles. The result is a self-fulfilling prophecy where consumers avoid EVs due to range anxiety, which then justifies the retreat from electric production. "EVs are going to win," Smith asserts, "and there will be a tipping point — different in each country — where the whole market just flips from combustion to electric." He warns that the decline of gas stations will accelerate this flip through a network effect, making the transition irreversible once it begins.

National Security and the Cost of Forfeiture

Perhaps the most urgent part of Smith's commentary is the link between consumer electronics and national defense. He argues that "drones are taking over the modern battlefield, and drones require batteries and rare-earth electric motors — the same components that go into the EVs that America is now refusing to build." By treating the electric transition as a partisan issue, the U.S. risks becoming dependent on Chinese supply chains for critical defense hardware. "China generates much more electricity than the U.S. does," Smith notes, partly because it is willing to build solar infrastructure where American permitting laws and political hostility block it.

"Because we collectively decided that EVs are hippie-dippy climate bullshit, we ignored the key physical technologies that increasingly underlie all of manufacturing, including defense manufacturing."

Smith's historical perspective underscores the gravity of this failure. He reminds readers that America has led every major technological revolution, from railroads to the internet. "But now we are missing the big one," he writes. "We are missing the Electric Tech Stack." The implication is stark: if the U.S. forfeits this stack, it forfeits its ability to lead in AI, robotics, and defense. The argument suggests that the administration's focus on political signaling over industrial capacity is a strategic error that threatens long-term national security.

Bottom Line

Smith's strongest move is decoupling the electric revolution from climate politics to reveal its true nature as a contest for industrial supremacy and military capability. His argument is vulnerable to the counterpoint that market forces, not just policy, are driving the hesitation of legacy automakers, yet his data on global adoption and component convergence is compelling. The reader should watch for whether the U.S. can decouple its industrial policy from partisan gridlock before the "tipping point" leaves the country permanently behind in the race for the electric age.
