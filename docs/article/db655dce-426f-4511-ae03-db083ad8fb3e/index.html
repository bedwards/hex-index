Deep Dives

Explore these related deep dives:

  • Working capital

    The article hinges on Bezos's unique ability to fund growth by collecting cash from customers before paying suppliers, a specific financial mechanic that distinguishes Amazon's burn rate from companies like Uber that lack this structural advantage.

  • Present value

    Understanding this core finance concept is essential to grasping Bezos's 1997 argument that sacrificing current earnings is rational only if the discounted future cash flows exceed the cost of capital, a calculation that often fails for modern AI startups.

  • Customer lifetime value

    The article critiques the blind application of the 'Amazon playbook' by highlighting that Bezos's strategy relied on predictable, high-margin recurring revenue from Prime, a metric that differs fundamentally from the volatile unit economics of generative AI services.

Bad Analogies

I saw an interaction on twitter the other day that I’m not going to share here because these specific people don’t deserve to be singled out because almost everyone is guilty of something similar.

Basically, one person said, “It’s amazing that OpenAI was able to raise $122 billion when they don’t have a single business that works,” and the other person replied, “Yeah they do they have a bunch of different business lines doing billions of dollars in revenue,” and the OP responded, “Yes but none of them is profitable, they’re losing a lot of money,” and the replier replied, “You could have said the same thing about Amazon!”

Amazon’s success has done a great deal of harm to a great number of companies.

Obviously, it’s done more good. AWS is a miracle. But go with me.
