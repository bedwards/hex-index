Devin Stone doesn't just dissect a movie; he exposes how Hollywood's theological dramas collapse under the weight of basic property law. While the film God's Not Dead 3 leans heavily on a narrative of religious persecution, Stone reveals that the central conflict is actually a mundane, and legally flawed, dispute over land ownership and eminent domain. For busy readers navigating a world of complex legal claims, Stone's breakdown offers a rare clarity: the movie's villains aren't a shadowy government conspiracy, but rather a university administration that has failed to follow the most elementary rules of civil procedure.

The Illusion of Persecution

Stone immediately dismantles the film's premise that the government is waging a "Witch Hunt" against religious leaders. He points out that the plot hinges on a mandate for pastors to turn over sermon transcripts, a scenario he identifies as legally impossible in the United States. "The only thing that this could possibly be related to is What's called the Johnson Amendment which it's not a mandate against churches being political," Stone writes. "It is that if you explicitly get into politics then you potentially lose your status as a non-profit."

This distinction is crucial. Stone argues that the film conflates tax-exempt status with free speech rights, creating a false sense of crisis. The narrative suggests that police will raid churches to seize sermons, but Stone notes that "no one's going to like the IRS police aren't going to come around and force you to turn over your sermon uh that's just not a thing." This highlights a significant gap between the film's dramatic stakes and the actual legal landscape. Critics might argue that artistic license allows for exaggerated scenarios to drive a plot, but Stone's point stands: the movie relies on a fundamental misunderstanding of how the Internal Revenue Service operates to manufacture tension.

The Rube Goldberg of Legal Absurdity

As the film progresses, the legal errors compound, moving from constitutional misunderstandings to procedural impossibilities. Stone zeroes in on the scene where a church is threatened with demolition and the pastor produces an injunction obtained through a private meeting with a judge. Stone calls this out as a gross violation of ethics. "If you talk to the judge without opposing counsel present uh generally that is a gross violation of both court rules and ethics rules that's an ex-party communication," he explains.

He further dissects the timeline, noting that emergency injunctions require strict adherence to due process, not a casual Sunday morning chat in a church. "Even that takes 48 Hours usually," Stone adds, dismantling the idea that a judge could simply sign off on a stop-work order instantly to save a building. The film treats the legal system as a magical tool that can be wielded by anyone with the right moral conviction, ignoring the rigid structures that prevent exactly this kind of chaos. Stone's commentary here is particularly sharp because it doesn't just say "this is wrong"; it explains why the mechanism fails, turning a plot hole into a teachable moment about judicial ethics.

Property Rights vs. The Narrative

The most damning critique Stone offers concerns the land itself. The film suggests the university has the power to seize the church simply because they want the land for a new student center. Stone clarifies that while eminent domain is real, it is not a blank check for government entities to take private property without cause or fair compensation. "The church owns the land this doesn't involve the first amendment in any way because the church owns this land," Stone states. "The public university is going to try to use what's called eminent domain where you know a government entity can take over land to repurpose it for some other."

He points out that the university would need a "good faith uh you know non-anti-religious reason" to proceed, and the church would be entitled to fair market value. The movie ignores the valuation process entirely, jumping straight to a moral showdown. Stone notes that the film's characters are so focused on the "holy war" aspect that they miss the actual legal leverage they hold. "You would want someone who specializes in property law property law is is very complex uh there's a lot of ins and outs," he remarks, mocking the movie's decision to have a "social justice attorney" handle a complex real estate dispute. This oversight strips the film of its intellectual credibility, turning a potential courtroom drama into a fantasy where legal strategy is irrelevant.

You would want someone who specializes in property law property law is is very complex uh there's a lot of ins and outs you would want someone who has that built-in institutional knowledge you would you would definitely not want to hire this guy.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's greatest strength is his ability to translate dense legal concepts into accessible, common-sense critiques that reveal the fragility of the film's plot. The strongest part of his argument is the exposure of the "Rube Goldberg" legal mechanics, showing how the movie ignores the very real, very boring rules of property law to sustain its drama. However, his critique assumes the audience cares about legal accuracy over emotional resonance, a trade-off the filmmakers clearly made. For the discerning reader, Stone's analysis serves as a vital reminder that even in fiction, the law has limits that cannot be wished away by good intentions.
