In 2026, will Americans finally turn against oligarchs?

Matt Stoller · ·Dec 26, 2025 ·14 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Matt Stoller makes a startling claim: the path to democratic renewal isn't found in sunny optimism, but in the collective anger of a public finally waking up to systemic theft. While most observers see the current economic malaise as a sign of despair, Stoller argues we are witnessing the necessary, painful birth of a new anti-monopoly movement that could reshape American politics by 2026.

The Myth of Cynicism

Stoller begins by dismantling the idea that cynicism is a sign of political sophistication. He turns to the winter of 1932, quoting Supreme Court Justice Louis Brandeis, who told a visitor during the depths of the Great Depression, "the worst took place before the crash." Stoller uses this historical pivot to argue that the true danger to democracy isn't the economic collapse itself, but the "numbness to injustice" that precedes it. He writes, "Disillusionment, a numbness to injustice, is the great villain of democracy." This framing is powerful because it reframes current public frustration not as apathy, but as the first spark of recovery.

The author draws a sharp parallel between the 1920s and today. Just as voters in the roaring twenties became "docile, and they will not recover from being so for many years," Stoller suggests we are now breaking out of a similar trance. He points to the 1920s as a time when "the imbalances and corruption unmasked in the 1930s built up" precisely because the public had lost its ability to be outraged. The core of his argument is that material fixes cannot happen until the public recovers its belief in collective action. As Stoller puts it, "It was the public learning and regaining its liberty that had to come before the material part could be fixed."

From Numbness to Viral Outrage

The piece shifts from historical theory to immediate, tangible evidence with the story of Katie Van Dyck, an antitrust lawyer who testified before Congress about the privatization of youth sports. Stoller admits he initially doubted the hearing would gain traction, noting that Congress is often "close to finalizing legislation in this area, and there's no scandalous news peg." Yet, the testimony went viral, resonating because it exposed a universal pain point: the commodification of childhood.

Stoller highlights how private equity firms have applied a ruthless playbook to youth hockey, football, and baseball, driving participation costs up 46% in just five years. He quotes Van Dyck's observation that this is "the same playbook that private equity used to consolidate industries as varied as veterinary clinics, nursing homes, hospitals, and firetrucks—stripping assets, raising prices, and degrading quality." This connection is the article's strongest analytical move, linking the abstract concept of market consolidation to the very personal experience of parents drowning in debt for their children's activities.

A sad story of corruption is not new, or unusual. What I believe is new and unusual is a popular recognition that it is systemic in the American economy, and a widespread revulsion towards the people doing it.

The author notes that this isn't just a niche issue; it has captured the attention of unlikely allies, from the "bro network" of Barstool Sports to major news outlets. This broad coalition suggests a shift in the Overton window. However, critics might argue that viral moments often fail to translate into lasting legislative change, especially when the beneficiaries of these monopolies hold immense lobbying power. Stoller counters this by pointing to the fire truck industry, where his previous reporting helped spur nine lawsuits and a Senate hearing, proving that public outrage can indeed force institutional accountability.

The Limits of the Establishment

Stoller does not shy away from the failures of both major political parties. He acknowledges that while the public is angry, the political machinery is often designed to protect the very interests causing the pain. He notes that the Democratic National Commission recently buried a review of their 2024 loss to avoid embarrassment, and that figures like Chuck Schumer "do not believe their job is to do anything but be nice to the wealthy." Similarly, he critiques the Republican approach, stating that despite populist rhetoric, the party has "largely supported the interests of the most extractive industries in the U.S., from gambling to private equity to crypto to big tech."

The author argues that the prevailing political ideology has shifted away from economic justice toward a focus on social identity and corporate-friendly "human resources" frameworks. He writes, "For nearly my entire life... politics has been about social identity, forms of dispute that push us into ethnic grievances against one another." This critique is sharp, suggesting that the establishment's refusal to address market concentration is a deliberate choice to maintain the status quo. Yet, Stoller sees cracks in the armor. He points to unlikely figures like Erik Prince and Mitt Romney questioning the current economic model, and local politicians in New York and New Jersey beginning to govern based on anti-monopoly principles.

The Turning Point

The article concludes with a cautiously optimistic view of the future. Stoller suggests that the public has reached a tipping point where they no longer believe that "powerful people will always do whatever they want" is an unchangeable fact, but rather a solvable problem. He cites an NBC poll where 54% of Americans felt powerless, but interprets this not as defeat, but as a clear-eyed recognition of the enemy. "The public has started to see the superrich as loafers, as corporate welfare cheats," Stoller writes. "Political leaders can make big and bold claims, and the public will give them a hearing."

He warns that the coming years will likely get worse before they get better, as consolidation continues in sectors like AI and healthcare. "There's a huge consolidation boom; last week, for instance, Nvidia just openly bought Groq, which will likely preserve its monopoly in AI chips." Despite this, the emergence of an "anti-monopoly framework" in the political mainstream is a phenomenon Stoller has "never seen" before. The argument rests on the belief that the "sad experience" of the last decade is finally forging a new political consciousness.

The anti-monopoly framework is entering the political mainstream, and that's something I've never seen.

Bottom Line

Stoller's most compelling contribution is his reframing of public anger not as a symptom of democratic decay, but as the essential precondition for its restoration. While the piece perhaps underestimates the structural inertia of the political establishment and the sheer financial power of the oligarchs it seeks to challenge, its diagnosis of the public mood is spot-on. The reader should watch for whether this newfound revulsion can survive the next election cycle and translate into concrete antitrust legislation, or if it remains a fleeting moment of viral outrage.

In 2026, will Americans finally turn against oligarchs?

by Matt Stoller · · Read full article

In the winter of 1932, during the depths of the Great Depression, a visitor asked Supreme Court Justice Louis Brandeis if he thought the worst was over. “Oh yes,” he said, “the worst took place before the crash.”

Brandeis, who had predicted the disaster before it occurred, believed that the sunny lies of the roaring twenties had deluded the American people. The depression, as horrible as it was, as shameless were the business tycoons who caused the crash, was fostering a great debunking of myths. As a result, he believed, America would “gain much from her sad experience.”

There’s a misplaced belief that optimism about democratic politics is sunny and naive, reflecting of joyful circumstances, while cynicism is savvy and realistic, reflecting the grittiness of real life. The truth, as Brandeis knew, is the opposite. Disillusionment, a numbness to injustice, is the great villain of democracy. In the 1920s, after two decades of reform and a great war, the voters had become cynics. As one famous Senator, Hiram Johnson, put it, the people had become “docile, and they will not recover from being so for many years.”

It was during that time of great cynicism that the imbalances and corruption unmasked in the 1930s built up. Only when the public recovered its ability to be outraged, to believe in collective action, that Americans could once again see their society as a free people and act upon it. The actual economic collapse started in 1929, and it continued and worsened even as Brandeis became more optimistic. It was the public learning and regaining its liberty that had to come before the material part could be fixed.

Two weeks ago, I helped a colleague, an antitrust lawyer named Katie Van Dyck, prepare for a Congressional hearing on youth sports titled “Benched: The Crisis in American Youth Sports and Its Cost to Our Future.

I’ve helped prep a lot of people to testify. Most get little attention. Van Dyck was talking about private equity and its roll-up of youth hockey, football, baseball, cheer, and so forth. The cost of youth sports participation has increased 46% in just five years, twice the rate of inflation, with parents often going into debt so their kids can play. The strategies to extract are varied, from overpriced cheerleading outfits to monopolization of hockey rinks to questionable promises to high school athletes they’ll get college scholarships.
