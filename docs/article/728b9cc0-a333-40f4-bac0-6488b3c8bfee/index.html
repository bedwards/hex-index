The Last American Gangster Epic

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Aug 31, 2023 · 50 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden makes a provocative claim that cuts through the noise of modern crime cinema: American Gangster is not just another entry in the genre, but the definitive "Last American Gangster Epic" that bridges the gap between the mythic Italian mob stories of the 1970s and the fragmented, television-dominated crime dramas of the 2000s. While others see a standard biopic, van der Linden identifies a structural pivot where the genre stops glorifying the family and starts dissecting the business, all while introducing a radical new dynamic: the honest cop as a tragic, isolated figure rather than a mere plot device.

The Fragmentation of the Mob Myth

Van der Linden begins by contextualizing the shift in the 2000s, noting that the era was defined by a "democratization" of the genre. He observes that the focus moved away from the "mob boss sitting at the top of the mountain" to the "middle guy and the smaller guys." This is a crucial distinction. The grand narratives of The Godfather gave way to smaller, international, and often more cynical tales like City of God or Eastern Promises. As van der Linden puts it, the gangster movie in this decade became "just about the business... getting the quick buck and then getting out." This reframing is effective because it acknowledges a cultural fatigue with the romanticized mob family; the audience no longer believes in the loyalty of the organization, only the transaction of the crime.

However, van der Linden argues that American Gangster stands apart because it refuses to shrink the scope. Instead, it offers a "sweeping crime saga" that feels like a direct continuation of the epic tradition. He notes that while the film follows the classic "rise and fall" structure, it executes a "strong deviation" by centering on a Black gangster in Harlem rather than the traditional Italian-American narrative. This choice is not merely cosmetic; it fundamentally alters the power dynamics and the cultural landscape of the story, forcing a confrontation with race that the older epics often sidestepped.

"The gangster movie at this point... is just all about the business. It's not about becoming part of some family or rising to the top."

Critics might note that by calling this the "last" epic, van der Linden risks ignoring the massive cultural footprint of television shows like The Wire and Breaking Bad, which arguably took over the mantle of the crime saga entirely. Yet, his point holds weight when considering the specific constraints of the feature film format, which struggled to find its footing in the post-Goodfellas era.

The Mirror of Corruption

The most compelling part of van der Linden's analysis is his examination of the film's dual protagonists. He highlights how the film deliberately inverts the moral compass of its characters to create a unique tension. Frank Lucas, the gangster, is portrayed with a "cool demeanor and calm stoic intelligence," almost a "Michael Corleone" figure, who is a devoted family man and churchgoer. Conversely, Richie Roberts, the detective, is an "incorruptible" force professionally but a "deadbeat dad" in his personal life, mired in divorce and isolation.

This inversion serves a specific narrative purpose. Van der Linden explains that the film uses this contrast to show how the "mask" of virtue eventually crumbles for the criminal, while the honest cop finds redemption through his professional integrity. He writes, "Richie gets to have this Revelation... to enact the same principles that he uses on the job to his private life." This is a sophisticated reading of the character arcs. It suggests that the film is not just about catching a criminal, but about how the environment of crime corrupts everyone, even those who think they are immune.

The author also points out the historical distortion at play. While the names are real, the details are often fabricated to fit the genre tropes. The real Frank Lucas was reportedly more violent and less intelligent, but the film elevates him to a mythic status. Van der Linden notes that this pressure to "contort the characters so that they feel like a gangster movie" is a testament to how deeply ingrained the genre's expectations have become. The film had to force reality into the shape of a myth to satisfy the audience's hunger for the epic.

"The real life story itself might have been very... there's this pressure to contort the characters so that it feels like a gangster movie."

This observation is vital. It reminds the listener that American Gangster is as much a product of Hollywood's need for a specific narrative arc as it is a historical account. The "truth" is secondary to the "epic" feel.

The Lone Wolf Within the System

Perhaps the most significant addition van der Linden identifies is the role of the police. In previous eras, corrupt cops were background noise or plot devices. In American Gangster, the honest cop is a central, tragic figure. Van der Linden describes Richie Roberts as an "outsider within his own police force," a pariah because he refused to take a bribe. This isolation creates a unique friction: "whenever the cops feel like they cannot trust their own," the honest man becomes the enemy of the institution.

This dynamic adds a layer of psychological depth rarely seen in the genre. The film explores how the system punishes integrity. Van der Linden notes that Richie's partner, unable to cope with this isolation, turns to drug addiction, creating a personal link to the very criminal Richie is hunting. This is a brilliant narrative device that ties the two worlds together not just through a cat-and-mouse game, but through shared trauma and consequence. As van der Linden states, "The corrupt police never really become Central characters in the story which is kind of what we see here."

"It's one of the most interesting additions... the inclusion of the police force perspective and specifically of this one character Richie who is an outsider within his own police force."

This framing elevates the film from a simple crime thriller to a study of institutional decay. It suggests that the true villain is not just the drug lord, but the culture that makes honesty a liability.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's argument that American Gangster is the genre's final epic is persuasive because it correctly identifies the film's unique synthesis of the old and the new: the grand scale of The Godfather meets the cynical, fragmented reality of the 2000s. His strongest insight lies in the analysis of the inverted morality between the gangster and the cop, a structural choice that gives the film its emotional weight. The piece's only vulnerability is the assertion that the film is the "last" of its kind, a claim that might be challenged by the sheer volume of high-quality crime dramas that followed, even if none quite matched this specific blend of myth and reality. For the busy listener, this commentary offers a clear lens through which to view a complex film, revealing why it remains a singular moment in cinema history.
