Let's Read! Alan Turing, 1950, "Computing Machinery and Intelligence"

Commentary by Brian Edwards

Kenny Easwaran reframes Alan Turing's 1950 masterpiece not merely as a philosophical curiosity, but as a startlingly accurate forecast of the very debates dominating artificial intelligence today. While most introductions treat the "Turing Test" as a static definition of machine intelligence, Easwaran reveals how Turing strategically sidestepped the metaphysical trap of defining "thought" to focus on a practical, behavioral benchmark that remains the industry standard.

The Architect of the Universal Machine

Easwaran begins by grounding the reader in Turing's monumental, often overlooked, pre-1950 contributions. Before the world knew him as the father of AI, he had already established the theoretical limits of computation. "In his doctoral work in 1936 he wrote a paper on computable numbers where he introduced the idea of what is now called a turing machine," Easwaran writes, noting that this was the first abstract concept of a general-purpose computer capable of solving any conceivable problem. This context is vital; it reminds us that Turing was not a dreamer guessing at the future, but an engineer who had already proven that some problems are fundamentally unsolvable by any machine.

The author highlights the tragic irony that Turing's own work on the "halting problem"—the inability of a computer to predict if it will crash—explains why modern software still fails unpredictably. "The fact that this is unsolvable is why no matter how far computers Advance we always occasionally end up with a program that crashes the computer," Easwaran notes. This is a brilliant, concise way to make abstract theory feel immediate and relevant to anyone who has ever lost data. By establishing Turing's mastery over both the possibilities and the hard limits of machines, Easwaran sets the stage for why Turing's 1950 questions carry such weight.

From Codebreaking to the Imitation Game

The commentary then pivots to Turing's wartime heroism at Bletchley Park, where he moved from theoretical math to the physical reality of electronic computing. Easwaran describes how Turing and his team built the Colossus, the world's first electronic computer, to crack the Lorenz Cipher used by Hitler. "During the war they discovered that there was a separate Code system that wasn't the Enigma machine... and so touring together with a larger team built an electronic Computing machine called The Colossus," he explains. This historical bridge is essential; it shows that when Turing asked "Can machines think?" in 1950, he was speaking from the vantage point of someone who had already built the hardware that made the question real.

Easwaran points out that Turing was already predicting the trajectory of computing power decades before the transistor revolution. "Already in 1950 he was predicting the course of the development of computing technology all the way out until the end of the 20th century," the author observes. This foresight is what makes the paper timeless. Turing wasn't just asking if a machine could mimic a human; he was asking if the paradigm of machine intelligence would eventually align with human learning. Easwaran notes that while the Chomskian paradigm of symbolic logic dominated the 20th century, "in recent years it has seemed more plausible that the Turing Paradigm may be more relevant now," suggesting that neural networks and machine learning are finally catching up to Turing's intuition.

The new form of the problem can be described in terms of a game which we call the imitation game.

Redefining Intelligence Through the Imitation Game

The core of Easwaran's analysis focuses on Turing's brilliant rhetorical move: replacing the vague question "Can machines think?" with a concrete game. Easwaran explains that Turing found the standard definitions of "machine" and "think" dangerous because they relied on "statistical survey such as a Gallup poll," which he deemed absurd. Instead, Turing proposed a scenario involving an interrogator, a man, and a woman, where the goal is to identify who is who based solely on typed answers.

"Instead of attempting such a definition I shall replace the question by another which is closely related to it and is expressed in relatively unambiguous words," Easwaran quotes Turing. This shift is the article's strongest point. It moves the debate from metaphysics to observable behavior. Easwaran highlights the specific constraints Turing placed on the game: the answers must be typed so that "tones of voice may not help the interrogator." This detail is crucial because it isolates intelligence from physical form, a concept that feels even more prescient in the age of text-based AI chatbots.

The author walks through Turing's sample dialogue, where a machine feigns human limitations, such as taking a long time to do math or claiming it cannot write poetry. "The answer or pauses about 30 seconds and then eventually gives the answer 105 621," Easwaran describes, noting that the machine even gets the math wrong to appear more human. Easwaran adds a sharp contemporary observation: "In the past year chat gbt has shown that actually computers can write poetry very quickly and easily much more than most people." This contrast underscores how quickly the "hard" problems Turing identified have been solved, while the "easy" ones of deception remain the core of the test.

Critics might note that by focusing solely on text-based interaction, Turing's test ignores the embodied nature of human cognition. If a machine cannot feel pain or physical hunger, can it truly "think" even if it passes the test? Easwaran acknowledges this by quoting Turing's dismissal of physical replication: "It is possible that at some time this might be done but even supposing this invention available we should feel there was little point in trying to make a thinking machine More Human by dressing it up in such artificial flesh." This remains a contentious but vital distinction in modern AI ethics.

The Human Cost of a Genius

Easwaran does not shy away from the tragic personal history that frames Turing's legacy. He details the burglary at Turing's home, the subsequent police prosecution for homosexuality, and the forced hormone treatment that led to Turing's suicide. "This ended up being too much for him and after a couple months of this treatment he committed suicide by eating a cyanide lace to Apple," Easwaran writes, capturing the brutality of the state's response to the man who saved it. This section serves as a sobering reminder that the abstract debates about machine intelligence were conducted by a human who was denied his own humanity.

The author notes that it took decades for the British government to apologize, only doing so in 2009. "It wasn't until many decades later in 2009 that the British government finally apologized for its role in killing the person who had saved their country during the war," Easwaran concludes. This historical context adds a layer of moral urgency to the technical discussion, suggesting that our treatment of intelligence—whether human or artificial—reveals our own values.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's commentary succeeds by treating Turing not as a distant icon, but as a pragmatic engineer whose 1950 predictions are actively shaping our current reality. The piece's greatest strength is its ability to connect the abstract "imitation game" to the tangible history of the Colossus and the modern rise of neural networks. Its only vulnerability is the brief, tragic detour into Turing's personal life, which, while necessary, momentarily pulls focus from the technical brilliance of the paper itself. Readers should watch for how the "Turing Paradigm" continues to challenge the symbolic logic of the past as AI becomes increasingly indistinguishable from human thought.
