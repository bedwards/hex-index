The "Azizification" of London housing

Michael Macleod · London Centric ·Mar 7, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Michael Macleod has done more than just report on a housing dispute; he has exposed a systemic rot where the profit motive for temporary accommodation is actively dismantling the security of permanent tenants. While mainstream outlets are only just waking up to the scale of the evictions by Criterion Capital, Macleod's investigation reveals a chilling pattern of forged safety certificates and political maneuvering that threatens the very fabric of London's housing market. This is not a simple landlord-tenant squabble; it is a case study in how regulatory gaps are being exploited to turn a homelessness crisis into a revenue stream.

The Anatomy of "Azizification"

Macleod anchors his reporting on a term coined by Merton councillor Stuart Neaverson: the "Azizification" of the London property market. He defines this trend starkly, noting it is "the process by which a landlord evicts longstanding tenants in order to relet their flats to councils as more profitable temporary accommodation." This framing is crucial because it shifts the narrative from individual malice to a calculated business model that relies on the state's inability to house its own citizens. The author argues that this process sees "vast sums of public money flow from local authorities to landlords," creating a perverse incentive where evicting a secure tenant is more lucrative than keeping them.

The evidence Macleod presents regarding the safety of these buildings is particularly damning. He writes, "London Centric has seen five electrical safety certificates given to tenants in Criterion-owned buildings. Despite being issued in recent months, they were signed by an electrician who went out of business three years ago." This is not a minor administrative error; it is a direct threat to life safety. The investigation found that the paperwork was identical across separate buildings and referenced no actual electrical connections, suggesting a mass-produced forgery rather than genuine inspection. When the author notes that "tenants, some of them paying £1,700/month for a one bed flat, said they had not let anyone into their properties to do the tests on the days they were issued," the implication is clear: the certificates are fiction.

The mayor is concerned about reports of potentially fake electrical certificates issued to residents. City Hall is supporting boroughs to investigate any breaches of housing law that might be putting Criterion tenants at risk.

While Mayor Sadiq Khan has weighed in, Macleod highlights the limitations of his power, noting that Aziz's response to the mayor was to claim the evictions are "going full steam ahead" and to blame the media for "politicising a legitimate series of business transactions." This defensive posture underscores the disconnect between the billionaire's legalistic defense and the human cost of his actions. Critics might argue that landlords have a right to manage their assets and that the Renters' Rights Act creates uncertainty, but Macleod's reporting suggests that the speed and method of these evictions—particularly the targeting of vulnerable groups like a Ukrainian refugee with a disabled child—transcend standard asset management.

The Political and Financial Reckoning

The article details a shifting political landscape where the financial rationale for these evictions is beginning to crumble. Macleod reports that London Councils, a cross-party coalition, has written to Aziz warning they are considering refusing to use Criterion as a temporary accommodation provider. The councillors stated they have a "responsibility and a financial imperative to discourage landlords who exacerbate London's homelessness crisis." This is a significant escalation, as it threatens the very business model that relies on council contracts. The author points out that if councils refuse to work with Criterion, it could "undermine the entire financial rationale for the mass evictions and potentially cost Aziz's company millions of pounds."

Furthermore, the reputational damage is mounting. Macleod notes that youth homelessness charity Centrepoint has "cut ties with the Aziz family's charitable foundation," and Opportunity London has ended its partnership with the firm. The author observes that "from the Telegraph to the Independent, people are waking up to the impact that Criterion Capital has on so many aspects of London life." This broadening of the spotlight is a critical development. It suggests that the "Azizification" trend is no longer a niche housing issue but a mainstream political liability.

Our councils would view it as unacceptable if a major landlord is seeking to unnecessarily evict tenants in secure accommodation on a large scale in order to profit from London's homelessness crisis.

The piece also touches on the aggressive tactics employed by the landlord's legal team, mentioning that lawyers Carter-Ruck claimed Aziz reported the editor to the police. However, Macleod clarifies that the Met police have "no record of such incidents being reported to the authorities," casting doubt on the intimidation tactics and reinforcing the credibility of the reporting.

Beyond the Housing Crisis

While the housing crisis dominates the narrative, Macleod briefly pivots to other London stories that reflect the city's complex social fabric. He touches on the "red vs blue school wars," noting that while some see a viral trend, the police have arrested individuals for "encouraging or assisting in a crime," suggesting a reality more nuanced than social media hype. He also highlights a bizarre property listing in Blackheath featuring "a Grade II listed modernist house with a rare network of sand tunnels," a stark contrast to the grim reality of the evictions. Additionally, he raises concerns about the safety of the foxes featured in David Attenborough's documentary, questioning whether "people not being sensible about where they put rat poison down" is threatening local wildlife.

These side stories serve to contextualize the housing crisis within a broader tapestry of London life, where the struggle for space and safety extends from human tenants to the city's animal population and its educational institutions. The juxtaposition of a £175m penthouse sale attempt by Nick Candy against the backdrop of mass evictions and homeless families further illustrates the city's deepening inequalities.

Bottom Line

Michael Macleod's strongest argument is his ability to connect the dots between forged safety certificates, political inaction, and a lucrative business model that profits from homelessness. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in the sheer inertia of the housing market; even with political pressure, the financial incentives for landlords like Aziz remain powerful. Readers should watch closely to see if the cross-party coalition's threat to cut off Criterion as a temporary accommodation provider is actually enforced, as this will be the true test of whether the "Azizification" of London can be reversed.
