Six-Day War (1967) - Third Arab–Israeli War Documentary

Commentary by Brian Edwards

Kings and Generals reframes the Six-Day War not merely as a clash of armies, but as a masterclass in the lethal power of deception and timing. While many histories focus on the sheer scale of the territorial gains, the author zeroes in on the "false reports" and "intelligence" that turned a desperate gamble into a decisive victory. For the busy strategist or history buff, this account strips away the myth of inevitable Arab dominance to reveal a conflict won in the first three hours before the ground troops even moved.

The Architecture of Deception

The narrative begins by dismantling the idea that the war was a spontaneous eruption of tension. Instead, Kings and Generals highlights how the Soviet Union's "falsely informed" intelligence regarding Israeli troop concentrations acted as the spark. The author writes, "international interference and the inability of the sides to find a settlement made one of the most iconic conflicts of the modern era the six day war inevitable." This framing is crucial; it suggests the war was a structural failure of diplomacy rather than a simple miscalculation by one side.

The core of the argument rests on the Israeli strategy of "Operation Focus." The author details how Israeli media "published false reports claiming israeli soldiers were on vacation" while pilots prepared for a real strike. This level of operational security was staggering. Kings and Generals notes, "the israeli pilots were informed about the start of the operation only five hours in advance." This brevity in notification underscores the extreme secrecy required to achieve total surprise.

"Operation focus was a decisive success israel lost only 19 planes in this operation and guaranteed its total air dominance for the rest of the war."

The author's emphasis on the destruction of the Egyptian air force "within three hours" effectively shifts the war's narrative from a ground slog to an aerial annihilation. Critics might note that this focus on air power occasionally overshadows the brutal infantry fighting that followed, but the data supports the claim that air dominance was the war's true turning point. The destruction of 338 Egyptian aircraft left the ground forces vulnerable, a fact the author underscores by describing how the Egyptian air defense system was "effectively off" during the initial strike.

The Collapse of the Sinai Front

Moving to the ground war, the commentary shifts to the Sinai Peninsula, where the author contrasts the numerical superiority of the Arab coalition with the tactical brilliance of the Israeli Defense Forces. The text notes that the Arab coalition held a massive advantage in deployed troops, "240 000 against 100 000," yet the author argues that leadership and preparation trumped numbers.

The narrative describes the Israeli advance as a series of calculated encirclements rather than a frontal assault. Kings and Generals writes, "initially the egyptians offered little resistance since their intelligence concluded that this was a diversion rather than a main attack." This misreading of Israeli intent by Egyptian command proved fatal. The author details how the Israeli 38th armored division, led by Ariel Sharon, "sowed enough confusion to weaken the artillery of the egyptian defense."

However, the most harrowing section of the coverage concerns the aftermath of the Egyptian retreat. The author points out that Field Marshal Abdel Hakim Amer "panicked and ordered the retreat of all units from sinai" without a clear plan. This lack of coordination turned a withdrawal into a massacre. Kings and Generals states, "the uncontrolled retreat weakened the morale of the egyptian troops instead of catching retreating egyptians the idf decided to capture three passes from sinai to the egyptian mainland and face the egyptian troops there."

"The passes became a killing ground with ten thousand egyptians being killed in one day alone."

This stark statistic serves as the emotional anchor of the section. While the author focuses on the military efficiency of the Israeli forces, the sheer scale of the loss highlights the catastrophic nature of the Egyptian command failure. A counterargument worth considering is whether the Israeli decision to block the retreat was a military necessity or a humanitarian failure, though the author presents it strictly as a tactical victory that secured the peninsula.

The Battle for Jerusalem and the Golan

The coverage then pivots to the Jordanian and Syrian fronts, where the author argues that Israeli success was less about surprise and more about relentless pressure and the exploitation of enemy hesitation. The text describes how King Hussein of Jordan was persuaded by Egyptian President Nasser that "egypt had an advantage against israel," a belief that proved disastrously wrong.

Kings and Generals writes, "jordanian and iraqi aviation started shelling israeli-controlled west jerusalem which caused 16 military and 20 civilian casualties." This aggression prompted the Israeli response to secure East Jerusalem, a move the author describes as a fierce battle for "ammunition hill" where the IDF "lost all but two of their attacking officers." The author's inclusion of these specific losses humanizes the rapid advance, reminding the reader that the victory was not bloodless.

On the Syrian front, the author notes the difficulty of the terrain, describing the Golan Heights as a "literally uphill battle against a fortified enemy." Yet, intelligence regarding weakened positions led Defense Minister Moshe Dayan to "order an offensive on golan without government authorization." This detail adds a layer of political drama to the military narrative, suggesting that the war's outcome was also shaped by the boldness of individual leaders operating outside standard chains of command.

"The israeli victory came as a result of more efficient military leadership better preparation of troops and intelligence but the six-day war was by no means the last conflict."

The author concludes by listing the territorial gains and the human cost, noting that "about 1 million arabs were placed under israel's direct control." This statistic serves as a bridge to the future, hinting that the victory planted the seeds for the next war. Critics might argue that the author underplays the long-term political consequences of this occupation, focusing instead on the military mechanics of the six days.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals delivers a compelling, data-driven account that successfully attributes the Israeli victory to superior intelligence and the psychological collapse of the Arab coalition rather than just firepower. The piece's greatest strength is its granular detail on the timeline of the air strikes, while its main vulnerability is a tendency to view the conflict through a purely tactical lens, occasionally glossing over the complex political fallout that defined the decades following the war.
