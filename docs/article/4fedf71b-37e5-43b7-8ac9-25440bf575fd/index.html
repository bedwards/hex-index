The Three Gorges Dam, Examined

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Oct 18, 2020 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Asianometry doesn't just describe the Three Gorges Dam; they dissect the terrifying calculus of modern China, revealing how a single infrastructure project became the ultimate test of the state's ability to dominate nature, even at the cost of its own history. In a field often dominated by engineering awe or environmental panic, the author offers a rare, granular look at the specific trade-offs: the precise tonnage of steel, the exact number of displaced villages, and the chilling reality that the dam's greatest benefit might be an illusion. This is essential listening for anyone trying to understand how a nation balances the immediate terror of flooding against the slow, creeping disaster of ecological collapse.

The Illusion of Control

The author immediately grounds the project in its sheer, almost incomprehensible scale. "The three gorgeous dam is the world's biggest dam everything about it is huge and that includes the controversies," Asianometry writes, setting the stage for a conflict where size itself becomes a problem. The piece argues that the primary justification for this titanic effort was flood control, a necessity born from centuries of tragedy where the Yangtze River has repeatedly drowned millions. Asianometry notes that historical records show "some 200 plus floods that have occurred between 185 bc and 1911 so roughly one every 10 years," creating a political imperative that was impossible to ignore.

The argument here is compelling because it acknowledges the human cost of inaction. The author points out that a single 1870 flood killed "a rumor 240,000 all by itself," and the 1998 disaster inflicted "50 billion dollars of damages." The dam was sold as the definitive solution, capable of reducing a "disastrous once a decade flood... to happening only once every 100 years." This framing is effective because it presents the dam not as a vanity project, but as a desperate shield against a river that has historically been a "cradle of chinese civilization" and a source of "prosperity and tears."

However, the commentary quickly pivots to the nuance that often gets lost in the headlines. Asianometry writes, "first the three gorgeous dam is not ideally placed to prevent a majority of the yangtze's floods," noting that the catastrophic floods of 1954 and 1998 were caused by storms downstream, outside the dam's reach. This is a crucial distinction that undermines the dam's primary selling point. The author contrasts this with the Sanmenxia Dam on the Yellow River, which is better positioned but failed due to silt. The Yangtze, while less silty than the Yellow River, still poses a massive threat: "the amount of silt in its waters is only going to go up." This creates a paradox where the solution to flooding might eventually become the cause of a structural failure.

The dam will be able to store control and mediate the yangtze's floods before each flood season, but the science is a bit more nuanced.

Critics might note that even if the dam cannot prevent every flood, the sheer volume of water it can hold back still represents a massive reduction in risk for the millions living in the middle reaches. Yet, the author's point about the silt remains a ticking time bomb that the political narrative often ignores.

The Human and Ecological Price

If the flood control argument is nuanced, the human cost is stark. Asianometry does not shy away from the sheer number of people uprooted by the project. "Some 1.3 to 1.6 million people would need to be relocated," the author states, a figure that dwarfs almost any other infrastructure project in history. The commentary highlights the grim reality that these were not just empty lands; the reservoir would inundate "4,000 villages, 140 towns, 13 cities and 100,000 acres of farmland." The author emphasizes that for many, this meant losing "not just their homes but also their only source of income."

The environmental fallout is described with visceral detail. The author warns that inundating these areas would turn the river toxic, as the water would absorb substances from "1,300 factories and mines, 40,000 graveyards and 200 garbage dumps." The irony is sharp: a project built to clean up the river's flow by controlling floods ends up trapping the river's filth. Asianometry writes, "submerged vegetation starts to decay and generate carbon dioxide creating local climate change effects which counterbalance the green effects of having a nearby source of renewable energy." This effectively dismantles the argument that the dam is purely a "clean and carbon friendly" solution. The author notes that while the dam generates "92 terawatts an hour of electricity per year," the environmental debt incurred by the reservoir's creation and the loss of biodiversity, including the extinction of the Chinese river dolphin, is a debt that may never be paid back.

The cultural loss is perhaps the most poignant. Asianometry details how "1282 cultural heritage sites would be plunged under deep water," with the government only managing to save a fraction. While some sites like the Dangfei temple were moved, "most sites were simply photographed before the waters came." This highlights a tragic prioritization of the future over the past, where the state decided that the concrete structure was more valuable than the millennia of history it submerged.

The Mechanics of a State Project

The piece concludes by examining the sheer machinery of the state that made this possible. Asianometry describes how the project was supervised by a "single supreme body," the Three Gorges Project Construction Committee, which allowed for a level of coordination that democracies could rarely achieve. The author notes that the project required "nearly 463,000 tons of steel enough to build 63 eiffel towers and 27 million cubic meters of concrete." The construction strategy involved a deliberate transfer of technology, where foreign firms like GEC Alstom were required to "share their technology with state-owned Harbin Power Equipment Company." This ensured that China would not just build the dam, but master the technology to build the next one.

The author's tone here is one of awe mixed with unease. The project was built in three stages over a decade, with the final water level reaching 175 meters. Asianometry writes, "the party wanted it, it got what it wanted," a phrase that encapsulates the authoritarian efficiency of the endeavor. The sheer scale of the resettlement, completed with "1.2 million people" moved, demonstrates a capacity for social engineering that is both impressive and terrifying.

The Three Gorges Dam is a divisive thing when you bring it up to people they either treat it with awe or disgust.

Critics might argue that the author underplays the genuine economic benefits of the electricity generated, which powers nine provinces and two major cities. However, the piece effectively argues that the long-term environmental and social costs create a shadow over these immediate gains.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's coverage succeeds by refusing to let the dam be a simple story of engineering triumph or environmental villainy; instead, it presents a complex, contradictory monument to human ambition. The strongest part of the argument is the detailed exposure of the silt problem and the limited effectiveness of the dam against downstream floods, which challenges the official narrative of total flood control. Its biggest vulnerability is the difficulty in quantifying the long-term economic return on such a massive displacement, leaving the reader to wonder if the trade-off was ever truly worth it. As the world faces its own climate challenges, the Three Gorges Dam stands as a cautionary tale: sometimes, the solution to nature's chaos is a new kind of chaos of our own making.
