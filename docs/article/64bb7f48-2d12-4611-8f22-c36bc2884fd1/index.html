In from the cold

Roger Pielke Jr. · The Honest Broker ·Jan 13, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Roger Pielke Jr. argues that the climate debate is finally undergoing a necessary correction, not because of a sudden political shift, but because the alarmist narrative has collided with the stubborn reality of human welfare. In a profile by Swedish journalist Anders Bolling, Pielke Jr. presents a compelling case that the "iron law of climate policy"—the principle that welfare always trumps climate action when the two conflict—is now driving a global retreat from unrealistic energy mandates. This piece matters now because it suggests the era of apocalyptic urgency is ending, replaced by a pragmatic focus on decarbonization trends that are already happening without radical disruption.

The Telephone Game of Alarmism

Pielke Jr. frames the current crisis of credibility not as a failure of science, but as a failure of communication. He describes a distortion process where scientific nuance is stripped away as it moves from technical reports to press releases, and finally to soundbites from global leaders. "It is a bit like the children's game of 'telephone,'" he explains, noting that the dry pages of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) reports are transformed into dire warnings that often lack evidentiary support. He points specifically to claims by UN Secretary-General António Guterres that weather-related disasters have quintupled since the 1970s, a statistic Pielke Jr. argues is unsupported by the very data the UN coordinates.

The core of his argument is that the public is being fed a version of climate science that contradicts the actual research on extreme weather. While heatwaves and heavy rainfall have increased, Pielke Jr. emphasizes that there is no robust evidence that hurricanes, floods, droughts, or tornadoes have become more frequent or severe. "The message conveyed in the dry pages of the report is simplified and transformed... which is what typically ends up in the news and is consumed by most people," he writes. This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from the scientists to the messengers, suggesting that the "red alert" rhetoric is a political construct rather than a scientific consensus.

Critics might argue that focusing solely on frequency ignores the increasing economic cost of disasters due to population growth and development in vulnerable areas, a nuance Pielke Jr. touches on but does not fully unpack in this specific profile. However, his insistence on distinguishing between physical trends and economic exposure remains a vital distinction for policy makers.

The claim has been that warming makes weather ever more dangerous. But what Pielke and his colleagues show—and what emerges from IPCC reports—is that there is no evidence that hurricanes, storms, droughts, floods, tornadoes, hail, or lightning have become more frequent.

The Iron Law and the Retreat from Unrealism

The most significant contribution Pielke Jr. brings to the discussion is his "iron law of climate policy," a concept he introduced in his 2010 book The Climate Fix. The law posits that when climate policy conflicts with citizens' welfare, welfare will win every time. He observes that this principle is now visibly reshaping global politics, as governments back away from mandates that impose high costs without delivering immediate benefits. "Bad news for those who want an instant transformation of the energy system—but a claim increasingly supported by evidence," he notes.

He illustrates this with the European Union's recent decision to delay its 2035 ban on gasoline cars, suggesting that the "unreality" that dominated the last decade has been met with a hard "bite" from reality. Pielke Jr. argues that the shift is not merely a reaction to a specific administration in the United States, but a broader, cross-national pragmatism. "It's a much broader pragmatism," he states, pointing to polling data in Germany and Sweden showing a sharp decline in the share of people who view climate change as their top priority. This perspective is crucial because it decouples the policy shift from partisan identity, framing it instead as a rational response to economic pressure.

The Best-Kept Secret in Climate Projections

Pielke Jr. also tackles the technical underpinnings of climate forecasting, specifically the misuse of the RCP 8.5 emissions scenario. For years, this scenario—which assumed a six-fold increase in coal consumption by 2100—was treated as the "business as usual" baseline, despite coal use already peaking around the time the scenario was introduced. "I call it the best-kept secret in the climate debate," he says, noting that many politicians and journalists are shocked to learn how dependent projections are on such outdated assumptions.

By revealing that the most extreme forecasts were based on unrealistic premises, Pielke Jr. argues that the downward revision of warming forecasts is a result of real-world energy developments, not political pressure. "Official forecasts of warming by the next century have been revised downward in recent years... The truth, he says, is that forecasts were revised downward because of (1) real-world energy developments and (2) the fact that earlier projections were simply wildly unrealistic." This analysis provides a necessary corrective to the narrative that climate models are being manipulated for political gain; instead, he suggests the models were simply wrong about the trajectory of the energy sector.

A History of Exile and Vindication

The profile does not shy away from the personal cost Pielke Jr. paid for his non-alarmist stance. He recounts a decade of professional isolation, including a congressional investigation into alleged bribery by the oil industry—a claim he dismisses as unfounded—and his dismissal from a role at FiveThirtyEight following a Twitter mob campaign. "Even the White House singled me out. Things get crazy pretty fast then," he recalls. He describes a period where his own university refused to support him, a breach of academic freedom that he believes pushed him out of his professorship.

Yet, he frames this period not as a defeat, but as a catalyst for a new kind of public intellectualism. "I've learned that they can't cancel me," he asserts, pointing to his current influence as a widely read voice in climate research and the fact that his work is now cited across all three working groups of the latest IPCC report. This narrative of resilience adds a human dimension to the policy analysis, suggesting that the pressure to conform to alarmism was a systemic feature of the climate establishment rather than a reflection of scientific truth.

People don't have to agree with me, but academic freedom has to mean something. You must have the right to do your research and describe what you see.

The Future of the Debate

Looking forward, Pielke Jr. predicts that climate change will gradually recede from the center of public concern, much like the population panic of the 1960s and 1970s. He argues that as food availability increased and birth rates fell, the demographic crisis faded from view, and he expects a similar trajectory for climate anxiety as the problem shifts from "apocalyptic" to "problematic." "When climate change shifts from being seen as apocalyptic to being seen as problematic, it will recede into the background in the same way," he writes.

He emphasizes that emissions are already declining relative to economic growth, a trend that began long before the Paris Agreement. "Paris may have led to good things, but decarbonization is not one of them," he contends, arguing that the steady decline in carbon intensity is a result of market forces and technological innovation rather than international treaties. This challenges the prevailing narrative that political targets are the primary driver of decarbonization, suggesting instead that the world is on a path to lower emissions regardless of political rhetoric.

Bottom Line

Roger Pielke Jr.'s argument is strongest in its insistence on separating scientific data from political amplification, offering a necessary corrective to the "telephone game" that has distorted public understanding of climate risks. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its potential to understate the non-linear risks of tipping points, which some scientists argue could render the "iron law" irrelevant if catastrophic thresholds are crossed before pragmatic solutions take hold. The reader should watch for whether this pragmatic shift leads to sustained investment in decarbonization technologies or simply a delay in addressing the long-term physical risks of a warming planet.

A Black Sheep in Climate Research.

Consider the following statements:

“Climate change is a serious problem.”

“If the UN climate panel did not exist, we would have to invent it.”

Professor emeritus Roger Pielke Jr. has repeated them for decades to anyone willing to listen. In his research, he constantly refers to the scientific working groups of the IPCC. And yet, for many years, the climate establishment has regarded him as a black sheep.

One explanation is that Pielke tells us what the research actually says—including what emerges from the IPCC’s scientific assessments—and that is not popular in influential circles. Those sounding the alarm about climate change often convey something quite different.
