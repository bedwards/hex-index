The Economics of Airline Class

Sam Denby · Wendover Productions ·Mar 14, 2017 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sam Denby dismantles a universal assumption: that airlines exist to move bodies from A to B. Instead, he reveals a ruthless economic architecture where the majority of passengers are essentially ballast, subsidizing a tiny elite whose tickets fund the entire operation. This isn't just about comfort; it's a masterclass in price discrimination that explains why your cramped seat exists at all.

The Illusion of the Mass Market

Denby opens with a stark financial reality check using a specific British Airways flight. He notes, "Economy class is not how Airlines make money. The real money at least for the traditional Airlines is in premium cabins." The data is startling: on this route, 45% of passengers generate 84% of the revenue. The author argues that the average flyer is not the customer; they are the infrastructure.

This framing is effective because it flips the script on airline complaints. We often criticize airlines for being cheap, but Denby suggests they are actually being hyper-efficient. He writes, "A vast majority of the revenue comes from a minority of passengers." The core of the argument is that the economy cabin is a loss-leader or, at best, a break-even filler designed to maximize the density of the plane so the premium seats can be sold at a premium. Critics might note that this ignores the sheer volume of leisure travelers who drive the brand's market share, but Denby's focus on revenue rather than ridership is the more accurate metric for a publicly traded company.

The Birth of Segmentation

The historical context Denby provides is where the piece truly shines. He explains that early aviation had no classes because the experience itself was the luxury. "In the beginning of commercial Aviation there weren't really any classes because everything was premium." The shift didn't happen because planes got better; it happened because airlines needed to fill seats without cannibalizing high-fare business travelers.

Denby details how the industry perfected the art of selling the same flight for different prices based on flexibility rather than comfort. "Tourist class tickets had to be purchased in advance and had no flexibility... The full Fair tickets were for the other type of traveler the business person." This is a brilliant example of economic segmentation. By restricting the cheap ticket with rigid rules, airlines could charge business travelers—who are often reimbursed and value flexibility—a massive premium. The author argues, "Airlines have figured out a way to sell the same product for different prices to different people." This strategy allowed the industry to democratize flying while protecting its profit margins.

The Concorde Paradox

Perhaps the most fascinating twist in Denby's narrative is the role of the Concorde. One might assume the supersonic jet drove the creation of luxury cabins, but Denby suggests the opposite. "The Concord was going to be the first class plane for the Rich and Famous regular planes would be the business and economy class." Because the Concorde was perceived as the ultimate first-class experience, traditional airlines hesitated to invest in their own first-class cabins for decades, fearing they couldn't compete.

The imminent perceived competition of the Concord really invigorated Airlines to optimize that middle class business class.

Instead of competing on luxury, they competed on the "middle tier." This led to the explosion of business class as the primary revenue driver. However, Denby points out a modern reversal: the Concorde's failure actually cleared the path for airlines to eventually reintroduce first class, but only after realizing that business class was the true cash cow. The argument here is subtle but powerful: sometimes a failed competitor can reshape an entire industry by forcing incumbents to find a new niche.

The Economics of Square Footage

In the final analysis, Denby turns to the brutal math of floor space. He breaks down an Emirates A380, showing that while first class takes up 35 square feet per seat, it generates significantly less revenue per square foot than business class. "Economy class seats make $332 per square foot business class seats $65 per square foot and first class seats $43 per square foot." The gap between economy and business is a chasm of comfort; the gap between business and first is a mere luxury tax.

This data supports his conclusion that first class is a dying breed. "More and more airlines are taking out their first class to just put in more business class." The logic is undeniable: if an airline can fill a plane with business class passengers, they will. The only reason they don't is that demand simply isn't there. The author concludes, "Everyone in economy in the end is just there to fill the plane." This is a sobering reminder that for the average traveler, their presence is not a privilege, but a mathematical necessity to make the premium seats profitable.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's analysis is a masterful dissection of airline economics, proving that the hierarchy of the cabin is not about comfort, but about extracting maximum value from a specific demographic. While the piece occasionally glosses over the operational complexities of managing mixed-cabin fleets, its core thesis—that the economy passenger is a means to an end for the business traveler—is undeniable. The strongest takeaway is the realization that the "middle class" of air travel, business class, is the only segment that truly matters to the airline's bottom line.
