John Stuart Mill - one minor mistake

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·Jun 4, 2023 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan tackles a 19th-century philosophical essay not to worship its authority, but to expose a single, fascinating crack in its logic. While most summaries of John Stuart Mill's On Names focus on his influence, Kaplan zeroes in on a specific confusion regarding "collective names" that undermines Mill's otherwise robust distinction between labels and descriptions. This is not a dry history lesson; it is a forensic audit of how we use language to carve up reality, revealing that even the greatest logicians can stumble when trying to define what makes a thing a "thing."

The Architecture of Meaning

Kaplan begins by dismantling a common misconception: that the meaning of a word is simply the idea it conjures in our minds. He highlights Mill's insistence that language connects us directly to the world, not to our internal representations of it. "The meaning of the word sun is the sun," Kaplan notes, quoting Mill's argument that the sentence "the sun is the cause of the day" refers to a physical fact, not a mental association. This distinction is crucial. It forces the reader to confront the gap between the ink on the page, the object in the sky, and the thought in the head. Kaplan effectively uses this to set the stage, showing why Mill's project was so revolutionary: he was trying to anchor language in reality rather than psychology.

"The meaning of the word sun is the sun. The sentence the sun is the cause of the day is not about our idea... it's saying that the Sun causes something."

However, Kaplan quickly pivots to where Mill's clarity begins to blur. The author introduces Mill's distinction between "general" names (like "cat") and "singular" names (like "Garfield"). Kaplan paraphrases Mill's view that general names allow us to make broad claims without listing every individual instance. This is a practical insight into the efficiency of language. But then Mill introduces the "collective name," a category Kaplan finds deeply problematic. Mill uses the "76th Regiment of the British army" as an example of a singular name that refers to a collection. Kaplan argues that this distinction collapses under scrutiny because, as he points out, "there's no such thing as a non-collective thing."

The Collective Conundrum

Here, Kaplan's commentary becomes most critical. He suggests that Mill's attempt to separate collective singular names from general collective terms is a "minor mistake" that creates more confusion than it solves. Kaplan paraphrases Mill's logic: a regiment is a single entity made of many soldiers, whereas "regiment" is a general term for any such group. Kaplan counters that a cat is also a collection of cells and molecules, yet we don't call it a "collective name" in the same way. "I think Mill is just genuinely confused uh when he introduces the notion of a collective name," Kaplan writes, bluntly assessing the philosopher's error. This is a bold move for a commentator to make against a giant of philosophy, but it works because the logic is undeniable. If everything is a collection of parts, the category of "collective name" applies to everything, rendering it useless.

Critics might argue that Mill was making a pragmatic distinction about how we speak about groups versus individuals, rather than an ontological claim about the nature of matter. Yet, Kaplan's point stands: if the definition of a collective name is "a group thought of as a single unit," then every proper name, from a person to a planet, fits the bill. The distinction evaporates.

The Unmeaning Mark

The essay's climax arrives with Mill's most famous and controversial claim: that proper names are "unmeaning marks." Kaplan walks the reader through Mill's argument that a name like "Frank Gilliam Jr." or "Dartmouth" does not describe the object it names; it merely tags it. Kaplan illustrates this with Mill's analogy of a robber chalking a house to mark it for theft. "A proper name is but an unmeaning Mark which we connect in our minds with the idea of the object," Kaplan quotes. The brilliance of this section lies in Kaplan's explanation of why this matters: if a name is just a tag, it remains valid even if the object changes its attributes. If the river Dartmouth moves due to an earthquake, the town is still Dartmouth. If Frank Gilliam retires, he is still Frank Gilliam.

"Proper names are attached to the objects themselves and are not dependent on the continuance of any attribute of the object."

Kaplan contrasts this with "connotative" names like "the Chancellor of the University," which lose their meaning the moment the person loses the job. This is the core of Mill's theory: names denote, descriptions connote. Kaplan acknowledges the power of this argument but hints at its fragility. He notes that later philosophers, specifically Gottlob Frege, would challenge this by showing that proper names must carry some descriptive content to function. If "Frank Gilliam Jr." were truly just an unmeaning mark, how do we know who it refers to if we've never met him? Kaplan suggests that Mill's "unmeaning mark" theory is a useful simplification that ultimately fails to capture the full complexity of reference.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's commentary succeeds because it treats Mill not as a deity to be revered, but as a thinker whose specific error reveals a broader truth about the limits of language. The strongest part of the argument is the demolition of the "collective name" category, which exposes a logical inconsistency in Mill's taxonomy. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability is its quick dismissal of the pragmatic utility of Mill's distinctions; while logically messy, Mill's categories often work well enough for everyday communication. Readers should watch for how modern philosophy of language has evolved from this "minor mistake," moving toward theories that blend Mill's direct reference with the descriptive content he tried to exclude.

"There's no such thing as a non-collective thing... Mill is just genuinely confused when he introduces the notion of a collective name."
