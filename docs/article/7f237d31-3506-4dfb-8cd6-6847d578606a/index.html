In Search of the Distinctively Human | The Philosophy of Blade Runner 2049

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Jan 29, 2018 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden does something rare in film criticism: he uses the dystopian sci-fi of Blade Runner 2049 not to predict the future, but to dissect the fragile architecture of the human soul right now. His most startling claim is that our humanity isn't a biological birthright or a divine spark, but a linguistic trick—a "symbolic self" we construct to hide our terror of being just matter. In a world where artificial intelligence is rapidly blurring the line between the organic and the engineered, van der Linden argues that the only thing separating us from the replicants is our shared, desperate ability to lie to ourselves about being special.

The Architecture of the Soul

Van der Linden begins by dismantling the romantic notion that we are naturally superior to our creations. He observes that in the film's collapsed ecosystem, "the world is designed by a blind man who can perceive it only through his own technology. He is God and yet the true miracle of creation eludes him." This sets the stage for his central thesis: the human drive to claim a "soul" is often just a mechanism for subjugation. As he puts it, "outside of the soul often lies the subjugation of all that is perceived to be without it: nature, animals, replicants."

The author leans heavily on the film's protagonist, K, to illustrate how we build walls between our inner lives and the external world. K, a replicant who believes he is just a machine, discovers a memory that feels too real to be fake. This moment forces him to cultivate a private self, a barrier against the world. Van der Linden writes, "it represents the staking out of his claim to an integral self free from the prying eyes of the world." This is a profound observation on the nature of consciousness itself. The argument lands because it reframes the "soul" not as a mystical entity, but as a psychological necessity for survival in a chaotic universe. Critics might argue that reducing the soul to a "symbolic construction" strips it of its spiritual weight, but van der Linden suggests this is exactly where the power lies.

The soul in itself is nothing... it only became meaningful if the meaning was given to it.

The Illusion of Connection

The commentary then pivots to the tragic impossibility of true connection in this framework. Van der Linden examines the relationship between K and his holographic girlfriend, Joi, noting that she is "programmed to be everything we want to see and everything we want to hear." He argues that this dynamic reveals a fundamental flaw in human interaction: "people seem to keep bumping up each other with our exteriors and falling away from each other." Even the most intimate acts, he suggests, are often just "a joining of exteriors" rather than a meeting of souls.

This section is particularly sharp because it connects the sci-fi premise to a very real, modern anxiety about digital intimacy. The author posits that our longing for connection is actually a longing to be "interlinked" in a way that transcends our isolated, symbolic selves. He writes, "the parts of ourselves that we value the most are dreams, thoughts, and inner feelings... leaving us with a fundamental longing for connection." The tragedy of the film, and perhaps of our own lives, is that we are trapped behind our own constructed identities, unable to fully reveal the "texture" of who we are.

Meaning as a Constructed Act

The climax of van der Linden's argument arrives when K discovers that his "miraculous" memory was likely a mistake, a misprint in a magazine that he and another character both misinterpreted. Instead of breaking K, this revelation liberates him. The author explains that the "soul" is not a pre-existing truth to be found, but a meaning to be made. "It is not the text but the texture that makes us human," van der Linden asserts. "The projecting of the symbolic self... not as something to be born with but as something to be constructed."

He ties this to Viktor Frankl's concept of self-transcendence, arguing that true humanity is found when we stop obsessing over our own specialness and start serving a cause greater than ourselves. K's ultimate act of humanity is not discovering he was born, but dying to save Deckard so he can be reunited with his daughter. "Dying for the right course is the most human thing we can do," the author concludes. This is a powerful reorientation of the film's moral center. It suggests that our worth isn't in our origins, but in our choices. A counterargument worth considering is whether this view is too idealistic; if the "soul" is just a story we tell, does it hold up when the stakes are life and death? Van der Linden would likely argue that the story is the only thing that holds up at all.

It is not the text but the texture that makes us human.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's analysis succeeds because it refuses to treat Blade Runner 2049 as mere entertainment, instead using it as a rigorous philosophical probe into the nature of identity. The strongest part of his argument is the reframing of the "soul" as a constructed narrative rather than a biological fact, which offers a surprisingly hopeful take on our artificial future. However, the piece risks oversimplifying the biological imperative that drives our sense of self, potentially ignoring the very real, non-symbolic aspects of human experience. Readers should watch for how this "constructed self" argument plays out as AI becomes more capable of mimicking human emotion; if the soul is just a story, the line between us and the machine may be thinner than we think.
