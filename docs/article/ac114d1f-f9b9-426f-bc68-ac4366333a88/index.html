Why gen-Z men are turning far right and women far left

Joeri Schasfoort · Money & Macro ·May 29, 2025 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Joeri Schasfoort cuts through the noise of partisan bickering to reveal a startling, data-driven reality: the political polarization tearing apart Gen Z is not just a cultural spat, but a calculated response to economic despair and a specific crisis of male status. While most commentators dismiss the rise of populism as mere extremism, Schasfoort marshals historical economic data to argue that voting for populist leaders is a "really risky bet" that often erodes democracy and stagnates growth, yet remains the most logical choice for a generation facing sky-high housing costs and social isolation.

Defining the Populist Surge

Schasfoort begins by dismantling the vague, often contradictory labels we apply to modern politics. He insists we move beyond the binary of "radical extremists" versus "people who listen," offering a sharper, scientific lens. "Social scientists have come up with three more neutral definitions about what currently classifies as far-left, far-right, and populist," he notes. The core definition hinges on a specific narrative: a party is populist if it frames politics as a struggle between "the people and the elite establishment."

This distinction is crucial for understanding why figures as different as Bernie Sanders and Donald Trump fall under the same umbrella. Schasfoort explains that while Sanders targets "oligarchs" and Trump targets the "regime," both movements share a disdain for the status quo. However, the divergence lies in their solutions. Left-wing populists generally seek to reject the capitalist system entirely, advocating for major redistribution, while right-wing populists are defined by nativism and a desire for strict social order. "First, we can see that the first two points that it talks about are seal the border and stop the migrant invasion," Schasfoort writes regarding the far-right. This focus on immigration and authority distinguishes them from traditional right-wing parties that might simply favor lower taxes.

The author's decision to ground these definitions in academic literature rather than media soundbites is a strength. It forces the reader to confront the mechanics of these movements rather than just their rhetoric. However, one might argue that the line between "populist" and "traditional" is sometimes blurrier in practice than the data suggests, as many mainstream parties have adopted populist rhetoric to survive.

"Strikingly in the 1990s, social scientists typically considered populism a Latin American phenomena... but as this graph for populist parties in Europe clearly shows... support... has now surged to around 30%."

The Historical Cost of Populism

Once the definitions are set, Schasfoort delivers a sobering assessment of what happens when these movements win. He does not shy away from the data, citing studies that show populist leaders are "four times more likely to trigger democratic backsliding" than their non-populist counterparts. The evidence is stark: populists tend to stay in power longer, often refusing to leave office through democratic means.

The economic argument is equally damning. Schasfoort references a long-term study comparing populist regimes to "doppelganger countries" with similar starting conditions. The result? "On average, all populists reduce economic growth, where left-wing populists do slightly worse than right-wing populists." While he acknowledges outliers like Turkey's Erdogan or Israel's Netanyahu who have seen growth, the historical trend points toward stagnation. "The outcome of these types of studies is not that voting populist spells certain doom for your country," he clarifies, "but what these studies do show quite clearly is that whether or not you vote right or left-wing populist, it is a really risky bet."

This section is the piece's analytical anchor. By moving the conversation from "who is right" to "what are the consequences," Schasfoort elevates the discourse. Critics might note that the "doppelganger" method, while rigorous, can struggle to account for unique geopolitical shocks that affect specific nations, potentially skewing the economic comparison. Yet, the sheer volume of historical examples—from Chavez to Orban—makes the pattern difficult to ignore.

The Gen Z Divergence: Economics and Gender

The most compelling part of Schasfoort's analysis is his attempt to explain why Gen Z men and women are splitting so dramatically. He rejects the idea that this is purely a cultural war, pointing instead to a shift in the nature of economic insecurity. "Today's economic insecurity therefore comes from different aspects: sky-high house prices and a massively increased cost of living in general that is hitting young people especially hard."

For young women, the turn to the far-left is a reaction to this economic pressure combined with a desire for systemic change. For young men, the shift to the far-right is more complex. Schasfoort identifies three specific drivers: a backlash against what they perceive as extreme feminist narratives in media, a crisis of loneliness, and a decline in educational attainment relative to women. "These three trends may then have been supercharged by the rise of social media where polarizing messages tend to get far more clicks than nuanced ones," he argues.

This synthesis of economic data with sociological factors regarding male status is a sophisticated move. It avoids the trap of blaming "toxic masculinity" in a vacuum, instead linking it to tangible losses in education and social standing. A counterargument worth considering is whether social media is the cause of this shift or merely an amplifier of pre-existing grievances; Schasfoort leans heavily on the former, which may underestimate the depth of the structural economic changes affecting men.

"Cultural factors may then explain why young men in particular choose the populist right while young women chose the populist left."

The Bottom Line

Schasfoort's most valuable contribution is his refusal to offer a simple villain; instead, he presents a systemic failure where centrist politicians have failed to address the "cost of living crisis" and "rapidly increasing cost of housing." His conclusion is pragmatic: until these material conditions improve, the allure of oversimplified populist solutions will only grow. The strongest part of this argument is its integration of hard economic data with the nuanced reality of Gen Z's social alienation. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that economic solutions alone can reverse a cultural polarization that has become deeply entrenched in identity. Readers should watch for whether centrist parties can actually deliver on the housing and energy reforms Schasfoort deems necessary, or if the populist tide will continue to rise regardless of policy tweaks.
