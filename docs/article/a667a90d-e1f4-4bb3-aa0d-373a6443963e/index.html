The Apocryphal Gospel of Philip contains a line that has sparked more speculation than almost any other sentence in all of early Christian literature. It reads something like, "And the companion of the Savior is Mary Magdalene. He loved her more than all the disciples, and he used to kiss her often on her, and then the manuscript simply breaks off. The page is damaged right where the body part should be.

We just get an empty space where a single word once was. And of all the places for a manuscript to crumble, it had to be right there. Seriously, did it say mouth, face, forehead, feet? We don't know.

And barring the discovery of another copy, we can't know and we will probably never know. But that hasn't stopped people from trying. Everyone from serious scholars to Da Vinci Code fans have tried to fill in the blank with their preferred version of Jesus and Mary's relationship in the Gospel of Philip. But the Gospel of Philip is far less interested in romance than in rituals and less interested in who kissed whom and more about how a person becomes a Christ.

This text is full of paradoxes. Death leads to life. Division leads to union. And it also makes some pretty startling theological moves, arguing that Jesus himself needed redemption through baptism and even rejecting the traditional virgin birth.

So, we'll come back to that missing word of where Jesus kissed Mary. But for now, let's dive into the Gospel of Philip. Our only surviving copy of the Gospel of Philip was found in Egypt in 1945 as part of what's called the Nagamadi Library. A remarkable collection of leatherbound cotices containing early Christian writings, hermetic treatises, and other mystical or philosophical texts.

The Gospel of Philip appears in Nagamadi CEX number two, which also includes several other well-known apocryphal Christian texts like the Gospel of Thomas. Although the surviving manuscript is in Coptic, scholars agree that the text was originally composed in Greek, probably sometime the second or third century CE. Though interestingly, the author occasionally plays with Syriak words. Syriak being a dialect of Aramaic used widely by Christian communities in the eastern Mediterranean.
