Batteries in bodywork. Extending the range of cars and planes

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Dec 6, 2020 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace identifies a paradox that threatens to stall the green transport revolution: the very component designed to save the planet is too heavy to get us there efficiently. While governments and manufacturers race to ban fossil fuels, Borlace argues that the solution isn't just better batteries, but batteries that disappear entirely into the vehicle's skeleton. This is not merely an incremental improvement in energy density; it is a fundamental reimagining of how we build machines.

The Weight of Progress

The core of Borlace's argument rests on a stark comparison of mass. He notes that in a typical electric vehicle, the battery pack can account for "as much as a third of the total mass of the vehicle," whereas fuel in a conventional car is merely "about three percent of the mass." This disparity creates a vicious cycle where adding range requires adding weight, which in turn drains energy faster. Borlace writes, "What if you didn't need to bolt the batteries to any part of the bodywork at all? What if the bodywork itself provided all the energy the car needed?" This rhetorical pivot frames structural batteries not as a luxury, but as a necessity for scaling electric mobility.

The author traces the lineage of this technology from a 2010 European project involving Volvo to current breakthroughs at Imperial College London. He highlights the work of Emil Greenhouse, who realized that to make a battery structural, it must do double duty. "From a design point of view it's very important that your positive and negative electrodes face each other," explains Xi Xiao, a scientist cited by Borlace, noting that misalignment kills the circuit. Borlace details how the Imperial team solved this by infusing carbon fiber with iron phosphate and encasing it in a conductive polymer resin. The result is a material that is both a load-bearing panel and an energy storage unit.

"The relentless research and development that's taken place during the last decade... has resulted in vast improvements in the mechanical properties and energy density of the latest versions of the battery material."

This evidence holds up well because Borlace grounds his optimism in specific metrics rather than vague promises. He points out that current structural materials now possess "at least 20 to 30 percent of both energy storage capacity and the mechanical capacity of the systems we want to replace." Critics might note that 30 percent is still a long way from the 100 percent needed to fully replace traditional batteries, and the durability of these composites under repeated stress cycles remains a significant engineering hurdle. However, Borlace effectively argues that even partial integration yields massive gains.

The Flight Factor

The stakes are even higher for aviation, where weight is the enemy of lift. Borlace illustrates this with the Kitty Hawk Cora air taxi, noting that its current batteries represent "17 percent of the overall weight." To make air taxis commercially viable, energy density needs to double, a feat Borlace suggests is unlikely to happen soon with traditional cells. Instead, he presents the Massachusetts Institute of Technology's approach as a more pragmatic path forward. Rather than chasing impossible energy density targets, MIT aims to "flip that equation on its head" by making the battery part of the fuselage.

As Dave Borlace puts it, "Their goal is to bring that threshold back down to where it is today by making their structural batteries become components of the plane's body." This strategy effectively lowers the required energy density from a theoretical 400 watt-hours per kilogram to a current 210 watt-hours. This is a compelling reframing of the problem. It shifts the focus from a materials science bottleneck to a systems engineering solution. The implication is clear: we may not need to invent a new physics to fly electric; we just need to stop carrying our batteries as dead weight.

Beyond the Machine

The scope of Borlace's coverage extends beyond cars and planes into the microscopic realm of medical implants and robotics. He describes how structural batteries could reduce an iPhone's thickness from over seven millimeters to under five, or enable "electronic skin for prosthetics" that senses temperature and pressure. Perhaps most striking is the work at the University of Michigan, where researchers created a zinc-air battery that mimics animal fat and muscle. Borlace writes that their goal is to create "machines with power sources that can integrate with their robotic skeletons just like the fat and muscle in animals."

This section highlights the versatility of the technology, moving from heavy industrial applications to delicate biological interfaces. The University of Michigan team's claim that their structural batteries have "72 times the energy capacity of a conventional lithium ion cell of the same volume" is a staggering figure that underscores the potential of this approach. However, the transition from lab prototypes to mass production for such diverse applications will require overcoming significant manufacturing complexities. The chemistry of zinc-air, for instance, is notoriously difficult to control in open environments, a nuance Borlace mentions but does not deeply explore.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage succeeds by shifting the conversation from "how do we make batteries smaller" to "how do we stop treating batteries as separate components." The strongest part of his argument is the demonstration that structural integration can solve the weight problem in aviation and automotive sectors without waiting for a breakthrough in energy density chemistry. The biggest vulnerability lies in the durability and safety of these multi-functional composites, which must withstand crashes and fatigue while holding a charge. As the industry moves from concept to prototype, the next critical milestone to watch will be the long-term structural integrity of these panels under real-world stress.
