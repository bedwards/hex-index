DeepSeek's hidden agenda is suspicious

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Feb 5, 2025 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

The Hated One cuts through the noise of AI hype with a provocative thesis: a Chinese startup didn't just catch up to American giants; it shattered the economic model of the entire industry by building a superior model for a fraction of the cost. This isn't just another benchmark report; it is a challenge to the trillion-dollar assumption that AI dominance requires massive, proprietary compute clusters. If the author's numbers hold, the era of "move fast and break things" is over, replaced by an era where efficiency and openness might actually win.

The Efficiency Shock

The core of the argument rests on a staggering disparity in resource allocation. The Hated One writes, "Deep Seek managed to build such a powerful AI using only 2,000 second-rate chips from Nvidia... the total cost of compute power to build their AI was $5.5 Million." In contrast, the author notes that training OpenAI's flagship model cost between $41 to $78 million, while Google spent up to $191 million on Gemini. This comparison is the piece's most potent weapon. It forces the reader to question the necessity of the arms race that has defined the last two years of tech news. The author frames this as a "20-year star build is in the cave with the box of scratch situation," a vivid metaphor suggesting that US big tech has been over-engineering solutions while a leaner competitor found a shortcut.

Critics might note that the author glosses over the potential for hidden subsidies or the possibility that the "second-rate" chips were actually optimized in ways not immediately visible to outsiders. However, the sheer scale of the cost difference makes the skepticism difficult to sustain without concrete evidence of fraud.

"Deep Seek essentially gave away their high-end AI that beats open AI and the rest of the big Tech and they gave it away for free thus proving there is little incentive to spend so much money on expensive US tech when you can build it at the fraction of the cost."

The Open Source Gambit

The author pivots from cost to strategy, arguing that the release of DeepSeek under a standard MIT license is a deliberate move to dismantle the proprietary moats of Silicon Valley. The Hated One writes, "Anyone with a copy of deeps AI is allowed to use copy modify merch publish distribute sub license and or sell copies of the software without any any restrictions." This is not merely a licensing choice; the author argues it is a geopolitical maneuver. By releasing a model that rivals the best proprietary systems for free, DeepSeek removes the barrier to entry for the entire world. The author draws a parallel to Google's Android strategy, noting that "Android being built on top of Linux is also fully open Source... Google became a global Behemoth because of it."

This framing is compelling because it shifts the battlefield from hardware to ecosystem. The author suggests that while US companies are building walled gardens, China is building a platform. "It will essentially become a platform," the author asserts, predicting that "many if not most of the new breakthroughs and improvements will spawn out of deep seek open source AI." This is a bold prediction that relies on the assumption that the global developer community will prioritize cost and performance over political alignment or data privacy concerns.

The Hidden Agenda

However, the piece takes a sharp turn when addressing the motivations behind this generosity. The Hated One warns that "DeepSeek's hidden agenda is suspicious." The author points out that while the model is open, the underlying governance is not. "Once it reaches Mass adoption it is mandatory in China that all large language models that are facing customers are to be tested by officials from the cyberspace administration of China to ensure they embody core socialist values." The author highlights the contradiction of an "open" model that is trained to censor specific topics, such as "inciting the subversion of state power or undermining National Unity."

This section is crucial because it prevents the narrative from becoming a simple "China wins, US loses" victory lap. The author admits that the AI is "censored much like the proprietary lms from the US are censored but in the opposite direction." The danger, as The Hated One puts it, is that "China is hoping to build a momentum for Mass adoption" to "export its censorship power and party propaganda abroad." This is a sobering counterweight to the excitement about open source. It suggests that the "free" model comes with a hidden cost: the normalization of a specific political ideology embedded in the code itself.

Critics might argue that the author overstates the immediate risk of export, given the Great Firewall and existing trade wars that isolate much of Chinese tech. Yet, the history of software suggests that once a standard is established, it is incredibly difficult to dislodge, regardless of its origins.

"China's AI doesn't come without caveat though with all of its openness and lack of restrictions China has a second agenda once it reaches Mass adoption it is mandatory in China that all large language models that are facing customers are to be tested by officials from the cyberspace administration of China to ensure they embody core socialist values."

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is the undeniable economic disruption caused by DeepSeek's cost efficiency, which forces a re-evaluation of the entire AI investment thesis. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its assumption that the global community will embrace a model that is inherently censored for political reasons, a tension that may limit its true global dominance. Readers should watch for whether the open-source community can successfully fork or modify the model to remove these political constraints, or if the efficiency gains will be enough to override the ideological red flags.
