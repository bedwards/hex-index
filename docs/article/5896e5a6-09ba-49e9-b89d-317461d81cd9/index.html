These Stupid Trucks are Literally Killing Us

Jason Slaughter · Not Just Bikes ·Mar 6, 2023 · 31 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jason Slaughter doesn't just critique the rise of SUVs; he reframes them as a deliberate, profit-driven assault on urban safety that has been masquerading as consumer choice. While most debates focus on fuel economy or aesthetics, Slaughter brings a chilling statistical reality to the forefront: the very features marketed to make drivers feel safe are statistically more likely to kill pedestrians and other motorists. This is not a rant about bad driving; it is a forensic dismantling of how regulatory loopholes and psychological manipulation created a vehicle class that is fundamentally incompatible with city life.

The Regulatory Loophole

Slaughter traces the explosion of light trucks not to a sudden shift in American ruggedness, but to a cynical exploitation of legal definitions. He notes that "until fairly recently, pretty much everybody drove a car or maybe a van," yet today, light trucks and SUVs make up almost 80% of all new car sales in the US. The author argues this shift was engineered by the auto industry to dodge stricter safety and emission standards applied to passenger cars. By lobbying to classify SUVs as "light trucks," manufacturers found a backdoor to avoid Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) regulations. As Slaughter puts it, "American automakers were incentivized to get as many people to buy light trucks as possible" because these vehicles were built on the same stiff, heavy frames as commercial pickups.

This historical context is vital because it strips away the myth of organic demand. The argument holds up well against the common defense that consumers simply "wanted" these vehicles. Slaughter reveals that the classification was a legal fiction designed to protect profits, not to serve a genuine need for off-road capability in a suburban setting. Critics might argue that market demand is the ultimate arbiter, but the evidence suggests that demand was manufactured through regulatory arbitrage rather than genuine utility.

The Marketing of Fear

Once the regulatory path was clear, the industry faced a hurdle: convincing people to buy vehicles that were initially "impractical, uncomfortable, ugly, and dangerous." Slaughter explains that the solution was to pivot from selling an outdoor lifestyle to selling security. "The city is a big scary place," he writes, noting that manufacturers capitalized on a national obsession with crime. The marketing strategy was blunt and effective: "Think of it as a 4,000 lb guardian angel." This framing turned road safety into an arms race where the biggest vehicle was perceived as the safest.

The commentary here is sharp and necessary. By linking the rise of SUVs to a manufactured fear of urban violence, Slaughter exposes a deep psychological vulnerability in the American consumer. The argument that these vehicles are "guardian angels" is particularly potent because it highlights the irony that the vehicle designed to protect the driver is often the one causing the most harm to everyone else. The marketing didn't just sell a car; it sold a false sense of security that has had deadly consequences.

SUVs are oversized, ridiculous, unnecessary death machines that are literally killing people, even their own drivers.

The Physics of Death

Moving from psychology to physics, Slaughter details why the engineering of these vehicles is catastrophic for anyone outside the cabin. He points out that while cars are designed with crumple zones to absorb energy, light trucks have "stiff frames" that act like "battering rams." This creates a fatal mismatch in crash compatibility. "When a person gets hit by a car, they're typically thrown onto the hood," Slaughter explains, "but the higher front end of an SUV means the impact is centered near the torso and head, which is much more deadly."

The statistics he cites are staggering. If a pedestrian is hit by an SUV, they are three times more likely to die than if hit by a regular car. Furthermore, the high bumper height means that children are often invisible to the driver, leading to a surge in "frontovers." Slaughter notes that advocacy groups have documented that "over 500 American children were killed by being run over by SUVs, usually by their own parents in their own driveways." This evidence is undeniable and shifts the blame from individual negligence to vehicle design.

A counterargument worth considering is that modern technology like cameras and sensors can mitigate these risks. However, Slaughter dismisses this as a corporate deflection, arguing that "the industry solution for this is proximity sensors and front-facing cameras because car companies are happy for any regulations that means they can sell you more stuff." He correctly identifies that technology cannot fully compensate for the fundamental physics of a two-ton vehicle with a high center of gravity and poor visibility.

The Myth of the Outdoorsy Driver

Perhaps the most biting critique in the piece is the dismantling of the "outdoorsy" persona associated with SUV ownership. Slaughter points out that the vast majority of these vehicles are driven exclusively in cities and never see off-road terrain. "75% of light truck owners tow something once a year or less, and 70% go off-road once a year or less," he writes. The author argues that the target demographic was never the rugged hunter, but rather a consumer driven by status and fear. He cites market research indicating that the average light truck purchaser is "less likely to volunteer or feel a strong connection to their communities" and is more likely to engage in risky driving behaviors.

This section of the commentary is provocative but supported by the data Slaughter presents. The image of the SUV driver as a rugged individualist is exposed as a marketing fabrication. The reality is a driver in a city, carrying a briefcase and a "fat ass," in a vehicle that is ill-suited for the environment they actually inhabit. The comparison to the Mercedes G-Wagon as the "absolute dumbest luxury vehicle ever made" underscores the absurdity of the current market.

Bottom Line

Jason Slaughter's argument is a masterclass in connecting regulatory history, marketing psychology, and hard physics to explain a modern urban crisis. His strongest point is the revelation that the SUV boom was not a natural evolution of consumer preference but a calculated exploitation of legal loopholes that prioritized profit over public safety. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its tone, which risks alienating the very drivers it seeks to regulate by labeling them as "posers" or "jerks," potentially hardening resistance to necessary policy changes. However, the core message remains undeniable: until the regulatory framework treats SUVs as the heavy, dangerous machines they are, cities will continue to pay the price in lives lost.
