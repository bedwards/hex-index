Why Tokyo Has the Best Streets in the World

Dave Amos · City Beautiful ·Jul 31, 2024 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Amos makes a counterintuitive claim that defies the standard urbanist playbook: Tokyo's best streets aren't the wide, protected bike lanes of Amsterdam, but the narrow, car-free alleys where utility poles sit in the road and pedestrians walk freely. He argues that true urban tranquility isn't about engineering perfect infrastructure, but about a cultural and land-use shift that renders cars irrelevant. For busy listeners tired of the "build it and they will come" mantra, this offers a radical alternative: fix the density first, and the quiet streets will follow.

The Hierarchy of Quiet

Amos begins by dismantling the expectation that a megacity must be loud and chaotic. He observes that while Tokyo has the expected busy arterials, the real magic lies in the neighborhoods where "buildings are built directly up to the curb" and sidewalks are merely painted suggestions. The author notes that in these spaces, "there isn't even separated space for utility poles; they're just right in the street." This would be a pedestrian's nightmare in a car-centric culture, yet Amos explains that it functions because "there aren't a lot of cars."

The core of his argument rests on a simple, profound observation: "Cities aren't loud; cars are loud." He illustrates this by contrasting a quiet Tokyo street with a quiet American street, noting that both lack traffic, proving that density does not require noise. Critics might argue that this model is impossible to replicate in cities where zoning laws mandate wide setbacks and parking minimums, but Amos insists the result is a "peaceful" environment where he found himself "scolding my kids for being too loud." The evidence here is compelling because it shifts the blame from urban density to vehicle volume.

Cities aren't loud; cars are loud. You can have lots of people living in dense homes and still be quiet if you get rid of the cars.

The Bike and Transit Reality

When discussing transportation modes, Amos challenges the assumption that Tokyo is a cycling utopia in the same vein as the Netherlands. He points out that while Amsterdam prioritizes "cycle tracks on busier streets," Tokyo relies on a different equation: "Transit plus walking is the power combo." He cites data showing that "only 12% of trips are made by a private car," while 71% are walking or transit. Bicycles, he notes, "replace longer walking trips within the neighborhood" rather than serving as the primary long-distance mode.

This distinction is crucial. Amos writes, "when you have a mass transit network like this, you don't need to use a bike to reach longer destinations." The result is a unique ecosystem where bike theft is rare because "almost all bikes are registered," and riders feel safe enough to leave them unlocked. He describes a scene where "one car, a ton of bikes, and 11 stories of apartments" coexist, a stark contrast to American suburbs where a single car dominates the visual landscape. The argument holds up because it acknowledges that different cities solve mobility problems based on their existing infrastructure; Tokyo doesn't need to force cycling to work when its trains are so efficient.

Engineering for Small Vehicles

Perhaps the most practical insight Amos offers addresses the elephant in the room: emergency access. Skeptics often ask how fire trucks navigate such narrow passages. Amos reveals that the solution isn't wider streets, but smaller vehicles. "Their solution is smaller trucks," he writes, describing the fire engines he saw as "actually adorable" because they "fit down these narrow streets just fine." He contrasts this with the United States, where fire departments have moved to "fewer larger do-it-all trucks due to tighter budgets," necessitating wider roads.

This section highlights a critical failure in American planning: we design our infrastructure for the largest possible vehicle, ignoring the reality that most daily traffic is small. Amos notes that "garbage trucks are also tiny in Tokyo," and even when delivery trucks block a street, "bikes and pedestrians can navigate just fine." The implication is clear: if we want quieter, safer streets, we must rethink the size of the vehicles we allow to dominate our public space. A counterargument worth considering is that retrofitting American fire departments with smaller, specialized fleets would be prohibitively expensive, yet Amos suggests that the current "do-it-all" approach is a false economy that degrades street quality.

The Land Use Imperative

Amos concludes by addressing the elephant in the room: can the rest of the world, particularly the United States, learn from Tokyo? He is candid about the differences, noting that "Tokyo is a city where only 12% of trips are made by car," whereas Amsterdam is a more relatable model for cities with higher car usage. He admits that for a planner in "Milwaukee or Provo or Tallahassee," the context of Tokyo's density—over 15,000 people per square kilometer—is "unlikely to see a context that is at all familiar."

Despite these barriers, he offers a definitive lesson: "Street design does not exist in a vacuum." He argues that "land use and transportation are linked," and that "dense urban development is necessary to have these peaceful car-free streets." The author's final verdict is that Tokyo's success is "not a triumph of urban design and transportation planning but a result of a culture that supports urban density and quality mass transportation." This reframes the entire conversation from "how do we build better bike lanes?" to "how do we build denser neighborhoods?"

Street design does not exist in a vacuum. Land use and transportation are linked, and dense urban development is necessary to have these peaceful car-free streets.

Bottom Line

Amos's strongest contribution is the realization that quiet, walkable streets are a byproduct of density and transit, not just infrastructure design. His argument's biggest vulnerability is the sheer difficulty of replicating Tokyo's cultural and historical context in low-density American cities, yet his call to prioritize land use over street width remains a vital, actionable insight for any city hoping to reduce its car dependence.
