Monopoly round-up: A gunboat oligarchy goes after Venezuelan oil

Matt Stoller · ·Jan 4, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Matt Stoller delivers a startling thesis: the recent U.S. military intervention in Venezuela is not a rogue act of a single leader, but the latest iteration of a century-old "gunboat oligarchy" where Wall Street and the executive branch fuse to seize resources. While headlines focus on the shock of regime change, Stoller forces the listener to see the deep, unbroken lineage from the 1930s to today, arguing that the fusion of corporate and state interest is the true constant. This is essential listening for anyone who believes current events are merely the result of personality quirks rather than structural design.

The Historical Echo

Stoller anchors his argument in the specific actions of Treasury Secretary Andrew Mellon, a man who also happened to own Gulf Oil. He writes, "Ninety five years ago, in 1931, Treasury Secretary Andrew Mellon, who owned Gulf Oil (now Chevron), forced the President of Colombia to give his company the Barco oil concession, which borders Venezuela." The mechanism was blunt financial coercion: Wall Street banks and the government threatened to withhold loans unless the concession was granted. Stoller draws a direct line from this era of "gunboat diplomacy" to the present, noting that while the current administration is more explicit about its motives, the underlying dynamic remains identical.

The author contextualizes this by examining the political climate of the 1920s, a time of "deep reverence for the wealthy" where figures like John J. Raskob, head of the Democratic National Committee, encouraged the public to borrow money to invest in the stock market. Stoller observes, "Just as there is increasing support for cynical and nihilistic figures today, many in the 1920s felt warmly towards Mellon, Mussolini, and authoritarianism in general." This historical parallel is powerful because it strips away the novelty of current events, suggesting that the public's susceptibility to authoritarianism in service of capital is a recurring vulnerability. However, critics might argue that the 1920s were a unique period of pre-Depression speculation that doesn't perfectly map onto today's de-industrialized, digital economy.

"It has been said that it is hard to convict a million dollars in the criminal courts. It can also be said that it will be hard to impeach a billion dollars."

Stoller highlights the quixotic efforts of Congressman Wright Patman, who in 1929 sought to impeach Mellon for self-dealing. Patman's struggle underscores the difficulty of holding economic elites accountable, a theme that resonates deeply with modern anti-monopoly movements. The eventual collapse of the Mellon era came not from legal victory, but from the 1929 crash and the resulting "debunking" of myths, as Louis Brandeis put it. Stoller argues that we are now entering a similar phase of disillusionment, where the public is finally waking up to the reality of their exploitation.

The Modern Predation

Turning to the present, Stoller describes the U.S. military buildup in the Caribbean as the largest maritime operation of the century, aimed explicitly at toppling the Venezuelan government. He writes, "Wall Street hedge funds and energy investors are already on their way to the country, and Trump has said that large U.S. oil companies will control these natural resources." The author is unflinching in his assessment of the rationale, stating, "Ultimately, what the attack on Venezuela shows is that Donald Trump decided to use his 2024 mandate for change to revert back to a traditional gunboat diplomacy framework, both domestically and abroad."

Stoller points out a grim continuity: the Biden administration had already placed a $25 million bounty on Maduro, signaling that regime change sits within the bipartisan foreign policy consensus. He notes that even Democratic leadership has been largely silent or focused on procedural grievances rather than the morality of the intervention. "It's a very 1920s style of opposition party fecklessness," Stoller writes, capturing the frustration of those who expected a different response from the opposition. The human cost of this strategy is often obscured by the financial jargon, yet the reality involves the destabilization of a sovereign nation for the benefit of foreign capital.

A critical distinction Stoller makes is the shift in American industrial power. Unlike the Mellon era, where U.S. factories were robust, today's U.S. has "de-industrialized in favor of China." He argues, "We can't even produce missiles without samarium, a rare earth metal almost totally controlled by China. We're not good at making things anymore, and we're barely even trying, so dominated are we by lazy financiers." This adds a layer of strategic vulnerability to the aggressive foreign policy; the U.S. is projecting power without the industrial base to sustain it. A counterargument worth considering is that in a globalized economy, relying on specialized supply chains is efficient, but Stoller's point about the fragility of military capability in the face of geopolitical rivals is a sobering reality check.

The Dawn of Disillusionment

Despite the grim outlook, Stoller finds a kernel of optimism in the public's growing awareness. He writes, "It's that the public is finally cottoning on to the real power structure they inhabit, after a 50-year episode of amnesia." The emergence of new political figures like Zohran Mamdani, who Stoller describes as having a "Patman-esque vibe," suggests a potential realignment. The author believes that the "rules-based international order" is dead and that the resulting chaos could force a necessary reform.

Stoller concludes with a call to action, quoting Abraham Lincoln: "The occasion is piled high with difficulty, and we must rise with the occasion. As our case is new, so we must think anew and act anew." The piece suggests that the very aggression of the oligarchy is creating the conditions for its own undoing, as the public's anger begins to coalesce into a political force. This is a compelling, if risky, bet on the resilience of democratic sentiment.

Bottom Line

Stoller's strongest contribution is the unflinching historical parallel that frames current military aggression not as an anomaly, but as a feature of a system designed to protect capital. His biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that public anger will inevitably translate into effective political realignment, a transition that history shows is neither guaranteed nor linear. Readers should watch for whether the emerging anti-monopoly movement can successfully challenge the bipartisan consensus on foreign intervention before the next crisis deepens.

Monopoly round-up: A gunboat oligarchy goes after Venezuelan oil

by Matt Stoller · · Read full article

Ok, so I was going to do a year end review and predictions issue today for the anti-monopoly movement, but yesterday, the U.S. engaged in regime change in Venezuela. At the end of this post, I’ll have the usual monopoly round-up, with lots of news. But we’ll start with the situation in Venezuela, about which Wall Street is very excited.

The best way to understand what just happened is to start with history. Because while Trump is unusually explicit about his rationalization for seizing control over a resource-rich territory, U.S. domination of the oil reserves of South America is not new. And neither is the fusion of corporate and state interest.

Ninety five years ago, in 1931, Treasury Secretary Andrew Mellon, who owned Gulf Oil (now Chevron), forced the President of Colombia to give his company the Barco oil concession, which borders Venezuela. How? Well Wall Street banks and the U.S. government threatened to withhold vitally needed bank loans if Colombia did not cede the franchise.

The parallels to the situation today are there. When Mellon seized these reserves, in partnership with JP Morgan banking interests, gunboat diplomacy was the norm. In the prior two decades, the U.S. had finished brutally putting down a resistance movement in the Philippines, and had become the global economic and political power after a gruesome world war. Woodrow Wilson had tried to establish a global rules-based order, which the GOP in the 1920s sabotaged.

At the time, Democrats were incompetent and split, as it was an era of deep reverence for the wealthy and bitter culture warring over race and alcohol. For instance, the head of the DNC in the late 1920s, a Dupont executive named John J. Raskob, published a pamphlet titled “Everybody Ought to Be Rich” encouraging Americans to borrow money to invest in the stock market.

Just as there is increasing support for cynical and nihilistic figures today, many in the 1920s felt warmly towards Mellon, Mussolini, and authoritarianism in general. U.S. Steel chairman Judge Elbert Gary encouraged Americans to “learn something by the movement which has taken place in Italy,” while progressive and New Republic founder Herbert Croly called Mussolini as substituting “purposive behavior for drifting and visions of a great future for collective pettiness and discouragement.” Gunboat diplomacy fit in well.
