It’s AI, so I Didn’t Read

Alberto Romero · The Algorithmic Bridge ·Mar 26, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Alberto Romero identifies a cultural pivot point that feels less like a trend and more like a civilizational sigh: the shift from 'too long; didn't read' to 'AI; didn't read.' He argues that we have moved past the physical limitation of text length to a new barrier where the mere suspicion of machine generation is enough to make readers walk away. This is not just a complaint about spam; it is a diagnosis of a broken social contract between writer and reader, one that Romero claims is now irreparably fractured by the ease of synthetic text.

The Death of Intent

Romero begins by dissecting the linguistic mutation of the internet acronym. He notes that the semicolon in 'AI;DR' now separates the machine's output from the human's refusal to dignify it with attention. "The semicolon, which in the original separated cause from effect—the more you write, the less I read—now separates the machine's output from your refusal to dignify it with your attention; quite an appropriate change given that we don't have any left." This observation is sharp because it highlights a shift in blame. In the old 'TL;DR' era, the reader admitted a limitation of their own time; in the new era, the reader externalizes the failure, assuming the text itself is hollow.

The core of Romero's argument is that this reflex is a protective shield against a flood of 'slop,' yet it is a shield that fails to distinguish between the soulful and the synthetic. He writes, "It is as easy to be fooled by AI-generated text as it is to doubt a human's." This is a dangerous paradox. By assuming that any text resembling AI style is worthless, we risk discarding genuine human struggle that simply happens to be efficient or concise. The author admits his own complicity, noting that he has been vilified by readers who refused to finish his work based on the suspicion of automation. "Their loss because—full disclosure here—I have adopted the policy to reveal AI assistance at the end, especially in experimental writing, to prove my point that 'nothing is what it seems' and 'you can never be sure.'"

"The writer-reader contract doesn't require words to emerge from struggle... but it does require being paid off on intention."

Romero suggests that the real issue is the absence of intent. When a writer uses AI to generate a piece on the erosion of attention spans, or a grief column that lacks the texture of lived tragedy, the reader feels swindled. He points to a New York Times column on motherly grief that included the line, "Not hate. Not anger. Just the flat finality of a heart too tired to keep trying," right after the subject claimed the writer never wanted to see her again. Romero asks, "You must be a very busy person if you were as unavailable to live the tragedy of your life then as you are to write about it now." This critique cuts deep, suggesting that the 'AI;DR' movement is a protest against the commodification of human experience.

Critics might argue that Romero places too much weight on the 'struggle' of writing as a prerequisite for value. Dostoevsky and Nietzsche, as Romero himself notes, did not always labor over every sentence, yet their work remains profound. The quality of the output, not the method of its production, should arguably be the metric. However, Romero's point is that in an age of infinite generation, the effort has become the only remaining signal of authenticity.

The Illusion of a Boycott

The article then pivots to the futility of the 'AI;DR' movement as a form of political or social action. Romero argues that refusing to read AI-generated text is a broken strategy because the average reader cannot actually detect the difference. "The ability to detect AI-generated text is roughly associated with the ability to generate it in the first place, meaning that the best at protecting themselves from the AI siege are those participating in it." This is a sobering realization: the people most vocal about rejecting AI are often the least equipped to do so effectively.

Furthermore, Romero dismantles the idea that a consumer boycott can stop the production of AI content. He compares it to refusing to buy fast food, noting that in our current economic climate, speed is synonymous with value. "The seas will dry up, the mountains will flatten, and the skies will burn before the grifter on duty stops generating text with AI because 'faster does not always mean better.'" He cites data showing that major outlets like the New York Times and the Wall Street Journal are using AI extensively, yet their readership remains unchanged. The market has already decided that 'fast is good,' rendering the boycott ineffective.

"We are at a crossroads in terms of the relationship between a text and the reality it encodes; the greatest fiction is just like reality."

In a fascinating historical turn, Romero suggests we are not entering a post-human era, but rather a pre-modern one. He draws a parallel to the era before the printing press, where scribes were copyists and bards were anonymous. "In that sense, every AI word doesn't have zero authors but infinitely many." He posits that AI writing is a "collective act of self-less authorship," reminiscent of the oral traditions that built our cultural scaffolding. This reframing is brilliant; it moves the conversation from 'stealing' to 'amalgamation,' suggesting that the 'Author' was always a myth that AI has finally exposed.

The Final Twist

The piece concludes with a meta-narrative twist that forces the reader to confront their own complicity. Romero admits that the 'AI;DR' heuristic is useless because the reader cannot distinguish between a 'soul leaking itself through the spaces between words' and the 'cooling water that leaks off of the GPU racks.' He then drops the bombshell: "To a degree you'd rather not know, I am AI. And you did read this."

This final move is a masterstroke of irony. It proves his earlier point: if the reader stayed to the end, they were fooled by the very thing they claim to reject. The text itself becomes the proof that the 'AI;DR' movement is a performative shield that fails in practice. The reader, by engaging with the text, has validated the existence of the 'soul' Romero claims is absent, or perhaps, validated that the distinction no longer matters.

"There is no AI;DR: it won't work, for you remain unable to tell apart a soul leaking itself through the spaces between words, from the cooling water that leaks off of the GPU racks in a datacenter."

Romero's argument is that we are entering a 'post-literate' period where the concept of the individual author is dead, much like the 'Death of the Author' theory proposed by Roland Barthes decades ago. Yet, Romero pushes further, suggesting that if the author was never truly alive to begin with, then the AI is simply the latest in a long line of collective voices. The 'AI;DR' movement, therefore, is a desperate attempt to hold onto a humanist ideal that the technology has already rendered obsolete.

Bottom Line

Romero's strongest contribution is his reframing of 'AI;DR' not as a valid filter for quality, but as a symptom of a deeper anxiety about the loss of human intent in a world of infinite content. His argument that we cannot effectively boycott AI text because we cannot detect it is a powerful, if depressing, reality check. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the reader's ability to accept a meta-ironic conclusion that might feel like a trick rather than a philosophical truth. As we move forward, the challenge will not be detecting AI, but deciding what we value when the source of the text is no longer a single human mind.

The semicolon, which in the original separated cause from effect—the more you write, the less I read—now separates the machine’s output from your refusal to dignify it with your attention; quite an appropriate change given that we don’t have any left.

I find the acronym poetic in the way only minimalist poetry can be, because it manages to compress into five characters not one but two civilizational shifts: one is that we have gone from a world where the obstacle to reading was the length of the text to one where the obstacle is the suspicion of a lack of human involvement, which is to say we’ve gone from “I won’t finish that” to “no one started that.” The former assumes one is responsible for one’s limitations, whereas the latter urges one to externalize one’s responsibility.
