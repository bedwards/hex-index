Midweek Update #2: The Two Wars

Phillips P. O'Brien · Phillips P. O'Brien ·Mar 18, 2026 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Phillips P. O'Brien cuts through the noise of daily headlines to reveal a startling paradox: the US and Israel are winning every tactical battle while losing the strategic war, while Iran is losing every air defense engagement but winning the economic war. This is not a story of who has the better missiles, but of two entirely mismatched conflicts playing out simultaneously, where the side with the superior technology is running out of time while the side with the inferior military is tightening a noose around the global economy.

The Asymmetry of Air Power

O'Brien begins by dismantling the conventional view of war as a single struggle over territory. He argues that the current conflict is actually "two very different wars" where neither side can stop the other from fighting in their preferred manner. The US and Israel have achieved a level of air dominance that is almost absolute. "Building on the destruction of Iranian air defenses last year, they now have the ability to attack from the air almost any target that they want using aircraft or missiles." This capability allows them to strike with surgical precision, targeting leadership and infrastructure at will. The author notes that the US decision is no longer about what they can attack, but what they want to attack.

However, O'Brien points out a critical flaw in this dominance: the inability to translate kinetic success into political change. The US strategy relies on the assumption that decapitating leadership or causing enough chaos will trigger an internal uprising or a regime collapse. "So far they have not been able to work with forces within the regime to take power and end the fighting." The author highlights a significant disconnect between the military reality and the political goal. While the US can destroy anything it finds, it cannot find everything, and more importantly, it cannot force the Iranian people to rise up. "The Iranian people have been badly burned by him," O'Brien writes, referring to previous failed promises of support, making the hope for an internal revolution increasingly fragile.

Critics might argue that the sheer scale of destruction could eventually break the regime's will, regardless of internal coordination. Yet, O'Brien's evidence suggests that without a viable internal partner, air power alone is a blunt instrument that fails to achieve the necessary political concessions.

"The US and Israel have a great advantage in being able to attack what they want when they want, but they are struggling translating that advantage into political success."

The Economic Chokehold

While the US focuses on high-tech air superiority, Iran has pivoted to a strategy that leverages geography and low-cost asymmetric warfare. O'Brien describes this as a war against the world economy rather than against US military forces directly. "The Iranian war is to take advantage of two things, geography and cheap attack systems, to threaten the world economy to such a degree that the US and Israel will be forced to give them a political deal that they want."

This argument gains significant weight when considering the specific geography of the Persian Gulf. Iran controls the Strait of Hormuz, a chokepoint through which a massive portion of global oil flows. O'Brien notes that "any disruption to flows through the Strait would have huge consequences for world oil markets," affecting not just fuel prices but also food costs and semiconductor production due to helium shortages. The author draws a parallel to the vulnerability exposed in other conflicts, noting that "closing such a body of water if you have access to a shoreline such as Iran has become surprisingly and scarily cheap."

The reference to Operation Mosaic and the strategic importance of Kharg Island underscores the depth of this threat. Just as historical operations have shown the vulnerability of oil terminals, Iran's use of inexpensive Shahed drones and unmanned sea vessels allows them to harass shipping without engaging in a direct naval battle. "The BBC has this striking timelapse video of civilian shipping almost stopping completely in a few days," O'Brien observes, illustrating how quickly the economic pressure can mount. This strategy effectively bypasses the US military's strengths, targeting the political will of the US by threatening the global economy that the US has protected for decades.

A counterargument worth considering is that the US Navy has significant capabilities to clear mines and protect shipping lanes, potentially neutralizing this threat. However, O'Brien suggests that the cost and duration of such an operation would be prohibitive, creating a time pressure that favors the Iranian strategy of attrition.

The Trap of Escalation

The most chilling part of O'Brien's analysis is his assessment of the US strategic mindset. He argues that the Trump administration entered this conflict with a fantasy of a quick, Venezuela-style victory, expecting the Iranian regime to crumble within days. "Much like Vladimir Putin, who expected Ukraine to collapse in a few days in February 2022, Donald Trump expected a quick and easy win over the Iranian regime." The reality has shattered this expectation, leading to a state of panic in Washington.

O'Brien reveals that the administration is now trapped. They cannot simply walk away because the threat to the Strait of Hormuz remains, yet they cannot achieve a decisive victory through bombing alone. "He will do something to try and force the Iranians to [capitulate]," O'Brien predicts, suggesting that the only path forward for the US is further escalation. The author notes that the President is now "contemplating actions that were not in his mind just two weeks ago." This shift from a desire for a quick exit to a potential escalation is driven by the fear that a failure to reopen trade would be a "catastrophic defeat for the USA" with severe political ramifications.

The core of O'Brien's argument is that the US is fighting a war it did not plan for, against an enemy that has successfully changed the rules of engagement. "This war, no matter what they say, has gone down a completely different road than they expected," he writes. The US is running out of time to achieve its political objectives before the economic costs become unsustainable, while Iran is betting that the US will blink first.

Bottom Line

O'Brien's strongest contribution is the reframing of the conflict as two parallel, incompatible wars where the side with the superior military is actually the one under greater time pressure. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its heavy reliance on the assumption that the US political will is the primary limiting factor, potentially underestimating the resilience of the US military-industrial complex to sustain a long-term blockade. Readers should watch for whether the US administration chooses to escalate further to break the economic deadlock or if the economic pain forces a negotiated settlement that validates Iran's asymmetric strategy.

The Two Wars

War is often seen as a process engaging two sides in one struggle over specific places or strategic objectives. Thus we can have two armies fighting over a town (say Pokrovsk or Stalingrad) or two navies fighting it out on the high seas (say Midway or the Black Sea). However in this case we seem to have two armed forces in some ways fighting two very different wars and both struggling to actually stop the other from fighting in their preferred manner. In that way both are winning and both are losing. The dilemma that both face is trying to stop their enemy from fighting their enemy’s preferred war while using the war they have chosen to extract the political concessions they both crave. In that way we are seeing two distinct wars developing, not one.
