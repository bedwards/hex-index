The Cold War and nuclear weapons, they go together like peanut butter and jelly or decaf coffee and the trash. I've seen the Cold War described as being born from the atomic fires of Hiroshima and Nagasaki. And much of the period can be largely defined by a fear of their use and the potential for a humanity ending war. But like most things, there is more to the Cold War nuclear story than just their destructive capability.

I'm your host, David, and today we are going to look at some of the alternate ways that nuclear weapons were used or at least envisioned being used during the Cold War. So, what if I told you that during the Cold War, scientists from the United States Air Force sat in a meeting room and seriously posed the question, "What if we nuked the moon?" Seems improbable at first glance, right? Well, what if I then told you that at the same time the Soviets were asking themselves the exact same question. And no, this isn't the plot of a James Bond film.

This is project A119, a top secret US Air Force plan proposed in 1958 to detonate a nuclear bomb on the moon as a spectacular display of power. And as bizarre as that sounds, it was just one of dozens of unconventional or downright mad ideas for nuclear weapons dreamed up during the Cold War. The period between 1945 and late 1980s was a time when the nuclear bomb wasn't just a weapon. It was seen by some as that technological Swiss Army knife.

Couldn't dig a harbor? Use a nuke. Need to cap a gas fire? Use a nuke.

Explore the very real nuclear test in space, look at America's peaceful project plowshare, and the Soviet program of nuclear explosions for the national economy, and even peak at orbital bombs and nuclearpowered spacecraft.
