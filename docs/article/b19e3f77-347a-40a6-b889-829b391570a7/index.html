Dinosaurs AND Trains!

Jason Slaughter · Not Just Bikes ·Mar 10, 2024 · 22 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jason Slaughter doesn't just review a family vacation; he weaponizes a dinosaur obsession to expose the crumbling state of European rail infrastructure. While the premise sounds like a lighthearted travelogue, the piece delivers a stinging indictment of how decades of underinvestment in trains versus highways has turned a simple museum hop into a logistical nightmare. The evidence isn't found in dry statistics alone, but in the visceral frustration of a canceled sleeper train that forces a family to scramble for alternatives while standing in the world's largest dinosaur hall.

The Allure of the Rails

Slaughter begins by establishing the emotional core of the trip: the intersection of two great passions. "Dinosaurs are awesome really awesome so to make this video as awesome as a subject deserves I've enlisted the help of tioo nebula's Resident animal expert including dinosaurs." This sets a tone of genuine enthusiasm that makes the subsequent infrastructure failures feel more like a betrayal than a mere inconvenience. He frames the journey not as a commute, but as a pilgrimage to three of Europe's premier natural history museums, accessible only by the continent's most celebrated mode of transport.

The author's advocacy for rail travel is grounded in practical experience rather than abstract idealism. He notes that "the best place for information is the man in seat 61 this website is an absolute Gold Mine of information about train travel all over the world." By pointing readers to a specific, reliable resource, Slaughter moves beyond general praise to actionable advice. He contrasts the smooth, 300 km/h ride of the Eurostar with the friction of air travel, noting that while airports require security and passport control, the train experience is "much much more comfortable." This comparison is effective because it acknowledges the reality of border checks while still championing the superior user experience of the train.

"Europe desperately needs more sleeper trains and I will talk about that in a future video but for now when your sleeper train is canceled there isn't really much you can do about it as there normally isn't a second one leaving shortly like there is for a regular train."

The Illusion of Connectivity

The narrative shifts sharply when the planned overnight sleeper from Brussels to Berlin is canceled. This isn't a minor delay; it is a systemic collapse. Slaughter captures the absurdity of the moment: "I got the email just as we were rocking up to the largest dinosaur Hall in the world." The juxtaposition of the ancient, majestic creatures and the modern failure of the transport network creates a powerful irony. He explains that while airlines often rebook passengers on flights days later, the German rail system, despite its reputation, offers a different kind of chaos.

He highlights a crucial distinction in how different transport modes handle failure. "Fortunately there are a lot of Alternatives in this part of Europe we decided to take the train to Cologne later that day and I managed to find a great last minute deal on a hotel room right at the train station." This flexibility is a silver lining, but it comes at a cost. The author points out that while Deutsche Bahn allows passengers to hop on any train, the sheer unreliability of the network is the real story. He cites a shocking statistic: "the reliability of deoban has tanked over the past few years reaching an all-time low of 52% for long-distance trains in November of 2023." This data point anchors the personal anecdote in a broader, alarming trend.

Critics might argue that blaming the rail system ignores the complexities of maintaining aging infrastructure in a growing economy. However, Slaughter anticipates this by shifting the blame from incompetence to policy. He writes, "the truth is that European countries have spent significantly more on car infrastructure than they have on rail infrastructure over the past 30 years." He details how between 1995 and 2020, highway length grew by 60% while railways actually shrank by 6.5%. This is the piece's most devastating argument: the current unreliability is not an accident, but the direct result of thirty years of prioritizing cars over trains.

The Museum as a Mirror

Amidst the travel chaos, Slaughter provides a fascinating interlude on paleontology, using the museums to illustrate the deep time of dinosaurs versus the short time of human infrastructure planning. He notes that "we live closer in time to T-Rex than T-Rex did to stegosaurus," a fact that puts our modern transit struggles into a humbling perspective. The museum visits serve as a reminder of what humans are capable of preserving and understanding over millions of years, contrasting sharply with our inability to keep a train running for a single night.

He also touches on the evolution of scientific understanding, mentioning that "most dinosaurs had feathers dinosaurs used to be drawn as lizard-like or crocodile like but we know now from fossil records that many of them were feathered." This scientific nuance mirrors his argument about infrastructure: just as our understanding of dinosaurs has evolved, so too must our understanding of how to build a sustainable transport network. The failure to update our infrastructure, like the outdated image of a scaly T-Rex, leaves us ill-equipped for the future.

Bottom Line

Slaughter's strongest move is reframing a travel disaster as a case study in policy failure, using the emotional weight of a family trip to make the statistics of rail decline feel personal and urgent. His biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that the solution is simply more investment, without fully addressing the political will required to reverse decades of car-centric planning. Readers should watch for his promised follow-up on the systemic issues, as the current piece only scratches the surface of why Europe's trains are failing its people.
