13. The Assyrians - Empire of Iron

Paul Cooper · Fall of Civilizations ·Jun 14, 2021 · 113 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Paul Cooper doesn't just recount the rise and fall of the Assyrian Empire; he reconstructs the terrifying awe of stumbling upon its ghostly ruins in the middle of a desperate retreat. By anchoring the narrative in Xenophon's 401 BC Anabasis, Cooper reveals that the Assyrians were already ancient history to the Greeks, a civilization that had collapsed centuries before the Parthenon was even conceived. This framing transforms a standard historical survey into a meditation on the fragility of power, asking the listener to confront the silence of cities that once held hundreds of thousands of souls.

The Ghosts of the Desert

Cooper's most distinctive move is using the Greek mercenaries' perspective to highlight the sheer scale of Assyrian ambition. He writes, "The greeks continued their march unmolested through the remainder of the day and arrived at the tigris river it was a large deserted city this wall was 25 feet in breadth 100 in height the whole circuit of the wall was 11 kilometers." The sheer physicality of these numbers—walls of clay brick resting on stone foundations, pyramids of stone—serves as the primary evidence of an empire that had already vanished into dust by the time the Greeks arrived. This approach is effective because it bypasses dry archaeological data in favor of a visceral, human reaction to the unknown. The Greeks, terrified and fleeing for their lives, were forced to pause and stare at a monument to a power they could not comprehend.

The author emphasizes that for the Assyrians, their own past was already deep history. "The assyrians also had an incredibly rich awareness of their own ancient history and the cultural roots of their society." Cooper notes that while we see them as ancient, they saw themselves as the cutting edge, inheriting a legacy that stretched back to the Sumerians and the legendary Gilgamesh. This reframing is crucial; it prevents the listener from viewing the Assyrians as primitive savages and instead presents them as a sophisticated society conscious of their place in a long lineage of human progress. As Cooper puts it, "The assyrians did not think of themselves as we might think of them as an early culture at the dawn of human history but as a people on the cutting edge of human progress."

"Xenophon must have asked himself who were these people who built such vast cities out here in the desert how could such a great metropolis vanish so completely beneath the sands."

Critics might argue that relying on Xenophon's account risks romanticizing the ruins or projecting Greek confusion onto Assyrian reality. However, Cooper uses this limitation to his advantage, showing how the mystery of the ruins fueled local myths of gods bringing down fire and thunder, a narrative that underscores the total cultural erasure that followed the empire's fall.

The City as a Living God

The commentary then shifts to the internal mechanics of the empire, specifically the city of Ashur. Cooper paints a vivid picture of a bustling trading hub that evolved into a spiritual powerhouse. He describes the city's religious landscape: "The city would become home to no fewer than 34 to the various gods worshipped by all the people of gods like the sun god shamash and the moon god sin... and of course the king of the gods enlil." The argument here is that the Assyrian state was not just a political entity but a theological one, where the city and its patron deity were inextricably linked.

Cooper explains that in this worldview, "If two cities went to war it was thought that their gods were battling it out in the heavens just as their armies were fighting down on earth." This insight into the Assyrian psyche is vital for understanding their military aggression; conquest was not merely territorial expansion but a divine mandate. The author illustrates this by describing how the city of Ashur eventually named its god after itself, creating a deity that was the literal embodiment of the state. The evidence of this is found in the city's massive archives and libraries, which Cooper notes were "a clear indicator of a city overflowing with wealth." The juxtaposition of these intellectual treasures with the brutal reality of their military expansion creates a complex portrait of a civilization that was both a beacon of learning and an engine of iron.

Bottom Line

Paul Cooper's coverage succeeds by grounding the monumental history of the Assyrians in the intimate, terrifying experience of those who walked through their ruins. The strongest part of the argument is the reframing of the Assyrians not as distant ancestors but as a contemporary power that viewed itself as the culmination of human history. The biggest vulnerability lies in the reliance on later Greek and Babylonian sources to interpret Assyrian motivations, which may color the narrative with external biases. Listeners should watch for how Cooper navigates the transition from this spiritual and intellectual flourishing to the inevitable, violent collapse that turned these great cities into the very dust that Xenophon feared.
