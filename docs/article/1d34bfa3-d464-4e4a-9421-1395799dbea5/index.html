Kenny Easwaran delivers a rare, lucid diagnosis of why our most advanced image generators still fail at the things humans notice most: hands, text, and logic. While the public fixates on the viral "Pope Francis puffy jacket" moment, Easwaran traces the lineage back to a fundamental mismatch between how neural networks "see" and how human vision prioritizes detail. This isn't just a technical tutorial; it is a philosophical correction to our assumption that generative AI is a creative partner rather than a statistical mirror.

The Short History of a Long Dream

Easwaran begins by dismantling the myth of sudden invention. He reminds us that while 2022 felt like an explosion, the technology was merely the tip of a much older iceberg. "In March of 2023 a now famous AI generated picture of Pope Francis wearing a puffy jacket went viral and many people were inspired to produce various responses to it," he notes, using the meme as a cultural anchor before pivoting to the actual mechanics. The author argues that this moment was merely the point where "AI image generation became extremely well known to the general public," obscuring the fact that the tools had been maturing for years.

The lecture effectively reorients the reader's timeline, showing that the "history is surprisingly short" for these specific models, yet deeply rooted in decades of computer vision research. Easwaran writes, "up until very recently AI was primarily imagined either as a tool for classifying things or making recommendations or doing some sort of interactive Behavior." This distinction is crucial. He contrasts modern generative tools with the CGI of the 1990s, noting that in films like Toy Story, "the computer wasn't really generating the image it was working on behalf of humans."

Early computer Graphics... was not usually thought of as part of artificial intelligence. It was instead developed as tools for working artists.

This framing is powerful because it separates the tool from the agent. Easwaran traces the lineage from symbolic approaches in the 1970s to the neural networks of the 1980s, specifically highlighting Yan LeCun's work on handwritten digits. He explains that the real breakthrough wasn't just better algorithms, but better data: "much of the really important work in the 90s and 2000s was the generation of Tidy labeled data sets." The argument holds up well here; without the ImageNet dataset, the 2012 AlexNet breakthrough would have been impossible. However, one might argue that Easwaran slightly underplays the role of compute power scaling, which was just as critical as the data in enabling these massive models.

The Hallucination of Hands and Text

The core of Easwaran's commentary shifts to the limitations of these systems. He points out that while the images are stunning, they crumble under scrutiny. "Even though the images are very impressive at some level when you zoom in on the hand there's something a little bit weird," he observes. The author identifies a profound asymmetry: humans are hyper-critical of hands and text, while the AI treats them as just another pattern in the noise.

Easwaran writes, "the AI is just trained on a large amount of data just a bunch of images of all different sorts while we humans care particularly specially about human body parts and text." This is the piece's most insightful observation. He draws a brilliant parallel to historical art, noting that when a European painter first encountered a kangaroo in 1770, they painted it as a cross between a mouse and a deer because they lacked the visual vocabulary. "To the AI That's What humans are we're just some animal that shows up in its pictures," Easwaran argues.

To us who really care about our own language and our own hands these look really bad.

The author explains that neural networks are "much better at getting Impressions and patterns rather than things like counting or getting diagrams right." He illustrates this with real estate diagrams generated by Midjourney that "make no sense at all" if you try to count rooms or trace walls. This is a vital distinction for busy professionals: the AI captures the vibe of a floor plan but fails the logic of one. Critics might note that newer models are rapidly improving at text rendering, but Easwaran's point about the fundamental lack of symbolic reasoning remains valid for the architecture he is describing.

The Field of View Problem

Perhaps the most original contribution of this lecture is the explanation of why hands and text fail. It isn't just a lack of data; it's a lack of perspective. Easwaran points out that in the training data, hands are rarely the focus. "Look at these photographs of someone teaching some kids how to tie knots or someone reading at a park bench," he says. In these images, the hands are peripheral.

He argues that "since the hands and text aren't very often in photographs they don't generally make up the majority of a photograph but they do make up the majority of some parts of our daily life in our visual field." This is a sophisticated insight into the nature of human attention versus machine training. We spend our lives looking at our own hands and the text we are reading, giving us a "field of view" that the AI never experiences.

Human artists have also known for ages that drawing hands is remarkably difficult people are very harsh critics if you get hands wrong.

This section effectively bridges the gap between computer science and art history. By acknowledging that "people are very harsh critics if you get hands wrong," Easwaran suggests that the AI's failure is not just a bug, but a reflection of the fact that it hasn't been "trained" by the same social pressures that force human artists to practice drawing hands for years. The argument is compelling, though it leaves open the question of whether future models trained on first-person video data (rather than static images) might solve this specific "field of view" deficit.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's strongest move is reframing AI image generation not as a failure of creativity, but as a failure of perspective; the models see the world as a collection of peripheral patterns, not as a focal point of human experience. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on 2022-era examples, which may feel dated as multimodal models rapidly improve at text and spatial reasoning. However, the underlying lesson remains essential: until AI understands the logic of what it generates, not just the style, it will remain a brilliant mimic that cannot be trusted for precision.
