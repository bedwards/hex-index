A vertical axis wind turbine that doesn't need the prevailing wind! How do they do that?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Apr 18, 2021 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace challenges a fundamental assumption of renewable energy: that wind turbines require vast, open spaces and consistent prevailing winds. He introduces a British startup, Alpha 311, which proposes harvesting the chaotic, wasted air turbulence created by vehicles on busy motorways using compact vertical axis turbines. This is not merely a theoretical exercise; the author presents specific performance data suggesting these devices can power their own infrastructure while feeding surplus energy back into the grid, turning roadside clutter into a distributed power network.

Rethinking the Wind Resource

Borlace begins by distinguishing between the familiar horizontal axis turbines (HAWTs) that dominate our skylines and the less common vertical axis wind turbines (VAWTs). He notes that while HAWTs are efficient at scale, they require complex yaw systems to face the wind, making them ill-suited for urban environments. "Vertical axis turbines or vorts tend to fall into one of two sub subcategories," Borlace writes, "Savonius turbines like this one or Darius turbines like these." He argues that the historical inefficiency of VAWTs was largely a result of material limitations and poor design, not an inherent flaw in the concept.

The core of Borlace's argument is that the wind resource is far more ubiquitous than we assume. He highlights that Alpha 311's innovation lies in retrofitting existing infrastructure rather than building new towers. "The initial concept that Alpha 311 pursued was to retrofit existing infrastructure with low-level turbines that would get the benefit of traffic air flow," he explains. This approach bypasses the cost prohibitive nature of building entirely new wind farms. The author's framing is effective because it shifts the focus from finding the perfect wind to utilizing the wind that is already being created by our daily commute.

Critics might note that relying on vehicle-generated turbulence introduces a variable that is dependent on fossil fuel consumption. If traffic declines due to electrification or remote work, the energy source diminishes. However, Borlace counters this by pointing out that the airflow is essentially wasted energy regardless of the turbine's presence, making its capture a net positive for efficiency.

"Those vehicles are all punching a hole in the air and causing air flow around them regardless of whether there's a turbine or a building or anything else at the side of the road. And that air flow is essentially just wasted energy."

The Mechanics of the Alpha 311

Moving from theory to engineering, Borlace details how the Alpha 311 turbine operates. The device is designed to be lightweight and durable, constructed from recycled composites. "The A311 turbine blades start to turn in an airflow speed as low as 1.2 meters/s," Borlace states, emphasizing their sensitivity to low-speed turbulence. He contrasts this with the massive gearing systems of traditional turbines, noting that Alpha 311 uses a "neat little static magnetic stator housed in the base of the unit with a stainless steel rotor inside it which is attached directly to the base of the turbine."

The author provides concrete data to support the viability of the project. Based on traffic flow data from the M2 motorway, he calculates that a single turbine could generate nearly 6 kilowatt-hours daily. "The total annual electricity generation along that section worked out at just over 860 megatt hours, which is definitely worth having," he asserts. This specific calculation moves the piece from speculative tech to a plausible engineering solution. The inclusion of the Institute of Thermofluids assessment adds credibility, suggesting the numbers are not just marketing fluff but the result of rigorous testing.

Borlace also touches on the dual utility of these devices. Beyond power generation, the hollow structure houses sensors for air quality and traffic monitoring. "The space allocated for the magnetic stator also houses a center array for data collection from sensors that can be mounted onto the lampost which means Alpha system can also monitor air quality, weather conditions and traffic statistics," he writes. This transforms the turbine from a simple generator into a node in a smart city network, adding a layer of value that justifies the installation costs even if power generation fluctuates.

Scalability and Future Applications

The commentary expands to consider where else this technology could fit. Borlace suggests that telecommunications masts, which consume significant power for 5G networks, are an ideal secondary market. "A typical 5G mast uses about 7 kwatt hours of energy every day," he notes, pointing out that a single turbine could nearly meet this demand. This application addresses the intermittency of solar power by providing generation during non-daylight hours or cloudy weather.

He also envisions a future where these turbines are combined with solar and battery storage in domestic settings. "That solar system would pull a lot of people, even here in the UK, out of fuel poverty," Borlace argues, linking the technology to social equity. While this is an ambitious claim, it underscores the potential for distributed energy to reduce reliance on centralized grids. The author acknowledges the skepticism surrounding startups, admitting that "companies like Alpha 311 and so many others all over the world are all working towards the same basic end goal, which is to develop working alternatives to the existing dominance of fossil fuels on our electrical grids."

The piece concludes by positioning Alpha 311 not as a replacement for massive offshore wind farms, but as a vital complement. "Alpha 311's installations will never replace onshore or offshore wind turbines, and they're not designed to," Borlace clarifies. "But they can provide a very valuable complimentary power source that'll improve the diversity of renewable energy inputs onto those grids." This nuanced stance prevents the piece from sounding like a hype cycle and grounds it in a realistic energy transition strategy.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage succeeds by reframing wind energy from a matter of scale to a matter of opportunity, proving that the most valuable wind resources may be the ones we create ourselves. The strongest part of his argument is the concrete data regarding traffic-induced turbulence, which validates the technology beyond theoretical physics. The biggest vulnerability remains the dependency on traffic volume, but the potential for these units to serve as multi-purpose smart infrastructure nodes offers a compelling path forward for urban energy resilience.
