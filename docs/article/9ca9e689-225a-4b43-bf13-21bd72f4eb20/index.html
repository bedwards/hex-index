Bad r/Legaladvice - My Neighbor’s Bees are Stealing My Pollen

Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone transforms a Reddit thread about neighborly bee disputes into a masterclass on how the law distinguishes between wild nature and human intent. While most legal commentary stops at "you can't sue for bees," Stone digs into the specific tort theories of conversion, negligence, and strict liability to reveal why the plaintiff's logic collapses under the weight of legal precedent. This is essential listening for anyone who has ever felt wronged by an uncontrollable force of nature, as it clarifies the thin line between a nuisance and a lawsuit.

The Illusion of Theft

Stone immediately dismantles the Reddit user's primary analogy: that bees stealing pollen is equivalent to a dog stealing tools. He writes, "The elements of conversion... is that the person has a legal right to the property... the defendant intentionally interfered with possession of the property." This is the crux of the argument, and Stone is right to highlight the missing link of intent. You cannot have a theft claim when the "thief" is an insect acting on instinct rather than a human directing the action.

The commentary effectively points out the absurdity of attributing human malice to bees. Stone notes, "We can't know the intent of the bees unless of course they are voiced by Jerry Seinfeld." This humorous observation underscores a serious legal reality: without proof that the neighbor directed the bees to trespass, the claim of conversion fails. The author also correctly identifies that the plaintiff likely suffered no actual harm, arguing that "no harm was done to the flowers in fact you could argue that the plants were enhanced by the bees attention." Critics might note that in a monoculture farming environment, pollen theft could theoretically reduce yield, but in a residential garden setting, the benefit of pollination almost always outweighs the loss of a single flower's resources.

The Duty of Care

Moving to negligence, Stone explains that a lawsuit requires a breach of a "reasonable person standard." He contrasts the Reddit scenario with a real-world case in Miami Beach where a tenant slipped on honey dripping from a hidden hive, resulting in a $1.4 million settlement. Stone writes, "The building owners had a duty to act a billing owner must act as a reasonable landlord towards the tenants but here they didn't do anything about the problem." This distinction is vital; the law punishes inaction when a danger is known and preventable, not when nature simply does what nature does.

Stone argues that the neighbor had no duty to stop the bees from flying, stating, "Nobody had a clear duty to stop the bees from flying in the air to a different property." This is a powerful application of the "no general duty to rescue" principle to property disputes. The argument holds up because it prevents the legal system from becoming a tool for policing natural biological processes. If a homeowner could sue every time a bird ate their fruit or a bee took their pollen, the courts would be overwhelmed with cases against the ecosystem itself.

The Limits of Strict Liability

Finally, Stone addresses the concept of strict liability, which applies to "abnormally dangerous condition or ultra hazardous activities" like storing dynamite or keeping wild tigers. He clarifies that bees generally do not fit this category. Stone writes, "Owners of domesticated animals are generally only liable if they are negligent whether an animal is wild or domesticated varies from stat." This is a nuanced point that often trips up laypeople. While bees are insects, they are often treated as domesticated livestock in legal terms, meaning the owner is only liable if they were negligent in their care.

The author effectively uses the example of a "pet circus tiger" to illustrate where strict liability actually kicks in, contrasting it sharply with the beekeeper next door. He notes that "some jurisdictions impose strict liability if a pet attacks or bites someone" but emphasizes that this usually requires a history of aggression or a specific local ordinance. The Reddit user's fear that the bees are "trespassing and stealing" is legally unfounded because the law does not view pollination as a crime, even if the resulting honey is sold for profit.

Bottom Line

Stone's strongest contribution is his ability to translate complex tort theories into a narrative that exposes the absurdity of the plaintiff's claim without dismissing the frustration of the homeowner. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on general principles that may vary by specific local zoning laws, which Stone briefly acknowledges but does not deeply explore. Readers should watch for how local ordinances regarding beekeeping density might shift the balance from a non-actionable nuisance to a legitimate regulatory violation.
