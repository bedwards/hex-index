Three dilemmas of the Venezuela crisis and one proposal to found the united nations anew

Jeff Rich · ·Jan 6, 2026 ·38 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Jeff Rich delivers a searing indictment of a hypothetical future where the United States kidnaps the Venezuelan president, framing the event not as a rogue operation but as the inevitable culmination of decades of imperial overreach. While the premise is speculative, the piece forces a confrontation with the stark reality that the executive branch has increasingly operated outside the constraints of international law, treating the United Nations not as a partner but as an obstacle to American interests.

The Hostage Dilemma

Rich begins by dismantling the standard narrative of intervention, arguing that the administration's actions have reduced international diplomacy to a hostage negotiation with a criminal state. He writes, "The USA's kidnapping of the President of Venezuela and his wife is a gross breach of international law and the United Nations charter." This framing is deliberate and jarring; it strips away the euphemisms of "regime change" or "humanitarian intervention" to reveal the raw mechanics of coercion. The author suggests that the global community now faces a choice: normalize this breach or demand accountability.

The piece draws a sharp contrast between domestic law enforcement and international relations. Rich notes, "In the USA, this situation might be resolved by shooting first and avoiding questions later... But in the society of nations, beyond that rogue state, public authorities aim to resolve hostage situations peacefully, tactfully, and in accordance with the law." This distinction is crucial. It highlights how the executive branch's "war footing" mentality, as described by Rich, ignores the delicate fabric of global stability. The argument gains weight when Rich points out the disconnect between Washington's posturing and reality: "Claims by USA officials to be 'running Venezuela' appear inflated and designed for another 'mission accomplished' press conference... They seem disconnected from on-the-ground realities, the mood of Venezuelans, and global opinion."

The stunt has been staged, with an uncanny resemblance to the scenario presented in season two of Tom Clancy's Jack Ryan, but what now? There are no answers to that question.

Rich's reliance on the 2002 coup attempt against Hugo Chavez as a historical parallel adds necessary depth. He argues that "Operation 'Absolute Resolve' might yet fail just as it did when the USA attempted to remove Chavez in 2002," reminding readers that military stunts often devolve into diplomatic disasters. However, critics might argue that the analogy to a fictional TV show undermines the gravity of the alleged kidnapping, potentially trivializing the human cost of such a crisis. Yet, the core point remains: the administration's confidence in its own omnipotence is a dangerous illusion.

Confronting the Defiant Power

The second major thrust of Rich's commentary is the challenge of holding the United States accountable when it explicitly rejects the authority of the body it helped create. He cites the 2025 National Security Strategy, which declared a doctrine of "primacy of nations" and asserted that the US would "chart our own course in the world and determine our own destiny, free of outside interference." Rich interprets this not as a policy shift but as a formal declaration of secession from the international order.

He writes, "If the USA does not respect the UN, why should the UN respect the USA?" This rhetorical question cuts to the heart of the institutional crisis. Rich argues that the US has treated the UN with "undue contempt for decades," a sentiment that has now crystallized into a policy of exceptionalism. He points out that the US is "the worst laggard in paying its dues," yet continues to wield veto power to shield its actions from scrutiny. The author suggests that the only logical response is to treat the US as a pariah: "If the USA wants to de facto secede from the UN, fine. Leave or be expelled."

The historical context Rich provides is damning. He notes that "Regime change operations are in direct breach of Article 2(4) of the United Nations Charter," and lists a litany of past violations, from Panama to Iraq. He references Mikhail Gorbachev's memoirs, where the Soviet leader questioned the US right to "lay down the law, bring in the verdict and execute the sentence" in sovereign nations. This historical thread connects the current crisis to a 25-year pattern of aggression, suggesting that the kidnapping is not an anomaly but a standard operating procedure. As Rich puts it, "The USA may have helped to draft the UN Charter. But at the same time, no power in the world has done more to subvert the Charter, the UN and international law."

The USA is the worst laggard in paying its dues. But shamefully, as Jan Oberg has exposed, China and Russia follow suit despite high-minded rhetoric.

While Rich's diagnosis of US exceptionalism is compelling, his proposed solution—expelling the US from the UN and dismantling its global military presence—faces significant geopolitical hurdles. A counterargument worth considering is that such a move would fracture the international system entirely, leaving a power vacuum that could be filled by even less stable actors. The UN's utility often lies in its ability to engage even its most powerful, flawed members, rather than ejecting them.

Bottom Line

Jeff Rich's piece is a powerful, if provocative, call to stop treating the United States as the indispensable nation and start treating it as a violator of the very laws it claims to uphold. Its greatest strength is the unflinching connection between current executive actions and a long history of disregarding international norms, but its vulnerability lies in the feasibility of its proposed reforms in a fractured world. Readers should watch for whether the global community can move beyond rhetorical condemnation to actual institutional consequences for the US.
