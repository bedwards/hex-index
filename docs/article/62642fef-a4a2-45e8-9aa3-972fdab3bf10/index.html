Ocean Acidification : What's the threat?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Aug 27, 2018 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace dismantles a pervasive myth with a soda stream and a bottle of pH indicator, proving that ocean acidification isn't about the sea turning into a corrosive acid bath, but rather a subtle, logarithmic shift that threatens the very foundation of marine life. While the title "Ocean Acidification" conjures images of melting faces, Borlace's DIY demonstration reveals a more insidious reality: the ocean is becoming less alkaline, and that small change is enough to dissolve the shells of the creatures that feed the entire food web.

The Chemistry of a Fizzy Drink

Borlace's approach is refreshingly grounded in the tangible. He bypasses abstract data models to show how carbon dioxide interacts with water in a way anyone can replicate at home. "These things work by forcing carbon dioxide into water at pressure and that then dissolves in the water before bubbling up to try and escape and that's what makes all fizzy drinks fizzy but it also causes a chemical reaction that we can measure." By using a common household appliance to demonstrate a global phenomenon, he makes the invisible visible. The shift from a neutral pH of 7 to 4.7 in his experiment serves as a microcosm for the ocean's struggle, though he wisely notes that the ocean is far more complex than a jug of water.

The core of his argument rests on the chemistry of carbonic acid. He explains that as the oceans absorb our industrial emissions, the water becomes saturated with hydrogen ions, which disrupts the delicate balance required for marine organisms to build shells. "Carbon dioxide plus water becomes H2 CO3 which is basic chemistry that I can remember from school and which I can just about manage even now." This self-deprecating humor disarms the reader, making the subsequent explanation of bicarbonate and carbonate ions accessible without dumbing down the science. He correctly identifies that the problem isn't just acidity, but the depletion of carbonate ions, the building blocks for calcium carbonate.

"Our .11 reduction is actually a 30% increase in acidity and apparently that is significant but pH 8.14 is still well alkaline isn't it so why is it so important well it turns out that the whole reaction we looked at earlier is reversible it works both ways depending on temperature and alkalinity."

This is the piece's most critical insight. Borlace highlights the logarithmic nature of the pH scale, a concept often glossed over in mainstream reporting. A drop of 0.11 sounds negligible, but on a logarithmic scale, it represents a massive surge in acidity. He effectively argues that the ocean's chemistry is not a static state but a dynamic equilibrium that is being pushed toward a tipping point where shell formation becomes impossible.

The Biological Reversal

The narrative shifts from chemistry to biology as Borlace introduces the concept of the "gerum plot" (a likely transcription error for Bjerrum plot), a graph that visualizes how carbonate availability shifts as pH drops. He illustrates that as CO2 increases, the ocean moves from a state where carbonate is abundant to one where bicarbonate dominates. "Simple carbonate levels go down and bicarbonate levels go up and that means less carbonate available to combine with calcium to make calcium carbonate and that means that shellfish and corals are less able to grow and repair themselves."

Critics might note that marine ecosystems are resilient and that some species may adapt to lower pH levels over time, a nuance Borlace touches on but does not fully explore. He acknowledges the complexity, mentioning that warmer waters could theoretically boost phytoplankton growth, which would absorb CO2. However, he quickly counters this with evidence suggesting that thermal stratification prevents nutrient mixing, ultimately starving these organisms. "As the oceans get warmer you get more temperature separation between the different depths and so there's less mixing of the layers that make these nutrients available."

The stakes are made visceral through the example of the pteropod, a tiny sea snail often called the "canary in the coal mine" for ocean acidification. Borlace describes a time-lapse video where a pteropod's shell dissolves in water with a projected future pH of 7.8. "Essentially the shell dissolves as the carbonate reacts with the free hydrogen ions to make bicarbonate." This visual evidence is powerful, moving the threat from a theoretical future to a biological reality that is already occurring in parts of the Pacific.

The Trajectory of Crisis

Borlace grounds his warning in the worst-case scenario known as RCP 8.5, a pathway representing "business as usual" emissions. He projects that by 2100, the average pH could drop to 7.8, a level that would be devastating for calcifying organisms. Yet, he balances this doom with a note of cautious optimism regarding renewable energy and political will. "Renewable technology is advancing at breathtaking speed and social and political will is changing fast despite the noise coming out of the White House so it's unlikely that we'll stay on that trajectory all the way to 2100."

However, he immediately undercuts this optimism with data from the Royal Society, which found that pteropods in the Pacific Northwest are already suffering severe shell dissolution. "The incidence of severe terapod shell dissolution owing to anthropogenic ocean acidification has doubled in Nearshore habitats since pre-industrial conditions across this region and is on track to Triple by 2050." This juxtaposition creates a sense of urgency: we may not reach the worst-case scenario by 2100, but we are already living through a crisis that is accelerating faster than many models predicted.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace succeeds in demystifying a complex chemical process, using a soda stream to illustrate a global catastrophe that is far more subtle than it is dramatic. His strongest argument lies in the explanation of the logarithmic pH scale, which reframes a seemingly minor drop in alkalinity as a 30% increase in acidity. The piece's vulnerability is its reliance on a single worst-case emissions trajectory to illustrate the danger, potentially underestimating the speed of adaptation or the variability of local ocean conditions, though the existing data on pteropod dissolution suggests the threat is already immediate. The takeaway is clear: the ocean is not turning into acid, but it is losing the chemical capacity to sustain the life that depends on it.
