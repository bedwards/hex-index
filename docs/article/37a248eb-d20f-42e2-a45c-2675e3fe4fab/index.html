In a live conversation that feels less like a Q&A and more like a masterclass in navigating the modern information landscape, Benn Jordan and Mark SubbaRao tackle the most pressing anxieties of our time: from the existential threat of science denial to the very real possibility of trapping humanity in a debris field around Earth. What makes this exchange notable is not just the scientific expertise SubbaRao brings from the Adler Planetarium, but the radical empathy Jordan insists upon when discussing why people reject established facts. They argue that the path to truth isn't paved with mockery, but with understanding the emotional roots of identity that drive denialism.

The Psychology of Denial

The conversation opens with a heavy but necessary topic: the rise of science hatred, specifically regarding vaccines and climate change. SubbaRao, drawing on two decades of experience in science communication, argues that the approach has shifted from dismissing fringe theories to addressing existential threats. He notes that dismissing these groups as "stupid" only causes them to retreat further. "The first thing you really need to do is listen to them, hear where they're coming from, see if they're areas where you have some sort of common ground, and then use that to start to build up your credibility," SubbaRao advises. This is a crucial pivot in the discourse; it moves the goalpost from winning an argument to building a bridge. Jordan reinforces this by pointing out that these conclusions are often "emotionally derived" rather than logically constructed, meaning that rational data alone cannot dismantle them.

The authors correctly identify that for many, rejecting a scientific consensus is an act of preserving their identity and community. SubbaRao references the documentary Behind the Curve to illustrate how deeply embedded these beliefs are in a person's sense of self. "Being presented with having to reject that idea is also really rejecting who they are, and that's why they can be very defensive," he explains. This framing is powerful because it humanizes the opponent without validating the falsehood. However, critics might note that while empathy is essential, it risks being interpreted as a concession to bad-faith actors who use the "listen" tactic to stall necessary policy changes. The balance between understanding and enforcing scientific reality remains a delicate tightrope.

The Kessler Syndrome and the Trash Tomb

Shifting from the social to the physical, the pair addresses a terrifyingly plausible scenario: the Kessler Syndrome. This is the theory that low Earth orbit could become so saturated with debris that a single collision triggers a cascading chain reaction, rendering space travel impossible for generations. SubbaRao highlights the scale of the problem, noting that new satellite constellations are launching at a rate where even a small percentage of failures creates a dangerous density. "One collision will make many more things, some of those things will hit other things, and it's just a runaway effect," he warns. The imagery of a "trash tomb" sealing humanity in is a stark, effective metaphor for a problem that feels abstract until it becomes irreversible.

Jordan presses on the lack of international regulation, asking if there are plans to restrict the number of objects sent into orbit. SubbaRao admits that while the Outer Space Treaty exists, it is unclear if there are robust agreements to limit the sheer volume of private launches. "I don't think there are solid agreements to limit the number of stuff that goes up there," he concedes. This admission is the piece's most alarming takeaway; we are actively building the conditions for our own isolation without a global safety net. The argument holds up well against current events, where the commercialization of space is outpacing the regulatory framework designed to manage it.

The Martian Gamble

The tone lightens as they discuss the feasibility of human colonization on Mars within our lifetime. Jordan, a musician who once applied to the Mars One project, brings a personal, slightly jaded perspective to the table. He recalls feeling "scammed" by the project's business model but acknowledges the technological plausibility of the mission. SubbaRao provides the hard physics, explaining that the energy required to return from Mars is a "factor of eight" greater than the energy to get there. "The technical challenges aren't just bringing people back, it's staying alive once you're there," SubbaRao notes, highlighting the difficulties of radiation exposure and food production. Jordan jokes about being the "MacGyver of the Mars One mission," but the underlying sentiment is one of cautious optimism. "I suppose if I had to bet, I would guess that it would happen in our lifetime," he says. This section serves as a reminder that while the engineering is daunting, the human drive to explore remains a constant variable.

The Multiverse and the Speed of Light

Finally, the conversation dives into the theoretical: black holes, parallel universes, and the expansion of the cosmos. SubbaRao breaks down the concept of the multiverse, distinguishing between an infinite universe where every atom arrangement repeats and the "many worlds" hypothesis from quantum mechanics. He explains that black holes, through the curvature of space-time, could theoretically act as bridges—Einstein-Rosen bridges or wormholes—connecting these different realities. "The idea is that very early on there's a phase transition in the universe that causes runaway expansion... and this kind of goes in with the idea that if it happened once, it's likely to have happened many many times," SubbaRao explains regarding chaotic inflation.

Jordan raises the common confusion about the universe expanding faster than the speed of light. SubbaRao clarifies that while nothing can travel through space faster than light, space itself can expand at any rate, effectively separating regions of the universe faster than signals can cross them. This distinction is vital for understanding why we can never reach the "edge" of the observable universe. The discussion here is dense, but the speakers manage to translate complex cosmological models into digestible concepts without oversimplifying the math. A counterargument worth considering is whether these multiverse theories, while mathematically sound, are scientifically falsifiable; if we can never interact with these other universes, can they truly be considered science? SubbaRao doesn't fully address this philosophical hurdle, focusing instead on the mechanics of the theory.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan and Mark SubbaRao deliver a rare blend of high-level astrophysics and grounded social commentary, proving that the most difficult problems—from climate denial to orbital debris—require both data and deep human understanding. The strongest part of their argument is the insistence that empathy is a prerequisite for effective science communication, a point often lost in polarized debates. Their biggest vulnerability lies in the lack of concrete solutions for the regulatory gaps in space exploration, leaving the "trash tomb" scenario as a looming threat without a clear path to prevention. Readers should watch for how international policy catches up to the rapid commercialization of space, as the window to prevent a Kessler Syndrome event is closing fast.
