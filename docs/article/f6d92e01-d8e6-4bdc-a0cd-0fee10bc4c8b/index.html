Unicef: Over 100 children killed in gaza since “ceasefire”; mass funeral in Iran; ICE arrests…

Ryan Grim & Jeremy Scahill · Drop Site ·Jan 14, 2026 ·17 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill deliver a harrowing dispatch that refuses to let the world look away from the human cost of geopolitical maneuvering, revealing that the so-called "ceasefire" in Gaza has been a veneer for continued attrition. The most jarring revelation here is not just the continued violence, but the specific architecture of the postwar plans being drafted in Washington, which appear to rely on the very networks that oversaw the deadly aid distribution failures of the previous year. This is not a standard news recap; it is an indictment of a governance strategy that prioritizes political optics over the survival of a population already pushed to the brink.

The Illusion of Ceasefire in Gaza

The authors immediately dismantle the narrative of stability by presenting data that contradicts the official peace rhetoric. Grim and Scahill write, "UNICEF says more than 100 children have been killed in Gaza since the October ceasefire," a statistic that forces a re-evaluation of what "ceasefire" actually means on the ground. The coverage details how these deaths are not merely collateral but result from a mix of active military engagement and environmental neglect. "Children are still being killed by airstrikes, drones, tank shelling, and live fire, while others are dying in tent shelters from exposure to winter storms," the report notes, highlighting a dual crisis of violence and abandonment.

The framing is particularly effective because it juxtaposes the high-level diplomatic talk with the grim reality of the Ministry of Health's count: "The total recorded death toll since October 7, 2023 is now 71,439 killed, with 171,324 injured." This sheer scale of loss serves as a counterweight to the bureaucratic language of "governance frameworks." The authors argue that the administration's approach is fundamentally disconnected from the humanitarian reality, noting that hospitals are overwhelmed with shortages of medicine and a surge in respiratory illnesses. Critics might argue that in a conflict zone, some level of violence is inevitable during a transition, but the specificity of the data—32 deaths from cold and collapsing shelters alone—suggests a systemic failure of protection rather than mere combat fallout.

The so-called "ceasefire" has become a mechanism for continued attrition, where children die not just from bullets, but from the freezing cold in tents that the international community has failed to adequately support.

The Architecture of Postwar Governance

Perhaps the most disturbing element of the coverage is the revelation regarding who is designing the future of Gaza. Grim and Scahill expose a troubling continuity in personnel, writing, "Gaza's postwar planners tied to the Gaza Humanitarian Foundation," a group previously responsible for aid distribution that resulted in the deaths of over 2,600 Palestinians. The report identifies specific figures, such as "senior U.S. Envoy Aryeh Lightstone, who worked to secure funding and international backing for the GHF," now tasked with shaping the "second stage" of the ceasefire. This is a profound conflict of interest that the authors highlight with surgical precision.

The proposed plan involves a "Palestinian-led technocratic committee" to take over day-to-day administration, with Nickolay Mladenov as a "high representative." However, the authors point out the fragility of this structure: "Critics warn that the governance framework lacks international buy-in, peacekeeping commitments, and adequate funding, with only about $1 billion raised compared to the estimated needs totaling tens of billions." The argument here is that the administration is attempting to outsource the impossible task of rebuilding a destroyed territory to a small, underfunded committee staffed by individuals with a controversial track record. The omission of a robust international peacekeeping force in the plan suggests a strategy of containment rather than genuine reconstruction.

Escalation in Iran and Regional Instability

The coverage shifts to the volatile situation in Iran, where the administration's rhetoric has shifted from diplomacy to direct incitement. Grim and Scahill report that "President Donald Trump urges Iranian protestors to seize institutions, says talks are canceled with Iranian officials," a move that effectively burns bridges with the current government. The Iranian response is swift and personal, with the security chief naming Trump and Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu as "killers." The authors note the grim reality of the protests: "Thousands of mourners attend a mass state funeral in Tehran, as Iranian state television acknowledged reports of a high death toll."

The reporting digs into the financial underpinnings of the push for regime change, revealing that "dark-money networks back the push for regime change in Iran." The authors trace millions of dollars from conservative donors to think tanks advocating for this shift, suggesting that the policy is driven less by national security and more by ideological and financial interests. "From 2020–2023, Donors Trust gave more than $2.7 million to the far-right Center for Security Policy," they write, connecting the dots between private funding and public policy. This framing challenges the notion that the administration's stance is a spontaneous diplomatic reaction, presenting it instead as a calculated, funded strategy. A counterargument might be that supporting democratic movements is a moral imperative, but the authors rightly question the timing and the potential for catastrophic regional escalation, especially as Gulf states warn against military action.

Domestic Fallout and Human Rights

The piece does not shy away from the domestic consequences of these foreign policy shifts. The authors highlight a crackdown on refugees in the United States, noting that "Dozens of refugees are arrested in Minnesota amid Trump administration crackdown." This includes individuals who passed security screenings, challenging the narrative of a vetted and safe immigration system. "Most of those detained are Somali, including children, despite their lawful entry," the report states, illustrating the human cost of policy reinterpretations. Furthermore, the designation of Muslim Brotherhood branches as terrorist organizations is presented not just as a foreign policy move, but as a domestic legal maneuver that has ripple effects on civil liberties and community trust.

The coverage also touches on the legal defeat of the radical pro-Israel group Betar U.S., which was forced to dissolve its nonprofit operations in New York due to allegations of inciting violence. "A New York state legal settlement forces Betar U.S. to dissolve nonprofit operations," the authors note, providing a rare instance of accountability for extremist rhetoric. This juxtaposition of domestic crackdowns on refugees and the legal dismantling of violent groups creates a complex picture of a nation struggling with its own internal contradictions while projecting power abroad.

Bottom Line

Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill have crafted a narrative that exposes the dangerous gap between diplomatic announcements and the brutal reality on the ground, particularly in Gaza and Iran. Their strongest argument lies in the revelation of the personnel continuity between the failed aid distribution of the past and the proposed governance of the future, a detail that fundamentally undermines the credibility of the administration's peace plan. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on figures from organizations with their own political agendas, such as HRANA in Iran, though the authors do note the lack of independent verification. Readers should watch closely for the actual release of the governance framework, as the details of funding and security commitments will determine whether this is a genuine path to peace or a recipe for further collapse.
