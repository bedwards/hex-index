Why jd vance is both the realest and fakest politician

Richard Hanania · ·Jul 8, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Richard Hanania delivers a piercing psychological autopsy of JD Vance, arguing that the Senator's political power stems not from consistency, but from a unique and unsettling duality: a commitment to small, personal truths that masks a grand, self-deceptive narrative about his own ambitions. This is not merely a critique of flip-flopping; it is an analysis of how "meta-dishonesty" allows a politician to convince himself he is virtuous while dismantling the very institutions he claims to protect.

The Architecture of Self-Deception

Hanania's central thesis rests on a sharp distinction between Vance's "micro-honesty" regarding his daily life and his "meta-dishonesty" concerning his motivations. He notes that on basic facts, Vance is surprisingly candid, even when it costs him politically. Hanania observes that "Vance doesn't like lying in interviews, which is why he's capable of being embarrassed when inconvenient facts are presented to him, in ways [the former President] is not." This admission of error regarding the "cats and dogs" incident serves as a microcosm for Vance's approach: he will admit to a specific falsehood to preserve his self-image as an honest man.

However, this honesty is a trapdoor that leads into a much deeper fabrication about his career trajectory. Hanania argues that Vance constructs a narrative where he is an "Appalachian Chauncey Gardiner," a passive figure swept up by events rather than a strategist climbing the ladder. The author writes, "Vance says it was all kind of an accident... He ran for Senate in order to make a point about Christian economics! But then, oops, somehow he ended up winning his seat." This framing is crucial because it absolves Vance of the political calculation required to pivot from a fierce critic of the administration's populist turn to its most loyal lieutenant.

The micro-honesty serves to convince Vance, and those around him, that he is a fundamentally decent man, which makes the meta-dishonesty easier to pull off.

Hanania points out that this accidental rise defies the documented reality of his relationships with powerful figures like Peter Thiel and Donald Trump's son. If one accepts Hanania's reading, Vance's story mirrors the thematic struggle found in Hillbilly Elegy, where personal agency is often obscured by a sense of fatalism, yet here it is weaponized to hide ambition. The author suggests that Vance has internalized his own fiction so thoroughly that he believes he didn't want the power he now wields.

A War on Rational Thought

The commentary then shifts to how this self-deception corrupts Vance's policy positions, particularly in economics. Hanania identifies a "crank" element in Vance's worldview, where he rejects data-driven analysis because it contradicts his political base's prejudices. The author notes that Vance claims to have learned from a trip to Japan that "Maybe economics is just fake," reducing complex global trends to the quality of strawberries and the structure of Catholic theology.

This rejection of expertise is not presented as a novel insight but as a strategic necessity for Vance's demagoguery. Hanania writes, "The war on 'economics' is really just a war on rational thought or data being applied to critique the opinions of ignorant voters that Vance relies on." By framing economists as cold calculators who ignore human suffering, Vance creates a strawman that allows him to advocate for policies with no theoretical grounding. The author notes that "no economist takes these positions" regarding GDP maximization at all costs, yet Vance attacks this non-existent caricature to validate his own nativist views.

Critics might argue that dismissing the entire field of economics is an oversimplification of a legitimate debate about how growth metrics fail to capture social well-being. However, Hanania counters that Vance goes beyond critique into "pure economic flat-eartherism," ignoring consensus on trade and immigration that spans both major parties. This section highlights a dangerous trend where intellectual rigor is sacrificed for the comfort of a political tribe.

The Paradox of the Good Man

Ultimately, Hanania's analysis leads to a disturbing conclusion: Vance may be a "good husband" and "good father," yet his need to believe in his own virtue drives him toward destructive politics. He applies Robert Trivers' theory of self-deception, suggesting that Vance convinces himself he is acting out of moral necessity when he is actually pursuing power. The author states, "Ironically, it is that need to convince himself of his virtue that leads him to adopt such destructive political positions and makes him one of the most effective demagogues of our generation."

This framing connects Vance's personal journey to a broader historical pattern where religious or moral certainty can be twisted to justify political opportunism. Much like the Dominican Order's historical emphasis on truth-seeking, which Hanania references in the context of Vance's conversion stories, the pursuit of "truth" here becomes a tool for obfuscation. Vance's story is not one of accidental discovery but of calculated adaptation, disguised as a spiritual awakening.

Yes, politicians in general aren't known for being systematic thinkers... But Vance is unique in the degree to which he seeks to intellectualize his demagoguery, with the pretensions of innocent truth seeking.

The piece leaves the reader with the chilling realization that the most dangerous politician may not be the one who lies blatantly, but the one who believes their own lies so completely that they can deceive everyone else without a flicker of guilt.

Bottom Line

Hanania's strongest contribution is exposing how Vance uses "micro-honesty" as a shield to protect a facade of "meta-dishonesty," allowing him to navigate political contradictions with a straight face. The argument's vulnerability lies in assuming that self-deception is the sole driver, potentially underestimating the raw cynicism that often fuels such rapid ideological shifts. Readers should watch for how this duality shapes future policy: expect Vance to frame every strategic pivot as an accidental discovery of higher truth.

Why jd vance is both the realest and fakest politician

by Richard Hanania · · Read full article

I have a morbid fascination with JD Vance. Since I’ve thought about the man a lot, I didn’t think I’d get anything new from his most recent book, Communion. But I was wrong, as it led me to develop a novel theory about what makes him so unique and unsettling as a political figure.

There is a disparity between what we might call his meta-dishonesty and his micro-honesty. This is how Vance can be understood as an outlier in terms of both how genuine and how fake he seems at the same time.

On basic facts about his day-to-day life, I think Vance is honest. I believe his conversion story, because if any part of it were made up, it would have been a lot less lame. Vance cites two “miracles” for his conversion during the process of him contemplating whether to join a church. In one, while discussing theology with a conservative intellectual – later revealed to be Ross Douthat – a wine glass fell on the floor. In addition to this, he was listening to a psalm on the way to meet a Dominican friar, and then, when they went to a chapel together, they heard the same psalm! Vance is aware this isn’t going to convince anyone, and becomes defensive.

Now, I know it’s easy to make the skeptic’s case: “Oh, come on, JD, a group of Christians sang a song and then another group of Christians sang the same song in a different language? Total coincidence!”

But to quote Samuel L. Jackson from Pulp Fiction: “You don’t judge s*** like this based on merit. Now, whether or not what we experienced was an ‘according to Hoyle’ miracle is insignificant. What is significant is I felt the touch of God.”

The reader can decide whether a Samuel L Jackson quote refutes David Hume. As a reviewer for The Atlantic put it, “If I were Vance, I would be a little offended that God was not putting in more effort to recruit me.”

So it all rings true. Vance also doesn’t like lying in interviews, which is why he’s capable of being embarrassed when inconvenient facts are presented to him, in ways that Trump is not. When he was forced to defend the cats and dogs nonsense, he came out and admitted he was lying, which I guess is the most honest possible way one could’ve handled that ...
