Bill Bishop doesn't just report on the Chinese establishment's reaction to the Iran conflict; he exposes a dangerous schism between those celebrating American overreach and the sober economists warning that the fallout will crush China's own growth. The piece's most arresting insight is that while hawkish voices see a "tiger-riding predicament" for Trump as a strategic gift, the most credible voices in Beijing are terrified that the resulting oil shock could drag GDP to 3% or lower. This isn't a story about who wins the war; it's about who survives the economic aftershocks.

The Tiger and the Trap

Bishop captures the prevailing mood in Beijing with a vivid metaphor: the "tiger-riding predicament," or deng hu nan xia. He notes that early skepticism about US regime-change prospects has hardened into a feeling of "consternation and schadenfreude," yet he quickly pivots to the voices urging caution. "Shi Zhan and former Brookings 'returnee scholar' Li Cheng urge caution on US failure narratives and dismiss comparisons to the post-9/11 strategic distraction from China," Bishop writes. This is a crucial correction to the popular narrative. Just as the US invasion of Afghanistan once created a strategic opening for China, many now assume a quagmire in Iran will do the same. Bishop highlights why this logic is flawed: the geopolitical landscape has shifted. Li Cheng argues that the US will not soften its stance on China, regardless of the Middle East mess.

The commentary effectively contrasts this sober analysis with the more aggressive, opportunistic views. Wang Jiangyu, for instance, declares this "the last war America can launch with any semblance of dignity," suggesting that allies refusing to follow Washington's lead marks a terminal decline in US power. Bishop presents this view, but the tension is palpable. While Wang sees a weakening empire, Di Dongsheng argues that China is "following the script the United States played in the early stages of World War One to profit from the war." Di suggests that high energy prices actually improve the relative competitiveness of Chinese manufacturing against oil-dependent rivals.

"The dominant motif is Trump's 'tiger-riding predicament'—meaning that it is easier to climb on a tiger than to dismount and easier to start a war than to end one."

However, Bishop does not let the "profit from war" narrative stand unchallenged. He introduces a stark counter-argument from Morgan Stanley Chief China Economist Xing Ziqiang, who warns that markets are dangerously underpricing the risk. "Xing Ziqiang... warns that markets are underpricing the oil shock. His surprisingly stark warning is that $130 oil over a single quarter could drag China's GDP growth to 3% or below," Bishop reports. This is the piece's most vital economic insight. The idea that China benefits from high oil prices ignores the reality that China is still a massive net importer. A prolonged shock would not just hurt the US; it would strangle the very engine of Chinese growth. Critics might note that Di Dongsheng's argument about "energy autonomy" relies on China being more self-sufficient than its neighbors, but it glosses over the fact that global demand destruction hurts Beijing's export model more than it helps its domestic industry.

The Two Sessions and the Pension Paradox

Beyond the geopolitical theater, Bishop turns his attention to the domestic economy, specifically the recent Two Sessions and the new Five-Year Plan. The coverage reveals a deep anxiety about the limits of the current growth model. While the official plan was "expectedly boilerplate," the debate over rural pensions exposed a fracture in the consensus. Bishop highlights the debate between those calling for cash transfers and those fearing the social consequences. "Lü Dewen mounts an eight-point rebuttal, concluding that cash is not what the elderly actually need—and that pension transfers risk producing a 'crowding-out effect' on children's sense of filial obligation," Bishop writes.

This reference to the "crowding-out effect" is a fascinating application of economic theory to social ethics. It suggests that state welfare might inadvertently erode the traditional family structure that has long served as China's social safety net. Bishop connects this to a broader pessimism about the future. Zhou Tianyong puts a "damning number on China's reform deficit, forecasting an average growth of just 2.4% through 2035 unless institutional change occurs." The author uses this to illustrate the growing gap between the government's modernization targets and the reality of a stagnating economy.

The debate over consumption is equally contentious. Bishop notes that while many push for a pivot to consumption-driven growth, others argue this is a "red herring." Yu Yongding rejects the existence of a coherent "consumption-driven growth" category, while Zhu Tian argues that low consumption was actually a "key driver of four decades of growth rather than a pathology." Bishop frames this not as a policy disagreement but as a fundamental clash of economic philosophies. The establishment is struggling to find a new engine for growth, and the old models are showing their cracks.

"Zhou Tianyong puts a damning number on China's reform deficit, forecasting an average growth of just 2.4% through 2035 unless institutional change occurs."

Strategic Realignment and the Taiwan Question

The final section of Bishop's report extends these economic anxieties into broader strategic questions. The tone shifts from caution to a more assertive, perhaps even desperate, posture. Bishop notes that Wu Xinbo portrays a Beijing that "increasingly regards tariffs as a secondary issue and appears prepared to retaliate against US interests in third countries." This suggests a shift from defensive trade wars to offensive geopolitical maneuvering.

Zheng Yongnian calls for an "Interventionism 2.0," arguing that the PRC's traditional non-interference doctrine no longer matches the scale of its global interests. Bishop captures the urgency of this shift: "Zheng Yongnian, in turn, calls for an 'Interventionism 2.0', arguing that the PRC's traditional non-interference doctrine no longer matches the scale of its global interests." This is a significant departure from decades of Chinese foreign policy. The article also touches on the Taiwan issue, where the rhetoric has become increasingly blunt. Wei Leijie captures the sentiment that "common arguments for strategic patience are fallacies," signaling a growing impatience with the status quo.

The piece concludes with a look at the artificial intelligence sector, where the debate turns to the societal implications of rapid technological change. Liang Jianzhang and Wang Ciqiao warn of AI as a "fertility depressant," while Yao Yang describes it as a "massive bubble." Bishop weaves these disparate threads together to paint a picture of a nation grappling with multiple, simultaneous crises: a potential war in the Middle East, a stagnating domestic economy, and a technological revolution that threatens to upend the social contract.

"Zheng Yongnian, in turn, calls for an 'Interventionism 2.0', arguing that the PRC's traditional non-interference doctrine no longer matches the scale of its global interests."

Bottom Line

Bishop's greatest strength is his ability to juxtapose the hawkish optimism of China's strategists with the grim realism of its economists, revealing a leadership that is increasingly divided on how to navigate a volatile world. The piece's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that the "tiger-riding" metaphor fully captures the US position, potentially underestimating the US administration's willingness to escalate or de-escalate based on domestic politics rather than strategic logic. Readers should watch closely whether the "Interventionism 2.0" rhetoric translates into actual policy shifts in the coming months, or if it remains a theoretical exercise in a country that is still trying to figure out how to pay its own bills.

This monthly report is prepared for Sinocism by the excellent Sinification, an invaluable resource for understanding how domestic and international affairs are debated within the Chinese establishment. — Bill

The war in Iran predictably dominates commentary in March.

We covered initial reactions to the US-Israeli strikes in our briefing earlier last month, where we noted that assessments of China’s risk-opportunity balance hinged largely on Trump’s ability to turn action into success. We also featured a censored piece from the Intellisia Institute that pushed this logic further, arguing that a prolonged Middle East war could become a major strategic opportunity for China.

More than a month on, early scepticism about US regime-change prospects in Iran has hardened into a feeling somewhere between consternation and schadenfreude, with most analysts now framing the situation as a quagmire. The dominant motif is Trump’s “tiger-riding predicament” [骑虎难下]—meaning that it is easier to climb on a tiger than to dismount and easier to start a war than to end one.

Which makes it refreshing to read CFAU professor Shi Zhan and former Brookings “returnee scholar” Li Cheng urging caution on US failure narratives and dismissing comparisons to the post-9/11 strategic distraction from China. On the more familiar hawkish side we have Wang Jiangyu, who calls this “the last war America can launch with any semblance of dignity”.

Meanwhile, Di Dongsheng argues that high energy prices are a net gain for China—a case he was apparently unable or unwilling to make on his WeChat blog on the same theme a few days earlier, which promised but conspicuously withheld the argument. The economic counter-case comes from Morgan Stanley Chief China Economist Xing Ziqiang, who warns that markets are underpricing the oil shock. His surprisingly stark warning is that $130 oil over a single quarter could drag China’s GDP growth to 3% or below.

Coverage of the Chinese economy is also weightier than usual, driven by the Two Sessions and the new Five-Year Plan. Direct FYP coverage was expectedly boilerplate, but the rural pension question (the FYP’s token increase was arguably the biggest social policy disappointment of the Two Sessions) prompted a more pointed debate. Against the many calls among Chinese economists for significant pension increases, Lü Dewen mounts an eight-point rebuttal, concluding that cash is not what the elderly actually need—and that pension transfers risk producing a “crowding-out effect” [挤出效应] on children’s sense of filial obligation.
