Why you’re bad with money

The Walrus · ·Jan 4, 2026 ·16 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

This piece from The Walrus dismantles the most persistent myth in personal finance: that being "bad with money" is a character flaw. Instead, Kelley Keehn argues that financial distress is often a physiological and psychological response to systemic pressure, not a lack of willpower. By weaving together neuroscience, intergenerational trauma, and the crushing weight of modern economic insecurity, the article reframes debt not as a moral failure, but as a predictable reaction to a broken system.

The Physiology of Shame

Keehn opens with the story of "Michael," a marketing manager whose life unraveled not because he was reckless, but because his nervous system was hijacked by chronic stress. The Walrus writes, "When stress becomes chronic, it hijacks the brain's executive functioning, which makes it harder to plan, act, or solve problems." This is a crucial pivot. It moves the conversation from "why didn't you budget?" to "why is your brain unable to process the data?" The article effectively uses Michael's experience to illustrate that avoidance is a survival mechanism, not laziness. As Keehn notes, "Avoidance breeds more avoidance. And eventually, even opening his mailbox felt like a threat."

This framing is particularly potent because it aligns with historical data on cognitive load. Just as the companion deep dive on financial literacy suggests, the mental bandwidth required to survive poverty leaves little room for long-term planning. Keehn captures this perfectly: "It wasn't that Michael didn't know what to do. It was that his nervous system was overwhelmed." The argument holds up well against the common critique that financial advice is universally accessible; the article correctly identifies that the barrier isn't information, but the capacity to act on it.

"Broken people don't show up to sessions. Brave ones do."

The piece also tackles the isolation of debt with striking empathy. Keehn points out that shame thrives in secrecy, a dynamic that keeps millions silent. "The pain Michael felt wasn't his alone," she writes, citing data that seven in ten Americans have struggled with debt. By normalizing the experience, the article attempts to break the cycle of silence that often precedes financial collapse. Critics might argue that individual stories, while moving, don't address the structural mechanics of interest rates or wage stagnation, but Keehn's point is that without addressing the emotional paralysis, structural solutions remain out of reach.

The Inheritance of Fear

The commentary shifts to a deeper, more personal layer with the story of "Maria," a successful project manager who feels a constant, low-grade anxiety despite her financial stability. Here, Keehn introduces the concept of "money scripts"—unconscious beliefs inherited from family and culture. "These unconscious beliefs are known as money scripts, a term coined by financial psychologist Brad Klontz," The Walrus explains. "Some examples include: 'We can't afford that.' 'Money doesn't grow on trees.' 'People like us don't do things like that.'"

This section is the article's intellectual core. Keehn connects Maria's anxiety to her mother's history of sacrifice, noting that "her family hadn't just passed down frugality; they had passed down something heavier: Fear. Duty. Survival." The narrative powerfully illustrates how early conditioning overrides adult logic. Keehn writes, "Research from the University of Cambridge suggests that money habits are already set by age seven." This fact, often overlooked in standard financial literacy programs, explains why simply teaching adults to budget often fails. The emotional brain, wired for safety and familiarity, rejects new data that contradicts deep-seated family narratives.

The article also touches on the unique pressures facing first-generation immigrants. Keehn describes how Maria's guilt about having "more" than her parents created a paradox where she couldn't hold onto money. "There was nothing wrong with Maria. She was carrying history in her bones." This is a profound observation that challenges the meritocratic assumption that financial success is solely a result of individual choices. It suggests that for many, financial behavior is a form of loyalty to a past struggle, making it incredibly difficult to adopt new, wealth-building habits.

The Paralysis of Choice

Finally, Keehn addresses the modern investor, "Javier," who is paralyzed by the sheer volume of conflicting advice and the fear of making a mistake. The Walrus writes, "Behavioural economists call this decision paralysis—when too many options, conflicting advice, or high perceived stakes make us freeze instead of act." This is a timely critique of the current financial landscape, where social media algorithms amplify fear and confusion. Keehn notes that for younger generations, the stakes feel existential: "One wrong move, he feared, and he'd undo not just his own hard work but everything his parents had sacrificed for him."

The article highlights a stark reality: despite being the first generation expected to be worse off financially than their parents, millennials are expected to navigate a complex, volatile market without a safety net. Keehn writes, "Most financial advice still clings to outdated rules made for a world that no longer exists." This is a sharp critique of the industry's failure to adapt to seismic economic shifts, such as the disappearance of pensions and the skyrocketing cost of housing. While some might argue that the solution is simply better education, Keehn makes a compelling case that the problem is emotional, not just informational. "You can know what you 'should' do with money and still not do it," she asserts, because the limbic system prioritizes safety over logic.

"Money isn't just about math. It's about emotions. It's about stories. It's about who we believe we are—and what we believe we deserve."

Bottom Line

The Walrus delivers a necessary corrective to the self-help genre of finance, proving that compassion is a more effective tool than calculation. The article's strongest asset is its ability to humanize the data, showing that financial distress is often a symptom of deeper psychological and cultural wounds. Its biggest vulnerability is that while it diagnoses the emotional root of the problem, it offers fewer concrete policy solutions for the systemic issues that create the stress in the first place. However, for any reader feeling the weight of their own financial story, this piece offers the most valuable resource of all: the realization that they are not broken.

By Kelley Keehn

Michael used to sleep with his phone under his pillow—not because he was waiting for an important call, but because the vibration from his banking app alerts would jolt him awake. Another overdraft. Another minimum payment. Another “you’re out of money” moment that felt like failure.

By day, he was a charismatic twenty-nine‑year-old marketing manager with a magnetic LinkedIn profile and a talent for storytelling. By night, he was dodging collection calls, ghosting his student loan portal, and panic-refreshing his credit card balance before trying to buy groceries. He didn’t always live like this; it happened slowly. First came the student loans—he told himself everyone had them. Then the credit cards—just for textbooks, at first. Then Uber Eats, because he was too tired to cook after ten‑hour workdays. The car lease “to look professional.” The drinks to network. The vacations to “reward himself” for surviving burnout. The shame crept in quietly, disguised as lifestyle. As performance. As “normal.” But here’s the thing about shame: it loves secrecy. And the more Michael tried to fix it alone, the worse it got.

Research from the American Psychological Association shows that financial stress is one of the top contributors to anxiety and depression, especially among millennials and Gen Z. And when stress becomes chronic, it hijacks the brain’s executive functioning, which makes it harder to plan, act, or solve problems. In other words: stress about money exacerbates financial problems. And avoiding dealing with debt doesn’t mean you’re lazy—it means your nervous system is overwhelmed.

Michael kept telling himself, “I’ll open the bills tomorrow.” “I’ll fix this when I get a raise.” “Once I pay off one card, the rest will be easy.” But avoidance breeds more avoidance. And eventually, even opening his mailbox felt like a threat.

It wasn’t until he missed his sister’s birthday dinner—too embarrassed to say he couldn’t afford the meal—that the dam broke. He collapsed. Told a friend everything. And something surprising happened: instead of judgment, he got a story. The friend had been there too—once sobbing on the bathroom floor over an $18 overdraft fee. The pain Michael felt wasn’t his alone. Countless others had lived it; he just hadn’t known where to find help.
