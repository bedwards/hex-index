To Do Well In College: Don't Work Hard, Work Efficiently

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·Jun 15, 2021 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan flips the script on the most pervasive myth in higher education: that academic success is a direct function of grinding hours. He argues that the students who earn A's aren't necessarily the hardest workers, but the most efficient ones, transforming passive attendance into active data capture. This isn't just motivational fluff; Kaplan provides a mathematical breakdown of how distributed effort and strategic note-taking yield exponentially higher retention rates than the frantic cramming that defines the typical student experience.

The Efficiency Equation

Kaplan's central thesis is that time spent in class is a sunk cost that must be maximized, not just endured. He posits that simply showing up is a waste if the brain isn't actively engaged. "In order to get good grades, great grades in your college courses, you don't need to work hard. What you need to do is work efficiently," he writes. This distinction is crucial because it shifts the student's focus from the quantity of hours logged to the quality of cognitive processing during those hours.

He illustrates this with a hypothetical but compelling arithmetic model. A student who reads before class, takes notes during the lecture, and revises those notes immediately afterward might spend roughly 51 hours over a semester. Yet, this process yields a 94% retention rate. "That's an A minus or an A depending on how the grading scale works," Kaplan notes. The efficiency comes from layering comprehension: initial reading provides a 50% baseline, the lecture pushes it to 95%, and the revision locks it in at 99%. The commentary here is sharp; it treats learning as a compounding interest problem rather than a linear accumulation of facts.

Notes are a tool of efficiency. They are a way to take the time that you're already spending reading or attending lecture and squeeze out of that time more comprehension, more retention.

Critics might argue that Kaplan's numbers are overly optimistic or that not every student can absorb material at a 95% rate through note-taking alone. However, the underlying logic holds regardless of the specific percentages: the marginal gain of active engagement far outweighs the passive consumption of information. The model effectively demonstrates that the "efficient" student is actually doing more cognitive work per minute, even if the total clock time is lower.

The Cost of Inefficiency

The piece takes a darker turn when Kaplan contrasts the efficient student with the "crammer." He describes a scenario where a student skims readings, skips note-taking, and saves time during the semester only to panic before the exam. "You spend 30 hours cramming over the course of the few days before the exam," he explains. This approach, while seemingly time-saving in the short term, results in a catastrophic drop in retention. The student ends up with a 5% final retention rate, leading to a failing grade despite spending 60 hours total on the course.

Kaplan's analysis of the "smart but lazy" student is particularly biting. He suggests that skipping the reading entirely to save time is a false economy. "You spent 45 hours also to get an F. That is more efficient. You still failed, but this time you only spent 45 hours," he quips. This highlights a critical flaw in the modern student's mindset: the belief that efficiency means doing less, when true efficiency means getting the desired result with the least amount of wasted effort. The inefficient student is actually working harder in terms of stress and total hours, just with a much lower return on investment.

The Bodybuilder Metaphor

To drive the point home, Kaplan introduces a vivid anecdote about a conversation overheard between two bodybuilders. One, significantly more muscular, explains his success not by the time spent in the gym, but by what he does during that time. "The difference between you and me is that we both spend the same amount of time in the weight room, but I spend that time lifting weights," the bodybuilder says. Kaplan uses this to reframe the classroom as a gym for the mind.

He argues that students often treat lectures like the less disciplined gym-goer: "You're in here just as much as I am. But unlike you, when I'm in this gym, I'm not looking at myself in the mirror, and I'm not fussing with my headphones." The metaphor is effective because it strips away the romanticism of "studying hard" and replaces it with the brutal reality of focused execution. It suggests that the barrier to success isn't intelligence or time, but the discipline to eliminate distractions during the allocated work window.

When you go to class, you're gonna be there for an hour. You have to focus during that hour. You have to use that hour efficiently.

While the bodybuilder analogy is powerful, it risks oversimplifying the cognitive load of learning. Unlike lifting a weight, where the action is binary (lift or don't lift), understanding complex academic material requires periods of reflection and confusion that might look like "resting" to an observer. Nevertheless, the core message—that passive presence is useless—remains robust.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's argument succeeds by dismantling the guilt associated with not studying for 12 hours a day, replacing it with a rigorous framework for active learning. The strongest part of his coverage is the mathematical demonstration that distributed, high-focus effort beats concentrated, low-focus cramming every time. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on idealized scenarios where students can perfectly execute note-taking and revision strategies without external interruptions. Readers should watch for the practical application of these techniques, as the gap between knowing the strategy and executing it under pressure remains the true challenge.
