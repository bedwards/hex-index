Ipcc Climate Change : Countdown to Katowice

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Oct 21, 2018 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through the usual climate doom to deliver a startlingly pragmatic blueprint: the path to survival isn't just about stopping carbon, but about preventing the economic collapse that stopping carbon might trigger. While most coverage fixates on the terrifying science of tipping points, Borlace zeroes in on the "seismic technological changes" required to fix them, arguing that the real battle isn't against the atmosphere, but against the inertia of global inequality and corruption.

The Human Cost of Decarbonization

Borlace begins by acknowledging the deep cynicism many feel toward political leadership, noting that "climatological feedback loops and tipping points are accelerating so quickly in so many areas of the planet." Yet, he refuses to surrender to fatalism. Instead, he frames the upcoming UN climate conference in Katowice not as a diplomatic formality, but as a critical juncture where leaders must decide whether to "strengthen climate policies without inducing economic collapse or hardship." This is a crucial pivot. The author recognizes that the IPCC's central tenet—limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius—is scientifically sound but politically fragile if it worsens "existing short-term economic and social tensions including unemployment poverty inequality."

"The challenge is therefore how to strengthen climate policies without inducing economic collapse or hardship and to make them contribute to reducing some of the fault lines in the world economy."

Borlace's analysis here is sharp because it connects the abstract concept of "carbon-intensive assets" to the very real human experience of lost dignity. He argues that if fossil fuel companies are dismantled, the transition must include "massive government assisted retraining programs for normal hard-working people who find themselves displaced." This is not merely an economic adjustment; it is a social imperative. Critics might argue that the timeline for such retraining is unrealistic given the speed of the required transition, but Borlace correctly identifies that neglecting this human element is what doomed previous industrial shifts in coal and steel.

Decoding the Pathways

To make sense of the massive data ahead, Borlace wades through the IPCC's dense terminology, translating "Shared Socio-Economic Pathways" (SSPs) and "Representative Concentration Pathways" (RCPs) into a narrative of choice. He outlines five distinct futures, from the "sustainable development pathway" to the "fossil fuel development pathway," which he bluntly calls the "worst case scenario." He then introduces "archetypal pathway scenarios," including the "low energy demand scenario" (LED), which shifts the focus from just supply-side technology to "demand side measures" involving consumer behavior.

The data he presents is staggering. To hit the LED target, the world needs renewables to constitute 73% of primary energy and 77% of electricity by 2050. In the industrial sector alone, the low energy demand scenario requires a "91% reduction in CO2 emissions by 2050." Borlace emphasizes that these are not incremental tweaks but "unprecedented measures" that require "marked up scaling in supply-side energy system investments."

"Climate policies in line with limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius would require a marked up scaling in supply-side energy system investments between now and mid-century reaching levels of between one point six and three point eight trillion US dollars per year globally."

The financial scale is the most compelling part of his argument. He notes that investments in low-emission energy must overtake fossil fuel investments by 2025, with solar alone requiring a trillion dollars annually. Yet, he points out a glaring omission in the initial data tables: land-use change. While the report references it later, Borlace rightly questions why such a massive contributor to methane and nitrous oxide emissions isn't front and center in the energy consumption breakdown. This oversight suggests that even the most comprehensive reports struggle to fully integrate the complexity of the biosphere into their economic models.

The Governance Gap

Perhaps the most provocative part of Borlace's commentary is his diagnosis of the political failure. He argues that the Paris Agreement's reliance on voluntary national commitments was a fundamental error, noting that "allowing nations to decide unilaterally on their level of commitment... doesn't work." The result is a trajectory toward "3 degrees of warming or more by 2100," a reality that demands a shift to mandatory, global intervention.

"1.5 degrees Celsius consistent pathways would require robust stringent and urgent transformative policy interventions... We're essentially in an emergency not dissimilar to the one we faced in the Second World War where industries were transformed forever within a couple of years."

Borlace draws a powerful parallel to the WWII industrial mobilization, suggesting that the only way to achieve the necessary speed is through "global government intervention to make the necessary measures mandatory." However, he immediately identifies the Achilles' heel of this approach: corruption. He highlights the IPCC's warning that transitions are "inherently exposed to the risks of free riding and moral hazards," arguing that without "reliable national and sub-national governance based on reciprocity," the entire effort will fail.

"Governments sit down talk to each other identify the root causes of corruption apply legislation to stamp it out and then enforce that legislation ruthlessly."

This is a bold claim. While the economic and technological pathways are well-mapped, the political will to "stamp out" corruption on a global scale remains the biggest unknown. A counterargument worth considering is that the very concept of "ruthless enforcement" across sovereign nations is a diplomatic impossibility in the current geopolitical climate. Yet, Borlace's insistence that this is the "second priority" on the leaders' to-do list forces readers to confront the uncomfortable truth that technology alone cannot save us if the governance structures are rotten.

"The transitions require accelerated and coordinated action in multiple systems across all world regions. They are inherently exposed to the risks of free riding and moral hazards."

Bottom Line

Borlace's strongest contribution is his refusal to separate climate science from social justice, correctly identifying that the transition will fail if it exacerbates inequality or ignores the human cost of industrial change. His biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that the political will exists to enforce the "ruthless" governance required to stop corruption and mandate the necessary economic shifts. Readers should watch for whether the Katowice conference can move beyond voluntary pledges to the kind of binding, global enforcement Borlace argues is the only way forward.
