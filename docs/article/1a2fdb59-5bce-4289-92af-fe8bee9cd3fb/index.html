Why the US Doesn't Support Taiwanese Independence

PolyMatter · PolyMatter ·Aug 6, 2021 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

PolyMatter exposes a diplomatic tightrope walk so precarious that a single deleted tweet featuring a flag icon could theoretically ignite a military conflict. The author's most striking claim is that the United States has spent forty years maintaining a "mutually enforced facade of resolution" where both Beijing and Washington believe they have secured a victory, while the reality is a deliberate, strategic ambiguity that satisfies neither side's long-term desires.

The Architecture of Ambiguity

The piece begins with a seemingly mundane incident: the White House deleting a tweet listing vaccine donors because it included a Taiwanese flag. PolyMatter writes, "So fragile is this issue that merely tweeting an icon of a flag might be interpreted as a change in policy or even a recognition of statehood." This incident serves as the perfect entry point to dismantle the common misconception that the U.S. adheres to China's "One China principle." The author clarifies a critical linguistic distinction that often gets lost in translation: the U.S. "acknowledges" the Chinese position that there is one China, but does not "accept" or "recognize" it.

This distinction is the bedrock of the entire argument. PolyMatter notes, "The key word here being acknowledge which is not the same as accept or recognize." The author argues that this was not an accident of diplomacy but a calculated strategy of obfuscation. By using vague language, the U.S. could technically satisfy Beijing's demands while retaining the ability to support Taiwan's defense. As PolyMatter puts it, "The American position then and now is to obfuscate to narrowly satisfy China's stringent demands while giving up as little as possible in the process."

Critics might argue that this semantic gymnastics is dangerous, creating a false sense of security that could lead to miscalculation. However, the author suggests that the alternative—clarity—would force a confrontation neither side wants yet. The 1979 Joint Communique, which normalized relations with the People's Republic of China, is presented not as a resolution, but as a delay tactic. "Both sides only kicked the can further down the road," PolyMatter observes, creating a "superficial illusion of agreement" that has led to decades of confusion.

Both sides only kicked the can further down the road, creating a superficial illusion of agreement that leads to hurt feelings and accusations later on.

The Internal Politics of the Status Quo

The commentary then shifts to the internal dynamics of Taiwan, challenging the binary narrative of "pro-unification" versus "pro-independence." PolyMatter explains that the two main political camps—the Kuomintang (KMT) and the Democratic Progressive Party (DPP)—actually converge on the short-to-medium term strategy: maintaining the status quo. The author writes, "In reality both sides agree that maintaining the status quo is the best short to medium term strategy and neither side except for a few radicals advocates unification or independence."

This is a crucial insight for busy readers who often view the conflict through the lens of American or Chinese media, which tends to exaggerate the push for independence. The author points out that the DPP, often labeled as separatist, argues that Taiwan is "already an independent sovereign state and thus has no reason to declare independence." Declaring independence formally would be a self-destructive move that would trigger a war. The public data supports this nuance. PolyMatter highlights that for twenty years, the top three responses from the Taiwanese public have been variations of "maintain the status quo," with only a small minority favoring immediate unification or immediate independence.

The author notes the irony that "it's most often Americans halfway across the globe who panic when China flies fighter jets through taiwanese airspace not the taiwanese public for whom such shows of intimidation have become mundane." This suggests that the external pressure to take a side often overshadows the pragmatic reality on the ground. The U.S. position, therefore, is to oppose both a forceful unification by China and a unilateral declaration of independence by Taiwan, a stance that keeps the region stable but leaves the fundamental question unanswered.

The Ticking Clock

The final section of the piece addresses the sustainability of this limbo. While the status quo has survived eight American administrations, the author warns that time may not be neutral. PolyMatter writes, "Every day China's military and economy grow stronger... China may believe time is on its side that the balance of power will only shift in its favor." Simultaneously, the demographic reality in Taiwan is shifting, with younger generations feeling an increasingly distinct identity. The 2014 Sunflower movement and recent events in Hong Kong have only hardened this resolve.

The author suggests that the current arrangement is "historically unprecedented" but ultimately untenable. "Taiwan is stuck in a historically unprecedented limbo, a weird in-between state in which it can enjoy freedom only if it pretends not to," PolyMatter concludes. The tension lies in the fact that while the U.S. and Taiwan want to preserve the current arrangement, China views the refusal to negotiate unification as a red line. The delicate balance relies on the assumption that no one will force the issue, yet the growing power disparity makes that assumption increasingly risky.

Bottom Line

PolyMatter's strongest contribution is the dissection of the linguistic loopholes that have allowed the U.S.-China-Taiwan relationship to persist for decades without resolution, revealing that the "One China" policy is a myth constructed from carefully chosen words. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that both Beijing and Washington will continue to prioritize ambiguity over clarity, even as the power balance shifts dramatically. Readers should watch for how the next generation of Taiwanese leaders navigates the widening gap between their distinct identity and the shrinking window for peaceful negotiation.
