Legal Positivism - the dominant theory in jurisprudence

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·May 4, 2023 · 17 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan dismantles a common misconception in legal philosophy by arguing that the most dominant theory in jurisprudence—legal positivism—is not a cynical dismissal of morality, but a precise description of how law actually functions as a human invention. He challenges the intuition that law must be inherently moral to be valid, using the tangible, everyday mechanics of currency and elevator etiquette to prove that social reality depends entirely on collective belief. For busy professionals navigating complex regulatory environments, understanding this distinction is not merely academic; it clarifies why a law can be legally binding yet morally repugnant, and why that distinction is actually a tool for justice rather than an obstacle.

The Architecture of Social Reality

Kaplan begins by grounding abstract legal theory in the concrete reality of "social phenomena," a category he defines as things that exist only because people think they do. He illustrates this with a simple prop: a piece of paper. "If all of the human beings in the whole world disappeared today this paper could still keep on existing... but its status as money depends on the continued thoughts every minute of people," Kaplan writes. This analogy is effective because it strips away the mystique of value, revealing that money, like law, is a shared hallucination that functions only through sustained collective agreement.

He extends this logic to unwritten social rules, such as the "elevator rule" where passengers face the door. "The reason people would think that's so weird is because there really is this rule but this rule relies for its existence on the thoughts of people," he notes. This is a crucial pivot in his argument: he establishes that rules do not require a physical enforcement mechanism to exist; they require only the psychological state of the group. Critics might argue that this reduces complex legal systems to mere habits, but Kaplan's point is that the source of the law's authority is always this human consensus, not some divine or natural order.

Law is something that was posited into existence by human thought.

The Positivist Thesis and Its Proponents

With the foundation of social phenomena laid, Kaplan defines legal positivism as the thesis that "law is a social phenomenon" and that "legal facts... are ultimately social facts." He traces this view through history, citing John Austin's theory of commands and sanctions, and H.L.A. Hart's more sophisticated hierarchy of rules. Kaplan explains that for Hart, law exists because of "patterns of behavior and attitudes mental States the states of people's minds."

This framing is particularly sharp because it demystifies the legal system. Instead of viewing law as a mysterious force descending from a higher power, Kaplan presents it as a psychological and sociological construct. "Hart's Theory therefore is ultimately a psychological theory of law," he asserts. This clarity is vital for law students and practitioners who often get bogged down in the why of law when they should be analyzing the how. By identifying law as a product of specific human actions—voting, writing, raising hands—Kaplan makes the system feel tangible and, paradoxically, more changeable.

The Clash with Natural Law

The commentary shifts to the primary rival of positivism: natural law theory. Kaplan contrasts the positivist view with the natural law claim that "legal facts are determined ultimately by social facts and by moral facts." He uses the example of crossing the street to show that while a positivist sees the law's validity in the legislative vote, a natural law theorist adds a moral condition: the law is only valid if it is not "sufficiently immoral."

Kaplan brings in Martin Luther King Jr.'s "Letter from a Birmingham Jail" to illustrate this tension, quoting King's agreement with St. Augustine that "an unjust law is no law at all." Here, Kaplan's analysis becomes most nuanced. He acknowledges that natural law theory attempts to inject morality directly into the definition of law, creating a moral check on legal validity. However, he argues that this approach creates a logical problem: if a law is invalid because it is immoral, then one cannot describe the law as it exists without first judging it as it ought to be.

The Separation Thesis and Moral Clarity

Perhaps the most surprising part of Kaplan's argument is his defense of the "separation thesis." He anticipates the reader's fear that separating law from morality means endorsing evil laws. "Positivism is just a theory about what law is it just says in order for a law to be law it doesn't necessarily have to be morally good," Kaplan clarifies emphatically. He argues that this separation actually provides a clearer path for moral resistance.

He posits that a positivist can look at segregation laws and say, "That's what the law is and those laws are evil," without the cognitive dissonance required by natural law theorists who must claim the laws "are not law at all." This is a powerful rhetorical move. It suggests that acknowledging a bad law as a law is the first step to fighting it, rather than pretending it doesn't exist. "The positivist can just State the case like this like look there are these laws... they make interracial marriage illegal so on and so forth that's what the law is and those laws are evil," Kaplan writes.

Critics might note that this rigid separation risks legitimizing tyranny by treating evil laws as valid simply because they were enacted correctly. However, Kaplan's counter is that the alternative—denying their legal status—can obscure the reality of the oppression people face. By accepting the law's validity as a social fact, we can more accurately diagnose its moral failure.

An unjust law is no law at all, but a positivist can say: that is the law, and it is evil.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's commentary succeeds by stripping legal philosophy of its pretension, revealing law as a human-made social technology rather than a moral absolute. His strongest argument is that the separation of law and morality is not a moral failing but a strategic necessity for clear-headed critique. The biggest vulnerability in his approach is the potential for this clarity to be weaponized by those who wish to enforce unjust laws by hiding behind the shield of "it's just the law," a risk he acknowledges but does not fully resolve. For the busy professional, the takeaway is clear: understanding what the law is is the only way to effectively argue for what it ought to be.
