Maundy Thursday: My Heart at God's Altar

Various · Wayfare ·Apr 2, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In an era where spiritual reflection is often reduced to curated Instagram moments or quick-fix affirmations, this piece from Wayfare dares to expose the raw, unpolished friction of actual faith. The author, writing as Various, does not offer a serene meditation on Maundy Thursday but rather a brutal inventory of the gap between the desire for holiness and the reality of human pettiness. This is not a devotional for the comfortable; it is a confession for the distracted, arguing that true worship begins only when the mental gymnastics of self-justification finally collapse.

The Architecture of Distraction

Various begins by dismantling the modern reader's tendency to treat God as a background process. The author writes, "The call cracks my heart. Splices it, pares it down, strips it to bare essence." This violent imagery sets the tone immediately: the divine encounter is not a gentle nudge but a surgical intervention. The author argues that our spiritual displacement is fueled by a specific modern malaise, describing it as a plea from the "belly of this post-truth cavern." By invoking the concept of acedia—a spiritual sloth or listlessness often associated with the desert fathers—the piece grounds its modern anxiety in ancient history. This connection is vital; it suggests that the urge to scroll through polished lives on LinkedIn or Facebook is not just a bad habit, but a symptom of a centuries-old struggle against the void.

The narrative then pivots to the domestic sphere, where the author admits that their spiritual crisis manifests as passive-aggression toward family. "The anger leached out in passive-aggressive moves against my wife, who admitted she found it hard to breathe when she heard the edge in my voice," Various notes. This is a striking admission of how internal spiritual dissonance creates external relational toxicity. The author confesses to retreating into digital distractions, noting how "those photos scintillated with an intoxicating veneer, cruise-like waves, and opening night spotlights."

Critics might argue that this focus on personal guilt overshadows the systemic issues of the church or society, but the piece's strength lies in its refusal to look outward before looking inward. The author effectively uses the contrast between the "polished lives" online and the "robotic" feeling of entering church to illustrate the hollowness of performative piety. As Various puts it, "Entering church felt robotic ( almost like an afterthought )." The commentary here is sharp: without the internal work of repentance, the external ritual is merely a performance.

The words are something between a tackle and an embrace.

The Communion of the Flawed

As the piece moves into the sacrament, the focus shifts from the author's internal struggle to the collective reality of the congregation. The author observes that the ordinance is administered by "One is nearly sixteen, red-headed, taciturn, and staid," and others who are "somewhat tongue-tied. But earnest." This is a crucial theological point: the mechanism of grace is not broken by human imperfection. The author draws a parallel to the biblical figures of Enoch and Jeremiah, suggesting that these young, imperfect servers carry a weight far greater than their years.

The description of the congregation is a masterclass in inclusive observation. Various lists the "sister who uses a walker," the "stuttering young single adult," and the "calloused-handed brother who works with drill bits." The author notes that these individuals are "as common as salt. No less unruly, except maybe in appearance." This leveling of the playing field challenges the reader's potential assumption that holiness belongs to the eloquent or the physically able. The argument is that the "ordinance" is not a reward for perfection but a remedy for the fractured.

However, the author does not let the reader rest in this comfort for long. The narrative circle closes back on the self, with the admission, "And then, I hear something... Sometimes it's a good friend's voice. Sometimes it's wind chimes." The author describes a moment of recalibration where "stillness wells up like water in a well." This transition from the noisy chaos of the week to the quiet of the chapel is the emotional climax of the piece. The author writes, "My skull recalibrates. My hearing amplifies with an increased measure of attention." This suggests that the purpose of the ritual is not to escape the world, but to sharpen one's ability to perceive it clearly.

The Gap Between Willingness and Action

The final section of the piece confronts the most difficult question: what happens after the service? The author admits that the word "Willing" is "easy on the lips, especially here in this air-conditioned chapel." The contrast between the ease of the word and the difficulty of the deed is stark. "I'm willing to shovel snow in the middle of July," the author writes, "but I shrink when scorn flows fast, raw, and fresh." This is a devastatingly honest assessment of human nature. We are willing to perform heroic acts in the abstract but fail to offer basic kindness in the heat of conflict.

The author references the biblical moment in Gethsemane, quoting "let this cup pass . . . nevertheless . . ." to highlight the tension between human desire and divine will. The piece acknowledges that for many, the rhythm of Holy Week is easily broken by "budget analysis, travel songs, and baseball season." This is a poignant critique of how secular priorities constantly encroach on the sacred. The author notes that "Some days, I need stillness like I need breakfast, but don't admit it." This line cuts through the pretense of religious observance, admitting that the need for spiritual sustenance is often ignored until a breaking point is reached.

The author concludes by redefining the purpose of the name of God, moving away from it being a "punctuation, routine, or doorstop" to something that feels like "the feeling that arrives after a first snowfall." The piece ends with a reference to a personal memory of leaving a hospital with a newborn, leaning into a "pure singular presence" that kept a "broken heart then—and even now—secure." This personal anecdote grounds the high theology in the visceral reality of human vulnerability.

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this piece is its unflinching honesty about the disconnect between religious aspiration and daily behavior, using the specific context of Maundy Thursday to expose the universal struggle of acedia. Its vulnerability lies in its heavy reliance on personal introspection, which may feel inaccessible to readers seeking more concrete social or theological frameworks. However, for the busy, skeptical reader, the verdict is clear: this is a necessary reminder that faith is not found in the perfection of the ritual, but in the messy, often failed, attempt to bridge the canyon between who we are and who we are called to be.

MY HEART AT GOD’S ALTAR

O God

The call cracks my heart. Splices it, pares it down, strips it to bare essence. A primordial reaching, a beckoning for home, for release from displacement, loneliness, fear, acedia, ennui; it’s a plea from the belly of this post-truth cavern. A shout from one side of a canyon to another. I approach the bridge to cross, the way to fly over the cliffs, the path around the loose and falling sediment. I yearn for escape from the consistent brush with physical and spiritual death. This cry to God loops me into the fray, but still, the signifier can’t begin to capture the width and depth and height of the signified.

I meditate over the appellation, measure it with what and how I know, actually know, my own earthly father. A monument exists in the title and reveals granite in his justice, temperance, and honesty. He’s sent me here to breathe and bathe, winnow and receive, understand what’s gained and lost in time, money, mountains, and dust. No matter how much of eternity I see in my own parents, I’ve come to this communion table to seek out his span and reach, his care, work, and unwearyingness.

In the name of thy Son

I remember the grumblings from earlier in the week: a friend who couldn’t keep their mouth shut, a relative I assumed was sidewinding, a child who didn’t listen each time I told them to cut their siblings some slack. The anger leached out in passive-aggressive moves against my wife, who admitted she found it hard to breathe when she heard the edge in my voice, my facial lines crinkling, aging me ten years in five minutes. I rankled, pivoted, and left the room. I then gave way to trolling through MLB.com, LinkedIn, and Facebook. Hours passed through me into others’ polished lives; those photos scintillated with an intoxicating veneer, cruise-like waves, and opening night spotlights. When I couldn’t fall asleep, I remembered the rationalizations that kept me from saying sorry, the ballooning pride still rising. The mumbled murmurs, “But then . . .” and “I never . . .” and “How do you expect me to . . . ?”
