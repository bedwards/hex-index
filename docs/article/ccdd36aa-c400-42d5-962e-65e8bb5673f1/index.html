{"title": "China's Rare Earth Chokehold", "author": "Patrick Boyle", "output": "<p>China's Rare Earth Chokehold</p> <p><strong>The China-US Meeting That Changed Everything</strong></p> <p>In recent weeks, Donald Trump and Xi Jinping agreed to delay export controls on rare earths and computer chips. The one-year deal followed months of escalating tariffs, retaliatory restrictions, and stalled negotiations. The US and China also reached agreements on American tariffs related to fentanyl and on tit-for-tat levines affecting each other's shipping industries.</p> <p>Trump sounded triumphant after the meeting, but the agreement was largely seen as a win for China. The US accepted a negotiating link between its restrictions on high-tech exports like AI chips and China's restrictions on rare earths. With the 20% fentanyl tariff halved, the average tariff rate on Chinese goods falls to 31%, well below the 50% tariffs on Brazil and India and below the 35% tariffs on many Canadian goods.</p> <p>Markets breathed a sigh of relief, but underlying tensions remain. Economists described the agreement as sketchy, temporary, and built on sand.</p> <p><strong>Why Rare Earths Matter</strong></p> <p>Rare earth elements have become a key negotiating point in recent standoffs between major powers. These 17 metallic elements are crucial components in electric motors, smartphones, airplanes, fiber optic networks, medical imaging equipment, and much more. They also play a role in missile guidance systems and jet engines.</p> <p>China has spent decades building dominance in rare earth production, and that dominance is now being used as leverage. These elements aren't particularly rare in the literal sense of the word, but they're often found together in low concentrations, making mining and separating them a complex and expensive process.</p> <p>The refining stage is messy, energy-intensive, and often involves strong acids. This is partially why China dominates the industry. It built infrastructure over the years, accepted environmental costs, and scaled up production when other countries were reluctant to get involved.</p> <p>While rare earth production has grown massively over the last 25 years, demand has grown just as fast. These elements have become almost indispensable in modern manufacturing.</p> <p><strong>The Magnets That Power Everything</strong></p> <p>Neodymium magnets are used in electric motors, wind turbines, and hard drives. They're small, powerful, and efficient, ideal for miniaturizing components and reducing energy consumption. Building something like an electric drone would be difficult without them.</p> <p>Rare earths are also used in polishing powders, battery alloys, and medical imaging equipment. They play a critical role in producing fiber optic cables, which form the backbone of the internet and modern telecommunications networks.</p> <p>Different rare earths are used to dope glass fibers, enabling amplification of light signals over long distances without degradation. This is essential for high-speed data transmission across continents, powering everything from video streaming and cloud computing to financial transactions and global communications.</p> <p>Without rare earths, the efficiency and reach of fiber optic networks would be severely limited, undermining the infrastructure that supports the digital economy.</p> <p><strong>The Military Dimension</strong></p> <p>Military applications exist too, often hyped in articles whenever the supply of rare earths is threatened. But these applications are not the primary driver of demand. Harvard calculates that electric cars require six times the mineral inputs of conventional cars, and wind plants require nine times more minerals than gas-fired plants.</p> <p>While rare earths do have military uses like in missile guidance systems, jet engines, and radar equipment, the quantities needed for military applications are relatively small. The main demand comes from civilian industries like electronics, cars, energy, and telecommunications.</p> <p><strong>The Rise and Fall of American Production</strong></p> <p>From the 1960s through the 1980s, the United States was the dominant global producer of rare earth elements, largely due to the Mountain Pass mine in California. That mine shut down in 2002 after a toxic waste spill where radioactive wastewater leaked into the surrounding desert.</p> <p>The mine was no longer viable due to cheaper Chinese imports, environmental compliance costs, and pollution issues. The site revived in 2012, went bankrupt in 2015, and was taken over by financiers who founded MP Materials in 2017.</p> <p>Earlier this year, the US government acquired a 15% stake in the company for $400 million as part of a broader public-private partnership aimed at building a domestic supply chain for rare earth magnets. Apple has committed $500 million in a long-term partnership to source American-made rare earth magnets and to build a rare earth recycling facility at Mountain Pass.</p> <p><strong>How China Won</strong></p> <p>Starting in the 1990s, a combination of environmental regulations, corporate short-termism, and outsourcing led to a decline in US rare earth production. China, meanwhile, strategically invested in the industry, offering lower costs and fewer environmental constraints.</p> <p>By the early 2000s, China had overtaken the US. Today it supplies around 80% of the world's rare earths and handles over 90% of the refining. China also manufactures nearly all of the magnets that use rare earth materials.</p> <p>In China, the entire supply chain from ore to finish components is vertically integrated and geographically concentrated. China's dominance is possibly more strategic than economic in nature, as selling rare earths isn't hugely profitable.</p> <p>The US spends more than 20 times as much on avocado imports each year as it does on rare earths. The difference, of course, is that no one ever threatened to cut off the guacamole supply.</p> <p>Because rare earths are so deeply embedded in the supply chains of nearly every advanced industry, control over their flow means influence over production timelines, pricing, and provides huge geopolitical leverage when needed.</p> <p><strong>The Leverage Problem</strong></p> <p>China's geopolitical edge comes from its ability to cause huge disruptions. When export licenses are delayed or denied, supply chains around the world come to a halt.</p> <p>Rare earth elements, despite their name, aren't particularly rare. But the ability to refine and produce them at scale is. China has built that capacity through decades of industrial investment, environmental tolerance, and technical process development.</p> <p>The process is messy, expensive, and difficult to scale up quickly, making it extremely difficult to rebuild this industry under Western environmental and regulatory constraints. Environmental approvals can take years in Western countries. Labor costs are significantly higher than in China, and expertise in production is extremely limited as most of the engineers with experience in this space retired many years ago.</p> <p>Even with government backing and corporate investment, progress has been slow because rebuilding capacity requires more than just access to raw materials. It requires technical expertise, regulatory changes, and long-term investment.</p> <p><strong>The Licensing Regime</strong></p> <p>China's export licensing system has become a central tool in its trade strategy. Since 2023, the Chinese government expanded the scope of these licenses to cover more than 700 different products, including rare earths, manufacturing equipment, and other critical inputs.</p> <p>Licenses are typically issued for six months and require detailed disclosures about the buyer, the intended use, and the final product that will be built. In some cases, exporters are asked to collect technical documentation or proprietary information from their customers as part of the license application process.</p> <p>The licensing process gives Beijing a window into foreign supply chains. By collecting data on how and where rare earths are used, Chinese authorities can track demand patterns, identify potential stockpiling, and monitor for buyers who might be reselling the materials.</p> <p>The licensing system also allows for selective enforcement on a customer-by-customer basis. Licenses can be delayed or quietly withheld, creating uncertainty for buyers without the need for formal bans. In recent months, German firms have received more approvals than their American counterparts, and Indian buyers have faced even longer delays.</p> <p><strong>Learning From Japan</strong></p> <p>In 2010, during a maritime dispute with Japan, Beijing quietly cut off rare earth exports to Japanese manufacturers. The move caused immediate disruption. Japanese firms caught off guard were forced to halt production lines. The Japanese government caved in and released the Chinese fishermen that had been arrested as part of the dispute, and China resumed shipment shortly after.</p> <p>The episode left a lasting impression on Japanese firms who had until then relied on just-in-time manufacturing techniques. Japan began stockpiling rare earths, investing in alternative sources, and redesigning products to reduce reliance on Chinese materials.</p> <p>Japan's dependence on Chinese rare earths has fallen from 90% in 2010 to around 60% today. Japanese auto manufacturers developed electric motors for their hybrid cars that didn't require rare earth magnets.</p> <p>The 2010 Japanese embargo triggered a formal complaint at the World Trade Organization. The US, the European Union, and Japan jointly challenged China's export restrictions.</p> <p>In 2014, the WTO ruled against China, stating that its policies violated international trade rules. Beijing agreed to remove all quotas, but by then the message had been delivered: China was willing to use its control of rare earths as a geopolitical tool.</p> <p><strong>The Strategic Narrative</strong></p> <p>In 1987, China's former leader Deng Xiaoping famously said that while the Middle East has oil, China has rare earths, foreshadowing China's willingness to use its leverage over the supply chain of vital components.</p> <p>In 2019, a week after the Trump administration had blacklisted Huawei, Xi Jinping toured a rare earth processor in China and highlighted the importance of rare earths in global supply chains. This was widely interpreted as a threat to restrict Chinese exports to the United States.</p> <p>Chinese state media followed with editorials warning that China could cut off supplies if pushed too far. One headline read, "Don't say we didn't warn you."</p> <p>In Chinese political discourse, rare earths are often described as being both a strategic resource and a national treasure. The Financial Times has reported that Beijing views rare earths as both a sword and a shield, a means of defense and a tool of coercion.</p> <p>The term "assager's mace" has also appeared in official rhetoric referring to asymmetric trade capabilities designed to counter more technologically advanced adversaries.</p> <p>These signals aren't just for foreign audiences. They serve a domestic purpose too. The trade war has allowed Xi to frame China's economic challenges as the result of foreign hostility. This narrative has helped consolidate political support at home.</p> <p>Even as the country grapples with deflation, a property crisis, and slowing growth, rare earths in this context are more than a bargaining chip. They're a symbol of national strength and a reminder that China is prepared to fight on its own terms.</p> <p><strong>The Bottom Line</strong></p> <p>China's rare earth chokehold represents one of the most strategic bottlenecks in global manufacturing. The strongest part of this argument is clear: no other country can match China's vertically integrated supply chain for these critical elements, and rebuilding it would take decades under Western regulatory constraints.</p> <p>The biggest vulnerability is that the recent US-China deal, while temporarily easing tensions, doesn't address the underlying structural dependency. The one-year agreement kicks the can down the road, but leaves every advanced industry essentially hostage to Chinese supply chains.</p> <p>What readers should watch for next: whether Apple and other companies actually follow through on domestic rare earth commitments at Mountain Pass, and whether the US government stake translates into meaningful diversification away from Chinese dominance.</p>"}}
