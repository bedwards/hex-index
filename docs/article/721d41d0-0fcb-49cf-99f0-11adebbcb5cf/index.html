When I made my video on how they caught Luigi Manjone, I correctly identified the fact that the police were not able to track him down based on any kind of facial recognition. The only picture of Luigi's face the police got was a high-angle shot from a CCTV camera in a hostel where Luji is wearing a full hoodie and is completely covering his hair, ears, neck, and forehead. If you don't know who you're looking for, this frame is not enough to identify anybody. How Luigi was caught was by the police following his profile.

What I'm about to show you is an extremely powerful tool that automates this work beyond anything you could possibly imagine or might have seen in popular media. This is Track.

It's made by Verone, a video analytics and a surveillance AI company. In this demo, Track allows you to search for a target based on a wide range of characteristics, most notably accessories, body, clothes, or footwear, as well as key biometric markers. Track can not only identify this profile in a surveillance camera, but can also follow the subject's movement across different recordings. It does this in a way that leaves little manual labor for the operator.

It's fast, efficient, and much more precise than facial recognition alone, which in many street settings, like that fateful morning in New York City, is completely dysfunctional. Track is used by Veron's 400 plus customers, many of which of course include federal, state, and local police, but it's also used by universities and commercial customers from media and entertainment. Track can be legally used even in cities and states that ban the use of facial recognition by the police. Because track can follow so many features, it doesn't need to rely on faces at all.

For Veritone, Track is still in its beginning stage of development. The company promises they are only one year away from being able to run it on live video feeds with full automation. That year should be 2026. Tracked by Veritone is part of a newly emerging industry called whole body biometrics.
