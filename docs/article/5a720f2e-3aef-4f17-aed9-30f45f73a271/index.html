Introduction to Land Use — City Beautiful Basics

Dave Amos · City Beautiful ·Feb 12, 2025 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Amos reframes the mundane mechanics of city planning not as bureaucratic hurdles, but as the invisible architecture that dictates whether our daily lives are defined by isolation or connection. He argues that while most people view zoning as a personal annoyance regarding fences or room additions, it is actually the primary engine creating the "low density car oriented cities" that plague modern America. This perspective is vital now because the climate crisis and housing affordability collapse are not accidental; they are the direct results of planning decisions made decades ago.

The Invisible Rules of the Road

Amos begins by dismantling the assumption that our urban environment is natural. "It's only obvious to you that you can't open up a grocery store in your living room or convert your backyard into an oil refinery because we all live in a world that zoning has shaped and regulated." This observation is the piece's strongest hook; it forces the listener to realize that the separation of work, home, and play is a legal construct, not a biological necessity. He explains that zoning codes act as a "rule book about how a city can be built down to the last little detail," governing everything from building height to parking signage.

The author effectively highlights the tension between local clarity and regional dysfunction. While local governments love zoning because it "creates a lot of clarity for everyone who owns land," Amos points out that this clarity often comes at the cost of flexibility. He notes that "these codes can lead to low density car oriented cities that can cause traffic and generate a lot of carbon emissions." This is a crucial pivot in the argument: the very tool designed to protect property values is often the same tool that degrades the quality of life by forcing car dependency. Critics might argue that removing these strictures could lead to chaotic development, but Amos suggests the current chaos of traffic and emissions is already the result of rigid planning.

Zoning is how many people interact with city planning on a regular basis, but it is a simple tool that implements a broader city-wide plan.

The Constitution for Growth

Moving beyond the granular rules of zoning, Amos introduces the comprehensive plan as the "constitution for new growth and development in a community." He argues that this document is essential because it "ties in transportation, housing, land use and the environment" into a single, cohesive vision. The author uses the example of Charlotte, North Carolina, to illustrate how these plans function in practice. Their plan, the "Charlotte 240," organizes policies around "10 critical Community goals" ranging from transit-oriented development to the creation of "10-minute neighborhoods."

This section is compelling because it shifts the focus from restriction to vision. Amos writes that "comprehensive planning is done with extensive Community input throughout the process," often taking planners to "farmers markets, festivals, schools" to gather feedback. This humanizes the planning process, suggesting that the best plans emerge from genuine engagement rather than top-down mandates. However, the argument glosses over the difficulty of achieving true consensus; in reality, "community input" can sometimes be dominated by vocal minorities rather than representing a "representative group of community members." Despite this, the framing of the comprehensive plan as a shared vision is a powerful antidote to the fragmentation of modern urbanism.

The Regional Reality Check

The commentary then expands its scope to the metropolitan level, arguing that "many problems don't respect city limit boundaries." Amos uses the Bay Area as a case study, noting that regional agencies must coordinate across nine counties to manage systems like "BART and Cal Train." He highlights the ambitious nature of the "Plan Bay Area 2050," which allocates billions for affordable housing and transit to tackle region-wide issues.

Here, Amos identifies a critical weakness in the American planning system: "the regional agencies that produce them don't have the power to compel the cities within the region to comply with the plan." He explains that while these agencies cannot rewrite local zoning codes, they use "grant programs" to incentivize compliance. This is a pragmatic acknowledgment of the political reality in the United States, where local sovereignty often trumps regional efficiency. The author suggests that despite this weakness, regional planning is "no small thing" because it coordinates the "growing, changing urban areas" that single cities cannot manage alone.

Planting Seeds for the Future

In his conclusion, Amos returns to the human element of planning, comparing planners to "gardeners" who "plant the seeds of a city they want to see in the future." He illustrates this with recent reforms in Los Angeles and Seattle, where cities are abolishing parking minimums and allowing mixed uses to reduce car dependency. "Parking is one of those land uses that you don't really think about too much but just about every land has space for Park cars," he writes, noting that these requirements often waste land and money. By allowing businesses to determine their own parking needs based on market demand rather than arbitrary codes, cities can create "smaller parking lots better optimized for the use on the site."

This final section ties the technical aspects of zoning back to the lived experience of the city. The argument that "mixing uses can reduce travel times and traffic, decrease carbon emissions, and make for more pleasant walkable places" is both logical and emotionally resonant. It positions land use reform not as a technical adjustment, but as a pathway to a more humane and sustainable future.

Without planners, cities would grow in a halfhazard way with weeds crowding out the flowers.

Bottom Line

Dave Amos delivers a masterclass in making the invisible visible, successfully arguing that the mundane details of zoning codes are the primary drivers of our climate and housing crises. While the piece occasionally underestimates the political friction involved in implementing regional plans, its core message—that we must move from rigid separation to flexible, mixed-use communities—is the most urgent conversation in urban planning today. The strongest takeaway is that the "halfhazard" growth of the past is not inevitable; it is a design choice that we have the power to change.
