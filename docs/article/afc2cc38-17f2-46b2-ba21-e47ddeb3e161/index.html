The Most Complicated Thing in the Universe: What is the Brain?

Alex O'Connor · Cosmic Skeptic ·Mar 8, 2026 · 143 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Alex O'Connor doesn't just recount the history of neuroscience; he exposes a profound cognitive dissonance that persists in the human psyche today. The most surprising claim isn't that ancient people thought the heart controlled thought—it's that this intuition was so robust it survived direct experimental disproof for over a millennium. For the busy listener, this is a masterclass in why our gut feelings are often the worst guides for understanding our own biology.

The Illusion of Intuition

O'Connor anchors his argument in a deceptively simple observation: human beings feel their emotions in their chests, not their skulls. He notes that "it's not just that you feel it there, but also you can you can kind of measure it," pointing out that when we are frightened, our hearts pound while our brains remain physically silent. This sensory evidence was overwhelming for centuries. As O'Connor explains, "everyday experience, if you're just thinking about it, which is all that people had for most of the time, it would suggest strongly that there's something going on here, not up here."

This framing is effective because it validates the reader's own experience while simultaneously dismantling it. It explains why Shakespeare could joke about "fancy bread" being in the heart or head and expect the groundlings to laugh; the confusion was cultural, not just academic. However, O'Connor wisely avoids painting the ancients as ignorant. Instead, he frames their error as a rational conclusion based on the data available to them, much like the belief that the sun orbits the earth. The shift to a brain-centric view required a radical departure from sensory reality, a leap that took centuries to achieve.

The Pig That Changed Everything

The narrative pivot arrives with the Roman physician Galen, whose gruesome but definitive experiment on a live pig shattered the cardiocentric hypothesis. O'Connor describes the scene with stark clarity: Galen silenced a squealing pig not by stopping its heart, but by pressing down on its brain. "Push down on the brain," Galen commanded, and "instantly, of course, the poor pig becomes unconscious and no longer makes a noise." This was the moment the link between the brain and consciousness was empirically established, proving that the heart was merely a pump.

Yet, the most fascinating part of O'Connor's coverage is his observation that this proof didn't immediately change minds. "Despite this experiment being well-known, didn't change anybody's mind," he notes. The resistance wasn't due to a lack of data, but a lack of a coherent theoretical framework to replace the old one. People clung to the feeling that the heart was the seat of the soul because the brain felt like "inert matter." This highlights a critical vulnerability in scientific progress: facts rarely overturn intuition without a new story to replace the old one. It wasn't until the 18th century that the consensus finally shifted, long after the evidence was in hand.

"There is no brain centric moment. There isn't a moment at which the apple falls from the tree and Newton goes aha gravity... there's no experiment that convinces everybody."

O'Connor uses this to illustrate that scientific consensus is a slow accumulation of evidence, not a singular eureka moment. This is a crucial distinction for modern readers who often expect immediate paradigm shifts. The transition from the heart to the brain was a messy, centuries-long negotiation between what we feel and what we observe.

The Lingering Ghost in the Machine

Even after the science settled, the cultural hangover remains. O'Connor points out that our language is littered with "linguistic fossils" like "heartbroken" or "heart in my mouth," which betray our ancestral belief system. He argues that these phrases are not just metaphors but evidence of a deep-seated intuition that is "very deeply baked and hard to shake off." This connects to the later historical concept of phrenology, where the 19th-century attempt to map personality to skull bumps was essentially a desperate attempt to make the physical brain match our intuitive understanding of the mind's location.

Critics might note that O'Connor slightly underplays the role of dualism in delaying acceptance; thinkers like Descartes insisted the soul interacted with the brain via the pineal gland, a specific biological claim that was easily disproven, yet the philosophical separation of mind and body persisted. O'Connor touches on this, noting Descartes was "very certain" about the pineal gland because he believed only humans had one, a fact quickly corrected by observing other mammals. But the core of the argument holds: the brain's role was accepted long before the nature of thought was understood.

Bottom Line

O'Connor's coverage is strongest in its refusal to treat the history of neuroscience as a straight line of progress, instead revealing it as a struggle against our own sensory deception. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the pig experiment as the definitive turning point, which, while dramatic, glosses over the complex philosophical debates that kept the heart theory alive for centuries. Ultimately, this is a vital reminder that the most complicated thing in the universe is not the brain itself, but the human mind's ability to ignore what it cannot feel.
