Leaving the ipcc and unfccc is bad for the united states

Roger Pielke Jr. · The Honest Broker ·Jan 8, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Roger Pielke Jr. delivers a counterintuitive punch: walking away from the very climate institutions the U.S. helped build might actually hurt American interests more than it helps. While the political theater of withdrawal offers easy propaganda wins, Pielke Jr. argues that the practical reality is a loss of influence over global science and trade rules that will inevitably shape the American economy. This isn't about saving the planet; it's about ensuring the United States doesn't cede the rules of the game to rivals who are less skeptical of climate activism.

The Illusion of Obligation

Pielke Jr. begins by dismantling the assumption that the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) is a binding trap. He points out that for decades, the U.S. has treated the agreement as a "talking shop" with no legal teeth, a stance cemented by the 1997 Byrd-Hagel resolution which the Senate passed unanimously to block binding emissions targets. "The U.S. government — meaning the President and Congress, and both parties when in control of each — has for more than 30 years rejected the UNFCCC as little more than a talking shop, with no real policy teeth," he writes. This historical context is crucial; it reminds us that the current executive order is less a radical break and more a theatrical escalation of a long-standing bipartisan consensus.

Yet, Pielke Jr. warns that even a non-binding forum carries weight. By withdrawing, the U.S. surrenders its ability to steer the conversation. He notes that while the legal authority to withdraw unilaterally remains a "live issue" that the Supreme Court has sidestepped, the strategic cost is undeniable. "Why take that risk?" he asks, highlighting that without a seat at the table, other nations could adopt policies on trade and technology that are "penal to the U.S." Critics might argue that staying in a forum dominated by climate alarmists is a waste of time, but Pielke Jr. counters that isolation is far more dangerous than engagement.

The result could be policies adopted in other countries or by blocs of countries that are penal to the U.S. — particularly as related to trade, supply chains, and technology.

The Strategic Value of the IPCC

The argument deepens when examining the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). Pielke Jr. reveals a fascinating historical irony: the U.S. government actually championed the creation of the IPCC in 1988 not to advance climate activism, but to blunt it. He cites Michael Oppenheimer, who noted that the U.S. saw the panel as a way to "prevent the activism stimulated by my colleagues and me from controlling the policy agenda." The administration wanted a rigorous, peer-reviewed body to counter the "flawed or exaggerated scientific claims" of activists.

Now, Pielke Jr. warns, the pendulum has swung too far. He argues that without U.S. participation, the IPCC risks becoming "fully captured as an instrument of political advocacy." He is particularly critical of the panel's working groups, noting that while the science on temperature trends is sound, other areas rely on "implausible, outdated scenarios." He writes, "It is easy to imagine the IPCC becoming weaponized against the interests of the U.S., grounded in flawed but seeming authoritative science — This is not speculative, it is already happening." This is a bold claim, suggesting that the very mechanism designed to ensure scientific rigor is now vulnerable to political capture by the very activists the U.S. originally sought to moderate.

Multilateralism as National Security

Finally, Pielke Jr. broadens the scope to the concept of soft power. He observes that the executive order wisely spared economic and defense institutions like the World Bank and NATO, recognizing their utility. However, he argues that in 2026, scientific institutions are just as critical to national security as defense pacts. "Participation in and improving these institutions would difficult work. We should try it, not run away," he urges. He frames multilateral science not as a concession to globalism, but as a necessary arena for managing complex issues like supply chains and critical minerals.

He challenges the reader to look beyond the "propaganda benefits" of withdrawal. "On both sides, strong partisans will readily cheerlead along with the rhetoric," he notes, dismissing the partisan cheerleading of both "Globalists!" and "Hoax!" camps. The core of his argument is that real-world policies have real-world impacts on normal Americans, regardless of which party is in power. A counterargument worth considering is that the U.S. could simply fund these organizations without participating in the political wrangling, but Pielke Jr. insists that funding without a voice is a recipe for irrelevance.

If the IPCC did not exist, we'd have to invent it — so we should make it as robust as possible.

Bottom Line

Pielke Jr.'s strongest move is reframing climate diplomacy not as an environmental concession, but as a pragmatic defense of American economic and scientific influence. His argument is most vulnerable where it assumes that U.S. participation can effectively moderate the IPCC's political leanings, a task that has proven difficult even when the U.S. is fully engaged. The reader should watch whether the administration's withdrawal actually leads to the "weaponization" of climate science he fears, or if the U.S. simply loses the ability to shape the narrative that will define global trade for the next decade.

Leaving the ipcc and unfccc is bad for the united states

by Roger Pielke Jr. · The Honest Broker · Read full article

Yesterday, the Trump administration announced via executive order that the United States was withdrawing from 66 international organizations, of which 31 fall under the United Nations (UN).1 Among these organizations are the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC).2

Today, I explain a bit about these organizations and why it is contrary to the interests of the U.S. — and also, perhaps surprisingly, the stated interests of the Trump administration — to formally withdraw from each.

Before proceeding, let me acknowledge that there are surely considerable propaganda benefits to the Trump administration of announcing this rejection of multilateralism. Globalists! Woke! Hoax! Similarly, the embrace of these organizations by the Biden administration was also arguably more about propaganda than policy. Existential threat! Threat multiplier! Follow the science!3

On both sides, strong partisans will readily cheerlead along with the rhetoric. However, beyond the news cycle and team sport, there are real world policies with real world impact, including impact on the fortunes of normal Americans.

Today’s post addresses three points:

The U.S. government — meaning the President and Congress, and both parties when in control of each — has for more than 30 years rejected the UNFCCC as little more than a talking shop, with no real policy teeth. Even so it matters;

The U.S. helped to create the IPCC in the 1980s to push back against climate activism perceived to contrary to U.S. interests, notably that based on flawed or exaggerated scientific claims. A robust IPCC serves U.S. interests;

U.S. engagement in multilateral organizations is a source of soft power that has proven meaningful to advance U.S. interests — obviously in economics and defense, but also in 2026 in science as well.

Let’s take a closer look at each.

U.N. Framework Convention on Climate Change

The Trump administration’s withdrawal EO may not actually withdraw the U.S. from the UNFCCC.

It states:

For United Nations entities, withdrawal means ceasing participation in or funding to those entities to the extent permitted by law.

The UNFCCC is technically a “non-self-executing” international agreement. That means that as a signatory, the U.S. has no formal obligations other than those that might come in the aftermath of becoming a signatory, i.e., that Congress and the President together decide to implement in law.
