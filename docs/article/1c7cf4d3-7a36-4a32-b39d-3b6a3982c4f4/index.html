Designing and building warships is a tricky business, and it's abundantly clear that most nations out there don't get it perfect all the time. Canadian ships sometimes cost so much that you might wonder if the decimal points in the right place. Some German warships are so lightly armed, they might struggle with the whole war part of the job description, while with their combination of things like a tendency towards spontaneous combustion and 25-year refits. Some Russian warships seem to struggle with the ship part.

Oh, and then there's North Korea, which clearly wins this entire competition by building what they claim to be a modern destroyer and then managing to somehow fail to even get it into the water correctly, tipping the thing over on the slipway and requiring extensive recovery operation. The point is, when it comes to warships, happens. And that for big navies in particular, over a long enough period of time, you can afford the odd strike here or there. As the US Navy is discovering, though, the problems tend to mount when you start missing shot after shot.

Its program for advanced next generation stealthy destroyers and potentially cruisers, what gave us the Zumalt class, ultimately produced far fewer ships than planned at a higher per unit cost than planned that weren't ideal for the modern Navy's needs. While the LCS program produced fewer ships than planned, that had a variety of issues and which weren't ideal for the modern Navy's needs. Strike one, strike two. The US Navy's constellation class frigot program was meant to reverse that trend.

The idea this time was to play it safe. Take an existing internationally successful frigate design, adapt it a bit for United States service, and come up with something the US could crank out affordably and in huge numbers in order to help rebuild the size of the US combat fleet and augment the overstretched destroyers and cruisers. With Constellation, the US Navy shot for the stars, a ship that balanced affordability, accessibility, and capability. They missed.

The ship was morphed and altered until it barely resembled the base design. cost blew out, delays mounted. And so in November, the axe finally fell with the US announcing that far from spamming out the new constellation frig.
