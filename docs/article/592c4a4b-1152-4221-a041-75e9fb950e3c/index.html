What is a Theory of Everything: Livestream

Matt O'Dowd · PBS Space Time ·Jul 28, 2020 · 89 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Matt O'Dowd and Brian Keating have assembled a rare, high-stakes dialogue that cuts through the jargon to expose the fundamental fracture at the heart of modern physics. This isn't just another lecture on string theory; it is a live, unscripted confrontation between the two most successful yet incompatible frameworks in human history. For the busy mind seeking clarity, the value lies not in a solved equation, but in witnessing the precise moment where our best theories break down, revealing a universe that refuses to be neatly categorized.

The Two Pillars That Won't Touch

The conversation begins by establishing the staggering success of our current understanding, only to highlight its catastrophic failure when combined. James Beacham, the experimentalist at the Large Hadron Collider, sets the stage with a blunt assessment of the field's frustration. He notes that physicists possess "two completely fantastic wonderful theories" that describe reality at opposite ends of the spectrum. One is quantum field theory, which governs the subatomic world with such precision that it "makes your kind of brain explode how good it is." The other is general relativity, Einstein's masterpiece that perfectly predicts the behavior of galaxies and gravitational waves.

O'Dowd and his guests emphasize that these theories are not merely different; they are mutually exclusive when forced to interact. "When you try to marry them together naively everything breaks," Beacham explains, resulting in "infinite energies or probabilities greater than one." This is the crux of the Theory of Everything problem. The argument is compelling because it strips away the mystique of theoretical physics to reveal a practical engineering failure: the math simply doesn't add up. Critics might note that this framing assumes a single unified theory must exist, whereas some philosophers of science argue that nature might simply be fundamentally fragmented at different scales. However, the consensus among the panelists is that a deeper, underlying reality must connect them.

"Physicists for all of us... we seem sometimes kind of stuffy and nerdy and very kind of analytical deep down inside we're very very very very frustrated."

Divergent Paths to Quantum Gravity

As the discussion shifts to potential solutions, the panelists reveal their distinct philosophical approaches to the problem. Stefan Alexander, a cosmologist and saxophonist, describes his methodology as being "agnostic about that toolkit," inspired by Richard Feynman's pragmatic approach to puzzles. Alexander outlines two dominant strategies: string theory and loop quantum gravity. He explains that string theory starts with the assumption of symmetry and "quantize[s]" the movement of strings, causing gravity to "pop out of the equations" as a necessary consequence. This approach suggests that gravity is not a fundamental force but an emergent property of a deeper geometric structure.

In contrast, Alexander describes loop quantum gravity as taking the opposite route. It accepts general relativity as the starting point and attempts to apply quantum mechanics directly to it. "Let's take general relativity as it is... let's quantize that," he paraphrases the logic of the approach. The distinction is vital for the listener: one theory builds up from the quantum to find gravity, while the other breaks down gravity to find the quantum. O'Dowd's coverage effectively highlights that neither approach has yet produced a testable prediction that distinguishes it from the other, leaving the field in a state of "incomplete" theoretical development.

The Search for Symmetry and Scale

The dialogue circles back to the historical context, noting that today marks the centennial of the great debates of the 1920s, a time when physicists first grappled with the scale of the universe. The panelists suggest that the answer to the largest cosmic questions lies in the smallest particle interactions. "The universe's largest scale properties are determined in part by how it functions at the micro level," O'Dowd asserts, linking the fate of galaxies to the behavior of quarks and gluons. This synthesis of the very large and the very small is the ultimate goal of the Theory of Everything.

Max Tegmark, a theoretical physicist known for his work on the mathematical nature of reality, joins the fray, though his specific arguments are less detailed in this excerpt than Alexander's. The collective tone is one of cautious optimism mixed with deep intellectual humility. The panelists acknowledge that they are not solving the problem today, but rather "making a little scratch" on the surface of a century-old mystery. The strength of this coverage is its refusal to offer false closure; instead, it invites the audience to appreciate the complexity of the unsolved.

Bottom Line

O'Dowd and Keating succeed in transforming a dense theoretical debate into a human story of frustration and curiosity, proving that the search for a Theory of Everything is as much about the limits of human understanding as it is about the laws of nature. The strongest element is the clear articulation of why the two pillars of physics fail to merge, while the biggest vulnerability remains the lack of experimental data to guide the theorists toward a solution. Readers should watch for the upcoming discussion with Lisa Randall, as her expertise in extra dimensions may provide the missing link this panel identifies but cannot yet prove.

"When you try to marry them together naively everything breaks."

The path forward requires not just new math, but new ways of thinking about the relationship between the quantum and the cosmic. Until then, the universe remains a beautiful, broken puzzle.
