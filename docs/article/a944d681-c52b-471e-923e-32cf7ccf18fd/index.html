WEEKLYSTREAM73: Live Q&A @ University Of Denver!

Commentary by Brian Edwards

Benn Jordan transforms a casual live Q&A into a masterclass on creative constraint, revealing how a camera lost to the ocean and a modular synth system too large to count became the unlikely engines of his latest artistic project. While the setting is an impromptu pop-up at the University of Denver, the substance is a rigorous examination of why modern musicians must abandon the pursuit of perfection in favor of the chaotic, lo-fi reality of field recording. This isn't just a gear talk; it is a manifesto on the value of failure in the creative process.

The Architecture of Failure

Jordan opens by dismantling the myth of the perfectly planned performance. He admits that while he loaded a specific chord sequence and programmed beats into his sequencer, the melodic magic was largely accidental. "All of it was improvised," he explains, describing his method of sampling a low-frequency oscillator and running it through a scale quantizer to generate patterns that are "non-random but non-repeating." This approach highlights a crucial distinction in electronic music: the difference between randomness and complexity. By controlling the parameters of chaos rather than eliminating it, Jordan creates a sound that feels organic yet structured.

The conversation takes a sharp turn toward the physical toll of this lifestyle when discussing his recent road trip. Jordan recounts a harrowing incident where his Sony A74 camera, a piece of equipment he describes as "gorgeous" and essential, was lost to the Pacific Ocean. "The ocean wanted it more than I did," he jokes, but the underlying message is serious. He chose to continue his project using only the audio captured by the camera's built-in microphone rather than deploying his professional field recording gear. This decision to embrace "lo-fi" limitations was a deliberate artistic pivot, proving that the best sound sources often come from the environment itself, not a $3,000 microphone.

Critics might argue that relying on a camera's internal mic sacrifices too much fidelity for the sake of a story, but Jordan's work suggests that the texture of the recording is more important than its clarity. The imperfection becomes the aesthetic.

The Modular Rabbit Hole

The discussion then shifts to the heart of Jordan's sonic identity: the modular synthesizer. He traces his journey back to 2016, when he scored a film for the Adler Planetarium and decided to build an entire composition using logic gates. "I had no idea what I was saying," he admits, noting that he simply told the producers he wanted to use logic gates and they agreed. This audacity led him to invest his budget directly into modular gear, specifically the Mutable Instruments modules like Rings and Clouds, which he describes as mind-blowing.

Jordan's commentary on the current state of modular synthesis is particularly poignant. He expresses deep sadness over the fact that Emily of Mutable Instruments is no longer producing new hardware, stating, "I'm so sad that her creativity isn't going to be a tool I can use anymore." Yet, he finds hope in the open-source nature of the company's legacy. "If I were to make modules and then not continue to sell them, I would love for them to be open source too," he argues, ensuring that future generations can continue to modify and build upon these designs.

However, Jordan also offers a candid critique of his own accumulation. He confesses that his system has grown so large—powered by three separate power supplies—that he has stopped counting the size in "HP" (horizontal pitch). "I don't use 60% of them more than once in a great while," he reveals. This admission serves as a warning to enthusiasts: the joy of modular synthesis lies in the journey of building, not the hoarding of gear. He notes that some modules, like the Distinct, are powerful but have "painful" interfaces, leading him to unplug them in favor of more ergonomic alternatives.

The Future of Workflow

As the session winds down, Jordan reflects on the future of his workflow. He expresses a desire for a more fluid system where modules can be swapped instantly without the friction of screws and patch cables. "I wish I could just open up a lid or something and take a module off and put it here," he says, highlighting a friction point that many modular users feel but rarely articulate. He sees the future not in bigger systems, but in "smaller setups" that act as a "walled garden," forcing creativity through limitation.

This perspective challenges the common narrative that more gear equals more creativity. Jordan suggests that the "idea factory" of modular synthesis works best when the scope is constrained. "Having a walled garden is actually a nice thing," he concludes, arguing that it forces the artist to dig deeper into fewer tools rather than skimming the surface of many.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's commentary succeeds because it strips away the pretense of the professional musician, replacing it with a raw, honest look at the messy reality of creation. His strongest argument is that limitation—whether it's a lost camera or a small synthesizer—is the true catalyst for innovation. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the listener's familiarity with niche gear, but the underlying philosophy of embracing failure is universally applicable. Readers should watch for how Jordan's "lo-fi" experiment evolves, as it may redefine the standard for field recording in electronic music.
