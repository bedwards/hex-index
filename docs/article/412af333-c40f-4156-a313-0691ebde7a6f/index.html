Historically, it might be tempting to think of war economies as essentially a binary state. Either a country is at peace and producing fridges, PlayStations, and novelty water bottles, or it's at war, the military-industrial complex is humming, and suddenly your pre-war car factories are cranking out Shermans. The transition to war process has often varied from rough around the edges at best to an absolute crunching train wreck at worst, which at first glance might be a little hard to understand. Generals might sometimes cop flack for preparing for the last war and investing in the wrong doctrine and technology.

But technology and tactics constantly evolve and change, which might make picking winners difficult without the benefit of hindsight. By contrast, it can feel like every major industrial war has given us a shell crisis, a supply chain squeeze for critical inputs, labor issues, a procurement system that rapidly seizes up, and of course, corruption and con artists who come out of the woodwork and try and sell desperate for cashed up militaries. anything from fake bomb detectors powered by magic to bulletproof vests that happen to be missing the bulletproof parts.
