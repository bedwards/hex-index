Commentary by Brian Edwards

Most histories of Rome end with a funeral dirge for the West, but Kings and Generals flips the script on the Eastern Empire, arguing that its survival wasn't luck—it was a masterclass in strategic adaptation. The author's most striking claim is that the Byzantines didn't just endure; they evolved by deliberately discarding the rigid dogmas of their ancestors to embrace the very tactics of their enemies. This is not a story of decline, but of a military machine that learned to be surgical rather than blunt, a distinction that matters for anyone studying how organizations survive existential threats.

The Myth of the Static Legion

Kings and Generals opens by dismantling the nostalgic bias that plagues military history. The author notes that the Eastern Roman Empire was a "Survivor" rather than a conqueror, pressed on all sides yet outlasting its Western counterpart by nearly a millennium. This framing is crucial because it shifts the metric of success from territorial expansion to institutional resilience. The piece leans heavily on the 6th-century historian Procopius to make this point, quoting him as he "gives 6th Century Byzantine traditionalists a verbal slap in the face and telling them to get with the times."

The commentary here is sharp: it identifies that the true Roman spirit wasn't in clinging to the past, but in the willingness to change. Kings and Generals writes, "to change from Iconic Roman military Styles was in fact more Roman than maintaining outward trappings." This is a provocative assertion that redefines Roman identity not by its equipment, but by its pragmatism. The author argues that the transition from the heavy infantry of Caesar to the flexible forces of Justinian was a necessary evolution, not a corruption. Critics might note that this view risks romanticizing the loss of the massive citizen-legion, which provided a different kind of political stability, but the evidence of survival suggests the trade-off was worth it.

The drill and discipline Legions of Caesar and Augustus had been reformed by Emperors at the end of the 3r century crisis into the late Imperial forces of limitan comensis and more detailed in the notitia dignitatum fifth century disruption and collapse Barbarian incursion monetary shortage purposeful change and other factors led to the reborn small and Elite Army of banum in the early 6th Century where strategy and generalship were more important and Cavalry increasingly prominent.

The Hybrid Warrior

The core of the argument rests on the transformation of the soldier himself. Kings and Generals details the rise of the hippotoxotai, or heavy horse archers, describing them as "armor clad Archer[s] mounted on Horseback" who replaced the spear-armed cavalry of old. The author explains that this wasn't a haphazard adoption of barbarian styles but a calculated refinement. The Byzantines took the composite bow, a weapon of the steppe, and standardized it for mass production, swapping the asymmetrical Hunnic bow for a symmetrical model that was "simpler to manufacture in large numbers."

This section effectively debunks the idea that the Byzantines abandoned infantry. The author writes, "we must also quash the simplistic notion that the byzantines new emphasis on Cavalry led to the utter dereliction of infantry forces as nothing more than cannon fodder." Instead, the infantry evolved into the protata, a versatile force armed with spears and bows that could hold the line or harass the enemy. Kings and Generals illustrates this with a specific engagement where Gothic cavalry charged Byzantine spearmen, only to be met with a disciplined wall of shields and thrusts. "Most impressive is procopio's detailing of how none of the spearmen interfered with any of their comrades," the author notes, highlighting the superior training that replaced the sheer numbers of the past.

The recruitment strategy was equally adaptive. The author points out that while the Western Empire collapsed under the weight of unassimilated mercenaries, the East integrated them with surgical precision. "Such a Vigor for drawing the best of the Barbarian military Talent is exemplified clearly by the array of foreign leaders who became High Generals in Justinian service," Kings and Generals writes. This willingness to promote non-Romans to the highest command ranks—men like Belisarius and Narses—was a radical departure from earlier Roman taboos and a key factor in their military success.

The Double-Edged Sword of Private Armies

However, the author does not shy away from the structural weaknesses introduced by this adaptability. The piece introduces the bucellarii, the private household troops of generals, describing them as a "completely alien practice" where soldiers owed loyalty to their commander rather than the Emperor. Kings and Generals writes, "this kind of powerful private Army was an unquestionable danger to the emperor in Constantinople who risked deposition at the hands of soldiers who owed no loyalty to him."

This is a nuanced point that adds depth to the narrative. The author argues that while the bucellarii provided the elite core needed for campaigns, they eroded the state's monopoly on violence. The text notes that the suppression of the Nika riots relied on these private forces, meaning Justinian's survival depended on the very men who could overthrow him. A counterargument worth considering is that this decentralization was not just a weakness but a feature of a resource-constrained empire that could no longer afford a massive standing army. The author acknowledges this, stating that the decline in manpower was due to the disappearance of the "old class of Roman Imperial citizens," forcing the state to rely on smaller, more expensive, and more personal forces.

The Byzantine armies of Justinian's age of reconquest as we have detailed were often incredibly small by comparison while four centuries before Tran was able to Rally up to 200,000 men for his conquest of deia bisari was only able to bring about 7,000 to Bear during his initial Landings in Italy.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals succeeds in reframing the Byzantine military not as a shadow of Rome, but as a sophisticated successor that mastered the art of asymmetric warfare. The strongest part of this argument is the demonstration that adaptability was a deliberate, intellectual choice rather than a desperate reaction. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the romanticization of the bucellarii system, which, while tactically effective, ultimately contributed to the political fragmentation that plagued the later Empire. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: survival often requires abandoning the very traditions that made you great in the first place.
