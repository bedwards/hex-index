Imagine applying for a building permit, but before the project can move forward, you're told you'll need to consult with the elves. That's not the setup for a fantasy urban design novel, but a real headline from the New York Times in 2005. Building in Iceland, better clear it with the elves first. The article recounts case after case in Iceland where road crews were diverted, bulldozers mysteriously malfunctioned, or construction projects stalled because locals insisted that the land belonged to Iceland's hidden people or the hulafolk.

It's exactly the sort of paradox that foreign journalists apparently love. The New York Times breathlessly frames it as proof that a modern secularized nation remains stubbornly superstitious. But what often goes unsaid is just how much this fascination has been constructed from the outside. As scholars of religion have pointed out, the international obsession with Icelandic elves is a surprisingly recent phenomenon.

19th and early 20th century travel writers barely mention elves at all. Only in the late 20th century did guide books, traveal logs, and documentaries turn elf belief into a pillar of Iceland's tourism brand. Ranging from in-flight magazines directing tourists to elf dwellings to German television dramas about murdered elf commissioners. And yes, that's actually a thing.

Uh the German drama, not the elf commissioner. So, what's really going on here? Are we looking at the survival of authentic pre-Christian beliefs and practices, or a carefully curated tourist elf, satisfying foreign journalists and tourists looking for Nordic mystique? To keep our analysis manageable, I'll be focusing specifically on belief in elves and hidden people, or in Icelandic, Alvar, and Hulvoke, which are most often used interchangeably both in folklore and in the scholarly literature.

Now, the term Alvar, where we get the English word elves, was far less precise than modern readers might expect. Rather than referring to a single race of beings, as we might imagine from the Lord of the Rings, Alvar and medieval texts seem to have been a broad label for a variety of supernatural figures. When we turn to the medieval sources, especially the collection of old Norse mythological and heroic poems called the poetic eta, elves seem less like hidden neighbors in the hills and more like divine figures. In the Edic poems, the Olivar occasionally appear alongside the icier in stock formulas, which refers to the primary group of Norse gods.
