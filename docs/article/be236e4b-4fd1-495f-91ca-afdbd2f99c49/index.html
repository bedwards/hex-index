The Bridge Collapse That Shocked a Nation

Fred Mills · The B1M ·Mar 11, 2026 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Fred Mills delivers a jarring reality check: Germany, the world's third-largest economy, is literally falling apart. While the narrative often focuses on German efficiency, Mills exposes a critical infrastructure crisis where bridges collapse without warning and rail networks stall, all while a massive €500 billion rescue fund faces skepticism over whether it can actually fix the rot. This is not just a story of decay; it is a forensic look at how even the most advanced nations can neglect their foundations until they crumble.

The Illusion of Stability

Mills begins by dismantling the myth of German invincibility, noting that the country's transport backbone is in "dire need of repair or due to be replaced." He points to the September 2024 collapse of the Corolla Bridge in Dresden as the catalyst for national anxiety. "Just before 3:00 a.m. on September 11th, 2024, the Corolla Bridge in Dresden fell suddenly into the River Ela," Mills writes, highlighting the terrifying randomness of the event. The bridge, built in 1971, failed due to hydrogen-induced stress corrosion—a slow, invisible killer accelerated by decades of acid fog from coal heating. This technical detail is crucial; it shifts the blame from a single catastrophic error to a systemic, decades-long neglect of material science.

The author effectively uses the bridge collapse to illustrate a broader pattern of deferred maintenance. He notes that while defects are usually spotted and fixed, "recently, there have been some pretty big exceptions to that." This framing is powerful because it acknowledges that the system thought it was working until it wasn't. However, critics might argue that Mills understates the impact of the 2008 financial crisis and the subsequent austerity measures that hollowed out maintenance budgets long before the recent political shifts. The decay wasn't just an accident of time; it was a policy choice.

The €500 Billion Question

The centerpiece of Mills' coverage is the newly announced €500 billion infrastructure fund. He presents the sheer scale of the investment with a mix of awe and skepticism. "It's an enormous sum of money, one that you'd think should be able to solve these problems once and for all," he observes, immediately undercutting the optimism with the question: "will it be enough?" The breakdown of the funds reveals the complexity of the challenge: 300 billion for the federal government, 100 billion for states, and 100 billion for climate investments, spread over 12 years.

Mills digs into the mechanics of this funding, revealing a potential trap. He explains that the new money is an exception to Germany's strict "debt break" constitutional rule, which caps borrowing at 0.35% of GDP. "This new fund is an exception to the rule," he writes, allowing the government to bypass historical constraints. Yet, he quickly pivots to the reality that this is often a shell game. "What you can see for rail for example is that they employed another trick in the budget that actually whatever money they had so far for railways and for infrastructure was taken out of the core budget and now to be financed from this new fund." This is a devastating critique of political accounting: the fund may not be new money, but rather a reshuffling of existing resources to make the numbers look better.

Money isn't the only problem. As we mentioned earlier, projects here often get bogged down in bureaucracy and there's a shortage of skilled workers.

Mills correctly identifies that capital is useless without execution. He draws a parallel to the infamous Brandenburg Airport, which took decades to open due to bureaucracy, and warns that without fixing the "shortage of skilled workers," the €500 billion will sit idle. This is a vital nuance often missed in economic reporting: you cannot simply buy your way out of a capacity crisis. The reference to the Deutsche Bahn (German Rail) needing €150 billion by 2034, while only receiving €19 billion in the first year of the new fund, underscores the gap between ambition and reality.

A Glimmer of Engineering Hope

Despite the gloom, Mills finds a counter-narrative in Germany's engineering prowess. He highlights the A45 Rinsdorf fire duct replacement, where engineers used a "transverse shift" to slide a new bridge into place at 1 mph. "It was an astonishing achievement and showed that if the money is there, Germany can build big infrastructure as well as anyone," he concludes. This example serves as a proof of concept, proving that the technical capability exists even if the political will has been lacking.

He also touches on the energy sector, noting the Sud Link, a 500 km underground power line that will transport wind energy from north to south. "A 5 km tunnel is being excavated so the cable can pass 20 m under the Ela River," Mills notes, ironically pointing out that the same river that swallowed the Corolla Bridge is now the site of a massive engineering triumph. This juxtaposition reinforces his central thesis: the problem isn't a lack of ability, but a lack of consistent investment and maintenance.

Bottom Line

Fred Mills' coverage is a masterclass in connecting specific engineering failures to systemic political failures, using the Dresden bridge collapse as a microcosm for Germany's broader infrastructure crisis. His strongest argument lies in exposing the budgetary sleight of hand behind the €500 billion fund, revealing that the money may not be as additive as politicians claim. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the hope that bureaucracy can be fixed quickly; without addressing the deep-seated administrative inertia and labor shortages, even this massive injection of capital may struggle to prevent the next collapse.
