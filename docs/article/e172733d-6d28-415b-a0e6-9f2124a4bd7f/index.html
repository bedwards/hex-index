Take the masks off

Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, Bill Kristol · The Bulwark ·Jan 12, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

This piece delivers a jarring diagnosis of a democracy in retreat, arguing that the administration's latest moves aren't just political games but a fundamental erosion of the rule of law. The most striking claim isn't that the executive branch is overreaching, but that it is actively dismantling the mechanisms of accountability by forcing law enforcement into anonymity and attacking the independence of the Federal Reserve. For busy observers trying to parse the noise of the news cycle, this commentary cuts through the rhetoric to identify a coordinated strategy: the removal of masks from the public face of the state, only to put them back on the agents of that state.

The Mask of Authority

The authors anchor their analysis in a powerful, unexpected parallel: the evolution of the Iraqi security forces. Mark Hertling, contributing to the collective voice of Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol, draws on his two tours in Iraq to illustrate a counterintuitive truth about legitimacy. He notes that during the early, fragile years of the occupation, Iraqi police and soldiers wore scarves to hide their identities out of fear. "Those face coverings weren't about intimidation. They wore them due to fear and a lack of courage," Hertling writes. He contrasts this with the later period, 2007–2008, when security units gained confidence and public trust, leading to a deliberate campaign where "soldiers wore their uniforms openly and proudly."

The commentary argues that the current administration is reversing this historical trajectory. By deploying Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agents who are "masked, heavily militarized, and—most troubling—without any visible identification," the executive branch is severing the link between power and responsibility. Hertling asserts that "in democratic societies, the use of lawful force depends on legitimacy. Legitimacy depends on accountability. And accountability begins with identification." This framing is particularly effective because it moves the debate away from partisan bickering and toward the structural requirements of a free society. When agents conceal their faces, the authors contend, they turn authority into anonymity, a condition that "democratic systems are designed to prevent."

Critics might argue that anonymity is a necessary safety measure for officers facing doxxing and harassment in a polarized climate. However, the authors dismantle this by pointing out that "public scrutiny is not a threat to legitimate law enforcement; it is one of its safeguards." If the administration's actions were truly lawful and ethical, identification would protect the agents as much as the public. Instead, the lack of identification suggests a fear of the very scrutiny that defines democratic oversight.

"Once trust is lost, it is not easily rebuilt. In Iraq, scarves came off as confidence grew. Yet we seem to be moving in the opposite direction."

The Attack on Independence

The piece shifts from the physical anonymity of agents to the institutional anonymity the administration seeks to impose on the Federal Reserve. The authors highlight a direct confrontation where the Department of Justice has served subpoenas to Fed Chair Jerome Powell, threatening criminal indictment over building renovations. This is not a standard oversight dispute; it is a weaponization of the legal system to force policy compliance. Powell, in a rare direct-to-camera address, stripped away the administration's excuses, stating, "The threat of criminal charges is a consequence of the Federal Reserve setting interest rates based on our best assessment of what will serve the public, rather than following the preferences of the president."

Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol emphasize that the administration's actions are a transparent attempt to subordinate economic policy to political whims. They note that "no one is really disputing it," underscoring the clarity of the threat. This mirrors the historical fragility of central banks, much like the early days of the French Resistance where the very existence of independent institutions was under siege. Just as the French Resistance had to navigate a complex web of occupation and collaboration, the Federal Reserve now faces an executive branch that views its independence not as a feature of the system, but as an obstacle to be removed.

The commentary suggests that the administration's strategy relies on the public's fatigue and the assumption that institutions will crumble under pressure. However, the authors point to Republican Senator Thom Tillis, who has already signaled he will join Democrats to block any attempt to replace Powell. This bipartisan defense highlights that the threat to the Fed is not just a partisan issue but a constitutional one. The authors argue that the administration's aggression is a test of whether the system can withstand the pressure of a leader who views the rule of law as a tool for personal vendettas.

The Case for Resistance

Finally, the piece makes a bold call to action, urging the opposition to shed the stigma of being a "resist lib." Bill Kristol, writing with Longwell and Miller, acknowledges that the term has been used to dismiss critics as "unreflective knee-jerkiness." Yet, he argues that the current moment demands a reclamation of the concept. Drawing a parallel to the French Resistance, Kristol notes that while the movement included "dogmatic communists as well as humane liberals," the primary moral value during occupation was "courage." He writes, "After Trump's first year, and especially after the accelerationist authoritarianism of the last ten days, the primary moral value for our opposition party has to be courage."

The authors outline specific, high-stakes legislative maneuvers, such as refusing to fund the Department of Homeland Security unless ICE agents are required to wear identification and obtain warrants. They acknowledge the political risk, noting that this could lead to a government shutdown, but point to recent electoral victories as evidence that "Democrats won resounding electoral victories, by margins far greater than polling a month before suggested." This is a strategic gamble: betting that the public will prioritize accountability over the convenience of a functioning government if that government is enabling abuse.

The commentary does not shy away from the human cost of the administration's policies. It references the shooting of Renee Good, where Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem and the President defended the use of deadly force against a woman they labeled a "professional agitator" without evidence. The authors critique the administration's narrative, noting that "Trump is doing everything he can to flood ICE, border patrol, and other federal law enforcement into every American city" while explicitly telling them they have his backing for violence. This creates a dangerous dynamic where agents feel empowered to act with impunity, and victims are blamed for their own deaths.

"If not resistance now, then when?"

Critics might argue that a strategy of government shutdowns and legislative brinkmanship is politically suicidal and could alienate moderate voters. The authors counter that the alternative—passive acceptance of authoritarian overreach—is far more dangerous. They suggest that the political landscape is shifting, with Republicans defecting on issues like Obamacare subsidies, indicating that the public is ready to punish the administration for its excesses.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its synthesis of historical precedent with immediate, tangible threats to civil liberties, effectively framing the administration's actions as a systematic dismantling of democratic norms rather than isolated policy disputes. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the political feasibility of the proposed resistance strategies, which rely on a level of bipartisan cooperation and public outrage that may be difficult to sustain. Readers should watch closely to see if the proposed legislative hurdles on ICE funding and the defense of the Federal Reserve can translate from rhetorical calls to actual legislative victories, as the outcome will define the balance of power for the rest of the term.

It’s an excuse Trump has been using for going after Powell since last summer.

Masks, Courage, and Accountability.

by Mark Hertling

Between my two tours in Iraq, first in 2003–2004 and then in 2007–2008, one of the more subtle changes I saw in the Iraqi Army and Iraqi Police that U.S. forces were helping organize and train had to do with masks. During my first tour, many of the police recruits and the young soldiers—jundi—wore scarves pulled high across their mouths and noses, sometimes a traditional shemagh or keffiyeh, sometimes whatever cloth they could find. Those face coverings weren’t about intimidation. They wore them due to fear and a lack of courage. Cowardice.

Early on, these men were joining a fragile security force, and they were new to their mission. They understood that insurgents would target anyone cooperating with Americans. They also believed their families would be threatened. Covering their faces, masking their very identity, was a form of survival.

But things changed as security improved and legitimacy began to take hold across the country during the 2007–2008 period.
