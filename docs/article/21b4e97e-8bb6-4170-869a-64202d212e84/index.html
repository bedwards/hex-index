The Italian Wars 1494-1559 - Early Modern History Documentary

Kings and Generals · Kings and Generals ·May 26, 2024 · 135 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Kings and Generals reframes the Italian Wars not as a distant sequence of feudal squabbles, but as the violent birth pangs of modern European statecraft, where the collision of French artillery and Swiss pikes shattered the medieval order. The author's most striking claim is that the conflict began not with grand strategy, but with the personal incompetence of a child duke and the machinations of a regent who invited a foreign superpower into his backyard. This is a crucial distinction for the busy listener: the era's geopolitical earthquakes were often triggered by domestic dynastic failures rather than abstract national interests.

The Spark of Ambition

The narrative opens by dismantling the myth of the stable Italian Renaissance. Kings and Generals writes, "The Italian peninsula of the Middle Ages was divided between the fragmented North split into numerous thiefs and city states..." This fragmentation created a vacuum that the great powers of Europe were eager to fill. The author effectively argues that the balance of power, maintained by the "Italic League" and overseen by Lorenzo de' Medici, was a fragile construct that collapsed the moment the Medici died. The trigger was the succession crisis in Milan, where the regent Ludovico Sforza, described as tampering with the young duke's education to keep power, made a fatal miscalculation.

Kings and Generals notes that Ludovico "solicited for years an old friend of the Sforza family the king of France Charles VIII to come to Italy and take over the Throne of Naples." This decision is framed as a classic case of the "enemy of my enemy is my friend" logic backfiring spectacularly. The author highlights that Charles VIII was not merely a conqueror but a king with a reformed army and a centralized treasury, resources that had been impossible to muster just decades prior. The commentary suggests that the Italian states failed to recognize that the nature of warfare had fundamentally changed; they were playing a game of chess while France had brought a sledgehammer.

The king wanted to plow through the peninsula by making examples of what happened to resistance and frightening his foes.

The Shock of the New

The documentary's most compelling section details the French invasion of 1494, emphasizing the psychological impact of their tactics. Kings and Generals writes, "The complete destruction of the surrendered town and the slaughter of both the population and the Defenders was a calculated strategy by the French King not wanting to play by the Italian rules of War." This is a pivotal moment in the text, illustrating the shift from the "defense, containment, maneuver, attrition and backdoor diplomacy" of the Italians to the brutal efficiency of the French. The author describes this as the "fury of Franz," a tactic designed to terrorize rather than negotiate.

The narrative details how Charles VIII traversed the peninsula with "an army of around 30,000 men including 8,000 Swiss mercenaries and a modern artillery Park." Kings and Generals writes, "The modern fortress could withstand both the French army and their artillery for months..." only to be bypassed or destroyed by the sheer speed and firepower of the French advance. The author points out that the Italian city-states, accustomed to mercenary armies that fought with restraint, were utterly unprepared for an enemy that viewed the destruction of a town like Fivizzano as a strategic necessity. Critics might note that the account leans heavily on the French perspective of "liberation" while downplaying the opportunistic nature of Charles's crusade against the Ottomans, which was largely propagandized. However, the core argument—that the invasion shattered the illusion of Italian autonomy—remains robust.

The Coalition and the Retreat

The turning point of the first war arrives with the formation of the League of Venice. Kings and Generals writes, "Ludovico IL Moro who had not expected France to put so many resources into the Expedition found himself in a worrisome situation." This highlights the hubris of the Italian statesmen who invited the French in, only to realize too late that they had unleashed a force they could not control. The author details the rapid mobilization of Spain, Milan, Venice, the Emperor, and the Pope to counter French hegemony.

The retreat of Charles VIII is described with a focus on the tactical evolution of the battlefield. Kings and Generals writes, "The Italian Army was mostly composed of Keri hired by Venice with only a quarter of those being millanes as their main force was preoccupied at Novara." The author contrasts the heavy knights of the Italian League with the Swiss mercenaries who formed the "spearhead" of the French army. The Battle of Fornovo is presented not as a decisive victory for either side, but as a testament to the new reality of warfare. The French, despite being outnumbered and cut off, managed to break through the Italian lines. Kings and Generals writes, "Charles's Army at Fornovo was composed of 5,500 mounted men... 4,000 infantry including 3,000 Swiss mercenaries... and 42 cannons." This composition underscores the decline of the knight and the rise of combined arms.

In just 5 months the French King had traversed the Italian Peninsula nearly unopposed and any resistance against him had been pulverized.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals delivers a masterful overview of how the Italian Wars marked the end of the medieval world and the beginning of the early modern era, driven by the collision of French centralization and Italian fragmentation. The strongest part of the argument is the vivid depiction of the tactical shock caused by French artillery and Swiss pikes, which rendered traditional Italian mercenary warfare obsolete. The biggest vulnerability is the relative lack of depth on the social and economic consequences for the Italian populace, focusing instead on the high politics of dukes and kings. For the listener, the key takeaway is that the Italian Wars were not just a series of battles, but the moment Europe realized that the old rules of war no longer applied.
