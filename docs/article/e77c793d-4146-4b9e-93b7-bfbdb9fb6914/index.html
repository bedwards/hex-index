Stroads are Ugly, Expensive, and Dangerous (and they're everywhere) [ST05]

Jason Slaughter · Not Just Bikes ·Apr 26, 2021 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jason Slaughter delivers a brutal diagnosis for the American urban landscape: the most common road type in North America isn't just inefficient, it is actively suicidal. He argues that the "stroad"—a hybrid of street and road—is a design failure that kills people, bankrupts cities, and offers no viable alternative to driving. For busy professionals watching their cities decay, this is not just urban planning theory; it is an explanation for why your commute is getting slower and your local economy is stalling.

The Futon of Transportation

Slaughter's most striking metaphor arrives early, defining the stroad not as a compromise, but as a disaster. "Just like how a futon converts from a terrible uncomfortable couch to a terrible uncomfortable bed, the Strode is the futon of transportation," he writes. This analogy perfectly captures the dysfunction of the design: it tries to be two things and succeeds at neither. He contrasts the "road," designed for high-speed connection with wide clear zones and few exits, against the "street," a human-scale environment where buildings touch the sidewalk and life happens. The stroad, he argues, takes the wide lanes of a road and the chaotic access points of a street, creating a "hostile" environment for anyone not in a car.

The visual evidence he presents is damning. He points out that while traffic engineers obsess over the "clear zone" to prevent drivers from hitting trees, they casually accept that "hitting a cyclist is expected." This reveals a prioritization of vehicle flow over human life that is baked into the asphalt. Critics might argue that some of these designs were necessary for post-war economic expansion, but Slaughter's point is that the context has shifted while the infrastructure has not. The result is a space where "nobody actually sits and watches the world go by on this bench." It is a non-place, designed purely for transit, yet failing to move people efficiently.

The Illusion of Speed

Perhaps the most counterintuitive part of Slaughter's argument is that these wide, multi-lane thoroughfares do not actually make traffic move faster. "Because there are so many crossroads, entrances, and exits to the strode, traffic lights are required, meaning drivers are constantly stopping at red lights," he explains. The design creates a paradox: the wide lanes encourage speeding, but the frequent access points force constant stopping. This leads to "traffic signal induced congestion" where drivers are stuck in a cycle of acceleration and braking.

He drives this point home with a sobering statistic from the pandemic era. "During the coronavirus lockdowns... despite an estimated 355 billion fewer miles driven, fatal crashes per mile actually increased by up to 34%," Slaughter notes. This data suggests that the danger isn't just about volume; it's about the design itself. The wide, straight stretches of the stroad tempt drivers to speed up, only to encounter sudden conflicts at driveways and intersections. "The only reason these unsafe American stros aren't killing even more people regularly is because they're usually so jammed up with traffic that drivers can't get going fast enough to kill each other," he writes. This is a chilling admission: our safety is an accidental byproduct of gridlock, not design.

The stroad fails at every metric, but it's the default setting for transportation design almost everywhere in the US and Canada.

The Financial Black Hole

The economic argument is where Slaughter's analysis becomes most urgent for city leaders and taxpayers. He details how stroads are built to highway standards—requiring four or more lanes, wide shoulders, and massive turning radii—while serving the function of a local street. This creates a massive maintenance burden. "A strode environment is significantly less financially productive than a street, but with significantly more infrastructure," he argues. The wide asphalt and lack of density mean these areas generate low tax revenue while incurring high costs for grading, flood protection, and repairs.

He highlights the absurdity of the land use: "Parking lots don't employ anyone, and they don't earn much tax revenue either." By dedicating vast swaths of land to cars and parking, the stroad model ensures that the city is "literally putting American cities on the strode to financial ruin." This is not a minor inefficiency; it is a structural deficit that threatens municipal solvency. The argument holds up well against the common defense that cars are necessary for commerce; Slaughter shows that the stroad model actually suppresses the density required for vibrant commerce.

The Dutch Alternative

Slaughter doesn't just critique; he points to a proven alternative. He turns to the Netherlands, where a strict classification system prevents the stroad from ever being built. The Dutch divide infrastructure into three distinct categories: the snelweg (highway), the distributieweg (distributor road), and the woonerf or access street. "In the Netherlands, every road and street... has to be classified and designated as one of the following three options," he explains. This clarity forces designers to choose a purpose. If it's a road, access is limited. If it's a street, speeds are capped at 30 km/h, and the design prioritizes people.

He describes how the Dutch system seamlessly transitions between these types, using roundabouts and physical design to manage speed and access without the chaotic mixing found in North America. "Single lane roads are much safer because cars changing lanes introduces points of conflict between vehicles and they are easier for pedestrians to cross," he observes. This approach frees up space for sidewalks and bike paths, creating a network that is safer and more efficient. The Dutch example proves that the stroad is not an inevitability of modern life, but a choice—one that the Netherlands has wisely rejected.

Bottom Line

Jason Slaughter's strongest asset is his ability to reframe the familiar as dangerous, exposing the stroad as a design that is simultaneously lethal and economically ruinous. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in the sheer political inertia required to undo decades of car-centric zoning, a hurdle he acknowledges but does not fully solve. For the reader, the takeaway is clear: the gridlock and danger we face are not bugs in the system, but features of a failed design that we have the power to change.
