Digital security - threats, risks and how to protect yourself

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Dec 16, 2019 · 51 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

The Hated One and Daniel, CTO of Safing, deliver a sobering reality check: the internet is fundamentally broken because it was built on 1970s architecture that never accounted for malicious actors. While most security advice focuses on individual passwords, this conversation argues that the entire infrastructure of the web is operating without basic safety checks, much like an airline that never inspects its engines until a crash occurs.

The Car Analogy and the Legacy Problem

The discussion opens with a powerful comparison between modern cybersecurity and the early days of the automobile. Daniel argues that just as cars were initially lethal until safety became a non-negotiable design requirement, the internet is currently in a dangerous transition period. The Hated One notes that "we're still using loads of protocols and technology that was built back then and just not suitable for digital world that we have now." This historical context is crucial; it explains why the internet feels so fragile. The core of the argument is that security cannot be an afterthought or a patch; it requires a ground-up reworking of how data moves. This lands because it shifts the blame from individual users to systemic design flaws.

Critics might note that completely rewriting internet protocols is a logistical nightmare that could fracture the global network, but the authors suggest that the cost of inaction is already too high. The Hated One emphasizes the urgency, stating, "most people are just naked in the wild of the internet and they need to be protected otherwise they can get horribly abused or damaged by bad security." This framing effectively strips away the illusion of safety that many users feel.

"When we first had cars... people were just dying horribly and it took some time until well we realized that's not a thing and we really have to change how cars work to get them to cure and that's the same process we're going through now with with information technology."

The Business of Inaction

Why has this transition taken so long? Daniel points to a perverse economic incentive: security is invisible when it works. He explains that for business leaders, "if it works you don't feel it... it's only when they they there's a problem that they realize well we have to throw more money at it." This observation cuts through the noise of corporate responsibility. The argument is that companies only invest in security after a breach, often with an "unlimited budget" that could have been spent more efficiently beforehand. The Hated One highlights this reactive cycle, noting that even with regulations like GDPR, the motivation is often fear of fines rather than a genuine commitment to user safety.

The conversation also touches on the strange market where vulnerabilities are sold to the highest bidder, often nation-states. Daniel suggests that tech giants like Apple make a "tactical decision" not to match the prices of these black markets, fearing they would only drive up the cost of exploits. "If you just like pump the price there will just be more people looking for abilities and Apple is still not the one who's gonna be offered to them," he argues. This is a cynical but realistic take on the economics of zero-day vulnerabilities, acknowledging that the market for breaking security is more lucrative than the market for fixing it.

The Path Forward

Despite the gloomy outlook, the authors identify specific technical shifts that offer hope. They point to the slow but steady adoption of HTTPS and the encryption of DNS queries as critical steps toward a safer web. The Hated One describes the current state of internet routing as a system where "anybody can just come here and say hey well you know that Google Network I own that and should please send all your traffic to me," a vulnerability known as BGP hijacking. The solution, as Daniel outlines, involves new protocols that verify the identity of network operators, finally bringing a layer of trust to a system built on blind faith.

The discussion concludes by reinforcing the idea that while perfect security is impossible due to human error, the current baseline is unacceptable. The authors argue that we are finally moving from a world where security was a "hassle" to one where it is a necessity. As The Hated One puts it, "we're in this process and there are a couple of things I think that are happening at the moment" that signal a fundamental shift in how we approach digital safety.

"Imagine you go onto an airplane and you know like the airline that you're traveling with it's like not even checking their their their aircraft or you know making security checks or verifying and if something goes wrong in the airplane crashes they are not even responsible that's that that would be crazy you would never take a flight and somehow on the internet this is considered to be to be a norm or acceptable."

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its refusal to blame the user, correctly identifying that the internet's foundational architecture is the primary source of risk. Its biggest vulnerability is the sheer scale of the problem; fixing 50 years of technical debt is a generational challenge that may outpace the immediate threats. Readers should watch for the implementation of DNS encryption and BGP verification, as these are the first tangible signs that the internet is finally learning to protect itself.
