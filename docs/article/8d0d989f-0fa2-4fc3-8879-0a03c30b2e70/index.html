Commentary by Brian Edwards

Timothy Snyder, in this second lecture of his Yale course on Ukraine, dismantles the comforting myth that nations are ancient, natural entities. Instead, he argues that the very concept of a nation is a modern political invention that survives by fabricating a false, linear history to justify its existence today. For busy leaders trying to understand the current war, this distinction isn't academic nitpicking; it is the key to decoding why narratives of "restoration" and "golden ages" are so dangerous when wielded by modern states.

The Great Deception of National History

Snyder begins by tackling the fundamental paradox of nationalism: how do we get from "nothing" to a nation? He posits that once a nation exists, it immediately invents a past that never happened to legitimize itself. "The nation once it exists lays claim to the Past right so the nation didn't always exist but once it comes into existence it tells a story about the past and the story that the nation tells about the past is wrong," Yale University writes. This is a bold, necessary correction to the way most people consume history. We tend to accept national stories as facts, but Snyder insists they are functional fictions designed to make the modern political order feel inevitable.

The mechanism for this deception is often the education system, which Snyder describes as a circular trap. "The nation takes over the state the state takes over the education the education takes over the kids right then the kids believe the things which are commonsensical," he explains. This institutional loop ensures that by the time we are adults, the constructed nature of our nationality feels as natural as gravity. Critics might argue that this view is overly cynical, ignoring the genuine cultural bonds that predate modern states. However, Snyder's point is not that culture doesn't exist, but that the specific political form of the "nation" is a relatively recent invention that repackages that culture to serve modern power structures.

The nation is modern but it lays claim to the Past in a way which if we ourselves are at all nationally minded it feels comfortable and right and that makes it very hard to answer this question of where the nation came from.

The Myth of Innocence and the Three-Part Story

Snyder identifies two dominant narrative structures that nations use to hide their modern origins. The first is the story of "lost innocence," often favored by imperial or post-imperial powers. He notes that this narrative suggests, "there once was innocence and the innocence was lost that is a big story about the nation especially Nations that emerge out of Empires." This framing allows leaders to claim they are merely trying to return to a time when everything was "right," masking the fact that the "golden age" they seek to restore never actually existed in the way they describe.

The second, more common structure for anti-colonial or peripheral nations is a three-part biblical arc: a time of virtue, a period of loss or diaspora, and a promised redemption. Snyder points out that this story is so pervasive it feels like a universal truth, yet it is a specific tool for political mobilization. "The nation takes over this story very easily," he observes, citing the concept of a "National Renaissance" as a prime example. The idea of a "rebirth" is logically strange—how can a group be reborn if it never died?—but it is politically potent because it promises a future where the people reclaim a state they supposedly lost.

This narrative strategy is particularly effective because it addresses a real historical shift: the entry of the masses into politics. "The nation and the way we're talking about the nation is a modern form of politics which involves if not everybody it's meant to involve the masses," Snyder argues. As political power shifted from monarchs to the people, nations needed a story that made the masses feel like the rightful heirs to history. The three-part story provides exactly that, suggesting that the current struggle is just the final chapter in a long, pre-ordained journey back to power.

The Reality of History vs. The Utility of Myth

Despite the power of these stories, Snyder is clear that they do not reflect historical reality. "It's not that there ever was a pure Nation it's not that every that there was an ethnicity which existed a thousand years ago and still exists today," he states bluntly. He acknowledges the awkwardness of this revelation for many listeners, noting that "marriage and you know I know that I'm it's I know I'm breaking something to you now." But the utility of the myth lies in its ability to make sense of the chaotic transition to mass politics, not in its factual accuracy.

The danger, as Snyder implies, is when these myths become the basis for policy and war. When a nation believes its own story of lost innocence or inevitable redemption, it stops seeing the present as a complex reality and starts seeing it as a problem to be solved by returning to a fictional past. This is not just a Ukrainian problem; it is a global phenomenon where the "originalist" desire to return to a pure founding moment blinds leaders to the complexities of the present.

Bottom Line

Snyder's most compelling contribution is his exposure of the "circular phenomenon" where nations invent their own history to justify their existence, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy that is nearly impossible for citizens to escape. While his deconstruction of national myths is intellectually rigorous, it risks underestimating the emotional power of these narratives, which often sustain communities through genuine hardship, not just political manipulation. For anyone trying to understand the current conflict in Ukraine, the takeaway is clear: the war is not just about land, but about which version of the past gets to define the future.
