The Iranians Backing Bibi and Trump

Novara Media · Novara Media ·Mar 11, 2026 · 26 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Novara Media exposes a jarring contradiction at the heart of the current geopolitical crisis: the loudest voices in the West demanding war on Iran are not the Iranian people, but a fractured diaspora faction that the article argues is being weaponized by Western media to manufacture consent. While the narrative of a unified Iranian front against the Islamic Republic dominates the news cycle, the author reveals a far more complex reality where anti-war Iranians are being silenced, doxxed, and labeled as regime agents by their own compatriots.

The Manufactured Consensus

The piece dismantles the assumption that the Iranian diaspora speaks with one voice. Novara Media notes that prominent talking heads like Massie Alenad have become huge backers of the war, claiming, "More than 32,000 have been killed by the Islamic Republic less than 24 hours." This hyperbolic statistic, repeated without context, serves to justify immediate, massive military intervention. The author points out that this rhetoric ignores the nuance of the conflict, particularly the fact that the regime's internal dynamics have shifted. As the author observes, "When you remove that layer of RGC commanders... they've been replaced by younger people with a point to prove the complete opposite of what she's saying." This suggests that the removal of moderate figures has actually hardened the state's stance, a critical detail often lost in the rush to condemn.

The media's role in amplifying these specific voices is scrutinized heavily. The author argues that outlets like the BBC prefer to platform pro-war figures while ignoring the diversity of opinion. "Unfortunately, if you are in a position where your country has a contentious or maybe good relationship with America, certain segments of the diaspora are highlighted," the text explains. This selective amplification creates a false narrative of unanimity. Critics might note that media bias is a perennial complaint, but the specific mechanism here—using diaspora voices to override the collective will of a brown nation—is a distinct and potent form of political gaslighting.

"They want to create this image that the entire Iranian diaspora supports the war. It's to gaslight you."

The Rise of a Far-Right Exile Movement

Perhaps the most startling revelation in the coverage is the characterization of the monarchist movement, led by Reza Pahlavi, as increasingly fascist. The author does not use the term lightly, pointing to a shift in rhetoric that goes beyond standard regime change advocacy. "In English, he's very liberal, very lysen, he's very French affectation... And in Persian, he's a bit more, you know, we won't forgive, we won't forget," the author explains, highlighting a dangerous duality in the movement's messaging. The text details how the base of support includes figures who espouse white supremacist ideologies, such as the claim that "Iran was a white Aryan nation and it was invaded and polluted by yellow Asians and brown semmites."

This historical context is crucial. The author draws a parallel to the Pahlavi era's own contradictions, noting that the regime once hosted a runaway SS officer who fought in Nazi Germany. "This isn't even foreign to the kind of the state ethos of the uh of the Palivist," the author asserts. This connection to historical fascism adds a layer of urgency to the current debate, suggesting that the alternative to the current theocracy might not be the liberal democracy many in the West assume. The article also touches on the suppression of other political factions, noting that ethnic minorities and leftists are being pushed out of the movement. "If you're a Kurd, but you're too passionate about your Kurdish identity... then you're a traitor Kurd," the author paraphrases the exclusionary logic of the hardliners.

The human cost of this polarization is immediate and terrifying. The author describes how anti-war voices are being targeted: "They're doxing them. They're coming for their family, me, death threats." This suppression prevents a genuine democratic discourse within the diaspora. The author challenges the media to do better: "If you're serious about anti-war Iranian voices, bring a credible humanist, left-leaning anti-war voice, not just some government lackey." This plea underscores the artificial nature of the current debate, where only the most extreme pro-war voices are permitted to speak.

The Tragedy of Cultural Destruction

Beyond the politics, the article laments the physical destruction of Iranian heritage. The author reflects on the bombing of cultural sites like Ozadi Square, formerly Shahiod Square, a monument built to commemorate the 2500-year anniversary of the Persian monarchy. "It's so so tragic to see yet another one of our cultural sites, our historic monuments, significant things to see in the city come under threat," the author writes. The irony is palpable: "To free us, they bombed Freedom Tower." This imagery serves as a powerful metaphor for the unintended consequences of foreign intervention, where the very symbols of national identity are erased in the name of liberation.

The author also addresses the psychological toll on the diaspora, noting that many who initially supported precision strikes now regret the escalation. "We supported, you know, precision strikes. We supported decapitation strikes. We supported one or two days, not 10 days, not oil raining in from the sky," the text quotes. This shift in public opinion suggests that the initial enthusiasm for war was based on a misunderstanding of its scope and consequences. The author predicts that future polling will reflect this change, with support for the war dropping significantly as the reality of the conflict sets in.

Bottom Line

Novara Media's coverage is a vital corrective to the simplistic narrative of a unified Iranian front, exposing the dangerous rise of a far-right exile movement and the systematic silencing of anti-war voices. The article's strongest asset is its willingness to name the fascist undercurrents within the monarchist movement, a detail often glossed over by Western media eager for a regime change victory. However, the piece's reliance on anecdotal evidence regarding the shift in public opinion, while compelling, would be strengthened by more immediate polling data to confirm the predicted 70-30 split. Readers should watch for how the media responds to the growing evidence of internal division within the diaspora, as this could fundamentally alter the political calculus for any potential intervention.
