The Iranians Backing Bibi and Trump

Novara Media · Novara Media ·Mar 11, 2026 · 26 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

In a media landscape saturated with monolithic narratives, Novara Media cuts through the noise to expose a startling fracture: the Iranian diaspora is not a unified front cheering for war, but a deeply polarized community where the loudest voices are often the most extreme. The author's most distinctive claim is that Western media platforms are actively gaslighting the public by amplifying a fringe, far-right faction of exiles while systematically silencing the anti-war majority, a distortion that risks fueling a conflict based on a fabricated consensus.

The Illusion of Consensus

The piece opens by dismantling the popular image of the Iranian diaspora as a monolith. Novara Media writes, "New polling from the National Iranian-American Council suggests that when asked whether they support the war, Iranian-Americans are about evenly split." This statistic is crucial because it immediately undercuts the prevailing media narrative that the entire community is clamoring for regime change. The author points out that this data was collected before the full-scale escalation, suggesting that as the reality of the bombing sets in, the anti-war sentiment is likely surging even further. This is a vital correction to the record, reminding readers that public opinion is fluid, not static.

The commentary highlights how the media selectively platforms specific voices to manufacture a false majority. As Novara Media puts it, "They want to create this image that the entire Iranian diaspora supports the war. It's to gaslight you." The author argues that this strategy relies on a psychological manipulation where American progressives are told that opposing the war is an act of racism against the "collective will" of a brown nation. This framing is particularly insidious because it weaponizes anti-racism to silence legitimate dissent. Critics might note that the author's reliance on social media anecdotes to gauge sentiment could be subject to selection bias, yet the polling data cited provides a necessary anchor of empirical evidence.

"They will tell you, well, look at all these Iranians in America who support the war and look at all these AI Iranians in Iran who support the war. How dare you, you white American, tell these brown people what's best for their country."

The Rise of a Far-Right Exile Movement

The article takes a sharp turn to dissect the political character of the pro-war faction, identifying them not as liberal democrats, but as a far-right nationalist movement. Novara Media notes that while figures like Reza Pahlavi present a "very liberal, very French" image in English, his inner circle and base espouse rhetoric rooted in "racial purity" and Aryan supremacy. The author cites specific examples, such as Jason Rezazadeh Gorgani, who helped found the alt-right and claims Iran was "polluted by yellow Asians and brown semmites." This revelation is jarring for readers who may have assumed the exile movement was a straightforward democratic alternative to the Islamic Republic.

The author draws a disturbing parallel to historical precedents, noting that the Pahlavi movement has a history of hosting Nazi fugitives, including a runaway SS officer who fled to Iran. This context adds a layer of historical depth, echoing the complex and often contradictory alliances seen in the National Iranian American Council's earlier analyses of diaspora politics, where the line between anti-authoritarianism and ethno-nationalism has frequently blurred. The piece argues that this is not an isolated case but a systemic feature of the movement. As Novara Media writes, "When you look in the Persian, and thankfully the auto translates becoming a thing now on many social media platforms, you see the type of rhetoric."

The author emphasizes that this rhetoric is often hidden behind a "smoke screen" of English-language liberalism. The commentary suggests that this duality is a deliberate strategy to gain Western support while maintaining a hardline stance at home. The text points out that the movement is actively purging leftists, Kurds, and other ethnic minorities, labeling them as traitors. This internal cleansing is a critical detail that challenges the notion of the diaspora as a unified democratic force. A counterargument worth considering is that the author may be overstating the influence of these fringe elements, as the movement's leadership often disavows such extreme views. However, the evidence of doxing and death threats against anti-war voices suggests a climate of intimidation that goes beyond mere political disagreement.

The Cost of a Manufactured War

The final section of the commentary focuses on the human and cultural cost of this manufactured consensus. The author describes the bombing of cultural sites like Ozadi Square, once known as Shahiyad Square, a monument built by the Shah to commemorate 2,500 years of Persian monarchy. The irony of bombing a "Freedom Square" to "free" the country is not lost on the author. Novara Media writes, "Does watching this video make you think Iran is being freed? All I see is death and destruction. What an irony. To free us, they bombed Freedom Tower."

This emotional appeal is bolstered by the testimony of ordinary Iranians who feel betrayed by the war. The author notes that many who initially supported precision strikes are now horrified by the scale of destruction. The piece argues that the media's failure to represent these voices creates a dangerous feedback loop where the war is justified by a consensus that does not exist. As Novara Media puts it, "They're suffocating the anti-war voices. We haven't seen them represented anywhere." The author concludes that the true danger lies in the West's willingness to accept a distorted view of Iranian society, leading to a war that serves the interests of a small, radicalized faction rather than the Iranian people.

Bottom Line

Novara Media's most compelling contribution is its exposure of the far-right, ethno-nationalist undercurrents within the pro-war diaspora, challenging the liberal veneer often presented in Western media. While the piece relies heavily on social media testimony, its integration of polling data and historical context regarding the Pahlavi movement's ties to fascism provides a robust foundation for its argument. The biggest vulnerability remains the difficulty of proving the exact current sentiment of a population under war, but the evidence of silencing and doxing strongly suggests that the anti-war voice is being systematically erased from the public square.
