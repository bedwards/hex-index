Are You a Useful Idiot?

Commentary by Brian Edwards

A Polemic Dressed as Geopolitical Analysis

The article opens with a confrontational challenge to right-wing commentators who celebrated the U.S. capture of Venezuelan President Nicolas Maduro, arguing that their enthusiasm reveals a fundamental ignorance of who actually benefits from the operation. The author's thesis is blunt: the Venezuela operation serves Israeli and Zionist interests, not American ones, and those cheering it on are, in the author's framing, "useful idiots" for a foreign policy apparatus they claim to oppose.

The piece is less an analysis than an extended argument, pulling from a wide range of sources -- journalist Jose Nino, historian Matthew Raphael Johnson, UN economic data, and various Substack commentators -- to construct a case that U.S. foreign policy in Latin America is an extension of Israeli strategic objectives. Whether readers find this persuasive will depend largely on how much connective tissue they require between correlation and causation.

The Isaac Accords and Latin American Diplomacy

The article's most substantive contribution is its examination of the Isaac Accords, a diplomatic initiative extending Israeli normalization efforts into Latin America. The author cites Genesis Prize Foundation Chairman Stan Polovets directly.

We must end Israel's isolation on the world stage. Together with President Milei, we will start in Latin America and help make his dream of Isaac Accords a reality. Milei's support is not only symbolic. His Isaac Accords vision is a geopolitical strategy that can bring tangible results in Latin America.

The phased approach described -- Argentina, Costa Rica, Panama, and Uruguay first, with Brazil, Chile, Colombia, and El Salvador targeted for 2026 -- is documented through Jose Nino's reporting. The author frames this as evidence that Israel is deeply invested in Latin American geopolitics, countering the claim that "Israel doesn't care about Venezuela."

A counterpoint worth raising: diplomatic normalization initiatives are routine instruments of statecraft. Every major power cultivates regional partnerships. The existence of the Isaac Accords demonstrates Israeli interest in Latin America broadly, but the leap from "Israel wants diplomatic relationships in the region" to "the Maduro capture was orchestrated for Israeli benefit" requires several inferential steps the article does not fully bridge. Proximity of interests is not identity of interests.

The Clean Break Document and Neoconservative Foreign Policy

The author reaches back to the 1996 "Clean Break" strategy paper to argue that American foreign policy has been subordinated to Israeli interests for decades. The article quotes an analysis of the document's influence on the Bush administration.

The authors of A Clean Break, those driving American policy, derive their concepts based on Israeli security and Israeli interest so that American foreign policy under the Bush administration is primarily serving the interest of Israel and secondarily that of the United States.

This is a well-trodden argument in foreign policy criticism, and the Clean Break document is a real paper with real authors who did hold influential positions. The connection between the document's recommendations and subsequent U.S. actions in Iraq, Libya, and Syria is a subject of legitimate scholarly debate. Where the article weakens its own case is in treating this connection as self-evident proof of a single controlling interest rather than one factor among many in complex policy decisions shaped by oil interests, defense industry lobbying, bureaucratic inertia, and genuine -- if misguided -- ideological commitments to liberal interventionism.

The Oil Question and Paul Singer

On the practical question of Venezuelan oil, the author presents a strong factual case that the resource is no quick windfall for American consumers. The article quotes commodity analyst Gus Vasquez of Argus Media.

There is no realistic prospect of immediately increasing Venezuela's crude output. Venezuelan oil infrastructure would take years and possibly hundreds of billions to bring up to something close to its former capacity. Repairing refineries would be even harder.

The discussion of Paul Singer's acquisition of CITGO at a steep discount -- reportedly paying $5.9 billion for assets valued between $13 billion and $18 billion -- is among the article's most concrete reporting. The detail that the court-appointed special master overseeing the auction sits on AIPAC's national board of directors is the kind of fact that, regardless of whether it influenced the outcome, raises legitimate questions about the appearance of impartiality in proceedings of this magnitude.

Venezuela's characterization of the sale as "the theft of the century" may be hyperbolic, but the numbers tell their own story. A discount of 55 percent on a nation's crown jewel energy assets, acquired through a legal process the asset's owner rejects as illegitimate, is the kind of transaction that generates lasting resentment whether or not it meets the technical requirements of legality.

Sanctions and the Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

The article's strongest analytical thread concerns the circular logic of U.S. sanctions policy. The author, drawing on Jose Nino's reporting, argues that Washington created the very conditions it now cites as justification for intervention.

To understand Venezuela's current strategic partnerships, one must first grasp the systematic campaign that preceded them. Since 2005, Washington has imposed 12 distinct rounds of sanctions, creating one of the most comprehensive economic warfare campaigns in the Western Hemisphere.

The statistics the author marshals about pre-sanctions Venezuela are striking: poverty falling from 44 percent to 26 percent between 1999 and 2011, the GINI coefficient dropping to the lowest in Latin America, food consumption increasing 81 percent, malnutrition falling from 21 percent to under 3 percent. These figures, drawn from Dr. Johnson's analysis, paint a picture of genuine economic progress under Chavez-era policies -- progress that was systematically dismantled by external pressure.

The counterpoint here is that Venezuela's economic model was always dangerously dependent on oil revenues, and the decline in global oil prices beginning in 2014 would have stressed the economy regardless of sanctions. Maduro's own policy decisions -- currency controls, price fixing, constitutional manipulation -- compounded the damage. Sanctions accelerated a crisis, but they did not create it from whole cloth. The author's framing omits this contributing factor entirely, which weakens an otherwise potent argument.

The Fabricated Cartel

Perhaps the article's most damaging factual point concerns the Cartel de los Soles, the organization cited as justification for narco-terrorism charges against Maduro. The author cites reporting from Common Dreams.

Cartel de los Soles is not a literal organization, according to a range of specialists in Latin American criminal and narcotics issues, from think-tank analysts to former Drug Enforcement Administration officials. It is instead a figure of speech in Venezuela, dating back to the 1990s, for Venezuelan military officials corrupted by drug money.

The fact that neither the DEA's National Drug Threat Assessment nor the UN's World Drug Report has ever mentioned this organization is a significant detail. Building a legal case for regime change on the premise that a president leads a cartel that law enforcement agencies do not recognize as existing is the kind of creative statecraft that tends to erode institutional credibility over time. The juxtaposition with Trump's pardon of Honduran President Juan Orlando Hernandez -- convicted of actual cocaine trafficking -- further undercuts the administration's stated rationale.

Where the Argument Overreaches

The article's weaknesses are as instructive as its strengths. The author's insistence that neoconservatism and Zionism "are the same thing" collapses a complex ideological landscape into a single variable. Neoconservatism has roots in Trotskyist anti-Stalinism, Cold War liberal hawkishness, and Straussian political philosophy that are not reducible to a single national interest. Many of its most consequential policy failures -- Iraq reconstruction, Afghan nation-building -- produced outcomes that harmed Israeli security as much as they served it.

The author's characterization of Chavez and Maduro as "Social Nationalists" -- with the explicit comparison to National Socialism -- is similarly provocative rather than clarifying. Dr. Johnson's quoted analysis that "Socialism of the 21st century is nothing less than National Socialism" conflates economic nationalism with a specific historical ideology that carried rather different implications. Venezuela's Bolivarian socialism shares more with Peronism, Mexican PRI-era corporatism, and Nasserist Arab socialism than with the movement Johnson invokes.

The article also relies heavily on sources from a narrow ideological band -- The Occidental Observer, Unz Review, Patreon posts from individual commentators -- which limits its persuasive reach beyond an audience already sympathetic to its conclusions. The strongest claims in the piece are the ones that could be verified through mainstream reporting: the CITGO acquisition terms, the nonexistence of Cartel de los Soles as a recognized organization, the Isaac Accords initiative. The weaker claims are those that depend on interpretive frameworks available only within the author's particular political ecosystem.

Bottom Line

The article raises several legitimate questions about the Venezuela operation that deserve more attention than they have received in mainstream commentary: the strategic context of the Isaac Accords, the deeply discounted acquisition of CITGO, the fabricated narco-terrorism justification, and the self-defeating cycle of sanctions and intervention. These are facts, not conspiracy theories, and they complicate any straightforward narrative about American interests being served. However, the author's insistence on reducing all of these threads to a single explanatory variable -- Israeli orchestration -- prevents the analysis from achieving the credibility its factual foundations could support. The strongest version of this argument would acknowledge that U.S. foreign policy in Latin America serves multiple overlapping interests, of which Israeli strategic objectives are one significant but not singular factor. By refusing that complexity, the article becomes a mirror image of the simplistic thinking it criticizes.
