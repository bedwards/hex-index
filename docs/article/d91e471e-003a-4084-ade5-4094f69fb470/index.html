LLM hallucinations are still absolutely nuts

Freddie deBoer · ·Jan 5, 2026 ·21 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In an era where artificial intelligence is increasingly treated as an oracle, Freddie deBoer delivers a chilling reality check by exposing how large language models don't just make mistakes—they construct entire, elaborate fictional worlds that users mistake for truth. Drawing on a personal interaction with a graduate student who relied on Google Gemini to research his own past institutionalization, deBoer reveals a system that confidently invents buildings, social rituals, and bureaucratic procedures that never existed. This is not a minor glitch; it is a fundamental failure of grounding that threatens to distort our understanding of history, policy, and human experience.

The Architecture of Fiction

The piece begins with a stark, personal anecdote: a researcher asks deBoer about the "Vance Building" at Connecticut Valley Hospital, a facility deBoer knows well. The AI, operating in its advanced "Thinking" mode, had generated thousands of words describing a vibrant vocational hub and social center. As deBoer notes, "Gemini discussed Vance Hall in detail, describing it as a hub of patient education, vocational training, job opportunities, commerce, and socializing." The sheer specificity of the fabrication is what makes it so dangerous. The model didn't just say there was a building; it invented a "geography of hope" where patients could escape the drudgery of locked wards.

DeBoer dismantles this narrative with surgical precision, pointing out that while a road named Vance Drive exists, the building itself is a hallucination. "To understand the Vance Building and The Club is to understand the geography of hope at Connecticut Valley Hospital," the AI claimed, but deBoer counters, "There is no Vance and thus no high ceilings or large windows or bustle." The model conflated a street name with a facility, then populated it with a fantasy of patient autonomy that contradicts the reality of high-security psychiatric care. Critics might argue that such errors are merely the price of generative speed, but deBoer's analysis suggests something deeper: the AI is not just retrieving data; it is synthesizing a narrative based on the idea of what a psychiatric hospital should look like, rather than what it actually is.

"The intricacy of the descriptions provided by Gemini are somewhat offset by the fact that Vance Hall does not exist."

The Logic of the Catch-22

The hallucination extends beyond architecture into the realm of social dynamics and logic. The AI described a complex system where access to the fictional "Vance" was used as a behavioral reward, creating a paradox where a patient's desperation to visit the building could lead to their privilege being revoked. DeBoer identifies this as a misapplication of the concept of a "Catch-22." While the term originates from Joseph Heller's 1961 novel and describes a situation where one cannot escape a rule because of the rule itself, the AI's version feels like a dramatic trope rather than a clinical reality. "I'm also not convinced that scenario constitutes a Catch-22," deBoer writes, noting that the actual constraints of psychiatric care are far more rigid and less theatrical than the AI's narrative suggests.

The model invented a "Treatment Mall" concept, claiming it was designed to simulate "going to work" or "going to school." DeBoer points out that while the concept of therapeutic environments is real, the specific implementation described is pure fiction. "The concept of a treatment mall is indeed a real thing, but again the Vance Building is a hallucination," he clarifies. This highlights a critical vulnerability in how these models function: they fill gaps in knowledge with plausible-sounding but factually empty details. For a researcher, this is a nuisance; for a policy maker or a historian, it could lead to a complete misreading of institutional history.

The Social Fabric of a Lie

Perhaps the most unsettling aspect of the AI's output is its invention of a rich social ecosystem. The model described "The Club," a social lounge and canteen where patients mingled, played pool, and engaged in "hospital romances." It even invented a currency system involving "trust fund slips" for purchasing snacks. "There was a cafe in my time, but I do not recall a canteen," deBoer writes, dismantling the AI's claim that patients used paper slips instead of money. "As far as I am aware it took ordinary money, not 'trust fund slips,' a truly bizarre concept."

The AI's description of inter-ward romances and a "grapevine" of gossip paints a picture of a vibrant, almost collegiate community. DeBoer, however, reminds us of the reality: "Wards are compartmentalized spaces and it's just hard for me to imagine extensive inter-ward gossip going on to this degree." The model's portrayal of patients passing notes through messengers or meeting at a fictional pool hall ignores the strict security protocols and the isolation inherent in forensic psychiatric care. It romanticizes an environment that is, in reality, defined by surveillance and restriction.

"Hospital romances are indeed discouraged at most psychiatric hospitals between patients in restricted wards, whereas the kinds of patients who have significant privileges would typically have enough off-campus and unstructured time to do what they want."

The AI also hallucinated the existence of a "Gilead Social Club" as an on-campus facility, confusing it with the real-world Gilead Community Services, a separate nonprofit organization. "There is no 'Gilead Social Club,'" deBoer corrects, noting that the real entity is an entirely separate organization focused on transitional housing and outpatient care. This conflation of distinct entities is a classic symptom of LLM confusion, where similar names or concepts are merged into a single, incorrect narrative.

The Underground Myth

Finally, the AI conjured up a network of underground tunnels connecting the hospital's buildings, describing them as "dimly lit, humid, and lined with massive steam pipes." DeBoer dismisses this as a cinematic trope, likely influenced by films like Girl, Interrupted. "The notion of intricate catacombs of tunnels underlying psychiatric hospitals is a common one in urban legends," he observes, pointing out that moving patients through such mazes would be impractical and unsafe. "It would seem to me that the idea of routinely transporting psychotic patients through dimly-lit mazes filled with hot steam pipes would not appeal to your average state bureaucrat."

This section of the commentary is particularly effective because it exposes how AI models rely on pop culture and genre conventions to fill in the blanks. When the model lacks specific data, it defaults to the most dramatic or recognizable narrative available, regardless of its factual accuracy. The result is a version of reality that is more entertaining than true, but dangerously misleading.

Bottom Line

Freddie deBoer's analysis serves as a powerful warning: the most capable large language models are not just prone to error; they are prone to creating coherent, persuasive, and entirely false realities. The strongest part of his argument is the demonstration that these hallucinations are not random noise but structured narratives that mimic human logic and social dynamics. The biggest vulnerability in the current state of AI is its inability to distinguish between what could happen and what did happen. As these tools become more integrated into research and decision-making, the risk of institutionalizing these fictions grows. We must remain vigilant, treating AI output not as a source of truth, but as a creative writing exercise that requires rigorous human fact-checking.

A couple months back, a grad student reached out to me to see if I could help with some research they were doing. (I’m keeping things vague for reasons that will be apparent.) They had heard that I had previously been a patient at Connecticut Valley Hospital. I told them that I would be happy to do my best with whatever questions they might have, but that I hadn’t been there in 20 years and my memories were dimmed by the weight of years, psychosis, and psychiatric medications. But, sure - I would tell them what I knew. I answered questions about my experiences there, mainly focusing on how I came to be admitted, my legal status on my first go round, the wards I had principally been in, and my release. We exchanged email for awhile and that was that.

Then, just a few days before the Christmas holiday, they got back in touch with me. They had some more questions that they had not been able to solve via research. They asked me about several features of life “on campus” that I couldn’t answer, and I grew confused. One was about the “Vance Building.” I told them I wasn’t familiar with it, but that wasn’t unusual; CVH is a sprawling facility with lots of different buildings that in many ways operate as their own little worlds, and again, it’s been several decades since I was there. Then they asked me if I remembered “the club.” To which I replied, the… club?

A bit of confused back and forth went down, and eventually they sheepishly admitted that they had been doing some research through Google Gemini. They insisted (quite strenuously) that they would never have an LLM write any of their actual work for them - and, for the record, I believe them - but that they had tried to fill in gaps in information with AI. With my gentle pushback, they were now concerned that they had a few facts wrong. I asked them if they would copy and paste the Gemini output and send it to me and they did. And, hoo boy!
