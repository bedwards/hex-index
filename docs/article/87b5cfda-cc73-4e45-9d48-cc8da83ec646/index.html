Why balogun's world cup red card was incorrect

Various · Reason ·Jul 2, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In a sports landscape often dominated by emotional outbursts or tactical breakdowns, this editorial from Reason takes a surprisingly judicial turn, applying the rigorous standards of criminal law to a split-second soccer foul. The piece argues that the red card ejected against American striker Folarin Balogun was not just a bad call, but a fundamental misapplication of the rules regarding intent and the standard of review for video intervention.

The Time Framing Problem

The article's most distinctive move is reframing the incident through the lens of "time framing," a concept familiar to legal scholars but rarely applied to athletic officiating. Reason reports, "In law, a common issue is deciding the appropriate time frame to assess a disputed action." The editors argue that focusing exclusively on the moment Balogun's boot landed on the defender's ankle creates a misleading impression of culpability.

Instead, the piece insists the analysis must begin earlier: at the launch of the jump. "Almost by definition, the assessment must involve a broader time frame then when Balogun landed," the article notes. This is a compelling argument because it highlights how the defender's own illegal arm extension altered Balogun's trajectory mid-air. By ignoring this preceding contact, the officiating crew effectively treated the player as an airborne object rather than an athlete reacting to a foul against him.

Critics might note that in high-speed sports, the moment of impact is often the only objective data point referees can rely on without over-analyzing the chain of causation. However, the piece's insistence on context mirrors how courts handle self-defense cases, where the shooter's action is judged by the threat preceding it.

Mens Rea and the "Guilty Mind"

The commentary then pivots to a crucial legal concept: mens rea, or the "guilty mind." The editors point out that while soccer rules on "serious foul play" are strict, they implicitly require an element of intent or recklessness. "Any player who lunges at an opponent... with excessive force or endangers the safety of an opponent is guilty of serious foul play," Reason quotes from the International Football Association Board (IFAB) rules.

However, the article argues that endangerment alone should not trigger a red card if the danger was unintentional and unforeseeable. The piece draws a sharp parallel to another World Cup incident involving Lionel Messi, noting that his contact with an opponent was deemed non-foul because it lacked intent. "It would be hard to describe the mere act of jumping in the area of an opponent as 'endangering' an opponent," the editors write.

This legalistic breakdown adds necessary depth to a debate often reduced to "he hit him, he's out." By invoking former referees Mark Clattenburg and Andy Davis, who also questioned the call based on intent, Reason strengthens its position that the ejection was inconsistent with how the game is actually played at the elite level. The argument holds weight because it exposes a gap between the letter of the rulebook and the practical application of fairness.

If neutral and skilled soccer observers disagree with the call, it is hard to believe that the error was, in fact, 'clear and obvious.'

The Standard of Review and the One-Way Ratchet

Perhaps the most damning critique in the piece concerns the Video Assistant Referee (VAR) protocol itself. The editors highlight a specific constraint: VAR can only overturn an on-field decision if it constitutes a "clear and obvious error." Reason reports, "The original decision given by the referee will not be changed unless the video review clearly shows that the decision was a 'clear and obvious error.'"

Given that even experts disagree on whether Balogun's challenge warranted a red card, the piece argues the threshold for overturning the call was never met. The initial referee saw no foul; the VAR introduced significant subjectivity where none should exist under the protocol.

The article goes further to critique the suspension system as a "one-way ratchet." Under current FIFA rules, a team can appeal to increase a punishment but cannot appeal to decrease it. "This one-way rachet allowing an increase in the punishment but not a decrease is ill-conceived," the editors argue. This structural flaw prevents the correction of errors like Balogun's, leaving the system unable to self-correct even when new evidence or expert consensus suggests a mistake was made.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its successful translation of legal due process into sports officiating, exposing how the "clear and obvious" standard was violated by subjective interpretation. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the practical reality that introducing formal appeals for suspensions could slow down an already bloated tournament schedule. Still, as the piece notes, accuracy and consistency are paramount when a player's career trajectory hangs in the balance.

Because it is interesting to think about how legal rules apply in this situation, I thought a short blog might be appropriate and timely.

After slow motion review of the video, the referee changed his earlier call and gave Balogun a red card for "serious foul play."
