Vfyw: Zen and the art of window sleuthing

Andrew Sullivan · The Dishwasher ·Dec 6, 2025 ·15 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Andrew Sullivan transforms a weekly geography puzzle into a profound meditation on the quiet, persistent human need for connection in an increasingly fragmented digital age. While the premise is a simple "where is this window?" contest, the coverage reveals a hidden ecosystem of community, intellectual curiosity, and shared vulnerability that defies the isolation often associated with online life.

The Architecture of Community

Sullivan does not merely list correct answers; he curates a tapestry of human stories that orbit the central mystery of the image. He highlights the emotional weight carried by these participants, noting how the contest has become a ritual for many. "First, the VFYW book arrived, and what a lovely way to relax at the end of the day," Sullivan writes, capturing the tangible comfort readers find in the physical artifact of their shared hobby. This observation is crucial; it grounds the digital activity in a physical reality, suggesting that the value lies not just in the solving, but in the pause it creates.

The piece excels in its portrayal of the "angst" of the modern knowledge worker. Sullivan paraphrases a contributor's struggle to balance the joy of the hunt with the demands of a busy life: "I have now experienced the angst of having the correct research ready for its final form regarding the contest … and then getting too busy to finish it out." This admission resonates deeply with the target audience of busy professionals. It reframes the contest not as a trivial distraction, but as a cherished intellectual pursuit that often gets sacrificed to the relentless pace of work. Sullivan's commentary validates this tension, suggesting that the act of returning to the puzzle is an act of reclaiming one's own time and curiosity.

We are not just solving riddles; we are carving out moments of wonder in a world that demands our constant attention.

The coverage also touches on the global nature of this community, weaving in diverse perspectives from Aruba to Sydney. One contributor notes, "It's the one holiday 'they' haven't been able to commercialize (yet)," referring to Thanksgiving. Sullivan uses this to underscore a broader point about the contest itself: it remains a space of genuine human exchange, untouched by the algorithms that usually dictate our attention. This framing is effective because it positions the "View From Your Window" as a rare sanctuary of authenticity.

The Detective's Mindset and Historical Echoes

As the piece delves into the specific mechanics of the sleuthing, Sullivan showcases the intellectual rigor required to solve these puzzles. The current week's challenge involved a "Zen Den" and a "Phoenix Block," leading to a labyrinth of false leads and red herrings. Sullivan recounts the journey of a participant who initially mistook a sign for "Love Den," sending them on a "goose chase to some adult entertainment stores." This anecdote serves as a humorous reminder of the fallibility of human perception, even in the age of Google Maps.

The commentary draws a subtle but powerful parallel between this digital detective work and the literary traditions of the past. Sullivan notes that one participant, while guessing the location of a photo in Petoskey, Michigan, recalled that "Ernest Hemingway spent many summers in the Petoskey area at his family's cottage on Walloon Lake." This reference is not merely decorative; it elevates the act of looking at a window to an act of literary pilgrimage. Just as Hemingway used the region as inspiration for his first novel, Torrents of Spring, these modern sleuths are using the landscape to construct narratives of their own. The connection suggests that the urge to understand our surroundings is a timeless human trait, linking the 21st-century digital explorer with the 20th-century literary giant.

The piece also touches on the darker, more complex history of the locations involved. A contributor from Hinckley, Ohio, shares a dramatic story of a prank gone wrong involving the "Old Phoenix Bank" and the FBI, where teenage boys stole cash and were eventually caught. Sullivan includes this narrative to illustrate how a simple building name can unlock a portal to local history, tragedy, and humor. "The feds were most likely expecting adults driving a car rather than teens on bicycles, so they completely missed their get-away," the contributor recalls. This detail adds a layer of human drama that a simple map search could never provide. It reminds the reader that every window looks out onto a world filled with stories, some of which are decades old and deeply embedded in the local psyche.

Critics might argue that focusing on such niche historical anecdotes distracts from the broader appeal of the contest. However, Sullivan's inclusion of these stories demonstrates that the contest's strength lies precisely in its ability to reveal the unexpected depth of the ordinary. The "Phoenix Block" is not just a building; it is a repository of community memory.

The Human Cost of the Digital Hunt

Beyond the intellectual challenge, Sullivan weaves in poignant moments of personal grief and family connection that give the piece its emotional core. The story of a father attending a university tour at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, only to be distracted by the view of the State Capitol, is particularly moving. "I politely yet agitatedly listen about how they are training the next generation of teachers and ed-policy leaders to change the state and nation and yada yada yada … as I witness this gorgeous view slowly fade to dusk and then night," the contributor writes. This admission of distraction is not framed as a failure, but as a testament to the enduring power of beauty and curiosity.

The piece also addresses the profound grief of Michael Lewis, whose daughter died in a car accident, and how his generosity in speaking to a young aspiring writer speaks to the resilience of the human spirit. Sullivan uses this to highlight the community's capacity for empathy. "The fact that he so generously gave his time to a young aspiring writer speaks volumes about his character," Sullivan observes. This moment of connection between a grieving father and a young writer mirrors the connection between the contest participants and the author. It suggests that in a world often defined by division, these small acts of attention and kindness are vital.

The coverage also touches on the absurdity of modern digital culture, noting the rise of "Fortnite" as a cultural phenomenon where a "naked anthropomorphized banana" can be considered obscene and where Tarantino shorts are premiered. Sullivan writes, "I think I need to go lie down," in response to the sheer surrealism of a world where Peely appears in a Tarantino film. This moment of exhaustion is relatable, serving as a counterpoint to the grounded, human-scale interactions of the contest. It highlights the contrast between the chaotic, algorithm-driven digital landscape and the deliberate, human-curated world of the "View From Your Window."

Bottom Line

Andrew Sullivan's commentary succeeds because it refuses to treat the "View From Your Window" contest as a mere game; instead, it presents it as a vital practice of attention in a distracted world. The strongest part of the argument is its ability to connect the mundane act of identifying a building with the profound human needs for connection, history, and beauty. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the reader's willingness to engage with these slow, detailed narratives in an era of rapid information consumption. However, for those willing to spend the time, the payoff is a rare glimpse into a community that values the depth of the world over the speed of the feed.

In a world of infinite scrolling, the most radical act is to stop, look closely, and ask: where am I, and who else is looking with me?"
