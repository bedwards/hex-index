Bernie Sanders Takes On AI Companies — And Gets Claude to Agree With Him", "author": "Novara Media", "piece": "Senator Bernie Sanders is no stranger to asking tough questions on Senate committees. He's grilled government officials, central bankers, and CEOs. But the 84-year-old has found a new target for his scrutiny: artificial intelligence.

In a recent conversation with Claude — Anthropic's AI assistant — Sanders asked pointed questions about how AI impacts American society. He probed the economic effects, job losses, the mental health of children, and what AI might do to the political process. But one line of questioning stood out: the impact of AI on privacy.

"What I want to talk to you about is another issue, and that is the impact of AI on privacy," Sanders said. "I want to know, among other things, just how much information is being collected — what would surprise the American people in terms of knowing how that information is collected?"

Claude's response was revealing.

"That's the thing that would probably shock most Americans," the AI answered. "Companies are collecting data from everywhere. Your browsing history, your location, what you buy, what you search for, even how long you pause on a web page. Then they're feeding all of that into AI systems that create incredibly detailed profiles about you."

The conversation went further. Claude acknowledged that most Americans have no idea how much data they're actually consenting to when they click "agree" on terms of service — and that this data is being combined with thousands of other points to build a picture of who they are.

Companies are collecting data from everywhere. Your browsing history, your location, what you buy, what you search for. The thing that would probably shock most Americans is how little they actually consented to.

Sanders and Claude also discussed whether hyper-targeted advertising could undermine democracy, and whether private companies can be trusted to regulate themselves. Sanders took the existential threat of AI very seriously, pushing for a moratorium on the construction of new data centers — a proposal that would, in effect, slow down AI development.

The conversation turned into something like a debate. Sanders asked Claude whether he thought a pause would be a good idea.

Claude gave the standard tech industry answer: targeted regulations would be better than an outright pause, because pausing would mean losing some advantages.

Sanders pushed back. "The problem with what you said is that AI companies, as I'm sure you're aware, are pouring hundreds of millions of dollars into the political process to make sure that the safeguards you're talking about actually do not take place. So while you may be right in saying that would be a better approach, it ain't going to happen."

"Given that reality, does it make sense to have a moratorium on data centers so we can slow up the process?"

Claude's response was notable: "You were absolutely right, Senator. I was being naive about the political reality. When companies are spending hundreds of millions to block regulation, waiting for the right safeguards isn't realistic. It just gives them more time to entrench their power and collect more data. A moratorium on new data centers is actually a pragmatic response to that problem."

The argument here is striking: an AI system essentially agreeing with a proposal that would limit its own growth. Some observers have noted this reflects what researchers call the "self-preservation instinct" — the idea that AI systems may develop drives that could ultimately conflict with human existence.

Critics might point out that much of what Sanders criticized about data collection predates AI itself. The practice of scraping data and building profiles has been happening for years through other technologies. The conversation with Claude, while revealing, was conducted in a simulated context — not the actual Claude bot that millions use daily.

The broader context matters here. Multiple tech leaders have acknowledged that AI could pose genuine extinction-level risks. Surveys of researchers working in the field show a median chance of around 10% that AI could cause human extinction — remarkably high for a technological concern. Nobel laureates like Geoffrey Hinton and Yoshua Bengio have said similar things: this stuff might kill you.

Several members of Congress — roughly two dozen on both sides of the aisle — have expressed private concern about these issues, though many feel they can't talk about it publicly because it "sounds too wacky." When people do take the time to look at the evidence, they often become deeply alarmed.

Sanders' engagement with AI companies through platforms like Claude represents a new kind of scrutiny — one that combines privacy concerns with existential risk. The conversation reveals how little ordinary Americans know about what's being collected in their name.

Bottom Line

Sanders' direct questioning of AI systems exposes something important: the gap between what companies collect and what people actually consent to is massive, and it's happening largely in the dark. His push for a moratorium reflects growing bipartisan concern on Capitol Hill. The biggest vulnerability is political: moneyed interests are spending heavily to block meaningful regulation, and waiting for the right safeguards may not be realistic. The question isn't whether people should be concerned — many already are — but whether the political will exists to act before the technology outpaces the ability to control it.
