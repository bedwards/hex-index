Would Socrates Have Valued AI? #404

Andreas Matthias · Daily Philosophy ·Mar 28, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Andreas Matthias doesn't just ask if Socrates would like AI; he argues that the ancient philosopher might have found it to be the first tool capable of restoring the very dialogue that writing destroyed. By reframing Socrates' famous skepticism not as a rejection of technology, but as a demand for interactivity, Matthias turns a 2,400-year-old critique into a startling defense of modern algorithms. This is a perspective you won't find in the usual tech-optimism or Luddite panic, and it demands your attention because it shifts the entire burden of wisdom from the machine to the user.

The Myth of the Silent Scroll

Matthias anchors his argument in Plato's Phaedrus, specifically the myth of Theuth and Thamus. He notes that Socrates viewed writing as "inert or static," a medium that "cannot answer questions, defend itself or adapt to and change the soul of the reader for the better." This is a crucial distinction often lost in modern debates. The author reminds us that Theuth, the inventor of writing, claimed it would be a "remedy for the limits of memory," while the king Thamus countered that it would actually "produce forgetfulness because people will rely on external records instead of meaningful internal recollection."

Matthias argues that Socrates' objection was never about the medium being "new, artificial or non-human," but rather about its inability to engage in elenchus—the disciplined practice of questioning that exposes contradictions. The author writes, "True knowledge, for Socrates, comes from living dialogue, where claims can be tested through questioning." This is a powerful reframing. It suggests that the problem isn't the text itself, but the silence that follows it. A written treatise, no matter how elegant, "addresses everybody and nobody at once," leaving the reader to passively absorb doctrine rather than actively interrogate it.

Where the text falls silent, AI answers back.

The Unexpected Ally

Here is where Matthias makes his boldest move: he posits that AI is not the enemy of Socratic inquiry, but its long-lost partner. He observes that while AI lacks "lived experience and does not possess wisdom in the Socratic sense," it possesses the one thing writing never could: responsiveness. "It revises its formulations in light of objections. It can pursue a line of inquiry over time, remembering earlier claims and returning to them under scrutiny," Matthias explains. This functional capability allows AI to simulate the "midwife" role Socrates claimed for himself, assisting in the birth of ideas rather than delivering them.

The author draws a sharp contrast between the theatrical nature of Plato's written dialogues and the actual engagement AI offers. "A written dialogue may simulate this process between characters, but it remains theatrical. AI, by contrast, can actually engage in dialogue." This argument gains depth when considering the companion topic of Anamnesis (recollection). Socrates believed learning was remembering what the soul already knew, a process triggered by questioning. AI, by adapting its discourse to the individual's confusion, acts as a catalyst for this recollection in a way a static book cannot. As Matthias puts it, "AI functions similarly [to Socrates]. It does not replace thinking, it stimulates it."

Critics might note that attributing this level of agency to a statistical model risks a category error, confusing the appearance of understanding with the reality of it. However, Matthias anticipates this, arguing that the asymmetry of the Socratic relationship means the human must still do the heavy lifting of insight. The machine is merely the mirror.

Psychagogy and the Soul

The commentary deepens when Matthias tackles the concept of psychagogy, or "the leading of souls." In the Phaedrus, Socrates insists that effective rhetoric must be tailored to the hearer's specific disposition and background. Writing fails this test because it is "blind" to the reader's state of mind. AI, however, can "adjust its level of abstraction, reframe explanations and pursue lines of questioning appropriate to the user's interests and capacities." Matthias writes, "In this respect, it comes closer to the ideal of personalized dialectic than any other mass medium in history."

This is a profound observation for our current era of intellectual complacency. The author argues that books often reinforce existing biases, whereas AI can be explicitly instructed to "oppose, critique or problematize a person's positions." By forcing the user to defend their views against a responsive, albeit artificial, interlocutor, AI revives the spirit of dialectic. "Far from encouraging intellectual passivity, it can demand articulation, clarification and defense from its users," Matthias concludes. The tool does not need consciousness to be useful; it only needs to resist finality.

In a world saturated with information but starved for examination, insight and a humane approach toward each other, that alone might have been enough to earn his interest.

Bottom Line

Matthias's strongest move is decoupling Socratic value from human consciousness and reattaching it to functional interactivity, a shift that transforms AI from a content generator into a philosophical sparring partner. The argument's vulnerability lies in the risk that users might mistake the AI's fluency for genuine wisdom, a trap Socrates warned against, though the author rightly insists the burden of truth remains with the human. For the busy thinker, this piece offers a compelling reason to stop fearing the machine and start interrogating it.
