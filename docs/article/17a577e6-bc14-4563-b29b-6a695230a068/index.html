Michael huemer is wrong about AI hype

Bentham's Bulldog · ·Jan 14, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Most debates about artificial intelligence get stuck in a loop of doomsday prophecies versus utopian fantasies, but this piece cuts through the noise with a startlingly specific claim: the technology is not overhyped, it is being hyped for the wrong reasons. Bentham's Bulldog argues that while fears of short-term copyright infringement or electricity usage are largely distractions, the real, underappreciated danger lies in an exponential acceleration of research capabilities that could fundamentally restructure the global economy within a decade. For the busy professional trying to discern signal from noise, this is a crucial pivot away from philosophical anxiety toward hard data on growth rates.

The Math of Exponential Growth

The author begins by dismantling the skepticism of philosopher Michael Huemer, who recently argued that AI is overhyped. Bentham's Bulldog counters that Huemer's view relies on a misunderstanding of how exponential trends work. "The tasks AI can perform consistently have been getting twice as long roughly every seven months," the author notes, highlighting a metric that suggests a rapid shift in capability rather than a plateau. The core of the argument rests on the idea that we are not waiting for a singular breakthrough, but rather witnessing a compounding effect where the number of AI models running simultaneously is increasing at a staggering pace.

Bentham's Bulldog writes, "The number of AI models that can be run has been going up 25x per year. So even if it simply reaches human level and plateaus, it will be as if the population of human researchers is going up 25 times a year." This framing is powerful because it translates abstract computing power into a tangible economic variable: a sudden, massive expansion of the global research workforce. The implication is that this isn't just about faster computers; it is about a fundamental change in the rate at which human knowledge is generated.

Critics might note that extrapolating current trends ignores potential bottlenecks in energy, hardware, or data availability. However, the author anticipates this by pointing out that even if growth slows considerably, the trajectory still points toward a massive disruption. The argument holds weight because it relies on observed data rather than speculative timelines.

"Most people aren't predicting GDP doublings every year. So if GDP doublings are imminent, or AI is going to cause human extinction, then it is currently underhyped."

The Bias of History

A significant portion of Huemer's original argument rests on the historical tendency for humans to overhype technological revolutions, citing past failures like environmental catastrophe predictions. Bentham's Bulldog dismantles this by pointing out that the bias cuts both ways. "Lots of people underestimated Covid, for example, even when it was pretty clear it was going to take over the world," the author observes, reminding us that the human default is often to underestimate the speed of change just as often as we overestimate it.

The commentary suggests that relying on general heuristics about "hype" is a dangerous strategy when specific evidence contradicts them. Bentham's Bulldog argues that unlike previous predictions made in a vacuum, today's AI forecasts are grounded in visible, accelerating metrics. "Our evidence about AI capabilities is sufficiently good and specific that one should analyze specific arguments instead of broad, high-level heuristics," the author asserts. This is a compelling call to move beyond philosophical skepticism and look at the actual performance of the systems.

The author also addresses the counter-argument that early AI pioneers were wrong, noting that predictions from the 1950s are irrelevant to the current landscape. Instead, the focus should be on recent shifts in expert consensus. "Metaculus predictions for when artificial general intelligence will arrive went from 50 years to five years in just a few years," Bentham's Bulldog points out, using this data to show that the timeline for transformative AI is compressing, not expanding.

The Myth of Mere Mimicry

Perhaps the most contentious point addressed is the philosophical objection that AI is merely predicting text sequences without true understanding. Huemer argues that because AI mimics reasoning, it cannot produce genuine breakthroughs. Bentham's Bulldog rejects this distinction as practically irrelevant. "Even if it's just mimicry of true understanding, it can still be useful," the author writes, arguing that the output matters more than the internal mechanism.

The piece draws a striking analogy to human evolution to illustrate this point. "We evolved to reproduce. But it turned out that the best way to do that was to gain all sorts of advanced reasoning capabilities," Bentham's Bulldog explains. Similarly, AI is trained to predict text, but to do so accurately, it must develop a deep, functional understanding of the world. This reframing suggests that the "Chinese Room" argument—a classic philosophical objection to machine intelligence—is a distraction from the practical reality of what the technology can achieve.

Furthermore, the author highlights that modern AI systems are no longer just predicting the next word; they are optimized through reinforcement learning to provide answers that satisfy human users. "It executes a layer of thinking before typing out any text to you," the author notes, emphasizing that the architecture of these systems has evolved to simulate complex reasoning processes. Whether this constitutes "consciousness" is a question for philosophers, but the author insists it is a question of little consequence for the economy.

"Most things on Earth won't blow you up, but this is not a strong argument for standing on a land mine."

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its refusal to get bogged down in the binary of "AI will kill us all" versus "AI is just a fancy chatbot," replacing it with a data-driven case for exponential economic disruption. Its biggest vulnerability is the assumption that current hardware and energy trends will continue uninterrupted, a variable that could easily derail the projected timeline. Readers should watch for how quickly the gap between predicted and actual AI capabilities continues to close, as this metric will likely dictate the pace of the coming transformation.

Michael huemer is wrong about AI hype

by Bentham's Bulldog · · Read full article

Michael Huemer is one of my favorite philosophers. I’ve read every book, paper, and article he has ever written, and enjoyed them all (this is the sort of statement that sounds hyperbolic but is not). However, even very smart and reasonable people are wrong sometimes (for example, I have, upon occasion, been wrong).1 Huemer recently wrote an article called Is AI Over-Hyped? (his conclusion was yes) which I didn’t think was correct. For this reason, I thought it would be worth explaining my disagreement—to answer the question is “Is AI Over-Hyped?” overhyped?

My general view on AI is that it is overall underhyped, but in particular, people are hyping the wrong stuff.2 There’s lots of discussion of short-term harms like AI plagiarizing art, water use, and electricity use. This stuff is mostly bogus. But there is a real, non-trivial chance of near-term AI that will completely upend the world and bring about economic growth at a speed never seen before in human history. Even if timelines are long, so AI comes in 40 years rather than 5, when it comes, it will probably massively upend the world.

Why do I think transformative AI soon is likely? The basic reason is: if you just project that current trends continue, or even if they slow considerably, AI progress balloons through the moon soon. Does balloon through the moon=doom? Foom? I’m skeptical of those predictions, but you don’t need to assume them for AI to massively upend the world.

The tasks AI can perform consistently have been getting twice as long roughly every seven months. Right now they can do tasks that take hours. With a few more doublings, AI will be able to, at will, effectively replace any human researcher. The number of AI models that can be run has been going up 25x per year. So even if it simply reaches human level and plateaus, it will be as if the population of human researchers is going up 25 times a year.

Or, put more concisely: it’s possible to run more AIs performing longer tasks. If these trends continue, then we’ll have a bunch of AIs that can do long tasks, effectively replacing most human researchers. That will radically change the world. So why does Huemer disagree?
