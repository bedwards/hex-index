OpenAI Five Beats World Champion DOTA2 Team 2-0! 🤖

Karoly Zsolnai-Feher · Two Minute Papers ·May 18, 2019 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Karoly Zsolnai-Feher does not merely report on a video game victory; he documents a terrifying inflection point where artificial intelligence stopped playing by human rules and started rewriting the game's fundamental logic. The most startling claim here is not that a machine won, but that it won by making moves that looked like mistakes to the world's best human players, only to reveal them as calculated gambits with a 99% certainty of success. This coverage matters because it proves that in complex, strategic environments, AI is no longer just faster than us—it is operating on a different plane of foresight entirely.

The Confidence of the Machine

Zsolnai-Feher frames the match not as a standard esports event, but as a psychological dismantling of the human ego. He highlights how OpenAI Five, an artificial intelligence developed to play the multiplayer online battle arena game Dota 2, challenged OG, the reigning world champion team, without the pressure of a looming deadline that had hampered previous attempts. "First they didn't just challenge some local team of formidable players no no they flat-out challenged og the reigning world champion team an ambitious move that exudes confidence from their side," Zsolnai-Feher notes. This framing is crucial; it shifts the narrative from a technical demonstration to a bold assertion of dominance. The author effectively uses the contrast between the AI's calm, data-driven confidence and the human team's visible confusion to illustrate the gap in capability.

The commentary dives deep into the AI's unique decision-making process, specifically its liberal use of "buybacks"—a mechanic that resurrects a dead character at a high cost. Humans view this as a desperate measure, but the AI treats it as a standard tactical tool. "It plays unusually aggressively from the get-go and uses buybacks quite liberally at times where human players don't really consider it to be a good choice," the author observes. This is the core of the piece's argument: the AI is not optimizing for the same metrics as humans. It is willing to sacrifice immediate resources for long-term statistical dominance, a strategy that initially baffled the audience. Critics might note that this aggressive style relies on the AI's ability to calculate outcomes in a controlled environment, which may not translate perfectly to the chaotic unpredictability of real-world strategic problems, yet the performance against top-tier humans remains undeniable.

"If you engage with it and it fights you it probably means that you are going to lose that must be quite confusing for the players some mind games for you."

The Illusion of Human Intuition

As the match progresses, Zsolnai-Feher describes a pivotal moment where the AI, despite appearing to be behind, confidently predicted a 95% win probability. "Someone noted that we should perhaps ask the bots what they think about their chances and then the AI said yeah no worries we have a higher than 95% chance to win the game," he recounts. This moment serves as the article's emotional anchor, transforming the bots from mere code into entities with a terrifying self-assurance. The author argues that this confidence is not bluffing; it is the result of millions of self-play games that have taught the AI to recognize winning patterns invisible to the human eye.

The coverage details how the AI executed this prediction with surgical precision, wiping out the human team immediately after their declaration of intent. "Shortly after they won match number one can you believe this this is absolutely amazing," Zsolnai-Feher exclaims, capturing the collective shock of the room. The second game, however, reveals an even more disturbing trend: the AI's ability to bait human players into fatal errors. The author describes a sequence where the AI lost a hero but immediately traded it for two human heroes, a maneuver that looked like a mistake until the numbers revealed it as a deliberate trap. "We wonder whether this was a deliberate bait it pulled on the humans," he suggests, pointing out that the AI's understanding of risk and reward has surpassed human intuition.

The author also touches on the post-match analysis, where human players admitted that the AI's planning in team fights was superior to anything they had ever seen. "In five versus five fights they seem better in planning than any human team is and there is quite a lot to learn from the AI for us humans," Zsolnai-Feher writes. This admission from the losing side is perhaps the most significant evidence of the AI's superiority, as it acknowledges a fundamental gap in strategic depth that cannot be bridged by practice alone.

Beyond the Game

Zsolnai-Feher wisely pivots from the spectacle of the match to the broader implications of the technology. He emphasizes that the ultimate goal is not to create better gamers, but to solve complex, real-world problems. "Dota 2 is a wonderful testbed to see how their AI compares to humans at complex tasks that involve strategy and teamwork however the ultimate goal is to reuse parts of this system for other complex problems outside of video games," he explains. This contextualization is vital, preventing the piece from becoming a mere celebration of a gaming milestone and instead framing it as a breakthrough in general artificial intelligence.

The author concludes by noting the sheer scale of the event, with over 15,000 games played and a 99.4% win rate for the AI. "There are still ways to beat it but given the rate of progress of this project likely not for long," he warns. This final thought leaves the reader with a sense of urgency and awe. The argument is that we are witnessing the dawn of an era where machines can out-think us in domains requiring long-term planning and teamwork, and the implications for fields like logistics, finance, and science are profound.

"It is inevitable that this AI will become unbeatable at some point."

Bottom Line

Zsolnai-Feher's coverage succeeds by focusing less on the mechanics of the game and more on the psychological and strategic shockwaves of the AI's performance. The strongest part of the argument is the evidence that the AI is not just playing better, but playing a fundamentally different game based on a deeper understanding of probability and risk. The biggest vulnerability is the assumption that these specific strategic gains will translate seamlessly to non-gaming environments, a leap that remains to be proven. Readers should watch for the release of the full research paper to see if the "baiting" strategies observed here can be generalized to solve complex real-world coordination problems.
