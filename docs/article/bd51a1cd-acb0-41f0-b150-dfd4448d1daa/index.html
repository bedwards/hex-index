Active Neutrality in the Middle East – Chinese Commentary on the US-Iran war

Various · Sinification ·Mar 8, 2026 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In a landscape of predictable geopolitical analysis, this curated collection of Chinese expert commentary on the 2026 US-Iran war offers a jarring, necessary contradiction: while officially condemning the strikes, Beijing's intellectual elite is quietly admiring American military precision. The piece is notable not for what it says about Iran, but for what it reveals about China's own strategic anxiety—a realization that the "rules-based order" is dead, replaced by a Hobbesian reality where moral restraint is a liability.

The Death of Moral Restraint

The authors make a startling admission that cuts against the grain of China's traditional diplomatic posture. Zheng Yongnian, one of the country's most prominent public intellectuals, argues that the international system has shifted into a "fear-based international order" that China must reckon with rather than merely deplore. This is a profound departure from the standard rhetoric of multilateralism. The commentary suggests that Beijing is no longer interested in debating the legality of the strikes as much as it is analyzing the efficacy of the US response.

"Not using it is equivalent to not having it."

Zheng Yongnian delivers this blunt assessment regarding China's failure to leverage its own technological and military capabilities. He warns against the "trap of excessive moralisation and self-restraint," invoking a historical analogy where China invented gunpowder but used it for fireworks while the West turned it into cannons. This framing is effective because it strips away the diplomatic veneer to expose a raw, realist calculation: in a world where the US can execute a targeted killing of a head of state with surgical precision, moral high ground is a strategic weakness. Critics might note that this view dangerously underestimates the diplomatic capital China has built through its "non-interference" principle, which has been its primary shield in the Global South.

The Israeli Proxy and the Offshore Balancing Act

The text navigates a complex tension regarding who is actually driving the conflict. While the official Chinese narrative blames the US, several authors argue that Israel has achieved "effective command over US military assets." Shen Yi, a professor at Fudan University, puts it most bluntly, suggesting that Washington is acting as a proxy for Tel Aviv. This perspective allows Chinese analysts to maintain a distinction between the American state and its allies, preserving a sliver of hope for future US-China engagement.

"The US might replicate this model in East Asia—with Japan playing Israel's role."

Zheng Yongnian extends this logic to a terrifying conclusion for Beijing: the "offshore balancing" strategy used in the Middle East could be applied to the Indo-Pacific. He warns that Japan, Taiwan, and the Philippines could serve as the new instruments of containment, mirroring Israel's role in Iran. This is the piece's most chilling insight. It reframes the conflict not as a regional dispute, but as a template for a global strategy that China must now prepare to counter. The authors' ability to see the Middle East as a rehearsal for East Asia adds a layer of urgency that is often missing from Western analysis.

The Conditional Future

Perhaps the most sophisticated argument in the corpus comes from Zheng Ge, who refuses to predict a winner. Instead, he frames the strategic outcome as entirely dependent on the war's conclusion. The logic is binary and stark: if the US succeeds in reshaping the Middle East, it reverses China's narrative of American decline; if the US gets bogged down, it accelerates that decline.

"If the US succeeds in reshaping the Middle East order, it could reverse China's vision of gradually declining American hegemony."

Zheng Ge's analysis highlights that China's biggest fear is not the war itself, but a US victory that frees up American resources to focus on the Asia-Pacific. This is a nuanced take that avoids the wishful thinking common in nationalist circles. While many authors predict a US quagmire, the text acknowledges the possibility that a successful political realignment in the Middle East could actually strengthen US regional dominance. This uncertainty is the central tension of the commentary, reflecting a deep anxiety that China's strategy of waiting for American overextension might be a gamble that fails.

"The strategic outcome for China is entirely conditional on how the war ends."

Bottom Line

This collection of commentary is a masterclass in strategic realism, stripping away diplomatic niceties to reveal a China that is simultaneously condemning US actions and studying them with intense admiration. Its greatest strength is the unflinching admission that moral posturing is no longer a viable strategy in a Hobbesian world. However, its biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that the US will inevitably overextend; if Washington manages a swift, decisive victory in Iran, China's entire strategic calculus regarding American decline could collapse overnight.

Executive Summary

  1. Chinese expert commentary largely recommends pursuing a position of neutrality and mediation in the Iran-US war, with only two senior academics making subtle arguments for a more assertive posture.

  2. Zheng Yongnian writes that Beijing should “demonstrate the strength and policies befitting a great power”.

  3. Zheng Ge claims that the sustainability of China’s “active neutrality” depends on the war’s direction, noting that meaningful mediation “requires China to transcend its traditional ‘non-interference’ principle”.

  4. Legal condemnation of the strikes coexists with something closer to strategic respect, with several authors arguing that China must learn from this demonstration of American power.

  5. Several authors read the operation through the lens of US “offshore balancing”, with one warning that the Middle East template could be replicated in East Asia with Japan playing Israel’s role.

  6. Others view the conflict as a strategic opportunity for China, with several predicting that the US will become entangled in the Middle East.

  7. Authors broadly agree that the strategic implications for China hinge on how the war ends: US success would challenge China’s vision of declining American hegemony, while a quagmire would accelerate it.

  8. Commentary is sceptical that regime change is achievable without ground troops—a red line all authors agree Trump will not cross.

  9. Most analysts view US-Iran nuclear negotiations as cover for a premeditated attack—though many of the same analysts simultaneously portray the war as primarily Israel-driven.

  10. One author argues that China’s overriding priority should be preventing the Iran crisis from spilling over into the US-China bilateral agenda.
