This is the Loheed Martin F35. It is designed with 418 kg of rare earths which are embedded in its weapons, lasers, and engine. An Arley Burke destroyer needs about 2600 kg while a Virginia class submarine uses nearly 4600. Different systems, but they all lean on the same elements.

Neodymium, preodmium, samarium, dsproium, and turbium. To most people, these names mean little, but to defense technologies, they mean everything. And yet, nearly all of these elements trace back to China, the undisputed leader in rare earth supply chains. Around 85% of rare earths are refined there, and the country produces more than 90% of the world's rare earth magnets.

Automakers, aerospace manufacturers, semiconductor companies, and military contractors around the globe rely on these metals. Without them, most modern devices would either fail to function or operate far less efficiently. For the defense sector, the risks are even greater because in some shape or form, the world's strongest military now rests on its rival supply chain.
