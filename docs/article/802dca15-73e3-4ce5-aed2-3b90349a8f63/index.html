Nuclear Fusion: Revolutionary new breakthrough

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Mar 22, 2020 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through decades of fusion fatigue by spotlighting a radical departure from the massive, superheated magnets that have defined the field. He argues that an Australian firm, HB 11 Energy, may have finally cracked the code not by building bigger, but by getting smarter, claiming a reaction rate a billion times higher than predicted without the need for extreme heat. This is the hook for anyone tired of the "twenty years away" cliché: a potential path to clean energy that fits in a city rather than a desert.

The Magnet Trap

Borlace begins by dismantling the prevailing narrative of nuclear fusion, which has long been dominated by two massive approaches: the tokamak and the stellarator. He describes the Wendelstein 7-X in Germany, a machine so complex it required supercomputers to design and precision robots to weld, noting that "the idea behind these incredibly intricate twists and turns is to overcome a problem encountered in these standard tokamak Donuts." The author effectively illustrates the physical limitations of magnetic confinement, where imbalances in magnetic fields cause particles to drift and crash into the reactor walls, ending the reaction.

The core of Borlace's critique is that these traditional methods rely on "brute force of enormous energy inputs to raise temperatures to levels that are about seven times higher than the center of the Sun." He points out the fundamental economic flaw: "reaching and maintaining those conditions generally requires more energy than the reaction itself generates." While he acknowledges that the International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor (ITER) and China's EAST device have achieved fleeting moments of success, he rightly frames them as "some way off providing our civilization with the ultimate solution."

Critics might note that dismissing the decades of work on tokamaks and stellarators as mere "brute force" overlooks the essential physics lessons learned from these failures, which are likely prerequisites for any future success. However, Borlace's framing is effective because it highlights the urgent need for a paradigm shift rather than just incremental improvements.

The enormous energy savings could be a game-changer.

The Laser Alternative

The commentary pivots sharply to HB 11 Energy, a company led by Emeritus Professor Heinrich Hora, which Borlace presents as the potential disruptor. Unlike the magnetic confinement models that heat plasma to millions of degrees, this approach uses lasers to trigger a non-thermal fusion reaction between hydrogen and boron. Borlace explains that the company is "sidestepping all the scientific challenges that have held fusion energy back for more than half a century" by avoiding the need to create a superheated soup of plasma.

He details the mechanism with clarity: two lasers are used, one to establish a magnetic field and the other to trigger an "avalanche fusion chain reactor." The result is the creation of helium atoms that are "naked," meaning they lack electrons and carry a positive charge. Borlace notes the significance of this: "the helium atoms have a two charge which HB 11's machine harnesses to create a current that can be used directly." This bypasses the inefficient step of using heat to boil water and drive steam turbines, a process that defines both current nuclear fission and fossil fuel plants.

The author emphasizes the role of Chirped Pulse Amplification (CPA), the Nobel Prize-winning technology that allows lasers to reach petawatt levels of power. He writes, "it's only possible now because the brand-new CPA lasers are capable of doing it," suggesting that the physics was understood decades ago but the tools were simply not ready. This is a compelling argument for why this specific breakthrough is happening now rather than in the 1970s when Hora first theorized it.

We are not using the laser to heat things up to crazy temperatures; we are using the laser to massively accelerate the hydrogen through the boron sample using nonlinear force.

The Reality Check

Despite the excitement, Borlace grounds his enthusiasm in a healthy dose of skepticism regarding the timeline. He quotes Dr. Warren McKenzie, the managing director of HB 11, who warns against the hubris of setting arbitrary deadlines. Borlace writes, "I don't want to be a laughingstock by promising we can deliver something in 10 years," a sentiment that resonates deeply in a field plagued by overpromising.

The path forward is described as a series of milestones: first demonstrating the reaction, then proving an energy gain by counting the helium output, and finally engineering a working reactor concept. Borlace acknowledges that while the simulation results are "returning reaction rates a billion times higher than predicted," the transition from simulation to a physical, net-positive energy reactor remains the "elusive prize."

A counterargument worth considering is that the "nonlinear force" and "avalanche" effects described in simulations may not scale linearly in a real-world environment where material constraints and laser precision introduce new variables. Yet, Borlace's balanced approach—celebrating the theoretical breakthrough while respecting the engineering hurdles—makes the piece credible rather than purely promotional.

The time line question is a tricky one.

Bottom Line

Borlace's strongest contribution is reframing nuclear fusion not as a problem of scale, but of method, offering a plausible route to compact, waste-free energy that bypasses the thermal inefficiencies of the past. The argument's biggest vulnerability remains the unproven nature of scaling the HB 11 concept from a laboratory experiment to a commercial power source, a gap that history suggests will take far longer than a decade to bridge. Readers should watch for the upcoming demonstration of net energy gain, which will be the true litmus test for this revolutionary claim.
