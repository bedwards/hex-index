Which planets in Warhammer 40k could really exist?

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Jul 1, 2020 · 26 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis doesn't just ask if Warhammer 40,000's nightmare worlds are possible; he asks if they are plausible under the strict, unyielding laws of astrophysics, stripping away the magic to reveal the terrifying science beneath. By applying a PhD in atmospheric physics to a setting defined by gothic excess, Francis transforms a fan theory into a rigorous stress test of planetary science, proving that even in the grim darkness of the far future, physics still demands an answer.

The Physics of a Dying Earth

Francis begins with the most familiar horror: Terra itself. He argues that the canonical description of a waterless, desiccated Earth is scientifically catastrophic unless massive intervention occurred. "If you were to remove all water from Earth's hydrosphere things would go very badly," Francis writes, noting that water is not just a resource but the planet's primary thermostat. He explains that without oceans to store thermal energy or atmospheric vapor to trap heat, the planet would swing between freezing nights and scorching days, eventually suffocating as the biosphere collapses.

The author's assessment is that Terra's current state is only possible through "huge amounts of arcane technology reshaping the atmosphere." This is a crucial distinction. Francis isn't saying the world is impossible; he is saying it requires a level of geoengineering that borders on the miraculous. He suggests that for Terra to exist as described, humanity must have spent millennia pumping oxygen and artificial greenhouse gases into the air to counteract the loss of natural moderating influences. This reframes the Imperium not just as a military machine, but as a desperate planetary life-support system.

"Terra is a nightmare world kept alive by huge amounts of arcane technology reshaping the atmosphere."

Critics might argue that the sheer scale of this engineering makes the scenario less about physics and more about plot convenience, but Francis wisely leans into the "arcane technology" aspect of the setting to bridge the gap. He acknowledges that while the loss of water is a death sentence for a natural Earth, the Imperium's refusal to accept natural limits makes the scenario a grim possibility.

Darkness, Smog, and the Red Giant

Moving from a dry Earth to a frozen one, Francis tackles Nostromo, a world shrouded in toxic smog orbiting a dying star. He identifies the star as a likely red giant—a star that has expanded and cooled, emitting less energy than our Sun. "Nostromo may have started off like earth but could certainly have become dimmer and colder," Francis notes. The real scientific weight, however, lies in the atmosphere. He draws a parallel to volcanic eruptions on Earth, where sulfur dioxide reflects sunlight and causes cooling, arguing that a permanent, dense layer of industrial pollutants could replicate this effect on a global scale.

The author's analysis of radiation is particularly sharp. He distinguishes between short-wavelength radiation from the star and long-wavelength radiation emitted by the planet, explaining how different atmospheric components trap or reflect these energies. "If Nostromo had, for example, a low stratosphere and so could keep a large density of pollutants in the atmosphere at all times then it can definitely largely block out the Sun and cause very dark cold world," he writes. This is a compelling application of real-world climate science to a fictional hellscape, grounding the lore in observable atmospheric dynamics.

The Tidal Locking Paradox

Perhaps the most rigorous section of the commentary concerns Mauryan, a tidally locked world where one side faces the sun eternally while the other faces the void. Francis points out a glaring error in the source material: the claim that such a planet has no Coriolis force and therefore no wind. "That's just plain wrong," he asserts, explaining that tidal locking requires rotation, which inevitably generates atmospheric dynamics. He clarifies that while the rotation period matches the orbital period, the planet is not stationary.

Francis uses this correction to highlight a fascinating atmospheric phenomenon: a temperate habitable band existing between the scorching day side and the freezing night side. "Research suggests that tidally locked planets around bright stars could have strong convection and cloud formation which would lessen the difference between the day and the night sides," he writes. This insight transforms Mauryan from a simple binary of hot and cold into a complex system of wind and cloud cover that could support life in a narrow strip. The author's willingness to correct the source material while still finding a way for the planet to exist demonstrates a deep respect for both the setting and the science.

"This planet is so cool that we're inclined to give Games Workshop two writers a pass on this one... but also because of another thing on the wiki page where they were actually spot-on."

The Impossible Impact

The analysis takes a hard turn toward the impossible with Valhalla, a frozen world supposedly knocked out of the habitable zone by a comet impact. Francis dismantles this narrative with cold, hard math. He calculates that to move a planet like Earth to the outer edge of the Goldilocks zone would require an impactor of staggering proportions. "It would need to be around 12,000 kilometers across... that's more than ten times the size of the largest asteroid we've yet observed Ceres," Francis writes.

He further notes that the lore describes an "iron-rich comet," which is a contradiction in terms, as comets are typically icy while asteroids are rocky. The energy required to shift a planet's orbit is so immense that no known celestial body in our solar system could deliver it without destroying the planet entirely. "Perhaps Valhalla is a smaller planet orbiting a less massive Sun but for the reasons that will come active later in the video even then an impact of sufficient ferocity wouldn't have the effect of knocking Valhalla into a new orbit," he concludes. This section serves as a reminder that while fiction can bend reality, physics has a breaking point that even the Imperium cannot ignore.

"Planets can be flung to new orbits or out of their star systems entirely by gravitational interactions... but Valhalla's specific backstory just doesn't add up."

Bottom Line

Francis's greatest strength is his ability to treat the absurd with absolute seriousness, using the rigorous framework of planetary science to validate the most outlandish elements of Warhammer 40,000. His biggest vulnerability lies in the few instances where the lore's internal logic is simply too broken to be fixed by physics, as seen with Valhalla. For the busy reader, this piece offers a rare thrill: the realization that the universe of 40k is not just a fantasy, but a collection of extreme, yet theoretically possible, astrophysical scenarios waiting to be discovered.
