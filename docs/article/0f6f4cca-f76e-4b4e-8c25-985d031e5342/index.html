Injecting Cocaine Into Your Spine

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Jun 25, 2021 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis does something rare in medical history: he takes a century-old archive of self-experimentation and reveals it not as a triumph of science, but as a grotesque comedy of errors driven by addiction and arrogance. By juxtaposing the dry, clinical prose of 1898 German physicians with the manic energy of an early American surgeon, Francis exposes how the very foundation of modern anesthesia was built on a drug that was simultaneously a miracle cure and a poison.

The Theater of Self-Experimentation

Francis frames the historical record through a biting satire of British imperial superiority, mocking the "sterling fortune" of Sir William Osler while he tolerates the "uncouth" American Dr. William Halsted. The author highlights a disturbing reality: Halsted, a founding father of American surgery, was a cocaine addict who "insisted on bringing with him one of those uncouth americans... who by jove is a ruddy addict." This isn't just gossip; it is the lens through which Francis views the birth of modern surgical rigor. He argues that the notorious 5 AM ward rounds and grueling residency hours of today are a direct legacy of Halsted's "manic nature and work ethic driven in part by cocaine."

The piece then pivots to the German contribution, specifically Dr. August Bier, who proposed the radical idea of injecting cocaine directly into the spinal cord. Francis notes that Bier's initial subjects included a tuberculosis-ridden laborer and a young baker, both of whom suffered "crashing depression and severe headache" after their surgeries. The author writes, "I shall read directly... after administration he felt nothing in the lower half of his body and I sawed off his leg and he felt no pain." The horror lies in the casual delivery of such violence. Francis points out that Bier, unsatisfied with patient data, decided to "perform some cocaine investigations upon myself," eventually recruiting an assistant, Dr. Hildebrand, to undergo the same torture.

"The study was a great success and its performance on a well-informed physician resulted in an excellent account of the action of cocaine on the spinal cord."

Francis uses the transcript of Hildebrand's self-inflicted ordeal to illustrate the absurdity of the era's safety standards. The text describes a man having his legs struck with an iron hammer and his testicles crushed with forceps, all while the observer notes, "strong pressure and traction on the testicles was not painful." Francis observes that despite this "excellent account," the aftermath was catastrophic: Hildebrand developed "posies" (pus-filled sores) and a headache so severe he could barely function, yet Bier ordered him to operate on patients anyway. The author's commentary here is sharp: "These germans are an absolute riot to waste all that good cocaine on an assistant surgeon." It underscores a systemic blindness to suffering in the pursuit of medical breakthrough.

The Myth of Safety and the Rise of Addiction

Moving beyond the operating room, Francis traces the commercial explosion of cocaine, noting how the German pharmaceutical giant Merck scaled production from 50 grams in the 1870s to over 83,000 kilograms by 1885. The author highlights the bizarre marketing of the time, where cocaine was sold as a cure for morphine addiction and a treatment for depression. "The U.S. surgeon general recommended that cocaine be used to treat depression claiming that there was no such thing as cocaine addiction," Francis writes, a claim that sounds almost comical to the modern ear but was deadly serious policy.

He connects this historical naivety to the present day, warning that cocaine remains a potent cardiovascular threat. "Cocaine is not advisable," he states, explaining that it causes blood vessels to "spasm and kind of clamp shut" while making blood "more sticky," leading to heart attacks in healthy young people. This section serves as a stark counterpoint to the historical narrative, grounding the reader in the biological reality that the 19th-century doctors ignored. Critics might note that Francis's transition from historical satire to a direct health warning feels slightly abrupt, but it effectively prevents the audience from romanticizing the era's "adventures."

The commentary also touches on the cultural ubiquity of the drug, mentioning how Coca-Cola originally contained cocaine and how Sigmund Freud wrote a 70-page document extolling its virtues. Francis notes that Freud's friend, Dr. Koller, realized its utility as a topical anesthetic for the eye, a discovery that reduced bleeding and pain. Yet, the author reminds us that this medical utility came at a high cost. "I literally wrote the book on recreational drugs and the heart," Francis asserts, lending weight to his warning that the drug's benefits were vastly outweighed by its potential for harm.

"German medicine is like German automobiles unlikely to last British manufacturing now that will last forever oh germans they really have no ambition."

This quote, delivered in the voice of the satirical British narrator, captures the arrogance of the era. Francis uses it to illustrate how medical progress was often stifled by nationalistic pride and a refusal to learn from the failures of others. The narrative suggests that the "meek country" of Germany, despite its scientific rigor, was dismissed by the British, who believed their own methods were superior. This dismissal, Francis implies, delayed the understanding of the true risks of spinal anesthesia and cocaine addiction.

Bottom Line

Francis's greatest strength is his ability to use humor to dissect a dark chapter of medical history, making the grotesque self-experimentation of the 1890s both accessible and horrifying. His argument that modern medical culture's grueling hours are a byproduct of a surgeon's drug addiction is a compelling, if provocative, historical thesis. However, the piece occasionally risks letting the satire overshadow the genuine tragedy of the patients and physicians who suffered from the lack of safety protocols. The reader is left with a clear verdict: the pursuit of medical innovation without ethical guardrails is not just dangerous, it is a recipe for disaster that we are still paying the price for today.
