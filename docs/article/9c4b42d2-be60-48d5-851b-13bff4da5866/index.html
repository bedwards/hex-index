These Aren’t Laws

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Sep 9, 2020 · 18 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just debunk legal myths; he exposes how pop culture has rewritten the rulebook for millions of Americans. In a world where courtroom dramas and crime procedurals dominate our entertainment, Stone argues that our collective understanding of the law is dangerously detached from reality. His most striking claim isn't just that we are wrong, but that our confidence in these errors prevents us from protecting our rights when it matters most.

The Theater of Justice

Stone begins by dismantling the idea that the law consists of hard and fast rules, noting that "if you ask a lawyer a legal question the answer is usually it depends because it usually depends." This admission of complexity is the foundation of his entire argument: the law is fluid, not a set of rigid commandments. He tackles the myth of cousin marriage first, revealing that it is legal in over half the United States, a fact that surprises even those who assume they know the boundaries of family law. As Stone puts it, "cousin marriage has always been popular around the world gross but for hundreds of years it was considered a relatively good bet for a long-lasting union."

The commentary here is sharp because it highlights how cultural prejudice, rather than legal logic, often drives these misconceptions. Stone notes that in America, the practice was stigmatized partly because it was associated with the poor, whereas in England, it remained acceptable among the aristocracy. This historical context adds necessary depth to a simple "true or false" question. However, critics might note that while the law permits it, the genetic risks remain a valid public health concern that Stone glosses over in favor of the legal technicality.

Next, Stone addresses the cinematic trope of the "reading of the will." He writes, "there is no legal requirement that a last will and testament be read aloud to family members or anyone else for that matter." This is a crucial distinction for anyone navigating the grief of losing a loved one. The drama of the family gathering is, as Stone explains, a fabrication designed for soap operas: "it's a great way to create dramatic tension and there's a lot riding on this dead person's estate." In reality, beneficiaries simply receive copies of the document. The author uses the Trump family estate battle to illustrate the real-world consequences of will contests, showing how the lack of a dramatic reading can actually lead to prolonged legal warfare over asset valuation.

The Casino and The Street

The piece shifts to the high-stakes environment of gambling, where Stone debunks the idea that card counting is a crime. He asserts, "counting cards while playing blackjack though widely considered to be illegal is not really you're just playing the game that the casino is offering at a much higher level than they're expecting." This is a powerful reminder that using one's brain is not illegal, even if it upsets the house. Stone clarifies that while casinos in Nevada can ban players for counting cards because they are private property, there is no law against the act itself. As he dryly observes, "there's even a court case making it legal at least in new jersey because everything is legal in new jersey."

The most urgent correction Stone offers concerns missing persons. He dismantles the pervasive myth that police require a 24-hour waiting period before investigating. "A lot of people think that if a person is missing you have to wait 24 hours to report it to the authorities but in reality there is no state or federal law requiring police departments to wait 24 hours before investigating a missing person's case." Stone cites the Suzanne's Law and the Protect Act to show that for minors and young adults, immediate reporting is not just allowed but mandated. He connects this to the tragic case of Suzanne Lyall, whose disappearance was initially dismissed by authorities who believed the "waiting period" myth. This is the most life-saving information in the piece, transforming a legal technicality into a matter of immediate safety.

You can be convicted or found not guilty based on circumstantial evidence alone but that brings us to the next legal misconception which is that everybody knows that if you're doing something illegal and you think you might be interacting with an undercover cop you should ask them if they are a police officer because everyone knows police can't lie to you false.

The Weight of Evidence

Finally, Stone tackles the misconception that circumstantial evidence is too weak to secure a conviction. He explains that the standard is "beyond a reasonable doubt," which can be met entirely through indirect evidence. He uses the Casey Anthony trial to illustrate how a jury can weigh circumstantial clues—like internet searches and chemical traces—and still reach a verdict that defies public intuition. Stone writes, "the prosecution can prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt by building a case through direct evidence circumstantial evidence or a combination of both." The argument here is that the law relies on inference, not just eyewitness testimony, a nuance that is often lost in the court of public opinion.

The piece concludes with a final, jarring correction: police are legally allowed to lie to suspects. Stone notes that the idea that "police can't lie to you" is false, a fact that fundamentally changes how one should interact with law enforcement. While he cuts off before fully expanding on the legal precedents for police deception, the implication is clear: the rules of engagement are far more permissive for officers than the average citizen realizes.

Bottom Line

Stone's strongest asset is his ability to translate dense legal statutes into relatable, life-or-death realities, particularly regarding missing persons and police deception. The argument's only vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that viewers will apply these corrections to their own lives rather than treating them as trivia. Ultimately, this is not just a myth-busting exercise; it is a necessary update to the public's operating system for navigating the legal world.
