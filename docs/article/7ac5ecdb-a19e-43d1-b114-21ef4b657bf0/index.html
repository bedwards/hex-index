The Twins who Terrorized Physicists with a Sock Puppet Army

Commentary by Brian Edwards

BobbyBroccoli uncovers a bizarre chapter in scientific history where two French twins didn't just fake their credentials; they weaponized the internet to gaslight the entire physics community. This isn't merely a story of academic fraud, but a masterclass in how bad actors can manipulate public perception when they refuse to engage in good faith. The author's forensic breakdown of the twins' sock puppet army reveals a level of deception that transforms a simple scandal into a surreal revenge feud.

The Art of the Mistranslation

The narrative begins by exposing how the Bogdanoff twins, Igor and Grichka, systematically distorted the words of their critics to manufacture false support. BobbyBroccoli highlights a particularly egregious example involving physicist Peter White, noting, "his sentence it C possible that you have some new worthwhile results on Quantum groups got translated into the following certainly possible became absolutely certain a polite sendoff had transformed into a glowing review." This manipulation wasn't accidental; it was a calculated strategy to turn polite skepticism into glowing endorsements. The author effectively demonstrates that the twins treated language as a malleable tool rather than a medium for truth.

The pattern extends to other critics, where the twins altered the meaning of their statements to suggest their work was valid. BobbyBroccoli writes, "John D Georges had a quote mistranslated as well incomprehensible articles cannot pass through such a procedure and certainly not five times now if I read that quote out of context I'd assume he was defending their papers." By flipping "cannot pass" to "not supposed to pass," the twins created a narrative where their papers had somehow survived a rigorous review process. This framing is particularly effective because it relies on the reader's assumption that scientific journals have high barriers to entry, a barrier the twins had clearly bypassed through deception.

It's one thing to mistranslate the words of your critics and have them call you out of course they'd be mad but it's another thing entirely to get called out by people who once defended your work.

Critics might argue that the twins' poor command of English could explain these errors, but BobbyBroccoli dismantles this defense by pointing out the specificity of the changes. The author notes that even committee members like Roman Jackie and Sha Majid flagged the translations as "non-authorized and a fabrication." The evidence suggests that the twins were not merely clumsy translators but active architects of a false reality.

The Golden Ratio Debacle

The commentary then shifts to the twins' technical incompetence, specifically their claim that the golden ratio is a transcendental number. BobbyBroccoli explains that this is mathematically impossible, stating, "the golden ratio isn't actually a transcendental number because it's the solution to this equation therefore making it an a rational number." When challenged, the twins doubled down, first with convoluted proofs and then with a claim that they meant the word in a "common usage" sense. The author finds this defense absurd, asking, "I mean I can think of a few other adjectives you could have used that aren't already mathematical terms like legendary Universal iconic like any of those would have been fine."

The most damning evidence of their dishonesty came when they attempted to prove they had used quotation marks in their draft. BobbyBroccoli writes, "ybm again going the extra mile here pointed out that if you viewed the pictures in an image editor Igor i' left in clear signs of photoshopping the spacing around the quotation marks are out of whack and you can actually see part of the letter T is cut off by the rectangular Snipping Tool." This moment crystallizes the twins' approach: when caught, they don't admit fault; they fabricate evidence to cover their tracks. The author's analysis of this Photoshop fail serves as a microcosm for the entire scandal.

The Sock Puppet Army

The story escalates from academic dishonesty to a coordinated online harassment campaign. BobbyBroccoli details how the twins created a legion of fake personas to defend their work and attack critics. The author identifies specific patterns in these fake accounts, noting, "the three giveaways tend to be a grammar mistakes characteristic of a French speaker B enthusias fantastic praise for the bov's work and that everyone else clearly just hasn't read the papers and C long detailed replies where they nitpick every sentence of a critique." This systematic approach reveals a level of organization that goes beyond simple trolling.

The investigation into the IP addresses of these accounts exposes the twins' location and identity. BobbyBroccoli writes, "bizarre isn't it that an email ending with the domain for Hong Kong appears to be coming from a want too subscriber in Paris huh maybe that's why Dr Yang's speech is full of grammatical errors that are out of place for a Hong Kong native but common for a native French speaker." The author uses this evidence to show that the "International Institute of mathematical physics" was a complete fabrication, with the twins operating out of their own apartments in Paris.

It's fairly easy to spot a boggin off sock puppet purely from the writing style the three giveaways tend to be a grammar mistakes characteristic of a French speaker B enthusias fantastic praise for the bov's work and that everyone else clearly just hasn't read the papers and C long detailed replies where they nitpick every sentence of a critique.

The twins' campaign even extended to Wikipedia, where they engaged in a massive edit war to control their narrative. BobbyBroccoli notes that the situation became so severe that "Wikipedia's arbitration committee had to get involved... effectively Wikipedia's Supreme Court." The fact that the committee had to ban the twins and their sock puppets from editing their own page underscores the extent of their disruption. The author's coverage of this digital battleground highlights how the twins turned the internet into a weapon, using anonymity to amplify their nonsense.

Bottom Line

BobbyBroccoli's account is a compelling dissection of how fraud can metastasize when fueled by ego and a lack of accountability. The strongest part of the argument is the forensic evidence of the sock puppet army, which proves the twins' intent to deceive rather than just their incompetence. The biggest vulnerability in the twins' defense was their refusal to engage with critics in good faith, a strategy that ultimately backfired by exposing their dishonesty to the world. Readers should watch for how similar tactics of disinformation are used in other fields, as the Bogdanoff twins' playbook remains eerily relevant today.
