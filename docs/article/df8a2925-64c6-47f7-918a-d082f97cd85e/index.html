It's 2026, which means it's time to talk about project 2025 again. When the Heritage Foundation published its conservative blueprint in 2024, the Trump campaign jumped to distance itself from the 900page manifesto. Trump surrogate Howard Lutnik told the New York Post that no one associated with Project 2025 would come anywhere near the Trump White House. They were radioactive.

A year later, Lutnik is Secretary of Commerce, and he works alongside a whole castle of those supposedly banned Project 2025 guys. So, how much of the manifesto did these people operationalize in year 1? A lot. The website project 2025.observer estimates that the Trump administration has already carried out about 50% of the documents goals.

Hooray. And they couldn't have done it without a big assist from the Supreme Court. Thanks, Chief Justice Roberts. Okay, we're going to break it all down, but first, let's talk about the one weird trick that made it all possible.

The authors of Project 2025 have a lot of political and cultural goals. They want to criminalize abortion, end gay marriage, do away with environmental regulations, and lower taxes for corporations and billionaires, but most Americans are not down with that. Polls consistently show broad support for abortion rights, LGBTQ equality, protecting the environment, and funding the government, particularly when that involves rich people paying their fair share. Clearly, the Project 2025 agenda was never going to get passed by democratic means.

And so, the authors pinned their hopes on undemocratic means. Specifically, they sketched out an agenda for a president with sweeping powers, effectively unbound by the law. And the law that they most want the president not to be bound by is the constitution spending clause which says that no money shall be drawn from the treasury but in consequence of appropriations made by law. That is you pay your taxes into the US treasury >> and all of us have to pay our share >> and Congress tells the executive branch how to spend the money.

>> Our taxes must provide the necessary funds to improve and expand our school system to pay for city streets. >> That's what the federal budget is. It's a law passed by Congress telling the executive branch what to do with your tax dollars. >> None of us can escape.
