Henry Vaughan

Close Reading Poetry · Close Reading Poetry ·Jan 17, 2024 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a landscape often dominated by secular interpretations of poetry, Close Reading Poetry makes a striking claim: Henry Vaughan's "The Retreat" is not merely a nostalgic look at childhood, but a sophisticated theological argument that fuses Christian typology with Neoplatonic philosophy to argue for the soul's pre-existence. While many readers might mistake the poem for a simple lament about lost innocence, the author reveals how Vaughan uses obscure biblical imagery and hermetic thought to construct a radical vision of spiritual regression as the only path to salvation. This is not just literary analysis; it is a guide to decoding a worldview where the material world is a distraction and the past is the future.

The Metaphysics of Memory

The author immediately grounds the analysis in the poem's unique philosophical DNA, distinguishing Vaughan from his more famous predecessor, George Herbert. Close Reading Poetry writes, "Henry Vaughan goes a bit further and incorporates neoplatonic and hermetic philosophy and we'll see a little bit of that popping up in the retreat." This distinction is crucial because it shifts the reader's expectation from a standard Anglican devotional to something more esoteric and universal. The author notes that while Herbert relies on the liturgy and sacraments of the Church of England, Vaughan taps into the idea of the "preexistence of the soul," a concept not formally taught by Orthodox Christianity but central to the poem's emotional weight.

The commentary effectively unpacks the poem's opening lines, explaining how the meter itself mimics the speaker's emotional state. The author points out the shift from a trochaic start to iambic rhythm, noting that this pattern "has the effect of Leaping happy those early days signaling towards a sense of excitement." This technical observation supports the broader argument that the poem's form mirrors its content: a soul trying to break free from the constraints of earthly time. By connecting the phrase "second race" to both the Greek Olympic metaphor for life and the hermetic idea of earthly existence, the author clarifies a confusing line that often trips up modern readers.

"The shadows of the world, the book of nature, is prefiguring eternal truths in what is true of Eternity."

Typology and the Book of Nature

A significant portion of the coverage focuses on how Vaughan reads the natural world through a biblical lens. The author explains that Vaughan sees nature not as an end in itself, but as a series of "Shadows of Eternity." Close Reading Poetry writes, "Here he's reading it and what's he reading he's seeing glimpses of his My First Love implicitly God his bright face within the Gilded cloud or flower." This interpretation of typology—the method of reading Old Testament events as prefigurations of New Testament truths—is extended by Vaughan to the physical world. The author argues that for Vaughan, a flower or a cloud is a "weaker glory" that points toward a greater, spiritual reality.

The commentary draws a compelling parallel between Vaughan and William Wordsworth, noting that while Wordsworth's "Intimations Ode" is often seen as the pinnacle of this theme, Vaughan was doing it a century and a half earlier. The author suggests that Vaughan's "angel infancy" could be read as literal childhood, a favored state among Caroline poets, rather than just a mystical pre-existence. This dual reading adds depth, allowing the poem to resonate with both secular readers who value childhood innocence and religious readers who seek spiritual truth. However, critics might note that over-emphasizing the hermetic elements risks obscuring the poem's deep roots in specific biblical texts, such as Revelation 2, which the author does mention but could have explored in greater depth.

The Paradox of the Retreat

The most powerful section of the commentary addresses the poem's central paradox: the desire to move backward to move forward. The author highlights the antithesis in the couplet, "Some men a forward motion love but I by backward steps would move," describing it as a beautiful example of Hebrew parallelism where contrast creates meaning. Close Reading Poetry writes, "He's trying to get here he's trying to get back but he's drunk with this life and the things that crowd upon this life and that weigh one down and that Stupify the Mind and Spirit." This metaphor of spiritual intoxication is particularly striking, as the author connects it to the modern experience of information overload, noting, "I feel this way after too much social media really... you watch too many reals or something and it's just you feel drunk after it."

By framing the "retreat" as a necessary rejection of the "forward motion" of worldly progress, the author positions Vaughan as a counter-cultural voice for the modern age. The commentary suggests that Vaughan's vision of returning to the "ancient track" is not a rejection of God, but a return to the source of spiritual clarity. The author concludes that while George Herbert is great for those familiar with Anglican liturgy, "Henry Vaughan has this neoplatonic hermetic sensibility that I think is really meaningful for the Contemporary experience of living as a as a modern um in this in this world today whether your religious or not."

Bottom Line

Close Reading Poetry successfully argues that Henry Vaughan's "The Retreat" is a complex theological work that transcends its 17th-century origins to speak directly to the modern condition of spiritual disorientation. The strongest part of the argument is the demonstration of how Vaughan blends biblical typology with Neoplatonic philosophy to create a unique vision of salvation through regression. The biggest vulnerability is the occasional reliance on the "pre-existence of the soul" concept without fully addressing its tension with orthodox Christian doctrine, though the author does acknowledge this nuance. Readers should watch for how this specific blend of mysticism and poetry continues to influence modern spiritual literature, particularly in how it reframes the past not as a lost era, but as a recoverable state of grace.
