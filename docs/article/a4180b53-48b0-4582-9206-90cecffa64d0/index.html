How to secure your network

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Jan 13, 2020 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a landscape of fear-mongering cybersecurity clickbait, The Hated One cuts through the noise with a startlingly pragmatic thesis: your digital safety doesn't require a degree in computer science, but it does require treating your home network as a shared public good. The author argues that individual security creates a "herd immunity" effect, suggesting that the most dangerous vulnerability isn't a sophisticated state actor, but a neighbor with an unencrypted router. This is a refreshing pivot from the usual "you are doomed" narrative to a community-focused call to action.

The First Line of Defense

The core of the argument rests on the router, the often-overlooked gateway to every device in a home. The Hated One writes, "if you only do this you're already way ahead in your security," referring to the simple act of changing default passwords and enabling strong encryption. This is a powerful framing because it lowers the barrier to entry; the author acknowledges that "securing your network is essential but it isn't difficult even if you are not the best friends with technology." By demystifying the process of logging into a router's web interface, the text transforms a daunting technical task into a manageable checklist.

The author is particularly sharp on the distinction between a Wi-Fi password and an admin password, a confusion that plagues millions. "Beware that the admin password is not the same as the password you use to log into Wi-Fi," The Hated One warns, noting that default credentials like "admin/admin" are an open invitation to intruders. This advice is sound, yet it highlights a systemic failure in consumer hardware where manufacturers ship devices with predictable defaults. The recommendation to use a passphrase of at least 16 characters is standard, but the suggestion to change the network name (SSID) to something generic, like a Marvel movie title, adds a layer of psychological obfuscation that is often overlooked.

"Security of computers also has something of an equivalent to herd immunity; if everybody around you has a secure network each individual network is that much more difficult to target."

Critics might argue that changing a network name or password offers only marginal security gains against a determined adversary with physical access to the neighborhood. However, for the vast majority of threats, which are automated scripts scanning for low-hanging fruit, these basic hygiene steps are disproportionately effective. The author correctly identifies that most attacks are not targeted but opportunistic.

Beyond the Basics: Encryption and Traffic

Once the perimeter is secured, The Hated One shifts focus to the data flowing through it. The text insists that "encryption however only works with a strong password," emphasizing that WPA2 is the minimum standard and that older WEP encryption is "broken years ago." This is a crucial distinction, as many users still cling to outdated hardware thinking it is sufficient. The author goes further, advocating for HTTPS Everywhere and DNS encryption, noting that "most DNS queries are unprotected much like most HTTP traffic used to be unencrypted before the Snowden leaks in 2013."

The coverage of DNS (Domain Name System) encryption is particularly valuable. By recommending DNS over HTTPS or DNS over TLS, The Hated One addresses a blind spot where Internet Service Providers can still see which websites a user visits, even if the content is encrypted. The suggestion to use tools like Pi-hole on a Raspberry Pi to block trackers from giants like Google and Facebook takes the argument from personal defense to active data sovereignty. "This step is easy but it is not available on all systems yet," the author admits, showing a realistic understanding of the current technological landscape.

"The most secure network is one that doesn't exist; that is why it is most recommended to ditch Wi-Fi all together and use Ethernet cables for all devices."

This radical suggestion to eliminate Wi-Fi entirely for maximum security is the piece's most provocative claim. While it may be impractical for the average user, it effectively illustrates the inherent risks of wireless communication. The author notes that for those facing "advanced adversaries," using Wi-Fi is "out of the question," and suggests booting from Tails OS and using Tor. This moves the conversation from consumer tips to high-stakes operational security, acknowledging that the threat model varies wildly depending on who is watching.

The Limits of Convenience

The Hated One is honest about the trade-offs involved in hardening a network. "Changing other settings adds more inconvenience for smaller marginal increase security," the author concedes, advising readers to stop at the essentials unless they have a specific reason to go further. This is a rare moment of restraint in a genre that often encourages paranoia. The text also warns against the false sense of security provided by hiding an SSID, stating, "hiding your Wi-Fi doesn't stop its broadcast and doesn't make it invisible to network analysis tools."

The discussion on Virtual Private Networks (VPNs) is nuanced. The author expresses deep skepticism about free services, noting that "most VPNs cannot be trusted," and recommends specific paid options or Tor for public networks. However, there is a tension here: the author admits that using a VPN on a phone disables the application firewall, making mobile browsing "way too insecure." This creates a dilemma for the modern user who relies on smartphones, a gap that the text acknowledges but cannot fully resolve without suggesting the impracticality of avoiding public networks entirely.

"If somebody gets into your home network they could potentially collect your private information or send death threats to the President of the United States from your IP address."

While this example of potential misuse is hyperbolic, it serves to underscore the severity of the risk: your IP address is your digital identity, and if hijacked, you become the perpetrator in the eyes of the law. The author's tone here is urgent but grounded in the reality of how easily identity theft and fraud can occur. The mention of the "six trillion dollars" global cost of cybercrime by 2021 provides a macro-economic backdrop that reinforces why individual actions matter.

Bottom Line

The Hated One succeeds by stripping away the mystique of cybersecurity and presenting it as a series of logical, achievable steps that anyone can take. The strongest part of the argument is the emphasis on "herd immunity," shifting the focus from individual paranoia to collective responsibility. The biggest vulnerability, however, lies in the assumption that users have the technical literacy or the hardware to implement advanced measures like open-source firmware or Pi-hole setups. Ultimately, this is a vital guide for the average person, provided they remember that while these steps drastically reduce risk, they are not a silver bullet against a determined, well-resourced attacker.
