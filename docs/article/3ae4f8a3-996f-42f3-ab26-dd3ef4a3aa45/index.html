Transcript excerpt on Religion For Breakfast

If you picture Jesus right now, chances are you're imagining a man with long flowing hair. That image is everywhere. From Byzantine icons to Renaissance paintings to modern film and TV. It's so familiar that it barely even registers as an artistic interpretation.

It's just how Jesus looks. As the scholar Joan Taylor puts it, this image of Jesus has become quite literally iconic. In an earlier video, I talked about where some of that imagery comes from. The visual language of divinity in the ancient world.

Long hair and a big beard were already linked with almighty gods like Zeus. Over time, those visual cues got folded into how Christians depicted Jesus. But that's art history. Today, I want to ask the more anthropological question.

Would a man walking around first century Galilee actually look like this? Did Jesus actually have long hair? Now, to get oriented, we need a baseline. What did men's hair usually look like in the first century Mediterranean world?

And here the answer is pretty clear. Short hair was the norm for men across the Roman Empire. We can see this almost everywhere we look. Roman statuary from the late republic and early imperial period consistently depict men with closely cropped hair.

So for example, check out these statues from the reigns of Augustus and Tiberius. That same pattern shows up in wall paintings from Pompei preserved when the city was destroyed in 79 CE by a volcano. In domestic fresco depicting banquetss, shopkeepers, musicians, and everyday urban life, men overwhelmingly appear with short hair. And unlike those statues, these paintings are closer to snapshots of how ordinary people expected men to look like in public.

We see something similar in the mummy portraits from Roman a Egypt. These are naturalistic painted portraits attached to mummies, mostly dating from the 1st to 3rd century CE. They represent real individuals, often middle to upper class Roman Egyptians living far from Rome, but still fully embedded in Roman cultural norms. And once again, men are shown with shorter hair.

So even across ethnic and regional differences, the grooming norms are relatively consistent. And even when ancient sources do talk about men with long hair, they don't mean shoulderlength hair in the modern sense.
