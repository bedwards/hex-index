Is Covid racist? | How Science & Medicine Get It Wrong

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Jun 5, 2020 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis, a practicing physician and scientist, dismantles the comforting but dangerous myth that the coronavirus is a "great equalizer" by exposing how medical science itself has been complicit in racial bias. While many scrambled to find genetic explanations for why Black and South Asian communities were dying at disproportionate rates, Francis argues that the answer lies not in biology, but in the structural failures of society and the healthcare system. This is essential listening for anyone who believes that science is inherently neutral, as Francis reveals how deeply embedded racism is in the very textbooks and protocols used to save lives.

The Myth of Biological Difference

Francis immediately challenges the instinctive reaction of the medical community to look for a biological culprit. "People are racist but viruses can't be right," he notes, highlighting the absurdity of assuming a virus would discriminate based on race while humans do not. He points out that the medical establishment has long operated under the false premise that race is a biological category, teaching students that Black people have different kidney functions or heart structures without sufficient evidence. "If we could see DNA the way Neo sees the Matrix we'll see that skin color is a fraction of a percentage of what makes us different," Francis writes, emphasizing that genetic variation within the African continent is greater than the variation between all other continents combined.

The author's critique here is devastatingly effective because it targets the source of the error: the education system itself. He argues that doctors are trained with "blinkers on," viewing human diversity through a pseudo-scientific lens that dates back to the era of biological taxonomy. "We've got this whole generation of people who've dedicated their lives to helping others by going into fields like medicine operating with an erroneous bias," he states. This is a crucial distinction; the problem isn't just individual prejudice, but a systemic failure to update medical science to reflect modern genetic understanding. Critics might argue that acknowledging biological differences in drug metabolism is necessary, but Francis clarifies that these differences correlate poorly with race, making race an unreliable proxy for biology.

Race is a real thing, the same way government or money are real things, they're social constructs, but they're important and we agree we all know what they mean but race does not exist biologically.

Social Determinants Over Genetic Destiny

Once the biological myth is dispelled, Francis pivots to the actual drivers of mortality: deprivation and occupation. He explains that when Black and South Asian patients were admitted to hospitals in the UK, their outcomes were statistically similar to white patients, proving the virus itself does not discriminate. The disparity arises before the hospital doors even open. "Black and minority ethnic people are more likely to be poor, they're more likely to live in cities and in crowded accommodation with less personal space," he paraphrases the data, noting that these groups are vastly over-represented in front-line jobs like taxi drivers, cleaners, and care workers.

Francis shines a particularly harsh light on the care sector, where the most vulnerable are protected by the least equipped. He contrasts the adequate personal protective equipment (PPE) received by hospital doctors with the "pointless plastic aprons and flimsy masks" given to care workers. "The people who care for the most vulnerable in society have died in vast numbers," he observes, linking this directly to the demographic makeup of the workforce, which relies heavily on staff from former British colonies. This section of the argument is powerful because it moves the conversation from abstract statistics to the grim reality of who is expected to sacrifice their health for society's functioning. The evidence holds up firmly against the counter-argument that lifestyle choices are to blame; Francis correctly identifies that these "choices" are often constrained by economic necessity and systemic inequality.

The Persistence of Structural Racism in Medicine

The commentary then broadens to address how racism manifests in the treatment patients receive, even after they enter the healthcare system. Francis cites stark disparities in the US, where Black patients are less likely to receive appropriate cardiac medications, bypass surgery, or pain management for fractures. "Even after controlling for insurance status, income, age, severity of condition, black people receive a different level of care compared to white patients," he writes, underscoring that bias persists regardless of socioeconomic status.

He draws a direct line from historical atrocities like the Tuskegee syphilis trials to modern-day complacency, warning that science has always been a tool that can be used to legitimize discrimination. "Science and medicine have always been and continue to be structurally racist systems through which discrimination and mistreatment is inflicted upon black people," Francis asserts. He challenges the self-congratulatory narrative of the "woke" scientific community, asking why so few Black voices are followed by science communicators. "I can't tackle scientific racism in this short video but I would refer you to the reading list below," he suggests, pointing to the need for active engagement rather than passive acknowledgment. A counterargument worth considering is that individual doctors may not intend harm, but Francis effectively argues that good intentions are insufficient when the system itself is flawed.

Saying hey I've got black friends I don't even see race is not being anti-racist; not seeing race will blind you to the mistreatment of black friends and co-workers.

The Call to Active Anti-Racism

Francis concludes by dismantling the idea that being "non-racist" is enough. He invokes Angela Davis's distinction, arguing that true progress requires active opposition to racist structures. He addresses the specific guilt of non-Black minorities, urging them not to opt out of the conversation. "Defeating racism isn't a passive process, it's something that requires active effort," he insists, calling for introspection and tangible action beyond social media hashtags. He acknowledges his own shortcomings as a science communicator, admitting he didn't follow enough Black creators, which adds a layer of authenticity and humility to his plea.

The strength of this piece lies in its refusal to let the medical community off the hook. By framing racism as a structural and scientific failure rather than just a moral one, Francis forces a reckoning that is uncomfortable but necessary. The argument is most compelling when he connects the dots between the lack of diversity in senior medical positions and the poor health outcomes of minority populations. While some might argue that the focus on race distracts from universal healthcare access, Francis demonstrates that without addressing racial bias, universal access alone will not solve the disparity.

Bottom Line

Rohin Francis delivers a searing indictment of the medical establishment's failure to recognize race as a social construct rather than a biological one, effectively proving that the pandemic's racial disparities are a symptom of systemic neglect rather than genetic fate. His strongest contribution is the exposure of how scientific training itself perpetuates bias, a vulnerability in the profession that few are willing to admit. The reader must watch for how this argument translates into policy changes in medical curricula and hospital protocols, as the diagnosis is clear but the cure remains unimplemented.
