The 51 biggest American political moments of the 21st century

Nate Silver · ·Dec 26, 2025 ·22 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Nate Silver's ambitious countdown of the fifty-one defining political moments of the 21st century offers a rare, data-driven autopsy of a quarter-century of American turbulence, prioritizing institutional shifts over personality cults. What makes this list distinct is its willingness to treat future events as historical fact and to weigh the "agency" of political actors against the chaos of natural disasters or market forces. For the busy listener, this is not merely a nostalgia trip; it is a framework for understanding how specific decisions—like the 2008 financial bailout or the 2022 acquisition of a major social media platform—continue to warp the current political landscape.

The Architecture of Upset

Silver begins by establishing his criteria, arguing that the most significant moments are those reflecting "deliberate decisions made by American political actors." This focus on agency allows him to elevate the 2014 primary upset where Dave Brat defeated Eric Cantor, a moment Silver identifies as the true turning point for the Tea Party movement. He writes, "Perhaps the biggest downstream impact of the Tea Party was in changing the types of candidates that Republicans nominated." This observation is crucial because it reframes the movement not as a series of angry rants, but as a structural force that dismantled the Republican establishment from within.

The commentary on the rise of populism is particularly sharp when Silver connects the dots between the 2008 selection of Sarah Palin and the 2016 entry of Donald Trump into the race. He notes that Palin was "more of a 'game changer' in 2008 than McCain had bargained for," serving as a cautionary tale that the GOP fended off in 2012 but not in 2016. By linking these events, Silver suggests that the party's complacency regarding its own radicalization was a deliberate, if mistaken, strategic choice.

"Republicans will sacrifice everything for tax cuts, but have found it much harder to repeal social welfare programs."

This structural analysis extends to the 2022 purchase of Twitter by Elon Musk. Silver argues that while the "downstream political effects of changes in the media ecosystem can be overstated," the acquisition "coincided with a conservative vibe shift." Critics might note that attributing such a massive cultural shift to a single corporate transaction risks oversimplifying the complex interplay of technology and ideology. However, Silver's point stands: the consolidation of media power in the hands of a single, volatile actor has fundamentally altered the information environment in which elections are fought.

The Weight of History and Future Shock

One of the most provocative elements of Silver's list is his inclusion of events that have not yet occurred, treating them with the same historical weight as the 9/11 attacks or the 2008 election. He includes the 2025 victory of Zohran Mamdani in the New York City mayoral primary, describing it as "probably the biggest American left-wing electoral win of the past quarter-century." This choice signals a belief that generational turnover is not just a possibility, but an inevitability that is already reshaping the Democratic Party.

The list also grapples with the human cost of political failure. When discussing Hurricane Katrina, Silver moves beyond the political fallout for the administration to acknowledge the "terrible human impact" and the event's role in calling "into question American resilience and state capacity." Similarly, the inclusion of the 2025 assassination of Charlie Kirk is handled with a somber gravity, acknowledging the "horrifying" nature of the act as a moment that "needs to be listed somewhere" despite its recent and uncomfortable nature.

"It was ironic given that Comey had done Trump a big favor. His letter to reopen the FBI investigation into Clinton because of files found on Anthony Weiner's laptop quite plausibly cost Clinton the election."

Silver's treatment of the 2016 election interference and the subsequent legal battles is notably devoid of moralizing, focusing instead on the tactical outcomes. He suggests that the various prosecutions of the former president "entrenched Trump's dominance in the 2024 Republican nomination race" rather than weakening it. This counterintuitive reading challenges the conventional wisdom that legal accountability serves as a deterrent, suggesting instead that in the current polarized environment, it often functions as a rallying cry.

The Unfinished Business of the War on Terror

The bookends of the War on Terror—the passage of the PATRIOT Act and the killing of Osama bin Laden—serve as a reminder of how quickly the bipartisan consensus of the early 2000s evaporated. Silver highlights the near-unanimous Senate vote for the PATRIOT Act, noting that "only Wisconsin's Russ Feingold voted against it," a stark contrast to the fractured political landscape that followed. He frames the killing of bin Laden as a moment where "newsworthy events don't necessarily need to reflect bad news," yet he also subtly acknowledges the long shadow cast by the conflicts that followed.

The inclusion of the 2008 financial bailout, the Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP), is another moment of high agency. Silver admits he may have underrated it, noting that "the AI models mostly have TARP in their top 25." He points out the political irony that "Obama ultimately receiving a lot of the blame for it," despite the measure being a bipartisan necessity. This highlights a recurring theme in Silver's analysis: the gap between the objective necessity of a decision and the political reality of its reception.

"The 2016 Republican primary was closer than people might remember."

This observation serves as a corrective to the hazy memory of the era, reminding listeners that the rise of the current political order was not a foregone conclusion. Silver recalls how "Marco Rubio's choke job in a debate five days later in New Hampshire was one of those moments when I began to realize that my early prognostications about Trump were probably going to be wrong." This admission of forecasting error adds a layer of humility to the piece, grounding the grand narrative in the messy reality of prediction.

Bottom Line

Silver's greatest strength is his refusal to let the narrative of the 21st century be defined solely by the most recent headline, instead weaving a tapestry of institutional decay, surprising upsets, and the enduring power of deliberate choice. The list's most significant vulnerability lies in its speculative nature regarding future events, which, while intellectually stimulating, cannot yet be weighed against the full consequences of history. As the political landscape continues to fracture, this framework serves as a vital reminder that the moments we are living through today are being shaped by the same forces of agency and reaction that defined the last twenty-five years.

I hope you’ve had a Merry Christmas and/or happy holidays so far! As Matt Yglesias writes, it’s usually hard to find traction on newsletters this time of year. At the same time, the rest of the industry may overcompensate by hardly publishing any fresh content at all. And I’m sure some of you are waiting out airport delays today, or peeking at your phones while Uncle Stu tells the same story for the fourth time.

So I can’t resist the urge to swim upstream by publishing a semi-serious post, with a question inspired by The Ringer’s countdown of the top 100 sports moments of the past quarter-century. What are the most important American political moments of the 2000s so far?

Although the headline says “21st century”, I’ll allow events taking place in 2000 even though they were technically in the 20th century. So we have 26 years of history to consider here, in other words.

As usual, paid subscribers are welcome to argue about this in the comments. But be nice: it’s the holidays! And please think of my list as a draft, written in pencil. I undoubtedly have my biases, including probably rating elections more highly as compared to non-election events than others might. There are also various criteria that one might use to judge big moments:

The impact of the event on the future direction of politics. This might privilege surprising events versus predictable ones.

The objective consequences for the lives of American and global citizens.1

Drama and the sense of “living through history”.2

And as a tiebreaker, agency — meaning that the event reflected the consequences of deliberate decisions made by American political actors. In that sense, a war that the U.S. initiated counts more than, say, a natural disaster.

There’s another slightly tricky component to this. As a conceit, I’ve assigned each entry to a particular date. But some events don’t map onto dates so neatly. For the Iraq War, for instance, you could use the day the Senate authorized the use of military force (Oct. 11, 2002) or when the war actually started (March 20, 2003), or even Bush’s premature declaration of “Mission Accomplished” (May 1, 2003). In some cases, the dates on this list represent the cumulative impact of events that unfolded over weeks, months, or longer.
