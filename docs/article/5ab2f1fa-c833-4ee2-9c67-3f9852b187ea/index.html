The United Kingdom Is a Dystopian S*!Thole!

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Mar 2, 2025 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

The Hated One makes a startling claim that cuts through the usual noise of political commentary: the United Kingdom has evolved into a surveillance state more aggressive than China or Russia, and it is doing so by legally forcing a global tech giant to compromise the privacy of every single user on Earth. This is not just a story about British politics; it is a warning about how a single jurisdiction can weaponize its laws to erode digital rights worldwide, and the evidence presented suggests the mechanisms are already in motion.

The Unprecedented Global Backdoor

The core of the argument rests on a specific, terrifying order issued in January 2025. The Hated One writes, "The UK government sent a Secret order to Apple to build a backdoor access to all encrypted content of iCloud backups." What makes this claim so alarming is not just the request itself, but the scope. Unlike previous requests from nations like China, which were geographically limited, this order demands global compliance. As The Hated One puts it, "UK authorities want Apple to hand over user data on any user anywhere in the world, whether or not they are a UK citizen." This distinction is crucial; it means a dentist in Ohio or an accountant in Slovenia could have their private data exposed simply because they use a service that operates in the UK.

The author argues that the legal framework behind this is uniquely draconian. The Investigatory Powers Act, often called the "Snooper's Charter," grants the government powers that bypass standard judicial oversight. The Hated One notes, "Under UK law, Apple can appeal to a judge but they cannot delay complying with the request during the appeal process." This removes the critical buffer of time that usually allows companies to mount a defense or seek higher court intervention. In the United States, Apple was not forced to comply with the FBI's backdoor request until a judge ruled on the matter, but the UK system forces immediate action. This legal asymmetry is what allows the government to bypass the mathematical impossibility of breaking encryption directly. Instead of cracking the code, the state forces the creation of a new software version that uploads data before it is ever encrypted.

Critics might note that the author's comparison to authoritarian regimes like China and Russia relies heavily on the scope of the request rather than the intent or the actual data access rates, potentially oversimplifying the nuances of national security laws in different democracies. However, the mechanism of forcing a company to secretly undermine its own security protocols for global users remains a distinct and dangerous precedent.

"This is not Apple selling out your privacy to the UK government; they literally had no other choice."

The Collapse of Corporate Defense

The commentary shifts to the role of technology companies, specifically challenging the narrative that Apple voluntarily compromised user privacy. The Hated One argues that the removal of Advanced Data Protection for UK users was not a betrayal of principle but a legal surrender. "Apple didn't sell out your privacy to the UK government; they literally had no other choice," the author asserts. This reframing is vital because it shifts the blame from corporate greed to state coercion. The author highlights that Apple was forbidden from even acknowledging the order, a stark contrast to their public campaigns in the US where they fought for transparency. "If Apple says anything about the UK order, people could face prison time," The Hated One writes, illustrating the severity of the gag order.

The argument here is that the UK has created an environment where transparency reports are impossible, effectively silencing the very companies that serve as the last line of defense for digital rights. This silence is not accidental; it is a feature of the "authoritarian surveillance state" the author describes. The Hated One contends that the government's ability to remotely hack any device, including those of non-citizens, transforms the UK into a cyber-attack vector. "Dozens of British authorities can hack iCloud accounts of any US person," the author warns, suggesting that the global implications are that any adversary could potentially compromise a UK agency to access the entire British surveillance apparatus.

From Surveillance to Survival

Having established the severity of the threat, the author pivots to solutions, rejecting the idea that self-hosting data is a viable fix for the average person. The Hated One writes, "Self-hosting is not the silver bullet as people present it to be." The reasoning is that individual efforts cannot compete with the resources of state-sponsored surveillance or the security infrastructure of major tech companies. Instead, the author proposes a strategy of geographic and service fragmentation. "To protect your data in a country that will punish you for it means that you have to use a service outside of that country," The Hated One advises. This involves moving away from monolithic accounts like iCloud or Google and adopting a suite of privacy-focused tools like Proton, Signal, and Tutanota, potentially accessed through Tor or VPNs to bypass censorship.

The commentary also addresses the societal dimension, noting that the surveillance state thrives on public apathy. The author cites statistics suggesting that a majority of the British public supports intrusive measures, believing they protect safety. "80% believe public CCTV surveillance should be allowed," The Hated One points out, arguing that this consent is the foundation of the regime's power. The proposed societal solution is aggressive lobbying and political pressure, urging citizens to "protest, bother, and humiliate" politicians who advocate for these laws. The author warns that the "safety of children" is often used as a rhetorical shield to justify overreach, a tactic that must be flipped to expose the true cost to civil liberties.

Critics might argue that the call to "humiliate" politicians could deepen polarization rather than foster the bipartisan consensus needed for privacy reform, and that the reliance on foreign-based services may not be feasible for all users due to cost or accessibility. Yet, the urgency of the situation described suggests that traditional political channels have already been compromised.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's most compelling argument is that the UK has crossed a line from domestic surveillance to a global threat, using its legal system to force a fundamental weakening of encryption for the entire world. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on a specific, future-dated scenario (January 2025) that requires the reader to accept the premise of an already-issued secret order as established fact. However, the detailed breakdown of the Investigatory Powers Act and the specific legal mechanisms that prevent Apple from defending itself provides a chillingly plausible roadmap for how such a dystopia could be realized, making the call to action for digital self-defense both urgent and necessary.
