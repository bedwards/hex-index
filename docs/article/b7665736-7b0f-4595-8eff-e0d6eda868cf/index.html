A conversation with Claude

Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

Noah Smith is publishing his actual conversation with Claude, and it's one of the most substantive AI discussions I've seen from a Substack essayist this year. The hook isn't just whether we should be talking to AI — it's what Smith believes AI will discover that humans literally cannot comprehend.

Smith opens with a claim that's been nagging at me ever since I read it: "I suspect, however, that AI is going to discover some truly groundbreaking science that humans never could have discovered on their own." This isn't the usual AI hype about faster iteration or cheaper synthesis. He's saying AI will find different knowledge — patterns in nature so complex that even if we found them, we couldn't understand them.

To support this, Smith makes an argument about compressibility that's worth sitting with. "Human science is all about compressibility," he writes. We take natural phenomena and boil them down to simple formulas — conservation of momentum, the laws of thermodynamics — because human communication requires simplicity. These are what we call the "laws of nature." But here's the unsettling part: there's no reason why every natural principle needs to obey simple laws that can be written down in a few lines.

Smith uses LLMs themselves as an example. We figured out how to create and apply human language without ever being able to write down simple "laws" of how it worked. The model works — we can use it — but the underlying mechanism is too complex to compress into human-understandable rules. He's asking: what if there are other complex-but-useful patterns in other domains, like materials science and biology?

If they exist, I think AI will be able to find them and apply them.

This is where the conversation gets genuinely speculative. Smith admits that "the question — as Claude notes near the end of our discussion — is whether AI would be able to communicate those laws to humans." His answer: probably not. Just like a dog will never be able to understand quantum mechanics, humans may never be able to understand some of the scientific principles that AI discovers and harnesses.

The conversation then turns concrete. Smith asks Claude for ten transformative advances in materials science where AI could accelerate progress. The list is impressive — room-temperature superconductors, solid-state electrolytes for batteries, direct air capture sorbents, catalysts for green hydrogen, ultra-high-performance structural alloys, programmable self-healing materials, designer proteins, topological and quantum materials, radiation-tolerant materials for fusion and space, thermoelectric materials, biodegradable plastics, neuromorphic computing substrates, metamaterials, and ultra-efficient phosphors.

Then Smith asks for timelines — proof of concept versus commercial scale production. This is where the conversation gets genuinely useful as a forecasting tool. Claude estimates room-temperature superconductors at 5-15 years for proof of concept, 15-30+ years for commercial scale. Solid-state electrolytes are almost already here for proof of concept — 3-8 years to commercial scale. Direct air capture sorbents are largely done at the proof-of-concept stage but face a brutal gap between "it works in a lab" and "you can make wire out of it."

What makes this piece different from the hundred other AI conversations flooding Substack? Two things.

First, Smith isn't just asking what AI can do — he's asking what happens when AI finds patterns we literally cannot understand. This reframes the entire AI safety debate. The fear isn't that AI will become superintelligent and take over. It's that AI will become a kind of alien intelligence that discovers true facts about the universe but can't translate them into human language.

Second, Smith is actually bullish on AI's capacity for breakthrough ideas — more bullish than Claude is. Claude thinks humans will retain the edge in creativity and invention. Smith bets AI will get good at this very quickly. He expects AI to discover "some truly groundbreaking science that humans never could have discovered on their own."

Critics might note that Smith's timeline estimates are necessarily speculative, and he's the first to admit it — "treat these as informed guesses with wide error bars." But that's exactly what makes this conversation valuable: someone actually putting out specific numbers for when we might see room-temperature superconductors, solid-state batteries, or AI-designed catalysts.

A counterargument worth considering: if AI discovers patterns too complex for humans to understand, how do we avoid the trap of building a science that we can't validate or build upon? If we can't understand what AI discovers, we're essentially building an oracle — useful but not knowledge we can actually use. The history of science is built on understanding; compressibility isn't just about communication, it's about cumulative progress.

Bottom Line

Smith's strongest move is the philosophical argument about compressibility — the idea that human science has always been limited by our need for simple, communicable laws. His biggest vulnerability is practical: even if AI finds useful patterns we can't understand, what good is scientific knowledge if it stays locked in the machine? Watch next for whether AI labs start prioritizing "explainability" as a research goal — because right now, Smith's conversation suggests we're building a future where AI tells us what's true but not why.
