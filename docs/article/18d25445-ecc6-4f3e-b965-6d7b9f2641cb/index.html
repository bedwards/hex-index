Lots to be thankful for

Freddie deBoer · ·Nov 26, 2025 ·16 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In a media landscape obsessed with performative outrage and algorithmic doom-scrolling, Freddie deBoer offers a startlingly sincere counter-narrative: a gratitude list that refuses to ignore the wreckage of his own career while celebrating the specific, tangible things that still work. This isn't a generic holiday platitude; it is a forensic examination of what survives when the professional world turns its back on you, anchored by a defense of deep ownership in an age of ephemeral streaming and a plea for intellectual curiosity that transcends the binary traps of modern political discourse.

The Architecture of Exclusion and the Miracle of a Book

The piece opens with a raw admission of professional stagnation. deBoer acknowledges that despite his self-awareness regarding past mistakes, the industry's "petty clubbiness" has effectively closed doors that might otherwise remain open. He writes, "I have accepted that I am probably never going to fully leave behind the constraints that have been imposed on me by my past behavior... and by the wages of an industry that's still governed by petty clubbiness and networking." This framing is crucial because it refuses the victim narrative while simultaneously indicting a system that lacks a statute of limitations for human error. It is a stark reminder that in the current cultural economy, reputation management often outweighs redemption.

Yet, amidst this self-imposed exile, deBoer finds a profound victory in his novel, The Mind Reels. He describes the book's existence as a "cosmic clerical error," noting that it has managed to claw back its modest advance despite "unique marketing challenges." The author's gratitude here is not for fame, but for the simple fact that "actual readers have actually found the book and actually liked it." This sentiment cuts through the noise of the attention economy. In an era where digital platforms can erase a creator overnight, the physical reality of a book that "looks that good" and sells well enough to survive is a radical act of defiance. Critics might argue that focusing on a single book ignores the systemic nature of the exclusion deBoer describes, but the point is precisely that individual connection remains possible even when institutional gatekeeping is absolute.

"I've spent every day of my adult life assuming that the floor is about to fall out in front of me, and the fact that actual readers have actually found the book and actually liked it feels like benefiting from some kind of cosmic clerical error."

Owning the Signal in a World of Streaming Noise

The commentary then pivots to a defense of digital ownership, using the Android app Poweramp as a case study for why enthusiasts still matter. deBoer contrasts this with the modern trend of "streaming ephemera," arguing that when you own your music as files, "it can't vanish because some licensing deal expired." This is a potent argument for digital sovereignty. He praises Poweramp for being "ferociously overdesigned in the best way," a precision instrument in an era of "beige minimalism." The sheer number of knobs and dials is not a bug; it is a feature that respects the user's agency.

This preference for control over convenience echoes a broader historical tension in media consumption. Much like the early days of the MP3 format, which democratized music distribution by allowing users to rip and own their own files, deBoer champions a return to that spirit. He notes that the app treats ownership "like a sacred right," a phrase that carries weight in a subscription economy where access is constantly renegotiated by corporate licensing deals. The argument holds up because it highlights a tangible loss in the shift to streaming: the loss of the library as a permanent, curated collection. While some may argue that cloud storage offers similar permanence, deBoer's point is about the feeling of control and the ability to manipulate the media itself, a luxury stripped away by the black-box algorithms of major streaming platforms.

The Stubbornness of Long-Form Journalism

Turning to print media, deBoer addresses the financial precarity of Harper's Magazine while celebrating its literary ambition. He admits the publication "drives me crazy" with its "self-serious essay" tendencies and low salaries that suggest a "haunted-house internship." Yet, he insists that "there is simply nothing else like Harper's." The magazine's refusal to publish all its best material online is framed not as elitism, but as "guarding a little island where writing still matters." This aligns with the historical role of publications like Harper's, which have long served as a counterweight to the speed of daily news cycles. By keeping content offline, the magazine forces a slower, more deliberate engagement that mirrors the depth of the essays themselves.

The author's appreciation for this "admirable stubbornness" is a critique of the modern media model, which prioritizes click-through rates and algorithmic optimization over depth. deBoer writes that the magazine is a "monthly reminder that smart, artful, beautifully composed prose still exists." This is a vital observation in a time when long-form journalism is increasingly difficult to fund. A counterargument worth considering is that this model is unsustainable for most publishers, but deBoer's point is that the value of such work justifies the struggle to keep it alive, even if it means living on "baling wire, passion, and a willingness to live on lentils."

Escaping the Algorithmic Terrarium

Perhaps the most politically charged section of the piece is the praise for Breaking Points, a show that deBoer argues has "escaped the algorithmic terrarium of 'left vs. right'." He commends hosts Krystal Ball and Saagar Enjeti for discussing topics like labor issues and antitrust fights without "tipping over into red-pill theatrics or conspiracy cosplay." The show is described as "refreshingly adult, skeptical without being nihilist, populist without being reactionary." This is a rare description in a polarized media landscape where nuance is often punished. deBoer challenges readers who have filed the show under "Antiwoke" to "look deeper," suggesting that the show's true value lies in its ability to critique elites without resorting to conspiracy theories about "lizard people."

This framing is effective because it identifies a genuine gap in the media ecosystem: the space for populist critique that is not reactionary. By focusing on the "questions underneath the questions," the show offers a model for political discourse that moves beyond the prefab outrage of cable news. However, critics might note that the line between "populist without being reactionary" and "reactionary without admitting it" is often thin, and the show's critics would argue that its skepticism of mainstream institutions can sometimes veer into the very conspiracy-adjacent territory deBoer claims to avoid. Nevertheless, the core argument—that there is room for a politics that is critical of power without being cynical—remains compelling.

The Restorative Power of Learning and Play

The final sections of the piece return to personal rituals that defy the efficiency-obsessed modern world. deBoer describes online Korean classes as a "structured intellectual stimulation adulthood quietly strips away," a place where he can "struggle through grammar and pronunciation" and remember the dopamine hit of learning. This is a powerful endorsement of lifelong learning as a social and psychological necessity, not just a professional asset. Similarly, his defense of fantasy football podcasts, specifically The Ringer Fantasy Football Show, highlights the human need for connection in a digital age. He acknowledges that "listening to people talk is no substitute for real human connection," but argues that in a "fallen post-Cum Town world," these shows represent a "pinnacle of 'three guys hanging out' podcasting."

The piece concludes with a nod to the novels of Jules Verne, whose "vast back catalog" offers a "mixture of curiosity, optimism, and adventure" that is "restorative" in a world "engineered to scatter your attention." deBoer writes that he is "growing old alongside his books," finding comfort in a writer who represents a time when science fiction was about wonder rather than dystopia. This is a fitting end to a gratitude list that values depth, ownership, and human connection over the frantic pace of the news cycle.

Bottom Line

Freddie deBoer's argument is strongest when it connects the personal struggle of exclusion to the broader structural failures of the cultural economy, offering a blueprint for finding meaning in the margins. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on niche, enthusiast-driven solutions—like specific apps or independent magazines—that may not be scalable or accessible to everyone. However, the core message remains vital: in a world designed to fragment attention and commodify identity, the act of owning your media, engaging with deep literature, and finding genuine connection in unlikely places is a radical form of resistance.

"In a world engineered to scatter your attention to the wind, there's something restorative about a writer with a vast back catalog you can page through over the years, returning again and again to that mixture of curiosity, optimism, and adventure."

The reader should watch for how these small, intentional acts of curation and connection might scale into a broader cultural shift, challenging the dominance of algorithmic feeds and ephemeral content. deBoer's gratitude is not just for what he has, but for the possibility that others might find similar anchors in their own lives.

That My Novel Exists, Looks Beautiful, & You Can Buy It

I have gotten to the point where I have accepted that I am probably never going to fully leave behind the constraints that have been imposed on me by my past behavior, by what others make of my past behavior, and by the wages of an industry that’s still governed by petty clubbiness and networking. This is all boring to you and boring to me. The reality though is that this has been a year where a lot of potential professional opportunity has come up and then eventually slipped away, thanks to a reputation for bad behavior that seems not to have a statute of limitations. Well, we forge the chains we wear in life, as a wise hotdog restaurant once said. I just find myself more and more looking for the door out of whatever this career of mine is, because at this point it’s clear that I can’t move on if others aren’t willing to let me. I apologize for being arch.
