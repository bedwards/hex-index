Heather Cox Richardson delivers a stark warning: the current administration's approach to the Middle East has shifted from strategic diplomacy to a volatile mix of personal fantasy and potential war crimes. While the White House claims "great progress" in negotiations, the evidence suggests a leader flailing without a plan, threatening to obliterate civilian infrastructure while the global economy reels from the very chaos he created.

The Fantasy of Regime Change

Richardson exposes the disconnect between the administration's rhetoric and the grim reality on the ground. She notes that when the executive branch decided to engage Iran, it apparently "fantasized that the operation would look like his strike on Venezuela," expecting a quick decapitation of leadership followed by a compliant successor. Instead, the initial strikes killed leadership only for the regime to replace them from within, rendering the administration's core objective "as imaginary as his claim that the U.S. and Iran have been at war for 47 years."

This failure of imagination has led to a dangerous escalation. Richardson points out that the administration is now reduced to threatening attacks on civilian infrastructure to force a reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, a goal that existed before the war started. "Trump appears to be trying to force his will on the Iranians by threatening to commit war crimes," she writes. The specific threat to destroy desalination plants is particularly alarming, as it targets the very water supply of the population. As Shashank Joshi of The Economist notes, such an act is "not only stupid because Iran could do the same to other Gulf states, but also, quite obviously,...very illegal." This framing is crucial; it moves the discussion from political disagreement to the realm of international criminal liability.

International law recognizes attacks on civilian infrastructure as war crimes, yet the administration threatens to blow up electric plants and desalination facilities as a matter of policy.

Critics might argue that the threat is merely bluster intended to strengthen the administration's negotiating position. However, Richardson counters this by highlighting the administration's history of following through on aggressive posturing and the fact that troops are already being massed in the region. The threat is not just rhetorical; it is a tangible escalation that invites retaliation.

The Hollowing of Institutions

The article argues that this foreign policy disaster is a direct symptom of a broader domestic shift: the concentration of power in the executive branch and the systematic removal of expertise. Richardson traces this back to the "Movement Conservatives" of the 1980s, a faction that sought to dismantle the regulatory state and centralize authority in the presidency. "Now we are seeing what that sort of a government, devoid of experts and beholden to the whims of a single man, looks like," she observes.

The consequences are visible in the administration's reliance on billionaires rather than diplomats. Richardson reports that when the President spoke with Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi, billionaire Elon Musk participated in the call, despite official readouts omitting his presence. This suggests a government where policy is increasingly dictated by the personal interests of the ultra-wealthy rather than the State Department. Furthermore, the administration's internal dynamics are fracturing. Insiders have leaked that White House deputy chief of staff Stephen Miller allegedly demanded confrontations between agents and protesters to "win the 'PR battle,'" while Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth has cast the conflict as a "holy war," praying for "overwhelming violence of action against those who deserve no mercy."

This erosion of institutional norms is compounded by a lack of congressional oversight. Republicans in Congress have "abdicated their authority to oversee the war," leaving the administration to escalate a conflict that has already caused inflation to rise and job growth to stall. The administration's attempt to fund this war by cutting federal health care spending further illustrates the disconnect between policy priorities and public welfare.

The Global and Domestic Backlash

The administration's isolation is becoming palpable. Richardson notes that allies, whom the President has long disdained, are refusing to participate in a war started without consultation. Spain has closed its airspace to U.S. planes involved in the operations, a clear signal of diplomatic rupture. Domestically, the public is turning away. An AP-NORC poll shows that 59% of Americans believe the U.S. has "gone too far" in Iran, with only 13% supporting further escalation.

Yet, the administration seems trapped in a cycle of self-justification. Richardson writes, "There's no telling what President Trump will resort to doing to save face, create the mirage of victory, and extricate himself from the box canyon into which he so triumphantly galloped." The administration's focus has shifted to internal enemies, with the President posting repeatedly about prosecuting New York Attorney General Letitia James, while simultaneously allowing a Russian-flagged tanker to bypass a blockade intended to strangle Cuba's energy sector. This contradiction highlights a foreign policy driven by personal grievance rather than strategic coherence.

The administration has entered a war of choice that has created an extraordinary global energy crisis, only to find itself begging for help from allies it has alienated.

Bottom Line

Richardson's most compelling argument is that the current crisis is not an anomaly but the inevitable result of decades of conservative efforts to gut the federal government's expertise and centralize power in a single, unaccountable executive. The strongest part of her coverage is the link between the administration's illegal threats against Iranian water supplies and the broader collapse of institutional checks and balances. The biggest vulnerability in the administration's position is its lack of a viable exit strategy; with allies refusing to cooperate and the American public opposing escalation, the President is left with only the option of further, potentially catastrophic, escalation. Readers should watch closely for any move toward the threatened destruction of civilian infrastructure, as that would mark a definitive crossing of the line into war crimes.
