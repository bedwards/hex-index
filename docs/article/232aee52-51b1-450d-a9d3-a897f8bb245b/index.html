The Inner Chronicle of What We Are – Werner Herzog Documentary

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Feb 28, 2019 · 26 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden does not merely recount the wild anecdotes of Werner Herzog's career; he argues that the director's chaotic life is a deliberate strategy to capture a specific kind of truth that polished cinema misses. The piece stands out by connecting Herzog's obsession with "adequate images" to a broader philosophical crisis in modern civilization, suggesting that without them, we are doomed to extinction. For a busy mind seeking depth in fifteen minutes, this is a rare synthesis of film criticism and existential philosophy.

The Struggle Against the Indifferent Universe

The core of van der Linden's argument is that Herzog's entire filmography, spanning fiction and documentary, revolves around a single, brutal conflict: the human spirit versus a universe that is "inherently violent and indifferent." He traces this from the soldier in Signs of Life who goes mad from boredom on a Greek island to the deaf and blind Finns in Land of Silence and Darkness who exist in total isolation. The author writes, "There is no harmony in the universe we have to get acquainted to this idea that there is no real harmony as we have conceived it." This framing is powerful because it strips away the comforting narrative of cosmic justice, replacing it with a raw, almost spiritual struggle.

Van der Linden suggests that Herzog's characters are not just individuals but archetypes of a universal condition. He notes that these figures are "desperate and solitary rebels with no language with which to communicate," doomed to fail yet persisting anyway. The author's insight here is that Herzog elevates these outsiders to the status of saints. "Those people on the furthest fringes of our societies... do not become outsiders in the films of werner herzog instead they become saints," he writes. This is a compelling redefinition of heroism, one that resonates with anyone who has felt the sting of being misunderstood. Critics might argue that romanticizing trauma risks aestheticizing suffering, but van der Linden's focus on the longing to communicate rather than the tragedy itself keeps the tone humanistic rather than exploitative.

"I've always thought of my films as really being one big work that i've been concentrating on for 40 years. The characters in this huge stories are all desperate and solitary rebels with no language with which to communicate."

The War for Adequate Images

The commentary shifts to Herzog's method, specifically his rejection of the "worn out images" of modern media. Van der Linden highlights Herzog's declaration of a "holy war" against the destructive nature of television and Hollywood sets. The author explains that Herzog believes we lack a "new grammar of images" and that without developing "adequate images," civilization will "die out like dinosaurs." This is the piece's most provocative claim: that the quality of our visual language determines the survival of our culture.

To illustrate this, van der Linden details Herzog's unorthodox techniques, from hypnotizing his cast in Fitzcarraldo to casting a man with a traumatic past to play Casper Hauser. The author argues these are not gimmicks but essential tools for stylization. "The hypnosis therefore was not a tool of manipulation but one of stylization, a way to emphasize the underlying story," van der Linden explains. This distinction is crucial. It reframes Herzog's eccentricities as a rigorous pursuit of emotional truth rather than mere spectacle. The evidence holds up well when paired with the description of the cattle auctioneer's speech as "real poetry of capitalism," showing how Herzog finds rhythm and meaning in the raw data of reality.

However, the argument glosses over the ethical gray areas of Herzog's methods. While van der Linden defends the use of real rats in Nosferatu and the physical toll on actors as necessary for authenticity, a counterargument worth considering is whether the pursuit of "truth" ever justifies the exploitation of vulnerable subjects. The author briefly mentions the controversy but ultimately sides with the artistic necessity, which may leave some ethical questions unanswered.

Reality, Fiction, and the Deeper Truth

In the final section, van der Linden tackles the paradox of Herzog's non-fiction work. He points out that while Herzog demands reality in his fiction, he curates and fictionalizes his documentaries. The author describes how Lessons of Darkness turns burning oil fields into an "apocalyptic science fiction film" to reveal a deeper truth. "The stylization serves to reveal a deeper truth to capture something essential about the human experience," he writes. This challenges the traditional definition of documentary filmmaking, which often prioritizes literal accuracy over emotional resonance.

The author contrasts Herzog's approach with the "direct cinema" and "cinema verite" movements of the 1960s, which sought objective truth. Van der Linden argues that Herzog rejects this objectivity in favor of a "poetic truth" that feels more real to the audience. This is a sophisticated distinction that elevates the discussion from film technique to epistemology. The piece effectively argues that what we perceive as "real" is often a construct, and Herzog's job is to break that construct to show us something more profound.

"We lack adequate images our civilization doesn't have adequate images and i think a civilization is doomed or is gonna die out like dinosaurs if it does not develop an adequate language or adequate images."

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's commentary succeeds by treating Werner Herzog not as a mere eccentric filmmaker but as a philosopher of the visual age, making a strong case that his chaotic methods are a necessary antidote to a stagnant culture. The piece's greatest strength is its ability to connect specific film techniques to a grand, existential warning about the death of civilization's language. Its only vulnerability is a slight reluctance to fully grapple with the ethical costs of Herzog's relentless pursuit of truth, but this does little to diminish the power of the central argument.
