Chinese Titanium

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Mar 24, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider cuts through the geopolitical noise to deliver a counterintuitive truth: China's dominance in titanium isn't a sign of imminent supply-chain collapse for the West, but rather the result of a decades-long, state-driven industrial accident that left Beijing with too much low-grade metal and too little high-grade capacity. While Washington frets over export licenses, Schneider reveals that the real bottleneck isn't Chinese production volume, but the insurmountable regulatory and technical moats keeping Chinese titanium out of American cockpits.

The Strategic Misunderstanding

The piece begins by dismantling the assumption that raw material abundance equals strategic leverage. Schneider writes, "China, the world's biggest titanium producer (~70% of global production), currently requires exporters of high-performance titanium alloys... to obtain licenses." This sounds ominous on paper, a classic resource weaponization scenario. However, the author quickly pivots to the historical reality, noting that this dominance was born not from market efficiency, but from Mao Zedong's 1958 directive that "there are 64 nonferrous metals and we can't do without them."

This historical grounding is crucial. It reframes the current export controls not as a sudden, aggressive maneuver, but as the bureaucratic tail end of a Cold War survival strategy. Schneider explains that while the US pivoted away from titanium sponge production in the late 1950s to focus on missiles, China doubled down, driven by Zhou Enlai's 1968 assertion that "the production of titanium is a matter of life and death." The author effectively uses this timeline to show that China's capacity was built for self-reliance against the USSR and the US, not for global market disruption.

"China, being relatively isolated on the global stage... needed to pursue metallurgical self-reliance from the ground up if the country was to develop both industry and defense."

The narrative deepens with a fascinating look at the geography of this industry. Schneider details how Panzhihua, a city hidden behind mountains in Sichuan, was developed as a secret industrial base where "planners placed the city's train station behind a mountain so that civilian riders could see the mines from train windows." This connects directly to the broader Third Front strategy, where heavy industry was dispersed to remote western provinces to survive a potential nuclear exchange. It's a vivid reminder that today's commercial supply chains were forged in the fires of existential paranoia.

The Quality Gap and the Certification Wall

The most compelling part of Schneider's analysis is the distinction between volume and value. He argues that while China churns out massive quantities of titanium, it struggles with the "high-purity feedstock" required for aerospace. The author notes, "Less than 5% of Baoji's titanium processing output is destined for high-value-add industries like medical applications or aerospace." This is a critical nuance often missed in policy debates that focus solely on tonnage.

Schneider writes, "It will be years before any Chinese producer gets past the complicated regulatory process, navigates almost-guaranteed political headwinds, and wins certification." This highlights a dual barrier: technical and bureaucratic. Even if China could magically produce aerospace-grade sponge tomorrow, the US acquisition regulations require metals to be melted in the US or allied nations. Japan remains the primary non-US source, creating a "procedural quagmire" that protects the West more effectively than tariffs ever could.

Critics might argue that this reliance on Japanese and Saudi certification is a single point of failure, especially given the volatility of global alliances. If Japan were to face its own supply shocks, the US aerospace sector would be exposed. However, Schneider's point stands that China's current overcapacity in low-grade materials does not translate to a viable threat in the high-end defense sector.

"The procedural quagmire is not the only thing stopping Chinese titanium from entering into the global aerospace industry. Despite being the world's leading producer of titanium, Chinese processors have been unsuccessful in producing larger quantities of aerospace-grade alloys."

The author also touches on the economic irony of China's situation. The industry, once a strategic necessity, has become a victim of its own success. "State media openly admit to an 'overcapacity' crisis in Baoji," Schneider observes, noting that the sector is now "too big for its own good." This mirrors the broader dynamic in many Chinese industries where state subsidies drive production beyond market demand, leading to price crashes and the shuttering of smaller players.

Bottom Line

Schneider's strongest contribution is reframing the titanium panic as a misunderstanding of industrial quality versus quantity; the West is safe not because China lacks volume, but because it lacks the specific high-grade certification and purity that defense contractors demand. The argument's vulnerability lies in the speed of technological catch-up, as China's recent breakthroughs in high-purity extraction suggest the quality gap may narrow faster than bureaucratic certification processes can adapt.

Titanium! Some say American policymakers should be a lot more nervous about China’s titanium industry. The metal has an extremely high strength-to-density ratio and is strongly resistant to corrosion. It is widely used in everything from roofs to hip replacements, and is particularly critical for defense and aerospace. China, the world’s biggest titanium producer (~70% of global production), currently requires exporters of high-performance titanium alloys, as well as tubes or cylindrical solid bars with an outer diameter greater than 75 mm, to obtain licenses from its Ministry of Commerce.

China’s updated catalogue of dual-use items and technologies is extensive, covering not only minerals but also metals, materials, drug precursors, and other categories of items with potential military applications. Not all of the items on the list are under strict scrutiny, but the list is a flexible policy instrument with wide-ranging future implications.

Are the concerns justified? It depends on who you ask, and we will get to that in Section 3. But first, let’s understand what titanium is and why it is valuable.

Titanium is the ninth-most-abundant element in the Earth’s crust. Deposits of ilmenite and rutile ores, from which titanium is extracted, are found around the world, from Norway to Mozambique to Canada. How did China even become the world’s biggest titanium exporter? History of Chinese titanium

“There are 64 nonferrous metals and we can’t do without them.” 64种有色金属，没有它不行。 — Mao Zedong, 19581

Nonferrous metals do not contain iron in appreciable amounts. They are usually lighter, more conductive, and resistant to corrosion. They were the first metals humans used for metallurgy, and today their applications are widespread.

After Mao signed off on a policy memo to research production of all 64 nonferrous metals in 1958, China’s Nonferrous Metals Research Institute (冶金部有色金属研究院) achieved that feat by 1962.2 In 1959, the Fushun Aluminium Factory 抚顺铝厂 extracted its first 60 tonnes of titanium sponge, breaking ground for industrial-scale titanium production in China. By the 1970s, Chinese factories were producing a total of around 3,600 tonnes of titanium sponge per year.
