Does History Progress?

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Oct 21, 2023 · 28 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now confronts a jarring paradox that defines our era: how can the most life-saving medical breakthrough in human history emerge from a messy accident, while the same decade witnessed industrialized genocide? The piece challenges the comforting myth that progress is a straight line, arguing instead that civilization is a turbulent river where blood and beauty flow side by side. For the busy mind seeking clarity, this is not just a history lesson; it is a framework for understanding why our world feels so contradictory and how we might actually steer it toward something better.

The Paradox of Chance and Catastrophe

The author anchors the discussion in a harrowing 1940 case: a London policeman whose life was saved by penicillin, only to die days later when the supply ran out. This story serves as the entry point for a deeper inquiry into the nature of discovery. Then & Now writes, "Not only was it discovered by accident the mold had floated up through a window accidentally left open onto a petri dish left accidentally out on a bench rather than in an incubator." This anecdote is powerful because it strips away the illusion of inevitable scientific triumph. The author argues that while the discovery was serendipitous, the capacity to recognize and utilize it was not; it required a scientist embedded in a culture of inquiry.

Yet, the piece immediately juxtaposes this miracle with the horrors unfolding in Europe. "Compare this story to what was going on at precisely the same time only a few hundred miles away in Germany and Poland Millions were being systematically murdered while the Innovations of Science and Technology were being put to good use by European slaughtering each other on battlefields." This contrast is the article's emotional and intellectual core. It forces the reader to abandon the idea that technological advancement equals moral progress. A counterargument worth considering is whether this framing creates a false dichotomy; one could argue that the same rationality that built the gas chambers also built the penicillin, suggesting that reason itself is a tool, not a moral compass.

"Civilization is a stream with banks the stream is sometimes filled with blood from people killing stealing in shouting and doing the things that historians usually record while on the banks unnoticed people build homes make love raise children sing songs write poetry and even Whittle statues."

Defining the Measure of Good

To navigate this chaos, the author proposes a specific metric for progress: Liberty. They define this not as a rigid political ideology, but as "the freedom to think to speak to do to act to be oneself to go where one chooses to strive in the way one wants to strive to have as many as the primary Goods of life as possible." This is a crucial pivot. By moving away from abstract debates about happiness or wealth, the piece grounds progress in the tangible expansion of human possibility. The argument suggests that a society with better access to medicine is objectively "better" than one with gas chambers, not because of GDP, but because it expands the realm of what a human can do.

The commentary then shifts to the philosophical underpinnings of this view, drawing heavily on G.W.F. Hegel. Then & Now explains that for Hegel, history is not random; it is "the unfolding of Reason Through Time." The author paraphrases Terry Pinkard's interpretation, noting that freedom is the "relationship between desire reasoning acting on your desires and recognition and Authority." This is a dense but vital point: we are not isolated atoms. Our freedom is negotiated through our interactions with others and the systems we build. The piece argues that humans are unique because we have "concepts" of our actions, allowing us to reflect and improve. "A mouse has a past but it has no real history," the author notes, highlighting that our ability to categorize and judge our own behavior is what drives historical movement.

The Arc from Shadows to Light

The narrative traces the slow, uneven evolution of this concept of freedom. The author describes the "Greek Miracle" as the moment humanity first achieved "reflective critical distance" from the world, asking "who are the people what does freedom mean who rules and so on." This self-consciousness, the text argues, was the seed of progress. However, the piece is quick to acknowledge the limitations of the ancient world, noting that for the Greeks, "Freedom meant not being compelled it meant being self-directing and crucially it meant not being a slave but of course women and slaves were excluded." This admission prevents the argument from becoming a naive celebration of the past.

The author then moves through the Middle Ages, describing them as a regression where "monarchism absolutism even the Catholicism of the period don't fit well under our broad idea of what Liberty means." The turning point arrives with the Renaissance and the Scientific Revolution, fueled by the rediscovery of ancient texts and the invention of the printing press. Then & Now writes, "It was essentially an explosion of ideas the discovery of America by Europeans in 492 also revolutionized attitudes of many across the continent." This section effectively links the material conditions of history (printing presses, migration) to the intellectual shifts that expanded human liberty.

Critics might note that this Hegelian view of history risks being too teleological, implying that history must move toward freedom, which can obscure the very real forces of regression and tyranny that persist today. The author acknowledges this by stating that history is "contingent subject to chance random messy," but insists the direction is toward greater freedom. This tension between chaos and direction is where the piece remains most compelling.

Bottom Line

Then & Now successfully reframes the question of progress not as a measure of technology, but as the expansion of human agency and the capacity for self-reflection. The strongest part of the argument is its refusal to separate scientific advancement from moral context, forcing the reader to confront the duality of human capability. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on a Hegelian narrative that assumes a linear trajectory toward freedom, a view that feels optimistic in the face of modern authoritarianism. Readers should watch for how this framework applies to the digital age, where the tools for connection and the tools for surveillance are often indistinguishable.
