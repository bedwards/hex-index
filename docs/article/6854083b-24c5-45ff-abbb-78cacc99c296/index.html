Does history progress?

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Oct 21, 2023 ·28 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now tackles the most persistent question in historical analysis: whether human progress is a guaranteed trajectory or a fragile accident. The piece's most striking move is reframing the discovery of penicillin not as a triumph of inevitable scientific destiny, but as a paradox where a life-saving miracle emerged from a messy, open window while the world burned. This is essential listening for anyone trying to make sense of a world that feels simultaneously advanced and collapsing.

The Paradox of Chance and Catastrophe

The narrative opens with a harrowing account of a London policeman in 1940, whose life was saved by a moldy petri dish before the supply ran out and he died. Then & Now uses this tragedy to dismantle the comforting myth that history moves in a straight line toward improvement. "Not only was it discovered by accident... if the discovery of such a life-saving drug is the result of chance how can we think about progress at all," they ask. This framing is powerful because it forces the reader to confront the randomness of survival. The author juxtaposes this medical miracle with the simultaneous industrial slaughter in Europe, noting that "the most important innovation in history... were happening at the same time as the most devastating catastrophe in history."

The piece leans heavily on historian Will Durant's metaphor of civilization as a river of blood, with the good things happening quietly on the banks. Then & Now writes, "The story of civilization is the story of what happened on the banks." This is a compelling visual, but it risks romanticizing the quiet moments while ignoring that the river often floods the banks. The argument suggests we are too pessimistic because we only watch the violence, but this overlooks how often the "banks" are the very places where tyranny is constructed.

Defining the Metric: Liberty as the North Star

To navigate this chaos, the author proposes a specific metric for progress: liberty. They argue that while values like health or wealth are debatable, "Liberty broadly speaking is the freedom to think to speak to do to act to be oneself" is a neutral ground most can agree upon. This is a pragmatic pivot. By defining progress as the expansion of possibility—"more possibility is better than less"—the piece avoids getting stuck in ideological dead ends. The core of the argument is that we must identify which historical conditions expanded this freedom and which shrunk it.

The commentary draws on Hegel and Terry Pinkard to suggest that freedom is not just individual whim, but a social negotiation. "Freedom is the relationship between desire reasoning acting on your desires and recognition and Authority," the text explains. This inter-subjective view is crucial. It moves the conversation away from "I can do what I want" to "We can build a world where my reasons matter." However, critics might note that defining liberty as the primary good can sometimes clash with the need for collective security or economic equality, a tension the piece acknowledges but doesn't fully resolve.

The society that has better access to penicillin is better than the one where you're more likely to be sent to a gas chamber.

The Greek Miracle and the Birth of Self-Reflection

The piece traces the roots of this reflective freedom back to the ancient Greeks, specifically the invention of the polis. Then & Now argues that the "Greek Miracle" was not just about democracy, but about the capacity for self-criticism. "Without these mechanisms for self-criticism there can no movement in history," they assert. The author highlights how the Greeks introduced the idea that rules should be interrogated, not just followed. "The Greeks he thinks were self-conscious they had a particularly acute idea of the self and asked questions about it."

This section is the intellectual engine of the piece. It suggests that progress isn't about technology alone, but about the concept of the self. The author notes that while Aristotle's definition of freedom excluded women and slaves, the idea of being "a law unto himself" set a trajectory that would eventually expand. The piece argues that the "Dark Ages" were indeed a regression in terms of liberty, where "monarchism absolutism even the Catholicism of the period don't fit well under our broad idea of what Liberty means." This is a bold claim, one that historians of the Middle Ages might challenge by pointing to the rise of universities and guilds, but it serves the author's broader point about the centrality of individual agency.

The Explosion of Ideas

The narrative concludes by linking the Renaissance, Reformation, and Scientific Revolution to the rediscovery of these ancient texts and the invention of the printing press. Then & Now describes this as "an explosion of ideas" that fundamentally altered how humans categorized the world. The argument is that once we start categorizing and questioning—moving from "the sun goes around the earth" to empirical observation—we create the conditions for progress. "Hegel says that we emerge from a realm of Shadows and move towards the light of the space of reasons."

This optimism is tempered by the reality that these ideas didn't just appear; they were fought for. The piece implies that the current state of liberty is not guaranteed, but a fragile achievement that requires constant maintenance. The author's reliance on Hegel's teleological view—that history has a direction toward freedom—is its most vulnerable point. If the "end of history" is freedom, why does it feel like we are sliding backward? The piece doesn't fully answer this, leaving the reader with a sense of hope that is more philosophical than empirical.

Bottom Line

Then & Now succeeds in reframing progress not as a straight line, but as a struggle to expand the "space of reasons" where human liberty can flourish. Its strongest asset is the synthesis of medical history, ancient philosophy, and modern political theory to define a clear metric for what matters. The biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a Hegelian faith that history must move toward freedom, a belief that feels fragile in the face of current global instability. The reader should watch for how this definition of liberty holds up when tested against the urgent, non-liberal demands of climate change and economic survival.
