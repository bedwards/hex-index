Gatekeepers of law: Inside the westlaw and LexisNexis duopoly

Tom Blakely · ·Dec 31, 2025 ·21 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Tom Blakely exposes a quiet crisis in American democracy: the law, which belongs to the public, has been effectively privatized behind a $500-per-search paywall. This isn't just a story about expensive software; it is a forensic look at how a century of corporate consolidation and regulatory failure has turned the foundational rules of our society into a proprietary product. For the busy professional relying on the rule of law, Blakely's account reveals that the very tools needed to navigate justice are held hostage by a duopoly that regulators failed to stop.

The Illusion of Access

Blakely, a federal judicial law clerk and former Department of Justice attorney, begins by dismantling the modern assumption that the internet has democratized legal knowledge. He writes, "The law in America does not belong to lawyers, it belongs to all of us. As citizens, we must be able to read the law, understand it, obey it, and bring complaints against those who wrong us." This is the moral core of his argument, and it lands with force because it contrasts sharply with the reality he describes: "Today, the law is hidden behind paywalls."

The author traces the mechanics of this exclusion back to the physical limitations of the past. In the common law tradition, where judicial interpretations bind future cases, knowing the law required navigating a "cumbersome if necessary system" of physical books. Blakely notes that prior to digital tools, accessing a specific ruling like United States v. Alcoa meant physically locating the 148th volume of the Federal Reporter and turning to page 416. While early publishers like West Publishing solved the problem of access by organizing these decisions, they simultaneously built a "taxonomical monopoly" through their proprietary Key Number system and page numbering.

This historical context is crucial. Blakely explains that West's system became so entrenched that "all research paths began with West's system of Key Numbers and pagination, and ended in its reporters." Critics might argue that this was simply efficient market evolution, but Blakely suggests it was a strategic capture of the legal infrastructure itself. The system worked so well that it became the only way to speak the language of the law, effectively locking out competitors who couldn't match the proprietary citation standards.

"West's Key Number System on the other hand, was curated by West's attorney-editors, who summarized and organized key legal points and opinions into headnotes... This system gave West a sort of taxonomical monopoly over U.S. case law."

The Digital Moat and the Feist Ruling

The narrative shifts dramatically with the advent of the computer age. When Lexis launched in the 1970s, it threatened to break the print monopoly by making cases searchable. However, the duopoly solidified not through innovation, but through the legal defense of West's page numbers. Blakely highlights a bizarre legal reality where "prior to 1991, U.S. copyright law actually held that West's business of putting page numbers over compendiums of public domain court cases was eligible for copyright protection."

This created a paradox: the law itself is public domain, but the specific way it was paginated was owned by a private corporation. Blakely writes, "While theoretically anyone could mass publish court opinions, without West's authoritative page numbers, any such reporter was mostly useless." The stakes were incredibly high because the legal profession relies on "pinpoint citations to page numbers in official reporters" for everything from court filings to academic writing.

The turning point came with the 1991 Supreme Court decision in Feist Publications, Inc. v. Rural Telephone Service Co., which established that mere compilations of facts lack the creativity required for copyright. Blakely notes that this ruling was the "starting gun" for a decade of consolidation. Following Feist, courts ruled that West's page numbers "did not entail even a modicum of creativity," stripping the company of its primary legal moat. Yet, rather than opening the floodgates to competition, this legal clarity coincided with a massive wave of mergers that left only two players standing.

The Regulatory Failure

The most damning section of Blakely's piece concerns the merger that created the modern Lexis/Westlaw duopoly. When Thomson Corporation moved to acquire West in the 1990s, it was a clear antitrust violation on paper. Blakely cites antitrust litigator Gary Reback, who argued that "using nearly any approach to analyze the anticompetitive effects of the proposed Thomson/West acquisition... would have condemned the tie up."

Instead of blocking the deal, the Department of Justice under the Clinton administration allowed it to proceed. Blakely points to a disturbing detail: the former owner of West had pledged over $150,000 in campaign contributions to Democrats while the deal was pending. He writes, "One does not need to be much of a cynic to connect the dots between these backroom political dealings on the one hand, and how the merger seemed to glide through regulators—against the balance of the law—on the other."

The regulatory failure was one of perspective. Blakely quotes a Justice Department deputy who claimed, "A treatise on torts is not a substitute for a treatise on contracts," illustrating how the agency focused on narrow market definitions while ignoring the broader picture. As journalist John Morris observed, the government "appears to have become obsessed with competing seedlings and overlooked how the giants of the forest can block out the light." This myopic view allowed a single entity to control the primary access points to the entire American legal system.

"The Antitrust Division could not have missed the issue. Legal newspapers wrote editorials about it. Law librarians publicly criticized the deal in bar publications."

Bottom Line

Blakely's strongest contribution is his ability to connect the dry mechanics of copyright law and corporate mergers to the fundamental health of democracy, showing how a $500 search fee effectively disenfranchises the citizenry. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on historical antitrust failures to predict future solutions; it identifies the problem with surgical precision but offers less clarity on how to dismantle a duopoly that is now deeply embedded in the legal profession's daily workflow. The reader must watch for whether current antitrust enforcement has learned from the "seedlings and giants" error of the 1990s, or if the gatekeepers remain unchallenged.

Introduction

Hi there! My name is Tom Blakely, I’m a federal judicial law clerk and attorney. I previously worked in big law and the United States Department of Justice. You might remember me from last year, when I wrote about the US v. Google ad tech antitrust trial at Big Tech on Trial. I’m excited to be writing a guest piece for BIG, this time on a particular competition problem I (and nearly every lawyer) deals with on a daily basis: the Lexis/Westlaw duopoly.

The law in America does not belong to lawyers, it belongs to all of us. As citizens, we must be able to read the law, understand it, obey it, and bring complaints against those who wrong us. That is the basis for a coherent political order, and why the rule of law is such a foundational aspect of a democratic system. Moreover, it would be hard to find a technological era more suited to the democratization of law than the one we’re in today. The computer lets anyone query instantly, the internet lets anyone do it from anywhere, and generative artificial intelligence can decipher and explain complex legal questions if it has access to credible databases.

And yet, today, two powerful companies dominate and restrain access to key aspects of our legal order, from case opinions to legislative history. To give you a sense of what I mean, Lexis, one of two dominant legal research platforms, charges up to $469 for a single search. That’s the equivalent of a Google query, for almost $500. Moreover, beyond LexisNexis and its rival Westlaw, the Federal judiciary itself has used its privileged position to charge high fees for court records that should be available to all. Today, the law is hidden behind paywalls.

But it wasn’t always this way.

In this piece, I’m going to explain how and why America keeps the law from its citizens, and what we can do about it.
