Algae - natures answer to fossil fuels and plastics!

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Feb 20, 2022 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace doesn't just present algae as a biological curiosity; he frames it as the planet's original climate engineer and its most promising, yet underfunded, repair kit. While most climate discussions focus on stopping emissions, Borlace argues that the very organism responsible for creating our breathable atmosphere and the fossil fuels we burn might be the key to reversing the damage. This is a compelling pivot from doom-laden statistics to a tangible, biological solution that operates on a scale far more efficient than traditional forestry.

The Irony of the Carbon Cycle

Borlace opens by dismantling the common perception of algae as mere "green slime" or the cause of toxic red tides. He reminds us that this "ability to proliferate so rapidly is precisely what made algae one of the most successful organisms ever to exist in nature." The author's most striking observation is the historical irony: the same process that created the breathable atmosphere and the oil deposits we are now burning is the one we must now harness to fix the climate. "So you could say it's quite ironic that an organism resulting in a substance that enabled human beings to cause catastrophic changes to our climate might just turn out to be one of the key materials to help us fix the problem," Borlace writes. This reframing is powerful because it connects deep geological time with immediate policy needs, suggesting the solution has been here all along.

The efficiency metrics Borlace cites are staggering. He notes that "a kilogram of algae absorbs 1.83 kilograms of carbon dioxide every day," which is exponentially faster than land-based trees. He points out that algae grows ten times faster than land plants, uses a tenth of the space, and requires significantly less water. This evidence suggests that if we can solve the engineering challenges, we could decouple carbon capture from the competition for arable land. However, critics might note that these laboratory or theoretical efficiencies often falter when faced with the chaotic variables of open-ocean farming or large-scale pond management.

The Commercial Hurdle

The narrative shifts from biological potential to commercial reality, where the story becomes one of missed opportunities and corporate hesitation. Borlace explains that while governments and oil giants like Shell and ExxonMobil invested heavily around 2015, the project stalled. "It turns out there are upwards of 30,000 different types of algae... and some estimates suggest there could be as many as a million different species on the planet," he notes. This biodiversity, while a sign of nature's resilience, has become a scientific bottleneck. The research required to genetically engineer a "superstrain" that is both fast-growing and oil-rich is described as "extremely expensive and extremely time-consuming."

The situation with ExxonMobil illustrates the complexity of the private sector's role. While they remain the only major oil company still pursuing this, their projected output of 10,000 barrels a day is a drop in the bucket compared to their 4 million barrels of crude oil production. Borlace highlights this disparity to show the sheer scale of the challenge: "when you set that against Exxon's current daily production of 4 million barrels of crude oil it kind of puts the scaling problem into quite stark context." This is a crucial point; the technology exists, but the economic model to make it competitive with cheap fossil fuels is still elusive. A counterargument worth considering is whether waiting for a perfect, genetically modified strain is the right strategy, or if we should be deploying existing, less efficient strains immediately to start the carbon drawdown.

Beyond Fuel: Food and Plastics

Perhaps the most promising section of Borlace's coverage is the pivot away from fuel toward bioplastics and food. He highlights a breakthrough where scientists used "metabolic engineering techniques" to increase the proportion of a biodegradable polymer in microalgae from 10 percent to 80 percent. "Unlike polypropylene which will be swimming around in our oceans for hundreds of years... phb breaks down in a normal household composter and if it did make its way into the ocean then it'd dissolve within a year or so," Borlace writes. This offers a direct solution to the plastic pollution crisis, not just the carbon crisis.

Furthermore, the potential for algae as a protein source is presented not as a dystopian "Soylent Green" scenario, but as a high-efficiency alternative to livestock. Borlace contrasts the emissions of beef production with algae, noting that producing protein from algae "actually removes 320 grams of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere." Even more compelling is the application in animal feed: adding a specific red algae to cow diets can reduce methane emissions by up to 90 percent. "Adding just two percent of this red seaweed to the diets of those animals has been shown to reduce their methane emissions by as much as 90 percent," he states. This is a rare instance where a solution addresses a specific, hard-to-abate sector of the economy without requiring a total overhaul of human dietary habits.

Unlike polypropylene which will be swimming around in our oceans for hundreds of years causing damage wherever it goes, this algae-based plastic breaks down in a normal household composter.

The Scale of the Solution

The piece concludes with a vision of massive oceanic farming. Borlace references a study suggesting that if we ramped up seaweed aquaculture to cover just 15 percent of suitable ocean areas, it could "completely offset all the greenhouse gas emissions from current global agriculture." This is a bold claim that reimagines the ocean as a carbon sink rather than just a resource to be harvested. However, he wisely cautions that such expansion requires "extremely careful stewardship to ensure it didn't get out of control and start killing off existing sea life and ecosystems." The argument here is not that algae is a silver bullet, but that it is a massive, necessary component of a broader strategy.

The final call to action is directed at both investors and lawmakers. Borlace suggests that the technology is ready, but the capital and policy incentives are lagging. He urges commercial investors to "grow some kahunas and speculate some of their billions" and for lawmakers to create the necessary incentives. This framing places the onus on human decision-making rather than scientific limitation, a refreshing and empowering conclusion to a technical discussion.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest argument lies in connecting the biological efficiency of algae to specific, scalable applications in plastics, food, and methane reduction, moving beyond the stalled promise of biofuel. The piece's biggest vulnerability is the reliance on future genetic breakthroughs and massive capital injection to overcome current economic hurdles, which remains a significant risk for immediate climate action. Readers should watch for the next wave of pilot projects in ocean farming, as these will determine if the theoretical carbon capture numbers can survive the reality of the open sea.
