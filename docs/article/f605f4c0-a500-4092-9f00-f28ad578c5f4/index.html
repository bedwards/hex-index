El Niño 2023 could be a monster!

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Apr 30, 2023 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace delivers a stark warning that cuts through the noise of seasonal forecasts: the ocean has been silently hoarding heat for three years, and it is now preparing to unleash it all at once. While many headlines focus on the immediate weather, Borlace's most distinctive claim is that the energy transfer from the 2023 El Niño event could rival the most extreme climate disruptions in human history, effectively turbo-charging global warming after a brief pause. This is not just a weather report; it is a data-driven alarm about the moment the planet's thermal battery finally discharges.

The Mechanics of a Triple Dip

Borlace begins by dismantling the assumption that the recent three-year cooling trend was a sign of climate stabilization. He explains that the "triple dip La Nina" was an anomaly that merely suppressed, rather than reversed, atmospheric warming. "Even with that unusual duration of temperature suppression, our planet still experienced three of the warmest years ever recorded in 2020, 21 and 22," he notes, establishing that the underlying heat engine never stopped. This framing is crucial because it prevents readers from mistaking a temporary lull for a solution. The author then pivots to the current shift, noting that the system has moved into a neutral phase with all signs pointing toward a powerful El Niño.

The commentary here is effective because it simplifies complex atmospheric physics into a relatable narrative of pressure and wind. Borlace describes how the trade winds, which usually push warm water toward Asia, are now weakening, allowing that heat to slosh back toward South America. "It's the rising air in the eastern part of the Pacific that causes more stormy and rainy conditions over here, meanwhile the air over in the Western Pacific is descending which tends to cause conditions that are more stable than average," he writes. By anchoring the explanation in the Southern Oscillation Index (SOI)—the pressure difference between Darwin and Tahiti—he provides a concrete metric for readers to track, rather than relying on vague predictions. Critics might argue that early-stage forecasts are notoriously unreliable, but Borlace wisely hedges by focusing on the trend of sea surface temperatures rather than a guaranteed outcome.

The Global Domino Effect

The piece shines when it moves from the Pacific to the rest of the world, illustrating how a localized ocean event ripples into a global crisis. Borlace offers a nuanced take on North American weather, dispelling the myth that El Niño is universally bad or good. He points out a silver lining for the Atlantic: "Stronger wind shear and higher pressure over the Atlantic caused by our El Niño events tends to reduce the overall number of hurricanes and tropical systems." However, he immediately counters this by detailing the devastation awaiting the southern United States, where a shifted jet stream could bring "lower temperatures and extreme storms to these Southern States."

This section is particularly strong because it uses historical data to ground the fear in reality. Borlace compares the current trajectory to the 1997 and 2016 events, the latter of which was driven by a massive release of oceanic energy. "The amount of energy transferred across the ocean during that event was equivalent to 100 times the total energy consumption of the entire human species over a whole year," he states, a figure that puts the scale of the phenomenon into terrifying perspective. He details the human cost of the 2016 event, from mudslides in California to droughts in Ethiopia, reminding us that these are not abstract temperature anomalies but drivers of humanitarian crises. The argument holds up well against scrutiny because it relies on the consensus of both European and North American forecasting agencies, rather than a single outlier model.

The three years of the triple dip La Nina that we've just experienced suppressed the atmospheric warming effect of rising greenhouse gas emissions, but now that pent-up oceanic energy is being liberated to turbo-charge the warming of our atmosphere.

The Hidden Cost of Ocean Heat

Perhaps the most alarming part of Borlace's coverage is the focus on the ocean's role as a heat sink. He explains that over 90% of the extra heat from industrial emissions has been absorbed by the oceans, a fact that often gets lost in discussions about air temperature. "Heat absorbed into the tropical Pacific is now extending down to a depth of more than 100 meters," he writes, highlighting the sheer volume of energy now in the system. This depth is significant because it means the heat is not just a surface phenomenon; it is a deep, structural change that will drive marine heat waves and coral bleaching for years to come.

Borlace connects this directly to the current record-breaking ocean temperatures, noting that 2023 has already surpassed the 2016 peak. "Average global ocean surface temperatures in 2023 have already beaten that record; they now sit at 21.1 degrees Celsius," he reports. This data point is the piece's smoking gun: the ocean is hotter now than it was during the strongest El Niño on record, even before the full atmospheric effects of the current event have kicked in. The implication is that the coming year could see unprecedented extremes in weather, sea-level rise, and ecosystem collapse. While the author acknowledges that local weather systems can muddy forecasts, his insistence that "El Niño's effects are definitely felt in every corner of the planet" is a necessary corrective to regional complacency.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest asset is his ability to translate dry meteorological data into a compelling narrative about the release of pent-up planetary energy, making the abstract concept of ocean heat feel immediate and dangerous. His biggest vulnerability is the inherent uncertainty of long-range forecasting, though he mitigates this by focusing on the overwhelming consensus of temperature trends rather than specific storm tracks. Readers should watch for the confirmation of the "El Niño watch" in the coming months, as the transition from neutral to active phase will likely mark the beginning of a record-breaking year for global temperatures."
