Is Peña Parra’s new job an exile, or probation?

Various · The Pillar ·Mar 30, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

The Pillar's analysis of Archbishop Edgar Peña Parra's sudden reassignment cuts through the Vatican's usual fog of diplomatic euphemisms to ask a question no one else is daring to pose: Is this a promotion, or a polite exile? The author, writing as Various, doesn't just report the personnel shuffle; they dissect the legal and canonical contradictions that made Peña Parra's eight-year tenure as the pope's chief of staff a lightning rod for scandal. This is essential listening for anyone trying to understand the new Pope Leo XIV's vision of "by-the-book" governance, because the move signals a sharp break from the informal, sometimes chaotic style of his predecessor.

The Weight of Eight Years

The piece immediately grounds the reader in the sheer anomaly of Peña Parra's longevity in the sostituto role. Various writes, "Having served eight years as sostituto, he served past both the historical norm for the role of five years, and the seven year stint achieved by his immediate predecessor, Cardinal Becciu." This detail is crucial because it highlights that his departure was not a sudden whim of the new pontiff, but the inevitable conclusion of a term that had already stretched its welcome. The author notes that even before Pope Francis's death, the curia was buzzing about his exit, suggesting the writing was on the wall long before the announcement.

The commentary effectively frames the sostituto not just as a chief of staff, but as a position of immense, unchecked power. The author points out that Peña Parra "emerged as a quiet pole of obvious influence and power under Pope Francis, clearly trusted by the late pontiff." Yet, this trust came with a heavy price. The text details how the archbishop stepped into the role during the height of the London property scandal, inheriting a mess of "accounting sleight-of-hand" and officials who "stone-walled oversight." The author's assessment is damning: "His decision proved costly in the form of serious papal embarrassment for Pope Francis, and the loss of hundreds of millions of euros."

"Peña Parra also appeared at the center of several controversies in which he appeared to act well outside his own, or indeed any, legal scope of action."

This observation is the pivot point of the entire article. The author argues that Peña Parra's style of "no-holds-barred engagement" was tolerated, and perhaps even encouraged, by a Pope Francis who favored unorthodox methods. However, the new Pope Leo appears to have no patience for such improvisation. The text notes that the archbishop "conceded under oath that his actions were not always honest or necessarily lawful," a stunning admission for a high-ranking prelate that the author uses to underscore the severity of the situation.

The Shadow of the Principi Affair

Perhaps the most explosive section of the coverage is the recounting of the September 2024 intervention in the case of Alberto Ariel Principi, an Argentine priest convicted of sexual abuse. The author describes how Peña Parra signed a letter vacating the priest's laicization, claiming an "extraordinary procedure" without citing any legal norms. The author writes, "Despite his double conviction for sexual abuse of a minor, Peña Parra vacated the decisions and the laicization, saying the priest was guilty only of 'recklessness,' and ordered him reinstated."

This incident is framed not just as a bureaucratic error, but as a direct challenge to the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith. The author notes that Archbishop John Kennedy, the head of the disciplinary section, had to "publicly intervene to nullify Peña Parra's attempt to rehabilitate the abuser." This detail is vital because it shows the sostituto overstepping his jurisdiction in one of the most sensitive areas of church law. The author suggests a darker undercurrent: "the possibility that Francis had personally, albeit informally, ordered his attempted rehabilitation of Principi was arguably the most plausible explanation for an otherwise incredible act of overreach."

Critics might note that the article leans heavily on the assumption that Francis directed these actions, a claim that remains unproven and relies on "curial wisdom" rather than documentary evidence. However, the author's point stands: the sostituto's actions were so irregular that they could only be explained by a unique, personal mandate that has now evaporated with the old pontiff's death.

A Prestigious Side-Lining

The article then turns to the destination itself: the nunciature to Italy and San Marino. The author argues that while this is a top-tier diplomatic post in terms of rank, it is functionally a "prestigious non-job." The reasoning is sound: "As Bishop of Rome, the pope already directly selects the president of the local bishops conference... and often plays a more than usually direct role in selecting bishops." Unlike other countries where the nuncio is the primary conduit for information, the proximity of the Italian state and the Vatican means the sostituto's new role is largely ceremonial.

The author draws a sharp distinction between the power of the sostituto and the limitations of the nuncio. "The archbishop's reassignment to a nunciature... represents a step back in the Vatican civil service hierarchy," they write. Yet, it is a step back wrapped in velvet. The text explains that this move was likely a "face-savingly senior diplomatic posting" designed to avoid a public scandal while removing Peña Parra from the levers of power.

"The Roman nunciature is an interesting means of squaring that circle."

The author suggests that this assignment is a test of loyalty and adaptation. At age 66, Peña Parra faces a choice: serve a full decade and potentially earn a cardinal's hat, or step out of line and face an early, ignominious retirement. The author notes that "how willing he proves to separate himself from the daily reality of Vatican power politics remains an open question." This is a prescient observation, especially given the archbishop's history of using "freelancing members of Italy's intelligence community" for surveillance. The question of whether he will truly step back or continue to operate in the shadows is the piece's lingering tension.

The article also wisely contextualizes this move within the broader history of the Vatican's financial reforms. It mentions that the sostituto had to "help bed in a new apostolic constitution in 2021, reorganizing the Roman curia," a task that required navigating the very legalistic framework that now seems to be his undoing. The author implies that the rules of the game have changed, and the old players are no longer welcome.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its unflinching connection between Peña Parra's specific legal transgressions—particularly the Principi affair and the surveillance scandals—and the new Pope's stated preference for rule-of-law governance. The author successfully argues that this is not a random personnel change but a calculated reset of the Vatican's operational culture. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the reliance on anonymous "curial wisdom" to explain the motivations of the late Pope Francis, leaving some of the most dramatic claims slightly speculative. Readers should watch closely to see if Peña Parra respects the boundaries of his new role or if his history of overreach suggests he will continue to operate just outside the law, even from the Italian side of the river.

Excerpt from the original article on The Pillar

Pope Leo XIV on Monday ordered the biggest curial personnel change of his first year in office, transferring the sostituto of the Secretariat of State, Archbishop Edgar Peña Parra, to serve as apostolic nuncio to Italy and San Marino.

Peña Parra has served as sostituto, who functions effectively as papal chief of staff in day-to-day curial operations, since 2018, when he was appointed to replace then-archbishop, soon to be Cardinal Angelo Becciu, who was promoted to lead the Congregation for Causes of Saints.

The archbishop’s eight year tenure as sostituto was a period of remarkable upheaval, both for the curia and the office, with Peña Parra having immediately to step into the throws of the Secretariat of State’s financial scandal, as well as help bed in a new apostolic constitution in 2021, reorganizing the Roman curia.

During his time in office, he emerged as a quiet pole of obvious influence and power under Pope Francis, clearly trusted by the late pontiff. However, Peña Parra also appeared at the center of several controversies in which he appeared to act well outside his own, or indeed any, legal scope of action.

His new assignment appears to emphasize Pope Leo’s emerging preference for by-the-book governance and the rule of law, though it also provides a position of considerable prestige for the archbishop. And, even if it likely constitutes an effective side-lining for one of the curia’s previously most influential operators, it could yet hold out the promise of prospects for the future.
