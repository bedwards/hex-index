Live IN Taiwan 3: The Big Vote

Chris Chappell · China Uncensored ·Jan 11, 2020 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Chris Chappell doesn't just report on an election; he captures the visceral, high-stakes energy of a democracy that the world is watching with bated breath. In "Live IN Taiwan 3: The Big Vote," the author argues that the true story isn't just who won, but the sheer transparency and civic fervor of a system that stands in stark contrast to the opaque, often cynical American process. For a busy reader, this is a rare glimpse into a political moment where the mechanics of democracy feel tangible, immediate, and surprisingly hopeful.

The Theater of Transparency

Chappell's most compelling observation centers on the physical act of counting votes. He describes a scene that feels almost archaic yet profoundly effective: "the way it works in Taiwan is you can actually go to the polling stations after they close and watch them count the ballots by hand." This isn't a metaphor for transparency; it is the literal reality. The author notes that while the process is "very low-tech," it is "very efficient" and creates a "double failsafe system" where mistakes are corrected instantly and fraud is virtually impossible to hide.

The contrast with the United States is implicit but deafening. Chappell writes, "I wish they were doing the US before voting if you could go like what some counties we have a low voter turnout rate... imagine how that would work if you could actually you know." This observation lands hard because it highlights a fundamental disconnect in American political culture, where trust in the system is often eroded by complexity and distance. The Taiwanese system, by allowing anyone to watch the count, turns a bureaucratic necessity into a communal event.

Critics might argue that low-tech counting is prone to human error or slower than digital systems, but Chappell's on-the-ground reporting suggests the opposite: the human element here ensures accountability that machines often obscure.

"In democracy isn't everybody can official or anything I guess I just have a higher opinion of the Taiwanese people though... it's all very low-tech but very efficient get results quick and very transparent."

The Shifting Political Landscape

Beyond the mechanics of voting, Chappell tackles the complex ideological battle between the two major parties: the Democratic Progressive Party (DPP) and the Kuomintang (KMT). He addresses a common misconception among international observers regarding the KMT's stance. "A lot of that you know wasn't was in charge of China the Republic of China driven out to Taiwan so people were surprised that the KMT is now the more Pro China palace party," he explains. The author clarifies that while the DPP leans toward sovereignty and independence, the KMT has pivoted toward closer economic ties and the idea of a shared Chinese identity.

The narrative arc Chappell constructs is one of political resilience. He details how the DPP, after a crushing defeat in 2018 that forced the party chairwoman to step down, managed a "remarkable turnaround." This resurgence was fueled by a strategic appeal to younger voters, utilizing pop culture and "anime drawings" to rebrand the party. Chappell notes, "she has managed a remarkable turnaround both of the DPP political image appealing to the youth vote especially anime drawings of tangle."

This cultural strategy is significant. It suggests that in a modern democracy, political survival isn't just about policy platforms but about connecting with the cultural zeitgeist. However, one might question whether this appeal to youth culture is sustainable or merely a temporary electoral tactic that could alienate older, more traditional voters in the long run.

A Global Stage

The stakes of this election extend far beyond the island. Chappell emphasizes that "the whole world is kind of watching what happens in these elections because it does have a big global impact." He points to the unprecedented support from social media platforms, noting that "Twitter even made a special election emotive emoji for those who do not know it's it's like a little like Taiwanese ballot." This digital validation underscores the international community's desire to see Taiwan's democratic process succeed, even as geopolitical tensions with China loom large.

The author captures the emotional weight of the moment, describing the atmosphere as a "night market" of political engagement where people are "freaking out know what to make about" the results. He reflects on the historical context, reminding readers that Taiwan only emerged from 38 years of martial law in 1987. "You can really beat here you really get a sense of people still remember this and really cherish their democracy," he observes. This historical memory serves as a powerful motivator for the high voter turnout and the intense emotional investment of the electorate.

Bottom Line

Chris Chappell's coverage succeeds by grounding a complex geopolitical event in the human experience of democracy. His strongest argument is that the transparency of the ballot-counting process serves as a powerful antidote to the cynicism plaguing Western democracies. The piece's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the optimism of the moment; while the DPP's victory seems assured, the long-term implications of a pro-independence stance on cross-strait relations remain a volatile variable. For the busy reader, this piece offers a vital reminder: democracy is not just a set of rules, but a living, breathing practice that requires constant vigilance and participation.
