Here's a light-hearted question for a YouTube video. How much is a human life worth? At least from a military perspective. We've talked extensively on this channel about how expensive war can be in terms of the munitions and equipment that it chews up and the economic damage it inflicts.

But some of the most painful losses in just about any war are going to be the human ones. Historically, we've seen societies and leaders come up with very different answers to how you value that kind of cost. with commanders ranging from bloody-minded idiots who might give little thought to expanding men by the tens or hundreds of thousands to those who treated the minimization of casualties as a key part of the military art. A recurring theme they might argue is that in many cases leaders have a tendency to undervalue the cost of sending people to war.

because what they might perceive to be a relatively cheap and abundant resource that can be rapidly mobilized to generate combat power is actually one of a nation and an economy's most valuable resources that might cost relatively little in the short term to use but an immense amount in the long term to lose. But to understand the deeper economics behind that statement, we need to cover a couple points in turn. First, the temptation. Why is manpower so useful as a military resource?

And why might some leaders historically have been tempted to overexpend it? Then I want to work through some of the obvious and more hidden costs of doing so. Going from the cost of turning a civilian into a soldier to some of the more long-running economic and demographic costs of caring for those who return and the price of missing those who don't. Then as an illustrative example of all those costs, we'll look at the current Russian war effort.

Both in terms of the comparatively small costs of Russia raising additional infantry and throwing them at the Ukrainians in the here and now, and the potentially much larger costs the country is going to have to pay for that kind of action in years to come.
