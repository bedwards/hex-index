Acquisition Marketplaces Are Blooming

Various · Defense Tech and Acquisition ·Mar 28, 2026 · 84 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

This piece cuts through the usual bureaucratic fog to deliver a stark reality: the U.S. defense industrial base is finally waking up to the fact that winning future wars requires mass, not just marvels. The author's most distinctive claim is that the era of "exquisite" high-cost systems is colliding with a new reality of "precise mass," where cheap, autonomous swarms render traditional defense budgets obsolete. For busy leaders tracking the FY27 budget request, the evidence here is immediate and alarming: the Pentagon is scrambling to quadruple missile production while admitting that less than half a percent of its budget currently funds the very low-cost systems needed to counter them.

The Budgetary Pivot and the Reconciliation Gamble

The article opens by anchoring the reader in the immediate fiscal calendar, noting that the Office of Management and Budget will deliver the FY27 request on April 3. However, the real story isn't the base number; it's the political maneuvering behind it. Various writes, "The number one priority of the federal government has always been keeping our homeland safe and keeping our enemies at bay over there so they can't hit us here." This sentiment, attributed to Senator Lindsey Graham, frames the upcoming second reconciliation bill not as a spending spree, but as a survival mechanism.

The coverage effectively highlights the tension between traditional procurement and the urgency of modern threats. As Rep. Mike Rogers puts it, "We're not talking about something frivolous here. We're talking about national defense." The argument here is that the legislative branch is finally recognizing that the current funding structure cannot sustain the pace of conflict seen in recent years. This lands because it connects abstract budget numbers to the tangible reality of soldiers in the field.

However, the piece glosses over the historical difficulty of passing reconciliation bills for defense purposes, a hurdle that has stalled similar efforts in the past. Critics might note that relying on a second reconciliation bill introduces significant political risk; if it fails, the "surge" in funding could vanish before it begins.

"The purpose of the second reconciliation bill is to make sure there is adequate funding to secure our homeland and to support our men and women in the military who are fighting so bravely."

The Industrial Base: From "Exquisite" to "Precise Mass"

The core of the article's argument shifts to the industrial base, specifically the desperate need to balance "high" and "low" cost munitions. The author points out a startling statistic: the Pentagon's recent Munitions Acceleration Council allocated 97% of funds to high-cost, exquisite munitions, leaving only 3% for low-cost developmental systems. Lt Gen Steven Whitney acknowledges this imbalance, stating, "We call this a high-low mix, and to be perfectly clear, both are needed."

The commentary here is sharp, noting that the U.S. is trying to correct a decades-long bias toward expensive, complex systems. The text draws a powerful parallel to the Precision Strike Missile (PrSM), which was used for the first time in combat against Iran in Operation Epic Fury. Lockheed Martin is now tasked with quadrupling production of this system, a move that signals a shift from prototyping to wartime footing. This is a crucial detail; it shows that the "surge" isn't just theoretical.

Yet, the article also exposes the fragility of this transition. As VADM Seiko Okano notes, "Part of finding the balance is stabilizing the industrial base we have right now." The argument is that without multi-year procurements to send a clear demand signal, factories cannot ramp up fast enough. This overlooks the sheer time it takes to retool factories, a lag that could leave the U.S. vulnerable in the short term even if the long-term plan is sound.

The coverage of the "precise mass" concept is particularly strong. Michael Horowitz's analysis, cited in the text, argues that we are in a new era where "states and nonstate actors... will be able to field low-cost precision weapons and sensors at scale." The author uses the example of the Shahed-136 drone to illustrate how cheap systems can overwhelm expensive defenses. This is the piece's most compelling insight: the cost curve has flipped.

"Defenders are on the wrong side of the cost curve and there are limited numbers of air defense interceptors available to defend against the kinds of salvos countries can generate with precise mass systems."

Unmanned Systems: The Gap Between Ambition and Reality

The article then pivots to unmanned systems, specifically the Navy's struggle with Unmanned Surface Vehicles (USVs). The coverage is refreshingly honest about the setbacks. It details how the Navy is pivoting away from the MASC program to a new MUSV marketplace, even as it deploys BlackSea's Global Autonomous Reconnaissance Craft (GARC) in active conflict. The text notes that these vessels logged over 450 hours in Operation Epic Fury, marking the first time Washington confirmed using such craft in war.

However, the author does not shy away from the failures. The piece recounts how a GARC collided with another boat at speed during a test and how another became inoperable in the Middle East. This context is vital. It suggests that while the U.S. is deploying these systems out of necessity, the technology is not yet mature. The article draws a parallel to the Terminal High Altitude Area Defense (THAAD) system, noting that BAE Systems is ramping up production, but unlike THAAD, which has a long track record, USVs are still proving their worth in the crucible of combat.

The commentary on the "drone wash-up" in Turkey adds a layer of urgency. An armed AEGIR-W variant drifted ashore and had to be destroyed, highlighting the risks of autonomous systems in contested waters. The author argues that the Navy must prioritize quantity and vendor diversity over perfection. As the text suggests, "The only measure is the number of USVs... that can successfully perform key mission sets."

Critics might argue that deploying unproven systems in active combat is reckless, potentially compromising sensitive technology if the vessels are captured or fail catastrophically. The article acknowledges this risk but frames it as a necessary trade-off for gaining real-world data.

"It is vitally important the Navy get as many USVs from many vendors into the waters into exercises and operations ASAP."

The Silicon Valley Disconnect and the Path Forward

The final section of the piece tackles the political and cultural friction between the Pentagon and the private sector. Trae Stephens of Anduril Industries delivers a scathing critique of congressional dysfunction, warning that "our federal government is not doing its job." The author uses Stephens' words to highlight a strategic opening for China, suggesting that American legislative gridlock is ceding the technological race.

The article contrasts this dysfunction with the agility of companies like AeroVironment, which introduced the LOCUST X3 laser weapon capable of destroying drones for under $5 per engagement. This specific fact drives home the "precise mass" argument: the technology exists to solve the cost problem, but the procurement system is too slow to adopt it at scale. The piece also highlights the General Dynamics-led "Leonidas" truck, a commercial vehicle retrofitted with microwave counter-drone tech, as a model for the future.

The inclusion of the President's Council of Advisors on Science and Technology (PCAST) and its high-profile members like Jensen Huang and Mark Zuckerberg suggests a potential bridge between Silicon Valley and the defense establishment. However, the article notes that "Silicon Valley's years of resistance to Pentagon work" have already allowed adversaries to grow stronger. This is a powerful indictment of the tech sector's past neutrality.

"If all you have is a hammer, every problem looks like a nail. When your main tools are investigations and the bully pulpit, the rules they write are often already obsolete by the time they take effect."

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its unflinching diagnosis of the "cost curve" problem: the U.S. cannot afford to fight a war of attrition with expensive missiles against cheap drones. The piece effectively uses the "precise mass" framework to explain why the current budget and procurement models are failing. Its biggest vulnerability is the assumption that the political will exists to shift funding from "exquisite" to "low-cost" systems quickly enough to matter. The reader should watch for the FY27 budget request on April 3 to see if the rhetoric of "surge" translates into actual dollars for low-cost munitions and autonomous systems.
