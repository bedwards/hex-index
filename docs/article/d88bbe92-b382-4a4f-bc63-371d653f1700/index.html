Military Laws Broken: Top Gun

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·May 25, 2022 · 22 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just analyze a movie; he weaponizes a former Marine prosecutor's experience to expose the legal carnage hidden behind Hollywood's coolest dogfights. By bringing in Spencer, a real-life judge advocate who prosecuted pilots at the very airbase where Top Gun was set, Stone transforms a nostalgic rewatch into a chilling indictment of military insubordination. The result is a rare piece of pop-culture commentary that treats the UCMJ (Uniform Code of Military Justice) with the same gravity as the film treats its aerial maneuvers.

The Cost of Cool

Stone's central thesis is immediate and devastating: Maverick isn't a hero; he's a defendant waiting to happen. He argues that the film's most iconic moments are actually textbook violations of military law. Stone writes, "The crime is improperly hazarding an aircraft and the reality of that crime is that in time of war you could get the death penalty for that." This reframing is jarring because it strips away the cinematic gloss to reveal the bureaucratic and lethal reality of the military chain of command. Stone emphasizes that the rules of engagement are not suggestions; they are the thin line between a successful mission and an international incident.

The commentary shines when Stone dissects the "buzz the tower" scene. He notes that Maverick's actions constitute willful disobedience of a superior officer, reckless endangerment, and conduct unbecoming an officer. Stone points out the severity of this behavior: "In the real world not only would he get grounded he would probably lose his wings entirely and spend the rest of his time on his contract like filling sandbags or doing something very menial in an office." This is a crucial correction to the audience's perception. While the movie celebrates Maverick's rebellious spirit, Stone highlights that in a real military structure, such recklessness endangers not just the pilot, but the entire unit and national security.

Critics might argue that Stone applies a rigid legal standard to a fictional narrative designed for entertainment, potentially missing the point of the character arc. However, Stone's expertise lends weight to his analysis; he isn't just guessing at the rules, he has prosecuted pilots for exactly these infractions. His assertion that "every single soldier marine sailor is taught before going on a deployment is you are allowed to act in self-defense" grounds the film's tension in actual military doctrine, making the fictional stakes feel terrifyingly real.

"Rule number one in the military is look cool. Trouble come on man."

The Human Element and Legal Realities

Beyond the aerial violations, Stone and his guest Spencer tackle the interpersonal dynamics that often go overlooked in action films. They address the absurdity of Maverick's call sign, with Stone noting, "No one has a cool call sign like that... if you were to go into a place and say I want my call sign to be Maverick you would probably be hazed to the point of death." This observation adds a layer of cultural authenticity to the legal critique, showing that the military has its own social codes that are just as strict as its written laws.

The discussion also delves into the complexities of relationships within the ranks. Stone explains that while adultery is not a crime for civilians, it is a serious offense for service members if it is "prejudicial to good order and discipline." He illustrates this with a real-world anecdote: "I actually was a military prosecutor assigned to what is now Marine Corps Air Station Miramar... one pilot got entirely dismissed from the Marine Corps. He was a test pilot for an F-35 and it was for adultery." This anecdote serves as a stark reminder that the military justice system operates under a different moral and legal framework than civilian life, where personal conduct is inextricably linked to professional viability.

Stone also tackles the unrealistic portrayal of civilian expertise, specifically Charlie's PhD in astrophysics. He quips, "Unless they're charting with the stars, I'm not sure her PhD is going to help that much." While this might seem like a minor nitpick, it underscores Stone's broader point about the film's tendency to prioritize drama over technical accuracy. He balances this by acknowledging that civilian contractors are common, but their roles are usually more specialized than the film suggests.

The Verdict on Maverick

Ultimately, Stone's commentary serves as a defense attorney's closing argument against Maverick's actions. He advises the character to "remain silent" and notes that the evidence against him is overwhelming. Stone writes, "He certainly willfully disobeyed the order of a superior commissioned officer... some of those could carry the death penalty." The sheer volume of charges Stone lists—disobedience, reckless operation, hazarding an aircraft—paints a picture of a pilot who is a liability rather than an asset.

The piece effectively uses the film as a vehicle to educate the audience on the UCMJ without becoming a dry lecture. By weaving in direct quotes from the movie and contrasting them with the harsh realities of military law, Stone creates a compelling narrative that challenges the viewer to see the film anew. He concludes that while Maverick's actions make for great cinema, they would result in a career-ending court-martial in reality.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's analysis is a masterclass in applying real-world expertise to pop culture, successfully arguing that Maverick's rebellion is not just dangerous, but criminal. The piece's greatest strength is its reliance on authentic legal precedent and personal experience, which lends undeniable authority to its conclusions. However, the argument occasionally leans too heavily on the literal application of law to a fictional story, potentially underestimating the narrative necessity of Maverick's rule-breaking for the film's emotional core.
