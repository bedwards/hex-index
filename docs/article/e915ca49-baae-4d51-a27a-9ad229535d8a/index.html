Could the Second Mexican Empire have endured?

Matt Yglesias · Slow Boring ·Mar 14, 2026 · 18 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Matt Yglesias turns a casual question about a 19th-century Mexican emperor into a sharp critique of how we understand political stability and electoral reform. While the piece opens with a self-deprecating reflection on aging film tastes, the real value lies in Yglesias's counterintuitive assertion that tinkering with primary election systems is a distraction from the actual work of organizing for better ideas.

The Illusion of Electoral Mechanics

Yglesias begins by addressing a reader's query about the Second Mexican Empire, asking if Emperor Maximilian could have succeeded without U.S. intervention. He notes that while Maximilian was installed by French forces to collect debts, he "actually turned out to have fairly liberal views: sticking with freedom of religion, confirming the legitimacy of previous sales of church property, and so forth." This historical nuance is crucial; it reveals that the conflict wasn't a simple binary of conservative monarchy versus liberal republic, but a complex ideological struggle where the "conservative" emperor was more liberal than the opposition. Yglesias points out that Maximilian even "tried to pardon Ju&#225;rez and bring him into the political system," only to be refused by the republican leader who maintained U.S. support after the Civil War.

The author uses this to argue that the French intervention was a strategic blunder by Napoleon III, suggesting that "they should have just eaten the losses on the loans and not bothered with all of this." He posits an alternate history where the U.S. and France might have negotiated a deal to stabilize a liberal order, but ultimately concludes that the outcome was driven by the Monroe Doctrine and debt collection rather than any inherent stability of the monarchy.

"From a Mexican perspective, this was an ideological struggle between liberals and conservatives, but one in which the ostensible head of the conservative faction was actually pretty liberal."

This historical detour serves as a metaphor for Yglesias's broader argument: we often obsess over the wrong variables. Just as the fate of the Mexican Empire hinged on external geopolitical shifts rather than internal institutional design, Yglesias argues that modern American polarization is not caused by the mechanics of primary elections. He writes, "The main reason that primaries empower extremists is not that primary voters are particularly extreme themselves — it's that extreme actors are a lot more engaged and activated."

Critics might note that structural reforms like Alaska's top-four primary system have shown tangible results in electing moderates like Lisa Murkowski, suggesting that the rules of the game do matter. However, Yglesias remains skeptical that changing the rules is the silver bullet, arguing instead that the problem is a lack of informed voters who defer to advocacy groups.

The Real Barrier: Organizing Over Tinkering

Yglesias dismantles the idea that electoral reform is the primary solution to polarization. He argues that "we don't have an informed electorate; we have an electorate that is deferential to advocacy groups." He illustrates this by noting that being "bad" on labor might simply mean supporting teacher salaries over seniority, or that being "bad" on the environment could mean favoring natural gas exports for energy security. These nuances are lost when voters rely on brand-name organizations that may be "relentlessly hostile to nuclear power" or stuck in "'70s degrowth thinking."

The author's solution is not to redesign the primary system, but to engage in "first-order organizing projects on behalf of better ideas." He points to the YIMBY movement as proof that "a relatively modest amount of money invested in YIMBY organizing has made a huge difference to national politics." This is a pragmatic, if demanding, approach: the fix isn't a new voting algorithm, but a new coalition of people willing to do the hard work of persuasion.

"If people who think the Republicans are kinda crazy but Democrats are also kinda crazy in a totally different way actually bother to do the work, you can get different outcomes."

Yglesias suggests that if the goal is genuine reform, the focus should shift to proportional representation. He argues this would make gerrymandering irrelevant and force states to acknowledge their median voters, noting that "in a deep blue state like California, the center of political gravity is a moderate Democrat, not a far-leftist." This structural change, he believes, would allow for better policy outcomes like Medicaid expansion in Texas or fixing public universities in California, without relying on "weird unicorn figures" to break partisan logjams.

Bottom Line

Yglesias's most compelling contribution is the shift from procedural fixes to cultural and organizational organizing, arguing that "the proximate solution to extremism isn't to engage in a long-term organizing project to alter the primary system — it's simply to engage in first-order organizing projects on behalf of better ideas." While his dismissal of primary reform may underestimate the power of structural incentives, his emphasis on the need for an informed electorate and the specific failure of advocacy groups to represent nuanced policy positions offers a necessary corrective to the current reformist fervor. The piece succeeds in reframing the debate: the problem isn't how we vote, but what we are voting for and who is telling us how to think.
