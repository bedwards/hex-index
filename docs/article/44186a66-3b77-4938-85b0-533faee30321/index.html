The best movies of the century so far

Hal Johnson · ·Jan 4, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Hal Johnson doesn't just list movies; he weaponizes his own subjectivity to expose the absurdity of "definitive" rankings, arguing that the only honest approach to the last twenty-five years of cinema is to embrace the "foolish and embarrassing blunder." He challenges the very math of the century, admitting he lets the year 2000 count because he "doesn't believe in math," a playful admission that sets the stage for a list where the only rule is the rule of the author's gut. This isn't a curated canon for the academic; it is a passionate, messy defense of films that refuse to be polite, from the decade-long production of Boyhood to the surreal chaos of Princess Raccoon.

The Architecture of Excess and the Human Cost of History

Johnson's selection process is a deliberate rejection of the "three-act structure" as a moral imperative, favoring instead films where "characters' insane actions gel into something more than just the arbitrary requirements" of standard storytelling. He champions Samaritan Girl and Primer not for their polish, but for their willingness to demand that the viewer "take so many notes," suggesting that true engagement requires effort rather than passive consumption. This is a vital correction to the modern trend of algorithmic content designed to be effortlessly digestible.

The list takes a sharp turn into the realm of historical trauma with The Act of Killing, a documentary where Johnson finds a "humanist film" emerging from an "insane" premise: getting mass-murderers to reenact their crimes. He notes that by invoking the word "murderer," he is "asserting power over both them and to some extent the Indonesian government," a bold admission of the critic's role in shaping historical memory. This choice is particularly poignant given the real-world context of the 1965–66 Indonesian mass killings, where hundreds of thousands were slaughtered in a state-sponsored anti-communist purge that was long denied or minimized by the very officials featured in the film. Johnson argues that the film's power lies in its ability to persuade the viewer that they have "learned something about both Indonesian anti-communism and humanity," even if the source of that knowledge is a surreal nightmare.

"The mass-murderers here are, of course, often officially on the side of right and justice, and by invoking the word 'murderer' I am asserting power over both them and to some extent the Indonesian government."

Critics might argue that framing a film about genocide as a "humanist" work risks sanitizing the horror, but Johnson's point is that the film forces a confrontation with the banality of evil that traditional documentaries often miss. He extends this interest in the "persuasive" over the "realistic" to Apocalypto, dismissing complaints about Mel Gibson's personal controversies to focus on how the film depicts "stone age people as, you know, people, as opposed to positive or negative cliches." He finds the opening "fascinating" and the ending "terrifying," noting it is a "rare use of dramatic irony that doesn't feel pandering and cheap."

The Power of the Uncomfortable and the Mission

Johnson's commentary on performance is equally unflinching, particularly regarding Christine, where he admits he "barely understand acting" except in the case of Rebecca Hall, who "manages to look uncomfortable in her own skin for every moment of this movie's two hours." He acknowledges the pain of watching this biopic is "thematically intentional," a stark reminder that cinema can be a vehicle for suffering rather than just entertainment. This willingness to sit with discomfort extends to Mother!, which he describes as a "Kafkaesque nightmare" that is "as busy and excessive as its exclamation point." He asks, "Has any movie been this self-serious and yet this playful since Buñuel's catalog?" suggesting that the film's frustration is a feature, not a bug.

The list also celebrates the "hard-core" nature of character motivation, using a bizarre comparison to the 1973 Japanese film Lady Snowblood to explain the emotional core of Up. Johnson argues that Carl's refusal to help a child in peril until he achieves his goal is a "hard-core moment in all of American cinema," a testament to the power of a singular, obsessive mission. He writes, "She stabs them both! Yuki has a mission, and nothing is going to distract her from her mission," using this extreme example to highlight the purity of intent in Pixar's work. This is a refreshing take that elevates animation to the level of high-stakes drama, arguing that the "melodramatic opening" of Up is as compelling as any revenge fantasy.

"It was like jumping from the hot tub to the pool—of course the pool feels cold! When I was ready for the downward arc in quality, the contrast didn't seem as great, and, sure, it's a drop, but it drops from an empyreal height!"

Johnson's analysis of There Will Be Blood reveals his appreciation for films that sustain tension through contrast rather than consistency. He admits he initially thought the film was the "greatest movie ever made," then revised it to "the greatest movie of the last ten years," before settling on it as a top-three film of the year, only to realize the "downward arc" was part of the design. This self-correction mirrors his approach to the entire list: a willingness to be wrong, to be surprised, and to let the art challenge his own expectations. He concludes with 2046, a film he calls a "busy shaggy-dog portrait of a man in despair" that works despite its "execrable CGI," because the "slight distance provided by reading this stuff makes it sound beautiful."

The Verdict on a Century of Cinema

Johnson's ultimate claim is that the best films of the century are those that defy categorization, from the "rock-and-roll space western musical" of The American Astronaut to the "nutty grifter comedy" of Parasite. He argues that Get Out succeeds because it feels "thought out" and believes in its world, while Barb and Star Go to Vista Del Mar earns its spot for going "so much further off the rails" than its predecessors. The list is a testament to the idea that cinema is not about perfection, but about the "excess" that makes a film memorable.

"None of this should work, and if things had been a little different it wouldn't have, and the whole house of cards would have come tumbling down. But it works, and 2046 is the best film of the last twenty-five years."

Critics might note that Johnson's reliance on personal taste and obscure references like Lady Snowblood or The Atomic Brain could alienate readers looking for a more accessible guide. However, this exclusivity is precisely the point: he is writing for those who want to be challenged, not comforted. His refusal to apologize for his "foolish and embarrassing" choices is a call to arms for a more engaged, less cynical viewership.

Bottom Line

Hal Johnson's list is a masterclass in subjective criticism, proving that the most compelling arguments about art come from a place of unapologetic passion rather than objective consensus. Its greatest strength is the willingness to connect disparate genres and historical traumas through the lens of "persuasion" over "realism," while its biggest vulnerability is its reliance on niche references that may require a deep well of film knowledge to fully appreciate. For the busy reader, this piece offers a reminder that the best way to engage with the world is to let it surprise you, even if it means taking notes on a time-travel movie or watching a man stab his way through a mission.

The best movies of the century so far

by Hal Johnson · · Read full article

People keep making these lists and they’re all foolish and embarrassing blunders, so I thought I’d make my own blunder, as foolish and embarrassing as it may be. I let the year 2000 count as this century (because I don't believe in math), and I was open to letting 2025 count as well, but no current movie, not even the ones you like so much, cracked the top 25, which is where I stopped.

I limited things to one entry per director, for reasons unclear to me. This post is of course too long for an email, so click through to find out what is number one (“…will surprise you”).

As always: FIGHT ME!

25 Boyhood (2014).

I love a good gimmick, and spending a decade slowly making a film (you know, like they did with Stranger Things ha ha) is even better than a Tingler. Epic in the way that’s hardest to fake.

24 Another Year (2010).

Perhaps not the best Mike Leigh film, but his best of this century, a quiet, pleasant, melancholy year in the life of a couple and their friends. Compare this movie to the frenetic, unpleasant, implausible Four Seasons mini-series. Back in 2010 people could make movies about a worthwhile life that wasn’t overstocked with signifiers of wealth.

23 Samaritan Girl (2004).

Like many a Kim Ki-duk film, this is partially a horror story, partially a portrait of strange psychology, and partially…just weird. But unlike in most movies—not most KKD movies, I mean, but in most movies—the characters’ insane actions gel into something more than just the arbitrary requirements of the the three-act structure.

22 Primer (2004).

I’m not sure most movies should require a viewer to take so many notes, but maybe it’s good at least one does. Time travel without the nonsense.

21 Apocalypto (2006).

There’s a story beat I love, when the barbarian from the hinterlands enters Rome (or fantasy-Rome-analog) and looks around with that just where on Earth am I? only it doesn’t quite work because for viewers few things are less alien than Rome (or fantasy-Rome-analog). Well, Apocalypto squares that circle because the city our barbarians come to is nothing like Rome or (for most of us) like anything previously encountered.
