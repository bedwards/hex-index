"Notes on the state of Virginia" - chapter 3

PILCROW · ·Nov 20, 2025 ·32 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

PILCROW transforms a standard sports narrative into a visceral meditation on the fragility of the human body against the relentless machinery of competition. By embedding the story of a collegiate hockey team within a fictionalized retelling of Thomas Jefferson's Notes on the State of Virginia, the author creates a unique tension between the cold logic of statistics and the chaotic reality of illness. This is not merely a story about a game; it is an examination of how institutions demand perfection from individuals who are quietly falling apart.

The Architecture of Illness

The piece opens by establishing the stakes through a roster of elite athletes, treating their physical prowess with the reverence usually reserved for historical figures. PILCROW writes, "Time was not a healer; it was not a salve; it was merely the sad unwinder of uncertainty." This reframing of time as an antagonist rather than a restorative force sets a somber tone that permeates the entire narrative. The author meticulously details the protagonist, Emmy Noether, not just as a player, but as a complex entity defined by her chemistry major, her Jewish heritage, and her role as a maternal figure to her brothers.

By invoking the name Emmy Noether, the text subtly bridges the gap between the fictional narrative and the real-world mathematical genius who revolutionized physics and algebra. Just as the real Noether faced institutional barriers as a woman in science, this fictionalized Emmy faces a biological betrayal that threatens her place on the ice. The author notes that "corpuscular agents were transforming her lymphatic process to malevolence through white-celled abundance," a phrase that elevates the description of a cold to a biological coup d'état. This scientific precision mirrors the mathematical rigor of the real Noether, suggesting that the body, like the universe, follows laws that can turn against the observer.

"Change was clearly afoot in late February (a godless month of limited light, we of the north can agree) when the line's play suffered, because cohesion derives from the center position and that player—Emmy (of small physical stature)—was tiring and losing precious fragments of precision and pace."

The commentary here is striking because it refuses to romanticize the athlete's resilience. Instead, it highlights the systemic pressure that forces a sick player to compete. The coach's speech, urging the team to "push their bodies far beyond limits of normalcy," stands in stark contrast to Emmy's internal reality. PILCROW captures the dissonance perfectly: "Energy and purpose arose in the girls around her, but Emmy stood on a floor that felt skewing, spinning, falling." This moment underscores a critical vulnerability in high-performance culture: the expectation that the individual must suppress their humanity to serve the collective goal.

The Physics of the Game

As the game begins, the narrative shifts from internal monologue to the kinetic chaos of the rink. PILCROW describes the sport through the lens of a physicist, noting that "Hockey, for her, was rather like velocity—a scientific function, where the game was defined by an output relative to time, to wit, goals per period." This perspective aligns with the real Emmy Noether's life work, where she sought to understand the symmetries and conservation laws governing the physical world. In this fictionalized account, the ice becomes a laboratory, and the players are variables in a complex equation.

The author details a specific play where Emmy, despite her sickness, executes a pass with "facile ease," a moment where "due deliberation dissolved into remission and gave way to a state of innateness." This description suggests that muscle memory and instinct can transcend conscious suffering, a theme that resonates with the real Noether's ability to perceive deep mathematical truths that others missed. The narrative draws a parallel between the practiced drills of the rink and the rigorous training of the mind, suggesting that excellence is a form of transcendence over physical limitation.

However, the story does not shy away from the cost of this transcendence. The referee's "penchant for laxness in interpreting the prohibition against body checking" adds a layer of institutional danger to the game. Critics might argue that this focus on the officiating distracts from the internal drama of Emmy's illness, but PILCROW uses it to heighten the stakes. The physical danger is not just a risk of the sport; it is an externalization of the internal threat Emmy faces. As the author puts it, "The peril for Emmy was exacerbated by referee Pettis... Dartmouth would target the slight Emmy for an abundance of contact at the edge of legality."

"With her pass complete, Virginia continued ahead quickly on powerful strides. Emmy crossed the blue line with Virginia close behind... In rapid, unthinking calculation, Emmy turned herself left, briefly becoming completely blind to the puck."

This sequence is a masterclass in pacing. The short, staccato sentences mimic the speed of the game, while the description of Emmy's "unthinking calculation" highlights the disconnect between her mind and her failing body. The author effectively uses the setting of Lynah Rink, a real and iconic venue, to ground the fiction in a tangible reality. The specific detail of the ice being "chilled to twenty-five degrees" and the blades "cut sharply to a deep radius" adds a layer of sensory authenticity that makes the emotional weight of the scene more palpable.

The Cost of Perfection

The narrative culminates in a goal that feels less like a triumph and more like a desperate act of defiance. After scoring, Emmy "skated slowly to the bench, feeling as if her body was completely broken." This final image is the emotional core of the piece. It challenges the traditional sports trope of the heroic comeback, replacing it with a more nuanced reality where victory comes at a severe personal cost. The author writes, "Fires already burned in her lungs and legs and she feared failure," a line that captures the terror of an athlete who knows their body is failing them.

The piece also touches on the theme of memory and loss. Emmy's father, a German industrialist, and her late mother are woven into the narrative as sources of both strength and sorrow. The author notes that she practiced "every facet of play until due deliberation dissolved into remission," a phrase that echoes the mathematical concept of remission in disease, blurring the lines between her athletic training and her medical condition. This dual meaning adds a layer of poignancy to the story, suggesting that the drive for perfection is a double-edged sword.

"Hers, however, was a body in deep somatic rift—diseased by a significant misworking."

This line serves as a powerful indictment of the pressure placed on young athletes. The "somatic rift" is not just a physical ailment; it is a symptom of a system that demands the impossible. The author's choice to frame this through the lens of a fictionalized Notes on the State of Virginia invites readers to consider the broader implications of how we value human potential. Just as Jefferson's notes were a mix of observation and ideology, this story is a mix of athletic glory and human tragedy.

Critics might note that the heavy use of literary allusion and the specific historical references could alienate readers looking for a straightforward sports story. However, PILCROW's approach enriches the narrative, providing a depth that transcends the genre. The inclusion of Wallace Stevens' poetry and the real-life figure of Emmy Noether elevates the story from a simple tale of a hockey game to a broader meditation on the human condition.

Bottom Line

PILCROW's piece is a triumph of narrative voice, successfully blending the rigor of scientific observation with the emotional depth of literary fiction. Its strongest element is the refusal to glorify the athlete's suffering, instead presenting it as a tragic consequence of a system that demands perfection. The biggest vulnerability lies in its dense, allusive style, which may require a patient reader to fully appreciate the historical and mathematical parallels. Ultimately, this is a story that demands we look beyond the scoreboard to see the human cost of our pursuit of excellence.

  • Emmy Noether

    The fictional character shares her name with the pioneering mathematician Emmy Noether, who was also from Erlangen, Germany. This deliberate naming connects to the character's mathematics major and German origins, making the real Emmy Noether's groundbreaking contributions to abstract algebra and theoretical physics a rich contextual read.

  • Lynah Rink

    The championship game takes place at Lynah Rink, Cornell's historic ice hockey arena known for its intense atmosphere and devoted fans. Understanding the venue's significance in college hockey culture adds depth to the story's setting.

  • Wallace Stevens

    The chapter opens with an epigraph from Wallace Stevens' poem 'The Man with the Blue Guitar.' Stevens was a Pulitzer Prize-winning American modernist poet whose work explored imagination versus reality—themes that resonate with the story's literary ambitions.
