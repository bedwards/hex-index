More on US pedestrian deaths

Brian Potter · Construction Physics ·Oct 16, 2025 ·17 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Most analysis of the surge in US pedestrian deaths points to a single villain: the rising popularity of massive SUVs. But in this rigorous follow-up, Brian Potter dismantles that easy narrative, revealing a far more tangled and unsettling reality. The data suggests that while vehicle size matters, it is not the sole driver of a crisis where deaths have climbed nearly 80% since 2009. This piece matters because it forces us to confront a problem that defies simple fixes, leaving us with a confusing but critical truth: the streets are becoming deadlier for reasons we haven't yet fully understood.

The SUV Hypothesis Crumbles

Potter begins by revisiting the most compelling theory from his previous work: that larger vehicles are crushing pedestrians. He initially found that fatalities rose even as overall collisions remained flat, a pattern suggesting increased lethality per crash. However, a deeper dive into the data from the National Highway Transportation Safety Administration (NHTSA) complicates the picture. "The piece of evidence that most compelled me was this: the rate of pedestrian fatalities has increased substantially across many different states, even as overall pedestrian collisions have stayed close to constant," Potter writes. This logic initially pointed squarely at the rise of big SUVs.

Yet, the data refuses to cooperate with a simple explanation. Potter notes that "the number of pedestrians killed by sedans seems to be rising as well. That can't be caused by SUVs getting bigger and more popular." This observation is crucial; it suggests that the lethality of all vehicles is increasing, or that external factors are making every collision more likely to end in death. Critics might argue that the sheer mass of modern vehicles, regardless of type, is the issue, but Potter's analysis shows the trend is too broad to be pinned on one vehicle class alone.

Darkness and Speed: A Dangerous Shift

The investigation takes a sharp turn when examining when these deaths occur. Potter admits a significant error in his prior work regarding the time of day, correcting himself based on a New York Times report. "The NYT is correct; This was an error on my part that stemmed from not looking closely enough at the data behind a bad summary statistic," he concedes. The corrected data reveals a stark disparity: while daylight deaths rose 28%, fatalities in darkness surged by 103%.

This shift toward night-time tragedy changes the entire landscape of the problem. It implies that visibility, street lighting, or driver behavior after dark are central to the crisis. Potter explores the theory that minor increases in speed could be the culprit, noting that "going from 23 mph to 32 mph increases the risk of death from 10% to 25%." However, the data contradicts the idea that small speed bumps are the primary cause. Deaths have risen across all speed limits, including on roads with limits of 70 mph or higher where death was already nearly guaranteed. "This doesn't seem consistent with 'minor increases in speeding' as a cause of increased deaths," Potter concludes. The increase is most dramatic at the highest speed limits, where the risk has nearly tripled.

Deaths in daylight are only up 28% since 2009; deaths in darkness or lit darkness are up 103% and 87%, respectively.

Where the Danger Lives

Perhaps the most disturbing finding is the geographic shift in these tragedies. The danger is no longer concentrated in dense urban cores but is migrating to the suburbs. Potter cites a 2023 study showing that "pedestrian fatalities appear to be concentrating in lower-density suburbs with lower-income, lower-education, and minority populations." In major cities like Los Angeles and Chicago, the hotspots have moved from downtown areas to the periphery.

This demographic and geographic migration suggests that infrastructure designed for cars, rather than people, is failing vulnerable populations in low-density areas. The lack of sidewalks, poor lighting, and wide, fast roads in these suburbs create a death trap that didn't exist in the same way in the past. Potter notes that while some cities like New York and Minneapolis have reduced speed limits without seeing the same spike in deaths, others like Seattle have not. This inconsistency leaves policymakers without a clear playbook.

The Pandemic Paradox and Human Cost

The timeline of the data offers another clue, specifically around the 2020 pandemic lockdowns. One might expect fewer cars to mean fewer deaths, but the opposite happened for pedestrians. "In Arizona, for instance, the number of daylight pedestrian crashes declined by 27% from 2019 to 2020; nighttime crashes only declined by 3%," Potter observes. While people stayed home during the day, the traffic that remained at night was deadlier.

This period also highlights a tragic correlation with the homeless population. While the rise in homelessness began later than the rise in pedestrian deaths, the overlap is significant in major cities. Potter points out that in some jurisdictions, a staggering fraction of those killed are unhoused. "It also seems that while homeless deaths are a large fraction of pedestrian deaths in cities, they're still a relatively small fraction of pedestrian deaths overall," he writes, estimating they account for roughly 13% of the total. Yet, the rate of traffic fatalities among the homeless has quadrupled since 2011. This is not just a traffic statistic; it is a humanitarian crisis where the most vulnerable are being systematically removed from the streets.

A Puzzle Without a Solution

Potter's investigation ultimately leads to a frustrating conclusion: the easy answers are wrong. He examines theories ranging from distracted pedestrians to demographic shifts, finding little evidence to support them as primary drivers. Even the comparison with bicyclists, whose deaths have risen 86%, suggests a systemic issue affecting all vulnerable road users, yet the specific cause remains elusive. "Unfortunately, this exercise left me more confused than before," Potter admits. "What previously seemed like the most promising explanation (the rise of large SUVs) now looks less compelling. But no other explanation has emerged to take its place."

Unfortunately, this exercise left me more confused than before. What previously seemed like the most promising explanation (the rise of large SUVs) now looks less compelling.

This intellectual honesty is the piece's greatest strength. Potter refuses to force a narrative where the data doesn't fit. He highlights that while countries like Canada have similar SUV markets, they haven't seen the same explosion in pedestrian deaths, suggesting something uniquely American about our road culture, infrastructure, or enforcement is at play.

Bottom Line

Brian Potter's analysis is a masterclass in data-driven skepticism, proving that the rise in pedestrian deaths is a complex, multi-faceted failure rather than a single policy error. Its greatest vulnerability is the lack of a definitive solution; by debunking the SUV myth, it leaves readers with a problem that is harder to solve, not easier. The strongest takeaway is the urgent need to address the specific conditions of night-time driving and suburban infrastructure, where the most lethal trends are concentrated.

The human cost of this confusion is measured in lives lost every night on roads that were never designed for the people walking on them. Until we can identify the true drivers of this lethality, the trend will likely continue, turning our streets into increasingly dangerous spaces for the most vulnerable among us.

More on US pedestrian deaths

by Brian Potter · Construction Physics · Read full article

Last week I wrote about the unexplained rise in pedestrians killed by motor vehicles in the US. Since 2009, pedestrian deaths have increased by almost 80%, following multiple decades of falling rates.

I looked through data on pedestrian fatalities from the National Highway Transportation Safety Administration (NHTSA), but I couldn’t find a singular explanation that satisfied me. The piece of evidence that most compelled me was this: the rate of pedestrian fatalities has increased substantially across many different states, even as overall pedestrian collisions have stayed close to constant. This suggests that pedestrian accidents are getting more deadly, which in turn suggests that the increase in popularity of big SUVs might be causing the increase in deaths. However, the number of pedestrians killed by sedans seems to be rising as well. That can’t be caused by SUVs getting bigger and more popular.

This article got lots of attention, and readers had many theories about the drivers of the increase. In this follow-up post, I wanted to further investigate some of the most compelling theories and angles on the question.

Unfortunately, this exercise left me more confused than before. What previously seemed like the most promising explanation (the rise of large SUVs) now looks less compelling. But no other explanation has emerged to take its place.

Deaths and darkness.

Some folks pointed me to a New York Times article on the problem of US pedestrian deaths from 2023. Contra my original claims (that the increase in deaths doesn’t seem specific to one time of day), the NYT states that nearly all the increase in deaths is at night.

I was correct that there hasn’t been much change in the proportion of deaths during the hours of 6pm to 6am. But 6am is night at some times of year and location, and day in other times and places.. Lighting conditions are not just a function of the clock. If you look at deaths by lighting condition, you find that the increase in deaths is mostly in times of darkness.
