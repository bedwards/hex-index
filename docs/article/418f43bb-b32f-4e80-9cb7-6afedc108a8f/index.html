What is Lost in The Grand Budapest Hotel?

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Jun 16, 2023 · 53 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden and Thomas Flight do more than dissect Wes Anderson's visual symmetry; they argue that the director's most popular film, The Grand Budapest Hotel, is actually a profound meditation on the melancholy of preserving a world that never truly existed. While most critics fixate on the pastel palettes and rapid-fire dialogue, the pair uncover a hidden philosophy about the voluntary, yet doomed, labor of sustaining cultural illusions.

The Architecture of Melancholy

The conversation begins by acknowledging Anderson's reputation for whimsy, but quickly pivots to the emotional core that often gets overlooked. Van der Linden notes that while Anderson's style can feel like a barrier, this specific film marks a shift where the visual intensity serves a deeper narrative purpose. "I think this one has a really compelling character at its core," van der Linden argues, highlighting how the focus on Gustav H. makes the film "more emotionally accessible" than his previous ensemble-heavy works. This observation lands effectively because it reframes the film's famous aesthetic not as a shield against emotion, but as a vessel for it. The rigid, storybook quality of the visuals contrasts sharply with the "deep melancholy" underlying the plot, creating a tension that defines the movie's unique power.

The hosts draw a fascinating parallel between Anderson's filmmaking style and the literary works of Haruki Murakami or the atmospheric films of David Lynch, where "it's much more about the atmosphere of his films, the mood... than it is about the plot." Van der Linden suggests that Anderson treats the manner of storytelling as a valid pursuit in itself, distinct from the story's content. This is a bold claim for a director often accused of style over substance. However, the argument holds up when considering how Anderson's meticulous control creates a specific emotional resonance that a more traditional narrative might fail to achieve. It is, as they aptly put it, "Kubrick but for sad children."

"To be frank, I think his world had vanished long before he ever entered it, but I will say he certainly sustained the illusion with a marvelous grace."

The Outsider's Grace

The discussion takes a biographical turn, connecting the character of Gustav H. to Anderson's own journey as a filmmaker from Texas who became obsessed with a European culture he was never born into. Van der Linden posits that the film is a story about "trying to hold on to this thing or trying to participate in a culture in a society that they're not really a part of." This reading adds a layer of poignancy to the film's tragedy: Gustav is not just a hotel concierge; he is an artist trying to maintain a civilization that has already collapsed around him. The hosts suggest that Anderson, like his protagonist, is "adopting all these other cultures" and showcasing them, even as the version of that world he fell in love with is "vanishing in a sense."

Critics might note that projecting the director's personal biography onto a fictional character risks oversimplifying the text, turning a complex film into a mere allegory for Anderson's life. Yet, the evidence from the text supports this reading, particularly in how the film treats the past as a fragile, constructed memory rather than a solid reality. The argument that Anderson's style is a "voluntary position" to direct one's whole purpose to someone else or something else resonates deeply with the film's themes of service and devotion.

Bottom Line

Van der Linden and Flight successfully elevate the discussion of The Grand Budapest Hotel beyond a simple appreciation of its aesthetics, revealing it as a tragic study of the human need to sustain illusions in a changing world. Their strongest move is connecting the film's emotional accessibility to the director's own outsider status, though the argument occasionally leans heavily on biographical speculation. Readers should watch for how this lens of "sustained illusion" applies to Anderson's later, more surreal works like Asteroid City, which may continue this exploration of the artist's relationship with a vanishing past.
