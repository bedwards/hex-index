Laws Broken: Avengers - Sokovia Accords Illegal?

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·May 6, 2019 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just ask if the Avengers broke the law; he argues that the very document designed to stop them—the Sokovia Accords—is likely unconstitutional from the moment it was signed. By treating a fictional UN treaty as a binding legal instrument, Stone exposes a staggering array of civil liberty violations that would make any real-world Supreme Court justice pause. This is not a fan theory; it is a rigorous dismantling of superhero accountability that reveals how quickly good intentions curdle into authoritarian overreach.

The Constitutional Foundation

Stone begins by grounding the fantasy in the gritty reality of American treaty law. He points out a critical procedural hurdle that often gets lost in the cinematic spectacle. "An accord is not a US law," Stone writes, explaining that for an international treaty to become binding in the United States, "the Senate has to approve the treaty by a two-thirds vote under article 2 section 2 of the Constitution." He notes that the U.S. has signed, but never ratified, major agreements like the Kyoto Protocol and the Treaty of Versailles. This distinction is vital because it suggests the Accords might not even be valid federal law without a specific legislative act that never happened in the Marvel Cinematic Universe.

Even if we grant the Senate's approval, Stone argues the Accords still face a higher authority: the Constitution itself. He posits that "treaties cannot violate the US Constitution," a principle that immediately puts the Accords in the crosshairs. The author suggests that before analyzing the specific clauses, one must recognize that the Avengers were already operating in a legal gray zone. Vigilantism is illegal, and the justification of self-defense rarely applies when one "intentionally put[s] yourself in harm's way." Stone's analysis here is sharp because it strips away the moral high ground of the heroes; they aren't just breaking new rules, they are violating existing ones regarding deadly force and duty to retreat.

Critics might argue that the unique nature of extraterrestrial threats and super-powered individuals creates a sui generis legal category that standard criminal law cannot address. However, Stone effectively counters this by noting that even if the federal government granted them status, state laws prohibiting criminal conduct would still apply, creating a chaotic legal patchwork.

The Ten Constitutional Infirmities

Stone then moves to the core of his argument: ten specific constitutional violations embedded in the text of the Accords. He starts with the requirement for enhanced individuals to reveal their secret identities. "Those with secret identities must reveal their legal names and true identities to the United Nations," Stone quotes, labeling this a classic case of compelled speech. He draws a parallel to West Virginia School Board v. Barnette, where the Supreme Court ruled that students could not be forced to salute the flag. "Forcing individuals to give up their secret identities is the government compelling you to make a certain kind of speech," Stone asserts. This is a compelling application of First Amendment jurisprudence to a superhero context, treating a secret identity not as a security risk, but as a protected form of personal expression.

The argument deepens when Stone addresses the registration and monitoring provisions. He compares the mandatory registration of all enhanced individuals to the internment of Japanese Americans during World War II. "That executive order was tested at the Supreme Court level in a court case called Korematsu," Stone notes, acknowledging that while the ruling technically stands, "I doubt the Supreme Court would rule the same way today." He identifies the mandatory wearing of tracking bracelets as a clear Fourth Amendment violation, arguing that "warrantless monitoring without probable cause" is unconstitutional. The mere status of being "enhanced" does not constitute probable cause for surveillance. Stone also touches on the invasive nature of the required power analysis, suggesting it violates the right to privacy and potentially infringes on intellectual property rights, particularly regarding Tony Stark's technology.

Stone also tackles the Second Amendment, a point of intense contemporary debate. He argues that for heroes like Iron Man or Falcon, their suits are effectively "arms that they have created." Citing District of Columbia v. Heller, he suggests that the Accords' restrictions on military-grade technology might run afoul of the individual right to bear arms. "Given the makeup of the current Supreme Court and the deference shown to the individual right to bear arms," Stone writes, "you can bet that the Sokovia Accords probably run afoul of that right." This is a provocative take that bridges the gap between comic book logic and current political discourse.

The analysis extends to the First Amendment's right to associate. Stone points out that the Accords effectively ban non-enhanced members from joining the Avengers, violating the "right to voluntarily associate." Furthermore, he highlights the restriction on travel, quoting the text: "Any enhanced individuals who sign are prohibited from taking action in any country other than their own unless they are first given clearance." Stone argues this is a "clear violation of the privileges and immunities Clause" and international travel laws, noting that the vague term "taking action" could be interpreted so broadly it prevents a hero from even setting foot in a foreign country.

Perhaps the most damning critique concerns the vague language of the Accords. Stone invokes the "vagueness doctrine," which requires criminal laws to be explicit. "Vague terms like taking action... are vagueness issues that are probably so vague as to run afoul of the due process clauses," he argues. This lack of clarity makes the law unenforceable in a fair manner. Finally, Stone addresses the treatment of non-compliant heroes as "enemy combatants" and the potential for forced conscription. He argues that imprisoning U.S. citizens on a Helicarrier without a lawyer or arraignment is a "clear due process violation" and a breach of the Sixth Amendment right to counsel. "You're not gonna put me in a prison," he quotes the characters, underscoring the absurdity of denying basic legal rights to those who are supposed to be protecting the nation.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's most powerful contribution is his refusal to treat the Sokovia Accords as a plot device, instead dissecting them as a genuine legislative failure that would crumble under constitutional scrutiny. While he occasionally leans into the absurdity of applying real-world law to fictional technology, his core argument—that the Accords sacrifice fundamental civil liberties for a false sense of security—remains legally sound and deeply relevant. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that the Supreme Court would apply current precedents to super-powered beings, a variable that remains entirely speculative. However, Stone successfully demonstrates that in the Marvel universe, the law is not just broken; it is fundamentally flawed.
