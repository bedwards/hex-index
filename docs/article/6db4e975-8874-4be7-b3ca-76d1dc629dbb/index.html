If you think MAGA journalism is bad now, just wait

Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, Bill Kristol · The Bulwark ·Jan 14, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol deliver a chilling diagnosis of the modern right: it is no longer being led by seasoned strategists, but by an algorithmic ecosystem that rewards the "young and dumb." Their central claim is that the collapse of traditional editorial institutions has created a vacuum where viral propaganda now supersedes factual reporting, accelerating a slide toward authoritarianism. This is not merely a critique of a single bad video; it is an analysis of how the machinery of American politics is being hijacked by those who understand engagement metrics better than the truth.

The Collapse of the Safety Net

The authors anchor their argument in the recent chaos in Minneapolis, where a viral video by a 23-year-old YouTuber named Nick Shirley sparked a federal crackdown on Somali communities. Longwell, Miller, and Kristol note that while public-service fraud involving Somali-run centers had been a known issue for years, it was Shirley's "highly misleading gonzo video" that turned it into a national crisis. They write, "Within days, the White House was surging immigration enforcement to Minneapolis; Vice President JD Vance said Shirley had 'done far more useful journalism than any of the winners of the 2024 [Pulitzer] prizes.'"

This endorsement from the highest levels of the executive branch is the piece's most alarming data point. It signals a shift where institutional credibility is discarded in favor of raw, unverified viral momentum. The authors argue that Shirley's subsequent interview, where he couldn't define "benevolent," proves he lacks the intellectual foundation for journalism. Yet, they point out a crucial distinction: "It's not a crime to be young and dumb in America. It's not even a career-ending offense to be young and dumb in journalism."

The core of their argument is that in a different era, an editor would have reined in such a reporter. Longwell, Miller, and Kristol recall their time at the Weekly Standard, where editors acted as a "sounding board" to prevent young writers from "popping off with insane hot-take stuff." Today, that guardrail is gone. "Guys like Nick Shirley aren't trying to join a publication, they're picking up a camera and trying to go viral on their own," they observe. "They have no safety net, no sounding board, no mentorship, no way to grow beyond what they're doing this minute."

Critics might argue that the decline of traditional media is a broader societal issue affecting the left as well, not just a unique failure of the right. However, the authors contend that the right's specific ecosystem is uniquely vulnerable because it rewards "snappy, confrontational, fact-agnostic propaganda for the regime" above all else. The algorithm demands chaos, and the administration is now feeding it.

All they have is the zero-sum game of the algorithm: Get noticed or die. That's what the ecosystem rewards, so we're going to get more and more of it.

The Human Cost of Authoritarian Acceleration

The commentary shifts from media analysis to the tangible human consequences of this new dynamic. The authors highlight the killing of Renee Nicole Good in Charlotte and the subsequent cover-up attempts in Minneapolis. They detail how career prosecutors resigned in protest when political appointees refused to authorize a standard investigation into the shooting. "Six federal prosecutors in Minnesota quit rather than carry out orders to open a criminal investigation of Becca Good, Renee Good's widow, as well as over the refusal of their political superiors to authorize an investigation of the shooting," they report.

This institutional rot is contrasted with the aggressive posture of the executive branch. The authors cite Stephen Miller, a top advisor, telling ICE officers they have "immunity to perform your duties" and that any official interfering with them "will face justice." This rhetoric, Longwell, Miller, and Kristol argue, is not just political posturing; it is an invitation to lawlessness. They note that ICE officers are now operating with a sense of impunity, leading to "openly brutal" encounters with protesters and observers.

The human toll is further illustrated by the story of TJ Sabula, a factory worker who shouted "pedophile protector" at the President during a photo op. Sabula was suspended for his heckling, but the authors frame his action as a vital act of resistance. "He told the Washington Post, 'As far as calling him out, definitely no regrets whatsoever,'" they quote. "And today I think I did that."

The authors emphasize that this resistance is not monolithic. It ranges from the Federal Reserve chair defending institutional independence to civil servants resigning in protest, to ordinary citizens heckling a leader. "Freedom is complicated. Dictatorship is simple," they write. "Liberal and pro-democratic resistance efforts, by contrast, tend toward diversity, toward variety and heterogeneity."

This diversity is presented as the movement's greatest strength against a regime seeking uniformity. "Their message is one of simplicity and unity: 'Ein Volk, ein Reich, ein Führer,'" the authors write, contrasting it with the resistance's core message: "No Kings."

The Fragility of the Future

The piece concludes by warning that while the resistance is diverse and inspiring, the authoritarian momentum is dangerous. The authors point out that the administration is actively dismantling the norms that once checked executive power. They note that even popular, bipartisan legislation like the "Give Kids a Chance Act" for pediatric cancer research is being held hostage by political maneuvering and procedural obstruction.

Longwell, Miller, and Kristol also highlight the shifting allegiances within the right itself. They cite Joe Rogan, a prominent conservative voice, expressing horror at the violence in Minneapolis and asking, "Are we really gonna be the Gestapo? 'Where's your papers'? Is that what we've come to?" This suggests that the base is not entirely monolithic in its support for the administration's escalating tactics.

However, the authors remain sober about the path forward. "It's sobering to realize that success, while certainly possible, remains in doubt," they admit. The speed at which the administration is moving, combined with the collapse of journalistic standards, creates a volatile environment where facts are secondary to narrative.

If you think that's grim today, wait till you see the future.

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this commentary is its structural analysis: it identifies the collapse of editorial mentorship as the root cause of the right's descent into propaganda, rather than blaming individual personalities. Its biggest vulnerability is the assumption that the "diversity of the resistance" will automatically translate into political victory, a hope that history does not always guarantee. Readers should watch for whether the executive branch's claim of "immunity" for law enforcement becomes the new legal standard, effectively ending the concept of accountability for state violence.

MAGA’S Young, Dumb Future.

by Andrew Egger

The wilder the situation grows in Minneapolis—ICE growing openly brutal against protesters and observers, career prosecutors resigning in protest over the slanted investigation of the death of Renee Good, Stephen Miller proclaiming to ICE that “you have immunity to perform your duties” and that “no city official, no state official, no illegal alien, no leftist agitator or domestic insurrectionist” can stand in their way—the more remarkable it is that the whole situation really got going thanks to one smooth-brained 23-year-old YouTuber.

Minnesota’s public-services fraud and its connections to Minneapolis’s Somali population had been an ongoing scandal for years, and were already a large and growing story on the right before Nick Shirley headed there with a camera crew late last month. But Shirley’s highly misleading gonzo video confronting employees of Somali-run daycares and health care centers and claiming to have discovered $110 million in fraud went ultra-viral, racking up hundreds of millions of views and turning the scandal into the story of the moment for the MAGA right. Within days, the White House was surging immigration enforcement to Minneapolis; Vice President JD Vance said Shirley had “done far more useful journalism than any of the winners of the 2024 [Pulitzer] prizes.”

If this sort of person doing this sort of work can be so richly rewarded on the right right now, it’s safe to say both that Shirley will be a major fixture of the online right for a while, and that many others will try to follow in his footsteps. But if he’s the future of right-wing journalism, the future is very bleak indeed.
