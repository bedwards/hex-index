How much water is in my steak?!?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Sep 8, 2019 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace delivers a visceral shock to the system by quantifying the invisible water debt hidden in our dinner plates, arguing that our current food production methods are mathematically incompatible with a growing global population. He doesn't just cite abstract statistics; he physically demonstrates the staggering volume of water required to produce a single kilogram of beef, forcing the listener to confront the sheer inefficiency of modern livestock farming. This is not a gentle nudge toward sustainability; it is a stark warning that the hydrological cycle is being broken by our appetite for meat.

The Hidden Cost of the Steak

Borlace begins by grounding the conversation in the basics of the water cycle, noting that "the water that exists on our planet is all the water there ever was and all the water there ever will be." This framing is crucial because it dismantles the illusion that technology will simply create more fresh water as demand rises. He points out a grim reality: while 97.5 percent of Earth's water is salty, only a tiny fraction is usable, and that fraction is shrinking relative to population growth. As Borlace puts it, "the water that was shared between 1.5 billion people about a hundred years ago is the same amount of water that needs to be shared between seven point six billion people today." The argument here is effective because it shifts the blame from natural scarcity to human mismanagement of a finite resource.

To make this abstract scarcity tangible, Borlace moves to a hands-on demonstration in his garden, using a 1,000-liter tote to visualize the water footprint of common foods. He reveals that while a kilogram of apples requires 822 liters, "to produce a kilogram of beef takes fifteen thousand five hundred liters of water." The sheer scale of this number is the piece's most memorable takeaway. By contrasting the 380 liters his household uses for daily living against the 15,500 liters needed for a single kilo of steak, Borlace illustrates that our dietary choices are the primary driver of water stress. Critics might note that water footprint calculations can vary wildly depending on whether one counts rainwater (green water) or irrigation (blue water), potentially inflating the numbers for grazing animals. However, even with conservative estimates, the inefficiency of converting water into beef remains undeniable.

Ninety-seven point five percent of the world's water is in the oceans, and of course that water is salty and undrinkable without significant energy hungry industrial manipulation.

The Land Use Paradox

The commentary deepens as Borlace connects water scarcity to land use, exposing a profound inefficiency in how we allocate our planet's surface. He highlights that while 77 percent of agricultural land is dedicated to livestock, this massive footprint yields only 17 percent of the world's calories. "The 23 percent of land used for crops yields 83 percent of the total calorific supply and 67 percent of the protein supply," he observes. This disparity suggests that our current trajectory is not just environmentally damaging but logically flawed. The author argues that as disposable incomes rise in developing nations, the demand for meat is expected to double by 2050, creating a collision course with available resources.

Borlace warns that if we continue on this path, "by 2030 which is just 12 years from now the demand from water will be 40 percent higher than all of the available fresh water on the planet." This projection is alarming, yet it is rooted in the reality of how meat production amplifies water needs. The argument holds weight because it connects economic trends—rising middle classes in China and elsewhere—to ecological limits. A counterargument worth considering is that regenerative grazing practices can actually improve soil water retention, potentially mitigating some of these impacts. Borlace touches on this later, but the initial data on intensive farming remains the dominant narrative of the piece.

The Climate Feedback Loop

The final section of the piece tackles the irony of climate change: as the atmosphere warms and holds more moisture, the soil dries out faster, leading to a paradox of drought and flood. Borlace explains that "the extra water does mean more extreme flooding all over the world... rather than gently soaking into the ground the water hits rock hard dried-up earth and runs off." This description of water running off hard-packed soil rather than seeping in is a powerful visual that underscores the importance of soil health. He argues that without radical changes to land management, we will see more arid regions that are "completely uninhabitable."

Borlace concludes by pivoting to solutions, specifically regenerative agriculture, which he describes as a method that makes soil "spongy and absorbent" so it can hydrate deeper root systems. He suggests that practices like reforestation and regenerative tree planting are vital for drawing down carbon and holding water. This offers a glimmer of hope, but it requires a fundamental shift away from the "maximizing crop yield and monetary profit" model that currently dominates. The piece leaves the listener with a clear choice: continue a trajectory toward a water-starved future or adopt land management techniques that work with nature rather than against it.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's most compelling contribution is his ability to translate abstract hydrological data into a concrete, terrifying reality: the steak on your plate is a massive water bomb. While the piece relies heavily on the inefficiencies of industrial livestock farming, its greatest strength is the clear link it draws between soil health, water retention, and climate resilience. The biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that global dietary habits can shift quickly enough to meet the 2030 deadline, a political and cultural hurdle that is far higher than the technical one.
