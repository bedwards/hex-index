Rolls Royce Small Modular Reactor. Energy Revolution or delusional distraction?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Dec 19, 2021 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace doesn't just ask if Rolls-Royce's new nuclear reactor is a miracle; he asks if it's a mirage that could delay the actual work of decarbonization. In a landscape saturated with hype, Borlace offers a rare, data-driven tug-of-war between the seductive promise of factory-built power and the brutal economics of scaling new technology. This isn't a simple endorsement or a dismissal; it is a forensic audit of whether a 470-megawatt reactor can truly compete with the plummeting costs of wind and solar before the climate clock runs out.

The Allure of the Factory-Built Reactor

Borlace begins by acknowledging the pedigree of the company behind the proposal. Rolls-Royce, the largest employer of nuclear engineers in the UK with decades of experience building reactors for submarines, is no amateur. Their proposal centers on a Small Modular Reactor (SMR) that is compact enough to be manufactured in a factory and transported by truck. "The entire site will sit inside a protective canopy which will not only minimise local noise and air pollution but also protect the site itself from the elements," Borlace notes, highlighting a design choice that aims to eliminate the weather delays that plague traditional construction. This is a compelling argument for speed; while traditional plants take over two decades to build, Rolls-Royce claims they can deliver power in just four years.

The economic case hinges on this speed and the promise of standardization. Borlace points out that the claimed cost of electricity is "about eighty five dollars per megawatt hour," a figure that would make it competitive with coal and gas, and significantly cheaper than current nuclear projects. The visual of a reactor fitting on a seismic raft, insulated from land movement and built in a controlled environment, is undeniably attractive to investors and planners alike. However, this optimism relies heavily on the assumption that factory production lines will behave like car manufacturing, a leap that history suggests is fraught with difficulty.

"If Rolls-Royce were going after an architectural award then the computer renderings of their proposed nuclear power stations would certainly be a very good starting point."

The Safety Paradox and Waste Reality

The author tackles the elephant in the room immediately: safety. Borlace leans on historical data to counter the visceral fear of nuclear accidents, citing a chart from Our World in Data that shows nuclear power causes far fewer deaths per terawatt-hour than fossil fuels. He explains that the new SMR designs incorporate "passive safety features which allow them to describe their reactors as walk away safe." This means the reactors rely on natural forces like gravity and convection to shut down without human intervention, theoretically making a meltdown impossible.

Yet, Borlace does not let the safety argument off the hook entirely. He pivots to the persistent issue of waste management, noting that while the technology to contain waste exists, the political will to manage it in perpetuity is uncertain. "The international atomic energy agency or iaea calculates that there's about 38 million cubic meters of solid radioactive waste currently in existence," he writes, reminding readers that a significant portion of this remains in interim storage. The promise that Rolls-Royce's waste will be stored on-site permanently is a bold claim that assumes future generations will maintain the same level of diligence as the present one. Critics might note that the sheer volume of waste increases linearly with the number of reactors, potentially creating a logistical nightmare if hundreds of these units are deployed globally.

The Economic and Scaling Trap

The most critical section of Borlace's commentary comes when he introduces the skeptics, particularly Michael Barnard, who argues that the fundamental economics of nuclear power work against small modular reactors. Borlace explains that economies of scale usually require building thousands of identical units, a feat that is unlikely given that every country has its own preferred technology and geopolitical constraints. "Every country engaged in smr research has its own preferred technologies and its own companies to support so that kind of horizontal scaling at global level is extremely unlikely," Borlace paraphrases Barnard's core objection.

Furthermore, the author highlights a physical limitation: thermal efficiency. Larger reactors are generally more efficient because bigger pipes move more fluid with less material cost. By shrinking the reactor, Rolls-Royce may be sacrificing the very efficiency gains that make nuclear viable in the first place. The financial risk is also compounded by the reality of insurance; no private insurer covers nuclear disasters fully. "The total liability for that disaster has nevertheless run into the trillion dollar region," Borlace points out regarding Fukushima, noting that taxpayers ultimately bear the risk. This creates a situation where the private sector gets the profit, but the public absorbs the catastrophic downside.

"If nuclear eats all the pies which it's looking to be doing we won't have enough money to do the kinds of things we need to do which we know practically and technologically we can do now."

The Verdict on Timing

Ultimately, Borlace frames the debate around the urgency of the 2030 climate deadline. He cites experts like Tony Seba and Mark Jacobson who argue that renewables, combined with storage and transmission, can meet 100% of energy needs without nuclear. The timeline for Rolls-Royce's first operational site is the early 2030s, which Borlace suggests is too late to help with the most critical decade of emissions reductions. "These small modular reactors are not going to play any part in that crucial challenge at all," he quotes Paul Dorfman, a leading critic. The argument here is that investing billions in a technology that won't be ready for a decade is a distraction from deploying proven, cheaper solutions that can be installed today.

However, Borlace does leave room for a middle ground. He acknowledges that while renewables can cover 80% of the grid, the remaining 20%—the baseload gap—remains a challenge. He notes that even the skeptics concede that "it's very reasonable to make a side bet or two to ensure coverage of that last twenty percent." This nuanced conclusion prevents the piece from becoming a simple anti-nuclear rant, instead positioning the SMR as a potential, albeit risky, insurance policy rather than a silver bullet.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's analysis succeeds in stripping away the marketing gloss to reveal the hard economic and logistical constraints facing the nuclear industry. The strongest part of his argument is the timeline mismatch: the technology is too far away to solve the immediate climate crisis. His biggest vulnerability is the assumption that renewable storage and transmission will scale perfectly to fill the gap, a transition that has historically faced its own grid-integration hurdles. Readers should watch whether Rolls-Royce can actually achieve its four-year construction target, as that single metric will determine if this is a revolution or a delusion.
