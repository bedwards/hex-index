Top ten reasons NOT to buy an electric vehicle

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Jul 7, 2019 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace doesn't just debunk myths about electric vehicles; he dismantles the entire emotional architecture of resistance against them, arguing that the most persistent objections are not based on current reality but on outdated fears. While many commentators focus on the environmental benefits, Borlace zeroes in on the practical anxieties that keep buyers in the gas lane, using a relentless barrage of data from 2019 to prove that the transition has already happened, even if public perception hasn't caught up. This is essential listening for anyone who feels the market is moving too fast or is too expensive, because the numbers suggest the tipping point is not a distant horizon but the present moment.

The Cost and Choice Illusion

The author immediately tackles the twin myths of limited selection and prohibitive cost, framing them as relics of a bygone era. "There's a whole raft of brand new models in the development pipeline all the big firms and 2020 is the year when the industry is anticipating a tsunami of models to arrive on the market at every price point and in every category," Borlace writes. This assertion is powerful because it shifts the narrative from scarcity to abundance, suggesting that the lack of choice was a temporary glitch in the market's evolution rather than a permanent feature. He supports this by noting that even pickup trucks and the iconic VW Bus are joining the electric revolution, effectively closing the gap between niche and mainstream.

On the financial front, Borlace argues that comparing sticker prices is a flawed metric that ignores the true economics of ownership. He cites a report from the International Council for Clean Transportation which found that "in every case the pure electric vehicle came out as the least expensive overall" when factoring in insurance, maintenance, and fuel over four years. This evidence is compelling because it moves the debate from the showroom floor to the long-term ledger, where electric vehicles consistently win. He reinforces this with data from the US Department of Energy, noting that "recharging an electric vehicle cost about half as much per mile as a gasoline vehicle," a gap that widens significantly in regions with high fuel taxes like Europe.

Critics might argue that these calculations assume stable electricity prices and ignore the volatility of renewable energy markets, but Borlace's reliance on government-backed tools like the "Gallon" calculator grounds his argument in tangible, state-level data rather than theoretical models.

"By 2023 even the sticker price of a 150 mile range electric car will actually be cheaper on the car showroom forecourt some an equivalent internal combustion engine car."

The Grid and The Environment

Perhaps the most contentious point Borlace addresses is the environmental hypocrisy argument: the idea that electric cars are just "coal on wheels." He dismantles this by pointing out that even in coal-heavy grids, the efficiency of electric motors results in lower emissions. "Bloomberg ran a report in February of 2019 looking at exactly this accusation they found that carbon dioxide emissions from battery powered vehicles were about 40% lower than for internal combustion engines in 2018," he notes. This is a crucial distinction, as it separates the vehicle's operation from the grid's current mix, while simultaneously highlighting the grid's rapid decarbonization.

The argument takes a fascinating turn when discussing the electrical grid's capacity. Borlace counters the fear of a grid collapse with the concept of the vehicle as a mobile battery. "The software in electric vehicles will be able to communicate with the grid so that the grid can either send energy to the car to recharge its battery or take energy out of the car's battery to help smooth the spikes in the demand," he explains. This reframes the electric car from a burden on the infrastructure to a stabilizing asset, a perspective that is often missing from public discourse. He dismisses the idea that everyone will plug in simultaneously, noting that "most people park up for the evening and don't use the car again until the morning," allowing for smart, staggered charging that utilizes off-peak energy.

A counterargument worth considering is the sheer scale of battery production and the environmental cost of mining lithium, which Borlace touches on only briefly by mentioning battery recycling. While he focuses on the operational phase, the upstream environmental impact remains a valid concern for skeptics.

Range, Charging, and The Human Element

The final third of the commentary tackles the visceral fears of range anxiety and charging logistics. Borlace points out that the average commute is only 20 miles, while modern batteries offer "well over 200 miles or more," rendering the fear of running out of power largely irrational for daily use. He highlights the rapid evolution of charging speeds, noting that new stations can add "300 miles of range in 15 minutes," a speed that makes long-distance travel comparable to a gas stop.

Addressing the lack of home charging for apartment dwellers, he admits that induction charging is still futuristic but argues that the solution lies in public infrastructure and workplace charging, drawing a parallel to how people currently refuel without a home gas station. The most poignant part of his analysis, however, addresses the psychological barrier. "I know about internal combustion engine cars I know how they work I've been driving them for 33 years I know what to do when they don't work," he paraphrases the common sentiment, before countering that this comfort is merely a resistance to change. He suggests that the reluctance to switch is often rooted in social insecurity rather than technical reality.

"It's more likely that the car itself will get scrapped long before the batteries fail."

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest asset is his ability to replace emotional resistance with hard data, proving that the electric vehicle ecosystem is not a future promise but a current reality. His biggest vulnerability is the assumption that infrastructure will scale perfectly to meet demand without significant political or logistical friction. The reader should watch for how quickly the "tsunami of models" he predicts in 2020 actually materializes, as that will be the true test of whether the market has truly turned the corner.
