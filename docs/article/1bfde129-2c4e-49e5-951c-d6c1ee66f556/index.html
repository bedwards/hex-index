16. Bagan - City of Temples

Paul Cooper · Fall of Civilizations ·Oct 3, 2022 · 84 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Paul Cooper doesn't just recount history; he reconstructs a lost world through the eyes of a man who almost missed it entirely. The piece's most arresting claim is that the legendary city of Bagan, with its 4,000 golden temples, was already a ghost town when Marco Polo arrived, a fact that reframes the entire narrative of Southeast Asian decline. This is essential listening for anyone who assumes ancient empires simply faded away; Cooper argues they were often abandoned in a flash, leaving behind a landscape that felt both majestic and haunted.

The Witness in the Wilderness

Cooper anchors his narrative in the specific, sensory details of Polo's journey, transforming a dry historical account into a vivid expedition. He writes, "After the journey of 15 days that has been mentioned you reach the city of miyan which is large magnificent and the capital of the Kingdom." This quote serves as the pivot point for the entire episode, contrasting the traveler's expectation of glory with the reality he found. Cooper effectively uses Polo's status as an outsider to validate the scale of the ruins, noting that despite seeing the world's wonders, the Venetian was still struck by the sheer magnitude of the golden spires.

However, the author quickly complicates this image of splendor. He points out that the city had "fallen on hard times" and that "everywhere homes were abandoned and temples had fallen into disuse and disrepair." This juxtaposition is the core of Cooper's argument: the visual grandeur of the architecture masks a society that had already collapsed. The evidence holds up well because Cooper leans on the primary source's own confusion; Polo describes a place that looks like a capital but acts like a ruin. A counterargument worth considering is whether Polo's account was exaggerated for his Genoese jailers, but Cooper mitigates this by citing the collaboration with Rusticello de Pisa, which scholars generally agree resulted in a surprisingly high degree of accuracy.

The city that Marco Polo called Miyan had fallen on hard times, yet over these sorry sites the golden shrines of the city towered and attested to its former magnificence.

The River as the Engine of Empire

Moving beyond the ruins, Cooper shifts his focus to the geography that made Bagan possible. He argues that the Irrawadi River was not merely a backdrop but the literal lifeblood of the civilization. "The Irrawadi River winds for more than two thousand kilometers across the dry zone of Burma through palm forests and scrub lands and around sandy islands," he notes, emphasizing the river's role as a highway for trade and culture. This framing is crucial because it explains why the empire rose in such an arid region; the surrounding mountains trapped the rain, forcing the population to cluster tightly along the water's edge.

Cooper's description of the local ingenuity is particularly compelling. He paraphrases historical records to show how the people built "floating island gardens that rise and fall with the water level," turning the seasonal floods into agricultural assets rather than disasters. This detail highlights a sophisticated adaptation to the environment that modern readers often overlook. The author suggests that the empire's collapse was likely tied to a failure of this delicate hydrological balance, though he stops short of making a definitive claim, leaving room for the listener to ponder the fragility of such systems. Critics might note that focusing so heavily on the river risks downplaying the role of Mongol invasions, which are mentioned but not fully explored in this segment.

The Problem of Chronicles

Perhaps the most intellectually rigorous section of the piece is Cooper's treatment of the historical sources themselves. He is refreshingly honest about the bias in the "Chronicles of Burma," admitting they are "full of exaggeration, poetic license and mythology." He describes how these texts include "truly ludicrous exaggerations about the size of armies often describing battles involving tens of millions of soldiers." By acknowledging these flaws upfront, Cooper actually strengthens his credibility. He argues that despite the fantastical elements—ogres, ghosts, and demons—the chronicles preserve "relatively accurate dates and events" when cross-referenced with stone inscriptions.

The author illustrates this method by discussing the "Glass Palace Chronicle," commissioned in 1829 to "purify" previous accounts by "adopting the truth in the light of reason." This meta-commentary on history-writing is rare in popular media and adds significant depth to the narrative. It forces the listener to engage critically with the past rather than passively consuming a story. The argument lands because it treats the audience as intelligent partners in the investigation, rather than just consumers of a legend.

The chronicles are instruments of official propaganda, full of exaggeration and mythology, yet they combine with stone inscriptions to preserve relatively accurate dates and events.

Bottom Line

Paul Cooper's coverage excels by refusing to romanticize the past, instead presenting Bagan as a complex, flawed, and ultimately fragile civilization. The strongest part of the argument is the seamless integration of Polo's eyewitness account with the critical analysis of Burmese chronicles, creating a multi-layered historical portrait. The biggest vulnerability lies in the brief treatment of the actual collapse, which leaves the listener wanting more on the specific catalysts that ended the golden age. For a busy listener, this is a masterclass in how to listen to history not as a static fact, but as a contested, evolving story.
