How 2025 put the American golden age on hold

Jeff Rich · ·Jan 2, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Jeff Rich delivers a blistering diagnosis of American exceptionalism, arguing that the administration's 2025 proclamation of a "Golden Age" has not only failed but accelerated the nation's descent into global instability and internal decay. Rather than accepting the official narrative of renewed vigor, Rich reframes the United States not as a unique beacon, but as the volatile, abusive partner in a crumbling "Greater Europe" that includes Russia and the continent itself.

The Myth of the Golden Age

Rich opens by dismantling the administration's grandiose rhetoric, noting that while the executive branch declared a new era of American dominance in January 2025, the reality by year's end was a continent fractured by war and piracy. "History had disappointed American Greatness, again," Rich writes, setting a tone of historical inevitability rather than political failure. He argues that the administration's reliance on tired tropes of the frontier and manifest destiny is a dangerous delusion. The administration's own National Security Strategy claimed a desire for an America that "cherishes its past glories and its heroes, and that looks forward to a new golden age," yet Rich suggests this is merely a cosmetic adjustment to a failing project.

The author's most striking insight is that the "Golden Age" is a symptom of a deeper pathology: the inability of the American political class to confront the reality of a multipolar world. "The fall of American Primacy does not imply the collapse of American Power," Rich explains, "Rather, it means that America no longer ranks at the top of all sources of power." This distinction is crucial; it suggests that the administration is fighting a war for status in a theater where the rules have already changed. Critics might argue that Rich underestimates the resilience of American soft power, but his evidence regarding the breakdown of the "toxic threesome" between the US, Europe, and Russia offers a compelling counter-narrative to the idea of American invincibility.

The farcical world historical personage of this time is Donald Trump. He is a symptom of this time of monsters. He is not its cause, nor its champion, and he has next to no control over the events he seeks to stage manage.

The Abusive Partner in Greater Europe

Rich pivots to a geopolitical framework that challenges the standard "America vs. the World" binary. He adopts historian John Darwin's concept of "Greater Europe," defining it as a zone where the US, Europe, and Russia are inextricably linked. In this view, the US is not an outlier but the "Western Frontier of Greater Europe," a role that has historically made it an aggressive, expansionist force. Rich describes the current dynamic as a "toxic threesome" where the US has acted as an "abusive, violent partner."

The author draws a sharp parallel between modern US foreign policy and the machismo of the caudillo tradition in Latin America, referencing the 1807 trial of Aaron Burr for treason. "Burr took the machismo of the caudillo two steps further," Rich notes, connecting the Founding Fathers' era to the current administration's impulse to seize territory or dominate neighbors. This historical lens is effective because it strips away the moral superiority often claimed by US policymakers. "Unhappy empires twist their stories, but not in conditions of their own making," Rich argues, pointing to the structural inevitability of decline when a nation prioritizes "full-spectrum dominance" over reciprocal relationships.

However, Rich's characterization of Europe as "stuck or imprisoned" may overlook the agency of European nations to forge independent paths, such as the strategic autonomy movements currently gaining traction in Brussels. While the US may be the aggressor, the narrative of a passive Europe waiting to be liberated ignores the complex, often complicit role European powers have played in maintaining the transatlantic order.

The Social Crisis of the Empire

Perhaps the most damning section of Rich's commentary is his focus on the domestic rot that underpins foreign policy failures. He argues that the US cannot project strength abroad while its citizens suffer from the worst health outcomes among high-income nations. Citing Emmanuel Todd, Rich asserts that "Americans spend more to die more," framing the healthcare crisis not just as a policy failure but as a sign of the empire's "self-inflicted defeat."

Rich connects this social decay to the degradation of the state itself, moving from the "active state" to a "zombie state" and finally to a "zero state." "The degeneration of the soul of the deep state from Kissinger to Clinton to Blinken parallels the loss of collective ethos in the West," he writes. This is a bold claim that links the personal failures of individual leaders to a broader civilizational collapse. The author suggests that the administration's focus on grandiose projects, like planting the flag on Mars, is a distraction from the fact that "the world is too perplexing for even the finest minds."

The real truth is that the abuse will only end when the world breaks the spell of love of America.

Rich's analysis here is particularly potent because it refuses to separate foreign policy from domestic welfare. He argues that the "indispensable divided superpower of the world cannot govern itself," a paradox that renders its global leadership illegitimate. While some might argue that the US remains a beacon of innovation and opportunity, Rich's data on life expectancy and healthcare costs provides a stark, empirical rebuttal to the notion of American exceptionalism.

Bottom Line

Rich's strongest argument is his refusal to treat the administration's rhetoric as a unique aberration, instead framing it as the inevitable culmination of a century of imperial overreach and social neglect. His biggest vulnerability is a tendency toward fatalism, suggesting that the "spell of love" for America is the only barrier to global peace, which may underestimate the potential for diplomatic realignment. Readers should watch for how the "Greater Europe" concept evolves as the US continues to isolate itself; if the "abusive partner" dynamic holds, the next decade may see a rapid reordering of the global balance of power that leaves Washington behind.

Recap of the World Power World History Tour.

In 2025 I took readers on a world tour of the history of five great powers or ‘civilization states’: in reverse order, Russia, Europe, India, China and the USA. Through the weekly essays, deep dives and book recommendations, we explored an astonishing range of stories: from the volcano in Guatemala that created the Viking Age; to the 1926 visit of ballerina Anna Pavlova to my home town where she danced the Dying Swan and drank billy tea.

Since many readers have joined after the start of the world tour, I wanted to bring this world tour to you in the convenient form of a compendium post, where you can find all the stories about each region.

Two questions lurked behind all my essays of the World Power World History Tour:

How do the best current histories help you to reimagine the stories of these states, nations and cultures?

How does the concept of ‘civilization state’ improve, or impair, your understanding of those histories?

The power elites in each of these five states claim the status of ‘civilization state’. The idea has caught on in much popular commentary on international affairs. But the history underpinning such claims is often weak. For example, the claim that China has a 5,000-year, continuous, largely peaceful history as a civilization-state does not mention the Mongols or the Qing, or the many rebellions, state breakdowns, border wars or civil conflicts. I will suggest an alternative approach to the concept of the ‘civilization state’ in the first of my Long Reads at the end of January.
