Imagine it's 1905 and you're this guy. You're working to build Philadelphia's new metro system, which will open in a couple of years. The Chicago L system, Boston's T system, and the New York subway system have all recently opened. It's a good time to be in the metro construction field, right?

You figure that Detroit, St. Louis, Pittsburgh, and Buffalo, some of the largest cities of that time, are going to start construction next, right? Right. Well, that's not what happened.

And none of those cities have a metro system. In fact, the next metro system wouldn't be built for another 50 years in Cleveland. After Cleveland, there's another gap, then a moderate amount of new metro systems that bring us to the modern day with the most recent one opening in Honolulu. Why don't other major US cities have a metro system?

other cities around the world have been building them at a breakneck pace. The world's busiest metro system, Shanghai's, didn't even open until 1993. But while other countries are speedrunning new metro system construction, we here haven't. This video is all about answering that question and we'll get started after the bike bell.

Okay, so you might be asking yourself, is the United States really lacking in metro systems? By my account, there are at least 13 in the US. They're in the top three largest cities, New York, Los Angeles, and Chicago, as well as major cities like Atlanta, Washington DC, and Philadelphia. That's a lot of metro systems.

But only seven of the top 10 largest urban areas have metros. Dallas, Houston, and Phoenix don't. and only three of the next 10 do. Cities like Seattle, Detroit, Minneapolis, Tampa, San Diego, Denver, and Orlando are metro-free.

These are cities with millions of people. It would be unheard of for cities that size not to have a metro if they were located in Europe. The largest city in Europe without a metro is Leeds or the West Yorkshire area in the UK. It has 2.3 million people, which is smaller than all of the cities I just mentioned in the US.
