Why Sri Lanka is Collapsing: the Coming Global Food Crisis

PolyMatter · PolyMatter ·Jun 7, 2022 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

PolyMatter delivers a chilling diagnosis: Sri Lanka's collapse isn't a freak accident, but the first domino in a global chain reaction where food prices, debt, and currency instability converge. While most coverage fixates on the political drama in Colombo, the author argues that the island's inability to afford paper for school exams reveals a deeper, systemic failure that threatens the entire developing world.

The Currency Trap

The piece begins by dismantling the intuitive idea that a trade deficit is simply a failure to save. PolyMatter writes, "What all these countries do to make up the difference is simply borrow," highlighting that deficits are normal until the borrowing stops. The author's crucial insight is that for nations like Sri Lanka, the real currency is not their local rupee, but the US dollar, which is required to buy essential imports like oil and medicine. "If a country imports more than it exports you can see how it could just run out of usd," PolyMatter notes, explaining that this shortage creates a fatal bottleneck.

This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from abstract market forces to a tangible resource constraint. The author illustrates how Sri Lanka's government tried to maintain an artificial exchange rate to keep food prices low, a move that only accelerated the drain on foreign reserves. "The central bank in other words overvalued the rupee because the country depends so heavily on imports," the text explains. When the reserves ran dry, the currency collapsed, and the government could no longer pretend that the market value of the dollar was anything other than what it was.

Critics might argue that this analysis underplays the role of external shocks, such as the pandemic, in precipitating the crisis. However, the author counters that the groundwork was laid decades prior through poor governance and a reliance on new loans to pay off old ones. "Sri Lanka began paying off old loans with new ones in what the world bank has compared to a pyramid scheme," PolyMatter writes, emphasizing that the current chaos was inevitable once the external funding dried up.

"Sri Lanka in other words may simply be the first domino to fall this year."

The Perfect Storm

The commentary then broadens its scope, connecting Sri Lanka's specific failure to a global "perfect storm" driven by the war in Ukraine and rising interest rates. PolyMatter observes that the conflict has triggered "the largest commodity shock in half a century," with Russia and Ukraine supplying a massive portion of the world's wheat and fertilizer. "Ukrainian exports alone previously fed 400 million people," the author points out, underscoring the fragility of the global food supply chain.

This section is particularly strong in its ability to humanize macroeconomic data. The author describes how "millions of sri lankans went from living a fairly middle-class lifestyle... to wondering whether they can eat dinner this," illustrating the speed at which stability can evaporate. The argument suggests that while the rich world faces inconvenience from high gas prices, the developing world faces the "undoing of decades worth of progress in healthcare education and general prosperity."

A counterargument worth considering is whether the International Monetary Fund (IMF) can truly intervene given the current political turmoil. PolyMatter acknowledges this, noting that bailouts require a functional government to implement unpopular policies. "The IMF doesn't just write checks on the whim it wants to know that the country won't end up in the same situation six months later," the author states, implying that the window for rescue may have already closed.

The Global Ripple Effect

Finally, the piece warns that Sri Lanka is merely a preview of what is to come for other vulnerable nations. PolyMatter identifies Pakistan as a likely next casualty, describing it as a "domino... that is nuclear," given the country's strategic importance and debt load. The author argues that the confluence of a pandemic, financial tightening, and armed conflict creates a unique pressure cooker. "For which other country or countries will the confluence of the pandemic financial tightening and a drastic increase in commodity prices soon expose a weak foundation," the text asks, leaving the reader with a sense of looming uncertainty.

The argument holds up well because it refuses to isolate Sri Lanka as an outlier. Instead, it presents the crisis as a symptom of a global system where developing nations are increasingly exposed to shocks they cannot absorb. "What for the rich world will be an inconvenience... will for the developing world mean the undoing of decades worth of progress," PolyMatter concludes, driving home the stark disparity in how these economic forces play out.

Bottom Line

PolyMatter's strongest asset is the ability to translate complex balance-of-payments mechanics into a narrative of human survival, making the abstract threat of a trade deficit feel immediate and visceral. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that political instability will permanently block international aid, a variable that could shift with diplomatic maneuvering. Readers should watch closely for the next country to face a similar currency crunch, as the dominoes are already falling.
