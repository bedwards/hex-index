12. The Inca - Cities in the Cloud

Paul Cooper · Fall of Civilizations ·Jan 12, 2021 · 128 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Paul Cooper doesn't just recount the discovery of Machu Picchu; he reconstructs the sheer, breathless improbability of an empire that turned the world's most hostile mountain range into a cradle of civilization. While most histories focus on the Spanish conquest, Cooper anchors his narrative in the geological and biological constraints that forced the Inca to innovate or perish, arguing that their genius was born entirely from necessity.

The Discovery and the Landscape

Cooper begins with the 1911 expedition of Hiram Bingham, framing the moment not as a triumphant find, but as a desperate scramble against the elements. He captures the physical toll of the journey, quoting Bingham's own admission that "so lofty are the peaks on either side that although the trail was frequently shadowed by dense tropical jungle many of the mountains were capped with snow." This vivid imagery immediately establishes the stakes: the Inca didn't just build in the mountains; they built against the mountains. Cooper writes, "Presently we found ourselves in the midst of a tropical forest beneath the shade of whose trees we could make out a maze of ancient walls," a sentence that perfectly encapsulates the moment the hidden world revealed itself.

The author's coverage is particularly effective when he contrasts the lush, rain-soaked Amazon on one side of the Andes with the hyper-arid Atacama Desert on the other. He notes that "the desert that has formed on the western side of the mountains is the driest place on earth," a fact that underscores the fragility of the civilization that rose in the valleys between these extremes. By describing the Atacama as a place where "even though it contains several mountains higher than 6000 meters many are still completely free of glaciers," Cooper illustrates a landscape so barren it serves as a proxy for Mars, making the Inca's ability to farm there seem almost miraculous.

Critics might argue that focusing too heavily on the environmental determinism risks oversimplifying the cultural agency of the Inca, but Cooper balances this by detailing the sophisticated social structures that emerged from these constraints. He explains that "the steep rocky slopes of the Andes hold very little fertile soil," which necessitated a shift from individualism to a "reciprocal economy" where survival depended on collective labor. This is a crucial distinction; the empire wasn't built by a single genius, but by a society that understood that "a group of farmers worked much faster than one on his own."

The Inca didn't just build in the mountains; they built against the mountains, turning the world's most hostile environment into a cradle of civilization.

The Architecture of Survival

As Cooper delves into the mechanics of Inca society, he shifts from the physical landscape to the social engineering required to navigate it. He highlights the verticality of their world, noting how they divided the mountains into distinct zones: the quechua for maize, the sunni for potatoes, and the puna for grazing llamas. This stratification was not just agricultural; it was a logistical masterstroke. Cooper writes, "These camelid animals are incredibly strong for their size and are capable of carrying loads of up to 40 kilograms across some of the world's toughest terrain," emphasizing how the llama became the engine of a trade network that connected the coast to the jungle.

The author's description of the trade routes is compelling because it reveals the scale of their integration. He points out that "this system saw seashells decorating the clothes of people who lived a thousand miles from the sea and bright tropical feathers decorating the hair of people who had never laid eyes on the jungle." This detail does more than paint a picture; it proves that the Inca had solved the problem of distance in a way that modern logistics still struggle with in such terrain. The evidence holds up because it relies on the tangible artifacts—shells and feathers—that prove a unified economic system existed long before the Spanish arrived.

However, the narrative does gloss over the darker side of this centralization. While Cooper mentions that "life for these early people was hard" and that infant mortality was so high children weren't named until their third birthday, he doesn't fully explore the coercive aspects of the labor system that sustained this efficiency. A counterargument worth considering is whether the "highly controlled and centralized empires" were as benevolent as the text implies, or if the "reciprocal economy" was enforced through strict state control. Cooper hints at this by noting the "enormous labors that required large work gangs," but the human cost of that organization remains slightly in the shadows.

The Mystery of the Fall

The piece concludes by returning to the central mystery: how did such a sophisticated society vanish? Cooper sets the stage by describing the Inca as "the latest in a long string of human civilizations that rose and fell in the region," suggesting a cyclical pattern of rise and collapse. He references the Chronicle of the Incas, quoting the myth that "in ancient times they say the land and the provinces of Peru were dark and neither light nor daylight existed." This mythological framing serves as a powerful metaphor for the suddenness of their collapse, a theme he promises to explore in the full episode.

The author's decision to end with the question of "what in all the world had happened to the Inca people of the cloud forest" is a masterful hook. It leaves the reader with a sense of unfinished business, compelling them to seek the full explanation. Cooper's coverage is strongest here because he refuses to offer a simple answer, instead presenting the Inca as a civilization that was both magnificent and fragile, built on a foundation of stone and snow that could not withstand the cataclysm to come.

Bottom Line

Paul Cooper's coverage succeeds by grounding the grandeur of the Inca Empire in the brutal reality of their geography, proving that their civilization was a direct response to an environment that would have destroyed others. The argument's greatest strength is its vivid reconstruction of the logistical and social innovations required to survive, though it could benefit from a deeper examination of the internal social tensions that may have contributed to their fall. Readers should watch for how the full episode connects these environmental constraints to the specific political fractures that allowed the Spanish conquest to succeed.
