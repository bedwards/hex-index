Information and Technological Evolution

Brian Potter · Construction Physics ·Apr 2, 2026 ·30 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Brian Potter doesn't just ask how we invent things; he asks why the math of invention seems impossible, yet happens anyway. By dissecting a simulation that builds computer logic from scratch, he reveals that technological progress isn't a straight line of genius, but a desperate, efficient search through a universe of failure. For leaders navigating rapid change, this isn't abstract theory—it's a blueprint for how complex systems actually survive.

The Search for the Needle in the Haystack

Potter begins by noting a gap in our collective understanding. "If you want to learn about how some particular technology came into existence, there's often very good resources available," he observes, citing books on the transistor and Xerox. "But if you're trying to understand the nature of technological progress more generally, the range of good options narrows significantly." He turns to economist W. Brian Arthur and his 2006 paper on evolving boolean logic circuits to fill this void. The simulation is stark: it starts with only three basic elements—a NAND gate and two constants—and randomly wires them together to see if they accidentally form a useful function.

The author's most striking insight comes when he quantifies the difficulty of this task. "Finding complex functions like an 8-bit adder or a 6-bit equal requires successfully finding working functions amidst a vast ocean of non-working ones." Potter calculates that for a standard 8-bit adder, there are roughly 10^177,554 possible logic functions. To put that in perspective, the number of atoms in the observable universe is only about 10^80. The odds of stumbling upon a working circuit by pure random chance are effectively zero.

Fulfilling some goal from circuit space means finding one particular function in a gargantuan sea of possibilities.

This framing is crucial. It shifts the narrative from "invention as discovery" to "invention as navigation." Potter argues that the simulation succeeds not because it is lucky, but because it changes the rules of the game. Once the simulation finds a simple function, like an AND gate, it "encapsulates" it. This new, stable block becomes a building block for the next level of complexity. "We find that our artificial system can create complicated technologies (circuits), but only by first creating simpler ones as building blocks." This mirrors the concept of combinatorial explosion, where the potential combinations of existing technologies create a new, manageable layer of possibilities, preventing the system from being overwhelmed by the sheer scale of the search space.

The Architecture of Stability

To explain how this navigation works, Potter brings in a classic analogy from Herbert Simon's 1962 paper, "The Architecture of Complexity." He contrasts two watchmakers: Tempus, whose watches fall apart if interrupted, and Hora, who builds stable subassemblies. "Tempus will make far fewer watches than Hora," Potter writes, noting that Hora's hierarchical approach allows progress to accumulate rather than reset.

Potter applies this directly to the simulation. "Our experiment—or algorithm—arrives at complicated circuits by first satisfying simpler needs and using the results as building blocks to bootstrap its way to satisfy more complex ones." This is where the author's commentary shines. He suggests that the "partial fulfillment" mechanic in the original paper—where circuits that are almost right are saved—is less important than the hierarchical structure itself. Even when he removed the partial fulfillment rule in his own recreation, the system still succeeded. The key was the ability to lock in stable sub-components.

This connects deeply to the work of Lillian Hoddeson, who documented how the transistor didn't appear in a vacuum but emerged from decades of accumulated knowledge about semiconductors. Just as Hoddeson showed that the invention was a convergence of physics, chemistry, and engineering, Potter's simulation shows that complexity is a ladder, not a leap. "The emergence of circuits such as 8-bit adders seems not difficult," Potter notes, but only because the simulation has already solved the simpler problems that make the complex ones possible.

Critics might argue that biological evolution and technological design operate on different timescales and constraints, making the direct comparison to Simon's watchmakers slightly reductive. However, the mathematical reality of the search space remains the same regardless of the domain. The bottleneck is always the same: how do you traverse a space of 10^177,554 options without getting lost?

The Information Economy of Innovation

Ultimately, Potter reframes the entire concept of technological progress. It is not about the brilliance of the inventor, but the efficiency of the search. "When we look a little closer, we find what the paper is really showing us is that finding some new technology is a question of efficiently acquiring information." The simulation succeeds because it treats every solved problem as a new piece of information that shrinks the search space for the next problem.

This has profound implications for how we view institutional innovation. If the executive branch or a large corporation tries to innovate by randomly throwing resources at a complex problem without building stable sub-assemblies, they are playing the role of Tempus. They will fail because the search space is too vast. Success requires the deliberate, often boring work of encapsulating small wins and turning them into the foundation for the next step. Potter's analysis suggests that the "secret" to progress is not a sudden breakthrough, but the disciplined accumulation of stable, reusable components.

Bottom Line

Potter's strongest contribution is demystifying the "black box" of innovation by revealing the combinatorial math that makes it possible. His argument holds up because it replaces the myth of the lone genius with the reality of hierarchical accumulation. The biggest vulnerability is the assumption that all technological domains can be modeled as boolean logic circuits, but the underlying principle—that complexity requires stable stepping stones—is robust. For anyone trying to build something new, the lesson is clear: stop looking for the final answer and start building the ladder.

Information and Technological Evolution

by Brian Potter · Construction Physics · Read full article

I spend a lot of time reading about the nature of technological progress, and I’ve found that the literature on technology is somewhat uneven. If you want to learn about how some particular technology came into existence, there’s often very good resources available. Most major inventions, and many not-so-major ones, have a decent book written about them. Some of my favorites are Crystal Fire (about the invention of the transistor), Copies in Seconds (about the early history of Xerox), and High-Speed Dreams (about early efforts to build a supersonic airliner).

But if you’re trying to understand the nature of technological progress more generally, the range of good options narrows significantly. There’s probably not more than ten or twenty folks who have studied the nature of technological progress itself and whose work I think is worth reading.

One such researcher is Brian Arthur, an economist at the Santa Fe Institute.1 Arthur is the author of an extremely good book about the nature of technology (called, appropriately, “The Nature of Technology,”) which I often return to. He’s also the co-author, along with Wolfgang Polak, of an interesting 2006 paper, “The Evolution of Technology within a Simple Computer Model,” that I think is worth highlighting. In this paper Arthur evolves various boolean logic circuits (circuits that take ones and zeroes as inputs and give ones and zeroes as outputs) by starting with simple building blocks and gradually building up more and more complex functions (such as a circuit that can add two eight-bit numbers together).

I wanted to highlight this paper because I think it sheds some light on the nature of technological progress, but also because the paper does a somewhat poor job of articulating the most important takeaways. Some of what the paper focuses on — like the mechanics of how one technology gets replaced by a superior technology — I don’t actually think are particularly illuminating. By contrast, what I think is the most important aspect of the paper — how creating some new technology requires successfully navigating enormous search spaces — is only touched on vaguely and obliquely. But with a little additional work, we can flesh out and strengthen some of these ideas. And when we look a little closer, we find what the paper is really showing us is that finding some new technology is a question of efficiently acquiring information.
