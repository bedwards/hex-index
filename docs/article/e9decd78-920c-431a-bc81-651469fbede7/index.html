Information and Technological Evolution

Brian Potter · Construction Physics ·Apr 2, 2026 · 30 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Brian Potter cuts through the noise of technological optimism by revealing that innovation isn't magic—it's a brutal game of information retrieval played in a universe of impossible odds. While most histories of invention focus on the genius of the inventor, Potter argues that the real bottleneck is the sheer size of the search space we must navigate to find a working solution. This matters now because it reframes how we should invest in R&D: not just funding more ideas, but funding better ways to build stable stepping stones.

The Library of Broken Circuits

Potter begins by observing a gap in our intellectual toolkit. "If you want to learn about how some particular technology came into existence, there's often very good resources available," he writes, citing deep dives into the transistor and the Xerox machine. But when the question shifts to the general nature of progress, the literature thins. He points to economist Brian Arthur as a rare exception, specifically highlighting a 2006 paper that simulates the evolution of boolean logic circuits. Potter notes that while Arthur's paper is good, "the paper does a somewhat poor job of articulating the most important takeaways."

The author's own analysis of the simulation reveals the true mechanism of progress. The simulation starts with basic NAND gates and randomly combines them. If a combination works, it gets "encapsulated" and added to the pool of building blocks for future, more complex circuits. Potter observes that this process mirrors biological evolution, noting that "complex features can be created in biological evolution only if simpler functions are first favored and act as stepping stones." This is a crucial insight: you cannot jump straight to an 8-bit adder. You must first master the 4-bit adder. Critics might argue that biological evolution is a poor analogy for human engineering, where we can skip steps via theory, but Potter's simulation suggests that even in engineered systems, the combinatorial explosion forces a step-by-step approach.

Finding complex functions like an 8-bit adder or a 6-bit equal requires successfully finding working functions amidst a vast ocean of non-working ones.

Potter then dismantles the idea that this is merely a story of gradual improvement. He argues that the simulation's "partial fulfillment" mechanic—where circuits that are almost right are kept and refined—is less important than the hierarchical structure itself. "The most interesting aspect of this paper isn't showing how new, better technologies supersede earlier ones, but how the search for a new technology requires navigating enormous search spaces," he asserts. This reframing is powerful because it shifts the focus from the result of innovation to the cost of discovery.

The Combinatorial Abyss

To illustrate the scale of the problem, Potter dives into the math of truth tables. He explains that while a simple function has few possibilities, the complexity grows exponentially. "If you have 16 inputs and 9 outputs, like an 8-bit adder does, you have a mind-boggling 10^177554 possible logic functions," Potter writes. To put that in perspective, he notes that the number of atoms in the universe is only about 10^80. The probability of stumbling upon a working 8-bit adder by random chance is effectively zero.

This is where the argument gains its teeth. Potter connects this to Herbert Simon's 1962 parable of the watchmakers, Hora and Tempus. Tempus builds a watch part by part; if interrupted, the whole thing falls apart. Hora builds stable subassemblies first. Potter explains that "Hora makes completed watches about 4,000 times faster than Tempus" because Hora doesn't have to restart from zero every time he makes a mistake. In the context of technology, this means that the ability to encapsulate successful sub-circuits is the only thing that makes complex innovation possible. Without these stable subassemblies, the search space is too vast to ever cross.

The likelihood of such a circuit being discovered by random combinations in 250,000 steps is negligible.

Potter's recreation of the simulation confirms that without these stepping stones, the system fails. He notes that "if the simpler goals aren't met first, the simulation won't find solutions to the more complex goals." This validates the hierarchical nature of technological evolution. It suggests that the "combinatorial explosion" mentioned in related deep dives isn't just a mathematical curiosity; it is the fundamental constraint on how fast we can invent. A counterargument worth considering is that human intuition allows us to prune the search space more effectively than a random simulation, but Potter's point remains: even with intuition, we are still navigating a space larger than the universe.

Bottom Line

Potter's strongest contribution is exposing the hidden geometry of innovation: progress is not a straight line but a hierarchical climb through a near-infinite void of failure. His argument is most vulnerable where it relies on a simulation that may oversimplify the role of human creativity in pruning search spaces. However, the takeaway is clear: if we want faster technological progress, we must prioritize building and protecting stable subassemblies, because they are the only bridges across the abyss.

Excerpt from the original article on Construction Physics

I spend a lot of time reading about the nature of technological progress, and I’ve found that the literature on technology is somewhat uneven. If you want to learn about how some particular technology came into existence, there’s often very good resources available. Most major inventions, and many not-so-major ones, have a decent book written about them. Some of my favorites are Crystal Fire (about the invention of the transistor), Copies in Seconds (about the early history of Xerox), and High-Speed Dreams (about early efforts to build a supersonic airliner).

But if you’re trying to understand the nature of technological progress more generally, the range of good options narrows significantly. There’s probably not more than ten or twenty folks who have studied the nature of technological progress itself and whose work I think is worth reading.

One such researcher is Brian Arthur, an economist at the Santa Fe Institute.1 Arthur is the author of an extremely good book about the nature of technology (called, appropriately, “The Nature of Technology,”) which I often return to. He’s also the co-author, along with Wolfgang Polak, of an interesting 2006 paper, “The Evolution of Technology within a Simple Computer Model,” that I think is worth highlighting. In this paper Arthur evolves various boolean logic circuits (circuits that take ones and zeroes as inputs and give ones and zeroes as outputs) by starting with simple building blocks and gradually building up more and more complex functions (such as a circuit that can add two eight-bit numbers together).

I wanted to highlight this paper because I think it sheds some light on the nature of technological progress, but also because the paper does a somewhat poor job of articulating the most important takeaways. Some of what the paper focuses on — like the mechanics of how one technology gets replaced by a superior technology — I don’t actually think are particularly illuminating. By contrast, what I think is the most important aspect of the paper — how creating some new technology requires successfully navigating enormous search spaces — is only touched on vaguely and obliquely. But with a little additional work, we can flesh out and strengthen some of these ideas. And when we look a little closer, we find what the paper is really showing us is that finding some new technology is a question of efficiently acquiring information.
