Transcript excerpt on Economics Explained

The world is now more in debt than it ever has been before. Between governments, companies, and individuals, we owe $315 trillion to one another, which is roughly 300% of global GDP. At even a modest rate, that means 10% of all productive output for the entire planet is consumed by interest payments alone, which puts the global economy in a pretty vulnerable situation. Over recent decades, we've refined a system to smooth out natural economic booms and busts by pushing lots of money into the economy during bad times to get things moving again.

What we arguably haven't got as good at, though, is taking our foot off the accelerator during good times to make sure things don't go too far. The massive accumulation of debt this has resulted in is just one symptom of this problem. And controversially, it may not even be the biggest concern. We owe this debt to one another.

One person's liability is another person's asset, and beyond that, it's just numbers in a database. But this refusal to let the economy feel pain might be genuinely hurting our long-term prosperity. In the past, hard economic times cleared out unproductive businesses and offered an opportunity for economies to reallocate scarce resources to more promising areas. Today, in an age of meme coins worth more than centuries old car companies and endless businesses raising billions of dollars for no clear reason, it's understandable why more economists are calling for a bit of cold water to sober up the exuberance.

It's understandable to see why we may not think that resources are necessarily being allocated in the most efficient manner. And oh boy, what luck. A trade war combined with a growing number of actual wars in globally strategic regions could be exactly that opportunity. But is this the recession that we need to have?

Will it do any long-term good? And finally, will we be willing to let it get that bad? So, as you probably been following in the news in the past weeks, trade wars are on again, especially between China and the US. The US just rolled out new tariffs on Chinese goods at saying it's about trade imbalances and national security.
