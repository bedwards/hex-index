Commentary by Brian Edwards

Crash Course doesn't just recount the history of artificial intelligence; they reframe the last seventy years of computing as merely the prologue to a far more disruptive era. While most histories focus on the hardware, this piece argues that the true revolution lies in the shift from rigid, coded logic to systems that learn from data, a transition governed by predictable mathematical patterns that are now accelerating faster than our ability to regulate them.

From Refrigerators to Neural Networks

The narrative begins by establishing the sheer velocity of technological change, noting that the exponential growth of the last half-century might soon look trivial compared to what is coming. "The change was unbelievably fast. It was like nothing history had ever seen. And yet, it's possible that it won't even hold a candle to what's happening now," Crash Course writes. This framing is effective because it immediately dispels the notion that we are merely in a period of incremental improvement. The author anchors this in the familiar concept of Moore's Law, explaining how Gordon Moore's 1965 prediction about transistor density held true for decades, only to be superseded by a new frontier.

The piece then distinguishes between the early, "narrow" AI of the past and the modern systems we recognize today. It highlights the Bernstein chess program of 1957, which could beat a human but took eight minutes per move, and contrasts it with Deep Blue, which defeated a world champion in 1997. However, the crucial distinction lies in the methodology. "Deep Blue, like all those other early chess bots, was still an example of symbolic AI. It's a kind of AI that relies on the logic coded directly into its hardware and makes moves by hard-coded search and evaluation," Crash Course explains. This is a vital clarification for busy readers: early AI didn't learn; it was simply following a very complex instruction manual. The commentary here is sharp, pointing out that while Deep Blue was powerful, it lacked the adaptability of true intelligence.

The Deep Learning Revolution

The argument pivots to the modern era with the introduction of neural networks, which mimic the human brain's layered structure. The author uses the evolution of the chess bot Stockfish to illustrate the power of this shift. Unlike its predecessors, Stockfish utilizes an efficiently updatable neural network that allows it to learn from experience, revising strategies when a move leads to a loss. "Unlike symbolic AI, bots that run on any type of neural networks can change based on experience," Crash Course writes. This transition from hard-coded rules to deep learning is presented as the catalyst for the current explosion in AI capabilities.

The piece identifies three pillars that fuel this deep learning revolution: data, algorithms, and compute. It argues that the ability to process vast amounts of information is what separates modern general-purpose AI from its narrow ancestors. "In general, AI neural networks that are fed more data perform better. The more books a large language model reads, the better it'll be at writing its own," the author states. This observation is both a technical explanation and a warning. It suggests that the path to super-intelligence is not necessarily through better human engineering, but through the sheer accumulation of resources. Critics might note that this "bigger is better" approach ignores the diminishing returns of scaling and the environmental costs of massive compute requirements, but the author acknowledges these caveats while maintaining that the trend is undeniable.

In the world of neural networks and deep learning, being able to find patterns and learn from huge quantities of data is going to work better than waiting for a human expert to code them in.

The introduction of "transformers" is described as the tool that allows these systems to process entire sequences of data at once, rather than word by word. This efficiency is why general-purpose AI can now write books, generate images, and drive cars with unprecedented speed. The author notes that benchmark saturation is happening "freaky fast," meaning AI is quickly mastering tasks that were once thought to be exclusively human domains. This rapid progress raises a critical question: if the systems are improving so quickly, can we keep up?

Scaling Laws and the Future

The commentary culminates in the concept of "scaling laws," mathematical formulas that predict AI performance based on data and compute. Crash Course admits the formulas look like nonsense to non-experts but emphasizes their practical implication: "Bigger is better." This principle explains why tech giants like Microsoft and Google dominate the field, possessing the massive resources required to feed these models. "Scaling laws see a big future for AI. As algorithms improve and data and compute keep increasing, AI could deep learn its way to some pretty advanced stuff like finding new treatments for cancer or achieving super intelligence," the author concludes.

However, the piece ends on a note of cautious uncertainty. While Moore's Law held for fifty years, there is no guarantee that scaling laws will last indefinitely. The author warns that we may soon reach a point where these predictive models fail to tell us if AI is "harmlessly marching pawns around or coming for us all." This ambiguity is the piece's most compelling feature. It refuses to offer a utopian or dystopian certainty, instead presenting a future that is mathematically predictable yet fundamentally unknown.

Bottom Line

Crash Course successfully demystifies the technical leap from symbolic AI to deep learning, making the case that the current AI boom is driven by scalable, data-hungry architectures rather than sudden breakthroughs in human-like reasoning. The argument's greatest strength is its clear explanation of scaling laws, but its biggest vulnerability is the assumption that these laws will continue to hold without hitting a hard wall of diminishing returns or resource constraints. Readers should watch for the moment when the industry shifts from scaling up to scaling smarter, as that will be the true test of whether the current trajectory is sustainable.
