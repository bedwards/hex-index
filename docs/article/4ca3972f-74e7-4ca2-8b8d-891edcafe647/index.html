In the wake of violence

Jay Kuo · The Status Kuo ·Dec 15, 2025 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Jay Kuo cuts through the noise of recent tragedies to expose a dangerous double standard: how society and the state systematically punish entire minority groups for the crimes of individuals, while absolving the majority of any collective guilt. This is not just a moral critique; it is a forensic look at how policy is weaponized against refugees and minorities in real-time, offering a stark contrast to the inaction seen in other contexts.

The Architecture of Collective Guilt

Kuo begins by observing the silent, recurring prayer of minority communities after every mass shooting: "I hope he wasn't one of ours." He argues that this fear is not paranoia but a rational response to a system where a single act by a brown, black, or trans individual triggers immediate condemnation of their entire identity group. The author highlights how this dynamic plays out with chilling consistency, noting that while the LGBTQ+ community faced baseless rumors linking a killer to his trans roommate, and Muslims faced immediate Islamophobic rhetoric after the Bondi Beach attack, no such collective blame is ever applied to the demographic that actually commits the vast majority of mass shootings.

"When a white mass shooter is taken down by a white officer or bystander, we never focus on their race or religion. This only happens with ethnic, religious or sexual minorities—because our brains are wired to accept that minority communities are somehow responsible for the acts of any one of them, while whites are always absolved of any guilt by association."

This framing is powerful because it moves beyond individual prejudice to describe a systemic cognitive bias that has been codified into law. Kuo points out the absurdity of this logic by contrasting the reaction to a white shooter with the reaction to a Muslim hero. He notes that Ahmed al-Ahmed, a Muslim refugee who disarmed a killer in Australia, did not dispel the racist rhetoric surrounding the event. As Kuo writes, "You'd think that a Muslim man on Bondi Beach committing one of the most heroic acts ever caught on camera might dispel some of the otherwise inevitable racist rhetoric. But it won't." The evidence suggests that systemic bias is not about facts or counter-examples; it is about a pre-existing narrative that refuses to be dislodged.

Critics might argue that security concerns regarding specific nationalities are pragmatic rather than prejudiced, but Kuo dismantles this by highlighting the inconsistency of such policies. He points to the executive branch's decision to indefinitely suspend immigration requests for Afghans following a single attack by one individual. This move, which potentially stranded tens of thousands of asylum seekers, stands in stark contrast to historical precedents. Kuo asks the reader to imagine if an immigrant from Sweden had killed two U.S. soldiers and the administration had responded by cutting off all immigration from that country. He concludes, "It's nonsensical in the extreme. It would simply never happen."

Policy as Punishment

The commentary takes a sharper turn when Kuo connects this social dynamic to official government action. He describes the suspension of Afghan immigration processing not as a security measure, but as a form of collective punishment that mirrors tactics used by fascist regimes. This is a heavy claim, but Kuo supports it by showing how the policy punishes the innocent for the acts of the guilty, a hallmark of authoritarianism that has become normalized in the current political climate.

"Alarmingly, however, collective punishment has now become official White House policy without very much pushback or protest."

The author's choice to label this "official policy" is significant. It shifts the conversation from isolated incidents of hate speech to a structural failure of governance. By referencing the specific suspension of asylum processing, Kuo forces the reader to confront the tangible human cost of this rhetoric. The argument is bolstered by the historical context of the National Firearms Agreement in Australia. Kuo reminds us that thirty years ago, Australia responded to a tragedy in Tasmania not with collective blame, but with decisive legislative action. The National Firearm Restriction Act of 1996 banned assault rifles and mandated buybacks, removing as many as one million guns from circulation. The result was a dramatic drop in mass shootings, with none occurring for two decades.

"Thirty years ago, Australia as a nation responded decisively to a horrific mass shooting event in Tasmania by passing the National Firearm Restriction Act of 1996... Since passage of that Act, mass shootings in Australia have become extremely rare."

Despite this clear evidence, Kuo notes that opponents of gun regulation seized upon the Bondi Beach attack to argue that gun control doesn't work. He exposes the logical fallacy in their argument: the attackers used shotguns, which are still permitted, rather than the now-banned semi-automatic rifles that would have caused far greater carnage. The author argues that the failure to learn from this is not a lack of data, but a lack of political will. He writes, "All the evidence points to the fact Australia's firearm restrictions actually work, and that we ought to be doing the same here in the U.S. But we currently lack the political will and an adaptive governing system to get it done."

The Crisis of Moral Clarity

The final section of the piece addresses the moral vacuum left by political leaders who refuse to condemn violence unequivocally. Kuo contrasts the equivocation of the current administration with the clear moral stance taken by figures like Rob Reiner and New York City Mayor-elect Zohran Mamdani. He criticizes the tendency to shrug off violence as inevitable, quoting a dismissive response to the Brown University shootings: "Things can happen." Kuo argues that this fatalism is a failure of leadership.

"We frequently let comments like that go these days because it's just 'Trump being Trump,' but we as a nation should never tell ourselves we 'have to get over it' as Trump has urged before following mass shooting events, or simply accept that 'Things can happen.'"

Instead, Kuo champions the moral clarity displayed by Palestinian voices condemning antisemitic terror and by Mamdani's call to protect Jewish communities. The author highlights a viral post from a Palestinian man who stated, "Nothing the Israeli government has ever done can justify violence against Jewish people for the mere fact of their Jewishness." This distinction is crucial: it separates political grievances from the fundamental right to life. Kuo argues that true moral clarity requires governments to protect their people from extreme violence and to reject the politics of collective guilt.

"The actions of a few do not, and should not ever, condemn the many. The politics of a murder victim are irrelevant because no such violence should ever occur. And terrorist attacks upon innocent people are wrong and must be condemned without exception."

This section lands with particular force because it refuses to let the reader off the hook with vague platitudes. It demands that political leaders and citizens alike reject the normalization of violence and the scapegoating of minorities. The argument is that without this clarity, society remains trapped in a cycle of tragedy and reaction.

"Only by so doing, can we honor the legacy of those who tragically lost their lives by solemnly recommitting ourselves to a world with less horror, less hatred and less violence."

Bottom Line

Kuo's strongest asset is his ability to link the abstract concept of systemic bias to concrete, devastating policy outcomes, proving that collective punishment is not just a social ill but a governing strategy. The piece's primary vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that moral clarity alone can overcome entrenched political interests, a hurdle that history suggests is far higher than the author implies. Readers should watch for whether the momentum for gun reform in Australia can serve as a viable model for the U.S., or if the current political climate will continue to prioritize collective blame over evidence-based solutions.

In the wake of violence

by Jay Kuo · The Status Kuo · Read full article

Over the weekend, there was death and tragedy at every turn. From students in classrooms at Brown University, to Jews murdered while celebrating the first day of Hanukkah on Bondi Beach in Australia, to the violent death of a beloved filmmaker and his wife at the apparent hands of their own son—it’s a lot to absorb and try to make any sense of.

I won’t try to do so today. The violence arose from seemingly intractable issues: the easy availability of high powered rifles in the U.S., the rise of violent antisemitism, and the deep trauma of drug addiction, even among the most privileged in our society.

But while we cannot solve these things easily, we can at least take a few lessons from these horrific events, including how and how not to react. Clear-headedness during such crises helps us focus on and address the problems that lit these fires, rather than expend time stomping out the flames fanned by others.

The urge to collectively blame and punish

Nowadays when we learn about a mass shooting, every minority community makes the same silent prayer: “I hope he wasn’t one of ours.” We’ve grown used to this because we understand what typically happens in the wake of a violent act committed by any brown or black skinned, immigrant, trans or Muslim individual: condemnation of every other member of that community.

Rep. Randy Fine (R-FL) never misses a chance to do so. Following the Bondi Beach violence, he demanded, “How many more times is this going to happen until we wake up? Islam is not compatible with the West.”

The LGBTQ+ community endured the same leap to judgment and collective punishment when reports emerged that Charlie Kirk’s killer was in a romantic relationship with his trans roommate.
