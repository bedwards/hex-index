Criminal: Apple Literally Lied Under Oath

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Jun 2, 2025 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Most legal analysis of the Apple-Epic Games dispute focuses on antitrust economics, but The Hated One makes a far more explosive claim: that Apple's leadership didn't just lose a civil case, they committed criminal fraud. The author argues that top executives, including CEO Tim Cook, knowingly lied under oath and obstructed justice to maintain a monopoly, a level of corporate malfeasance that could theoretically send them to jail. This isn't just a complaint about high fees; it is a direct accusation of criminal contempt against the most valuable company on earth.

The Architecture of Extortion

The Hated One begins by dismantling the idea that Apple's 30% commission is merely a business policy, framing it instead as a coercive tool. "Apple would literally extort developers violating their policies with a complete ban and removal of their apps from the app store," the author writes. This characterization is effective because it shifts the narrative from a standard contract dispute to one of power imbalance, noting that for many developers, losing access to iOS means their business is effectively over. The author points out that while Android allows sideloading, Apple's ecosystem is a "damn near monopoly" where losing the platform is fatal.

Critics might note that Apple argues these rules are necessary for security and user experience, a point the author dismisses as a "wool garden" designed to hide costs. The Hated One contends that the real issue is the prohibition on "steering," where developers are banned from telling users they can pay cheaper prices elsewhere. "Apple didn't just create a wool garden. They purposefully built an information blackbox around their system so that they could lock their users in and squeeze more money out of them." This metaphor of an information blackbox is a strong rhetorical device, illustrating how the lack of transparency directly inflates prices for consumers. The argument holds weight because it connects the technical restriction to a tangible financial harm for the end-user, not just the developer.

The Smear of Oath-Breaking

The piece takes a darker turn when discussing Apple's response to the court order. The author asserts that Apple didn't just fail to comply; they actively subverted the law while pretending to follow it. "In later court testimonies, Apple's VP of finance, Alex Roman, outright lied under oath to hide the truth about these executive decisions." The Hated One details how Apple internally debated two non-compliant strategies: either restricting where payment links could appear or charging a new 27% fee, ultimately choosing both. The author highlights that even Philip Schiller, a top executive, advised full compliance, but Tim Cook allegedly ignored this advice. "Tim Cook chose to ignore Schiller and let his chief financial officer look Main Street to go with the non-compliant plan."

This section is the most damning, as it moves from civil antitrust violations to potential criminal liability. The author notes that the judge has already referred the matter to a US attorney for investigation into criminal contempt. "That's why the judge decided to pass the matter to a US attorney to investigate for criminal contempt. This mean Apple as a company as well as some of their top executives could be held criminally liable and even end up in jail." The gravity of this claim is underscored by the author's observation that such behavior would result in immediate prosecution for any individual or smaller corporation. "If you did a fraction of what Apple and their executives did, you'd have the book thrown at you so hard you'd become [Music] Facebook."

Apple is not your friend. It's an empire of evil.

The Political Shield

The final layer of the argument questions whether justice will actually be served, attributing potential impunity to political connections. The Hated One argues that the executive branch, which controls the criminal investigation, is compromised by a close relationship between Tim Cook and Donald Trump. "Cook even managed to convince Trump to scale back his original tariff plan in 2019 when he gave exceptions to the iPhone and other electronics." The author suggests that this long-standing personal rapport, including donations and private meetings, creates a conflict of interest that could derail the prosecution. "So, now that criminal prosecution is in the hands of the executive branch, how likely do you think this will go through?"

While the author's political analysis is speculative, it addresses a critical vulnerability in the legal process: the independence of the investigation. The Hated One concludes that the "elite class has no real politics," implying that corporate protectionism transcends party lines. This framing is provocative and aligns with a broader skepticism of corporate accountability, though it relies heavily on the assumption that personal relationships will override legal mandates. The author's solution to this systemic failure is a radical departure from the status quo: abandoning the iPhone entirely for "Graphine OS, a private and secure operating system that doesn't require an account."

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest contribution is the detailed account of Apple's internal deliberations on how to circumvent a court order, transforming a civil antitrust case into a potential criminal conspiracy. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on political speculation to explain why accountability might fail, rather than on legal precedent. Readers should watch closely to see if the US Attorney's Office actually pursues criminal charges, as that will determine whether this remains a high-profile civil dispute or a historic precedent for corporate criminal liability.
