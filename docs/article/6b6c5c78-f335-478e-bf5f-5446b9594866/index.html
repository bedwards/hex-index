Nuclear Energy in the 21st Century

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Aug 11, 2019 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace doesn't just present the case for nuclear energy; he exposes the brutal paradox at the heart of the climate crisis: the technology that could save us is also the one we are most afraid to build. While most discussions get stuck on the binary of "nuclear vs. renewables," Borlace forces a reckoning with the messy reality that Germany's anti-nuclear pivot actually increased coal consumption, a counterintuitive twist that demands immediate attention from anyone serious about decarbonization.

The Staggering Scale of the Opportunity

Borlace opens by grounding the debate in deep time, noting that humanity has been on a "technological Odyssey" for 180,000 years, yet our reliance on fossil fuels has only lasted 150. He quickly pivots to the solution discovered in 1934: "nuclear fission," a process with the potential to "transform energy generation forever." The sheer density of this energy source is his first major point. He explains that a single kilogram of nuclear fuel releases "millions of times more energy than a kilo of coal," making it the only non-renewable resource capable of matching the output of renewables without the intermittency issues.

The author's data on the United States is particularly striking. He notes that while gas and coal dominate, nuclear provides a steady "base load energy generation" that "doesn't fluctuate." This reliability is the technology's superpower. Borlace writes, "nuclear power is the world's second largest source of low-carbon power after hydro producing about 30 percent of the total global low-carbon mix." This statistic is crucial because it separates nuclear from the hype cycle of solar and wind, positioning it as the backbone of a stable grid.

"Nuclear power provides base load energy generation that means it doesn't fluctuate; it's a reliable and constant source of power for the grid."

Critics might argue that "base load" is an outdated concept in an era of smart grids and massive battery storage, but Borlace's point stands: without a constant, high-output source, the grid remains vulnerable to weather-dependent failures.

The Thorium Promise and the Chinese Surge

The commentary shifts to the future, where Borlace highlights a "resurgence in interest" in molten salt reactors fueled by thorium. He describes this as a "very smart piece of lateral thinking" because it "does away with the need to use water for cooling," addressing the primary failure mode of the Chernobyl and Fukushima disasters. The abundance of thorium is another key argument; Borlace points out that it is "at least three times as abundant as uranium" and comparable to lead in availability.

This technological optimism is mirrored by geopolitical reality. Borlace details how China is "catching up fast," with a plan to double its nuclear capacity by 2040. He notes that "58 percent of their energy still comes from coal," yet their strategy relies on a massive nuclear expansion to meet Paris Agreement goals. The author suggests that for China, switching to thorium isn't just environmental; it's strategic, allowing them to "save China massively on uranium import."

As Borlace puts it, "China's plan also relies on a doubling of its current nuclear power generation... and that put China just behind the US as the second largest nuclear energy producer in the world." This framing is effective because it removes the moralizing often found in Western debates and focuses on the pragmatic necessity of energy security.

The Weight of the Past: Waste and Fear

However, Borlace does not shy away from the "downside column." He confronts the elephant in the room: spent nuclear fuel. He states plainly that a typical plant generates "20 tons of used nuclear fuel every year" that is "highly radioactive and extremely hazardous to life." The logistical nightmare of this waste is undeniable. "You can't just dump it in a landfill site," he writes, noting that it must be managed for "hundreds or even thousands of years."

The psychological barrier is perhaps even higher than the technical one. Borlace references the "huge fear of nuclear accidents" stemming from Three Mile Island, Chernobyl, and Fukushima. He cites a poll showing that "63 percent of the population were opposed to nuclear energy" following the Fukushima disaster. The human cost of Chernobyl is laid out with grim precision: "115,000 people were immediately evacuated... and another 220 thousand people were subsequently relocated."

"The accident caused serious social and psychological disruption in the lives of those affected and vast economic losses over the entire region."

This section is where Borlace's argument is most vulnerable to criticism. While he accurately reports the death tolls and evacuations, he briefly touches on the "4,000 cancer deaths" estimate from Cancer Research UK without fully exploring the debate around long-term, low-dose radiation effects. A more robust analysis might have acknowledged that the fear often outpaces the statistical risk, a nuance that could strengthen his case for public education over policy paralysis.

The Cost of Complexity

The final hurdle Borlace addresses is economic. He argues that while operating costs are low, the "capital costs of a nuclear power plant are much higher than for energy sources such as coal and natural gas." He highlights a litany of failures in Western Europe, including the "fiasco" at Flamanville in France, where costs soared to "almost four times the original budget."

Borlace quotes the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists to drive the point home: "the annual cost of repaying the initial investment is substantially higher than the annual operating costs." He uses the Hinkley Point project in the UK as a prime example of "cost overruns and delays," noting the government's controversial move to force "British taxpayers to take up the burden of the project overspend."

He concludes this section with a sobering warning from adviser Dieter Helm: "no smart contracting or regulating framework can magic away the deep challenges that nuclear faces." This admission is vital. It prevents the piece from sounding like a sales pitch and instead frames nuclear as a high-stakes gamble.

"The very large upfront and sunk costs, the risk and the safety regulation and the challenges of getting rid of the waste."

Critics might note that the comparison with renewables is shifting rapidly; as solar and wind prices plummet, the argument for expensive, decade-long nuclear projects becomes harder to justify. Borlace acknowledges this, citing the IPCC's warning that we have "at most about 12 years" to transform our systems, a timeline that seems incompatible with the slow pace of nuclear construction.

The German Paradox

Perhaps the most compelling part of Borlace's commentary is his critique of Germany's "rash policy decision" to abandon nuclear power. He points out the irony that in its quest to reduce carbon emissions, Germany "had to lean heavily on its coal industry to bridge the results and energy gap." He notes that "nearly a quarter of all electricity produced in that country still comes from burning lignite," the "dirtiest of all fossil fuels."

This is the piece's strongest evidence. Borlace writes, "closing down existing nuclear power plants that provide safe low-carbon reliable base load energy for our electricity grids does seem like a rather rash policy decision." He contrasts this with the "ill-considered energy policy decisions" of Japan and Germany, suggesting that fear-driven politics can lead to worse environmental outcomes than the technology itself.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace delivers a balanced but urgent argument: nuclear energy is the only proven technology capable of providing the massive, reliable, low-carbon power needed to replace fossil fuels, yet it is being strangled by cost overruns, waste fears, and political cowardice. The strongest part of his coverage is the exposure of the German paradox, proving that anti-nuclear sentiment can inadvertently fuel coal consumption. However, the argument's biggest vulnerability remains the timeline; without a breakthrough in cost reduction or construction speed, the 12-year window to avert climate catastrophe may close before the next reactor comes online.
