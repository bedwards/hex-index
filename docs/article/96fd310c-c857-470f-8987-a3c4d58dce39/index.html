Bearing Across

Various · Wayfare ·Mar 28, 2026 ·30 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

In a literary landscape often obsessed with the ephemeral, this piece from Wayfare dares to suggest that the most urgent political act available to a human being is the disciplined study of the past. The author, writing under the collective pseudonym Various, does not merely recount a mystical vision; they construct a rigorous argument that true justice and societal stability are impossible without a deep, unmediated engagement with the canonical texts of human history. For the busy reader seeking clarity amidst modern noise, the claim is startling: that the path to understanding our current institutional fractures lies not in new policy papers, but in the ancient, often forgotten, dialogues of Plato, Shakespeare, and the prophets.

The Architecture of the Soul

The narrative begins not with a policy proposal, but with a radical redefinition of the human condition. Various writes, "I am not ashamed, afraid, or averse to tell you what ought to be told: that I am under the direction of messengers from Heaven, daily and nightly." This opening sets a tone of absolute authority, yet the substance of the message is surprisingly intellectual rather than purely devotional. The text argues that the human mind is not a blank slate created at birth, but an eternal entity that has always existed. "Intelligence, unmade and increate, and independently ensphered as man, is one of two composing all there is," the Messenger declares. This philosophical grounding is crucial; it suggests that the capacity for wisdom is innate, not granted by the executive branch or any earthly institution.

The author's framing of this eternal intelligence as a tool for social cohesion is particularly potent. They posit that to understand the divine, one must first understand the self, and to understand the self, one must look through the eyes of the "Other." As Various puts it, "To know thy self as Other doth is no more than all gods have done 'ere thee." This is a profound pivot from the self-help genre's focus on individual gratification to a communal ethic of empathy. It implies that the current political polarization stems from a failure to recognize the shared, eternal nature of the opposing side's humanity. Critics might note that this metaphysical approach risks alienating secular readers who prefer empirical over theological arguments, yet the text bridges this by grounding its theology in the rigorous study of history and literature.

"Learning is to choose and nothing else."

The Canon as a Political Imperative

Where the piece truly distinguishes itself is in its specific, actionable prescription for how to engage with the world. The Messenger does not offer vague platitudes about reading; they provide a curated syllabus that spans millennia. "Seek ye out from books the best to learn, of Gilgamesh in whom the greater love, that man can have, a friend for friend, subsists," the text commands, before moving through Beowulf, Plato, Aristotle, and the Attic tragedies. This is not a passive suggestion; it is presented as a moral duty. The author argues that these texts are the only reliable tools for "purging" fear and understanding the cyclical nature of nations and wars.

The commentary here is sharp: the text suggests that the modern tendency to deconstruct language and history has left society vulnerable. "The learned deconstruct; an Other's mind is thine and shall instruct," the Messenger warns, implying that the academic habit of tearing down texts has severed the connection to the wisdom they contain. Instead, the reader is urged to "translate them correctly as can be," treating the past not as a resource to be mined for bias, but as a living conversation. This aligns with the deeper themes found in the companion piece on Temenos, where the concept of a sacred, protected space for discourse is essential for the survival of a community. Without this shared intellectual ground, the text suggests, society fractures into warring factions.

The author's insistence on the unity of mind and matter is also a direct challenge to the modern siloing of disciplines. "Material is thinking self-begot and thought to things thought on identical," they write. This philosophical stance dismantles the barrier between the abstract and the practical, suggesting that the way we think about history directly shapes the physical reality of our laws and borders. The text argues that a nation that forgets its literary and philosophical heritage is a nation that has lost its soul, leaving it susceptible to the "usurping-knight" of force and tyranny.

The Rejection of Force

The climax of the vision brings the abstract into the concrete through a confrontation with a "mantled poet sovereign" who wields a sword. This figure represents the temptation to use violence or political force to impose one's will, a temptation the Messenger immediately rebukes. "And would ye strike another with a sword, though he be over thee set by thy Lord?" the Messenger asks, halting the conflict. The resolution is not a battle, but a revelation: the sword must be turned into a ploughshare, and the "canon" must be opened, not reformed by force. "The canon's such as not to be reformed, but opened and restored to Other's use," the text declares.

This section serves as a powerful allegory for the current state of political discourse. The author suggests that attempts to "reform" institutions through coercion or the destruction of shared truths are doomed to fail. Instead, the only viable path is the restoration of a shared heritage that belongs to everyone. The text references the "thirty-nine articles" of old, hinting at the historical attempts to codify belief, only to dismiss them in favor of a more fluid, inclusive understanding of truth. "Land for man was made, not man for land," the mantled man concludes, echoing the sentiment found in the Seraph deep dive regarding the transformative power of love over possession. The argument is clear: the health of a society depends on its ability to see the divine in the stranger, a realization that can only come through the disciplined study of the great human stories.

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this piece is its refusal to separate spiritual wisdom from intellectual rigor, presenting the study of the classics not as an academic exercise but as a vital political necessity. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its dense, archaic prose, which may obscure its urgent message for the time-pressed reader. However, the core verdict remains: in an age of fragmentation, the only path to unity is a shared return to the foundational texts that define our common humanity.
