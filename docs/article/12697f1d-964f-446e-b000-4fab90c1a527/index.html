Every Country in the World

Sam Denby · Wendover Productions ·Dec 6, 2016 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sam Denby transforms a simple alphabetical list into a masterclass on the absurdity of human geography, revealing how arbitrary borders and quirky history define our world more than maps suggest. Rather than a dry recitation of facts, this piece argues that the planet's political landscape is a chaotic patchwork of accidents, ambitions, and anomalies that defy logical design.

The Architecture of Absurdity

Denby opens by dismantling our intuitive sense of scale and time. He notes that "stepping over the 47m long Afghanistan China border jumps you forward by 3 and 1/2 hours," a detail that instantly reframes the concept of a border from a line on a map to a temporal rupture. This is the piece's most effective hook: it forces the reader to confront the physical reality of time zones, which are often treated as abstract conventions. The author uses this to pivot into the sheer scale of China, describing it as a nation so vast it maintains a single time zone despite its size, creating a dissonance between solar time and clock time.

The commentary then shifts to the strange micro-geography of North America. Denby highlights Point Roberts, a piece of the mainland US cut off by Canada, where "students have to cross into Canada then back into the US each day on their way to school." This anecdote serves as a perfect microcosm for the piece's broader theme: borders are often historical accidents that create daily logistical nightmares. It is a compelling argument that sovereignty does not always equate to connectivity.

"Canada happens to be the second largest country on Earth and has more Lakes than the rest of the world combined."

This statistic, while impressive, is used by Denby to illustrate the sheer physical dominance of the north, which in turn leads to a discussion of Croatia's islands and the tiny nation of San Marino. The author's ability to chain these disparate facts together creates a narrative momentum that keeps the listener engaged, even as the geography jumps from the Arctic to the Mediterranean.

The Paradox of Landlocked Nations

One of the most fascinating sections involves the concept of being "double landlocked." Denby explains that Uzbekistan is "one of only two countries worldwide to be double landlocked as in landlocked by landlocked countries." This is a rare geographical distinction that highlights the isolation of Central Asia. The author notes that "no River leads to the ocean" in Uzbekistan, emphasizing the hydrological dead ends created by these political boundaries.

The piece also touches on the irony of Lesotho, a nation completely surrounded by South Africa, which is the only country with three capitals. Denby writes that South Africa is "the only country in the world to have three capitals," a fact that underscores the complexity of its political structure. The author uses this to transition into the even smaller enclave of Eswatini (formerly Swaziland), where "roads are so bad that two of the last four transport ministers died in car accidents." This dark humor serves to humanize the statistics, reminding the reader that these borders are lived realities, not just lines on a screen.

Critics might note that the rapid-fire delivery of facts can sometimes obscure the deeper geopolitical reasons behind these anomalies. For instance, the reasons for North Korea's unique time zone or the specific history of the Poland-Germany island division are mentioned but not deeply analyzed. However, for a general audience, the sheer volume of surprising facts is likely more engaging than a deep dive into any single one.

The Fragility of Borders

Denby's coverage of divided islands and split nations reveals the fragility of international agreements. He describes a border between Finland and Sweden that exists because "fin accidentally built a lighthouse in Swedish territory and so they just readjusted the border to make everyone happy." This anecdote illustrates how diplomacy can be driven by practical necessity rather than grand strategy. Similarly, the discussion of the Panama Canal, which has its Atlantic end to the east and Pacific end to the west, challenges the reader's mental map of the world.

The author also points out the existence of Bir Tawil, a piece of land "claimed by no country since Egypt and Sudan disagree on where their borders are." This is a powerful example of how borders can be so contentious that they result in a terra nullius, a place where no nation claims sovereignty. Denby uses this to highlight the arbitrary nature of many international boundaries.

"The UK is home to the shortest regularly scheduled commercial flight in the world between westray and papa westray in the Scottish Isles. It cost 17 lbs, takes 53 seconds and traverses only 1.7 miles."

This detail encapsulates the piece's spirit: the world is full of tiny, specific, and often ridiculous exceptions to the rules we assume govern it. The author's tone is one of wonder and amusement, inviting the reader to share in the discovery of these oddities.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's "Every Country in the World" succeeds because it treats geography not as a static backdrop but as a dynamic, often chaotic story of human error and ambition. The strongest element is the relentless pace of discovery, which keeps the listener constantly surprised. The biggest vulnerability is the lack of deeper context for some of the more complex geopolitical issues, but this is a minor trade-off for the sheer entertainment value of the journey. For a busy listener, this is a perfect way to expand their understanding of the world in just a few minutes.
