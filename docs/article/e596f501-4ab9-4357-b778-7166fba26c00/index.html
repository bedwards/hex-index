Chip off the old block

Scott Alexander · Astral Codex Ten ·Jun 30, 2026 ·18 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Scott Alexander delivers a startlingly intimate diagnosis of parenthood that bypasses the usual sentimental platitudes to uncover a biological and psychological loop: children are not just successors, but living mirrors reflecting their parents' youth, obsessions, and even neuroses back at them. In an era where parenting advice often feels like a rigid set of rules or a performance for social media, Alexander frames the parent-child dynamic as a strange, feudal negotiation of rights and rituals, grounded in the raw data of his own family's quirks.

The Mirror of Youth

Alexander begins by challenging the linear perception of aging. He argues that having children shatters the "brute fact" that time only moves one direction, replacing it with a surreal experience where one sees their former self resurrected in a new body. He writes, "By the time you're a parent, you're on your way to being old, ugly, tired, and cynical... Then I had kids, and got confronted with people who were basically me, but young and beautiful and happy." This observation is striking because it reframes parenthood not as a burden of responsibility, but as an unexpected emotional resurrection. The author suggests that this phenomenon is gendered in his experience; he notes, "It's like Mudjekeewis says - you've got to have a son to see your youth rise up before you," echoing the Ojibwe epic The Song of Hiawatha where the father sees his own lost passion reflected in his son.

This framing is effective because it moves beyond abstract love into specific, almost uncanny recognition. Alexander illustrates this with his own childhood obsession with trains, a trait he believed was unique to him until his son began seeing railroad tracks in crib bars and brick walls. "Surely there can't be a gene for train obsession," he muses, yet the evidence mounts as his son recites facts about 1980s train speeds that Alexander himself memorized before he could use the potty. The piece suggests that inheritance is not just physical but behavioral, a transmission of specific fixations that bypass conscious intent.

"You bring back the days departed, You bring back my youth of passion."

Critics might argue that this focus on genetic mirroring overlooks the role of environment and shared experiences in shaping a child's interests. However, Alexander anticipates this by noting his daughter inherited her mother's traits while he remained distinct from her, creating a 2-2 balance in their household dynamics. The specificity of these anecdotes—the framed newspaper article about his train obsession, the OCD door-closing rituals—grounds the philosophical claim in tangible reality.

Feudal Rights and Domestic Rituals

The second major thrust of Alexander's commentary is the comparison of toddler negotiation to medieval feudalism. He rejects the binary view of parenting as either strict discipline or permissive chaos, proposing instead that families operate under a "byzantine code" of unwritten rights and duties. Drawing on historian James Scott's analysis of grain measurement disputes in early modern Europe, Alexander writes, "Living with small children occasionally resembles a sudden Viking invasion, but it more often feels like this kind of byzantine code of feudal rights and duties."

This analogy is the piece's most intellectually rigorous move. It reframes bedtime stalling not as a failure of authority, but as a stable equilibrium where both parties understand their obligations. For instance, his son has established that he must be granted "two more minutes" before bed, but in exchange, he must go to sleep enthusiastically once those minutes are up. Alexander notes the efficiency of this system: "This is a great trade on my part, especially since I can just announce 'Bedtime!' two minutes before I actually want him to go to bed." The author admits to exploiting loopholes—like his son's lack of time perception—but frames it as a necessary adaptation to the local laws of the household.

The article also touches on the transmission of skills, noting how his daughter inherited her grandmother's artistic prodigy while his son mimics Alexander's own verbal tics and judgmental phrases. "It very funny!" has become a family meme, a linguistic artifact passed down with high fidelity despite the child's limited grammar. This section highlights how parenting is less about molding a new person and more about curating an existing, chaotic inheritance.

The Online Courtroom

Alexander concludes by addressing the hostile environment of online parenting discourse. He contrasts his nuanced, feudal approach with the rigid, often childless moralizing found on social media platforms. "If I ever admit that I'm disciplining my children less than maximally hard... a horde of I-don't-have-children-myself-buts will show up to tell me that I'm a terrible dad," he writes. He recounts being labeled the "2020s Face Of Bad Parenting" for allowing bedtime stalling, only to watch his son naturally grow out of it without intervention.

This section serves as a critique of performative parenting advice. Alexander argues that the online community treats child-rearing as a non-iterated game where every move is judged in isolation, ignoring the long-term stability of family systems. He suggests that the "Danegeld" analogy—paying off invaders to avoid immediate destruction—is a poor model for parenting, which requires stable, evolving customs rather than one-off transactions. The counterargument here is that some online criticism stems from genuine concern about child welfare in an era where safety standards have shifted; however, Alexander's point remains that rigid dogma often fails to account for the unique equilibrium of individual families.

Bottom Line

Alexander's strongest contribution is reframing parenthood as a complex system of inherited traits and negotiated rituals rather than a moral test or a linear progression. The piece's vulnerability lies in its reliance on anecdotal evidence from a specific, neurodivergent family dynamic that may not translate universally to all parenting experiences. Ultimately, the reader is left with a compelling vision of the home as a micro-polity where love is expressed through the strange, specific language of shared obsessions and feudal compromises.

Chip off the old block

by Scott Alexander · Astral Codex Ten · Read full article

I.

Having kids has given me new appreciation for old poetry. The first time I read Song of Hiawatha, I skimmed over the part in Book 3 where Hiawatha first meets his father Mudjekeewis:

Filled with joy was MudjekeewisWhen he looked on Hiawatha,Saw his youth rise up before himIn the face of Hiawatha,Saw the beauty of WenonahFrom the grave rise up before him.

"Welcome!" said he, "Hiawatha,To the kingdom of the West-Wind!Long have I been waiting for you!Youth is lovely, age is lonely,Youth is fiery, age is frosty;You bring back the days departed,You bring back my youth of passion."

But this passage communicates a secret of parenthood, something I’ve never seen discussed anywhere else. By the time you’re a parent, you’re on your way to being old, ugly, tired, and cynical. I certainly was. This felt like a brute fact about the world: we all know time only moves one direction. Then I had kids, and got confronted with people who were basically me, but young and beautiful and happy. That part of them which wasn’t me was the other person I love best in the world, also transmuted into a young and beautiful and happy form. This was a completely unexpected delight which nothing besides this one fragment of poetry had ever tried to prepare me for.

I might never have noticed this if I’d only had girls. I love my daughter, but I’ve never been a little girl; it doesn’t bring anything back for me. It’s like Mudjekeewis says - you’ve got to have a son to see your youth rise up before you.

Sometimes this is fire and passion and beauty and so on. But also, I was a bit of a weird child. I understand lots of children love trains. But probably not many get an article in the local newspaper about how train-obsessed they are. My mother still has it, framed in the guest bedroom, to embarrass me whenever I visit. Beside it are little pictures of me in my train engineer’s cap and train t-shirt and train pants holding my train book in one hand and railroad caboose lantern in the other. Every so often I will find I still remember some weird fact about the maximum speeds of various 1980s train designs, memorized before I could consistently use the potty.

Surely there can’t be a gene for train obsession. And I certainly didn’t ...
