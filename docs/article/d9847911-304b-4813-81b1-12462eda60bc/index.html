On its 250th birthday, our constitutional republic is failing the people it was founded to serve

Lucian K. Truscott IV · The Warning ·Jan 2, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Lucian K. Truscott IV does not merely critique a political moment; he dismantles the foundational myth that America's current crisis is an anomaly caused by a single leader. Instead, he argues that the constitutional republic has been failing its people since its inception, driven by a system designed to concentrate wealth and power rather than serve the citizenry. For the busy listener seeking to understand the structural rot beneath the daily news cycle, this piece offers a stark, unflinching historical audit that reframes modern corruption not as a glitch, but as a feature.

The Architecture of Exclusion

Truscott begins by rejecting the comforting notion that the founders simply failed to anticipate a "madman" in the White House. He writes, "Our problem isn't just the unhinged lunatic whom 77 million of our fellow citizens saw fit to elect in 2024. It's the whole fucking system." This blunt framing is the article's engine, forcing the reader to look past personality cults and examine the machinery of governance itself. The author contends that the Constitution, the land, and the people have been in a state of conflict for centuries, with the legal framework consistently siding with the powerful.

He traces the origin of American land ownership not as a benign transfer of title, but as a violent seizure. "The United States started out with the 13 states that had been colonies of the British Empire. The country expanded as land was seized by force from indigenous people whose presence here predated settlers from Europe by thousands of years." This historical grounding is crucial; it reminds us that the very ground beneath the federal government was acquired through dispossession, a reality that echoes in the companion deep dive on the Tulsa race massacre, where Black wealth was literally burned to the ground to enforce white supremacy. Truscott argues that this pattern of exclusion was codified early on, noting that citizenship was originally restricted to white men, with full inclusion requiring a Civil War and subsequent amendments.

"It was never of the people, by the people, and for the people. It's been of the wealthy, by the well connected, and for their friends and children and relatives right from the start."

The author's analysis of the Supreme Court's recent actions is particularly biting. He points out that the judiciary has effectively removed a critical check on executive power. "The Supreme Court quite literally removed one check on the executive – the laws of the land – by granting the president immunity from prosecution for official acts taken as president." This refers directly to the Trump v. United States ruling, which Truscott argues creates a dangerous double standard where the president is the only government official exempt from ethics laws regarding self-dealing. Critics might argue that the Court's decision was a narrow interpretation of separation of powers rather than a carte blanche for corruption, yet Truscott's point stands: the practical effect is an executive branch that operates with unprecedented insulation from accountability.

The Surveyor's Legacy

To illustrate how privilege has always been baked into the system, Truscott turns to the profession of surveying, a trade shared by George Washington and Thomas Jefferson. He explains that these founders used their expertise to draw boundaries that favored their allies and enriched their networks. "George Washington and Thomas Jefferson, our first and third presidents, were famously surveyors before they were presidents. This gave them enormous power to draw land boundaries that would become political lines of demarcation such as counties and towns and cities." This historical anecdote serves as a powerful metaphor for how policy decisions are rarely neutral; they are tools for wealth transfer.

Truscott extends this logic to the Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956, a piece of legislation often celebrated for connecting the nation. He argues that the surveying of these routes was a political act that created winners and losers. "When President Eisenhower, working with Senator Albert Gore Sr. from Tennessee, got the Federal Aid Highway Act passed in 1956, surveyors set out to map it. Decisions were made within states about where the highway would go." The result, he notes, was that farms were bisected, reducing their value, while land near new exits skyrocketed in price. This mirrors the companion analysis of the Federal-Aid Highway Act, revealing how infrastructure projects have historically been used to manipulate property values and segregate communities.

He highlights the human cost of these decisions, noting that highways were often routed through poorer neighborhoods, exposing them to pollution and displacement. "A good example of this was the way interstate highways were surveyed to pass through poorer neighborhoods of cities, sparing more wealthy areas." This is a stark reminder that the "public good" is often defined by who holds the pen. While the author's tone is undeniably cynical, the evidence of systemic bias in infrastructure planning is well-documented and supports his broader thesis about the continuity of corruption.

The Modern Continuum

Truscott connects these historical threads to the present day, arguing that the corruption we see now is merely a continuation of past practices, albeit with new tools. He points to the Citizens United decision as a turning point that allowed corporations to effectively purchase political influence. "The Citizens United decision reduced the power of everyone's citizenship by allowing corporations to buy and sell not just individual politicians, but an entire political party." He further cites the recent appointment of a billionaire to formulate a government department without Congressional authority as evidence that the system is now openly serving its corporate masters.

The author's critique of the Senate's refusal to advise and consent on judicial nominees during the Obama administration, followed by their rush to confirm Trump's nominees, underscores the breakdown of institutional norms. "The Republican-controlled Senate denied President Obama his right to appoint a member of the Supreme Court by simply sitting on its constitutional duty to advise and consent for nearly a year until Obama was out of office." This selective application of constitutional duties, he argues, signals the end of the republic as we know it.

"The corruption we see today with Donald Trump is nothing new. The only change might be that Trump has had to move overseas to ply much of his corrupt self-enrichment because so much of what we have here in this country has already been carved up by powerful men at the expense of the less powerful."

In the face of this systemic failure, Truscott offers a call to action rooted in refusal and civic engagement. He urges citizens to withdraw their support from institutions that have been co-opted, citing the cancellation of performances at the Kennedy Center as a model of resistance. "The musicians and actors and dancers who have cancelled their appearances at the Kennedy Center are providing a good example of what it means to refuse." He also challenges the media landscape, specifically the takeover of CBS, urging listeners to reject the new direction. "Don't watch CBS News. The takeover of CBS by the Ellison family and their hand-puppet Bari Weiss will not work if we don't let it."

Bottom Line

Truscott's most compelling argument is his refusal to treat current events as an aberration; he successfully frames the erosion of democracy as the logical conclusion of a system built on exclusion and self-enrichment. However, the piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its near-total lack of faith in institutional repair, offering little more than protest and electoral hope as solutions to a problem he defines as foundational. The reader is left with a sobering truth: the system is not broken, it is working exactly as designed for a specific class, and the path forward requires a complete reimagining of citizenship rather than a return to a mythical past."

On its 250th birthday, our constitutional republic is failing the people it was founded to serve

by Lucian K. Truscott IV · The Warning · Read full article

We have become way too accustomed to the idea that the founders of this country did not foresee the damage that could be done by a madman serving as president. Our problem isn’t just the unhinged lunatic whom 77 million of our fellow citizens saw fit to elect in 2024. It’s the whole fucking system.

This country is supposed to be comprised of three essential elements: The Constitution, our set of governing laws, which is written on paper and is kept in Washington D.C., the only piece of land in the United States that is not a state, but the “District of Columbia.”

The second element is the land. The United States started out with the 13 states that had been colonies of the British Empire. The country expanded as land was seized by force from indigenous people whose presence here predated settlers from Europe by thousands of years. It was further expanded by the Louisiana Purchase, which was not a transfer of title to land, much of which was unexplored at the time of its sale, but instead a granting by sale of the right to take lands controlled by indigenous tribes by treaty or conquest by war. The rest of the land we now consider the Continental United States was seized forcibly from Indian peoples or gained by war with Mexico or treaties and purchases from Spain.

The third element is people. I don’t use the term “citizens” here, because the founders did not grant citizenship except to white men. Citizenship had to be gained for slaves by fighting a Civil War with slave-owning states, and by amendments to the Constitution granting citizenship to former slaves and those born within the boundaries of the country and by the 19th Amendment granting women the right to vote.

Our constitutional republic is failing because the Constitution is failing the other two elements or our country, the land and the people. The Constitution has been chipped away by the Supreme Court, which has imposed limits on citizenship of people by restricting the right to vote. The Congress, another branch, has abdicated its responsibility to provide a check on the other two branches by refusing to write laws limiting the power of either the judiciary or the executive.
