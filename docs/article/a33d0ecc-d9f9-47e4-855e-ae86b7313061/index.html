How The NFL Covered Up Player Concussions..And Made Billions

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Jan 12, 2023 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union delivers a searing indictment of the National Football League, arguing that the league's decades-long suppression of concussion science wasn't a bureaucratic failure, but a calculated business strategy. The piece distinguishes itself by connecting the dots between the NFL's monopoly power, its influence over media giants, and the systematic silencing of players who speak out, framing the sport not just as a game, but as a predatory enterprise. This is essential listening for anyone who has ever cheered a Sunday touchdown without considering the long-term cost to the athletes on the field.

The Architecture of Denial

The author anchors the narrative in the tragic reality of Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy (CTE), a degenerative brain disease linked to repeated head trauma. More Perfect Union writes, "The NFL is an unstoppable force with a monopoly on a pastime and physically linked with American culture and economy." This framing is crucial because it explains why the league could ignore medical consensus for so long; they weren't just a sports league, they were an institution too big to fail. The commentary highlights that while the public sees the on-field injuries, the real crisis is the quiet, decades-long cover-up.

The piece traces this cover back to 1915, noting that even then, a West Point coach warned against putting concussed athletes back in the game. Yet, in the 1990s, when high-profile players like Steve Young and Troy Aikman suffered career-ending injuries, the NFL responded by appointing a rheumatologist, not a neurologist, to lead their concussion committee. As More Perfect Union puts it, "They put somebody who wasn't a neurologist or neurosurgeon or brain scientist in charge of it... which should have told us something about how seriously they were taking this." This historical context effectively dismantles the idea that the league was simply unaware of the risks; they actively chose to downplay them.

"The NFL has made 180 billion dollars in that time they've barely changed anything about their protocol and continue to sacrifice players brains in the pursuit of profit."

Critics might argue that medical understanding of CTE has evolved significantly since the 1990s, suggesting that past decisions were based on the best available science at the time. However, the author counters this by pointing out that the league's own internal research was designed to produce specific outcomes, mirroring tactics used by the tobacco industry.

The Media and Player Silencing

The argument shifts to the immense leverage the NFL holds over the media ecosystem. The author notes that major networks like Comcast, Disney, and Amazon pay billions for broadcasting rights, creating a conflict of interest where critical journalism is stifled. More Perfect Union writes, "If we think back to 2007 it was the New York Times who took the leap of assigning somebody full time to the concussion and CTE beat that allowed this to become a national story." The piece suggests that even established outlets like HBO's Real Sports faced pressure, with the implication that negative coverage could jeopardize future business deals.

This dynamic extends to the players themselves. The author describes a system where speaking out against the league can end a career. "If you are in a leadership role the NFL players association and you fight back against the NFL or say bad things in the media you're much less likely to get signed later on in your career," the text states. This creates a chilling effect where the very people suffering the most damage are financially and professionally coerced into silence. The commentary on the union's negotiation tactics is particularly sharp, noting that players were forced to give up money to reduce practice hits, a move the author calls "an interesting window into how the NFL thinks about this."

The Pipeline of Fans and the Cost of Youth

Perhaps the most disturbing claim in the piece is the assertion that the NFL actively recruits children not just to build a talent pipeline, but to create a lifelong fan base. The author argues, "The NFL is actively recruiting children to game attack of football not because they're actually even trying to create a pipeline of future NFL players they're trying to create a pipeline of future fans." This reframes youth football from a developmental activity to a marketing strategy for a dangerous product.

The piece emphasizes that children cannot consent to these risks, starting as young as five or ten years old. "The idea that we are recruiting five-year-olds for a sport that will give them a brain disease when they have 25 other options that will not give them that brain disease has to change," More Perfect Union writes. This argument strikes at the heart of the sport's cultural dominance, suggesting that the "American identity" tied to football is built on a foundation of exploited youth and ignored health risks.

"The billionaires who own the NFL need to be held accountable for the damage they've reeked on their players brains players or who make football great and just like the rest of us they're workers too."

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this coverage is its unflinching connection between the NFL's financial monopoly and its moral failure to protect players, moving beyond individual negligence to systemic exploitation. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that the league's primary motive is purely profit-driven, potentially underestimating the complexity of institutional inertia and the genuine difficulty of changing a cultural phenomenon. Readers should watch for how upcoming collective bargaining agreements address head safety, as the league's next move will either validate or refute these claims of intentional neglect.
