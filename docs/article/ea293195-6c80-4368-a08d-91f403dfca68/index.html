In a literary landscape often obsessed with irony and detachment, John Pistelli mounts a surprisingly bold defense of earnest emotion. He challenges the modernist assumption that feeling must be earned through a labyrinth of cynicism, arguing instead that the highest form of literary sophistication is the ability to love the world despite its flaws. This is not a retreat into sentimentality, but a rigorous re-examination of what makes great fiction endure.

The Case Against Irony

Pistelli opens by addressing a common anxiety among readers: the fear that wanting to be moved by a story is a sign of bad taste. He rejects this premise entirely. "Fiction is not an intellectual exercise, not a joke," he writes, dismantling the idea that literature exists solely to test a reader's wit. He traces the development of Western taste over the 20th century, noting how it conditioned students to believe that "the earnest affirmation of love had been debased by commerce and propaganda, by Hallmark and by Stalin." This historical framing is crucial; it contextualizes our current aversion to sincerity not as an inherent virtue, but as a defensive reaction to political and commercial manipulation.

Yet, Pistelli argues that even the most difficult modernist works retain a core of affection. He points to Ulysses, Lolita, and Gravity's Rainbow as examples that, beneath their irony, are "fully as moving as anything permitted to be earnest in the prior century." The argument lands because it reframes the canon. Instead of viewing these books as cold, intellectual puzzles, he invites us to see them as secret love letters to humanity. As he puts it, "Madame Bovary is the model... a work more thoroughly and scabrously ironic than what followed, but a work secretly in injured love with the world and its inhabitants behind its disaffected armor." This connection to Flaubert's masterpiece adds necessary depth, reminding readers that the "new sincerity" of the 21st century may actually be a regression, or at least a misunderstanding of how irony and love coexist.

Critics might note that dismissing the "new sincerity" of authors like David Foster Wallace or Jonathan Franzen as "civilizational regression" risks oversimplifying a complex literary evolution. However, Pistelli's point is less about the quality of specific modern novels and more about the loss of a specific emotional texture—the ability to be moved without first deconstructing the mechanism of that movement.

The vaunted "new sincerity" of the 21st-century novel, its inability or refusal to sustain an ironic perspective, its finally flagrant sentimentalism, plausibly signifies civilizational regression. Yet love was always the point.

The Architecture of Erudition

Shifting from emotion to style, Pistelli tackles the criticism that his own novel, Major Arcana, is too intellectual. A reviewer noted the sheer density of vocabulary, listing words like "au gratin," "tarsus," and "scyphozoan." Rather than apologizing, Pistelli leans into the defense of the articulate character. He challenges the "Hemingwayesque" ideal of the taciturn hero, asking, "If you could take a ten-year-old child, and instantly upload a library's worth of knowledge into that child's brain, would they somehow cease to be a child?" His answer is a firm no. He argues that maturity is not about knowing less, but about realizing that knowledge does not guarantee understanding.

This section is particularly effective because it reframes erudition not as a display of the author's ego, but as a necessary tool for expressing the full range of human possibility. He cites Ralph Ellison, who wrote that "it would be necessary... to invent" eloquent characters because "by such attempts that [the novelist] fulfills his social responsibility as an American artist." Pistelli uses this to justify his own stylistic choices, suggesting that the ability to articulate complex thoughts is a form of empathy. He admits, "Some of my characters are smarter than I am—a paradox I am convinced is possible," and connects this to Thomas Mann's own admission that he might be "too good at display, the 'show window,' at the manipulation of esoteric material."

The inclusion of specific literary references, such as the connection between the word "tarn" and Poe, or "peignoir" and Stevens, grounds his defense in a tangible tradition of borrowing and synthesis. It suggests that a writer's vocabulary is not an organic, biological trait but a curated collection of influences. "There was a time in each of our lives when we didn't know any words at all," he notes, a simple reminder that all language is learned, and thus, all erudition is a valid part of the human experience.

The Physiology of Feeling

The piece concludes by exploring why certain media move us more than others. Pistelli observes that "language loses its ability to move us directly once the tricks of manipulation have been identified." He contrasts this with music and photography, which have a more direct, almost physiological access to emotion. He describes the audience reaction to a play like Hamnet, where laughter and tears are indistinguishable, noting that "those laughing in the theater at the end of Hamnet had to discharge the stimulated affect as well as those who were crying, as if smoke had gotten in their faces and made them cough, sneeze, and weep."

This observation offers a fascinating counterpoint to the idea of the "jaded" reader. Pistelli suggests that the barrier to feeling is not the complexity of the work, but the reader's awareness of the author's intent. He argues that once we recognize the "tricks," the spell is broken. This explains why he finds contemporary bestsellers like Colleen Hoover less moving than Thomas Mann; the former's directness feels simplistic, while the latter's stylistic complexity functions like music, creating a deeper, more resonant emotional experience. "After we've been initiated into the experience of being moved by a Mann, a Joyce, a Flaubert, we can't go back," he writes, capturing the irreversible nature of literary maturation.

After we've been initiated into the experience of being moved by a Mann, a Joyce, a Flaubert, we can't go back, or I can't, to being moved by Hoover, who is far more direct and unadorned in language than the 18th- and 19th-century sentimentalists.

Bottom Line

John Pistelli's commentary is a vital corrective to the modern obsession with ironic detachment, successfully arguing that true literary sophistication lies in the capacity for earnest love. While his dismissal of contemporary "new sincerity" may feel harsh to some, his defense of the articulate, emotional character offers a compelling vision for what fiction can achieve. The strongest part of his argument is the reframing of erudition as an act of empathy rather than pretension, a perspective that challenges readers to reconsider the value of complexity in an age of simplified narratives.
