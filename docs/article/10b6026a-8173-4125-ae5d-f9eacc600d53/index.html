Kings and Generals reframes the familiar myth of Alexander the Great not as a flawless demigod, but as a "list of contradictions" whose genius lay in a terrifying ability to pivot between brutality and rationality. While history often treats his early campaigns as a mere prelude to the Persian invasion, this analysis argues that those chaotic first months were actually a masterclass in psychological warfare and logistical improvisation that defined his entire reign.

The Weight of Inheritance

The narrative begins by dismantling the idea that Alexander's success was inevitable. Kings and Generals writes, "He began as a young king of Macedonia and then developed into a hero and god during his own lifetime." This framing is crucial because it reminds the reader that Alexander started with a precarious position, not a guaranteed throne. The author details how the young king inherited a powerful army and a bridgehead in Asia, but notes that "these inheritances were tenuous, and to keep them, Alexander would have to fight for them." This distinction is vital; it shifts the focus from Alexander's destiny to his agency.

The commentary highlights a specific moment of political ruthlessness: the execution of Attalus, a general loyal to Alexander's father but suspected of plotting against the new king. Kings and Generals notes that Alexander "was not appeased and had Attalus executed, simultaneously sending agents to Asia Minor to secure Parmenion's loyalty." This move demonstrates that Alexander's leadership was a blend of decisive violence and calculated diplomacy. Critics might argue that this early bloodshed set a precedent for the paranoia that would eventually consume him, but the author effectively uses it to show Alexander's immediate grasp of power dynamics.

He was a forward-thinking rationalist, but also superstitious and paranoid. He was in some ways terrible and in other ways truly great.

The Speed of Audacity

Once the internal threats were neutralized, Alexander turned his attention to the fractious Greek city-states. The author emphasizes the sheer velocity of his response to rebellion. "Advised by Antipater... to remain cautious, Alexander, as he ever would, instead chose boldness and audacity." This sentence captures the essence of his strategy: speed as a weapon. The text describes how he "carved steps into the seaward side of Mount Ossa" to flank a Thessalian force, a maneuver that shocked his enemies into submission.

This section of the coverage is particularly strong because it explains why his speed worked. The Greeks had underestimated him, expecting a slow, traditional campaign. Instead, Alexander "materialized outside Thebes, shocking the proud city and assuring its temporary submission." The author argues that this psychological shock was more effective than the battle itself. By moving faster than his enemies could process, he turned potential coalitions against him into isolated, terrified entities.

Tactical Genius in the North

The narrative then shifts to the brutal campaigns in Thrace and Illyria, where Alexander faced unconventional warfare. The author provides a vivid account of the battle at Mount Haemus, where Thracian defenders planned to roll wagons down the mountain to break the Macedonian phalanx. Kings and Generals writes, "Alexander precient both to his own weakness and the Thracian's offensive bent realized that it was their plan to send the wagons crashing into his locked phalanx."

The commentary here is excellent because it breaks down the mechanics of the counter-move. Alexander didn't just fight harder; he fought smarter. He ordered his men to "break formation and opened gaps to let the wagons go by harmlessly if terrain allowed and lay prone with their locked shields above their head if not." This detail underscores the discipline of his troops and the clarity of his command. The result was a victory where "not a single soldier was lost to the Thracian ploy," a testament to the effectiveness of his preparation.

However, the author also acknowledges the limits of ancient military records. When describing the crossing of the Danube against the Getae, the text admits that casualty numbers reported by Arrian are "certainly questionable." This moment of honesty strengthens the overall credibility of the piece, showing that the author is analyzing history rather than just retelling legend.

Thus provisioned with such a makeshift fleet, that night, masked by the darkness, Alexander boldly ferried 4,000 of his infantry and 1,500 cavalry across the Danube.

The Trap at Pelium

The final segment covers the siege of Pelium, a rare tactical blunder where Alexander found himself trapped by Illyrian forces. The author describes how Alexander's "hasty miscalculation" left his army ringed by enemies and cut off from supplies. This is a critical inclusion, as it humanizes the "invincible" commander. The text explains that Alexander had to rely on a psychological ploy to escape: "Like a programmed machine of war, they pivoted right, left, back, and forth again without a word being uttered."

The commentary suggests that this display of drill was designed to disorient the enemy. "Disconcerted and enthralled by this unprecedented display of martial prowess, the Illyrians were not ready when Alexander gave his final signal." This interpretation adds depth to the story, suggesting that Alexander's greatest weapon was often the sheer, terrifying discipline of his army rather than just his personal bravery. A counterargument worth considering is that this reliance on psychological tricks might not have worked against a more disciplined enemy, but against the tribal forces of Illyria, it was the perfect solution.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals succeeds in stripping away the romanticized veneer of Alexander the Great to reveal a commander defined by adaptive brutality and relentless speed. The piece's greatest strength is its focus on the tactical improvisation of his early campaigns, proving that his genius was forged in the chaos of survival rather than the comfort of destiny. The biggest vulnerability remains the reliance on ancient sources that may exaggerate his invincibility, but the author's willingness to question those numbers keeps the analysis grounded.

After his death, his legend increased still further, becoming the founding hero of many successor kingdoms and the ultimate aspirational figure for politicians and military leaders across the Greco Roman world. During the Middle Ages, he was viewed as a romantic figure of a bygone a, a paragon of chivalri values of virtue. Later still, he was viewed as a tragic figure, a Greek tragedy made real in a man who succeeded purely off the back of his own ambition and drive before dying tragically young. Today, views of Alexander are more nuanced.

Widely acknowledged as one of the greatest military commanders in history, Alexander is also recognized as being a list of contradictions. He could be as vicious and brutal as he could be generous and kind. He was a forward-thinking rationalist, but also superstitious and paranoid. He was in some ways terrible and in other ways truly great.
