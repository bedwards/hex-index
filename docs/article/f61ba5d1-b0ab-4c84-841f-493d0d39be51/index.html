This piece delivers a scathing, historically grounded indictment of how the executive branch effectively hijacked the nation's trade policy, bypassing Congress through a legal fiction that has lingered for decades. Brad DeLong doesn't just analyze the tariffs; he dissects the specific legal hallucination—the misreading of the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)—that allowed the White House to declare a phantom emergency and unilaterally rewrite the rules of global commerce. For busy readers trying to understand why the constitutional guardrails failed so spectacularly, DeLong offers a rare clarity: this wasn't a policy debate, it was a structural collapse enabled by cowardice and bad history.

The Phantom Emergency

DeLong anchors his argument in the stark reality that the power to tax imports belongs to the legislature, not the presidency. He writes, "The American people deserve an explanation on how the president of the United States on April 2 last year announced the largest tariff package in modern history with no action by Congress." The author's framing is crucial here: he strips away the political theater to reveal a procedural void. The administration declared an emergency, but as DeLong notes, "Nothing out of the ordinary had occurred. Perhaps US trade deficits had begun to add up. But there had been no single shocking event, no calamitous fall in the dollar's exchange rate." This distinction matters because it exposes the declaration not as a response to crisis, but as a pretext for power.

The core of the failure lies in the misuse of the International Emergency Economic Powers Act. DeLong explains that while the statute allows for sanctions against bad actors like Hamas or North Korea, it was never intended as a general trade tool. He points out a critical legislative detail: "The bill to create IEEPA was not referred to the committees with jurisdiction over tariffs. Congressional committees are very jealous of their jurisdictions." This suggests the omission of tariff language was deliberate, not accidental. The administration's reliance on this statute was a legal stretch that ignored the plain text of the law.

The claimed emergency tariff authority, not even mentioned in the statute, and not anywhere recorded in Congress's consideration of the legislative authority cited by the president's lawyers, never existed at all.

Critics might argue that in a fast-moving global economy, presidents need flexible tools to respond to unforeseen trade imbalances. However, DeLong counters this by highlighting that Congress had already passed a specific, limited mechanism for balance-of-payments crises in 1974, capping tariffs at 15 percent for 150 days. The administration's choice to ignore this narrow path in favor of a broad, undefined emergency power was a rejection of the very compromise Congress had built.

The Ghost of 1971

Perhaps the most fascinating historical thread DeLong weaves is the persistent myth surrounding the Nixon shock of 1971. The administration's legal team leaned heavily on the precedent of Richard Nixon's import surcharge, assuming it was a valid use of emergency authority. DeLong dismantles this, revealing that "President Nixon had been adamant in discussions with his aides at closed meetings at Camp David... that he was not going to invoke the wartime emergency authority because it was an insult to call our allies 'enemies.'" Nixon actually used different authority, but the legal lore forgot this nuance, creating a "botched folk memory" that the White House exploited.

This historical error had profound consequences. DeLong writes, "It was convincing to four Federal Circuit appellate justices (none appointed by President Trump) for them to find this last year that broad tariff authority did exist in the more recent statute IEEPA." The fact that judges appointed by other administrations were swayed by this flawed precedent underscores how deeply the legal fiction had taken root. It wasn't just political maneuvering; it was a failure of legal scholarship and judicial scrutiny that allowed the executive to operate in a gray zone for years.

The Failure of Checks and Balances

The commentary takes a darker turn as DeLong examines why no one stopped this. He argues that the breakdown wasn't just legal, but political. He describes a "Republican congressional leadership that wanted to avoid picking any fights with Trump they could avoid, no matter how stupid and destructive the policies." The author paints a picture of a legislature paralyzed by fear, where "Republican congressional sheeple scared of Trump's hold over their core electoral base" refused to vindicate the separation of powers.

DeLong extends this critique to the judiciary, describing a Supreme Court "anxious to blow up procedure whenever it delays something of Trump's that its six-member neofascist majority likes, and eager to slow-walk anything that might wind up with them picking a fight with Trump they think they have a chance of losing." This dual failure—legislative cowardice and judicial opportunism—created a perfect storm. The result, as DeLong starkly puts it, is that "constitutional government went completely out the window last April 2."

An entire political structure composed of very weak people indeed, without either testicles or ovaries.

To illustrate the gravity of this institutional collapse, DeLong invokes the 17th-century poet Andrew Marvell, drawing a parallel to the erosion of parliamentary power under Cromwell. He quotes Marvell: "Though justice against fate complain, And plead the ancient rights in vain; But those do hold or break As men are strong or weak." This literary reference elevates the argument from a policy critique to a meditation on the fragility of liberty when institutions are led by the timid.

Bottom Line

Brad DeLong's analysis is a powerful reminder that constitutional crises often begin not with a bang, but with a misreading of history and a lack of political will. The strongest part of his argument is the meticulous debunking of the Nixon precedent, which exposes the legal foundation of the tariffs as a house of cards. The piece's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that the courts and Congress will eventually reassert themselves; it offers little hope for immediate correction in the current climate. Readers should watch for how the Supreme Court navigates this specific separation-of-powers question, as the outcome will define the limits of executive emergency power for a generation.
