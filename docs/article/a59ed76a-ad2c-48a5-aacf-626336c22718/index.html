How Mercenaries Beat Empires

Kings and Generals · Kings and Generals ·Mar 5, 2026 · 17 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Kings and Generals presents a narrative where the most dangerous force in the medieval Mediterranean wasn't an empire, but a rogue army of unemployed soldiers who decided to become the empire themselves. The author's most striking claim is that the Catalan Company didn't just fight for the Byzantine Empire; they dismantled its authority from within, only to carve out a thriving, hybrid state in Greece that defied the religious and political norms of the 14th century.

The Anatomy of a Mercenary State

The piece excels at demystifying the "fantastical" reputation of medieval mercenaries by grounding their success in specific tactical innovations. Kings and Generals writes, "The unique style of the Al-Magavas stemmed from their participation in the reconista against the Islamic powers of Iberia." This connection to the Iberian Reconquista is crucial; it explains why these light infantrymen, armed with javelins and swords, could outmaneuver the heavy cavalry of Europe and the Turkic forces of Anatolia. The author effectively argues that their success was not merely luck, but a result of "shock and ore tactics" honed over centuries of border warfare.

However, the coverage also highlights the psychological dimension of their warfare, noting that they terrorized enemies by "striking flint against their swords to create vivid sparks" while shouting, "Light iron, it's time to kill." This detail transforms the mercenaries from mere hired guns into a psychological weapon, a force designed to break morale before the first spear was thrown. The author suggests that this "utterly insane persona" was as vital to their survival as their equipment.

"The combination of skilled warfare tactics and utterly insane personas made the Catalan company a force to be reckoned with."

Critics might argue that the focus on their "insane" behavior risks romanticizing the atrocities they committed. The text admits that the locals eventually viewed them as "even worse than the Turks," yet the narrative maintains a certain admiration for their military efficacy, potentially glossing over the human cost of their "rampaging the countryside."

The Betrayal and the Pivot

The turning point of the article is the betrayal of the company by the Byzantine Emperor, a moment that underscores the fragility of the mercenary-employer relationship. Kings and Generals describes how the Emperor, fearing the growing power of the mercenaries, orchestrated a massacre: "On April 30th, 1305 CE, the company entered Adrianople to join in an imperial banquet. Upon arriving, however, they were ambushed by Allen mercenaries hired by the new emperor." This event, where the leader Roger perished alongside his men, serves as the catalyst for the company's transformation from allies to conquerors.

The author's analysis of the aftermath is particularly sharp. Rather than dissolving, the survivors "escaped to Gallipoli and from there began raiding across Macedonia and Thrace." This pivot demonstrates the company's adaptability; they were no longer fighting for a paycheck but for survival and sovereignty. The text notes that they even forged alliances with former enemies, gaining support from "Turk mercenaries in the Byzantine army who defected to their side." This pragmatic approach to alliances, ignoring religious divides for strategic gain, is a recurring theme that challenges the typical East-West binary of the era.

The Conquest of Athens and the New Order

The narrative reaches its climax with the company's seizure of the Duchy of Athens. Kings and Generals writes, "He thus rose up against the Duke and killed him during the battle of Halmyros in 1311 CE. After that, the company moved into Athens and took over the duche." This act of insurrection against their own employer is the ultimate proof of the article's thesis: mercenaries can and do beat empires when the empire fails to pay or respect them.

The author details how the company established a unique political order, where "the Customs of Barcelona, a legal code from arrogant proper, became the law of the land." This is a fascinating historical footnote that illustrates the depth of their cultural imprint. They didn't just occupy the land; they imposed their own legal and linguistic framework, making Catalan one of the official languages of a Greek duchy. The text also notes the pragmatic nature of their rule, securing alliances with "Turk blics in the Aian" despite being Catholic, and even negotiating treaties with Venice to secure their hold on power.

"The alliances of the Catalan company were pragmatic rather than based on faith or ideology."

A counterargument worth considering is whether the "undisputed aristocracy" they established was truly stable or merely a prolonged period of chaotic occupation. The text admits that historical records become "patchy" by the 1360s and that the duchy eventually lost territory to Serbia, suggesting that their state-building efforts may have been more fragile than the narrative implies.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals delivers a compelling case that the Catalan Company was the ultimate expression of mercenary power, turning the Byzantine Empire's desperation into their own kingdom. The strongest part of the argument is the detailed breakdown of how tactical innovation and psychological warfare allowed a small force to dominate a region, but the piece occasionally leans too heavily on the "dastardly" and "fantastical" aspects, potentially obscuring the complex political realities of their rule. Readers should watch for how this model of mercenary state-building echoes in later history, where private armies often outlasted the states that hired them.
