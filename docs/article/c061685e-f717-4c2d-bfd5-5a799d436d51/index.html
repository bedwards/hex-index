Why central banks are increasingly buying gold and Bitcoin

Joeri Schasfoort · Money & Macro ·Mar 6, 2025 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Joeri Schasfoort cuts through the noise of financial jargon to reveal a quiet but seismic shift: central banks are not just hoarding gold out of nostalgia, but as a direct hedge against the weaponization of the US dollar. While the narrative often focuses on Bitcoin's volatility or gold's ancient allure, Schasfoort's analysis of World Bank data exposes a pragmatic, geopolitical driver that could reshape global power dynamics. This is not a story about getting rich quick; it is a story about nations preparing for a world where the rules of the financial game are no longer guaranteed.

The Three Pillars of Reserve Assets

Schasfoort begins by dismantling the assumption that central banks are investment firms. Their primary mandate is crisis management, not yield generation. He writes, "Reserve assets are assets that central banks use to defend the value of their own currency." This distinction is crucial. When the Japanese Yen plummeted or Sri Lanka faced hyperinflation, it was the ability to swap reserves for local currency that prevented total economic collapse.

The author then ranks potential assets—dollars, euros, gold, and Bitcoin—against three specific criteria: profitability, liquidity, and safety. On profitability, the US dollar wins hands down, offering interest rates around 4.4% on government debt, whereas gold carries a negative return due to storage costs and Bitcoin pays nothing. Schasfoort notes, "Gold is just a piece of metal... Bitcoin is a data entry and it does not pay an interest rate at all." This is a fair assessment of the current yield curve, though it overlooks the long-term capital appreciation potential that some institutions are beginning to prioritize over short-term yield.

However, the analysis pivots sharply when discussing liquidity. For a reserve asset to be useful in a crisis, a central bank must be able to sell it without crashing the market. Here, the US dollar is unrivaled. Schasfoort explains, "Bitcoin is not suitable for most major central banks right now as they would crash the Bitcoin market just by selling the massive quantities that they would in a crisis." This liquidity constraint is a massive hurdle for crypto adoption at the sovereign level. Critics might note that as Bitcoin's market cap grows, this liquidity gap could narrow faster than anticipated, but for now, the math holds.

"Unlike fiat currencies which governments can create in large quantities, the quantity of gold and especially Bitcoin is finite."

This scarcity, while a feature for investors, is a bug for central banks trying to accumulate reserves cheaply. Schasfoort points out that buying large quantities of gold or Bitcoin drives up the price, making it an inefficient tool for emerging markets trying to build a safety net. In contrast, the US has historically allowed countries to accumulate dollars through trade, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of liquidity.

The Safety Paradox and the Rise of Sanctions

The most compelling part of Schasfoort's coverage is his redefinition of "safety." It is not just about price stability; it is about physical and political security. He recalls how during the Cold War, nations stored gold in London or New York to avoid Soviet invasion, effectively outsourcing their security to the US. "If you trust America to hold your gold, it makes sense that you also trust them not to confiscate your US dollars," he writes.

But that trust has eroded. The turning point, according to Schasfoort, is the freezing of Russian assets following the invasion of Ukraine. He cites IMF research showing that "both the volume and value of gold reserves increases with the imposition of sanctions from the US, UK, Euro area and Japan." This is the smoking gun. Central banks are realizing that the most liquid asset in the world—the dollar—is also the most politically vulnerable.

Consequently, the shift to gold is not about beating inflation; it is about sovereignty. Schasfoort argues that for nations like Russia, "gold that is kept locally now looks more safe than dollars and Euros." This is a profound admission that the global financial system has fractured. While gold solves the political risk, it introduces logistical nightmares. Storing physical bullion in Moscow or Beijing is expensive and difficult to liquidate quickly.

This is where Bitcoin enters the theoretical conversation. Schasfoort suggests, "Unlike gold it is digital... it is much easier and cheaper to send than gold for central banks who send it in large quantities." Yet, he remains skeptical of its immediate utility. He notes that World Bank researchers found central bankers still view Bitcoin as "way too volatile and not liquid enough." The volatility argument is strong; Schasfoort points out that Bitcoin experienced "hyperinflation" in 2022 terms, swinging wildly in value.

"Central bankers are not investors. They are crisis fighters. A volatile asset like Bitcoin has been unacceptable for them because its volatility may prevent them from defending their own currency when they need it most."

This is the core tension. Bitcoin's fixed supply, which makes it attractive to investors, makes it dangerous for a central bank that needs a stable unit of account to manage a currency crisis.

The Future of the Dollar and the Long Game

Despite the surge in gold buying, Schasfoort is cautious about declaring the end of the US dollar. He argues that reserve currency status is sticky, driven by network effects that take decades to unwind. "I don't expect a big move away from the dollar anytime soon," he writes, noting that the transition from the British pound to the dollar took over 60 years.

The author suggests that while the dollar will not collapse, its dominance will be challenged by a more fragmented system. "In this increasingly fragmenting global order I do expect that the gold buying trend will continue as more and more central bankers want to ensure themselves against geopolitical moves." This is a nuanced take: the dollar remains the king, but the court is being populated by new players who are hedging their bets.

Schasfoort also touches on the potential for Bitcoin to find a niche not as a primary reserve, but as a "digital alternative to gold that is easier to transfer across borders." This is a pragmatic middle ground. It acknowledges Bitcoin's technological advantages without overstating its current readiness for sovereign balance sheets.

"Given that fiat currencies are much more stable due to their flexible supply, I don't think it makes sense to expect central bankers to fully switch to gold or Bitcoin unless the world completely turns to anarchy."

This final caveat is vital. It grounds the speculation in reality. Unless the global order collapses entirely, the flexibility of fiat currency to respond to crises will keep it relevant.

Bottom Line

Schasfoort's strongest contribution is reframing the gold rush not as a return to the past, but as a strategic defense against the weaponization of the modern financial system. His biggest vulnerability, however, lies in underestimating the speed at which Bitcoin's liquidity and stability could mature, potentially accelerating the timeline for its adoption. Readers should watch not for the immediate demise of the dollar, but for the slow, steady construction of a parallel financial infrastructure where gold and digital assets serve as the ultimate insurance policy.
