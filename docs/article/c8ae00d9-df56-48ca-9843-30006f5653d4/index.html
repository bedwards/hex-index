Da Wei: China does not want America’s job

Zichen Wang · Pekingnology ·Mar 27, 2026 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In a geopolitical landscape often obsessed with who will replace whom, Zichen Wang delivers a strikingly counterintuitive thesis: China has no desire to inherit the American mantle. Drawing from a rare, high-level interview with Da Wei, a leading scholar at Tsinghua University, the piece dismantles the Western anxiety that Beijing is waiting for a vacuum to fill. This is not a standard diplomatic script; it is a candid admission that China's strategy is one of integration, not hegemony, offering a necessary reality check for a world bracing for a new Cold War.

The Myth of the Successor

The article's most provocative claim arrives immediately, challenging the very premise of much Western foreign policy analysis. "China neither seeks nor intends to fill any 'vacuum' left by the United States," Wang reports Da Wei stating clearly. This is a direct rebuttal to the narrative that the US retreat automatically creates a power void that Beijing is eager to occupy. Wang highlights that Da Wei views the current era as one where "leadership is key," emphasizing that the personal chemistry between heads of state—specifically the recent phone call between the US and Chinese presidents—matters more than bureaucratic inertia.

The author effectively uses Da Wei's assessment of Donald Trump to complicate the standard "hostile China" narrative. Wang writes, "Personally, I do not think Trump holds a particularly hostile view of China. His view of China is relatively neutral, and he is willing to engage in certain 'deal-making'." This observation is crucial because it suggests that even a transactional leader like Trump might be more amenable to stability than the ideological hardliners often assumed to be driving US policy. However, the commentary wisely tempers this optimism by noting Da Wei's caveat that personal will is constrained by the broader environment. As Wang paraphrases, "Once a precedent is set or a policy direction is established, it can easily outlast the term of any individual office-holder." This is a sobering reminder that the tariffs of the first Trump term survived the Biden administration, proving that policy continuity often trumps political personality.

China is not a hegemonic power, and it does not aspire to become a superpower in the traditional sense.

Beyond the 'World Minus One'

Moving beyond bilateral relations, Wang tackles the macro-structure of global order. The piece argues against the simplistic idea of a "world minus 1," where the rest of the planet unites to exclude the US. Instead, Da Wei describes a fragmented, multipolar reality where Europe, India, and the Global South are realigning in complex ways. Wang notes that this is not a monolithic bloc against Washington but a "process of differentiation and realignment."

This section is particularly strong because it grounds abstract theory in concrete economic reality. Wang points out that China's massive trade surplus makes a purely absolute version of free trade unsustainable, forcing Beijing to seek a "rebalancing" of interests. The author connects this to the historical context of the Group of 77, a coalition of developing nations where China has long sought to lead not by domination, but by consensus—a strategy that mirrors Da Wei's call for an "inclusive and broadly beneficial form of globalisation." Critics might note that this vision of multipolarity ignores the friction caused by China's own economic dominance in the Global South, which often looks suspiciously like a new form of dependency. Yet, Wang presents Da Wei's argument that the goal is "equal and orderly" multipolarity, suggesting that the alternative is a chaotic free-for-all.

The Limits of Global Governance

The final pillar of the commentary addresses the gap in global governance. As the US pulls back from international organizations, the temptation for China to step in is high. Da Wei, however, rejects this. "China's position is that existing mechanisms, including the United Nations, should be made to function more effectively and be reformed," Wang writes, quoting the scholar. This stance aligns with the principles of the "Responsibility to Protect" debates, where the focus is on strengthening existing norms rather than creating new, parallel institutions that could fracture the world further.

Wang emphasizes that Da Wei sees the Western fear of a "new hegemony" as a projection of traditional power politics that doesn't fit China's actual strategy. The interview suggests that China's strength lies in its economic and technological momentum, which forces the US to "accept the reality of coexistence." This is a pragmatic shift from ideological confrontation to a recognition of mutual strength. The author notes that while high-level visits from European leaders signal a desire for "hedging diplomacy," they are not a magic bullet. "The effectiveness of these visits depends on whether the two sides can manage their differences," Wang observes, citing the unresolved issues of Ukraine and structural economic contradictions as persistent hurdles.

Bottom Line

Zichen Wang's coverage of Da Wei's interview succeeds in stripping away the sensationalism of "who will rule the world" to reveal a more nuanced, albeit fragile, path of coexistence. The strongest part of the argument is the rejection of the "vacuum" theory, which forces readers to confront the possibility that the US and China may simply be learning to share a fractured world rather than fighting for total control. The biggest vulnerability remains the gap between this diplomatic rhetoric and the hard realities of economic competition and security dilemmas that could still trigger conflict. Readers should watch whether China's commitment to "reforming" the UN system holds up as its own economic leverage grows, or if the pressure to lead becomes too great to resist.

Excerpt from the original article on Pekingnology

As the U.S. pulls back from parts of the international order it once helped to build, questions are growing over whether China will seek to fill the space Washington leaves behind. Speaking to The Paper, an influential Shanghai-based media outlet, Da Wei, one of China’s best-known international relations scholars, rejects that premise.

In his view, Beijing neither seeks nor intends to replace the United States in filling any so-called “vacuum”, nor should it be expected to do so. China’s position, he argues, is to integrate into the multilateral system and help improve it, not to replace one hegemon with another.

Da Wei is Director of the Center for International Security and Strategy (CISS) and a Professor at the Department of International Relations, School of Social Science, Tsinghua University.

The interview was published on 12 March 2026 by The Paper and is also available on CISS’s official WeChat blog. Da Wei reviewed the translation before publication.
