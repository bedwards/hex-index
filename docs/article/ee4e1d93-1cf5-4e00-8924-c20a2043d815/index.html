You have 100 'energy slaves'

Andrew Dessler & Zeke Hausfather · The Climate Brink ·Nov 18, 2025 ·14 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Andrew Dessler & Zeke Hausfather strip away the moral panic surrounding energy costs to reveal a stark, physical reality: the modern world runs on a subsidy of labor so vast it is invisible to the naked eye. By reframing fossil fuels not as a commodity to be priced, but as a force that replaced centuries of human drudgery, the authors force a confrontation with the sheer scale of our dependency. This is not a plea for complacency, but a necessary calibration of perspective before we can realistically engineer a future that preserves our standard of living without burning the planet.

The Invisible Workforce

The piece opens with a deceptively simple concept: embedded energy. Dessler & Hausfather argue that we fail to see the cost of our goods because we only pay for the final transaction, not the history of labor required to create them. "Consider an iPhone. Its constituents were once sand and ore, transformed through energy-intensive manufacturing into glass, metal, silicon, and countless other advanced materials," they write. This framing is crucial because it shifts the conversation from abstract carbon budgets to tangible physical effort. When they break down the energy required for a single Big Mac—roughly 10 megajoules—they translate that into a human equivalent: "It would therefore take a human roughly 10^5 seconds... three 8-hour days of sustained labor to generate 10 MJ of energy."

This calculation is the article's intellectual anchor. It effectively demonstrates that our current convenience is a historical anomaly. The authors remind us that "Before fossil fuels, this level of energy access was reserved for kings, nobles, or slaveholders—those who could literally have hundreds of people working to produce their goods and services." By invoking the term "energy slaves," coined by Buckminster Fuller, Dessler & Hausfather make the invisible visible. They are not glorifying slavery; rather, they are using the term to quantify the massive displacement of human toil by inanimate power sources.

"Without this, what feels routine to us today (i.e., ordering a Big Mac) would demand heroic effort."

The argument holds up under scrutiny because it relies on physics, not politics. A human can generate about 100 watts of power; a gallon of gasoline contains the energy equivalent of 14 days of non-stop human labor. The authors note that "drivers might complain about paying $2.50/gal for gasoline... it is actually an unbelievable bargain." This is a provocative stance in an era of green activism, but it serves a specific purpose: to establish the baseline of what we are trying to replace. If we cannot replicate the energy density of fossil fuels, we cannot maintain the current pace of global development.

The Scale of Modern Abundance

Moving from the burger to the backyard, the authors illustrate the exponential difference between muscle and machine. They describe the excavation of a swimming pool, a task that would require a team of four workers over 20 days, versus an excavator operator finishing in one day with a few gallons of diesel. "The fuel cost is practically a rounding error," they observe, highlighting the economic distortion created by cheap energy. This section effectively counters the narrative that energy is "expensive" by contextualizing it against the alternative: a world where every mile traveled or every ton moved requires a human to sweat.

The authors then aggregate these individual acts into a lifestyle metric. By calculating the average power consumption of an American—eating a hamburger daily, taking an international flight, and living in a standard home—they arrive at a staggering figure: 10 kilowatts per person. "Your life now requires 24 energy slaves working for you... each American relies on the equivalent of around 100 energy slaves," Dessler & Hausfather state. This reframing is powerful because it personalizes the abstract concept of "energy consumption." It forces the reader to acknowledge that their comfort is a massive, continuous output of work performed by machines.

Critics might argue that focusing on the benefits of fossil fuels risks minimizing the urgency of the climate crisis. However, the authors anticipate this, noting that acknowledging the past is not an endorsement of the future. They write, "Acknowledging the pivotal role of fossil fuels in the past is not an argument for their indefinite future use." Instead, they use this historical context to set a high bar for the transition: any new system must not only be clean, but it must also be capable of sustaining the complexity of modern civilization.

The Political Bottleneck

The commentary shifts from physics to policy in its final act, addressing the "disposable economy" and the political barriers to change. Dessler & Hausfather argue that our current economic model, which favors replacement over repair, is an energy inefficiency that exacerbates the problem. "Making a new product always consumes far more energy than repairing one that already exists," they explain. This is a compelling argument for design reform, suggesting that the solution lies not just in swapping power sources, but in rethinking how we build and consume.

The authors then tackle the global equity issue, noting that while the average American uses 10 kilowatts, the global average is only 2 kilowatts, with many in Africa and South Asia relying on less than 1. "These people deserve access to more energy to spur development," they assert. This creates a tension: how do we raise living standards in the developing world without repeating the carbon-intensive path of the industrialized nations? The authors' answer is blunt: "The solution is not to limit access to energy, but to change how energy is produced and used."

"The clean-energy transition is not a scientific or technical problem, it's a political one."

This is the piece's most incisive conclusion. It dismisses the idea that we lack the technology to decarbonize, pointing instead to the "enormous political influence of fossil fuels" as the primary obstacle. By grounding the argument in the reality of embedded energy, the authors make the political stakes clear: the fight is not about giving up comfort, but about preserving the capacity for comfort without the catastrophic side effects. The reference to the "energy slaves" serves as a reminder of what is at risk; if we fail to transition, we risk losing the very infrastructure that allows billions to escape the drudgery of pre-industrial life.

Bottom Line

Dessler & Hausfather succeed in making the invisible visible, using the metaphor of "energy slaves" to ground the abstract debate on climate policy in the physical reality of human labor. The strongest part of the argument is its refusal to apologize for the benefits of fossil fuels, instead using that history to set a rigorous standard for the transition ahead. Its vulnerability lies in the sheer difficulty of the political task it identifies; acknowledging that the problem is political does not solve the gridlock that prevents the solution. The reader is left with a clear mandate: the technology exists to maintain our 10-kilowatt lifestyle without the carbon, but the will to execute it remains the missing variable.

What is embedded energy?.

Consider an iPhone. Its constituents were once sand and ore, transformed through energy-intensive manufacturing into glass, metal, silicon, and countless other advanced materials.

The total energy required to create an iPhone is what we call its “embedded energy.” This includes the energy consumed in extracting raw materials, manufacturing components, transporting goods, maintaining infrastructure, and eventually disposing of or recycling products at the end of their useful life. It includes the energy consumed in writing the software and running the cloud services that the phone relies on.

Embedded energy is everywhere. A steel beam embodies the energy used to mine iron ore, smelt it into steel, and transport it to a construction site. A hospital building represents not just the energy used in construction, but the ongoing energy required to heat, cool, light, and operate it.

Every good (road, building, vehicle) or service (a university lecture, an hour of tax preparation) requires energy without which that good or service would not exist.

Example: a Big Mac.

Consider the humble Big Mac. The bread requires energy to grow the wheat, mill it into flour, bake it, store it, and ship it across supply chains. The hamburger patty requires energy to grow the grain that feeds cattle, as well as the energy to slaughter, process, refrigerate, and cook it. Even small ingredients like pickles and onions contribute.

When everything is added up, a single Big Mac embodies around 10 megajoules (MJ) of energy. To provide context for this number, know that an adult doing sustained, hard physical work might produce about 100 joules per second of useful power.

How big is 100 J/s? A joule of energy is about the same energy it takes to lift an apple from the floor to a nearby table. Thus, 100 joules per second is the same as lifting 100 apples (about 20 lb) from the floor to a table every second. As I said, that’s hard physical labor.
