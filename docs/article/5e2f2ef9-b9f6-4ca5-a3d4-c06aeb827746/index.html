Trapped in the hell of social comparison

Noah Smith · Noahpinion ·Jan 16, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Noah Smith cuts through the noise of economic indicators to ask a question that data alone cannot answer: why do Americans feel poorer even as inflation cools and rates fall? His answer is not a policy failure or a political scandal, but a psychological trap wired into the very devices we hold in our hands. By reframing consumer pessimism as a symptom of algorithmic social comparison rather than material deprivation, Smith offers a disturbing explanation for why the "good life" feels further away than ever.

The Shift from Peers to Strangers

Smith begins by dismantling the standard economic narrative. He notes that despite a decent real economy, "American consumers [are] more pessimistic than they were during the depths of the Great Recession or the inflation of the late 1970s." He dismisses easy political explanations to focus on a more insidious driver: the distortion of reality by social media. The core of his argument rests on the evolution of the platform itself. "Social media today is very different than social media in the 2010s," Smith writes. "It's a lot more like television — young people nowadays spend very little time viewing content posted by their friends."

Instead of seeing the curated highlights of neighbors, users are fed an algorithmic stream of strangers, specifically influencers who project a life of leisure and wealth. Smith illustrates this with examples like Rebecca Ma, whose posts feature private jets and spectacular weddings, noting that "Becca Bloom comes from a super-rich Hong Kong family... She's top 0.01% for sure." The danger, Smith argues, is not that these lifestyles are visible, but that they are now the default reference point. "Now, thanks to social media, they do [have contact with the super-rich]," he observes, contrasting this with previous eras where the ultra-wealthy were geographically and socially removed from the average person.

"On a day-to-day basis, we are more aware of the Becca Blooms of the world than we were thirty years ago, or probably even ten years ago."

This framing is powerful because it connects a modern digital phenomenon to a timeless economic concept: relative deprivation. Smith draws on historical research to show that humans have always compared themselves to others, but the scope of that comparison has exploded. He cites Luttmer (2005), who found that "having richer neighbors makes you less happy," but points out that neighbors were once a manageable, explainable group. Today, the comparison group is global, unexplained, and unattainable.

The Mystery of Wealth and "Money Dysmorphia"

The most compelling part of Smith's analysis is the psychological mechanism he identifies: the loss of context. In the past, if you saw a neighbor with a nice car, you understood the source of their wealth. "In 1995, if you knew a rich guy who owned a car dealership, you knew how he made his money," Smith writes. "If you envied his big house and his nice car, you could tell yourself that he had those things because of hard work, natural ability, willingness to accept risk, and maybe luck."

Today, that narrative is gone. Influencers like Merritt Beck or Jacey Duprie display lifestyles that are "economically out of reach for the average American," yet their source of income is often opaque. "It's not immediately obvious what they do for work, or how they could afford all those nice things," Smith notes. This ambiguity creates a sense of helplessness. The wealth appears "delocalized, in front of you — something that other people have that you don't." This leads to what Smith calls "money dysmorphia," a condition where the perception of success is warped by these floating images.

The evidence for this distortion is stark. Smith points to a recent survey where Gen Z reported a threshold for financial success of "$588,000," a figure that is "thirteen times the median personal income in the United States." While critics might note that survey wording can be tricky and that the definition of "success" may have shifted linguistically, the sheer scale of the gap suggests a genuine psychological disconnect. "It's also possible that Gen Z folks really do think they're losers if they don't make $588k," Smith argues, linking this directly to the influencers they consume daily.

"Not only can you not explain the wealth you're seeing on social media, but you probably don't even think about explaining it. It's just floating there, delocalized, in front of you."

Smith's argument here is that the algorithm has severed the link between effort and outcome in the public imagination. When the reference point is a lifestyle that cannot be explained by hard work or risk, it creates a permanent sense of inadequacy. This is distinct from the "Keeping up with the Joneses" of the past, where the Joneses were at least within the same socioeconomic stratum. Now, the Joneses are a global elite, and the middle class is left feeling like it is failing by a metric that was never designed for them.

The Limits of Economic Solutions

Smith concludes by addressing the inevitable question: what is the solution? He is refreshingly pessimistic about traditional economic fixes. "It's physically impossible to give every American an income anywhere near $588k, at least in the near future," he writes. He dismisses redistribution or growth as insufficient to close the gap created by social media comparison. "Neither redistribution nor growth nor any combination of the two will give regular folks the kind of lifestyle they see on TikTok and Instagram."

Instead, he suggests cultural and structural shifts: providing more public goods like parks and transit, or cultivating a culture where wealth display is less common, similar to Japan. "We can continue prodding young people to watch less TikTok and Instagram," he adds, though he admits this is a difficult sell. The piece ends on a note of uncertainty, acknowledging that while social comparison may not be the only reason for low satisfaction, it is a hypothesis that demands attention. "Hopefully over time people will learn that influencer lifestyles aren't a good barometer of reality," Smith writes, "that fancy Europe trips and cavernous mansions are as rare now as having a giant apartment in Manhattan was in the 1990s."

Bottom Line

Smith's most significant contribution is identifying the algorithm as a driver of economic despair, shifting the blame from policy failures to a distorted perception of reality. However, the argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that users are passively absorbing these images without critical filters; it underestimates the growing awareness of influencer curation among younger generations. The reader should watch for how this "money dysmorphia" evolves as the next generation of influencers begins to monetize authenticity and struggle, potentially resetting the comparison baseline.

"Social comparisons can put a lid on how happy we can feasibly make people."

The strongest part of this piece is its refusal to accept the standard economic explanation for consumer pessimism, forcing a confrontation with the psychological cost of the attention economy. The biggest risk is that it treats the symptom—the feeling of inadequacy—without fully addressing the disease: an algorithmic system designed to maximize engagement by exploiting human insecurity. Until that system changes, the gap between reality and the feed will likely continue to widen.

Trapped in the hell of social comparison

by Noah Smith · Noahpinion · Read full article

Interest rates have begun to come down. Inflation has mostly subsided, and the real economy is still doing decently well despite Trump’s tariffs. So why are American consumers more pessimistic than they were during the depths of the Great Recession or the inflation of the late 1970s?

It’s possible to spin all sorts of ad hoc hypotheses about why consumer sentiment has diverged from its traditional determinants. Perhaps Americans are upset about social issues and politics, and expressing this as dissatisfaction about the economy. Perhaps they’re mad that Trump seems to be trying to hurt the economy. Perhaps they’re scared that AI will take their jobs. And so on.

Here’s another hypothesis: Maybe Americans are down in the dumps because their perception of the “good life” is being warped by TikTok and Instagram.

I’ve been reading for many years about how social media would make Americans unhappier by prompting them to engage in more frequent social comparisons. In the 2010s, as happiness plummeted among young people, the standard story was that Facebook and Instagram were shoving our friends’ happiest moments in our faces — their smiling babies, their beautiful weddings, their exciting vacations — and instilling a sense of envy and inadequacy.

In fact, plenty of careful research found that using Facebook and Instagram made people at least temporarily unhappier, and there’s some evidence that social comparisons were the reason. Here’s Appel et al. (2016), reviewing the literature up to that point:

Cross-sectional evidence demonstrates a positive correlation between the amount of Facebook use and the frequency of social comparisons on Facebook…A similar pattern emerges for the impression of being inferior…Some of these studies…have documented an association between social comparison or envy and negative affective outcomes…

Causal relationships between Facebook use, social comparison, envy, and depression have also been established experimentally. For example, in a study about women's body image…women instructed to spend ten minutes looking at their Facebook page rated their mood lower than those looking at control websites. Furthermore, participants in the Facebook condition who had a strong tendency to compare their attractiveness to others were less satisfied with their physical appearance…

In summary, available evidence is largely consistent with the notion that Facebook use encourages unfavorable social comparisons and envy, which may in turn lead to depressed mood.
