Russia-NATO Confrontation - Drones over Poland & MiGs over the Baltic

Commentary by Brian Edwards

Perun cuts through the noise of daily headlines to argue that the war in Ukraine is no longer just a battle for territory, but a high-stakes political duel where "signaling and public opinion can carry the same kind of importance as economic output." This is not a standard recap of border incidents; it is a strategic autopsy of how Russia is testing the limits of NATO's patience without triggering a conventional war. For the busy professional trying to understand the next escalation, the distinction Perun draws between a "gray zone" probe and actual warfare is the critical lens needed to interpret the chaos.

The Gray Zone Gambit

Perun begins by dismantling the binary view of peace and war, describing a "murky space that exists between peace and war" where ambiguity becomes a weapon. He notes the contradictory rhetoric from Moscow, where officials like Sergey Lavrov simultaneously claim "there is no war" while asserting that "NATO and the European Union want to declare in fact have already declared a real war on my country." This cognitive dissonance is not a mistake; it is a feature of a strategy designed to "blur the lines between war and peace, an attempt to sew doubt in the minds of target populations."

The author's analysis suggests that Russia is exploiting its "escalation inferiority" against the alliance. Because the resource gap is so vast, Moscow cannot afford a direct fight. Instead, they are "probing with the bayonet," looking for soft spots in the alliance's political cohesion. Perun argues that the goal is often not to destroy an enemy's military capability, but to "destabilize and undermine societies" by increasing public nervousness. This framing is compelling because it shifts the focus from hardware to psychology.

"Hybrid methods are used to blur the lines between war and peace, an attempt to sew doubt in the minds of target populations. They aim to destabilize and undermine societies."

However, this strategy carries inherent risks. Critics might note that Perun's reliance on the "escalation inferiority" theory assumes Moscow acts with perfect rationality. If the Russian leadership miscalculates NATO's threshold for response, a "probe" could accidentally trigger the very kinetic conflict they seek to avoid.

The MiG-31 Incursion

The commentary then pivots to a specific, high-tension incident: three armed Russian MiG-31s entering Estonian airspace for twelve minutes. Perun highlights why this specific aircraft matters, noting that while a drone could be a navigation error, a manned MiG-31 leaves little room for doubt. "When you see a B1B Lancer bombing someone, no one's going to blame Argentina. Russia is currently the only country flying the big 31."

The author details how the Russian pilots ignored radio communications and flew with transponders off, forcing Finland, Sweden, and Italy to scramble interceptors. Perun points out that unlike drones, "with a manned aircraft, you do have a backup if the electronic systems go haywire. It's called the pilot navigating." This detail underscores the intentionality of the act. The incursion was not a glitch; it was a deliberate test of the "Air Defense Identification Zone" rules and the speed of NATO's response.

The analysis suggests that while these actions are not strictly illegal if confined to identification zones, they are designed to "divert resources from Ukraine to NATO's own defenses" and test the alliance's resolve. The fact that the Russian aircraft remained silent while being escorted by multiple NATO fighters demonstrates a calculated arrogance.

"When you see one, the game of Guess Who isn't particularly difficult."

Yet, the effectiveness of this tactic is debatable. While it certainly grabs headlines, it is unclear if it actually fractures NATO unity or if it merely reinforces the alliance's resolve to monitor the border more closely. Perun touches on this but stops short of declaring the strategy a failure, leaving the reader to wonder if the "big blue blob" is being poked to death or if the pokes are becoming strategically self-defeating.

Bottom Line

Perun's strongest contribution is the reframing of border incidents not as isolated accidents, but as deliberate components of a "gray zone" strategy aimed at eroding political will rather than winning military battles. The piece's vulnerability lies in its assumption that Moscow's caution is permanent; if the calculus changes, the "probe" could instantly become a war. Readers should watch whether NATO's response shifts from mere interception to more aggressive deterrents, which would signal that the gray zone is closing.
