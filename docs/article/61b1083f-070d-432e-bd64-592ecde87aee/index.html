We Played Connect 4 by Travelling to Actual US States

Sam Denby · Wendover Productions ·May 25, 2022 · 24 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sam Denby doesn't just play a board game; he turns the entire American West into a chaotic, high-stakes playground where geography meets absurdity. In this Wendover Productions special, the author argues that the true challenge of connecting four states isn't strategic planning, but rather surviving the logistical nightmares of early morning flights, rental car disasters, and the sheer unpredictability of random card draws. This isn't a travel guide; it's a masterclass in how to turn a simple concept into a narrative of human endurance and comedic failure.

The Illusion of Strategy

Denby opens by setting the stage with a premise that sounds deceptively simple: connect four states by visiting their capitals and completing random challenges. "We're playing Connect Four, but instead of connecting tiles, we're connecting actual American states," he explains, immediately grounding the absurdity in a concrete rule set. The teams, led by Denby and Brian from Real Engineering against Ben and Adam, must navigate the 22 states west of the Mississippi. The author's initial analysis suggests a clear path to victory: "Probably the most valuable combination is if you get California and Nevada, that's really good."

However, the narrative quickly pivots from strategic dominance to logistical reality. Denby admits that while California and Nevada are the ideal targets, "there aren't any good flights there" early in the day. This is where the piece shines; it acknowledges that in the real world, optimal strategy is often held hostage by airline schedules. The team decides to pivot to Denver, hoping to claim Wyoming by driving north, a move Denby describes as a way to "get an advantage" before the board gets "mixed up."

The core of the argument is that geography is not just about maps, but about the friction of movement.

Critics might note that the game's reliance on flight availability introduces a massive element of luck that undermines the strategic depth of Connect Four. Yet, Denby leans into this, arguing that the chaos is the point. The strategy isn't about winning the game; it's about surviving the journey to get there.

The Absurdity of the Challenge

Once the teams arrive, the random card draws transform the trip from a travelogue into a surreal scavenger hunt. In Wyoming, Ben and Adam draw a card that requires them to "catch three different local bugs." Denby captures the frustration of this task perfectly: "I'm not seeing a lot of bugs in... I'm not seeing a lot of bugs." The team's descent into desperation—searching under wood, spotting debris, and eventually forcing an ant onto a finger—highlights the ridiculous lengths they must go to for a digital victory.

Meanwhile, Denby's team faces a different kind of hurdle in Colorado. Their card demands they "get one team member intoxicated." "Ben's getting drunk," Denby notes, watching as his partner downs four shots of vodka in a dive bar. The scene is a stark reminder of the physical toll of their game. "I am now officially drunk in Colorado," Ben declares, a moment that underscores the absurdity of the task. The author doesn't shy away from the discomfort, noting that Ben "doesn't really drink a lot" and that this is "by far the most I've ever seen him drink."

The game forces them to confront the physical and mental limits of travel, turning a simple board game into a test of endurance.

This section of the piece is particularly effective because it juxtaposes the intellectual strategy of the game with the visceral reality of the challenges. The author's willingness to document the drunkenness and the bug-hunting struggle adds a layer of authenticity that a polished travel show would likely edit out.

The Unpredictable Outcome

As the game progresses, the initial strategy of claiming California and Nevada begins to crumble under the weight of reality. Denby realizes that his opponents' move to Wyoming might actually "screw him" by forcing him to wait for their next move. "I'm hoping that when they see that we're in California, they're going to panic," he admits, revealing the psychological warfare inherent in the game. The tension builds as Denby and Brian finally reach Sacramento, only to face another random challenge: "Eat spicy food."

The team's attempt to find the "spiciest thing on the menu" at an Indian restaurant ends in a mix of pain and humor. "Oh, that is hot," Denby exclaims after tasting the sauce, a moment that encapsulates the unpredictable nature of the game. The author's commentary on the situation is sharp: "We have to say verbatim I want the spiciest thing on the menu," highlighting the rigid rules that govern their chaotic journey.

The game is a metaphor for travel itself: a series of planned moves derailed by unexpected obstacles and random encounters.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's piece succeeds because it refuses to treat the game as a mere gimmick; instead, it uses the Connect Four framework to explore the messy, unpredictable reality of travel. The strongest part of the argument is the realization that strategy is secondary to adaptability, a lesson learned through bug hunting and drunken shots. The biggest vulnerability is the reliance on the randomness of the cards, which can sometimes overshadow the strategic elements, but this is precisely what makes the narrative so engaging. Readers should watch for how the author balances the humor of the situation with the genuine challenges of navigating a continent on a whim.
