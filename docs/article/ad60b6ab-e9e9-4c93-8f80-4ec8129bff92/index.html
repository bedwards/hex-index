Richard danzig on AI and cyber

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Jan 16, 2026 ·54 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

This conversation cuts through the noise of futuristic speculation to deliver a stark warning: the national security establishment is asleep at the wheel while a continuous revolution in cyber warfare unfolds right now. Richard Danzig, a former Secretary of the Navy and RAND board member, argues that waiting for a mythical "messianic" artificial general intelligence is a strategic error that leaves the United States dangerously exposed to present-day "spiky" breakthroughs. Jordan Schneider, the author of this ChinaTalk piece, frames the discussion not as a debate about distant sci-fi scenarios, but as an urgent call to action for the Department of Defense to "get out of bed" and master the tools available today.

The Fierce Urgency of Now

Schneider opens by highlighting Danzig's central thesis: the shift brought by AI is not a singular event waiting in the future, but a rapid, ongoing transformation comparable to the jump from feudalism to capitalism. Danzig draws a powerful historical parallel to the 16th century, noting that while the transition to capitalism took two centuries, AI's impact will likely compress into less than a decade. "If people sitting in 1500 tried to anticipate the consequences of the jump from feudalism to capitalism, they'd have an extraordinarily difficult job guessing what the next two centuries might look like," Danzig explains. "In the same way, I don't think we can predict the consequences of AI with much confidence."

This framing is effective because it rejects the paralysis of uncertainty. Instead of trying to predict the next two centuries, Danzig urges leaders to focus on the immediate "first-mover advantage" in cybersecurity. He argues that the ability to both attack and defend software is being automated right now, creating a window where early action determines long-term security. The urgency is palpable: "My proposition is, in some respects, let's just take a small corner of that to understand it... it's a representative case. It's suggestive and important."

Critics might argue that focusing too narrowly on current cyber capabilities risks missing the paradigm shift that true AGI could bring, potentially leading to a strategy optimized for yesterday's battlefield. However, Danzig's rebuttal is compelling: deferring action for a future that may never arrive is a luxury the nation cannot afford.

"The Department of Defense doesn't need a wake-up call about AI — they're well aware of it. What they need to do is to get out of bed."

The Trap of the "Messianic" Future

One of the most striking elements of Schneider's coverage is how he exposes the two opposing forms of bureaucratic inertia: the belief that nothing will change, and the belief that a future AGI will solve everything. Danzig identifies the latter as a dangerous rationalization for inaction. He describes the prevailing mindset as waiting for a "messianic" AGI that will sweep away all current problems, a concept he firmly rejects. "The coming of AGI or superintelligence will be uneven," Danzig states. "It's not like the coming of the Messiah, where it sweeps away everything in front of it. It's part of a larger ecosystem."

Schneider skillfully weaves in a historical analogy to illustrate the peril of this thinking. He references the post-Cold War assumption that nuclear superiority would negate the need for robust conventional forces, a miscalculation that left the U.S. ill-prepared for asymmetric conflicts. Danzig reinforces this by recalling his own experience in the 1990s, where he pushed for anthrax vaccinations despite resistance from a Joint Staff that wanted to wait for a perfect, all-encompassing vaccine. "I had in hand Josh Lederberg, a great figure, Nobel Prize winner... to say that's a fantasy," Danzig recalls. "But the tendency to wait for the fantasies is very strong."

This historical grounding adds significant weight to the argument. Just as the military failed to prepare for the messy reality of biological warfare by waiting for a perfect solution, it now risks failing to secure its digital infrastructure by waiting for a perfect AI. The parallel to the rigid, high-risk training of Imperial Japanese pilots in the 1930s—where exquisite quality was prioritized over mass producibility—further underscores the danger of optimizing for a specific, idealized future rather than the chaotic reality of the present.

The Schizophrenia of Bureaucracy

The conversation takes a sharp turn when co-host Teddy Collins introduces the concept of "limited incrementalist thinking" as a third failure mode. Collins notes that many officials acknowledge AI's importance but only imagine it speeding up existing processes by 30% over 18 months, missing the systemic transformation entirely. Danzig agrees, identifying this as part of the same problem: a lack of deep expertise and a disconnect from the frontier companies driving innovation. "Way too little real expertise on AI at senior levels," Danzig asserts. "A second thing would be general knowledge and awareness... A third problem is the distance from the companies."

Schneider's choice to include this critique of "incrementalism" is crucial. It suggests that even well-intentioned efforts to adapt are often too timid, treating AI as a tool for efficiency rather than a force that will fundamentally alter the nature of conflict. The Department of Defense's rigid, siloed structure is chronically underweighting domains like cyber and AI, creating a gap between the speed of technological change and the speed of institutional adaptation.

"You can't take a vacation from it. You can't say, 'Well, it's summertime — I'll wait till after Labor Day to come to grips with this.' You don't ever win."

Bottom Line

Schneider's interview with Danzig succeeds in stripping away the hype to reveal a sobering reality: the U.S. national security apparatus is trapped between the paralysis of waiting for a perfect future and the shortsightedness of ignoring the present. The strongest part of the argument is the historical grounding, which effectively demonstrates that waiting for a "perfect" solution is a recurring failure mode with catastrophic consequences. The biggest vulnerability, however, lies in the difficulty of translating this urgency into the slow-moving machinery of the Department of Defense. The reader should watch for whether the administration can move beyond incrementalist memorandums and actually restructure its approach to match the "fierce urgency of now."
