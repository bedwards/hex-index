OpenClaw Emperors

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Mar 17, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider doesn't just predict the future of artificial intelligence; he diagnoses a cultural fever that has already broken out in Shenzhen. By juxtaposing the "Qigong Fever" of the 1990s with the 2026 "OpenClaw" craze, Schneider argues that we are not witnessing a technological revolution, but a desperate, ritualistic search for agency in a collapsing economy. This is a vital read because it strips away the hype of "Web 4.0" to reveal the raw, bureaucratic anxiety driving China's most sophisticated developers.

The Architecture of Desperation

Schneider opens with a striking visual comparison that anchors his entire thesis. He describes two photographs separated by thirty years: one showing laid-off workers in the 1990s balancing aluminum cooking pots on their heads to channel cosmic energy, and another from March 2026 showing crowds wearing plush red "lobster claw" headbands to master AI agents. "Three decades ago, people wore 'antennas' to grasp at the mythical salvation of supernatural powers during a period of massive economic restructuring," Schneider writes. "Today, they wear red claws, queuing up to embrace the cyber-deity known as the AI Agent."

This parallel is not merely poetic; it is a structural critique of how humans interact with complex systems they do not understand. Schneider suggests that the "OpenClaw" phenomenon is less about software utility and more about psychological comfort. He notes that just as the Qigong movement offered a sense of control during the chaotic "Great Leap" of economic reform, the current rush for AI agents offers a "ticket to Web 4.0" for those terrified of being left behind. The author points out that this desperation is so palpable that black-market scalpers were charging 1,000 RMB just to install instances for tech workers, while giants like Tencent staged "charity installations" to grant "Birth Certificates" for digital lobsters.

"The future of AI is not about increasing the IQ of one brain; it is about organizing multiple average brains into an infallible corporate structure."

This observation shifts the conversation from the capabilities of the models to the architecture of their deployment. Schneider argues that relying on a single large language model is like "trying to build a city by stacking a single skyscraper infinitely high." Instead, the solution lies in "Multi-Agent orchestration," where the future belongs not to the smartest algorithm, but to the best-organized bureaucracy.

The Tang Dynasty in Silicon Valley

The most distinctive part of Schneider's coverage is his analysis of the "Edict" open-source project. While Western developers are experimenting with "flat hierarchies" where agents chat in group settings, Chinese developers have looked backward to the "Three Departments and Six Ministries" system of the Sui and Tang dynasties. Schneider explains that this ancient political architecture was designed to prevent the very chaos that plagues modern AI: "When AI agents are left to 'chat' freely, they exhibit the worst traits of a poorly managed startup: they engage in endless polite greetings, lose sight of the objective, and enter infinite loops of mutual agreement without producing deliverables."

He details how the "Edict" framework forces users to adopt the role of a "yellow-robed" Emperor presiding over a strict civil service. The system includes a "Secretariat" to plan, a "Chancellery" with the power to veto flawed plans, and "Six Ministries" to execute specific tasks like coding, security, and deployment. Schneider writes, "The Chancellery held the terrifying power of Fengbo, the right to veto and return flawed imperial edicts. In the OpenClaw Edict system, the Chancellery is the dedicated QA and anti-hallucination auditor."

This historical analogy is powerful because it reframes software engineering as political science. Just as the Tang Dynasty used institutional design to manage the "entropy of power," the "Edict" system uses adversarial auditing to manage the "entropy of generative AI." However, a counterargument worth considering is whether this rigid, top-down structure stifles the creative flexibility that often drives innovation in AI. By forcing a "one-way information flow," the system might solve reliability issues but at the cost of the serendipitous breakthroughs that occur in more chaotic, collaborative environments.

"You trade speed and cheapness for guaranteed, hallucination-free reliability."

Schneider acknowledges the heavy cost of this approach. Running a digital empire that mimics the Tang bureaucracy requires massive computational overhead, burning "API tokens like incense in a temple." The user must constantly pay for the friction of the system, revising and vetting every "edict" before it is executed. This mirrors the historical reality of Chinese bureaucracy, where the "most diligent emperors in Chinese history often died early from overwork, spending their lives fighting their own bureaucratic systems."

The Carnival of Shovel Selling

The article takes a darker turn as Schneider examines the commercialization of this anxiety. He highlights the viral sensation of "Huo Qubing," an AI-generated micro-short drama that was marketed as a miracle of automation but was, in reality, a labor-intensive project involving twenty professionals. "The reality, quietly admitted by the director days later, was a classic hallucination," Schneider notes. "There were no 80 episodes, just a few short promotional clips."

Despite the deception, the market response has been explosive. Schneider points out that the real money is being made not by the users of the technology, but by the "shovel sellers" monetizing the fear of obsolescence. He describes a bizarre marketplace where "AI Gurus" sell "OpenClaw Quick Installation" services for 299 RMB to terrified product managers, and even "OpenClaw Uninstallation and System Repair" for 199 RMB to those worried about their digital lobsters leaking passwords.

"From the 'Three Departments and Six Ministries' framework to tutorials on how to use AI agents to simulate a development team, 'AI Gurus' are ruthlessly monetizing this mass anxiety on social media platforms like Xiaohongshu and WeChat."

This section serves as a grim reminder of the "Carnival of Shovel Selling." The author draws a parallel to the "Intelligent agent" deep dives, suggesting that the technology is being sold as a cure for overwork and unemployment, even as it creates new forms of precarious labor. The "Web 4.0 citizen" is told they no longer need to learn Python, but they must learn the "pragmatic art of rulership" to manage their digital court.

"When AI starts mirroring the carbon-based political science of classical Chinese antiquity, the barrier to accessing raw intelligence has dropped to near zero; the defining skill of the future is no longer coding, but architecture, governance, and institutional design."

Schneider's warning is clear: as we build these digital empires, we risk replicating the worst flaws of historical autocracies. He asks, "What happens when the 'cyber-emperor' gets tired of the Chancellery agent rejecting their bad ideas?" Users may eventually dismantle the checks and balances to prioritize speed, creating a "monolithic 'Grand Council' model that simply tells them what they want to hear." This echoes the historical trajectory of the Ming and Qing dynasties, where emperors like Zhu Yuanzhang abolished the Prime Minister role to centralize power, turning strategic partners into sycophants.

Bottom Line

Schneider's most compelling argument is that the future of AI is not a technical problem but a governance one, requiring us to apply ancient political wisdom to modern code. His coverage is strongest when it exposes the human desperation driving the "OpenClaw" fever, revealing that the "lobster claw" is just a modern "aluminum pot." However, the piece slightly underestimates the potential for these bureaucratic systems to become so complex that they become opaque even to their "Emperor" users, potentially leading to the very "cyber-coups" Schneider warns against. The reader should watch for how quickly the "Edict" framework evolves from a niche developer tool into a standard for corporate AI, and whether the "shovel sellers" can sustain the hype when the computational costs become too high to ignore.
