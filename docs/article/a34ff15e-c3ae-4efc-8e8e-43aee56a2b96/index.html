Nature of Genius 114 Definition for Today

Yale University · Yale Courses ·Nov 2, 2022 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Yale University doesn't just define genius; it dismantles the romantic myth that it is merely a high IQ or a solitary flash of brilliance. In this lecture, the author argues that true genius is not a static trait but a dynamic force measured by its ability to force society to change, regardless of whether that change is for good or for ill.

The Elusive Target

The lecture begins by acknowledging the historical difficulty in pinning down the term. Yale University notes that definitions have shifted wildly, from John Milton's view of genius as a "special endowment" to John Addison's 1711 distinction of it as "native intellectual power of an exalted type." This historical sweep is effective because it sets the stage for a more rigorous, modern criteria. The author then introduces a powerful metaphor from Arthur Schopenhauer to distinguish talent from genius: "Person of talent it's a Target that no one else can hit; person of Genius it's a Target that no one else can see."

This distinction is the intellectual anchor of the piece. It reframes genius not as superior execution, but as superior vision. The author illustrates this with figures like Nikola Tesla, who foresaw the smartphone a century before Steve Jobs, proving that the "target" must be invisible to the rest of the world until the genius reveals it. Critics might argue that this definition is too reliant on hindsight; many visionaries were ignored in their time, suggesting that "seeing the target" is not enough without the societal machinery to act on it. However, the author's focus remains on the cognitive leap itself.

Impact Over Novelty

Moving beyond vision, the lecture insists that genius requires a tangible societal shift. Yale University proposes a working definition: "A genius is a person of extraordinary mental powers whose original works or insights change society in some significant way for good or for ill across cultures and across time." The author is careful to parse this, noting that originality alone is insufficient. One could invent a new, inefficient way to walk from Grand Central to Times Square, but without impact, it is merely novelty, not creativity.

The argument here is that creativity is a transaction requiring two parties: the creator and the receiver who adopts the change. "It takes two to effect an act of creativity," the author states, challenging the romantic notion of the genius as a solitary figure on a desert island. If Albert Einstein had developed relativity but kept it to himself, the author suggests he would be a genius "in potentia" but not in actuality. This is a crucial, if slightly rigid, distinction that forces the reader to consider the social nature of intellectual achievement.

Genius is imminent within the individual versus genius being impactful on the outside world.

The Ethics of Genius

Perhaps the most provocative section of the lecture tackles the moral dimension of genius. The author asks, "Does the Evil Genius exist?" and challenges the audience to consider if society should "cancel" creators who produce transformative works while behaving inhumanely. The comparison between penicillin, which saved 200 million lives, and Kanye West's Yeezy sneakers, which offer fleeting pleasure, highlights the metric of "significance." The author notes that penicillin's impact is global and enduring, whereas fashion is transient.

This leads to a philosophical dilemma about duration versus scale: "Who is the greater genius: someone who influences a billion people during one year or someone who influences a million people for a thousand years?" The author admits there is no easy answer, but the framework forces a re-evaluation of how we value cultural contributions. It suggests that the longevity of an idea is just as critical as its immediate reach.

A Formula for the Unquantifiable

Acknowledging that their own definition is too wordy to remember, the author offers a simplified formula inspired by Einstein's E=mc². Yale University proposes: "G equals S times N times T," where genius equals significance times the number of people impacted times the duration of impact. While the author admits that ranking geniuses like Rembrandt against Picasso is "pointless" and "silly," the formula serves as a heuristic tool to measure impact rather than to create a leaderboard.

The lecture concludes by empowering the audience to reject the provided definition if it doesn't resonate, stating, "Having your own values and sensibilities is important just as is having your own definition of genius." This is a rare moment of intellectual humility in a lecture series, turning the definition from a rule into a starting point for debate.

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this lecture is its insistence that genius is a verb, not a noun—a force defined by its capacity to alter the human experience across time. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its dismissal of the "uncommunicated genius," potentially undervaluing the internal cognitive breakthroughs that precede societal adoption. Readers should watch for how this framework applies to modern figures whose impact is digital and instantaneous rather than physical and enduring.
