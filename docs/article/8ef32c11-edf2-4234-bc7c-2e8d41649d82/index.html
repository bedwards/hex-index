The path to Caracas was paved in gaza

Jonathan Cook · ·Jan 9, 2026 ·15 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Jonathan Cook delivers a chilling diagnosis of a global shift: the legal and moral frameworks that once constrained state power are being systematically dismantled to protect a specific geopolitical agenda. He argues that the recent abduction of Venezuela's president and the escalating repression of dissent in the UK are not isolated incidents, but the inevitable result of a two-decade strategy forged in the fires of Gaza. This is not merely political commentary; it is a forensic mapping of how the "good cop, bad cop" theater of Western diplomacy has collapsed into open authoritarianism.

The Collapse of the "Honest Broker" Myth

Cook begins by dismantling the long-held illusion that the United States and Israel operate as opposing forces in the Middle East. He writes, "The central conceit of the good cop, bad cop narrative is that both the US and Israel are the ones upholding the law and fighting the criminals. Unlike the Hollywood version, neither of these real-world cops is in any way good." This framing is crucial because it strips away the comforting narrative that Western institutions are merely flawed but ultimately lawful. Cook contends that the public is not the intended audience for this performance; rather, it is a spectacle designed to maintain order among the populace while the state acts with impunity.

The author points to the recent confirmation of Nicolás Maduro's capture and the administration's admission that the operation was an oil grab as the moment the mask finally slipped. "The path to Caracas—and potentially next to Mexico, Colombia, Cuba, Greenland and Canada, other targets of Donald Trump's greed—was paved in Gaza," Cook asserts. This connection is the article's most provocative claim: that the normalization of extrajudicial violence in the Middle East has created a template for resource extraction and regime change elsewhere. Critics might argue that linking these disparate geopolitical events oversimplifies complex regional dynamics, but Cook's evidence suggests a deliberate, escalating pattern of impunity.

The Weaponization of Law in the West

The commentary shifts to the domestic consequences of this foreign policy, focusing on the United Kingdom as a case study for the erosion of civil liberties. Cook details how opposition to the war in Gaza has been reclassified from political dissent to terrorism. He notes, "First, opposition to Israel's apartheid rule over Palestinians was conflate with antisemitism. Now opposition to Israel's Gaza genocide is conflated with terrorism." This linguistic shift is not semantic; it is a legal tool used to justify the banning of organizations like Palestine Action and the arrest of activists.

Cook highlights the specific targeting of the judicial system to ensure convictions. He describes the removal of a skeptical judge from a high-profile case and the government's plan to scrap jury trials, arguing that "juries have a tendency to take a far more critical view of the British establishment's behaviour than the establishment does itself." This move to bypass juries echoes historical precedents where the state feared the unpredictability of the public. It recalls the dynamics of Operation Condor, where the removal of judicial independence was a prerequisite for state terror across Latin America. By removing the jury, the state removes the only check on its own narrative.

The human cost of this legal maneuvering is stark. Cook writes, "The cause of the disbelief and anger that spurred millions to take to the streets, and to campuses, remains unaddressed. Western powers are still colluding deeply in Israel's crimes." He illustrates the severity of the crackdown by noting that Greta Thunberg was arrested for holding a sign, and that the UK government has designated peaceful protests as "hate marches." This escalation creates a feedback loop where the state's inability to win the argument is met with force, further alienating the public.

The International Stranglehold

The scope of Cook's analysis expands to the international level, where the US administration is using financial sanctions to silence legal experts and judges. He focuses on Francesca Albanese, the UN special rapporteur, who was sanctioned by the US Treasury. "Her listing came a few days after she published her report on the complicity of western corporations in Israel's genocide," Cook explains. The result is that she has been effectively turned into a "non-person," unable to travel, bank, or work.

This tactic extends to the International Criminal Court (ICC). Cook notes that the administration has sanctioned eight judges and the chief prosecutor, Karim Khan, for pursuing accountability for crimes against humanity. He quotes French judge Nicolas Guillou, who described the impact of sanctions: "Being under sanctions is like being sent back to the 1990s." This is a direct assault on the rule of law, where the threat of economic strangulation is used to intimidate jurists into silence. Cook argues, "Like gangsters, Israel and the US are sending a very direct message to each of the ICJ judges: you will be punished too, if you dare to find us guilty."

The administration's response to the ICC's investigation into the aid blockade in Gaza—which the court found plausible as a crime against humanity—was to declare a "national emergency" and threaten the court's existence. Cook observes that this "all-out assault on one of the main pillars of international law has been barely mentioned" in European capitals, suggesting a complicit silence from Western allies.

Bottom Line

Jonathan Cook's most powerful contribution is his ability to connect the dots between the humanitarian catastrophe in Gaza and the rising authoritarianism in Western democracies, revealing a unified strategy of impunity. The argument's greatest strength is its refusal to treat these events as isolated political failures, instead presenting them as a coherent, escalating system of control. However, the piece's reliance on a deterministic view of state power risks underestimating the potential for grassroots resistance to disrupt this machinery, a factor that remains the only viable counterweight to the administration's momentum.
