AI Literacy - Lecture 8.2: Interpretations and Misinterpretations of Artificial General Intelligence

Kenny Easwaran · Kenny Easwaran ·Sep 16, 2024 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Kenny Easwaran does something rare in the crowded field of AI commentary: he treats our current panic not as a sudden crisis, but as the latest chapter in a four-thousand-year-old story of human overreaction. By tracing the lineage from Bronze Age brass statues to the "Sparks of Artificial General Intelligence" paper, Easwaran suggests that our fear of machines is often just a mirror reflecting our own desire to be fooled.

The Ancient Hype Cycle

Easwaran begins by dismantling the idea that modern technology has invented the concept of the thinking machine. He notes that "ever since the Bronze Age there are stories of people having built not just statues but statues that move themselves." This historical grounding is crucial; it reframes our current anxiety as a recurring cultural reflex rather than a unique technological threshold. The author points to ancient figures like Daedalus, who supposedly built a giant of brass, and the automata of Yan Shi, which "walked and sang and flirted with the ladies of the court."

These stories, Easwaran argues, were likely "overenthusiastic ancient hype about artificial intelligence comparable to many things that we've experienced in recent decades." This is a sharp, necessary perspective. We often assume that because the technology is new, the social reaction must be unprecedented. Easwaran reminds us that the gap between what a machine can do and what humans think it can do has always been wide. While we have no physical evidence of these ancient wonders, the discovery of the Antikythera mechanism suggests that the ancients were indeed capable of complex mechanical engineering, even if they lacked the software for true general intelligence.

Unfortunately none of the ancient automata that were built have survived so we don't know what their actual capacities were.

The author's handling of the medieval period is equally insightful. He contrasts the magical Golem of Jewish folklore with the "Brazen Head" attributed to Roger Bacon. Easwaran observes that "neither of these stories depict the being as a true general intelligence." The Golem could defend a community but not speak; the Brazen Head could answer questions but only about the past or future. These were not visions of a rival species, but rather limited tools with specific, narrow functions. This distinction is vital for today's readers who often conflate narrow AI (like a chatbot) with the sci-fi trope of a sentient overlord.

From Fiction to the Turing Test

As the narrative moves into the 19th century, Easwaran shifts from myth to literature, highlighting how fiction began to grapple with the emotional consequences of artificial life. He discusses Mary Shelley's Frankenstein, noting that the story was inspired by conversations about electricity and "experiments conducted by the doctor Erasmus Darwin in England using electricity that seemed to give life to a noodle."

Shelley and her contemporaries were not predicting the future; they were processing the anxiety of their present. Easwaran writes, "they are trying to come to terms with the idea that progress in modern machinery and science might someday either produce something emotionless that a human might be fooled by and fall in love with or might produce something truly alive that human might misinterpret and mistreat." This dual failure mode—either the machine is too human for us to handle, or we project humanity onto a machine that isn't there—remains the central tension in AI ethics today.

The introduction of the word "robot" by Karel Čapek in 1920 is another key pivot. Easwaran explains that Čapek was worried about political extremism and wrote a play where "oppressed workers undertaking a violent revolution but then destroying the ability to keep Society going." The robots in the play were not just tools; they were a mirror for human labor and class struggle. Easwaran notes that "Chapek is one of the first who means for us to identify just as much with the robot as with the human oppressor or victim." This humanization of the machine, even in a cautionary tale, set the stage for how we relate to technology now.

The Modern Illusion of Intelligence

The commentary then turns to the mid-20th century, where the abstract becomes concrete. Easwaran discusses Joseph Weizenbaum's ELIZA, a program designed to satirize psychotherapy. The program was simple, yet Easwaran points out the profound result: "many people including his own secretary started to Value the conversations that they thought they were having with this therapist."

This is perhaps the most critical point in the lecture. Weizenbaum intended to show how easy it was to fake understanding, but instead, he demonstrated how desperate people are to be understood. Easwaran concludes that this revealed a "failure mode where humans project an inner life onto a device that doesn't really have it." This dynamic explains why we still feel a strange connection to our voice assistants, even when we know they are just scripts.

This suggested that in addition to the failure mode suggested by Frankenstein and R.U.R. where artificial intelligence might have an inner life that isn't properly appreciated by humans there's also a failure mode where humans project an inner life onto a device that doesn't really have it.

Easwaran then critiques the modern corporate approach, specifically Google's strategy. He describes how Google expanded from search to email, maps, and video, creating a vast data ecosystem. Yet, despite this scale, he argues it "never really felt to anyone like a general intelligence." It felt like a tool. The shift happened with the rise of voice assistants and, more recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) based on the Transformer architecture.

The author highlights the moment in late 2022 when OpenAI released a chatbot interface, noting that "suddenly the public started paying attention." This was a user-interface breakthrough, not necessarily a fundamental leap in capability, yet it triggered a massive shift in perception. The subsequent release of GPT-4 led researchers to publish a paper titled "Sparks of artificial general intelligence," claiming that the models were showing signs of true reasoning. Easwaran presents this as the culmination of centuries of speculation, where the line between simulation and reality has become dangerously thin.

Critics might argue that Easwaran's historical determinism downplays the genuine novelty of current neural networks. While the reaction may be old, the capability of modern AI to generate coherent, novel text at scale is a qualitative shift that ancient automata could never achieve. However, his point stands that our interpretation of that capability is still filtered through ancient fears and hopes.

In a sense he show what he ended up showing was that even though the therapy was simplistic enough to be implemented by a computer program of that era it was still tapping into something important and emotional for people.

Bottom Line

Kenny Easwaran's strongest contribution is his insistence that the "AGI" debate is as much about human psychology as it is about computer science. His argument holds up well against the backdrop of history, proving that our tendency to anthropomorphize machines is a deep-seated human trait, not a glitch in the system. The biggest vulnerability in his coverage is the rapid pace of current development; the "Sparks" paper he cites at the end suggests the ground is shifting faster than historical analogies can fully capture. Readers should watch not just for what the machines can do, but for how quickly we are willing to surrender our judgment of what is "real" to the comfort of a conversation.
