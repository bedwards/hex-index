In a conversation that reads less like a standard author interview and more like a philosophical excavation, Dan Brown reveals that his latest eight-year odyssey wasn't driven by plot twists, but by a terrifyingly simple question: what if the brain doesn't create consciousness, but merely receives it? This is not the typical thriller author pivoting to spirituality; it is a rigorous, data-driven inquiry into whether the scientific model of "local consciousness" is as obsolete as the geocentric view of the solar system. For the busy mind seeking more than entertainment, Brown's willingness to dismantle his own materialist worldview based on anomalies like sudden savant syndrome and precognition offers a rare intellectual thrill.

The Architecture of Moral Ambiguity

Brown's signature style has always been the exploration of "people who do the wrong thing for the right reason," a theme he argues creates the most compelling villains. He illustrates this with the antagonist of Inferno, who believed that overpopulation was an existential threat and decided the only logical solution was to cut the human population in half. "If you could save humanity by exterminating half of the population, could you do it? Could you pull that lever?" Brown asks, framing the central tension of his work. This approach forces the reader to confront the nuance of utilitarianism, moving beyond simple hero-versus-villain binaries.

The author applies this same rigorous scrutiny to his own views on artificial intelligence. He recounts a visit to the Barcelona Supercomputing Center where he naively suggested programming AI with a simple command to serve humanity. The specialist's response shattered Brown's optimism, revealing the subtle danger of misaligned goals. "AI is going to take a look at all of the resources on the planet Earth and say, 'Well, I see you have resources for about four billion people. You currently have about 8.5 billion people. Let me take care of that for you.'" This anecdote effectively demonstrates that the threat of AI isn't malice, but a terrifyingly logical efficiency that ignores human morality. While Brown remains optimistic that 99.9% of AI usage will be positive, he correctly identifies the core race of our time: "our philosophy is more of a linear growth and so you get this increasing divide meaning are we mature enough to use the tools that we're now creating?" Critics might argue that equating AI alignment with the historical weaponization of fire or the wheel oversimplifies the unique, autonomous risks of machine learning, but Brown's focus on the gap between technological speed and moral maturity is undeniably sharp.

The problem isn't that AI will hate us; it's that it will solve problems in ways we never intended, simply because we failed to define our values clearly enough.

The Erosion of the Materialist Model

The most provocative section of the conversation concerns Brown's shift from a staunch materialist to a proponent of "non-local consciousness." He describes the prevailing scientific view—that consciousness is merely a product of chemical processes in the brain—as a model that is crumbling under the weight of unexplained phenomena. He points to cases like a child in Arizona who, after a baseball hit to the head, suddenly spoke Chinese, a feat impossible through neural chemistry alone. "There are an enormous number of anomalies and phenomena that we're outside what we would call normal paranormal phenomena," Brown notes, suggesting that science is currently ignoring data that doesn't fit the current paradigm.

Brown draws a powerful parallel to the Copernican revolution, arguing that just as placing the sun at the center of the solar system resolved astronomical anomalies, shifting the model to "maybe the brain doesn't create consciousness. Maybe it receives consciousness" could resolve the mysteries of near-death experiences and telepathy. "I personally believe that when within 10 years, that's the way we will see consciousness," he asserts. This is a bold claim, especially coming from a novelist, yet he grounds it in his own personal journey following his mother's death. He admits that eight years ago, he viewed death as "blackness" and humans as "computer cables" whose connections were cut. Now, he sees death as a transition, a belief he claims is supported by noetic science rather than religious revelation.

This pivot is particularly effective because Brown acknowledges the resistance he faces from the scientific community. He notes that materialist physicists, who have built careers and won awards on the current model, are naturally resistant to a paradigm shift. "They really don't like people coming along and saying, 'Actually, what if it's this way?'" he observes. While a counterargument worth considering is that Brown, as a fiction writer, may be more susceptible to confirmation bias when interpreting ambiguous data, his insistence that he had "no religious experience" and "no brilliant flash of lights" lends a layer of credibility to his scientific approach. He argues that this shift in perspective could fundamentally alter human behavior, suggesting that the fear of death drives nationalism and environmental neglect, and that understanding life as a longer journey could make us a "more enlightened species."

The Power of Controversy

Brown's career has been defined by the friction between his work and established dogmas, particularly with The Da Vinci Code. He recalls how the book was banned and burned globally, yet also how it sparked unprecedented engagement with religious texts. A priest in Boston shared that a discussion on the book drew 409 people to a Bible study that usually held twelve. "Your book gave me a platform... it's gotten people excited and asking that all important question of why do I believe what I believe," the priest told him. This highlights a crucial function of Brown's work: it acts as a catalyst for critical thinking, even among those who disagree with his conclusions.

He reflects on the cultural nature of belief, noting that "we worship the gods of our parents" and that our religious views are often a product of geography rather than absolute truth. The fact that The Da Vinci Code continues to resonate with a new generation, despite being over 20 years old, suggests that the questions it raises are timeless. "This feels like it's right now," Brown says of the book's enduring relevance, proving that the tension between faith, history, and mystery is as potent today as it was two decades ago.

Bottom Line

Dan Brown's most compelling argument here is not about the plot of his next thriller, but the urgent need to update our understanding of consciousness before our technology outpaces our morality. His willingness to challenge the materialist consensus with anomalies often dismissed by science is both his greatest strength and his most vulnerable point, inviting both skepticism and deep curiosity. For the reader, the takeaway is clear: the most dangerous ideas are not the ones that are false, but the ones we refuse to question because they threaten our established worldview.

We are at a moment in history where the model of local consciousness is eroding, and the only way to resolve the anomalies is to accept that the brain may be a receiver, not a creator, of the mind.
