Four Autopsies: Late Capitalism a Survival Guide

Wes Cecil presents four concrete examples of how late capitalism quietly extracts money from everyday transactions. The first two—a gift card and an NFL franchise—reveal systems designed to generate fees regardless of value delivered.

The Gift Card Trap

Wes Cecil received a $50 Visa gift card from his brother-in-law. Flip it over, and a $4.95 activation fee appears in fine print. Instantly, the card is worth only $45. That's a 10% loss before any purchase.

But the fees don't stop there. When his brother-in-law bought the card using a credit card, Visa collected merchant fees—roughly a dollar—on the sale. Then when Cecil used the card, more merchant fees applied: another dollar or so per transaction. The total? If he spends the full $50, Visa extracts roughly $7 in fees.

This is simply inflation—a 15% tax on your own money that feels invisible because it's built into prices we never see.

The real mechanism runs deeper. Merchants build these fee structures into the cost of everything they sell. Cash users pay more because merchants must cover credit card processing costs. The more people use credit cards, the higher prices rise—yet cash users also pay more since merchants need to recoup those fees regardless. One key driver: credit card usage massively increases what we pay for goods.

Worse, Visa holds $50 from his brother-in-law until he uses the card. That money sits in financial markets generating returns—the company earns interest on float it never had to earn. And if the recipient flies to a country where gift cards are illegal—like France with stronger consumer protections—and never uses it? The company keeps the full $51 for providing zero service.

That's the ideal structure: 100% profit, no value delivered.

The Chiefs' Stadium Deal

The Kansas City Chiefs are one of the NFL's most popular teams. They've sold out every home game for decades. Thousands wait on a season ticket waiting list. Fans tailgate in parking lots before games—many don't even have tickets.

The stadium is aging. The team wants upgrades: more seats, better pregame experiences, improved facilities. Sounds like reasonable commerce? That's not what happened.

Instead, the state of Kansas offered to pay 70% of the cost for an entirely new multi-billion-dollar stadium—if the team moved from Missouri to Kansas. Why? Revenue streams. Ticket sales, parking, concessions, taxes—all flow to whichever state hosts the team. Missouri loses that revenue stream if the team moves.

The state issues municipal bonds—relatively inexpensive—and pays billions to build a stadium it doesn't own. Wait: the state owns the building. The Chiefs get all revenue from it.

They're getting a free stadium while the public covers construction costs, and they collect every dollar the stadium generates.

The deal includes special zoning around the stadium with almost no taxes—a developer zone for the Hunt family, who are also real estate developers. They develop surrounding land, generate economic activity, pay zero taxes on that development, and collect all revenue from it.

And the seats? The new stadium holds 10,000 fewer seats than current venues—fewer but wealthier fans. Luxury boxes. Higher prices. Demand increases when supply shrinks. Ticket prices likely double.

This is late capitalism at its finest: public funding for private revenue streams, fewer seats for richer customers, and developers capturing value while citizens fund the infrastructure.

Critics might note that these examples represent specific industries rather than universal patterns—gift cards and sports stadiums don't capture how financialization touches healthcare, education, or housing. The piece also frames these as inherently negative when some could argue they simply reflect efficient market mechanisms that consumers chose to use.

The strongest argument runs through both examples: hidden costs accumulate invisibly until you know where to look. Gift card fees aren't scams—they're inflation we accept because we never see the fine print. Stadium deals aren't corruption—they're revenue streams capturing public value for private benefit. The vulnerability is structural, not individual.
