Is a mini ice age coming?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Dec 1, 2019 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace tackles a paradox that confuses millions: how can record global heat coexist with record-breaking cold snaps? His coverage cuts through the noise of political denialism by tracing a direct, physics-based line from melting Arctic ice to the erratic weather battering American farmers. This isn't just a weather report; it is a forensic dismantling of the "mini ice age" theory using the very mechanism that causes the cold: a warming planet.

The Jet Stream's New Groove

Borlace begins by grounding the reader in the stark reality of rising greenhouse gases, noting that the World Meteorological Organization confirmed carbon dioxide levels hit a new record high in 2018. He highlights the disconnect between this data and the lived experience of Americans facing freezing winters, a gap that fuels skepticism. "According to figures from the US National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, all of that information must sound very odd to millions of Americans who so far in 2019 have experienced one of the coldest winters on record." This observation is crucial; it validates the listener's confusion before correcting it, building trust rather than dismissing it.

The author then pivots to the mechanics of the jet stream, explaining it not as a new anomaly but as a system under stress. He quotes Dr. Jennifer Francis to clarify that the jet stream is driven by the temperature difference between the equator and the poles. "The bigger the difference in temperature between the equator and the pole, the bigger the gradient is for the air to travel along, and so the faster it goes." Borlace effectively simplifies complex fluid dynamics into a relatable concept: the engine of our weather is losing its pressure.

"The human emissions of carbon dioxide are causing warming in our atmosphere and that's been melting quite a lot of the sea ice up in the Arctic... causing even more warming and melting and so on and so on."

This section is the piece's strongest asset. Borlace doesn't just state that the Arctic is warming; he explains the feedback loop of dark water absorbing sunlight versus ice reflecting it. He argues that this "Arctic amplification" reduces the temperature gradient, slowing the jet stream and causing it to meander. Critics might note that while the link between Arctic warming and jet stream behavior is gaining traction, some climate models still debate the magnitude of this effect. However, Borlace's reliance on the physical reality of the temperature gradient makes his argument robust against claims of coincidence.

The Science of Blocking Events

Moving from theory to prediction, Borlace introduces recent research from Rice University that connects these weather patterns to hard data. He describes how scientists struggled to model "blocking events"—stagnant weather systems that trap cold air—until they factored in the jet stream's changing characteristics. "Once those factors were included in the calculations, the models retrospective predictions suddenly work beautifully." This "Eureka moment" transforms the discussion from theoretical physics to predictive certainty.

The implications are dire. The new models suggest that the area of these blocking events will increase by 17% due to human-caused climate change. Borlace uses this to dismantle the idea that cold snaps are a sign of cooling. "Mr. Trump, I'm really not sure the United States would benefit from a bit of that good old-fashioned global warming." This direct address to political rhetoric serves as a powerful rhetorical device, grounding high-level science in immediate political consequence.

"That good old global warming has also put huge amounts of extra energy and moisture into our atmosphere which causes far more violent life-threatening storms, hurricanes and tornadoes around the world, and most ironically of all, is the very cause of the Arctic blasts that are afflicting the United States in a more and more extreme way every year."

This irony is the core of Borlace's argument: the cold is a symptom of the heat. He effectively synthesizes the idea that a warmer atmosphere holds more moisture, leading to floods, while simultaneously destabilizing the wind patterns that usually keep cold air contained at the poles. The argument holds up well against the "solar minimum" counter-argument he briefly mentions, as the timing and frequency of these events align more closely with the jet stream's degradation than solar cycles.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage succeeds by turning a confusing contradiction into a coherent narrative of cause and effect. The strongest part of his argument is the clear explanation of how a warming Arctic weakens the jet stream, creating the very cold snaps that skeptics use to deny global warming. The biggest vulnerability remains the public's inability to intuitively grasp non-linear climate feedbacks, but Borlace's use of the "blocking event" research provides the concrete evidence needed to bridge that gap. Listeners should watch for how these blocking events evolve in the coming decade, as the models predict a 17% increase in their size, signaling a future of more persistent, extreme weather swings.
