Maybe you should have bought an electric car

Noah Smith · Noahpinion ·Mar 29, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Noah Smith reframes the electric vehicle debate not as a climate crusade, but as a stark lesson in economic security and supply chain resilience. While the mainstream narrative often fixates on emissions, Smith argues that the volatility of global oil markets—exacerbated by recent geopolitical conflicts in the Middle East—has made fossil fuel dependence a direct liability for the average American commuter.

The Illusion of Stability

Smith opens by dismantling the comforting notion that high gas prices are merely a temporary inconvenience. He points out that while $4 a gallon might seem manageable in a vacuum, the sudden 50% spike in weekly commuting costs for families is a severe shock to household budgets. The author contrasts this with the experience of electric vehicle owners, who remain insulated from these geopolitical tremors. "People who drive electric cars... don't even go to the pump," Smith writes, highlighting a fundamental shift in how energy is consumed. Instead of reacting to global supply chains, EV drivers simply "park their cars in their driveways and garages every night, and attach a little cable," ensuring the vehicle is ready the next morning.

This observation is powerful because it shifts the metric of value from the car itself to the stability of the fuel source. Smith notes that even with rising electricity costs, the economics favor the electric driver: "An analysis last December by Autoblog found that it cost EV drivers only 5 cents to drive each mile, compared to 12 cents for good old gasoline-powered cars." The author suggests that the hesitation to adopt this technology stems not from technical limitations, but from a decade of misinformation. "For years, whenever I'd say that EVs are the wave of the future, I was met with an absolute torrent of nonsense," he recalls. He systematically debunks the old arguments, noting that "EV range has tripled over the last decade" and that charging happens conveniently while the driver sleeps.

Critics might argue that the upfront cost of electric vehicles remains a barrier for lower-income households, a point Smith acknowledges only implicitly by focusing on the long-term operational savings. However, the core of his argument holds: the "blizzard of FUD" (Fear, Uncertainty, and Doubt) has successfully stalled adoption in the U.S. despite the technology maturing rapidly.

"The United States is pulling back from a transition that much of the world is accelerating."

The Industrial Competitiveness Gap

The commentary then pivots to the macroeconomic consequences of this domestic hesitation. Smith highlights a troubling divergence between U.S. policy and global market trends. He cites research from Hengrui Liu and Kelly Sims Gallagher to illustrate the financial pain of this disconnect: "Ford and General Motors had recently announced US$19.5 billion and $6 billion in EV-related write-downs, respectively." These numbers signal a retreat from the future, even as the rest of the world accelerates.

Smith argues that the U.S. has created an artificial bubble of protectionism that ultimately harms its own manufacturers. "In a free market, the end of those subsidies wouldn't have mattered, since Chinese batteries and EVs are much cheaper anyway, but U.S. tariffs are so high that they make Chinese batteries and cars extremely artificially expensive." This protectionism, combined with political polarization, has left American consumers "increasingly reluctant to buy EVs." The result is a "plateau and fall" in U.S. sales while global adoption skyrockets.

The author warns that this is not just a consumer choice issue but an industrial strategy failure. "Slower EV production and slower adoption in the U.S. can keep prices higher, delay improvements in batteries and software, and increase the risk that the next generation of automotive value creation will happen elsewhere." The stakes are high: if the U.S. cedes this ground, it risks losing the supply chains for not just cars, but the broader ecosystem of batteries, drones, and robotics that rely on the same manufacturing base.

Energy Security as National Defense

Perhaps the most compelling section of Smith's piece is the reframing of energy independence. He argues that the current geopolitical crisis demonstrates that oil is a fragile commodity, vulnerable to "supply shocks" that are impossible to predict. "Oil demand is very inelastic in the short run," he explains, meaning that when supply is cut, prices don't just rise; they "shoot up very quickly."

Smith draws a sharp contrast between the global oil market and the domestic natural gas market. While the U.S. benefits from abundant shale gas, the global oil supply is at the mercy of maritime choke points like the Strait of Hormuz. "The fact that oil is a global market means that any war, anywhere in the world, can shut down those choke points and send the price of gasoline skyrocketing everywhere in the world." He notes that the era of U.S. military dominance guaranteeing low oil prices is over, stating bluntly: "In 1991, you could count on Uncle Sam to use its military might to keep oil prices low; today, you can't."

This vulnerability is driving a global shift. Smith lists emergency measures taken by nations from the Philippines to South Korea, where governments are rationing fuel and cutting power to cope with the crisis. "Unlike in previous episodes of crisis and disruption in fossil fuel markets, countries now have another option — build more solar, wind, and batteries." The author suggests that the war, while tragic, serves as a catalyst for this transition. "The Iran War provides a vivid demonstration that the energy transition isn't a climate issue — it's an issue of national security."

"Choosing to disbelieve in technological innovation has real consequences."

The Bottom Line

Smith's strongest argument is the undeniable link between geopolitical instability and the economic vulnerability of the internal combustion engine. By framing EVs as a shield against global supply shocks rather than just an environmental tool, he makes a pragmatic case that resonates with the current moment. However, the piece slightly underestimates the political inertia required to reverse the U.S. retreat from EV subsidies and the cultural resistance to foreign-made vehicles. The reader should watch for whether the recent surge in global EV interest can overcome domestic policy barriers, or if the U.S. will remain wedded to an obsolete technology while the rest of the world moves on.

$4/gallon gas isn’t historically that high. If you measure relative to typical American incomes, it’s considerably lower now than it was in the early 2010s. But that’s cold comfort to people who have to commute every day to work, and who just saw their weekly gas bill increase by 50%. Those people have every right to be upset about Donald Trump’s war in Iran.

You know who’s not feeling the heat in their daily commute? People who drive electric cars. To them, the war in Iran isn’t a source of daily pain at the pump, because they don’t even go to the pump. Instead, they just park their cars in their driveways and garages every night, and attach a little cable to the back of the car, and in the morning the car is charged and ready to go.

And this means they get to drive around much more cheaply than people who fill up their cars at the pump. Yes, the price of electricity is higher than it was before the pandemic. But even so, an analysis last December by Autoblog found that it cost EV drivers only 5 cents to drive each mile, compared to 12 cents for good old gasoline-powered cars. And that was before the Iran War spiked the price of gas!

For years, whenever I’d say that EVs are the wave of the future, I was met with an absolute torrent of nonsense. “What about range anxiety?”, I’d hear from people who were unaware that EV range has tripled over the last decade. “But it takes so long to charge up,” I’d hear from people who don’t realize that EVs charge up while you sleep. “We’re going to run out of minerals!”, I’d hear from people who had never actually looked up the numbers. And so on.

This sort of nonsense failed to sway Yours Truly, obviously, but it did a number on the United States as a whole.
