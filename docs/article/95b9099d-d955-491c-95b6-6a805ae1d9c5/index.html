Maybe you should have bought an electric car

Noah Smith · Noahpinion ·Mar 29, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Noah Smith reframes the electric vehicle debate not as a climate crusade, but as a stark national security imperative, arguing that the volatility of global oil markets makes gasoline cars a dangerous economic liability. While most commentary focuses on emissions or charging infrastructure, Smith brings a visceral, immediate cost-benefit analysis to the table: in a world where geopolitical conflict can instantly double fuel prices, the electric vehicle is the only rational shield against supply chain chaos.

The Economic Shield

Smith cuts through the noise of political rhetoric by grounding his argument in the daily reality of the American commuter. He notes that while $4 gas isn't historically unprecedented, the sudden spike caused by conflict feels devastatingly personal. "Those people have every right to be upset about Donald Trump's war in Iran," he writes, immediately pivoting to the stark contrast in experience for EV owners. For them, the geopolitical storm is irrelevant; they simply plug in at home. The author highlights a compelling statistic from an Autoblog analysis: it costs EV drivers only 5 cents to drive a mile, compared to 12 cents for gasoline cars. This gap widens dramatically when oil prices surge, creating a financial moat that protects EV owners from the whims of foreign policy.

The core of Smith's argument is that the traditional objections to EVs have been debunked by reality, yet the public discourse remains stuck in the past. He dismantles the "range anxiety" myth by pointing out that "EV range has tripled over the last decade," and dismisses charging time concerns by reminding readers that "EVs charge up while you sleep." This reframing is effective because it shifts the conversation from technical limitations to lifestyle convenience. However, critics might note that this analysis assumes universal access to home charging, a luxury not available to the millions of Americans living in apartments or urban centers without dedicated parking.

If you drive a gas-powered car, you are economically vulnerable to these periodic price shocks. If you drive an electric car, you are not vulnerable. It's as simple as that.

Smith argues that the United States is uniquely vulnerable because it has allowed political polarization to stall its own industrial transition. He points out that despite the Inflation Reduction Act's initial momentum, the political landscape has turned hostile, with the Trump administration canceling support for battery factories. This policy flip-flop has real consequences: "Ford and General Motors had recently announced US$19.5 billion and $6 billion in EV-related write-downs, respectively," signaling a retreat from the technology just as the rest of the world accelerates. The author suggests that this isn't just a market correction but an "industrial competitiveness problem" that threatens the future of American manufacturing.

The Geopolitical Reality

The piece takes a darker turn when examining the fragility of the global oil market. Smith draws a sharp distinction between the fragmented natural gas market, which insulates the U.S. due to abundant shale production, and the global oil market, which is entirely at the mercy of maritime choke points. He explains that "oil demand is very inelastic in the short run," meaning that when supply is disrupted, prices don't just rise; they skyrocket because people have no choice but to pay. This inelasticity is the engine of economic pain during conflicts like the one in the Strait of Hormuz.

Smith connects this economic reality to military history, noting a profound shift in American power. "In 1991, you could count on Uncle Sam to use its military might to keep oil prices low; today, you can't," he writes. The argument here is that modern drone warfare and the rise of asymmetric threats have made it impossible for the U.S. to guarantee the free flow of oil, rendering the old strategy of "just go to war in the Mideast" obsolete. This is a powerful, if unsettling, historical pivot that elevates the EV debate from consumer choice to survival strategy.

The Iran War provides a vivid demonstration that the energy transition isn't a climate issue — it's an issue of national security.

The author illustrates this global shift by listing the emergency measures taken by nations like the Philippines, Sri Lanka, and Pakistan, where fuel rationing and rolling blackouts have become daily realities. In contrast, he points to the surge in EV inquiries in Europe and Asia as a pragmatic response to this instability. Smith warns that the U.S. is ignoring this lesson, risking a future where "American car companies will be cut off from global markets" because they cling to "obsolete internal combustion technology." The argument is bolstered by the fact that the battery supply chain is the same one needed for drones and robotics; by failing to scale EV production, the U.S. is effectively "delivering the industrial future to China on a silver platter."

Critics might argue that Smith underestimates the resilience of the internal combustion engine and the potential for new oil discoveries or alternative liquid fuels to stabilize prices. Yet, the sheer speed of the global transition, evidenced by the "flippening" in markets like Norway and Singapore, suggests that the window for a U.S. comeback is closing rapidly.

Bottom Line

Smith's most compelling contribution is his redefinition of the EV transition as a defense against geopolitical volatility rather than an environmental imperative. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its assumption that American consumers will prioritize long-term security over short-term political narratives, a gamble that has historically failed in the U.S. market. Readers should watch whether the current price shocks are enough to break the cycle of "fear, uncertainty, and doubt" that has stalled American adoption, or if the nation will continue to pay the premium for its refusal to adapt.

$4/gallon gas isn’t historically that high. If you measure relative to typical American incomes, it’s considerably lower now than it was in the early 2010s. But that’s cold comfort to people who have to commute every day to work, and who just saw their weekly gas bill increase by 50%. Those people have every right to be upset about Donald Trump’s war in Iran.

You know who’s not feeling the heat in their daily commute? People who drive electric cars. To them, the war in Iran isn’t a source of daily pain at the pump, because they don’t even go to the pump. Instead, they just park their cars in their driveways and garages every night, and attach a little cable to the back of the car, and in the morning the car is charged and ready to go.

And this means they get to drive around much more cheaply than people who fill up their cars at the pump. Yes, the price of electricity is higher than it was before the pandemic. But even so, an analysis last December by Autoblog found that it cost EV drivers only 5 cents to drive each mile, compared to 12 cents for good old gasoline-powered cars. And that was before the Iran War spiked the price of gas!

And so on.
