Can Earth sustain 11 billion humans?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Jan 13, 2019 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace delivers a counterintuitive truth that cuts through the noise of climate anxiety: the looming crisis isn't an explosion of births, but the inevitable momentum of the people already alive. While most headlines scream about population booms, Borlace reveals that fertility rates have plummeted globally, yet we are still barreling toward 11 billion people by 2100 simply because the world's youth bulge is maturing. This distinction is vital for busy minds trying to prioritize solutions, as it shifts the focus from controlling reproduction to managing the resource demands of a generation that is already here.

The Momentum of Demographics

Borlace dismantles the common misconception that we are facing a runaway population explosion. He notes, "you'd be forgiven for thinking that we had ourselves a full scale completely out of control population explosion going on but here's the remarkable thing there's been a massive drop in fertility rate since the second half of the 20th century." The data supports this; as living standards and women's education rise, the number of children per family has dropped to just below 2.5, less than half of what it was fifty years ago. This framing is effective because it corrects a pervasive error in public discourse, preventing the misallocation of energy toward population control when the real driver is demographic inertia.

Using a visual analogy of stacked boxes to represent age cohorts, Borlace explains why the population will continue to swell even without new babies. He describes how, over three generations, the current young population will age and reproduce, filling the "missing" billions that were never born in previous eras. "Within three generations with no increase in fertility rates and no increase in life expectancy just by the inevitable fill up of human beings based on how many people are on the planet today you get to 10 10 billion people," he argues. This logic is sound and highlights a critical nuance: the future population is largely a foregone conclusion based on current demographics, making adaptation more urgent than prevention.

The answer to that crucial question of course is that it depends on how we live and use the resources that are available to us now.

Critics might note that while fertility is dropping, the sheer scale of 11 billion people still presents unprecedented pressure on ecosystems, regardless of the rate of growth. However, Borlace's point remains that the trajectory is less about new births and more about the consumption patterns of the existing billions.

The Geography of Consumption

The most striking part of Borlace's analysis is his pivot from population numbers to land use, revealing that where we live matters far less than what we eat. He points out that if everyone lived at Manhattan's density, the entire global population would fit into New Zealand. "According to that Map There's masses of space all over the place for a population expansion and our planet will barely even notice 11 billion people right well of course it's not just space that we need but food and water," he writes. This reframing is powerful because it moves the debate away from Malthusian fears of running out of physical space and toward the inefficiencies of our resource distribution.

Borlace then exposes the staggering inefficiency of Western diets compared to plant-based ones. He cites data showing that if everyone adopted the diet of Bangladesh, we would need less than 15% of habitable land, whereas adopting the average American diet would require 140% of the planet's available land. "Our famously food passionate European Neighbors in France and Italy as well as in the Alpine regions have a very meat dominated diet as a result if we all live the same way as them we'd need 122% of our total habitable land to sustainers which I think you'll agree is arithmetically impossible," he states. This evidence is compelling and difficult to refute; it isolates diet as the primary variable in sustainability, rather than population size.

He further clarifies that a total shift to veganism isn't the only path forward, offering a pragmatic middle ground. "Even if you keep eating meat but reduce your overall intake by half for example by only eating meat 3 days a week you'd get your land use and greenhouse gas emissions down to levels that are not much more than people on a vegetarian diet," Borlace suggests. This approach avoids the trap of moral purity, making the solution accessible to a broader audience who might otherwise feel overwhelmed by the scale of the problem.

Sackcloth and Ashes and ritual weekly self flatulation are not actually required on this one we just need to have a think about what we consume and get ourselves out of our own healthy Western mindset.

A counterargument worth considering is that dietary shifts in the West are politically difficult to enforce and that developing nations are increasingly adopting meat-heavy diets as they grow wealthier, potentially negating Western gains. Borlace acknowledges this trend but emphasizes that the current disparity in consumption is the immediate lever we can pull.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest contribution is his ability to separate the myth of population explosion from the reality of resource mismanagement, proving that the path to 11 billion is paved with dietary choices rather than birth rates. His argument is vulnerable only to the political inertia of changing entrenched Western consumption habits, but the data he presents leaves no room for denial regarding the inefficiency of current food systems. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: the technology to sustain 11 billion people exists, but it requires a fundamental shift in what we put on our plates, not just how many of us there are.
