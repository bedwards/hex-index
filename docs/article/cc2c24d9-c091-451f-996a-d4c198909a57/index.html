China’s Financial Strategy: Power, Sovereignty and the Limits of Caution

Various · Sinification ·Mar 31, 2026 · 38 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Xia Bin, a veteran of China's financial establishment, delivers a rare and candid admission that the nation's economic might is currently held hostage by its own financial caution. He argues that China's "lame giant" status—huge in output but weak in currency power—is not a temporary glitch but a structural vulnerability born of an outdated fear of global markets. For busy leaders watching the US-China rivalry intensify, this piece is essential because it rejects the standard Western playbook, proposing instead that true sovereignty requires a paradoxical mix of aggressive domestic reform and deliberate, limited global exposure.

The Paradox of Caution

The core of Xia Bin's argument rests on a sharp diagnosis of China's current predicament. He writes, "China remains a 'weak financial power'—a 'lame giant' unable to freely convert its currency, price commodities, or shape the dollar-centred system it depends on." This is a striking concession from a senior State Council researcher, acknowledging that despite decades of growth, Beijing lacks the financial tools to protect its own interests. Xia Bin suggests that the traditional view of finance as merely a servant to the real economy is insufficient; instead, he posits that "policy competition among governments... will determine the course of history."

This framing is powerful because it elevates financial strategy from a technical economic issue to a matter of national survival. Xia Bin argues that in an era of great-power transition, "sporadic incidents and the accumulation of contingent factors become impossible to ignore." He is essentially telling his readers that the old rules of economics no longer apply when the geopolitical stakes are this high. However, this focus on political contingency risks underestimating the market's ability to punish overreach, a point critics might argue is just as dangerous as financial weakness.

"In the face of an uncertain future external environment, policy competition among governments, especially between major powers, will determine the course of history."

The Strategy of Limited Globalization

Where the argument becomes most distinctive is in its proposed solution. Xia Bin does not call for a full-scale opening of China's capital accounts, which would invite volatility, nor does he advocate for total isolation. Instead, he champions a middle path: "full domestic marketisation paired with limited cross-border globalisation." He explains that while the imperative is to globalize, "in the face of a turbulent and unpredictable future external landscape, China's financial globalisation must under no circumstances be rushed."

This approach reflects a deep-seated Chinese instinct that finance is "inherently unstable, politically consequential, and useful only if bound by the needs of the real economy." Xia Bin believes that rushing to make the Renminbi a global reserve currency would be a strategic error. He notes that "the realistic near-term target is regionalisation, using centres such as Hong Kong as the key offshore hub." This is a pragmatic, defensive stance that prioritizes "risk insulation" over the "exorbitant privilege" enjoyed by the United States.

Critics, such as Alicia García-Herrero, would argue that this caution is self-defeating. They might point out that Britain's 19th-century dominance and America's post-1945 financial supremacy were built on bold internationalization, not insulation. By refusing to fully embrace the risks of a global currency, Xia Bin's strategy may perpetuate the very dependency on the dollar he seeks to escape. Yet, his defense is that "using deficiency to ward off harm" is a valid strategy in Traditional Chinese Medicine terms, turning weakness into a shield against external shocks.

"The only viable path is full domestic marketisation combined with limited cross-border globalisation—domestic reform being the prerequisite for exchange rate flexibility, capital account opening and RMB internationalisation."

A Harmonious Path Forward

Despite the tensions in the global order, Xia Bin insists that China's rise does not require a zero-sum conflict. He writes, "A strategy does not have to be adversarial... it can be realised without fundamentally harming others and in a harmonious way." He envisions a "clear and harmonious financial strategy" that advances Chinese interests while sustaining global growth. This is a crucial nuance often lost in Western discourse, which frequently assumes that any Chinese financial expansion is inherently aggressive.

Xia Bin argues that China must offer a "credible answer to the question of how to sustain economic globalisation and promote inclusive growth across the globe." He believes that "the real financial world cannot be captured by mainstream Western textbooks" because finance is "systemic, innately unstable, dynamic and structurally unequal." By rejecting "bookish" analysis, he calls for a political economy framework that adapts to the "distribution of global economic power."

This perspective challenges the reader to consider that the "original sin" of currency mismatch is not just an economic flaw but a political constraint that can only be solved through a unique, non-Western approach. While some may argue that "harmonious" strategies are impossible in a bipolar world, Xia Bin's insistence on this path offers a distinct alternative to the current trajectory of decoupling.

"China should adopt a managed floating exchange rate: neither a hard peg nor a free float suits a rising but financially weak major economy, and no existing currency bloc fits China's scale."

Bottom Line

Xia Bin's most compelling contribution is his unflinching admission that China's financial system is the weak link in its rise to superpower status, and that the solution lies not in mimicking the West but in a carefully calibrated, defensive globalization. The argument's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that "limited" globalisation can ever fully insulate China from the volatility of the dollar-dominated system, a risk that history suggests may be unavoidable. Readers should watch to see if Beijing's next moves align with this cautious pragmatism or if the pressure to break the dollar's grip forces a more radical, risky shift.

Xia Bin’s call for “full domestic marketisation paired with limited cross-border globalisation” rightly identifies China’s structural vulnerabilities—currency mismatch, RMB non-convertibility, and exposure to dollar hegemony. Yet his analysis rests on a flawed premise: that finance (“the xū”) must remain subordinate to the real economy (“the root”), serving only as a cautious tool to avoid external shocks. This view underestimates finance’s autonomous strategic value. A developed financial system is not just supportive infrastructure; it is a core instrument of national power, sovereignty, and self-reliance—especially by breaking dependence on foreign currencies.

History demonstrates this truth. Britain’s 19th-century financial supremacy and America’s post-1945 “exorbitant privilege” arose from bold monetary internationalisation, not insulation. Dollar dominance grants the United States seigniorage revenue, sanction leverage, and immunity to external monetary shocks—precisely the autonomy Xia Bin seeks for China. By contrast, his strategy of managed floats, phased capital controls, and RMB “regionalisation” (rather than full internationalisation) perpetuates the very “financial weakness” he diagnoses. Gradualism keeps China tethered to dollar liquidity, foreign reserve accumulation, and external pricing power over commodities and energy. True self-reliance requires the opposite: decisive opening that transforms the RMB into a genuine reserve currency, allowing China to finance its growth on its own terms, recycle surpluses globally, and insulate domestic policy from U.S. Federal Reserve decisions.
