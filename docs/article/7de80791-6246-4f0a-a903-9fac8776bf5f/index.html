The Information State Isn’t Going Away

Various · Compact Magazine ·Apr 6, 2026 ·14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jacob Siegel's latest work, The Information State, delivers a startling thesis: the machinery of state propaganda never truly disappears; it merely changes hands and refines its tools. While the current political climate celebrates the dismantling of specific agencies, Siegel argues that the underlying infrastructure of digital control remains intact, waiting to be reactivated by whichever faction holds the levers of power. This is not a story about one administration's victory over another, but a structural analysis of how modern governance has become inextricably bound to the algorithms that shape our reality.

The Illusion of Dismantling

Siegel begins by dissecting the recent pivot in Washington, where the administration shuttered the Global Engagement Center (GEC), an entity originally created to counter foreign disinformation. The official rationale was that the GEC had become a "censorship industrial complex" targeting domestic critics. Yet, the author points out a glaring contradiction in the administration's subsequent actions. "Upon taking power last year, the Trump administration set out to dismantle an infrastructure initially set up a decade prior for the same purposes laid out in the cable," Siegel notes, only to see Secretary of State Marco Rubio immediately recommend teaming up with military psychological operations units and leveraging Elon Musk's X platform for "countering anti-American propaganda." This rapid reversal suggests that the desire to manage the information environment is bipartisan and institutional, not merely partisan.

The author draws a sharp line between the rhetoric of liberation and the reality of state power. He writes, "efforts to control the information environment overseas returned home," a phenomenon that became inevitable as digital technology blurred the lines between foreign and domestic threats. This argument is bolstered by historical context; just as the FBI's COINTELPRO operations in the 1960s left a legacy of surveillance that survived the era's political backlash, the current dismantling of the GEC may be more cosmetic than substantive. The "technical infrastructure of the information state, while dormant, remained fundamentally intact," Siegel observes, waiting for a new mandate.

The weapons of hybrid warfare were still lying around, waiting to be picked up.

Critics might argue that the shift to private platforms like X represents a genuine decentralization of power, freeing speech from bureaucratic oversight. However, Siegel counters this by highlighting the deep entanglement between the state and these private entities. He points to the "Twitter files" and the subsequent hearings as evidence that the public-private partnership was already well-established, with the government and tech giants collaborating on content moderation long before the current administration took office. The idea that removing state oversight automatically leads to freedom is, in his view, a dangerous fallacy.

The Historical Echo Chamber

To understand the present, Siegel takes readers back to the founding moments of modern state propaganda: Woodrow Wilson's presidency and the Vietnam era. He argues that these periods were not anomalies but prototypes for the current "information state." During World War I, intellectuals flocked to Washington to help the government "tame the unruly masses" using new media tools. "Both of these prior episodes ended in ignominy," Siegel writes, noting that while the specific agencies like the Committee on Public Information were disbanded, their functions diffused into the broader bureaucracy. The legacy of Wilson endured not in the agency itself, but in the permanent integration of propaganda, censorship, and publicity into the fabric of government.

This historical lens is crucial because it challenges the notion that the current era is unique. Siegel cites a 1996 RAND Corporation report that predicted the internet would make it impossible to "clearly distinguish between 'foreign and domestic' threats." This prediction has come to pass, turning domestic politics into "another theater of hybrid warfare." The author suggests that the terminology of "misinformation" and "disinformation," once the domain of intelligence analysts, has become a ubiquitous tool for managing domestic dissent, regardless of which party is in power.

The comparison to the "China convergence" theory adds a layer of global urgency. Siegel references the idea that all advanced technological societies are gravitating toward "totalizing techno-administrative governance." In the US, this manifested as a "whole of society" approach that circumvented normal legislative processes to enact policy through digital levers. "The notion of 'free and spontaneous' choices started to look like a farce in the context of an algorithmically controlled environment," he asserts. This framing forces the reader to confront the possibility that the very structure of our digital existence is designed to limit genuine autonomy, regardless of who is pulling the strings.

The functions of propaganda, censorship, and publicity diffused throughout countless government offices, public relations agencies, military and intelligence bureaus, and advertising firms.

However, Siegel's reliance on the philosopher Jacques Ellul introduces a tension in his argument. While Ellul critiques the pervasive role of "technique" in modern life, Siegel sometimes seems to endorse a cyberlibertarian view that freedom exists simply because the government is not directly directing algorithms. He writes that the fact that "the government was no longer directing how the social media companies deployed those algorithms … made public life freer." This claim feels contradictory to his earlier points about the enmeshment of state and private power. If the algorithms are still "tweaking people's fractional perceptions and influencing their behaviors," as he admits, can the environment truly be considered free?

The Unresolved Paradox

The most unsettling aspect of Siegel's analysis is the suggestion that the pendulum swing against the anti-disinformation crusade has not resulted in a more open society, but rather a more chaotic one. He notes that while the administration "demolished the central mechanisms of information control," it also "maintained the state of emergency" to target political opponents. The result is a system where the infrastructure of control remains, but the guardrails are gone. The "de facto alliance" between the administration and X, where the platform's algorithm favored political allies, serves as a prime example of this new, chaotic iteration of the information state.

Siegel leaves the reader with a difficult question: Is the information state a specific historical artifact of the Obama and early Trump eras, or is it an inevitable outcome of operating in the age of information technology? His analysis leans toward the latter, suggesting that "any state that operates in the age of information technologies, in other words, will by necessity become an information state." This deterministic view challenges the optimism of those who believe that removing government regulation will spontaneously lead to a flourishing of free speech.

Bottom Line

Jacob Siegel's The Information State offers a compelling, if unsettling, diagnosis of our current political moment: the machinery of control is too deeply embedded in our digital infrastructure to be dismantled by a change in administration. The book's greatest strength is its refusal to treat the current political shifts as a victory for liberty, instead revealing them as a reshuffling of the same deck. Its primary vulnerability lies in the unresolved tension between critiquing the power of algorithms and the hope that removing state oversight alone can restore freedom. Readers should watch for how the administration's new partnerships with private tech giants evolve, as this will likely determine whether the "information state" remains a dormant threat or an active engine of governance.

On March 30, The Guardian reported on a US State Department cable that directed embassies and consulates worldwide to “launch coordinated campaigns against foreign propaganda.” Secretary of State Marco Rubio recommended teaming up with “the Department of War’s Psychological Operations” unit and making use of Elon Musk’s X, which he described as an “innovative” tool that could assist with “countering anti-American propaganda.” The context for this missive, the article noted, was the war with Iran, as well as ongoing tensions with China and Russia.

There was an irony in Rubio’s recommendations. Upon taking power last year, the Trump administration set out to dismantle an infrastructure initially set up a decade prior for the same purposes laid out in the cable. The central node of this apparatus was the Global Engagement Center (GEC), established by executive order in the last year of Barack Obama’s presidency with a mandate to counter foreign propaganda. The month before Donald Trump’s second inauguration, Congress terminated the GEC’s funding, after which its operations were scaled back and absorbed into the State Department. In April 2025, Rubio shuttered what remained of it for good. 

The administration’s rationale for getting rid of the GEC was that it was part of a “censorship industrial complex” targeting Americans, specifically conservatives and other critics of the liberal establishment. In a statement to the House Foreign Affairs Committee from around the time the GEC was dissolved, the conservative journalist Benjamin Weingarten decried the “turning of federal agencies … tasked with targeting foreign adversaries instead on Americans and our core political speech.” The GEC, he said, had been a “key cog in such efforts.” 

Musk, whose platform Rubio is now recommending as a useful instrument of state-led information warfare, played a role in popularizing this line of criticism. After taking over what was then Twitter in 2022, he offered a number of journalists access to a trove of internal documents revealing the site’s content moderation decisions and interactions with various governmental and nongovernmental parties concerned with propaganda and disinformation. The following year, the “Twitter files” became the subject of a series of hearings in Rep. Jim Jordan’s “Weaponization of the Federal Government” House subcommittee.
