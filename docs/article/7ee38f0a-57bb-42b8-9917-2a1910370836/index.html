So, I went to South Korea recently. I used to come to Korea several times a year for business travel, but my last trip was almost 15 years ago. So, I was seriously impressed. Not just that the bedet toilets now come with an enema button, but also by how good the sole metro has become.

I took a lot of trains while I was there. Not just to the touristy stuff, but also to filming locations in dozens of neighborhoods and cities all around Seoul. Getting to all of these places wasn't just possible by Metro. It was also extremely fast, convenient, and actually enjoyable because of the way the whole system works together.

Like, there is so much great public transit design visible in just this one clip alone that I'd probably need a whole YouTube video to explain it all. So, I guess we better get started. Seoul is a city that experienced extremely rapid urbanization. In my previous video about Chongya Chong, I showed that this is what the area looked like in the 1950s.

And this is what it looked like by the 1970s. Absolutely wildly fast growth. And yet in 1970, there was no metro system here. None at all.

Metro Line 1 was the first to open in 1974. Now, it's one of the top five largest metro systems in the world with a higher yearly wrership than Paris, London, or New York. In fact, New York is the only one of those three that has more stations than Seoul. But, as I'll make it clear in this video, it's almost embarrassing to compare any of those other systems to here because of how good it is.

This is the latest Soul Metro map. It's relatively easy to read and understand, at least on a metro with this many lines and stations, but it hides just how extensive this system really is. You can see that line one goes from Shinchan to Yun Chun, which looks impressive enough on this map, but when you look at where these places actually are, it's crazy. These two stations are 150 km apart.

So this isn't just a metro for Seoul. It's for the entire region. The train system is exceptionally well integrated and the trains really blur the lines between commuter trains, intercity trains, and metro trains with several lines acting as multiple different types ...
