Why German History is Different

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Oct 10, 2022 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now makes a startling claim: the very fragmentation that made Germany a political backwater for centuries was the crucible that forged the modern self. While most histories focus on unification or war, this piece argues that Germany's lack of a single national narrative forced its thinkers to turn inward, inventing the concepts of individualism, nationalism, and the romantic imagination that now define global culture.

The Fractured Crucible

The author begins by dismantling the idea that Germany had a coherent national story until 1871. "Whereas England and France have a claim to having a single coherent National narrative and National story of A Sort Germany's is much more fragmented as it was a loose collection of Germanic principalities and States until it was unified only in 1871." This fragmentation, the piece argues, wasn't a weakness but a catalyst. Without a central state or empire to dictate culture, German intellectuals developed an "inferiority complex" that paradoxically fueled a massive creative explosion. They rejected the cold rationalism of the French Enlightenment and the empirical science of the British, seeking something more visceral.

"The ideas of the Enlightenment... were felt by many German thinkers to be distant abstract cold theories they seem to be completely void of the Vitality of ordinary life."

This framing is compelling because it reframes the German "irrationalist" turn not as a rejection of reason, but as a search for the "felt immediacy of everyday routines." The author suggests that the trauma of the Thirty Years' War and the Napoleonic occupation pushed thinkers to look for stability not in laws, but in the soil, the forest, and the vernacular language. Critics might note that this romanticizes a period of deep political instability, ignoring how this same cultural turn eventually fed into dangerous forms of ethno-nationalism. Yet, the piece effectively highlights how the search for identity in a fractured land birthed the modern obsession with the inner self.

The Power of the Inner World

The commentary shifts to how this cultural soil produced a unique relationship with language and imagination. The author points to Martin Luther's translation of the Bible into German vernacular as a pivotal moment that democratized reading and turned Germans inward. "Germans took refuge in the imagination more than anywhere else in Europe." This insularity, born of isolation, allowed for a flourishing of literature and philosophy that prioritized the "undecidable mystery" over technical precision.

The piece highlights the role of the forest as a central symbol, noting that "the forests were powerful and life-giving and beautiful and dangerous and contradictory all at the same time." This duality is captured in the works of the Brothers Grimm and Caspar David Friedrich, where nature is not just a backdrop but a character that embodies the contradictions of human existence. The author argues that this focus on the local and the immediate allowed German thinkers to construct a new kind of reality.

"Goethe wrote famously that I turned back into myself and I find a world that within us were forces more powerful more mysterious more voluminous than anything we find outside of us."

This is the core of the author's argument: the German philosophical tradition shifted the locus of truth from the external world to the internal mind. The piece describes how Johann Gottfried von Herder and Johann Gottlieb Fichte argued that we do not merely observe the world; we construct it. "Fichte wanted to spread among his listeners the desire to be an eye not a complacent sentimental passive eye however but one that was Dynamic World grinding world creating." This is a bold assertion that the modern concept of agency and self-determination is a direct product of this German reaction against external authority.

The Invention of the Modern Self

The final section connects these 18th-century ideas to our current understanding of the human condition. The author posits that Romanticism was not just an artistic movement but the "invention of a modern self." This self is defined by a tension between the finite (the everyday, the ordinary) and the infinite (the imagination, the spiritual). "The Romantic Theologian Frederick schliermacher wrote that imagination is the highest and most original element in us it's your imagination that creates the world for you."

The piece argues that this tension is what makes us human today. We are constantly striving for something absolute—truth, security, perfection—while knowing we are fallible and contradictory. "The human story wasn't just about science logic reason industry but it had to be holistic philosophies had to contain both these things and the imagination sentiments had to combine Grand National and Global narratives with everyday life." The author suggests that without this German contribution, our modern understanding of psychology, art, and even politics would be unrecognizable.

"By endowing the commonplace with a higher meaning the ordinary with a mysterious respect the known with the Dignity."

This concluding thought lands with force, reminding us that the ability to find meaning in the mundane is a cultural inheritance. However, the piece glosses over the darker side of this "world-creating" imagination, which can also lead to the construction of exclusionary myths and the justification of violence in the name of national destiny. The author acknowledges the "contradiction of life" but perhaps underplays how easily the "dynamic eye" can become a tool for domination.

Bottom Line

Then & Now delivers a fascinating, if slightly romanticized, account of how Germany's political weakness became its philosophical strength. The strongest part of the argument is the connection between national fragmentation and the birth of the modern inner self, a link often overlooked in standard histories. Its biggest vulnerability is the tendency to view this cultural shift through a purely positive lens, minimizing the toxic potential of an imagination untethered from shared reality. Readers should watch for how these same mechanisms of "world-creating" imagination play out in today's polarized digital landscape.
