Welcome to not boring world

Packy McCormick · Not Boring ·Jan 6, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

In an era where artificial intelligence can generate competent prose in seconds and video platforms dominate attention, Packy McCormick makes a defiantly counterintuitive claim: the written word is not dead, but rather the only medium capable of capturing the raw, unrefined ideas that actually drive progress. While the rest of the media landscape chases algorithmic engagement, McCormick argues that the most valuable insights are trapped in the minds of busy practitioners who lack the time to write them down, creating a critical gap between frontier innovation and public understanding.

The Bottleneck of Genius

McCormick identifies a structural failure in how we consume expert knowledge. He notes that while founders and researchers are "betting their prime years on certain ideas that no one else understands," their best insights rarely see the light of day because they are too busy building to write. "I am only one person and I have only ten fingers," he admits, acknowledging that his own capacity to translate these ideas into essays is a bottleneck. This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from the reader's attention span to the structural difficulty of knowledge transfer.

The proposed solution is a shift from solo authorship to a "liquid super team" model, where McCormick acts as a co-writer and editor for domain experts. He points to his own history, noting that his two most popular essays, "The Electric Slide" and "Excel Never Dies," were co-written with founders. "The trade is simple: they're smarter than me, I'm (usually) a better writer than them," McCormick writes. This approach leverages the unique strengths of both parties: the practitioner's deep, real-time knowledge and the writer's ability to structure and clarify complex thoughts. It is a pragmatic recognition that expertise does not automatically translate into communication skills.

Critics might argue that co-authorship risks diluting the original voice or introducing the writer's bias into the expert's data. However, McCormick anticipates this by emphasizing that the goal is not to polish the idea into a generic summary, but to preserve the "raw inputs and thought processes" so readers can form their own conclusions.

Ideas are meant to be written. They are meant to be put to paper, cross-examined, torn apart, rearranged, supplemented with data, edited, packaged, placed next to shitty hand-drawn illustrations.

The Case Against the Audio-Visual Default

A central pillar of McCormick's argument is a sharp critique of the podcast and video formats, which have become the default for sharing ideas. He challenges the assumption that these mediums are superior for deep thinking, asking readers to "quick: name the best idea you've heard on a podcast recently." He lists canonical texts of the tech and economic world—"Aggregation Theory," "The Bitter Lesson," "Attention is All You Need"—and notes that they were all written, not spoken. "I can't think of a single canonical idea born on a podcast or video, not one," he asserts.

This distinction is crucial for busy professionals. While audio is excellent for storytelling and building rapport, McCormick argues it is a "terrible medium for ideas" because it lacks the permanence and editability required for rigorous thought. "They are meant to be quoted and shared and built upon," he explains, highlighting that the written word allows for the cross-examination and rearrangement that drives intellectual evolution. This is a bold stance in a culture that increasingly prioritizes speed and consumption over depth and retention.

Feeding the Brain in the Age of AI

Perhaps the most timely aspect of the piece is its positioning against the rise of Large Language Models (LLMs). McCormick argues that as AI makes it easier to get "mediocre outputs on-demand," the value of high-quality, human-generated inputs skyrockets. He posits that the future belongs to those who can access "ideas you can't find in LLMs" because these are ideas being formed in real-time by people "out in the field building stuff."

The vision is to create a repository of "Long Inputs / Short Outputs," where the focus is on the raw material of thought rather than the final polished product. "My co-authors are figuring out new knowledge in real time, and we're going to share it fresh, before we're even sure it's right, while the hypothesis it still being tested," McCormick writes. This commitment to sharing unfinished, frontier thinking is a direct counter to the AI tendency to synthesize existing data into average conclusions. It suggests that human intuition and risk-taking are the only things that will remain truly "alpha" in an automated world.

We will write down ideas you can't find in an LLM. My co-authors are figuring out new knowledge in real time, and we're going to share it fresh, before we're even sure it's right, while the hypothesis it still being tested.

A World of Means and Meaning

Beyond the mechanics of writing and AI, McCormick frames this project as a philosophical stance on the future of technology. He introduces a worldview centered on "Means & Meaning," arguing that technology should not just provide greater capabilities (means) but also contribute to a life worth living (meaning). "If we are zipping around the world on supersonic planes depressed and arguing about politics or whatever, we have failed," he warns. This adds a layer of moral weight to the project, suggesting that the goal is not just to inform, but to guide the trajectory of innovation toward human flourishing.

The editorial infrastructure he plans to build—hiring editors, illustrators, and researchers—is designed to curate these inputs without sanitizing them. The aim is to balance the messiness of raw thought with the legibility required for a broad audience. "I'll curate the very best people I can find and present their ideas as coherently as possible, to balance the raw inputs with legibility," he states. This approach acknowledges that while raw data is valuable, it must be framed to be useful.

Bottom Line

McCormick's strongest argument is the identification of a critical market failure: the disconnect between the speed of human innovation and the slowness of written documentation. His bet that "the most biased narrator, the one betting his or her livelihood on his or her idea, is the most reliable narrator" offers a compelling alternative to the detached, synthesized content of the AI age. The project's biggest vulnerability lies in its execution; scaling a model that relies on the time and energy of busy founders without diluting the quality of the output will be a significant challenge. Readers should watch to see if this "liquid super team" can consistently deliver the frontier insights it promises, or if the model collapses under the weight of its own ambition.
