Why Star Wars Can’t Move Forward

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Jan 30, 2025 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden identifies a paradox that has plagued Star Wars for a decade: the franchise is so terrified of killing its most beloved characters that it has paralyzed them. His most striking claim is that the Jedi are suffering from a "Schrodinger Jedi" crisis, existing simultaneously as mythic heroes and problematic cultists, a contradiction that prevents any new story from moving forward.

The Identity Paradox

Van der Linden argues that the core issue isn't just bad writing, but a fundamental inability to choose a narrative lane. He writes, "The real issue points to a deeper problem of quality in the way that the Jedi story is told, the way their philosophy, their lore and general identity is progressed and expanded upon." This observation lands because it explains why shows like Andor or Rogue One felt fresh—they sidestepped the Jedi entirely—while Jedi-centric projects like The Acolyte or Obi-Wan Kenobi felt stuck in a loop of self-contradiction.

The author suggests that modern storytelling has shifted from myth to sociology, stripping the Jedi of their spiritual weight. "In the original trilogy and in the prequels the Jedi were an ideal complicated by politics, whereas in this new vision they have become politics complicated by idealism." This distinction is crucial. It explains why deconstructing the Jedi feels different now; when you treat them as a political organization rather than a mystical order, their "child abduction" recruitment practices and dogmatic rigidity become glaring flaws rather than noble sacrifices.

In a more sociological context that battle for the soul becomes just another aspect of the material reality.

Critics might argue that this shift toward realism is exactly what modern audiences crave, citing the success of grounded superhero deconstructions. However, Van der Linden counters that unlike other franchises, Star Wars cannot fully commit to this deconstruction because the brand relies on the mythic appeal of the lightsaber. The result is a story that wants to have its cake and eat it too.

The Oscillation of Vision

The most damning evidence Van der Linden presents is the franchise's refusal to let the conflict resolve. He points to the sequel trilogy as the moment the fracture became permanent. "The sequel gave us not one but two visions about the Jedi: one that indeed tried to set in motion this more sociological course... and also another one that is actively pulling back against it." He describes this as a constant friction where "Luke throwing the lightsaber away in one movie and catching it in the next" creates a narrative whiplash that alienates viewers.

This oscillation is not just a creative choice but a commercial imperative. The author notes, "Disney wants to keep selling their action figures," which necessitates a return to the "uncritical nostalgia bait" even after a movie like The Last Jedi tried to dismantle the myth. Van der Linden writes, "By sitting right next to that other trend, the result is a constant friction between two conflicting visions that now feels like it has cemented itself in Star Wars as a whole."

The argument holds up under scrutiny: you cannot have a story where the hero must end the order, and then immediately have the next hero restore the order without breaking the internal logic of the universe. The franchise is stuck in a state of "artificially patting that runtime," stretching the conflict to avoid a conclusion.

The Path Forward

So, how does the franchise escape this loop? Van der Linden suggests that the solution requires a hard choice. "First and foremost they have to commit to a vision. They have to set a direction and follow it through." He posits that the creators must decide if Star Wars is a "mythological reflection of matters of the heart and soul or a social political examination of matters of the world."

He draws a parallel to the Batman franchise, noting that "a more grounded real world approach worked great for The Dark Knight... much less so for Man of Steel." The implication is clear: Star Wars must pick a lane. If it chooses the sociological route, it must fully embrace the flaws of the Jedi as a political entity. If it chooses the mythological route, it must stop pretending the Jedi are a problematic cult and lean into their aspirational nature. Trying to do both, as the author argues, is the very thing that is "preventing the Jedi from progressing forward in a meaningful way."

The constant oscillation between competing perceptions of what the Jedi are supposed to be is the true identity crisis.

Bottom Line

Van der Linden's diagnosis is sharp: Star Wars is stuck because it refuses to let the Jedi die or be reborn, preferring a safe, contradictory middle ground that satisfies no one. The argument's greatest strength is its identification of the commercial pressure to maintain the brand's mythic status while simultaneously trying to appear culturally relevant through deconstruction. The biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that a single, unified vision is even possible in a franchise now managed by a committee of directors with different styles, suggesting the "Schrodinger Jedi" may be the only version of the character that can survive the modern corporate landscape.
