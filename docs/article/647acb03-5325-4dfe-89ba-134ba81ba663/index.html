#60: Two Craft Talks, a Celebration of Life, and an Old Story Inspired by Great Art (from AWP Baltimore)

Matt Bell transforms a standard conference recap into a masterclass on the ethics and mechanics of historical fiction, arguing that the writer's job is not to judge the past but to inhabit its blind spots. He bypasses the usual noise of the literary world to offer a specific, actionable philosophy: that sensory reality bridges centuries while analytical certainty destroys them.

Rewriting the Past from the Inside Out

Bell begins by dismantling the idea that historical research is merely about gathering facts. Instead, he frames it as a psychological exercise in building a temporary worldview. "The most important thing historical research does for me is help put me in the mind of people from the time I'm writing about," he writes. This approach shifts the goal from accuracy to empathy, asking writers to understand what a medieval peasant knew that we do not, and what we know that they could not.

The core of his argument rests on the tension between the character's limited perspective and the author's modern knowledge. Bell suggests that a novel can be smarter than its characters without breaking the illusion of the era. "Your book can be smarter, more ethical, and more curious than its characters," he asserts. This is a crucial distinction for writers who fear anachronism; the narrative voice can hold a broader truth while the character remains trapped in their time.

The historical novel is a model of the world in a particular time—but it's a model built in a later time, which has its own way of seeing, seeing deeper, seeing around.

However, Bell warns against the trap of over-researching analysis. He argues that reading modern critiques of history often ruins the "verisimilitude" of the work because it imposes a future certainty on a present uncertainty. "Analysis comes from a future looking backward at the past with a kind of surety no one in the present can have about their own time," he notes. This is a provocative stance that challenges the academic instinct to contextualize everything. Critics might argue that ignoring modern analysis risks perpetuating harmful biases, but Bell counters that the "blind spots" are exactly where the drama lives.

To bridge the gap between eras, Bell points to the unchanging nature of the human body. "The salt smell of the sea. The scent and feel of a leaf crushed between the fingers," he lists, suggesting that these sensory details are the true time machines. He goes further, noting the visceral reality that "Sex. Blood and guts. Farts. Weird as it sounds, your body probably smells a lot like Cleopatra's or Jesus Christ's did." This grounding in physical reality is the piece's most effective tool for making history feel immediate rather than distant.

The Architecture of Revision

The commentary then pivots to Bell's own experience writing craft books, specifically his guide Refuse to Be Done. He reveals that the book was not born in a vacuum but evolved from a decade of teaching and a personal struggle with the revision process. "In all that time, I was never actually taught how to revise, only to do it," he recalls, highlighting a gap in standard creative writing education that his book aims to fill.

Bell's method is practical and rigorous, breaking the novel-writing process into three distinct drafts. He emphasizes that the second draft, which often involves a complete rewrite, is where the real work happens. "The kind of people who write novels aren't afraid of hard work. They just want help knowing what work to do next," he observes. This reframes revision not as a punishment for a bad first draft, but as a necessary structural phase.

The evolution of his craft book serves as a case study in testing ideas. He notes that the content was "battle-tested" over nine years, moving from a private practice to a public talk, then to a classroom, and finally to a manuscript. "There's nine years between the first version of the craft talk that became RTBD and the book appearing on shelves," he explains. This longevity suggests that the advice is not theoretical but proven in the trenches of actual writing.

Writers are so hungry for practical advice and tangible steps they can take to better their writing. They don't want an easy way out of the work, but they do want a way forward.

Bell also challenges the notion that only experts can write about craft. He shares an anecdote about assigning craft essays to beginner students who initially protested they had no authority to teach. "My guess is that everyone in the room at the conference has a great craft essay in them, if they thought hard about what they're learning from the project they're writing," he says. This democratization of craft knowledge is a generous and empowering conclusion to his panel notes.

A Community Remembered

The piece concludes with a somber but uplifting reflection on the death of Dan Wickett, a mentor and founder of the Emerging Writers Network. Bell describes the memorial gathering not as a eulogy, but as a celebration of a legacy that shaped an entire generation of writers. "I did some ugly crying at the podium," he admits, grounding the emotional weight of the event in a raw, human moment.

This section serves as a reminder that the literary world is built on relationships, not just books. Bell highlights the importance of the "people in the picture" and the community that gathered to honor Wickett. It is a brief but powerful interlude that connects the technical advice on writing back to the human necessity of connection.

Bottom Line

Matt Bell's commentary succeeds because it refuses to treat historical fiction as a museum exhibit, insisting instead on the visceral, bodily connection between past and present. His strongest argument is that sensory truth trumps analytical certainty, a lesson that applies to all genres of storytelling. The piece's only vulnerability is its reliance on the writer's personal intuition over structural theory, which may leave some readers wanting more concrete frameworks for navigating historical bias.

Excerpt from the original article on Matt Bell

I returned home yesterday evening from a week spent in Baltimore at the AWP Conference, where (if you haven’t been) 10,000 or so writers and writing teachers gather annually to talk writing and reading, eat and drink literally everything in the city, shout ourselves hoarse, and go to innumerable late night off-site events in cafes and bars, each of which features thirty geniuses reading for seven minutes apiece, plus one bad actor who reads for fifteen. It’s one of my favorite events, in large part because so many of the people I love and admire will be there at the same time.

At this year’s conference, I gave one reading at an event generously organized by Ben Niespodziany (where I shared a little bit of the novel I just finished writing—thanks to everyone who said kind things afterward!) and two panels, one on “The Art and Craft of the Craft Book,” where I spoke alongside Heidi Czerwiec, Jehanne Dubrow, Jason Schneiderman, and Nicole Walker, and one on “Rewriting Stories That History Forgot,” with Sandra Simonds, Maurice Carlos Ruffin, and Joseph Scapellato. I also spoke at a memorial gathering for Dan Wickett, an old friend and mentor who was also my boss when I worked as an editor at Dzanc Books, as well as the founder of the Emerging Writers Network, an important early literary blog that championed so many writers of my generation.

Several people who attended my panels asked if I’d be publishing my comments somewhere, and this seems to be the appropriate spot! Thanks to everyone who came and wanted to see these notes again—including the lady in the audience who yelled at me to slow down my very fast speaking voice, haha. These are my notes for speaking, so they’re rougher than my usual fare here, but hopefully they’re still useful. After that, there’s a brief note about Dan’s celebration of life, plus an old story of mine and the Baltimore art that inspired it almost 20 years ago (and that I visited again this past week). It’s kind of a grab bag newsletter! Thanks for reading, as always, and thanks to everyone who said hi in Baltimore this past week. I came home feeling renewed and enthused to get back to writing, and I hope you all did too.
