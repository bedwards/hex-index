Confused Notes on the War on Iran: Everyone Is Losing Except for Surviving IRGC Officers Getting Swift Promotions

Brad DeLong · DeLong's Grasping Reality ·Mar 30, 2026 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Brad DeLong does not merely analyze the war on Iran; he dismantles the very premise that this conflict is a contest where one side can win. His most jarring claim is that this is a "slow, grinding competition in which the prize is to lose the most," a grim reality where the only true beneficiaries are surviving IRGC officers securing swift promotions. For the busy listener tracking global stability, this piece cuts through the noise of diplomatic posturing to reveal a strategic trap that threatens to sink the global economy.

The Illusion of Victory

DeLong immediately confronts the chaotic rhetoric emanating from Washington, contrasting the administration's bluster with the strategic reality on the ground. He highlights the absurdity of current communications, quoting a transcript where Donald Trump claims Iran has "given us most of the points" of a 15-point plan, only to reveal the "concession" was simply the delivery of oil tankers. "This is what happens every day in terms of communications from the U.S. government," DeLong writes, exposing a disconnect between the administration's perception of leverage and the actual mechanics of the conflict.

The author argues that the administration is operating under a delusion that the Iranian regime has collapsed or is about to, a belief that drives a dangerous escalation. DeLong notes that while Trump threatens to "blow up and completely obliterat[e] all of their Electric Generating Plants," the reality is far more constrained. He points out that the administration believes it is negotiating with a "new, and more reasonable regime," a notion DeLong treats with deep skepticism. This framing is effective because it strips away the political theater to show a leadership structure that is reactive, volatile, and disconnected from the grim constraints of military logistics.

"This is not a war about 'victory'; it is a slow, grinding competition in which the prize is to lose the most."

Critics might argue that DeLong underestimates the psychological impact of regime change rhetoric on Iranian hardliners, suggesting that the threat of total destruction could force a capitulation. However, DeLong counters this by emphasizing the "great deal of ruin in a nation," suggesting that the Iranian leadership is willing to absorb catastrophic damage rather than surrender sovereignty.

The Strategic Trap

The core of DeLong's argument rests on the geography of the conflict, specifically the Strait of Hormuz. He invokes the insights of historian Bret Devereaux to explain that the Middle East's strategic value to the U.S. is binary: if the Suez Canal and the Persian Gulf oil arteries remain open, the region is manageable; if they close, the U.S. is dragged into an unwinnable quagmire. DeLong writes, "The gamble here was that because the regime would simply collapse on cue, the United States could remove Iran's regional threat without having to commit to a major military operation."

He argues that this gamble failed spectacularly. By bombing nuclear facilities in June 2025, the U.S. created a scenario where Iran was forced to respond, not with a surrender, but with a strategy to close the Strait. DeLong notes that "20% of liquid natural gas and around 20% of the world's fertilizer passes through the Strait of Hormuz," making any disruption a global economic catastrophe. This context is crucial; it moves the discussion from a regional skirmish to a systemic threat to global supply chains.

The author draws a chilling parallel to recent naval history, noting that escort operations in the strait are "deeply unpromising." He suggests that American destroyers would be vulnerable to the same swarm tactics that sank the Russian cruiser Moskva. "It isn't even hard to imagine what the attack would look like: essentially a larger, more complex version of the attack that sunk the Moskva," DeLong writes. This comparison grounds the abstract threat of missiles in a concrete, recent historical precedent, making the danger feel immediate and tangible.

"Once the strait was effectively closed, the United States could not back off out of the war."

A counterargument worth considering is that the U.S. Navy possesses superior technology and could theoretically clear the strait with overwhelming force. DeLong addresses this by pointing out the sheer volume of Iranian anti-ship missiles and the difficulty of protecting slow-moving tankers in constrained waters. He argues that the U.S. spent a year hammering the Houthis without fully removing their capabilities, suggesting that a state actor like Iran presents an even more formidable challenge.

The Cost of Chaos

DeLong's most damning critique is reserved for the internal dynamics of the U.S. administration. He describes a leadership vacuum where a "reckless, senile or non compos mentis prince" dictates policy without adult supervision. He paints a picture of a White House where senior staff act as "courtiers guessing which way he will jump this afternoon" rather than strategists. "The traditional realist admonition is: do not look at the prince's preferences, look at his constraints," DeLong writes, "But this advice assumes the prince is at least minimally rational and goal-directed."

The author suggests that the lack of a coherent strategy has led to a situation where the U.S. is trapped in an escalation spiral. He notes that "neither party can back down unilaterally and survive politically," creating a deadlock where both sides suffer. This analysis is particularly potent because it explains why rational actors might continue a war that is clearly detrimental to their interests. The political cost of backing down is simply too high for either side to absorb.

"It is not possible for two sides to both win a war. But it is absolutely possible for both sides to lose."

DeLong also touches on the human cost, noting that while the Iranian people suffer the most, the U.S. bears significant diplomatic and economic costs. He argues that the world will remember that the U.S. "unilaterally initiated by surprise a war of choice which set off severe global economic headwinds." This historical perspective serves as a warning that the immediate tactical gains of the war will be overshadowed by long-term strategic losses.

Bottom Line

DeLong's strongest argument is his reframing of the conflict not as a battle for victory, but as a mutual suicide pact where the only winners are the opportunistic elites within the Iranian military. His biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that the U.S. administration is incapable of a rational pivot, a view that may underestimate the pressure of economic collapse to force a policy change. Readers should watch for the next move in the Strait of Hormuz; if shipping disruptions accelerate, the theoretical "escalation trap" DeLong describes will become a concrete global crisis.
