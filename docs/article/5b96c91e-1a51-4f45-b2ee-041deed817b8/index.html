Early 2020, life as we knew it took an unexpected turn. Talk of viruses. Compulsory masks. Lockdown. Confusion. Fear. A long list of new rules. Can I walk the dog? Can my kids still play sport? Is the gym open? Friday night drinks? What about my niece’s wedding? Welcome to Victoria, Australia, the lockdown capital of the freakin’ world.

Used to backyard barbies, most Aussies were prepared to ‘do their bit’ to beat ‘Rona. But days in iso—isolation—turned into weeks. The gym remained closed, weddings were postponed indefinitely, and we started losing touch with our friends and family. Students were skipping online lessons to lose themselves in gaming, doom scrolling, or lying semi-comatose on their unmade beds amid a nest of food scraps and apathy.

With hubby still off to his job in ‘essential services’ each day, I felt the isolation like everyone else. There I was, clinging to the edge of the curtains in the front room, staring mindlessly out at the deserted street.

My elderly neighbors, Jim and Gail, were quite active in our area before ‘Rona came to town. Worried about Gail, who suffered from depression, I scanned the street before sneaking across to their door. Gail’s pale smile contrasted with the dark smudges under her eyes.

After we’d shared tea, chocolate bikkies1 and a few belly laughs, I felt better. We all felt better – for a while. Gail was doing it much tougher than me and it occurred to me that for many old folks still living at home, my short term iso was their normality. A scary thought, but what could I do to help?

I decided to contact Ageless, a community group that matches volunteers with elders as buddies. On the form, I ticked “female”, “lives locally”, “creative”, “educated”, and “a reader”, as my preferences. Two days later I received a response. They matched me with an old bloke, an ex-motor mechanic, living an hour and a half away. Really? I had had a difficult relationship with my own father, similar vintage, and was unsure what I could offer this old guy.

“I don’t know,” I told Fiona from Ageless.

“Look, Doug’s a lovely old man, easy to talk to, and he’s happy just to have a phone chat once every week or two.”
