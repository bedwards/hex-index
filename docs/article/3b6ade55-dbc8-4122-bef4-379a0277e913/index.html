Meetups Everywhere Spring 2026: Times & Places

Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Scott Alexander does something rare in the digital age: he transforms a scattered list of coordinates into a map of a global intellectual renaissance. While most online communities remain trapped in comment sections, this piece documents the physical manifestation of the Rationalist and Effective Altruism movements, proving that 180 cities now host people willing to meet in person to discuss complex ideas. This isn't just a calendar; it is evidence of a decentralized network finally finding its feet.

The Architecture of Trust

The most striking element of the text is not the sheer volume of locations, but the radical inclusivity Alexander explicitly engineers into the event design. He writes, "If you're reading this, you're invited. Please don't feel like you 'won't be welcome' just because you're new to the blog, demographically different from the average reader, or hate ACX and everything it stands for." This is a deliberate counter-measure to the insularity that often plagues niche intellectual communities. By explicitly welcoming those who might disagree with the blog's core tenets, the author attempts to break the echo chamber before it even forms.

The logistical instructions reveal a community that values efficiency and clarity over social friction. Alexander advises organizers to "bring a sign that says 'ACX MEETUP' and prop it up somewhere," and to "have people type their name and email address in a spreadsheet or in a Google Form." These are not just administrative details; they are the scaffolding of a movement that prioritizes data and accessibility. The instruction to "schedule a followup meetup while you're having the first" reflects a deep understanding of human psychology and the difficulty of maintaining momentum in volunteer-led initiatives.

"You don't have to RSVP or contact the organizer to be able to attend... RSVPs are mostly to give organizers a better sense of how many people might show up."

This approach lowers the barrier to entry significantly. In a world where social anxiety and bureaucratic hurdles often prevent participation, removing the requirement to contact a stranger beforehand is a powerful signal of openness. However, critics might note that this low-barrier approach could lead to no-shows or logistical chaos in smaller venues, a risk organizers must mitigate with the suggested RSVP systems.

From Theory to Practice

The geographic spread of these meetups offers a fascinating glimpse into the global reach of ideas that originated in online forums like LessWrong. The list spans from Baghdad to Hobart, from the "forboding street-level doorway" in Tokyo to the "grass area next to Max Brenner" in Tel Aviv. This distribution mirrors the historical trajectory of the Effective Altruism movement, which grew from a small group of philosophers in Oxford and Berkeley into a global network influencing billion-dollar donations and AI safety research.

Alexander notes that in Hobart, organizers plan to "combine this with the local EA group (which grew out of the 'Spring' ACX meetup)." This detail is crucial; it shows that these gatherings are not merely social clubs but incubators for organized action. The transition from reading a blog post to coordinating a meetup in a wildlife park in Jos, Nigeria, or a writers' club in Chengdu, demonstrates a level of agency that is rare in the digital landscape. The author's instruction to "ask people about a recent post or book review that they liked" serves as a simple but effective icebreaker, leveraging shared intellectual history to build immediate rapport.

"If it's the first meetup, people are probably just going to want to talk, and you shouldn't try to organize some kind of planned workshop or anything like that."

This advice is a masterclass in community management. It acknowledges that the primary value of these gatherings is the human connection, not the curriculum. By resisting the urge to over-structure the event, the organizers allow the organic dynamics of the community to take center stage. This organic growth is what allowed the Rationalist community to scale from a few hundred people on a forum to a global network capable of organizing in over 180 cities.

The Human Element

Despite the focus on logistics and lists, the human stories embedded in the notes are the most compelling part of the piece. The organizer in Baghdad writes, "I tried to host the meet up a few times before, and no one came. so if you are coming please inform me so I would actually go." This vulnerability highlights the fragility of these connections and the genuine effort required to sustain them. Similarly, the organizer in Perth notes, "I am totally blind. Please look for the ACX meetup sign and announce yourself when you arrive," showcasing the community's commitment to accessibility and inclusion.

The sheer variety of venues—from a "writers' club" in Chengdu to a "parking lot" in Tel Aviv, where a secure location is noted "in case of a missile alert"—underscores the diverse realities of the participants. The Tel Aviv organizer's note about adhering to safety guidelines regarding missile alerts adds a layer of gravity to the event, reminding readers that these intellectual gatherings are taking place in a world of real-world stakes. The instruction to "bring blank labels and pens for nametags" is a small detail that speaks to the desire for personal connection in an increasingly anonymous world.

"We'll be in the back room at Our Local on Kloof Street and will have an ACX meetup sign, and a pile of boardgames."

This image of board games and signs in Cape Town encapsulates the spirit of the movement: serious ideas discussed in a casual, welcoming environment. The inclusion of "dogs and kids" in the Tel Aviv meetup further reinforces the idea that these are community events, not exclusive academic conferences.

Bottom Line

Scott Alexander's list is more than a directory; it is a testament to the power of decentralized organization and the enduring human need for intellectual community. The strongest part of this coverage is its ability to translate abstract online engagement into concrete, physical reality across 180 cities. The biggest vulnerability lies in the reliance on volunteer energy, as seen in the Baghdad organizer's plea for attendance, but the sheer scale of the response suggests a resilient and growing movement. For the busy reader, this piece confirms that the ideas discussed in the blog are not just theoretical—they are being lived, discussed, and acted upon in coffee shops and parks around the world.
