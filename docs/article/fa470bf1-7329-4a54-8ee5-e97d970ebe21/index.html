Under the Vienna Convention, diplomats have full immunity for criminal prosecution in the country where they serve. So, local police generally cannot arrest or charge them even for serious offenses. And if a diplomat commits a crime, all the host country can do is kick them out of that country.

Now, why would countries agree to give diplomats such sweeping immunity from persecution? Well, because they want their own diplomats to have the same protections abroad. The concept of special status of foreign envoys and messengers dates back to antiquity when Greeks and Romans wanted to ensure that their envoys would safely carry messages to foreign countries without fear of retribution. Today, diplomatic immunity typically includes all diplomatic agents, including administrative personnel and service staff.

Immunity also extends to family members who are part of the diplomat's household. And under international and federal law, diplomats also enjoy broad immunity for many kinds of civil suits. A diplomatic agent isn't even required to give evidence as a witness. Seems like a pretty sweet deal.
