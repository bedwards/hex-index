Hydrogen Will Not Save Us. Here's Why

Sabine Hossenfelder · Sabine Hossenfelder ·Jan 14, 2023 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a climate conversation dominated by hype, Sabine Hossenfelder delivers a necessary reality check: hydrogen is not the silver bullet we are told it is. While governments and corporations pour billions into a "hydrogen economy," Hossenfelder argues that the physics, economics, and geopolitics of the technology make it a deeply flawed solution for mass transport. For busy decision-makers, this is not just a technical critique; it is a warning against betting the future on a fuel that is expensive, inefficient, and potentially just as dirty as the fossil fuels it aims to replace.

The Efficiency Trap

Hossenfelder begins by dismantling the romanticized image of hydrogen as a clean, simple alternative. She notes that while the exhaust is indeed "pure water," the process of getting there is anything but simple. "Hydrogen is a gas that at normal atmospheric pressure and temperature takes up a lot of volume and it's some not impractical to drag a zeppelin behind your car," she writes. This vivid analogy underscores the fundamental storage problem: to make hydrogen viable, it must be compressed to 700 bar, a process that consumes massive amounts of energy and results in a fuel with only one-sixth the energy density of gasoline by volume.

The author's logic here is airtight. She points out that while hydrogen has a higher energy content by mass, the heavy tanks required to store it negate that advantage, making hydrogen cars "somewhat heavier than gasoline powered ones." This is a critical distinction often missed in marketing materials that focus solely on emissions. The technology itself is not new; Hossenfelder reminds us that fuel cells were developed over 200 years ago and perfected by NASA for the Apollo missions. Yet, as she notes, "they've never been particularly popular" because "fossil fuels are considerably more convenient." The convenience gap remains, and closing it requires overcoming significant physical barriers that no amount of political will can easily erase.

"Hydrogen is therefore not really a source of energy but a storage system."

The Color of Carbon

Perhaps the most damning section of Hossenfelder's analysis concerns the source of the hydrogen itself. She explains that while hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe, it rarely exists in a pure form on Earth. To get it, we must break chemical bonds, which requires energy. She introduces the industry's "color scale" to track production methods, revealing a stark reality: "at the moment almost all hydrogen is produced in one of those Ways by using fossil fuels."

Hossenfelder writes that "more than 95% of the hydrogen worldwide was assigned one of those colors black brown or gray," a process that releases 830 million tons of carbon dioxide annually. This is a crucial piece of context that often gets lost in the excitement over "green hydrogen." She then scrutinizes "blue hydrogen," which the industry touts as a climate-friendly alternative because it captures carbon emissions. However, she cites research showing that due to methane leaks and the energy required for carbon capture, "blue hydrogen performs so poorly" that its greenhouse effect is comparable to burning natural gas directly.

Critics might argue that blue hydrogen is a necessary bridge technology while green hydrogen scales up. However, Hossenfelder's data suggests that even under ideal conditions, the emissions reduction is marginal, and the reliance on fossil fuel infrastructure remains a significant vulnerability. The economic argument is equally grim; she notes that producing hydrogen via solar or wind is "pretty much the most expensive way you can do it," and efficiency losses from fluctuating renewable energy sources make it unlikely to ever be economically competitive with dirty hydrogen.

The Geopolitical Bottleneck

Beyond the chemistry and economics, Hossenfelder identifies a looming geopolitical crisis. The fuel cells that power these vehicles rely on platinum and iridium, rare metals that are difficult to replace. "The entire hydrogen economy hinges on the availability of those two metals," she argues. This creates a fragile supply chain concentrated in just three countries: South Africa, Russia, and Zimbabwe. She highlights a report from Wood Mackenzie predicting that by 2030, "demand for idium will be several times higher than the supply."

This is a strategic blind spot for many nations investing heavily in hydrogen. While the technology promises energy independence, it actually creates a new dependency on a handful of nations for critical minerals. Hossenfelder also touches on the "cold start problem," where water produced in the fuel cell freezes in sub-zero temperatures, degrading the system. This basic physics issue means hydrogen vehicles are ill-suited for many climates without expensive pre-heating systems, further limiting their practicality.

"The city of rebart in Germany for example recently retired its six new hydrogen powered buses because The Filling Station broke down sinking a few million euro."

This anecdote serves as a microcosm of the broader infrastructure failure. It is not just about the cars; it is about the entire ecosystem required to support them, which Hossenfelder describes as incredibly expensive and prone to breakdowns. The need for specially coated, thick tanks to prevent hydrogen embrittlement adds to the cost and weight, creating a cycle of inefficiency that is hard to break.

Bottom Line

Sabine Hossenfelder's argument is compelling because it strips away the marketing veneer to reveal a technology that is physically inefficient, economically unviable, and geopolitically precarious. Her strongest point is the demonstration that hydrogen is a storage medium, not a source, and that the energy required to produce and compress it often negates its environmental benefits. The biggest vulnerability in the current push for hydrogen is the assumption that technology will solve these fundamental physics and supply chain problems before it is too late. For now, the evidence suggests that betting on hydrogen to save us is a gamble we cannot afford to make.
