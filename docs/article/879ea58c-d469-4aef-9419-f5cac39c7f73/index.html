The Rise and Fall of the Mainstream Media

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Aug 1, 2024 · 90 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now delivers a startling diagnosis for our current media chaos: the rise of the internet didn't break the corporate media monopoly; it merely completed a centuries-long evolution where profit motives systematically crushed diverse, radical voices. This isn't just a history lesson; it's a blueprint for why your news feed feels so manipulated today, revealing that the 'free' internet is walking the same path as the 19th-century press toward consolidation and sensationalism.

The Illusion of Linear Progress

The author immediately dismantles the comforting narrative that media history is a straight line from ignorance to enlightenment. "In history we too often think of the news the media information as becoming progressively more free linearly Through Time stone tablets Papyrus printing press newspapers radio television the internet," Then & Now writes. This framing is crucial because it forces the listener to abandon the idea that technology alone guarantees freedom. The piece argues that every new medium, from the printing press to the web, initially promises liberation but quickly develops a darker, controlling side.

The commentary suggests that understanding the present requires a deep dive into the past, specifically the moment when states lost their grip on information. "The media really becomes interesting when states start to lose control of its grip on news and information creating an ostensibly separate fourth estate," the author notes. This is a sharp observation: true media diversity flourished not when governments were absent, but when censorship collapsed during events like the English Civil War, allowing for a "republic of letters" where pamphleteers and philosophers debated freely.

"It lines up conveniently with the progressive March from ignorance superstition backwardness Dark Ages despots through to democracy connection reason progress and light."

However, this golden age of diversity was short-lived. Then & Now points out that the original explosion of opinion was "truly diverse and truly influential," citing Thomas Paine's Rights of Man selling 200,000 copies in a population of 10 million. The argument here is that the sheer volume of competing voices was the norm, not the exception, until economic and legal forces intervened. Critics might note that this romanticized view of the 18th-century press overlooks the fact that literacy was still low and access was limited to the propertied classes, but the sheer density of debate remains a powerful contrast to today's algorithmic silos.

The Industrialization of Opinion

The pivot point in the narrative is the invention of the steam-powered printing press, which shifted the landscape from pamphlets to mass-market newspapers. Then & Now explains that while this technology allowed for larger runs, it also made publishing expensive, forcing papers to rely on advertising. "Any money thrown upon them is so much thrown away," an advertising executive is quoted as saying regarding publications with readers who couldn't afford products. This single sentence encapsulates the birth of the modern media bias: the audience is no longer the customer; the advertiser is.

The author traces how this economic reality led to the rise of "yellow journalism," where sensationalism became a profit strategy. "Murder stories made up 50% of the pages of London's Lloyd's weekly despite the rates in violent crime decreasing across century," Then & Now observes. This evidence is compelling because it proves that the media's focus on crime and scandal isn't a reflection of reality, but a reflection of what sells. The shift from political ideas to entertainment was a calculated move to capture the bourgeois audience that advertisers coveted.

"Commercialization plus industrialization plus populism equal sales and profit."

This formula explains why working-class papers like the Daily Herald struggled and eventually failed, while tabloids like The Sun thrived. The commentary highlights a tragic irony: the most popular working-class paper had the least advertising revenue because its readers couldn't buy the products being sold. The result was a media landscape that increasingly ignored the working class in favor of the middle class, a dynamic that still shapes newsrooms today. A counterargument worth considering is that the rise of sensationalism also democratized news by making it affordable and entertaining for the masses, even if the content was shallow.

Manufacturing Moral Panics

As the 20th century progressed, the media's role shifted from reporting news to manufacturing consent and distraction. Then & Now cites sociologist Stuart Hall, arguing that "moral panic was a way for the ruling class to distract away from the real problems facing British Society." The author details how tabloids focused on superficial scare stories about sexuality, gangs, and drugs while ignoring oil shocks, poverty, and nuclear war. This is a devastating critique of the media's function: to provide "dramatic narratives with a strong moral content" that serve as a distraction from systemic failures.

The piece concludes by noting that professional groups often provide the evidence for these crises, but the media subjects them to market pressures. "Sections of the mass media subjected to Market pressures have responded by presenting dramatic narratives with a strong moral content," the author writes. This connection between market forces and social anxiety is the piece's most vital insight. It suggests that the outrage we see online today isn't an accident; it's the modern equivalent of the 19th-century murder mystery, designed to keep us engaged and clicking.

"The press was pandering to the worst in its readers tastes."

The argument holds up well against the backdrop of modern social media, where algorithms prioritize engagement over truth, mirroring the old tabloid formula. The only gap in the coverage is a brief mention of how digital platforms have accelerated this cycle, but the historical parallel is strong enough to stand on its own.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's strongest contribution is exposing the economic machinery that turned the media from a diverse marketplace of ideas into a consolidated engine for profit and distraction. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its heavy reliance on British history, which, while relevant, might obscure the unique regulatory differences in the American media landscape. Readers should watch for how these historical patterns of sensationalism and consolidation are playing out in the current algorithmic era, where the stakes are higher and the reach is global.
