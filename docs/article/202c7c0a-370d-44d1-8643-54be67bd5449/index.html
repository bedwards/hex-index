A brutal flu season, new dietary guidelines, and why cancer screenings are at risk

Dr. Katelyn Jetelina · Your Local Epidemiologist ·Jan 13, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

This week's briefing from Your Local Epidemiologist cuts through the noise of a record-breaking flu season to expose a disturbing disconnect between scientific evidence and federal policy. While the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention tracks the highest rates of respiratory illness in nearly thirty years, the administration has simultaneously released dietary guidelines that ignore its own scientific review and tightened safety nets just as cancer screenings begin to slip away.

The Viral Surge and Policy Blind Spots

Dr. Katelyn Jetelina, writing through her guest host Jess Steier, frames the current respiratory crisis not as a temporary blip, but as a historic anomaly driven by a specific viral evolution. The data is stark: "Influenza-like illness is the highest it has been since 1997-98, and the highest on record since the CDC started tracking." This isn't just a statistical spike; it represents a tangible threat to pediatric health, with emergency department visits surging among children and seventeen pediatric deaths already recorded. The author notes that the current strain, a subclade of H3N2, is "better at evading this season's flu shot and prior immunity," accounting for roughly 80% of tested samples.

The commentary highlights a particularly jarring policy decision: the removal of the flu shot from the official Health and Human Services childhood vaccination schedule. Jetelina argues that "HHS taking the flu shot off the list amidst a flu season that could break records for pediatric deaths is cruelly tone deaf." This creates a dangerous friction where federal guidance contradicts the American Academy of Pediatrics, which still recommends the shot for everyone six months and older. While the administration may be prioritizing other health metrics, the immediate consequence is a potential drop in uptake during a season where "the flu shot might not fully prevent an infection, but it can greatly reduce severity and reduce spread in the community."

Critics might argue that changing vaccination schedules is a routine administrative process, but the timing here suggests a failure to align bureaucratic updates with real-time epidemiological data. The human cost of this misalignment is measured in hospitalizations and the seventeen children who have already died.

The situation is compounded by a resurgence of measles, with over 2,200 confirmed cases this season. The author warns that the U.S. risks losing its measles elimination status, a milestone held since 2000, if transmission continues uninterrupted. The outbreaks in South Carolina and the Utah-Arizona corridor are not isolated incidents but symptoms of a broader erosion of herd immunity.

A Divergence in Dietary Science

The piece then pivots to the newly released Dietary Guidelines for Americans, revealing a significant break from established scientific norms. Historically, these guidelines have aligned with the independent Scientific Report produced by an advisory committee. This cycle, however, the Department of Agriculture and HHS "diverged from that report, openly criticized it, and introduced a separate Scientific Foundation developed privately by contracted scientists."

The new guidelines, reduced to a mere ten pages from the previous 164, have sparked confusion. The author notes that while the intent was to make the advice "simpler and more accessible," this has come "at the expense of detail, guidance, and nuance that clinicians, schools, and policymakers rely on." The new recommendations include a heightened focus on protein, an encouragement of animal fats, and a softening of alcohol guidance, removing specific daily limits despite evidence that "no amount of alcohol is safe."

Perhaps most concerning is the explicit removal of health equity as a consideration. The guidelines now exclude factors like "socioeconomic status, race, culture, research representation, and diverse population needs" from the review process. This shift means that federal nutrition programs, which serve one in four Americans, will be guided by a framework that ignores the structural barriers many populations face in accessing "nutrient-dense food groups."

A counterargument worth considering is that the administration sought to empower individuals with "real" whole foods rather than prescriptive limits. However, by stripping away the context of equity and the rigor of the independent review, the guidelines risk becoming a political statement rather than a public health tool. As the author points out, "Most Americans don't follow the guidelines anyway," yet the guidelines shape the food served in schools and the nutrition assistance programs that sustain vulnerable families.

The Erosion of Preventive Care

The final section of the briefing connects policy changes to a tangible loss of life through the lens of Medicaid eligibility. The "Big Beautiful Bill" introduces new work and renewal requirements that will take effect in January 2027. An analysis in the Journal of the American Medical Association predicts that "more than one million missed screenings in the next two years" will result in "155 preventable deaths."

This is not a theoretical risk; it is a projection based on the loss of coverage for breast, colorectal, and lung cancer screenings. The author emphasizes that "this estimate is conservative," as it does not even include cervical cancer screening. The impact will be uneven, with states that previously expanded Medicaid facing the steepest decline in screening rates. While there is hope that community health centers might step in, the author cautions that this will "require investment in already stretched systems."

Amidst this contraction of access, there is a glimmer of progress in cervical cancer prevention. The Health Resources and Services Administration has endorsed at-home human papillomavirus self-collection tests. This move acknowledges the barriers to care, noting that "an estimated 1 in 4 adults are not up to date with cervical cancer screenings." The at-home kits offer a "less invasive" alternative for those who dread clinical exams, potentially increasing participation. However, the author warns that "an HPV test is not a Pap smear," and for high-risk groups, clinician-collected samples remain the gold standard.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this briefing is its unflinching connection between administrative decisions—whether in vaccination schedules, dietary science, or Medicaid eligibility—and their direct, often fatal, impact on public health. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on projections for future policy impacts, which, while grounded in data, cannot yet be fully verified by real-world outcomes. Readers should watch closely as the new Medicaid requirements take effect in 2027, as the gap between policy intent and health reality threatens to widen significantly.

Infectious disease “weather report”.

Flu. Buckle up, folks. We’re in the thick of a very rough flu season. The CDC tracks “influenza-like illness” (ILI)—doctor visits for fever, cough, and sore throat—as a proxy for respiratory virus activity. Most of those cases right now are flu. ILI is the highest it has been since 1997-98 (nearly 30 years), and the highest on record since the CDC started tracking. Last season, infections peaked at the beginning of February, and we’re already seeing similar rates a month earlier this year. And infections will continue to increase, since they haven’t peaked yet.

Emergency department visits are highest among kids ages 5-17, with children under 5 close behind. Seventeen children have died from the flu so far this season. For context, last year set the record for pediatric flu deaths (280). Looking at this horrible season, we might see similar rates this year, unless uptake of the flu shot—which greatly reduces the risk of hospitalization and death—catches up.

Subclade K, the latest offshoot of H3N2 (type A)—basically a slightly updated version of the virus we already know—emerged over the summer and is to blame here. It’s better at evading this season’s flu shot and prior immunity. It has accounted for roughly 80% of flu samples tested so far.

What this means for you: The best thing you can do is get a flu shot, and make sure everyone in your family 6 months and older has received one. It’s not too late.
