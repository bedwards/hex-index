The creation of instant coffee

Various · Works in Progress ·Apr 6, 2026 ·14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Most people assume instant coffee is a compromise—a bland, powdered afterthought born of convenience. This piece from Works in Progress flips that narrative, arguing that the history of instant coffee is actually a history of high-stakes chemical engineering where the goal was never just to remove water, but to preserve the very molecules that make coffee worth drinking.

The Physics of Flavor Loss

The authors, Oscar Sykes and Benjamin Stubbing, begin by dismantling the assumption that drying coffee is simple. They correctly identify the central paradox: "Coffee's appeal lies in the hundreds of volatile compounds that create its flavor and aroma, exactly the substances most likely to disappear during processing." This is a crucial distinction that many overlook. The article effectively traces the early failures not as mere bad recipes, but as fundamental misunderstandings of thermodynamics and chemistry.

The narrative moves through a series of disastrous experiments, from John Dring's 1771 attempt to mix coffee with butter and tallow, to the "coffee essence" of the mid-1800s which tasted more like "coffee flavored molasses." The authors note that these early methods relied on boiling, which they rightly cite as the enemy of flavor: "That's why they were syrups. Boiling away all the water to create a dry powder would have destroyed whatever coffee flavor remained." This framing is sharp; it reframes the history of instant coffee as a battle against heat.

The article's treatment of the American Civil War context adds necessary depth. It explains how the logistical burden of transporting 250 tons of coffee for 100,000 troops drove the military to seek alternatives, even if the result was a concentrate that soldiers compared to "axle grease." This historical pivot point highlights how necessity drove innovation, even when the technology wasn't quite ready.

The Industrial Breakthrough

The commentary shifts to the first genuine powder, created by David Strang in New Zealand in 1889. The authors describe his "Dry Hot-Air" method, which cleverly used the physics of evaporation to keep the coffee cool: "Because this energy comes from the air rather than the liquid, the coffee surface actually gets cooler as it evaporates." This is a sophisticated explanation of a counter-intuitive process. However, the authors are honest about the trade-off, quoting expert Arjun Haszard: "This process, given what we know about what happens to coffee with heat and air would have undoubtedly resulted in heat damaged, oxidized coffee. Portable, yes, but also most likely horrible."

The piece then details the rise of George Constant Louis Washington and his Red E Coffee. While the taste was "disagreeable," the industrial scale was the real innovation. The authors capture the soldier's perspective perfectly: "I am very happy despite the rats, the rain, the mud, the draughts, the roar of the cannon and the scream of shells. It takes only a minute to light my little oil heater and make some George Washington Coffee." This quote underscores the article's central thesis: the value of instant coffee wasn't culinary excellence, but morale and logistics.

"The convenience of instant coffee masks a surprisingly difficult problem."

Solving the Sticky Problem

The narrative takes a dramatic turn with the 1929 Wall Street Crash and the Brazilian coffee surplus. The authors vividly describe the destruction of 10.3 billion pounds of coffee, noting that the surplus led to the invention of the modern instant coffee we recognize today. The story of Max Morgenthaler, who continued his research at home after Nestlé cut funding, is a compelling human element. His breakthrough involved spray drying, a technique that had to be adapted because coffee's natural sugars become sticky at high temperatures.

Morgenthaler's solution was to mix coffee with carbohydrates like maltodextrin to raise the sticky point. The authors explain that "despite using much higher air temperatures than previous methods... spray drying actually causes less heat damage to the coffee as the droplets spend only seconds in the heated chamber." This is a nuanced point about residence time versus temperature that is often missed in simpler explanations. The result was Nescafé, which sold out in two months and became a "commodity vital to the war effort" during World War II.

Critics might note that the article glosses over the environmental and economic impact of the massive coffee burning in Brazil, focusing instead on the technological silver lining. While the innovation is fascinating, the human cost of the surplus crisis is a darker backdrop that deserves more than a passing mention.

From Ice to Vapor

The final section explores the evolution toward quality, moving from spray drying to freeze drying. The authors explain the science of sublimation, where ice turns directly to vapor under low pressure, a process used by the Inca for potatoes centuries ago but industrialized for medical supplies during World War II. The comparison of flavor retention is stark: "One study found that freeze drying maintained 77 percent of volatile compounds compared with 57 percent for spray drying."

The piece concludes by looking at the modern premium market, where specialty roasters use aroma recovery technologies like the spinning cone column to capture delicate volatiles. The authors note that even with these advances, "flavor compounds are inevitably lost during the drying process," but the gap between instant and fresh is closing.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its reframing of instant coffee not as a culinary failure, but as a triumph of chemical engineering and logistics. The authors successfully demonstrate that the struggle to preserve volatile compounds drove some of the most significant industrial innovations of the last century. The biggest vulnerability is the occasional glossing over of the economic and social costs of the crises that spurred these innovations, but the technical depth more than compensates. Readers should watch for how these same drying and aroma-recovery technologies are being applied to other fragile food products in the coming years.

The convenience of instant coffee masks a surprisingly difficult problem. Coffee’s appeal lies in the hundreds of volatile compounds that create its flavor and aroma, exactly the substances most likely to disappear during processing. Creating instant coffee required developing techniques to extract the soluble molecules in coffee from the insoluble plant matter without destroying the fragile compounds that make coffee worth drinking.

The first attempt at the drink was, by all accounts, terrible. In 1771, over two centuries after coffee reached Europe, Londoner John Dring filed a patent for a ‘coffee compound’. Dring’s method involved mixing ground coffee with butter and tallow, then heating the mixture on an iron plate until it thickened into a paste that could be shaped into cakes. These cakes were then dissolved in hot water to make coffee. The purpose of the animal fats isn’t entirely clear. They may have been intended to extract and carry soluble compounds from the coffee grounds or to preserve the ground coffee from oxidation. Whatever Dring’s aim, the method wasn’t commercially viable because the fats went rancid, causing the cakes to spoil quickly.

During the mid-1800s, several firms produced instant coffees as thick liquid concentrates that could be reconstituted with water. In 1840, the Scottish company T & H Smith developed a ‘coffee essence’ by brewing coffee and reducing it to around a quarter of its original volume. This thick liquid was mixed with chicory extract and burnt sugar syrup, creating a molasses-like concentrate. One or two teaspoons mixed with boiling water made a drink, though it tasted more like coffee flavored molasses than proper coffee.

Another attempt came during the American Civil War. In 1832, President Andrew Jackson had replaced soldiers’ daily spirit rations with coffee beans and sugar. This created a heavy logistical burden for the army, with a 20-day supply for 100,000 troops weighing 250 tons, all needing transport by horse-drawn wagon. Roasting, grinding, and brewing coffee in the field was also time-consuming for soldiers.
