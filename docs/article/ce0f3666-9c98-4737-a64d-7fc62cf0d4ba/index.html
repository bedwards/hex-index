Erik olin wright and the lessons of the shmoo

Ben Burgis · Philosophy for the People ·Jan 11, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Ben Burgis transforms a 1940s comic strip about magical creatures into a rigorous framework for understanding why the wealthy fear abundance more than scarcity. By applying Erik Olin Wright's analytical Marxism to the fictional "shmoo," the piece reveals a stark truth about modern capitalism: the system relies not just on the exploitation of labor, but on the artificial scarcity of basic necessities. This is not a whimsical detour; it is a precise dissection of why universal healthcare and guaranteed income remain politically toxic to the ruling class, even when they would theoretically stabilize the economy.

The Mechanics of Exploitation

Burgis begins by rigorously distinguishing between oppression and exploitation, a nuance often lost in broader political discourse. He leans on Wright's definition from Class Counts, noting that true exploitation requires a specific causal link: "The 'material welfare' of one group 'causally depends on the material deprivations of another.'" This is a crucial pivot. It means that simply being poor while others are rich isn't enough to constitute exploitation in the Marxist sense; there must be an active mechanism where the rich get richer because the poor are kept poor.

The author explains that this mechanism involves the exclusion of workers from the means of production. Burgis paraphrases Marx's "doubly free" formulation, describing workers as free to move and sign contracts, yet "free" from any realistic way of supporting themselves except by submitting to a capitalist. This framing is effective because it strips away the moralizing and focuses on the structural dependency. It forces the reader to confront the idea that the current economic arrangement isn't a natural state of affairs, but a designed exclusion.

"The capitalists in the comic rightly see the shmoos as an existential threat to their profits and (mostly) succeed in wiping them out."

This observation lands with particular weight because it reframes the capitalist's motivation. It isn't about greed in a vacuum; it is about the preservation of the mechanism of exploitation itself. If a creature existed that could provide food, shelter, and clothing without labor, the worker's dependence on the wage would vanish, and with it, the capitalist's leverage.

The Shmoo Parable and Class Interests

The core of Burgis's argument lies in his analysis of the "shmoo," a creature from the Li'l Abner comic strip that multiplies rapidly and provides only basic necessities. Burgis uses this absurdity to test the material interests of different classes. He outlines a scenario where shmoos provide "modestly generous" support—enough to survive, but not enough to live luxuriously. In this middle ground, the interests of workers and capitalists diverge sharply.

Burgis writes, "If the shmoo 'provides less than bare physical subsistence,' it probably benefits both groups... enabling capitalists to pay lower wages because part of the workers' needs are met outside the market." This is a chilling insight. It suggests that a partial safety net can actually reinforce the power of the employer by reducing the worker's bargaining power. The worker is less desperate, yes, but they are also less capable of demanding a living wage because their survival is partially subsidized by the "shmoo" rather than their labor value.

However, the author notes that if the shmoos were truly abundant, providing for every desire, the dynamic would shift again. But the danger zone is the middle ground. Burgis illustrates this with a preference ordering where capitalists prefer the destruction of the shmoos over any scenario where workers have access to them. This logic holds up under scrutiny: the capitalist's goal is to maximize profit, and any resource that allows a worker to opt out of the labor market, even partially, is a threat to that profit.

Critics might argue that this model oversimplifies human motivation by assuming everyone acts on "rational self-interest." Burgis acknowledges this, noting that "human beings are very often motivated by things other than rational self-interest." Yet, he defends the abstraction as a necessary tool for class analysis, much like a political map simplifies terrain to show borders. The value isn't in capturing every individual nuance, but in revealing the structural incentives that drive collective behavior.

"The workers' preference ordering makes sense once you realize that everyone getting a shmoo is at least a little bit better for workers than no one getting a shmoo... since the capitalists having to spend less on their own consumption means they have more left over to spend on hiring workers."

This point is subtle but vital. It suggests that even in a world of pure self-interest, workers have a rational stake in universal abundance. Their preference for "everyone gets a shmoo" aligns with what Burgis calls "universal human interests," echoing the Marxist concept of the "universal class." This is a powerful reframing of class solidarity: it's not just about altruism, but about the only logical outcome for a system where the alternative is mutual destruction.

From Dogpatch to Detroit

Burgis then bridges the gap between the comic strip and real-world labor history, drawing a parallel to the "Treaty of Detroit" of 1950. He details how Walter Reuther and the United Auto Workers demanded a 30 percent wage increase with no price hikes, a move that threatened the profit margins of the auto industry. Reuther's strategy was to force companies to pay for benefits that would eventually pressure the government to assume those costs.

The author highlights the irony of the outcome: while the UAW secured pensions and health insurance, they did so by making these benefits a private contract between employer and employee. "It's been 76 years since the Treaty of Detroit, and General Motors has yet to endorse Medicare for All," Burgis observes. The reason, he argues, is that the capitalist class has no incentive to band together to solve the problem of healthcare costs if the current system allows them to offload the risk onto individual workers.

Burgis cites Paul Heideman's Rogue Elephant to explain why employers don't form a unified front for public goods. "The incentives for capitalists to band together in class-conscious pursuit of their collective interests have actually been fairly weak in the United States." This is a counterintuitive but compelling point. Capitalists don't need to organize against the state when the state is already doing their work of suppressing wages and maintaining scarcity. They only organize when the labor movement threatens to break the machine.

"It could be that they're moved by at least a dim awareness that it might not be the best idea to let their workers have health-care shmoos."

This final analogy ties the entire argument together. The resistance to universal healthcare isn't just about cost; it's about the fear of the "shmoo." If healthcare becomes a right, detached from employment, the worker's dependence on the employer for survival is severed. The administration and the corporate sector may not articulate this in Marxist terms, but their actions—fighting Medicare for All, resisting universal benefits—align perfectly with the logic of the shmoo parable.

Bottom Line

Burgis's use of the shmoo parable is a masterclass in making complex theory accessible without losing its analytical bite. The strongest part of the argument is the demonstration that capitalist opposition to universal benefits is not a moral failing but a rational response to the threat of worker independence. The biggest vulnerability is the assumption that workers will always recognize this alignment of interests, a hope that history shows is often fragile. The reader should watch for how the current political battles over healthcare and wages are not just about money, but about the fundamental architecture of exploitation itself.
