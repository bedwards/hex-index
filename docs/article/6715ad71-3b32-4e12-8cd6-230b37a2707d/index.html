What the new global economic order will look like

Joeri Schasfoort · Money & Macro ·May 20, 2025 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Joeri Schasfoort cuts through the noise of headlines to argue that the coming global shift isn't just about politics, but about a fundamental change in how economic power is weaponized. He brings a rarely cited lens—geoeconomics—to explain why the transition from a US-dominated order to a multipolar one will likely be far more volatile and protectionist than optimists hope. This matters now because the rules governing your portfolio, supply chains, and inflation are about to be rewritten by a logic that standard economic models often miss.

The Mechanics of Influence

Schasfoort begins by dismantling the idea that economic strength alone dictates global outcomes. Instead, he leans on the work of Yale economist Christopher Clayton to define the true engine of power: the ability to force action. "Geoeconomics is the study about how countries use their economic strength to exert influence on foreign entities to achieve geopolitical or economic goals," Schasfoort writes. He argues that hegemons—dominant powers—succeed not just by being big, but by manipulating the "inside" and "outside" options for other nations. If a country stays inside the hegemon's system, it gets market access but must comply with political demands; if it leaves, it faces sanctions or tariffs.

This framework is crucial because it explains why aid and sanctions work differently than pure economics predicts. Schasfoort notes that while US aid to Egypt didn't make the country rich, it successfully raised the value of staying "inside" the American system, steering Egypt away from the Soviet Union. "US aid to Egypt makes total sense if we look at it through the lens of geoeconomics," he observes. Critics might note that this view reduces complex development histories to transactional bargaining chips, potentially overlooking genuine humanitarian motives or local agency. Yet, the analytical payoff is high: it reveals that foreign policy is often a calculation of costs and benefits rather than ideology.

"Geoeconomics does not tell us that the multi-polar order will be a disaster for sure. But crucially, it will depend on what type of multipolar order we end up with."

The author's most incisive point concerns the nature of threats. He argues that sanctions are often deemed ineffective because they don't always force immediate compliance, but this misses the broader signal they send. "If the hegemon does not carry out its threat against one entity today then other entities may think they will also be let off the hook in the future," Schasfoort explains. The threat itself maintains the system's integrity, even if the specific target doesn't fold. This reframes the "failure" of sanctions on Russia or Iran as a necessary cost of maintaining the credibility of the entire global order.

The Three Scenarios of a Multipolar World

With the mechanics established, Schasfoort pivots to the future, outlining three distinct paths the world could take. He dismisses the binary of "good vs. bad" globalization, suggesting instead that the structure of the new order will dictate the level of chaos. He identifies a "Russian vision" of competing spheres of influence, a "Brazilian vision" of inclusive multilateralism, and a "Cold War II" scenario of two distinct blocs.

The Russian vision, Schasfoort argues, is the most dangerous. It envisions great powers carving out exclusive spheres where they can use economic threats freely. "Great powers do not agree on the borders of these spheres of influence," he writes, pointing to conflicts in Ukraine and the South China Sea as evidence that this path leads to "economic trade and finance crashing down." This aligns with the pessimistic view of leaders like Singapore's Prime Minister, who warns that the era of rules-based globalization is over.

"Singapore is not a hegemon and it is very dependent on both global trade and finance. On the other hand, bigger nations like Brazil, Russia, India, and China are logically more optimistic as they have far larger home markets."

In contrast, the Brazilian vision of "inclusive multipolarity" relies on great powers cooperating within existing institutions. Schasfoort finds this "naive," noting that while hegemons working together is possible, it usually requires a shared existential threat or a dominant power like the US to enforce it. He assigns this scenario only a 5% probability. The most likely outcome, he posits, is a return to bipolarity: "I personally think the middle scenario, a second cold war scenario is the most likely." In this world, the US and China lead separate economic blocs, creating friction but avoiding the total chaos of the Russian vision.

The implications of this shift are stark. Schasfoort draws on economic models to predict that multipolar worlds are inherently less globalized. "A multipolar world order which is by definition dominated by multiple intermediate hegemons will have more economic threats and they will be acted upon more," he states. This means more tariffs, more sanctions, and more volatility. The "intermediate" nature of these new powers means they lack the overwhelming dominance of the post-Cold War US, forcing them to use threats more frequently to maintain influence.

The Verdict on Volatility

Schasfoort's analysis lands heavily on the side of caution. He suggests that while the US coalition has weakened—making the "inside option" less attractive and the "outside option" less painful—the result isn't a freer world, but a fractured one. The loss of coalition partners like Europe and Japan in relative economic terms has eroded the US ability to enforce rules without direct confrontation.

"These two predictions can explain why Singapore's prime minister was so pessimistic about the new global order."

The author's confidence in the "Cold War II" scenario (55% probability) over the "spheres of influence" chaos (40%) offers a glimmer of stability, but the cost is high. We are moving toward a world where economic interdependence is a vulnerability rather than a bridge. For investors and policymakers, the takeaway is that the era of predictable, rules-based trade is ending, replaced by a landscape where economic tools are constantly deployed as weapons of statecraft.

Bottom Line

Schasfoort's strongest contribution is applying the "inside/outside" option framework to explain why sanctions and aid function as political leverage rather than pure economic development tools. His biggest vulnerability lies in the uncertainty of his probability bets; the 55% chance of a Cold War II scenario assumes rational actors who will avoid the total fragmentation of the Russian vision, a gamble that history does not guarantee. Readers should watch for how the US and China manage their respective coalitions, as the cohesion of these blocs will determine whether we drift into chaos or settle into a tense, managed rivalry.
