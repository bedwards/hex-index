SBIR's Back and New PAEs

Various · Defense Tech and Acquisition ·Mar 21, 2026 ·85 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

The most striking revelation in this issue of Defense Tech and Acquisition is not the scale of the budget request, but the stark realization that the United States is fighting its first modern war against an adversary with a genuine, hidden long-range precision strike capability. The author, Various, moves beyond the usual tactical chatter to expose a fundamental mismatch: the U.S. is burning through multi-million dollar interceptors to stop cheap, expendable drones, a dynamic that threatens to bankrupt the defense industrial base before the conflict even reaches its climax.

The Legislative Lifeline and the Security Trap

The piece opens with a critical legislative development that often gets lost in the noise of daily operations: the reauthorization of the Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) and Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR) programs. After a five-month lapse, Congress has stepped in to secure funding through fiscal year 2031, but with a significant new caveat. Various writes, "If an agency denies an application for security reasons, the agency must provide the small business with the basis for such determination." This requirement for transparency is a double-edged sword; while it protects innovators from arbitrary exclusion, it forces the government to reveal its security assessment criteria, potentially giving adversaries insight into what the Pentagon fears most.

The article highlights a persistent failure in the innovation pipeline: the "Phase III" gap. These programs successfully fund early research but often lack the mechanism to transition those technologies into actual fielded systems. To fix this, the new bill establishes "strategic breakthrough allocations" allowing agencies to award up to $30 million for technologies that have already proven effective and secured matching funds. This is a necessary pivot. As Various notes, the current system suffers from "SBIR mills"—companies that receive awards year after year without ever commercializing their tech. By capping the number of proposals a single business can submit and mandating better training for contracting officers, the legislation attempts to break the cycle of perpetual research without production.

However, the timing is precarious. The bill arrives as the defense industrial base is already straining under the weight of current operations. The author points out that the federal government does not take equity or intellectual property ownership in these companies, a feature that has historically attracted high-risk innovators. Yet, with the new emphasis on evaluating "foreign ownership and financial ties," the window for global collaboration may be narrowing just as the need for diverse supply chains is widening. This tension between security and innovation is the central drama of the reauthorization.

"The U.S. led the long-range precision strike revolution, and this is the first war where we're seeing the adversary have that kind of capability. It's putting stress on the system we haven't seen before."

The Budgetary Reality of Protracted Conflict

The financial implications of the ongoing conflict in the Middle East are laid bare with startling clarity. The administration is preparing a supplemental request that could reach $200 billion, a figure that dwarfs previous estimates of $50 billion. This isn't just about paying for current strikes; it's about refilling the magazines. Various explains that the Pentagon plans to ask lawmakers for these funds to "procure many more aircraft during the FYDP, more ships, tens of thousands of critical munitions."

The sheer scale of the expenditure highlights a structural weakness in the U.S. defense posture: the lack of surge capacity. The article draws a sobering parallel to historical conflicts, noting that from the Punic Wars to World War II, "industrial capacity—not simply tactical or operational acumen—tipped the scales in a protracted war." Today, the U.S. faces a manufacturing deficit. While the European Union employs 30 million people in manufacturing and Japan and South Korea dominate shipbuilding, American manufacturing constitutes a mere 11% of the gross value of the economy.

Critics might argue that the U.S. can rely on its technological edge to offset these industrial gaps, but the article dismantles that notion. The enemy is not relying on exquisite systems; they are using "cheap, plentiful weaponry" to overwhelm expensive defenses. The text notes that "the cost of one Patriot interceptor at $4M... could supply 115 one-way drones." This cost asymmetry is the defining challenge of the era. The administration's request for $1.5 trillion in the base budget, potentially supplemented by the $200 billion for operations, signals a recognition that the era of "just-in-time" logistics is over.

The article also touches on the human element of this industrial crisis. "Roughly a quarter of the U.S. defense industrial workforce is at retirement age," the author writes, "with many employable workers choosing to leave or declining to enter it." This demographic cliff threatens to make any budget increase ineffective if there are no skilled hands to build the weapons. The solution proposed is a "Deterrence at Scale" strategy that integrates allied production. The recommendation to "map allied production capacity in those sectors and integrate it into U.S. contingency and surge planning" is a pragmatic acknowledgment that the U.S. cannot win this industrial race alone.

The Fog of Battle Damage Assessment

Perhaps the most intellectually rigorous section of the piece is its critique of how the U.S. military assesses success in Iran. Following a significant drop in Iranian drone launches, there was a temptation to declare victory. The article warns against this, drawing a sharp lesson from the 1991 Gulf War where commanders "mistook reduced enemy activity for the physical destruction of enemy capacity."

Various breaks down the three levels of battle damage assessment: physical, functional, and target system. The current data only shows a reduction in activity, not a destruction of capability. "Tehran could be using this time to learn, adapt, and refine its strategy and tactics," the author suggests. Or, more dangerously, "Iran's lower daily launch rates could reflect deliberate stockpiling for larger coordinated attacks later."

This analysis is crucial because it challenges the narrative of air superiority. The article details how the U.S. has shifted from "stand-off" strikes to "stand-in" strikes only after establishing dominance, yet the threat remains. The MQ-9 Reaper drones, while effective in reaching targets with Small Diameter Bombs, have suffered losses, with "about a dozen MQ-9s... lost in the air or on the ground." This loss rate is a stark indicator that the adversary's air defenses are far from neutralized.

The piece also highlights the strategic depth of the Iranian threat. "Tehran is still hitting valuable military installations and energy infrastructure across the Mideast daily," the author notes, part of a strategy to "raise oil prices to economically punishing levels." This is not a tactical skirmish; it is a strategic campaign designed to bleed the U.S. economy and its allies. The administration's focus on "drone dominance" and "big investments" is a direct response to this reality, but the article implies that technology alone cannot solve a problem rooted in industrial asymmetry and strategic patience.

"History is replete with examples of when industrial capacity—not simply tactical or operational acumen—tipped the scales in a protracted war."

Bottom Line

The strongest argument in this piece is its unflinching diagnosis of the cost-asymmetry trap: the U.S. is attempting to win a war of attrition with a system designed for precision, a mismatch that threatens to exhaust the defense industrial base regardless of budget size. The article's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on allied integration as a solution; while logically sound, the political and regulatory friction of merging supply chains across borders remains a formidable, unquantified hurdle. Readers should watch closely for the details of the $200 billion supplemental request, as it will reveal whether the administration is truly prepared to fund a long war of attrition or if it is merely reacting to the immediate crisis.
