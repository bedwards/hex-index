SBIR's Back and New PAEs

Various · Defense Tech and Acquisition ·Mar 21, 2026 · 85 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In a landscape often dominated by abstract strategy, this edition of Defense Tech and Acquisition delivers a jarring, necessary reality check: the United States is fighting a war of industrial attrition against an adversary that has spent decades preparing for exactly this scenario. The author, writing as Various, does not merely report on budget numbers or legislative wins; they expose a terrifying asymmetry where American precision is being exhausted by Iranian volume, forcing a fundamental rethink of how the Pentagon buys, builds, and fights.

The Legislative Lifeline and the Security Paradox

The piece opens with a critical legislative development that often gets lost in the noise of daily headlines. Various writes, "The House on Tuesday passed a bill to reauthorize the SBIR and STTR programs for five years, following the Senate's passage of the legislation two weeks ago." This Small Business Innovation and Economic Security Act is more than a bureaucratic renewal; it is a direct response to the need for agility in a contested environment. The author highlights a crucial shift: the bill now mandates that agencies evaluate the security risks of small businesses applying for awards, a move designed to close loopholes that could allow foreign influence to seep into the defense supply chain.

This is a smart, if painful, evolution. The author notes that the bill "establishes strategic breakthrough allocations for critical technology areas," allowing for awards up to $30 million to push promising tech toward commercialization. This directly addresses a long-standing failure in the system. As Various points out, the current model suffers because "while designed to steer companies toward a commercial or government buyer in Phase III they don't award funding in this final stage." By injecting capital specifically for Phase III, the legislation attempts to bridge the "valley of death" that kills too many promising startups before they can scale.

However, the security provisions introduce a new friction. The requirement to deny applications based on security concerns and provide the "basis for such determination" is a double-edged sword. It protects the ecosystem but could slow down the very speed the military desperately needs. Critics might argue that adding layers of due diligence contradicts the "ASAP" mandate the article itself champions. Yet, the author makes a compelling case that speed without security is a liability, especially when the text warns that "China, Russia, Iran, North Korea, and ransomware groups will continue to pose critical threats to U.S. networks."

The federal government does not take equity or IP ownership in companies funded through SBIR or STTR, yet now demands a deeper look into who owns the companies themselves.

The Budgetary Gamble and the Bomber Backbone

The commentary then pivots to the financial reality of the conflict, presenting a stark picture of the costs involved. Various writes, "The Pentagon plans to ask lawmakers for some $200B in supplemental funds to pay for operations in Iran and refill American munition stockpiles." This figure is staggering, especially when contrasted with earlier reports suggesting a request of only $50 billion. The author suggests this isn't just about paying for the current fight but about rebuilding a depleted industrial base.

The narrative connects this spending directly to the performance of the bomber fleet. Gen Dan Caine is quoted describing the bombers as "the backbone of America's long-range strike capability, and their contributions to Epic Fury have been decisive." The author details how the B-2 stealth bombers conducted penetrating strikes while B-1 and B-52s shifted from "stand-off" to "stand-in" strikes once air superiority was established. This tactical evolution is significant. It shows a military adapting in real-time, moving from safe distances to closer, more cost-effective engagements.

Yet, the author is careful not to paint this as a total victory. The text notes that the "budget's going to procure many more aircraft during the FYDP, more ships, tens of thousands of critical munitions," but questions whether the $1.5 trillion request will be enough. The underlying argument is that the U.S. is trying to buy back the industrial capacity it lost over decades. The author writes, "Manufacturing represents 16.4% of gross value across the EU and ~ 21% in Japan, whereas it constitutes a mere 11% in the United States." This statistic serves as a sobering reminder that no amount of software or strategy can replace the physical need to build things, a lesson echoed in the deep dive on PaR Systems, where the historical struggle to scale production lines has repeatedly hampered U.S. readiness.

The Asymmetry of Cost and the Illusion of Victory

Perhaps the most chilling section of the piece is the analysis of the cost exchange ratio. The author presents a brutal equation: "The cost of one Patriot interceptor at $4M…could supply 115 one-way drones." This disparity is the central challenge of the modern battlefield. Various writes, "The U.S. led the long-range precision strike revolution, and this is the first war where we're seeing the adversary have that kind of capability. It's putting stress on the system we haven't seen before." The reliance on expensive, exquisite systems to counter cheap, disposable threats is a strategic vulnerability that the article exposes with clarity.

The author challenges the conventional wisdom that a drop in enemy fire equates to a drop in enemy capability. Referencing the Gulf War, the text warns, "After the Gulf War's air campaign concluded in February 1991, U.S. commanders were confident that airpower had destroyed the bulk of Iraq's Republican Guard... The reality was that commanders had mistaken reduced enemy activity for the physical destruction of enemy capacity." This historical parallel is vital. It suggests that the current 83% drop in Iranian drone launches might be a tactical pause or a stockpiling maneuver rather than a defeat.

The piece argues that "Tehran could be using this time to learn, adapt, and refine its strategy and tactics given Russia's assistance." This is a nuanced take that avoids the trap of overconfidence. It acknowledges that Iran's strategy is to "raise oil prices to economically punishing levels" and that a lower launch rate might be sufficient to maintain coercive pressure. The author's use of the term "protraction" is key here, framing the conflict not as a series of battles but as a long war of industrial endurance. This connects to the deep dive on Qusecure, where the focus on securing the supply chain against such long-term, adaptive threats is critical.

The U.S. faces a country with force projection capability for the first time in many decades. That capability is also well hidden.

The author also highlights the human cost of this industrial mismatch. With "roughly a quarter of the U.S. defense industrial workforce at retirement age," the capacity to scale production is physically constrained. The text notes that "manufacturers struggle to replace departing technicians with workers who possess comparable skills and experiences." This is a structural weakness that money alone cannot fix immediately. It suggests that even with the $200 billion supplemental request, the U.S. may struggle to ramp up production fast enough to match the pace of consumption in a prolonged conflict.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its refusal to treat the conflict as a series of tactical victories, instead framing it as a test of industrial resilience where the U.S. is currently outmatched in volume. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that the political will exists to sustain a $200 billion supplemental request and a decade-long industrial rebuild. Readers should watch for how the Pentagon balances the immediate need to refill stockpiles with the long-term necessity of diversifying its supply chain, a challenge that goes far beyond the SBIR reauthorization.

SBIR Reauthorization Bill

Small Business Innovation and Economic Security Act

BLUF: This bill reauthorizes through FY31 and modifies the SBIR / STTR program, and related pilot programs.

  • Specifically, the bill expands the requirements for federal agencies administering these programs to evaluate the security risks of the small businesses that apply for awards under the programs. If an agency denies an application for security reasons, the agency must provide the small business with the basis for such determination.

  • Each agency must set a maximum number of proposals per fiscal year that a small business concern may submit in response to Phase I and Phase II solicitations.

  • The bill expands the training requirements for agencies’ contracting officers and acquisition workforce with respect to Phase III (commercialization of technologies) awards under the SBIR and STTR programs.

  • The bill also establishes strategic breakthrough allocations for critical technology areas. These are additional Phase II SBIR and STTR awards to small businesses that demonstrate an effective technology and receive matching funds.

House Passes SBIR/STTR Reauthorization Bill

The House on Tuesday passed a bill to reauthorize the SBIR and STTR programs for five years, following the Senate’s passage of the legislation two weeks ago and months of bipartisan, bicameral negotiations.

  • The programs support 4,000 companies annually at roughly $4B per year. The federal government does not take equity or IP ownership in companies funded through SBIR or STTR.
