Clinic-in-the-loop

Ruxandra Teslo · ·Dec 29, 2025 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Ruxandra Teslo challenges a pervasive myth in modern medicine: that clinical trials are merely a bureaucratic hurdle to clear before a drug can be sold. Instead, she argues they are the very engine of discovery, a claim that could reshape how we fund, regulate, and understand the future of life-saving therapies.

The Broken Funnel

Teslo begins by confronting a frustrating reality known as Eroom's Law, where the cost of bringing a new drug to market doubles roughly every nine years despite scientific breakthroughs. She writes, "With the goal of upending Eroom's law, I have spent the last year studying the structural bottlenecks that shape how new medicines are tested and the FDA's role in such decisions." This framing is crucial because it shifts the blame from a lack of scientific genius to a broken process. The prevailing "funnel metaphor" suggests a linear path where basic science yields a perfect drug, which is then merely validated by the clinic. Teslo dismantles this, arguing that this view "obscures the clinic's role as an active engine of discovery."

The author's critique of the status quo is sharp. She notes that critics of her "Clinical Trial Abundance" project fear that faster trials are like running a "casino," risking patient safety for speed. Teslo counters this by reframing failure not as a waste, but as data. "When a trial fails, it provides a unique physiological stress test that reveals exactly where a drug's design fell short," she explains. This is a powerful pivot. It suggests that the current system, which often discards negative results or treats them as dead ends, is actually throwing away the most valuable information we have. Critics might argue that loosening regulatory guardrails invites danger, but Teslo's point is about efficiency of learning, not recklessness. The goal is to learn faster, not to skip steps.

The clinic is not an endpoint of discovery but a central component of it.

The CAR-T Case Study

To prove her point, Teslo turns to the history of CAR-T cell therapy, a field that perfectly illustrates the "clinic-in-the-loop" concept. She details how early versions of these therapies, which genetically reprogrammed a patient's own immune cells to fight cancer, failed miserably in the first human trials. The initial designs looked perfect on paper but vanished from patients' bodies too quickly to work. "Progress in understanding why early CAR-T therapies did not live up to their promise came from treating first-in-human trials not simply as therapeutic attempts, but as opportunities to learn," Teslo writes.

This section is where the argument gains its strongest empirical weight. Teslo describes how researchers used tools like quantitative PCR to track the cells, discovering that while the cells entered the body, they lacked a "second signal" to stay alive and active. This insight led to the addition of costimulatory domains, transforming the therapy. She highlights the work of Carl June and his team, noting that the breakthrough came from a "first-in-human study" that was small, expensive, and deeply informative. The result was a therapy that now cures children with blood cancers who previously had months to live. "It was unexpected," the researchers reported, "that the very low dose of chimeric antigen receptor T cells that we infused would result in a clinically evident antitumor response."

This narrative arc demonstrates that the "casino" critique is fundamentally flawed. As Teslo puts it, "It assumes that experimentation simply reveals a fixed probability of success. But trials can change those probabilities." The data from the clinic didn't just validate a theory; it corrected the theory, creating a feedback loop that made the eventual success possible. Without those early, messy, "failed" trials, the life-saving Kymriah therapy would never have existed.

The Bureaucratic Bottleneck

If the logic is sound, why isn't everyone doing this? Teslo identifies the culprit: a regulatory and manufacturing environment that treats early, small-scale academic trials with the same heavy-handed requirements as massive commercial rollouts. She points out that researchers are often forced to use full "good manufacturing practice" (GMP) facilities for studies with only a handful of patients, driving costs into the millions. "Many researchers argue that such custom, tightly monitored studies do not need full commercial-scale GMP," she notes, citing Australia's more flexible regulatory approach as a successful alternative.

The author's frustration with the current institutional inertia is palpable. She argues that the "design-build-test loop" is broken not because of science, but because of policy. "If we want biomedical progress to accelerate, we should stop treating clinical trials as an afterthought or as a binary 'yes/no' part of drug development," she urges. The implication is clear: the executive branch and agencies like the FDA need to distinguish between a pilot study designed to learn and a commercial launch designed to sell. The current one-size-fits-all approach is stifling the very innovation it claims to protect.

Bottom Line

Teslo's argument is a compelling call to reorient the entire biomedical ecosystem, moving from a culture of fear and validation to one of rapid, data-driven iteration. The strongest part of her case is the historical evidence from CAR-T therapy, which proves that "failure" is often just a necessary step in the design process. However, the biggest vulnerability remains political: convincing regulators and institutional review boards to accept higher risks in the name of faster learning will require a cultural shift that is notoriously difficult to engineer. The next critical test will be whether the Clinical Trial Abundance project can translate this intellectual framework into concrete policy changes that lower barriers for investigator-initiated studies without compromising patient safety.

Clinic-in-the-loop

by Ruxandra Teslo · · Read full article

For the last several years, I have been trying to understand why biomedical progress, especially in therapeutics, has become less productive despite staggering advances in basic science.

I am not the only person vexed by this. In 2012, biotechnologist Jack Scannell formally described the dwindling returns on therapeutic investments, coining the term Eroom’s Law (Moore’s Law in reverse). Eroom’s law states that the inflation-adjusted cost to bring a new drug to market roughly doubles every nine years: a trend that has held since the 1950s. With the goal of upending Eroom’s law, I have spent the last year studying the structural bottlenecks that shape how new medicines are tested and the FDA’s role in such decisions.

Much of my time has been focused on clinical trials, which, despite their central role in the creation of pharmaceuticals, receive remarkably little systematic attention. This led me to launch the Clinical Trial Abundance Project, a framework aimed at increasing not only the number of clinical trials, but also their speed and how much we learn from them. Recently, I co-authored an essay with Scannell, arguing that making trials more efficient and informative is essential to breaking Eroom’s Law.

Critics of our essay, however, argued that making clinical trials more efficient risks treating biotechnology like a casino. In their view, making it easier to run clinical trials would risk allowing more potentially harmful drugs to be tested in patients and, instead, biotechnologists should focus on making better drugs that are more likely to gain approval. These critics see Clinical Trial Abundance as accepting the status quo of drug development rather than challenging it.

But this is a misunderstanding.

In fact, Clinical Trial Abundance and better hypotheses for drugs are not merely compatible, but self-reinforcing. Faster testing in the clinic creates a feedback loop: ideas become trials, trials generate rich data (including both successes and failures), these data improve models, and better models inform the next generation of ideas. In this view, the clinic is not an endpoint of discovery but a central component of it.

To understand why clinical abundance is important, we must step outside the prevailing view of clinical testing as a mere “validation step” for scientific ideas. The familiar funnel metaphor of drug discovery, depicting a linear progression from basic science to regulatory approval, reinforces the flawed notion of clinical testing as a passive filter designed to screen pre-existing ideas.
