A Visual Guide to Attention Variants in Modern LLMs

Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Sebastian Raschka does not merely catalog the evolution of Large Language Model architectures; he exposes the hidden economic engine driving their current design: the desperate, mathematical battle against memory bottlenecks. While many observers focus on parameter counts or benchmark scores, Raschka argues that the true differentiator in modern AI is how efficiently a model manages its "KV cache" during inference. This is not a theoretical exercise; it is the practical constraint that dictates whether a model can run on consumer hardware or requires a data center the size of a warehouse.

The Original Bottleneck

Raschka begins by dismantling the assumption that attention is a new invention, grounding the discussion in the limitations of older Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). He writes, "Attention predates transformers and MHA. Its immediate background is encoder-decoder RNNs for translation." He explains that these earlier systems suffered from a critical flaw: they had to compress an entire sentence into a single, finite hidden state before generating a translation. "The hidden state can't store infinitely much information or context," Raschka notes, "and sometimes it would be useful to just refer back to the full input sequence."

This historical context is vital. It reminds us that the 2017 "Attention Is All You Need" paper was not just an architectural tweak but a fundamental shift from sequential processing to parallel access. Raschka illustrates how the decoder in an RNN was forced to guess the next word based on a compressed summary, whereas the transformer allows the model to "revisit the full input sequence." This distinction is the bedrock of modern capabilities. Without this shift, the long-context reasoning we expect today would be impossible.

"Attention breaks the RNN bottleneck by letting the current output position revisit the full input sequence instead of relying on one compressed state alone."

However, this freedom came with a price. As Raschka points out, the mechanism that solved the RNN problem created a new one: the attention matrix grows quadratically with sequence length. "For a sequence of T tokens, attention needs one row of weights per token, so overall we get a T x T matrix." This mathematical reality means that as models get smarter and context windows get longer, the memory required to store the "keys" and "values" for every token explodes.

The Efficiency Wars: GQA vs. MLA

The core of Raschka's argument lies in the trade-off between modeling quality and memory efficiency. He identifies Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) as the current industry standard for balancing these needs. "In GQA, we keep a larger set of query heads, but we reduce the number of key-value heads and let multiple queries share them," he explains. This approach, popularized in 2023, allows models like Llama 3 and Mistral to run faster and cheaper without a catastrophic drop in performance.

Raschka frames GQA not as a perfect solution, but as a pragmatic "sweet spot." He writes, "That lowered both parameter count and KV-cache traffic without making drastic implementation changes." This is a crucial insight for practitioners: the best architecture is often the one that is robust and easy to implement, not just the one with the highest theoretical ceiling. Critics might note that this pragmatism could stifle innovation, but Raschka counters that GQA remains "robust, easier to implement, and also easier to train" than its more complex rivals.

Yet, as context windows expand further, even GQA hits a wall. Enter Multi-Head Latent Attention (MLA), the technique championed by DeepSeek. Raschka distinguishes this sharply from GQA: "MLA shrinks the cache by compressing what gets stored rather than by reducing how many K/Vs are stored by sharing heads." Instead of sharing heads, MLA stores a compressed "latent representation" of the keys and values.

"MLA is a preferable attention mechanism for DeepSeek not just because it was efficient, but because it looked like a quality-preserving efficiency move at large scale."

The evidence Raschka presents suggests that while GQA is the safe bet for models under 100 billion parameters, MLA becomes the superior choice at massive scales. He cites ablation studies where "GQA looked worse than MHA in terms of modeling performance, while MLA held up much better." This is a nuanced take: efficiency is not a zero-sum game with intelligence. In fact, at the right scale, the compression of MLA may actually help the model focus on the most relevant signals, discarding noise that a full-resolution cache might retain.

The Practical Reality

Raschka's commentary is grounded in the reality of deployment. He notes that while MLA offers better theoretical efficiency, it is "more complicated to implement than GQA, more complicated to serve." This complexity is a significant barrier. He observes that "some of the newer releases still stay deliberately classic here," citing models like MiniMax M2.5 that stick to GQA because the engineering overhead of MLA is too high for their current needs.

This creates a bifurcated landscape. On one side, you have the "classic" architectures using GQA, which are easier to tune and deploy. On the other, the cutting-edge, massive models using MLA, which demand more sophisticated infrastructure. Raschka writes, "For smaller models, let's say <100B, GQA seems to work better, or, is at least easier to tune and get right." This is a vital warning for developers: chasing the most efficient architecture on paper does not guarantee the best results in practice if the implementation complexity introduces bugs or training instability.

"GQA remains appealing because it is robust, easier to implement, and also easier to train."

The author's visual guide serves as more than a reference; it is a map of these strategic choices. By organizing 45 different architectures, he reveals that the "best" model is entirely dependent on the constraints of the user. Is the goal maximum context length? Then MLA is the answer. Is the goal rapid deployment on limited hardware? GQA is the king.

Bottom Line

Raschka's strongest contribution is reframing the narrative of LLM progress from "bigger is better" to "smarter compression is better." He successfully argues that the future of AI depends less on raw parameter counts and more on how elegantly we can manage the memory required to process them. The article's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that current hardware constraints will remain the primary driver of architecture, potentially underestimating how quickly new memory technologies could render these trade-offs obsolete. For now, however, the choice between GQA and MLA is the defining strategic decision for any serious AI builder.

1. Multi-Head Attention (MHA)

Self-attention lets each token look at the other visible tokens in the sequence, assign them weights, and use those weights to build a new context-aware representation of the input.

Multi-head attention (MHA) is the standard transformer version of that idea. It runs several self-attention heads in parallel with different learned projections, then combines their outputs into one richer representation.

The sections below start with a whirlwind tour of explaining self-attention to explain MHA. It’s more meant as a quick overview to set the stage for related attention concepts like grouped-query attention, sliding window attention, and so on.
