Kings and Generals delivers a sobering autopsy of the Eastern Roman Army, arguing that its collapse was not a sudden military defeat but a slow, systemic rot driven by economic bankruptcy and a failure to adapt to the gunpowder age. The piece is notable for its granular dissection of the pronia system, revealing how a once-mighty provincial force was replaced by a patchwork of absentee landlords and foreign mercenaries who could not hold the line against the Ottoman tide.

The Shift from Themes to Pronia

The narrative begins by contrasting the robust provincial themes of the middle era with the fractured reality of the late empire. Kings and Generals writes, "despite the reforms that were enacted as early as during the reign of Alexios the first... the Byzantine army would never be able to reclaim its past glory." This sets a tragic tone immediately, framing the subsequent military history as a long, slow decline rather than a series of isolated battles. The author pivots to the pronia system, the fiscal engine of this new army, explaining that "fiscal rights were bestowed to an individual by the emperor and they were generally claims on taxes burdening the peasants and immovable property such as mills, mines and docks."

This distinction is crucial. Unlike Western feudalism, where land ownership transferred, the Byzantine state retained title, granting only revenue rights. Kings and Generals notes that "the pronia holders characterized the military of their age... yet unlike the thematic soldiers their employer was the central government not the local administration." This centralization was a double-edged sword; while it theoretically strengthened imperial control, it created a class of "absentee landlord[s]" who were reservists rather than full-time professionals. The author effectively highlights the fragility of this model: "the wealthiest were also accompanied by small retinues... while the norm was probably a mere handful." A counterargument worth considering is whether the pronia system was a failure of design or simply a desperate adaptation to a treasury that could no longer pay soldiers in cash. The author leans toward the latter, suggesting the system was a necessary evil that ultimately failed to produce a cohesive fighting force.

The Fragmented Guard and the Rise of Mercenaries

As the empire shrank, so did the regular army, replaced by a hodgepodge of specialized units and foreign hires. Kings and Generals details the survival of the Varangian Guard and the integration of the Vithariote, noting that "although viewed as foreigners in their early days they were gradually hellenized until they stopped being considered mercenaries and became citizens of the empire." This cultural assimilation is a fascinating microcosm of the empire's resilience, yet it could not compensate for the lack of numbers. The author points out that the organization of forces became "entirely ad hoc," with units like the allagia varying wildly in size and composition. The reliance on foreign contingents, such as the Catalan guard mentioned in the work of Theodore Paleologus, underscores a desperate reliance on external power to fill internal voids.

The commentary on the infantry is particularly damning. Kings and Generals asserts that "the infantry branch constituted the achilles heel of the late Byzantine armies." The author explains that spearmen, or contorati, lacked the archer support that had been standard in previous centuries, forcing them into vulnerable, four-rank formations. Furthermore, the archers themselves had degraded from disciplined professionals to "unarmored archers" who were often just peasants called up for duty. This erosion of professional standards is a recurring theme; the army was no longer a machine but a collection of disparate parts held together by loyalty to the emperor rather than training or doctrine.

The Gunpowder Trap

The most striking section of the commentary addresses the technological gap that sealed the empire's fate. Kings and Generals writes, "despite the fact that the byzantines knew of the existence of firearms by 1392 at the latest the virtually non-existent economic sources of the empire meant that it was impossible for them to participate in this late medieval arms race." This economic reality is the silent killer of the Byzantine military. The author illustrates this with the tragic story of the Hungarian engineer Orban, who offered his services to the Byzantines but "could not afford his high salary demands nor could he supply the materials necessary for the gun's construction." Instead, Orban built the massive cannon for the Ottomans that would eventually breach the walls of Constantinople.

The irony is palpable: the empire's poverty prevented it from mastering the very technology that would destroy it. Kings and Generals notes that "the only known record of a cannon founder in the area of constantinople is that of the hungarian orban who had offered his services to the byzantines in 1452." The author further details the chaotic deployment of the few cannons the Byzantines did possess, citing incidents where internal friction and a lack of expertise led to catastrophic failures, such as a cannon exploding and killing its own crew. Critics might argue that the Byzantines were simply outnumbered and outmatched regardless of technology, but the author's focus on the inability to manufacture and effectively use artillery suggests a deeper systemic collapse that went beyond mere numbers.

The virtually non-existent economic sources of the empire meant that it was impossible for them to participate in this late medieval arms race.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals succeeds in portraying the late Byzantine army not as a heroic last stand, but as a shadow of its former self, crippled by a fiscal system that could not sustain a professional force and a technological stagnation born of poverty. The strongest part of the argument is the clear link between the pronia system's limitations and the eventual inability to field a cohesive defense against the Ottomans. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the slight tendency to treat the pronia holders as uniformly ineffective, overlooking the moments where these local levies did manage to mount surprisingly resilient defenses. Readers should watch for how this economic-military nexus played out in other declining empires, where the cost of modernization often outpaced the state's ability to pay for it.
