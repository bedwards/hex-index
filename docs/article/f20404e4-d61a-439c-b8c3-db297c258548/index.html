Perhaps people are cynical about success in the creative arts for a reason

Freddie deBoer · ·Jan 16, 2026 ·14 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Freddie deBoer confronts a silence that defines modern publishing: the unspoken machinery behind sudden literary fame. While most critics debate whether a new novel is good, deBoer asks a more uncomfortable question—how did it get noticed at all? His argument cuts through the usual meritocratic myths to expose a system where access, not just artistry, dictates who becomes a star.

The Illusion of Organic Success

The piece centers on the explosive media rollout for Madeline Cash's debut novel, Lost Lambs. deBoer observes that the simultaneous praise across major legacy outlets is statistically improbable for a work of fiction. "This level of immediate, simultaneous media penetration does not happen with literary fiction without people in positions of power making it happen," he writes. This is not a claim about the book's quality, but about the mechanics of its promotion. The author notes that coverage of novels in legacy media is "almost dead," making this sudden blitz an anomaly that demands explanation.

The argument gains traction when deBoer contrasts this with the trajectory of Vincenzo Latronico's Perfection, a translated novel that succeeded through a "slow-rolling snowball" of word-of-mouth rather than a coordinated press assault. "That is the kind of publicity for a book by an ambitious young novelist that feels organic to me," deBoer notes. By juxtaposing these two paths, he highlights a structural shift in how cultural capital is distributed. Critics might argue that high-profile launches are simply the result of aggressive marketing, a standard industry practice. However, deBoer suggests the scale here points to something deeper: a system where "access rises" while the myth of the meritocracy fades.

Nobody just falls into this coverage. That doesn't happen. Someone with connections and influence sweated their way to ensure that this rollout happened.

The Mechanics of the Machine

deBoer identifies the specific ingredients of this phenomenon: billionaire lineage, powerful marketing firms, and the reciprocal nature of favors among elites. He points out that the verbiage in the coverage seems "implausibly similar," suggesting a coordinated narrative rather than independent critical consensus. The author writes, "The book might be a masterpiece, and its readers might adore it, and also by the way, this kind of conveniently-coordinated media blitz is proof of Astroturf, sorry, that's just true." Here, he invokes the concept of astroturfing—manufactured grassroots support—to describe a literary launch that feels engineered from the top down. This parallels historical concerns about "manufacturing consent," where media narratives are shaped to serve specific interests rather than reflect organic public sentiment.

He further illustrates this dynamic by referencing the coverage of Laura Delano, whose family name and wealth seemingly guarantee attention for her views on mental health, while social workers laboring in the same field remain invisible. "Her last name is, yes, the same Delano found in 'Franklin Delano Roosevelt,'" deBoer observes, noting that she tells a story "the Boerum Hill brownstone liberals who keep the New York Times in business want to hear." This comparison underscores his broader thesis: media coverage is often a transfer mechanism from social capital to cultural capital, laundered through rhetoric of diversity and authenticity.

The High DM Ratio and the Fear of Truth

Perhaps the most revealing part of deBoer's commentary is his admission that this argument will likely be met with public hostility but private agreement. He describes the "DM ratio"—the phenomenon where people agree with a controversial take in private messages but disavow it publicly. "This post is a classic high DM ratio piece," he writes. "Book world people and media professionals will have very little incentive to agree with it publicly, but they will tell me privately, because what I'm observing is real." This insight exposes the fragility of the industry's self-image. The fear is not that the system is flawed, but that admitting it is flawed would undermine the legitimacy of everyone who has succeeded within it.

He acknowledges his own position, noting that while he has achieved success, it pales in comparison to the "massive rollout" afforded to Cash. "I am of course jealous of someone like Cash, who has been allowed to cut the line," he admits. Yet, he insists that jealousy does not invalidate the observation. The core issue is the "political economy" of the arts, where "networking, patronage, nepotism, and plain old rank corruption play such an outsized role." This reframing shifts the blame from individual talent to institutional design, suggesting that the system is rigged not by accident, but by design.

Never before have networking, patronage, nepotism, and plain old rank corruption played such an outsized role in determining who gets seen, published, optioned, streamed, featured, or taken seriously at all.

Bottom Line

deBoer's most compelling contribution is his refusal to separate the art from the apparatus, forcing readers to confront the reality that "access rises" in an era where merit is increasingly secondary. The argument's greatest vulnerability is its potential to dismiss genuine talent as merely a product of privilege, a risk deBoer carefully navigates by insisting the book itself may still be excellent. Ultimately, the piece serves as a necessary corrective to the industry's self-deception, urging a honesty that could only lead to a fairer, if more cynical, cultural landscape.
