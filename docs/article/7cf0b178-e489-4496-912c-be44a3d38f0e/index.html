The dsa candidate who won down South

Hamilton Nolan · ·Nov 15, 2025 ·15 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In a political landscape often defined by national polarization, Hamilton Nolan uncovers a surprising counter-narrative: the rise of democratic socialism in the American South is not a theoretical exercise, but a tangible victory rooted in labor solidarity and hyper-local issues. While the national conversation fixates on federal gridlock, Nolan's reporting on Kelsea Bond's City Council win in Atlanta reveals that the most effective path for progressive power may lie in bypassing partisan labels to focus on the concrete mechanics of displacement and transit.

The Power of the Picket Line

Nolan frames Bond's entry into politics not as an ideological leap, but as a direct response to the tangible power of organized labor. The author highlights a pivotal moment in 2021 when Bond witnessed Nabisco workers in a Georgia suburb successfully win a contract through a strike. "That was really powerful for me, and solidified my understanding of how crucial labor organizing is if we want to win anything that we want in this country," Bond tells Nolan. This anecdote is crucial because it grounds the abstract concept of "socialism" in the immediate reality of the workplace, a strategy that resonates deeply in a region where union density has historically been suppressed.

The piece effectively argues that Bond's victory was built on a foundation of mutual aid between the candidate and the labor movement, rather than top-down party machinery. Nolan notes that Bond managed the campaign of Gabriel Sanchez, who became the second DSA member elected to the Georgia statehouse, creating a rare pipeline of socialist power in the Deep South. This connection is significant; it suggests that the Democratic Socialists of America (DSA) are no longer just a protest movement but a viable electoral force when anchored in local labor struggles.

"We had Starbucks stores organizing in Midtown, and those Council members never came out to a single picket line or sip-in, or gave meaningful support in any way."

This quote underscores a sharp critique of the incumbent political class in Atlanta. Nolan uses Bond's voice to expose a disconnect between the city's leadership and its working class, particularly regarding the massive union drives at the world's busiest airport. The argument here is that the administration's silence on Delta and Teamsters organizing is not an oversight, but a calculated response to corporate influence. Critics might note that city councils often have limited jurisdiction over state-level labor laws, but Nolan's framing suggests that moral leadership and vocal support are necessary precursors to legislative change.

Beyond the Label: Housing and Transit

The coverage shifts to the specific policy battles that defined Bond's campaign, moving away from ideological purity tests to the gritty details of urban planning. Nolan details how Bond identified the affordable housing crisis and the stalled transit expansion as the true litmus tests for voters. The author points out a critical failure in Atlanta's development model: "Developers systematically opting out of building deeply affordable units because there's this loophole that they can just pay a small fine, and it goes into this trust fund, and the trust fund itself has not really been spent the way that it's supposed to."

Nolan weaves in the historical context of the Atlanta Beltline, noting that the original vision included light rail as a transit corridor, not just a pedestrian trail. The piece highlights how that vision was sacrificed to gentrification, turning a potential public good into a "super expensive" enclave for the upper middle class. "Over the past decade it's just really acted as a gentrification engine," Bond explains. This reframing of the Beltline from a success story to a cautionary tale is a masterstroke of local political analysis, connecting the dots between infrastructure spending and the displacement of long-time residents.

The argument that transit is the number one priority because it "affects so many other things" is compelling. It suggests that without mobility, other progressive goals—access to jobs, healthcare, and education—remain out of reach for the working class. Nolan's reporting suggests that Bond's success came from translating the broad label of "democratic socialist" into the specific, universally desired goal of a functional light rail system.

The Myth of the Red-Baiting

One of the most striking elements of Nolan's piece is the debunking of the narrative that socialist candidates are doomed by red-baiting in the current political climate. While the national media often focuses on the fear-mongering surrounding candidates like Zohran Mamdani in New York, Nolan reports that Bond faced little resistance on her ideological identity in Atlanta. "Weirdly, no," Bond says when asked about red-baiting. "I think we're really lucky that democratic socialism, since Bernie's first run, has become very normalized in many ways."

Nolan attributes this shift to the normalization of progressive ideas through national figures and the specific demographics of Bond's district. The author notes that the opponent, a multimillionaire's wife, actually polled on the issue and found that "democratic socialism is popular and did not harm our chances at all." This finding challenges the conventional wisdom that the South is an impossible terrain for the left. Instead, Nolan suggests that the barrier is not ideology, but viability and the ability to articulate a clear policy vision.

"I think if I had just run saying 'I'm a socialist, I'm a socialist,' people would have said, 'and so what? What are you going to do?'"

This insight, attributed to Bond by Nolan, is perhaps the most actionable lesson for progressives everywhere. It argues that ideology must be secondary to the delivery of tangible results. The piece implies that the "Boogeyman effect" of the DSA is fading as local chapters become visible in protests and community organizing, demystifying the organization for the average voter.

The National Implications

Finally, Nolan connects Bond's local victory to the broader national mood, specifically the frustration with the Democratic Party's inability to stand up to the executive branch. The author notes that the campaign launched the week of the inauguration, channeling the anxiety of the moment into local action. "If we have ever had a political opening for socialists, I think that moment is now," Bond asserts. Nolan presents this not as a call for a third party, but as a strategy of independent action within a nonpartisan municipal system, allowing candidates to avoid the constraints of the two-party duopoly.

The piece concludes with a look at the future, where Bond hopes for a unified progressive and labor left to draft a candidate for the next presidential election. Nolan's framing suggests that the path forward is not to wait for the national party to change, but to build power from the ground up, starting with city councils and transit authorities. The argument is that these local wins create the momentum necessary to challenge the status quo at higher levels.

"People will be like, 'You've brought me hope! I haven't felt hope in so long.' I think these wins will create the momentum for more political wins in the future, as long as we stick with the strategy."

Critics might argue that relying on nonpartisan municipal races limits the ability to enact state-wide or federal policy changes, as Bond herself admits regarding the lack of collective bargaining rights in Georgia. However, Nolan's reporting suggests that these local victories are the necessary first step in building the coalition required to eventually shift those larger levers of power.

Bottom Line

Hamilton Nolan's coverage of Kelsea Bond's victory offers a vital corrective to the narrative that the American South is impervious to progressive change, proving that the key lies in anchoring socialist principles to the immediate, material needs of labor and housing. The piece's greatest strength is its focus on the mechanics of winning—choosing the right district, prioritizing transit over ideology, and leveraging labor solidarity—rather than getting lost in abstract debates. The biggest vulnerability remains the structural limitations of city government in a right-to-work state, but the article makes a convincing case that these local footholds are the essential foundation for any future national resurgence. Readers should watch how Bond navigates the tension between her progressive platform and the entrenched corporate interests that dominate Atlanta's development landscape.

The dsa candidate who won down South

by Hamilton Nolan · · Read full article

Zohran Mamdani was not the only Democratic Socialist who just won a citywide election. In Atlanta, 31 year-old DSA member Kelsea Bond (who uses they/ them pronouns) handily won a race for City Council. That makes them the only socialist to win an election this year in the South, and just the second DSA candidate to win elected office in Georgia, after Gabriel Sanchez was elected to the statehouse there—in a campaign that Bond managed.

We spoke to Bond about their priorities, building socialist power down South, and the prospects for a glorious DSA political wave in the USA.

How Things Work: You were involved in the labor movement. How did that draw you into politics?

Kelsea Bond: I got involved in DSA in 2020. So much was happening with the Covid pandemic, Bernie not making it past the primary, and then of course the Black Lives Matter protests all over the country. In 2021 Nabisco workers went on strike, and they had a picket line out in a Georgia suburb. That was really my first time being on a picket line, growing up in the South and not being super familiar with unions. I remember at the time being disappointed with how many losses there had been on the left. Especially here in Atlanta, with the original Cop City ordinance getting passed in 2021. And then just a month later, seeing a bunch of regular, ordinary people win a good contract just because they went on strike and withheld their labor. That was really powerful for me, and solidified my understanding of how crucial labor organizing is if we want to win anything that we want in this country.

From then, I went on to help organizing Starbucks workers when they filed their first union election in Midtown Atlanta, and I went on to help them organize more stores in the area. Then was organizing strike solidarity for the Teamsters during their practice pickets in 2022, and UAW, and went on to work with almost every major labor union in the city. I was really happy to have had those same unions support me in this election. That felt very powerful to me.
