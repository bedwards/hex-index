How did tvs get so cheap?

Brian Potter · Construction Physics ·Jan 8, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Most people assume the plummeting cost of televisions is simply a story of fierce market competition, but Brian Potter reveals a far more mechanical and counterintuitive truth: we got cheap screens by making the manufacturing batches larger, not smaller. Potter dismantles the popular business school dogma of "one-piece flow" to show how the TV industry achieved a 90% price drop per pixel by scaling up the size of the glass sheets used to make them. This is not just a history of consumer electronics; it is a masterclass in how physical constraints and geometric scaling can override conventional economic wisdom.

The Geometry of Cheapness

Potter begins by establishing the sheer magnitude of the price collapse. He notes that "TVs have fallen in price more than any other major category on the chart," pointing out that a 50-inch screen that cost $1,100 in 2001 now sells for under $200. But the real insight lies in how he normalizes this data. Rather than just looking at the sticker price, he analyzes "dollars per area-pixel," a metric that accounts for the fact that modern TVs are both bigger and sharper. "The price per area-pixel has fallen by more than 90%," Potter writes, a statistic that forces the reader to confront the fact that we are getting exponentially more value for every dollar spent.

The core of Potter's argument challenges a deeply held belief in modern manufacturing. For decades, business literature has championed the idea that efficiency comes from minimizing batch sizes. Potter argues the opposite for this specific industry: "Several decades of lean manufacturing and business schools assigning 'The Goal' have convinced many folks that you should always aim to reduce batch size... But as we see in several processes... increasing your batch size can, depending on the nature of the process, result in substantial cost savings." This reframing is crucial. It suggests that the "one-size-fits-all" approach to efficiency is a fallacy; sometimes, the most efficient path is to do more at once.

"Mother glass scaling effects have, as I understand it, been the largest driver of LCD cost declines."

The mechanism behind this is the evolution of "mother glass." Potter explains that LCD panels are cut from massive sheets of glass, and the industry has relentlessly increased the size of these sheets. He details how "Generation 1" sheets were a modest 12 by 16 inches, while today's "Generation 10.5" sheets are nearly 100 times larger, measuring 116 by 133 inches. The math here is unforgiving but favorable: because equipment costs rise slower than the area of the glass, the cost per unit drops precipitously. Potter illustrates this with a striking comparison: "Going from Gen 4 to Gen 5 mother glass sheets reduced the cost per diagonal inch of LCD displays by 50%." This is a geometric advantage that has nothing to do with labor savings and everything to do with physics.

Process Innovation and the Semiconductor Link

While the size of the glass is the headline act, Potter weaves in a supporting cast of process innovations that made the scaling possible. He describes the manufacturing environment as one of extreme precision, noting that "manufacturers have had to find ways to keep very large surfaces pristine," necessitating cleanroom conditions and robotic handling to prevent defects. The industry didn't just get bigger; it got smarter. Potter highlights the shift from capillary action, which could take days to fill a panel with liquid crystal, to the "one drop fill" method. This innovation "reduced the time required to fill a panel from days to minutes," a dramatic compression of the production timeline that mirrors the speed gains seen in semiconductor fabrication.

The connection to the semiconductor industry is a recurring theme that adds significant depth to Potter's analysis. He notes that "the display manufacturing process is a lot like the semiconductor manufacturing process, it's just simpler and bigger." This parallel allowed the TV industry to piggyback on decades of R&D in chip manufacturing, utilizing equipment like steppers for photolithography and adopting similar yield-improvement strategies. Potter points out that yields have skyrocketed from 50% in the early days to over 90% today, a testament to the transfer of high-tech expertise.

However, this relentless drive for efficiency came at a steep price. Potter describes the industry landscape as a "25 year suicide pact for display manufacturers," where companies were forced to continuously invest billions in larger factories and newer technology just to survive. "Manufacturers have been required to continuously make enormous investments in larger fabs and newer technology, even as profit margins are constantly threatened," he writes. This dynamic was often fueled by national strategic interests rather than pure market logic, with various governments propping up domestic industries to secure a foothold in this critical technology sector.

Critics might argue that Potter's focus on the technical and economic mechanics of cost reduction overlooks the human cost of this "suicide pact." The pressure to cut costs and scale up likely drove labor practices and consolidation that squeezed suppliers and workers, even if the end consumer benefited from lower prices. Potter acknowledges the negative margins and the fierce competition but focuses primarily on the engineering triumph rather than the social friction it caused.

"It's thanks to the enormous market for LCD screens that these huge, efficient fabs can be justified, and the investment in new, improved process technology can be recouped."

The Bottom Line

Potter's analysis is a powerful reminder that the cheapest goods often come from the most expensive and complex industrial feats. The strongest part of his argument is the clear demonstration that "virtually all the major mechanisms that can drive efficiency improvements"—from geometric scaling to yield optimization—were deployed in the TV industry, creating a perfect storm of cost reduction. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the assumption that this model is universally applicable; the "suicide pact" nature of the industry suggests that such extreme scaling is only sustainable when backed by massive capital and strategic state support. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: the cheap TV on your shelf is not a miracle of the free market, but the result of a highly engineered, capital-intensive industrial strategy that prioritized scale above all else.

How did tvs get so cheap?

by Brian Potter · Construction Physics · Read full article

You’ve probably seen this famous graph that breaks out various categories of inflation, showing labor-intensive services getting more expensive during the 21st century and manufactured goods getting less expensive.

One of the standout items is TVs, which have fallen in price more than any other major category on the chart. TVs have gotten so cheap that they’re vastly cheaper than 25 years ago even before adjusting for inflation. In 2001, Best Buy was selling a 50 inch big screen TV on Black Friday for $1100. Today a TV that size will set you back less than $200.

The plot below shows the price of TVs across Best Buy’s Black Friday ads for the last 25 years. The units are “dollars per area-pixel”: price divided by screen area times the number of pixels (normalized so that standard definition = 1). This is to account for the fact that bigger, higher resolution TVs are more expensive. You can see that, in line with the inflation chart, the price per area-pixel has fallen by more than 90%.

This has prompted folks to wonder, how exactly did a complex manufactured good like the TV get so incredibly cheap?

It was somewhat more difficult than I expected to suss out how TV manufacturing has gotten more efficient over time, possibly because the industry is highly secretive. Nonetheless, I was able to piece together what some of the major drivers of TV cost reduction over the last several decades have been. In short, every major efficiency improving mechanism that I identify in my book is on display when it comes to TV manufacturing.

How an LCD TV works.

Since 2000, the story of TVs falling in price is largely the story of liquid crystal display (LCD) TVs going from a niche, expensive technology to a mass-produced and inexpensive one. As late as 2004, LCDs were just 5% of the TV market; by 2018, they were more than 95% of it.
