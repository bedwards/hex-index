American capernaum

Justin Smith-Ruiu · Hinternet ·Jan 11, 2026 ·15 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Justin Smith-Ruiu delivers a startling cultural diagnosis: the current political trajectory of the United States is not a return to tradition, but a descent into a hollow, postmodern simulacrum that mirrors the worst excesses of petro-state authoritarianism. This is not a standard political critique; it is a sweeping historical argument that links the aesthetic sensibilities of the White House to the global rise of "vapid consumerism," suggesting that the true threat to American democracy is not just policy, but a fundamental rot in the national spirit.

The Aesthetics of Betrayal

Smith-Ruiu begins by dismantling the idea that the current administration represents a distinct American conservatism. Instead, he frames the executive branch's cultural alignment as a bizarre fusion of Russian kitsch and Gulf petro-state opulence. He points to the future president's cameo in a 2013 music video with Azerbaijani artist Emin Agalarov as a telling early warning sign. "You could know already, from the support that the future president lent to this gross display of Eurasianist pop kitsch, that something was deeply rotten in our Republic," Smith-Ruiu writes. This observation reframes the administration's foreign policy pivots not as strategic realignments, but as an aesthetic surrender to a specific brand of globalist corruption.

The author argues that this shift is most visible in the embrace of global soccer, specifically the influence of FIFA and the migration of star players to Saudi Arabia. He posits that the administration's withdrawal from traditional international norms parallels a move toward the "louche shadow-government" of international football. "Is there any globalist conspiracy more powerful, and more out in the open, than fucking FIFA?" he asks, using the rhetorical question to underscore the absurdity of the situation. The argument suggests that the "beautiful game" has been stripped of its working-class roots and repackaged as a tool for soft power by regimes with little regard for human rights. Critics might note that this conflates a popular sport with a geopolitical conspiracy, potentially oversimplifying the complex economic forces driving global football. However, Smith-Ruiu's point is less about the sport itself and more about what the sport represents: a top-down, pre-fab version of culture that lacks organic roots.

Bad taste, in sufficiently concentrated dosage, can be as great a threat to our American way of life as any subversive ideology.

The Cargo Cult of Power

The commentary then pivots to the internal cultural decay of the United States, contrasting the "high modernity" of European and Persian intellectual traditions with the "cargo-cult imitation" of American wealth. Smith-Ruiu draws a sharp distinction between the genuine, albeit conflicted, cultural history of Iran and Russia and the derivative, superficial opulence of the American elite. He notes that while the Soviet Bloc suppressed avant-garde art, it paradoxically preserved certain bourgeois values of secular modernity that have since vanished in the West. "Such quaint Austro-Hungarianism as would warm the bourgeois heart of Peter Gay likewise survived, to some extent, in the Ashkenazi diaspora," he observes, highlighting the irony that the "enemy" preserved a form of culture that the West has abandoned.

In contrast, the author describes the current American leadership's aesthetic as a "distant ancestral connection to the aesthetic sensibility once predominant at Versailles," but one that is entirely hollow. "Trump's gilded decor, I mean, is really nothing more than a cargo-cult imitation of the way aristocratic wealth and power appear when you have no real connection to the historical legacies that produced this wealth and this power," Smith-Ruiu writes. This is a devastating critique of the administration's self-presentation: it is not a restoration of order, but a performance of power by those who do not understand the history of the power they are mimicking. The argument extends to the paramilitary forces and the general atmosphere of the White House, which the author compares to the cheap, performative violence seen in other authoritarian regimes.

The Illusion of Escape

Perhaps the most provocative section of the piece is the rejection of the progressive desire to flee to Europe. Smith-Ruiu argues that the idea of Europe as a sanctuary is a "childish delusion" and a "postmodern simulacrum" that exists only in the minds of Americans who have never actually lived there. He describes the reality of Europe as a place where people are "stocking up on canned goods and, on Ursula van der Leyen's recommendation, iodine pills" in preparation for real war. "The idea that one could somehow escape the crisis that is currently swallowing their own country whole, that they would no longer be plagued by dread for the future if they were simply to relocate to 'Europe', is nothing but a childish delusion," he asserts.

This section serves as a brutal reality check for those who view the transatlantic divide as a moral one. Smith-Ruiu suggests that the focus on domestic political squabbles and "electoral horserace chatter" is a form of repression, a way to avoid the "existential predicament" of the geopolitical landscape. He compares the American intellectual class to Israeli academics protesting pension cuts while ignoring the existential threat looming over their country. "Americans, similarly, excel at electoral horserace chatter... while remaining basically incapable of thinking about their true plight: as inhabitants of the world," he concludes. The argument is that the crisis is global, and there is no safe haven to run to.

The problem is that it is pretty much only within the boundaries of the United States, which remain essentially inviolable by any conventional military means, that such a simulacrum can appear real for long.

Bottom Line

Smith-Ruiu's strongest contribution is his refusal to treat the current political moment as a simple policy dispute, instead framing it as a cultural and historical catastrophe where the United States has become the global epicenter of a hollow, imitative consumerism. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its sweeping generalizations about European life and its dismissal of the tangible differences in political stability between the US and its allies, which may alienate readers seeking concrete policy analysis. Ultimately, the reader must watch for whether this cultural diagnosis translates into a coherent political strategy, or if it remains a lament for a world that has already been lost.
