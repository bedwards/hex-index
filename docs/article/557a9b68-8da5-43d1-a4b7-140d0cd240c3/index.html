Supreme cinema

Sam Jennings · ·Jan 6, 2026 ·14 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In a cultural landscape often defined by exhaustion with sequels and superhero fatigue, Sam Jennings makes a startlingly optimistic claim: the best films of 2025 are not the blockbusters, but two deeply personal, arthouse-adjacent works that defy the very genre conventions critics claim are dying. Jennings argues that while the industry chases children and nostalgia, the true vitality of cinema has migrated to films that are simultaneously "resolutely contemporary" and "timeless," offering a lifeline to audiences hungry for substance over spectacle.

The Critic's Reckoning

Jennings opens with a candid admission of their own pessimism, noting that their only positive review in a previous tenure was for a film released fifty years ago. This sets the stage for a dramatic pivot. "I owe it to my readers to finally bestow some genuine praise on the best films of 2025," Jennings writes, acknowledging that the year was not the "banner year" many predicted, but one where quality was hidden in plain sight. The author dismisses the idea that 2025 was a return to form for the industry at large, pointing out that the highest-grossing movies remain aimed at children, and that "independent" adult cinema has often become "much duller than it ought be."

This self-correction is crucial. It lends weight to the subsequent praise because it comes from a place of established skepticism. Jennings doesn't just like these films; they like them despite their own expectations. The author notes that the subject of serious art today is often a "barely-suppressed, ongoing reckoning with the anxiety unleashed by the last century," yet these new films manage to avoid the trap of "flogging the dead historical horse." Instead, they offer genuine entertainment that hasn't sacrificed its artistic integrity.

"Genuine, grand works of popular entertainment that escaped the Arthouse with all their ideas and techniques intact, sacrificing none of their artistic bona fides as they reach outward to meet their (hopefully sizable) audiences."

This distinction is the core of Jennings' argument: these films bridge the gap between high art and popular appeal without diluting either. It is a rare feat, one that reminds us that "the newest things often feel the most peculiarly familiar."

The Quiet Power of Sentimental Value

The commentary then shifts to Joachim Trier's Sentimental Value, a film Jennings describes as "still, quiet, melancholic, and filled with light." The piece highlights how Trier and his collaborator Eskil Vogt use the family home as a central metaphor, treating intergenerational tragedy with a "characteristically light touch." Unlike the heavy-handed drama often associated with family sagas, the film reveals its history through a "dazzling prologue" that visualizes the house's past, a technique Jennings compares to the "novelistic" digressions found in Trier's previous work, The Worst Person in the World.

Jennings praises the film's visual language, noting that the cinematography by Kasper Tuxen achieves a "flawless marriage of arthouse observation, brief flashes of punk immediacy, and occasionally thrilling push-ins." The review specifically lauds Renate Reinsve, Inga Ibsdotter Lilleaas, and Stellan Skarsgård for their performances, but emphasizes that the film's true strength lies in its tone. "So many of the most beautiful images in the film are soft and warm, as close to natural light as Trier and his cinematographer... can get," Jennings observes. This is a film that feels "exquisite" and "timeless," proving that a story about a "Bergman-esque film director" can feel urgently modern. The reference to Bergman is apt; just as Ingmar Bergman explored the psychological fractures of the family in the mid-20th century, Trier updates this tradition for a new era, using the house as a vessel for memory rather than a setting for melodrama.

Critics might argue that the film's reliance on a "light touch" risks underplaying the severity of the trauma it depicts, but Jennings counters that this approach allows the audience to grasp the "underlying structure of the film" without being overwhelmed by suspense. The result is an ending that feels "both surprising and conclusive," a rare balance in a genre often prone to ambiguity.

The Frenetic Energy of Marty Supreme

In stark contrast, Jennings turns to Josh Safdie's Marty Supreme, a film described as "lurid, frenetic, exhausting, and linear." If Trier's film is a meditation on the past, Safdie's is a "one-way slalom" of "insane and exhausting logic." The review identifies the protagonist, Marty Mauser, as an allegorical figure representing "every hustler, every American man, every American Jew, every single hopeful winner of every sports game ever." Jennings argues that the film is not just about a table tennis hustler, but about the "burdens and follies of ambition" itself.

The author draws a parallel to the Safdie brothers' previous work, noting that Marty Supreme feels like the culmination of their "single-drill exercises" in Uncut Gems and Good Times. However, this film elevates the stakes, combining disparate elements like "ping-pong, Chalamet's ego, sports movies, Hiroshima & Nagasaki, the Holocaust" into a narrative that feels "grandly life-sized." Jennings praises the casting, particularly Timothée Chalamet, who embodies the "obsessive actorly will" of the character. "Chalamet's story of himself... is the idea of abolishing all doubt and all cringe in the reaching for unironic greatness," Jennings writes. This performance is described as a "gift to a writer-director like Safdie," whose career has been obsessed with blurring the line between "realism" and "real life."

"Marty Supreme... is a film of immense ambition, itself about the burdens and follies of ambition. It is Raging Bull and The Hustler and Boogie Nights and any other cocky/tragic American epic of the low road, of the burnt-out hope and faith in the self."

The review also highlights the film's technical achievements, from the "long, crashing telephoto lenses" reminiscent of Robert Altman to the production design by Jack Fisk, which grounds the film in a "felt past." The soundtrack, a "carousel" of synthesizers and jukebox numbers, drives the film forward, creating a sense of a "future of coked-up and sweaty sports films." This is a film that demands to be seen on the big screen, where its "insistent, bleary-eyed, midnight darkness" can be fully appreciated.

The Verdict on Contemporary Cinema

Jennings concludes by asserting that these two films, despite their differences, are "future classics." They represent a shift away from the "elegiac century" of mourning and toward a new mode of storytelling that is both "popular" and "serious." The author suggests that the best of 2025 is not found in the return of old formulas, but in the willingness of artists to take risks and explore the "plight of cinematic artists themselves."

The piece ends with a call to recognize the value of these works, which have managed to "escape the Arthouse" without losing their soul. "I did not see any films in 2025 better than Joachim Trier's Sentimental Value and Josh Safdie's Marty Supreme," Jennings states, a bold claim that serves as a testament to the enduring power of cinema when it is made with courage and vision.

"Genuine, grand works of popular entertainment that escaped the Arthouse with all their ideas and techniques intact, sacrificing none of their artistic bona fides as they reach outward to meet their (hopefully sizable) audiences."

Bottom Line

Sam Jennings' argument is compelling because it reframes the current state of cinema not as a decline, but as a migration of quality to unexpected corners. The strongest part of the piece is its nuanced comparison of Sentimental Value and Marty Supreme, using their contrasting styles to illustrate the breadth of contemporary storytelling. The biggest vulnerability is the assumption that these films will find a "sizable" audience, a hope that may be optimistic given the current dominance of franchise filmmaking. However, the verdict is clear: for those willing to look beyond the headlines, 2025 offered two masterpieces that redefine what it means to be a "great film."

Discerning readers will note that the only film I’ve reviewed positively so far was released 50 years ago. They’ll note, too, that I’ve been entirely ambivalent about the overall quality of this past year’s cinematic output. I took the chance in my Superman review to air my exhaustion with the whole superhero genre (a sentiment that at least seems somewhat shared by the general moviegoing public — for now). On the back of Seth Rogen’s The Studio, I waved away any talk about 2025 being a banner year for the return of good cinema, opining that most of the highest-grossing movies every year continue to be films aimed at children. Then I wrote about finding Guillermo del Toro’s Frankenstein only fitfully interesting; I wrote about Clint Bentley’s Train Dreams, and why what passes for adult cinema in the world of small-sized and “independent” films — though far better than the average blockbuster — is still, on the whole, much duller than it ought be.

On the one hand, I think it’s appropriate to opine like so when given the right stage. Ours is an elegiac century, down to its bones. The subject of “serious” arts these days is most often a barely-suppressed, ongoing reckoning with the anxiety unleashed by the last century, and with the weight of all our self-conscious failures to live up to its most sterling achievements in popular culture. To add to this general, mournful air is, to some readers, understandably, the veritable flogging of the dead historical horse—one we’re all quite aware has long since passed away beneath us. Still, on the other hand, to do otherwise would mean telling a lie. And that is the work of artists, not critics.
