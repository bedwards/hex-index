CROSSPOST: DAN DREZNER & No 1: Welcome to the Lax Americana & March 19-21: God Is a Comedian

Brad DeLong · DeLong's Grasping Reality ·Mar 24, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Brad DeLong presents a terrifying thesis: the fog of war has not just obscured the conflict in the Persian Gulf, it has been replaced by a "policy smog" where the United States government operates with no coherent strategy, only a series of contradictory impulses. He argues that the current administration's behavior is not merely incompetent, but actively deconstructed, creating a reality where the US is simultaneously bombing Iran, lifting sanctions on its oil, and sending Marines to seize an island that handles 90% of its exports—all within the same hour.

The Architecture of Chaos

DeLong begins by dismantling the idea that this is a standard geopolitical crisis. He writes, "It is, I think, one of the more disorienting features of our current moment that we cannot even say with confidence who is attritting whom over the Persian/Arabian Gulf." This is a profound admission from a seasoned observer. Usually, the fog of war is a temporary condition of combat; here, DeLong suggests it is a permanent state of governance. The administration is not hiding facts; it is generating them at a rate that outpaces comprehension.

He highlights the absurdity of the President's rhetoric, noting how Trump "zigzagged his way through various frustrations and policy reversals" in a single day. The sequence is dizzying: demanding NATO help, calling allies cowards for refusing, then claiming the Strait of Hormuz is unnecessary, then sending 5,000 Marines to die for it. DeLong captures the surreal nature of this with a brutal observation: "This sequence of statements was delivered, as far as the public record shows, by the same person, using the same mouth, within roughly 24 hours."

The allies are cowards for not helping with the thing he doesn't need, which is why he's sending Marines to die for it, unless the countries that do need it do it themselves, which they won't, because they're cowards.

This logic loop is not just confusing; it is strategically paralyzing. Critics might argue that such erratic behavior is a negotiating tactic designed to keep adversaries off-balance. However, DeLong's evidence suggests a deeper rot: the administration lacks the basic machinery of statecraft to sustain even a simple deception. When the President claims a "simple military maneuver" is safe but requires "a lot of help" from the very allies he just insulted, the policy ceases to be strategy and becomes performance art.

The Deconstructed State

The commentary shifts to the institutional decay that allows this chaos to persist. DeLong cites Dan Drezner to argue that the problem is not just bad policy, but a lack of care for the work itself. "When a lazy, incurious administration starts doing things with a deconstructed state," Drezner writes, the result is a government that "don't care that they haven't put in the work."

This is where the historical parallel to the 25th Amendment becomes unavoidable. DeLong notes, "We know that it is appalling that there is even a single vote against 25th-Amendmenting Trump right now." The text implies that the threshold for invoking the 25th Amendment—a mechanism designed for when a president is unable to discharge powers—has been lowered to the point where the mere existence of the administration is treated as a "normal parameter of American politics."

The evidence of this deconstruction is found in the specific details of the conflict. DeLong describes a scenario where the US Treasury lifts oil sanctions on Iran for 30 days while simultaneously bombing the country. He writes, "The United States is purchasing, with Chinese currency, oil from the country it is currently bombing?!" The logic, he explains, is a circular trap: the war crashed the oil market, so the administration must buy the enemy's oil to stabilize prices, even though that oil funds the missiles shooting down American planes.

The sanctions were necessary to stop Iran funding the war, but the war made the sanctions too effective, so the sanctions had to be lifted to fund the war effort against the country that no longer needs sanctions.

This is not a policy failure; it is a policy hallucination. The administration is trying to solve an economic problem created by a military campaign that was supposed to solve a security problem, all while ignoring the fact that the military campaign is failing. The mention of Kharg Island, a speck of land handling 90% of Iran's oil exports, serves as a grim focal point. The US is preparing to seize it, yet the very act of seizing it requires navigating a strait that Iran has turned into a toll road, charging up to $2 million per transit.

The Illusion of Control

Perhaps the most damning section of DeLong's analysis is the timeline of the President's press gaggle. He lists a series of contradictory statements made in minutes: "At 12:03 PM, President Trump told reporters he wanted a ceasefire... At 12:05 he declared victory. At 12:07 he announced he was sending Marines."

DeLong points out that this isn't just confusion; it is a mechanism for market manipulation. He notes that 13 minutes after futures markets closed, the President posted on Truth Social that the US was "winding down" efforts, causing the S&P to reverse 1% in seconds. "A coincidence that should be studied in every securities fraud textbook," DeLong writes. This suggests that the chaos is not accidental but a feature of a system where insider information is the primary currency.

The military reality on the ground contradicts the President's narrative of "winding down." DeLong describes the USS Gerald R. Ford, the most expensive warship in history, retreating to Crete due to a "laundry fire" after 266 days at sea. "The crown jewel of the US Navy is fleeing the theatre, not because of being damaged in combat... But because someone's skivvies got too hot." This detail underscores the fragility of the entire operation. While the President claims victory, the F-35 stealth fighters, the cornerstone of US air dominance, are being hit by Iranian air defenses.

Someone forgot to tell the Iranians the planes were invisible.

The irony is palpable. The US is fighting a war based on the assumption of technological invincibility, only to find that the adversary has doubled their missile range and is now within striking distance of London and Paris. DeLong notes that Iran claimed a 2,000-kilometer range, but the missiles reached Diego Garcia, 4,000 kilometers away. "They were lying by a factor of two, which, in the context of ballistic missile capabilities, constitutes what experts call 'a very bad surprise'."

The Silence of the GOP

DeLong concludes by addressing the political silence that enables this chaos. He argues that the most appalling aspect is not the policy itself, but the lack of resistance from within the Republican party. "We know, further, that nobody inside the Trump administration appears willing to take any career risk at all," he writes. The administration has become a "hall of mirrors" where officials are more interested in "cashing in on their official connections" than in governing.

He contrasts this with the behavior of J.D. Vance, who is doubling down on divisive rhetoric while the country burns. DeLong writes, "J. D. Vance has decided that his road to power lies in doubling down on 'Great Replacement Theory,' thereby implicitly declaring his own mother-in-law, father-in-law, wife, and children to be at best second-class citizens." This personalizes the political failure, showing how the rhetoric of the party directly attacks the families of its own leaders.

In the end, DeLong turns to the source of his information: a newsletter called "No1" (pronounced "no one"). He admits uncertainty about the source's reliability but argues that in this environment, "he is certainly not less reliable and grounded than everyone currently engaged in sanewashing Donald Trump and the Trump administration."

That, today, is a very low bar—and a very telling one.

This admission is the piece's most powerful moment. It suggests that the baseline for truth has collapsed so completely that a fringe newsletter is the only place to find a coherent account of events. The "sanewashing" by mainstream commentators has become indistinguishable from the chaos itself.

Bottom Line

DeLong's strongest argument is that the administration's incoherence is not a bug but a feature of a "deconstructed state" where the separation between policy, profit, and performance has dissolved. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on a single, unverified source ("No1") for the most explosive claims, which could allow critics to dismiss the entire narrative as fiction. However, the sheer volume of contradictory facts—sanctions lifted while bombing, allies insulted while being asked for help, stealth planes shot down—creates a pattern that is difficult to ignore. Readers should watch for whether the "policy smog" clears or if this state of perpetual contradiction becomes the new normal for American foreign policy.
