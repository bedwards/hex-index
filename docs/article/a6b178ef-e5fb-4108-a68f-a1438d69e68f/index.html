Solving the electroporation bottleneck

Niko McCarty · ·Jan 15, 2026 ·14 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

This piece cuts through the noise of incremental biotech news to expose a fundamental, yet often ignored, bottleneck: we are blind to 99.999% of life on Earth because we lack the tools to manipulate it. Niko McCarty argues that the scientific community's obsession with a handful of "model" organisms is not just a habit, but a structural failure that prevents us from discovering the next generation of life-saving tools. The evidence is stark: despite a century of work, we still do not understand a quarter of the genes in our most studied bacterium, E. coli. If we cannot decode the familiar, how can we possibly hope to harness the unknown?

The Great Cultivation Gap

McCarty begins by dismantling the assumption that modern biology has a comprehensive map of the microbial world. He writes, "Rounding to the nearest whole number, we have deeply studied zero percent of lifeforms on this planet." This hyperbole serves a critical purpose: it forces the reader to confront the sheer scale of ignorance. The author points out that while we have cataloged a few thousand species, the vast majority of the estimated one trillion microbial species remain inaccessible because they refuse to grow in standard laboratory conditions.

The article effectively reframes the problem not as a lack of curiosity, but as a lack of incentive and capability. McCarty notes that for a PhD student, "It's much easier to take a labmate's vial of cells and get to work straight away on a well-defined research problem." This observation highlights a systemic issue where the scientific career structure rewards working with established tools over pioneering new ones. The combinatorial complexity of biology is described as a "vast and difficult to navigate" space, where finding the perfect growth conditions for a new microbe is akin to searching for a needle in a universe of needles.

"If we don't yet understand the inner workings of a bacterium that scientists have obsessed over for so long, consider how little we are likely to know about everything else."

The historical context McCarty weaves in is particularly potent. He reminds us that the thermostable DNA polymerase used in modern PCR was discovered in a Yellowstone geyser, and CRISPR was first found in a salt-loving microbe in Spain. These breakthroughs did not come from studying the familiar; they came from the obscure. Yet, as McCarty argues, the current infrastructure makes it nearly impossible to replicate this success with new organisms. The barrier is not just growing the cells; it is the ability to genetically transform them.

The Electroporation Bottleneck

The core of the argument shifts to the technical hurdle of electroporation—the process of using electrical pulses to punch holes in cell membranes so DNA can enter. McCarty explains that while this method is standard, it is far from foolproof. "If you get no colonies at all, it could be the plasmid, it could be the conditions, it could be the machine, it could be the buffer, it could be the voltage, and you just don't know," says Nili Ostrov, CSO of the nonprofit Cultivarium, in a quote that encapsulates the frustration of the field.

The author illustrates the magnitude of the problem with a striking statistic: a single commercial electroporator machine has over 4.2 million possible settings for voltage, resistance, and capacitance, not even accounting for biological variables like pH or temperature. This creates a search space so vast that human trial-and-error is effectively blind. McCarty draws a parallel to the Hodgkin-Huxley model of the 1950s, where scientists used giant squid axons to understand electrical signaling, noting that the same electrical principles that govern neurons can be repurposed to "program" a cell. However, unlike the squid axon, which is large and visible, modern microbial manipulation requires navigating a microscopic maze of parameters.

Critics might note that relying on automation to solve biological problems risks oversimplifying the nuanced, context-dependent nature of cellular life. A machine might find a setting that works in a lab but fails in the wild. However, McCarty counters this by showing that the current manual approach is already failing to produce results for non-model organisms.

The Algorithmic Breakthrough

Enter Cultivarium, a nonprofit attempting to automate the discovery of growth and transformation protocols. McCarty details how they built a custom robot capable of testing 24 different conditions simultaneously, varying buffers, voltages, and waveforms. The results were immediate and surprising. The team found that for certain microbes, changing the wash buffer could improve transformation efficiency by 100-fold, and adjusting the voltage could yield 1,000-fold improvements.

The most compelling part of the coverage is the introduction of an "active learning framework." Instead of a human guessing the next step, a Bayesian optimizer suggests which parameters to test next based on previous results. McCarty quotes lead author Stephanie Brumwell: "The goal is to let the model suggest what we cannot rationalize." This approach allowed them to transform species of ocean-dwelling microbes that had never been worked with before, including Cupriavidus necator, achieving transformation efficiencies 8.6-fold higher than anything previously described.

"The goal is to let the model suggest what we cannot rationalize."

This section of the article is a masterclass in explaining complex automation without losing the human element. McCarty emphasizes that this is not just about speed; it is about expanding the horizon of what is biologically possible. By giving these protocols away for free, Cultivarium is attempting to democratize access to the "dark matter" of biology.

Why Native Hosts Matter

The final argument of the piece addresses the limitations of metagenomics, the practice of sequencing environmental DNA and inserting those genes into a familiar host like E. coli. While useful, McCarty points out that "many fascinating biochemical mechanisms, especially those involving multiple genes, can only be understood in context; they stop working entirely when transplanted into another cell." This is a crucial distinction. To truly understand a plastic-eating microbe, one must work with the plastic-eating microbe, not a proxy.

The author concludes by reinforcing the idea that the next great discoveries—perhaps a new antibiotic, a more efficient carbon-capture enzyme, or a novel way to degrade waste—are likely locked inside organisms we currently cannot manipulate. The bottleneck is not a lack of potential; it is a lack of method. As McCarty writes, "Biologists find useful things nearly everywhere they look."

"If our hypothetical PhD student makes a growth media, dips cells into it, and nothing grows, then that failed experiment also returns little useful information; was it the pH or oxygen to blame, or something else?"

Bottom Line

Niko McCarty's analysis is a powerful call to action, exposing how the scientific community's reliance on established models is actively stifling discovery. The strongest part of the argument is the demonstration that automation and machine learning are not just efficiency tools, but essential keys to unlocking the vast, unexplored diversity of life. The biggest vulnerability remains the biosecurity and ethical implications of making these powerful genetic tools accessible for a wider range of organisms, a tension the article acknowledges but does not fully resolve. The reader should watch for how this new wave of automated biology reshapes the biotech landscape in the coming decade, potentially turning the "zero percent" of studied life into a new frontier of innovation.

  • Thermus aquaticus

    The article mentions the thermostable DNA polymerase from a Yellowstone geyser used in PCR - this is Taq polymerase from Thermus aquaticus. This specific organism exemplifies the article's broader point about discovering useful biology in extreme environments.

  • Genetic transformation

    While the article focuses on electroporation, it mentions other transformation methods like calcium-phosphate treatment and microinjection. Understanding the broader history and variety of genetic transformation techniques provides essential context for why electroporation became dominant.

Science’s most studied organism is, without contest, E. coli. It has prompted at least half a million academic papers, spanning nearly a century of work. And yet a quarter of its genes still do not have an experimentally-determined function, according to a 2019 study.1

If we don’t yet understand the inner workings of a bacterium that scientists have obsessed over for so long, consider how little we are likely to know about everything else.

Scientists have estimated that about one trillion microbial species inhabit the Earth, of which 99.999 percent remain undiscovered.2 Of those that have been cataloged, perhaps a few thousand have been grown in a laboratory. In other words, a vast chasm remains between the microbes one can see under a microscope or locate in the dirt and those we can grow on a petri dish.3 Rounding to the nearest whole number, we have deeply studied zero percent of lifeforms on this planet.

Even if such rounding is hyperbolic, it points to the vast number of possibly useful discoveries still on the table. CRISPR was first found in a salt-loving microbe in Spain. The thermostable DNA polymerase used in modern PCR was discovered in a Yellowstone geyser. Biologists find useful things nearly everywhere they look.So why don’t we grow and study more organisms in the laboratory?

A few reasons. First, the genetic tools for manipulating more “established” organisms, like E. coli, yeast, and HeLa cells, are quite reliable. Why would a PhD student spend the formative years of her career mixing up liquids to grow an obscure microbe that nobody else has ever heard of? It’s much easier to take a labmate’s vial of cells and get to work straight away on a well-defined research problem.

Another reason is that the combinatorial complexity of biology is vast and difficult to navigate. A typical protein in E. coli is 300 amino acids long, with 20 possible amino acids at each position. The number of possible sequences for this hypothetical protein is thus 20300, more options than there are atoms in the Universe. And that’s just for proteins!4
