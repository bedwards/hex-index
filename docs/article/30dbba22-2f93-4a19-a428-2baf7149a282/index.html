In a landscape saturated with polished gear reviews, Benn Jordan offers something far more valuable: an unvarnished confession about the gap between an instrument's reputation and its actual workflow. Filming live from Perfect Circuit in Burbank, Jordan dismantles the myth that expensive or cult-favorite synthesizers guarantee creativity, arguing instead that intuitive design matters more than brand loyalty.

The Myth of the "Greatest" Machine

Jordan's central thesis is provocative: owning a legendary piece of hardware does not make you a better producer. He challenges the blind worship of specific gear by admitting his own struggles with high-end instruments. "I would never tell someone to get the Tempest because I know that's starting you know what I'm saying," Jordan states, highlighting the danger of leading beginners down a path of frustration. He notes that while the machine has the tools to be the greatest, its surface-level usability is a barrier. "Once you get the muscle memory down of it sure it's fine but then at the same time you're like I don't want to lead someone down this path because I know you're going to be disappointed."

This honesty is rare in a genre often defined by hype. Jordan suggests that the allure of these machines is often a trap for those who haven't used them. He points out that the interfaces on devices like the Elektron Machine Drum and Mono Machine are "anti-workflow," slowing down the creative process rather than enhancing it. "It's just anti-workflow you know you just slows you down and everything that you make on it kind of sounds like a machine drum or a mono machine like you can't really escape that almost like the digitone like you can't escape that like little world." By admitting he considered selling his own Machine Drum because he never used it, he validates the experience of countless producers who feel guilty for not utilizing their expensive gear.

I would never tell someone to get the Tempest for $2,500 no way that's like in the world of NPCs as well people like oh I'm safe enough to get like an MPC60 and I'm like dumb.

Critics might argue that mastering difficult interfaces is a rite of passage that deepens a musician's understanding of sound design. However, Jordan's point stands that in a modern production environment, friction can be the enemy of inspiration. He acknowledges that while the workflow becomes manageable after years of practice, the initial barrier is too high for many.

The Allure and Limitation of Constraints

The conversation shifts to the paradox of limitations in music production. Jordan reflects on the enduring appeal of older samplers like the Akai MPC and the SP-1200, which are celebrated for their specific sonic character rather than their ease of use. He recalls how J Dilla used the Akai MPC 3000, noting that the machine's 12-bit crunch and low-end heaviness define its sound. "It's super crunchy it's like an SP-1200 it's super bright yeah but less aliasy it's more like low end heavy and there's not so much of the like ringy top unless you really push it."

Jordan appreciates that these constraints force creativity, a sentiment he carries over to modern remakes like the Isla Instruments S-2400. "I love that there are limits because that brings on its own sense of creativity when it comes to things." Yet, he remains skeptical about whether these limitations are still necessary in an era of unlimited digital tracks. He questions why modern hardware still imposes arbitrary caps, asking, "Dude it's 2021 like why is there a limit sure but at the same time I love that there are limits."

This tension between the romanticism of limitation and the practicality of modern tools is the piece's most nuanced argument. Jordan admits that while he understands the workflow of the SP-1200 thanks to educational content from artists like Alex Ball, he still struggles to justify the purchase for his own needs. "I always just was like oh it's a old drum machine that the Dilla used and that's why it's good... but at the same time I love those machines."

The Danger of Blind Imitation

Perhaps the most striking part of Jordan's commentary is his critique of the "follow the leader" mentality in gear acquisition. He observes that many buyers purchase instruments simply because a famous artist used them, without understanding the context or the workflow. "People with that really I don't I don't I don't do Polly and stuff really people are very passionate about it and I and I'm fine with that it's just I don't want to do it wrong." He recounts a controversial video where he compared the Elektron Digitakt and Digimono, noting that the backlash was intense because he formed an opinion before even owning the gear.

Jordan's willingness to admit his own missteps adds significant weight to his warnings. He describes how he returned a Digitone Keys because it felt like "the ugliest keyboard ever" and didn't click for him, despite its reputation. "Every time I used it I was trying to use everything on it you know I was like oh it's a great FM synth so in my head I'm like okay cool I'm gonna do like Yamaha DX70 tones and stuff but then I'm like making kick drums and next thing you know I'm making a techno track and using all the voices and I'm like wait what was I working on."

I think both of those things have died a very respectful funeral and I don't think that they have too much place today.

He argues that the industry should stop treating these older, difficult machines as holy grails. "I think both of those things have died a very respectful funeral and I don't think that they have too much place today." This is a bold stance for a musician known for his deep dive into electronic music history, suggesting that the best path forward is to find tools that fit the current workflow rather than clinging to the past.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's commentary succeeds because it prioritizes the human experience of making music over the technical specifications of the gear. His strongest argument is that the "cult of the machine" often obscures the reality that difficult interfaces can stifle creativity rather than inspire it. The piece's vulnerability lies in its potential to alienate purists who believe that mastering difficult tools is essential to artistic growth, but Jordan's personal anecdotes provide a compelling counter-narrative. For the busy producer, the takeaway is clear: do not buy a machine because it sounds good on paper or because a legend used it; buy it because it helps you make music without fighting the interface.
