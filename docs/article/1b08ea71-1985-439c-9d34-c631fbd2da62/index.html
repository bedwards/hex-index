How to Read a College Syllabus - And Strategize for How to Best Approach the Course

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·Jul 6, 2021 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan delivers a counterintuitive truth that most college students miss: the syllabus is not a contract to be endured, but a strategic blueprint to be weaponized. While most students skim for due dates, Kaplan argues that the document's true value lies in reverse-engineering the professor's expectations before the first lecture begins. This is not about compliance; it is about gaining a tactical advantage that separates high performers from the rest of the pack.

The Calendar as a Survival Tool

Kaplan begins with a seemingly mundane suggestion—listing office hours on a personal calendar—but quickly escalates the stakes to existential levels. He writes, "you need to start keeping a calendar or else you will be sad for your entire life... and you will die alone." While the hyperbole is comedic, the underlying logic is sound: the syllabus reveals hidden opportunities for engagement that vanish if not scheduled in advance. By treating office hours as recurring events rather than emergency exits, students transform a passive resource into an active strategy.

This approach reframes the student-professor relationship. Instead of waiting for confusion to set in, the proactive student identifies gaps in understanding weeks before they become crises. Critics might argue that not every student needs or wants to attend office hours, and forcing a rigid schedule could feel burdensome. However, Kaplan's point is not about mandatory attendance, but about visibility; simply seeing the slot on the calendar lowers the barrier to entry when help is actually needed.

Decoding the Professor's Mindset

The commentary takes a sharp turn into the psychology of the classroom when Kaplan addresses the infamous "It's on the syllabus" meme. He notes, "one thing that you can do at the beginning of the semester in order to make sure that you're not one of the students bringing these uh these undesired questions to your professors." This observation cuts through the frustration many faculty feel regarding logistical queries. By reading the syllabus thoroughly, students avoid asking questions that have already been answered, preserving their credibility for asking the "cool ones" about course material.

Kaplan suggests that professors are eager to discuss subject matter but weary of administrative clarifications. He writes, "if you're a geologist and you like geology so much that you go on to become a professor of geology... you're going to be like awesome i love talking about tectonic plates." This insight is crucial for students who want to build rapport with instructors. It shifts the dynamic from a transactional exchange of information to a collaborative intellectual pursuit.

"When you read the policies, come up with the corresponding strategies for taking the course."

Strategic Policy Analysis

Perhaps the most valuable section of Kaplan's argument is his insistence on treating policies as strategic levers. He argues that students should not just read the rules but actively plan around them. "For example the policies about the exams say that the exams are open note you need to recognize that at the hey the exams are open note that means i've got to take notes," he explains. This simple realization changes the entire semester's workflow, turning note-taking from a passive habit into a deliberate exam-preparation strategy.

The author emphasizes that understanding the grading calculation, late policies, and attendance rules allows students to optimize their efforts. "You get to the midterm exam you know it's open note you have all of these beautifully written notes and then you breeze through the midterm exam," Kaplan describes. This is where the syllabus transitions from a static document to a dynamic game plan. Students who miss this nuance often find themselves scrambling, trying to apply a generic study method to a specific, rule-bound environment.

The Hierarchy of Syllabus Sections

Kaplan offers a controversial take on "student learning outcomes," dismissing them as administrative boilerplate. "I now give you permission... to never read the student learning outcomes," he declares, arguing they are meant for administrators, not students. While this advice might seem reckless to some, it reflects a pragmatic approach to time management. In a busy semester, distinguishing between bureaucratic requirements and actionable data is a vital skill.

Conversely, he elevates the course schedule to the most critical component. "The course schedule is the part of the syllabus that gets looked at the most," he notes, urging students to transfer every due date to their personal calendars immediately. This act of transcription is not mere busywork; it is an act of commitment. "Many students i've discovered now that i teach college many students don't do this and those students they get worse grades because you're going to forget all of this stuff if you don't have it written in one place," Kaplan warns. The argument holds up: the syllabus is the source of truth, but the personal calendar is the execution engine.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's strongest contribution is the shift from passive reading to active strategizing, particularly the insight that policies dictate study methods rather than just rules. His biggest vulnerability is the dismissal of learning outcomes, which, while often dry, can sometimes reveal the specific skills a professor prioritizes. The reader should watch for the specific policies in their own syllabi that fundamentally alter how they should prepare, rather than just what they need to memorize.
