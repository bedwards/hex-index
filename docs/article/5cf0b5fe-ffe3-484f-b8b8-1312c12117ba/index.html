Can Tsmc Make Enough Chips?

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Dec 3, 2020 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In an era where consumers blame supply chains for empty shelves, Asianometry reframes the crisis as a fundamental bottleneck in advanced semiconductor manufacturing. The author's most provocative claim is not that demand is simply too high, but that the sheer physical limits of TSMC's cutting-edge capacity could force industry giants like Intel to abandon their decades-long strategy of in-house production. This matters now because the chips powering the next decade of AI, 5G, and gaming are being built in a handful of factories in Taiwan, creating a single point of failure for the global economy.

The Capacity Crunch

Asianometry opens by contextualizing the holiday rush, noting that "the chips for the first three products those from Apple and the gaming consoles are exclusively made by TSMC." This observation is crucial; it strips away the noise of marketing and reveals a singular dependency. While shortages are common with new launches, the author argues that the pandemic has created a "unprecedented" demand spike that traditional supply chain fixes cannot solve quickly.

The piece dives into the technical reality of TSMC's operations, explaining that the company produces roughly "12 million 12-inch equivalent wafers a year," yet the most advanced processes are concentrated in specific facilities like Fab 18 in Tainan. Asianometry writes, "Fab 18 is the cutting edge of cutting edge facilities costing an estimated 17 billion dollars." This massive capital investment highlights the barrier to entry; competitors cannot simply snap their fingers and replicate this capacity overnight. The author notes that while TSMC is building a facility in Arizona, it will be small and likely reserved for government-sensitive work, meaning the bulk of high-performance computing will remain in Taiwan for the foreseeable future.

"Half of winning the game is just showing up. The customers might love your products but they can't get their hands on them. It doesn't matter. They'll buy something else."

This analogy effectively shifts the conversation from product quality to availability. The author argues that even if a competitor like Samsung offers better terms, as seen with Nvidia and Qualcomm defecting for some products, the inability to secure volume is a fatal flaw. Critics might note that this view underestimates the potential for older, less advanced nodes to handle many consumer needs, but for the high-margin, high-performance market, the author's point about capacity being the ultimate moat holds significant weight.

The Intel Dilemma

The commentary takes a sharp turn when examining Intel's position. Historically, Intel has prided itself on vertical integration, controlling its own fabrication. However, Asianometry points out that "Intel's 2020 chip production numbers are estimated to be over 60 percent higher than TSMC's," yet Intel is now facing delays in its own 7-nanometer process. The stakes are high with the "500 million dollar Aurora supercomputer" project, which relies on chips that Intel has failed to deliver on time.

The author suggests a paradigm shift: "The sheer size of Intel's capacity makes me wonder about the likelihood of the rumors of Intel tapping TSMC's five nanometer process." This is a massive potential disruption. If Intel, the former king of manufacturing, must outsource its most critical advanced chips to its biggest rival, it signals a fundamental change in the industry's power dynamics. Asianometry speculates that Intel might adopt a "mix and match" approach, combining dies from its own foundries with advanced dies from TSMC, a strategy that would require TSMC to provide significant capacity.

The Capital Constraint

Finally, the piece scrutinizes the financial limits of expansion. TSMC has increased its capital expenditure to $17 billion, but Asianometry questions if this is enough. The author notes, "The flat capital expenditure number makes me wonder if the Intel customer is actually coming or if it's coming at all in small numbers." The bottleneck isn't just money; it's hardware. TSMC relies on ASML for its extreme ultraviolet lithography machines, and "ASML only makes a few of these machines every year."

This supply constraint creates a ceiling on growth that no amount of corporate strategy can easily bypass. Asianometry concludes that while TSMC aims to maintain a "50 percent market share," the reality of equipment shortages and the time required to build new fabs means they may simply be unable to make enough chips to satisfy everyone. The author writes, "TSMC is likely to be able to sell every chip they can make," implying that the problem is not sales, but pure production volume.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's strongest argument lies in exposing the fragility of the global chip supply, where a single factory's output dictates the availability of everything from iPhones to supercomputers. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on speculation regarding Intel's future strategy, which remains unconfirmed. However, the core insight remains vital: in the semiconductor world, the ability to manufacture at scale is becoming more valuable than the ability to design the chip itself.
