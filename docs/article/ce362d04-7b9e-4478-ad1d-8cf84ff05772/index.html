Iran

Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider and his co-hosts on the "Second Breakfast" podcast dissect a hypothetical US-Iran war not as a triumph of military might, but as a catastrophic failure of strategic planning and readiness. Their most startling claim is that the US military is currently fighting a war it cannot win because it has forgotten how to fight a real war, relying on "body counts" instead of clear objectives while leaving the world's most critical oil chokepoint vulnerable to mines and drones. For busy leaders tracking global stability, this analysis strips away the noise of daily headlines to reveal a terrifying reality: the US has no "save point" for its current strategic blunders.

The Illusion of Victory

Schneider and his guests immediately dismantle the administration's narrative of success. They point out that despite CENTCOM striking 6,000 targets, the Iranian regime remains intact, and the war has merely escalated without a clear path to victory. Schneider writes, "This sounds very MACV, very Vietnam body count. It's not about effects... It's about inputs. The number of bombs you've dropped, the number of people you've killed. Not what you're achieving, even if you knew what that was." This comparison to the Vietnam War is a powerful rhetorical device that forces listeners to confront the disconnect between activity and progress. The argument lands hard because it exposes a fundamental flaw in modern military metrics: counting strikes does not equal winning a war.

The discussion highlights that the US has failed to anticipate how an adversary like the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) actually operates. As Schneider notes, "We did not plan for the IRGC being able to operate like the IRGC — in small groups, under mission command. That's the whole point of the IRGC. You cut the head off the snake and the snake's still there." This observation suggests that decades of planning for conventional state-on-state warfare have left the US unprepared for the asymmetric, decentralized tactics that define the Iranian threat. Critics might argue that the sheer volume of strikes has degraded Iranian capabilities more than the hosts admit, but the evidence of continued missile launches and the inability to secure the Strait of Hormuz suggests a significant strategic gap.

There are no loot drops from which you can upgrade. We did not plan for the IRGC being able to operate like the IRGC.

The Strategic Blunder of the Strait

The most alarming section of the coverage focuses on the failure to keep the Strait of Hormuz open, a mission that is CENTCOM's primary reason for existing. The hosts mock the administration's claim that the strait is "open for transit" as long as Iran stops shooting, comparing it to declaring the beaches of Normandy open while the Germans are still firing. Schneider argues, "The whole point of CENTCOM is to keep that strait open... Either this was not the con-op they wanted... or they just took a backseat and said, 'We can do this with minimal forces.'" This critique is devastating because it points to a lack of basic contingency planning. The removal of dedicated mine-countermeasure ships and the reliance on unproven Littoral Combat Ships creates a massive vulnerability that Iran could exploit instantly.

The hosts emphasize that the battlefield is not as transparent as military leaders claim. Even with advanced surveillance, Iran can use fishing vessels and dhows to lay mines that jam the strait. As one host puts it, "You may not be focused with all your Reapers and space-based ISR on Iranian fishing vessels in a congested waterway full of civilian shipping." This highlights a critical blind spot in US intelligence and operational focus. The economic consequences of a closed strait would be immediate and severe, potentially driving oil prices to $200 a barrel and crippling global economies, a scenario the US seems ill-equipped to prevent.

The Nuclear Gamble

Perhaps the most chilling aspect of the analysis is the potential outcome regarding Iran's nuclear program. The hosts argue that the war has not eliminated the threat but may have exacerbated it. Schneider writes, "If you are a wounded, grieving Iranian regime left in power at the end of this... you have a powerful incentive to double down on your nuclear ambitions." This insight challenges the assumption that military strikes will deter nuclear proliferation. Instead, the war could create a political environment where the Iranian leadership feels compelled to accelerate their nuclear program to ensure regime survival.

The discussion also touches on the logistical nightmare of recovering the 400 kilograms of highly enriched uranium (HEU) buried under rubble. Schneider notes, "This is not five guys from JSOC with a black bag. It's a task force-sized event at minimum, and that's assuming everything goes right and you hit all the sites simultaneously." The lack of a concrete plan to secure this material suggests that the US is gambling on a best-case scenario that is unlikely to materialize. The risk is that the US ends up with a nuclear-armed Iran and a shattered global economy, achieving neither of its strategic goals.

If the option truly were an Iran on the cusp of a nuclear weapon versus a major war causing an energy spike rivaling 1973, I could understand the trade-off. But the Iranian nuclear program had not substantially advanced... The likeliest outcome is the regime stays intact... and it won't be clear that Iran's nuclear ambitions have been extinguished.

Bottom Line

Jordan Schneider's analysis delivers a stark verdict: the US is engaged in a war of attrition without a theory of victory, driven by overconfidence and a failure to plan for the realities of modern asymmetric warfare. The strongest part of the argument is the exposure of the disconnect between military metrics and strategic outcomes, particularly the failure to secure the Strait of Hormuz. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a hypothetical scenario that, while plausible, assumes a level of incompetence in US planning that some might find exaggerated. Readers should watch for whether the US military can actually mobilize the necessary mine-countermeasure forces and whether the Iranian regime can be pressured into a negotiated settlement before the nuclear threshold is crossed.
