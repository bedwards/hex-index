Real Lawyer Reacts to Legal Memes

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Jul 19, 2019 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just react to legal memes; he uses them as a Trojan horse to dissect the very real, very human anxieties of the legal profession. While most content creators treat pop culture references as mere punchlines, Stone leverages them to explain complex procedural concepts like summary judgment and the rule against prejudicial evidence, proving that humor is often the most effective vehicle for legal literacy.

The Court of Public Opinion

Stone opens by dismantling the idea that courtroom decorum is purely about seriousness. He points out that while lawyers must build rapport, the power dynamic is skewed: "You always want to build up a rapport with the jury and when you have a joke that lands with the jury or the judge it is a really really great feeling." Yet, he immediately contrasts this with the terrifying reality of judicial authority, noting that "you're basically obligated to laugh whether the joke is funny or not and all Federal Court judges think that they are just absolutely the funniest people on Planet."

This observation highlights a critical tension in the legal system: the human element versus the institutional hierarchy. Stone's analysis of a meme involving a seal prosecuting a killer whale illustrates his point about evidence admissibility. He explains that names can be "more prejudicial than probative and they are not allowable in court," a concept that often confuses laypeople but is central to fair trials. By grounding this in a silly cartoon scenario, he makes the abstract rule of evidence tangible.

Critics might argue that focusing on the humor of the courtroom trivializes the gravity of the proceedings, but Stone's approach suggests that understanding the human quirks of judges and juries is essential for anyone navigating the system.

The Anxiety of the Brief

The commentary shifts from courtroom dynamics to the grueling reality of legal practice, specifically the pressure of deadlines and the fear of sending a flawed email. Stone captures the universal dread of the "nasty gram" sent to opposing counsel: "It will cause an asthma attack or a panic attack in so many different lawyers thinking about the email that you sent as opposed to the email that they they intended to send."

He uses a meme about a lawyer turning a masterpiece brief into a "converting picture of a cheetah into the mascot for Cheetos" to illustrate the compromise between perfection and productivity. The core of his argument is that "sometimes getting it done is more important than getting it perfect," a sentiment that resonates deeply in any high-stakes profession. This is a refreshing departure from the polished, infallible image of lawyers often portrayed in media.

"Sometimes getting it done is more important than getting it perfect."

Stone also tackles the misconception that legal work is always dramatic, using a meme about the Law & Order theme song to remind readers that real courtrooms lack the dramatic orchestral swells. He admits, "sadly it does not play when you walk into a courtroom dramatically," and shares a personal anecdote about convincing his parents that watching the show was "educational TV." This self-deprecating humor humanizes the profession, stripping away the mystique to reveal the mundane reality of litigation.

Pop Culture as Precedent

Perhaps the most effective part of Stone's coverage is his ability to bridge the gap between pop culture and black-letter law. When discussing a meme about Kanye West teaching contracts, Stone explains the doctrine of "consideration"—the requirement that something of value be exchanged for a contract to be valid. He notes that the law prevents "gratuitous contract where you write down a contract but it's only the one side doing something and the other side isn't getting anything as a result."

By using the absurdity of a celebrity teaching law school, he clarifies a concept that law students spend semesters mastering. Similarly, his reaction to a meme about a client showing up to court with a ridiculous appearance serves as a cautionary tale about first impressions. He warns that "you're gonna swim upstream when your face is covered in tattoos," emphasizing that while the law should be blind to appearance, the human element of the jury and judge is not.

Stone's analysis of a meme regarding the Simpsons character Lionel Hutz further demonstrates his range, noting that the character's "works on contingency? no money down" sign is a perfect satire of the ethical gray areas some attorneys navigate. He laments the loss of the voice actor, Phil Hartman, but uses the moment to reinforce the difference between cartoon incompetence and real-world ethical standards.

Bottom Line

Stone's piece succeeds because it refuses to treat legal memes as mere jokes; instead, he treats them as case studies in legal procedure, ethics, and human psychology. The strongest part of his argument is the demonstration that humor does not undermine the law but rather illuminates its complexities for a general audience. The only vulnerability lies in the occasional reliance on sponsorship segments, which momentarily breaks the immersion of the legal analysis, though the core content remains robust and insightful.
