There's a phenomenon in which people transcend geographical and cultural boundaries, uniting under a shared identity with a shared set of mythologies and symbols. An identity marked by transcendent devotion to something greater, something that can shape their world view, influence their ethics, and even dictate their behaviors. And no, I'm not describing religion.

Seriously, who's choosing this footage? Well, I'm not describing religion as it's stereotypically defined. I'm talking about Swifties, Disney adults, or mega Star Wars nerds, or any experience of pop culture fandom at its most intense. The communal empowerment of singing the fight song at a college football game, making a trip to Graceand to connect with Elvis Presley, getting married at Disney World.

Are these religious behaviors? Can a person's fandom of a sports team, a corporate brand, a singer, or a cinematic universe be religious? Well, it all depends on how we define what religion is, or rather, what it does. See, I'm not a fan of the simplistic matching game you sometimes see online.

Wokeism is a religion. Environmentalism is a religion. Here are 10 reasons why. These arguments usually flatten both religion and the thing being compared, turning them into a checklist of surface level similarities.

But at the same time, I'm the same guy who published videos saying that baseball players engage in magical rituals, Silicon Valley tech companies can be religious, and the American flag can be a religious symbol. So what gives? Am I being a hypocrite? What I'm getting at is the classic distinction in religious studies between an essentialist definition of religion and a functionalist definition of religion.

Essentialist or substantive definitions of religion are based on the idea that there's something inherently different about religion than any other cultural phenomenon. In other words, religion is a human experience that's unique in its characteristics. A lot of scholars of religion back in the day, including Edward Tyler, William James, and Rudolph Otto, based their definitions of religion on a substantive foundation. Tyler defined religion as belief in spiritual beings.
