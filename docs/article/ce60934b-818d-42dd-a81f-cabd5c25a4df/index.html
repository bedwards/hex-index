Democratic realism, inclusiveness, and subjugation

Cyril Hédoin · ·Jan 8, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Most debates about democracy focus on how to make elections work better. Cyril Hédoin flips the script entirely, arguing that the real crisis isn't voter incompetence, but the hidden cost of forcing people to obey rules they had no hand in making. This piece offers a rare, mathematical approach to political philosophy, suggesting that the solution to democratic fatigue isn't more voting, but a radical restructuring of how power is distributed across different levels of society.

The Cost of Being Ignored

Hédoin begins by dissecting the stalemate between two dominant schools of thought: deliberative democrats, who believe in the wisdom of crowds, and democratic realists, who argue that voters are too irrational to govern themselves. He finds both sides missing the point. While realists correctly identify that voters often act on inconsistent impulses, they wrongly assume this behavior is fixed. Hédoin writes, "Realists are right that behavioral aspects limit what electoral democracy can achieve. They are wrong to assume that these behavioral aspects are inherent and independent of the democratic institutions in place."

This is a crucial pivot. It shifts the blame from the citizen to the system. The author suggests that when institutions fail to include people, the result is not just bad policy, but a form of coercion he terms "subjugation." He defines this starkly: "Lack of inclusivity entails subjugation, i.e., the coercion of individuals to abide by rules or decisions they regard as unjustified and would prefer to ignore."

The argument gains historical weight when Hédoin connects this to the "epistemic turn" in democracy theory. He notes that while some argue "diversity trumps ability," meaning a random group often outperforms experts, this ignores the friction of disagreement. As he puts it, "The more the deliberation process is open to a diversity of views, the more difficult it will be to reach a sensible decision." This creates a tension: we need inclusiveness to avoid subjugation, but too much inclusiveness can paralyze decision-making.

Subjugation is especially costly when political decision-making touches on aspects of human flourishing or domains of social life that individuals regard as private.

Beyond the Ballot Box

Hédoin turns his attention to Hélène Landemore's concept of "open democracy," which champions mechanisms like sortition—selecting citizens by lottery, much like jury duty—to replace or supplement elections. He acknowledges the appeal of Landemore's case studies, such as Iceland's constitutional process, where randomly selected citizens drafted a new constitution. However, he questions whether a single, monolithic system of open democracy can handle the complexity of modern governance.

He argues that the stakes of politics determine the necessary level of inclusiveness. When the government decides on matters of deep personal significance, the cost of subjugation is high, demanding maximum inclusion. But for routine or technical issues, the cost of constant deliberation outweighs the benefit. Hédoin suggests, "A polycentric (rather than open) democracy could lower the stakes of politics – thus making demands for inclusiveness less pressing, but also making inclusiveness less costly to implement."

This is where the piece moves from critique to construction. Hédoin proposes a "polycentric democracy" where decision-making is scaled to the issue. In such a system, citizens could move between jurisdictions if they disagree with local rules, effectively lowering the stakes of any single political decision. He notes, "If subjugation is too costly, they can change jurisdiction, at least for a large range of issues." This echoes the federalist traditions of thinkers like James Madison and the economic theories of Charles Tiebout, though Hédoin applies them to a modern context of radical inclusion.

Critics might argue that this model privileges the wealthy, who have the resources to move, while trapping the poor in unfavorable jurisdictions. Hédoin anticipates this, noting that the effectiveness of the system depends heavily on "how costly mobility between jurisdictions is." Without low barriers to movement, the "exit option" becomes a privilege rather than a right.

The Stakes of Politics

The most compelling part of Hédoin's analysis is his re-evaluation of where democracy should be most intense. He suggests that the current obsession with national elections is misplaced because the stakes are often too high for meaningful deliberation, yet too low for true inclusion to matter. Instead, he argues for a system where the most inclusive mechanisms are reserved for local issues and constitutional foundations, while intermediate levels rely on more traditional representative methods.

He writes, "It is plausible that in this context, most individuals broadly agree on the general ends to be pursued and only disagree on means to reach them." In these local settings, deliberation is more likely to succeed because the shared goals reduce the friction of disagreement. This challenges the realist view that democracy is inherently unstable. As Hédoin concludes, "Rather than assuming that we know subjugation, deliberation, and disagreement costs, the institutional competition between jurisdictions can serve as a mechanism to reveal them."

The point of polycentricity is also that it facilitates experimentation with collective decision-making procedures.

This experimental approach is a refreshing alternative to the binary choice between "more elections" or "more experts." By allowing different regions to try different methods of inclusion, the system itself becomes a learning machine. It acknowledges that we don't have a perfect formula for democracy yet, but we can find one through trial and error.

Bottom Line

Hédoin's strongest contribution is reframing the debate from "how do we fix voters?" to "how do we structure institutions to minimize the pain of being governed?" His polycentric model offers a pragmatic path forward that respects the need for inclusion without ignoring the reality of human disagreement. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on mobility; without a robust safety net for those who cannot move, this system risks deepening inequality. Watch for how real-world experiments with local governance and sortition test the limits of his theory in the coming years.

Very short summary: This essay explores a neglected aspect of the debate between deliberative democrats and democratic realists regarding the possibility and relevance of more inclusive democratic institutions. I reformulate the debate in terms of the subjugation costs due to a lack of inclusiveness. Hélène Landemore’s open democracy argues that demands for more inclusiveness justify non-electoral democratic mechanisms. Reasoning in terms of subjugation costs makes it possible to evaluate this argument. I argue that subjugation costs depend on the stakes of politics. From this perspective, a polycentric (rather than open) democracy could lower the stakes of politics – thus making demands for inclusiveness less pressing, but also making inclusiveness less costly to implement.

There have been a few significant paradigmatic innovations in the theory of democracy over the last couple of decades. One is the so-called “epistemic turn” in deliberative democracy, which acknowledges that the justification of political institutions largely depends on their epistemic properties for tracking the truth.[1] The other is the emergence of the “realist” account of democracy, which urges us to reconsider our normative expectations about what a democratic regime can and should achieve, especially in light of what we know about voters’ competences.[2]

The interaction between these two innovations can lead to unexpected outcomes. In particular, democratic realists can easily raise skepticism about democratic mechanisms’ ability to produce good outcomes or to uncover the truth. It is then only a small step to the more radical conclusion that some undemocratic regimes are epistemically superior to democracy.[3] [3] Epistemic democrats have countered by arguing that “diversity trumps ability,” meaning collective decisions made by a diverse electorate through democratic mechanisms tend to track the truth better than a more competent but less diverse committee of experts.[4] Also, screening for competence can inadvertently (or, for the most cynical, purposively) introduce social biases in the electorate, as socioeconomic and demographic characteristics are likely to be correlated with competence, however the latter is defined.[5]
