The Wrong Way to Set Speed Limits [ST06]

Jason Slaughter · Not Just Bikes ·Sep 6, 2021 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jason Slaughter dismantles a decades-old traffic engineering myth with a single, uncomfortable truth: the speed limits governing our cities are not based on safety, but on the arbitrary speeds drivers happen to choose. He argues that the standard "85th percentile rule"—which sets limits based on the speed of the fastest 15% of drivers—is a relic of 1960s rural planning that is actively killing people in modern urban environments. For busy leaders and citizens navigating the built environment, this is not just a technical critique; it is a revelation that the very geometry of our streets is designed to encourage the behavior that kills our neighbors.

The Flawed Metric

Slaughter identifies the core mechanism of the problem immediately. He describes the "85th percentile rule" as a process where engineers "build a road, call your lowest paid engineering intern and send them out with a radar gun... then you throw away the top 15 of speeds and the highest speed left is rounded down and that becomes your new speed limit." This approach, he notes, is fundamentally circular: it assumes that if most people are speeding, the limit must be too low, rather than the road design being too dangerous.

The author's distinction between a "road" and a "street" is the intellectual pivot of the piece. He explains that a road is a "high-speed connection between two places," whereas a street is a "complex environment with businesses, houses, and lots of people outside of cars." By applying a rural road metric to a complex urban street, engineers create what Slaughter calls a "strode"—a dangerous hybrid that prioritizes vehicle throughput over human life. This framing is effective because it forces the listener to re-evaluate every wide, straight avenue in their city not as a convenience, but as a design failure.

Critics might argue that lowering speed limits without changing infrastructure could lead to widespread non-compliance and driver frustration. Slaughter anticipates this, noting that simply changing a sign is ineffective because driving is largely a subconscious activity. He writes, "The vast majority of drivers will drive at whatever speed feels comfortable for the road they're driving on." If the road looks fast, people drive fast, regardless of the sign.

Speed is the single biggest factor in the fatality of car crashes. The higher the speed, the more likely it is that a crash will be fatal.

Designing for Safety, Not Compliance

The commentary shifts from diagnosing the problem to exposing the solution, which lies in changing the physical environment rather than the legal one. Slaughter points out that in North America, towns often react to speeding by installing flashing signs, a tactic he dismisses as a "temporary solution" that fails to address the root cause. He argues that drivers need to feel the risk, not just be told about it. "To be safe the street must communicate the real level of risk to the driver," he states, quoting Chuck Marone. "In other words, the driver must feel discomfort driving in a manner that is unsafe."

He contrasts the North American approach with the "civilized countries" like Sweden and the Netherlands, where road geometry dictates speed. He describes how a narrow road with trees planted close to the edge "immediately triggers a subconscious response" that slows drivers down without a single sign. This is the crux of his argument: we do not need better policing or more signs; we need better design. The evidence he presents suggests that when streets are "narrow, complex and twisty," people naturally drive slowly.

A counterargument worth considering is the potential impact on emergency response times or the efficiency of freight movement if all urban streets are narrowed. However, Slaughter suggests that the trade-off is necessary, noting that "the very best streets are those with almost no motor vehicles at all" and that moving vehicles is not the primary purpose of a street.

The old approach was to design the street, check the 85th percentile speed, and set the speed limit accordingly. The modern, safer approach is to choose the speed limit that's appropriate for the context of the street, and if drivers are consistently going faster, the street design should change until drivers are going the desired speed.

Bottom Line

Slaughter's most compelling contribution is his insistence that traffic engineering must stop being a reactive exercise in managing driver behavior and start being a proactive discipline of shaping it through design. The argument's greatest strength is its clarity in separating the function of a road from the function of a street, a distinction that has been blurred for decades. The only vulnerability lies in the political difficulty of retrofitting existing infrastructure, but the moral imperative he outlines is undeniable: we cannot design streets for cars and then blame drivers for the resulting fatalities.
