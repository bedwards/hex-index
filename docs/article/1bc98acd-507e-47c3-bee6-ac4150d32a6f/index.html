Legislating Reality

Michael Huemer · Fake Nous ·Mar 28, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Michael Huemer delivers a blistering critique of a modern intellectual taboo: the belief that moral urgency can rewrite empirical truth. He argues that the drive to suppress hereditarianism—the study of genetic differences in group traits—is not just a strategic error, but a fundamental category mistake that threatens the integrity of all inquiry. In an era where academic appointments are being overturned by legal threats, Huemer's insistence that "reality doesn't care about our values" is a necessary, if uncomfortable, corrective.

The Fallacy of Moral Engineering

Huemer identifies a pervasive error he calls "legislating reality," where the desire to prevent bad outcomes is used to declare certain facts false or unspeakable. He writes, "This is the error of using practical aims, including moral or political goals, to determine what the descriptive facts are, or what one may say the descriptive facts are." This distinction is vital; he is not arguing for the right to publish nuclear bomb plans, but rather against the logic that "Claim x could be used to justify bad behavior; therefore, ~x."

The author anchors this abstract argument in the high-stakes case of philosopher Nathan Cofnas, whose appointment at Ghent University sparked demands for his firing and criminal prosecution. Huemer notes that Belgian Justice Pierre Thiriar argued Cofnas's work violated the "anti-racism law of 30 July 1981," claiming that "packaging a discourse as 'scientific', 'philosophical', or 'critical' does not prevent it from being punishable." This legal maneuver is a stark example of the phenomenon Huemer describes: using the threat of social harm to criminalize a specific line of empirical inquiry. Critics might note that the Belgian legal framework has historically been robust in combating hate speech, suggesting the state sees a genuine link between such theories and incitement. However, Huemer's point remains that the truth of the scientific claim is being adjudicated by its potential misuse, not its evidence.

"Reality doesn't care about your feelings. Nor does it care about our political values. We cannot count on nature to conspire to produce the most politically convenient patterns of facts."

The Logic of the "Racist Argument"

To dismantle the opposition's case, Huemer reconstructs the "Racist Argument" that his critics fear. He breaks it down into six steps, from the empirical claim that Black people have lower average IQs to the moral conclusion that they should be discriminated against. He concedes that the first premise is an "empirical fact that no one likes but is rarely disputed," but he isolates the real moral rot in premises four and five, which assume that genetic differences justify differential treatment.

Huemer argues that the suppression of Cofnas rests on a non sequitur: the idea that because a bad conclusion can be drawn from a fact, the fact itself must be false. He writes, "If the recognition of some fact would lead people to adopt bad policies, that will not stop nature from throwing that fact at us." This is a powerful rhetorical move, comparing the suppression of hereditarianism to ignoring the weather before a dictator's nuclear threat. The argument holds weight because it exposes the irrationality of hoping that ignorance will prevent harm. If the goal is to stop discrimination, attacking the moral premise that "people should be evaluated based on the race that they belong to" is far more effective than attacking the genetic data.

The Epistemological Poison of Guilt-by-Association

Perhaps the most striking part of Huemer's commentary is his attack on "guilt-by-association." He observes that hereditarianism is often rejected simply because it "sounds like stuff that the Nazis said." He counters this by asking if we should deny the law of non-contradiction just because an evil person might use it to justify destroying the human species.

He draws a sharp parallel to the Unabomber, Ted Kaczynski, whose manifesto argued that industrial society harms the environment and destroys freedom. Huemer writes, "Do we now have to put a taboo on anyone's arguing that industrial society harms the environment, etc.? (You don't want to sound like the Unabomber, do you?)" This reference serves as a brilliant historical anchor, reminding readers that the Left itself often champions environmental critiques despite the fact that a mass murderer also held them. By invoking the Unabomber, Huemer forces the reader to confront the absurdity of silencing ideas based on the character of their proponents.

"Morally bad people are not guaranteed to always be factually wrong about everything important that they say."

The author suggests that the current campaign to silence hereditarianism is not only logically flawed but strategically counterproductive. He argues that in a society where "elite culture... is utterly against Jim Crow," the threat of a return to widespread racial discrimination is "close to zero." Instead, he posits that suppression creates a martyr effect, leading observers to think, "Oh, it looks like the hereditarian case is so unanswerable that the left-wing elites are afraid to let us hear it."

Bottom Line

Huemer's strongest contribution is his rigorous separation of moral imperatives from empirical facts, a distinction that is increasingly blurred in modern discourse. His argument is most vulnerable to the counterpoint that some ideas, even if factually true, carry such a high risk of social destabilization that their suppression might be a pragmatic necessity. However, his core verdict stands: if we cannot discuss the facts of human variation without fear of criminal prosecution, we have already lost the battle for a rational society.

1. Legislating Reality

“Legislating reality” is a favorite thought pattern on the left. This is the error of using practical aims, including moral or political goals, to determine what the descriptive facts are, or what one may say the descriptive facts are. The former disjunct is fallacious; the latter is tyrannical and foolish.

Note: I’m not talking about ideas like “you’re not allowed to publish the plans to make an atomic bomb, because that could lead to bad consequences.” I’m talking about arguments like “Claim x could be used to justify bad behavior; therefore, ~x” or “… therefore, no one should be allowed to say x.”

Perhaps there are extreme cases where the second disjunct would be justified, but I haven’t seen any in reality.

2. The Cofnas Case

A recent case is the reaction to the appointment of philosopher Nathan Cofnas to Ghent University in Belgium. This led to demands for Cofnas to be fired, and even criminally prosecuted. For background, Cofnas has argued for hereditarianism, the view that there are real, on-average differences in psychological traits, including cognitive abilities, across groups, including races, and genetic differences are at least part of the cause of this.

Pierre Thiriar (a Justice on the Court of Appeal in Antwerp) wrote about Cofnas:

“… in the light of Belgian criminal law and more specifically the anti-racism law of 30 July 1981 … This is not a borderline case of protected speech, but a coherent discourse that can be qualified as punishable under Articles 20 and 21 of that law. … [P]ackaging a discourse as ‘scientific’, ‘philosophical’, or ‘critical’ does not prevent it from being punishable when it objectively incites discrimination or propagates ideas of racial superiority.”

Thiriar thinks that Cofnas is advocating racial discrimination. I don’t think so; I read him as advocating for an end to the discrimination advanced by the woke left.
