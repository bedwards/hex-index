How New Addictions are Destroying Us

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Feb 23, 2023 · 24 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now delivers a startling diagnosis: our modern crisis isn't just about drugs, but about a civilization engineered to exploit our ancient brain wiring for profit. The author argues that we have shifted from a world of scarcity to one of overwhelming abundance, creating a "post-modern problem" where our evolutionary instincts are hijacked by "limbic capitalism." This is not a passive observation of bad habits; it is a structural critique of an economy that treats human attention as a resource to be mined through mathematically perfected hooks.

The Evolutionary Mismatch

The piece begins by dismantling the traditional view of addiction, expanding the definition far beyond substances to include work, social media, and gaming. Then & Now writes, "The modern world... was an attempt in many ways to fix a pre-modern problem scarcity. What happens when for some of us the problem of scarcity is overcome?" The author suggests that our survival mechanisms, evolved for the savannah, are now fighting a losing battle against a landscape of infinite digital excess. This reframing is powerful because it removes the stigma of personal failure, placing the blame on a mismatch between our biology and our environment.

The argument gains weight when it cites the biological reality: "Studies have shown that substance addictions to nicotine say and behavioral addictions to phones say indistinguishable in brain scans." By equating the neural pathways of a cocaine user with a gaming addict, the author forces us to take behavioral addictions seriously. Critics might note that equating all addictions risks diluting the specific medical severity of substance abuse, but the neurological evidence presented here is compelling. The core issue is that "dopamine is Central to addiction," and modern technology has mastered the art of triggering this neurotransmitter without the natural consequences that once kept our behavior in check.

We've built civilizations on the pursuit of dopamine... but usually evolutionarily speaking it was also rare, a treat.

The Mechanics of Limbic Capitalism

The most distinctive contribution of this piece is the concept of "limbic capitalism." Then & Now explains that this is "a technologically advanced way of doing business that encourages excessive compulsion and addiction targeting of the limbic system." The author traces a direct line from the mathematicians who professionalized gambling in the 17th century to today's tech giants who use algorithms to predict exactly when you will open an app. This is not accidental; it is a calculated strategy. As the author puts it, "Big Tech Giants work on the probabilities of you opening an app at a certain time of day."

The commentary highlights how this system is designed to exploit vulnerability. The text notes a disturbing statistical pattern: "eighty percent of alcohol sales go to 20 of the heaviest users... in social media use the top one percent of users create the majority of content." This reveals that the business model relies on the most addicted individuals to sustain the ecosystem. The author argues that these "limbic hooks appeal to the most vulnerable Among Us," including those with psychological needs, poverty, or genetic predispositions. This is a crucial insight: the market is not serving the average user, but rather harvesting the compulsions of the most susceptible.

Critics might argue that this view removes agency from the individual, suggesting we are helpless against corporate design. However, the author balances this by emphasizing the "compulsion" and "diminishing of our free will" as the defining features of addiction itself, suggesting that the environment has simply become too potent for our unaided willpower.

The Siren Song of the Attention Economy

The piece concludes by framing the attention economy as a modern version of the Sirens in Homer's Odyssey. Then & Now writes, "Odysseus on his way home gets his crew to tie him to the mast to hear the siren song... is it any wonder that this temptation towards sweetness and doom... is such a well-known part of that story?" The author suggests that our modern notifications, red badges, and autoplay features are the digital equivalent of those songs, designed to bypass our "controlled attention" and trigger "automatic attention."

The text points out that even our goals have been weaponized. We are no longer just seeking connection; we are chasing "Duolingo streaks," "inbox zero," and "high scores." The author observes that "the internet has exposed people to goals they barely knew existed," turning self-improvement into a source of endless compulsion. This is the ultimate trap of the post-modern age: we are constantly being pulled toward a horizon of rewards that, once reached, immediately reset, leaving us in a state of perpetual craving.

We live in an attention society that seeks to take possession of our minds over and over from all directions.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's strongest argument is the identification of "limbic capitalism" as a systemic force that has turned human biology into a profit center, making addiction a feature rather than a bug of the modern economy. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its somewhat deterministic tone, which can make the reader feel that resistance is impossible without acknowledging the growing movement for digital minimalism and regulatory reform. Readers should watch for how this framework is applied to future technologies, particularly virtual reality, which promises to make these hooks even more immersive and inescapable.
