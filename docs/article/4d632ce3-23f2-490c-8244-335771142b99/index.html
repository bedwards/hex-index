The Iran Air and Missile War - Ballistic Missiles, Interceptors & Munition Stockpiles

Perun · Perun ·Mar 8, 2026 · 64 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

In a conflict where the fog of war is thickened by generative AI and competing narratives, Perun cuts through the noise not by predicting the outcome, but by exposing the mechanics of the information war itself. While headlines scream of hypersonic barrages and carrier strikes, the author argues that the most critical variable isn't the next missile launch, but the sheer exhaustion of munition stockpiles and the reliability of the data we consume. This is a rare, grounded analysis that treats the 2026 Iran war as a logistical puzzle rather than a video game, forcing readers to question the very footage they are watching.

The Fog of Generative AI

Perun opens with a stark assessment of the current information landscape, noting that "almost as soon as American and Israeli forces launched their attacks on Iran in the closing days of February, the fog of war descended and the propaganda and announcements from both sides seem to be describing almost parallel universes." The author's most distinctive contribution is the detailed breakdown of how modern technology has democratized deception. Unlike previous conflicts where state propaganda required massive infrastructure, Perun points out that "you don't need an expensive globe spanning propaganda arm in order to run an online bot farm" anymore. The argument is bolstered by specific examples of AI-generated content, such as fake satellite images and short, physics-defying videos of missile barrages that have already racked up millions of views.

The author warns that relying on AI tools to verify AI fakes is a dangerous game. Perun illustrates this with a case where an AI tool confidently labeled a fake video of Iran's "Futter" hypersonic missile as real, despite the footage actually being recycled from a TikTok post made weeks before the war began. "Generative AI wasn't built to be an intelligence analysis tool, and it is entirely capable of saying things are real when they're not," Perun writes, highlighting a critical vulnerability in modern media consumption. This insight is vital for busy readers trying to navigate the news cycle; it shifts the burden of proof from the headline to the viewer, demanding a level of skepticism that is often absent in the rush to share.

Critics might argue that focusing so heavily on AI fakes distracts from the very real, unedited atrocities and strategic failures occurring on the ground. However, Perun's point stands: when the baseline of truth is so easily manipulated, distinguishing reality from fabrication becomes the first step in understanding the conflict.

"AI fakes are out there in enormous numbers. And so, if something looks a bit odd, whether it says something you want to be true or not, take the second to check it wherever possible."

The Logistics of Exhaustion

Moving beyond the digital battlefield, Perun pivots to the kinetic reality of the air and missile war, arguing that the narrative of "super weapons" is often a smokescreen for a grinding war of attrition. The author contrasts the Iranian claim of holding back their best arsenal—"We have no intention of deploying all our advanced weapons and equipment from the outset"—with the American assertion of overwhelming success, where President Trump reportedly rated the war a "12 to 15 out of 10." Perun suggests that these conflicting narratives converge on a single, terrifying point: everyone is running out of weapons.

The core of the argument is that the duration and outcome of this conflict will be dictated not by the next dramatic strike, but by inventory levels. Perun writes, "I don't think munition shortages are going to bring this thing to an end in a matter of days," challenging the popular expectation of a swift resolution. This perspective is strengthened by the author's refusal to accept the hype around new Iranian capabilities without evidence, noting that claims of Russian technology transfer to Iranian drones in Cyprus were debunked by matching debris patterns to a drone destroyed in Ukraine long before this war started.

The author also contextualizes the naval threat, referencing the strategic importance of the Straits of Hormuz and drawing a parallel to the 1987 Operation Praying Mantis, where the US Navy engaged Iranian forces in a similar high-stakes environment. Just as that conflict tested the limits of naval air power and missile defense, the 2026 war is testing the limits of global supply chains. Perun notes that while the US claims to have broken the "scoring scale," the reality is a complex contest where "the multi-directional contest between American and Israeli air power, the Iranian missile and drone force, and the air and missile defense systems" is the true measure of success.

A counterargument worth considering is that the sheer volume of cheap drones and missiles might overwhelm even the most robust defense systems, rendering stockpile counts less relevant than the rate of fire. Yet, Perun's focus on the economic war and energy prices suggests that the ability to sustain a high-tempo conflict is the ultimate bottleneck.

The Economic Endgame

The commentary concludes by widening the lens to the economic implications, arguing that the "economic war, energy prices, and the all-important straits of Hormuz" are just as critical as the missile exchanges. Perun emphasizes that the information war is not just about shaping public opinion, but about obscuring the true state of the economy and the global supply chain. The author notes that tools like Ground News can help readers see "what other parts of the internet at any given time might be seeing," breaking the echo chamber that often surrounds such conflicts.

Ultimately, Perun posits that the only honest answer to many of the strategic questions—"Why now? What will happen with the naval war? What about related battlefronts?"—is that "we don't know." This admission of uncertainty is the piece's greatest strength, as it resists the urge to provide a false sense of clarity in a chaotic situation. The author's call to "zoom in specifically on the immensely important air and missile war" serves as a reminder that while the headlines may focus on the spectacular, the war will be won or lost in the mundane details of logistics and verification.

Bottom Line

Perun's analysis is a necessary corrective to the hyperbolic coverage of the 2026 Iran war, shifting the focus from speculative super-weapons to the hard realities of munition depletion and information verification. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that both sides are rational actors managing finite resources, a premise that may not hold if the conflict escalates into a total war of survival. Readers should watch for the next major shift in oil prices and the verification of any new missile claims, as these will likely be the true indicators of the war's trajectory.
