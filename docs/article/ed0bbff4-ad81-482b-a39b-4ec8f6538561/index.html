Hal Johnson doesn't just rank the 1960s; he dismantles the myth of a monolithic decade by curating a list where a train disaster parody sits comfortably next to a posthumous blues recording from a dying man. The piece's most striking claim is that great art transcends its political or historical context, a stance Johnson takes even while highlighting songs that are deeply rooted in the civil rights movement and the counterculture. This is not a nostalgic trip for the uninitiated; it is a sharp, often irreverent dissection of why specific recordings still rattle our nerves decades later.

The Architecture of Mystery and Madness

Johnson begins his deep dive with a song that defies easy categorization, using Frank Zappa's "Let's Make the Water Turn Black" to illustrate how ambiguity can be more potent than clarity. He writes, "There are a few exceptions, though, such as this irresistible little song about the strange pastimes of two young brothers." Rather than accepting the decoded lyrics, Johnson embraces the chaos, noting, "In my mind, Kenny and his buddies are literally taking water and adding various disgusting household items to it until, you know, it turns black." This approach highlights a specific strength in Johnson's commentary: he trusts the listener's imagination over the critic's footnotes. By refusing to explain away the absurdity, he captures the spirit of the era's experimentalism.

This willingness to lean into the bizarre continues with Peter LaFarge's "Trail to Mexico." Johnson acknowledges the song's bitter undertones but frames the performance as deceptively light. "Maybe I'm bad at reading people's tones," he admits, "but LaFarge makes the experience sound so light-hearted and pleasant." This observation is crucial; it suggests that the stoicism of the cowboy tradition often masks deep tragedy, a theme that resonates with the Carter Family's own history of loss and resilience. Just as the Carter Family's legacy was built on the foundational work of the 1920s, LaFarge's song draws from a deep well of folk tradition, even as Johnson playfully misinterprets the emotional weight of the delivery.

"Great art transcends politics. Great art is just soulful enough."

The Politics of Sound and the Ghosts of the Past

When Johnson tackles The Impressions' "We're a Winner," he walks a fine line between acknowledging the political message and prioritizing the sonic experience. He notes the "weird grammar" and the crowd cheers, arguing that the song succeeds because it is "a rousing anthem complete with crowd cheers and weird grammar." He admits his own limitations, stating, "I'm sure I'm missing a lot of political subtext (or, you know, text), but great art transcends politics." Critics might note that this dismissal of subtext risks erasing the specific urgency of the 1967 civil rights context, yet Johnson's point stands: the song's energy is what makes it timeless, not just its message. The track's insistence that "we're moving on up" mirrors the trajectory of Curtis Mayfield's later solo work, creating a narrative arc that Johnson traces with a casual, conversational flair.

The commentary takes a darker turn with Big Bill Broonzy's "This Train." Here, Johnson confronts the anomaly of a 1960s hit recorded by an artist who died in 1958. He questions the categorization: "Does this even count?" before answering with the grim reality of the recording session. "Broonzy was quite literally dying when this song was recorded, his face (the liner notes again) a rictus of pain as he sang." This is the piece's most human moment, stripping away the nostalgia to reveal the physical toll of the music. The song becomes a meditation on mortality, with Johnson noting that Broonzy gives the track "his most poignant performances" precisely because he was facing the end.

The Absurdity of the Everyday

Johnson finds equal value in the trivial and the terrifying, as seen in his analysis of Dave Van Ronk's "Georgie and the IRT." He describes the song as a parody of a grisly train disaster, where the protagonist is "decapitated (alternately: bisected) by the train doors." The humor is dark, but Johnson grounds it in historical specificity, explaining the distinction between the Interborough Rapid Transit and other subway lines. He writes, "His body, it lies between the ties, amidst the dust and dew, And his head it rides the IRT to Flatbush Avenue." This juxtaposition of the macabre with the mundane is a hallmark of the 1960s folk revival, a movement that often used humor to process the harsh realities of urban life.

Even the pop hits get the Johnson treatment. On Nancy Wilson's "How Glad I Am," he argues that the song is "110% only successful because of the performance." He praises her ability to overcome "cloying instrumentation and especially annoying percussion" to deliver a vocal that is "sui generis." This focus on the performer's agency over the production choices is a recurring theme, reminding the reader that the human voice remains the most powerful instrument in the mix.

Bottom Line

Hal Johnson's list is a triumph of selective memory, proving that the 1960s were not just a time of protest and peace but also of absurdity, pain, and pure sonic joy. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to treat the era as a museum exhibit; instead, it treats the music as a living, breathing entity that still has the power to shock and delight. The biggest vulnerability lies in Johnson's occasional dismissal of political context, which may alienate readers looking for a deeper sociological analysis, but for those seeking the raw emotional core of the decade, this commentary is an essential listen.

119 Trail to Mexico by Peter LaFarge (1963).

A genuine rodeo rider and folk singer, Peter LaFarge died young but left behind a legacy…perhaps mostly of songs he wrote (many of which, including “Ira Hayes,” his friend Johnny Cash later covered) but also of songs he recorded, like this great bitter cowboy ballad. In the great stoic cowboy tradition, LaFarge sounds equanimous despite his hardships, climaxing in a faithless love.

Well, damn your gold and the bullets too!God pity a woman who can’t prove true.I’m heading back where the bullets fly,And stay on the trail till the day I die.

118 The Shoop Shoop Song by Betty Everett (1964).

I don’t know how had the idea of naming the song after the background nonsense syllables and not for the chorus hook, but it’s a genius move. The shooping is the best part of an already great song! The whole song is so up-tempo that it kind of rattles my nerves, but it really ratchets up in the…is that the bridge? It happens twice…are there two bridges? Well anyway, Betty Everett may as well be defusing a bomb the way I’m on the edge of my seat. I mean that as a compliment.
