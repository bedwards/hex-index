Excerpt from the original article on The Baseball Nerd

This is Part 8 of our nine-week investigation. We’ve documented Congressional inaction, proven statistical cases for Bonds, Clemens, and Rodriguez, exposed MLB’s ten billion dollar profit from the steroid era, and questioned whether steroids even enhanced performance as dramatically as assumed. This week we examine the Baseball Hall of Fame’s selective application of character standards. They display steroid-era artifacts while excluding the players who created them. They inducted Bud Selig while excluding Barry Bonds. They celebrate Gaylord Perry despite his admitted cheating. They ignore segregation advantages and amphetamine use but exclusively punish steroid users. The Hall of Fame doesn’t uphold moral principles. It protects institutional reputation at players’ expense.

The National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum is located in Cooperstown, New York. It’s a beautiful building filled with artifacts from baseball’s 150-year history. Bats, balls, gloves, uniforms, photographs, plaques. Everything that tells the story of America’s pastime.

Including artifacts from the steroid era.

Barry Bonds’s 756th home run ball, the one that broke Hank Aaron’s all-time record, is on display at the Hall of Fame. Mark McGwire’s bat from his 70-home-run season is there. Sammy Sosa’s equipment is there. The Hall charges admission to see these artifacts. They market them. They use them to tell baseball’s story.

But Barry Bonds isn’t in the Hall of Fame. Mark McGwire isn’t in the Hall of Fame. Sammy Sosa isn’t in the Hall of Fame. The institution displays their achievements while excluding them as achievers. It profits from their history while denying them recognition.

This is the Hall of Fame’s core hypocrisy. They want to be a museum when convenient and a church when expedient. Museums preserve all of history. Churches celebrate moral examples. You can’t be both.

The Artifacts Without the Men

Walk into the Hall of Fame and you’ll see Mark McGwire’s 70th home run bat from 1998. It’s in a display case celebrating one of baseball’s most memorable seasons. The caption describes the home run chase between McGwire and Sammy Sosa that captivated the nation and saved baseball after the 1994 strike.

What the caption doesn’t mention: McGwire later admitted to using steroids during that season. The achievement on display was chemically enhanced. The Hall knows this. They keep the bat and ball on display anyway. They charge people to see it. They market it in their promotional materials.
