What Eating the Rich Did For Japan

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Oct 10, 2021 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Asianometry delivers a startling historical pivot: the collapse of Japan's ultra-wealthy families wasn't a revolution, but a series of calculated, almost bureaucratic dismantlings that turned plutocrats into regular taxpayers. The piece stands out by tracing the precise mechanics of how state assets were privatized into oligarchic empires, only to be systematically stripped away by war, inflation, and occupation policy. For busy readers tracking modern wealth inequality, this is a masterclass in how quickly concentrated power can evaporate when the state decides it must.

The Architecture of the Zaibatsu

The narrative begins not with greed, but with desperation. Following the arrival of Commodore Matthew Perry, Japan faced an existential threat that required rapid industrialization. The Meiji government initially tried to lead this charge through state-owned enterprises, but the strategy failed financially. "Thus in the early 1880s the government tried transferring these government-managed assets to private hands," Asianometry writes, noting that these sales were often conducted at book value or to the highest bidder. This was the genesis of the zaibatsu, or "financial clans," who acquired mines, shipyards, and textile mills for pennies on the dollar.

The author effectively highlights the sheer scale of this transfer, comparing it to the creation of Russian oligarchs in the 1990s. Families like Mitsui and Sumitomo leveraged their political connections to secure exclusive rights to natural resources. "The merchant families who took on these assets and benefited from them would come to create the zaibatsu," the text explains. These were not just companies; they were vertically integrated mini-economies where a family's mining profits funded their chemical plants, which in turn supplied their shipbuilding subsidiaries. This structure allowed them to dominate the domestic market with little government pushback.

The wealth of the top 1 percent grew 3.5 times faster than the national average.

This disparity was tolerated because the zaibatsu served the state's ideological goals of militarization. However, the author points out a critical flaw in this arrangement: the families' focus on short-term profits eventually clashed with the military government's long-term war aims. As the state tightened its grip, the families found their investment decisions de facto controlled by the government, signaling the beginning of the end for their unchecked power.

The Great Unraveling

The dismantling of the zaibatsu was not a single event but a multi-stage process involving war damage, land reform, and aggressive taxation. Asianometry details how the government confiscated agricultural land to boost food production, inadvertently stripping landlords of their rental income. "The intention was to stimulate food production for the war effort but it was also accidental land reform," the author notes. This was followed by post-war measures that taxed family capital at rates as high as 85 percent.

The most dramatic moment came when the Holding Company Liquidation Commission (HCLC) physically removed the stocks and bonds of the zaibatsu families. "They loaded up 1.25 billion yen worth of stocks and bonds belonging to the zaibatsu holding companies into two trucks and drove them to a bank," Asianometry writes. This was a literal seizure of the financial engine that powered the families' empires. Executives were purged, and the families were forced to sell off their assets.

Critics might note that the author somewhat glosses over the moral ambiguity of the families' wartime collaboration, suggesting they "escaped larger punishment" despite their role in the war machine. While the text acknowledges the families profited from aggression, it emphasizes the lack of a clear paper trail, which may have softened the blow. Yet, the economic outcome remains undeniable: the families were financially neutered.

The families were sucked out of their companies like meat out of an alaskan king crab leg at a boiling crab restaurant.

The final blow was hyperinflation. The families were allowed to withdraw their compensation only after years of triple-digit inflation, which wiped out 97 percent of the real value of their cash holdings. By 1950, the top 1 percent of earners were no longer living off passive wealth; they were earning their income through employment. The plutocracy had been dissolved, not by a mob, but by policy.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's strongest argument lies in its granular accounting of how wealth concentration can be reversed through a combination of asset seizure, taxation, and inflation, offering a stark counter-narrative to the idea that wealth is permanent. The piece's vulnerability is its slight tendency to frame the families as passive victims of circumstance rather than active participants in a system they helped build and exploit. Readers should watch for how this historical precedent informs current debates on wealth taxes and antitrust enforcement in the modern era.
