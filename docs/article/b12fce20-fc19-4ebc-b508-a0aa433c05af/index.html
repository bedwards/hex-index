What Happened to Singapore's Tsmc?

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Dec 22, 2020 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Asianometry delivers a sobering autopsy of a national ambition that never quite found its footing: Singapore's attempt to build its own TSMC. The piece is notable not for revealing a secret failure, but for dissecting exactly how a competent, well-resourced state strategy can still be crushed by the sheer scale and speed of global semiconductor consolidation. In an era where governments are rushing to subsidize chip manufacturing, this story serves as a critical case study in why capital and policy alone cannot guarantee dominance in a winner-take-all industry.

The Whale Fall Strategy

The author begins by contrasting Singapore's industrial philosophy with that of its neighbors. While Taiwan nurtured small private entities to commercialize technology, Singapore relied on attracting massive multinational corporations to create a "whale fall" ecosystem. Asianometry writes, "You start getting spillover effects transfers of technology and skill sets coming over from foreigners to singaporean locals as the theory goes... these multinationals like ibm or uh hp they're your whale and then they spawn a whole ecosystem around them." This metaphor is striking because it visualizes the passive nature of Singapore's approach: waiting for a giant to die and decompose to feed the local economy. The commentary effectively highlights the risk in this strategy; when the whale is healthy and mobile, it can simply swim away.

The narrative then pivots to the birth of Chartered Semiconductor Manufacturing (CSM) in 1987, launched by the state-owned entity ST Engineering. The government hoped to create a national champion similar to its success with Singapore Technologies Group in defense. Asianometry notes that CSM was founded with outdated technology from a US partner, forcing an aggressive internal R&D push to catch up. "Almost immediately csm began an in-house r d lab in order to improve the process tech they got from sierra highly motivated to advance as fast as possible they pushed ahead quickly." This rapid ascent is impressive on paper, with the company climbing from 3.0-micron to 0.5-micron processes in less than a decade. The author's framing here is crucial: it establishes that Singapore had the talent and the drive. The failure was not a lack of capability, but a shift in the global battlefield.

The Singapore government is extremely competent patient and effective at what it does. They deserve success but success is never guaranteed.

The Pincer Movement

The core of the argument lies in the "pincer movement" that eventually squeezed CSM out of the market. Asianometry identifies two distinct forces converging on the Singaporean champion. First, the entry of low-cost foundries from mainland China, particularly Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation (SMIC). The author explains that China offered the same technology licenses as the West but at a fraction of the cost. "China can offer just as much as singapore but on even bigger scale." This observation is vital for modern readers; it underscores that in the semiconductor foundry business, scale often trumps efficiency. If a competitor can undercut you by 20% while offering the same node, the market will inevitably migrate.

Simultaneously, the high-end market was being locked down by giants with deeper pockets. TSMC and Samsung entered the fray with massive capital expenditures that CSM could not match. Asianometry writes, "Samsung stole the business of servicing high-end customers looking for either diversification additional capacity or better pricing from tsmc... samsung kicked off a war that csm could not compete in." The author provides a stark financial reality check: gross margins for CSM plummeted to 11% in 2005, while TSMC enjoyed 44%. The commentary effectively uses these numbers to illustrate the impossibility of competing in a capital-intensive race where the cost of a leading-edge fab jumped from $3 billion to $19.5 billion in a single generation. A counterargument worth considering is whether Singapore could have succeeded if it had focused on niche, specialized processes earlier rather than chasing the leading edge, but the author suggests the market dynamics made this difficult.

The Exit and the Legacy

The narrative concludes with the 2009 acquisition of CSM by GlobalFoundries, a venture backed by Abu Dhabi's sovereign wealth fund. The deal marked the end of Singapore's dream of an independent semiconductor champion. Asianometry writes, "Global foundries never managed to execute on their business strategy... unable to keep up with the tech race and now locked out of the premium market." The author points out a lingering, uncomfortable question: did the local population actually benefit from the decades of investment? "A significant percentage of the jobs including on the senior level went to highly paid expat foreigners that did not much change throughout the 22 years of losses." This is a sharp critique of the "whale fall" model; while the ecosystem grew, the highest-value roles often remained with foreign experts, leaving the domestic workforce with a mixed legacy.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's strongest asset is its refusal to blame the failure on incompetence, instead attributing it to the brutal economics of an industry where scale and speed are the only currencies that matter. The piece's biggest vulnerability is a slight underestimation of how Singapore successfully pivoted to become a hub for equipment and materials rather than manufacturing, a nuance that might soften the defeat. For any government currently drafting chip subsidies, the lesson is clear: building a foundry is not just about money; it is about surviving a race with no finish line where the competitors keep moving the goalposts.
