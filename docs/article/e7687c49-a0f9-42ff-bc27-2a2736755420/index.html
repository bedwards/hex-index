The U.S. Operation in Venezuela - Maduro's Capture & what next for Venezuela?

Perun · Perun ·Jan 12, 2026 ·66 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Perun delivers a startling narrative: a U.S. special forces raid that extracted Venezuela's president in under two hours, an event the author argues was not a fluke but the opening move of a broader geopolitical earthquake. While many observers expected a quiet 2026, Perun asserts that the rapid deployment of American assets to Caracas reveals a military capability gap so vast it renders traditional resistance nearly impossible. This is not just a story about one raid; it is a case study in how modern superpowers can dismantle regimes with surgical precision, leaving the rest of the world to scramble for a new security order.

The Buildup and the Bluff

Perun begins by dismantling the idea that this operation was a surprise. The author meticulously details the months of military posturing that preceded the raid, noting that the United States had been steadily concentrating assets in the region since late 2025. "The US relationship with the government in Karacus going into 2026 was absolutely dismal and what we saw through late 2025 was the United States steadily concentrate more and more military assets in the region." This framing is crucial because it shifts the narrative from a sudden shock to an inevitable culmination of pressure. The author points to the arrival of the USS Ewima, a Wasp-class amphibious warfare ship, and the massive Gerald R. Ford supercarrier as undeniable signals of intent.

The commentary here is sharp, distinguishing between the theoretical threat of an invasion and the practical reality of a targeted strike. Perun writes, "What they were capable of doing though was bringing a lot of air and surface warfare assets to the table, parking a giant threat in being off the Venezuelan coast and enforcing the evolving tanker blockade." This distinction matters. It suggests the U.S. did not need to occupy the entire country to achieve its goals; it only needed to dominate the skies and seas long enough to execute a specific mission. The author effectively uses satellite imagery and ship movements to build a timeline that makes the raid feel less like a gamble and more like a calculated execution.

The Asymmetry of Power

The core of Perun's argument rests on the sheer disparity between the U.S. military machine and Venezuela's defensive capabilities. The author dissects the Venezuelan inventory with a critical eye, noting that while they possessed decent hardware on paper, the reality was far grimmer. "Readiness rates though are a massive question mark. A lot of those F-16s probably aren't operational." This point is vital for understanding why the operation was so swift. The author argues that even if the Venezuelan Air Force had scrambled, the engagement would have been less a dogfight and more "a game of one-sided blip hunter."

Perun goes further, analyzing the ground-based air defense systems. Despite rumors of Russian upgrades, the author finds little evidence of the cutting-edge technology needed to counter stealth aircraft. "In fact, I checked CRI's transfer data for Venezuela going back two decades and you will find much older systems like JY1's delivered in the early 2000s, but so far nothing more modern." This evidence supports the claim that the operation was not a setup or a match-fix, but a genuine display of overwhelming force. The author suggests that the Venezuelan leadership likely knew their aircraft were obsolete and chose not to risk them, a decision that speaks volumes about the psychological impact of U.S. air superiority.

"The qualitative gulf between the Venezuelans and the Americans would have been wider than it was between the Russians and Ukrainians back in 2022."

This comparison is striking and perhaps the most provocative claim in the piece. By placing the U.S.-Venezuela dynamic above the Russia-Ukraine conflict in terms of technological disparity, Perun underscores the unique position of the United States as a military hegemon. Critics might note that this comparison overlooks the complexities of asymmetric warfare, where a weaker force can still inflict significant costs through guerrilla tactics or urban combat. However, Perun's focus on the specific context of a high-speed extraction raid makes the argument compelling: in a contest of speed and air dominance, the gap is indeed insurmountable.

The Shadow Fleet and the Future

The author concludes by looking beyond the immediate raid to the broader implications for global security. Perun introduces the concept of the "Shadow Fleet"—a network of tankers used to bypass sanctions—and notes that the U.S. is now actively targeting these vessels. "Then we'll look at the aftermath, where the United States and Venezuela stand now, before switching over to some of the ways in which this arguably very much isn't over yet, introducing America's ongoing campaign of systemically touching other people's boats in the form of Shadow Fleet Tankers." This shift from a kinetic operation to a campaign of economic strangulation highlights the multifaceted nature of modern warfare.

The commentary here is forward-looking, suggesting that the raid on Maduro is just the beginning. The author warns that the global security order is shifting, with countries from Colombia to Denmark paying close attention. "And so today, with the smoke starting to clear on the entire affair, I thought it was time to talk about it. Realistically, I'd argue there are at least three big elements to this story." The author's ability to connect a specific military event to broader geopolitical trends is the piece's greatest strength. It moves the reader from the immediate shock of the raid to a deeper understanding of the changing rules of engagement in the 21st century.

Bottom Line

Perun's analysis is a masterclass in connecting military details to strategic outcomes, offering a clear-eyed view of how modern superpowers project power. The strongest part of the argument is the evidence-based dismantling of the idea that Venezuela had a realistic chance of resisting, which reframes the raid as a demonstration of capability rather than a desperate gamble. The biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that such overwhelming force will lead to stable outcomes, a question the author hints at but does not fully resolve. Readers should watch for how the U.S. continues to target the shadow fleet, as this could signal a new era of aggressive economic warfare that extends far beyond Venezuela's borders.
