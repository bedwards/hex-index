In a year where climate funding feels stagnant and cancer research often hits biological walls, Packy McCormick identifies a paradoxical truth: the market's retreat from hype is actually creating the discipline required for genuine breakthroughs. This week's dose doesn't just list good news; it reframes the current economic tightening as a necessary filter that is separating vaporware from the companies and scientists actually building the future.

The Great Climate Filter

McCormick opens by challenging the narrative that the climate tech sector is dying. While capital is indeed tightening, the author argues this is a feature, not a bug. Citing the Silicon Valley Bank Future of Climate Tech report, McCormick writes, "The market is cutting hype, forcing discipline, and rewarding the companies that actually build." This is a crucial distinction for investors and policymakers alike. The data shows that while $29 billion was invested last year, the conversation has quieted significantly, with mentions of climate on public earnings calls down 70%.

This silence, McCormick suggests, is healthy. "Government support is pulling back, making things slower and harder," he notes, pointing out that 52% of companies are cutting burn rates. The argument here is that the era of easy money masking inefficiency is over, and the survivors will be those with viable unit economics. A counterargument worth considering is that this retrenchment might stifle the high-risk, long-term R&D that only government subsidies can support, potentially slowing the pace of innovation in critical areas like carbon capture. However, the author's framing holds weight: the category needs to prove it can stand on its own before it can scale.

The Pancreatic Breakthrough

Shifting from economics to biology, McCormick highlights a trial that challenges a decades-old dogma in oncology. For years, pancreatic cancer was considered an "immune desert," biologically incapable of responding to immunotherapy. McCormick writes, "Pancreatic cancer was supposed to be the cancer that immunotherapy couldn't touch." Yet, new data from a Phase 1 trial of a personalized mRNA vaccine suggests otherwise.

The trial, led by Dr. Vinod Balachandran, involved 16 patients who received a custom-built vaccine alongside a checkpoint inhibitor. The results were stark: of the eight patients who mounted a T-cell response, seven are still alive six years later. McCormick captures the human stakes perfectly: "One of those seven — Donna Gustafson, who was the first person in the world to receive one of these vaccines in February 2020 — just hiked Mt. Etna for her 50th wedding anniversary."

This success story connects to the broader history of checkpoint inhibitors, a class of drugs that first showed promise in melanoma before struggling in other cancers. The key insight here is that the "wall" of pancreatic cancer isn't impenetrable; it just needed the right map. McCormick argues, "The personalized mRNA playbook that Moderna/Merck have been running in melanoma is generalizable, and generalizing fast." While critics might note the small sample size and the lack of randomization in this Phase 1 study, the persistence of the T-cell clones into the memory phase offers a mechanistic explanation for the survival benefit that goes beyond statistical noise.

If this holds up at scale, it's a twofer for our anti-cancer canon: pancreatic cancer is not immunologically untouchable, and the personalized mRNA playbook is generalizable.

Autonomy and the Complete Loop

The commentary then pivots to the convergence of AI and physical infrastructure, highlighting a massive strategic move by SpaceX. McCormick details a deal where SpaceX effectively purchased a call option on Cursor, a coding AI tool, for a potential $60 billion. "SpaceX basically purchasing a call option on Cursor for $10 billion is the kind of story we would have covered in the Dose anyway," McCormick writes, framing the deal as a necessary step for vertical integration.

The logic is compelling: Cursor has the software but lacks the compute, while SpaceX has the compute but needs the software to manage its massive operations. "Put the two together, and you might have something that can compete with the leading labs in a use case that they all agree is incredibly important to win." This mirrors the broader trend of designing hardware specifically for autonomy rather than retrofitting human-centric designs. McCormick points to Humble, a startup that removed the driver's cab entirely to optimize for autonomous trucking. "Trucks were never designed to be autonomous," founder Eyal Cohen says, a sentiment McCormick embraces as the key to the next generation of logistics. By removing the cab, Humble achieves 360-degree sensor coverage and lighter weight, proving that "when you design around the actual constraint instead of the inherited one, you end up with a different machine."

The TSMC of Biology

Finally, McCormick explores the physical infrastructure of drug discovery with the launch of Medra, a robotic bio-lab. The bottleneck in AI-driven biology has never been the design of molecules, but the physical testing of them. "A designed molecule is not a validated molecule," McCormick writes, emphasizing that every candidate still needs a human to pipette and test. Medra aims to solve this by deploying 100 robotic arms that can run experiments 24/7, using computer vision to operate legacy lab equipment that wasn't built for robots.

The ambition is to become the "TSMC for biology," a foundry where drug companies can run experiments without owning a wet lab. McCormick notes that this could push the share of automated bio-tech tasks from 5% to 75%. The implication is profound: if the speed of iteration increases, the timeline for cures shrinks. "Lee believes that with more throughput, we 'cure cancer, Alzheimer's, infectious disease,'" McCormick reports. While the challenge of integrating AI with the messy reality of wet labs is non-trivial, the move to instrument every arm and bench with sensors to log exact protocols suggests a level of data granularity previously impossible.

Bottom Line

Packy McCormick's strongest argument is that the current market contraction and technological bottlenecks are not signs of failure, but the necessary friction required to separate viable solutions from hype. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its optimism about the speed of scaling these complex technologies, particularly the personalized mRNA vaccines and robotic labs, which face regulatory and manufacturing hurdles that a single trial or prototype cannot overcome. However, the core thesis remains compelling: we are moving from a phase of speculation to a phase of disciplined execution, and the results are already visible in the data.

The market is cutting hype, forcing discipline, and rewarding the companies that actually build.

Kaitlin Sullivan, Marina Kopf and Anne Thompson for NBC News

Pancreatic cancer is often a death sentence. It kills about 87% of the people it’s diagnosed in within five years. Worse, it’s sometimes called the “silent killer” because symptoms don’t show up until very late, and by the time they do, only about 1 in 10 patients has a tumor that’s still operable. For the rest, there are no good options. And for a long time, oncologists thought the disease was biologically incapable of mounting a meaningful immune response at all. Pancreatic cancer was supposed to be the cancer that immunotherapy couldn’t touch.

This week at the AACR annual meeting in San Diego, Dr. Vinod Balachandran of Memorial Sloan Kettering presented six-year follow-up data from a Phase 1 trial of a personalized mRNA vaccine for pancreatic cancer that strongly suggests otherwise.

The trial enrolled 16 patients with early-stage, operable pancreatic cancer. After surgery, each got a custom-built mRNA vaccine (autogene cevumeran, developed by BioNTech and Genentech) alongside the checkpoint inhibitor atezolizumab and standard chemotherapy.
