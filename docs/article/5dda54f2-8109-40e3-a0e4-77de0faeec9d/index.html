Turkey is building its first fullyfledged aircraft carrier, a vessel more than 300 m long. Once completed, it will be shorter only than American and Chinese super carriers. This powerful mu class ship whose construction began in 2025 stands as a symbol of the path Turkey has taken under Rip Taipe Erdan. The new Sultan of the Bosphorus has ruled the country for more than two decades.

During that time, Turkey has undergone the most profound transformation since the era of the republic's [music] founder, Mustafa Kemal Ataturk. Erdogan has fundamentally redefined Turkeykey's international position. After decades spent on the margins of the geopolitical chessport, the country has emerged as a textbook example of an assertive regional power, one determined to leverage its full potential and play for the highest stakes. Today, much like in the days of the Ottoman Empire, Turkey once again projects power far beyond the Anatolian hardland.

Turkish influence now stretches from Syria and Libya through the caucuses all the way to the Horn of Africa. But every empire exacts a price. Turkey is no exception. The foundations of the power structure built by Erdogan are showing increasing signs of erosion.

A deep economic crisis, the concentration of authority in the president's hands, and routine crackdowns of political opposition have become the defining features of Erdogan's third decade in power. Together, these trends have driven down support for both the president and his justice and development party, AK, making it progressively harder for them to retain control. Under constitutional changes introduced by Erdogan several years ago, 2028 should mark the end of his presidency. Although the president has publicly declared that he does not intend to seek another term, the ruling camp is simultaneously pushing for a new constitutional overhaul, one that could either clear the way for Erdogan to remain in office or the very least secure his influence for years to come.

These suspicions are not unfounded. As work on new constitution proceeds, potential rivals are being systematically neutralized. The most striking example has been the arrest of Ekram Imamoglu, who in some polls was projected to defeat Erdogan in a future presidential election. It remains unclear whether we are witnessing the final chapter of Erdogan's rule, but the current geopolitical moment offers a timely opportunity to examine the mechanisms that have allowed him to stay in power for more than 20 years.
