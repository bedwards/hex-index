9 Takeaways from the JP Morgan Chase Energy Study You Won't Want to Miss

Various · Energy Bad Boys ·Mar 28, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

The energy debate has shifted overnight from a moral crusade for decarbonization to a brutal calculus of reliability and cost. Various, writing for Energy Bad Boys, delivers a stinging critique of the current grid trajectory, arguing that the rush toward intermittent renewables has collided with an insatiable, 24/7 demand from data centers. This isn't just another market update; it is a warning that the "green transition" as currently structured may be the largest misallocation of capital in American history.

The Data Center Dilemma

The core of the argument rests on a single, undeniable fact: data centers are breaking the grid's economics. Various highlights that the PJM Interconnection region, home to "data center alley," now hosts 67 gigawatts of existing and planned capacity. This concentration has triggered a crisis in capacity payments—essentially insurance premiums paid to generators to ensure power is available during peak times. Without a cap, recent auctions would have cleared at a staggering $530 per megawatt per day.

"Last point: some utilities within PJM are questioning whether re-regulation would be the better option (Exelon, First Energy, PPL, and PSEG); I agree with them."

This quote is the piece's most provocative pivot. It suggests that the decades-long experiment with deregulation, which began in earnest in the 1990s to spur competition, may be collapsing under the weight of its own complexity. The author argues that the market mechanism is failing to price in the true cost of reliability. Critics might note that re-regulation often leads to political interference and inefficiency, but the sheer scale of the data center load makes the current market design look increasingly fragile.

The Solar and Storage Mismatch

Perhaps the most damning section addresses the economics of solar and storage. The author points out a fundamental flaw in the current build-out: data centers drive load at night, precisely when solar panels are useless. Despite seven states attempting to impose higher rates on data centers to offset these costs, the specialized rates are still too low to fund new solar-plus-storage projects.

"It is increasingly looking like the rush to build solar and storage in the United States may go down as one of the largest misallocations of capital in our nation's history."

Various supports this with hard data: solar power purchase agreements (PPAs) have skyrocketed from under $30 per megawatt-hour in 2019 to over $60 today. The report notes that even with these rising costs, specialized rates can cover a natural gas plant but fail to cover the cost of solar plus battery storage. This is a crucial distinction. While proponents argue that battery costs are falling, the author counters that the duration of storage required to back up a 24/7 load makes the math impossible with current technology.

"Nighttime load can be viewed as positive as it represents consistent load that allows utilities to monetize capital deployed assets and does not put additional strain during peak hours; but it's another sign of rising data center demand."

This observation reframes the narrative. Instead of seeing nighttime load as a grid asset, the author reveals it as a symptom of a deeper problem: the grid is being forced to build expensive, specialized infrastructure to serve a load profile that intermittent renewables cannot serve.

The Reliability Gap and the Gas Resurgence

The commentary then turns to the supply side, noting a global shortage of gas turbines. Demand for these machines now exceeds production capacity through 2030. Major manufacturers like GE Vernova, Siemens, and Mitsubishi are ramping up production, yet the backlog remains a bottleneck.

"The U.S. is now at pre-2005 levels of firm capacity on the grid at a time when electricity demand is projected to have the largest increases in over a decade due to data center and AI growth and electrification efforts."

This comparison to pre-2005 levels is striking. It underscores that while the headline number for new capacity builds is high, the reliability of that capacity is plummeting. New additions are increasingly wind and solar, which require "derating" because they don't produce power on demand. The author argues that the U.S. is effectively running a grid with less reliable power than it had two decades ago, just as demand is exploding.

"On this kind of topic, stick to peer-reviewed pieces in publications like Joule. EMBER's article is more a reflection of the world the authors want to exist rather than the world as it really is."

Here, the author takes a direct shot at the think tank EMBER, which claimed "baseload" solar was competitive. By dismissing this as wishful thinking, the author reinforces the central thesis: the market is not a theoretical model; it is a physical reality where physics dictates that you cannot run a 24/7 data center on 90% solar without massive, currently uneconomic storage.

The Nuclear Reality Check

Finally, the piece addresses the "silver bullet" of Small Modular Reactors (SMRs). While investor enthusiasm is high, the economic reality is grim. The report cites a 2025 analysis estimating SMR costs between $200 and $400 per megawatt-hour, far above the $130 threshold for affordability.

"The burden of proof for economic viability still lay with the SMR industry."

This is a sobering reminder that optimism does not equal economics. The author notes that even the Tennessee Valley Authority's analysis of a "first of its kind" SMR resulted in a price of $196 per megawatt-hour. While nuclear is essential for long-term decarbonization, the author argues it cannot solve the immediate affordability crisis.

"2025 was a bad year for battery revenues. The reason: 9 GW of new battery capacity in ERCOT saturated Ancillary Services and pushed storage into diurnal energy market competition, compressing spreads across the grid and increasing storage capacity by 70x since 2020."

The reference to the "Donner Party" in the context of ERCOT battery revenues is a vivid metaphor for cannibalization. As more batteries flood the market, the price spreads they rely on for profit vanish, potentially leaving the grid without the incentive to build the very storage it needs.

Bottom Line

Various delivers a necessary corrective to the prevailing optimism about the green transition, grounding the debate in the hard constraints of physics and economics. The strongest part of the argument is the clear link between data center load profiles and the failure of solar-plus-storage economics. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability is its heavy reliance on current market signals, which may shift if policy interventions or technological breakthroughs in storage occur faster than anticipated. The reader should watch closely to see if the "re-regulation" trend in PJM becomes a national precedent or an isolated reaction to a unique crisis.

On March 3rd, JPMorgan Chase released its 16th Annual Eye on the Market Energy Paper. This year’s report, written by Michael Cembalest, is titled “Fighting Words,” and it is a 98-page analysis with hundreds of graphs and charts on the state of the energy industry.

It spans most aspects of the energy industry, but as with all things energy, much of this year’s report centers on the impact of data centers on cost and reliability. Also of note are discussions on the cost of solar and storage, conventional fuels, small modular reactors (SMRs), electricity prices, and battery storage economics.

Here are the nine takeaways we found most interesting from the study, hereafter referred to as the JPMC report.

1. The Data Center Price Debate: A PJM Deep Dive

Data center impacts on customer costs continue to dominate the headlines for electricity affordability. The JPCM report notes:

The PJM region (data center alley: VA, PA, MD, OH) has 67 GW of existing and planned data center capacity, the largest cluster in the U.S. PJM has attracted attention due to spikes in its capacity payments, which are “insurance premiums” paid to generators to commit future supply or commit to demand response reductions during peak demand. Without the cap, the recent PJM auction would have cleared at $530 per MW per day.

While capacity payments take place in wholesale markets, they’re partially flowing through to retail power prices in MD and NJ. Factors driving the spike in PJM capacity payments include retirement of thermal assets, data centers and declines in capacity accreditation for solar and storage.

The reductions in capacity accreditation for solar and storage were overdue, and the recent increase in accreditation for onshore wind seems risky to us, as MISO, which, in fairness, has much more wind capacity than PJM, has the capacity value in the mid-teens.
