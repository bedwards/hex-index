The Epidemic of Fake Disease

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Feb 25, 2020 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis delivers a jarring but necessary truth: the medical tests we trust to save our lives may actually be harming us by manufacturing "fake disease." In an era obsessed with early detection, Francis argues that our most advanced screening tools are often guilty of lead time bias and overdiagnosis, turning healthy people into patients for conditions that would never have killed them.

The Illusion of Early Detection

Francis begins by dismantling the comforting narrative that finding a problem early is always a victory. He illustrates this with two stark case studies: Kevin, a man who undergoes a risky heart stent procedure for a "ticking time-bomb" artery that was actually stable, and Mary, a woman who loses a breast to chemotherapy for a cancer that likely never would have caused symptoms. "In both cases we know that in fact the likely outcome of them undergoing those initial tests is that they've been harmed more than they've benefited," Francis writes. This is a devastating claim, yet it rests on solid clinical evidence that stable, asymptomatic blockages rarely lead to heart attacks, and that aggressive screening often catches indolent tumors that would have remained dormant.

The author's use of anecdote here is strategic. By humanizing the statistics, he forces the reader to confront the physical reality of medical intervention—bleeding, swelling, infection, and permanent disability—rather than abstract survival rates. Critics might argue that this approach risks causing unnecessary anxiety or discouraging people from seeking legitimate care, but Francis is careful to distinguish between symptomatic patients who need treatment and asymptomatic ones who are being over-treated. He notes that for unstable patients having chest pain, stents are indeed "life saving and one of the best therapies we have in medicine," but the danger lies in applying that same logic to healthy bodies.

The Trap of Statistics

The piece's most technical yet vital section tackles how we measure medical success. Francis exposes the flaw in using five-year survival rates as a metric for screening efficacy, a statistic often weaponized by politicians and advocates to claim victory. He points to Rudy Giuliani's comparison of US and UK prostate cancer survival rates, where the US boasted an 82% survival rate versus the UK's 44%, despite identical death rates. "When we hear five-year survival rates have improved you assume it's because people are dying later but actually it's because we're diagnosing them earlier," Francis explains. This phenomenon, known as lead time bias, creates a statistical mirage where patients appear to live longer simply because their disease was named sooner, not because their death was delayed.

"There's no link between five-year survival and how many people are dying from a disease which is of course the thing that any sufferer cares about."

Francis argues that we must stop using survival rates and focus entirely on mortality—the actual number of people who die from the disease. This is a crucial distinction that cuts through the noise of marketing and political spin. The correlation between improved survival rates and reduced death rates is, as he notes, effectively zero for many common cancers. This reframing is powerful because it shifts the goalpost from "how long do we know you have the disease?" to "did the test actually keep you alive?"

The Rise of "Fake Disease"

As diagnostic technology becomes more sensitive, Francis warns of a surge in "overdiagnosis," where we detect abnormalities that are biologically cancerous but clinically harmless. He describes these as "fake disease," defining them as conditions that are "not causing and would not cause that patient any problems whatsoever." He uses a compelling graph to illustrate that while cancer diagnoses have skyrocketed over the last forty years, death rates have remained remarkably stable. "Either our treatments are getting better at exactly the same rate as the increase in cases or we're diagnosing things that aren't causing any harm," he posits. The evidence strongly suggests the latter.

He extends this logic to modern technology, comparing high-risk lung cancer screening trials that showed no mortality benefit to the Apple Watch's detection of atrial fibrillation in low-risk users. "While I've made it clear that I think the potential for what we can do with the Apple watch is very exciting and it's a fantastic technology when I say that it's unlikely to save lives I'm normally met with complete incredulity," Francis admits. This highlights a cultural blind spot: we are so enamored with the ability to detect anything, anywhere, that we forget to ask if the detection matters. The rise of "incidentalomas"—unexpected findings on scans like cardiac MRIs—further complicates the landscape, turning healthy individuals into patients with a single, often unnecessary, scan.

"Disease from the Latin literally meaning without ease is not present if the pathology is not causing any symptoms nor adversely affecting the person's life."

Francis concludes by acknowledging the power of anecdote, noting that while statistics can be dry, the emotional pull of a celebrity's survival story often overrides the data. He warns that when even doctors are confused by these statistics, it is unfair to expect the public to navigate them alone. The result is a system where "patients with fake disease confuse the statistics," inflating survival rates without saving a single life.

Bottom Line

Francis's argument is a masterclass in cutting through medical marketing to reveal the hidden costs of our obsession with early detection. His strongest point is the ruthless exposure of how survival rates can be manipulated to make ineffective screening look like a triumph. The biggest vulnerability in the piece is the emotional difficulty of accepting that doing nothing might be the safer option, a counterintuitive truth that runs against decades of public health messaging. Readers should watch for the growing tension between technological capability and clinical necessity as we move forward.
