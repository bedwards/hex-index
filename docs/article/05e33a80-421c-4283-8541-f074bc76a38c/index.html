Google vs DuckDuckGo | Search engine manipulation, censorship and why you should switch

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Oct 13, 2018 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

The Hated One makes a startling claim: that Google's most beloved features are not innovations but stolen concepts, and that the search giant actively sabotages competitors to protect a surveillance-based business model. This piece doesn't just compare two websites; it frames the search engine as a political gatekeeper that manufactures consent and curates reality, arguing that the price of Google's convenience is the loss of a neutral internet.

The Illusion of Neutrality

The author opens by dismantling the perception of Google as a helpful tool, suggesting instead that it is an insecure monopoly. "Google copied that from DuckDuckGo," The Hated One writes regarding instant answers, immediately flipping the script on who the innovator is. The core argument here is that Google's dominance allows it to dictate not just what we find, but how we think about what we find. By placing its own tools and automated responses at the top of the page, Google effectively pushes organic results down, forcing users to scroll past an agenda before seeing the actual internet.

This framing is effective because it shifts the focus from technical performance to user autonomy. The Hated One argues that "Google artificially manufactures user consent through its vague and broad Terms of Service," a point that resonates deeply in an era of data fatigue. The author contends that even when users try to opt out, Google "proactively tricks them without our consent," collecting location data, email logs, and offline purchasing records. This accumulation of data is described not as a service, but as "stalking, spying, tracking, snooping, snatching."

Critics might note that Google's personalization algorithms are designed to improve relevance, not necessarily to manipulate ideology, and that the sheer volume of data helps refine search quality. However, the author's insistence that this creates a "filter bubble" where users only see results aligning with their existing beliefs presents a compelling counter-narrative to the idea of a neutral search engine.

Google wants to control what people get to see. DuckDuckGo strikes this issue head-on.

The Architecture of Censorship

Moving beyond data privacy, The Hated One tackles the issue of search neutrality and the manipulation of results. The author highlights how Google's "instant answer" feature often provides a single, fabricated narrative, such as claiming Google doesn't listen to conversations, which the author calls a "total fabrication" designed to create a "delusion" of privacy. The piece argues that "Google never tells you" when it manipulates results, a lack of transparency that is central to the critique.

The author provides a stark example involving climate change, noting that while Google suppresses denialist sites, DuckDuckGo ranks them higher because its algorithm is non-judgmental and keyword-driven. "Google is making unilateral decisions on what should appear in its search engine," The Hated One writes, taking over the user's responsibility to find the truth. This is a provocative stance, suggesting that Google's "good intentions" to stop extremism or misinformation actually amount to censorship.

This argument holds weight when examining the 2016 election, where Google allegedly filtered autocomplete suggestions for Hillary Clinton to hide terms like "crime" and "scandal," while doing the same for Donald Trump and Bill Cosby. The author asserts that "Google could have decided to stay away out of this by letting the algorithm do what it does best," but instead, "Google made a conscious decision to show the Internet how Google thinks it should be."

The Anti-Competitive Reality

The final section of the commentary focuses on the practical barriers to switching engines. The Hated One details how Google allegedly engaged in anti-competitive tactics, such as buying the domain duckduckgo.com to redirect users to an empty page with "ugly looking links" for years. "The search Goliath stopped redirecting to its main domain after six years," the author notes, only after a massive EU fine. This evidence paints a picture of a corporation using its market dominance to stifle competition rather than competing on merit.

The author contrasts this with DuckDuckGo's approach, which places organic results in the center and relegates ads or instant answers to the side, ensuring they don't crowd out independent content. "Google wants to keep you in its own ecosystem, a walled garden," The Hated One writes, arguing that the price of this hand-holding is a "massive surveillance state where nothing you do is private anymore."

While the author's call to action is clear—switch to DuckDuckGo to vote with your clicks—the piece acknowledges that the growth of the privacy engine is still slow compared to Google's historical trajectory. Yet, the argument remains that the mere existence of a choice is vital for a healthy internet.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its unflinching exposure of how search engine design choices inherently favor the platform's own interests over user neutrality. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its binary framing of "organic" versus "manipulated" results, potentially oversimplifying the complex algorithms that balance relevance, safety, and commercial viability. Readers should watch for how search giants respond to this growing demand for transparency, as the battle for the soul of the internet is no longer just about speed, but about who controls the truth.
