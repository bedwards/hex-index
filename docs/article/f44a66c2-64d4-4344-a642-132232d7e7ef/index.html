Elena ferrante's 'my brilliant friend'

Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Oz delivers a startlingly unromantic autopsy of the literary life, arguing that in Elena Ferrante's world, the written word is not a ladder to salvation but a trap for the powerless. While many critics praise the Neapolitan series for its depiction of female friendship, Oz identifies a far more corrosive thesis: that beauty and physical agency utterly eclipse intellect, rendering the writer's struggle pathetic and irrelevant. This is not a celebration of the artist; it is a warning that the pen is mightier only in the imagination, while in reality, the body rules.

The Trap of the Written Word

Oz frames Ferrante's novel as a brutal deconstruction of the writer's fantasy. The author argues that the protagonist's belief in the redemptive power of education is systematically dismantled by the raw force of the physical world. Oz writes, "Agency as the sole defense against mimesis," suggesting that without the ability to act independently, one is merely copying the desires of others. The text posits that the protagonist, Elena, defines her entire existence through the hope of publication, yet Ferrante reveals this hope to be a lie.

The commentary highlights how the novel's most celebrated writer, Donato Sarratore, is portrayed not as a sage but as a predator. Oz notes that "the grand writer is reduced to a disgusting worm," a figure whose poetry is merely a tool for sexual manipulation rather than a vessel for truth. This framing is effective because it strips away the romantic veneer often placed on literary figures, exposing the vulnerability of those who rely on words in a world governed by force. Critics might argue that this reduces the complexity of Ferrante's critique of class, but Oz's focus on the physical danger facing the writer adds a necessary layer of urgency.

Writing is not redemptive. In Ferrante's universe, the sun and the sea and a momentary taste of flesh, however fleeting, will always defeat the bookish compensatory life.

The Monopoly of Beauty

The piece shifts to analyze how beauty functions as a form of absolute power, creating a hierarchy that intellect cannot penetrate. Oz describes a world where "a woman's superior beauty defeats other women and enslaves men," turning social interaction into a transaction of physical dominance. The author draws a sharp distinction between the invisible, bookish Elena and the luminous Lila, whose very presence commands the attention of the town.

Oz writes, "They seemed to have breathed another air, to have eaten other food, to have dressed on some other planet," capturing the alien nature of the beautiful elite. This observation connects to the broader theme of exclusion, where the poor and the ugly are rendered literally invisible. The commentary suggests that Ferrante is not just describing a neighborhood but exposing a universal human tendency to worship physical perfection over intellectual depth. This aligns with the historical context of Italian neorealism, which often focused on the gritty reality of the poor, yet Ferrante inverts this by showing how the poor are mesmerized by the very beauty that excludes them.

The author argues that this beauty is a "local monopoly," a temporary but totalizing force that dictates social reality. Oz points out that "beauty even enthralls a father, not sexually, but with its sheer magnetism," demonstrating how deeply this power runs. This is a provocative claim: that the most potent force in the human experience is not justice, love, or truth, but the ability to be seen.

The Escape of Agency

In the final analysis, Oz identifies a singular path to freedom that bypasses both the trap of writing and the tyranny of beauty: pure agency. The commentary focuses on the character of Nino, who escapes the town not through intellect or looks, but through a refusal to participate in its mimetic games. Oz writes, "He was Abraham leaving his father's people to found a nation of his own," framing his departure as a radical act of self-creation.

The author contrasts this with Lila, whose agency is powerful but precarious, often tied to her beauty and the men she manipulates. Oz notes that Lila "seemed as usual to have no need of male attention," but ultimately, her power is still reactive to the world around her. Nino, however, is defined by his indifference. "He didn't care. He's not bound by town gossip," Oz observes, suggesting that true freedom comes from a lack of attachment to the social script.

This section offers a nuanced critique of the feminist reading of the novel. While Lila is often celebrated as the ultimate feminist icon, Oz argues that her power is still constrained by the very system she navigates. The true escape, the commentary suggests, is the ability to walk away entirely, to reject the game of beauty and status altogether. This connects to the themes in The Leopard, where the decay of the old order is marked by a nostalgia for a time when power was more straightforward. In Ferrante's world, the only way to survive is to stop playing by the rules of beauty and status.

She knew how to be autonomous whereas I needed her.

Bottom Line

Oz's commentary succeeds in reframing My Brilliant Friend not as a story of female empowerment, but as a stark warning about the limits of intellect in a physical world. The strongest part of the argument is the unflinching exposure of the writer's vulnerability, challenging the reader to reconsider the value of the literary life. The biggest vulnerability lies in the potential overstatement of beauty's power, which may obscure the structural economic forces at play. Readers should watch for how this analysis of agency applies to modern discussions of influence, where physical presence and social media often trump deep thought.

Elena ferrante's 'my brilliant friend'

by Oz · · Read full article

WARNING: SPOILERS

My Brilliant Friend is about the frenetic mimesis of female friendships, the humiliation of writers, the impotence of tracked study, and the overwhelming power of beauty, money, sheer physicality, and agency over words.

There is much to appreciate in Elena Ferrante’s first novel in her Neapolitan series. There is perhaps no greater portrait of mimesis in literature. Agency as the sole defense against mimesis. The uneven intensity of memory and experience is delivered in a charming patter of youthful Italian and ‘dialect’ audible even to a reader in English. The outsized, monstrous effect of ideas and words from near-history (monarchy, communism, fascism) and the people that lie on the periphery of the familiar (like the strange neighbour you had as a child). The irresistible power of a woman’s beauty. But most ironic of all is how one of the greatest literary authors of the 21st century totally denigrates the written word and the writer in her great literary work.

Ferrante chuckles at herself often enough: through her child protagonist’s eyes a woman a little over 30 is old and a writer must be rich:

Evidently his father, although he had written a book of poems, was not yet wealthy.

No, writing will not bring you riches. Writing does not even make you better. In fact, Ferrante says, writing can be a trap, its lure a lie.

The book’s sole author is Donato Sarratore, a sexual predator who mangles a widow’s mind and molests our underage protagonist Elena. He writes poems and a book, which seems wildly romantic and idealistic and successful to Elena until that fantasy careens into the older man’s sexual approach, and is left pathetic as he desperately begs for her affections, promising poems, threatening suicide. The grand writer is reduced to a disgusting worm.

Our two girl friend protagonists, Elena and Lila, are initially enthralled by books and stories. They represent an intellectual, economic, and status ladder out of their backward little town, out of the vortex of village gossip in which they’re stuck. Elena, our narrator with no sense of self or agency, defines herself exclusively through the prism of such aspirations:

I was secretly convinced that I would truly exist only at the moment when my signature, Elena Greco, appeared in print.

The narrative structure hammers her lack of agency endlessly: Elena describes their world, Lila commands it from its centre.
