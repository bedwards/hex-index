The liberal man and the masses

Cyril Hédoin · ·Jan 2, 2026 ·14 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Most political analysis treats populism as a sudden outbreak of irrational anger, but Cyril Hédoin argues it is actually the logical, tragic conclusion of a promise liberalism made but cannot keep. In this dense, three-part essay, Hédoin contends that the very individualism we celebrate has inadvertently created a "mass society" where individuals feel powerless, driving them toward the very authoritarianism they claim to fear. This is not a story about a specific leader or a single election cycle; it is a structural diagnosis of why modern democracies feel so broken to the people living in them.

The Paradox of Freedom

Hédoin begins by dismantling the standard definition of liberalism. He suggests that while we often point to tolerance or pluralism as its core, the true engine is "normative individualism"—the belief that the individual is the primary unit of moral and political value. He writes, "The first part argues that normative individualism, rather than tolerance, pluralism, or public reason, is the core ideal of liberalism." This framing is crucial because it sets up the central conflict: a system built on the idea of the sovereign individual operating in a world that has become too complex for any single person to control.

As the division of labor expanded and knowledge dispersed, Hédoin notes that top-down control became inefficient, leading to a rise in individual rights. But this victory came with a hidden cost. "The liberty of the ancients, where individuals (at least a significant number of them) are directly involved in the governance of the polity, had to be traded off for private freedom." Here, Hédoin echoes a tension Alexis de Tocqueville identified in the 1830s, where the rise of equality led to a retreat into private life, leaving the public sphere vulnerable. The author argues that we are now reaping the consequences of that trade-off.

"The rise of the liberty of the moderns has given individuals greater control over their private life and affairs... This would not have been possible without the division of labor and the dispersion of knowledge."

This observation holds up well against historical trends, yet it risks oversimplifying the agency of citizens. Critics might note that people have always navigated complex systems without losing their sense of civic duty; the issue may be less about complexity and more about the deliberate dismantling of civic institutions. Hédoin, however, insists the structural shift is the primary driver.

The Birth of the Mass Man

The essay's most compelling section explores the psychological toll of this shift. Hédoin introduces the concept of the "mass man"—not as a brute or a fool, but as an isolated individual who has lost their connection to the social fabric. He draws heavily on Hannah Arendt's analysis of totalitarianism, noting that "the masses grew out of the fragments of a highly atomized society whose competitive structure and concomitant loneliness of the individual had been held in check only through membership in a class." This is a powerful reframing: the danger isn't that people are too angry, but that they are too lonely.

In this environment, the individual feels they owe nothing to anyone and expect nothing from anyone. Hédoin writes, "In mass democratic societies, while individuals have become impure forms of the liberal man, their agency is literally 'flooded' into an ocean of entangled causal effects mediated by a no less complex institutional nexus of impersonal rules." The result is a profound sense of disempowerment. The liberal man is taught to be autonomous, but the reality of the mass society renders that autonomy an illusion.

"The inability of contemporary liberal democracies to respond to this predicament may account for the rise of populist politics."

This connection between isolation and political volatility is the piece's strongest insight. It suggests that populism is not a rejection of liberal values, but a desperate, often distorted attempt to reclaim the self-governance that the system promised but failed to deliver. However, Hédoin's reliance on "ideal types" like the "liberal man" and "mass man" can sometimes feel abstract, potentially glossing over the very real economic inequalities that also drive political unrest.

The Myth of Merit and the Path Forward

Hédoin turns his critique toward the concept of meritocracy, arguing that in a complex mass society, the belief that success is purely a result of individual merit is a dangerous fiction. He points out that this belief is "self-serving for the lucky few who enjoy social success, and a source of personal humiliation for the rest." By treating merit as an institutional construction rather than an absolute truth, we might better understand why people feel wronged when the system fails them.

He suggests that the solution lies not in abandoning liberalism, but in revising how we govern. "The stability of the liberal society depends on its ability to strike the right balance between mechanisms that effectively confer self-governing abilities to individuals and social constructions that justify mass outcomes over which individuals have barely any control." This is a nuanced call for institutional innovation, perhaps looking toward polycentric governance or new forms of deliberative democracy that can restore a sense of agency.

"Populism is the political expression of a demand for self-governance."

This conclusion is provocative. It forces liberal elites to stop viewing populists merely as a problem to be managed and start seeing them as a symptom of a deeper institutional failure. While Hédoin admits that "it's easier to diagnose a disease than to provide a remedy," his diagnosis is sharp and necessary. He challenges the reader to consider that the anger we see in the world today is a rational response to a broken social contract.

Bottom Line

Cyril Hédoin's essay offers a sophisticated, structural explanation for the crisis of modern democracy, moving beyond personality clashes to examine the fundamental tension between individual autonomy and mass complexity. Its greatest strength is reframing populism as a demand for agency rather than a failure of character, though it occasionally leans too heavily on abstract philosophical categories. For anyone trying to understand the current political moment, the key takeaway is clear: until liberal democracies find a way to make individuals feel like they actually govern their own lives, the cycle of resentment and reaction will continue.
