The Liar Paradox - an explanation of the paradox from 400 BCE

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·Mar 15, 2023 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan doesn't just explain a 2,400-year-old logical puzzle; he dismantles the most popular modern solution to it, revealing why our intuitive understanding of truth might be fundamentally broken. While many pop-philosophy explanations stop at the simple contradiction of "this sentence is false," Kaplan pushes further to show that even sophisticated workarounds—like declaring a sentence "neither true nor false" or banning self-reference—collapse under their own weight. This is not a history lesson; it is a live demonstration of why professional logicians are still wrestling with a problem that threatens the very foundations of reasoning.

The Trap of the Strengthened Liar

Kaplan begins by establishing the classic paradox: a sentence that claims its own falsity creates an inescapable loop where truth implies falsehood and vice versa. He notes the obvious temptation to escape this binary by introducing a third option. "Since it's clear that in this case neither of those two options works, it's tempting to introduce a third option: a sentence or a claim can be true, it can be false, or it can be neither true nor false," Kaplan writes. This move, known as abandoning the principle of bivalence, seems like a clean exit strategy for the logician.

However, Kaplan immediately pivots to show why this escape is an illusion. He introduces the "strengthened liar sentence," which claims simply that it is "not true." By broadening the category from "false" to "not true," the sentence swallows the new third option. "Being neither true nor false is one version of being not true," he explains, demonstrating that the sentence now accurately describes itself, which paradoxically makes it true. The elegance of this counter-argument is striking; it proves that you cannot simply sidestep the paradox by changing the rules of the game. The paradox regenerates itself instantly.

Critics might argue that this reliance on the "strengthened" version is a semantic trick rather than a fundamental flaw in logic, but Kaplan's point is that any system allowing a sentence to refer to its own truth value will eventually face this regeneration.

The problem of course comes from this kind of circular thing, but it's not really a problem for self-reference.

The Illusion of Self-Reference

A common defense against the liar paradox is to ban self-reference entirely, arguing that a sentence cannot meaningfully talk about itself. Kaplan systematically tears down this defense by showing how easily we can construct the paradox without a single sentence referring to itself. He proposes a scenario where two sentences refer to each other in a circle: one says the other is false, and the other says the first is true. "If this is possible then we can generate a pair of sentences that work together to do the same thing that the liar sentence did in the first place," Kaplan asserts.

This is the piece's most significant contribution to a general audience. It shifts the blame from the structure of individual sentences to the structure of truth itself. By proving that circular reference between different sentences creates the exact same logical explosion as self-reference, Kaplan eliminates the "self-reference" ban as a viable solution. The paradox is not a glitch in how we name things; it is a feature of how truth operates in a circular system.

The Final Dilemma: Truth or Logic?

Having ruled out the easy fixes, Kaplan forces the reader to confront the uncomfortable reality: the paradox points to a deep fracture in our logical framework. He identifies two primary ways out, both of which are deeply unappealing. The first is to abandon the "T-schema," the intuitive rule that allows us to say "P is true" if and only if P. "One way to get out of the liar Paradox is just to drop this but that seems crazy," Kaplan admits, acknowledging that doing so would make our language unintelligible.

The second option is to abandon classical logic itself, moving toward "dialetheism" (where a statement can be both true and false) or "gap theories" (where statements are neither). "They're less intuitive, they're weird, and they seem less logical and that's bad if you're developing Logic," he writes. This is a bold admission. Kaplan is effectively telling the reader that the tools we use to understand the universe might be insufficient to handle the concept of truth.

The argument is compelling precisely because it refuses to offer a neat resolution. Instead of a tidy conclusion, we are left with a live debate that has persisted since Aristotle and continues in graduate seminars today. Kaplan's reliance on the work of Tim Maudlin and Ethan Jerzak grounds his explanation in serious academic discourse, moving beyond the "Star Trek" analogies that often plague this topic.

Critics might note that Kaplan's dismissal of non-classical logics as "weird" is itself a bias toward classical intuition, potentially closing the door on the very solutions that could save the system. However, his point stands: any solution requires a sacrifice of something we currently hold dear.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's analysis succeeds because it refuses to let the reader off the hook with a simple "it's a paradox" shrug; he forces us to see that the paradox is a symptom of a broken definition of truth. The strongest part of his argument is the demonstration that circular reference, not self-reference, is the true culprit, rendering many popular solutions obsolete. Its biggest vulnerability is the lack of a constructive path forward, leaving the reader with the unsettling realization that our logical foundations may be inherently unstable. For the busy thinker, this is a vital reminder that some of the oldest questions in philosophy remain the most dangerous.
