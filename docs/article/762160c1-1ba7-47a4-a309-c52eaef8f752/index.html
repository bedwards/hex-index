How would London's "Range Rover tax" work?

Michael Macleod · London Centric ·Mar 16, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Michael Macleod delivers a dual-pronged exposé that cuts through London's political noise, revealing how a single landlord's aggression has reached the Prime Minister while the city simultaneously debates taxing the very vehicles that symbolize its inequality. This is not just a report on housing or traffic; it is a study in how power operates when scrutiny finally lands. Macleod argues that the era of silent evictions and unchallenged SUV dominance is ending, driven by a rare convergence of investigative journalism and political pressure.

The Landlord's U-Turn

The piece opens with a startling revelation: the mass evictions carried out by billionaire Asif Aziz's Criterion Capital have become a matter of national security for the housing market. Macleod writes, "Silently and in semi-secrecy, London and the south east of England are experiencing the largest mass eviction by a private landlord in decades." This framing is crucial; it transforms a local dispute into a systemic crisis. The author details how Labour MP Siobhain McDonagh forced the issue into the House of Commons, prompting Prime Minister Keir Starmer to condemn the practice and pledge to abolish "no-fault evictions."

The narrative takes a sharp turn when Macleod describes Criterion Capital's response. After the Prime Minister's intervention, the landlord sent a letter to tenants blaming "media reports" for creating "concern or uncertainty." Macleod notes the cynicism of this move: "The note denied that issuing a section 21 notice – commonly known as 'no-fault eviction' – necessarily meant there had ever been an intent to evict the tenants." This is a classic deflection, attempting to reframe a legal threat as a misunderstanding. The author rightly points out the skepticism of residents who have been burned before, noting that Criterion previously threatened legal action against the very publication that exposed them.

"Residents who no longer trust the company and want to move elsewhere have been unable to get references for their new properties."

Macleod's coverage goes beyond the immediate eviction crisis to question the origins of Aziz's empire. He asks, "How did Asif Aziz come to dominate so many aspects of life in the capital with barely anyone noticing?" This is the piece's most provocative angle. By linking the evictions to other controversies, such as tax-evading gift shops and threats to cinemas, Macleod suggests a pattern of unchecked influence. Critics might argue that focusing on one landlord distracts from broader housing policy failures, but the author effectively demonstrates that individual malfeasance can exploit systemic gaps. The piece concludes this section by highlighting a persistent, unanswered question about fake electrical safety certificates, a detail that underscores the landlord's potential disregard for tenant safety.

The SUV Tax Paradox

Shifting gears, Macleod tackles the Mayor's "Vision Zero" plan, specifically the proposed tax on large SUVs like Range Rovers. The author identifies a fundamental tension in the policy: while the goal is to eliminate road deaths, the method—taxing vehicles—clashes with the political reality of car dependency. Macleod writes, "One problem is that 'SUV' – which stands for sports utility vehicle – is more of a marketing term than a set of explicit criteria." This technical hurdle is significant. If London defines an SUV by size or weight, it could inadvertently set a national standard that manufacturers might game.

The commentary highlights the disparity between London and other cities. In Paris, parking fees for heavy cars have tripled, but Macleod points out that London lacks the centralized control to replicate this easily. "Instead, this suggests any London-wide system would have to be enforced using TfL's existing ULEZ and Congestion Charge cameras," he explains. This reliance on existing infrastructure is pragmatic but raises questions about fairness for those in outer London who rely on cars. The author notes that the fastest way to reduce car use is to make it "more expensive and annoying," yet this approach risks alienating voters who feel they have no alternative.

"TfL could be doing a lot more to point out that there are alternatives to buying huge cars."

Macleod's analysis of the political fallout is particularly sharp. He reveals that detailed plans for per-mile road charging were scrapped ahead of the 2024 election due to fears of hurting Labour's campaign. This admission exposes the gap between the Mayor's safety goals and his political survival. A counterargument worth considering is that taxing cars without improving public transport is simply a revenue grab, but Macleod suggests the primary motivation is genuine safety, albeit hamstrung by political cowardice. The piece lists 43 commitments in the Vision Zero report, from 20mph speed limits to better pedestrian crossings, but the SUV tax remains the most contentious and visible element.

The Battle for Newham and the Death of Optimism

The final section of Macleod's coverage zooms in on the upcoming Newham elections, where the debate over car usage has become a referendum on climate policy. The author describes a political scramble where both Labour and the Independents are pledging to scrap emissions-based parking charges. "Car is a necessity not a luxury," says the Independent candidate, a slogan that resonates in a borough where public transport alternatives are often lacking. Macleod observes that even the Green Party is considering a shift to weight-based systems, signaling a retreat from strict environmentalism.

This local political shift is contextualized by the broader cultural phenomenon of "Londonmaxxing." Macleod describes how a viral trend on X (formerly Twitter) briefly celebrated London's potential before collapsing under the weight of negativity. He writes, "The moment reveals a strange story: X and many other platforms have become overwhelmed with videos portraying London as being in a state of complete collapse." This cultural observation adds depth to the political analysis, suggesting that the city's identity is fractured between those who see opportunity and those who see decay. The author's inclusion of this trend serves as a reminder that policy debates do not happen in a vacuum; they are fueled by the public's emotional state.

"If control of the council does switch in May, there'll be a lot written about the big factors at play – but parking charges might be just as important."

Macleod's ability to weave together a housing scandal, a traffic policy debate, and a cultural trend creates a comprehensive picture of London's current state. The coverage is not just informative; it is a warning that the city's institutions are under stress from multiple directions. The author's insistence on asking the unanswered questions—about fake certificates, about the funding for parking schemes, about the definition of an SUV—keeps the reader engaged and skeptical.

Bottom Line

Michael Macleod's piece is a masterclass in connecting disparate threads of London life into a coherent narrative of power and resistance. The strongest part of the argument is the exposure of how political pressure can force even the most entrenched landlords to hesitate, while the biggest vulnerability lies in the political unwillingness to fully implement the necessary traffic reforms. Readers should watch for the outcome of the Newham election, as it may signal a broader retreat from climate-focused urban planning in favor of short-term political expediency.

London Centric’s reporting on the mass evictions being carried out by Asif Aziz’s Criterion Capital has reached prime minister’s questions.

Labour MP Siobhain McDonagh, a longstanding critic of the billionaire landlord, raised the issue with Keir Starmer in a packed House of Commons.

The Mitcham and Morden MP said:

“Silently and in semi-secrecy, London and the south east of England are experiencing the largest mass eviction by a private landlord in decades. Criterion Capital have issued at least 130 no fault evictions across their portfolio including in Britannia Point in Colliers Wood in my constituency. Those affected have done nothing wrong. They have paid their rent, looked after their homes and worked hard. They are simply victims of a voracious landlord who always wants more. Will the prime minister ensure that in the final weeks before the abolition of no fault evictions his government do all they can to hold Criterion Capital to account?”

In response, the prime minister said he would “condemn any unfair evictions” and asked the housing minister to look into Criterion Capital: “Renters should have security… I’m proud to be abolishing section 21, a practice that’s pushed thousands of renters into homelessness.”
