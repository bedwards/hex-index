How would London's "range rover tax" work?

Michael Macleod · London Centric ·Mar 16, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Michael Macleod does more than report on London's housing crisis; he exposes a systemic rot where private capital exploits legal loopholes to displace tenants while the state hesitates to intervene. The piece is notable not for the eviction numbers alone, but for the chilling revelation of how a single landlord, Asif Aziz, has weaponized the legal system to silence the very journalism that uncovered his tactics. This is a story about the fragility of tenant security in a city where policy moves slower than profit.

The Shadow of Section 21

The article anchors its argument in a dramatic confrontation at Prime Minister's Questions, where Labour MP Siobhain McDonagh challenged the government on the scale of displacement. Macleod writes, "Silently and in semi-secrecy, London and the south east of England are experiencing the largest mass eviction by a private landlord in decades." This phrasing is crucial; it frames the event not as a series of isolated disputes, but as a coordinated campaign of removal that has gone unnoticed until now. The Prime Minister's response—promising to "condemn any unfair evictions" and pledging to abolish Section 21—highlights the gap between political rhetoric and the immediate reality for tenants.

The author notes that the government is preparing to end "no-fault evictions," a practice that has "pushed thousands of renters into homelessness." Yet, the article reveals a disturbing delay tactic by Criterion Capital. After the Prime Minister's comments, the landlord sent a letter blaming "media reports" for creating "concern or uncertainty" among tenants, while simultaneously denying any intent to evict. Macleod points out the cynicism here: "Many of these residents are sceptical that this is a genuine U-turn. They have been stung before." This skepticism is well-founded, given that just weeks prior, the landlord's lawyers at Carter-Ruck threatened the publication with libel for suggesting the evictions might be paused. The reference to Carter-Ruck is significant; it reminds readers of the legal heavy artillery often deployed by London's elite to suppress scrutiny, a tactic that echoes the aggressive legal posturing seen during the Housing Act 1988 debates, which originally cemented the very Section 21 powers now under fire.

"Concern or uncertainty" is the euphemism for a landlord who has issued 130 no-fault notices and now claims they never intended to use them.

The piece also raises a critical question about the landlord's opacity. Macleod asks, "How did Asif Aziz come to dominate so many aspects of life in the capital with barely anyone noticing?" The author lists a string of allegations, from tax-evading retail ventures to undermining local cinemas, culminating in the unanswered question about fake electrical safety certificates. Critics might note that without a regulatory body stepping in to audit these claims, the public is left relying on investigative journalism to fill the enforcement vacuum. The administration's reliance on the housing minister to "look into" the matter feels insufficient against a landlord who has already spent millions on legal defense.

The SUV Tax Paradox

Shifting from housing to transport, Macleod dissects the Mayor's "Vision Zero" plan, specifically the proposal to tax large SUVs. The argument is grounded in safety data: larger vehicles are "more likely to cause serious injuries or death," with occupants safer while pedestrians are left vulnerable. However, the author identifies a fundamental implementation hurdle. "One problem is that 'SUV'... is more of a marketing term than a set of explicit criteria." If London defines the tax by size, it risks becoming a de facto national standard that manufacturers will simply game by designing cars that sit just under the threshold.

The commentary highlights the political tension at the heart of the proposal. While Transport for London admits that cutting private car usage is "one of the most effective ways to cut road danger," the political reality is that "people who rely on their car... don't like the idea of having to pay more." Macleod notes that detailed plans for per-mile road charging were scrapped ahead of the 2024 mayoral election due to fears of backlash. This reveals a recurring theme in London governance: the gap between what is necessary for safety and what is politically palatable. The article suggests that any London-wide system would likely have to piggyback on the existing Congestion Charge cameras, a logistical workaround that feels like a compromise rather than a solution.

The Battle for Newham

The narrative takes a sharp turn to the borough of Newham, where the debate over car usage has become the central battleground for local elections. Macleod observes a startling reversal in political strategy. "Labour and its main challenger [are] outbidding each other with pledges to make it cheaper to park a car in the borough." The incumbent Labour leadership, which once declared a climate emergency, now faces a candidate promising to scrap emissions-based permits and build a multi-storey car park. The opposition candidate, Mehmood Mirza, frames the issue starkly: "Car is a necessity not a luxury."

This shift underscores the difficulty of implementing green policies in outer London, where public transport alternatives are often perceived as inadequate. The article notes that even the Green Party is now considering a shift from emissions-based to weight-based parking systems, signaling a retreat from the most aggressive climate measures. The author connects this local fragmentation to a broader trend, noting that Labour's dominance is cracking as voters prioritize immediate convenience over long-term climate goals. This mirrors the national dynamic where the executive branch struggles to balance environmental mandates with the economic anxieties of the electorate.

"Londonmaxxing" briefly went viral, a desperate attempt by tech workers to counter a narrative of collapse with a pitch of economic potential.

The piece concludes with a brief, almost surreal detour into the phenomenon of "Londonmaxxing," a term coined to counter the doom-scrolling on social media. Macleod writes that the term "appears to have died" within a week, yet it reveals a deeper truth: "anyone trying to be remotely positive about the city has to come up with a whole new term if they want to cut through the negativity." This fleeting trend serves as a metaphor for the city's current state—a place where optimism requires a rebranding because the default setting has become despair.

Bottom Line

Michael Macleod's coverage is at its strongest when connecting the dots between legal loopholes, landlord aggression, and political hesitation, particularly in the Criterion Capital eviction saga. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the hope that public pressure will force the administration to act, despite a history of regulatory capture and legal intimidation. Readers should watch whether the abolition of Section 21 is implemented with enough teeth to actually protect tenants, or if it becomes another symbolic victory that leaves the underlying power dynamics untouched.
