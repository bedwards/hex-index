The Failed Logistics of Russia's Invasion of Ukraine

Sam Denby · Wendover Productions ·Mar 5, 2022 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sam Denby delivers a masterclass in military logistics, arguing that Russia's invasion of Ukraine failed not because of a lack of firepower, but because of a fundamental inability to move it. While most analysis focuses on the bravery of Ukrainian defenders or the geopolitical blunders of Vladimir Putin, Denby zeroes in on the unglamorous reality of fuel trucks, rail gauges, and supply chains. He brings a rare forensic precision to the narrative, using satellite imagery and digital footprints to prove that the Russian military's greatest weakness was its own infrastructure.

The Illusion of Readiness

Denby begins by dismantling the myth that the invasion was a spontaneous reaction. He meticulously traces the buildup over months, noting how Russia used its vast rail network to amass forces while maintaining a facade of routine exercises. "The likelihood that this was a simulation plummeted in January," Denby writes, pointing to the arrival of medical units and fuel reserves that signaled a shift from training to war. This evidence is compelling because it highlights a deliberate, state-sponsored mobilization that was visible to the world yet ignored by many analysts until it was too late.

The author emphasizes the unique role of Russia's railway system, which acts as the backbone of its military machine. "This control and extensiveness combined means that Russia's military is able to rely heavily on the rail network," he explains. However, he quickly pivots to the critical flaw: this system is a domestic asset that becomes a liability once the war moves beyond the railheads. The reliance on trains allowed Russia to gather 175,000 troops, but it also created a bottleneck that could not be easily resolved once the front lines stalled.

"Russia's forces under their current configuration are simply incapable of properly supporting a fight more than 90 miles or 145 kilometers from supply dumps."

This specific metric is the article's most devastating insight. It suggests that the Russian strategy was mathematically doomed from the start, as their logistical tail was too short to sustain the offensive push they envisioned. Critics might argue that Denby underestimates the adaptability of military forces in the field, but the sheer volume of abandoned tanks and looted food supplies on day two strongly supports his thesis of systemic failure.

The Clash of Systems

The commentary shifts to the tactical level, where Denby contrasts the Russian "push" system with the Western "pull" system. In a push system, supplies are sent based on a pre-determined schedule, whereas a pull system allows frontline units to request what they actually need in real-time. "Western forces let strategy lead logistics while Russian forces let logistics lead strategy," Denby asserts. This distinction is crucial for understanding why the Russian advance bogged down so quickly; they were fighting a war of rigid planning against a fluid, adaptive enemy.

Denby details how Ukrainian forces exploited these logistical cracks with remarkable ingenuity. By targeting unarmored fuel trucks and destroying key bridges, they turned Russia's own supply lines into their greatest vulnerability. "If you stop the fuel trucks you stop the tanks," he notes, highlighting how cheap, accessible weapons could neutralize expensive armored divisions. This part of the argument is particularly effective because it reframes the conflict not as a clash of armies, but as a clash of supply chain management.

The author also touches on the psychological and informational warfare that compounded these logistical failures. From hacking radio frequencies to painting over street signs, Ukrainian tactics were designed to disorient an enemy that relied on analog maps and unsecured communications. "The Russian military was looking disorganized and disconnected," Denby observes, painting a picture of an army that was physically present but operationally blind.

The Historical Echo

Denby draws a powerful parallel between the current conflict and the Soviet Union's failure in Afghanistan, suggesting a historical through line of logistical incompetence. "Poor logistics performance is an offside reason for the USSR's failure to achieve its objectives," he writes, reminding readers that this is not a new problem for Russian military doctrine. The argument gains weight by showing that despite decades of modernization, the core structural issues of the Soviet era persist.

However, Denby acknowledges that Russia still possesses the potential to shift gears. He notes that the Russian military has the capacity to build pipelines and repair rail lines to support a prolonged conflict, suggesting that the war is far from over. "The single factor that can best address these issues however is time," he concludes, warning that Russia could eventually adapt if the West allows them the breathing room to do so. This adds a layer of urgency to the analysis, moving beyond a simple critique of past failures to a warning about future possibilities.

"Western forces let strategy lead logistics while Russian forces let logistics lead strategy."

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's analysis is a definitive explanation of why the Russian invasion stalled, proving that modern warfare is won or lost in the supply chain long before the first shot is fired. His strongest argument lies in the stark contrast between Russia's domestic rail dominance and its inability to project power beyond its borders, a vulnerability that Ukraine brilliantly exploited. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that Russia will not successfully transition to a prolonged logistical model, a risk that remains the most critical variable for the future of the war.
