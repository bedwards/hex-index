Venezuela

Dr Michael John-Hopkins · ·Jan 7, 2026 ·16 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

This piece cuts through the noise of daily headlines to ask a terrifyingly simple question: when does American foreign policy stop looking like diplomacy and start looking like organized crime? Dr Michael John-Hopkins argues that the current escalation in Venezuela is not a chaotic aberration, but the logical endpoint of a strategic culture that prefers "contracts to conquest" until it doesn't. For the busy reader, the value here is the exposure of a hidden script: the shift from managerial control to raw coercion is not a mistake, it is a feature of the new 2025 National Security Strategy.

The Illusion of Management

Dr Michael John-Hopkins begins by dismantling the comforting myth that the United States prefers soft power. The author posits that for decades, American influence was exercised through the "managerial model," where control was embedded in finance, insurance, and supply chains rather than boots on the ground. "Whoever controls the rules of access can often control the resource without deploying a single soldier," the author writes. This framing is crucial because it explains why sanctions and economic isolation were the default tools for so long; they were designed to be invisible mechanisms of dominance.

However, the commentary notes that this model has a breaking point. When the target state refuses to comply, the machinery of the state does not retreat; it accelerates. The author observes that "power, at its most comfortable, does not look like domination at all," but when consent is withheld, the preference for management evaporates. This is where the argument gains its teeth. The transition is not accidental. As Dr Michael John-Hopkins puts it, "When sanctions do not compel compliance, and when leverage runs out, power does not dissolve, but rather it escalates according to the dominant strategic culture of the time."

When consent is not forthcoming, contracts fail, and power does not dissolve, but rather it escalates.

The piece effectively contextualizes this by drawing a parallel to the 1954 Guatemalan coup d'état, where agrarian reforms threatening foreign corporate interests were met with a covert invasion. The author suggests we are witnessing a return to that era, but with a modern legal veneer. Critics might argue that the comparison to historical coups ignores the complexity of modern international law, but the author's point stands: the underlying logic of protecting strategic assets remains unchanged regardless of the legal dressing.

The Western Hemisphere as a Zone of Enforcement

The article's most provocative claim centers on the 2025 National Security Strategy's explicit reassertion of hemispheric dominance. Dr Michael John-Hopkins identifies a shift from the Monroe Doctrine to what the text calls a "Trump Corollary" or "Donroe Doctrine," where the Western Hemisphere is treated not as a community of equals, but as a zone where stability must align with American interests. The author writes, "The United States will deny non-hemispheric competitors control over strategically vital assets... and it will use financial, technological, and security, including military leverage to ensure compliance."

This section is particularly striking because it strips away the rhetoric of partnership. The author argues that sovereignty is now conditional: "States are free to govern, provided they do not disrupt strategic supply chains... When they do, sovereignty becomes conditional. It is no longer a shield but an obstacle to be overcome."

The commentary finds this analysis compelling because it reframes recent maritime interdictions and threats of force not as isolated incidents, but as the standard operating procedure of a new imperial logic. The author notes that "practices that might once have been labelled gunboat diplomacy or colonial intervention are instead normalised as law enforcement." This normalization is the key to understanding the current escalation. It allows the executive branch to act with the force of a police action while pursuing the goals of an empire.

From Contracts to Coercion

The heart of Dr Michael John-Hopkins' argument is the timeline of escalation. The author traces a direct line from the Hamiltonian logic of commercial governance to the Jacksonian instinct of brute force. "This helps explain why escalation does not feel, internally, like a rupture. It is experienced instead as a shift between co-existing strategic cultures," the author explains. The shift is seamless because the strategy anticipated it.

The text lists a grim chronology of interventions in the Americas—from Guatemala to Haiti—where reforms challenging US dominance were met with regime change. "This pattern reflects a recurring structural logic, that when reforms threaten strategic assets, capital flows, or geopolitical interests, and when indirect pressure fails, escalation follows," Dr Michael John-Hopkins writes. The author's choice to list these historical precedents serves as a stark warning: the current situation in Venezuela is not an anomaly, but a recurrence of a deep-seated pattern.

The commentary highlights the human cost often missing from such strategic analyses. While the text focuses on statecraft, the implication is clear: when "contracts give way to coercion," the result is often decades of instability for the local population. The author notes that in Guatemala, the overthrow of Árbenz led to "decades of instability," a fate that looms over Venezuela if the current trajectory continues.

Control without consent remains control—the task now is to dress this up as being consensual.

The Criminalization of Strategy

The final and most damning section of the piece addresses the legal implications of this shift. Dr Michael John-Hopkins argues that the move from competition to coercion crosses the line into illegality under the UN Charter. The author writes, "Resource control, strategic pre-eminence, and supply-chain security do not constitute lawful justifications" for the use of force. This is a bold assertion that challenges the administration's framing of these actions as necessary security measures.

The piece goes further, suggesting that the systematic extraction of resources under coercive conditions may constitute "pillage," a war crime. "Modern international humanitarian law does not require looting by individual soldiers to establish pillage," the author states. "Systematic extraction, enforced contractual arrangements, or resource exploitation carried out under coercive conditions by private corporations may meet the same threshold."

This is where the argument reaches its peak intensity. The author suggests that what looks like strategic necessity is, in legal terms, "a form of organised predation." The commentary notes that while this is a severe charge, the evidence presented—the explicit language of the 2025 strategy and the history of resource-driven interventions—makes it a difficult claim to dismiss. The author concludes that the strategy is not just aggressive, but potentially criminal: "It is therefore unsurprising that contemporary strategies emphasise avoiding the appearance of occupation, and instead seek to identify or install ostensibly consenting partners within Venezuelan political and military elites."

Critics might argue that international law is often a tool of the powerful and that the definition of "pillage" is subject to political interpretation. However, the author's reliance on the specific prohibitions of the Geneva Conventions provides a solid legal anchor for this critique.

Bottom Line

Dr Michael John-Hopkins delivers a chilling diagnosis: the current crisis in Venezuela is the inevitable result of a strategy that treats sovereignty as conditional and force as a backup for failed economics. The strongest part of the argument is the exposure of the seamless transition from "managerial" sanctions to "coercive" force, revealing that the latter was always the intended fallback. The biggest vulnerability is the piece's reliance on the assumption that the administration will follow this logic to its conclusion, potentially underestimating the political backlash or logistical hurdles. The reader should watch for the next escalation not as a surprise, but as a scheduled event in a strategy that has already decided that compliance is non-negotiable.

Venezuela

by Dr Michael John-Hopkins · · Read full article

When does permissible strategic competition become illegitimate coercion, and when does state power begin to resemble organised crime? What is the Trump administration’s logic in pardoning former Honduran President Juan Orlando Hernández while indicting the sitting Venezuelan president Nicolás Maduro on similar allegations of drug trafficking? This article examines the recent US escalation from maritime interdiction in the Gulf of Mexico to the use of armed force against Venezuela as an unfolding crisis of responsible statehood.

There is a recurring instinct in American strategic culture when it comes to power, resources, and order. In some contexts it prefers management to mess, contracts to conquest, and leverage to occupation. The ideal outcome is not formal control but compliance so that oil flows, markets stabilise, rivals are excluded, and all of it happens through arrangements that appear legal and technical. Power, at its most comfortable, does not look like domination at all.

Yet Venezuela exposes the limits of that instinct. Because when consent is not forthcoming, contracts fail, when sanctions do not compel compliance, and when leverage runs out, power does not dissolve, but rather it escalates according to the dominant strategic culture of the time. What emerges is a movement from managerial control to coercive authority that is neither accidental nor aberrational. It is anticipated, structured, and in many respects brazenly and openly articulated in the 2025 US National Security Strategy (NSS). Venezuela is not a deviation from American strategic culture, but very much demonstrative of its extremities.

What makes this moment significant is not simply the possibility of intervention or resource seizure. It is what the transition from contracts to coercion reveals about the nature of contemporary state power, its relationship to legality, and the fragility of statehood as a normative project in a world increasingly crowded with alternative, often brutal, forms of governance.

The managerial model of power

For decades, in certain places and at certain times, US power has been exercised primarily through indirect means. Control has rarely required installation of puppet regimes, annexation or formal occupation. Instead, it has been embedded in contracts, finance, sanctions regimes, supply chains, insurance markets, export controls, licensing frameworks, security partnerships, and dispute resolution through multilateral bodies.
