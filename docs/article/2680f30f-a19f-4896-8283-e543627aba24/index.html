Where does a liberal go from here?

Noah Smith · Noahpinion ·Jan 4, 2026 ·15 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Noah Smith delivers a rare, unflinching autopsy of the modern American left, arguing that the progressive movement has not just drifted from its roots but actively sabotaged the very liberal ideals it claims to champion. In an era where political discourse is often reduced to partisan shouting matches, Smith offers a sobering historical lens, asking whether the arc of history has bent so far toward chaos that the old guard of liberalism must now chart a course through a conservative era they did not create. This is not a plea for unity; it is a diagnosis of a movement that has lost its way, leaving its original supporters stranded on the beach of a wrecked ship.

The Ghost of 1815

Smith opens by transporting the reader to a specific moment in history to frame the current crisis. "Imagine being a French liberal in the year 1815," he writes, asking us to consider the despair of watching the French Revolution devolve from a dream of liberty into the Terror and then the Napoleonic wars. He uses this historical parallel to suggest that the current American political landscape feels like the Congress of Vienna—a moment where the old order has returned, but the liberal dream seems dead. The comparison is striking because it reframes the current political fatigue not as a temporary setback, but as a potential historical turning point where the ideals of the Enlightenment appear to have been defeated by their own excesses.

Yet, Smith insists that history is longer than a single election cycle. "Liberalism may have lost the first French Revolution, but it ended up winning the world — at least, for a while," he notes, reminding readers that the arc of history often bends slowly and unevenly. This historical grounding provides a necessary counterweight to the immediate despair of the moment, suggesting that the current conservative resurgence does not necessarily mean the end of liberal progress, but rather a painful correction.

The Record of Success

Before dissecting the failures of the present, Smith meticulously catalogs the victories of the late 20th century to establish what has been lost. He describes a generation raised on the belief that America could be a place of deep inequality solved by a real welfare state. "Beginning in the 1990s, America became a more redistributive, generous nation, under both Democratic and Republican presidencies," he writes, pointing to the Earned Income Tax Credit and the expansion of Medicaid as the fulfillment of those dreams. The argument here is that the center-left achieved its goals: poverty rates fell, racial employment gaps narrowed, and gay marriage became law.

This section is particularly effective because it grounds the author's nostalgia in hard data rather than sentimentality. He argues that "I would not force millions of Americans to return to the grinding, desperate poverty they suffered in 1985," a statement that anchors his critique of modern progressivism in a tangible moral imperative. The success of these policies, he suggests, created a foundation that the current movement has failed to build upon, instead choosing to dismantle the very institutions that made those gains possible.

The progressivism that emerged in the 2010s seems to view anarchy as a form of welfare, believing that the best way to help the poor and unfortunate was to allow the worst and most violent among them to terrorize the rest of them without restraint or reprisal.

The Great Divergence

The core of Smith's argument lies in his distinction between the liberalism of his youth and the progressivism of the 2010s. He observes that "my fellow liberals had stopped using the word 'liberal', and begun to use 'progressive' instead," a semantic shift that masked a profound ideological departure. He details how the quest to end discrimination evolved into what he sees as institutionalized discrimination, and how the desire for universal healthcare morphed into "endless deficit-funded subsidies for overpriced service industries."

Smith's critique of urban governance is perhaps his most visceral. He recounts a specific incident in Seattle where a homeless man repeatedly attacked an elderly woman, noting that a police officer described the attacker as a "regular" who "usually punches." He asks, "How has progressive governance allowed the people of America's greatest cities to live like this?" This anecdote serves as a microcosm for his broader argument: that the pursuit of ideological purity has come at the cost of public safety and basic state capacity. He argues that "Blue cities failed to build housing, choosing instead to embrace the progressive myth that new construction fuels 'gentrification'," a policy choice he views as a betrayal of the working class.

Critics might note that Smith's characterization of progressive policies as uniformly destructive overlooks the complex structural factors driving urban decay, such as decades of disinvestment and the opioid crisis, which cannot be solved by policing alone. However, his point about the degradation of state capacity and the failure to deliver tangible improvements in quality of life remains a potent challenge to the current political establishment.

The Vacuum of Opposition

Smith identifies a critical structural failure: the collapse of the conservative rival allowed liberalism to overgrow its bounds. "The self-immolation of Reaganite conservatism in the 2000s... left liberalism with no real check on its ideological overgrowth," he writes. He argues that the rise of a "shambolic rightist cult" did not provide a compelling alternative but rather excused the worst excesses of the left by making the alternative seem even worse. This dynamic, he suggests, has created a political environment where the only choice is between "an insanely corrupt authoritarian" and "a gaggle of ineffectual ideologues."

This analysis of the political ecosystem is crucial. It suggests that the current crisis is not just about the failures of the left, but about the absence of a viable center-right that can hold the left accountable. Without a sane opposition, the progressive movement has been free to pursue policies that alienate the very voters they seek to help. Smith notes that "if you absolutely have to choose between voting for an insanely corrupt authoritarian... and a gaggle of ineffectual ideologues who spend all day canceling each other... you vote for the latter," but he warns that this is a choice made in desperation, not conviction.

The Path Forward

As he concludes, Smith rejects the easy path of defecting to the right. "The project of making America sane again is a good and important one, but I'm not going to spend the rest of my political life as a pure pragmatist," he asserts. He acknowledges that the conservative era is here to stay, but he refuses to abandon the liberal ideals of his youth. "The liberal ideals I grew up with still have power, and I still believe in them, even if the progressive movement stopped navigating toward those ideals a while ago," he writes.

This refusal to capitulate is the piece's most powerful moment. It is a call to rebuild liberalism from the ground up, to reclaim the values of tolerance, intellectual argument, and broad-minded discourse that have been lost. Smith suggests that the future of liberalism lies not in chasing the latest cultural trend, but in returning to the fundamentals of human dignity and practical governance. He warns that "every social and political movement, if unchecked, tends to take things too far," and that the only way forward is to recognize when the pendulum has swung too far and to begin the hard work of correction.

Bottom Line

Smith's most compelling argument is his historical framing of the current moment as a necessary, albeit painful, correction to a movement that has lost its way. His willingness to critique his own side with such specificity gives the piece its weight and credibility. However, his reliance on a binary choice between a corrupt authoritarian and a chaotic progressive movement risks oversimplifying the complex realities of modern governance. The strongest takeaway is not a policy prescription, but a moral imperative: to rebuild a liberalism that prioritizes human dignity and practical results over ideological purity. Readers should watch for how this "old school" liberalism attempts to reorganize in the face of a conservative resurgence, and whether it can offer a viable alternative to the current political chaos.

Where does a liberal go from here?

by Noah Smith · Noahpinion · Read full article

“You," Said Dr. Yavitch, "are a middle-road liberal, and you haven't the slightest idea what you want.” — Sinclair Lewis

Imagine being a French liberal in the year 1815. You spent your youth dreaming of an end to tyranny and the stultification of the estate society, reading the works of Voltaire and Rousseau and Montesquieu and Diderot, talking of liberty with your friends in cafes. Yours was not among the names that history would remember from that era, but you once attended a salon in a rich woman’s house in Paris. You were not part of the mob that stormed the Bastille in 1789, but you felt your heart leap when you heard the news, because you knew that now everything would change. When you read the terms of the Constitution of 1791, you saw the fulfillment of your youthful daydreams become the solid fabric of a new reality.

Imagine, then, standing in 1815, a quarter century after the Revolution, looking back at what it had all become. That first bright rush of freedom had given way, first to the murderous insanity of the Terror and the Committee for Public Safety, then to the thuggish new imperialism and endless bloody wars of Napoleon, and finally to the fall of all Europe to conservative reaction under the Congress of Vienna. Imagine looking back on the arc of your beliefs, your movement, and your life, now as an old man, with no prospects for another, better Revolution ahead of you.

Would you think your dreams had failed? Would you decide that everything you had believed had been an illusion, and that freedom, democracy, and the Rights of Man were false idols that led only to chaos and bloodshed?

If so, you would be utterly wrong. The two centuries after 1815 would see the ideals of the early French Revolutionaries continue to advance across the world — unevenly, in fits and starts, and with many reversals, yet almost always leaving society better off than before. Those centuries would also see plenty of successors to Robespierre and Napoleon, but just like the originals, they would usually go down to defeat or see their legacies overturned by people weary of war and oppression. Liberalism may have lost the first French Revolution, but it ended up winning the world — at least, for a while.
