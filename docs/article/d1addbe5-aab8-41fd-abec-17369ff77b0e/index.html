The racial justice case for zoning reform

Matt Yglesias · Slow Boring ·Mar 20, 2026 · 22 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Matt Yglesias makes a provocative pivot in a crowded policy debate: the strongest argument for zoning reform isn’t economic efficiency—it’s racial justice. And he delivers the uncomfortable truth that the movement most invested in racial equity has largely ignored it. This matters now because housing segregation remains America’s most durable racial caste system, yet activists keep fighting battles on other fronts.

The Unfinished Civil Rights Revolution

Yglesias cuts through progressive orthodoxy by highlighting a buried historical pivot point. "The first-ever big anti-segregation victory at the Supreme Court was a case striking down explicit racial zoning, and the decision was handed down all the way back in 1917"—a full forty-six years before the Civil Rights Act. He connects this to Richard Rothstein’s The Color of Law, showing how zoning survived the civil rights era because it operated through ostensibly race-neutral rules like lot sizes and single-family mandates. The core insight lands with force: "Precisely because this all happened before the Civil Rights Act, it was not unwound by the events of the early 1960s and remains a huge deal today."

This reframing is vital. While redlining (1930s) and restrictive covenants made segregation explicit, zoning became its stealth successor—embedding racial exclusion in local land-use codes that still dictate who lives where. Yglesias wisely notes these dynamics aren’t secret; scholars like Jessica Trounstine have meticulously documented them. But he asks the right question: Why hasn’t this galvanized the racial justice movement?

The Coalition Conundrum

Here’s where Yglesias delivers his sharpest political analysis. He argues the YIMBY movement initially leaned too hard on racial justice appeals—a strategic error. "If people develop the impression that the purpose of zoning reform is to help African-Americans while making white people worse off, that’s going to mean zoning reform doesn’t happen." Connecticut’s rebranding from Desegregate CT to Pro-Homes Connecticut proves his point. "Most of the big land-use-reform success stories have involved bipartisan coalitions that see it as a win-win policy change," he observes, noting how framing matters: Emphasizing unsustainable public pensions or crushing property taxes builds broader coalitions than leading with structural racism.

Critics might note that deprioritizing racial justice risks losing moral urgency—but Yglesias anticipates this. He concedes Connecticut’s policies are promoting segregation, yet insists the political reality demands pragmatism. The tension is real: Can you fix a racist system without naming racism? His evidence suggests not in today’s polarized climate.

Injecting concepts like structural racism into the mix is not helpful for building the coalitions that pass reform.

Why Criminal Justice Dominates

Yglesias then dissects why racial justice activism fixates on policing over housing. Two factors stand out: First, "it’s always easier to push yourself into lanes where you’re avoiding coalition tensions"—like targeting police unions (right-wing) over teachers unions (left-wing allies). Second, racial profiling hits high-achieving Black professionals hardest, making it visceral for movement leaders. "Every Black man I meet in media or politics or academia has stories about this," he writes, citing Senator Tim Scott’s experiences. This personal resonance explains the focus, even if housing policy affects more people.

The counterargument—that mass incarceration’s scale dwarfs profiling—is valid but misses Yglesias’ point: Movement priorities reflect whose pain is loudest in the room. He’s not dismissing criminal justice reform; he’s exposing why housing gets sidelined despite its deeper roots in inequality.

Bottom Line

Yglesias’ greatest strength is exposing housing policy as the civil rights movement’s unfinished business—with evidence even skeptics can’t dismiss. His biggest vulnerability? Assuming economic arguments alone can sustain reform without the moral clarity of racial justice. Watch whether states like Oregon (which banned single-family zoning statewide) can replicate Connecticut’s bipartisan playbook while still acknowledging the racist foundations they’re dismantling.

Excerpt from the original article on Slow Boring

I published a New York Times op-ed this week about new research from David Broockman and Josh Kalla detailing exactly which kinds of moderation are most electorally potent. Something I mention in the piece is that they do find that on a handful of issues, moving to the center actually backfires on the Democratic Party — candidates should ditch unpopular issue positions, not popular ones.

At a very superficial level, this lends credence to the left-populist thesis that rather than moderating on cultural issues, Democrats can just sort of pound the table with popular economic views.

But it’s worth emphasizing exactly which progressive economic policies are actually popular. The big ones are: allow more doctors and engineers to immigrate, don’t cut Social Security, raise the minimum wage to $15 an hour, expand Medicaid, and raise the income tax rate on people earning more than $400,000 per year to 45 percent.

I think most self-identified progressive Democrats would support all of those ideas, but they would probably also find them objectionably moderate as the totality of a Democratic Party economic agenda. Joe Biden and the bulk of congressional Democrats supported a much more far-reaching agenda, only to see their ambitions curtailed by Joe Manchin.

So while it’s true that there is a progressive economic agenda that is popular enough that it would be counterproductive to back down from, I’m not sure it’s the economic agenda that progressives have in mind. Broockman and Kalla specifically test the idea of promising stepped-up antitrust enforcement, for example, and find no benefits to it and some potential that it backfires. To be fair, it’s not like abundance agenda ideas are super-popular either.

The point is just that in electoral terms, all the juice is in moderating on key issues. Then if you win, you get to govern and you need to try to do a good job.
