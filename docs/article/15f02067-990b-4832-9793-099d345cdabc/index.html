Russia is Running Out of People

PolyMatter · PolyMatter ·Jul 6, 2024 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

PolyMatter uncovers a demographic catastrophe that is far more visceral than simple statistics: Russia is not just shrinking, it is actively hemorrhaging its future through a lethal cocktail of alcohol, war, and systemic neglect. While most analyses focus on birth rates, the author's most disturbing revelation is that the real killer is the "Russian cross"—a demographic phenomenon where death rates surge precisely as birth rates plummet, creating a population collapse that no amount of propaganda can fix.

The Illusion of Resilience

The piece opens by dismantling a carefully staged narrative from the Kremlin. PolyMatter notes that President Vladimir Putin recently visited the remote town of Anadyr to celebrate a "Rising number of large families," portraying it as a beacon of hope. However, the author immediately undercuts this with hard data: "a net population increase of 157 between 2010 and 2021 or 14 people per year." This stark contrast between political theater and statistical reality sets the tone for the entire argument. The author effectively argues that while a net gain is technically better than a loss, it is a drop in the bucket compared to the massive shrinkage occurring elsewhere.

The core of the argument rests on the timeline of the Soviet collapse. PolyMatter writes, "because the Soviet Union collapsed 33 years ago birth rates fell 33 years ago which means today 33 years later Russia is missing a whole lot of 33y olds." This observation is crucial because it highlights a structural labor shortage that cannot be solved by short-term incentives. The country is missing its prime working-age demographic, leading to a labor market where "a mere 15% of all workers are under 30 years old." This isn't just an economic headwind; it is an existential threat to a state that relies on a young population to fund pensions and fight wars.

"These are warlike casualties in peacetime."

The Russian Cross and the Alcohol Epidemic

Where the commentary becomes truly distinctive is in its comparison of Russia to other shrinking nations like Italy and Japan. PolyMatter explains that while Italy's demographic decline is a "human victory" born of choice and better living standards, Russia's is a "human failure" driven by despair. The author points out that "deaths have remained High ever since" the initial drop in births, creating the infamous "Russian cross." This divergence is the piece's most powerful insight: Russia is being squeezed from both sides simultaneously.

The author identifies alcohol as the primary driver of this excess mortality, specifically the culture of binge drinking vodka. PolyMatter writes, "every Russian male has a roughly 25 to 30% chance of dying one way or another thanks to alcohol." The argument is bolstered by historical context regarding Mikhail Gorbachev's failed anti-alcohol campaign. The author notes that the campaign initially worked, increasing male life expectancy by over three years, but was abandoned because "the Soviet Union at the time truly ran on vodka." The tax revenue from alcohol was so critical that the state could not afford to lose it, leading to a decision that prioritized short-term fiscal survival over long-term human life.

Critics might note that focusing heavily on alcohol overlooks other systemic issues like poor healthcare infrastructure or environmental factors. However, PolyMatter counters this by citing specific data: "researchers estimate that between 1990 and 2001 over half of all adult male deaths in Siberia were caused by alcohol." This specificity makes the alcohol argument difficult to dismiss as mere stereotype.

The Cost of War and the Brain Drain

The piece concludes by connecting these demographic trends to current geopolitical events, arguing that the war in Ukraine is accelerating an already dire situation. PolyMatter writes, "estimates of Russian casualties from its war with Ukraine range between 100,000 and 500,000 in either case a massive chunk of the demographic its economy needs the most." This is a devastating point: the war is consuming the very young men the country desperately needs to sustain its economy.

Furthermore, the author highlights a secondary crisis: the exodus of the educated elite. "Only 29% of the population the elite hold a passport," yet those leaving are "disproportionately young wealthy well educated and have skills." The loss of "100,000 software Engineers on its own will cause chaos in Russia's once thriving technology industry." This brain drain compounds the labor shortage, creating a feedback loop where the country loses the people most capable of innovating their way out of the crisis.

"The government diverts money toward the military... rather than fund its outdated hospitals."

Bottom Line

PolyMatter's strongest asset is the ability to connect historical policy failures, like Gorbachev's anti-alcohol campaign, to modern demographic collapse, showing that today's crisis was decades in the making. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on estimates for war casualties and pandemic deaths, which remain contested, but the underlying trend of a shrinking, aging, and unhealthy population is undeniable. The reader should watch for whether Russia's leadership can ever prioritize long-term demographic health over short-term military and fiscal goals, or if the country is now on an irreversible path toward becoming a demographic ghost town.
