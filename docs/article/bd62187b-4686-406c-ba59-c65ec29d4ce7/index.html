Chartbook 426 greenland v. The price of lab monkeys

Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Adam Tooze performs a remarkable act of intellectual juggling, using the absurdity of a geopolitical crisis over Greenland to illuminate a far more consequential, yet invisible, economic signal: the skyrocketing price of lab monkeys. While the headlines scream about diplomatic farce, Tooze argues that the true story of our era is found in the supply chain of non-human primates, where the rise of Chinese biotech and the erratic policies of the U.S. executive branch are colliding with brutal market realities.

The Divergence of Rationality

Tooze begins by framing the current political moment not as a series of isolated scandals, but as a "historic divergence in rationality." He contrasts the chaotic, idiosyncratic decision-making of the current administration with the calculated, massive scale of China's green energy and biotech expansion. "Trump, by contrast, simply denies the issue. Bans wind turbines and has his family money invested fusion," Tooze writes, highlighting a fundamental disconnect between policy and scientific reality. This framing is powerful because it moves the reader past the personality cult and toward a structural analysis of how different nations are approaching the future. The administration's approach is characterized by denial and disruption, while China is doubling down on industrial capacity, even if that capacity relies on ethically complex foundations.

The core of Tooze's argument rests on the idea that trying to rationalize the administration's behavior is a trap. "Does one type of (ir)rationality condition the other? Is this another instance of uneven and combined development?" he asks. This is a sophisticated way of suggesting that the chaos in Washington is not just noise; it is a symptom of a deeper systemic failure that is actively ceding ground to competitors. Critics might argue that attributing such grand historical shifts to the whims of a single administration overstates the case, but Tooze effectively uses the "monkey price" as a concrete metric to prove that policy decisions have immediate, tangible consequences in the global scientific ecosystem.

MAGA's darkness makes more conventional modernity seem more clean-cut than it really is.

The Economics of the Hog Cycle

Tooze then pivots to the data, treating the price of non-human primates (NHPs) as a leading indicator for the global biotech sector. He explains the "hog cycle," an economic phenomenon where supply lags behind demand, causing wild price oscillations. "The cost of the monkeys — a proxy for the volume of clinical trials — is projected to reach Rmb150,000 ($21,000) at the start of 2026," he notes, citing a report by the Financial Times. This detail is crucial; it transforms an abstract concept of "biotech growth" into a hard dollar figure that investors and policymakers can track.

The narrative deepens when Tooze connects these market dynamics to the specific actions of the U.S. government. He points out that while China is expanding its primate breeding facilities to meet surging demand, the U.S. is dismantling its own infrastructure. "In his strongest condemnation yet of animal research, U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. said over the weekend that his department... is 'deeply committed to ending animal experimentation,'" Tooze reports. The irony is palpable: at the very moment the U.S. needs to compete with China in drug discovery, the executive branch is signaling a retreat from the very tools required for that competition.

The human and scientific cost of this policy shift is significant. Tooze quotes researchers warning of a catastrophic setback: "What a disaster this would be for not only infectious disease, but also neuroscience, behavior, reproductive biology, and transplant research," says microbiologist JoAnne Flynn. This is not merely a bureaucratic squabble; it is a potential threat to national security and public health. The administration's push to replace animal models with artificial intelligence, while theoretically sound, ignores the current reality that "such models currently 'cannot replicate the complexity of multiple systems working together in a living organism,'" as Joyce Cohen of the Emory National Primate Research Center argues. The administration's reliance on unproven alternatives while cutting funding for established centers reveals a dangerous gap between political rhetoric and scientific necessity.

The Supply Chain Crisis

The commentary also highlights the fragility of the global supply chain, exacerbated by the pandemic and geopolitical tensions. "COVID left the cupboard bare, and we're still struggling to recover," notes immunologist Jonah Sacha. The U.S. lost access to Chinese monkeys during the pandemic, and subsequent scandals in Cambodia have further tightened the supply. Yet, while the U.S. struggles to import, China is rapidly expanding its own breeding programs. "China has been rapidly expanding its primate breeding and research facilities," Tooze observes, noting that Western neuroscientists are already considering moves to China to access these resources.

This creates a stark picture of "uneven and combined development," where the U.S. is simultaneously the home of the world's most advanced biotech research and a place where that research is being actively hindered by domestic policy. The administration's decision to phase out monkey studies at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and the push to convert National Primate Research Centers into sanctuaries are framed not as progressive victories, but as strategic blunders. "We have to be ready for new biological and infectious disease threats, and we need to be able to compete with China," warns Jay Rappaport, director of the Tulane National Biomedical Research Center. The administration's actions, driven by a mix of animal rights advocacy and political posturing, risk leaving the U.S. unprepared for the next pandemic.

The polycrisis of our age is expressed not in the gyrations of Greenland, but in the price of lab monkeys.

Bottom Line

Adam Tooze's piece is a masterclass in connecting the absurd to the essential, using the price of a lab monkey to expose the strategic vulnerabilities of a nation in decline. The strongest part of the argument is its ability to bypass the noise of political theater and focus on the hard metrics of scientific capacity, revealing a U.S. government that is actively dismantling its own competitive edge. However, the piece perhaps underestimates the moral weight of the animal welfare arguments driving the administration's policy, which could resonate with a public increasingly concerned about ethical research practices. The reader should watch for the next major infectious disease outbreak, as it will serve as the ultimate stress test for a U.S. biotech sector that has been starved of its most critical resource.
