When I was a kid, I learned that the three things a human needed were food, clothing, and shelter. Shelter, or housing, is still as essential as ever. Yet, we seem to have a hard time providing enough high-quality, affordable housing to everyone who needs it. We have people who sleep on the streets without it, and people who live with their parents unable to afford their own.

This video will discuss several important facets on the topic of housing. We'll touch on home ownership, housing affordability, and homelessness. Let's get started after the bike bell. Let's start by talking about home ownership.

The United States is currently a nation of homeowners. About 65% of households own the home they currently live in. That figure isn't true everywhere. College towns often have higher rates of renting than owning due to all the student rentals.

But in general, more households own than rent. This is in part due to cultural reasons. Our country has attributed certain values to home ownership, like stability and family, but also for more practical ones. Once a household pays off their home loan, they don't have to pay for that housing anymore.

Same for taxes and insurance. That's a nice benefit. They also don't have a landlord telling them they can't paint their walls. Finally, home ownership is the primary form of wealth accumulation in the United States.

For many, homes are an investment. Renting has its perks, too. You won't be burdened by expensive repairs to your home, as your landlord has to take care of that, and it makes your housing situation more flexible if you need to move frequently. Renters and owners tend to have different needs, interests, and demographics.

Homeowners are disproportionately wealthy and white. And because there are more homeowners in the US, they tend to have a lot more political power locally. This is part of the reason communities across the United States are dealing with the not in my backyard movement or nimism. Homeowners are concerned about their property values, and it's such an important form of wealth accumulation, they're nervous about any change to their neighborhood that could lower their property values.
