How somalis became the new ‘welfare Queens’

Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, Bill Kristol · The Bulwark ·Jan 13, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

This piece cuts through the noise of current immigration crackdowns by revealing a chilling historical pattern: the administration is repurposing a fifty-year-old racist trope to justify dismantling the social safety net. Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol argue that the revocation of protected status for Somali refugees is not a genuine response to fraud, but a calculated political maneuver that fuses anti-immigrant sentiment with a war on welfare. For the busy listener, the value here is the context that transforms a breaking news scandal into a predictable cycle of political scapegoating.

The New Welfare Queen

The authors immediately challenge the official narrative surrounding the administration's decision to strip more than 2,000 Somali refugees of their legal status. They write, "The ostensible rationale is that 'country conditions in Somalia have improved,' which is a strange thing to say about a place very much still wracked by political turmoil and in the throes of a humanitarian crisis." This dismissal of the government's stated justification is crucial; it forces the reader to look past the bureaucratic excuse and examine the political utility of the move.

The commentary highlights how the administration is leveraging a specific local scandal to fuel a national narrative. As Longwell, Miller, and Kristol put it, "The spark—or, at least, the excuse—for the crackdown and rhetoric was a welfare fraud controversy involving mostly Somali-run service organizations stealing money that's supposed to finance programs to support needy Minnesotans." While they acknowledge that fraud did occur, they argue the reaction is disproportionate and targeted. The authors suggest the escalation is a direct response to the killing of Renee Good, a native-born citizen, by an immigration officer. "The uptick in just the last week is almost certainly linked to the administration's desperation to justify the killing of Good, a native-born, white U.S. citizen." This reframing shifts the focus from policy to political survival, suggesting the administration is using a minority group as a shield for its own violent overreach.

What you're seeing and hearing is the fusing of two parts of the Trump administration's agenda: a war on immigration and a war on the welfare state.

Echoes of the Past

The piece's most compelling section draws a direct line from the current events to Ronald Reagan's 1976 campaign. The authors explain how Reagan popularized the "welfare queen" trope, using a distorted story about a woman named Linda Taylor to argue that the government was being bled dry by lazy, undeserving recipients. Longwell, Miller, and Kristol note, "The backlash against anti-poverty programs specifically and government programs more generally had a lot to do with the fact that, after the civil rights movement and the Great Society reforms, large numbers of white voters believed that their hard-earned tax dollars were going to black people who didn't deserve it."

This historical parallel is not just an academic exercise; it explains the mechanics of the current outrage. The authors point out that just as the "welfare queen" story was a "political Rorschach test," the current narrative about Somali fraud is being constructed to serve a similar purpose. They write, "But the narrative that Trump and his allies are selling has a relationship with reality that is, at best, distant." They detail how a viral video by a self-described "citizen journalist" was used to manufacture a crisis, ignoring professional inspections that had already verified the safety of the centers. The authors argue this is a deliberate strategy: "Layered on all of this is the singling out of the entire Somali community as collectively responsible for the behavior of the scam artists in their midst, in a way Trump and his supporters would never generalize about native Mississippians."

Critics might note that acknowledging the reality of the $250 million fraud scheme weakens the argument that the administration is entirely fabricating the issue. However, the authors counter this by emphasizing the selective outrage and the racialized nature of the response, which distinguishes it from a standard policy critique.

The Human Cost and Political Backlash

The commentary concludes by examining the potential political fallout of this strategy. While Reagan successfully used the welfare trope to reshape the political landscape, the authors suggest the current administration may have overplayed its hand. They observe, "Trump seems to be using outrage over welfare fraud to fuel his policies singling out a minority group, rather than using anger at a minority group to fuel an agenda of rolling back the welfare state." This reversal suggests a more aggressive, less subtle approach that risks alienating moderate voters.

The authors cite recent polling and public reaction to the violence in Minneapolis as evidence of this backfire. "Even Joe Rogan is getting angry," they note, highlighting how the brutality of the crackdown is turning public opinion against the administration. The piece ends on a somber but hopeful note regarding the limits of this political playbook: "It may be that stealing a page from Reagan's playbook doesn't guarantee political success, even in this MAGAfied era."

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this argument is its ability to connect the dots between a specific local tragedy and a decades-long strategy of racialized political mobilization. Its biggest vulnerability is the assumption that public outrage over the killing of Renee Good will translate into a sustained political shift, rather than a temporary flashpoint. Readers should watch to see if the administration's aggressive rhetoric continues to erode its support among the very voters it seeks to mobilize.

How somalis became the new ‘welfare Queens’

by Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, Bill Kristol · The Bulwark · Read full article

HOMELAND SECURITY SECRETARY KRISTI NOEM announced on Tuesday that the federal government is revoking “temporary protected status” for more than 2,000 Somali refugees. The ostensible rationale is that “country conditions in Somalia have improved,” which is a strange thing to say about a place very much still wracked by political turmoil and in the throes of a humanitarian crisis.

Not that anybody takes the rationale seriously. The move comes after weeks of hostile Trump administration rhetoric toward Somali immigrants, including the president calling them “garbage” and “lowlifes” ” and vowing to “send them back from where they came.” Lately his administration has been trying to do just that through a massive immigration crackdown in Minneapolis, home of a large Somali diaspora—and where last week an immigration officer killed local resident Renee Good, who was protesting the enforcement action.

The spark—or, at least, the excuse—for the crackdown and rhetoric was a welfare fraud controversy involving mostly Somali-run service organizations stealing money that’s supposed to finance programs to support needy Minnesotans. The decade-old scandal attracted national attention in December following new indictments from federal prosecutors and a now-viral video from a MAGA-aligned, self-described “citizen journalist.”

Animus toward Somalis is nothing new for Trump and his supporters. The uptick in just the last week is almost certainly linked to the administration’s desperation to justify the killing of Good, a native-born, white U.S. citizen. But something else is going on here too. What you’re seeing and hearing is the fusing of two parts of the Trump administration’s agenda.

One is its war on immigration, which every day seems less like an attempt to control the border and more like an effort to minimize—and bully—America’s non-native, non-white population. The other is Trump’s war on the welfare state, which feels less like an effort to cut waste and more like an attempt to gut core programs that provide health care, childcare, and other critical services to many millions of Americans

You can imagine why Trump and his supporters are putting these two narratives together. And that’s especially true if you’re familiar with a political trope that first became part of the national conversation fifty years ago—one that, like the Somali fraud story, twisted some real facts and mainstream policy arguments into a fictional tale that leveraged ugly sentiments against a minority group in order to promote a more radical agenda.
