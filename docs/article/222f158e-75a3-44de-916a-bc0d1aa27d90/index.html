This is somewhere no human being was ever meant to go. Deep in the foothills of the Himalayas, temperatures near Reesei vary in the extremes, dropping from 50° C all the way down to freezing. Everywhere you look, jagged cliff faces create these deep, dangerous canyons. You can imagine the challenge faced by crews attempting to build a record-breaking bridge here.

To access civilization, you have to trek the dusty tracks by foot or take a boat journey along the river. Simply getting to the Janab Bridge site was an enormous undertaking, let alone maneuvering the 30,000 tons of steel necessary to build it. No motorways, no nearby factories, and not a flat, stable surface in sight. This is construction in one of mother nature's most unforgiving environments.

It might sound a bit cliche, but sometimes cliches do ring true. Constructing Chennab Bridge should have been impossible. From searing temperatures to dangerous ice and snow, working conditions really don't come much tougher than this. And it's not just the crews who suffer.

When it's exposed to extreme temperatures, steel can expand and contract, making it really difficult to manipulate and accurately line up. But the truth is, while crews are battling the snow and the sun, there's an even bigger challenge looming, and that is the wind. You see, this canyon can experience gusts of up to 165 mph. Now, that's unbelievably dangerous for people working hundreds of meters up in the mountains as it is, let alone when you consider the fact they're maneuvering thousands of tons of steel into place.

And even if the weather decides to take it easy on you from above, there's no guarantee an earthquake won't shake the ground to its core. Any one of these factors in their own right could have put a stop to the notion of building a canyon crossing bridge in this location.
