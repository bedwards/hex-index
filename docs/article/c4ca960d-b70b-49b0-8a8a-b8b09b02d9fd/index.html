How China courted Iran

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Jan 12, 2026 ·14 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider cuts through the noise of geopolitical grandstanding to reveal a relationship defined not by shared values, but by a cynical, high-stakes transaction of survival. While the world focuses on the internet blackouts tearing through Iran and the regime's desperate isolation, Schneider exposes the quiet machinery of how Beijing manages a partner it barely trusts. This is not a story of ideological allies, but of two regimes calculating how much they can extract from each other before the bill comes due.

The Architecture of Distrust

Schneider immediately dismantles the romanticized notion of a natural alliance between Tehran and Beijing. He notes that "for two countries so closely aligned, China and Iran don't have all that much in common," pointing out the glaring contradiction of a theocracy partnering with a state that imprisons Uyghur and Kazakh Muslims. The author traces the roots of this friction back to the 1979 Islamic Revolution, where the new government's slogan "Neither East nor West" was a direct rebuke to foreign powers, including China, which had backed the deposed Shah.

The piece highlights a specific historical irony: "Chairman Hua Guofeng was among the last foreigners to meet with the Shah before he was deposed by the revolution." This detail serves as a potent reminder that the current partnership is built on the ashes of past betrayals. Schneider argues that the relationship survived not through friendship, but through the "personal charisma" of figures like Zhang Guoqing, who leveraged arms sales during the Iran-Iraq War to build a foundation of mutual utility. The author writes, "Between 1987 and 1989, Zhang was promoted twice for his work at Norinco," illustrating how commercial opportunism often trumps diplomatic protocol in this sphere.

"With friends like these, who needs enemies?" ~ A Persian proverb from the poet Saadi

This proverb, invoked by Schneider, perfectly captures the Iranian public's growing resentment. The relationship is maintained through a mix of "material incentives, narrative control, and appeals to specific power centers within Iran," even as favorability among ordinary Iranians plummets. Critics might note that Schneider underplays the sheer lack of alternatives for Tehran; without Beijing, the Iranian economy might have collapsed entirely under the weight of sanctions, making the "friendship" less a choice and more a necessity.

The Economics of Survival

The core of Schneider's analysis lies in the brutal reality of the economic exchange. He details how China has become the primary lifeline for the Iranian regime, purchasing an estimated "77% of Iran's crude oil in 2024." However, this is not a standard trade relationship. Schneider explains that to evade US secondary sanctions, the two nations have developed a shadowy system involving a "dark fleet" of tankers that turn off transponders and falsify documentation.

The author describes a barter system where "China has paid for Iranian oil through the provision of cheap consumer items," a move that has flooded Iranian markets with low-quality goods. Schneider quotes Tehran-based journalist Mohammad Hashemi to illustrate the domestic fallout: "For more than a decade, inexpensive and low-quality Chinese consumer goods—ranging from vehicles to homeware—have flooded Iranian markets, putting local manufacturers and artisans out of business." This dynamic creates a paradox where the regime relies on Beijing for survival, yet the very goods Beijing sends are destroying the local economy that sustains the regime's base.

The 25-year partnership agreement, often touted as a strategic masterstroke, is revealed to be opaque and controversial. Schneider notes that "the text of the partnership agreement still has not been publicly disclosed," fueling skepticism on both sides. While some Chinese experts worry about the risks of "long-arm jurisdiction" from the US, Schneider points out that Chinese investment in Iran remains a fraction of what is poured into Saudi Arabia or Iraq. The author writes, "China's investments in Iran grew to US$3.924 billion by the end of 2023 (although this is peanuts compared to China's other regional investments)." This disparity suggests that for Beijing, Iran is a strategic hedge rather than a primary economic engine.

Narrative Control and the Cost of Silence

Perhaps the most chilling aspect of Schneider's coverage is the mechanism of "narrative control" used to suppress dissent. When the coronavirus pandemic hit, and Iran's health ministry accused China of lying about the virus's origins, the Chinese ambassador's response was a masterclass in diplomatic pressure. Schneider recounts how the ambassador suggested the Iranian ministry "read their news carefully in order to draw conclusions," a thinly veiled threat that signaled Beijing's influence.

The result was a swift and humiliating reversal by the Iranian foreign ministry, which praised China's "bravery, dedication & professionalism." Schneider argues that this appeasement was part of a broader strategy to secure Iranian silence on human rights abuses in Xinjiang. He notes that "not a single Muslim-majority country voted to condemn China's use of re-education camps for Uyghurs," with Iran signing a letter of support in exchange for economic aid and political cover.

The author highlights the absurdity of this arrangement, describing how Iranian officials were sent to Xinjiang to "tweet about Uyghur food" and how state media aired interviews defending the camps as "vocational centers." Schneider writes, "The narrative on the Uyghur issue is essentially, 'everyone's war on terror is their own business.'" This framing allows Tehran to ignore the suffering of Muslims in China because it serves the regime's own interest in suppressing its own separatist movements. The author connects this to China's broader playbook, noting that Beijing has used similar tactics since the 1990s to "guarantee Xinjiang was starved of support from pan-Turkic or pan-Islamic sympathizers."

"It seems China's statistics were a bitter joke as many in the world thought this was a flu-like disease with a smaller mortality rate," said Iran's health ministry spokesman, before the regime was forced to backtrack.

This episode underscores the fragility of the relationship. Schneider points out that while the regime may be appeased, the Iranian public is not. The environmental damage caused by Chinese operations, such as the destruction of the al-Azim wetland and the backlash against bottom trawling, has led to direct intervention by the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps. Schneider quotes a general who warned, "One cannot close their eyes to these realities and we have to stand up firmly against those who seize bread from the table of traditional fishermen." This suggests that the regime's ability to manage the relationship is hitting a ceiling where domestic unrest threatens to override diplomatic expediency.

Bottom Line

Jordan Schneider's analysis succeeds in stripping away the geopolitical mystique to reveal a relationship built on mutual exploitation and deep-seated suspicion. The strongest part of the argument is the detailed exposure of how Beijing uses economic leverage and narrative control to neutralize Iranian dissent, turning a potential human rights ally into a silent partner in repression. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its assumption that the Iranian regime can indefinitely manage the domestic backlash against Chinese economic dominance; the rising tide of public anger over environmental destruction and the flood of cheap goods suggests the transactional bargain is becoming increasingly unstable. The reader should watch for whether the regime's need for regime survival will eventually force a break with Beijing, or if the cost of isolation will drive them deeper into a partnership that offers little more than a slow, suffocating decline.
