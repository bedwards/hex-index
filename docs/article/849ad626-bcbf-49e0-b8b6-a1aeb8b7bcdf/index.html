By certain operational metrics, flight scheduled or passengers flown, American Airlines is not only the largest air carrier in the US, but the largest in the world. And yet, by most financial metrics, margin, revenue, profit, it is the least effective of the Delta United American triop at actually making money. It's a curious dichotomy, but it's not explained by simple bad luck. Since 2020, at almost every major moment in which the airlines management could have made the right or the wrong decision, they made the wrong one.

It started with co in those earliest days of the pandemic. The big strategic call each airline had to make revolved around timeline. The aviation industry quickly understood that the demand for short-haul travel would likely recover sooner than the demand for long haul travel due to a combination of government regulation and passenger preference. Therefore, it was clear the widebody and long haul planes predominantly used for intercontinental flights would not be necessary anytime soon.

While each airline kept a portion of these planes operational for the skeleton international schedules they continued to operate, they each had to decide what to do with the rest of them. United kept them all. Some sat in short-term storage at their maintenance hubs. Others were flown to longerterm storage at desert airports like Roswell, New Mexico and Victorville, California.

Delta took a broadly similar approach with the exception of its trip 7 fleet. These were old, comparatively inefficient aircraft that might not have remained in the airlines fleet for too much longer regardless. So Delta decided to just retire all 18 of them. American, meanwhile, clearly had a much more pessimistic prediction of what demand recovery would look like, and therefore they retired all of their A330, 767, and 757 aircraft, representing a massive 30% reduction in their longhaul fleet.

It wasn't long before they regretted this decision. Fast forward to summer 2022, and Americans and Europeans were vaxed and ready to travel. Transatlantic travel was up to about 90% of normal and airlines were able to charge some of the highest fairs ever thanks to the pent-up nature of this demand. People were willing to spend a lot after 2 years largely devoid of long haul leisure travel.
