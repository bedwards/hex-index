Trump's Tiger-Riding Predicament | Digest: March 2026

Various · Sinification ·Apr 6, 2026 · 23 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

This digest does not merely report on the March 2026 geopolitical landscape; it exposes a dangerous cognitive dissonance in Beijing's elite circles regarding the war in Iran. While the media narrative has settled on the idea that American failure in the Middle East is a strategic windfall for China, the authors here reveal a far more fractured reality where economic vulnerability threatens to outweigh geopolitical opportunity. The most striking revelation is not the war itself, but the stark warning from Morgan Stanley's Xing Ziqiang that a prolonged conflict could drag China's GDP to a catastrophic 3%, shattering the illusion that Washington's quagmire is a free lunch for Beijing.

The Tiger's Back

The central metaphor driving the analysis is the ancient Chinese idiom "riding a tiger," which perfectly captures the Trump administration's dilemma. As Wang Jiangyu observes, "Trump is 'riding a tiger and unable to get off': he underestimated Iran's resilience, but Iranian surrender is impossible without a large-scale ground invasion that he does not dare launch." This framing is crucial because it moves the conversation beyond simple "US failure" narratives to a more nuanced understanding of a conflict that cannot be won but also cannot be abandoned.

The authors are quick to dismantle the comforting notion that an American distraction in Iran will soften US policy toward China. Li Cheng explicitly rejects the historical parallel to the post-9/11 era, noting that "this is not the post-9/11 moment, when Bush recognised that the PRC was not the primary adversary." Instead, the conflict has hardened into a strategic competition where Beijing faces "many paradoxes and policy choices." This is a vital correction to the wishful thinking that plagues much of the commentary on US-China relations; the war does not create a vacuum for China to fill, but rather a complex trap where both sides are constrained.

The claim that a Gulf shock would collapse US AI financing, let alone US financial hegemony, is overstated: petrodollars account for less than 10% of AI financing.

Shi Zhan reinforces this by arguing that it is "premature to predict that the US will lose the war without first analysing its objectives." If Washington's goal is merely to degrade nuclear capabilities rather than achieve regime change, the US may still succeed. This perspective is often missing from the "schadenfreude"-driven analysis that dominates the field, reminding readers that American strategic aims are often narrower and more achievable than they appear on the surface.

The Economic Reality Check

While the geopolitical strategists debate the balance of power, the economists are sounding the alarm on the immediate material costs. The most sobering analysis comes from Xing Ziqiang, who warns that markets are "severely underpricing the risk of a prolonged oil shock." He posits a terrifying scenario: "If oil were to average around US$130 for a quarter... real GDP growth could fall to around 3% or lower without policy intervention." This is not a theoretical exercise; it is a direct challenge to the optimistic growth targets set in the new Five-Year Plan.

In contrast, Di Dongsheng offers a more aggressive, hawkish view, suggesting China is "following the script the United States played in the early stages of World War One to profit from the war." He argues that higher oil prices improve the competitive position of Chinese manufacturing relative to more oil-dependent rivals and transfer wealth to regions where Chinese goods are most competitive. However, this argument feels precarious when weighed against the sheer scale of the supply shock Xing predicts. Critics might note that Di's optimism relies on China's ability to insulate its economy from global inflation, a feat that becomes increasingly difficult as energy costs rise non-linearly.

The debate over domestic policy further highlights the tension between growth and social stability. The digest notes that the "token increase" in rural pensions during the Two Sessions was a major disappointment. Lü Dewen mounts a compelling rebuttal to calls for cash handouts, arguing that transfers risk producing a "crowding-out effect" on children's sense of filial obligation. This echoes the economic concept of crowding out, where government spending displaces private or social investment, but here it is applied to the moral economy of the family. As Zhou Tianyong starkly puts it, without institutional change, China faces "an average growth of just 2.4% through 2035," a figure that renders the current trajectory unsustainable.

Strategic Recalibration

Beyond the immediate crisis, the digest captures a profound shift in China's strategic posture. The traditional doctrine of non-interference is crumbling under the weight of global interests. Zheng Yongnian calls for an "Interventionism 2.0," arguing that the PRC can no longer afford to stand aside when its overseas assets are threatened. This represents a fundamental break from the past, moving from a reactive to a proactive global stance.

On the issue of Taiwan, the tone has shifted from strategic patience to urgent impatience. Wei Leijie captures this bluntness, arguing that "common arguments for strategic patience are fallacies" and calling for the "recovery" of the island at the earliest opportunity. This suggests that Beijing is no longer waiting for a perfect moment but is preparing to act on a timeline dictated by its own internal political pressures.

The refusal of US allies to follow Washington's lead in the Strait of Hormuz sets a precedent that will make it much harder for America to rally allies in future wars.

Wang Jiangyu's observation that this may be "the last war Washington can launch with any semblance of dignity" underscores the fragility of the US-led order. Yet, the authors are careful not to overstate this weakness. Wu Hailong warns that the US-Israeli strikes are part of a broader strategy to weaken aligned states one by one, requiring a "coordinated Sino-Russian response" that goes beyond rhetoric. The consensus is clear: the world is moving toward a more fragmented, volatile order where China must navigate a minefield of its own making.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this digest is its refusal to accept the "US failure equals Chinese victory" narrative as a given, instead highlighting the severe economic risks that a prolonged Middle East war poses to China's own growth targets. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the tension between the hawkish desire for geopolitical gain and the pragmatic economic warnings that such gains may come at a cost Beijing cannot afford. Readers should watch closely for how the Chinese government balances the call for "Interventionism 2.0" against the stark reality of a potential 3% GDP contraction.

The war in Iran predictably dominates commentary in March.

We covered initial reactions to the US-Israeli strikes in our briefing earlier last month, where we noted that assessments of China’s risk-opportunity balance hinged largely on Trump’s ability to turn action into success. We also featured a censored piece from the Intellisia Institute that pushed this logic further, arguing that a prolonged Middle East war could become a major strategic opportunity for China.

More than a month on, early scepticism about US regime-change prospects in Iran has hardened into a feeling somewhere between consternation and schadenfreude, with most analysts now framing the situation as a quagmire. The dominant motif is Trump’s “tiger-riding predicament” [骑虎难下]—meaning that it is easier to climb on a tiger than to dismount and easier to start a war than to end one.

Which makes it refreshing to read CFAU professor Shi Zhan and former Brookings “returnee scholar” Li Cheng urging caution on US failure narratives and dismissing comparisons to the post-9/11 strategic distraction from China. On the more familiar hawkish side we have Wang Jiangyu, who calls this “the last war America can launch with any semblance of dignity”.

Meanwhile, Di Dongsheng argues that high energy prices are a net gain for China—a case he was apparently unable or unwilling to make on his WeChat blog on the same theme a few days earlier, which promised but conspicuously withheld the argument. The economic counter-case comes from Morgan Stanley Chief China Economist Xing Ziqiang, who warns that markets are underpricing the oil shock. His surprisingly stark warning is that $130 oil over a single quarter could drag China’s GDP growth to 3% or below.

Coverage of the Chinese economy is also weightier than usual, driven by the Two Sessions and the new Five-Year Plan. Direct FYP coverage was expectedly boilerplate, but the rural pension question (the FYP’s token increase was arguably the biggest social policy disappointment of the Two Sessions) prompted a more pointed debate.
