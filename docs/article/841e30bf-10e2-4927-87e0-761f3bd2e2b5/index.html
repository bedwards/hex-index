Tiger-Riding Predicament

Various · Sinification ·Apr 6, 2026 ·23 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Sinification's March 2026 digest gathers voices from across China's strategic commentariat to answer a single question: what does the US-Israeli strike on Iran mean for the global balance of power, and for Beijing's place within it? The phrase supplying the title—"riding a tiger and unable to get off" (鯲虎难下)—captures the consensus Chinese reading. The White House, in this view, has launched a war it can neither conclude on favorable terms nor walk away from, and the fallout is reshaping how Chinese analysts measure American capacity.

An Alliance That Refused to Follow

The most striking claim running through the digest is not that the strikes were strategically unwise—though several contributors say so—but that Washington could not assemble allies to participate. Wang Jiangyu of City University of Hong Kong treats this as a threshold event. In his reading, the refusal of traditional partners to supply combat forces, basing rights, or even diplomatic cover marks the end of an era in which the United States could credibly promise an alliance response on short notice.

"This sets a precedent that will make it much harder for America to rally allies in future wars—this may be the last war Washington can launch with any semblance of dignity."

Wang's formulation is deliberately strong, and the digest treats it as representative rather than outlying. Shi Zhan, writing from China Foreign Affairs University, urges more analytical restraint, arguing it is "premature" to declare American defeat without first understanding what Washington actually sought. But even Shi's caution concedes the underlying point: the objective function is no longer legible to allies, and that itself is the news.

A counterpoint the digest does not develop, but one worth raising, is that allied non-participation can reflect political constraint at home rather than any judgment about American leadership. European governments facing domestic opposition to Middle Eastern wars may be showing the limits of their own coalitions, not of the transatlantic relationship. The Chinese reading collapses that distinction because its analytical interest lies elsewhere: whatever the cause, the observable result is the same, and it is the result that matters for strategic planning.

China as the Beneficiary of Disorder

If Wang supplies the diagnosis, Di Dongsheng of Renmin University supplies the framework for exploiting it. His contribution is the digest's most provocative, and it draws an analogy Chinese strategists rarely reach for in public.

"China is following the script the United States played in the early stages of World War One to profit from the war."

Di's argument is that higher oil prices stretch the fiscal and strategic resources of the United States and its allies while boosting the competitiveness of Chinese manufacturing. He expects accelerated Chinese exports of "new energy vehicles, storage, photovoltaics and wind power equipment"—the four categories Beijing has placed at the center of its industrial strategy. The historical parallel is meant to be uncomfortable: the United States, in 1914–1917, sold to both sides of a European war while husbanding its own capacity, and emerged as the dominant industrial and financial power. Di is suggesting the same script can be run, with China in the role the United States once played.

The weakness of the analogy, which the digest lets pass, is that 1914 America was an insulated continental economy with no entangling alliances and a financial system that benefited directly from European borrowing. Contemporary China is deeply integrated into the very supply chains now being disrupted, and its growth model remains export-dependent in ways that a global demand shock would damage, not reward. Yet the appetite among Chinese commentators for a narrative of American self-exhaustion is strong enough that the historical awkwardness is not a deal-breaker.

The Oil Shock Beijing Actually Worries About

Morgan Stanley's China chief economist Xing Ziqiang offers the sharpest rebuttal to the optimistic reading, and the digest gives him prominent placement. His concern is not strategic but mechanical: Chinese GDP growth is more exposed to oil prices than the triumphalist accounts acknowledge.

"$130 oil for a quarter could drag China's GDP growth to around 3% or lower. Markets are severely underpricing this risk."

Xing's policy recommendation—"ease credit conditions through reserve requirement cuts"—positions him against contributors who want fiscal tightening to preserve optionality. The debate matters because the gap between the strategic commentary and the economic commentary reveals a fracture in Chinese thinking: the analysts who track great-power dynamics see opportunity, while the analysts who track balance sheets see fragility. Both camps are reading the same war and reaching different conclusions because they are measuring different things. A reader who consumes only the strategic commentary would miss how carefully Beijing is watching the yuan, the property sector, and the expected reaction of central banks elsewhere.

The Doctrine Question: Interventionism 2.0

A different line of argument runs through Zheng Yongnian's contribution from the Chinese University of Hong Kong, Shenzhen. Zheng is not writing about Iran directly; he is writing about what Iran reveals. His phrase "Interventionism 2.0" signals a proposal that Beijing develop a formal doctrine permitting intervention abroad when Chinese overseas interests face threats.

The proposal is uncomfortable for official Chinese rhetoric, which has long defined non-interference as a distinguishing virtue of its foreign policy. Zheng is arguing that the distinction cannot survive the scale of Chinese overseas commercial exposure—Belt and Road infrastructure, energy contracts, diaspora populations. Wu Xinbo of Fudan University, in the same digest, notes that "Beijing is increasingly concerned about US pressure on overseas economic interests." Jia Qingguo, quoted alongside, suggests a middle path: shifting Chinese foreign investment from wholly-owned models to joint ventures, on the theory that host-country partners provide political protection that intervention capability otherwise would.

None of these three propose anything the state apparatus is ready to adopt. What they do propose is a conversation about the gap between China's declared principles and its actual footprint—a gap that the current war, by demonstrating what happens when a great power is seen as irresolute, makes harder to defer.

The Domestic Ceiling

The digest's second half turns inward, and the tone changes. Where the strategic commentary is confident to the point of triumphalism, the economic commentary is anxious to the point of gloom.

Zhou Tianyong projects that, absent serious institutional reform, Chinese GDP growth will average 2.48% over 2026–2030—roughly half the official 5% target. Yao Yang, whose work on rural-urban inequality has been influential in policy circles, dismisses the AI boom as "a massive bubble" and suggests a Chinese photonic chip breakthrough within two to five years could collapse it. The digest juxtaposes these assessments with the Two Sessions 2026 announcement of a rural pension increase—from 143 to 163 yuan per month, a sum so modest that several contributors treat it as evidence of Beijing's reluctance to shift toward consumption-led growth.

"Token pension increases are crowding out the very consumption transition they are meant to enable."

That judgment, in various forms, recurs across multiple contributors. Tang Dajie argues for rural pension parity; Lü Dewen warns the increases risk a "crowding-out effect" on filial obligation. The debate is a miniature of the larger one: Chinese analysts believe the war creates external opportunity, but they also believe the domestic model is approaching a structural ceiling that no external opportunity can lift.

Taiwan and the Closing Window

The digest includes a sharper voice on Taiwan from Wei Leijie of Xiamen University Law School, who argues that "recovery" (his word) is becoming more strategically pressing because "time will not resolve the issue naturally." The framing is notable in Chinese strategic writing because it rejects the patient-waiting posture that has characterized official commentary for decades. Wei's argument is that the longer integration is deferred, the more Taiwanese identity solidifies, the more US arms sales accumulate, and the more the status quo calcifies against Beijing's preferred end state.

A planned summit in May is mentioned in passing; Wu Xinbo notes that Beijing will prioritize technology controls and Taiwan arms sales restraint over tariff questions. The hierarchy is the story. Chinese strategists treat tariffs as noise and semiconductor export controls and Taiwan arms sales as substance, which is the inverse of how the American political conversation frames the relationship.

Bottom Line

The Sinification digest is valuable less for any single claim than for the collective picture it assembles. Chinese strategic analysts are reading the Iran war as confirmation of a structural shift they have been anticipating for years: allies that will not follow, an American leadership whose objectives are no longer legible, a global order in which Beijing's relative position improves without Beijing having to act. The economic commentators, reading the same events, see instead the fragility of a growth model that cannot absorb an oil shock and cannot be easily reformed in time. The gap between those two readings is where the interesting policy choices live. The external world is offering China opportunity; the internal world is offering constraint; and the contributors collected here are, collectively, trying to work out which matters more. Readers outside China should pay less attention to the triumphalist lines—they are the easiest to caricature and the least predictive—and more to the debate over Interventionism 2.0 and the doctrinal shift on Taiwan. Those are the moves that would actually change the map.
