How Afghanistan became a failed state

Shirvan Neftchi · CaspianReport ·Aug 24, 2021 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Shirvan Neftchi dismantles the popular myth that Afghanistan was always a war-torn wasteland, revealing a decade where the nation stood on the precipice of genuine democracy. His most striking claim is not that the country failed due to inherent cultural flaws, but that it was actively derailed by a fatal collision of foreign ideologies and incompetent domestic policy. For busy readers trying to make sense of today's headlines, this historical context is not just academic; it explains why the current collapse feels so inevitable.

The Golden Age and the Fatal Compromise

Neftchi paints a vivid picture of the 1960s, a period often erased from the modern narrative. He writes, "Kabul was a flourishing city in the center of the hippie trail... Afghan culture was booming and western icons were on display in every corner." This imagery serves a crucial purpose: it establishes that modernization was not an alien imposition but an organic desire of the Afghan people. The author highlights a rare moment of political maturity when King Zahir Shah voluntarily limited his own power. "Leaders don't go about restricting their own power voluntarily," Neftchi notes, "but that's exactly what the Afghan king did." This self-restraint was the foundation of the 1964 constitution, which freed political prisoners and established a parliament.

However, the author identifies a critical flaw in this progress: the state's reliance on foreign patronage to fund its ambitions. Prime Minister Sardar Daud Khan, seeking to modernize without raising taxes, decided to "play both sides" of the Cold War. Neftchi captures this precarious balancing act with a memorable quote: "I feel happiest when I can light my American cigarette with Soviet matches." While this strategy initially brought infrastructure and education, it sowed the seeds of internal division. The country adopted American curricula for its technocrats while simultaneously training its military officers in Soviet Marxist-Leninist ideology.

The country was on course to become a democracy, yet good intentions are ubiquitous in politics; what is scarce is good policy making.

This dual education system created a fractured elite. Neftchi argues that the discrepancy between Western-educated civilians and Soviet-trained officers was a "discrepancy that would later come to haunt Afghan politics." Critics might argue that the author places too much blame on foreign influence, overlooking the agency of local factions who eagerly adopted these ideologies for their own power struggles. Nevertheless, the narrative effectively demonstrates how external powers weaponized the very institutions meant to build a nation.

The Collapse of Order and the Rise of Chaos

The turning point arrives with Daud Khan's return to power and his subsequent purge of the very communists who helped him seize the presidency. Neftchi describes the ensuing violence with chilling clarity: "The tank turrets shifted towards the presidential palace and a firefight broke out." The death of Daud Khan marked the end of the experiment in organic democracy. The new communist leadership, led by Nur Muhammad Taraki and Hafizullah Amin, attempted to force a "workers paradise" onto a society unprepared for it.

The author's analysis of the failed reforms is particularly sharp. He points out that while Taraki's intentions to cancel debts and emancipate women were noble, the execution was disastrous. "By abruptly cancelling debts, Afghan landlords stopped lending money," Neftchi explains, noting that this uprooted centuries of socioeconomic balance. The result was not liberation but famine and social ostracization. The government's heavy-handed response to the resulting unrest, specifically the bombing of Herat, turned a political crisis into a full-scale civil war. Neftchi writes, "The destruction of Herat was so abrupt, so extreme, that it flipped the country into a state of war."

This section highlights a recurring theme: the disconnect between urban ideologues and rural reality. The author suggests that the communist leadership, "a man who had no experience in governance," tried to implement abstract theories that destroyed the traditional safety nets of the countryside. As Neftchi puts it, "The socio-economic reforms had not affected rural communities; now with the communist programs, millions of Afghans were left feeling ashamed and dishonored."

The Soviet Intervention and the End of the State

The final descent into failure is marked by the Soviet invasion in 1979. Neftchi frames this not as a sudden external shock, but as the inevitable conclusion of internal fragmentation. The military hierarchy had already collapsed, with "low-ranking corporals began disrespecting high-ranking superiors." When the Soviets finally rolled in, they were stepping into a vacuum of authority. The author notes that the invasion was a desperate move by Moscow to salvage a failing ally: "The Soviets realized that they had to replace Amin before he discredited communism beyond repair."

Decades of combat readiness collapsed in a matter of days.

The commentary here is stark. The author argues that the state didn't just fail; it was actively dismantled by a combination of internal purges and external intervention. The rise of the Mujahideen was a reaction to this chaos, unified initially by anti-communism rather than pure religious zeal. Neftchi's account suggests that the "failed state" label is a result of specific policy choices made over a few critical decades, rather than an inherent characteristic of the nation.

Bottom Line

Neftchi's strongest asset is his ability to humanize the political history, showing how specific policy errors—like debt cancellation and dual military training—cascaded into national ruin. The piece's vulnerability lies in its relative silence on the role of Pakistan and the US in fueling the subsequent insurgency, focusing heavily on the Soviet-Afghan dynamic. Readers should watch for how this historical pattern of external interference and internal overreach continues to shape the region's geopolitical instability today.
