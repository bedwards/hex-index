How the Internet was Stolen

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Dec 20, 2022 · 79 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now delivers a provocative historical indictment, arguing that the internet was not merely commercialized but systematically "stolen" from its public origins through political maneuvering and corporate capture. This is not a standard tech history; it is a forensic accounting of how taxpayer-funded infrastructure was dismantled to serve private profit, a narrative that demands attention as we grapple with the monopolistic realities of today's digital landscape.

The Public Roots of a Private Empire

The author begins by dismantling the myth of the internet as a spontaneous market creation. "The history of the internet has been one of big Capital pilfering from public investment and National infrastructure," Then & Now writes, immediately reframing the narrative from innovation to expropriation. The piece meticulously traces the lineage from the Department of Defense's Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) to the National Science Foundation (NSF), highlighting that the network was born of Cold War military strategy and academic collaboration, not Silicon Valley entrepreneurship.

The argument gains traction by contrasting the original vision with the eventual outcome. As Then & Now puts it, "The internet was meant to be a new Utopia, a tool of Liberty, fraternity, equality of radical democracy and freedom." This idealistic framing is crucial because it establishes a moral baseline against which the subsequent privatization feels like a betrayal rather than an evolution. The author notes that early researchers, frustrated by the scarcity of computing power, envisioned a "scholarly Utopia" where data could be shared freely across institutions.

"The web as we've come to know it is a great ocean we know that we only swim, paddle on its surface, never diving beyond the first few pages of search results... but its depth, what it's made of, how it was made, the history that gave shape to it, remain like the ocean surprisingly unknown to us."

This metaphor effectively captures the disconnect between user experience and historical reality. However, critics might note that the author's focus on "theft" occasionally glosses over the genuine necessity of private capital to scale the infrastructure once the public sector reached its limits. The transition was messy, but was it purely malicious, or a pragmatic response to the exploding demand for bandwidth that the government could no longer fund?

The Betrayal of the Information Superhighway

The narrative shifts sharply to the 1990s, identifying the moment the public-private partnership was hijacked. Then & Now details how Senator Al Gore initially championed a model where 20% of the infrastructure would remain public, modeled after public broadcasting. "Senator Daniel Inouye argued that legislation should preserve 20 of the infrastructure for public use to provide libraries, education, non-profits... directly to the public without charge," the author notes, illustrating a viable alternative path that was discarded.

The turning point is attributed to a confluence of lobbying and ideological shifts. The text claims that after meeting with Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan, Gore abandoned the public model in favor of deregulation. "Suddenly Gore changed his mind about the public private partnership... the NSF Net infrastructure should be sold off and all left to the whims of the market," Then & Now writes. This section is the piece's most damning, suggesting that the decision to privatize was less about economic efficiency and more about political influence.

The evidence presented regarding lobbying spending is stark. The author points out that telecommunications giants spent $66 million on lobbying between 1996 and 1998, surpassing the tobacco and aerospace industries combined. "The money was donated to candidates across both parties," the text observes, underscoring the bipartisan nature of the capture. This framing is effective in showing that the outcome was not an accident of history but the result of deliberate financial pressure.

"It was decided that NSF Net would be carved up and transferred to the corporations with the infrastructure handed over, it was officially decommissioned in 1995 with hardly a whimper of protest."

The phrase "hardly a whimper of protest" is a powerful rhetorical choice, highlighting the public's disengagement or lack of awareness during the transfer of a national asset. While the author paints a clear picture of corporate greed, a counterargument worth considering is that the commercial explosion of the internet, driven by this very deregulation, created the connectivity and services that billions rely on today. The trade-off between public control and private innovation remains a complex, unresolved tension.

From Hackers to Monopolies

The commentary also touches on the cultural shift from the open, collaborative ethos of early hackers to the closed, proprietary systems of the modern era. The author recalls how early researchers fought against locked terminals, with one student leaving a note saying, "What a big inconvenience it is to have to go under the floor so please do not inconvenience people by locking the door any longer." This anecdote serves as a microcosm for the broader struggle between open access and proprietary control.

As the network grew, the ban on commercial use became untenable. "Commercial use was banned, the network was to be used for research and education only," the text explains, noting that this rule was eventually ignored and then formally abandoned. The author argues that the transition was driven by a belief that the market could solve problems the government could not, a belief that ultimately led to the concentration of power in a few tech giants.

"In truth, no private company would have been capable of developing a project like the internet, which required years of R&D effort spread out over scores of far-flung agencies and which began to take off only after decades of investment."

This assertion is the backbone of the piece's argument: the private sector did not build the internet; it inherited it. The author uses this to challenge the narrative of Silicon Valley as the sole engine of progress. By emphasizing the role of the Pentagon and universities, Then & Now forces the reader to reconsider who truly deserves credit for the digital age.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's strongest asset is its rigorous documentation of the public funding that underpinned the internet's creation, effectively dismantling the myth of purely private innovation. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its somewhat monolithic portrayal of the privatization process, which may understate the genuine challenges of government scaling and the benefits of market competition. Readers should watch for how this historical framing influences current debates on net neutrality and antitrust regulation, as the question of who owns the digital public square remains as urgent as ever.
