Heather Cox Richardson does not merely report on a chaotic week in Washington; she exposes a deliberate, nostalgic fantasy being weaponized against the reality of 2026. By anchoring President Trump's bizarre social media post about Davy Crockett to the actual history of the 1950s right-wing backlash, she reveals that the current administration is not improvising but executing a decades-old playbook to dismantle the modern state. This is not just political noise; it is a structured attempt to replace federal responsibility with a violent, exclusionary myth.

The Frontier Myth as Political Weapon

Richardson begins by dissecting Trump's claim that the 1950s TV hero Davy Crockett is a "distant relative" of Representative Jasmine Crockett, a connection that has no historical basis. "It feels frighteningly appropriate for a 1950s television western to seem more important to Trump right now than the real world of April 2026 does," she writes. This observation is the key to the entire piece: the administration is prioritizing a cartoonish version of American history over the actual crises facing the nation. The author notes that the original "Davy Crockett" craze coincided with a political movement eager to dismantle the New Deal, championing the "western hero as the true American" against federal regulation.

She connects this cultural nostalgia directly to the ideological framework established in Barry Goldwater's 1960 book, The Conscience of a Conservative. Richardson points out that Goldwater, despite his cowboy persona, grew up with a live-in maid and argued that the Constitution was designed to prevent "the tyranny of the masses." The author argues that Trump is now echoing this exact sentiment, specifically regarding social safety nets. "I said to [Office of Management and Budget director] Russell [Vought], 'Don't send any money for daycare because the United States can't take care of daycare,'" Trump is quoted saying, insisting that such responsibilities belong solely to the states. Richardson interprets this not as fiscal prudence, but as a strategic retreat from federal power that would inevitably raise taxes on the very states Trump claims to help.

Trump is trying to bring to life a right-wing political fantasy of the 1950s, and Americans in the present are making clear they reject it.

Critics might argue that states' rights are a legitimate constitutional debate, but Richardson's framing suggests this specific invocation is a dog whistle for the same racial anxieties that fueled the original backlash. The evidence she presents—that this rhetoric is paired with aggressive military spending and domestic cuts—supports the idea that this is a coherent, albeit destructive, political strategy rather than mere inconsistency.

The Violence of the New Frontier

The commentary shifts sharply from rhetoric to the physical reality of this ideology, focusing on the Department of Homeland Security. Richardson highlights a disturbing trend where agents trained for high-risk manhunts are now deployed for civilian immigration sweeps. She cites a Wired investigation revealing that Border Patrol Tactical Unit agents are "as a group, the most violent of the hundreds of federal agents deployed to Chicago," engaging in tactics like punching protesters and shooting unarmed civilians.

The human cost of this "frontier" mentality is stark. Richardson details the death of Nurul Amin Shah Alam, a Rohingya refugee left in a freezing parking lot by Border Patrol agents, which a medical examiner ruled a homicide. When a DHS spokesperson dismissed the ruling as a "hoax," Richardson notes the administration's refusal to acknowledge the brutality of its own enforcement. "Racism was central to the rhetoric of cowboy individualism, and the institutionalization of that racism in the mass deportations and incarcerations of the Department of Homeland Security under Trump has created a backlash," she writes. This section effectively bridges the gap between abstract political theory and the lethal consequences of its implementation.

The Isolation of Power

As the administration consolidates authority, Richardson observes a dangerous erosion of institutional checks and balances. She points to a new Office of Legal Counsel opinion claiming the Presidential Records Act is unconstitutional, an argument that would allow the president to hide official business from public scrutiny. "The PRA is not a valid exercise of Congress's Article I authority and unconstitutionally intrudes on the independence and autonomy of the President," the memo reads, according to Richardson. This move to concentrate power is immediately followed by a disastrous foreign policy venture.

Richardson describes Trump's recent address on the war in Iran as a moment of profound disconnect. "Sounding tired and speaking in a monotone, Trump reiterated his claim that the U.S. doesn't need the oil that travels through the Strait of Hormuz and demanded that other nations who need the oil more force Iran to reopen it," she writes. The result was immediate market chaos and international isolation, with forty nations discussing a coalition to secure the strait without the United States. "What the hell did he just say?" one Republican strategist is quoted asking, capturing the bewilderment of the administration's own allies.

The fallout from that concentration of power is showing now in Trump's disastrous adventure in Iran, undertaking to attack the country without consultation either with Congress or with allies.

The author also details the purge of military leadership, noting that Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth fired Army Chief of Staff General Randy George for refusing to remove qualified officers from a promotion list. This is framed not as a routine personnel change but as an effort to install leadership that will "implement President Trump and Hegseth's vision for the Army," a vision that appears increasingly detached from military reality. Similarly, the firing of Attorney General Pam Bondi for failing to punish Trump's enemies underscores the administration's shift from rule of law to personal vengeance.

Bottom Line

Richardson's strongest argument is her ability to trace the direct line from 1950s cultural nostalgia to the lethal policies of 2026, proving that the current chaos is a feature, not a bug, of a long-planned ideological project. Her biggest vulnerability lies in the sheer speed of the narrative; the article covers so many simultaneous crises—from the Iran war to the firing of the Army Chief of Staff—that the reader may struggle to grasp the full strategic picture without prior context. The reader should watch for the upcoming 2027 budget plan, which Richardson identifies as the next major battleground where this fantasy of a militarized, non-welfare state will be tested against the reality of American voters' demands.

This afternoon, President Donald J. Trump posted on social media a video of the theme song of the Davy Crockett TV series from 1954–1955 starring Fess Parker. Over the clip, he wrote: “Davy Crockett, obviously a distant relative of Jasmine Crockett, and a very High IQ Frontiersman, would be proud of the legacy that he began long ago, and especially Jasmine’s Great Success as a Politician from the Great State of Texas! President DONALD J. TRUMP”

The Walt Disney Studio designed the Davy Crockett western series for children when Trump was about nine, an age that put him in the right demographic to have been part of the Davy Crockett craze that put “The Ballad of Davy Crockett” at the top of the Hit Parade and spurred the sale of $300 million of Davy Crockett merchandise as little boys begged their parents for raccoon caps that would make them look like a western hero.

Jasmine Crockett is a current Democratic U.S. representative from Texas. There is no evidence she is related to David Crockett, who served as a U.S. representative from Tennessee from 1827 to 1835 and who died at the Battle of the Alamo in 1836. Trump mused about their possible relationship before, in 2025.

It feels frighteningly appropriate for a 1950s television western to seem more important to Trump right now than the real world of April 2026 does. Davy Crockett was only one of the many westerns on television in the 1950s and 1960s as those eager to dismantle the New Deal government championed the idea of the western hero as the true American. Trump is trying to bring to life a right-wing political fantasy of the 1950s, and Americans in the present are making clear they reject it.

After World War II, Republican businessmen, southern racists, and religious traditionalists hated the government that both Democrats and Republicans had embraced since 1933, one that leveled the American social and economic playing field by regulating business, providing a basic social safety net, promoting infrastructure, and protecting civil rights. They insisted that such a system of government action was socialism or even communism, and contrasted it with their fantasy of an independent white man on the frontier who wanted nothing of the government but to be left alone.
