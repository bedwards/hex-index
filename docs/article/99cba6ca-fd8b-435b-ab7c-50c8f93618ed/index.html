Your 11-hour flight from Istanbul lands at JFK at midnight and you are back in the snug West Village after two weeks in Egypt. You’re able to get to sleep by four and are manage to sleep till 10 am (4 pm according to your body). You move through the day in a haze, unpacking, taking laundry to cleaners, getting organized.

Your beloved can’t even do that as she’s teaching tonight and has papers to read. Her class starts at 7 pm and you’re hoping you can stay awake until she returns as you’re still on Egypt time, six hours ahead. You can’t imagine teaching a class in this state. You can barely make dinner, though you long for a home-cooked meal.

So, you make the first proper Martini you’ve had in weeks; you finish that excellent book you started on the boat down the Nile, and you plan one of the most easy and satisfying Italian pastas you know, one of the Big Four: Pasta Amatriciana: tomato and bacon.

A reader on social media asked what ingredients I used, and though I’ve written about this dish before, I don’t know that I’ve written the recipe down. So here you go, dear reader.

Pasta Amatriciana

  • 1 pound bacon (or a guanciale-bacon mix, which is preferred) cut in strips or lardons, as you wish

  • crushed red pepper (optional)

  • 1 red onion large dice

  • 1 28-ounce can whole peeled tomatoes (preferably San Marzano)

  • 1 poind pasta (your choice; Ann likes short and squat, so I used cavatappi.)

  • 1 to 1-1/2 cups freshly grated parmiginao reggiano

  1. Cook the bacon or bacon-guanciale mix) till the fat is rendered and the pork is crisp. Remove to a towel-lined plate.

  2. If you like it spicy, add 1/2 teaspoon of red pepper flakes to the fat, then add the onion. (Yes, keep all that fat.) And here is something I do so reflexively I often forget to include it in the recipe: as soon as onion goes into a pan of hot fat, they immediately get a four-finger pinch of salt. This helps draw out water and begins the seasoning process.

  3. When the onion is tender, add the tomatoes and their juices, one-by one, pulverizing them in your hands as you do so.
