A home-cooked meal ...

Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Michael Ruhlman does not merely recount a trip to Egypt; he constructs a profound argument that our modern disconnection from nature is a culinary and spiritual crisis, using the simple act of making pasta as the anchor for a journey through ancient wisdom. While most travel writing focuses on the spectacle of the Pyramids, Ruhlman suggests that the true revelation lies in the quiet moments of a dahabiya boat and the philosophy of a local guide who sees the universe as a communicative gift. This is not a guidebook; it is a meditation on how we eat, how we travel, and how we have lost the ability to listen to the world around us.

The Alchemy of Return

The piece begins in the disorienting haze of jet lag, a state Ruhlman uses to highlight the human need for grounding rituals. He describes the exhaustion of returning from a two-week immersion in Egypt to a West Village midnight, noting that he is "still on Egypt time, six hours ahead." In this vulnerable state, the author argues that the solution is not complex planning but a return to fundamental, sensory comforts. He writes, "You can barely make dinner, though you long for a home-cooked meal," before settling on Pasta Amatriciana. This choice is deliberate; it is one of the "Big Four" Italian pastas, a dish defined by its simplicity and reliance on high-quality, rendered fat.

Ruhlman's recipe instructions are less about culinary technique and more about mindfulness. He emphasizes a reflexive habit: "as soon as onion goes into a pan of hot fat, they immediately get a four-finger pinch of salt." This small detail serves as a metaphor for the entire article: the importance of attending to the immediate moment and the ingredients at hand. He insists on keeping the fat, a controversial move for modern health-conscious cooks, but one that honors the dish's history. The author notes, "If your sauce looks like it's getting too thick, add some pasta water (moist is better as you'll be adding cheese)." This instruction underscores a philosophy of abundance and connection rather than restriction. The meal becomes a bridge between the traveler and the loved one waiting at home, a way to say, "Your hard-working beloved will thank you!" without speaking a word.

The boat was the place I always longed for. The day began at seven or so, coffee above deck, watching and listening to the quiet on the Nile.

The River of Time

The narrative then shifts from the kitchen to the Nile, where Ruhlman details a journey on the Dendera, a traditional sailing vessel. Here, the author contrasts the frantic pace of modern tourism with the "lazy" float of the dahabiya. He describes the boat as a sanctuary where "on the boat was nothing but beauty and comfort and quiet." This section is crucial because it sets the stage for the philosophical core of the piece: the difference between observing history and feeling it. Ruhlman contrasts the grand tombs of Pharaohs with the more intimate tombs of noblemen in El Kab, where the hieroglyphics told "the story of daily life, not the life of gods and goddesses." This distinction mirrors his own experience; he is less interested in the monumental than in the human scale of existence.

The author's description of the boat's movement is evocative. He notes that the shallow-bottomed vessel allowed them to stop along the river to see "temples, rock quarries, even a farming family's home." This accessibility creates a sense of intimacy with the land that is often lost in curated tours. Ruhlman writes, "Many of the group were serious board and card gamers, and so some kind of fun was always going on." This mundane detail grounds the high-minded travelogue, reminding the reader that even in the presence of millennia-old history, life continues in its simple, joyful forms. The journey is not just about seeing the past but about finding a rhythm that allows one to think and write, a luxury he describes as "a great luxury to relax and write and think and read as we floated lazily toward Aswan."

The Voice of the Guide

The centerpiece of Ruhlman's argument is his conversation with Sayed Ismael, a guide who challenges the Western notion of human dominance over nature. Sayed's perspective is radical: he suggests that humans have lost a vital connection to the world. "We should understand that when we arrive as a human and this universe, we have different creatures, and it's not only just the creatures—they are a gift for you," Sayed explains. This is not merely poetic; it is a theological and ecological claim that redefines the human role from master to participant. Ruhlman captures the depth of this insight by quoting Sayed's observation that ancient Egyptians understood the importance of hygiene, nutrition, and sustainable agriculture, leading to a civilization that lasted "seven, ten thousand years."

Sayed's most striking point concerns the crocodile, a creature often viewed as a monster but revered by the ancients as a predictor of the Nile's flood. "The crocodile somehow knew the level the Nile would rise to, so they could lay their eggs near its bank," Sayed explains, linking animal behavior directly to agricultural survival. This connection is reinforced by a historical nod to the broader context of the region's spiritual life; just as the author later references the prophet David in Islam, who could speak to animals, Sayed argues that this ability to communicate with nature was once universal. Ruhlman writes, "You will not forget, as it says in the Bible, in the Jewish book, in the Muslim book, that once the human, they succeed to communicate with these animals, these birds." This interweaving of religious texts with ecological observation creates a powerful argument for a lost human capability.

Sayed's critique of modern civilization is blunt: "Unfortunately, we are the human today. When we talk, we think that it is only just sentence... But there are other ways to communicate with other creatures, it's not only just question and answer." He argues that the modern obsession with cleanliness and technology has severed this connection, leading to disease and environmental destruction. "We are so far from nature," Sayed laments, suggesting that the ancient ability to "smell" and "feel" the world was the key to their longevity. Ruhlman presents this not as a nostalgic fantasy but as a serious critique of contemporary life, noting that Sayed believes we are "reaching to the edge, to our end."

Critics might note that Sayed's romanticization of the past risks ignoring the harsh realities of ancient life, such as high infant mortality or the brutal nature of some religious practices. However, Ruhlman's framing is careful; he presents Sayed's views as a specific, culturally rooted wisdom rather than a universal historical fact. The value lies not in the literal accuracy of every claim but in the challenge it poses to the reader's assumptions about progress. The author acknowledges the difficulty of this conversation, admitting, "I can't say Sayed was a barrel of laughs... but he was jovial, knowledgeable and seemed deeply humane."

The Taste of Connection

The article concludes by returning to the theme of food, this time on the boat during the end of Ramadan. Ruhlman describes the meals as "surprisingly good," listing a bounty of dishes that range from "fried fish from the Nile" to "roasted eggplant, okra stew, fava bean falafel." The food serves as a tangible manifestation of the connection between the travelers and the land. The breaking of the fast is a communal event, a moment of shared relief and joy. "Each evening, the crew would break fast just after six," Ruhlman writes, highlighting the discipline and faith of the crew who fasted in the heat. This experience reinforces the idea that food is not just fuel but a ritual that binds people together and connects them to the cycles of nature.

The author's final reflection on the journey is a call to reclaim this connection. He suggests that the answer to our modern malaise lies in a return to the senses, to "feel, to smell," and to engage with the world in a way that is more than intellectual. "We believe in the childhood time. We should lie in the dust, we should taste the dust," Sayed advises, a radical suggestion that challenges the modern aversion to dirt and disorder. Ruhlman ends with a nod to the spirits that accompany such journeys, from Grappa to Cha Cha, suggesting that the journey is as much about the internal transformation as the external destination.

Unfortunately, we humans have been stuck with this technology. To feel, to smell. Nowadays I got some news that senor or senora, some countries, they reach to a time, they are worried, they are scared from the dust, you know. But it should be you should communicate with the dust.

Bottom Line

Ruhlman's piece is a masterful blend of culinary instruction, travelogue, and philosophical inquiry, using the specific lens of an Egyptian journey to argue for a broader reconnection with nature. Its greatest strength is the integration of Sayed Ismael's voice, which provides a compelling counter-narrative to the Western obsession with control and efficiency. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on a romanticized view of the past, which may not fully account for the complexities of ancient life, but this does not diminish the power of its central message: that we have lost something vital, and the path back may be through the simple, sensory acts of cooking, eating, and listening. Readers should watch for how this theme of connection develops in Ruhlman's upcoming work, particularly in his Maine writers workshop, where he plans to explore "What We Learn About Writing (and Ourselves) When We Write About Food."
