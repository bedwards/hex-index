A Storied Russian Muckraker On Oil, Iran, Ukraine, and More

Matt Taibbi · Matt Taibbi's Racket ·Apr 2, 2026 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Matt Taibbi doesn't just interview a Russian muckraker; he unlocks a geopolitical Rosetta Stone that rewrites the script on every major conflict of the last century. Leonid Krutakov, a journalist who once risked his life to expose a $3 million bribe in 1997 Moscow, now argues that the American Civil War, the World Wars, and the current crisis in Ukraine are not battles of ideology, but a single, continuous war over the "mineral" energy that powers our civilization. This is not a dry economic history; it is a terrifying diagnosis of a world running on a finite battery that we are draining at a rate that defies sustainability.

The Math of Modern Life

Krutakov's most unsettling contribution is his refusal to frame the energy crisis solely through the lens of climate change. Instead, he forces the reader to confront the sheer physical impossibility of our current lifestyle. He argues that the high-energy existence enjoyed by the West is a historical anomaly, built on the back of cheap extraction in developing nations. "The energy density of oil is incredible," Krutakov explains, noting that a single month of driving by an average American consumes as much energy as it would take twelve people to build a three-story mansion in a year.

This perspective shifts the debate from environmental morality to survival logistics. Taibbi highlights how Krutakov views our dependence not as a choice, but as a trap. "One gallon of the gasoline we use today contains 90 metric tons of ancient plant substances," Krutakov states. "In one year, humanity burns a volume of fossil fuel equivalent to all the animal and plant life that inhabited the Earth over 400 years." The implication is stark: we are eating the future to fuel the present.

Critics might argue that this "doomed math" ignores the rapid potential of renewable energy transitions, but Krutakov's counter is that our entire industrial and agricultural base is chemically dependent on petrochemistry. "Without nitrates and 'targeted' pesticides, industrialized farms cannot exist," he warns, suggesting that a drop in oil yields would lead immediately to hunger and epidemics, not just a shift to electric cars.

We are eating through the lives and well-being of future generations.

The Invisible Hand of Oil

The interview takes a bold turn when Krutakov reinterprets American history through the prism of energy economics. He dismisses the traditional narrative that the Civil War was fought over slavery, instead framing it as a clash between "muscle and machine," or biological versus mineral energy. "The oil underpinning... ties to the Civil War of the North and South," Krutakov asserts, pointing out that the nascent oil empire led by John D. Rockefeller was heavily invested in the Union's victory to secure control over Southern oil fields.

Taibbi notes that this is a controversial take, one that might make American readers raise an eyebrow, yet it aligns with Krutakov's broader thesis that material resources drive history more than political will. He traces a direct line from Rockefeller's vertically integrated pricing systems to modern geopolitical maneuvering. "Rockefeller built the world in which we live," Krutakov says. "He laid the foundations of the oil industry as vertically integrated with an internal pricing system that allows controlling the cost of the final product."

This framing suggests that the "Great War for the planet's global market" is not a new phenomenon but a 150-year-old struggle for control over the rules of trade. Krutakov argues that oil became the first global commodity traded in a single currency, making it the core of international relations. "It has always been so, for at least the last 150 years," he claims, linking the invasion of Russia by Hitler to the search for Caspian petroleum fields just as he links the wars in Iraq and Libya to pipeline politics.

The Pipeline Wars

Perhaps the most chilling section of the interview is Krutakov's breakdown of modern conflicts as disputes over pipeline routes. He suggests that the wars in Syria and Libya were not about democracy or human rights, but about the construction of specific energy infrastructure. "The Libyan story began with a project to construct an oil pipeline to Italy along the bottom of the Mediterranean Sea," Krutakov explains. "Syria should have become part of the oil pipeline from Iran through Iraq."

This theory casts the "Special Military Operation" in Ukraine and the destruction of the Nord Stream pipelines as strategic moves in a game of energy dominance. Krutakov draws a parallel between the current tension in Ukraine and the Cuban Missile Crisis, arguing that the West fails to understand the existential threat Russia feels regarding NATO expansion. "For Russia, the situation of Ukraine potentially joining NATO was an existential threat," he says. "It's somewhat like placing Chinese or Russian military contingents in Mexico or Venezuela."

Taibbi uses this to highlight a massive disconnect in American discourse: the government likely understands these energy realities, while the public is fed a narrative of pure moral crusade. "The fact that these reasons are unclear to the American people only shows that these reasons have not been made public," Krutakov observes. He warns that retreating from the information war by cutting off access to Russian media is a sign of weakness, not strength. "In a war of meanings, victory can be achieved only through meaning, content, arguments, ideas. Retreat from discussion does not mean victory; it means admission of defeat."

The revolver has always been and remains the last argument in a debate when you have nothing left to respond with.

The Russian Failure

Despite his sharp critique of Western energy policy, Krutakov offers a searing indictment of his own country. He argues that Russia has failed to convert its immense natural wealth into long-term power. "Russia remains fundamentally a rentier country," he admits. "Over the past 30 years Russia has failed to convert its energy opportunities into cognitive capital."

This is a poignant admission from a man who once risked his life to expose corruption. He contrasts Russia's trajectory with that of the United States under Rockefeller, noting that while America used oil revenues to build an autonomous technological and industrial base, Russia simply spent the money. "Genuine supremacy is formed not in raw materials or the military sphere, but in education," Krutakov concludes. "Knowledge and science make a country truly great and significant for the whole of world civilization."

Critics might note that this overlooks the structural constraints Russia faced from Western sanctions and the sheer difficulty of diversifying an economy built on resource extraction. However, Krutakov's point remains that without a shift toward "cognitive capital," Russia's military might is built on a foundation of sand.

Bottom Line

Taibbi's interview with Krutakov succeeds by stripping away the moralizing rhetoric of modern geopolitics to reveal the cold, hard engine of oil that drives it. The strongest part of the argument is the reframing of historical conflicts as energy disputes, a lens that makes sense of seemingly irrational wars. Its biggest vulnerability is the deterministic view that reduces complex human struggles to mere resource calculus, potentially ignoring the genuine ideological drivers that also shape history. Readers should watch for Krutakov's upcoming second book, which promises to detail the "oil triangle" and the specific primary sources backing his claim that Saudi Arabia belongs to America.

Excerpt from the original article on Matt Taibbi's Racket

In Moscow in July 1997, reporter Leonid Krutakov had a scoop. Anatoly Chubais, the Harvard-educated privatization boss and right hand of Boris Yeltsin, had been given a $3 million loan from Stolichny Bank just before that same bank — controlled by an intimidating “businessman” named Alexander Smolensky — won an auction for a state-owned agricultural property, Agroprombank.

It was a great story, but Krutakov had to make an epic journey to print it. First fired from Chubais-friendly Komsomolskaya Pravda, he was rejected by Moskovsky Komsomolets, then appealed to the legendary editor of Top Secret, Artem Borovik, before that magazine, too, turned him down. Finally Krutakov struck a deal to publish a piece called “Credit… Or Lose” — a play on the USAID-inspired “Vote or Lose” commercials crucial to Yeltsin’s recent re-election — in Izvestia. Immediately after, Izvestia editor Igor Golombiyevsky was fired, and in a town full of hotshot muckrakers, Krutakov was feted for turning four newsrooms upside down in pursuit of a single political bribery story.

I was eventually fortunate to get a chance to work under Leonid at his paper Stringer, and watched in admiration as he and a handful of colleagues took big risks at a time when Russian reporters were routinely shot, beaten, blown up, stuffed in drainpipes, or worse. Decades later, the writer I remembered for short-form breaking news has published a massive, meticulously researched history of the oil business called “Oil and Peace,” analogous in scope to books like Daniel Yergin’s The Prize. The book argues human history has become intertwined with oil to the point where there’s virtually no line between petroleum and politics itself, while underscoring the lunatic inefficiency of the oil-dominated world.

American analyses of these questions tend to focus obsessively on global warming, but Krutakov’s book spends more time focusing on the doomed math of tying so much of our lives — everything from light to food to antihistamines to dentures to transportation — to the production of one hydrocarbon.
