Plato's Euthyphro - Which comes first: God or Morality?

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·Feb 25, 2020 · 20 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan transforms a dusty ancient Greek courtroom drama into a razor-sharp investigation of the very foundation of ethics, asking the question that keeps theologians and philosophers up at night: does morality exist independently of God, or is it merely a reflection of divine whim? The piece is notable not for discovering new ground, but for stripping away the academic jargon to reveal the brutal logic of the "Euthyphro Dilemma" in plain, conversational terms. Kaplan forces the reader to confront a paradox that threatens to dismantle the concept of objective morality if one isn't careful.

The Setup: A Father, A Ditch, and a Moral Crisis

Kaplan opens by setting a scene that is as bizarre as it is instructive. He introduces Euthyphro not as a sage, but as an arrogant figure prosecuting his own father for murder after the father left a dying laborer in a ditch to await a diviner's advice. "What a douche," Kaplan writes regarding Euthyphro's self-aggrandizing claim to possess "exact knowledge of all such matters." This blunt assessment immediately disarms the reader, making the subsequent philosophical rigor feel like a necessary correction to human hubris rather than an abstract exercise.

The author explains that Socrates uses this high-stakes family drama to pivot to a fundamental question: what is piety? Kaplan notes that Euthyphro initially fails the test by offering a list of examples rather than a definition. "I wasn't asking for a list of examples I was asking for the nature of piety itself," Kaplan paraphrases Socrates, comparing the request to a child asking for the definition of a building and receiving a list of famous skyscrapers instead. This analogy is effective because it instantly clarifies the distinction between specific instances and the underlying essence of a concept, a distinction that is often lost in casual moral debates.

"Virtue is that which the gods love or like or love."

The Divine Command Trap

Once the definition is sharpened, Kaplan guides the reader through Euthyphro's proposed solution: piety is simply what the gods love. The author highlights the immediate logistical failure of this view in a polytheistic context, noting that the Greek gods are "like reality TV stars" who constantly disagree. To salvage the argument, the dialogue shifts to a monotheistic framework, asking what happens if there is only one God. Kaplan writes, "we're gonna settle on this the things that all of the gods love those are good those are virtuous," effectively bridging the gap between ancient myth and modern theology.

However, the real meat of the commentary arrives with the pivotal question that gives the dilemma its name. Kaplan breaks down the logic with surgical precision: "is not piety according to your definition loved by all the gods because it is holy or holy because it is beloved by the gods?" This is the crux of the argument. Kaplan uses a brilliant analogy involving a sentence and grass to explain "explanatory priority." He argues that just as grass is green independently of the sentence "grass is green," moral acts might be good independently of God's approval.

Critics might note that this analogy assumes a naturalistic view of morality that a believer would reject, but Kaplan's strength lies in showing how the logic holds regardless of one's starting point. If the gods love something because it is good, then goodness exists outside of them, making God a recognizer rather than the creator of morality. If they love it because they love it, then morality is arbitrary. Kaplan captures this tension perfectly: "if the acts are virtuous independently of whether or not the gods love them... mentioning the fact that the gods love the virtuous acts doesn't explain their virtuousness."

The Circular Dead End

As the dialogue reaches its conclusion, Kaplan illustrates the frustration of the participants. Euthyphro admits defeat, realizing he has gone in circles. Kaplan summarizes the fatal flaw in the divine command theory: "Socrates is response to Euthyphro answer... is something like that doesn't explain that's no explanation at all." The author emphasizes that if virtue is just what God loves, we are left with no standard by which to judge God's love as good in the first place. The argument collapses into a tautology.

The commentary effectively demonstrates that the dilemma is not a problem to be solved, but a lens to be used. By the end, the reader understands that the question is not about which option is correct, but about the implications of choosing either one. Kaplan's ability to translate 2,400-year-old Greek logic into a modern, accessible narrative makes the stakes feel immediate and personal.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's commentary succeeds by refusing to let the reader off the hook with easy answers; he exposes the logical trap at the heart of divine command theory with clarity and wit. The piece's greatest strength is its use of the "grass is green" analogy to demystify complex philosophical concepts, while its vulnerability lies in the fact that it leaves the reader with a paradox rather than a resolution. For the busy thinker, this is a masterclass in why the question of God and morality remains the most difficult puzzle in philosophy.

"If the virtuousness explains the gods' love, then there's got to be something earlier that explains the virtuousness, and that's what we want to know about."

The strongest part of this argument is its relentless focus on explanatory priority, forcing the reader to admit that either morality is arbitrary or God is not its source. The biggest vulnerability is the assumption that a single, coherent definition of "virtue" is even possible to find, a point that modern moral realists and anti-realists alike would debate. Readers should watch for how this dilemma resurfaces in modern debates about human rights and religious law.
