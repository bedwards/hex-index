In a world where the creative life is often framed as a binary choice between soulless commerce and pure, isolated art, George Saunders offers a startlingly practical third way. He reframes the terrifying prospect of rejection not as a verdict on one's worth, but as a necessary diagnostic tool for artistic growth. This isn't just advice for writers; it's a masterclass in compartmentalizing the ego from the work, a strategy that feels urgently necessary for anyone navigating a fragile creative path.

The Architecture of Detachment

Saunders begins by dismantling the myth that publication is a young person's game or a soul-crushing grind. Instead, he suggests that the anxiety surrounding the business of writing stems from a lack of structural boundaries. "Becoming a better artist, in my experience, is a matter of learning, over and over, what you just plain like, no reasons needed, and then doing that thing as if it's all that matters," he writes. This distinction is crucial: the act of creation must remain sacred and irrational, while the act of submission must be treated with cold, clinical efficiency.

He proposes a specific, almost bureaucratic solution to the emotional chaos of seeking publication. Saunders describes creating a physical matrix, a grid where stories are matched against target magazines, and rejections are marked with a simple "R" before moving to the next slot. "It keeps a firewall between your creative worries... and your publishing worries," he explains. This approach is brilliant because it transforms the subjective pain of rejection into a neutral data point. By treating the submission process as a "household task," he removes the emotional weight that often paralyzes writers. Critics might argue that this industrial approach risks sterilizing the romanticism of the artistic struggle, but Saunders counters that the romance is in the writing, not the waiting.

"It keeps things clean and separates the inevitable rejections from your working life, which, theoretically, should be full of fun and hope and joy and a reasonable level of self-encouragement."

Rejection as a Creative Catalyst

The most provocative element of Saunders' argument is his reimagining of failure. Rather than viewing rejection as a sign to quit, he posits it as a "shock to the system" that forces an artist to confront their own habits. He admits to a very human, perhaps even "guilty," desire for attention and approval. "I have a need to attract attention, be liked, make something that is fun or enlightening for another person," he confesses. This honesty disarms the reader; he doesn't pretend to be above the market, but rather argues that the market's indifference can be a useful mirror.

When the world rejects a piece of work, Saunders suggests it is often telling the artist that their approach has become too cautious or "rule-bound." He recounts his own experience with a 700-page novel that his wife could not bring herself to like. That failure led to a moment of "total artistic clean-out," freeing him to start over with a new goal: writing something a reader would not only finish but love. "Rejection, and our acceptance of it as meaningful, can, maybe, make us see what about our current approach is merely habitual," he argues. This is a powerful reframing: the market is not a judge of truth, but a feedback loop for engagement. A counterargument worth considering is that this view assumes the market is always right about engagement, potentially discouraging truly avant-garde work that takes time to find its audience. However, Saunders balances this by suggesting that even the most difficult work must eventually "charm" someone to survive.

The Human Cost of the Creative Life

Ultimately, Saunders' advice is rooted in a deep empathy for the fragility of the creative spirit. He acknowledges that the world can seem "cruel, dark, discouraging," but insists that this reality gives artists "work to do." He urges writers to use whatever drives them, even if it is a desperate need for validation. "As hard as making art is, we have to use whatever we've got, and especially, maybe, whatever we've got in excess," he writes. This perspective validates the messy, irrational motivations that often fuel great art, rather than demanding a sterile, purely intellectual approach.

He concludes with a reminder that the pursuit of publication is not just about career advancement, but about the connection between the writer and the reader. The goal is to create something that has a "positive effect on someone else's day." This shifts the focus from the writer's ego to the reader's experience, grounding the entire process in human connection rather than professional status.

"The world can seem cruel, dark, discouraging, but, on the other hand, that gives us all work to do, in every moment."

Bottom Line

Saunders' strongest argument is the practical utility of treating submission as a business process to protect the creative self, a strategy that transforms rejection from a personal failure into a diagnostic tool. His biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that market rejection is always a signal of artistic stagnation, which may not hold true for work that challenges the status quo. Readers should watch for how they can apply this "firewall" technique to their own creative anxieties, separating the joy of making from the grind of selling.

Q.

Hi George! I hope you are well and looking forward to your upcoming book tour!

I love Story Club. Your generosity and warmth are a balm in a world that often feels both surprisingly savage and terribly fragile. You are a dear person.

My question is less about craft and more about commerce. Or maybe, when you strip away the details, at its heart, it’s really a question about existential dread? Maybe you can help me figure that out.

I came to writing late in life and rather recently. I knew, even as a little girl growing up in Chicagoland, that I wanted to be a writer. Instead, I became a divorced mother with two little girls and no education. Fast forward: The kids are grown, I have excellent health insurance, and I can afford groceries every week. (Yay!) So, now I am writing.

Of course, finding time to write can be difficult. I assume this is the case for all writers, especially those with full-time jobs outside of writing. Still, I’m not seeking encouragement to keep carving out time to write because to older people, I think, writing is like getting to go outside to play. Instead, I am wondering about spending time pursuing publication.

After all, time becomes more precious when there is less of it to lose.

Let’s say you, George, were the age you are now, but you were currently working at that engineering firm as a technical writer and counting the days until you could retire, hopefully, if all went well, at age seventy. Of course you would keep writing. But would you keep trying to get published? It seems incredibly time-consuming and perhaps a bit soul-crushing.

Sure, it looks like great fun to be in conversation with Deborah Treisman or signing books after a successful reading, but the effort to get there seems superhuman.

Perhaps, like tearing off a roof or curing a hangover with fast food, publishing may be a young man’s game.

Yes, the argument exists that one can simply write for the enjoyment of it and never seek publication but if I were a chef who spent a year or more perfecting recipes to prepare a feast, I would want, eventually, to share that meal with others.
