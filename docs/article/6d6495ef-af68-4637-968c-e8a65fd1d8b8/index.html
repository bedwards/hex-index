Amtrak's subtle, surprising success

Sam Denby · Wendover Productions ·Oct 17, 2025 ·17 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original video →

Sam Denby challenges the pervasive narrative that American rail is a hopeless failure by arguing that Amtrak's quiet, imperfect progress is actually a strategic triumph. While the media fixates on the glitches of the new Acela train, Denby reveals a deeper story: Amtrak is thriving not by chasing revolutionary speed, but by mastering the art of "deaf maneuvering" within a broken system. This is essential listening for anyone tired of doom-scrolling about infrastructure, as it reframes recent ridership records not as a post-pandemic fluke, but as the result of decades of creative, state-level partnerships.

The Myth of the Profit Mandate

Denby begins by dismantling the most persistent criticism of Amtrak: that it is a failing business because it never turns a profit. He traces this misconception back to the 1970 Rail Passenger Service Act, noting how the language was subtly amended in 1978 to change the mandate from being a for-profit corporation to being managed as one. "The myth that Amtrak exists to turn an eventual profit is not only an oversimplification, but also an obfuscation of the service's guiding purpose," Denby writes. This distinction is crucial because it shifts the metric of success from the bottom line to the public good.

The author argues that for over fifty years, the service has been judged by a standard it was never meant to meet. "Amtrak has not failed a mandate to turn a profit, but more accurately has failed to procure a quality intercity passenger rail system largely on account of the myth that it's supposed to turn a profit." This is a powerful reframe. It suggests that the real failure lies not in the train cars, but in the political will to fund them properly. Critics might note that this semantic shift lets Congress off the hook too easily, as the lack of profitability still invites political attacks that starve the agency of capital. However, Denby's point stands: the agency is fulfilling its actual legal purpose of providing an interconnected system better than ever, despite the noise.

The Quiet Power of State Partnerships

Moving beyond the federal gridlock, Denby highlights how the real growth is happening in state-supported routes. These are regional lines, often under 750 miles, where individual states pitch the idea and fund the operations while Amtrak provides the expertise. The author points to the Borealis line in the Midwest as a case study in success. "Without new stops or untested routings and requiring just two train sets, the Amtrak and multi-state partnership boosted regional ridership and seemed to offer proof that the more options offered, the more people will choose to take the train."

The argument here is that frequency and convenience matter more than speed. By adding a midday option between Chicago and St. Paul, the Borealis didn't steal riders from existing trains; it created new demand. This "induced demand" is a subtle win that flies under the radar. Denby notes similar success in the Pacific Northwest, where increased frequency on the Cascades route made the train a viable alternative to driving. "Already an established and popular routing, increasing frequency only made it more convenient for the average Pacific Northwest traveler to opt for the regularly available 3 and 1 half hour train ride instead of the 3-hour drive." The evidence holds up: when you make the train easier to catch, people take it. This approach bypasses the need for massive federal legislation, relying instead on local political will.

Infrastructure as the Silent Engine

The final piece of Denby's puzzle is infrastructure, specifically the long-delayed tunnel projects in the Northeast Corridor. He describes the Frederick Douglass Tunnel project as a game-changer, replacing the bottleneck of the Baltimore and Potomac Tunnel which forces trains to crawl at 30 miles per hour. "Projects that require heavy capital funding upfront, projects that aren't deemed absolutely necessary because the infrastructure in place technically already works. But that changed in 2023 with the announcement of the Frederick Douglass Tunnel." This shift in mindset is critical. For decades, the argument was that the old tunnel "worked," so why spend billions to fix it? Denby argues that "working" isn't enough when it creates a speed bump for 150 trains a day.

The author suggests that the new five-year plan is finally aligning with reality. "Nearing the end of those 5 years, this fairly lofty, fairly vague plan is actually looking surprisingly fulfilled." The combination of new rolling stock, state-funded expansions, and finally tackling the tunnel replacements suggests a path forward that is incremental but tangible. While the new Acela trains may have arrived with a stutter, the underlying network is becoming more robust. A counterargument worth considering is that these infrastructure projects take decades to complete, meaning the "success" Denby celebrates today might not yield full benefits until the 2030s. Yet, the momentum is undeniable.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's most compelling argument is that Amtrak's success is not measured in headlines or top speeds, but in the quiet accumulation of ridership through smart, localized strategies. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to accept the binary of "total failure" or "revolutionary success," offering a more nuanced view of a system that is slowly, stubbornly winning. The biggest vulnerability remains the political fragility of state-level funding, but the trend is clear: when Amtrak is given the tools to operate, it works.

Amtrak, within its narrow confines, is fulfilling its mandates better than ever to far less media fanfare than the Acela launch.

Amtrak's subtle, surprising success

by Sam Denby · Wendover Productions · Watch video

On the morning of Thursday, August 28th, 2025, Amtrak train 2154 pulled out of DC's Union Station at 6:50 a.m. headed north, like it does every weekday. And like it does almost every other day, it quickly got delayed. By just its first stop, the train was 13 minutes behind schedule.

In some cars, the automated doors weren't automating, so employees had to manually articulate it at each stop. The delays began to compound. 35 minutes late leaving here, 44 here, and by the time it arrived in Boston, the train was nearly an hour behind. Now, for service that's not on time, about a quarter of the time, such delays weren't particularly notable on their own.

But while the route wasn't new, the train set was, and it came with big promises. This glitching northbound service was the maiden commercial voyage of the next gen Asella. A much anticipated brand new train set boasting increased capacity, increased comfort, and increased top speeds over the aging Asellas they were poised to replace. A decade in the making, the roll out of these new trains was anything but subtle.

As the so-called a celebration was marked by music, fireworks, speeches articulating that for Amtrak, for the future of passenger rail in the US, this was a moment in history. The moment, however, felt slightly less revolutionary on the ground. Beyond the slightly embarrassing malfunctions on Route 2154, riders were left unimpressed by the stiff chairs. They were amused by the outdated USBA offerings that hinted at how long delayed the project had been, and they were quick to note that the train wasn't reaching the much anticipated top speeds of 160 mi or 260 km/h for long sustained periods.

The lukewarm reception continued from first-person reviews published everywhere from YouTube and Reddit to the New York Times. There were only five of these new active out of the 28 promised. There were no new routes or rail lines and thus no time gains. The trains were roomier.

The new tilting technology was more comfortable. The food and Wi-Fi was better. The trains were fine. But they didn't feel revolutionary.
