Amtrak's Subtle, Surprising Success

On the morning of Thursday, August 28th, 2025, Amtrak train 2154 pulled out of DC's Union Station at 6:50 a.m. headed north, like it does every weekday. And like it does almost every other day, it quickly got delayed. By just its first stop, the train was 13 minutes behind schedule.

In some cars, the automated doors weren't automating, so employees had to manually articulate it at each stop. The delays began to compound. 35 minutes late leaving here, 44 here, and by the time it arrived in Boston, the train was nearly an hour behind. Now, for service that's not on time, about a quarter of the time, such delays weren't particularly notable on their own.

But while the route wasn't new, the train set was, and it came with big promises. This glitching northbound service was the maiden commercial voyage of the next gen Asella. A much anticipated brand new train set boasting increased capacity, increased comfort, and increased top speeds over the aging Asellas they were poised to replace. A decade in the making, the roll out of these new trains was anything but subtle.

As the so-called a celebration was marked by music, fireworks, speeches articulating that for Amtrak, for the future of passenger rail in the US, this was a moment in history. The moment, however, felt slightly less revolutionary on the ground. Beyond the slightly embarrassing malfunctions on Route 2154, riders were left unimpressed by the stiff chairs. They were amused by the outdated USBA offerings that hinted at how long delayed the project had been, and they were quick to note that the train wasn't reaching the much anticipated top speeds of 160 mi or 260 km/h for long sustained periods.

The lukewarm reception continued from first-person reviews published everywhere from YouTube and Reddit to the New York Times. There were only five of these new active out of the 28 promised. There were no new routes or rail lines and thus no time gains. The trains were roomier.

The new tilting technology was more comfortable. The food and Wi-Fi was better. The trains were fine. But they didn't feel revolutionary.
