Commentary by Brian Edwards

Timothy Snyder, the Yale historian, dismantles the comforting myth that nations are inevitable, arguing instead that the Ukrainian state emerged from a brutal calculus of slavery, competing empires, and the desperate need for legal protection. In a lecture recorded during an active war, Snyder offers a chilling yet clarifying thesis: history is not a predetermined script, but a record of choices made when the alternative was being sold into bondage. This is not just a history lesson; it is a direct rebuttal to the fatalism that fuels modern conflict.

The Math of Survival

Snyder begins by rejecting the notion that history is about "what has to be." He insists that we must understand the past as a landscape of possibilities, where human agency still mattered. "History is not about predetermination," he states, "it's not about what has to be. History can do is it can be a kind of guide to what was possible." This framing is crucial because it strips away the deterministic narratives often used to justify current violence. If the Ukrainian state was not inevitable, then its existence today is a testament to specific, hard-won choices rather than a cosmic destiny.

The core of Snyder's argument rests on a grim economic reality: the globalization of the slave trade in the ninth and tenth centuries. He explains that for pagan societies, the "math" was increasingly stacked against them. As neighbors converted to Christianity, they gained immunity from enslavement, while pagans remained "fair game for everyone." Snyder notes, "What a state can do is put a stop to that locally. A state can say we recognize you... we have the coercive means to make sure that you are not be sold into slavery." This is a stark, materialist definition of statehood: the government exists first to protect its people from being harvested by others. The irony, which Snyder highlights with characteristic bluntness, is that "the same people who do this enslaving are also often the same people who start the state." The very elites who once captured humans for profit eventually built the legal structures to stop it, provided those humans were their own subjects.

Critics might argue that reducing state formation to a transaction against slavery ignores the spiritual or cultural motivations for conversion. However, Snyder's focus on the "math" of survival provides a more pragmatic explanation for why pagan rulers, who might have otherwise resisted foreign influence, suddenly embraced Christianity. It was a survival strategy, not just a theological shift.

The Clash of Empires and the Birth of Kingship

Snyder then pivots to the geopolitical forces that shaped this new order: the competition between two versions of Christianity. He describes a world where "Christianity was a plurality," split between the Frankish West and the Byzantine South. These were not merely religious sects but "two strong political units that were pushing outward imperially." The choice of which Christian path to follow was a choice of which empire to align with. Snyder points out that the theological differences mattered less than the political recognition each offered. "When you convert as a ruler... you're generally converting because the religion in question is bringing something to you that your native cult does not have," he argues, citing the need for written language, bureaucrats, and legitimacy.

This competition created a unique pressure cooker in Eastern Europe. The region was caught between the Franks, who claimed to have restored the Roman Empire, and the Byzantines, who claimed it never fell. Snyder illustrates the profound cultural impact of this rivalry by tracing the etymology of the word for "king." He notes that the name of the Frankish leader Charlemagne, or "Charles the Great," became the Slavic word for king—"karel" in Ukrainian and "król" in Polish. "The very idea of kingship," Snyder observes, "is imported from the West, yet adapted to the East." This linguistic legacy serves as a tangible reminder that the political structures of modern Eastern Europe were forged in the fire of imperial competition.

A counterargument worth considering is whether this focus on external pressures downplays the internal agency of Slavic peoples. While Snyder acknowledges that "a huge amount of politics is copying" neighbors like Moravia and Bulgaria, one might wonder if he underestimates the indigenous innovations that allowed these societies to adapt those models so effectively. Nevertheless, his emphasis on the external threat of enslavement and the internal need for legitimacy provides a compelling framework for understanding the rapid state formation in the region.

Bottom Line

Snyder's most powerful contribution is his redefinition of the state not as a vessel of national destiny, but as a shield against the chaos of the slave trade. The strongest part of his argument is the "math of slavery," which explains the urgency of conversion and state-building in a way that abstract theology cannot. The biggest vulnerability lies in the potential over-simplification of pagan resilience, as some groups managed to delay conversion for centuries without immediate collapse. As readers digest this history, they should watch for how these ancient choices between competing empires continue to echo in the geopolitical alignments of the region today.
