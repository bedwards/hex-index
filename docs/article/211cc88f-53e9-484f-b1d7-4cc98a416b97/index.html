BlackNest: Inside canary mission’s secret web of unlisted sites

Ryan Grim & Jeremy Scahill · Drop Site ·Jan 6, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

This investigation pulls back the curtain on a shadowy digital infrastructure that treats human lives as corporate metrics. Ryan Grim & Jeremy Scahill reveal that a notorious doxxing outfit operates not just as a vigilante group, but as a professionalized enterprise with internal dashboards celebrating deportations and firings as "company impact." For anyone concerned about the erosion of civil liberties and the weaponization of data, this is the definitive map of how private surveillance fuels state repression.

The Corporate Architecture of Repression

The most chilling aspect of this coverage is the banality of the evil described. Grim & Scahill do not paint the operators as rogue hackers in hoodies, but as a hierarchical organization with marketing teams, quarterly plans, and KPIs. They write, "BlackNest is just one of the names of several unlisted websites and content management systems used by Canary Mission, whose doxxing operation is run out of Israel and used by the highest levels of the Trump administration." This reframing is crucial: it moves the story from a fringe internet phenomenon to a structured component of the executive branch's enforcement apparatus.

The authors meticulously detail how the group categorizes its "wins." The website lists impacts such as "Change of behavior," job loss, and "deportation/forced to flee." Grim & Scahill observe, "The website categorizes the group's impacts into categories: 'Change of behavior,' job loss, denials of entry to the U.S., arrests, and 'deportation/forced to flee.'" This corporate tone transforms human suffering into a spreadsheet. It is a stark reminder of how modern authoritarianism often wears a business suit, optimizing the destruction of lives for efficiency.

"BlackNest's web development content reveals how Canary Mission thinks of its 'wins' in its efforts to influence U.S. policy."

The evidence presented suggests a direct pipeline between private data harvesting and government action. The authors note that when the Department of Homeland Security testified in court about using Canary Mission to select deportation targets, the group simply uploaded the article to their internal "Impacts" section. This isn't just speculation; it is a documented feedback loop where private harassment becomes public policy. Critics might argue that the administration's use of such data was reactive rather than proactive, but the internal documents showing strategic planning for "scale-up" suggest a coordinated effort to expand this influence.

From McCarthyism to Digital Blacklisting

The historical resonance of this investigation is impossible to ignore. Grim & Scahill weave in the legacy of COINTELPRO and McCarthyism, showing how the tactics of the past have been digitized. They point out that the group's strategic document lists its purpose as "dismantle the anti-Israel network by attacking the messenger, not the message." This mirrors the mid-20th-century strategy of targeting individuals to silence entire movements, a tactic that once destroyed careers and lives under the guise of national security.

The authors highlight the specific targets of this campaign, noting, "A campaign timeline hints at future areas of focus, including MIT, University of New Mexico, and 'Harvard (Major Campaign).'" By naming specific institutions and individuals, the group creates a chilling effect that extends far beyond those directly profiled. The internal updates celebrate the identification of activists from viral videos and the creation of "Stanford Arrest Profiles," treating the arrest of students as a corporate milestone. This professionalization of surveillance creates a permanent record that can be used to deny entry, employment, or freedom.

The scale of the operation is staggering. The investigation uncovered over 100 gigabytes of data, including the identities of over 30 "doxxers" who created fake social media accounts to infiltrate and monitor pro-Palestine groups. Grim & Scahill write, "Over 30 unique 'doxxers' appear to have created fake Facebook and other social media accounts to track and screenshot information about profiled pro-Palestine activists and students." These operatives, often using pseudonyms like "Ned Flanders," function as a decentralized intelligence network, feeding data into a central hub that then sells or shares it with government agencies. This echoes the historical practice of using informants to dismantle political organizations, but with the added speed and permanence of digital archiving.

The Future of Private Surveillance

Perhaps most alarming is the glimpse into the group's future plans. The authors discovered a strategic planning document that outlines a goal of "150 new profiles to be written per week" by 2025. Grim & Scahill note, "The year-plan for 2024 noted 'facial rec software combined with scraping is efficiently producing named POIs,' or persons of interest." This indicates a shift toward automated, large-scale surveillance that could overwhelm any individual's ability to protect their privacy.

The investigation also uncovers a failed spinoff project called the "Museum of Online Antisemitism," intended to be a "private Wayback Machine for alleged antisemitism." This project, which utilized the same software developers as the main operation, highlights the group's ambition to control the digital narrative permanently. Grim & Scahill explain, "The creators of MOA added code to cover up social media user names of anyone screenshotting content, to help keep doxxers private." This dual focus—harassing targets while protecting the harassers—reveals a sophisticated understanding of the legal and technical landscape.

"Anonymity as a tool to scare the enemy... We change the behavior of the enemy."

The authors' exposure of the funding trail, which funnels money through American nonprofits to Israeli entities, raises serious questions about foreign influence on domestic speech. While the group claims to act in defense of Jewish people, the internal documents reveal a broader mission to "fight those who hate the Jewish people" by dismantling specific political organizations. This broad definition allows for the targeting of anyone who criticizes Israeli policy, effectively chilling legitimate political discourse.

Bottom Line

Ryan Grim & Jeremy Scahill have delivered a masterclass in investigative journalism, exposing a shadowy infrastructure that turns human rights violations into corporate KPIs. The strongest part of their argument is the undeniable evidence of a feedback loop between private doxxing and government enforcement, proving that these are not isolated incidents but a coordinated strategy. The biggest vulnerability in the current landscape is the lack of legal frameworks to stop private entities from acting as de facto intelligence agencies for the state. Readers should watch for how the administration continues to integrate these private databases into official deportation and enforcement protocols, as the technology is already in place and the targets are already identified.
