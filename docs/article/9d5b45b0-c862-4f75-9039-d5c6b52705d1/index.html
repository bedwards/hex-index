The Net Zero Myth. Why Reaching our Climate Goals is Virtually Impossible

Sabine Hossenfelder · Sabine Hossenfelder ·Nov 11, 2023 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sabine Hossenfelder delivers a sobering reality check that cuts through the political euphoria surrounding climate targets: the concept of "Net Zero" is not a magic switch that stops global warming, and achieving it by 2050 is virtually impossible without a technological leap we haven't yet made. She dismantles the comforting narrative that simply balancing emissions with removals will cool the planet, revealing instead a complex lag where temperatures stabilize but do not drop. This is essential listening for anyone tired of vague policy promises and hungry for the hard physics behind the headlines.

The Definition Trap

Hossenfelder begins by stripping away the buzzwords to expose the technical reality of the term. She notes that until recently, "Net Zero" had no official definition, but the International Standards Organization eventually clarified it as "the condition in which human caused residual greenhouse gas emissions are balanced by human lead removals over a specified period and within specified boundaries." This precision is necessary because the term is often weaponized to sound like a solution when it is merely a mathematical state. The author points out a critical misconception: reaching this balance does not mean temperatures stop rising immediately. "Temperatures will not start to decrease once we reach it," she writes, explaining that the planet retains excess energy and the oceans act as a massive heat sink, causing a delay between emission cuts and temperature stabilization.

This distinction is vital. It reframes the goal from "stopping the problem" to "managing a slow bleed." The author argues that Net Zero only makes sense when paired with a total emissions budget, noting that "if we reach Net Zero with 600 parts per million carbon dioxide in the atmosphere that would be very different from reaching it at 800." This exposes the flaw in treating the date as the metric rather than the cumulative impact. Critics might argue that focusing on the lag time creates fatalism, but Hossenfelder's point is that without understanding the physics, policy becomes theater.

Net Zero only makes sense in combination together with a total emissions Target basically you need a requirement for the integral over the annual emissions as well as a requirement for the time by which the integral stops growing.

The Budget Reality

Moving from definition to data, Hossenfelder paints a stark picture of where we stand relative to the Paris Agreement goals. With global warming already at 1.1 degrees Celsius, the remaining carbon budget to have a 50% chance of staying under 1.5 degrees is shockingly small. She calculates that we have "roughly 400 to 800 billion tons of carbon dioxide left that we can emit," a number that is vanishing fast. The trajectory of current emissions suggests we are heading toward "net Infinity" rather than zero, with the 1.5-degree budget likely exhausted by 2030.

However, she offers a nuanced view on regional progress to avoid total despair. While global numbers are dire, she highlights that "the EU has reduced greenhouse gas emissions by 23% while the economy has grown by 61%" between 1990 and 2018. This evidence counters the argument that decarbonization requires economic collapse. Yet, she tempers this optimism by citing the Climate Change Performance Index, which ranks the EU highly not for results, but for "writing policies for doing things." The gap between policy and implementation remains the elephant in the room.

The Removal Imperative

The core of Hossenfelder's argument shifts to the only remaining path forward: active carbon dioxide removal. She asserts that "reaching Net Zero by 2050 is virtually impossible without carbon dioxide removal," a conclusion shared by the International Energy Agency and the IPCC. Here, she draws a sharp, necessary line between two often confused technologies. "Carbon dioxide removal should not be confused with Kermit the Frog," she jokes, before clarifying that Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) at fossil fuel plants is not removal; it is merely a reduction method. True removal requires "bioenergy with carbon capture and storage" or direct air capture.

The scale of the challenge is where the argument hits its hardest wall. Current technological removal stands at a mere 2.3 million tons a year, while the goal for 2050 is nearly 5 billion tons. "Those 2.3 million need to go to about a billion in 7 years," she notes, adding with characteristic bluntness, "yeah I can't see that happening either." The political friction is also real; environmental groups often oppose CCS entirely, fearing it prolongs fossil fuel dependence. Hossenfelder argues this is a mistake, stating that "some environmental groups are lobbying against carbon capture and storage at fossil fuel plants and then they're throwing out the baby that's the carbon removal with the bath water that's fossil fuse with ccs."

We've seen it's possible to decarbonize even large economies without sacrificing Prosperity still the challenge is enormous and we're not doing remotely enough.

Bottom Line

Hossenfelder's strongest contribution is her refusal to let "Net Zero" serve as a euphemism for inaction; she forces the reader to confront the sheer impossibility of the current timeline without a massive, unproven industrial scaling of carbon removal. Her biggest vulnerability lies in the political feasibility of the solution she proposes, as the required speed of technological deployment faces immense regulatory and public opposition. The reader must watch for how governments navigate the tension between admitting the goal is currently impossible and continuing to promise it is achievable.
