How Red Bull Built Its Empire

by Sam Denby · Wendover Productions · Read full article

If you buy a can of Red Bull, you'll probably pay about $3 or your local currency's equivalent. 9 cents of that goes towards paying for actually making the beverage you consume. The rest goes towards everything else. And as you undoubtedly know, in Red Bull's case, everything else is a tremendous amount.

Some slim portion of that $3 goes towards paying for this, the company's headquarters. And this company headquarters is so far from normal. It's not in a major city. It's not even in a minor city.

It's in the small Austrian mountain town of Fushani. This is a town with one supermarket, two coffee shops, one bank, and one globally known multinational beverage enterprise. For one of the world's most recognizable brands, it's a remarkably tiny global HQ. It'd take no longer than 3 minutes to walk from one side of the property to the other, and it's bounded by an insurance broker on one side, residential homes on another, and a small hotel on the third.

It's got no walls or fences or gate houses. In fact, the public is even permitted to stroll through its gardens. There's no great strategic logic behind this location. Rather, Red Bull's co-founder, Dietrich Matitz, just liked Foo.

He liked the privacy it afforded and the outdoors it gave access to and he wanted to live there. The headquarters's small scale may be surprising compared to the global stature of the company, but less so when considering its actual operating structure. After all, at its core, Red Bull is a pretty simple company. It sells its traditional flavor, a sugar-free variant, a slightly different zero sugar version, then 14 different configurations of traditional and sugar-free fruit flavored editions.

Each of these is sold in a couple different sizes to fit different distribution channels and markets, but that's pretty much it. That's their entire product lineup. Compare this to their closest yet smaller competitor, Monster Energy, which sells so many different variants, seasonal, co-branded, and regional flavors that there's not even a definitive count of how many different beverages they sell, though the rough estimate is around 150 at a given time. Adding to their simplicity, Red Bull doesn't even manufacture their own product.
