"The Permanent Problem of the Human Race": Yglesias, Keynes, Lindsey; the Quest for Valued Identities & Societal Roles; & How to Move from "Abundance" to "Flourishing"

Brad DeLong · DeLong's Grasping Reality ·Mar 9, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Brad DeLong tackles the most unsettling paradox of our time: we have solved the economic problem of scarcity, yet we are failing the human problem of meaning. While many economists celebrate the end of poverty, DeLong argues that our very success has created a "middle flourishing trap" where mass abundance coexists with mass unhappiness because our institutions are too large and impersonal to sustain human-scale connection.

The Identity Trap of Retirement

DeLong begins by dissecting a counterintuitive finding from Matt Yglesias: people often report higher well-being when they transition from "unemployed" to "retired," even though their income and daily activity levels may not change drastically. DeLong writes, "The important thing is not to be rich or even to be working, but rather to have a valued social identity." This observation cuts through the standard economic assumption that leisure is the ultimate good. Instead, it suggests that the label we attach to our time matters more than the time itself.

The author uses this to challenge the notion that John Maynard Keynes was naive about human nature. Keynes famously predicted that within a century, humanity would face the "permanent problem" of how to live wisely once work was no longer necessary for survival. DeLong corrects Yglesias's assumption that Keynes missed the modern obsession with work. In fact, Keynes saw it clearly. As DeLong notes, Keynes described the wealthy heirs of his day as "drunkards, gamblers, and whoremasters" who failed to solve the problem of leisure because they lacked "associations or duties or ties." The core of the argument is that without a socially validated role, freedom from work feels like emptiness, not liberation. This lands powerfully because it explains why even the wealthy feel compelled to "work" at philanthropy or hobbies rather than simply "chilling."

Critics might argue that this focus on identity ignores the structural barriers that prevent many from finding such roles, but DeLong's point is that the cultural framework itself is broken, not just the individual's ability to adapt.

"We cannot successfully outsource the solutions to the problem of how to live wisely and agreeably well to markets, bureaucracies, & parasocial algorithm-driven media feeds."

The Tyranny of Mass Society

The commentary then pivots to Brink Lindsey's diagnosis of modernity, which DeLong presents as the necessary next step in understanding our crisis. The problem, DeLong explains, is that the institutions responsible for our prosperity—markets, democratic states, and now algorithms—are "structurally inhuman." They are designed for efficiency and scale, not for the "intermediate associations" that make life feel worth living. DeLong writes, "All human associative groups and markers of cohesion and particularity have been steamed away." We have become "producer-cogs" and "consumer-cogs," stripped of agency and reduced to data points in a system that demands our attention but offers no genuine connection.

This framing is effective because it moves beyond the usual complaints about technology or capitalism individually. Instead, it identifies a systemic flaw: the very mechanisms that liberated us from scarcity have also atomized us. DeLong highlights Lindsey's view that we are facing a "tyranny of the majority" and an "aristocracy of manufactures" where top-down control replaces the messy, vital web of local communities. The author suggests that our current "mass society" is terrifying precisely because it is so efficient at producing goods while so inefficient at producing happiness.

A counterargument worth considering is whether DeLong and Lindsey are romanticizing the past, ignoring that intermediate associations in previous eras were often exclusionary or oppressive. However, the authors are careful to distinguish between the structure of community and the content of that community, arguing that we need the former to survive the latter.

From Abundance to Flourishing

Ultimately, DeLong's piece is a call to stop viewing the future solely through the lens of economic growth. The goal must shift from "abundance" to "flourishing," which requires creating new social identities and roles that are not tied to traditional employment. DeLong writes, "We will solve this—if we do—by creating other slices of life besides 'retirement', 'schooling', and raising children that are sources of internal purpose and of external respect." This is the "permanent problem" Keynes warned us about: the challenge of using our freedom to live "wisely and agreeably and well."

The author's synthesis of Keynes, Yglesias, and Lindsey offers a rare clarity: the solution to our unhappiness is not more money, but better stories about who we are and how we belong. The argument holds up because it aligns with a growing body of psychological research showing that social connection is a stronger predictor of well-being than income. Yet, the path forward remains vague. How do we build these new institutions in a world dominated by global algorithms? DeLong admits this is the hard part, leaving the reader with a challenge rather than a policy prescription.

"Man will [then] be faced with his real, his permanent problem—how to use his freedom from pressing economic cares, how to occupy the leisure, which science and compound interest will have won for him, to live wisely and agreeably and well."

Bottom Line

DeLong's strongest contribution is reframing the crisis of modernity not as a failure of production, but as a failure of meaning, effectively bridging the gap between Keynesian economics and the sociology of community. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a cultural shift that current political and economic incentives actively discourage, leaving the "how" of this transition unresolved. Readers should watch for how this diagnosis of "mass society" influences future debates on AI and automation, as the technology will only accelerate the very alienation DeLong describes.
