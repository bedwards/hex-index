Can we survive the coming decades?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Mar 6, 2022 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through the diplomatic fog of climate summits to deliver a stark verdict: the window for avoiding catastrophic loss is not just narrowing; it is slamming shut. Drawing on the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change's latest synthesis of 14,000 studies, he argues that we have already crossed critical tipping points, turning theoretical risks into immediate, irreversible death and destruction. This is not a plea for optimism but a forensic accounting of a system in freefall, making it essential listening for anyone trying to understand the true scale of the coming decades.

The Anatomy of a Vicious Circle

Borlace begins by dismantling the comforting notion that climate science is merely about predicting the future. Instead, he frames the current crisis as a present-tense emergency where the data is undeniable. "The report finds that climate change is already killing human beings, destroying nature, and making the world poorer," he states, grounding the abstract concept of global warming in immediate human cost. This direct linkage between emissions and mortality is the piece's most powerful rhetorical tool, stripping away the distance that often allows readers to dismiss climate change as a distant threat.

He describes the current trajectory as a "vicious circle" where human activity degrades ecosystems, which in turn lose their capacity to support life, forcing more desperate and often harmful human interventions. The author visualizes this using the IPCC's risk framework, noting how "climate hazards, vulnerability, and exposure" overlap to create an "anti-wheel of fortune" of doom. This metaphor effectively conveys the interconnectedness of the crisis; it is not a series of isolated events but a cascading failure of planetary systems. Critics might argue that such fatalistic framing could induce paralysis rather than action, yet Borlace insists that acknowledging the severity is the only prerequisite for survival.

"We've now gone past many of the tipping points that scientists have been warning us about for decades, meaning that in many cases the damages we'll see in the coming years will go beyond levels that humans and nature can adapt to."

The Geography of Irreversible Loss

Moving from the abstract to the specific, Borlace challenges the assumption that climate suffering is reserved for the Global South. He points out a startling reversal in the data: "It turns out that it's actually Europe and North America that are the two regions where a high level of impact is affecting all natural systems." This counter-intuitive finding forces a reckoning with the reality that no developed nation is insulated from the collapse of its own life-support systems.

He details the mechanisms of this collapse, from the disappearance of kelp forests that store carbon to the migration of marine species toward the poles. "Approximately half of all marine species assessed globally are now shifting towards the poles to escape the uninhabitable warm waters," he notes, illustrating the speed at which biological systems are being upended. The argument here is that the pace of change is outstripping the ability of nature to adapt, leading to "increasingly irreversible losses." This is not a slow drift but a sudden break, a distinction that is crucial for policymakers who still plan for gradual transitions.

The Trap of Maladaptation

Perhaps the most nuanced part of Borlace's commentary is his warning against "maladaptation"—well-intentioned solutions that make the problem worse. He argues that "some of those adaptations may actually be doing more harm than good," citing examples like seawalls that lock in future risks or indiscriminate tree planting that destroys local albedo. "Building a seawall is a great idea for reducing the impacts on a coastal town in the short term," he explains, "but it might end up locking in increased levels of exposure to climate risks in the long term."

This section serves as a critical check on the "techno-optimist" narrative that suggests we can engineer our way out of the crisis without changing our fundamental behaviors. Borlace emphasizes that without careful, long-term planning, our reactions will be "knee-jerk and reactionary," exacerbating the very vulnerabilities we seek to fix. The inclusion of indigenous perspectives is vital here, as he notes that these poorly conceived policies often "impact negatively on the lives and livelihoods of already very vulnerable indigenous people."

"The virtuous circle that the IPCC are urging us all to move towards looks more like this: a transition in human systems will mean that our species will no longer be a net emitter of greenhouse gases, and our adaptations will be carefully considered and targeted instead of knee-jerk and reactionary."

The Narrowing Window for Survival

Borlace concludes by quantifying the remaining time we have to alter our course, moving from the qualitative to the quantitative. He highlights the concept of the "shared socioeconomic pathways" to show that even a moderate warming scenario carries catastrophic risks. "If global warming exceeds 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, even if we only overshoot that limit briefly, then some of these impacts will themselves cause the release of yet more greenhouse gases," he warns, underscoring the point of no return.

The urgency is driven by the math of the carbon budget. "Our global carbon budget gives us fewer than eight years of emissions left at current rates," he asserts, framing the next decade not as a period for debate but as the final deadline for action. He argues that the only path forward requires a fundamental shift in how humanity organizes itself, including "peace and facilitation of human mobility" for the millions who will be displaced. While this sounds idealistic, he grounds it in the harsh reality that "if we fight amongst ourselves, then we can pretty much be assured of mutual destruction."

"We really have reached the end of the road down which we've been kicking the metaphorical can for about the last three or four decades or so. Time's up."

Bottom Line

Borlace's coverage is most effective in its refusal to offer false comfort, replacing vague hopes with a rigorous, evidence-based assessment of irreversible damage. Its greatest vulnerability lies in the sheer magnitude of the societal transformation required, which borders on the impossible given current political inertia. Readers should watch for how global leaders respond to the "maladaptation" warning, as the next few years will likely be defined by whether we choose thoughtful resilience or reactive destruction.
