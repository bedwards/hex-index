The Shock of Modernity

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Feb 27, 2020 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now reframes the Industrial Revolution not as a triumph of progress, but as a collective psychological trauma that rewired the human nervous system. By weaving together Victorian medical diagnoses, existential philosophy, and the visceral horror of early railway accidents, the author argues that modernity didn't just change how we live—it fundamentally broke how we feel. This is a crucial intervention for anyone feeling the weight of the current digital age, suggesting that our anxiety is not a personal failure but a historical inheritance.

The Architecture of Anxiety

The piece opens with a striking juxtaposition: the pastoral idealism of William Blake's poem against the "dark satanic Mills" of the 19th century. Then & Now writes, "within a generation the entire consciousness of life had changed and science challenged deep held views of the world." The author effectively uses this rapid shift to set the stage for a deeper exploration of the human cost of industrialization. By citing Søren Kierkegaard, the commentary grounds the historical upheaval in a timeless existential dread.

As Then & Now puts it, "anxiety may be compared with dizziness. He whose eye happens to look down into the yawning abyss becomes dizzy... hence anxiety is the dizziness of freedom." This metaphor is the intellectual anchor of the piece. It suggests that the sudden explosion of choices—political, commercial, and personal—was not liberating but paralyzing. The author connects this philosophical concept directly to the physical symptoms of the era, noting that the "freedom" of the modern world was experienced as a terrifying loss of grounding.

Critics might note that this focus on elite anxiety risks overlooking the sheer physical brutality faced by the working class, whose struggles were often about survival rather than existential dread. However, the author's point remains valid: the psychological shock of the new world order was a universal phenomenon, even if its manifestations differed by class.

The perfection of clocks and the invention of watches have something to do with modern nervousness since they compel us to be on time and excite the habit of looking to see the exact moment.

The Diagnosis of the Age

The article shines when it details the medicalization of this stress, specifically through the lens of "neurasthenia." Then & Now explains that this condition, characterized by fatigue and irritability, was viewed by physicians like Charles Beard as a depletion of "nervous energy" caused by the speed of modern life. The author lists the culprits with a rhythmic intensity: steam power, the telegraph, and the "mental activity of women."

The argument here is compelling because it highlights how society blamed the tools of progress rather than the structure of the new economy. As Then & Now writes, "machines to make machines production to expand production... faster and faster better and better." The author suggests that the human body was simply not evolved to handle the "cacophony of unbearable noise" and the relentless pace of industrial time. This framing is particularly resonant today, as we grapple with our own "digital neurasthenia" driven by smartphones and instant connectivity.

The piece also touches on the gendered nature of this diagnosis, noting that Beard included the "mental activity of women" as a cause of national nervousness. While this reflects the biases of the era, the author uses it to illustrate how deeply the fear of change permeated every layer of society, from the factory floor to the drawing room.

The Spectacle of Danger

Perhaps the most visceral section of the commentary focuses on the railway. Then & Now describes how the train became an emblem of the "shock of modernity," transforming travel from a slow, local experience into a high-speed, standardized nightmare. The author cites the "dizzying speed" of the railways and the way they forced the standardization of time, erasing local identities in favor of a single, rigid clock.

The cultural impact of this danger is illustrated through the popularity of "sensation" plays and novels that featured train crashes. Then & Now writes, "The Times described them as a perfect fever of excitement... The very same transport they were about to be thrilled by their fear." This paradox—finding entertainment in the very thing that terrified them—is a profound insight into the human psyche. The author argues that these stories allowed people to "practice the new emotions required in a modern fast unexpected world."

The inclusion of Charles Dickens's personal trauma from a train crash adds a layer of authenticity to the argument. Then & Now notes that after the accident, "he was never the same again." This personal anecdote serves as a microcosm for the broader societal shift: the modern world was not just a place to live, but a place that could shatter the mind in an instant.

The genre offers us one of the first instances of modern literature to address itself primarily to the sympathetic nervous system where it grinds its characteristic adrenaline effects.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's most powerful contribution is the assertion that our modern anxiety is a historical artifact, a "dizziness of freedom" born from the sudden collision of the old and the new. The argument's strength lies in its ability to connect abstract philosophy with the gritty reality of Victorian medical diagnoses and popular culture. The biggest vulnerability is a slight tendency to romanticize the pre-industrial past as a time of peace, ignoring the hardships of agrarian life. Yet, as a diagnosis of the modern condition, the piece remains a vital reminder that the shock of the new is not a bug of progress, but a feature of its very nature.
