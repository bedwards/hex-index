To The Moon!

Various · Defense Tech and Acquisition ·Apr 5, 2026 ·81 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Pete Modigliani and Matt MacGregor frame their latest Defense Acquisition dispatch around a single premise: the Pentagon's procurement machinery, long mocked for sclerosis, is being rewired in real time by the pressure of an ongoing Iran war and a $1.5 trillion FY27 budget. The newsletter reads less like analysis than like a field report from inside a reorganization, and that immediacy is both its strength and its limit.

Reform Under Fire

The central claim is that Portfolio Acquisition Executives, Direct Reporting Program Managers, and rapid acquisition mechanisms are finally closing what insiders call the valley of death—the gap between prototype and production. The authors quote CENTCOM commander ADM Brad Cooper on the effect: "We don't see their navy sailing. We don't see their aircraft flying, and their air and missile defense systems have largely been destroyed." The operational success in Operation Epic Fury is treated as validation of acquisition reform, not just military planning.

General Dan Caine's tribute to the industrial base lands in the same register. "In every military option," he says, "we could not and cannot do our jobs without the men and women across our country who show up every day, around the clock, to a factory floor." Modigliani and MacGregor use this to argue that the mobilization of dormant manufacturing capacity—tripling PAC-3 seeker output, quadrupling PrSM production—represents deliberate strategy rather than scramble.

The Metric That Matters

The sharpest moment in the newsletter is Major General Edward Vaughan's warning about how success gets measured.

"Don't measure the success of a program merely on cost, schedule and performance. If there are no metrics for operational impact, you've lost."

This is the kind of line that should haunt every program manager. The authors fold it into a broader argument that DRPMs and PAEs work precisely because they collapse the distance between requirements and fielding. General Dale White's framing reinforces the point: "Everything I do is in partnership with the current Air Force acquisition structure. I choose to use the authorities only when and if I need to." The reform, in other words, is a scalpel, not a sledgehammer.

The Private Capital Turn

A significant portion of the dispatch tracks the flood of institutional money into defense tech—Saronic's $9.25 billion valuation, SpaceX's confidential IPO filing, Carlyle's defense-focused fund. Shield Capital's Mike Brown captures the thesis: "The world is more dangerous. Technologies that were on the drawing board a decade ago have now proven themselves on the battlefield." Applied Intuition co-founder Qasar Younis adds a sharper operational note: "The cycle of standing up and tearing down autonomy stacks between events is over."

The authors clearly welcome this influx, but they let Brigadier General Jason Bartolomei add ballast. "We are not just building a single missile," he says of Lumberjack; "we are forging a new path toward a cost-effective, mass-producible deterrent for the nation." Mass production, not bespoke craftsmanship, is the watchword.

The Questions The Newsletter Skips

For all its enthusiasm, the dispatch has structural blind spots. The Iran war exposed a punishing cost exchange ratio—$4 million PAC-3 interceptors against $35,000 Iranian drones, roughly 114 to 1. The newsletter celebrates surge production of expensive interceptors without asking whether the economics are actually sustainable at wartime tempo. A factory that can triple output still can't repeal arithmetic.

Vendor lock-in is another quiet problem. Enterprise license agreements and consolidated contracts accelerate fielding, but they also hand contractors unusual influence over how threats get defined inside the force. The authors note this risk in passing and move on. The same goes for the firing of General Randy George mid-war, which they mention without exploring what it suggests about friction inside the new structures.

The biggest omission is sustainment. Compressed development cycles produce fielded systems fast, but those systems then require decades of parts, training, and upgrades. The A-10 doubling is invoked as triumph; it could equally be read as a warning that yesterday's rapid-fielding decisions become tomorrow's legacy burdens. Bill Greenwalt's critique of PPBE inflexibility is cited but not pressed. Congress, not acquisition reformers, ultimately controls whether any of this endures past the current emergency.

Bottom Line

Modigliani and MacGregor have produced a useful snapshot of a Pentagon in motion, and the quotes they assemble from Cooper, Caine, Vaughan, and White give the piece more authority than most acquisition commentary. The celebration of speed and private capital is earned, but it is also incomplete. Readers should treat this dispatch as evidence that reform is possible during wartime, while remembering that wartime is precisely when sustainability questions get postponed. The test of the new acquisition architecture won't come during Epic Fury. It will come five years later, when the budget tightens and the factories ask who still wants what they built.
