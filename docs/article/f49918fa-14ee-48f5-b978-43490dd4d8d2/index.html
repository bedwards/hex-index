To The Moon!

Various · Defense Tech and Acquisition ·Apr 5, 2026 · 81 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

This collection of briefs from Defense Tech and Acquisition does more than list budget numbers; it frames a decisive pivot from the era of Low Earth Orbit stagnation to a high-stakes, multi-domain war economy. The most striking claim isn't the $1.5 trillion price tag, but the assertion that the United States is finally treating defense production as a scalable industrial imperative rather than a boutique procurement exercise. For busy leaders tracking the shift from peacetime planning to active conflict readiness, the convergence of a historic lunar return and a massive munitions buildup offers a rare glimpse into the new strategic reality.

The Return to Deep Space and the Budgetary Leap

The coverage opens with a triumphant note on the Artemis II mission, positioning it not just as a scientific endeavor but as a strategic reclamation of deep space dominance. Various writes, "Since the Apollo 17 mission in Dec 1972, humans have only operated in Low Earth Orbit, roughly 250 miles." This context is vital; it reminds the reader that for over five decades, American human spaceflight has been confined to a narrow orbital band, leaving the lunar far side—where critical communication and surveillance advantages lie—untouched by human eyes. The article notes that the upcoming crew will be "the first people to lay eyes on some areas of the far side," a detail that echoes the historical secrecy of the Soviet Luna missions and underscores the strategic value of this 10-day voyage.

The financial backing for this ambition is staggering. The text details a request for "$1.5T in total budgetary resources for 2027," a figure that includes a "$445B or 42% increase from the 2026 total resource level." This is not merely inflation; it is a structural shift. The authors highlight that this budget relies heavily on "$350B in additional mandatory resources through reconciliation," a legislative mechanism typically reserved for domestic social policy, now repurposed for defense industrial expansion. This parallels the massive legislative maneuvers seen during the Reconciliation process in the United States Congress, where budget rules are bent to force through major policy shifts, signaling that the administration views defense spending as a non-negotiable priority.

The mandatory funding protects key priorities such as providing flexibility in maturing technology for delivery and allowing for acquisition approaches for portfolios of capabilities that broaden opportunities for new entrants.

The commentary here is sharp: by using reconciliation, the budget attempts to bypass the usual gridlock that stalls long-term defense projects. However, critics might note that relying on reconciliation for defense spending is politically fragile; if the political winds shift, the funding for these critical munitions and industrial base expansions could vanish overnight, leaving the "Golden Dome" missile defense system and other initiatives underfunded.

Industrial Mobilization and the "Golden Dome"

The piece pivots from space to the ground, focusing on the "Golden Dome," a next-generation missile defense system. The coverage is blunt about the funding disparity: "Golden Dome gets $17.5B but only $400M in base budget; the rest depends on reconciliation." This reveals a risky strategy where the core of America's future defense architecture hinges on temporary legislative fixes rather than stable, base-budget funding.

The article argues that the private sector is now a critical partner in this mobilization. It cites the recent fundraising of Saronic, an autonomous ship maker, which "raised $1.75B and more than doubled its valuation to $9.25B." The authors quote Saronic CEO Dino Mavrookas, who states, "We're seeing a real shift in demand towards unmanned systems that can be delivered at scale and at a fraction of the price point of traditional vessels." This is a crucial distinction. The military is no longer just buying expensive, bespoke platforms; it is seeking to replicate the scalability of commercial tech. The article notes that Saronic aims to "build more than 20 ships a year by 2027," a pace that dwarfs traditional naval construction timelines.

The coverage also touches on the human element of this industrial surge. It quotes Chairman Gen. Dan Caine, who emphasizes that "the distance from that factory floor and that assembly line, to the front line, is incredibly short." This framing is effective because it collapses the abstract concept of "defense industrial base" into a tangible reality: the weapons being built today are needed for conflicts happening now. The text supports this with data on the Army's missile procurement jumping from "$7B to $37B," driven by needs for PrSM seekers and HIMARS systems.

The Reality of Active Conflict

The tone shifts dramatically as the article moves from budget projections to the active theater of war in the Middle East. The authors describe the conflict with Iran as "a live, global game of Risk with real forces, real supply chains, and active shooting wars." This metaphor is powerful, stripping away the diplomatic euphemisms often used in defense reporting. The coverage details significant losses, noting that "Battle damage and replacement of losses over the first three weeks of the war likely costs roughly $1.4B to $2.9B."

The intelligence assessment presented is sobering. The text states, "Roughly half of Iran's missile launchers are still intact and thousands of one-way attack drones remain in Iran's arsenal." This contradicts the narrative of a decapitated enemy, suggesting that the conflict is far from resolved. The article quotes an intelligence source who warns, "They are still very much poised to wreak absolute havoc throughout the entire region." This quote serves as a stark counterpoint to the optimistic budget projections, reminding the reader that money and new systems take time to deploy, while the threat is immediate.

The coverage also highlights the resilience of legacy systems. Despite the Air Force's long-standing desire to retire the A-10, the text notes that "it once again proves a vital capability for the Joint Force." The authors argue that if the service needed a "new A-10" today, it would take "10+ years from Idea to FOC under normal acquisition processes." This highlights a critical vulnerability in the current defense model: the inability to rapidly field simple, effective weapons in a fast-moving war. The article suggests that the U.S. must be able to "design, develop, and mass produce a new A-10 in only a few years," a challenge that the current bureaucracy is ill-equipped to meet.

The Innovation Gap

The final section, drawing on the work of Steve Blank, addresses the structural flaws in how the military identifies and funds new technologies. The authors argue that current reforms, such as the Portfolio Acquisition Executives (PAEs), focus too much on the "middle phases of the innovation cycle" while neglecting the source of the requirements. Blank writes that "each PAE needs four things the current organization reforms don't provide: Forward-deployed Problem Discovery Teams in the Combatant Commands." This is a prescient critique. The military can buy all the drones and missiles it wants, but without a mechanism to rapidly identify what is actually needed on the battlefield, it risks solving yesterday's problems.

The article suggests a new model: "Fusion Cells – collect data from the field, industry and labs and rapidly do due diligence." This approach would allow for "lateral distribution at operational speed," moving away from the slow, top-down requirement generation that has plagued the Pentagon for decades. The authors warn that "Solving Yesterday's Problems Will Kill You," a blunt reminder that the speed of technological change in modern warfare outpaces the traditional acquisition cycle.

The innovation targeting cycle phases run continuously by a fusion cell, each rotation generating the input for the next.

Critics might argue that creating new organizational structures like Fusion Cells adds another layer of bureaucracy rather than solving the root cause of slow acquisition. However, the article's emphasis on "rapid operational assessment" suggests a move toward agility that could fundamentally change how the military adapts to threats.

Bottom Line

This collection of briefs succeeds in connecting the dots between a historic return to the Moon, a massive budgetary overhaul, and the brutal reality of active conflict. Its strongest argument is that the U.S. defense establishment is finally attempting to treat industrial capacity as a strategic weapon, leveraging private capital and legislative tools to scale production. However, its biggest vulnerability lies in the reliance on reconciliation for critical funding and the slow pace of acquisition reform, which may leave the military ill-prepared for the speed of modern warfare. The reader should watch closely to see if the "Golden Dome" and the new unmanned systems can actually be fielded at the scale promised, or if the gap between budget and reality will widen as the conflict intensifies.
