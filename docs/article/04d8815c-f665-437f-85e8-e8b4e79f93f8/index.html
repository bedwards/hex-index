America's chip export controls are working

Noah Smith · Noahpinion ·Jan 2, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Noah Smith delivers a chilling assessment of a policy pivot that could redefine the balance of power in the Pacific: the potential dismantling of U.S. export controls on advanced semiconductors. While much of the political discourse focuses on trade deficits or election rhetoric, Smith zeroes in on a specific, technical decision—the sale of the H200 chip to China—as a litmus test for whether the executive branch truly intends to maintain a technological edge over its primary rival. This is not a story about tariffs; it is a story about the very architecture of future warfare and the terrifying possibility that short-term corporate profits could inadvertently arm a strategic adversary.

The Stakes of Silicon

Smith frames the semiconductor industry not merely as an economic sector, but as the bedrock of modern military capability. He argues that the U.S. cannot out-produce China in volume, so it must out-innovate them in quality. "Chips are the foundation of all modern weaponry, from missiles to drones to satellites to advanced fighter jets," Smith writes, grounding the abstract concept of "AI" in the concrete reality of autonomous drone swarms and missile guidance systems. The argument is stark: if the technological gap closes, the deterrent that prevents a conflict over Taiwan or Japan evaporates.

The author's analysis of the H200 chip sale is particularly damning. He notes that while the administration initially resisted selling the H20, a rumor surfaced in mid-2025 that the H200—a chip nearly six times more powerful—would be approved. Smith highlights the Institute for Progress's data, which suggests that unrestricted exports would shrink the U.S. compute advantage from a massive 21-to-49x lead down to a precarious 1.2x. "If the U.S. cared about staying ahead of China in the AI race, why would we sell H200s to China?" Smith asks, forcing the reader to confront the contradiction between stated security goals and actual policy actions. The implication is that the administration may be prioritizing immediate revenue over long-term national survival.

If that technological edge evaporates, China is much more likely to start a war of conquest.

Critics might argue that keeping China dependent on American chips is a strategic masterstroke, forcing Beijing to rely on foreign supply chains. Smith dismantles this view with historical precedent. He points out that China's leadership has made technological self-sufficiency a non-negotiable mandate. "Rather than grow dependent, China will take Nvidia chips while they are available, use them to train models to compete with American frontier variants and continue to invest heavily in domestic alternatives," he writes. This is a crucial distinction: the strategy of "keeping them hooked" fails because the target is not a passive consumer but a state actor with a singular, long-term goal of independence.

The Illusion of Breakthroughs

A significant portion of Smith's commentary is dedicated to debunking the narrative that China is already winning the chip race, a narrative often used by lobbyists to justify loosening restrictions. He describes China's reported 7nm breakthrough in 2022 as a "Potemkin breakthrough," noting that while the technology was announced, the yields were poor and the production stalled. "Even SMIC's 7nm process, hailed as a catastrophic failure for export controls, is actually not achieving good yields, and is reportedly having reliability issues," Smith observes. This evidence suggests that the export controls are working exactly as intended, slowing China's progress without stopping it entirely.

Smith draws on the concept of "Galapagos syndrome" to explain why isolation might actually benefit the U.S. in the long run. If Chinese companies are cut off from the global ecosystem, their chips may become optimized only for the domestic market, limiting their scale and global competitiveness. "If Chinese AI companies buy only from Chinese chip companies, then Chinese chips may become optimized for the domestic market rather than the international market," he explains. This creates a natural barrier that preserves the advantage for American and allied chipmakers.

However, the author acknowledges the danger of information warfare. He notes that China's government frequently announces "stunning breakthroughs," such as a recent claim of an Extreme Ultraviolet Lithography (EUV) prototype, to pressure the U.S. into lifting bans. Smith is skeptical, pointing out that the prototype "has not yet produced working chips" and that the timeline for a functional machine has slipped to 2030. "It seems only a matter of time before opponents of U.S. export controls are using this stunning 'breakthrough' to argue that the U.S. should allow ASML to sell EUV machines to China," he warns. The fear is that the administration will fall for these feints, sacrificing a hard-won strategic advantage based on incomplete or misleading data.

The Human Cost of Strategic Error

The most gravity-laden section of Smith's piece connects the technical details of chip sales to the potential for catastrophic human loss. He argues that the primary reason to maintain a technological edge is deterrence. "The main thing stopping it from starting a war is the chance that it would lose such a conflict," Smith writes, referring to the Chinese leadership's calculation regarding a potential invasion of Taiwan. If the U.S. erodes its lead in AI and computing, the calculus changes. "Selling China H200s thus makes World War III a bit more likely in the next decade," he concludes, a statement that elevates the debate from economic policy to existential risk.

This framing forces the reader to consider the human cost of a policy error. A war in the Pacific would not be a distant abstraction; it would involve millions of lives and the potential destruction of the global order. Smith's argument is that the "capitalists will sell us the rope with which we will hang them," a quote he uses to describe the danger of prioritizing corporate sales over national security. "The notion that restrictions 'accelerate Beijing's move toward alternatives' misses a critical reality: Technological self-sufficiency is a Xi Jinping mandate," he asserts, emphasizing that the U.S. cannot rely on market forces to stop China's rise.

Selling China H200s thus makes World War III a bit more likely in the next decade.

Bottom Line

Noah Smith's analysis is a powerful, evidence-driven warning that the U.S. is flirting with a self-inflicted strategic defeat by allowing the sale of advanced AI chips to China. His strongest move is connecting the technical specifications of the H200 chip directly to the likelihood of a global conflict, making the abstract stakes of semiconductor policy undeniably concrete. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that the administration will consistently prioritize long-term deterrence over short-term economic gains, a gamble that history suggests may be too high. As the debate over export controls intensifies, readers must watch whether the executive branch continues to fall for the "Potemkin breakthroughs" of its rival or holds the line on the technological edge that keeps the peace.

America's chip export controls are working

by Noah Smith · Noahpinion · Read full article

“The capitalists will sell us the rope with which we will hang them.”

In December of 2024, before Trump took office, I wrote about the disturbing possibility that the man once known as a consummate China hawk would sell America out to its great rival. I wrote that the key tell would be whether Trump dropped the export controls on chips and chipmaking equipment implemented during Biden’s presidency:

[T]here’s one big important thing Trump could do to sabotage America’s effort to stand up to Chinese power. He could cancel the export controls that the Biden administration placed on the Chinese semiconductor industry. Removing export controls wouldn’t require legislative action — Trump could just do it whenever he wanted. And because the policy is not really in the limelight, there probably wouldn’t be a popular backlash to its cancellation. So export controls are pretty much a pure test of Trump’s China policy — if he keeps them, it’s because he wants to stand up to China, and if he cancels them, it means he doesn’t…

[E]xport controls are doing what they’re designed to do. They’re not killing China’s chip industry, but they’re slowing it down in important ways, and letting the U.S. retain its technological edge…These export controls [are] absolutely crucial if the U.S. is going to maintain any kind of a military-technological edge over China. Chips are the foundation of all modern weaponry, from missiles to drones to satellites to advanced fighter jets. And AI itself, which depends on advanced chips for training and inference, is rapidly becoming an essential weapon of war. When autonomous drone swarms hit the battlefield, AI will become even more crucial to the military balance.

The U.S. probably cannot out-manufacture China, even with all the tariffs and industrial policies in the world. America needs to retain a technological edge to balance out its productive weakness — an advantage in quality to balance out its deficiency in quantity. Semiconductors are that edge. If Trump cancels the export controls, it will mean he’s destroying America’s best chance to keep its weapons ahead of China’s weapons.
