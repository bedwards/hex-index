Rkul: Time well spent, 1/1/2026

Commentary by Hex Index staff

Razib Khan cuts through the noise of the modern information diet by arguing that the most valuable use of time is not chasing the latest trend, but engaging with dense, data-rich histories of scientific controversy. In a landscape saturated with superficial takes, Khan's curated list offers a rigorous defense of intellectual endurance, suggesting that the battles fought in academia decades ago are the very same ones raging in our institutions today.

The Architecture of Scientific Controversy

Khan begins by framing the new year not as a time for vague resolutions, but as an opportunity to "sprint through" books that have "stood up to repeat reading." His first major recommendation, Ullica Segerstråle's Defenders of the Truth, serves as a historical mirror for current academic friction. He notes that while the central players in the 1970s sociobiology debate—E.O. Wilson, Richard Lewontin, and Stephen Jay Gould—have largely passed, the "dynamics to those currently seen in academia's 'woke' period" remain hauntingly familiar.

"Carole Hooven's recent cancellation saga at Harvard could have been in Defenders of the Truth, though a further shift to the Left in academia since the 1970s makes it hard to imagine a modern-day 'sociobiology controversy' even seeing the light of day in the 2020s."

This observation is the piece's most provocative hook. Khan suggests that the mechanism of silencing dissent through moral accusation is not a new phenomenon, but a recurring cycle in the history of ideas. By linking the suppression of sociobiology to modern cancellations, he implies that the stakes of these intellectual battles are often higher than the immediate controversy suggests. However, critics might argue that equating the 1970s debate over evolutionary biology with today's debates on gender and identity risks oversimplifying the distinct social contexts that drive each movement.

"It's not for nothing that ancient Rome, both at its height and in its fall, continues to haunt people's thoughts over 1500 years later."

Khan pivots from the history of science to the history of civilization, recommending Bryan Ward-Perkins' The Fall of Rome. Here, he champions an approach that prioritizes material evidence over textual interpretation. He highlights how Ward-Perkins uses "ingenious and memorable measures as the increase in coin hoards after 400 AD" and pollution data from British ponds to map the empire's decline.

"The Fall of Rome makes it clear exactly how and when the Roman Empire went into decline based on graphs reporting widely varied data, rather than arguments based on narrow archival analysis."

This emphasis on hard data over narrative speculation is a recurring theme in Khan's selection. He values works that allow the reader to see the "material basis" of historical shifts, a method that feels particularly relevant in an era where political narratives often outpace empirical reality. The connection to the earlier sociobiology debate is subtle but potent: just as Wilson's theories were attacked for ignoring social construction, Ward-Perkins' archaeological data cuts through the "complaints about economic stress" found in ancient texts to reveal the actual collapse of infrastructure.

The Mechanics of Culture and Intelligence

Moving into the intersection of biology and culture, Khan introduces Joe Henrich's The Secret of Our Success. He positions this work as a potential catalyst for a new synthesis in anthropology, one that treats cultural units with the same rigor as genetic ones.

"While evolutionary psychologists discuss the impact of our cognitive hardware on our culture, cultural evolutionists like Henrich treat our memes like protean units of evolutionary analysis."

Khan argues that this distinction is crucial for understanding why societies diverge. He contrasts the universalist view of evolutionary psychology with the variation-focused approach of cultural evolution, suggesting that the latter offers a more nuanced tool for analyzing the "cultural variation we see around us." This aligns with the broader "dual inheritance theory" referenced in the piece's background, where genes and culture co-evolve.

"A book like A Primer of Population Genetics and Genomics pays long-term dividends, allowing you to understand dynamics observable everywhere in the world, and to make sense of patterns and trends that otherwise might mystify."

Khan does not shy away from technical depth, recommending Daniel Hartl's A Primer of Population Genetics and Genomics for readers willing to tackle "genuinely academic text." He frames this not as an exercise in obscurity, but as an investment in analytical literacy. By understanding concepts like "genetic drift, bottlenecks or natural selection," the reader gains the ability to "construct models off the top of their head." This is a bold claim for a reading list, suggesting that true literacy in the 21st century requires a grasp of population genetics.

"The search for excellence will always exclude."

This line, appearing in the context of An Apology for Philology, serves as a sharp rebuke to the argument that lowering standards is a form of inclusion. Khan critiques the trend in classics departments, such as Princeton's elimination of language requirements, as a concession to political radicalization rather than a genuine pedagogical shift. He points out the irony that scholars continue to use statistical tools developed by "unabashed eugenicists" like Francis Galton and Karl Pearson, yet reject the methods of philology based on the personal views of past practitioners.

"Surely the enterprise of scholarship is robust enough to accept that the measure of a scholar's value is in the utility of their insights or methods, not the merit of their personal views."

Here, Khan's commentary becomes a defense of the autonomy of scholarship against political interference. He draws a parallel to the controversy surrounding Napoleon Chagnon and The Darkness in El Dorado, where an "embittered journalist slandered Chagnon, permanently damaging his reputation." Khan notes that while colleagues knew the criticisms were baseless, they "could not bring themselves to support someone perceived as regressive." This historical parallel reinforces his argument that the pressure to conform to current political sensibilities often overrides the pursuit of truth.

Unintended Consequences and Global Shifts

The piece concludes with a rapid-fire survey of current events, tying them back to the theme of unintended consequences and long-term data trends. Khan highlights the tragedy of the vulture collapse in India, where the use of painkillers for cattle led to a population crash from "4 million to 32,000 in a generation."

"Millionth installment in the tragic annals of unintended consequences."

This brief but devastating summary underscores the fragility of ecological systems and the heavy price of human intervention. He also touches on geopolitical shifts, noting India's "Himalayan Building Spree" in preparation for a clash with China, a move driven by the reality of past conflicts like the 1962 Sino-India War.

"Humans killed millions of vultures. We're now living with the consequences."

Khan's inclusion of genetic history papers, such as those on the "Genetic history of Rus'" and "Upper Palaeolithic genomes," reinforces his central thesis: that deep time and hard data provide the only reliable map for understanding the present. Whether it is the "extensive admixture of Finnic people into northeastern Russians" or the "deep roots of modern Eurasians" dating back 45,000 years, these findings challenge simplistic narratives of national or racial purity.

"The rise, apogee, decline and fall of the first Europe-based multi-ethnic empire has served as a warning over the last two centuries of Western imperialism."

By weaving together ancient history, modern genetics, and contemporary academic politics, Khan constructs a worldview where the past is not a dead weight, but a living dataset. He challenges the reader to look beyond the "slop" of daily news and engage with the structural forces that shape human civilization.

"The genius of [Ward-Perkins'] work is its interdisciplinary scope... Instead of looking at ancient texts where contemporary commentators complain about economic stress, The Fall of Rome reports such ingenious and memorable measures as the increase in coin hoards after 400 AD."

Critics might argue that Khan's reliance on "data-heavy" arguments sometimes overlooks the human element of history, reducing complex social movements to graphs and genetic markers. However, his defense is that without this rigorous foundation, our understanding of the world remains shallow and susceptible to manipulation.

Bottom Line

Razib Khan's commentary is a powerful call to intellectual discipline, arguing that the only way to navigate a world of "slop" is to immerse oneself in the dense, often difficult, data of history and science. Its greatest strength lies in its refusal to separate the past from the present, revealing how the battles over sociobiology and the fall of Rome are not ancient history, but active templates for understanding today's institutional crises. The piece's vulnerability is its potential to alienate readers who prefer narrative over data, but for the busy, smart reader seeking clarity, it offers a roadmap to a more rigorous understanding of the world.
