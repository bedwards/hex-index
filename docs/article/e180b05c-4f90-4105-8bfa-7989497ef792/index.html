Transcript excerpt on Wendover Productions

California, by just about any metric, is a powerhouse. But it's also a state with an inherent and perhaps fatal flaw. Just look at the state's longest waterway and its two terminal reservoirs. These two fairly modest human-made lakes mark the end of the state's most important and most complicated water system and signal the major disconnect that defines California, that most of its people live where the water isn't.

Of California's 58 counties, just these four alone, home to Los Angeles and San Diego, contain about 19 million Californians, half the people in just 10% of the area. This relatively young mega region caught up to the historically dominant San Francisco over the past century. Rail connections linked the area to the rest of the nation. Projects to establish the ports of Long Beach and Los Angeles connected it to global trade.

Fissures in the ground brought oil bubbling to the surface. World War II brought about a regional aerospace boom. But a base reality underlies it all. Southern California boomed because it was simply an unbeatable place to live.

Whether it was boosters on the LA Times editorial board, postcard artists, railroads looking to sell property and up writership, Midwesterners convincing family to follow them, or sanitans selling their services to sick urbanites spread across the states. Southern California has always sold the fact that it has plenty of sunshine, pleasant temperatures, and a generally hospitable climate for living and growing. It's an image that has been maintained in popular culture. It's an idea that locals swear by today.

And it's a view that's hard to argue against. By daily mean temperature, Los Angeles's coldest month only differs from its hottest by 16° F or 9° C. It's as if the area doesn't have seasons. And with the ocean nearby helping to regulate, it's just never too terribly hot and never quite cold.

And it's always sunny as the city cites and sells the number 263 as the average count of sunny days a year. It's just about a perfect place to live. But boosterism has a way of turning alluring characteristics into cartoonish oversimplifications. What doesn't come up in talks as to how many sunny days there are is the downside linked to this lack of cloud cover, a lack of water.
