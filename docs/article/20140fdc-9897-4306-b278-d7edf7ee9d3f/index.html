Naming and Necessity by Saul Kripke - Part 1

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·May 9, 2024 · 22 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a cold January of 1970, a thirty-year-old mathematical prodigy named Saul Kripke walked into Princeton and dismantled a theory that had convinced the world's leading philosophers it was genius. Jeffrey Kaplan captures the sheer audacity of this moment, framing Kripke's lectures not merely as an academic correction, but as a philosophical demolition of how we understand language itself. Kaplan's coverage is essential because it translates dense metaphysical arguments into a narrative of intellectual revolution, showing how a simple question—"What does a name actually mean?"—can upend centuries of thought.

The Illusion of Description

Kaplan begins by setting the stage for the dominant theory Kripke attacked: descriptivism. As Kaplan explains, this theory, championed by John Searle, posits that a proper name like "Abraham Lincoln" is synonymous with a cluster of descriptions, such as "the 16th president" or "the issuer of the Emancipation Proclamation." Kaplan writes, "Abraham Lincoln is a guy and we could describe him... descriptivism is the idea that proper names like Abraham Lincoln are really just synonymous they really have the same meaning as descriptions like the 16th president of the United States." This framing is effective because it immediately grounds an abstract concept in a relatable historical figure, making the stakes clear: if the theory is wrong, our entire understanding of reference is flawed.

The core of Kaplan's analysis lies in how he reconstructs Searle's "cluster theory" into six distinct theses to expose their fragility. He paraphrases the central claim that the name refers to whoever fits the "weighted majority" of these properties. Kaplan notes, "whichever thing fits with enough of these that is the reference of the name that guy whoever meets enough of these characteristics that's Abraham Lincoln." This is the pivot point of the argument. Kaplan's commentary here is sharp; he highlights the vagueness of the theory, calling it "very vague and amorphous," which serves as a perfect setup for Kripke's surgical strike. A counterargument worth considering is that Searle's theory was never meant to be mathematically precise, but rather a psychological account of how people actually use language. However, Kaplan correctly identifies that Kripke's attack targets the logical necessity of the theory, not just its psychological plausibility.

If the name Abraham Lincoln just means by definition the person who was the 16th president of the United States... then you should be able to know that Abraham Lincoln did these things a priori.

Kaplan then tackles the most damaging thesis: the idea that these descriptions are necessary truths. He distinguishes between contingent truths (things that happened to be true) and necessary truths (things that must be true in any conceivable reality). Kaplan writes, "even though this is true it's only true contingently or by chance... compare that with this the winner of the election of 1860 won the election of 1860 this sentence is also true but it's not true contingently it's not just True by chance it has to be true no matter how history played out." This distinction is the engine of Kripke's argument, and Kaplan explains it with remarkable clarity, stripping away the jargon to reveal the logical heart of the problem. The argument lands because it forces the reader to imagine a world where Lincoln never became president, yet remains Lincoln.

The Rigid Designator and the Fictional Counterexample

Moving to the attack on uniqueness, Kaplan illustrates how ordinary speakers often lack the specific descriptions required by descriptivism to uniquely identify a person. He uses the example of Cicero and Richard Feynman to show that we can refer to people meaningfully without knowing enough to distinguish them from everyone else. Kaplan writes, "most people when they think of Cicero just think of a famous Roman orator without any pretension to think either that there is only one famous Roman orator or that one must know something else about Cicero to have a reference for the name." This observation is devastating to the cluster theory because it suggests that reference is not a matter of internal mental descriptions, but of something external and causal.

The commentary reaches its climax with Kripke's famous "Gödel-Schmidt" thought experiment. Kaplan describes a fictional scenario where the man we call "Gödel" did not prove the incompleteness of arithmetic; rather, a man named Schmidt did, and Gödel stole the credit. Kaplan writes, "imagine the following blatantly fictional situation... this super famous mathematician Kurt Gödel... is saying like I'm going to make up this story about Gödel I hope Gödel's not here in the audience today." This anecdote serves as the ultimate refutation of the idea that the name "Gödel" means "the prover of the incompleteness theorem." Even if the description is false, the name still refers to the man who stole the credit. Kaplan's delivery of this point is masterful, capturing the moment where the audience realizes the theory has collapsed.

Critics might note that Kripke's reliance on "possible worlds" and "rigid designators" introduces its own metaphysical baggage that some philosophers find problematic. Kaplan acknowledges this complexity, admitting, "in order to really understand this you have to know what a philosopher means when they say necessary... and you have to understand possible worlds." He wisely defers the technical explanation to another piece, keeping the focus on the logical intuition rather than the metaphysical machinery. This is a smart editorial choice for a busy reader, prioritizing the "why" over the "how."

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's commentary succeeds because it treats Kripke's lectures as a dramatic narrative of intellectual conquest rather than a dry recitation of theses. The strongest part of the argument is the clear demonstration that names function as "rigid designators" that stick to the same object across all possible worlds, regardless of the descriptions we associate with them. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the reliance on the listener's ability to intuitively grasp the concept of "possible worlds" without a deeper technical dive. Readers should watch for how this shift from description to causal history reshapes not just philosophy of language, but our understanding of identity and truth itself.
