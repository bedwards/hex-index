How Malaysia is becoming the next global chip giant

Shirvan Neftchi · CaspianReport ·Jul 20, 2024 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Shirvan Neftchi delivers a stark warning wrapped in a boom story: Malaysia is not just catching a wave of semiconductor investment; it is standing on a precipice where a single misstep could turn a golden opportunity into a geopolitical hostage situation. While most analysis celebrates the billions flowing into the country, Neftchi argues that without a radical shift in domestic capability and political stability, Malaysia risks becoming a glorified warehouse for foreign chips rather than a true innovation hub. This is a high-stakes narrative for anyone tracking the future of the global supply chain, because the "China Plus One" strategy is not a guarantee of safety—it is a gamble that requires more than just cheap land.

The Middle-Income Trap Revisited

Neftchi frames the current moment as a second chance for a nation that has historically struggled to break out of the "middle-income trap." He notes that while South Korea successfully pivoted from low-end manufacturing to high-tech dominance in the 1980s, Malaysia "was unable to develop Innovative Industries in time and remained trapped." The author's comparison is sharp and effective, highlighting that the country's early industrialization was built on a foundation that lacked a crucial component: research and development. As Neftchi writes, "a crucial component was left out... this mistake sealed Malaysia's Fate."

The commentary here is vital because it moves beyond the headline-grabbing investment figures to the structural weaknesses that could undermine them. Neftchi points out that past wealth redistribution policies, while politically necessary for social stability, "inadvertently led to a brain drain" where skilled non-ethnic Malay workers fled the country. This historical context explains why the current labor shortage is so severe. Critics might note that the author places significant blame on domestic policy without fully exploring the external pressures of global capital flight that affect all developing nations, but the link between the 1970s policies and today's engineering deficit is compelling.

"Malaysia needs to go big or go home yet large-scale plans have a mixed history of success in the country."

The Penang Paradox and Regional Rivalry

The piece takes a fascinating turn when it dissects the internal geography of Malaysia's tech ambitions. Neftchi describes how the state of Penang, once the "Silicon Valley of the East," was allowed to decline as the capital, Kuala Lumpur, aggressively siphoned off resources. He argues that "Malaysia scored one of the biggest own goals in history" by diverting talent and wealth away from the region that actually had the ecosystem to succeed. Now, the government is trying to revive Penang, but the competition has moved on.

The author contrasts Malaysia's volatility with the stability of its neighbor, Singapore. Neftchi writes, "Singapore's Edge over Malaysia is its proximity and higher pay which stands at $1,495 per month compared to just $4169 per month in Malaysia." (Note: The source text contains a likely transcription error here, as $4,169 is higher than $1,495; the context clearly implies the Malaysian wage is significantly lower, likely around $416). Regardless of the specific numbers, the point stands: the wage gap is driving a massive exodus, with Malaysian immigration to Singapore jumping 62% in early 2024. This is a critical vulnerability. If the local workforce cannot be retained, the billions in foreign investment will hit a hard ceiling.

Furthermore, Neftchi highlights the political instability that scares off long-term investors. He notes that "in the last four years Malaysia has seen four different Prime Ministers," creating an environment where "multi-billion dollar companies looking to do business prefer political stability Above All else." The example of the goods and services tax, introduced and then repealed, illustrates a legislative whiplash that no tech giant wants to navigate. A counterargument worth considering is that Malaysia's federal system allows for regional experimentation that a centralized state like Singapore cannot match, but Neftchi convincingly argues that in the race for high-tech manufacturing, consistency wins over experimentation.

The Geopolitical Tightrope

Perhaps the most urgent part of Neftchi's analysis is the geopolitical dimension. Malaysia sits "smack in the middle of an American Chinese trade War," and the author warns that the country cannot simply play both sides indefinitely. He writes, "Washington has already voiced its disapproval of prime minister anoir Ibrahim's cooperation with Chinese firms and there are fears that the sanctions regime May expand to include Malaysia." The stakes are existential: "should Washington feel inclined they can end Malaysia's semiconductor boom with the stroke of a pen."

Neftchi suggests that the only way out is to build enough domestic economic clout to become indispensable, but this requires a level of coordination that has eluded the country for decades. He proposes a potential symbiosis with Singapore, where Malaysia handles assembly and testing while Singapore focuses on design, but admits this would require Malaysian firms to accept a subordinate role. "In Practical terms terms that would mean embattled Malaysian firms such as unism Carm and Inari aratron would be left out in the cold," he writes. This is a painful trade-off that domestic politicians may be unwilling to make, even if it is the only rational economic path.

"The only way to escape that fate is to gather enough economic clout at home and so Malaysia must go big or go under."

Bottom Line

Neftchi's strongest contribution is his refusal to treat the semiconductor boom as a guaranteed victory, instead framing it as a fragile window of opportunity that could slam shut due to domestic brain drain and political volatility. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its somewhat deterministic view of the geopolitical landscape, potentially underestimating the leverage Malaysia holds as a neutral ground for Chinese and Western firms alike. Readers should watch closely for the next wave of engineering graduate retention rates and any shifts in US sanctions policy, as these two factors will likely determine whether Malaysia becomes a global giant or a cautionary tale.
