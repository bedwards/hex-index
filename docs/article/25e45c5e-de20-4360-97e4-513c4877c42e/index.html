Beyond the thucydides trap: Understanding China's unique development path

Jeff Rich · ·Jan 9, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Jeff Rich challenges a pervasive Western delusion: the belief that China is merely a delayed version of the United States, destined to follow the same liberal democratic trajectory. In a tour of modern Chinese history, Rich argues that the dominant geopolitical theories used by Washington are not just wrong, but dangerously narcissistic, projecting American history onto a civilization that has charted a distinct, often contradictory, path to modernity.

The Trap of American Projection

The core of Rich's argument targets the intellectual rigidity of mainstream international relations theory. He contends that scholars like John Mearsheimer and proponents of the "Thucydides Trap" fail because they assume China will inevitably replicate the US hegemony project. "The great mistake that IR theorists like John Mearsheimer make is to assume China will follow the US trajectory," Rich writes. "They make this error because they generalize the unique history of the USA and its hegemony project into a universal theory of great power politics."

This framing is powerful because it shifts the blame for misunderstanding from Chinese opacity to Western arrogance. Rich suggests that the West has fallen into a "narcissistic illusion that China would become like America." However, this perspective risks underestimating the genuine strategic competition that exists regardless of ideological alignment. Critics might note that even if China does not become a liberal democracy, its rise still fundamentally disrupts the US-led order, making the "trap" real in terms of power dynamics, if not in terms of ideological destiny.

Rich draws on Klaus Mühlhahn's Making China Modern to support the idea that China's development represents a "scalar shift in production" that may exceed the Euro-American Industrial Revolution in impact. He argues that the West's mental models are obsolete, noting that the "cartoon form of history used to educate the US American Empire's military and intelligence officials" is insufficient for understanding a civilization-state. This is a crucial intervention; it suggests that our current geopolitical vocabulary is ill-equipped to describe the reality on the ground.

The Myth of Omniscient Planning

A significant portion of Rich's commentary dismantles the romanticized view of Chinese strategic genius. He points to the one-child policy not as a masterstroke of long-term planning, but as a catastrophic error that contradicts the narrative of a perfectly foresighted state. "Remember: the one-child policy was the work of the great reformer, Deng Xiaoping, not Chairman Mao," Rich writes. "Though there is a lot of nonsense spoken about demographic collapse and population aging in China, the one-child policy is a counter to the myth of omniscient Chinese planning and foresight."

By highlighting this policy failure, Rich humanizes the Chinese state, stripping away the aura of infallibility that often surrounds it in Western discourse. He connects this to the Cultural Revolution, describing it not just as a political aberration but as a "human puzzle" with parallels to global upheavals like Paris in 1968 or the US in 2020. He asks, "Why are intellectuals sometimes the cruellest instigators of violence? Why do we destroy our culture and our history?"

This line of questioning is essential for a nuanced understanding. It moves beyond the binary of "communist dictatorship" versus "democratic transition" to explore the messy, often tragic, human experience of modernization. Rich notes that while Mao was "the mastermind behind the pandemonium," the Cultural Revolution was not solely his work, but a complex social phenomenon involving millions. This complexity is often lost in the "memory struggle sessions of the Second Cold War," where historical tragedies are enlisted as political weapons.

The errors of the Thucydides Trap debate stem from this mapping of unique US American experiences to universal theories.

Rich also touches on the Opium Wars, challenging the deterministic view that conflict between China and the West was inevitable. Citing Stephen Platt, he describes how key players "stumbled and blustered into a tragic war," rather than following a preordained script. This historical nuance is vital; it suggests that current tensions are not the result of an ancient, unchangeable destiny, but of specific, avoidable choices made by leaders on all sides.

The Riddle of the Modern

Ultimately, Rich posits that the central question is not whether China will become a liberal democracy, but how we understand its unique form of modernity. "The key question to ask is the riddle of the modern," he asserts. He rejects the idea that China is repeating the Soviet or fascist paths, arguing instead that its institutions emerged from a "unique way" of combining domestic and external events after the trauma of the Cultural Revolution.

He warns against the "doomsayers" who predict China's collapse due to demographics, calling them experts who "know nothing, about social history." Rich argues that the world does not play by the rules of a zero-sum game where rivals can be voted out. "You cannot exclude a great power from the one sphere of influence we all dwell in on this planet," he writes. This is a sobering reminder of the limits of containment strategies.

The piece concludes by emphasizing the human cost of these historical shifts, from the 14 to 20 million deaths during the war for national sovereignty to the ongoing struggles of ordinary people. Rich's approach is to listen to the "many stories of China's history" rather than forcing them into a single, comforting narrative. He suggests that films like Farewell My Concubine offer a more authentic entry point into this complexity than turgid textbooks, capturing the "irreducible plurality" of the Chinese experience.

Bottom Line

Jeff Rich's most compelling contribution is his rejection of the "American mirror" through which the West views China, exposing the intellectual laziness of assuming a universal path to modernity. While his dismissal of demographic doomsaying is persuasive, the piece could benefit from a deeper analysis of how China's unique path might still lead to conflict, even if not through the lens of the Thucydides Trap. The reader is left with a clear imperative: to abandon the expectation that China will become like us, and to prepare for a world where it remains fundamentally, and perhaps permanently, different.
