Laws Broken: Dark Knight (Can Batman Use Self Defense? How Many People Did the Joker Kill?)

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Mar 28, 2019 · 17 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just dissect a movie; he dismantles the entire legal fantasy of the vigilante. In a genre where the hero's violence is often framed as necessary justice, Stone applies the cold, hard logic of New York Penal Law to argue that Batman is not a hero, but a dangerous criminal who should be behind bars. This isn't a fan theory; it's a prosecutorial brief that forces the audience to confront the reality that "doing the right thing" does not grant immunity from the law.

The Joker's Legal Liability

Stone begins by establishing a clear jurisdictional framework, noting that while Gotham is fictional, it functions as a stand-in for New York City. He immediately pivots to the bank robbery scene, using the Pinkerton doctrine to argue that every member of a conspiracy is responsible for the acts of their partners. "Assuming that the Pinkerton doctrine applies all of the members of the conspiracy including the Joker are on the hook for all of the criminal acts of their co-conspirators," Stone writes. This is a crucial distinction that elevates the Joker from a chaotic actor to a mastermind liable for every death that occurs during the heist, including those he didn't personally commit.

The commentary shines when Stone analyzes the "felony murder rule," explaining that unintentional killings during an inherently dangerous crime like armed robbery still count as murder. He notes that the Joker's manipulation of the bus driver to cause a fatal crash constitutes murder under the mental state of "knowingly." Stone writes, "even though the Joker didn't pull the trigger he knowingly engaged in actions that would lead to the death of his co-conspirator which would qualify as at least second-degree murder if not first-degree murder." This reframing strips away the Joker's theatricality, reducing his crimes to cold, calculable legal elements. Critics might argue that the Joker's insanity would be a viable defense, but Stone correctly points out that the insanity defense is incredibly difficult to prove and doesn't negate the initial criminal liability.

"Everything is illegal."

Stone also tackles the Joker's use of terror, identifying his threats against the civilian population as a specific violation of anti-terrorism statutes. He argues that the Joker's goal to "intimidate or coerce a civilian population" fits the definition of terrorism under New York law, a charge that carries far more weight than simple murder. This is a powerful move, elevating the narrative stakes from a personal vendetta to a threat against the state itself.

The Myth of the Vigilante

The most provocative section of Stone's analysis targets Batman directly. He dismantles the popular notion of self-defense, explaining that New York law requires a reasonable belief of imminent deadly force and, crucially, a duty to retreat if possible. "New York law does not look kindly on vigilantes you can't just interject yourself into crime and and pretend to be police it just doesn't work that way," Stone asserts. This is the core of his argument: Batman's training makes his physical force deadly, yet he lacks the legal authority to use it.

Stone points out that Batman's code against killing is legally irrelevant because his actions are inherently lethal. He writes, "he has the kind of training that makes his actions I think deadly and even though he doesn't try to kill people I think it's clear that people die when he gets involved." This observation is devastating to the hero's moral standing; the intent doesn't matter if the result is death. Stone further complicates the picture by discussing the "fruit of the poisonous tree" doctrine. While evidence obtained illegally is usually excluded, Stone notes that because Batman is a private citizen and not a state actor, the police can still use the evidence he gathers. "The fruit of the poisonous tree doctrine doesn't apply because the police here were blameless," he explains. This is a subtle but vital legal nuance that often gets lost in pop culture discussions.

Stone also highlights Batman's violations of aviation and kidnapping laws, noting that his gliding and unauthorized extraction of Lau from Hong Kong are clear felonies. He writes, "Batman just straight-up kidnapped Slough whatever the Hong Kong equivalent of kidnapping I think that's probably what Batman just engaged in." The analysis extends to the interrogation of the mobster, where Stone distinguishes between New York's lack of a specific "torture" statute and California's, yet concludes that the battery charges would be equally severe regardless of the state.

The Verdict on Gotham

Stone's coverage culminates in a systematic indictment of Batman's methods. He argues that Batman's use of the Bat-cycle to destroy property constitutes reckless endangerment, and his masked presence violates loitering statutes. "Clearly Batman is violating the law during statute here," Stone states, pointing out that the disguise itself is a crime. The commentary is relentless, leaving no room for the "ends justify the means" defense. Stone writes, "he had a duty to withdraw because he didn't he's likely guilty of several dozen counts of battery." This is a stark reminder that the law does not recognize a private right to enforce justice through violence.

Critics might argue that Stone's application of real-world law to a fantasy setting ignores the unique context of Gotham's corruption, where the legal system has failed. However, Stone's point remains valid: even in a broken system, a private citizen cannot unilaterally decide to become the judge, jury, and executioner without facing legal consequences. The tension between moral necessity and legal prohibition is the heart of the piece.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's analysis is a masterclass in applying rigid legal logic to fluid narrative tropes, successfully arguing that Batman is a criminal who happens to be on the side of good. The strongest part of his argument is the dismantling of the self-defense justification, proving that vigilante justice is legally indistinguishable from assault. The biggest vulnerability is the assumption that New York law perfectly maps onto a fictional universe, but this limitation only serves to highlight the absurdity of the vigilante ideal. Readers should watch for how this legal realism continues to challenge the romanticized view of superheroes in future media.
