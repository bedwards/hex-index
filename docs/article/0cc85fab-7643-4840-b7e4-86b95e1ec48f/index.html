Single member districts, the electoral college, and the jurist prudence of the Supreme Court. But hey, we don't have all day. Now, when Vice President Aaron Burr killed Alexander Hamilton in 1804, you would have thought that the murder would have put an end to dueling, especially after Burr escaped and then tried to crown himself the king of the Western States, which is a thing that actually happened. But Congress wouldn't put dueling out of pasture just yet.

Congress didn't really get worked up about the dueling until two members of the House of Representatives killed each other. That's right. Kentucky Representative William Graves killed main representative Jonathan Silly in a duel right here at the Bladensburg dueling grounds just northeast of Washington DC. But what's crazy is these two didn't even have a direct grievance with each other.

However, Graves was sent to deliver a dueling invitation from a New York newspaper editor who accluded Silly of Defamation. But Graves and Silly decided that since they were already hanging out at the dueling grounds, they should duel each other to the death because why not? What could go wrong? Well, here's what went wrong.

They went beyond the customary two rounds and in round three, Graves shot Silly dead. A funeral was held in the House of Representatives and the Supreme Court boycotted it in protest.
