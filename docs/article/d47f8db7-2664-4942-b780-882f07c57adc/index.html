We Put 7 Uber Drivers in One Room. What We Found Will Shock You

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Sep 9, 2024 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union doesn't just ask if the gig economy is broken; they prove it with a controlled experiment that exposes a hidden layer of wage theft. By gathering seven drivers in a single room to test ride-share algorithms simultaneously, the author moves beyond theoretical complaints to deliver hard data on "algorithmic wage discrimination." This is not a story about bad luck or market fluctuations; it is a forensic look at how technology is being weaponized to pay different workers different rates for the exact same labor, a practice that threatens the very foundation of fair competition.

The Shadow Economy of Algorithms

The investigation begins with a startling premise: the transparency of the gig economy is a mirage. More Perfect Union writes, "once that technology is in there they can raise prices on a hot summer day... they can raise the price of milk even your paycheck, all controlled by invisible and unaccountable algorithms." This framing is crucial because it shifts the narrative from individual driver struggles to a systemic economic manipulation that affects everyone, from the person buying lemonade to the person driving the car.

The author notes that for years, the evidence was elusive, hidden behind "a single study, a hint in an earnings call, a story from a driver." But the stakes changed when the author connected with Sergio Aedan, a senior contributor at the Ride Here Guy, who claimed the industry was "at the tip of the iceberg." The resulting experiment was simple yet devastating in its clarity. Seven experienced drivers were placed inches apart in Los Angeles, all activating screen capture software to record their offers in real-time.

"The algorithms are set up in a way to charge the rider as much as it's possible and to pay the driver as little as possible."

The results were immediate and damning. More Perfect Union reports, "we found that Uber offered the same rides 46 times to multiple drivers, 63% of the time, one of the drivers was offered a bit less money for the exact same ride." The disparities weren't trivial; Lyft showed gaps of up to $3 to $4 per ride after bonuses. This evidence holds up because the experiment controlled for the variables the companies usually hide behind: location, time, and demand. By eliminating these excuses, the author forces the reader to confront the possibility that the pricing model is intentionally designed to exploit information asymmetry.

Critics might note that algorithmic pricing is standard in dynamic markets, where supply and demand fluctuate by the second. However, the experiment specifically tested simultaneous offers for the same route, neutralizing the argument that a driver's location or timing caused the price difference. The data suggests the variance is not market-driven but algorithmically induced.

The Legal Loophole of "Independence"

The most insidious aspect of this system, according to the author, is the legal gymnastics companies perform to avoid accountability. Uber and Lyft classify drivers as independent contractors to deny them benefits, yet they exert total control over their wages and pricing. More Perfect Union highlights this contradiction, quoting legal expert David Segman: "from our perspective Uber's and Lyft's business model is based on control without responsibility."

The author argues that this is a cognitive dissonance that the law has yet to catch up with. "If these drivers are as you say independent then they need to have true economic independence," the text notes, pointing out that the companies have stripped drivers of their ability to set prices. This is a powerful argument because it attacks the core of the "gig" model: the promise of freedom. The reality, as the author illustrates, is that drivers are "Your Own Boss" only in name, while the platforms dictate every financial variable.

"Uber and Lyft classify drivers as independent contractors, not employers... but as an independent contractor you're supposedly making money for yourself with no benefits or protections but Freedom."

The author points out that this model relies on a legal shield: forced arbitration. When drivers try to sue, they are sent to private arbitration where the results are never public. This creates a black box of justice where companies can allegedly violate labor laws without public scrutiny. The author suggests that while companies may be exempt from certain employment laws, they are not exempt from antitrust laws. "Federal and state enforcers aren't subject to forced arbitration agreements," the text reminds us, suggesting a path for regulatory intervention that has been largely ignored.

A Broader Threat to the Economy

The commentary concludes by widening the lens, arguing that this is not just a ride-share problem. More Perfect Union warns, "this issue of algorithmic pricing stretches beyond ride share... we're just at the beginning stages of what I feel is like a battle between the common folk and then that manager class and upwards." The author cites credible reporting that giants like Walmart, Amazon, and McDonald's are experimenting with similar surveillance pricing, where algorithms might charge a desperate parent more for children's Tylenol based on search history.

The Federal Trade Commission is investigating these practices, but the author argues that the same scrutiny must be applied to wages. "The power of these Giants lies in their ability to keep everyone consumer drivers Regulators blindfolded," the author writes. This is the piece's most urgent call to action. Without transparency, the economy becomes a rigged game where the rules are written by algorithms that no one can see or challenge.

"Maybe there's an innocent explanation to our Fair experiment but we won't know until the federal government shines a light."

The author's tone here shifts from investigative to cautionary. The evidence presented is compelling, but the solution requires a level of regulatory courage that has been absent. The piece effectively argues that the current legal framework is insufficient to handle the complexity of algorithmic wage discrimination, leaving workers vulnerable to a new form of exploitation.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union delivers a compelling, evidence-based indictment of the gig economy's pricing models, using a controlled experiment to expose a practice that has long been suspected but rarely proven. The strongest part of the argument is the direct link between the "independent contractor" classification and the algorithmic suppression of wages, revealing a business model built on regulatory arbitrage. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the difficulty of translating this evidence into immediate legal action, given the entrenched power of these corporations and the complexity of antitrust law. Readers should watch for how the Federal Trade Commission responds to these findings, as the outcome could redefine the relationship between technology, labor, and the law for decades to come.
