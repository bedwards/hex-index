Rules for Interacting with College Professors - Office Hours, Email, Letters of Recommendation

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·Mar 9, 2022 · 20 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan doesn't just list rules; he exposes the invisible social contract that separates successful students from those who get lost in the noise. By framing email etiquette not as arbitrary bureaucracy but as a mirror of professional reality, he offers a survival guide that transcends the classroom. His most striking insight? That the way you address a professor today is the exact same way you will address a future boss, and getting it wrong signals a lack of readiness for the white-collar world.

The Digital Handshake

Kaplan opens with a vivid, slightly humorous anecdote about his father, Harold, who awkwardly signed text messages with "Dear so-and-so" and a full signature. He uses this to illustrate a fundamental truth about communication norms: "Email conversations that you will be having with your college professors also have their own etiquette. There's just a way you do it. And if you don't do it the right way, then you'll look weird." This comparison is effective because it strips away the intimidation of academia and replaces it with the relatable awkwardness of a generational tech gap. He argues that the stakes are higher than just looking silly; they are about professional viability.

He dismantles the common student habit of starting emails with "Hey," noting that while it isn't malicious, "it's just that they don't know what is appropriate in this professional environment." Instead, Kaplan advocates for a consistent, respectful approach: "The best bet, the one that I recommend in every case in all college courses is Professor So-and-so." He clarifies that this applies even to instructors without the formal rank of professor, a nuance that often trips up students who are trying to be precise about titles. The advice is practical, but it also serves a deeper purpose: it forces students to adopt a professional persona immediately.

You want to start in this somewhat formal way and then you want to see how they respond. If they dial down the level of formality, right? Then that's a signal to you that you can dial it back.

This observation about matching the professor's tone is crucial. Kaplan explains that a professor signing off with initials like "JD" does not mean they want to be called "JD." Rather, "they still want you to call them professor so-and-so, but they don't want to seem like the kind of stuffy person who signs their emails professor so-and-so." This distinction is often missed by students who mistake a casual sign-off for an invitation to drop all decorum. Kaplan's guidance here prevents a common social faux pas that could damage a student's reputation before it's even formed.

The Logistics of Respect

Beyond greetings, Kaplan zeroes in on the most frustrating administrative hurdle for faculty: the missing context. He paints a clear picture of a professor juggling multiple sections and courses, noting that when a student sends an email without identifying their class, "I have to open up my course lists for for my courses and look through the course lists alphabetically to find this student's name in all three of those course lists. That's super annoying." He is careful to note that he won't punish the student, but the friction is real. The solution is simple but often ignored: "Remind them which class you're in." This small act of empathy saves the professor time and signals that the student respects the professor's workload.

He also addresses the medium of communication itself, drawing a hard line against informal channels. "The only electronic ways that you should contact your college professors are by email or in the messaging, you know, part of your learning management software." He explicitly warns against texting or social media contact, framing these boundaries as essential to maintaining a professional environment. Similarly, he advises against using "weird fonts" in emails, arguing that society has settled on default fonts because "that's actually super annoying" when people try to be creative with typography. The goal, he insists, is for the content to stand out, not the "shapes of the letters."

Critics might argue that this rigid adherence to formality stifles the unique personalities of students or that the academic world is slowly becoming more casual. However, Kaplan's point is that until the norms shift, the safest and most effective path is to mirror the professional world's current standards. He treats these conventions not as arbitrary rules but as a necessary code for navigating a professional hierarchy.

Office Hours as a Strategic Asset

Perhaps the most valuable section of Kaplan's commentary is his reframing of office hours. He describes them as "an incredible resource, an educational resource that's available to you," yet acknowledges the intimidation factor. His advice is to "feel the intimidation, swallow it, and go anyway." But he doesn't just encourage attendance; he provides a strategy for making it count. The key, he argues, is preparation: "The best way to use office hours is to do the readings and assignments in advance so that when you go to the office hours, you have specific questions."

Kaplan highlights a pervasive meme among faculty: students asking questions that are clearly answered in the syllabus. He notes that while professors are happy to answer, "If someone got a PhD in chemistry they love talking about chemistry. They didn't do it because they love talking about when the midterm is scheduled." This is a powerful reminder that professors are scholars first and administrators second. By asking substantive questions about the material, students signal that they are serious learners, which is the primary way to build the relationship necessary for future letters of recommendation.

He suggests a tactical move for students: list office hours on their personal calendar. "If you list their office hours on your personal calendar... most of the time you won't go. But you'll see those office hours and you'll remember they exist." This simple habit ensures that when a question arises, the student remembers the resource is available. The payoff, he promises, is tangible: "And then you'll get that question right on the exam. And then you'll get an A in the course."

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's argument is strongest when he connects the micro-behaviors of email etiquette to the macro-goals of professional success, making the rules feel less like arbitrary hurdles and more like essential training. His biggest vulnerability is the assumption that all professors respond to the same cues, though his advice to "match" the professor's tone mitigates this risk. For any student aiming to navigate college not just as a student but as a future professional, this piece is an indispensable roadmap to the hidden curriculum of academic interaction.
