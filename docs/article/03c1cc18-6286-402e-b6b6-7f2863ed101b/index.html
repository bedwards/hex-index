G. Elliott Morris has done something rare in the polling world: he turned a methodological exercise into a mirror for the public's deepest anxieties about the future. By crowdsourcing over 200 questions for a 2026 survey, the author reveals that voters are no longer just asking who will win, but whether the system itself is still functioning. The resulting list is less a political forecast and more a diagnostic tool for a nation grappling with artificial intelligence, energy grid fragility, and the very nature of democratic trust.

The Infrastructure of Anxiety

The most striking section of the proposed survey moves beyond traditional partisan bickering to address the physical realities of the digital age. Morris highlights a specific question regarding the energy demands of technology: "If a candidate proposed to require large technology companies to pay for energy grid upgrades needed to power their data centers, would that make you more or less likely to vote for them?" This framing is brilliant because it forces a direct line of sight between the abstract concept of AI and the tangible cost of electricity on a household bill.

Morris notes that the questions were compiled for transparency and fun, yet the underlying data points to a serious policy rift. The survey asks, "Who do you think should primarily pay for the additional infrastructure required to power large data centers?" offering options ranging from the technology companies themselves to the federal government. This shifts the debate from ideological purity to fiscal responsibility. Critics might argue that poll questions are too abstract to predict real-world voting behavior, but the specificity here—linking data centers to grid upgrades—suggests voters are ready to hold tech giants accountable for their physical footprint.

The Erosion of Trust

Perhaps the most unsettling portion of the list is the direct inquiry into the character and conduct of the current executive branch. Morris includes questions that bypass policy specifics to probe the psychological state of the electorate regarding leadership. One question asks respondents to rate the president's "mental sharpness," while another probes whether the administration has "misused the powers of the presidency." These are not standard approval ratings; they are existential checks on the stability of the office.

The author also includes a blunt inquiry into foreign policy aggression: "Would you approve or disapprove if President Trump ordered military action against Venezuela?" and "Do you support or oppose using the U.S. military to forcibly remove the president of Venezuela from power?" By placing these alongside questions about ethical conduct, Morris captures a public that is deeply skeptical of executive overreach. The survey asks, "How concerned are you, if at all, about ethical conduct in the Trump administration?" This suggests that for many, the integrity of the administration is a primary metric, not a secondary concern.

A counterargument worth considering is that polling on hypothetical scenarios, such as military action against a specific foreign leader, often yields volatile results that don't translate to actual policy support. However, the inclusion of these questions signals that the public is acutely aware of the stakes involved in potential conflicts, treating them as immediate threats rather than distant geopolitical maneuvers.

The Human Cost of Policy

Beyond the machinery of government, the survey list delves into the personal toll of economic and social shifts. Morris ensures that the human element is not lost in the data, asking, "Since January 20, 2025, would you say your mental health has been: Much better... Much worse?" This question acknowledges that political decisions have a direct impact on the psychological well-being of citizens. It reframes the election not as a contest of ideas, but as a determinant of daily life quality.

The list also tackles the looming threat of automation with surprising nuance. It asks, "How concerned are you, if at all, that artificial intelligence could eventually replace your job or jobs like yours?" and "Do you think advances in artificial intelligence will help or hurt economic opportunities for middle-class Americans over the next decade?" These questions move past the hype of AI to address the fundamental fear of obsolescence. Morris's inclusion of a question on Universal Basic Income (UBI) as a potential remedy shows that the electorate is already looking for structural solutions to technological displacement.

The Bottom Line

G. Elliott Morris's curated list of questions reveals a public that is less interested in partisan theater and more focused on the structural integrity of their lives and government. The strongest part of this approach is its refusal to shy away from uncomfortable truths, from the mental sharpness of leadership to the potential for military intervention. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the sheer volume of hypotheticals; while they illuminate fears, they may struggle to predict the chaotic reality of a 2026 election. Readers should watch for how these anxieties crystallize into actual voting patterns as the midterms approach.

We (Elliott + Verasight, my survey partner!) just went into the field with our January poll, and part of the process for that survey was compiling all the questions y’all sent in so we could pick a few to run. For transparency (and fun!), I figured I’d share the complete list of your questions below. I have done a very rough edit of the question text and response options for clarity. Not all of these questions will be used this year, and most are not in the final form we would use in a poll; I’m just sharing them for fun.
