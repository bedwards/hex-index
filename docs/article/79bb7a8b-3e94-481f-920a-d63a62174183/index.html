Are Boomers The Most Selfish Generation In History?

Economics Explained · Economics Explained ·May 18, 2025 · 16 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a landscape dominated by generational finger-pointing, Economics Explained makes a startling pivot: instead of blaming Baby Boomers for the struggles of Gen Z, the channel argues that the real culprit is a broken economic system that punishes workers regardless of age. This isn't another rant about selfish elders; it's a data-driven dismantling of the very concept of "generations" as a useful economic metric, suggesting that the narrative of intergenerational war is a dangerous distraction from the true crisis of inequality.

The Myth of the Generational Divide

The author immediately challenges the foundation of the current debate, noting that most generational labels are statistically meaningless. "It's getting real close to astrology," Economics Explained writes, pointing out that the arbitrary date ranges used to define Millennials or Gen Z ignore the vastly different market conditions individuals face even within the same cohort. This is a crucial distinction. By treating a 20-year span as a monolith, we obscure the fact that a Millennial born in 1981 faced a very different economic reality than one born in 1996.

The commentary argues that these labels are not just imprecise; they are actively harmful because they distract from structural issues. "These groupings... are unfortunately a really entertaining distraction from larger problems," the author asserts. This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from a specific group of people to a systemic failure. However, critics might note that while the labels are imperfect, they do capture shared cultural and economic shocks—like the 2008 financial crisis or the pandemic—that genuinely differentiate cohorts in ways that pure birth-year data cannot.

"Beefing between the whippersnappers and the fuddy-duddies is very human, but it's not good economics. This is an inequality problem, not a generational problem."

The Paradox of Wealth and Well-being

Perhaps the most provocative claim in the piece is the assertion that younger generations will technically be the wealthiest in history, even as they feel poorer. Economics Explained explains that this is due to the accumulation of assets over time and the concentration of wealth at the top. "Millennials can be the richest generation in history and also be struggling," the author notes, highlighting how a small group of ultra-wealthy individuals drags up the average while the majority fights over scraps. This paradox is vital for understanding why GDP growth doesn't translate to personal security.

The piece further complicates the narrative by distinguishing between "luxuries" and "essentials." While technology has made travel, entertainment, and information cheaper and more accessible, the cost of fundamental stability has skyrocketed. "Luxuries may have become more accessible, but essentials haven't," Economics Explained writes. This distinction is the core of the argument: we are comparing the Boomer experience of high-cost essentials with low-cost luxuries against the Gen Z experience of low-cost luxuries with impossible-to-afford essentials. The author suggests that blaming Boomers for this shift ignores the fact that Boomers also benefited from a time when the cost of living was lower relative to wages.

Critics might argue that dismissing the "wealth" of younger generations as merely statistical ignores the psychological toll of debt and the lack of autonomy. Having a high net worth on paper means little if you are drowning in student loans and cannot afford a down payment on a home. The author acknowledges this tension but maintains that the root cause is the financialization of the economy, not the age of the population.

The Mechanics of Inequality: Debt and Demographics

The analysis deepens when examining the mechanisms that have accelerated inequality: debt and demographic shifts. The author points out that the banking system has fundamentally changed, moving from relationship-based lending to algorithmic, high-volume consumer debt. "Back then, the role of a bank manager was quite prestigious because they had a lot of discretion about who got financing and who didn't," Economics Explained writes. Today, that discretion has been replaced by easy credit, allowing people to "dig their own financial graves" with consumer debt that offers no corresponding asset.

Furthermore, the piece highlights a demographic twist: declining birth rates mean that inheritances are becoming more concentrated. "Statistically, this means that wealthy parents are on average leaving their fortunes to a smaller group of their children," the author explains. This creates a feedback loop where wealth gravitates to the already wealthy, leaving the rest of the generation with less. This is a sophisticated point that moves beyond the usual "housing is too expensive" talking points to address the mathematical inevitability of wealth concentration in a low-fertility, high-debt environment.

"Wealth naturally gravitates to more wealth. Debt and self-concentrating populations have only really accelerated a trend that naturally happens over time."

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its refusal to let the audience off the hook with a simple villain; by reframing the issue as a systemic failure of asset ownership and debt structures, Economics Explained provides a more durable framework for understanding modern economic anxiety. Its biggest vulnerability, however, is the potential to minimize the very real, lived experience of younger generations who feel that their "wealth" is a statistical fiction while their daily struggle is concrete. The reader should watch for how policymakers respond to this distinction between aggregate wealth and individual security, as that will determine whether the "intergenerational bargain" can ever be repaired.
