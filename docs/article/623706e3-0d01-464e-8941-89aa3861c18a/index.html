Real Lawyer Reacts to Law & Order

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Nov 22, 2023 · 21 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just dissect a TV episode; he exposes the terrifying gap between Hollywood drama and the rigid, often absurd, mechanics of actual criminal law. In this collaboration with Dr. Mike, Stone reveals that the most dramatic courtroom moments in Law & Order are frequently the least legally sound, turning a murder mystery into a masterclass on why "obvious" guilt often fails in a real courtroom.

The Anatomy of a Legal Mistake

Stone begins by dismantling the show's opening premise: a doctor who kills a patient while drunk. He notes that while the show jumps straight to criminal charges, the legal reality is far murkier. "Your daughter is dead and he likely gave that medication under the influence of alcohol so it's time to prosecute," Stone observes, before immediately pivoting to the specific mental states required for conviction. He explains that the show correctly identifies the difference between criminal negligence and manslaughter, but fails to appreciate how difficult it is to prove the defendant's state of mind when they are intoxicated.

The commentary highlights a crucial legal nuance: intoxication is rarely a defense for the act itself, but it complicates the proof of intent. Stone writes, "Failing to perceive a substantial and unjustified risk that might cause suzan Morton death criminally negligent homicide... consciously disregarding that substantial and unjustified risk man slaughter too." He argues that the show's writers nailed the terminology but glossed over the evidentiary nightmare of proving a doctor "consciously disregarded" a risk while their brain was impaired by alcohol. This distinction is vital because, as Stone points out, a jury might struggle to differentiate between a medical error and a criminal act when the defendant's judgment was chemically altered.

"There is no law that says you can't practice while drunk probably not it would be a more generalized rule like Reckless homicide."

The Theater of Cross-Examination

The piece shifts to the courtroom tactics, where Stone finds the show's portrayal of cross-examination particularly grating. He critiques the defense attorney's inability to control the witness, noting that real lawyers would never allow a witness to ramble or offer non-responsive answers. "You don't have to ask every doctor that worked with him exactly do you know my client went to Harvard," Stone quips, mocking the irrelevant character attacks that plague the episode. He argues that the show prioritizes dramatic tension over procedural accuracy, allowing witnesses to evade direct questions without consequence.

Stone's most biting critique targets the defense's strategy of attacking the witness's credibility rather than the facts of the case. "That is one of the least effective cross-examinations," he states, pointing out that the attorney fails to challenge the medical facts and instead focuses on the doctor's background. This approach, Stone suggests, is a fatal error in a real trial where the goal is to create reasonable doubt about the specific actions taken, not the doctor's alma mater. The show's writers seem to believe that a dramatic objection is more important than a logical argument, a mistake that would get a real lawyer sanctioned.

Critics might note that Law & Order is designed for entertainment, not legal education, and that strict adherence to procedure would make for a boring hour of television. However, Stone's point remains that the show's inaccuracies can mislead the public about how justice is actually administered.

The Absurdity of Expert Testimony

As the episode progresses to expert testimony, Stone finds the dialogue increasingly ridiculous. The defense calls a doctor to testify about the defendant's sobriety, leading to a hypothetical scenario that Stone deems legally nonsensical. "Objection my aunt Minnie my aunt Minnie objection Aunt Minnie," Stone mocks, highlighting the absurdity of the defense's attempt to introduce irrelevant personal anecdotes. He explains that expert witnesses are allowed to answer hypothetical questions, but only if they are grounded in the facts of the case and the expert's specific knowledge.

The commentary underscores the danger of allowing experts to speculate without a factual basis. Stone writes, "Obviously that's impossible to say you'd have to be that 55-year-old man to know." He argues that the show's writers use these hypotheticals to create drama, but in a real court, such testimony would be stricken from the record. The defense's failure to establish a proper foundation for their expert's opinion is a glaring error that Stone uses to illustrate the importance of procedural rules in maintaining the integrity of the trial.

"If you're going to do nonsense like that to Pander to the jury you got to say you know objection relevance you have to have some grounds for the objection you can't just say objection my aunt Minnie."

The Final Verdict on Fiction

The piece concludes with Stone's analysis of the defendant's own testimony, where the doctor takes the stand to defend his actions. Stone notes that the defendant's inability to remember how many drinks he had is a devastating piece of evidence, yet the show treats it as a minor plot point. "I lost count of 49," Stone recounts, emphasizing how this admission would be a goldmine for a real prosecutor. He argues that the show's failure to capitalize on this contradiction undermines the entire narrative arc, leaving the audience with a sense of unresolved tension rather than a logical conclusion.

Stone's final observation is that the show's reliance on dramatic tropes often comes at the expense of legal realism. "This is a a a potentially very dangerous cross-examination because we've now established has been drinking a lot during lunch," he notes, pointing out the irony that the defendant's own testimony is used to convict him in the show's logic, but the legal system would likely find the evidence inadmissible due to the manner in which it was obtained.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's commentary is a sharp, necessary correction to the public's perception of criminal law, proving that the most dramatic moments in Law & Order are often the least credible. While the show succeeds as entertainment, Stone's analysis reveals that its legal inaccuracies could have real-world consequences for how we understand justice. The strongest part of his argument is the detailed breakdown of cross-examination tactics, which exposes the gap between TV drama and courtroom reality; his biggest vulnerability is perhaps a slight overemphasis on the show's failures rather than its successes in raising public awareness of legal issues.
