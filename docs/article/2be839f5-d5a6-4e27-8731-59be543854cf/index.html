LG Chem's Plan To Stay Solvent

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Sep 30, 2021 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Asianometry cuts through the noise of the electric vehicle hype cycle to reveal a critical truth: while CATL dominates China, the global battle for battery supremacy is being fought on a different terrain by LG Energy Solution. The author's most distinctive claim is that LG's survival and profitability hinge not just on chemistry, but on a decades-long, loss-leading strategy of geographic decentralization that defies the centralized manufacturing models of the semiconductor industry.

The Long March from Losses to Profit

The narrative begins by dismantling the myth of instant success. Asianometry writes, "for 20 years the company's battery business gained infamy for being an endlessly loss-making enterprise." This context is vital; it reframes LG's current profitability not as a lucky break, but as the result of a deliberate, painful long-term bet. The author details how LG Chem, originally a conglomerate making everything from toothbrushes to petrochemicals, recognized early that batteries were simply applied chemistry. They leveraged this vertical integration to build a supply chain capable of rapid adoption of new chemistries.

The pivot point was 2009, when General Motors selected LG for the Chevrolet Volt. Asianometry notes, "a huge breakthrough since then lithium-ion battery costs have fallen very quickly." This timing was serendipitous but also strategic. While competitors clung to nickel metal hydride batteries for hybrids, LG doubled down on lithium-ion, betting on higher energy density despite early concerns about cost and safety. The author effectively argues that this early commitment allowed LG to ride the wave of falling costs as the industry matured.

"Batteries are basically chemistry... LG Chem saw an opportunity to leverage their chemical expertise to gain share in the battery space."

Critics might argue that this vertical integration is a double-edged sword, locking the company into specific chemical pathways that could become obsolete if solid-state batteries or other technologies leapfrog current lithium-ion designs. However, the author's emphasis on LG's R&D spending suggests a company prepared to pivot its chemical expertise rather than its entire manufacturing infrastructure.

The Geography of Batteries

Perhaps the most insightful section of the piece concerns the logistics of battery manufacturing. Asianometry makes a compelling distinction between electronics and automotive supply chains. "With laptop and cell phone components... they are small enough that you can manufacture them at home... before shipping them abroad," the author explains. "Such a thing is not logistically or economically feasible for electric vehicles because their batteries are so heavy."

This observation explains why LG had to build factories in Michigan, Poland, and China, rather than relying on a single hub in South Korea. The author points out that car manufacturers demand local production to avoid shipping heavy, expensive batteries across oceans. This geographic dispersion is a massive capital burden but a necessary one for market access. The text highlights the 2010 investment in a Michigan factory serving GM, followed by expansions in Poland and China, illustrating a strategy of "following the customer" rather than optimizing for lowest-cost labor.

The Battle of Cell Formats

The commentary then dives into the technical nuances of battery cell shapes, a section that demystifies the industry jargon for the layperson. Asianometry breaks down the three main types: cylindrical, prismatic, and pouch. The author notes that while Tesla and many Chinese firms use standardized cylindrical cells, traditional automakers prefer prismatic cells because they are custom-fit to the vehicle's chassis.

However, the author identifies LG's true ace in the hole: the pouch cell. "Because they are flat thin and light you can stuff them to the max into the battery pack," Asianometry writes. This design offers superior energy density and a unique safety feature where the cell swells rather than explodes. The piece details LG's proprietary "stack and fold" technology and their "safety reinforced separator" (SRS), which uses a ceramic coating to prevent fires. These innovations are presented as the technical moat protecting LG's market share against competitors like Samsung SDI and SK Innovation.

"The separator if you recall separates the anode from the cathode... in LG's SRS a ceramic coating is applied that acts to provide superior abuse tolerance."

A counterargument worth considering is the recent shift by Volkswagen away from pouch cells toward prismatic designs, a move Asianometry acknowledges as a "big deal." This suggests that even LG's proprietary advantages are not immune to the shifting preferences of massive customers who may prioritize manufacturing simplicity over energy density. The author admits that competitors like CATL are catching up with "cell-to-pack" technology that skips the module stage entirely, threatening LG's lead.

The Financial Reality and the IPO

The final section addresses the bottom line, literally. After years of losses, LG Energy Solution turned profitable in 2020. Asianometry cites record revenues and an operating profit margin of eight percent, noting that the company is now a "$15 billion run rate business." The author frames the upcoming initial public offering (IPO) as the ultimate test of this strategy. With a valuation target of roughly $90 billion, the question is whether investors will reward a company that is profitable but faces intense competition and massive capital requirements.

The author leaves the valuation debate open, stating, "whether an auto supplier making just about a billion dollars annualize profit... is worth $90 billion is up for debate." This honesty is refreshing; it acknowledges the speculative nature of the EV sector while validating the company's operational success. The piece concludes that LG's focus on profit generation is likely a strategic move to appease institutional investors before going public, rather than a sign of complacency.

"Institutional investors will want to know if this business can make money... it does demonstrate the company's value and outsized importance in an extremely dynamic and world-changing future industry."

Bottom Line

Asianometry's strongest argument is the connection between LG's chemical heritage and its successful geographic expansion, proving that battery dominance requires physical presence, not just intellectual property. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that pouch cells will remain the preferred format for major automakers, despite recent signals from Volkswagen suggesting a shift toward prismatic alternatives. Readers should watch closely whether LG can defend its technological moat as competitors like CATL close the gap on cost and efficiency.

"The long march eventually bore fruit... LG Energy Solution has an energetic future ahead of it."

The author successfully argues that LG's path to solvency was not a straight line but a calculated, decades-long gamble on chemistry and logistics that is only now paying off in the black.
