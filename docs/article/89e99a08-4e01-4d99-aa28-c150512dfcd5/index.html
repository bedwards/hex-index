Civil Service: A History!

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Mar 31, 2026 · 28 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider and Kevin Hawickhorst dismantle the most enduring myth in American administrative history: that the Pendleton Act of 1883 single-handedly birthed a competent civil service. Instead, they argue that real capability emerged not from a single law, but from "subject-matter states" where agencies organized around specific vocations like entomology or engineering, creating a culture of expertise that politics could not easily penetrate. This is a vital correction for a moment when the federal government is under siege, suggesting that the path to rebuilding state capacity lies not in dismantling the bureaucracy, but in returning to a model where technical mastery commands respect.

The Pendleton Myth and the Real Inflection Point

The conversation begins by challenging the standard textbook narrative. "The history of the U.S. civil service is defined by the people who were hired to do jobs for the government, whether they did well or poorly, and whether they had training," Hawickhorst asserts. The authors contend that focusing on the Pendleton Act misses the forest for the trees; the Act was a necessary legal framework, but it was slow to implement and covered only a fraction of federal jobs for decades. The true story of competence is found in the agencies that managed to rise above the "patronage morass" of the Jacksonian era, where tens of thousands of postal workers were fired after every election.

Schneider and Hawickhorst point to the late 1870s and 1880s as the actual start of the upswing, driven by agencies that adopted military-style professionalism long before the law caught up. They highlight the U.S. Public Health Service, which restructured itself into a "paramilitary corps of surgeons" with ranks and uniforms, and the Department of Agriculture's bureaus. "The first agency where professionalization was a really big story was the U.S. Public Health Service," Hawickhorst notes, explaining how these entities recruited from medical schools and technical societies rather than political machines. This framing is compelling because it shifts the metric of success from legal statutes to organizational culture.

Critics might argue that ignoring the Pendleton Act downplays the role of legal protections in preventing the worst abuses of patronage, but the authors' data-driven approach suggests that culture preceded legislation. The real breakthrough was creating jobs so specialized that the average political hack simply could not do them.

"If you're a Bureau of Entomology filled with hard-charging experts going around putting a stop to outbreaks of weevils, that's clearly impressive."

The Power of the Subject-Matter State

The core of the argument rests on the concept of the "subject-matter state." In the early 20th century, agencies like the Bureau of Entomology and the Bureau of Soils did not just hire experts; they organized the entire government around a single vocational community. "They combined every function of government, all related to a single subject, and were then able to draw on technical vocations," Hawickhorst explains. This structure allowed them to pitch careers that were intellectually irresistible: "You're going to see every corner of it in your career — from research to enforcement to helping people on the ground."

This approach created a self-reinforcing cycle of competence. By partnering with state land-grant colleges and regulators, these agencies embedded their expertise into the fabric of American society. The result was a bureaucracy that European visitors in the 1900s considered "possibly the best-run bureaucracy on the planet." The authors draw a sharp contrast between the generic "I could do that" attitude toward postal work and the awe inspired by PhD-level entomologists. "That's basically the attitude that lets some agencies rise above the morass of patronage," Hawickhorst says.

The historical parallel to the Quartermaster Bureau under General Montgomery C. Meigs strengthens this point. Just as Meigs's logistical mastery during the Civil War proved that a professionalized, long-tenured bureaucracy could outperform a political one, these civilian agencies showed that technical depth could survive political volatility. The lesson is clear: competence is not a byproduct of tenure or exams, but of mission alignment and deep subject knowledge.

What Competence Actually Delivered

The payoff of this system was tangible economic and social progress. The authors detail how the USDA's investment in research and outreach transformed American agriculture, moving it away from "laissez-faire rugged individualism" toward a data-driven model. "The growth of productivity for American farmers was not quite the laissez-faire rugged individualism we remember," Hawickhorst writes, noting that two-thirds of top appointees at the USDA in 1910 held graduate degrees. This level of expertise drove infrastructure development as well, with the Bureau of Public Roads setting technical standards that eventually evolved into the Interstate system.

The argument here is particularly potent because it connects bureaucratic quality to national prosperity. "It wasn't just 'they could do the thing' — it was 'they can do the thing, and everyone knows they can do the thing because they are doing the thing throughout the U.S.'" The authors suggest that the hollowing out of these mission-driven agencies in the mid-century, replaced by process management and org charts, was a strategic error.

A counterargument worth considering is that this "subject-matter state" model could lead to regulatory capture or insularity, where agencies become too cozy with the industries they regulate. However, the authors imply that the broad outreach to state universities and the public served as a check against this, keeping the agencies accountable to a wider constituency of experts and citizens.

"The history of civil service law is not the history of the civil service."

Bottom Line

Schneider and Hawickhorst offer a necessary corrective to the nostalgia for a golden age of bureaucracy, arguing that the peak of American state capacity was built on vocational excellence, not just legislative reform. The strongest part of their argument is the evidence that agencies organized around specific technical missions could command respect and deliver results even in a patronage-heavy political environment. The biggest vulnerability is whether this model can be replicated in an era of hyper-polarization, where the consensus on "technical truth" is increasingly fractured. For anyone looking to rebuild government capacity, the lesson is not to pass more laws, but to rebuild the subject-matter state.

We should start the clock at the major inflection points of the federal bureaucracy — where agencies became competent and managed to set up recruitment pipelines of civil servants who could actually do the job and command respect across the country.
