How to keep your house clean

The Walrus · ·Jan 8, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Courtney Shea of The Walrus dismantles the modern obsession with domestic perfection, arguing that our relentless pursuit of a spotless home is not a sign of virtue, but a symptom of misplaced anxiety in an era of algorithmic pressure. This piece is notable because it reframes cleaning not as a chore to be optimized, but as a psychological coping mechanism that often fails to address the actual sources of our stress.

The Psychology of the Spotless Home

Shea begins by recounting a moment of visceral discomfort when a friend arrived early, exposing her home's "flotsam and jetsam of everyday existence." She captures the specific modern malaise: "It wasn't. Messy, that is. I just hadn't had time to do a pre-company spit polish." The author argues that this reaction is not about hygiene, but about a deep-seated fear that our lives are diminished by imperfection. The Walrus writes, "You live here —as in, life is precious and short and rare, and not one single person ever said on their deathbed, 'I wish I had spent more time with my Swiffer.'" This stark reminder serves as a powerful counter-narrative to the curated lives presented on social media.

The commentary effectively traces how the definition of a "good home" has shifted from functional to aesthetic. Shea notes that while past media allowed for imperfection, today's aspirational households are "sterile, Scandi design–influenced spaces." The piece highlights how even resistance movements, like the "messy girls aesthetic," have been co-opted by the same logic, turning chaos into a curated style. As The Walrus puts it, "Now even mess is supposed to be pretty." This observation is crucial; it suggests that the pressure to perform domesticity is so pervasive that even rejecting it requires a performance.

Tidying up is Sisyphean. You can either decide to lighten your boulder or get crushed by it.

Shea draws a parallel to the myth of Sisyphus, a connection deepened by the article's companion deep dives on the subject. Just as Sisyphus was condemned to roll a boulder up a hill only for it to roll back down, the modern housekeeper faces a task that is inherently endless. The author argues that the solution is not to work harder, but to redefine the goal. This framing is effective because it moves the conversation from productivity to mental health, suggesting that the "clean enough" standard is a personal choice rather than a societal mandate.

The Gendered Weight of Clutter

The piece takes a sharper turn when examining the gendered dimensions of mess. Shea cites a 2010 University of California, Los Angeles study which found that while men often view scattered items as neutral, women tend to experience disorder as a "physical manifestation of personal failings." The Walrus writes, "For women, living in a cluttered environment was associated with chronic stress." This evidence is compelling because it contextualizes the "stress-cleaning" behavior not as a personal quirk, but as a systemic response to cultural conditioning.

Shea references Jennifer McCartney's 2016 book, The Joy of Leaving Your Sht All Over the Place*, to bolster the argument that the link between mess and moral failure is a relatively recent construct, cemented by figures like Marie Kondo. The author notes that "the KonMari Method was an important milestone—in cementing the relationship between mess and moral failure." However, the commentary also acknowledges that this dynamic is deeply rooted in history, noting that "for homemakers (mostly women), the inclination to gauge self-worth with a feather duster goes back a lot further." This historical grounding prevents the argument from feeling like a fleeting trend critique.

Critics might note that focusing on individual psychological reframing ignores the structural lack of support for caregivers, such as affordable childcare or domestic labor rights, which often leave women with no choice but to manage the entire household alone. While the article offers a therapeutic approach, it does not fully address the material conditions that make the "MIA" (Most Important Areas) method a necessity rather than a choice for many.

Redefining Success

The solution proposed by Shea is practical and grounded in the work of cleaning expert Melissa Maker. The Walrus writes, "When we identify areas of focus, we are redefining what success looks like and giving ourselves permission not to tick every box." This "MIA" approach suggests that by selecting two or three key spaces, individuals can reclaim a sense of calm without succumbing to the "cleaning Olympics." The author's personal experiment with this method reveals a profound shift: "For months, every time I walked by my overflowing laundry bin, I would experience a twinge of anxiety, but now that wasn't happening."

The piece concludes by suggesting that the energy spent on cleaning could be redirected toward meaningful life tasks. Shea describes setting a timer to address anxieties, resulting in booking a driving test and handling financial planning. "Instead of yet another suction spree, I sat down and completed this very article," she writes. This pivot from the domestic to the existential is the article's strongest move, reinforcing the idea that a clean house is not a prerequisite for a meaningful life.

We don't need to clean more, we need to care less.

Bottom Line

Shea's argument is a necessary corrective to the toxic positivity of the domestic sphere, successfully identifying the psychological toll of the "perfect home" myth. Its greatest strength lies in connecting the personal act of cleaning to broader cultural and gendered pressures, though it could have explored the economic barriers that make maintaining even a "messy" home difficult for low-income families. Readers should watch for how this shift in mindset might influence the next wave of interior design trends, which may finally begin to prioritize lived-in comfort over sterile aesthetics.

By Courtney Shea

Last summer, a friend was visiting from Australia. Our plan was to meet at my place at noon, and then he showed up thirty minutes ahead of schedule. “You’re early. I need to tidy up, it’s a mess,” I lamented like some kind of 1950s fridge-magnet lady come to life. It wasn’t. Messy, that is. I just hadn’t had time to do a pre-company spit polish. And now here was my dear pal, standing in my kitchen, and I was spinning out over coffee rings. As we hugged, I surveyed the dishes in the sink from over his shoulder, the high chair caked with egg-something, the bowl on the butcher’s block overflowing with the flotsam and jetsam of everyday existence. I wasn’t embarrassed. It was more like a visceral discomfort, like this reunion I had been looking forward to was somehow diminished against an imperfect backdrop.

My friend was unfazed: “You live here, don’t you?” he said, shrug-smirking in a way that suggested maybe he hadn’t flown for almost twenty-four hours to spend time with quartz countertops. For him, it was a throwaway comment. My daughter barrelled in—a tornado of snot and sand and uncapped magic markers—and we got on with our day.

But for me, his words landed like a transgressive truth bomb, a much-needed reminder that chasing picture perfect in the domestic sphere is absurd. Like going to a barnyard and being disappointed by the smells. You live here—as in, this is a home, not an Architectural Digest photo shoot or house porn on social media. You live here—as in, meeting the needs of three people who eat and sleep and do laundry and leave toothpaste spray all over the bathroom vanity every day is a messy business. You live here—as in, life is precious and short and rare, and not one single person ever said on their deathbed, “I wish I had spent more time with my Swiffer.”

So why can’t I stop stress-cleaning?

It’s a serious question, if not one I ever thought I’d be asking myself. I am not a naturally tidy person. My brain tends toward the chaotic, and so does my T-shirt drawer.
