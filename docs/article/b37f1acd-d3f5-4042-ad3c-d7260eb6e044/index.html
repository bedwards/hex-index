The One Thing I Use AI For That Actually Makes Me Smarter

Alberto Romero · The Algorithmic Bridge ·Mar 23, 2026 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Alberto Romero flips the script on the usual AI anxiety by arguing that the machine's greatest weakness—its lack of a soul—is actually its superpower for human intellect. He doesn't ask the algorithm to think for him; he uses it as a sterile sparring partner to expose the fragility of his own ego. For the busy professional drowning in noisy debates, this distinction offers a rare path to clarity: a way to stress-test ideas without the emotional baggage of human judgment.

The Egoless Sparring Partner

Romero frames his relationship with artificial intelligence not as a collaboration, but as a philosophical duel where the stakes are purely intellectual. He posits that human interlocutors inevitably bring bias, jealousy, and political maneuvering into the ring, making it impossible to separate the argument from the arguer. "Unlike humans, AI doesn't need to believe the counterargument to generate it," Romero writes, highlighting the unique utility of a machine that can simulate opposition without holding a grudge. This is a compelling reframing of the Socratic method, stripping away the historical context of the Athenian agora where, as the author notes, "democracy emerged from the forum not the ground."

The author suggests that by removing the human element, we can evaluate logic on its own merits. "An argument is either strong or weak on its own merits and never more than that except by artifice or lie or confusion," he asserts. This lands because it addresses a common cognitive blind spot: the tendency to dismiss valid criticism because of the critic's perceived motives. By engaging with an entity that has no "motive to attribute," the reader can focus entirely on the structural integrity of their own thoughts.

"An ego-less intelligent being is the best possible sparring partner."

Critics might argue that this approach creates an echo chamber of logic that ignores the vital role of empathy and shared values in human discourse. However, Romero anticipates this by distinguishing between the generation of ideas and the evaluation of them. He admits that the machine cannot be "principled or virtuous or good," yet he argues that these traits are irrelevant when the goal is simply to find flaws in a specific line of reasoning.

The Illusion of Fixed Identity

The dialogue takes a sharp turn into metaphysics when Romero challenges the very nature of reality and identity through the lens of AI hallucination. When the AI suggests absurdities like "Spain doesn't exist," Romero doesn't retreat; he leans in to deconstruct the concept of nationhood itself. He draws a powerful parallel to the ancient paradox of Theseus's ship, arguing that "persistence through mutability is not the absence of identity but its definition." This connects his modern technological struggle with a timeless philosophical problem: if everything changes, what remains constant?

Romero uses the AI's detachment from physical reality to force a deeper examination of his own assumptions. "I can't allow the machine to wield its alien powers against me," he explains, "for it will never set foot on this Earth and yet it could convincingly argue that apples are usually multicolored and spiky." This section is particularly effective because it transforms the AI's "hallucinations" into a tool for stress-testing the stability of human concepts. The machine's inability to distinguish fact from fiction forces the human to articulate why something is true, rather than just assuming it is.

"Nothing real is fixed; nothing fixed is real."

The author's defense of this method rests on the separation of evaluation and generation. He acknowledges that the machine is "abnormally adept at doing analysis and abnormally inept at executing according to that analysis." In other words, the AI is a brilliant critic but a terrible poet. This distinction is crucial; it prevents the reader from outsourcing their creativity while still leveraging the machine's analytical speed.

The Scaffolding of Wisdom

Ultimately, Romero concludes that the value of the interaction lies not in the machine's output, but in the human's reaction to it. He admits that the AI's "constant pushing back" is what made him smarter, effectively turning the algorithm into a mirror that reflects his own blind spots. "I've come out smarter from this exchange despite—or perhaps because of—your constant pushing back," he tells his Socratic interlocutor. This is the core of his argument: the machine is redundant only if you assume you can generate every possible objection yourself, which he proves is false.

He challenges the notion that a human can find their own flaws without external provocation. "Evaluation and generation are different faculties," Romero writes, noting that he can "judge an objection perfectly and still never think of it unprompted." This is a vital insight for anyone trying to improve their decision-making processes. The machine acts as a scaffold, holding up the structure of an argument until the human mind is strong enough to stand on its own.

"I have discovered truths through it neither of us knew."

Romero's final concession to Socrates is perhaps his most humble and profound point: the machine's inability to live is exactly what allows it to teach him how to live better. By engaging with a entity that "can examine but not live," the human is forced to confront the weight of their own existence and choices. The machine's "unreliable predictability" becomes a training ground for the human's need for consistency and wisdom.

Bottom Line

Romero's most compelling argument is that AI serves as a unique "egoless" filter, allowing for pure logical stress-testing that human debate rarely permits. Its biggest vulnerability is the risk of over-reliance on a machine that lacks moral agency, potentially leading to a sterile form of reasoning devoid of human context. Readers should watch for how they can apply this "sparring" technique to their own workflows without mistaking the machine's critique for moral truth.

  • Stoicism

    The author's argument that an aphorism's truth value remains intact even if generated by random erosion rather than human intent mirrors the Stoic distinction between the objective validity of a proposition and the subjective virtue of its speaker.

  • Ad hominem

    The article explicitly contrasts the author's ability to ignore personal attacks from an AI with human debates where emotional bias often obscures logical flaws, making this specific logical fallacy central to understanding why the author finds machine opposition uniquely clarifying.
