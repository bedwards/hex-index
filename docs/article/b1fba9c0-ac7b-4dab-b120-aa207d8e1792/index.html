Are Taipei's Roads Still a "Living Hell"?

Jason Slaughter · Not Just Bikes ·Sep 8, 2024 · 30 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jason Slaughter returns to Taipei with a question that cuts through nostalgia: has the city truly shed its reputation as a "living hell" for pedestrians, or are the improvements merely cosmetic? Drawing on his unique perspective as a former semiconductor executive who raised a family there, Slaughter offers a rare before-and-after analysis that moves beyond tourist impressions to examine the gritty mechanics of urban safety. His conclusion is nuanced and urgent: while enforcement has finally caught up with the law, the city's fundamental design still prioritizes cars over people in ways that threaten to stall progress.

The Enforcement Revolution

Slaughter's most compelling observation centers on a cultural shift driven by strict legal consequences rather than moral persuasion. He recalls a time when traffic laws were suggestions, noting that in 2011, he witnessed a taxi driver honking at pedestrians for blocking his path on a sidewalk directly in front of a police station. Today, the dynamic has flipped. "The one thing I noticed immediately the first time I stepped outside was that cars would actually stop when I was crossing the street," Slaughter writes. This isn't just a change in driver politeness; it is the result of a new law mandating a three-meter buffer between vehicles and pedestrians, backed by real fines.

The author argues that this enforcement is the catalyst for everything else. "Most importantly those laws were actually being enforced," he notes, contrasting the current reality with the past where drivers aggressively pushed through intersections. This evidence holds up well; the visible change in driver behavior suggests that policy, when backed by consistent policing, can rapidly alter street culture. However, critics might note that relying solely on enforcement without addressing the root cause—excessive vehicle volume—creates a fragile safety net that could collapse if police resources are diverted.

"As long as they're driving slowly this may not be a problem, but the streets of Taipei are still a living hell because of its abysmal road safety record."

The Trap of Temporary Solutions

While the main arteries show improvement, Slaughter identifies a troubling trend in how the city handles narrower streets. He observes that Taipei has begun painting green stripes on the road to designate sidewalks, a move he finds deeply problematic. "These painted areas were supposed to be only temporary until they were replaced with physical sidewalks but as they say there's nothing more permanent than a temporary solution," he argues. The author contends that painting a line implicitly tells drivers they own the rest of the road, creating a psychological barrier that discourages pedestrians from walking freely.

Slaughter contrasts this with the Japanese model, where narrow lanes are treated as shared spaces with minimal car traffic. He suggests that the real solution for Taipei is not painted lines but "modal filters that prevent cars from passing but allow people walking and cycling to go through." This is a sophisticated urban planning argument that challenges the city's current trajectory. The piece effectively highlights the danger of low-cost, low-impact fixes that look like progress but fail to restructure the street hierarchy. A counterargument worth considering is that Taipei's density and the ubiquity of mopeds make the Dutch-style filtering model significantly harder to implement than in European cities.

The Bicycle Paradox

Perhaps the most frustrating section of Slaughter's coverage is his critique of Taipei's new bicycle infrastructure. Despite the city having some of the widest roads in the world, the new bike lanes are often squeezed onto existing sidewalks, creating conflict between cyclists and pedestrians. Slaughter points to Ren Yi Road as a prime example of wasted potential. "It's almost impossible to capture just how large this road is on camera," he writes, describing a multi-lane thoroughfare that still manages to place bike paths on the sidewalk rather than taking a lane from cars. "It's so frustrating that they couldn't even take just one of the many car lanes to build a proper bicycle path."

This critique lands hard because it exposes a political reluctance to inconvenience drivers. Slaughter notes that while the city is adding bike lanes, "they're only doing it where it doesn't inconvenience drivers." This suggests that the city's vision of safety is limited to protecting pedestrians from cars, but not necessarily prioritizing cyclists or removing cars from the equation entirely. The author's observation that Taipei is essentially "Amsterdam if all the bicycles were replaced with mopeds" adds a layer of cultural context that explains why the moped box at intersections exists, yet it also underscores the difficulty of transitioning to a car-free future.

The Bottom Line

Jason Slaughter's coverage succeeds because it refuses to accept the binary of "Taipei is dangerous" versus "Taipei is fixed." He acknowledges the genuine strides in enforcement and pedestrian yielding while exposing the structural flaws in sidewalk design and bicycle infrastructure. The strongest part of his argument is the distinction between behavioral change, which has happened, and design change, which has largely stalled. The biggest vulnerability remains the city's unwillingness to reclaim road space from the massive volume of motor vehicles, a hesitation that may ultimately limit how safe Taipei can ever truly become.

"The core of the argument is that enforcement has fixed the behavior, but the design still prioritizes the car."

Final Verdict

Slaughter's piece is a vital check on the narrative of rapid urban transformation in Asia. His evidence that drivers now yield to pedestrians is undeniable, yet his warning about painted sidewalks and sacrificed bike lanes serves as a crucial reminder that safety cannot be painted on top of a car-centric design. Readers should watch for whether Taipei's city planners eventually find the political courage to narrow roads and widen sidewalks, or if the "temporary" green stripes become the permanent reality.
