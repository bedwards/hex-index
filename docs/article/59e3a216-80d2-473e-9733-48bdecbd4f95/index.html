Opening Pokemon Packs — Q & A Livestream!

Dave Amos · City Beautiful ·Nov 28, 2023 · 49 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Amos, a city planning professor, does something almost unheard of in the niche of urban policy: he opens Pokémon card packs while answering serious questions about high-speed rail and zoning. This isn't a gimmick to distract from the work; it is a deliberate attempt to humanize the often dry, academic discourse surrounding how we build our cities. By weaving the hunt for a specific "urban" Pokémon—the Pidgey, which he dubs the "most urban Pokemon that exists"—into a live Q&A, Amos creates a unique bridge between pop culture nostalgia and rigorous planning theory.

The Urbanist's Mascot

Amos frames the entire livestream around a specific, quirky goal: collecting Pidgeys to represent the pigeon, a creature he argues is the closest real-world equivalent to the urban experience. "My unofficial mascot for the channel is actually a Pokémon... I determined to be the most urban Pokemon that exists," he explains, noting that it is "the closest to a pigeon." This choice is not random; it is a strategic nod to the reality that cities are defined by their density and the creatures that thrive within them. The argument here is subtle but effective: urban planning is not just about concrete and steel, but about the chaotic, living ecosystems that form within them.

Critics might argue that mixing card games with policy dilutes the seriousness of the subject matter. However, Amos uses the card opening as a rhythmic device, allowing him to pivot between the tension of a rare card pull and the tension of a policy debate. He admits the Pidgey hunt is a "nod to my kids," grounding his academic persona in the relatable reality of parenthood. This makes the content accessible without dumbing it down.

"I'm going to pick density on that one. Density does really seem to be the thing that you need to make transit happen."

The Chicken or the Egg of Transit

When the conversation shifts to the mechanics of city building, Amos tackles the perennial debate: does transit create density, or does density create transit? He takes a firm stance, arguing that "density does really seem to be the thing that you need to make transit happen." This aligns with a growing consensus among urban planners that high-frequency public transport is economically unviable without a critical mass of residents to support it. The evidence he brings is observational rather than statistical, relying on his experience in medium-sized cities like his current home, San Luis Obispo, California.

He illustrates this by describing his own city: "It's about 50,000 people. It's 5 miles across, which is a pretty easy bike ride." In this context, the argument holds up well; in smaller, walkable environments, the distance between destinations is short enough that active transport becomes a primary mode of travel. However, this perspective might overlook the complexities of sprawling metropolitan areas where density is fragmented, and transit is the only viable link between disconnected job centers. The focus on medium-sized cities provides a clear, optimistic view, but it may not fully address the challenges of retrofitting massive, car-dependent megalopolises.

The Reality of High-Speed Rail

The tone shifts from optimistic to cautious when Amos addresses the future of high-speed rail in the United States compared to China. He is blunt about the disparity: "China is way out in front and moving faster than ever, so I don't know." He expresses hope for California's project, noting that "Bright Line stuff is actually making me somewhat hopeful," but admits that the US is "moving so slowly." This candid assessment is refreshing in an era of often hyperbolic infrastructure promises. He acknowledges the financial realities, stating that cost is a "main concern" and that the Florida-to-Miami route currently costs more than a flight while taking twice as long.

"I wish that was the case. We're moving so slowly on high-speed rail, ironically."

Amos's commentary on the speed of US infrastructure development serves as a necessary reality check. While he remains hopeful, he does not shy away from the fact that political and logistical inertia is a massive barrier. This is a crucial distinction for busy readers who might be looking for quick fixes; Amos suggests that the path forward is slow, expensive, and fraught with challenges. The counterargument here is that technological innovation or new funding models could accelerate the timeline, but Amos's experience suggests that the bottleneck is often bureaucratic and political rather than purely technical.

Gaming the System

Beyond the cards and the rail, Amos reveals a deeper layer of his engagement with the field: his attempt to gamify urban planning. He discusses a card game he created, a "Top Trump style game" where players compete based on city attributes like density and elevation. "This is not actually a pitch to sell my card game at all," he jokes, admitting it is "somewhat overpriced." This segment highlights his belief that planning concepts should be accessible and engaging. He mentions that he is working on a new game but has been "pretty busy lately," a common struggle for academics trying to bridge the gap between theory and public engagement.

He also addresses the confusion between his channel and "City Planner Plays," another popular creator. "I am glad that that channel exists because the amount of people who ask if I would do City Skylines videos was too damn high for a long time," he says. This distinction is vital: Amos focuses on the real-world application of planning, while others focus on simulation. By clarifying this, he reinforces the value of his own work—analyzing the actual, messy reality of cities rather than the idealized versions found in video games.

Bottom Line

Dave Amos's livestream succeeds because it refuses to choose between being entertaining and being informative. By using the Pidgey hunt as a narrative thread, he keeps the audience engaged while delivering substantive insights on density, transit, and infrastructure. The strongest part of his argument is the insistence that density is the prerequisite for viable transit, a point that is often lost in debates about funding and technology. His biggest vulnerability is the reliance on medium-sized city examples, which may not fully translate to the complexities of the world's largest urban centers. For the busy reader, this piece offers a rare combination: a deep dive into urban theory that feels as casual as opening a pack of cards.

"I just want to be able to show them a video instead of having to develop a presentation myself and give it."

Amos's journey from a planner needing presentation materials to a professor creating a global community of urban enthusiasts demonstrates the power of accessible education. The Pidgey may be the mascot, but the real prize is the clarity he brings to the future of our cities.
