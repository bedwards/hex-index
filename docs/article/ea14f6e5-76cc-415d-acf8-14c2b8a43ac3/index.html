Funders Behind Far Right AI Rapper Exposed

Novara Media · Novara Media ·Mar 14, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Novara Media has uncovered a disturbing new frontier in political warfare: a shadowy AI rapper named Danny Bones, whose catchy, algorithmically generated tracks are spreading far-right rhetoric faster than any human campaigner could. The author's most startling claim isn't just that this exists, but that the music is deliberately designed to be "catchy" enough to embed itself in your head, regardless of how "dross" the lyrics might be. This isn't a theoretical future; it is happening now, funded by the same networks backing Britain First, and it is already influencing real-world elections.

The Anatomy of AI Slop

The investigation, conducted by Simon Charles and Harriet Williamson, peels back the layers of "The Node Project," a creative agency masquerading as an independent storytelling collective. Novara Media writes, "Every word sounds like it comes from somewhere real despite carrying the feeling that nothing really is." This observation cuts to the core of the deception. The agency produces content that feels authentic to the aggrieved voter while being entirely synthetic. The result is a character, Danny Bones, described as a "skin head regularly wrapped in the Union Jack or the St. George's flag" who "cosplays as a mass deportation unit officer."

The author argues that the effectiveness of this propaganda lies in its ability to bypass our skepticism. As Novara Media puts it, "It's not the music. It's not so obvious that the music is AI created. Um, like it sounds like rubbish, but then so much music does." The commentary highlights a critical vulnerability in our media consumption: we are conditioned to accept bad music as human error, making it harder to spot bad, AI-generated political messaging. The visuals, described as "hyperreal" and reminiscent of video games, further blur the line between reality and fabrication.

"The song This Is England... is catchy. Like it has got in my head this week when I've been working on this story."

The Political Machinery Behind the Mask

While The Node Project claims to be an "independent creative agency," the investigation reveals a direct financial link to Advance UK, a far-right political party backed by Elon Musk. This connection mirrors the trajectory of other right-wing movements that have leveraged digital platforms to bypass traditional gatekeepers, much like how Britain First utilized Facebook in the 2010s to amplify its message. Novara Media notes that the Node Project was "commissioned by Advance UK to make a video," which the party's leader, Ben Habib, praised for being so convincing that "you couldn't even tell that it was CGI."

The article details how these AI-generated interventions targeted the Gorton and Denton bi-election, where the Green Party's campaign in Urdu and Bengali was met with a barrage of AI content from Bones. The rapper's lyrics, a "list of every complaint you ever heard from your permanently agrieved boomer uncle," framed the election as a cultural threat. As Novara Media observes, "The left are getting used and don't even realize it. All this our community, their community. You lot got [expletive] all in common except a postcode." This strategy exploits social fractures with a speed and volume that human activists cannot match.

Critics might argue that labeling all AI political content as inherently dangerous ignores the potential for legitimate artistic expression or satire. However, the author's evidence suggests a coordinated effort to monetize division rather than engage in dialogue. The Node Project's denial of being "Islamophobic" rings hollow when their content explicitly targets minority communities and celebrates "mass deportation."

The Regulatory Vacuum

Perhaps the most alarming aspect of the coverage is the lack of accountability. When questioned, the Electoral Commission offered little more than a suggestion to "think critically," a platitude that feels woefully inadequate against an onslaught of algorithmically optimized disinformation. Novara Media points out the inconsistent responses from tech giants: "TikTok did take the Danny Bones stuff down. Um, I think YouTube added a like a label... Spotify said that humans had reviewed the song and it hadn't broken any rules."

This patchwork of responses creates a safe haven for bad actors. The author warns that as AI technology improves, the "boundaries between what is real and what is completely fake are kind of fraying anyway." The risk is not just the spread of false information, but the erosion of shared reality itself. As Novara Media concludes, "Having such an easy mechanism to disseminate a kind of divisive message is quite scary."

"The algorithm kind of rewards people for stirring things up. And so I think, you know, that's where I would look personally."

Bottom Line

Novara Media's investigation succeeds in exposing the industrial scale of modern disinformation, proving that the tools of hate have been upgraded for the AI age. The piece's greatest strength is its demonstration that the quality of the content is irrelevant; the only metric that matters is the emotional hook. The biggest vulnerability remains the regulatory gap, where platforms prioritize engagement over truth, leaving voters to navigate a landscape where reality is just another variable in the algorithm.
