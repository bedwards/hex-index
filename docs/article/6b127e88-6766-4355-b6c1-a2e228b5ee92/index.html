Two Fault Lines in the Global Economy: China's Hidden Fragility and Washington's Tariff Gambit

Multiple sources: Economics Explained Economics Explainedand 1 other
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

Two recent video essays, viewed side by side, sketch the contours of a global economic order under severe strain. Economics Explained turns its lens inward on Beijing, arguing that the long-prophesied Chinese century may be stalling before it arrives. Joeri Schasfoort of Money & Macro trains his attention on Washington, where the executive branch has unveiled a sweeping regime of so-called reciprocal tariffs. Together, the two analyses describe a world in which the two largest economies are each wrestling with structural problems they would rather not name.

Beijing's Quiet Unraveling

Economics Explained opens with a concession to conventional wisdom: For decades, many believed it was only a matter of time before China became the world's dominant superpower. That expectation, the narrator argues, has quietly collided with reality. China's economy is slowing, debt is piling up, and cracks are forming in its once unshakable financial system.

The essay's sharpest claim is not that China is collapsing but that its problems are camouflaged. China has its own problems that are just as, if not more serious than the USA's. If anything, it's just better at hiding them. Reported GDP figures, local-government financing vehicles, and a property sector propped up by rolling credit are all presented as evidence that the official numbers understate the fragility beneath.

The Chinese century, in this telling, is not cancelled — only postponed, and possibly hollowed out before it begins.

Washington's Tariff Shock

Schasfoort, by contrast, is preoccupied with a policy rupture emanating from the White House. The administration has rolled out what it brands reciprocal tariffs — duties calibrated, at least rhetorically, to mirror what trading partners impose on American goods. Schasfoort is blunt about their scale: These tariffs are unprecedented. Genuinely, I think they represent a watershed moment in the global economic system.

He frames the stated rationale in the executive branch's own terms — an effort to re-industrialize the US by tariffing the rest of the world into oblivion — while noting the alternative framing offered by the policy's defenders, that this is simply a leveling of the playing field. Either way, Schasfoort insists the consequences will ripple through financial markets, inflation expectations, growth forecasts, and the retaliatory calculus of every major capital.

Two Stories, One System

Read together, the two commentaries illuminate a single uncomfortable fact: the post-1980 bargain that bound the American consumer to the Chinese factory is fraying from both ends simultaneously. Economics Explained describes a producer whose growth model — debt-fueled investment, export dependence, suppressed household consumption — is running out of runway. Schasfoort describes an importer whose political class has concluded that the same arrangement hollowed out its industrial base and must be forcibly reversed.

Neither author quite says what the juxtaposition implies. If Beijing is weaker than its statistics suggest, the tariff shock lands on an economy less able to absorb it than the White House assumes. If Washington's gambit succeeds in re-shoring even a fraction of manufacturing, the external demand that has masked China's domestic imbalances disappears faster than Beijing's planners can pivot.

The risk is not that one side wins. It is that both sides discover, too late, how much of their apparent strength depended on the other.

What the Sources Leave Out

Both essays underplay the role of third parties. The European Union, Japan, South Korea, and the ASEAN bloc are not passive spectators; their responses — whether accommodation, retaliation, or quiet arbitrage through transshipment — will shape the final incidence of the tariffs far more than bilateral posturing suggests. Schasfoort gestures at retaliation but does not dwell on the possibility that Europe declines to follow Washington's lead, leaving American exporters isolated rather than vindicated.

Economics Explained, for its part, says little about the Chinese state's considerable tools for managing a slow-motion deleveraging: capital controls, state-directed lending, and a banking system that answers to the Party rather than to shareholders. Hidden problems can also be hidden losses, absorbed quietly over decades in ways a Western balance sheet cannot.

And neither essay reckons seriously with the climate and energy transition now reorganizing industrial policy everywhere. Tariffs on Chinese EVs, batteries, and solar panels are not merely trade instruments; they are bets about which economy will own the next generation of manufacturing. That contest may matter more than the headline duty rates.

Bottom Line

The two videos, taken together, describe a global economy in which the conventional narratives — inexorable Chinese ascent, stable American hegemony — have both quietly expired. Beijing is not the juggernaut its critics feared a decade ago, and the executive branch in Washington is no longer willing to pretend the old arrangement served American workers. What replaces it is unsettled, improvised, and unusually dangerous, because both sides are acting on assumptions about the other's strength that may already be out of date. Readers would do well to treat every confident forecast about the next five years — from Beijing, from Washington, or from YouTube — with the skepticism both of these essayists, in their better moments, recommend.
