Collaborative Capability Development

Various · Defense Tech and Acquisition ·Mar 31, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

This piece cuts through the noise of defense procurement by challenging a decades-old binary: that the government must choose between a single prime contractor or a chaotic patchwork of vendors. The author's most provocative claim is that a third path exists—a "Collaborative Integrator" model where the government orchestrates a consortium of competitors who police each other's performance in real time. This isn't just theoretical; the article points to the "Golden Dome" missile defense initiative as a live, high-stakes experiment proving that peer pressure can replace bureaucratic oversight.

The Third Path

The author begins by dismantling the traditional acquisition landscape. "Acquisition professionals traditionally only see two models for delivering a complex weapons system," they note, outlining the rigid Prime Integrator and the often-faltering Independent Integrator. The critique of the Prime model is sharp: it "stifles competition as subcontractors are usually locked-in," creating a monopoly on innovation within the supply chain. Conversely, the Independent Integrator model suffers from "low accountability," where third-party integrators often lack the technical depth to screen out mediocrity until it is too late.

The proposed solution is the Collaborative Integrator. "Rather than relying on a single prime, the government manages the effort by coordinating contributions across vendors, maintaining regular updates, enforcing a common data architecture and incentivizing teamwork." This approach mirrors the agile software development principles championed by figures like Trae Stephens, who has long argued that defense must adopt the speed and modularity of commercial tech. By breaking the monolithic contract into manageable pieces, the government can swap out underperformers without collapsing the entire program.

"They operate as a unit. They decide what they're going to build, when they're going to build it, how they're going to build it, and who the best athlete among them is to build it."

This quote from General Michael Guetlein, the program manager for Golden Dome, captures the radical shift in power dynamics. The author highlights how the consortium, including tech giants like Palantir and Anduril alongside traditional giants like Lockheed Martin, holds itself accountable through a "vote that individual off the island" mechanism. It is a startling admission that peer pressure can be more effective than government micromanagement.

Critics might note that this model places an immense burden on the government to possess the technical acumen to manage these interfaces, a skill set that has atrophied in many acquisition offices. The author acknowledges this risk, warning that "without a single prime contractor, the government must take a more active role in ensuring that all components work together."

Lessons from the Past

The article wisely grounds this new model in historical precedent, arguing that "this is not an entirely new model, just one not often employed in DoD/DoW." The author points to Project Maven, where the government orchestrated an ecosystem of vendors including AWS, Microsoft, and L3Harris to refine AI models iteratively. "Instead of a single monolithic defense contractor, Maven operated as a kind of modular consortium — lean, flexible, and fast," the author writes, noting its designation as a formal program of record.

The text also draws a parallel to the Space Development Agency's (SDA) tranche-based approach. While the SDA attempted a similar multi-vendor strategy for satellite networks, the author candidly admits that "they were not organized as well as they could have been which resulted in delayed fielding." This nuance is crucial; it suggests that the model works only when the governance structure is as rigorous as the technology.

"Competition doesn't end at contract award or only happen at key points. Rather vendors must consistently perform to remain part of the effort, which helps maintain high performance standards, encourage innovation and prevent complacency."

This continuous competition is the engine of the model. Unlike the Eurofighter program, which succeeded in performance but struggled with the "complex governance" of multi-nation workshare agreements, the Golden Dome model aims to streamline decision-making. The author argues that for rapidly evolving technologies like AI and cyber, "speed matters more than perfect upfront design," making this iterative, field-and-iterate strategy essential.

The Execution Gap

The final section of the article shifts to the practicalities of implementation, offering a checklist for success. The author emphasizes that "the government does not need to possess as deep of expertise since that is mitigated through the demonstration process." This is a bold assertion that challenges the traditional requirement for deep in-house technical mastery. Instead, the focus shifts to "clear performance metrics" and "flexible contracting approaches" that allow for vendor on-ramps and off-ramps.

However, the risks remain significant. "Diffused accountability" is a persistent threat; when issues arise across interdependent components, assigning responsibility becomes a nightmare. The author warns that "replacing vendors can improve performance but may also introduce transition delays, knowledge gaps and a storming phase." The success of this model hinges entirely on the quality of the government managers and their willingness to write adaptive contracts that break from standard acquisition templates.

"The government owns the technical baseline, but the group of nine partners are operating independently of each other and holding each other accountable through peer pressure, if you will, to perform."

This balance of government oversight and vendor autonomy is the delicate tightrope the author asks readers to watch. It requires a level of trust and agility that is rare in the defense industrial base.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its rejection of the false choice between a monolithic prime and a fragmented mess, offering a proven, albeit difficult, middle ground that leverages commercial speed for national security. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that the government can consistently find managers with the courage to execute such adaptive contracts and the technical savvy to manage a consortium without becoming a bottleneck. Readers should watch closely to see if the Golden Dome program can sustain this high-wire act through the 2028 operational deadline, as its success or failure will define the future of defense acquisition.

Excerpt from the original article on Defense Tech and Acquisition

Acquisition professionals traditionally only see two models for delivering a complex weapons system.

  1. Prime Integrator: Award to a prime who selects the key suppliers and with government oversight delivers an integrated solution.

  2. Independent Integrator: Award is made to multiple vendors with each providing a piece of the capability and the Government acts as integrator or hires a third-party as integrator to deliver a full solution.

There is a third path that can provide the government greater flexibility to maintain best of breed providers delivering the latest and greatest tech also while mitigating the inherent risk with the government acting as integrator or outsourcing that risk to a usually less capable company.

The third path can be called Collaborative Integrator. In this approach, the government awards contracts to multiple vendors, each responsible for a specific portion of a broader capability. Rather than relying on a single prime, the government manages the effort by coordinating contributions across vendors, maintaining regular updates, enforcing a common data architecture and incentivizing teamwork.

Golden Dome Example

This collaborative approach is now being actively used to build the foundational C2 layer for Golden Dome in what is being termed a “software development consortium.” The companies forming the consortium include Palantir, Anduril Industries, Aalyria Technologies, Scale AI and Swoop Technologies. It also includes traditional defense contractors Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman and RTX, although it is understood that they are operating in more of a support role and were added later.

The new consortium has already conducted a live demonstration of their capability, and according to the Golden Dome Direct Reporting Program Manager, Gen. Michael Guetlein, has proved it’s on target to deliver an operational capability by 2028.

The way Gen Guetlein has organized the different contractors and constructed the program governance is unique. As he described it:

“They operate as a unit. They decide what they’re going to build, when they’re going to build it, how they’re going to build it, and and who the best athlete among them is to build it. Then they hold themselves accountable on a weekly, biweekly basis. If at any point during that week, one of them did not carry their load, they can vote that individual off the island (adding that his chief engineer makes the final call).
