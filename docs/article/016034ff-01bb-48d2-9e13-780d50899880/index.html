Collaborative Capability Development

Various · Defense Tech and Acquisition ·Mar 31, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

This piece cuts through the fog of defense acquisition bureaucracy to propose a radical truth: the government doesn't need a single prime contractor to build complex weapons; it needs a self-policing consortium. The author's most distinctive claim is that peer pressure among vendors can be more effective than government oversight, a shift that turns the Department of Defense from a passive buyer into an active orchestrator of a dynamic ecosystem. For leaders watching the clock on modernization, this isn't just theory—it's the operational blueprint for the Golden Dome initiative, proving that the old models are no longer just slow, they are structurally incapable of keeping pace with AI and autonomous threats.

The Third Path

The author begins by dismantling the binary choice that has long paralyzed defense procurement: the Prime Integrator, where a single giant locks in subcontractors, or the Independent Integrator, where the government or a third party struggles to glue disparate parts together. "Acquisition professionals traditionally only see two models for delivering a complex weapons system," Various writes, "but there is a third path that can provide the government greater flexibility to maintain best of breed providers delivering the latest and greatest tech."

This third path, termed the "Collaborative Integrator," is not a minor tweak but a fundamental reimagining of incentives. The core of the argument is that by awarding contracts to multiple vendors for specific portions of a capability, the government can enforce a common data architecture while letting the vendors compete and cooperate simultaneously. This lands powerfully because it addresses the stagnation of the Prime Integrator model, where "subcontractors are usually locked-in... with little room for new entrants." By contrast, the Collaborative model ensures that "competition doesn't end at contract award," forcing vendors to constantly prove their worth or risk being replaced.

"They operate as a unit. They decide what they're going to build, when they're going to build it, how they're going to build it, and who the best athlete among them is to build it. Then they hold themselves accountable on a weekly, biweekly basis."

The author illustrates this with the Golden Dome project, where a mix of tech giants like Palantir and Anduril alongside traditional giants like Lockheed Martin are operating under a unique governance structure. The text notes that the group can "vote that individual off the island" if a member fails to carry their load, a mechanism that shifts the burden of quality control from the government to the consortium itself. This is a profound departure from the "Independent Integrator" model, where third-party integrators often lack the technical depth to screen out low performers until it is "too late in the game."

Historical Echoes and Modern Application

The piece wisely grounds this innovation in precedent, arguing that "this is not an entirely new model, just one not often employed in DoD/DoW." The author points to Project Maven, which successfully used a modular consortium approach to integrate data from Palantir, AWS, and Microsoft, proving that "instead of a single monolithic defense contractor, Maven operated as a kind of modular consortium — lean, flexible, and fast." This historical context is crucial; it suggests that the technology to execute this model has existed for years, but the institutional courage to adopt it has been lacking.

However, the author also offers a sobering counterpoint by examining the Space Development Agency (SDA). While SDA attempted a similar tranche-based approach, the text admits that because they "were not organized as well as they could have been," the program suffered delayed fielding. This nuance prevents the argument from becoming a utopian sales pitch. It acknowledges that while the model is powerful, it requires a level of government sophistication that is rare. "The government program office has a very deep understanding of the problem set," the author notes, warning that without this expertise, the coordination function can collapse under the weight of its own complexity.

"The government owns the technical baseline, but the group of nine partners are operating independently of each other and holding each other accountable through peer pressure, if you will, to perform. So far, it's working phenomenal."

The comparison to the Eurofighter program further deepens the analysis. While the multinational consortium achieved performance goals, the author admits it introduced "complex governance" and "schedule delays" due to workshare agreements. This serves as a critical warning: the Collaborative Integrator model works best when the government can enforce strict boundaries and rapid iteration, rather than when political workshare agreements dictate the pace. The text correctly identifies that this model is ideal for "rapidly evolving technologies such as AI, autonomy, software-defined systems, space, and cyber," where requirements are "not fully knowable upfront."

The Execution Trap

The author does not shy away from the risks, particularly the "Increased Government Integration Responsibility." Without a single prime to blame, the government must possess the "knowledge and courage to write adaptive contracts." This is where the argument faces its steepest challenge. The text admits that "not all acquisition structures and personnel are designed or prepared for frequent vendor changes." A counterargument worth considering is whether the federal workforce can truly scale to manage this level of dynamic coordination without becoming a bottleneck itself.

The piece outlines a clear path forward: breaking capabilities into manageable pieces, establishing a strong coordination function, and implementing regular evaluation cycles. "Use these cycles to inform decisions about continuing, expanding, or replacing vendors," the author advises. This emphasis on "continuous competition" is the model's lifeblood. It transforms the acquisition process from a one-time transaction into a living ecosystem where "performance is hard to specify but easy to measure using frequent tests."

"Speed matters more than perfect upfront design. This model is ideal for 'field early capability and iterate' style strategies."

Critics might note that this approach places an immense burden on the program manager to act as both referee and architect. If the government fails to define clear boundaries or if the "coordination function" becomes micromanagement, the system could fracture. Yet, the author remains convinced that the alternative—sticking with models that "stifle competition" and "lock-in" outdated technology—is a far greater risk in an era of rapid technological change.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its rejection of the false dichotomy between government control and private sector innovation, replacing it with a hybrid model where the government sets the rules and the market competes to solve the problem. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that the government possesses the institutional agility to manage a self-policing consortium without becoming a bottleneck. Readers should watch closely to see if the Golden Dome program can sustain this momentum beyond the initial demonstration, as the true test will be whether the bureaucracy can adapt its contracting and oversight mechanisms to match the speed of the technology it seeks to deploy.
