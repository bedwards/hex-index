Thanos, Fertility, and the Gift of Life

Various · Wayfare ·Mar 25, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In an era where climate anxiety and demographic collapse dominate the headlines, this piece from Wayfare delivers a jarring, counter-intuitive thesis: human beings are not a drain on the planet's resources, but the very engine of their creation. By weaving together the history of the Simon–Ehrlich wager with the theological concept of agency, the author challenges the Malthusian panic that has haunted thinkers from Thomas Robert Malthus to the Marvel villain Thanos, arguing instead that population growth correlates directly with increased abundance.

The Malthusian Ghost and the Thanos Calculus

The author begins by dismantling the most persistent fear of our time: that more people inevitably mean less for everyone. The text draws a striking parallel between historical economists and modern pop culture, noting that "Thanos frames his argument as a mathematical inevitability—implying that unchecked population growth leads inexorably to catastrophe." This is a powerful rhetorical move, grounding abstract economic theory in a recognizable cultural touchstone that millions of readers have already engaged with.

However, the piece quickly pivots to the historical record to refute this "simple calculus." The author reminds us that for centuries, the Reverend Thomas Robert Malthus and his followers believed scarcity was unavoidable. As the text notes, "The power of population is indefinitely greater than the power in the earth to produce subsistence for man." This Malthusian view was updated in 1968 by Paul Ehrlich, who declared in The Population Bomb that "the battle to feed all of humanity is over." The author effectively uses these dire predictions to set the stage for the data that follows, highlighting the sheer confidence with which these experts were wrong.

"If life is left unchecked, life will cease to exist. It needs correcting."

The Simon–Ehrlich Wager: A Decade of Proof

The core of the argument rests on the legendary bet between Julian Simon and Paul Ehrlich, a historical event that serves as the empirical backbone of the piece. The author details how Simon, an economist, challenged Ehrlich's doom-mongering by betting on the future prices of five nonrenewable metals. The stakes were high, and the timeline was set for a decade—a period that saw the global population surge by 19 percent.

The outcome is presented with dramatic flair: "In early October of 1990, Simon went out to his mailbox and found an envelope with no return address... a check for $576.07 signed by Paul Ehrlich's wife." The author uses this moment to underscore a critical insight: "The decade of the 1980s recorded the highest growth in human population... According to Malthus, Thanos, and Ehrlich, this wasn't supposed to happen." This is where the article shines, using a concrete historical fact to dismantle a theoretical fear. The data shows that as people multiplied, resources became cheaper, not scarcer.

Critics might argue that this bet focused on specific metals and ignored environmental externalities like carbon emissions or biodiversity loss, which are not captured in price tags. Yet, the author counters this by expanding the scope to the "Simon Abundance Index," which analyzed 50 basic commodities and found that "time prices fell by 64.7 percent over the 38-year period." This reframing suggests that what matters most is not the physical quantity of atoms, but the human capacity to access and utilize them.

Wealth is Knowledge, Not Atoms

Moving beyond economics, the author introduces a philosophical dimension through the work of George Gilder, arguing that "wealth is knowledge—not gold, not oil, and not even money." This distinction is crucial for the piece's ultimate conclusion. The author illustrates this with a memorable analogy about a piano: "If you asked Thanos, he would say, 'There are 88 keys, so there must be 88 songs.' This is actually a trick question—there are no songs in a piano. Songs don't exist in the instrument itself. They exist in the minds of human beings."

This section effectively bridges the gap between hard economics and human potential. The author posits that resources are not finite because knowledge is infinite. As the text puts it, "Since resources are the product of knowledge, and there are no limits to the amount of undiscovered knowledge, resources can be infinite." This argument is bolstered by the example of light, where the cost of an hour of illumination has dropped from three hours of labor in 1830 to less than a second today. The author concludes that "every one percent increase in population corresponded to a 22,474 percent increase in light abundance."

The Divine Imperative of Agency

The piece takes a distinct turn by integrating religious scripture to explain the mechanism of this abundance. The author cites Doctrine and Covenants 104:17, where the Lord declares, "For the earth is full, and there is enough and to spare; yea, I prepared all things, and have given unto the children of men to be agents unto themselves." This theological pivot reframes the economic argument: the limiting factor is not the earth's capacity, but human agency.

The author argues that "without agency, there is no innovation—and without innovation, there is no sustained prosperity." This connects the secular success of the Industrial Revolution, which Angus Maddison noted catapulted humanity out of poverty after 1820, with a spiritual mandate. The text suggests that the "Great Enrichment" was not just about capital accumulation but about a cultural shift where "dignity and liberty were extended to those who used their minds and hands to improve life."

"Human life is not a problem, it is a gift."

The Crisis of Fertility

In its final act, the commentary shifts from the abundance of the past to the scarcity of the future: a lack of people. The author points out that while the global fertility rate is barely above replacement, developed nations are plummeting. "The U.S. rate is 1.62, Mexico is 1.9 and Canada is just 1.4," the text notes, warning that without intervention, populations like Utah's could shrink by half in four generations.

This section serves as a call to action, quoting Pope Francis to reinforce the idea that "the birth rate is the first indicator of the hope of a people." The author argues that the old fears of overpopulation have been replaced by a new, equally dangerous fear: that we are running out of the very minds needed to solve our problems. The piece concludes that we must "place family at the top of our cultural value hierarchy" to sustain the engine of innovation.

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this piece is its seamless integration of hard economic data, specifically the Simon–Ehrlich wager, with a compelling philosophical and theological framework that redefines human beings as the ultimate resource. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its optimistic assumption that technological innovation will always outpace environmental degradation, a premise that remains hotly debated in climate science. Readers should watch for how this "abundance gap" narrative holds up against the tangible realities of ecological limits in the coming decades.

Excerpt from the original article on Wayfare

Imagine a world where more people meant more suffering. For centuries the Reverend Thomas Robert Malthus and his followers believed this was inevitable. But what if they were wrong? What if more people meant more abundance?

Seven years before Joseph Smith was born, Malthus anonymously published An Essay on the Principle of Population. At the time there were around a billion people on the planet. He predicted a growing “Scarcity Gap” between population and food resources. “The power of population is indefinitely greater than the power in the earth to produce subsistence for man. Population, when unchecked, increases in a geometrical ratio. Subsistence increases only in an arithmetical ratio.”

Fast forward 170 years to 1968. Global population had grown to over 3.5 billion people, and Stanford biology professor Paul Ehrlich updated the Malthus theory with his international bestseller The Population Bomb, exclaiming, “The battle to feed all of humanity is over. In the 1970s and 1980s hundreds of millions of people will starve to death in spite of any crash programs embarked upon now.”

By 2018 global population had grown to 7.6 billion. Avengers: Infinity War was the highest-grossing movie that year, starring the arch-villain Thanos. He tells his adopted daughter, “It’s a simple calculus. This universe is finite, its resources finite. If life is left unchecked, life will cease to exist. It needs correcting.” Like Malthus, Thanos frames his argument as a mathematical inevitability—implying that unchecked population growth leads inexorably to catastrophe.

Today with the global population now exceeding 8.2 billion, one might ask: What would Malthus or Ehrlich, or even Thanos, think of the divine commandment to be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the earth? Each of these figures predicted catastrophe from population growth—fearing that more people would mean fewer resources, more suffering, and inevitable collapse. But the facts tell a much different story.

In the 1970s an obscure economist named Julian Simon read Ehrlich’s book and decided he should check the historical facts on the prices of nonrenewable resources as far back as he could find data. Quantities are interesting to economists, but prices are much more important, because prices reveal relative scarcity. What Simon discovered surprised him. As population increased, the inflation-adjusted prices of nonrenewable resources actually went down. They were becoming more abundant, not less.
