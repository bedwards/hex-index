Unraveling Death Stranding's Existentialist Themes

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Dec 22, 2019 · 40 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden makes a startling claim: the most tedious part of Death Stranding—the slow, balance-heavy act of walking—is actually its most profound philosophical statement. In a landscape of video game criticism obsessed with combat mechanics or narrative twists, van der Linden argues that the game's true genius lies in forcing players to physically feel the weight of connection. This is not just a review; it is a meditation on how digital loneliness can be cured by shared, silent effort.

The Physics of Metaphor

The author begins by dismantling the game's complex lore to reveal a simple, terrifying truth about isolation. He describes the "Death Stranding" not merely as a plot device, but as a literalization of our social fractures. "The death stranding refers to a cataclysmic event that entangled our world with the beach, a dimension existing as a sort of limbo somewhere between life and death," van der Linden writes. By turning abstract grief into "beached things"—souls made of antimatter that trigger catastrophic explosions when they touch the living—the game makes the consequences of disconnection visceral. This framing is effective because it transforms a sci-fi trope into a psychological reality: ignoring the dead or the lost doesn't just hurt them; it destroys the living world around us.

The commentary highlights how the game's setting, a fractured America where survivors live in isolated "not-cities," mirrors our current political and social polarization. Van der Linden notes that Hideo Kojima, the game's creator, is rarely subtle. "Bridges, borders, strands, and knots all reveal a central role of connection," he observes. The author argues that the game's mechanics are not just obstacles but metaphors. The "Chiral Network," an evolved internet that links people, is presented as a promise of unity. "It is essentially a promise, a chance to rebuild the isolated cities back into a united nation and save humanity from extinction," van der Linden asserts. This lands with particular weight because it suggests that technology, often blamed for isolation, can be the very tool that reconnects us if used with the right intent.

The most fascinating thing about Death Stranding as an event is that it seems to take abstract social realities and subjective experiences and turns them into tangible physical phenomena.

Critics might argue that Kojima's tendency to over-explain his metaphors through character dialogue undermines the subtlety of the gameplay. Van der Linden acknowledges this, admitting that the narrative can be "needlessly complicated." However, he counters that the physical experience of the game transcends the clunky exposition. The "Timefall," a rain that ages everything it touches, serves as a perfect example. It makes time relative not just in physics, but in human experience, turning a moment into an eternity. This metaphorical layering gives the game a depth that pure action titles lack.

The Burden of the Journey

The core of van der Linden's argument shifts to the gameplay itself, challenging the modern expectation that travel in games should be frictionless. He contrasts Death Stranding with typical open-world titles where movement is a mere transition between exciting moments. "In other open-world games where you can carry along huge inventories without it affecting your mobility, every movement is often as simple as pointing yourself in the right direction and pressing forward," he writes. In Death Stranding, the journey is the point. The player must manage balance, cargo weight, and terrain, turning a simple delivery into a critical thinking exercise.

Van der Linden draws a powerful parallel to filmmaker Werner Herzog, noting that "the world reveals itself to those who travel on foot." This is the piece's most distinctive insight. By making the player feel the strain of the terrain, the game forces a connection to the earth that is rarely felt in digital spaces. "Every contract, every delivery requires your full attention," van der Linden explains. This demand for presence transforms the player from a passive observer into an active participant in the world's survival. The tediousness is not a flaw; it is the mechanism that makes the eventual success feel earned.

The author also touches on the moral dimension of the game's "Strand System," an asynchronous multiplayer feature where players can leave structures like bridges or ladders for others. "You never actually meet any other players," van der Linden points out. "You can see a sign left a vehicle or placed a structure, but you can't reach out to them directly." This limitation is intentional, creating a space where interaction is purely altruistic. The only way to communicate is through a "like," stripping away the ego and toxicity often found in online spaces. "It sanctifies online interactions," he argues, suggesting that the game creates a unique form of digital empathy.

It connects people to the best parts of themselves and by only being as visible as you are helpful to others, it takes the ego out of the equation.

A counterargument worth considering is that this sanitized version of online interaction is a fantasy that ignores the complexities of real-world cooperation. Real communities require conflict resolution, negotiation, and sometimes confrontation, none of which are present in the Strand System. Yet, van der Linden's point holds: the game offers a glimpse of what online interaction could be if it were designed around mutual aid rather than engagement metrics.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's analysis succeeds in reframing Death Stranding not as a confusing sci-fi mess, but as a profound exploration of human connection through the medium of movement. His strongest argument is that the game's physical friction is its emotional engine, forcing players to feel the weight of their responsibilities. The piece's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the player's willingness to engage with the game's slower pace, which may alienate those seeking traditional action. Ultimately, this is a compelling case for video games as a unique art form capable of simulating empathy in ways no other medium can.
