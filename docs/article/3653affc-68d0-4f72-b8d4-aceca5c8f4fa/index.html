124: How to manage your phone use around your kids

Various · Two Truths ·Mar 13, 2026 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

The most liberating claim in this piece isn't a new study or a strict rule, but a permission slip: the idea that parental phone use is an "impossible situation" where perfection is not only unattainable but unnecessary. While the digital age often frames technology as the villain of childhood development, the authors, Cassie Shortsleeve and Kelsey Haywood Lucas, pivot quickly to a more nuanced reality by interviewing psychologist Jacqueline Nesi. They argue that the real danger lies not in the device itself, but in the guilt that paralyzes parents from being intentional. This distinction is vital for busy listeners who are already drowning in contradictory advice about screen time.

Dismantling the Guilt Machine

Shortsleeve and Lucas begin by acknowledging the inherent contradiction of discussing digital disconnection through a digital newsletter, a self-aware move that builds immediate trust. They set the stage by noting that "so much of our lives are online and so much of our lives are not," a sentiment that resonates with anyone trying to balance remote work with family life. Instead of offering a binary choice between total abstinence and total immersion, they introduce Nesi's perspective that "any phone use around our kids is problematic" is a myth we have internalized.

This framing is effective because it shifts the burden from the parent's character to the context of their usage. As Nesi explains, "If our phones are getting in the way—distracting us during moments when we want to be connected, interfering with family time, causing stress for our kids or us—then it's probably time to reevaluate how we're using them." The authors wisely avoid fear-mongering, a trap many parenting guides fall into, and instead focus on the quality of interaction.

"So, first things first: Let's just acknowledge that this is an impossible situation! I think it speaks to some of the unrealistic standards that we have for parents—and as parents, that we have for ourselves."

The commentary here is sharp: it recognizes that the pressure to be constantly present is a modern invention that ignores the logistical realities of modern parenting. By validating the struggle, the authors make the subsequent advice feel actionable rather than judgmental.

From Willpower to Situational Design

The piece moves from philosophy to practical strategy by addressing the mechanics of addiction. The authors correctly identify that "our devices are designed to suck us in," making the reliance on sheer willpower a losing battle. Nesi suggests a shift toward "situational self-control," a concept that aligns with behavioral psychology. Instead of vague resolutions to "use less," the authors advocate for specific, value-driven goals: "I want to reduce the time I spend checking my email during the evenings, because it's taking away from time spent playing with my kids."

This specificity is the article's strongest tactical advice. It transforms a moral failing into an engineering problem. The authors list concrete steps like disabling Touch ID to increase friction or deleting apps, which are far more effective than simply telling parents to "put the phone down."

"Ultimately, the research points to the need for us to be mindful of our phone use around our kids, to be intentional about it, and, most importantly, to make time to nurture positive relationships with our kids. It does not point to a need to feel guilty every time we glance at our screens."

Critics might argue that this approach lets parents off the hook too easily, potentially normalizing the very "technoference" that studies show can lead to behavioral issues in children. However, the authors counter this by emphasizing that the goal is connection, not absence. They note that while short-term effects of parental distraction are documented, "we do not have any solid evidence on long-term impacts," suggesting that the panic is often disproportionate to the data.

Reclaiming the Human Element

The final section tackles the broader cultural loss of physicality, referencing the decline of the postal system and the rise of cashless transactions. The authors and Nesi reframe this not as a loss of technology, but as a loss of specific human interactions. Nesi points out that the sadness over kids not using cash isn't about the paper bills, but about "human connection" and the chance to "strike up conversations."

This insight connects deeply to the concept of "phubbing" (phone snubbing) explored in related deep dives on the topic. Just as phubbing research highlights how ignoring a partner for a phone damages relationship satisfaction, this piece argues that we must model the values of the lost physical world, not the objects themselves. The authors suggest that even while using Apple Pay, parents can "model for them the important stuff: striking up conversations, saying thank you, and connecting with the people around us."

"I don't think we need to forego the conveniences of technology for some of these things; rather, we need to be intentional about replacing the values or skills we worry are being lost."

This is a crucial distinction. It prevents the conversation from becoming a Luddite rant about returning to the 1990s. Instead, it asks parents to curate their digital lives to preserve the human skills that technology tends to erode. The argument holds up because it acknowledges that the medium changes, but the need for connection remains constant.

Bottom Line

Shortsleeve and Lucas succeed by replacing the toxic narrative of parental failure with a framework of intentional design. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to demonize technology, instead positioning it as a tool that requires management rather than elimination. The only vulnerability lies in the difficulty of execution; knowing that "situational self-control" is better than willpower doesn't make the friction of changing habits any easier, but it does provide a clear path forward for parents ready to try.
