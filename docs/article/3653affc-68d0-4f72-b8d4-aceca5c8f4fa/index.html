124: How to manage your phone use around your kids

Various · Two Truths ·Mar 13, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

In a landscape saturated with alarmist warnings about screen time, this collaboration between health journalists Cassie Shortsleeve and Kelsey Haywood Lucas and psychologist Jacqueline Nesi offers a rare, necessary antidote: permission to be imperfect. Rather than demanding digital abstinence, the piece argues that the real crisis isn't the device itself, but the guilt parents internalize when they use it. This shift from prohibition to intentionality is the most valuable takeaway for any busy parent trying to navigate a world where technology is both a lifeline and a distraction.

The Myth of the Perfect Digital Parent

The authors immediately dismantle the pervasive anxiety surrounding parental phone use. They interview Nesi, a psychologist who studies the intersection of technology and family life, to challenge the notion that any screen time is inherently damaging. "So, first things first: Let's just acknowledge that this is an impossible situation!" Nesi writes, pointing out that the expectation for parents to be perpetually offline is a modern fantasy. This framing is effective because it validates the reality of modern life, where phones are essential for work, logistics, and safety, rather than treating them as a moral failing.

The commentary highlights a crucial distinction often missed in popular discourse: the difference between technoference—when technology actively disrupts connection—and the benign act of checking an email. Nesi notes that while studies show technoference can lead to short-term behavioral issues in children, there is no solid evidence of long-term harm from occasional glances at a screen. "Ultimately, the research points to the need for us to be mindful of our phone use around our kids, to be intentional about it, and, most importantly, to make time to nurture positive relationships with our kids," she asserts. This nuance is vital; it moves the conversation from a binary of "good parent/bad parent" to a spectrum of mindful engagement.

Critics might argue that normalizing phone use risks blurring the lines for children who lack the self-regulation to distinguish between necessary and excessive use. However, the piece counters this by emphasizing that modeling balance is more effective than modeling perfection. The authors suggest that the problem isn't the phone, but the unrealistic standards parents have set for themselves.

"So, first things first: Let's just acknowledge that this is an impossible situation! I think it speaks to some of the unrealistic standards that we have for parents—and as parents, that we have for ourselves."

Engineering Intentionality Over Willpower

The second major thrust of the article is a practical guide to changing behavior without relying on sheer willpower. The authors and Nesi argue that because devices are engineered to be addictive, parents must rely on "situational self-control" rather than internal discipline. The advice is specific and actionable: disable biometric logins to increase friction, delete distracting apps, or use physical devices like "The Brick" to lock away the phone.

This approach aligns with broader trends in behavioral psychology, echoing the principles found in deep dives on minimalism, where the focus is on curating one's environment to support desired habits rather than fighting against them. The authors write, "Rather than going in saying, simply, 'I want to use my phone less,' it can be more effective to say something specific, i.e., 'I want to reduce the time I spend checking my email during the evenings [the what], because it's taking away from time spent playing with my kids [the why].'" This specificity transforms a vague aspiration into a concrete behavioral target.

The piece also addresses the nostalgia for a pre-digital world, such as the decline of physical mail or cash payments. Nesi reframes this loss not as a disappearance of objects, but as a potential loss of human connection. "A lot of times, we tie that feeling to the actual physical item (cash or a map), but really, it's about something else," she explains. The solution isn't to force children to use cash, but to model the underlying values: striking up conversations, saying thank you, and engaging with the community. This connects to the concept of phubbing (phone snubbing), reminding readers that the act of ignoring someone for a device is the true injury, regardless of the technology used.

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this coverage is its refusal to demonize technology, instead offering a pragmatic framework for integrating it into family life without sacrificing connection. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the difficulty of execution; while the advice to "set your environment for success" is sound, it requires a level of discipline that is hard to maintain when devices are designed to be irresistible. Readers should watch for how they can apply these principles of situational self-control to their own routines, focusing on the quality of interaction rather than the quantity of screen time."
