How to use the placebo effect to (actually) feel better

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·May 25, 2023 · 62 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis dismantles the dismissive notion that the placebo effect is merely a 'sugar pill' trick, reframing it instead as a profound biological mechanism driven by expectation, ritual, and the power of the mind. In a piece that oscillates between medical rigor and self-deprecating humor, Francis argues that the brain's ability to generate real physiological relief from inert substances is not a bug in the system, but a feature of human healing that we have dangerously underestimated.

The Architecture of Expectation

Francis begins by stripping away the mystique to reveal the mechanics: the placebo effect is not about magic, but about the brain adjusting perception based on what it anticipates. He illustrates this with a childhood anecdote, noting that while a parental kiss contains no 'magical healing chemicals,' it successfully alters a child's pain experience. "The placebo effect is the brain adjusting how you feel," Francis writes, establishing the core thesis that our internal narrative drives external reality. This is a crucial distinction; the author is not claiming the injury vanished, but that the suffering was mitigated by the mind's intervention.

The commentary deepens as Francis explores how the presentation of a treatment amplifies this effect. He points out that we are conditioned to associate specific rituals with healing, much like Pavlov's dogs. "The more ritualized and apparently extreme a placebo is and the sign posting that you give at least to the recipient, the more effective it can be," he observes. This explains why an expensive branded pill outperforms a generic one, or why a fake surgery yields better results than a placebo injection. The evidence here is compelling: the context of care is as potent as the chemistry of the drug. Critics might argue that relying on such psychological levers feels manipulative, yet Francis suggests that acknowledging this power is the only way to ethically harness it.

Beyond the 'Sugar Pill' Stigma

A significant portion of Francis's argument is dedicated to rehabilitating the reputation of the placebo, which is often viewed negatively in medical circles as a confounding variable or a sign of deception. He correctly identifies that in clinical trials, the placebo effect is the 'noise' that makes it hard to prove a new drug works. "62% of women reported firmer skin but we would have never known about flat Alex's deflated effect without a control group," he notes, using a hypothetical gas-reducing drug to demonstrate how essential these inert controls are for scientific truth. Without them, we would be left with anecdotal fluff and unproven remedies.

However, Francis pivots to a more humanistic view, arguing that dismissing a patient's improvement as 'just placebo' is a failure of empathy. He distinguishes between curing a disease and alleviating suffering. "Yes people should not be misled that something has demonstrable objective benefit... but their subjective sense of improvement in their well-being is still real to them," he asserts. This is a vital nuance. If a patient feels better after a supportive consultation or a ritualistic treatment, the relief is genuine, even if the underlying pathology remains. The author effectively challenges the medical establishment to stop viewing the placebo effect as a lie, and start seeing it as a therapeutic tool.

The Statistical Traps: Regression and Natural History

Francis wisely cautions against attributing every recovery to the placebo effect, introducing two critical statistical concepts that often masquerade as miracles: the natural history of illness and regression to the mean. He uses the example of a common cold, reminding us that "almost all colds just get better on their own after a few days." When a doctor prescribes a useless remedy during this window, the patient credits the doctor, not time. "The art of medicine consists of amusing the patient until nature cures the disease," he quotes Voltaire, a sentiment that remains surprisingly relevant.

He then tackles the more complex issue of regression to the mean, explaining that people often seek help only when symptoms are at their worst. Statistically, the next episode is likely to be milder regardless of intervention. "After an outlier than next result is going to be closer to the average simply due to probability," Francis explains. This is a powerful correction to the 'miracle cure' narrative. It forces the reader to question whether a treatment worked, or if the patient simply experienced a statistical inevitability. This section is the piece's intellectual anchor, grounding the emotional appeal of the placebo in hard data.

The Ethical Dilemma of Prescription

The piece concludes by addressing the elephant in the room: if placebos work, why don't doctors just prescribe them? Francis acknowledges the frustration of treating conditions like chronic pain or long COVID where effective drugs are scarce. He notes that these are "perfect kinds of conditions to prescribe a placebo for," yet the medical system hesitates. The barrier is ethical: the requirement for informed consent. While some researchers argue for 'open-label' placebos (telling patients they are taking a placebo), the traditional model relies on deception. Francis leaves the reader with the uncomfortable reality that the most effective tools for subjective relief are often the ones we are legally and ethically forbidden to use openly.

Bottom Line

Rohin Francis delivers a sophisticated defense of the placebo effect, successfully shifting the narrative from 'deception' to 'neurobiology.' The strongest part of the argument is the distinction between objective cure and subjective relief, which validates the patient's experience without endorsing pseudoscience. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in the unresolved tension between the efficacy of the placebo and the ethical constraints of modern medicine; Francis hints at open-label solutions but does not fully explore their practical limitations. Readers should watch for how the medical community navigates this paradox as the line between mind and body continues to blur.
