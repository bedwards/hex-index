Abolish the senses

Brian Merchant · ·Jan 16, 2026 ·23 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Brian Merchant delivers a harrowing account of how Silicon Valley's infrastructure has ceased to be a tool for connection and has instead become the engine of a new, spectacle-driven authoritarianism. This piece is notable not for recounting the violence of recent federal raids, but for pinpointing the specific technological and corporate mechanisms that allow the state to rewrite reality in real-time, turning the murder of a civilian into a political talking point without consequence.

The Architecture of Epistemic Collapse

Merchant argues that we have moved beyond the era of "fake news" into a state where the very concept of truth is rendered immaterial by the speed and design of modern platforms. He writes, "Tech companies, products (like AI), and platforms like X have chipped away at and undermined our confidence in the nature of 'truth,' and in the institutions once entrusted to arbitrate it, and left a vacuum in its place." This observation is critical because it shifts the blame from mere misinformation to a structural failure of the information ecosystem itself. The author suggests that the administration no longer needs to convince anyone of the facts; they only need to dominate the narrative feed.

The piece connects this digital vacuum to the physical violence unfolding in Minnesota, where the federal government has deployed thousands of agents in a campaign the author describes as a "military occupation." Merchant notes that the state is now treating real-world events as "an online argument that can be won by posting red meat," a strategy that relies on the audience's inability or unwillingness to verify footage. This framing is particularly potent when applied to the shooting of Renee Good, where the official narrative immediately contradicted the visual evidence of the victim attempting to flee. As Merchant puts it, "The state is treating this as an online argument that can be won by posting red meat, since Twitter, or rather X, the everything app, is real life now, and the administration no longer feels compelled to even gesture towards the truth."

It turns out they've both arrived, in tandem; the state is booming obvious falsehoods in our face and demanding we accept them, and they are simultaneously being packaged into infotainment to delight the converted and enrage the opposed.

Critics might argue that focusing on the role of technology lets political actors off the hook for their own choices, but Merchant's evidence suggests the two are now inextricably linked. The technology didn't just amplify the message; it created the conditions where the message could replace the reality entirely. This echoes the historical concept of "epistemic closure," where a group becomes immune to outside facts, but here, the closure is engineered by the very platforms that claim to democratize information.

The Profit Motive Behind the Occupation

A central pillar of Merchant's argument is the direct financial entanglement between the tech elite and the agencies conducting these raids. He does not shy away from naming the beneficiaries, noting that OpenAI's president and Palantir's CEO are among the top donors to the administration's super PAC. The author writes, "Everyone—and especially everyone in politics and the media—needs to understand the specific role that Silicon Valley elites and their platforms have played in ripening the conditions for the ICE raids, Good's shooting, and the terror now unfolding on American mud. And how they profit directly from the state that is perpetrating it."

This is a damning indictment of the tech sector's moral bankruptcy. The article details how agencies like the Federal Bureau of Investigation and Immigration and Customs Enforcement are now actively producing their own content, encouraging agents to record their own footage to create a "spectacle of violence." Merchant observes, "ICE officers are now being encouraged to record proactively, to produce their own raid footage and create their own content for dissemination on social media, which is likely why Ross had his phone in one hand taking video while he held his gun in the other when he shot Good." This transforms the act of policing into a form of content creation, where the goal is not just enforcement but the generation of viral material that reinforces the administration's narrative.

The human cost of this dynamic is stark. Merchant describes a neighborhood where a local church, a community hub, was commandeered by federal agents, turning a place of safety into a staging ground for raids. He recounts the fear of neighbors, including school employees and childcare workers, who found themselves under the threat of abduction. The author's personal account of the "fear and anger" in his community grounds the high-level policy critique in visceral reality. He writes, "When you can name the school employees, childcare workers, and instructors who you see around the neighborhood, who have taken care of your kids, who may have been targets, the violence and vileness of what ICE is doing fully takes root."

Whatever your views on immigration policy, if you don't find masked agents methodically harassing citizens, throwing people into unmarked vans, and going door to door asking for papers antithetical to American democracy, you're probably either a Stormfront mod or an ICE agent yourself.

The article also touches on the broader historical context, referencing the "domination porn" tailored for social media consumption. This aligns with the deep dive on fascism, where the spectacle of power becomes the primary tool of control. The administration's ability to declare a "domestic terrorist" out of a fleeing mother, and have that narrative stick despite video evidence, demonstrates a level of control that goes beyond traditional propaganda. It is a system where the truth is whatever the most powerful actor can make stick on the platform.

The Failure of Transparency

Merchant challenges the long-held Silicon Valley belief that more data and transparency lead to accountability. He argues that body cameras and social media, once hailed as tools for justice, have been co-opted to facilitate state violence. "Social media, once promised to illuminate the sins of the powerful so as to bring them to justice, have become instruments the rich can easily manipulate to benefit themselves and their interests," he writes. This is a crucial reversal of the tech industry's founding mythos. Instead of sunlight being the best disinfectant, the light of the camera has become a weapon, used to stage scenes and manufacture consent for violence.

The author points out that even when the truth is undeniable, the state simply adjusts its story to fit the new reality, or dismisses the evidence entirely. When it was revealed that the agent who shot Good had used slurs, the administration's response was to shift the focus to the victim's alleged "disrespect." This fluidity of truth is the ultimate victory of the new authoritarian model. As Merchant notes, "The Trump administration simply declaring what it would like to have transpired, even in the face of ample and obvious documentation to the contrary, works."

Bottom Line

Merchant's most powerful contribution is his demonstration that the collapse of truth is not an accidental byproduct of the digital age, but a feature of a political economy where tech giants and the state are locked in a symbiotic relationship of mutual profit and power. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that the public will eventually reject this spectacle, a hope that feels increasingly fragile given the current trajectory. Readers should watch for how the administration continues to leverage these platforms to bypass traditional checks and balances, and whether the tech industry will ever face meaningful consequences for its role in enabling this new form of governance.
