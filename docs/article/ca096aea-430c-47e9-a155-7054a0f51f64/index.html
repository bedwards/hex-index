How Big Tech is Ruining Your Attention

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Jan 31, 2023 · 18 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now delivers a jarring diagnosis for the modern age: our attention isn't just distracted; it is a currency being mined, bid on, and captured by corporations that have turned our psychology into a battlefield. The piece stands out not merely for listing the symptoms of our fractured focus, but for tracing a direct historical line from the sensationalist tabloids of 1833 to the algorithmic precision of today's tech giants, proving that the weaponization of human attention is a century-old project that has simply become more efficient.

The Historical Blueprint of Distraction

The author argues that the mechanisms we blame on Silicon Valley are actually the culmination of a long evolution in advertising. Then & Now writes, "The attention economy is everywhere... where corporations spend billions on the latest tricks to draw our eyeballs and to perk our interests." This framing is crucial because it shifts the blame from individual willpower to a systemic industrial design. The piece effectively dismantles the idea that we are failing to resist; rather, we are facing an opponent that has spent decades refining the art of breaking our resistance.

To illustrate this, the author reaches back to the early days of mass media, noting that when radio first entered homes, President Hoover declared it "inconceivable that we should allow so great a possibility for service... to be drained in advertising chatter." The contrast between that early hesitation and the current reality is stark. Then & Now points out that NBC executives quickly realized the commercial goldmine, with Frank Arnold noting, "Here you have the advertiser's ideal: the family group and its moments of relaxation awaiting your message." This historical pivot is the piece's strongest evidence that the erosion of privacy was not an accident, but a deliberate choice made by industry leaders who prioritized profit over the sanctity of the home.

"The ad man inevitably empties himself of human qualities... no man can give his days to barbarous frivolity and live an ad man."

The commentary on the psychological toll on the advertisers themselves is a fascinating, often overlooked angle. By quoting James Warty, the author suggests that the industry dehumanizes not just the consumer, but the creator, turning them into "demonic fantasts and sadists." This adds a layer of moral complexity to the argument: the system is toxic for everyone involved, not just the victim.

Critics might note that the piece leans heavily on a deterministic view of human nature, suggesting we are helpless against these "tricks." While the historical parallels are strong, it overlooks the agency of media literacy movements that have successfully pushed back against sensationalism in the past, such as the muckraking journalism that led to the 1906 Food and Drugs Act.

The Polygon of Personalized Capture

As the narrative moves to the digital age, Then & Now introduces the concept of the "polygon," a metaphor for the digital environment surrounding us. The author explains that unlike the passive ads of the past, modern technology uses data to create "personalized attention capture," vacuuming up keystrokes, locations, and purchases to fine-tune exactly what will grab our focus. Then & Now writes, "We're evolutionarily wired to want more of a good thing and like doors full of sugar these doors knock, they cool us, entice us."

This metaphor is powerful because it visualizes the invisible. The author argues that the problem isn't just that we are distracted, but that our brains are physically rewiring themselves to anticipate interruption. "Studies have shown that your brain trains itself to predict distractions and so we'll distract itself in anticipation," the author notes. This is a devastating insight: the mere presence of a notification changes our cognitive baseline, lowering our effective IQ by ten points even before we look at the screen.

The piece highlights the sheer scale of this intrusion, citing that the average person touches their phone 2,500 times a day. Then & Now quotes Netflix CEO Reed Hastings to illustrate the scope of the competition: "At Netflix we're competing for our customers' time so our competitors include Snapchat, YouTube, sleep, etc." This admission from an industry titan underscores the zero-sum nature of the attention economy. The argument here is that the "choice" we feel we have is an illusion; we are choosing between different forms of capture, all designed to drain our finite attentional resources.

"The attention economy is an over-determination, a dizziness, a promise of freedom to pursue desires that ends up as a new type of slavery to our deepest desires."

This line serves as the emotional core of the piece, reframing our digital habits not as a failure of discipline but as a structural trap. The author suggests that the "freedom" of the internet is actually a mechanism for enslavement to our own impulses, engineered by algorithms that know us better than we know ourselves.

Reclaiming the Self

In the final section, the author pivots from diagnosis to prescription, urging readers to either "leave the polygon or attend to the polygon" with intention. The core of the solution lies in protecting the "sanctity of the self." Then & Now quotes essayist William Deresiewicz to drive the point home: "You are marinating yourself in the conventional wisdom in other people's reality for others, not for yourself. You're creating a cacophony in which it's impossible to hear your own voice."

This is the most actionable part of the commentary. The author argues that without carving out space for silence, we lose the ability to distinguish our own needs from the demands of the market. The piece suggests that privacy was once sacred, but now "companies compete in a race to the bottom of tricks to grab our attention." The call to action is not just to use apps less, but to fundamentally restructure our relationship with technology to ensure our personhood can thrive.

Critics might argue that the solution of "leaving the polygon" is impractical for many people whose livelihoods depend on constant connectivity. The piece offers a binary choice that may feel impossible for those who cannot afford to disconnect from the digital economy.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's most compelling contribution is the historical continuity it draws, proving that the current crisis of attention is the logical endpoint of a century-long industrial project rather than a sudden technological glitch. The argument's greatest vulnerability is its somewhat fatalistic tone, which risks paralyzing readers with the sheer scale of the problem rather than empowering them with practical, incremental steps to regain control. The piece succeeds in making the invisible machinery of the attention economy visible, leaving the reader with a necessary, if uncomfortable, clarity about the cost of their own focus.
