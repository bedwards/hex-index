Aurelian: Emperor Who Restored the World

Kings and Generals · Kings and Generals ·Aug 6, 2020 · 18 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Kings and Generals frames the third-century Roman Empire not as a slow decline, but as a catastrophic fracture that required a specific, brutal kind of genius to fix. The author's most distinctive claim is that Aurelian did not merely patch the cracks; he fundamentally reimagined the empire's geography and psychology, turning a crumbling domain into a fortress state. In an era where history often focuses on the fall of Rome, this piece forces the reader to confront the moment it was saved, arguing that without Aurelian's "iron grip," the classical world would have ended decades early.

The Rise of the Iron Hand

The narrative begins by setting the stage for total collapse, noting that "at the height of rome's period of anarchy during the third century... the empire [was] on the very brink of total collapse." Kings and Generals writes, "one of these formidable men aurelian took the roman empire with an iron grip glued together its crumbling domain and set the stage for another two centuries of dominance." This framing is crucial because it shifts the focus from the chaos of the Crisis of the Third Century to the singular agency of one man. The author details Aurelian's background not as a noble birthright, but as a meritocratic ascent, earning the nickname "Manu ad Ferrem" or "hand on sword" through sheer battlefield prowess.

The commentary here is effective because it strips away the mystique of imperial divinity and presents Aurelian as a pragmatic soldier. The author notes that after the assassination of Emperor Gallienus, Aurelian was "simultaneously acclaimed emperor by the legions who were with him," highlighting that his power base was the army, not the senate. This lands because it explains why Aurelian could act with such decisiveness; he answered to no one but the men who held the swords. A counterargument worth considering is that this reliance on the military made the empire perpetually unstable, as it set a precedent where the next strongman could simply march on Rome again. Yet, the author suggests Aurelian was unique in his ability to channel that military power toward restoration rather than mere plunder.

The Strategic Retreat and the Walls of Rome

Perhaps the most surprising argument in the text is Aurelian's willingness to admit defeat and retreat. The author describes how, facing the Vandals and Goths, Aurelian made a "big step for the conservative romans to voluntarily and pragmatically admit to" that the province of Dacia was a "pointless exertion of imperial resources." Kings and Generals writes, "aurelian ordered that all legionary forces and citizens withdraw from the region effectively abandoning the province the border was consolidated and shortened on the near side of the river." This is a radical departure from the traditional Roman narrative of eternal expansion. It suggests a leader who understood that overextension was a greater threat than any barbarian horde.

This strategic contraction is paired with a massive defensive investment. The text notes that "individual cities now needed their own fortifications so the construction of a series of practical non-aesthetic defensive walls began around rome which still remain in the modern day." The author emphasizes the "practical non-aesthetic" nature of these walls, contrasting them with the vanity projects of earlier emperors. This focus on utility over glory is the defining characteristic of Aurelian's reign. Critics might note that abandoning Dacia was a psychological blow to Roman prestige, potentially emboldening other tribes to test the new borders. However, the author argues that this move was essential to "remedy" the vulnerability of the empire's heartland, prioritizing survival over pride.

Aurelian acknowledged that the province of dasia which was beyond the danube frontier was a pointless exertion of imperial resources and a vulnerable gateway into the empire.

The Campaign Against Zenobia and the Art of Mercy

The narrative then shifts to the East, where Aurelian faced the Palmyrene Empire under Queen Zenobia. The author paints a picture of a commander who could switch from ruthless efficiency to calculated mercy. When the city of Tyana refused entry, Aurelian initially pledged to spare "not a dog alive," but upon the city's surrender, he realized "leniency would be a more prudent course of action in the long term." Kings and Generals writes, "he ordered that his triumphant army spare the citizens rather than slaying them he was to be a liberator of these roman lands rather than a vicious foreign conqueror." This anecdote serves as a microcosm of his entire strategy: use fear to break resistance, then use clemency to secure loyalty.

The military tactics described are equally vivid. The author details the Battle of Emesa, where Aurelian used his lighter cavalry to bait the heavy Palmyrene cataphracts into a trap. "When the overburdened cataphracts and their horses began to slow down from exhaustion aurelian's cavalry turned and counter-charged them scattering the palmarine forces winning a decisive victory." This analysis highlights Aurelian's tactical genius, showing how he exploited the weaknesses of his enemy rather than meeting them head-on. The author also notes the capture of Zenobia, who was "paraded through serious cities chained to a camel" to "snuff out any lingering support." This brutal display was necessary to end the cycle of rebellion, yet the author balances it by noting that Aurelian spared the queen herself, preserving her for a triumph rather than executing her immediately.

The Restoration of Order

The piece concludes by addressing the internal reforms that made the military victories possible. Aurelian's crackdown on corruption was severe; when a riot broke out over financial mismanagement, "up to seven thousand were dead by the time aurelians subdued the city with an iron fist." The author argues that this violence was a necessary precursor to stability, as Aurelian "attempted to stabilize the coinage and harshly deal with corruption." The relocation of mints to strategic locations like Milan is cited as a key logistical reform, ensuring that pay could reach the armies efficiently. This section underscores that Aurelian's restoration was not just about winning battles, but about fixing the broken machinery of the state.

The author's tone remains admiring but grounded, acknowledging the bloodshed required to achieve peace. The text states that Aurelian assumed the title "restitutor orbis restorer of the world," a claim that the author supports by detailing how he reclaimed the East, secured the West, and reformed the economy. The argument holds up well because it relies on specific military and administrative actions rather than vague praise. However, the piece briefly glosses over the fact that Aurelian himself was soon assassinated, suggesting that while he could fix the empire, he could not fix the culture of assassination that plagued the third century.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals presents a compelling case for Aurelian as the pivotal figure who prevented the total collapse of the Roman Empire, blending military brilliance with pragmatic statecraft. The strongest part of the argument is the emphasis on Aurelian's willingness to abandon lost causes like Dacia to save the core, a move that redefined Roman strategy. The biggest vulnerability is the lack of discussion on the long-term instability caused by his reliance on military force and his eventual assassination, which suggests the restoration was fragile. Readers should watch for how later emperors, particularly Diocletian, built upon Aurelian's foundations to create the more stable, albeit authoritarian, empire of the late third century.
