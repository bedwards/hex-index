Is America good?

Stephen G. Adubato · ·Jan 8, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In a cultural moment defined by performative cynicism, Stephen G. Adubato delivers a stinging critique not of the American project itself, but of the privileged despair that has come to define its critics. He argues that the current wave of declinism—worn by both post-liberals and progressive activists alike—is less a sober analysis of reality and more a psychological coping mechanism for the comfortable. This is a rare, self-reflective intervention that challenges the reader to stop treating the collapse of civilization as a trendy aesthetic and start asking if their own moral outrage is actually a form of immaturity.

The Paradox of Prosperous Despair

Adubato opens by dissecting the specific mindset of the modern intellectual who finds it "cringe" to express love for their country. He identifies a dangerous feedback loop where the very success of liberal democracy breeds a "deadly combination of pessimistic cynicism and utopian idealism." As he puts it, "To say that you love democracy or that you love America... is to be decidedly uncool." This observation cuts deep because it exposes the social cost of patriotism in elite circles: it is viewed as a failure of critical thinking rather than a commitment to the common good.

The author leans heavily on Shadi Hamid's The Case for American Power to illustrate this phenomenon. Hamid suggests that the freedom to panic is actually a luxury of democracy, a point Adubato initially resists but eventually accepts. He writes, "Just as things are getting better — perhaps the best they've ever been — they can still feel awful. A sense of doom spreads precisely at the pinnacle of success." This reframing is crucial. It suggests that the feeling of impending doom is not evidence of systemic failure, but a symptom of the paradox of progress, where cleared bars simply lead to higher demands.

"We'd rather deconstruct — preferably from behind the comfort of a screen. A hashtag, infographic, or photo of myself at a protest will do."

Adubato argues that this digital deconstruction is easier than the hard labor of building. He posits that many who claim to want to overthrow the system are actually addicted to the drama of decline. This aligns with the historical concept of oikophobia, a term coined by Roger Scruton to describe a contempt for one's own home, which Adubato notes is endemic among elites who have forgotten that "the founding of any country involves unforgivable acts of violence." By invoking the decline narratives of Oswald Spengler, Adubato suggests that the belief that "the world might be ending" is more thrilling than the mundane reality that it will just be "bad, but it won't be that bad."

The Prophetic Failure of Modern Activism

The commentary then shifts to the moral implications of this attitude. Adubato contends that true prophetic voices do not parade their virtues but instead call out the sins of the community, including their own. He contrasts modern performative activism with the work of Dorothy Day, noting that "the prophets of yore were less interested in parading their virtues than in waking up their fellow humans to recognize the very sins they knew they were in no way exempt from." This is a sharp rebuke to the current trend of zero-tolerance moralism, where the focus is on signaling purity rather than engaging with the messy reality of human complexity.

He acknowledges that movements like Black Lives Matter or campus protests address real grievances, yet he argues their methodologies betray a "psychological and moral immaturity." The core of his critique is that "if you're going to take up the call of the prophet and preach about lofty, utopian ideals, you need to first be mature enough to recognize the 'log in your own eye' before calling out others." This framing challenges the reader to consider whether their outrage is a genuine drive for justice or a projection of their own unresolved issues and aversion to commitment.

Critics might note that Adubato risks dismissing legitimate structural critiques by attributing them too readily to individual psychological fragility. While the distinction between deconstruction and construction is vital, systemic violence often requires dismantling before it can be rebuilt, a nuance that his focus on personal maturity might underplay.

The Case for a Flawed Resilience

Despite his initial resistance, Adubato finds himself compelled by Hamid's argument that the lack of a single, unified notion of the "good" in American democracy is actually its source of vitality. Hamid admits that this pluralism leads to "profound individual-level unhappiness and political dysfunction," but insists the system is "remarkably resilient." Adubato wrestles with this, wondering if the spiritual suffocation he feels is an inevitable result of liberal democracy or merely a personal reaction to it.

He notes that Hamid's argument is compelling because it is rooted in personal experience, specifically the impact of 9/11 on his family as Muslim-Americans. This transparency allows for a genuine dialogue rather than a polemic. As Hamid asks himself, "I won't pretend that I'm not still conflicted. Because I am." Adubato concludes that this willingness to admit conflict and seek a "compromise position" is exactly what is needed to move beyond the binary of blind patriotism and cynical rejection.

"The only crisis is our inability to see what's right in front of our very eyes."

This quote, attributed to Hamid but embraced by Adubato, serves as the pivot point of the essay. It suggests that the crisis is not in the institutions, but in our perception of them. The author argues that we have become so obsessed with the "features" of corruption and injustice that we fail to see the "bugs" are actually manageable flaws in a system that, despite its failures, remains the best option for human flourishing.

Bottom Line

Stephen G. Adubato's piece is a necessary corrective to the self-indulgent despair that has infected modern political discourse, successfully arguing that our obsession with decline is often a mask for our own unwillingness to do the hard work of civic repair. Its greatest strength lies in its willingness to turn the critical lens inward, challenging the reader to distinguish between genuine moral outrage and the comfort of performative cynicism. However, the argument's vulnerability remains in its potential to minimize the very real, structural failures that justify deep skepticism, risking a slide into a defense of the status quo simply because it is familiar.

Is America good?

by Stephen G. Adubato · · Read full article

I wouldn’t be caught dead wearing American flag-themed apparel, let alone flying the flag in public. I go out of my way to make sarcastic, biting jokes about America’s hypocrisy on national holidays, and scoff at my parents — loyalists to the Democratic Party — when they repeat the well-known bit about how “despite its many flaws, this is still a great nation.”

Yes, I’m a millennial. How could you tell? Like many of my peers, I’m prone to a deadly combination of pessimistic cynicism and utopian idealism. To most of us, the claim that corruption and injustice are bugs and not features of the American Project is a mark of being démodé, in denial of the truth, and insufferably cringe.

Mind you, my brand of millennial idealism is a special one. Just like a snowflake, I am unlike all the others. Being a moderate liberal or conservative was cringe, but so was being a woke SJW (or a far-right anti-woke reactionary). I was determined to be more countercultural than those who postured themselves as the vanguard of the counterculture — thus my attraction to the emerging post-liberal movement. There was something so alluringly “meta” about the narrative that American liberal democracy is problematic not just for systematically barring certain groups of people from accessing its promises, but because the promise itself is a deceptive one. It claims to respect freedom of religion, speech, and local institutions, while in reality it cuts us off from that which is most essential: God, family, and local community.

Of course, it’s very easy to play this game while living in the comfort of a liberal democratic country. “Among the many freedoms enjoyed by citizens in a democracy” writes the journalist Shadi Hamid in his latest book The Case for American Power, “one of the least appreciated is the freedom to panic and assume that the worst is yet to come. This is a right (and luxury) that is generally lacking under dictatorship.”
