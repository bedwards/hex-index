All kidding aside, i find the creative arc of stranger things to be quite sad

Freddie deBoer · ·Jan 13, 2026 ·22 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Freddie deBoer delivers a stinging critique of modern serialized storytelling, arguing that the decline of Stranger Things is not just a creative failure but a symptom of a broken industry model driven by fan entitlement. He posits that the show's final seasons collapsed under the weight of its own success, sacrificing narrative integrity for the sake of appeasing an online audience that demands endless expansion.

The Cost of Authenticity

DeBoer opens his analysis by dismantling the show's central premise: its claim to be a loving homage to 1980s pop culture. He argues that the creators prioritized what feels "cool" to a 2020s audience over historical accuracy, a choice that undermines the show's credibility. "If you're demanding I take 80s pop culture references seriously, which the show is very much doing, then I demand that we tell the truth about 80s pop culture," deBoer writes. He points out the absurdity of a suburban Indiana teenager in the mid-1980s being an expert on the Butthole Surfers, an obscure and chaotic band that sold fewer than 75,000 albums by 1986. "Where did 16 year old Mike, perhaps the biggest normie in the core cast, learn about the Butthole Surfers in the mid 1980s?!?" he asks, highlighting how the show's nostalgia is curated for modern consumption rather than historical reflection.

This observation lands hard because it exposes a fundamental dishonesty in the show's DNA. The Duffer Brothers claim to celebrate a specific era, yet they sanitize and retrofit it to fit contemporary tastes. Critics might note that television is inherently a product of its time and often takes liberties with history for dramatic effect, but deBoer's point is that the show explicitly markets itself on its authenticity, making these deviations feel like a betrayal of its own contract with the viewer.

The Bloat of Expansion

The commentary then shifts to the structural rot that set in as the series expanded. DeBoer identifies a common trajectory in modern franchises: a tight, self-contained story that devolves into an impenetrable mess of lore and retcons. He contrasts the first season's grounded premise with the later seasons' convoluted mythology, where magic blood and haunted rocks replace the original, elegant sci-fi concept. "In its later seasons, Stranger Things did the worst thing a long-running television show can do: it retroactively made its earlier seasons worse," deBoer asserts.

He draws parallels to other franchises like Westworld and The Terminator, noting how they all suffered from the same "drift from clear themes and efficient storytelling towards sprawling, shaggy, seemingly directionless plots." The argument is that the industry's obsession with "worldbuilding" has become a trap, where creators feel a duty to fill in every gap rather than serve the story. "I really hate the term 'worldbuilding' at this point, with its suggestion that authors have a duty to ponderously build settings and backgrounds that are filled-in and complex for their own sake," he writes. This is a sharp rebuke of the current creative zeitgeist, suggesting that complexity is often mistaken for depth.

The show was originally intended to serve as a limited series... But the first season was a massive hit... and in the process of making more episodes, they gave viewers more of everything: more characters, more plotlines, more monsters.

The Tyranny of the Fanbase

Perhaps the most provocative part of deBoer's argument is his indictment of fan culture's influence on creative decisions. He suggests that the Duffer Brothers allowed online demands to dictate the narrative, leading to bloated character rosters and illogical plot resolutions. The "Justice for Barb" campaign is cited as a prime example of fans demanding that a narrative device be given a full arc, resulting in a story that felt forced and unearned. "The absurd 'Justice for Barb' campaign online demonstrates fans influencing fiction in the worst possible way," deBoer notes.

He argues that this dynamic prevents characters from having natural endings, forcing them to remain on screen long after their utility has expired. The result is a finale that feels like a checklist of emotional beats rather than an organic conclusion. "It felt like the Duffers had a literal checklist," he observes, pointing to the inclusion of marginal characters like Karen Wheeler and the forced heroism of side characters like Murray Baumann. This critique resonates with anyone who has watched a beloved show drag on until it lost its soul. While some might argue that fan engagement is a vital part of modern media, deBoer makes a compelling case that it can become a straitjacket that stifles genuine artistic risk.

Bottom Line

DeBoer's strongest contribution is his diagnosis of the structural forces that turn promising stories into bloated franchises, identifying the tension between artistic vision and audience expectation as the primary culprit. His argument is vulnerable only in its absolute dismissal of fan influence, which can occasionally spark genuine creative breakthroughs, but his core thesis—that the industry's hunger for endless content is killing storytelling—remains a vital warning. As streaming platforms continue to prioritize quantity over quality, this piece serves as a necessary reminder that sometimes, the most powerful story is the one that ends when it should.

All kidding aside, i find the creative arc of stranger things to be quite sad

by Freddie deBoer · · Read full article

My critical thoughts on Stranger Things have proven to be one of the more popular posts in the history of this newsletter. It was, of course, written in a somewhat hyperbolic fashion, which is something I like to do sometimes. (I don’t know why some readers seem unable to grasp that writers sometime exaggerate their feelings for argumentative or comedic or stylistic effect.) It seems a lot of people were also dissatisfied with that first batch of Season Five episodes too. Well, while I thought the finale was a bit better than the rest of the season, I still think the show really fell on its face in the final lap - and I think the specific ways the show got worse speaks to a lot of problems with modern pop culture storytelling. Stranger Things suffered from specific and particular issues, but also from structural problems with how serialized stories are written and produced now.

To be clear, and to be fair, Stranger Things was never going to be my… thing. That’s largely thanks to the nerd-worship issues I discussed in that earlier piece, which is kind of the heart of the show. And I am really deeply bothered by the way the Duffer Brothers play fast and loose with 80s pop culture history. I’ve mentioned several times that the idea of a 14 year old from suburban Indiana being obsessed with Kate Bush bothers me, and a few people have asked me why that particular detail would be a problem in a show about telekinetic teenagers and demons from another dimension. The reason is pretty straightforward: Stranger Things is an act of celebration and worship of 1980s pop culture. That’s superficially obvious and the Duffer Brothers have said it explicitly many times. Well, if you’re demanding I take 80s pop culture references seriously, which the show is very much doing, then I demand that we tell the truth about 80s pop culture. An honest show would have had that character be obsessed with Bon Jovi’s “Living on a Prayer” or “Higher Love” by Stevie Winwood, not “Running Up That Hill.” The trouble is that those songs aren’t cool to a 2020s audience, while Kate Bush will always be cool, so they did the cool thing instead of the authentically 80s thing. But the whole show is about 80s culture! It’s a stake in the heart of the show’s credibility ...
