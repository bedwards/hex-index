I recently made a video about trams and why I believe that they are the absolute best transportation method for cities, especially because of the way they act as a catalyst to great urbanism and how they're so compatible with great urban places, even pedestrianized streets. Today I live in Amsterdam with its efficient tram network where trams often run in dedicated lanes separated from car traffic and traffic lights automatically changed to allow them to sail unimpeded through intersections. I love taking the tram in Amsterdam, but I spent most of my adult life living in Toronto where the street cars are not like that. Riding the street car in Toronto is so immensely frustrating for me.

Not just because they're slow and inefficient, but because I know how much better they could be. And while Toronto isn't the only city with poorly designed street cars, they have somehow managed to do almost everything wrong, which means they're a great way to learn about what not to do when building and operating a transit service. So, let me explain how Toronto screwed up its street cars. About a hundred years ago, pretty much every city and town in the US and Canada had a street car, but almost all of them were torn out to make way for car infrastructure.

And there were only a handful of cities that retained any of their street car lines. Today, Toronto has the largest historic street car network in North America. But Toronto also tore out many street car lines despite strong opposition from residents because the Metro Roads Department said that the street cars were obstructing their progress in making the city more accessible to cars. There were also several lines that were replaced with subways and this was actually a stated goal of the Toronto subway system at the time.

On the opening day of the Young Subway in 1954, the guy who was in charge of the TTC, that's the Toronto Transit Commission, was quoted as saying, >> "The Queen Street subway should be started at once, eliminating 80% of the remaining street car operation in the downtown area and freeing many main streets for one-way traffic." Fortunately, after immense public pressure and the election of several pro- street car politicians, Toronto stopped removing street car lines in the 1970s.
