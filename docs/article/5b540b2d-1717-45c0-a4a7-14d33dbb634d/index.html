On November 4th, 2010, at 9:57 a.m., an Airbus A380, the largest commercial jet ever built, packed with 440 passengers, lifted off from Chani airport and rose into the warm sky above Singapore. 4 minutes later, Captain Richard Drepppney was about to turn off the seat belt signs when boom, an explosion. What was that? Boom!

A second explosion. Much louder. The whole plane shook. Was it an engine failure?

Two explosions. Two engine failures. No time to think. Bing.

The whole cockpit upted in a concert of alarms and warning lights. They were at 7,000 ft, far above the mountain tops. So, Decrepney hit the altitude hold button, which automatically brings the plane level and slows the engines. They felt the nose dip, but the engines were still running at full speed.

Bing. Dozens of alarms going off all at once. Decrepney pulled back on the thrust levers. The engines slowed.

Good. But if he had to slow the engines manually, then that meant that the auto thrust system wasn't working. Something was seriously wrong. In addition to Captain Drepne, there were actually four other pilots in the cockpit that day.

A first officer, a second officer, and two extra pilots who wouldn't normally be there, but they were there to conduct routine evaluations. What none of them knew at the time was that an oil pipe in engine 2 burst, melting and fracturing a 350 lb metal turbine disc. That was the first bang. And hurling metal pieces out of the engine at one and a half times the speed of sound.

That was the second bang. This engine explosion caused the most damage that any Airbus aircraft had ever experienced in the air. Metal pieces tore through the left wing. punctured fuel tanks because the fuel tanks in commercial aircraft are located inside the wings.

Shrapnel shredded wires, destroyed electronic and computer systems and splintered into over 200 pieces that pelted the side of the passenger compartment. The head flight attendant, Michael von Reth, said that it sounded like a thousand marbles being thrown onto a corrugated iron roof. Passengers seated on the left side of the plane from row 31 back looked out their windows and saw the left wing bent open like a sardine can with jet fuel spewing out into the air.
