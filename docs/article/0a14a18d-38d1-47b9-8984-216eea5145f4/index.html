I can't get the image out of my head. A little 5-year-old standing in the cold wearing his bright little blue bunny hat and his Spider-Man backpack surrounded by federal agents. He was on his way home from preschool when Immigration and Customs Enforcement, ICE, or CBP or whatever agency is running these things these days, took him and his father into custody. Now, this thing didn't look like law enforcement.

Not even a little bit. It looked like a huge mistake. the kind that shouldn't happen in a country that claims to care about children or due process, if those things mean anything. Now, when I posted the image of little Liam in my Instagram stories, other journalists I know from around the world couldn't believe their eyes.

I can't blame them. One of them simply responded, "His name is Liam Ko Ramos. Ko actually means bunny in Spanish, and his family was lawfully in the United States. But what happened to him next trigger a legal battle that exposed just how far immigration enforcement is willing to go to violate the constitution.

You might say this is not immigration enforcement, not in the sense that we know it. This wasn't a mistake. It was real and it was deliberate. ICE agents abducted Liam and his father by force and transported them more than 1300 miles from their home in Minnesota to a detention facility in Dilly, Texas.

Dilly, that notorious detention facility where children and parents do not have access to sufficient portable water or potable food. Where bright lights are left on 24 hours a day and officers threaten to separate families if they don't follow every order. I'll tell you more about Dilly later, but that's where they sent Liam and his father. Afterward, Marcos Charles, an acting executive associate director of ICE, told reporters that Liam's father tried to flee the authorities, effectively abandoning his child.

His school said that ICE then try to use him as bait to get at other people in the house. Supposedly, the mom was in there and ICE tried to get her, too. ICE could have left him there. They could have exercised what is known as prosecutorial discretion, but instead they took a preschooler to a detention camp.
