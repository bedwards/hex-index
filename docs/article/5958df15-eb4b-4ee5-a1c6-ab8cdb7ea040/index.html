Tesla batteries : are they the holy grail of climate change?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·May 26, 2019 ·11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace reframes the global energy transition not as a distant policy goal, but as an immediate engineering necessity driven by the fragility of our aging infrastructure. While much of the discourse focuses on carbon reduction, Borlace argues that the true catalyst for battery adoption is the sheer inability of centralized, fossil-fuel-dependent grids to withstand extreme weather and sudden demand spikes. This is a pragmatic, ground-level view that cuts through the political noise to reveal a market already in motion.

The Engineering Imperative

Borlace opens by grounding the abstract concept of "grid storage" in a personal reality: the need to capture solar energy when the sun is shining but the user is at work. He notes that "once you scale all this up to the level of a National Grid... it's this basic principle that makes the whole renewable energy thing a genuinely realistic option for displacing fossil fuels." This framing is crucial because it shifts the conversation from environmental idealism to functional reliability. The argument holds up well when examining real-world disasters; Borlace points to Hurricane Maria in Puerto Rico, where "Tesla installed or commenced installing no fewer than 11,000 solar and storage projects on the island" within a year of the storm. This rapid deployment highlights how decentralized storage offers resilience that traditional, centralized power plants simply cannot match during catastrophic events.

The speed of response in these systems is the hidden hero of the story. Borlace contrasts the sluggish reaction of conventional power stations with the lightning-fast capabilities of modern batteries. "Do we sense something on the system and the automated protection kicks in then a battery can operate at around 200 to 250 milliseconds so very much sub 1 second response," he explains. This technical detail is the piece's strongest evidence; it proves that batteries are not just backup generators, but active grid stabilizers that prevent cascading failures. Critics might note that while battery response times are impressive, the sheer volume of storage required to replace baseload fossil fuel generation entirely remains a significant economic hurdle, though Borlace suggests the cost curve is already bending.

The battery is far swifter and more accurate than the conventional power station, turning a potential grid collapse into a managed fluctuation.

The Global Race and the Smart Grid

The narrative then expands to a global competition, where the author observes that "it's actually South Korea thanks to LG Chem and Samsung who topped the charts for the most energy storage installed in 2018." This detail disrupts the common assumption that the United States is the undisputed leader in energy innovation. Borlace uses this to illustrate that the race for supremacy is driven by pragmatic grid operators who need to smooth out the "peaks and troughs in energy demand," regardless of the political climate.

In Europe, the focus shifts to the "smart grid," a system that integrates domestic storage with smart meters to create a decentralized network. Borlace describes how these meters "know when excess energy is being generated in the home and can send that energy back to the grid to be used almost instantaneously wherever it's most needed." This vision of a distributed network, where electric vehicles act as mobile storage units, represents a fundamental shift in how energy is consumed. However, the author acknowledges a significant barrier in the United States: the regulatory framework. He notes that while support for a smart grid became federal policy in 2007, "very little has happened to actually implement such a system" because utility companies find it easier to replace old parts than to justify the cost of a new infrastructure model.

The Human and Economic Dimension

Perhaps the most compelling part of Borlace's analysis is the connection between energy infrastructure and human development, particularly in the Global South. He argues that the most significant beneficiaries of this revolution could be "the vast continent of Africa," where nearly 600 million people lack reliable electricity. Borlace suggests that "energy storage that will really be transformational to people's lives down there" by enabling off-grid and mini-grids that bypass the need for massive, centralized infrastructure.

The socioeconomic implications are profound. Borlace writes that better access to power "provides essential power to build better houses and infrastructure all of which will greatly increase the resilience and ability of communities to cope with future extreme weather events." He takes this a step further, linking energy access to education and demographics: "history also shows that better educated populations particularly with better educated women and girls have lower birth rates." This is a bold, holistic argument that positions battery technology not just as a climate solution, but as a tool for stabilizing global population growth and improving quality of life. While some might argue that technology alone cannot solve deep-seated poverty, the correlation between energy access and educational outcomes is well-documented, lending weight to Borlace's optimistic outlook.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest contribution is his insistence that the energy transition is already happening, driven by engineering necessity and economic opportunity rather than political mandates. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its relative silence on the supply chain constraints for battery materials, which could slow the exponential growth he predicts. Readers should watch for how regulatory bodies in the US eventually adapt to the reality that replacing old grid components is no longer a viable long-term strategy.

Robots aren't going to do this; we have to take the entire energy complex of the United States and convert all of that infrastructure from fossil fuel nuclear to distributed renewable energy.
