Tesla batteries : Are they the Holy Grail of Climate Change?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·May 26, 2019 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace doesn't just ask if batteries are the Holy Grail of climate change; he proves they are the only thing keeping the lights on when the grid collapses. While most discussions focus on solar panels or wind turbines, Borlace zeroes in on the unsung hero of the renewable revolution: the ability to store energy instantly. He argues that without massive, rapid-response storage, the transition away from fossil fuels is a fantasy, not a plan.

The Speed of Survival

Borlace opens with a personal anecdote about his own cabin, noting that "if you scale all this up to the level of a National Grid and it's this basic principle that makes the whole renewable energy thing a genuinely realistic option for displacing fossil fuels." This framing is crucial because it shifts the conversation from environmental idealism to engineering necessity. He illustrates this with the aftermath of Hurricane Maria in Puerto Rico, where Tesla deployed 11,000 solar and storage projects to restore power to hundreds of locations. The scale of the disaster highlighted a terrifying vulnerability: our centralized power generation facilities are fragile in the face of extreme weather.

The argument gains even more weight when Borlace contrasts the sluggishness of traditional power plants with the lightning speed of battery systems. He cites grid operators in South Australia who revealed that "a battery can operate at around 200 to 250 milliseconds so very much sub 1 second response to be able to protect the systems." This is a game-changer. While skeptics like Australian billionaire Mike Cannon-Brookes initially dismissed the world's largest battery as a "gimmick," the reality of grid stabilization proved otherwise. The battery is "far swifter and more accurate" than conventional stations, a fact that has silenced many detractors.

"The battery is far swifter and more accurate... it's not just solving the problem, it's redefining how we keep the lights on."

Critics might note that while batteries are fast, they are not yet cheap enough to replace all fossil fuel peaker plants globally. However, Borlace's evidence suggests that the cost curve is dropping faster than the political will to upgrade infrastructure is rising.

The Global Race and the Smart Grid

The narrative then expands to a global stage, where the race for energy supremacy is heating up. Borlace points out that while Tesla grabs headlines, "it's actually South Korea thanks to LG Chem and Samsung who topped the charts for the most energy storage installed in 2018." This detail is often overlooked in American-centric coverage, reminding us that the technology is a worldwide effort. The growth is no longer steady; it is "climbing a curve that looks pretty exponential."

In Europe, the focus has shifted to the "behind the meter" approach, where small battery systems in homes combine with solar panels to create a decentralized network. Borlace describes the emerging "smart grid" as a system where "smart meters know when excess energy is being generated in the home and can send that energy back to the grid to be used almost instantaneously." This transforms every home into a potential power plant. Furthermore, he envisions a future where electric vehicles act as mobile batteries, set to "charge or discharge depending on the needs of the individual driver and the overall needs of the grid system."

The American Stagnation

Despite the global momentum, the United States faces a unique hurdle: an aging infrastructure built in the 1890s. Borlace notes that "70% of the United States electrical network has components that are more than 25 years old," yet regulatory frameworks stall the transition to a smart grid. He suggests that utility companies find it "much easier... to justify the cost of simply replacing existing components with new parts than it is for them to show how they'll recoup the cost of implementing an entirely new smart grid system." This regulatory inertia is a massive bottleneck, potentially fueled by political lobbying to slow the shift away from fossil fuels.

Yet, the market is pushing forward regardless. Borlace highlights a record-breaking 33 gigawatts of energy storage projects in the pipeline. He quotes economist Jeremy Rifkin, who predicts a massive employment surge: "we have one last surge of massive employment involving semi skilled unskilled professional and conceptual labor." This reframes the energy transition not as a job killer, but as the largest infrastructure project in human history. Robots, Rifkin argues, "aren't going to do this."

The Human Impact

The most compelling part of Borlace's coverage is the focus on the developing world. In Africa, where 600 million people lack reliable electricity, energy storage is not just about climate change; it is about survival and development. Borlace writes that "new technologies that combine renewables like solar and wind with energy storage... will give these people greater independence and provide a secure source of heat light and power." This moves the conversation beyond carbon emissions to human dignity.

He connects access to electricity with education, noting that "better educated populations particularly with better educated women and girls have lower birth rates." This is a profound insight: the battery is a tool for demographic stability and economic growth. As India aims to become the "global solar hub" with 14 new solar parks, the integration of storage is the key to unlocking this potential for 1.4 billion people.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest argument is that energy storage is the linchpin that turns intermittent renewable sources into a reliable, global power system. His coverage effectively bridges the gap between high-level engineering and human impact, showing that batteries are as vital for a child studying in rural Africa as they are for stabilizing the grid in South Australia. The biggest vulnerability in his narrative is the political and regulatory inertia in the United States, which threatens to slow the very transition that the rest of the world is racing to embrace.
