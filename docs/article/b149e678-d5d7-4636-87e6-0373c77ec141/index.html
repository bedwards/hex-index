Private Equity’s Ruthless Takeover Of The Last Affordable Housing In America

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Sep 18, 2024 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union exposes a quiet crisis where the last affordable housing in America is being systematically stripped of its dignity by financial engineers who view human beings as captive revenue streams. The author's most disturbing revelation is not just that rents are rising, but that the business model relies entirely on the physical immobility of the residents, turning a home into a trap. This is not a story about market forces; it is a story about the weaponization of poverty against those who have nowhere left to run.

The Anatomy of a Trap

The piece opens by dismantling the stigma often attached to manufactured housing communities, reframing them as the "last Oasis of affordable housing in this country." More Perfect Union writes, "22 million Americans call them home for a reason... they're often the last line of defense against homelessness." This framing is crucial because it shifts the narrative from a niche real estate issue to a national safety net failure. The author effectively argues that these parks are not merely housing options but the foundation of the American housing market for the working class.

The core of the argument rests on the unique vulnerability of the residents. Unlike apartment dwellers who can move to a cheaper unit, manufactured home owners own their structures but rent the land. Moving a home costs between $10,000 and $20,000, a sum most residents cannot access. More Perfect Union notes, "Wall Street investors... figured out that folks living in manufactured housing communities are trapped." This insight is the linchpin of the entire piece; without this lack of mobility, the predatory pricing model collapses. The author illustrates this with the chilling admission of an industry insider, Frank Rolfe, who is quoted as saying, "owning a mobile home park is like owning a waffle house and having your customers chained to the booth." This quote is devastating in its clarity, stripping away any pretense of a "free market" and revealing the coercion at the heart of the transaction.

"It's like you genetically bred a superior predator and let him loose in a target-rich environment."

The author's coverage of the financial mechanics is equally sharp. Private equity firms are not buying these parks to improve them; they are buying them for the "reliable stream of payments from captive tenants." More Perfect Union points out that these assets offer "one of the highest annual returns of any asset in real estate, higher than offices, commercial, industrial, you name it." This data point is vital because it explains why the influx of capital is accelerating despite the human cost. Critics might argue that private capital is necessary to maintain aging infrastructure, but the author counters this by showing that investors often neglect maintenance entirely to maximize short-term yields.

The Human Cost of Financial Engineering

The piece grounds its economic analysis in the harrowing stories of residents like Meline Beckett, a retiree who watched her rent jump 50% after a private equity firm took over her community. More Perfect Union writes, "I haven't been poor... I worked for 45 years. There is no American Dream anymore. All it is is survival." This personal testimony transforms abstract statistics into a visceral reality. The author details how residents are being forced to choose between rent and essentials, with one resident, Holly Hook, having to take on two roommates just to avoid homelessness. The narrative effectively highlights the cruelty of the situation: these are people who have followed the rules their entire lives, only to be punished by a system designed to extract their remaining wealth.

The coverage also exposes the corporate structures used to hide accountability. The author traces the ownership of a park in Flint back to Alden Global Capital, a hedge fund known for stripping newspaper assets. More Perfect Union writes, "Homes of America is just a shell within a shell within a shell." This structural opacity is a key part of the problem, as it allows executives like Randall Smith to live in mansions while residents suffer from contaminated water and evictions. The author notes that the county prosecutor had to charge the local LLC with operating without a license because the parent company refused to fix the water problem. This detail underscores the author's point that these corporations are "putting profits ahead of the safety and welfare of our local citizens."

The Political Battle for Survival

The final section of the piece shifts from diagnosis to resistance, detailing the legislative efforts in Michigan to protect residents. The author describes a bill that would require landlords to justify rent increases above inflation and limit fees. More Perfect Union writes, "The legislation would require landlords to justify any rent increase above the rate of inflation... and force landlords to pay fair market value for a home if a resident is evicted." This is presented as a "modest start," a realistic assessment that acknowledges the difficulty of the fight. However, the author reveals a dark turn in the political process: the industry association, which initially supported the bill, withdrew its backing after receiving significant donations from the very landlords the bill sought to regulate. More Perfect Union writes, "That money is some of the people that I know... their grocery money... that's the medicine that they're doing without." This juxtaposition of corporate donations against residents' survival needs is a powerful indictment of the political system.

A counterargument worth considering is whether state-level regulation can truly compete with the capital power of national private equity firms. The author hints at this difficulty by noting that the bill is currently stalled, but the focus remains on the moral imperative of the residents' fight. The piece concludes by framing this local struggle as part of a larger battle for "meaningful control of our neighborhoods," suggesting that the fate of manufactured housing is a bellwether for the future of American housing affordability.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union delivers a searing indictment of how private equity has turned affordable housing into a predatory extraction machine, supported by irrefutable evidence of resident entrapment and corporate negligence. The piece's greatest strength is its ability to connect the cold mechanics of financial engineering with the warm, desperate reality of human survival, making the abstract concrete. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the political reality that the proposed solutions are being actively sabotaged by the very industry they aim to regulate, leaving the ultimate outcome uncertain despite the moral clarity of the argument.
