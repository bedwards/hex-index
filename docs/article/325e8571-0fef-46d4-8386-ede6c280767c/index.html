Xcel Energy's Green Advocacy Backfires, Beautifully

Various · Energy Bad Boys ·Mar 21, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

This piece delivers a stinging indictment of corporate strategy, arguing that a utility giant's current crisis is not a victimization by policy, but the direct result of its own aggressive lobbying. The author's most distinctive claim is that Xcel Energy's plea to keep coal plants open is a calculated pivot to protect profits after years of championing the very regulations that now threaten grid stability. For busy listeners tracking the intersection of energy markets and public policy, this reframes a standard reliability warning into a story of institutional self-sabotage.

The Self-Inflicted Wound

The author, writing under the byline Various, opens by dismantling the narrative that Xcel Energy is a victim of circumstance. Instead, the piece posits that the utility is "simply reaping the entirely foreseeable consequences of its own self-serving actions." This framing is crucial because it shifts the blame from abstract market forces to specific, deliberate corporate choices. The commentary highlights a stark contradiction: while Xcel publicly advocated for carbon-free mandates and funded climate groups, it simultaneously built a business model dependent on ratepayer-funded infrastructure expansion.

The author points to the company's "Steel for Fuel" initiative as a prime example of this duality. Described as a strategy to swap fossil generation for wind and solar, the initiative was marketed with the promise that "everybody benefits." Yet, the text argues that the reality has been a divergence of interests where "only Xcel seems to have benefited, as consumers have been stuck with incredibly high-cost energy while Xcel's profits have exploded." This observation lands with force because it connects the abstract concept of ESG goals to the tangible reality of rising electricity bills.

Xcel is not a victim, nor is it brave for finally bringing attention to the need to maintain our existing generating resources.

Critics might note that utilities operate within complex regulatory frameworks where they must balance competing mandates, and that blaming a single company for systemic grid challenges oversimplifies the role of state legislators and market dynamics. However, the author's evidence regarding Xcel's active lobbying for the very laws that accelerated coal retirements suggests a level of agency that goes beyond passive compliance.

The Comanche Report and the Reliability Gap

The core of the argument rests on a detailed analysis of "The Comanche Report," a filing Xcel submitted to the Colorado Public Utilities Commission. The report admits to a "capacity deficit," a technical term the author translates bluntly as the system not meeting reliability requirements. The commentary notes that Xcel found "there are no reasonable alternatives to returning Comanche Unit 3 to service," a admission that undermines the company's previous assertions that renewable resources could seamlessly replace baseload power.

The author draws a sharp parallel to historical context, referencing the accelerated retirement of Comanche Unit 3 from 2040 to 2030. This decision, the piece argues, was a "disaster of Xcel's own making" that increased costs by $8 billion alone. By weaving in the concept of resource adequacy, the commentary explains that the utility underestimated the time and capital required to replace reliable thermal generation with intermittent sources. The author writes, "The cost increase occurs because it accelerates the timeline in which investments must be made to replace the plant, which earn a rate of return for the utility paid for by ratepayers."

This section effectively uses the utility's own data against it. The author notes that under all analyzed scenarios, "the loss of load probability exceeds the planning standard of less than 0.1." In some cases, the risk is "over 10 times greater than planning best practices." This statistical evidence supports the author's thesis that the reliability crisis was predictable, not accidental.

The beatings will continue until policies improve.

The commentary suggests that the utility's investor presentation reveals a different priority than the somber tone of the reliability report. While the Comanche Report warns of operational challenges, the investor deck is described as "practically giddy" about the rate base expansion. The author points out that spending on generation, transmission, and distribution has surged nearly threefold since 2009, driven by the very wind and solar projects that now fail to meet reliability standards without coal backup.

The Profit Motive Behind the Green Pivot

The final layer of the argument examines the financial incentives driving these decisions. The author argues that Xcel's strategy was designed to increase the rate base, which directly correlates to corporate profits. The text states that the company is "planning to spend huge sums on solar, wind, battery storage, and transmission lines" with the expectation that these investments will generate returns for shareholders, regardless of whether they solve the reliability issue.

The commentary highlights a potential 55 percent spike in residential electricity prices by 2029 if load growth does not meet forecasts. This projection serves as the ultimate consequence of the strategy. The author concludes that the utility's dilemma is a result of trying to "feed the crocodiles by funding green advocacy groups and lobbying for carbon-free mandates," only to find that the new gas plants intended as a backup were blocked by the same groups they cultivated.

If there are blackouts, there will be backlash, and Xcel will deserve every bit of it.

This conclusion ties the financial analysis back to the public interest, suggesting that the current situation is a failure of accountability. The author implies that the utility gambled on the ability to reverse course when the risks materialized, a gamble that has now left the grid vulnerable.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its relentless focus on corporate agency, successfully reframing a grid reliability crisis as a direct outcome of strategic lobbying and investment choices rather than external policy failures. Its biggest vulnerability lies in potentially underestimating the speed of renewable cost declines and the difficulty of securing regulatory approval for new gas infrastructure in a low-carbon policy environment. Readers should watch for how the Colorado Public Utilities Commission balances the immediate need for reliability extensions against the long-term mandates Xcel itself helped create.
