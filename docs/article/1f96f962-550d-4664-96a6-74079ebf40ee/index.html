Ukraine's Submarine & Shadow Fleet Strikes - UUVs, Novorossiysk & The Energy War at Sea

Commentary by Brian Edwards

Perun delivers a rare, granular dissection of a naval first: a drone submarine striking a manned submarine inside a heavily fortified Russian port. While much of the war coverage focuses on land battles or financial aid, this piece zeroes in on the silent, underwater revolution that is quietly reshaping naval doctrine. The author doesn't just report the explosion; they reconstruct the entire tactical puzzle, from the geography of Novorossiysk to the specific paint jobs of Russian submarines, offering a level of forensic detail rarely seen in open-source analysis.

The Asymmetry of the Deep

The core of Perun's argument rests on the evolution of Ukraine's naval strategy. He notes that while Ukraine lacks a traditional fleet, it has mastered "highly asymmetrical" warfare using drones and missiles to push the Russian surface fleet back. However, Perun points out a critical gap in this strategy: "They don't work particularly well if the target is submerged." This limitation forced a strategic pivot. The Russian Black Sea Fleet relies on "improved Kilo" class diesel-electric submarines, which are quiet, dangerous, and capable of launching land-attack cruise missiles or laying mines to strangle Ukrainian trade.

Perun writes, "The first is that some of Russia's improved kilos can launch land attack cruise missiles... that missile attack might come with limited warning and from an unexpected angle." This threat, combined with the potential for Russian submarines to interdict merchant shipping, made them high-value targets despite their submerged nature. The author effectively argues that Ukraine's previous success in hitting the submarine Rostov-on-Don while it was on the surface was a fluke of circumstance that couldn't be repeated once the fleet retreated to the more secure port of Novorossiysk. This sets the stage for the technological leap that followed.

"Ukraine has managed to achieve their first major success against a Russian submarine by just hitting it when it was above water... but there are some reasons it might not have been easily repeatable."

The Fortress Breached

The narrative then shifts to the geography of the attack, where Perun's analysis shines. He describes Novorossiysk not just as a port, but as a "fortress inside a fortress," protected by natural barriers and boom defenses. The author meticulously walks the reader through the challenge: an approaching vessel must navigate a constrained opening without detection. Yet, Ukrainian footage reveals the breach happened exactly where it was supposed to be impossible. Perun observes, "If a subsurface threat did make it into this harbor, then reaching those civilian vessels probably wouldn't be that difficult," highlighting the vulnerability of the entire fleet to a single point of failure.

The evidence presented is compelling. Perun analyzes the released video, noting, "Pretty significant detonation that does appear to begin below the surface of the water." He cross-references this with satellite imagery to identify the specific target, even going so far as to suggest the victim was the B-271 Kolpino based on unique paint jobs—a level of detail that transforms a news report into a historical record. Critics might note that without official confirmation from the Ukrainian military, the identification of the specific submarine remains speculative. However, the convergence of visual evidence and the timing of the strike makes the case for a successful UUV (Unmanned Underwater Vehicle) attack overwhelmingly strong.

The Unmanned Future

Perhaps the most significant takeaway is the implication for future naval warfare. Perun suggests that the specific design of the weapon remains a mystery, but the capability is undeniable. He posits that the system was likely a hybrid of a torpedo and a drone, potentially the "larger models" of the Toka series, capable of traveling long distances while carrying a substantial warhead. The author writes, "The Ukrainians are claiming this was an attack by a UV, an unmanned underwater vehicle, basically a submarine drone. With the footage we have, I think we can rule out a lot of other potential explanations."

This event signals a paradigm shift. As Perun puts it, "Navies around the world might be taking from the fact that once again another naval warfare first has been carried out by a country without much of a navy." The barrier to entry for naval power has been lowered; a small nation can now threaten the most expensive assets of a superpower without needing a single manned submarine. The author concludes that this technology is "unlikely to get put back in the bottle anytime soon," suggesting that the era of the invulnerable submarine is over.

"This is the most interesting part of the piece: a country without a navy just demonstrated a capability that traditional naval powers are still struggling to develop."

Bottom Line

Perun's coverage is a masterclass in connecting tactical details to strategic implications, proving that the most decisive battles of this war may be fought in the dark, silent depths of the Black Sea. The piece's greatest strength is its forensic reconstruction of the attack, which lends undeniable credibility to the claim of a drone-submarine strike. Its only vulnerability is the inherent uncertainty of open-source intelligence regarding the specific technical specifications of the UUV used, but the operational result speaks for itself. For busy readers, this is the definitive analysis of how the underwater domain has been irrevocably changed.
