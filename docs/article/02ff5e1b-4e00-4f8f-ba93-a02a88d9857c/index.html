Kenny Easwaran challenges a deeply ingrained intuition: that memory is a faithful archive of the past. Instead, he argues that our minds function less like a closet of folded sweaters and more like a dynamic Lego set, where pieces are disassembled, stored separately, and reassembled with inevitable variations. This distinction isn't just a psychological curiosity; it upends centuries of philosophical assumptions about how we justify our beliefs and what it means to "know" something based on recollection.

The Lego Metaphor and the Failure of the Archive

Easwaran begins by dismantling the "closet drawer" model of memory, where experiences are stored intact and retrieved unchanged. He writes, "we often might think the way that memory works is something like the way our closet drawers work... however the basic idea is that memory works a bit more like lego storage." This analogy is strikingly effective because it immediately visualizes the fragility and reconstructive nature of recall. If we accept this premise, the philosophical project of defining memory shifts from finding a static record to understanding a generative process.

The author notes that this constructive nature poses a "significant challenge to the causal theory of memory," which has long been the dominant view in philosophy. While many philosophers assume memory is purely preservative, Easwaran insists that "our confidence in the causal theory has been to a certain extent unfounded" because it ignores the empirical reality of how memory actually functions. He argues that we must update our theories to account for the fact that memories are not just retrieved; they are built.

"If there is nothing lost by construction at one stage there need be nothing lost by reconstruction at the other; if there is something gained by construction at one stage there might also be something gained by reconstruction at the other."

This insight forces a re-evaluation of what counts as knowledge. If a memory is built from fragments and filled in with general knowledge, can it still justify a belief? Easwaran suggests that the answer lies in accepting that memory is a "generative epistemic source," capable of producing new justified beliefs rather than just preserving old ones.

The Mechanics of Construction and Reconstruction

To make his case, Easwaran leans on the work of psychologists Alba and Hasher, breaking down the constructive process into four distinct phases: selection, abstraction, interpretation, and integration. He explains that "selection only certain incoming stimuli are selected for encoding," meaning we never store the full sensory reality of an event. Furthermore, "abstraction the meaning of a message is abstracted from the syntactic and lexical features," which explains why we remember the gist of a conversation but rarely the exact words.

The author highlights that "interpretation relevant prior knowledge is invoked," allowing us to fill in gaps with what we already know. This is where the danger—and the utility—of memory lies. As Easwaran puts it, "integration a holistic representation is formed from the products of the selection abstraction and interpretation processes." The result is a memory trace that is a hybrid of the original event and the mind's current state.

Critics might note that emphasizing the constructive nature of memory risks sliding into skepticism, where no memory can be trusted. However, Easwaran carefully distinguishes between the process of construction and the validity of the outcome. He argues that even though "whatever information was selected for representation and is still accessible is used together with general knowledge roughly to generate a hypothesis about what might have happened," this hypothesis can still be a reliable source of knowledge if the generative process is properly constrained.

From Preservation to Generation

The most provocative claim in the piece is the redefinition of memory's role in epistemology. Easwaran writes, "generative here is going to mean you can become justified in believing things through your memory that you were never actually believing before." This flips the script on the traditional view that memory only preserves what was already believed. By acknowledging that memory can add content during encoding or retrieval, he opens the door to a more flexible understanding of how we learn and reason over time.

He distinguishes between "construction," which can happen during encoding or consolidation, and "reconstruction," which occurs at retrieval. "I will modify this usage slightly allowing construction to refer also to relevant processes if there are any occurring during consolidation," he clarifies, ensuring that the philosophical framework captures the entire lifecycle of a memory trace. This nuance is crucial; it prevents the theory from collapsing into a simple "reconstruction is bad" narrative.

"As long as we are in the grip of a picture on which a memory trace or engram is a faithful record of experience we will be inclined to suppose that remembering must be a matter of retrieving the trace unaltered."

Easwaran's argument is that we must let go of this "grip" to make progress. The evidence from psychology suggests that the trace itself is constructed, and therefore, the act of remembering is inherently active. This doesn't mean our memories are fiction; it means they are dynamic simulations that serve a functional purpose in navigating the world.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's strongest move is successfully bridging the gap between empirical psychology and abstract epistemology, proving that the "Lego" model of memory demands a revision of how we define knowledge. His biggest vulnerability lies in the difficulty of establishing clear boundaries between helpful construction and destructive fabrication, a line that the paper sketches but does not fully draw. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: memory is not a video recording, but a creative act that we must trust with a grain of critical awareness.

"If there is something gained by construction at one stage there might also be something gained by reconstruction at the other."
