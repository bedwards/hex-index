Spotify's phony war on bots

Commentary by Brian Edwards

Benn Jordan exposes a terrifying reality for the modern independent creator: your livelihood can be erased by an algorithm with zero recourse, no warning, and no apology. This isn't just a story about a glitch; it is a forensic dismantling of the multi-layered middlemen that now sit between artists and their audience, revealing a system where financial incentives are misaligned to the point of self-destruction.

The Anatomy of a Digital Erasure

Jordan begins with a personal nightmare that serves as a microcosm for a systemic failure. After two decades of grinding as a professional musician, he woke up to find his entire catalog—23 albums and over 100 million streams—scrubbed from every major platform. "My life's work was scrubbed," he writes, describing the panic of discovering that his income had vanished without a single notification. The distributor, TuneCore, initially claimed the removal was due to an email sent on a weekend, a bureaucratic excuse Jordan rightly dismantles: "The person who deletes 23 albums with hundreds of millions of streams works weekends, but the staff that notifies or warns the musician that their income is being removed doesn't work weekends."

This incident highlights a critical vulnerability in the digital economy: the centralization of power in the hands of intermediaries who operate with opaque algorithms. Jordan notes that while the music was eventually reinstated, the damage was permanent. "The dead songs and albums got booted from playlists and radio stations and my Spotify listeners permanently dropped by 18.1%," he explains. This isn't a temporary setback; it is a long-term algorithmic scar that will haunt his career for years, representing millions of dollars in lost revenue that can only be recovered through litigation.

The core of the problem is that it's literally more cost-effective for these platforms to not have you as a customer than it is to try and solve your issue.

The Middleman Industrial Complex

Jordan's analysis then pivots to the historical context, tracing how the industry evolved from a curated gatekeeper model to a bloated, inefficient pyramid scheme. He points out that when Apple launched iTunes, Steve Jobs boasted about curating quality, but the economics quickly forced a shift to volume. "We needed to create a multi-billion dollar industry between the musician and the person who listens to their music," Jordan argues, mocking the necessity of the digital distributor layer. He details how fees stack up: Apple takes 30%, the distributor takes a cut, and the label takes another, leaving the artist with a fraction of the pie.

The consolidation of these entities has created a conflict of interest that is difficult to untangle. Jordan reveals that TuneCore's parent company, Believe, is being acquired by Warner Music Group, which also holds deep investments in Spotify and Amazon. "When Warner and Believe acquisition goes through," Jordan notes, the lines between the platforms hosting the music, the distributors delivering it, and the labels profiting from it become dangerously blurred. This vertical integration means that the entities responsible for policing fraud are often the same ones profiting from the very ecosystem they claim to protect.

Critics might argue that digital distributors provide essential infrastructure that artists cannot manage alone, such as metadata handling and royalty collection. However, Jordan's data suggests that for many, the cost of this infrastructure outweighs the benefits. He found that "well over half of the musicians on streaming services are paying money for you to pay money to listen to their music," a paradox where the transaction costs exceed the value generated.

The Witch Hunt for Artificial Streams

The most damning part of Jordan's coverage is his examination of Spotify's crackdown on "fake streams." To combat fraud, the platform has stopped paying royalties on tracks with fewer than 1,000 annual streams, a move Jordan suggests actually incentivizes the very behavior it seeks to punish. "Congratulations Spotify, you may have just actually made fake streaming a net positive for musicians," he writes, noting that the threshold creates a perverse incentive for artists to game the system just to get paid.

Even more troubling is the financial penalty structure. Jordan alleges that Spotify is now fining distributors $10 per song suspected of receiving 90% artificial streams, a cost that is passed directly down to the independent artist. "Now DistroKid... are acting as a mouthpiece for Spotify and fining the musicians $10 per song to be considered to go back onto Spotify," he explains. This creates a scenario where independent artists are financially penalized for the actions of bad actors, while major labels, who own significant equity in Spotify, are shielded from these costs.

If you are an independent musician who is participating in the streaming economy both you and your listeners are paying these companies in the transaction of selling your music, chances are that your intellectual property actually has negative value right now.

Jordan's data dive reveals a stark disparity in visibility: artists on major labels are roughly 2,000% more likely to be featured on top playlists than those using independent distributors. This suggests that the platform's algorithms are not neutral arbiters of quality but are engineered to favor the entities that hold the most financial leverage. The result is a system where 9% of musicians have had their music banned or suspended, and the vast majority of those affected are paying subscription fees to distributors who offer no safety net.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's piece is a powerful indictment of a broken infrastructure where the risk is borne entirely by the creator while the rewards are siphoned off by a consolidated oligopoly. The strongest part of his argument is the demonstration of how algorithmic opacity and financial penalties create a hostile environment for independent work. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the lack of a clear alternative; without a viable, decentralized distribution model, artists remain trapped in a system that can delete their livelihoods with a single click. Readers should watch for the outcome of the Warner-Believe acquisition, as it may further cement this concentration of power, making the next wave of artist-led innovation even more critical.
