How Corporations Are Secretly Poisoning Our Food Supply

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Jun 4, 2025 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union delivers a harrowing indictment of the American food supply, arguing that a corporate cover-up spanning decades has turned family farms into toxic waste sites. The piece's most chilling claim isn't just that "forever chemicals" are ubiquitous, but that the very fertilizer spreading them across the nation was legally encouraged by the government while manufacturers knew it was lethal. For busy listeners trying to navigate a complex health crisis, this narrative cuts through the noise by anchoring a global environmental disaster in the personal devastation of a single Michigan cattle farmer.

The Human Cost of Regulatory Failure

The article opens not with statistics, but with the visceral fear of Jason Grastic, a third-generation farmer whose livelihood was erased overnight. More Perfect Union writes, "Without warning, the state of Michigan had shut down Jason's farm, declaring it contaminated and unusable." This framing is effective because it immediately personalizes a bureaucratic failure, transforming abstract policy into a family tragedy. The author details how Grastic received a seizure notice via Zoom, a modern, impersonal method for delivering life-destroying news. The commentary here is sharp: the system protected the chemical, not the farmer.

The core of the argument rests on the mechanism of contamination: biosolids. These are the leftover byproducts of wastewater treatment, which the EPA has long promoted as cheap fertilizer. More Perfect Union explains that "the EPA has encouraged the use of biosolids as a cheap fertilizer," despite the fact that these sludge piles are essentially concentrated sinks for industrial waste. This is a crucial distinction often missed in broader coverage; the poison didn't just fall from the sky or leak from a pipe, it was legally spread on the land as a resource. The author notes, "I'm not the only one that took biosolids. The EPA said it's clean. State of Michigan said it was clean." This repetition of official reassurance highlights the systemic betrayal felt by victims.

Critics might note that the article focuses heavily on the worst-case scenarios of farm shutdowns, potentially underplaying the economic necessity of biosolids for sustainable agriculture if properly regulated. However, the piece counters this by emphasizing the lack of regulation and the known toxicity of the inputs.

The Decades-Long Cover-Up

The narrative then pivots to the historical root of the crisis, tracing the lineage of these chemicals back to the mid-20th century. More Perfect Union writes, "They knew about the problem for decades. And now they've got a chemical that can't be destroyed." This sentence encapsulates the article's moral outrage: the danger was not an accident, but a known variable ignored for profit. The author details how DuPont and 3M conducted internal studies on rats and workers as early as the 1960s, finding evidence of cancer and toxicity, yet kept the data hidden.

The piece relies heavily on the testimony of Rob Bulot, a former corporate lawyer turned whistleblower, who uncovered 110,000 pages of internal documents. More Perfect Union quotes Bulot's realization: "What I began to see there really kind of changed my entire perception of what I thought I knew about environmental law in the United States." This shift from defender of industry to accuser provides a credible, insider perspective that validates the author's claims. The author argues that the settlement of the Wilbur Tennant case in 2001 was a turning point, yet it failed to stop the flood of new chemicals.

"We were dealing with a chemical that had been invented by the 3M company right around really the time of World War II... The concern became at that point, well, what would happen to living things that might get exposed to this?"

The article effectively uses this historical timeline to show that the current crisis is the result of a deliberate, long-term strategy of obfuscation. The health study that followed the Tennant settlement confirmed links to six different diseases, including kidney cancer and ulcerative colitis. More Perfect Union notes that today, "there are more than 10,000 varieties of PFAS on the market," suggesting that the problem has metastasized rather than been solved. This is a powerful point: the industry didn't stop; they just swapped one toxic chemical for another.

The Political Battle for Accountability

The final section of the coverage examines the political machinery that allows this to continue. The author introduces Cindy Bole, a Wisconsin resident who discovered her family's blood levels were five times the national average. Her story illustrates the personal toll of the "patchwork" of state regulations. More Perfect Union writes, "You're either going to fight this yourself with whoever will help or you're going to leave this for your kids to deal with." This quote captures the desperate, binary choice facing ordinary citizens.

The commentary here turns critical of the political landscape, specifically the revolving door between industry and the EPA. The author points out that under the Trump administration, key appointments included former lobbyists and executives from the very companies being regulated. More Perfect Union writes, "If you'd raise your right hand. There's David Fatuhi, deputy administrator of the EPA, who once represented a company that had been accused of spreading 93,000 tons of PFAS containing biosolids throughout Maine." This specific detail serves as a smoking gun for the argument that regulatory capture is the primary barrier to safety.

A counterargument worth considering is that industry lobbyists argue these chemicals are essential for modern life, from waterproofing to electronics. The article acknowledges this, quoting the industry stance that "society is just going to fall apart" without them. However, the author dismisses this as a "vicious cycle" designed to protect profits over public health. The piece concludes by highlighting the fragility of progress, noting that while the Biden EPA set federal limits, the political pendulum threatens to erase them. More Perfect Union warns, "Under Trump everything is up in the air," emphasizing that the solution is not just scientific, but deeply political.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union's strongest asset is its ability to weave the personal trauma of farmers and families with the cold, hard evidence of corporate malfeasance, creating a narrative that is both emotionally resonant and factually dense. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on a binary view of political will, which may oversimplify the complex economic trade-offs involved in banning industrial chemicals. Readers should watch for how the next administration handles the EPA's new limits, as the article suggests the battle is far from over. The most striking takeaway is the realization that the poison in our food supply was not an accident, but a calculated decision made decades ago by people who knew exactly what they were doing.
