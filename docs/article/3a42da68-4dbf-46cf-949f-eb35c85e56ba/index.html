I Got To Make A Beat For My Favorite Rapper

Benn Jordan · Benn Jordan ·Apr 16, 2024 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Benn Jordan, a composer known for complex electronic soundscapes, attempts a radical pivot: creating a hip-hop beat for a professional rapper by flipping obscure samples. The piece stands out not as a simple tutorial, but as a psychological autopsy of the creative process, revealing that the "chill" reputation of beat-making is a facade for intense neurosis. For the busy listener, this is a rare glimpse into how modern technology is democratizing the legal minefield of sampling, turning a career-ending risk into a manageable workflow.

The Myth of the Chill Producer

Jordan begins by dismantling the casual perception of his craft. He admits that while he has composed music in hip-hop tempos, he has never taken "the art of sample flipping seriously until now." His hesitation stems from two major barriers: the legal nightmare of clearing samples and his own perfectionism. He notes, "I have worked with sync licensing long enough to know how problematic and potentially catastrophic it can be to flip a sample without clearance." This is a crucial insight for any creator; the barrier to entry isn't just talent, it's the fear of litigation.

He contrasts the traditional, high-stakes clearing process with modern solutions like Tracklib, a platform that aggregates rights to thousands of obscure tracks. Jordan writes, "Traditionally the way Tracklib worked was you paid a small subscription fee and then if you chose to use a song you could then license it for a variable fee." He explains that the platform has evolved to offer unlimited clearances for premium members, effectively removing the legal anxiety that has historically stifled innovation in sampling. This technological shift is the engine of the entire piece, allowing an outsider to step into the genre without the threat of a lawsuit.

Critics might note that relying on a single platform for all samples creates a homogenized sound, as everyone has access to the same "virtual record crate." However, Jordan's argument is that the tool is secondary to the ear; the platform merely enables the digging, it doesn't dictate the creativity.

The Neurosis of Perfection

Once the legal hurdles are cleared, Jordan confronts his own internal barriers. He describes his process not as a relaxed jam session, but as a surgical procedure. "I wanted to do things my way and surgically pull them out of tracks in a way that would be perfect or imperfect in time and avoid any pops if you're as anal retentive or insufferable as I am," he confesses. This admission reframes the genre: the "lo-fi" aesthetic often associated with hip-hop is frequently the result of hyper-precise, neurotic editing.

He details the technical rigor involved, using software to zoom into the "microscopic scale" of waveforms to find "zero crossings" where the audio cuts are seamless. The result is a collection of over 50 loops, a testament to his inability to settle for the first option. As he puts it, "I went overboard here too and made over 50 different loops and that was the moment when I realized that I'm doing precisely what I said I wouldn't do with this project." This self-awareness adds a layer of authenticity; he is not pretending to be a laid-back beatmaker, but rather exposing the obsessive work ethic required to produce something that sounds effortless.

Maybe beat making is not chill after all. Maybe those who are great at it are probably perfectionists and incredibly neurotic but they possess the ability of making it look easy.

The Collaboration and the Verdict

The climax of the narrative arrives when he sends the beat to the rapper, Superlative. The stakes are high, as Jordan acknowledges, "I have very little room for error here as I very little experience in this genre and somebody else's valuable time and energy is relying on me." The rapper's reaction validates Jordan's approach. Upon hearing the track, Superlative exclaims, "I love that when the syn opens up and becomes like it's almost it's like super reverberated but all of a sudden it's like your mind feels Clarity."

This feedback loop confirms Jordan's hypothesis about the nature of great production. He reflects on the legendary producer J Dilla, noting that while Dilla seemed to power through beats effortlessly, "if you dissect his recordings the amount of deep digging and raw creativity to find his samples and sound sources is nothing short of phenomenal." Jordan concludes that the "chill" persona is a misdirection; the reality is a deep, often anxious engagement with sound. He writes, "I can't be sure but maybe beat making is not chill after all."

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's piece succeeds by stripping away the mystique of hip-hop production to reveal the rigorous, anxiety-driven labor underneath. Its strongest argument is that modern licensing tools have lowered the barrier to entry, but the barrier to quality remains the creator's own neurosis. The biggest vulnerability is that this specific workflow—relying on a paid library—may not translate to the gritty, underground origins of the genre, yet it offers a viable path for the modern composer to cross-pollinate genres without legal peril.
