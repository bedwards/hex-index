Ryan Grim & Jeremy Scahill deliver a harrowing dispatch that strips away the diplomatic veneer to reveal a coordinated strategy of regime change and resource extraction, all while the human cost in Gaza and Venezuela mounts. This is not a story about policy shifts; it is a report on the mechanics of a new imperial architecture where oil revenues are parked in Qatar and war powers are quietly dismantled by a Senate that has lost its nerve.

The Architecture of Extraction

The most disturbing revelation in the coverage is the seamless integration of military pressure with economic looting. Grim & Scahill detail how the administration is moving to effectively run Venezuela, noting that President Trump has stated the U.S. will "control and market up to 50 million barrels of oil, with proceeds held in accounts overseen by the U.S. government." The authors highlight the sheer audacity of this arrangement, where a Justice Department memo explicitly cites calls from the opposition to oust President Nicolás Maduro as the legal rationale for U.S. intervention. This framing suggests a departure from traditional sovereignty norms, replacing them with a corporate-style takeover of a nation's primary asset.

The authors do not shy away from the financial engineering involved. They report that the first Venezuelan oil cargo, valued at $500 million, has been sold with proceeds parked in an account in Qatar, overseen by Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and directed by the President and Secretary of State Marco Rubio. This is a profound shift in how the executive branch engages with foreign nations. As Grim & Scahill observe, the White House has indicated that further oil deals are expected, with Chevron suggesting it can expand production by 50 percent within two years. The implication is clear: the conflict is being managed not just for security, but for profit, with the interim leadership in Caracas described as having "fully cooperated" with the arrangement.

The move has drawn sharp criticism from Democrats, including Senator Elizabeth Warren, who questioned the legality of offshore accounts controlled by the president.

Critics might argue that this approach stabilizes a chaotic region by securing energy supplies and punishing a hostile regime. However, the authors present a counter-narrative where the legal basis for such actions rests on the demands of a U.S.-backed opposition, raising serious questions about the legitimacy of the governance being installed. The human cost of this financial maneuvering is often obscured by the talk of barrels and accounts, yet the authors remind us that this is happening in a country already fractured by decades of crisis.

The Erosion of Congressional War Powers

The coverage shifts to the domestic political machinery, exposing how the legislative branch has failed to check the executive's military ambitions. Grim & Scahill report that Senate Republicans blocked a war powers resolution aimed at limiting further U.S. attacks on Venezuela, a move that effectively handed the President carte blanche to escalate. The authors note that Republican Senators Josh Hawley and Todd Young "flipped under pressure from the Trump administration," abandoning a bipartisan effort that had previously gained traction.

This is a critical moment for the balance of power. The authors point out that Vice President J.D. Vance had to break a 50-50 deadlock to dismiss the measure, a procedural maneuver that underscores the fragility of institutional checks. The article draws a parallel to historical precedents, referencing the War Powers Resolution to contextualize how rare it is for Congress to successfully assert its authority in the face of executive overreach. The failure of this resolution signals a dangerous precedent where the executive branch can pursue military action without meaningful legislative constraint.

The authors also highlight the broader context of this legislative failure, noting that it comes amidst a surge in violence in Gaza, where the UN reported over 300 incidents of airstrikes and shelling in a single week. The juxtaposition of a blocked war powers resolution with the ongoing slaughter in the Middle East creates a stark picture of American foreign policy. Grim & Scahill write that the administration is pushing forward with "demilitarization, technocratic governance, and reconstruction" in Gaza, a plan that includes the disarmament of all unauthorized forces. Yet, as they note, "assistance remains stockpiled at the border," and the death toll continues to rise.

The Senate on Thursday voted against a war powers resolution aimed at limiting President Trump’s ability to conduct further attacks on Venezuela after two Republican senators withdrew their support for the legislation.

A counterargument worth considering is that the executive branch requires flexibility to respond to rapidly evolving threats. However, the authors' reporting suggests that this flexibility is being used to bypass democratic accountability, with the administration leveraging its control over the Senate to push through controversial policies. The failure of the war powers resolution is not just a political defeat; it is a structural weakening of the system of checks and balances.

The Human Toll and the Silence of Diplomacy

Amidst the geopolitical maneuvering, the authors return to the human cost, detailing the suffering of civilians in Gaza and the impact of sanctions on Iran. Grim & Scahill report that the total recorded death toll in Gaza since October 7, 2023, is now 71,441, with 171,329 injured. They highlight the surge in violence, noting that since the ceasefire began, Israel has killed at least 451 Palestinians and wounded 1,251, with 710 bodies recovered from under the rubble. The authors do not mince words when describing the situation: "The UN reported that airstrikes, shelling, and other attacks in Gaza surged last week, with more than 300 incidents causing mass casualties—the highest weekly figure since the ceasefire."

The coverage also touches on the diplomatic fallout, particularly regarding Iran. The authors describe how Iran's foreign minister, Abbas Araghchi, appeared on Fox News to stress that Tehran is open to diplomacy but cautioned Washington against repeating a "failed experience" from June. Grim & Scahill note that Araghchi argued that "terrorist forces" had infiltrated protests to trigger a conflict, a claim that echoes the administration's narrative. However, the authors also report that the U.S. ordered a carrier strike group to transfer to the Middle East, signaling a readiness for escalation despite the diplomatic overtures.

The human impact of these tensions is felt far beyond the battlefield. The authors detail how India-Iran rice trade has stalled, with exporters reluctant to deal due to non-payment risks and renewed U.S. pressure. This has led to a deepening food crisis in Iran, with rice prices up 155 percent year-on-year. As labor activist Nader Moradi told ILNA News Agency, soaring prices are "breaking the backs of poor and defenseless people." The authors use this detail to illustrate how economic sanctions and trade disruptions are weaponized, causing suffering among the most vulnerable populations.

Children’s educator Ms. Rachel called on the United States and Israel to end what she described as the deliberate suffering of one million Palestinian children, urging the immediate entry of lifesaving shelter and medical aid into Gaza.

Critics might argue that the administration's hardline stance is necessary to deter aggression and protect national security. However, the authors' reporting suggests that this approach is exacerbating humanitarian crises and undermining long-term stability. The failure to deliver aid in Gaza, coupled with the economic strangulation of Iran, points to a strategy that prioritizes coercion over resolution.

Bottom Line

Ryan Grim & Jeremy Scahill have produced a vital piece of journalism that exposes the stark reality of an administration willing to bypass democratic norms and inflict severe human suffering in pursuit of its geopolitical goals. The strongest part of their argument is the unflinching connection between the blocking of war powers and the escalation of violence, revealing a system where accountability has been sacrificed for expediency. The biggest vulnerability in the administration's approach is the assumption that military and economic pressure can achieve lasting stability without addressing the root causes of conflict. Readers should watch closely for the next phase of this strategy, particularly as the U.S. moves to solidify its control over Venezuelan oil and as the situation in Gaza continues to deteriorate despite the promise of a ceasefire.
