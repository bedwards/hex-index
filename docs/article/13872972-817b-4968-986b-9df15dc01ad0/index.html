How Doctors Redefined Death

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Aug 2, 2020 · 18 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis doesn't just ask how we define death; he exposes the terrifying gap between the binary legal definition and the messy, gray reality of modern medicine. Drawing from a career on the front lines of emergency rooms and operating theaters, he argues that our reliance on a stopped heart is an archaic model that technology has already shattered, leaving families and doctors stranded in a moral limbo.

The Illusion of the Binary

Francis opens by dismantling the comforting simplicity of medical dramas, contrasting the clean lines of television with the chaotic reality of a hospital ward. He recalls a pivotal moment from the show Scrubs where a junior doctor rushes to pronounce a death, highlighting the absurdity of the legal fiction that a patient is "alive" until a doctor checks their watch. "The time that it's a lapse between his biological death at the end of a resuscitation attempt until jd's arrival is a kind of limbo where life had left his body but from the hospitals and the law's perspective he was alive," Francis writes. This observation is crucial because it reveals that death is often a bureaucratic event, not just a biological one. The author's point lands hard: we prioritize the living, meaning a dead patient can wait for hours while a doctor attends to the sick, creating a strange temporal disconnect.

The physical act of confirming death is described not as a clinical procedure, but as a psychological ordeal. Francis notes the pressure of standing motionless in a silent room while a family watches, waiting for a sign that never comes. He admits that the silence can be so profound that doctors start hearing things, or worse, succumb to the urge to rush the process. "Strictly speaking you're also supposed to inflict pain and listen for three whole minutes," he explains, noting that even textbooks are inconsistent on the duration. This section is effective because it humanizes the clinician, showing that the fear of making a mistake is a constant companion. Critics might argue that modern monitoring equipment has made this ritual obsolete, yet Francis insists that the human element of witnessing the end remains a profound, often awkward, burden.

In a modern hospital you've got experienced nurses who know very well when someone's died but they can't legally confirm it has to be the doctor.

When the Heart Stops, But Life Continues

The argument shifts dramatically when Francis introduces the technology that has blurred the lines of life and death: ECMO (external corporeal membrane oxygenation). He describes a patient named Judy, whose heart stopped completely, yet she remained alive because a machine was oxygenating her blood. "She had no pulse but she had a blood pressure... I don't think anyone would argue that judy was dead would they but she had no cardiac activity," Francis states. This is the core of his thesis: the cessation of a heartbeat is no longer a reliable marker for death. The evidence is irrefutable; if a stopped heart meant death, then anyone revived by CPR or defibrillation would have technically been dead for a moment, a notion that defies logic.

Francis takes this further with a visceral description of a heart-lung transplant, where he stood over a patient whose chest was open, heart and lungs removed, yet the body was kept alive by a bypass machine. "This woman's entire torso is empty and yet she's alive," he recalls thinking. This imagery is powerful, stripping away the biological assumptions we hold about what keeps us alive. It forces the reader to confront the idea that the body is a collection of systems that can be temporarily replaced, challenging the ancient definition of death as the failure of the pump.

The Brain as the New Frontier

As the heart becomes a replaceable part, the focus shifts to the brain. Francis traces the history of this shift to the 1960s, when the first heart transplants forced the world to redefine death to allow for organ donation. He contrasts the American approach, which required the heart to stop, with the South African approach, which accepted brain death. "Norm Shumway... had been beaten to the post he criticized the american boy scout definition of death," Francis writes, highlighting the political and theological resistance to changing the definition. The argument here is that the definition of death was not just a medical evolution but a pragmatic necessity to save lives through transplantation.

However, the author is careful not to present this as a settled victory. He points out the cultural friction, particularly in Japan, where the concept of brain death was rejected for decades due to deep-seated beliefs about the soul and the body. "Japan did not perform any beating heart organ transplants for three decades until finally in 1999 brain death was legally recognized," he notes. This historical context adds necessary nuance, showing that what is considered "dead" is as much a cultural construct as a biological fact. A counterargument worth considering is that the push for brain death criteria was driven as much by the need for organs as by medical certainty, a tension Francis acknowledges but suggests was a necessary trade-off.

Ultimately one will always struggle to define death without defining life another deeply complicated and unresolved area of philosophy and biology and if you're so inclined theology.

The Gray Zone of Survival

Francis concludes by addressing the most difficult part of the equation: the outcomes of resuscitation. He critiques the binary nature of television, where patients either die or return to normal, ignoring the vast middle ground of severe disability. "In reality there's a huge range of outcomes between someone walking out of the hospital feeling fine and death," he argues. This is perhaps the most poignant part of his commentary, as it addresses the ethical dilemma of resuscitation. The author suggests that the real question isn't whether a patient can be brought back to a heartbeat, but whether they can be brought back to a life worth living.

He touches on the involuntary signs of life, like the Lazarus sign, where a brain-dead body may still move, giving false hope to families. "Just like religion this involuntary sign can provide hope without actually affecting the likelihood of recovery," Francis writes. This observation underscores the tragedy of the situation: the body can mimic life long after the person is gone, complicating the grieving process. The author's stance is clear: we must move beyond the simple question of "alive or dead" and embrace the complex reality of survival.

Bottom Line

Rohin Francis delivers a compelling, evidence-rich argument that the traditional definition of death is obsolete in the age of advanced life support. His strongest point is the vivid demonstration that a stopped heart no longer equals death, forcing a necessary but uncomfortable shift toward brain-based criteria. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in the unresolved tension between medical pragmatism and cultural or religious beliefs, a gap that will likely continue to fuel controversy as technology advances further.
