The Ultimate Iran War Briefing, Part II

Dan Perry · Dan Perry ·Apr 5, 2026 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Dan Perry cuts through the noise of escalating rhetoric to argue that the real danger isn't just a military clash, but a strategic trap where cheap technology could force a global economic surrender. He posits that Donald Trump's erratic threats to "walk away" might inadvertently validate a scenario where a non-state actor or a rogue regime holds the world's energy supply hostage without firing a single major shot. This is not a standard war update; it is a chilling assessment of how modern asymmetry could dismantle the post-war order.

The Mediator's Paradox

Perry immediately challenges the plausibility of Pakistan stepping in as a neutral broker. He describes Islamabad not as a savior, but as a "shifty ally" with a "reliability deficit that has long been the subject of quiet despair in Washington." The author's skepticism is well-founded when he points out the deep contradictions in Pakistan's foreign policy: "formally aligned with the United States and hosting some US operations... while tolerating, and at times enabling, militant actors across its region."

The historical weight of this duplicity is hard to ignore. Perry notes that "no one was surprised when al-Qaeda leader Osama bin Laden was discovered and killed in 2011 by US Navy SEALs in Abbottabad, just steps from the Pakistan Military Academy." This specific reference to the Abbottabad Commission Report's context underscores why Washington views Pakistani mediation with deep suspicion. The country's civilian government says one thing while its military intelligence services often do another, creating a "duplicity" that makes them an unlikely peacemaker.

"It has flirted with supporting the Taliban and giving its members refuge, in effect, in its almost lawless tribal regions."

Perry argues that Pakistan's sudden offer to host talks is less about peace and more about "regaining relevance at the expense of India." While this cynical take fits the geopolitical pattern, critics might note that desperate times often force even unreliable actors to the table, and a failed mediation attempt could still buy valuable time for de-escalation.

The Four Scenarios of Collapse

The core of Perry's analysis lies in his ranking of potential war outcomes, moving from the ideal to the catastrophic. He suggests the "best-case scenario was a fall of the regime," which he calls a "monumental, historical favor to the world and to the Iranian people." Failing that, he hopes for a "weakened regime... agreeing to surrender terms: ending its nuclear weapons program, ending the ballistic missile program, ending its support and funding for proxy militias."

However, Perry warns that the world is sliding toward the third option: "ending the war merely in exchange for Iran ceasing its disruption of traffic through the Strait of Hormuz." He views this as "a case of giving in to blackmail," where the US and Israel would have to declare victory despite the regime's continued ability to terrorize. The worst-case scenario, which Perry fears Trump is flirting with, is a US withdrawal "even without an end to the blackmail," which would be "impossible to spin as anything other than an Iranian victory."

"The lesson will not be confined to the Gulf. Other actors—state and non-state—will draw the obvious conclusion: strategic disruption no longer requires great power capabilities."

This argument is the piece's most potent warning. Perry illustrates that if cheap drones can halt a fifth of the world's crude oil, the precedent is set for global instability. He writes, "you do not need to win a war to reshape the environment in which it is fought." This reframes the conflict from a traditional military engagement to a test of economic resilience, where the cost of insurance and logistics becomes the true battlefield.

The Strait of Hormuz as a Global Chokepoint

Perry shifts to the economic mechanics of the crisis, describing the Strait of Hormuz as "the single most important artery in the global energy system." He details how even "sporadic drone strikes, near-misses, or credible threats" would force tanker traffic to slow, creating "delays, bottlenecks, and higher transport costs that ripple immediately through global pricing benchmarks."

The author's analysis of the secondary effects is particularly sharp. He explains that insurance markets would "reprice aggressively," with premiums spiking to reflect a new baseline assumption that the Gulf is a contested environment. "Some insurers would simply refuse coverage without state guarantees," he notes, which would effectively shut down smaller operators. This creates a "global tax on commerce" where efficiency is replaced by redundancy.

"In other words, a handful of idiots with drones can impose real costs — especially when there is no central target to strike and no appetite to occupy territory."

This line captures the essence of the modern threat: the asymmetry of cost. While Perry's focus on economic paralysis is compelling, it arguably underestimates the speed with which naval coalitions could adapt to escort duties, potentially mitigating the "bunching up" of traffic he predicts.

The Gulf's Fragile Paradise

Turning to the Gulf states, Perry paints a vivid picture of their vulnerability. He describes their business model as a "monumental tax boondoggle" built on "hundreds of glistening towers, dozens of artificial islands, and even indoor ski slopes." The threat is existential: "Only about a tenth of the population of the United Arab Emirates is local — good luck keeping the rest there if Iranian drones can hover outside the office window."

Despite this fragility, Perry observes that "the Gulf nations want the war to go on; they have concluded that the Iranian regime must go." This suggests a calculated risk where they believe the regime is internally weak enough to collapse without a ground invasion. This contrasts sharply with the European reaction, where Perry notes Trump is unleashing "the Trumpiest of ire" over their refusal to join the war.

"Trump has a long history of indifference to the fact that NATO is a very good deal for the US, allowing it to project leadership and power and giving it access to dozens of bases in strategic locations."

Perry highlights Trump's confusion regarding Article V, noting that he seems to think it means "all must join any member if that member chooses to attack someone else." This misunderstanding fuels his personal attacks on leaders like British Prime Minister Keir Starmer and French President Emmanuel Macron, whom he mocks for their diplomatic caution.

Bottom Line

Dan Perry's strongest contribution is his reframing of the conflict from a military standoff to an economic siege, where the Strait of Hormuz becomes the ultimate leverage point for asymmetric warfare. His argument is most vulnerable in its assumption that the US and its allies lack the naval capacity to secure the strait against drone swarms, potentially underestimating the resilience of global shipping networks. Readers should watch closely for whether the "quartet" of Pakistan, Turkey, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt can actually deliver a diplomatic off-ramp or if they merely serve as a distraction while the economic clock ticks down.
