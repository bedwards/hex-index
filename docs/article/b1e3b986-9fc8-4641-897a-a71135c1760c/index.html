Would You Fall for It? [ST08]

Jason Slaughter · Not Just Bikes ·Jan 9, 2023 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jason Slaughter doesn't just critique a 1954 General Motors propaganda film; he exposes it as the original blueprint for the financial and urban disasters plaguing North American cities today. By juxtaposing the film's utopian promises with the grim reality of modern "stroads," Slaughter reveals that the traffic congestion we blame on too many cars was actually the intended outcome of a system designed to sell vehicles, not move people. This is a crucial historical correction for anyone tired of hearing that we simply need to build wider roads.

The Myth of the Infinite Highway

Slaughter opens by dissecting the film's rhetoric, which frames highways as "magical new technology that will bring freedom to everyone." He notes how this language persists today, even though the reality is a gridlock where "nobody is getting anywhere quickly." The author's insight here is sharp: he identifies that what we now call "induced demand" was known to planners in the 1930s as "traffic generation," yet the film ignores this to push a car-centric agenda. Slaughter writes, "This film doesn't talk about induced demand because it was made by GM and they love the idea that more people will drive because then they'll sell more cars."

This framing is devastatingly effective because it shifts the blame from individual drivers to the systemic incentives of the automotive industry. The film argues that the problem is a lack of road capacity, but Slaughter points out the fundamental flaw: "we didn't dream big enough."

The problem isn't that we haven't built enough roads; it's that we've built a car-dependent system with no viable alternatives to driving.

Critics might argue that in the 1950s, the economic benefits of connecting rural areas to markets were genuine and necessary, but Slaughter counters that the long-term cost of maintaining this sprawl far outweighs the initial gains. He highlights the film's admission that two-thirds of roads were already "obsolete, worn out, inadequate in width, inferior in condition, capacity and safety" just thirty years after construction. This foreshadows the modern crisis where cities face massive infrastructure debt.

The Stroad Trap and the Parking Fallacy

The commentary deepens as Slaughter tackles the concept of the "stroad"—a street designed like a road that fails at both functions. He connects the film's pitch to improve "Farm to Market roads" with the modern reality where these corridors become congested arteries for personal vehicles, not just trucks. Slaughter observes, "It's very obvious that the majority of the traffic here is not commercial trucks and if anything the trucks are being slowed down by all the personal vehicles."

He then pivots to the film's solution for downtown congestion: more parking. The film features a scripted testimonial from a merchant claiming, "What brings up store sales today is parking space. It's as important to volume as shelf space and display windows." Slaughter dismantles this logic by explaining that no amount of parking can replicate the foot traffic of a walkable neighborhood. He writes, "The fundamental fallacy here though is that you can't build enough parking to replace the number of customers you would get from foot traffic in a walkable neighborhood."

This is perhaps the most vital takeaway for urban planners today. The film's vision of a "heart attack" of congestion was met with the solution of widening streets, a strategy Slaughter notes has led to cities literally destroying their downtowns to make them "car friendly." The result, as he puts it, is that "places where downtown people used to live were turned into places where suburbanites could park."

The Financial Time Bomb

Slaughter concludes by exposing the financial mechanics behind the highway boom, specifically the role of Robert Moses and the illusion of toll roads paying for themselves. He notes that while the film celebrates the "miracles of concrete," the reality is that "after about 30 years any road needs to be resurfaced and refurbished which typically cost more than the price to build it in the first place." The film's promise that toll roads would "pay itself off way ahead of schedule" is revealed as a trap, as many were converted to free highways once the debt was paid, leaving taxpayers with the maintenance bill.

He cites the film's prediction that there would be 85 million cars by 1975, a figure that was vastly underestimated at 106 million. Slaughter uses this to illustrate the self-fulfilling prophecy of car dependency: "The car companies that made this piece of propaganda got everything they asked for and more and it didn't solve any of the problems mentioned in this video." The ultimate irony is that the solution proposed in 1954—building more highways—only created the very problems it claimed to solve.

You cannot build your way out of traffic congestion, but many American cities have literally bankrupted themselves trying.

Bottom Line

Slaughter's strongest argument lies in his ability to connect historical propaganda to current fiscal liabilities, proving that the "growth Ponzi scheme" of road building was a known risk that was ignored for profit. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that the public is aware of these historical failures, when in reality, the myth of the highway as a solution remains deeply entrenched in political discourse. Readers should watch for how this historical context is used to challenge current proposals for new highway expansions, which often repeat the same 1954 playbook.
