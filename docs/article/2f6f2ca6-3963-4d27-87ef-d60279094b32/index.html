Commentary by Brian Edwards

Kenny Easwaran tackles a concept that often feels like a mathematical abstraction but is, in reality, the hidden engine of human reasoning: the idea that uncertainty itself can be measured. In this commentary on his 2011 paper, Easwaran argues that we do not need to wait for perfect data to form rational beliefs; instead, we can treat our confidence in any hypothesis—from the fairness of a coin to the existence of God—as a precise numerical value that updates logically with new evidence. This is not merely a lesson in statistics; it is a philosophical claim that the messy, subjective nature of human belief can be tamed by the rigorous laws of probability.

The Architecture of Belief

Easwaran begins by dismantling the common intuition that probability only applies to random events like dice rolls or card shuffles. He reframes the entire conversation around the work of Thomas Bayes, an 18th-century minister whose posthumous essay shifted the focus from predicting outcomes to inferring causes. "Bayesianism is a collection of positions in several related fields centered on the interpretation of probability as something like degree of belief as contrasted with relative frequency or objective chance," Easwaran writes. This distinction is crucial because it allows us to assign a probability to things that are not random at all, such as whether a specific war will end this year or if a particular political candidate will win an election.

The author illustrates this with a vivid example of a coin from a magic shop. If the coin lands on heads three times in a row, a traditional observer might say the coin is likely double-headed, but a skeptic like David Hume would argue we can never be justified in believing anything about the future based on past observations. Easwaran counters this by showing how the math works: "the bayesian probability of the coin being double-sided is going to go up by a factor of 8 compared to the bayesian probability of the coin being fair." This is the core innovation: the evidence (three heads) does not change the physical coin, but it must rationally change the observer's degree of belief.

Bayesian probability reflects your uncertainty about the hypothesis while the probabilities of the coin flips are supposed to reflect some sort of objective chance or randomness in the world.

Critics might note that this approach risks conflating subjective ignorance with objective risk. If two people start with different initial beliefs about the coin, they will end up with different conclusions even after seeing the same evidence. Easwaran acknowledges this tension, noting that while Bayesians agree on how to update beliefs, they often disagree on what the starting point, or "prior probability," should be. Is there an objectively correct prior, or is it purely a reflection of one's biases? The author leaves this as an open question, suggesting that the power of the framework lies in the update rule itself, regardless of the starting point.

The Mathematics of Rationality

Moving beyond the intuition, Easwaran outlines the structural requirements for a rational mind. He posits that for an agent to be perfectly rational, their degrees of belief cannot be arbitrary; they must adhere to the strict axioms of probability theory. "For an agent to be perfectly rational her degrees of belief must obey the axioms of probability theory," he states. This is a bold normative claim. It suggests that if your beliefs about the world are internally inconsistent—say, you believe A is likely, B is likely, but A and B together are impossible—you are not just mistaken, you are irrational.

The author leans heavily on the work of Andrey Kolmogorov, whose 1933 axioms provide the mathematical backbone for this system. Easwaran explains that Kolmogorov defined probability as an abstract mathematical structure, a "field of probabilities" that assigns real numbers to sets of events. By mapping our mental states onto this structure, we gain the "precision and power" to calculate exactly how much our confidence should shift when new data arrives. This is the concept of "conditionalization," where beliefs are updated in a way that is logically determined by the evidence.

However, the reliance on mathematics also invites skepticism. Easwaran admits that "the fact that degrees of belief are represented mathematically... is also the source of much skepticism towards these views among other philosophers." The critique is that human belief is often fuzzy, binary, or context-dependent, not a smooth curve of numbers between zero and one. By forcing our messy intuitions into a rigid mathematical box, do we lose something essential about how humans actually reason? Easwaran suggests that while the model is an idealization, it serves as a necessary benchmark for rationality, much like a frictionless plane in physics.

Beyond the Coin Toss

The scope of Easwaran's argument extends far beyond gambling or simple logic puzzles. He explicitly lists hypotheses that lack mathematical precision but are central to human life: "the bayesian probability that god exists or the bayesian probability that a republican will win the next presidential election or the bayesian probability that the war in ukraine will be resolved this year." By applying the same logic to these high-stakes, unobservable realities, Easwaran bridges the gap between abstract philosophy and the urgent questions of our time.

He distinguishes between two types of constraints on belief: synchronic (at a single moment in time) and diachronic (across time). The synchronic constraint ensures that your beliefs don't contradict each other right now, while the diachronic constraint ensures that you change your mind in the right direction when you learn something new. "Conditionalization... is the standard way the beliefs change over time," he explains. This dynamic view of knowledge challenges the static notion of "justified true belief" that has dominated epistemology for centuries. Instead of asking "Do I know this?" the Bayesian asks "How confident should I be, and how should that confidence shift?"

The important thing to note is this bayesian probability is very different from the probability of the outcome the coin you picked up either is a double-headed coin or it is a fair coin there's nothing random about that at all.

This distinction is vital. The coin is either double-headed or it isn't; the randomness is in our ignorance, not in the object. Easwaran's coverage makes it clear that Bayesianism is a tool for managing ignorance, not for predicting the future with certainty. It is a framework for navigating a world where we never have all the facts but must still make decisions.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's commentary on his own work succeeds in demystifying Bayesianism, presenting it not as a dry mathematical exercise but as a profound philosophical stance on the nature of belief. Its greatest strength is the rigorous way it connects subjective uncertainty to objective logic, offering a clear path for how we should reason even when we cannot know for sure. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the "prior probability"; without a consensus on where to start, the system can be manipulated to justify almost any conclusion, a risk Easwaran acknowledges but does not fully resolve. For the busy thinker, the takeaway is clear: rationality is not about having the right answers, but about updating your confidence in the right way.}
