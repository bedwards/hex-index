Did China Just Drop The Ball On Global Dominance?

Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →
Transcript excerpt on Economics Explained

Two decades ago, China's economy was home to the most intense economic growth ever seen. No economy in history that large had grown that quickly. But today, the IMF expects growth to hover around 4.8% for 2025 and fall further to about 4.2% in 2026. The IMF also warns that without major reforms, China's potential growth could sink to as low as 3% a year by the 2030s, less than a third of what it was experiencing a decade ago, and also probably not enough for it to close the gap on the USA.

What's more is that given its geopolitical complications and the threats it's now presenting to global advanced industries, a lot of its biggest export customers are trying their very best to reduce their dependence on Chinese manufacturing with various levels of subtlety. However, at the same time, China's trade surplus has reached its highest level ever, becoming the first economy in history to have net exports of more than a trillion dollars in a single year. So, for now, it's not that countries want to buy from China. It's just that as much as they try to fight it, they often don't have another option, which is an opportunity.

So with China's growth slowing and western restrictions tightening, a major gap is opening in global supply chains for the first time in decades. Countries like Vietnam, Mexico, and Indonesia are racing into fill it. But one has emerged as the front runner. For the first time in a generation, India stands at a center of the world's biggest economic shift.

The Indian government set a goal of reaching a $5 trillion economy by 2025, which now that we're actually at the end of the year feels a little optimistic. But even without hitting that mark, the progress has been remarkable. India is already the fifth largest economy in the world. And as growth rates slow for other countries in the top five, the IMF estimates growth of around 6.4% for India in 2025 and 2026.

Major companies are already moving in. Apple is assembling iPhones there. LG is building a manufacturing complex that will produce more than 5 million appliances a year. Toyota, Honda and Suzuki are investing billions of dollars to build new cars and factories in India.
