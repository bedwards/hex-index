10 Steps to Becoming AI Proof

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Jun 12, 2025 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now cuts through the noise of AI anxiety not by promising a utopia, but by arguing that the only way to survive the coming wave is to become aggressively, unapologetically human. While most guides focus on mastering the tools, this piece flips the script, suggesting that our greatest defense lies in the very things machines cannot replicate: physical dexterity, the ability to make singular decisions, and the cultivation of deep, local trust.

Mastering the Machine and the Hand

The author begins by dismantling the fear that ignorance is safety, insisting instead that one must "know the technology, know AI intimately" to avoid becoming "the tool of tools." This is a crucial distinction; the argument posits that AI is best used as an ideation engine rather than a fact-checker, noting that while it "might give me examples of things that I've missed," it "hallucinates a lot" and cannot be relied upon for trusted data. The author's approach here is pragmatic: use the machine to point the way, but walk the path yourself.

However, the most compelling pivot in the text occurs when the author shifts from digital literacy to physical reality. They argue that while creative skills were once thought safe, "one of the last things that it's going to be able to replicate is dexterity." The human hand, described as "one of the most advanced things on the planet," remains a formidable barrier to automation. Consequently, the author suggests that "if you can somehow tap into that, that will provide a strong line of defense," pointing toward care work and crafts as future-proof industries.

"The human hand is one of the most advanced things on the planet other than the human brain and the human physiology in general."

Critics might argue that robotics is advancing faster than the author anticipates, potentially eroding the "dexterity" advantage sooner than expected. Yet, the author's broader point holds weight: the friction of the physical world is currently a moat that digital intelligence cannot easily cross.

The Human Edge: Decisiveness and Trust

Moving beyond physical skills, the commentary tackles the psychological gap between human and machine. The author observes that AI "always tries to be balanced," acting as a synthesizer that "can only say you should do this based on this set of priorities." In contrast, humans are forced to "make singular decisions out of complex scenarios." The advice is stark: "Always take a stance. Be good at making decisions." In a world of information overabundance, the ability to act without perfect data becomes a rare and valuable commodity.

This leads to a discussion on trust. The author notes that "at the moment AI is low trust," creating an opening for humans to become "high trust" authorities. Trust is built not just through competence, but through reliability and the willingness to "cite sources" and show the "conscientiousness to deliver information that they can rely on." The argument suggests that in a flood of AI-generated content, the "value comes from something that is rare," and that rarity is found in verified human judgment.

"Be the person that can make the quick decisions in a world of information overabundance where everyone else is swimming in too much information."

A counterargument worth considering is that "high trust" is increasingly difficult to maintain in a polarized digital environment, where bias is often mistaken for decisiveness. The author acknowledges the need for "due diligence," but the challenge of scaling that trust in a fragmented media landscape remains a significant hurdle.

The Political Imperative

The piece concludes by rejecting the notion that AI adoption is inevitable and unchangeable. The author urges readers to "kick up a fuss, complain about it, moan about it," and to "be both a technologist and a neolite at the same time." The core warning is economic: if AI makes content easy to produce, "the cultural sphere... will be flooded with it," rendering it valueless. The author posits that "if everyone will be doing it... who's paying you to do this?"

This section shifts from career advice to a critique of capitalism, noting that the pressure to use AI often stems from a "race to the bottom" designed to "impress investors over humans with flesh and blood." The author's final plea is for a democratic approach to regulation, ensuring technology produces "human flourishing" rather than just speeding up the rat race.

"Technology needs to be shaped to our needs, not the other way round."

Bottom Line

Then & Now offers a refreshing antidote to AI fatalism by grounding the conversation in human agency, physical reality, and the economics of scarcity. While the argument may understate the rapid pace of robotic advancement, its insistence on the value of decisive human judgment and deep trust provides a robust, actionable framework for navigating an automated future.
