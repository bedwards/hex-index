“Deport Them All” — Who’s to Blame for Springfield’s Immigrant Crisis?

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Feb 6, 2025 · 22 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union dismantles the viral narrative that Haitian immigrants are devouring pets in Springfield, Ohio, by tracing the city's actual crisis back to decades of deindustrialization and political neglect. The piece is notable not just for debunking the absurdity of the "cat-eating" rumor, but for exposing how a desperate plea for federal aid was weaponized into a campaign of hate by sitting politicians. In an era of soundbite politics, this deep dive forces listeners to confront the uncomfortable reality that Springfield's pain predates the arrival of its newest neighbors by thirty years.

The Anatomy of a Lie

The author begins by confronting the sensationalism that turned a small Midwestern city into a national lightning rod. "I knew that there was going to be chaos, I had no idea how bad it was going to be," the narrator admits, setting the stage for a story where political theater eclipsed human reality. More Perfect Union writes, "JD Vance claims Haitian migrants are draining social services and causing chaos," only to immediately undercut this by revealing the source of the panic: a single, resolved incident involving a missing cat named Miss Sassy. The coverage effectively illustrates how a mundane misunderstanding—a cat hiding in a basement—was amplified by Senator Vance into a national talking point about "eating dogs and cats."

This framing is crucial because it shifts the blame from the vulnerable to the manipulative. The article argues that the rumor gained traction not because it was true, but because it offered a convenient scapegoat for genuine suffering. "Rumors like that get traction among people who are struggling and that they're looking for someone to blame for their struggle," the piece observes. This is a powerful psychological insight that holds up under scrutiny; when communities feel abandoned, they often turn their frustration toward the most visible "other," even when that other is working harder than anyone else.

The Ghost of Manufacturing Past

To understand why the rumor stuck, the author takes listeners on a historical tour of Springfield's economic collapse, arguing that the city was already a "tinderbox" before the Haitian arrival. More Perfect Union writes, "All these people who want you to believe this was just Utopia before that are liars," dismantling the nostalgic myth of a perfect past. The piece details how Springfield once thrived on unionized manufacturing jobs, with International Harvester employing 10,000 people and offering a middle-class life that included pensions and free healthcare. "Life was like The Truman Show," one former worker recalls, capturing the illusion of stability that was about to shatter.

The commentary here is sharp: the author correctly identifies that the city's decline was engineered by policy choices, not demographic shifts. As the article notes, "Corporations and their allies in government engineered trade deals that made it cheaper to move jobs abroad." This argument is bolstered by the specific example of the 2005 Ohio tax cuts, which drained $12 billion from state revenues, directly impacting the schools and hospitals that are now under strain. A counterargument worth considering is whether the city's leadership could have done more to diversify the economy before the manufacturing exodus, but the evidence of systemic state disinvestment makes the "blame the immigrants" narrative difficult to sustain.

Housing policy was built on racist foundations, and we never tore them up. We just stopped talking about it.

The New Economy and the New Scapegoat

The piece then pivots to the arrival of Latino and Haitian workers, framing them not as a burden but as a lifeline that was mismanaged. More Perfect Union writes, "The promise of a growing workforce along with generous subsidies from the state attracted companies like Amazon, Dole, and Topre America, bringing an estimated 8,000 jobs to the region." However, the author is quick to note the cost: these were non-union jobs with grueling hours. One worker at Topre America describes leaving after 98 consecutive days of 12-hour shifts, highlighting the exploitative nature of the "revival."

The coverage makes a vital distinction between the economic reality and the political narrative. While the city's infrastructure was indeed stretched thin by the rapid population increase following the 2021 assassination of Haiti's president, the author argues that the solution was always federal funding, not deportation. "We needed Federal help with that," a city official states, only to be met with political grandstanding instead of resources. The article effectively points out the irony that the United States' own history of intervention in Haiti contributed to the very instability that drove people to Springfield. "Haiti is where it is today in part because of the United States' intervention," the text asserts, adding a layer of historical accountability that is often missing from the debate.

Critics might argue that the sheer speed of the population growth made the strain on services inevitable regardless of federal aid, but the piece counters this by showing how the city had successfully integrated previous waves of immigrants when resources were available. The failure was one of political will, not demographic inevitability.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union's strongest move is connecting the absurdity of the "cat-eating" rumor to the very real, decades-long failure of American economic policy. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that federal intervention could have solved the infrastructure crisis overnight, which may oversimplify the logistical challenges of rapid urban growth. Ultimately, the reader should watch for how this narrative of "blame the immigrant" will be deployed in other Rust Belt cities facing similar structural decay.
