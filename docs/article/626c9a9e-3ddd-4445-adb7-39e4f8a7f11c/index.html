The Lamentations of a Rocking Chair

Various · Animation Obsessive ·Mar 22, 2026 · 13 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

In an industry obsessed with technical spectacle, this piece makes a startling claim: the most influential animated film of the last forty years is a silent, hand-drawn meditation on a rocking chair. The author, writing as Various for Animation Obsessive, doesn't just recount the history of Frédéric Back's Crac; they reveal how a Canadian short film, born from a daughter's school assignment, fundamentally shattered the creative confidence of Isao Takahata and Hayao Miyazaki, forcing them to rethink the very soul of their own work.

The Shock of the Real

The narrative hinges on a specific, almost cinematic moment in 1982. While Takahata and Miyazaki were struggling with a frustrating Hollywood collaboration, they stumbled upon Crac in a Los Angeles theater. The author captures the gravity of this encounter perfectly. "It was a shock to both of us," Miyazaki recalled, admitting to Takahata, "So, I guess we're failures, aren't we…" This admission is powerful because it comes from giants who defined an era of animation. The author argues that this wasn't just admiration; it was a revelation that their own medium had been lacking something essential.

The piece effectively contrasts the rigid, industrial nature of Japanese cel animation at the time with Back's organic approach. As the author notes, Takahata and Miyazaki realized that in Crac, "the story, theme and presentation all matched," creating a "single, total vision" that their own industry struggled to replicate. This observation is crucial. It suggests that the film's success wasn't just about pretty pictures, but about a unified artistic intent that transcended the medium's technical limitations.

Critics might argue that attributing the entire trajectory of Studio Ghibli to a single short film is a stretch, yet the evidence provided—that the DNA of My Neighbor Totoro and later works like The Tale of the Princess Kaguya is rooted in Back's influence—makes the connection hard to dismiss. The author writes, "You can read even My Neighbor Totoro as an answer to Crac," a bold assertion that reframes Ghibli's history through a Quebecois lens.

"They'd discovered a filmmaker, Takahata later wrote, whose 'content and presentation are inseparably linked.'"

The Cost of Beauty

The article deepens by exploring the physical toll of Back's artistry. This isn't a story of easy inspiration; it is a story of sacrifice. The author details how Back developed a unique technique using frosted cels and colored pencils to achieve a sketch-like texture, a method that required him to draw over 7,000 frames by hand. But the most harrowing detail is the cost of his vision. "I washed my eye out with water, because it was 10 or 11 at night and all the drug stores were closed," Back remembered regarding the chemical fixative that damaged his sight. He continued working despite the injury and ultimately lost his eye.

This section of the commentary is vital because it contextualizes the film's emotional weight. Back wasn't just making art; he was fighting a battle against injustice and environmental decay. The author points out that Back was a "lifelong activist" who believed that "one must counterbalance violence." The film's quiet beauty, therefore, is not passive; it is a deliberate act of resistance. The author notes that Back felt he didn't have the talent to make "revolutionary films," so he chose instead to make films that "communicate something, films that renew and give new life to the viewer."

The historical context here is subtle but significant. While the article focuses on the film's universal appeal, it briefly touches on the specific cultural moment: Back was a tribute to a rural Quebec way of life that was vanishing, a theme that resonated deeply with the Quebec sovereignty movement of the era, which was fiercely debating the preservation of local identity against modernization. Yet, as the author observes, the film transcended these borders, surprising Back when "Americans found it amusing, and that the Japanese found it interesting."

The Legacy of the Rocking Chair

The piece concludes by tracing the ripple effects of Crac through the animation world, from Disney veterans like Glen Keane to festival circuit directors. The author emphasizes that Back's influence is often invisible, woven into the DNA of modern animation. "The film's DNA is inside Studio Ghibli — and inside almost all animation, everywhere," the author writes. This is a compelling argument for the enduring power of the auteur vision in an era increasingly dominated by corporate formula.

The author also highlights the role of composer Normand Roger, noting that the folk music in Crac plays a "storytelling role" that compensates for the lack of dialogue. This musical choice, featuring traditional musicians who "learned by ear very fast," adds a layer of authenticity that reinforces the film's connection to the land and its people. The author's description of the film's ending—where the rocking chair finds a new life in a museum despite the destruction of its world—serves as a poignant metaphor for the survival of memory and culture.

"I always felt weak in relation to the dimension of injustice. … One must counterbalance violence. One must react. We can't accept it."

Bottom Line

This commentary succeeds in elevating Crac from a forgotten classic to a pivotal moment in animation history, using the reactions of Takahata and Miyazaki as a powerful lens to examine the film's impact. Its strongest argument is that true innovation comes not from technical perfection, but from the inseparable link between an artist's message and their method. The piece's only vulnerability is its heavy reliance on the Ghibli connection, which, while compelling, might overshadow the film's independent merit for readers unfamiliar with Japanese animation history. Ultimately, the author makes a convincing case that Crac is not just a film to watch, but a philosophy to live by.

1. Speaking through beauty

In 1982, Isao Takahata and Hayao Miyazaki were in the United States. They’d come to work with Hollywood on an animated Little Nemo feature. The experience was a frustrating one.

But something good came of it. While in Los Angeles, Takahata went to a screening of The Adolescent (1979), a live-action movie from France. It was touring American theaters as a double bill with an animated short from Canada. Takahata didn’t realize that the short had already won an Oscar earlier in 1982.1

Its title was Crac (1981). Takahata was floored — “completely captivated,” he later said. He had to watch it again, pulling colleagues along.

Miyazaki’s reaction was similar. In his words:

It was a shock to both of us. As we trudged home after the film, I remember saying to Takahata-san, “So, I guess we’re failures, aren’t we…”2

He and Takahata became evangelists for Crac when they got back to Tokyo. Toshio Suzuki, then a magazine editor, heard the raves. “They said that the story, theme and presentation all matched,” he remembered. Even the characters and backgrounds were united in the film’s single, total vision — something Japanese cel animation didn’t do.3

They’d discovered a filmmaker, Takahata later wrote, whose “content and presentation are inseparably linked.”4

Crac’s quiet beauty and emotional pull, and its message about the value of rural living in Quebec, strongly resonated with Takahata and Miyazaki. It became one of their favorite films — and biggest influences. From that point, at least a little (often a lot) of this film showed up in their work.

Crac is about a rocking chair. It’s also about history, and what it means to be happy. The story begins when a man cuts down a tree and lovingly carves a chair from its wood. It stays in his family for decades. We watch life go on: a wedding celebration, the change of seasons, the birth of children, the games those children play. The chair is part of it all — getting painted, broken and repaired along the way.
