Let's Read! Laurence BonJour, 2014, "In Defense of the A Priori"

Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Kenny Easwaran tackles one of philosophy's most stubborn divides by stripping away the mystique of "pure reason" to reveal a surprisingly practical definition of how we know things without looking. He doesn't just defend the existence of a priori knowledge; he radically redefines it, arguing that even Descartes' famous "I think, therefore I am" relies on experience, while true a priori insight is reserved for the logical architecture of reality itself.

Redefining the Rational

Easwaran begins by narrowing the battlefield. He isn't arguing about whether we know things without experience, but whether we have reasons for beliefs that don't derive their weight from the senses. He sets up a clear distinction between the ability to understand a concept—which might require sensory experience—and the justification for believing it true, which might not.

"A priori reason for thinking that a claim is true is one whose rational force or cogency does not derive from experience either directly as in sense perception or indirectly as by inference of any sort."

This distinction is the piece's intellectual anchor. By separating the possession of a concept from the justification of a belief, Easwaran sidesteps the common objection that humans can't think without sensory input. He acknowledges we need experience to learn language, but insists that once we grasp a mathematical truth, the reason we believe it stands apart from the world of senses. Critics might argue this line is too thin; if we need experience to learn the concepts, can the reasoning truly be independent? Easwaran anticipates this, suggesting the dependency stops at the door of understanding.

The Introspective Trap

Perhaps the most provocative move Easwaran makes is his treatment of introspection. While many philosophers treat looking inward as a special, non-empirical faculty, Easwaran drags it back into the realm of experience. He argues that knowing your own thoughts is just another form of sensing, dependent on causal relations and temporal facts.

"Introspective awareness of one's thoughts sensations and other mental states should also count as a variety of experience and the reasons for belief that such experience provides as empirical rather than a priori."

This is a bold claim that dismantles the Cartesian foundation. When he turns to Descartes' cogito, Easwaran doesn't dismiss it as false; he reclassifies it. He argues that realizing "I exist" because "I am thinking" is based on an internal observation of a specific mental event, making it a posteriori knowledge. This reframing forces the reader to reconsider what counts as "pure" reason. If even our most certain self-knowledge is empirical, where does the a priori survive? Easwaran's answer is sharp: it survives only in the realm of logical and mathematical necessity.

"Even the reason for belief in one's own existence that is supplied by the cartesian cogito... is based on introspective awareness of the occurrence of specific thoughts and sensations."

Insight Over Intuition

Having cleared the deck of empirical intruders, Easwaran defines what remains. He rejects the vague, psychological notion of "intuition"—often confused with hunches or gut feelings—in favor of a rigorous concept of "insight." For Easwaran, a priori reasons aren't just feelings of certainty; they are revelations of why something must be true.

"A priori insights at least purport to reveal not just that the claim is or must be true but also at some level why this is and indeed must be so."

This is the strongest part of his defense. By demanding that a priori reasons explain the essential nature of things, he elevates the standard from "I feel sure" to "I see the necessity." He illustrates this with the example of mathematical proofs, where we start with basic premises we can immediately see to be true and chain them together. The resulting knowledge is a priori not because it ignores the world, but because its validity rests entirely on the logical structure of the premises.

"The traditional view which i believe to be essentially correct is that in the most basic cases such reasons result from direct or immediate insight into the truth indeed the necessary truth of the relevant claim."

However, Easwaran admits a potential vulnerability here: the reliance on "insight" can sound mysterious. He tries to ground it by referencing Lewis Carroll's paradox of deduction, suggesting that these insights are often about the rules of inference themselves, not just the propositions. A counterargument worth considering is whether this "insight" is truly distinct from a highly trained form of empirical pattern recognition, or if it is a unique cognitive faculty. Easwaran leans heavily on the latter, a stance that remains contentious in modern cognitive science.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's defense succeeds by shrinking the scope of the a priori to make it defensible, sacrificing the grandiose claims of historical rationalism for a precise, minimal version that actually holds water. His biggest vulnerability is the reliance on "insight" as a self-evident faculty, which may feel circular to skeptics who demand a naturalistic explanation for how we access necessary truths. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: pure reason exists, but it is far more austere and logical than we usually imagine, and far less reliable as a foundation for self-knowledge than Descartes hoped.
