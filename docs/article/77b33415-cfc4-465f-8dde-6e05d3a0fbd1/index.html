Weekend update #165: Why the pantomime?

Phillips P. O'Brien · Phillips P. O'Brien ·Dec 28, 2025 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Phillips P. O'Brien cuts through the diplomatic theater to argue that the current "peace" push is not a genuine negotiation, but a strategic delay tactic designed to buy the Russian war machine time while the West feigns engagement. The piece's most disturbing claim is that the United States is effectively acting as an agent for Moscow, running a two-track diplomacy where friendly back-channel talks with Russian officials contradict the public pressure placed on Kyiv. For busy readers tracking the human cost of this conflict, O'Brien provides a chilling framework: the "pantomime" of diplomacy is not a failure of communication, but a feature of a policy that prioritizes Russian security concerns over Ukrainian survival.

The Architecture of Deception

O'Brien begins by dissecting the recent meeting between President Zelensky and the US administration in Florida, framing it not as a breakthrough but as a performance. He argues that while the public is shown a 20-point peace plan from Kyiv, the real negotiations are happening elsewhere. "The US is simultaneously running a two-track diplomacy," O'Brien writes, noting that talks with Russian negotiator Kirill Dmitriev are "friendly and show common understandings," while talks with Ukrainians feel like "an agent of Russia, trying to find out what they can get the Ukrainians to agree to on Putin's behalf." This framing is potent because it challenges the standard narrative of US mediation as neutral; instead, it suggests the US is actively filtering Ukrainian demands to fit a Russian agenda.

The author points to specific interactions to bolster this claim, highlighting how US negotiator Steve Witkoff met with Ukrainian officials only to report "no breakthrough," while simultaneously praising Russia's commitment to peace in meetings with Kremlin figures. "Witkoff was very positive about the Russians, using language that must have been so pleasing to the Kremlin that one wonders if Dmitriev dictated it," O'Brien observes. This observation forces the reader to reconsider the language of US officials; when they refer to the invasion as the "Ukrainian conflict," it is not a semantic slip, but a deliberate erasure of Russian aggression. Critics might argue that diplomatic ambiguity is standard practice to keep channels open, but O'Brien's evidence of a stark disconnect between public US posturing and private Russian alignment suggests something more calculated is at play.

"The US talks with Ukraine, blusters here and there, and then turns to Putin for his opinion and the Russian dictator says 'no'. Once Putin rejects any deal, Trump either blames the Ukrainians for being unreasonable or at at best 'both sides' for their instransigence."

The Anchorage Shadow

The commentary deepens by revisiting the "Anchorage summit," a meeting often dismissed by Western observers as inconclusive. O'Brien contends that this gathering was actually the cornerstone of a new, grim reality where the US and Russia have aligned on key territorial concessions. He notes that Russian Deputy Foreign Minister Sergei Ryabkov explicitly rejected the Ukrainian 20-point plan because it "radically differs from the 27 points which we have been working on with the American side over the past few weeks." This reveals a disturbing consistency in the Russian narrative: they believe they have already secured a framework that the US is now trying to enforce on Kyiv.

O'Brien recalls his own earlier analysis of the Anchorage meeting, where he predicted that the US was moving toward forcing Ukraine to cede territory. "What has come out of Anchorage is that the US government believes that the key step in any peace deal is to force Ukraine to give up territory to Putin while getting practically nothing in return," he writes. This is the piece's most sobering insight: the "understandings" reached in August were not about stopping the war, but about managing its outcome in Russia's favor. The author suggests that the "spirit of Anchorage" is now the unspoken rule of engagement, where the US provides just enough aid to prevent total collapse but not enough to win, effectively incentivizing the continuation of the conflict on Russian terms.

The human cost of this diplomatic maneuvering is implicit but heavy. If the US is indeed waiting for the war to "swing Russia's way," as O'Brien suggests, then every month of delay translates to more shelling of Ukrainian cities and more civilian casualties. The author notes that the US has "waited/hoped for the war to swing Russia's way" while issuing "fake sanctions" and "public deceptions." This characterization of US policy as a form of "play-acting designed to string Ukraine and European states along" is a stark indictment of the administration's strategy.

The Weaponry Gap

Shifting from diplomacy to the battlefield, O'Brien examines the recent Ukrainian strikes on Russian refineries using British and French Storm Shadow/Scalp cruise missiles. He argues that these attacks highlight a critical failure by the US and Germany to provide superior long-range systems like the Tomahawk or Taurus missiles. "The Ukrainians attacked Russian refineries... which was revealing," O'Brien writes, pointing out that these strikes were necessary because Ukrainian systems are often insufficient for high-value targets. The author emphasizes that while Ukraine has shown what it can do with "inferior systems," the refusal of Western allies to share more capable technology is a strategic choice.

The commentary here is particularly sharp regarding the disparity in military aid. O'Brien notes that the US "is more on Russia's side these days, so was just bluffing about Tomahawks earlier." This suggests that the restriction of advanced weaponry is not a logistical hurdle but a political one, intended to limit Ukraine's ability to strike deep into Russian territory. The author contrasts this with Germany, which he notes "understand the real peril Europe is in" and has the capacity to help significantly more. The implication is clear: the West is fighting a war of attrition with one hand tied behind its back, not out of necessity, but out of a desire to avoid escalating the conflict in a way that might threaten the "Anchorage" understandings.

"The US does the minimum to give Ukraine help, actually supports Russia materially in different ways, and waits/hopes for the war to swing Russia's way."

Critics might argue that providing long-range missiles could provoke a broader escalation with nuclear risks, a valid concern in any conflict involving a nuclear power. However, O'Brien's argument rests on the premise that the current strategy of "minimum help" is already failing to protect Ukrainian lives and is instead prolonging the suffering without achieving a stable peace.

Bottom Line

Phillips P. O'Brien's analysis is a devastating critique of the current diplomatic landscape, arguing that the "pantomime" of peace talks is a deliberate strategy to legitimize Russian gains while the West disengages. The piece's greatest strength is its willingness to connect the dots between private diplomatic "understandings" and the public suffering on the ground, suggesting that the war's prolongation is a feature, not a bug, of current US policy. However, the argument relies heavily on the interpretation of back-channel communications and Russian statements, which leaves it vulnerable to claims of speculation. Readers should watch for whether the "Anchorage framework" is ever officially acknowledged by the US administration, as that would confirm the worst fears O'Brien outlines.

Why The Pantomime?.

President Zelensky will meet with President Trump in a few hours. It was another hastily scheduled meeting between the two and has ostensibly been called to see if Ukraine can fully agree a new “peace” plan to present to Putin for the Russian dictator’s approval. We know a little of where the plan is, or at least what the Ukrainians want us to think what the plan says, because its 20 points were outlined to the press by the Zelensky government.

Of course, this Ukrainian version is probably not a reflection of where the Trump administration and Putin are. The US government has not acknowledged these points, and it is clear that the US has a completely separate dialogue going with the Russians. In fact, in a Politico story, Trump was asked about the status of the 20-point plan and the US president turned the tables on the Ukrainians. Trump said that he had not given approval to any new plan, and that Zelensky will have to wait and see what he does.

“He doesn’t have anything until I approve it,” Trump said. “So we’ll see what he’s got.”
