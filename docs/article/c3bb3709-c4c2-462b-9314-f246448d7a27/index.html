We saw the future of b2b marketing...here are the takeaways

Emily Kramer · ·Jan 7, 2026 ·16 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Emily Kramer doesn't just predict the future of business-to-business marketing; she hands out the blueprint for survival in an era where artificial intelligence has flattened the barrier to entry for content creation. Her central thesis is startlingly simple yet operationally complex: the era of the specialist is over, and the "Gen Marketer"—a hybrid of deep craft and broad orchestration—is now the baseline requirement for relevance. This isn't a call for more tools, but a demand for a fundamental shift in how value is delivered to audiences who are increasingly skeptical of generic noise.

The Death of the "Mid" Generalist

Kramer reframes the concept of being a generalist, stripping away the stigma of mediocrity that often plagues the title. She argues that in the past, being a generalist was an excuse for not mastering any single discipline, but the AI era demands the opposite. Citing Ting Ting Luo, Head of Product Marketing at Clay, she writes, "Being a generalist is not an excuse to be bad at multiple things or be mid at multiple things." This distinction is crucial. The new standard requires marketers to possess deep expertise in one or two areas while layering on the ability to connect those dots across the entire go-to-market function.

The argument holds weight because it addresses a specific friction point in modern organizations: the bottleneck of handoffs. When a product marketer must wait for a growth marketer to tweak a landing page, which then waits for a designer to format an email, velocity dies. Kramer suggests that the "Gen Marketer" removes these friction points by owning the workflow end-to-end. She notes, "Gen Marketer isn't just another job title—it's actually the new baseline skill set for marketers." This is a bold claim, effectively stating that the traditional siloed roles of product, growth, and content marketing are becoming obsolete unless they evolve into this integrated model.

Critics might argue that expecting one person to master deep technical execution, strategic positioning, and AI orchestration is a recipe for burnout rather than efficiency. However, Kramer counters this by emphasizing that the tools now handle the heavy lifting of execution, freeing the human mind for strategy. The real risk here isn't the workload, but the failure to adapt; as she bluntly puts it, "The AI era isn't rewarding marketers who know one narrow thing in isolation; it's rewarding generalists who can connect the dots."

Gen Marketer isn't just another job title—it's actually the new baseline skill set for marketers.

From Random Acts to Connected Flywheels

The second pillar of Kramer's analysis targets the chaotic nature of modern marketing campaigns, which she dubs "Random Acts of Marketing." She argues that the proliferation of disconnected tools has led to a strategy of throwing content at the wall, hoping something sticks. The solution, she posits, lies in the new generation of go-to-market tools that integrate enrichment, workflows, and AI prompting directly into the product.

Kramer highlights how modern platforms are moving beyond simple data storage to active intelligence. "Instead of just focusing on my one swim lane, I'm thinking about the whole swimming pool," she quotes Jenny Thai, Head of Marketing at Pear VC. This shift is enabled by tools that can pull in firmographic and behavioral data to create a full picture of the audience without manual research. The implication is profound: the excuse of "we didn't have enough context" is no longer valid.

The commentary here is particularly sharp when discussing the evolution of search. Just as search engine optimization (SEO) shifted from keyword stuffing to user intent in the early 2010s, Kramer suggests we are now entering an era of "Answer Engine Optimization" (AEO), where AI models synthesize answers rather than just listing links. She points out that tools are now built to generate content tailored to specific signals, ensuring that "prompting can remove creative bottlenecks, but only when paired with the right structure and inputs." Without this structure, the result is not efficiency, but "slop."

The danger of ignoring this shift is clear. Kramer warns that "If you're just doing random one-off activities, you're toast." The market is moving toward connected activities that compound over time. This requires a move away from isolated campaigns toward repeatable loops where data from one channel informs the next. As she illustrates with examples from companies like Tofu and Unify, the future belongs to those who can trigger automated follow-ups based on real-time user behavior, ensuring that high-intent visitors are never lost in the funnel.

The Metric That Matters: Learnings Per Minute

Perhaps the most compelling part of Kramer's argument is her proposed metric for success: "learnings per minute." In a world where AI can generate infinite content in seconds, the volume of output is meaningless without the quality of insight. She anchors this argument in a quote from Dane Vahey, Head of B2B Marketing at OpenAI: "I have this informal metric when I think about content, which is learnings per minute…And I don't think enough marketers really optimize for learnings when they think about their content."

This reframing is essential. It moves the conversation from "how much did we produce?" to "how much did we teach?" Kramer argues that the speed of AI should not be used to ship mediocre work faster, but to iterate on high-quality ideas more rapidly. She writes, "The death of a marketing team is when every single idea takes 20 people to execute." By empowering a smaller, more versatile team to own the entire process, organizations can maintain a high quality bar while moving with unprecedented speed.

However, this approach places a heavy burden on human judgment. While AI can generate the "fuel" for campaigns, Kramer insists that "humans are needed here" for the creative spark and strategic direction. The tools can orchestrate the flywheel, but they cannot decide which direction to spin it. This is where the "Gen Marketer" earns their keep: by using their cross-functional knowledge to ensure that every piece of content adds genuine value to the audience.

Bottom Line

Emily Kramer's analysis succeeds in cutting through the hype of AI marketing tools to identify the human skills that will actually drive results. Her strongest argument is that velocity without value is a liability, and that the only way to survive the flood of AI-generated content is to become a "Gen Marketer" who prioritizes deep understanding over shallow execution. The biggest vulnerability in this vision is the sheer difficulty of finding and training talent capable of this hybrid role; if organizations cannot upskill their teams, they risk being left behind by competitors who can. The takeaway is clear: the future belongs to those who can connect the dots, not just the ones who can push the button.

The death of a marketing team is when every single idea takes 20 people to execute.
