Revisiting Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Jun 29, 2023 · 53 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden cuts through the noise of nostalgia to reveal that Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull is less a failed sequel and more a self-aware critique of the very genre it inhabits. While many critics dismiss the film as a jarring departure from the franchise's roots, van der Linden argues that its awkwardness is actually a deliberate, if clumsy, attempt to grapple with the colonialist baggage of treasure hunting. This is not a defense of the movie's plot holes, but a compelling re-evaluation of its thematic ambition in a post-9/11 world.

The Colonial Blind Spot

The conversation begins by dismantling the assumption that Indiana Jones is a straightforward hero. van der Linden points out the absurdity of the franchise's mechanics, noting how the protagonist seems to possess an inherent right to loot simply because he can solve the puzzles. "It's almost like the artifacts were waiting for somebody to like come in and like put in the right combination for the safe and then take them," he observes. This framing exposes the film's underlying logic: the artifact exists only to be taken by the Western hero who is "smart enough" to find it.

van der Linden suggests that Crystal Skull attempts to correct this dynamic, even if it stumbles. He argues that the film tries to "steer a little bit more sensitively" into the implications of colonial dynamics by having the protagonist return an artifact rather than keep it. However, he acknowledges the film cannot fully escape its history, as it still relies on "nameless and faceless" savages to populate the periphery. This tension is the film's defining characteristic: it wants to be a modern critique of the past while still delivering the same old adventure beats.

"There's a certain denial there of or a certain dismissal of the value of those cultures and of our own past essentially."

This observation is particularly sharp. van der Linden identifies a specific trope where ancient civilizations are credited with alien intervention rather than human ingenuity. He argues this reflects a "linear progression" of history that denies the wisdom and technical know-how of the past. By attributing ancient achievements to aliens, the film inadvertently insults the very cultures it claims to explore. Critics might note that this is a common flaw in pulp adventure fiction, but van der Linden's point is that Crystal Skull is uniquely aware of this flaw, even if it doesn't fully resolve it.

The Melancholy of the Legacy Sequel

The discussion then shifts to the broader context of legacy sequels, a genre defined by the aging of the original hero. van der Linden places Crystal Skull in a specific lineage of films like Rocky Balboa and Rambo, which were driven by a "inherent undercurrent of melancholy." These stories are not just about action; they are about the sadness of a hero who is "now an old man and not who he used to be."

He contrasts this with the "second wave" of legacy sequels, which focus on passing the torch to a new generation. In Crystal Skull, the attempt to introduce a new adventurer, Mutt, is ultimately subverted. van der Linden highlights the moment where Indy swipes the hat back from Mutt as a symbolic refusal to let go. "It's like yeah not yet at least," he says, describing the scene where the torch is offered but immediately retracted. This refusal to fully embrace the new generation leaves the film in a liminal space, neither a pure adventure nor a true deconstruction.

"We're at this time more defined by also having the old creators come back it was more of a sort of like one more like final round for your classic beloved Heroes."

This analysis explains why the film feels so disjointed to many viewers. It is caught between the desire to honor the past and the need to move forward. The "melancholy" van der Linden describes is not just a mood; it is a structural element that prevents the film from achieving the clarity of a standard sequel or the freshness of a reboot.

The Alien Trope and the Denial of History

Perhaps the most provocative part of van der Linden's commentary is his critique of the alien civilization trope. He argues that the idea of aliens facilitating ancient human civilizations is a "denial of... the ingenuity that and the resourcefulness and the just the wisdom" of those cultures. This is not just a plot device; it is a philosophical stance that assumes human history is a linear march toward the present, where the past is incapable of great achievements on its own.

"To therefore look back and say or look at all the things that we were able to do or that people used to be able to do that we now no longer know how they were done and say oh it's probably aliens or something that's there is a certain denial there of or a certain dismissal of the value of those cultures and of our own past essentially."

This argument holds significant weight. By framing the alien trope as a form of historical erasure, van der Linden elevates the critique from "bad writing" to "problematic worldview." He suggests that the film's reliance on this trope is a symptom of a larger cultural inability to respect the complexity of ancient civilizations. While some might argue that the alien plot is just a fun sci-fi twist, van der Linden insists it reveals a deeper bias in how we view history.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's analysis succeeds by reframing Crystal Skull not as a failure of execution, but as a confused attempt at cultural self-correction. The strongest part of his argument is the identification of the film's "melancholy" as a structural flaw that prevents it from fully committing to either the past or the future. However, his critique of the alien trope, while insightful, may overstate the film's intentionality; it is possible the writers simply wanted a twist, not a philosophical statement on historical erasure. Readers should watch for how future legacy sequels navigate this tension between honoring the past and respecting the cultures they depict.
