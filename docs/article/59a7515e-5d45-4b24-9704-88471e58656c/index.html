Michael Tracey uncovers a disturbing paradox at the heart of the Jeffrey Epstein saga: the very mechanisms designed to deliver justice for the accused's victims have arguably dismantled the foundational civil liberties protections that shield every citizen. While the media fixates on the lurid details of the crimes, Tracey argues that the real story is a judicially-authorized spectacle where the presumption of innocence was discarded for a dead man, and taxpayer funds were deployed to subsidize unverified accusations. This is not just a legal curiosity; it is a blueprint for how the state can bypass due process when public outrage reaches a fever pitch.

The Erosion of Due Process

Tracey begins by challenging the assumption that the Epstein case is an exception to the rule of law. He posits that the "vindictive moralistic frenzy" surrounding the case created a vacuum where constitutional safeguards were deemed expendable. "The more untamable the public animus against a particular category of wrongdoer, the more readily civil liberties can be chucked aside," he writes. This framing is crucial because it shifts the focus from the specific depravity of the crimes to the systemic danger of allowing public sentiment to override legal procedure.

The author zeroes in on Judge Richard M. Berman's decision to hold a hearing after Epstein's death, a move Tracey describes as "wildly aberrational." With the defendant dead, the criminal charges were technically moot, yet Berman convened a proceeding where self-identified accusers could speak without cross-examination. Tracey notes that the judge transformed the courtroom into a "complete free-for-all," effectively creating a "judicially-authorized 'struggle session.'" This term, borrowed from the historical context of political purges where individuals are publicly shamed and coerced into confession, is a powerful rhetorical choice. It suggests that the hearing was less about fact-finding and more about performative condemnation.

"Using this bogus pretext, Berman allowed his courtroom to transform into a complete free-for-all, with a parade of self-declared 'victims' and lawyers filing in for what could only be described as a judicially-authorized 'struggle session.'"

The argument holds weight when examining the specific testimony allowed. Tracey highlights a claim by an anonymous "Jane Doe" who alleged Epstein recruited her in Texas, despite the fact that Epstein had no known property in that state. The judge, Tracey points out, affirmed these individuals as bona fide "victims" without requiring "minimal verification of the facts." This abandonment of the qualifier "alleged" is significant. As Tracey observes, "'Victimhood' was now an unchallengeable metaphysical status that could be conferred on anyone who desired it." Critics might argue that in high-profile sex crime cases, the trauma of victims necessitates a more flexible standard of engagement, but Tracey rightly questions the precedent set when the court itself validates uncorroborated claims as fact.

The Economics of Accusation

The piece takes a sharp turn into the financial mechanics of the Epstein proceedings, revealing a system where the government actively funded the prosecution's narrative. Tracey details how Bradley Edwards, a lawyer representing accusers, successfully lobbied the government to pay for the travel and lodging of victims attending the August 2019 hearing. "The government has never done this before," Tracey quotes prosecutor Maurene Comey as saying, yet the request was granted within an hour. This creates a perverse incentive structure where the state subsidizes the very people making claims against a deceased defendant, raising questions about the neutrality of the process.

Tracey provides specific, jarring examples of this expenditure. He notes that Virginia Giuffre, a central figure in the case, requested and received a business-class ticket costing over $10,000, citing a "medical condition" that Tracey suggests was merely a preference for comfort. Similarly, Sarah Ransome was flown in from Barcelona for roughly $1,500. The author points out the linguistic shift accompanying these payments, where the term "survivor" replaced "victim" to convey a sense of moral profundity. "It also seems to carry a certain sense of enhanced moral profundity, not unlike 'Holocaust Survivor' — and certainly the Epstein affair has come to be regarded as of comparable historical magnitude," Tracey writes. This comparison, while provocative, underscores the author's concern that the case has been elevated to a level of historical inevitability that discourages scrutiny.

"Taxpayers were also going to subsidize the brouhaha."

The involvement of high-profile attorney Gloria Allred further complicates the picture. Tracey scrutinizes her role, noting that she read the Texas "Jane Doe" statement in court only to drop the client weeks later without explanation. When Tracey asked for clarification, Allred's response was a flat "No comment." Yet, the author reveals that Allred continued to interface with federal prosecutors, sending emails about "new clients" and requesting documents for potential civil litigation. This suggests a potential conflict of interest where the line between seeking justice for victims and building a civil case for profit may have blurred. The "Epstein Files" released by the Department of Justice show Allred coordinating with the U.S. Attorney's office, yet no clients of hers played a material role in the criminal trial of Ghislaine Maxwell. This disconnect between the high-profile media spectacle and the actual legal outcomes is a critical point Tracey drives home.

The Cost of Unchecked Frenzy

Tracey's central thesis is that the Epstein case serves as a cautionary tale for civil liberties. The "neat trick" he identifies for prosecutors and politicians is simple: "if you want to make the Constitution vanish, just say you're punishing 'pedos.'" This argument resonates because it connects the specific events of the Epstein case to a broader pattern of rights erosion. The author reminds readers that this pattern has repeated throughout history, targeting "terrorists," "drug dealers," and other culturally reviled groups. The danger, Tracey argues, is that the public's desire for retribution blinds them to the procedural violations that could eventually be used against anyone.

The piece concludes by highlighting the lack of accountability for the unverified claims made during the hearing. Despite the judge's affirmation of "victimhood," many of the stories, including the Texas recruitment tale, contained factual impossibilities. The government's reliance on these unvetted accounts, funded by taxpayer money, sets a dangerous precedent. As Tracey puts it, "Unchecked degradation of civil liberties affects the entire body politic, whether or not you give a hoot about Epstein." This is the most compelling part of his analysis: the realization that the mechanisms used to destroy a reviled figure can be turned on any citizen if the political climate shifts.

"Here's a neat trick for prosecutors and politicians: if you want to make the Constitution vanish, just say you're punishing 'pedos.'"

Bottom Line

Michael Tracey's analysis is a vital corrective to the media's uncritical celebration of the Epstein proceedings, exposing how the pursuit of justice for the accused can paradoxically undermine the very legal principles that protect the innocent. While his focus on procedural anomalies is sharp, the piece could benefit from a deeper exploration of the genuine trauma of the victims, whose voices may have been instrumentalized by the spectacle rather than heard. The strongest takeaway is the warning that when the state abandons due process in the name of moral outrage, it creates a dangerous precedent that threatens the rule of law for everyone.
