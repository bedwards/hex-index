It’s dangerous to overestimate the left’s strength

Eric Blanc · Labor Politics ·Jan 5, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Original article →

Eric Blanc and Bhaskar Sunkara deliver a sobering reality check to a movement intoxicated by a historic victory in New York City. While the election of Zohran Mamdani, a young socialist and member of the Democratic Socialists of America (DSA), marks a high-water mark for the left, the authors argue that celebrating this win without addressing the movement's structural fragility is a dangerous gamble. They contend that unlike the deep, century-long roots of early 20th-century socialists in Milwaukee, today's victory rests on a surprisingly shallow foundation of organized labor and community ties.

The Paradox of Electoral Strength

The core of the argument rests on a stark historical comparison. Blanc and Sunkara note that while Mamdani's win feels like a breakthrough, it occurred "despite far lower levels of union and left organization" than the socialist movements of the past. In 1910, Milwaukee's socialists boasted one member for every one hundred residents; today, New York's DSA has one member for every 670. The authors suggest that this numerical gap reveals a critical disconnect: the left has achieved electoral success without the corresponding "on-the-ground power" necessary to sustain it.

This framing is compelling because it challenges the narrative that a single high-profile victory signals a paradigm shift. Blanc writes, "The core of the argument is that we are a Left with ballots cast but few shop floors won, with more cultural importance than class power." This distinction is vital for busy readers trying to understand the limits of current political momentum. It suggests that charisma and viral moments, while potent, cannot replace the slow, grinding work of building durable institutions.

"Things were very different when American socialism had its first big electoral breakthrough... Milwaukee's triumph was the culmination of decades of escalating working-class militancy and socialist growth."

Critics might argue that the comparison to the early 1900s is anachronistic, given the vastly different political landscape and the decline of industrial unions that once anchored such movements. However, the authors use this history not to demand a return to the past, but to highlight the specific deficit in organizational depth that threatens future gains.

The Hollowed-Out Coalition

The commentary then traces the historical erosion of the link between labor and the left, attributing it to the "mass expulsion of leftists from union leadership nationally during the McCarthyite purges of the 1950s." Blanc explains how this divorce left American unions bureaucratized and focused on narrow member interests, while socialists retreated into academia and nonprofits. The result, as the authors describe it, is a society where workers have turned to "individualist survival strategies" rather than collective action.

The rise of the modern left, sparked by the 2008 financial crisis and Bernie Sanders's 2016 campaign, is presented as a resurgence that has nonetheless failed to fully repair this broken bridge. Blanc notes that while DSA membership surged, "unions, for their part, continued to decline in number and mostly stayed aloof from, if not actively hostile toward, these electoral insurgencies." This observation is crucial for understanding the current political dynamic: the left is winning elections by mobilizing a specific demographic of college-educated voters and young minorities, rather than by leading the broader working class.

As Eric Blanc puts it, "Zohran proved able to win over much of the working class despite NYC-DSA's disproportionate concentration among college-educated voters in a handful of neighborhoods." This paradox is the piece's most urgent warning. It suggests that the administration's ability to govern effectively will be hamstrung if it cannot translate electoral enthusiasm into a broad, organized base capable of withstanding corporate pushback.

Reverse Engineering Power

The authors propose a path forward that involves "reverse engineering a working-class movement" using the momentum of the mayoral victory. The strategy relies on leveraging the mayor's platform to drive people into unions and local organizing hubs like "Our Time." Blanc argues that "changing the relationship of forces through outward-facing organizing will do far more to help make Zohran's agenda a reality than denunciations of the administration's inevitable limitations and compromises."

This pragmatic approach acknowledges the limits of what a single official can achieve against entrenched corporate interests. The authors warn that "just getting elected and pursuing smart insider politics isn't enough to pass even modest social democratic policies." They point to Governor Kathy Hochul's hesitation to support taxing the rich as evidence that establishment politicians will only move when pressured by a mass movement, not just by a popular mayor.

"When the going gets rough — and it certainly will in the face of concerted billionaire opposition — even the best communications game won't be able to assuage the doubts, misunderstandings, and fears that will arise among ordinary people."

The text also touches on the external pressures facing the city, noting that the left must maintain its popularity "in the face of inevitable attacks and crises (including those imposed by Donald Trump)." By reframing the threat as an external force of chaos rather than focusing on the individual, the authors keep the focus on the resilience of the movement itself. The argument holds that without a deep layer of trusted organizers, the public will be too easily swayed by media narratives or overwhelmed by daily struggles to support bold reforms.

The Power of Hope

Ultimately, the piece pivots to the psychological impact of the victory. Blanc and Sunkara suggest that the most significant outcome of Mamdani's win is the erosion of the despair that has long dominated working-class life. They quote the mayor's victory speech to anchor this point: "There are [some] who see politics today as too cruel for the flame of hope to still burn. New York, we have answered those fears… Hope over tyranny. Hope over big money and small ideas."

This emotional resonance is the article's strongest asset. It acknowledges that while organization is the hard machinery of politics, hope is the fuel. However, the authors are careful not to let hope become a substitute for strategy. They conclude that the real test is whether this renewed optimism can be channeled into the "broad layer of well-rooted working-class activists" needed to sustain the movement.

"We won because New Yorkers allowed themselves to hope that the impossible could be made possible. And we won because we insisted that no longer would politics be something that is done to us. Now it is something that we do."

Bottom Line

Blanc and Sunkara offer a necessary corrective to the euphoria surrounding the New York election, correctly identifying that electoral wins without organizational depth are fragile. The argument's greatest strength is its historical grounding, which exposes the gap between modern socialist victories and the deep-rooted power of the past. Its vulnerability lies in the immense difficulty of executing the proposed "reverse engineering" of a movement while simultaneously governing a complex city; the transition from hope to durable power remains the movement's most precarious challenge.

It’s dangerous to overestimate the left’s strength

by Eric Blanc · Labor Politics · Read full article

By Eric Blanc and Bhaskar Sunkara

Even with a couple of months’ distance, Zohran Mamdani’s election still feels almost unreal, like a dispatch from an alternate universe. But it’s happening: America’s largest city is now led by a young socialist, a Jacobin subscriber, and a committed member of Democratic Socialists of America (DSA).

For many of us on the New York left, this is the high point of our political lives thus far, a vindication of patient, often grinding organizing. But amid the celebration, there is a note of worry — the feeling that we have won the sprint, and now the marathon begins. For November’s result to mark the beginning of a lasting transformation of not only one city but national politics, we have to start by acknowledging an uncomfortable truth: despite our impressive electoral reach, our movement’s roots are still shallow.

Things were very different when American socialism had its first big electoral breakthrough. The 1910 election of Socialist mayor Emil Seidel in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, initiated nearly fifty years of leftist governance in the city. Milwaukee’s triumph was the culmination of decades of escalating working-class militancy and socialist growth. Mamdani’s, by contrast, has taken place despite far lower levels of union and left organization. Consider the raw numbers: while Milwaukee’s Socialists had roughly one member for every one hundred city residents, New York’s chapter of DSA has, even after a big surge in the past decade, one member for every 670 residents.

But organizational size isn’t the biggest difference. In the early twentieth century, Milwaukee’s Socialists led nearly every major union in the city and were woven into working-class neighborhood life. It was this on-the-ground power that made it possible for them to sustain their movement for so long and to pressure intransigent legislators from other parties to pass some of their policy planks, like improving infrastructure and strengthening workers’ legal protections. In contrast, New York’s democratic socialists don’t lead any of the city’s largest unions. And Zohran proved able to win over much of the working class despite NYC-DSA’s disproportionate concentration among college-educated voters in a handful of neighborhoods.

How did we get to this paradox of electoral strength without organizational depth? And what does it mean for Mamdani’s ability to govern, as well as for rebuilding a socialist movement worthy of the name?
