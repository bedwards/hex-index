How nature became a 'prestige' journal

Asimov Press · ·Jan 5, 2026 ·28 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Asimov Press dismantles the myth that scientific prestige is a pure reflection of truth, revealing it instead as a carefully constructed fiction born from financial patronage and the desperate need for speed. In an era where research output has exploded beyond human capacity to evaluate, this historical autopsy of Nature offers a crucial lens for understanding why we still worship certain journals while ignoring others.

The Architecture of Prestige

The piece opens by challenging the romantic notion that science builds on "the solid ground of nature" to create eternal truths. Asimov Press writes, "Prestige in this system is not simply a reflection of pure scientific merit, as one would hope, but a fiction that attempts to command attention in an era where the quantity of research far exceeds an individual's capacity to assess it." This is a striking admission from a publication deeply embedded in the scientific ecosystem. The argument suggests that what we call "excellence" is often just a signal of scarcity and access, a mechanism to ration attention rather than a measure of quality.

The author traces this back to the chaotic landscape of the 19th century, where approximately 10,000 periodicals competed for survival. Most failed, but Nature, launched in 1869, succeeded not because it was the most rigorous, but because it had the right backing. Asimov Press notes that the journal's survival rested on "patronage that insulated it from commercial failure, elite networks that conferred early credibility, and a publication speed responsive to the increasingly competitive scientific environment." This reframing is vital: it moves the conversation from the abstract idea of "merit" to the concrete realities of economics and networking. The journal was essentially subsidized by the Macmillan publishing empire, allowing it to absorb losses for thirty years while competitors folded.

"Survival was not inevitable, ultimately resting upon three contingent advantages: patronage that insulated it from commercial failure, elite networks that conferred early credibility, and a publication speed responsive to the increasingly competitive scientific environment."

The Speed Game and the X-Club

The most compelling part of the analysis is how Nature weaponized speed. While traditional society journals operated on quarterly schedules tied to meeting proceedings, Nature adopted a weekly cycle. Asimov Press explains that this speed was possible because "Macmillan absorbed the financial losses that more frequent publication required, losses that would have crippled competitors." This economic reality created a strategic advantage that reshaped how science was communicated. Researchers like George Romanes used the journal to establish priority, publishing abstracts to beat rivals to the punch.

However, the path to prestige was not a straight line of scientific consensus. The journal initially relied on the X-Club, an informal dining society of nine prominent scientists, but the relationship soured when the editor, Norman Lockyer, refused to be a "mere mouthpiece of a clique." Asimov Press writes, "Lockyer argued that the journal could not be 'the mere mouthpiece of a clique,'" a stance that alienated his early backers but ultimately secured the journal's independence. This moment is critical: it shows that the journal's authority was forged in conflict, not just collaboration. The X-Club eventually disbanded in 1892, but by then, a new generation of scientists had already adopted Nature as their primary venue for debate.

Critics might note that this focus on speed and debate sometimes came at the expense of depth or peer review rigor, a trade-off that still haunts modern high-impact publishing. The pressure to be first can incentivize haste over accuracy, a tension the article hints at but does not fully explore in the context of today's replication crisis.

From National to Global

The transition to the 20th century saw Nature evolve from a British-centric publication to a global institution, largely due to the editorial vision of Richard Gregory. Asimov Press highlights Gregory's role as a "spokesman of science" who took principled stands, such as criticizing the Nazi regime for expelling Jewish scientists. This moral stance was not just ethical; it was strategic. By positioning the journal as a defender of scientific freedom, it attracted top-tier international talent.

The shift was dramatic. Asimov Press notes, "By 1933, half of Nature's letters came from outside Britain," a stark contrast to American journals like Science, which remained predominantly domestic for decades. This internationalization became a key pillar of its prestige. The article points out that the journal's ability to "keep in the race" for discoveries was essential for physicists like Ernest Rutherford, who used the rapid publication schedule to announce findings before competitors could scoop them.

"By taking such a principled stance, Gregory established Nature as a venue where scientific merit transcended national interests, drawing cutting-edge researchers from around the world and elevating the journal's international profile."

Yet, this global vision was fragile. Asimov Press observes that under later editors, the focus drifted back toward domestic British laboratories, suggesting that the international prestige was a deliberate construction that could easily unravel without active maintenance. The fact that English eventually became the lingua franca of science also played a role, but the article implies that editorial choices were the primary driver of the journal's global reach.

Bottom Line

Asimov Press delivers a powerful corrective to the idea that scientific prestige is an organic outcome of truth-seeking; it is, in fact, a historical artifact built on money, speed, and political maneuvering. The strongest part of the argument is the exposure of how financial patronage and editorial independence were leveraged to create a self-reinforcing cycle of authority. Its biggest vulnerability is the lack of a clear roadmap for how the scientific community can dismantle these entrenched metrics without collapsing the entire system of communication. Readers should watch for how these historical dynamics play out as new, open-access models challenge the traditional gatekeepers of science.

By Robert Reason

Science seeks truths that outlive those who discover them. English poet William Wordsworth’s observation that “to the solid ground of nature trusts the Mind that builds for aye [forever]” — a line that appeared beneath the masthead of the scientific journal Nature for 75 years — captures this romantic ambition: the dream that building on nature’s foundations yields truths durable enough to transcend their discoverers.

And yet, for many of us, when we think about scientific prestige, we cannot help but focus on the individual. Often, we imagine institutions such as Oxford or Harvard as metonyms for the “brilliant mind,” or perhaps envision a Nobel Laureate or Fields Medal winner. One component of this prestige is selectivity — from many, few are chosen. The same is true for scientific journals. While both journals and individuals project their prestige through reputation, journals have found ways to signal prestige through metrics such as impact factor, acceptance rate, and journal rankings; each suggesting the difficulty of publication and the likelihood that an individual’s work will receive institutional acclaim as a result.

At the dawn of the 19th century, approximately 100 scientific periodicals existed worldwide, and by its close, this number had risen to an estimated 10,000. Such growth marked a new era of independent scientific periodicals, distinct from traditional society publications. In such a competitive landscape, most of these publications clung to a tenuous existence, with prominent titles like The Reader (founded 1863) and Scientific Opinion (1864) struggling financially before ultimately folding. Even established and reputable periodicals, such as Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society, which had enjoyed royal patronage since it was formally taken over by the Royal Society in 1752, rarely made money and struggled to publish without assistance.

When Norman Lockyer and Alexander Macmillan launched Nature on November 4th, 1869, they did so amidst a graveyard of failed scientific periodicals. That Nature survived while so many publications collapsed reflects not only scientific merit, but patronage, elite networks, speed of publication, and a talent for capturing an international audience.
