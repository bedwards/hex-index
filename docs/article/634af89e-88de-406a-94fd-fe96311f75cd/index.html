There are a couple of features that are common to a lot of history's arms races. They can be easy to start, hard to stop, and incinerate cash with the kind of zeal you'd normally associate with venture capital during a tech bubble. A couple of weeks ago, we talked about a country that demonstrates that effect in action. The North African nation of Algeria, which according to Cypri, is managing to shovel a greater percentage of its GDP into the defense spending furnace during peace time than Russia officially is during wartime.

What we didn't cover in that episode though were the details of one of the countries that might be most concerned by that break neck pace buildup. The neighboring kingdom of Morocco hasn't had formal diplomatic relations with Algeria since 2021. It stood on opposite sides of multiple geopolitical issues, chosen different friends internationally, adopted a different system of government, and amidst a variety of disputes and tensions that stretch back many, many years, they probably have a fairly strong incentive to try and counterbalance any buildup by Alger. The key problem from a defense economic standpoint is how to do it.

In that sense, Morocco is different from a lot of the other countries we've looked at on the channel. Most of them tend to be relatively wealthy industrialized countries with a lot of resources available to spend on defense with the main challenge being spending it efficiently. But Morocco with a smaller population, a smaller economy, and less GDP dedicated to defense just isn't in a position to match Algeria's dollar for dollar. mean that Moroccan defense policy is often a story of hard procurement choices, difficult decisions, and a need to prioritize, which from a defense economics perspective sounds like an interesting kind of story to me.
