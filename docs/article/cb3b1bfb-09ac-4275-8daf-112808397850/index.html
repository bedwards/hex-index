World Models: Computing the Uncomputable

Packy McCormick · Not Boring ·Mar 19, 2026 · 94 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Packy McCormick makes a provocative claim that cuts through the current hype cycle: while Large Language Models dazzle with words, they are fundamentally ill-equipped to navigate the physical world, whereas "World Models" offer a path to computing the uncomputable. He argues that the industry's obsession with text generation has obscured a more critical bottleneck—simulating dynamic, stochastic environments where actions change outcomes in real-time. This is not just another AI trend report; it is a technical reorientation suggesting that the next leap in artificial intelligence requires machines that can "dream" and intervene, not just predict the next word.

The Computational Bottleneck

McCormick opens by highlighting a fundamental limitation in traditional computing that has stalled robotics for decades. He illustrates this with a vivid scenario: simulating a soccer stadium where thousands of fans interact unpredictably. "Mathematically, in a traditional engine, simulating N fans is at least an O(N) or O(N 2) problem," he writes, noting that every interaction requires explicit calculation. This complexity creates a barrier that prevents machines from responding to the real world with human-like fluidity.

The author's insight here is that human brains bypass this cost by using actions as a form of compression. Instead of calculating every possible future state, we observe, decide, and act, letting the environment provide the rest of the simulation. McCormick explains that World Models learn to predict dynamics from video and action-labeled clips, reducing these chaotic scenarios into a "single fixed cost operation in a neural network." This reframing is powerful because it shifts the focus from raw processing power to architectural efficiency.

"Actions act as a form of compression to predict unfolding dynamics: they hold the information to unroll future states in an environment, until more actions take place and add new inputs into the environment."

Critics might note that this "fixed cost" claim relies on the assumption that training data can perfectly capture the infinite variability of the physical world, a hurdle that has historically plagued simulation engines. However, McCormick's distinction between passive observation and active intervention remains the piece's strongest theoretical anchor. He contrasts standard video models, which predict the next frame based on probability, with World Models that predict the next state based on intervention: "P(s t+1 | s t , a t ). That a t , the action at time t, is the magic."

From Dreams to General Intelligence

The essay pivots to a philosophical and historical context, grounding the technical argument in the age-old question of reality. McCormick draws a parallel between human cognition and the concept of lucid dreaming. "Think about models like dreams," he suggests, distinguishing between watching a video (passive) and shaping a dreamscape (active). "Have you ever had a lucid dream in which you were able to shape the story inside the mind-generated dreamscape? That's a World Model."

This analogy serves as a bridge to the broader implications for Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). McCormick argues that while LLMs can solve complex logic problems, they lack the spatial and temporal reasoning required for embodied agents. He points to the massive capital influx into this specific niche as validation, noting that "Yann LeCun's AMI raised $1.03 billion" and "Fei-Fei Li's World Labs raised $1 billion." This investment surge signals a shift in consensus among experts who have long been skeptical of the LLM-only path.

"World models — these systems that learn from watching the world and the actions taken in it — are a fundamentally new kind of foundation model. They can compute what was previously uncomputable."

The author weaves in historical depth by referencing the "Allegory of the Cave" and Zhuangzi's "Butterfly Dream," suggesting that the question of whether we are agents in a simulation is no longer just philosophy but a technical challenge. He even references the original script for The Matrix, where humans were conceived as a neural network, to underscore the cultural resonance of this idea. This contextual layering elevates the piece from a tech briefing to a cultural critique, though it risks over-romanticizing the technology before it has proven its utility.

"I bet with full confidence that 2026 will mark the first year that Large World Models lay real foundations for robotics, and for multimodal AI more broadly."

McCormick acknowledges the current confusion in the field, where "World Models" risks becoming a buzzword for any 3D or video generation tool. He quotes NVIDIA's Jim Fan to highlight the gap between current "AI video slop" and the future potential of robotics. The author's willingness to admit that "the future is not yet determined" adds credibility to his otherwise optimistic forecast.

The Path Forward

The core of McCormick's argument is that World Models offer a solution to the "uncomputable" nature of the physical world by learning from human interaction rather than explicit programming. He posits that "Being human, after all, is spending a lifetime taking actions based on what we experience, observe, and learn." This observation-driven approach allows machines to internalize the physics and social dynamics of the world without needing to code every variable.

"If human brains are much more efficient than best-in-class LLMs, then we can get all of that computation practically for free by observing how humans respond to the countless variables in their environments."

The piece effectively argues that the next generation of AI will not be defined by how well it writes poetry, but by how well it can navigate a room, avoid obstacles, and manipulate objects. While the timeline McCormick proposes (2026) is aggressive, the direction of travel seems clear. The convergence of massive funding, theoretical breakthroughs in predictive coding, and the limitations of current language models creates a fertile ground for this shift.

"This ability to compute the uncomputable is why we believe World Models will unlock progress in embodied AI in a way that current model architectures can't."

Bottom Line

McCormick's strongest contribution is his clear distinction between passive video prediction and active, action-conditioned modeling, a nuance often lost in the current AI hype. His argument is vulnerable to the immense difficulty of training models on the chaotic, unstructured data of the real world, but his conviction that this is the necessary path to embodied intelligence is compelling. Readers should watch for the 2026 milestone he predicts, as that will be the true test of whether these models can move from "cool videos" to functional robotics.
