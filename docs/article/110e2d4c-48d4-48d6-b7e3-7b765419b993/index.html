100 things that made my year

Commentary by Hex Index staff

In a media landscape obsessed with breaking news and viral outrage, Austin Kleon offers a radical alternative: a year measured not in headlines, but in the quiet, tactile rituals of a life well-lived. This isn't merely a list of hobbies; it is a deliberate argument for the necessity of slowing down, curating one's attention, and finding profound meaning in the mundane. For the busy professional drowning in digital noise, Kleon's inventory of 100 small joys serves as a corrective to the modern anxiety of missing out.

The Architecture of Attention

Kleon structures his year around the deliberate curation of sensory experiences, arguing that our environment shapes our internal state. He writes, "Keeping the calendar clear to write. ('Open the document, stay in the document.')" This simple instruction highlights a core thesis: creativity requires the removal of friction, not the addition of more input. By prioritizing the act of showing up over the pressure of producing a masterpiece, Kleon reframes productivity as a form of presence.

The author suggests that technical constraints often unlock spiritual breakthroughs. He notes, "How often the spiritual problems of a piece of writing are connected to its technical problems, and how often a fix to a technical problem also solves a spiritual problem." This observation cuts through the mystique of the creative process, grounding it in the physical reality of the work. It is a reminder that the struggle to find the right word is often the struggle to find the right thought.

"How often the spiritual problems of a piece of writing are connected to its technical problems, and how often a fix to a technical problem also solves a spiritual problem."

Critics might argue that this focus on the domestic and the local ignores the broader, urgent crises facing the world. However, Kleon counters this by embedding his personal joy within a context of civic engagement, such as "Staple-gunning hand-printed flyers to telephone polls all over our neighborhood to try to save our son's middle school." The argument holds that one cannot engage with the world effectively without first tending to the immediate, tangible realities of one's own life and community.

Analog Resistance in a Digital Age

A significant portion of the commentary centers on the tension between digital convenience and analog depth. Kleon describes the generational gap he bridges by creating physical artifacts in a screen-dominated world. He recounts, "Sharing a mixtape, then getting a comment from a young man who said he didn't understand what a mixtape was or how you make one." This moment of confusion is not framed as a failure of the younger generation, but as a loss of a specific type of intentional curation.

Kleon doubles down on this by investing in physical media, writing, "Splurging on a brand-new Tascam cassette deck." He argues that the friction of physical media—the need to flip a tape, the tactile nature of a record—forces a deeper engagement with the art. As he puts it, "There are all kinds of mix tapes. There is always a reason to make one." This is a subtle critique of the algorithmic playlists that dominate modern listening, suggesting that human selection carries an emotional weight that code cannot replicate.

The author also touches on the danger of artificial intelligence in art, noting, "How AI slop is making us really think about what it means to be human and make art." Rather than rejecting technology outright, Kleon uses the threat of automation to sharpen his appreciation for human imperfection and effort. He finds value in the struggle, observing that "Looking up the answer to the question, 'How did this even get made?' (Usually, the answer is: 'Well, it wasn't made like anything else was made.')" This emphasis on the unique, non-replicable process of creation is a defense of human agency.

The Practice of Low Expectations

Perhaps the most striking aspect of Kleon's year is his philosophical shift toward lowering expectations to find joy. He writes, "Keeping my expectations low. ('Are the lights on? Is the water running? Is there food in the fridge?')" This stoic approach reframes happiness not as the achievement of grand goals, but as the appreciation of basic stability.

He reinforces this with a quote from C.S. Lewis: "It is only our daily bread that we are encouraged to ask for." This perspective challenges the relentless drive for optimization and growth that characterizes modern professional life. Instead, Kleon advocates for a "long-term pessimist, short-term optimist" mindset, which allows for resilience in the face of uncertainty. He writes, "Trying to replace my dread with curiosity," suggesting that the antidote to anxiety is not certainty, but an open-minded engagement with the unknown.

"Trying to replace my dread with curiosity. Being a long-term pessimist, short-term optimist. Believing in the wild possibilities of a single day."

Some might argue that this philosophy risks complacency, encouraging a retreat from the harder fights of the world. Yet, Kleon's list includes moments of active resistance and community building, proving that low expectations for the self do not equate to low standards for society. The balance he strikes is between accepting what cannot be changed and fiercely protecting what can.

Bottom Line

Austin Kleon's list succeeds because it treats the ordinary as extraordinary, offering a blueprint for a life that resists the frantic pace of the digital age. While the focus on personal rituals might seem escapist to some, the underlying message is one of profound resilience: by anchoring ourselves in the tangible and the local, we build the capacity to navigate the chaotic and the abstract. The strongest part of this argument is its practicality; it is not a call to abandon the world, but a guide on how to inhabit it more fully. Readers should watch for how this philosophy of "low expectations" translates into action when the stakes are high, as Kleon hints that the two are not mutually exclusive.

Hey y’all,

Here’s my annual list of 100 things that made my year:

Walking cookies to friends on New Year’s Day.

Accepting the sad fact that my nervous system can only handle one cup of coffee a day. Drinking lots of herbal tea instead. Peppermint. Trader Joe’s Harvest Blend. A cup cap accessory for my Yeti Rambler that turns it into a thermos like workers take to construction sites.

Having tweens! One of them now a teen! 6-7!

Taking the boys to see the movies they love at the Village Alamo Drafthouse. Sonic The Hedgehog 3. A Minecraft Movie. FNAF 2.

Pizza and a movie every Friday night. Wallace & Gromit: Vengeance Most Fowl. Mousehunt. Freaky Friday. The Adventures of Tintin. The Wild Robot. Flow. Harry and The Hendersons. KPop Demon Hunters. Napoleon Dynamite. (The kids still shout, “Make yourself a danged quesadilla!”) Rango. The Gold Rush. Good Burger. Young Frankenstein. Who Framed Roger Rabbit? Addams Family Values. Galaxy Quest.

Building fires in our fireplace. Chopping kindling. Dipping into The Book of Building Fires and experimenting with the top-down method.

Riding bikes with Marty.

Making mixtapes to listen to in the studio. Sharing a mixtape, then getting a comment from a young man who said he didn’t understand what a mixtape was or how you make one. Recording an explanatory video for him that made me feel a million years old. Splurging on a brand-new Tascam cassette deck. Rob Sheffield’s memoir Love is a Mix Tape. (“There are all kinds of mix tapes. There is always a reason to make one.”)

Texting old photos to college pals on a Friday night.

Spaghetti every Monday night. Making a “Red Sauce” mix for Meghan to play while she’s making the meatballs. Discovering the Ultra-Lounge series of compilation CDs.

Mixing gin martinis with three olives.

Getting through winter with soup and heat. Soup lessons. Seeing The Soup Peddler sign with an elegant edit of something I wrote: “Soup teaches us some things get better tomorrow.”

Flowers on the kitchen table. The way Meg buys bouquets and deconstructs them into mini-bouquets.

Keeping the calendar clear to write. (“Open the document, stay in the document.”)
