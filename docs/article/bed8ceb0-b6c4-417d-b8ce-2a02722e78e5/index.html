The Batsh*t Software Aphex Twin Used

Benn Jordan · Benn Jordan ·May 26, 2024 · 20 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Benn Jordan doesn't just list the software Aphex Twin used; he argues that the tools themselves are a testament to a level of obsessive, almost masochistic creativity that modern producers have forgotten. While most discussions about electronic music legends focus on the final sound, Jordan insists that the "batsh*t" methods Richard James employed to create it are the real story, revealing a genius who treated code as a canvas and command lines as paintbrushes.

The Myth of the Secret Weapon

Jordan immediately dismantles the common musician's obsession with gear, noting that the question "what software do you use" often carries an "unintentional suggestion that there's some secret tool out there that made all of my music." He counters this by emphasizing that James's output came from "countless hours of experimenting and editing and learning until my eyes and ears bled." This framing is crucial because it shifts the narrative from technical acquisition to human endurance. The author's point lands hard: the magic wasn't in the box, but in the person inside the box, willing to suffer through the limitations of 1990s technology.

Critics might argue that this romanticizes the struggle, suggesting that modern tools could achieve the same results with less friction. However, Jordan's demonstration of the software proves that the friction was the point. The difficulty forced a unique creative path that easy, modern interfaces might never have inspired.

Painting with Sound: Media101 and the Visual Approach

When discussing the Windowlicker EP, Jordan describes how James didn't program the music in a traditional sense but rather "painted" it using software called Media101. He explains that this tool "allows you to use sound as your medium and then color represents panning," turning audio engineering into a visual art form. Jordan demonstrates this by drawing a French horn and manipulating its spectrum to create reverb, noting that the result is "pretty chaotic" yet structurally brilliant.

"I'm fairly certain that I could be locked in a room for a month with the much more featured modern version of Media101 and I still would not come close to making anything that sounds as structured or frankly as awesome as this."

This comparison is the piece's emotional core. By juxtaposing his own inability to replicate the sound even with superior modern tools against James's 1999 output, Jordan validates the idea that the artist's vision transcends the software's capabilities. The evidence holds up because he actually attempts the workflow, showing the viewer the clunky, non-linear nature of the process rather than just describing it.

The Command Line as a Creative Instrument

The most striking section of the commentary focuses on the album Come to Daddy, specifically the track "Buis Bouncing Ball." Jordan reveals that the chaotic, tearing sounds were created using Composer's Desktop Project (CDP), a command-line interface software that ran on DOS. He details the grueling process of typing commands like "Reverb ball.wav" with specific parameters for reflection gain and absorption, noting that "probably the most difficult way that you could put Reverb on anything in 2024" was the standard method for James.

Jordan paraphrases the experience of using CDP as "subjecting himself to hours, days, maybe even weeks of trial and error." He argues that this lack of a graphical user interface (GUI) forced a level of precision and experimentation that visual editors discourage. The author's demonstration of the command-line interface, followed by a look at the dated GUI front-ends like "Sound Shaper," illustrates just how alien the workflow was.

"Now you may start to understand how the Masterpiece Buis Bouncing Ball was created."

This section effectively bridges the gap between technical obscurity and artistic triumph. It forces the reader to confront the reality that some of the most innovative music in history was made by people who refused to use the "easy" buttons, preferring to type code until the sound broke in interesting ways.

Linear Drumming and the Tracker Legacy

Jordan also explores the rhythmic precision of James's work, introducing the concept of "linear drumming"—a technique where only one sound plays at a time per track. He attributes this style to the use of trackers, software that sequences music in vertical columns where each line can only hold one voice. He speculates that for tracks like those on the Richard D. James Album, James likely used a tracker to trigger a SCSI sampler, creating intricate patterns that would be difficult to replicate on standard piano-roll sequencers.

He highlights a specific melody from the track "V Hbin," noting that the software used, Player Pro, required splitting notes into separate lanes to play chords, a "tracker's weakness" that James "breezes right through." This observation is vital because it reframes a software limitation as a stylistic choice. The constraint of the tool dictated the linear, staccato nature of the drums, which became a signature of the genre.

Critics might note that attributing specific software to James is speculative, as the artist rarely confirmed his exact toolkit. However, Jordan mitigates this by presenting the evidence as a logical deduction based on the sonic characteristics and the era's available technology, rather than claiming definitive proof.

The Hidden Code: SuperCollider and Custom Systems

The commentary concludes by touching on the more obscure, code-based tools James likely utilized, such as SuperCollider. Jordan mentions that James's roommate, Chris Jeffs, used a custom SuperCollider system for live performance, featuring a "TB303 style vocal sequencer with text to speech functionality" that was "next level stuff" even two decades ago. Jordan emphasizes that James's access to these custom, often unreleased tools gave him a distinct advantage.

"The whole reason I'm making this video in the first place is an attempt to expand my viewers' palette of tools off of the beaten path territory."

This final point serves as a call to action for the reader, urging them to stop chasing the latest plugin and start exploring the "beaten path" of older, stranger, and more difficult software. It suggests that the future of innovation lies in looking backward at the tools that demanded more from their users.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's commentary succeeds because it treats the history of software not as a timeline of features, but as a map of creative constraints that shaped a legend. The strongest part of the argument is the demonstration that modern ease of use has come at the cost of the unique sonic textures born from struggle. The biggest vulnerability is the inevitable speculation regarding James's exact workflow, but Jordan's willingness to admit uncertainty while providing deep technical context makes the speculation feel grounded rather than conjectural. Readers should watch for the tools Jordan recommends, not to copy James, but to rediscover the friction that sparks innovation.
