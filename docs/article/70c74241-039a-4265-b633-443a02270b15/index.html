Why China Is Falling Behind in AI | by PKU Scholar Hou Hong

Various · Sinification ·Feb 12, 2026 · 26 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

Paul Triolo, a technology policy expert who recently toured China's AI sector, delivers a crucial correction to the prevailing narrative that China is simply losing the artificial intelligence race. While many analysts fixate on chip shortages, Triolo argues the real danger lies in a structural mismatch between China's manufacturing prowess and its inability to monetize AI in a service-based economy. This piece is essential listening because it moves beyond the "chip war" headlines to expose a deeper, more insidious cycle of low growth that could trap Chinese innovation for years.

The Vicious Cycle of Ecosystems

The article challenges the assumption that model quality is the primary battleground. Instead, Triolo highlights Hou Hong's thesis that "factor conditions, demand conditions, and competitive structure are interacting in a mutually reinforcing negative cycle." This is a sophisticated argument: it suggests that even if Chinese firms build a model as good as the American version, the surrounding business environment prevents it from generating value. The author notes that while China can "reproduce [technological approaches] quickly and even optimise locally," the real bottleneck is "upstream and downstream."

This framing is powerful because it shifts the blame from engineering failure to market failure. In the United States, businesses are willing to pay a premium for AI because "productivity gains map directly to economic value." In China, however, "business to business in China is hard," and consumer AI is often treated as a mere "search upgrade rather than a deep productivity tool." The consequence is stark: Chinese productivity plays are forced into overseas markets because the domestic ecosystem cannot support them.

"The constraint is no longer just model ideas, but the surrounding system—compute supply chains and commercialisation."

Critics might argue that this view underestimates the speed at which state-directed economies can force adoption, but the data on token consumption suggests a real gap. With China processing only 30 trillion tokens daily compared to Google's 980 trillion monthly, the scale of engagement is simply not there yet.

The Agentic Future and the Hardware Myth

A significant portion of the commentary addresses the "pessimists" who believe China is doomed by a lack of advanced semiconductors. Triolo pushes back, suggesting that "major breakthroughs within the domestic semiconductor industry over the next two to three years" will narrow the gap, particularly as the industry moves from training to inference. He points to the recent launch of an agentic AI smartphone by ByteDance and ZTE as proof that China is not behind in deployment.

However, the author acknowledges a critical friction point: the "closed, app-based ecosystem" in China, shaped by giants like Tencent and Alibaba, prevents the "Internet of Agents" from flourishing. In the US, the architecture is open, allowing agents from different platforms to interact. In China, "platform monopolies stifle 'creative destruction'." This is a vital distinction. The US is shifting to a paradigm of "AI using AI," while China remains stuck in a model where "humans using AI" is the primary interaction.

"China will be the first to deploy smartphone-based agentic platforms at scale, but the transition to an open Internet of Agents is stalled by China's closed ecosystem."

This observation is particularly sharp. It suggests that China's strength in hardware and infrastructure could become a weakness if the software layer remains walled off. The "Internet of Agents" requires a level of interoperability that Chinese tech giants, who want to keep users within their own "gardens," are unlikely to provide voluntarily.

The Talent Paradox and the Path Forward

The article also weighs in on the "optimists" who point to China's massive talent pool and industrial chain. While acknowledging China's "advantage in terms of both the quantity and quality of its higher education students," Triolo argues that this advantage is being neutralized by a lack of risk capital and a manufacturing-dominated economy. The core issue is that China's economy is better suited for physical goods than for the "service-oriented AI" required for the next generation of innovation.

The author suggests that the lack of "AI readiness" in Chinese enterprises is a double-edged sword. While it is a current disadvantage, the combination of government signals and competitive pressure "could ultimately turn into an advantage" by forcing a rapid, top-down digitalization. Yet, the risk remains that "DeepSeek cannot disrupt China's tech ecosystem" because its open-source software primarily benefits established platform companies rather than spawning new unicorns.

"Prioritising AGI research over service innovation... benefits established platform companies to the detriment of potential unicorns."

This is a sobering assessment of the Chinese innovation model. It implies that without a shift in how capital is allocated and how platforms are regulated, China's AI industry may remain a "follower" in the most lucrative segments of the market.

Bottom Line

Triolo's most compelling contribution is the argument that the AI race is no longer about who has the best model, but who has the most open and monetizable ecosystem. The strongest part of the analysis is the identification of China's "closed garden" problem as a barrier to the "Internet of Agents." The biggest vulnerability, however, is the assumption that domestic hardware breakthroughs will arrive quickly enough to offset the current commercial stagnation. Readers should watch for whether China can actually break the monopoly of its tech giants to allow for the "creative destruction" that drives true AI innovation.

Recently there has been much debate within Chinese industry and academia over how the country stands with respect to the United States in developing and deploying AI. A recent commentary by Hou Hong, titled Personal insights on the US-China AI gap, falls on the side of a group, including within large Chinese AI companies, who hold that major structural issues will keep Chinese companies behind their Western, mostly US counterparts. They hold that especially as competition shifts from “who has the best model” to “who builds the strongest ecosystem for deployment and compounding usage”, Chinese companies will be at a disadvantage.

The Pessimists: China’s Disadvantage in Compute and Commercialisation

For Hou, China is not portrayed as losing primarily because it cannot produce strong models. Rather, the author’s main thesis is that factor conditions, demand conditions, and competitive structure are interacting in a mutually reinforcing negative cycle—keeping China on a lower trajectory in agent deployment and especially in building an Internet-of-Agents-style ecosystem, while the US compounds advantages through faster deployment, stronger rivalry dynamics, more open ecosystem surfaces, and heavier infrastructure/ecosystem investment.

In a recent roundtable, technologists from Alibaba, Tencent and Zhipu also converged on a pragmatic view of what still holds China back from “catching up” with the US: the constraint is no longer just model ideas, but the surrounding system—compute supply chains and commercialisation.
