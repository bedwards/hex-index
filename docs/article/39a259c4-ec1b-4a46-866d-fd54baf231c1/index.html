Here's a factoid for you. According to the United Nations, Africa possesses 30% of the essential minerals needed for renewable technologies and enjoys no less than 60% of our planet's best sunlight exposure. In July 2025, the UN Secretary General Antonio Gut said this. >> By 2040, Africa could generate 10 times more electricity than it needs entirely from renewables.

That's quite a claim, isn't it? And surely an opportunity for any enterprising capitalist entrepreneur looking for a potentially lucrative gap in the market somewhere. And it turns out somewhat ironically that the most enterprising capitalist entrepreneur operating on that vast continent in recent years has been China. To paraphrase the International Monetary Fund, there was a vacuum and China has been far quicker than Western nations to exploit that vacuum in the renewables and infrastructure space.

The 12 month to June 2025 saw a 60% increase in solar panel imports into Africa. And now folks in off-grid areas are generating their own power for less than half the cost of a diesel generator. And if you're wondering why that is in the slightest bit relevant to you, then stick around because the answer might surprise you. Hello and welcome to Just Have a Think.

So, is that it then? China just swoops in and scoops up Africa like some kind of token on a geopolitical board game? Well, not quite. But it's easy to see why that metaphor might seem compelling.

For decades, most Western nations treated African development like a bit of a side quest. A dribble of aid money here, a few development projects there, but very little real investment in the sort of hard infrastructure that actually powers modern economies. By contrast, China offered the construction of roads and ports and railways as well as really great energy deals, not as charity, but as mutually beneficial commerce, at least on the face of it anyway. Because while there's no doubt those investments have resulted in visible progress in many African nations, they also raise some tough questions about debt and dependency and who really calls the shots when the loans come due.
