Housing villains

Various · Reason ·Jun 30, 2026 ·14 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

In a political landscape often defined by gridlock, this piece from Reason identifies a rare moment of bipartisan cooperation on housing supply that is now being held hostage at the finish line. The article's most striking claim isn't just that legislation passed, but that the primary obstacle to its enactment is not ideological polarization between parties, but a unilateral demand for unrelated partisan leverage from the executive branch.

The Spoiler in the Oval Office

The editors frame the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act as an anomaly in modern governance. "This week's newsletter thus focuses on two of these housing villains," they write, immediately setting a tone that prioritizes policy outcomes over political theater. The first target is the White House's refusal to sign a bill that passed with overwhelming majorities: 85 yeas to five nays in the Senate and 358–32 in the House.

Reason reports, "Trump canceled a signing ceremony for the bill and said that he would not sign the legislation until Congress passed the SAVE America Act, a piece of partisan election legislation." This move effectively turns a bipartisan supply-side reform into leverage for a separate political agenda. The article notes that even if the president vetoes it, Congress has the votes to override, yet questions whether the Republican-controlled chamber would defy their own leader.

The coverage details how this bill was the product of unlikely alliances, specifically between Senator Tim Scott and Representative Elizabeth Warren. It began as a vehicle for supply-side reforms, including deregulation of manufactured homes and grants for states that liberalize zoning codes—a nod to the broader historical struggle where local land-use restrictions have long stifled construction, similar to the complex rent regulation battles seen in New York history.

"The whole saga is a good reminder that cynicism about federal politics is rarely misplaced."

The piece argues that the administration's stance ignores the bill's core utility: increasing housing construction. By demanding an unrelated partisan priority be met first, the executive branch risks derailing a package that includes a ban on large investor purchases of single-family homes—a policy the president himself championed in his State of the Union address. Critics might note that using legislative leverage is a standard political tactic; however, the article effectively highlights the unique irony of blocking a bill that fulfills one's own stated priority simply to extract concessions elsewhere.

The Double-Edged Sword of Rent Control

The commentary then pivots to New York City, where Mayor Zohran Mamdani has fulfilled a campaign promise with a vote by the Rent Guidelines Board to freeze rents on one- and two-year leases. "Everyone likes ice cream at the moment," the editors observe, using the mayor's dessert-themed publicity stunt as a metaphor for short-term political gains that mask long-term economic toxicity.

The article points out the dangerous disconnect between this freeze and rising operational costs. According to the board's own estimates, operating costs for rent-stabilized buildings increased 5.3 percent in 2026. "Such is the case with the adopted rent freeze," Reason argues, warning that while tenants get a temporary reprieve, the policy accelerates the physical deterioration of housing stock.

The editors highlight the structural trap created by the state's 2019 rent law updates, which eliminated most avenues for landlords to raise rents even when facing financial distress. "Absent changes to the 2019 rent law, one can expect more buildings to go bankrupt and more units to deteriorate into uninhabitability," the piece warns. This creates a paradox where the mayor's push for new supply through zoning reform is undermined by his simultaneous destruction of existing housing viability.

"Mamdani's supply-side socialism is merely trading more homes for fewer homes."

A counterargument worth considering is that without rent freezes, vulnerable tenants face immediate displacement in an unaffordable market. However, the article's focus on the financial health of the building stock suggests that preserving affordability today may result in a total loss of housing tomorrow.

The Judicial Horizon

Finally, the editors address the speculation surrounding the Supreme Court's potential intervention. While Justice Clarence Thomas has signaled openness to reviewing rent control cases under the Fifth Amendment's Takings Clause, the article cautions against high hopes. "Past challenges to New York's rent law have not fared well in the courts," Reason notes, citing the exceptionally high bar for proving regulatory takings.

The piece explains that while a new lawsuit filed by property owners and the Small Property Owners of New York Association is underway, the legal system has historically been slow to dismantle these regulations. The editors suggest that waiting for judicial salvation is less effective than addressing the legislative roots of the crisis.

Bottom Line

This commentary succeeds in reframing housing policy not as a battle between left and right, but as a clash between supply-side pragmatism and political obstructionism from both the executive branch and local populists. Its strongest argument lies in exposing how short-term political wins—whether holding a bill hostage or freezing rents—undermine the long-term goal of affordable, habitable housing. The biggest vulnerability remains the legal reality that courts are unlikely to rescue the market quickly enough to prevent immediate deterioration, leaving policymakers as the only viable agents of change.
