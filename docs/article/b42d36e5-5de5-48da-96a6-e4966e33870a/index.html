ICE Gets Hammered In Court

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Mar 6, 2026 · 23 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Devin Stone delivers a rare legal exposé: federal judges are not just criticizing the Trump administration's immigration enforcement—they are threatening to jail its officials. This is not standard judicial pushback; it is a historic breakdown of the rule of law where courts must repeatedly threaten contempt to force the government to obey basic orders.

A System in Collapse

Stone opens with a startling claim that sets the stakes immediately. "For the past year, ICE has been breaking people's lives, and now it's breaking the entire justice system." He supports this by detailing how federal judges across the political spectrum, including those appointed by Donald Trump, are sounding a "five alarm firebell." The author argues that the court is facing a situation "unlike anything I've ever seen in my legal career," where the government must be threatened with contempt "again and again and again" just to comply with court orders. This framing is effective because it moves beyond policy disagreement to a constitutional crisis, suggesting that the executive branch is operating as if it is above the law.

"The court is not aware of another occasion in the history of the United States in which a federal court has had to threaten contempt again and again and again to force the United States government to comply with court orders."

Stone illustrates this collapse through specific cases in Minnesota, where ICE's "Operation Metro Search" led to indiscriminate sweeps. He notes that Judge Jeffrey Bryant ordered the return of seized property—cash, phones, passports—yet the government filed "weak declarations" claiming they were "currently not aware of any property return issues," despite evidence to the contrary. The author's point is clear: the administration's response was not a mistake but a pattern of evasion. Stone writes, "The government's responses are incomplete and evasive and do not comply with the court's February 20 order." This highlights a dangerous precedent where the government treats court orders as suggestions rather than mandates.

Critics might argue that the sheer volume of cases created an administrative bottleneck, making perfect compliance impossible. Stone anticipates this by citing a prosecutor who blamed "enormous volume" and lack of experience, but he rightly dismisses this as a "problem of its own making" that does not justify "flagrant disobedience."

The Contempt Escalation

The commentary deepens as Stone explains the gravity of contempt proceedings. He distinguishes between civil contempt, which is meant to coerce compliance, and criminal contempt, which is punitive and can lead to imprisonment. Stone notes that judges are now resorting to "show cause" hearings where officials must appear in person to explain their defiance. He references a similar precedent where Judge James Boasberg found probable cause for criminal contempt against the administration for deporting people under the Alien Enemies Act despite court orders.

As Devin Stone puts it, "Criminal contempt is a very big deal in federal court... It's about vindicating the authority of the court itself." This distinction is crucial because it signals that the judiciary is no longer just correcting errors but punishing willful defiance. Stone details how Judge Laura Provenino held a federal prosecutor in civil contempt, ordering a $500 daily fine until a detained man's documents were returned. The judge rejected the government's excuse of being understaffed, stating, "I don't believe I need to do additional handholding on this."

Stone also highlights the absurdity of the government's reaction to judicial scrutiny. When Chief Judge Patrick Schiltz listed 96 prior violations by ICE, the U.S. Attorney claimed the assessment was "beyond the pale." Stone writes, "Imagine saying, 'Judge, I did my own math and my numbers indicate that we only messed up in four of the 11 cases.' That's that's not going to fly." This anecdote powerfully illustrates the administration's refusal to accept judicial reality, forcing the court to independently verify every single violation.

"If anything is beyond the pale, it is ICE's continued violation of the orders of this court."

The author connects this local chaos to broader legal trends, referencing the Supreme Court's decision in Trump v. CASA. Stone warns that this ruling made it "near impossible for federal courts to stop illegal government action nationwide," leading to a clogged system where policies are challenged repeatedly until the courts break. This context adds depth, showing that the current crisis is not just about bad actors but about a legal framework that has been eroded to the point of failure.

The Human Cost and the Media Blind Spot

Stone shifts to the human impact, describing "Operation Country Roads" in West Virginia, where agents stop cars and grab people without warrants. He quotes Judge Goodwin, who described the scene as "masked, anonymous, armed with military weapons, operating from unmarked vehicles, acting without warrants of any kind." Stone uses this to argue that the government has eliminated "every structural feature that distinguishes constitutional authority from raw force."

To ensure readers understand the full scope of this narrative, Stone pivots to the role of media framing, using Ground News to show how different outlets report the same events. He notes that while some sources highlight bipartisan outrage, others frame it as a partisan attack. Stone writes, "Facts don't shape public opinion. Framing does." This section serves as a meta-commentary on how the legal crisis is being obscured by political narratives, urging readers to look beyond headlines to see the systemic failure.

Critics might suggest that Stone's reliance on a specific sponsor (Ground News) dilutes the legal analysis, but the underlying point about media fragmentation remains valid: without a clear understanding of the facts, public accountability is impossible.

Bottom Line

Stone's most compelling argument is that the rule of law is not just strained but actively being dismantled by an administration that treats court orders as optional. His strongest evidence lies in the independent verification of over 100 violated orders, which dismantles the government's claim of accidental non-compliance. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its heavy reliance on specific, localized cases in Minnesota, which, while damning, may not fully capture the scope of federal enforcement nationwide. However, the pattern of contempt threats from judges of both parties suggests a systemic issue that demands immediate attention.
