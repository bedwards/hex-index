2025 in review: The year the US changed sides

Phillips P. O'Brien · Phillips P. O'Brien ·Dec 31, 2025 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Phillips P. O'Brien presents a chilling thesis that cuts through the noise of annual reviews: 2025 was not just a year of policy shifts, but the moment the United States fundamentally changed sides in a global conflict. While most analysis focuses on the mechanics of a new administration, O'Brien argues that the US has transitioned from a defender of democratic sovereignty to a protector of authoritarian aggression, a move with immediate, lethal consequences for civilians. This is not a story about personality, but about a structural realignment that has left the world's democracies leaderless and vulnerable.

The Anatomy of a Betrayal

O'Brien's framing is stark and unapologetic. He rejects the narrative that the administration is merely seeking "peace," arguing instead that the diplomatic posture has shifted to actively undermine Ukrainian sovereignty. "In 2025 all of that ended. The US ended direct support for Ukraine and provided a great deal of diplomatic support and cover for Russia," he writes. This is a deliberate pivot from the previous year's strategy, where the executive branch had committed to Ukraine's territorial integrity and NATO membership. The author suggests that the American political system has effectively handed a "blank check" to remake the nation's global standing, a choice that O'Brien insists is now an American reality, not just a political maneuver.

The human cost of this diplomatic shift is the piece's most harrowing element. O'Brien connects the withdrawal of military aid directly to the surge in civilian suffering. He notes that the United States, once the primary backer of Ukraine's air defense, has now become an "unreliable, arguably destructive, actor." The result is measurable and tragic: "In 2025 through mid-November, Ukrainian civilian casualties were up by 27%." This statistic is not presented as a footnote but as the direct outcome of a policy decision to restrict the supply of Patriot missiles and 155mm ammunition. Critics might argue that diplomatic pressure is a necessary evil to end a war, but O'Brien contends that the current terms—demanding Ukraine surrender unconquered lands and forbidding NATO membership—are not peace, but a recipe for permanent instability.

The best that the US now offers to Ukraine is now far far bleaker than the worst that would have been on offer last year.

The Strategic Vacuum and the Patriot Gap

The commentary delves into the military mechanics of this retreat, highlighting a specific vulnerability that has catastrophic implications. O'Brien points out that Ukrainian air defense systems were constructed around US-made hardware, specifically the MIM-104 Patriot. He draws a parallel to the concept of "Finlandization," where a smaller nation is forced to accommodate the foreign policy of a powerful neighbor, noting that the US is now actively engineering this outcome for Ukraine. By reducing the supply of Patriot ammunition, the administration has left Ukrainian cities exposed to Russian cruise and ballistic missiles.

The author describes the administration's behavior as "erratic and dangerous," oscillating between halting aid and slow-walking deliveries. "The US went from Ukraine's most important military backer to an unreliable, arguably destructive, actor," O'Brien writes. This inconsistency has not only weakened the front lines but has also emboldened the Russian war machine, allowing it to wage a "war-crime riddled strategy" with renewed confidence. The administration's refusal to allow the use of seized Russian assets, partly due to US influence, further illustrates how the executive branch is prioritizing relations with Moscow over the survival of Kyiv.

A Global Realignment

Beyond the battlefield, O'Brien argues that this shift represents a rupture in the post-1945 world order. The 2025 National Security Strategy, according to the author, has codified this new reality by identifying European democracies as the "great problem" while treating Russia and China with "kid gloves." This is a profound departure from the alliances that defined the Cold War and its aftermath. The author warns that this has left democratic states "leaderless and divided," forcing them to reconsider their security architectures in the absence of American protection.

The piece suggests that the administration is engaging in a "pantomime" of threats and posturing that ultimately serves to buy time for the Russian dictator. "All of this playacting had massive implications for the war as it gave Putin protected time," O'Brien observes. By keeping Europe guessing and delaying decisive action, the US has effectively allowed the conflict to drag on under terms favorable to the aggressor. The author's conclusion is stark: the world must acknowledge that the US is no longer a friend to liberal democracy and is, in many ways, working to subvert it.

The USA has changed sides, is not their friend and in many ways is out to subvert their futures as the US becomes an ally of authoritarians and dictators.

Bottom Line

Phillips P. O'Brien's argument is at its strongest when it refuses to separate policy from its human toll, grounding the abstract concept of "changing sides" in the 27% rise in civilian casualties. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its absolute certainty that the shift is permanent and that no internal political resistance can reverse it, potentially overlooking the resilience of democratic institutions. However, the core warning remains urgent: the world is watching a superpower dismantle the very alliances it once built, and the cost is being paid in blood by those left behind.

However when you step back and look at things from a broad perspective, one thing towers over the others.

2025 was the year that the USA changed sides.

In 2024 the US was a strong (and growing) supporter of Ukraine and an opponent of Russian aggression. In 2024 the US worked hard to organize and support its NATO allies, it was increasingly committed to helping Taiwan and was working assiduously to increase its friendships/alliances throughout the Indo-Pacific region.

In 2025 all of that ended. The US ended direct support for Ukraine and provided a great deal of diplomatic support and cover for Russia. It weakened, perhaps fatally, much of its alliance structure around the world. It entered trade wars with its friends and economically enhanced states that it used to see as its competitors. The USA even ended up codifying this new international reality in its 2025 National Security Strategy, which named European democracies as the great problem and handled Russia and China with kid gloves, arguably kindness. The changing of sides was being proclaimed from the rooftops.
