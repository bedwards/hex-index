Mick Ryan identifies a chilling evolution in Chinese military strategy: the shift from passive border defense to an aggressive, proactive posture designed to shape the battlefield before a conflict even begins. This piece is not merely a recap of recent drills; it is a forensic breakdown of how the People's Liberation Army (PLA) is using live-fire exercises to test the limits of American deterrence and psychologically dismantle the resolve of democratic allies. For busy leaders tracking the Pacific, the critical takeaway is that these maneuvers are no longer just about signaling; they are rehearsals for a specific, high-stakes campaign of isolation and decapitation.

The Architecture of Coercion

Ryan frames the recent "Justice Mission 2025" exercise not as an isolated event, but as the physical manifestation of a strategic pivot that began in earnest after 2022. He notes that authoritative PLA texts now emphasize "enhancing the aggressiveness of the military strategic guidelines," a phrase that signals a fundamental departure from decades of defensive doctrine. As Ryan writes, "The implementation of this guidance can be seen in China's more assertive military posture in the South China Sea and around Taiwan."

This is a crucial distinction. The PLA is no longer waiting for a threat to materialize; it is actively constructing the conditions for its own victory. Ryan points out that the exercise posters themselves are telling, with shields shaped like blades intended to "cut off the delusional dream of Taiwan independence." This visual rhetoric underscores a grim reality: the Chinese leadership views the separation of Taiwan not as a political dispute, but as an existential imperative that justifies extreme measures. The exercise zones, which cover a much larger area than in previous years, are designed to simulate a complete quarantine of the island, blocking any potential aid from the United States or Japan.

The Chinese are messaging the Taiwanese that 'the noose is tightening' over time and that the sooner they negotiate a return to the PRC, the better it is for them.

The scale of this buildup is quantifiable and alarming. Ryan details how the naval fleet allotted to the Eastern and Southern theater commands has expanded significantly since 2022, with the number of cruisers doubling and the bomber fleet growing by 50 aircraft. This expansion is not merely about quantity; it is about the capacity to sustain a multi-domain campaign. Critics might argue that China's rapid expansion faces logistical bottlenecks and training gaps that could undermine these capabilities in a real-world scenario. However, Ryan's analysis suggests that the sheer volume of these exercises is itself a weapon, designed to normalize aggression and desensitize the international community to the threat of invasion.

The Human Cost of Decapitation

Perhaps the most disturbing aspect of Ryan's coverage is his focus on the specific objectives of the drills, particularly the simulated "decapitation" strikes. This is not abstract military jargon; it refers to the targeted killing or capture of political and military leadership to paralyze a nation's ability to resist. Ryan cites National Defense University professor Zhang Chi, who confirms that the exercise included "simulated strikes on symbolic targets associated with the leaders of the 'Taiwan independence' separatist forces."

This reflects a brutal lesson learned from the war in Ukraine: the Chinese leadership is determined to prevent the emergence of a "Taiwanese Zelenskyy" who could rally the population and secure foreign support. As Ryan puts it, "The CCP cannot afford the emergence of a 'Taiwanese Zelenskyy' in the event of a military invasion of the island, and is sure to have multiple contingency plans to prevent this." This strategic intent carries a heavy human cost. It implies a willingness to target civilian leadership and potentially cause mass casualties in the opening hours of a conflict to ensure a swift, uncontested victory. The focus on "decapitation" transforms the exercise from a show of force into a rehearsal for the systematic dismantling of a democratic society's command structure.

One of the key lessons the Chinese leadership have learned from the war in Ukraine is to not let enemy leaders live to rally their people and gain foreign support.

The timing of the exercise, launched just as high-level diplomatic talks were concluding in the United States, further highlights the calculated nature of this aggression. Ryan suggests that the administration is being tested on its reaction times and decision-making protocols. The goal is to create a "fog of war" where the line between routine patrol and pre-invasion preparation becomes indistinguishable. This ambiguity is a strategic tool, forcing democratic nations to make impossible choices about when to intervene and when to stand down.

Cognitive Warfare and the Battle for Perception

Ryan argues that the most important location for these exercises is not the physical waters of the Taiwan Strait, but the minds of observers in Washington, Tokyo, and Taipei. The PLA is engaging in a sophisticated campaign of cognitive warfare, aiming to project an "inevitable victory" narrative that discourages intervention before the first shot is fired. He writes, "Xi's key objective is to project an 'inevitable victory' narrative (similar to Putins) and that American intervention would be expensive, bloody and ultimately futile."

This psychological dimension is as critical as the military one. By normalizing large-scale, complex joint exercises, the PLA is attempting to erode the political will of potential adversaries. The exercise serves as a constant reminder of the costs of resistance, designed to make the idea of defending Taiwan seem like a futile sacrifice. Ryan notes that the Chinese are analyzing the responses of Taiwan and other nations in the information domain, using media releases and propaganda videos to shape the global narrative. This dual-track approach—military pressure coupled with information dominance—creates a comprehensive environment of coercion.

Truly effective modern military institutions need to integrate the operations of forces from across the air, land, sea, space and cyber domains.

The integration of these domains is the ultimate goal. Ryan emphasizes that the Eastern Theatre Command is working to refine its joint integration, ensuring that air, missile, and drone defenses, as well as offensive fires and logistics, operate as a single, unified machine. This is a massive undertaking, and while the PLA has made significant strides, the complexity of coordinating such a diverse force remains a potential vulnerability. However, the sheer ambition of the "Multi-Domain Precision Warfare" concept suggests that the Chinese military is preparing for a conflict that is far more integrated and lethal than previous regional disputes.

Bottom Line

Mick Ryan's analysis provides a sobering look at the PLA's transition from a defensive force to an offensive machine capable of executing a complex, multi-domain invasion. The strongest part of his argument is the identification of "decapitation" and cognitive warfare as central pillars of Chinese strategy, revealing a willingness to target leadership and manipulate perception to achieve political ends. The biggest vulnerability in this assessment, however, lies in the assumption that the PLA's internal cohesion and logistical capabilities can fully support such an ambitious operational concept under the stress of actual combat. As the administration continues to navigate this shifting landscape, the focus must remain on the human cost of these maneuvers and the urgent need to maintain a credible deterrent that can withstand the pressure of an "inevitable victory" narrative.

On 29 December, just as the summit between President’s Zelenskyy and Trump was finishing at Mar-a-lago, and hundreds of journalists were filing their initial analyses of the meeting, the Peoples Liberation Army Eastern Theatre Command announced that it had commenced exercise "Justice Mission 2025" in the Taiwan Strait.

Similar to other joint exercises conducted in 2025 and 2024, this was an activity to rehearse future military campaigns to “shield” Taiwan from outside military forces that might come to its aid. At the same time, the bottom of the shields in the PLA posters for the exercise are shaped like blades. This is intended by the Chinese to “cut off the delusional dream of Taiwan independence.” Thus, Justice Mission 2025 is intended to project the image of an advanced, capable PLA that can both attack Taiwan while fending off assistance from the military forces of America, Japan and elsewhere.

The exercise is rehearsing a range of military activities and features live fire drills.

The exercise is one of President Xi’s ways of demonstrating his displeasure at the 17 December 2025 announcement by the U.S. government of a massive weapons sale to Taiwan. With a value of US$11.1 billion, this is the largest single U.S. sale to Taiwan in history, and includes highly symbolic weapon systems such as the HIMARS long-range precision strike system.

Finally, we should not discount that Justice Mission 2025 is also designed to message China’s continuing displeasure with Japan. Two of the exercise zones for this PLA activity would (theoretically) permit operations against Japanese military targets on Yonaginajima to the east.
