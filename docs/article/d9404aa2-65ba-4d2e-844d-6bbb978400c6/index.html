Can You Legally Buy a Real Human Skeleton?

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·May 11, 2020 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis does not merely ask if you can legally buy a human skeleton; he exposes a global supply chain where the dead are commodified, the law is ignored, and the medical profession looks the other way. While most assume modern anatomy relies on synthetic models or ethical donations, Francis reveals that the bones in many classrooms may be the result of a centuries-old trade rooted in colonial exploitation and modern negligence. This is not a history lesson; it is an investigative reckoning with the physical reality of medical education.

The Colonial Ghost in the Classroom

Francis begins by dismantling the sanitized image of medical training. He notes that while television shows depict pristine classrooms, the reality of learning anatomy often depended on "grave robbing in the 19th century" and the "insatiable hunger for fresh corpses." As he writes, "for hundreds of years doctors have depended on real human bones to learn from." This historical context is crucial because it sets the stage for the modern trade, which did not vanish with the 1832 Anatomy Act in the UK but merely migrated.

The author traces this migration to India, specifically Kolkata (then Calcutta), where a brutal business model emerged. "The promise of some extra cash was a no-brainer quite literally," Francis observes, describing how the poorest communities were incentivized to process bodies for export. He highlights the scale of this operation, noting that "medics at least here in the UK will be familiar with the name Adam Hui as the makers of just about every anatomical model found in hospitals across the country," yet these companies began by selling real human specimens. The argument here is devastating: the foundation of modern Western medicine in the 20th century was built on the bones of Bengalis, often sourced without consent. "British medical students especially right here in London where Adam Hui was based learned their Anatomy almost exclusively from the bones of Bengalis that had seen better days," Francis states. This reframes the standard narrative of medical progress, suggesting it was powered by a specific, exploitative geography.

Critics might argue that the medical necessity of the time justified these means, but Francis effectively counters this by showing how the trade persisted long after ethical standards supposedly evolved, driven by profit rather than pure necessity.

The Ban That Never Happened

The narrative takes a darker turn when Francis addresses the 1985 ban on exporting human remains following the scandal of 1,500 child skeletons being shipped to the US. One would expect the trade to cease. Instead, Francis argues it went underground. "Slowly but surely the trade returned to India only now it was under the dissection table and behind closed doors." He supports this with his own investigative trip to Kolkata, visiting "Young Brothers," a company that had weathered the ban for decades.

Francis describes the location vividly: "tucked away on a side street between one of Calcutta's largest graveyards and its busiest hospitals." The building appeared abandoned, yet inside, the trade continued. He recounts his attempt to pose as a lecturer to purchase specimens, revealing the casual nature of the illegal market. "Ideally go if I want to take some specimens back to England then that's on me and my problem and I suppose that's fair enough," a dealer told him, dismissing the legal and ethical implications with chilling nonchalance. When pressed on the source of the bodies, the dealer claimed they were "unclaimed bodies," a justification Francis finds dubious given the demographics of the city. "Compared to the 1990s when I saw way more people sleeping on the streets in Calcutta I would be surprised if there are that many dead bodies with nobody to speak for them," he writes, suggesting that bodies are likely being diverted from other sources.

The author's on-the-ground reporting adds a layer of authenticity that pure historical analysis cannot match. He notes the police inaction: "the understaffed and underfunded police in India turn a blind eye to what they probably consider a minor crime. The victims are dead after all." This highlights a systemic failure where the lack of a living voice renders the dead vulnerable to commerce.

The Global Shadow Market

Francis broadens his scope to show that India is not an anomaly but part of a larger, grim ecosystem. He references Scott Carney's book The Red Market to illustrate that "each chapter is about a different human organ or tissue bought and sold as a commodity all over the world." He draws parallels to scandals in the US and UK, such as the removal of organs from babies without consent, to show that the ethical rot is not confined to the Global South. "I'll put some links in the description about what I consider far more macabre scandals in the US where body parts were removed sometimes without consent for use in own grafts," he notes, ensuring the reader understands this is a universal issue of consent and regulation.

He also touches on the cultural nuances that facilitate this trade. "In Hindu and many other Eastern philosophies human remains are not as taboo as they are in the West," Francis explains, noting the belief that the soul leaves the body, leaving only an "empty vessel." While this cultural perspective offers a different framework for understanding the trade, Francis is careful not to use it as an excuse for the lack of regulation. Instead, he uses it to explain why the trade persists despite bans. "Three decades after the ban was imposed Indian merchants continue to supply medical schools with specimens certainly domestically but possibly around the world," he concludes, emphasizing the resilience of the illegal market.

The victims are dead after all and this is just a purely personal observation here but in Hindu and many other Eastern philosophies human remains are not as taboo as they are in the West.

Bottom Line

Francis's investigation is a masterclass in connecting historical exploitation to modern negligence, proving that the skeleton in the corner of the classroom may have a much more sinister origin story than we admit. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to let the reader off the hook with a simple "it's the past" dismissal, instead showing that the trade is alive, well, and largely unregulated. Its vulnerability lies in the inherent difficulty of proving the exact provenance of every bone, but the circumstantial evidence and the casual admissions of dealers make a compelling case that the medical community is complicit in a quiet, ongoing scandal.
