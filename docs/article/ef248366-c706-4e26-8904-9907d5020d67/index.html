Liquid Hydrogen Jet Aircraft : A Carbon-Free Flying Future?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Dec 8, 2019 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace cuts through the moralizing noise of climate activism to confront a harder truth: we aren't going to stop flying, so we must figure out how to fly without burning the planet. While Greta Thunberg's boat trip to the 2019 climate conference was a powerful symbol, Borlace argues it offers no scalable solution for the billions of people who will inevitably choose air travel over sea voyages. The real story isn't about personal sacrifice; it's about the terrifying math of an industry where efficiency gains are completely swallowed by a doubling of passenger numbers every fifteen years.

The Math of Insanity

Borlace begins by dismantling the comforting narrative that aviation is getting greener. He points out that while engines are more efficient, the sheer volume of travel has exploded. "The IATA also optimistically tells us that airlines have cut average emissions per passenger journey in half compared to 1990 levels. Sounds great, admit but according to Tim Johnson... co2 emissions from aviation have doubled over the last twenty to twenty-five years." This is a crucial distinction often missed in headlines; the technology is improving, but the system is growing faster than the technology can save.

The author identifies the root cause not as a lack of innovation, but as a distorted economic reality. "That statistic is driven by massive subsidies that mean for example that it's cheaper to fly 600 miles from London to Geneva in Switzerland than it is to travel 200 miles by train from London to Manchester in the UK." This pricing insanity makes short-haul flights artificially cheap, locking travelers into carbon-intensive choices before they even step on a plane. Critics might argue that raising prices would hurt low-income travelers, but Borlace suggests the current subsidy structure is the true inequity, artificially propping up an unsustainable industry.

"Before any technological solution is even considered the insanity of our current pricing system has to change urgently."

The Battery Dead End

When the conversation shifts to technology, Borlace delivers a sobering reality check on electric aviation. The industry is buzzing with prototypes from Rolls-Royce, Airbus, and others, but the author warns that these are limited to short hops. "All of which is extremely encouraging for short-haul flights all the way up to about a thousand miles but 80% of the aviation industries emissions come from a long haul and that's where battery-powered Electric planes hit the buffers."

The physics simply don't work for long-distance travel with current battery tech. Borlace explains that kerosene packs 43 megajoules per kilogram, whereas lithium-ion batteries manage a "comparatively poultry one mega Joule per kilogram." The weight penalty is insurmountable; a battery-powered plane could only fly about a third of the way across the Atlantic before running out of power. "Power failure in mid-flight is a major risk engineers want to avoid which strikes me as an extremely reasonable point of view." This section effectively tempers the hype around electric planes, clarifying that they are a niche solution, not a silver bullet for global aviation.

The Hydrogen Hope

If batteries fail, Borlace points to a fuel source that sounds like science fiction but has a proven track record in space travel: liquid hydrogen. He draws a parallel to the space shuttle program, noting that "the fuel that filled up those famous orange rocket boosters with liquid hydrogen with a pure oxygen oxidizer... basically allow the liquid hydrogen to come into contact with the pure oxygen and then lissa big-match underneath it all."

The energy density of liquid hydrogen is the game-changer. "It's three times as energy dense as jet a kerosene which means much less fuel weight on an aircraft potentially." This allows for a radical redesign where the fuel is stored in the fuselage rather than the wings, trading some aerodynamic drag for a massive reduction in takeoff weight. A jumbo jet using this system could weigh 90,000 kilograms less at takeoff. "Dr. Bobby Sethi... view is that as soon as conventional fuel prices emission taxation scenarios and the sheer necessity of transformation are taken into account the cost of transition is going to look remarkably modest for such a fundamental long-term solution."

However, the path forward is not without significant hurdles. The infrastructure required to produce, store, and transport cryogenic hydrogen is non-existent at the scale needed. "Ambient temperatures hydrogen is a gas so it has to be cryogenically chilled to get it into liquid form and that takes a massive amount of energy which are current renewable infrastructure isn't yet set up to handle it." Furthermore, the volatility of hydrogen raises safety concerns, though Borlace notes that unlike kerosene spills, "if there was ever a spill lh2 quickly vaporizes and disperses without the same kind of contamination and threat to health of airport staff."

The Long Wait

Despite the promise of the "enable h2" initiative led by Cranfield University, Borlace tempers expectations with a dose of realism. We are not seeing carbon-free long-haul flights tomorrow. "Don't hold your breath just yet because best estimates are that it could take more than a decade to bring a fully functional commercial version of one of these planes to market." In the interim, the industry will rely on incremental gains in fuel efficiency and air traffic management, which may only yield a 10 to 15 percent reduction in emissions.

The ultimate conclusion is that technology alone cannot solve the crisis. The most immediate and impactful change requires political will to fix the pricing model. "The biggest and quickest impact that we can make as a society is the one we looked at right at the top of the program which is to apply a pricing structure that doesn't contain huge subsidies and that accurately represents the carbon cost involved."

"It's cheaper to fly 600 miles from London to Geneva in Switzerland than it is to travel 200 miles by train from London to Manchester in the UK."

Bottom Line

Borlace's strongest argument is his refusal to let the "electric plane" narrative distract from the physics of long-haul travel, correctly identifying liquid hydrogen as the only viable path for decarbonizing the bulk of aviation emissions. His biggest vulnerability is the timeline; relying on a technology that is a decade away from commercial viability leaves a dangerous gap in climate action. The reader should watch for how quickly governments move to price carbon into ticket costs, as that political shift is the only thing that can accelerate the hydrogen transition before it's too late.
