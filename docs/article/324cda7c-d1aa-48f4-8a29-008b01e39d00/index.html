How much of the reading do you have to do in college courses?

Jeffrey Kaplan · Jeffrey Kaplan ·Aug 6, 2021 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Jeffrey Kaplan, an assistant professor of philosophy, drops a bombshell that contradicts the standard academic playbook: you are not actually expected to read every word of every assignment. Drawing on his own trajectory from a top-tier undergraduate to a PhD holder at Berkeley, Kaplan argues that the "standard line" faculty give students is a myth, and that true academic efficiency comes from understanding the hidden codes of how much to read in different disciplines.

The Myth of the Full Read

Kaplan opens by dismantling the assumption that more reading equals better grades. He admits he initially planned to give students the conventional advice but pivoted after reflecting on his own success. "I graduated summa cum laude... with a 3.96 GPA from arguably the most elite, most competitive small college in the country and I did that without doing all the reading," Kaplan writes. He details a strategy of starting with full engagement and then dialing back as he learned the specific expectations of each subject. This is a provocative claim, one that challenges the guilt many students feel when they cannot finish a syllabus. The argument lands because it is backed by the very people who set the rules: professors. Kaplan notes that if you press faculty on this, "they will always admit that in their various subjects... there are little codes for how much of the reading you're supposed to do."

Critics might argue that this approach risks students missing crucial nuances, but Kaplan's point is that the "nuance" is often in the specific sections that matter most, not the filler. The real failure, he suggests, is the lack of transparency. "What is infuriating and frankly unacceptable is that they don't tell you this," he asserts, shifting the blame from student laziness to institutional opacity.

What is infuriating and frankly unacceptable is that they don't tell you this; you're just sort of expected to pick it up on your own.

Discipline-Specific Strategies

The core of Kaplan's commentary lies in his granular breakdown of reading strategies by field. He argues that a "one-size-fits-all" approach is inefficient. In philosophy, the rule is strict: "the readings are short and dense and you have to read every word of them, often multiple times." This makes sense given the nature of the texts, which are often written for peers, not students. However, the strategy shifts dramatically for history. When assigned entire books, Kaplan advises students to "read the introduction and the conclusion and you sort of skim the chapters in the middle." He clarifies that skimming is not about skipping, but about building a map: "you want a vague sense for the topic so that if the professor spends 20 minutes of class talking about [a specific event], you know where that chapter is." This tactical reading allows students to focus their deep reading on the specific topics covered in lecture, turning passive listening into active verification.

The Textbook Trap in Science

Perhaps the most counterintuitive section of Kaplan's argument concerns science and social science textbooks. While professors often claim, "you don't have to even bother reading the textbook," Kaplan insists this is dangerous advice. He offers three reasons why students should ignore this permission. First, professors "overestimate their own ability to explain things well" compared to the edited precision of a textbook. Second, faculty have forgotten the struggle of learning the material for the first time. Third, and most pragmatically, exams are often generated by textbook publishers, meaning the "phrasing of everything should match what's going to be on the exam."

Kaplan provides a clever litmus test for students to determine if their professor is trustworthy on this front. If a student points out a discrepancy between the text and the lecture, a professor who says, "trust the textbook," is signaling that the exam will likely follow the book. Conversely, a professor who dismisses the text as the work of a "fool" is signaling that their lecture is the sole authority. This distinction is vital for students trying to optimize their study time.

The Efficiency Paradox

Despite advocating for selective reading, Kaplan emphasizes that doing nothing is a recipe for failure. He warns against strategizing to do the "least" amount of work possible. The goal is efficiency, not avoidance. "If you've done the reading in advance then you have a sort of background base of knowledge that you can use to absorb more out of that one hour," he explains. This transforms the lecture from a source of new information into a reinforcement tool, making the course easier and more interesting. He notes that when students do the reading, they can "find the interesting stuff" even in a "mediocre lecture," a benefit that is "almost always ignored."

You're only going to find that if you do the reading and understand the material, and then your whole college career will be filled with interesting, fun, stimulating stuff.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's argument is a necessary correction to the anxiety-inducing culture of over-reading in higher education, offering a pragmatic framework for strategic engagement. While his advice to skim history books and trust textbooks over lectures requires a level of student discernment that not all undergraduates possess, the core message—that transparency is lacking and efficiency is key—is undeniable. Students who adopt his discipline-specific codes will likely find their academic burden lighter and their learning deeper, provided they remain vigilant about the specific demands of their professors' exams.
