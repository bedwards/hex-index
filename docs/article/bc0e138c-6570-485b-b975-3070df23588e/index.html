We Went to America’s Biggest Copper Mine: The Corruption Will Shock You

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Apr 9, 2024 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union exposes a disturbing paradox at the heart of America's green energy transition: the very minerals needed to power a sustainable future are being extracted through a process that threatens to destroy the communities and cultures that have stewarded the land for generations. This isn't just a story about copper; it is a forensic look at how federal legislation was weaponized to bypass environmental safeguards, turning a sacred Apache site into a corporate asset with the stroke of a pen.

The Scale of the Subsidy

The author begins by grounding the reader in the physical reality of the proposed mine, using a 3D model to illustrate the sheer magnitude of the destruction. More Perfect Union writes, "That's the size of the or body that's going to be pulled out from underground... you're telling me that this mountain that we're looking at is the volume of stuff they want to pull out of that?" This visual comparison immediately dismantles the abstraction of "mining" and replaces it with the image of a mountain being erased. The argument is effective because it forces the reader to confront the physical impossibility of the project being anything other than catastrophic for the landscape.

The piece then pivots to the human cost, drawing a parallel between the current situation and the history of boom-and-bust cycles in mining towns. The author notes that while the company promises a golden age, the reality of the past suggests otherwise. "People of Superior were ready to have these jobs you know they they promis that the jobs are going to last for 40 years and ain't going to happen," the author observes, highlighting the recurring pattern of broken promises. This lands with particular force because it is backed by the testimony of locals who have already lived through the collapse of the Magma Copper Mine in 1982, which wiped out half the town's population. Critics might argue that modern automation and different market conditions could alter this outcome, but the author rightly points out that the parent company, BHP, has a history of abandoning operations once profitability dips, leaving behind toxic tailings and unemployment.

"When you go down deep in there you're going to see the ugliness that we're going to be left with once the damage is done they never look back."

The Legislative Heist

The most damning section of the coverage details the legislative maneuvering that allowed Resolution Copper to bypass standard environmental reviews. The author describes how a land swap, which would strip federal protections from Oak Flat, was slipped into a massive defense spending bill in 2014. More Perfect Union writes, "At the last minute the land swap was slipped in to an 1100 p must pass Bill to fund the US military... added by Senator John McCain who had taken campaign contributions from resolution copper and by Senator Jeff Flake who was once a lobbyist for Rio Tinto." This is the piece's strongest move: it connects the dots between campaign finance, lobbying, and the erosion of democratic process, showing that the decision wasn't made by the people living there, but by politicians with financial ties to the mining giants.

The author emphasizes the absurdity of the timeline imposed by the legislation. "A federal environmental impact statement would need to be created and within 60 days of that happening no matter what the statement said Oak flat would be free to be mined." This detail underscores the futility of the supposed safeguards; the outcome was predetermined before the study even began. The framing here is crucial—it reframes the issue from a simple resource dispute to a constitutional crisis where the rule of law was suspended for corporate profit.

The Cultural and Environmental Stakes

Beyond the politics, the commentary delves into the cultural significance of the land, centering the voices of the San Carlos Apache. The author captures the gravity of the situation through the words of Winsler Noi, the former chairman of the tribe. "If there was copper ore under the Vatican they wouldn't want to destroy the Vatican," the author paraphrases, a comparison that instantly makes the stakes clear to a broad audience. This analogy is powerful because it translates a specific Indigenous struggle into a universal concept of sacred space that any reader can understand.

The environmental risks are then laid out with stark clarity. The proposed "block cave" mining method would leave behind over 1.2 billion tons of toxic waste and consume 250 billion gallons of water in a state already suffering from a 23-year drought. More Perfect Union writes, "The amount of water this mine will use a day is enough to sustain a city of 60,000 people... that's the City of Tempe Arizona." This statistic is devastating because it highlights the zero-sum game: the mine's operation is directly incompatible with the survival of the surrounding communities and the region's water security. The author notes that the company's promise of jobs is a "facade," masking the reality that the mine will be largely automated, operated by robots from hundreds of miles away.

"We're waking up America to make them realize that you're living in a corporate world and you're destroying everything and do you want to be a part of that."

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union's strongest argument is its unflinching exposure of the legislative corruption that enabled this project, proving that the "green energy" transition is currently being built on a foundation of legal loopholes and broken promises. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that public outrage alone can overturn a deal that has already been codified into federal law, a challenge that will likely require a Supreme Court victory or a complete reversal of political will. The reader should watch for the outcome of the Apache-led lawsuit, as it represents the final, desperate line of defense for a community facing total erasure.
