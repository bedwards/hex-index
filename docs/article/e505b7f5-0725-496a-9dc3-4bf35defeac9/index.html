The rise of masturbation, the decline of sex

Richard Hanania · ·Jan 5, 2026 ·13 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Richard Hanania presents a jarring paradox that defies the standard liberal-conservative divide: as society has become more permissive regarding solo sexual acts, it has simultaneously erected higher barriers to real-world intimacy. The piece is notable not for its moralizing, but for its structural argument that "safetyism"—the obsession with eliminating all risk of emotional or physical harm—is driving a retreat from the messy reality of human connection into the sterile safety of digital consumption.

The Great Divergence

Hanania opens by identifying two parallel trends that usually seem contradictory. On one hand, there is an explosion of acceptance for masturbation and the tools of self-pleasure, with legal restrictions on sex toys becoming "practically unthinkable." On the other, there is a surge in "sex-negativity" regarding real-life interactions, particularly those involving age gaps or power disparities. He notes that while the mid-1980s saw stable rates of pornography consumption, the cultural landscape has shifted to make actual sex increasingly problematic.

The author argues that this isn't a return to traditionalism, but a new form of risk aversion. "Masturbation is the safest sex of all," Hanania writes. "Putting aside those who accidentally asphyxiate themselves, pleasuring yourself ensures that no one gets emotionally or physically harmed." This framing is provocative because it reframes the decline in sexual activity not as a moral failure, but as a rational response to a culture that penalizes any potential for emotional friction. Critics might note that this glosses over the very real history of coercion in unequal relationships, but Hanania's point is that the pendulum has swung so far toward protecting against potential harm that it now stifles actual consent between adults.

"The point of terms like grooming and trafficking is to obscure what is going on, and make it impossible to even conceive of a woman voluntarily engaging in prostitution in the same way she might open an OnlyFans account."

This observation cuts to the heart of the legal and cultural confusion Hanania describes. He suggests that the language of "trafficking" and "grooming" has been expanded to the point of absurdity, effectively criminalizing the very agency that the digital economy celebrates. He points out that while a woman can legally sell content on OnlyFans, the same transaction in a physical space is often reclassified as a crime against a "victim," regardless of her actual intent.

The Institutionalization of Fear

The commentary deepens as Hanania connects these sexual norms to broader institutional dynamics. He draws a sharp analogy to the economic realm, asking us to imagine a world where any transaction between parties of unequal power is deemed suspicious. "I would not be able to buy things from Amazon, or any major corporation," he posits. "Obviously, economic life would ground to a halt." Yet, he argues, this is exactly what is happening in the dating sphere, where the power dynamics of age, wealth, or career status are treated as inherent signs of abuse rather than natural variations in human attraction.

Hanania highlights the shift in how we view historical figures like Hugh Hefner or the intern scandals of the past. "A generation ago, much of the mainstream press brushed off the president's affair with an intern; now any relationship between a boss and a subordinate by necessity involves an abuse of power." This shift reflects a culture that prioritizes the appearance of safety over the reality of adult agency. The reference to the Mann Act and FOSTA-SESTA in the broader context of this debate underscores how legal frameworks, originally designed to combat coercion, are now being weaponized to police consensual adult behavior under the banner of protection.

"Young men complain about not knowing how to approach women in the post-MeToo era, and although this attitude can be exaggerated and self-defeating, it is true that norms have changed."

Here, Hanania acknowledges the validity of the anxiety felt by many young men without dismissing the legitimate concerns of the MeToo movement. He argues that the solution to this anxiety has been a retreat into the digital, where the risks of rejection, awkwardness, or misinterpretation are eliminated. He describes a young man who prefers pornography because a "zip file can't lie to you," a sentiment that reveals a deep exhaustion with the ambiguity of human interaction.

The Paradox of Safety

The most striking part of Hanania's argument is his claim that the very things that make sex "unsafe"—the unpredictability, the power imbalances, the potential for emotional pain—are also the things that make it transformative. He contrasts the "gooning" lifestyle, where men retreat into isolated digital loops, with the experience of visiting a sex worker. "Actually being exposed to a woman in the flesh is a transformative experience," he writes. "Moreover, visiting the brothel requires you to get out of your room."

He argues that even a transactional encounter forces a man to engage with social norms, hygiene, and eye contact, building habits that transfer to other areas of life. In contrast, the digital alternative offers no such friction. "What does masturbation get you?" he asks rhetorically. "Why is this the thing we embraced, while problematizing sex?" The answer, he suggests, lies in a culture that has decided that the risk of emotional harm is worse than the stagnation of social development.

"The prostitute is a hero. For natural reasons, men lack confidence and feel inferior if they don't have any sexual experience... So, for a market price, the prostitute elevates men while protecting her fellow women."

This is perhaps the most controversial claim in the piece, yet it serves as the logical conclusion of his safetyism argument. Hanania suggests that by criminalizing or stigmatizing sex work, society is removing a crucial outlet for men to learn how to interact with women, thereby driving them further into isolation and digital fantasy. He notes that while the enablers of masturbation face few restrictions, the prostitute is subject to political prosecution, creating a system that is "unjust, and indicative of what has gone wrong in a culture that is increasingly retreating into the digital."

Bottom Line

Hanania's strongest contribution is his reframing of the sexual decline not as a moral panic, but as a structural failure of "safetyism" that prioritizes the elimination of risk over the necessity of human connection. His argument is vulnerable to the critique that it romanticizes transactional sex and underestimates the genuine power imbalances that can exist in such arrangements. However, the piece forces a necessary conversation about whether our current legal and cultural frameworks are inadvertently creating a generation that is safer, but also profoundly lonelier and less capable of navigating the complexities of real life.

The rise of masturbation, the decline of sex

by Richard Hanania · · Read full article

The last few decades have seen two parallel developments that are usually considered naturally at odds with one another.

An increase in the acceptance of masturbation. This means more options available, less social stigma on the practice, and a decline in any cultural or legal restrictions on engaging in self-pleasure.

An increase in real life sex-negativity. When it comes to cisgendered heterosexuals at least, fewer conditions under which people are supposed to have sex, and increasing taboos on more kinds of relationships: namely age gaps and those involving power disparities, particularly bosses and their subordinates. Even behavior that might lead to sex like aggressive flirting is seen as more problematic.

In Harper’s Magazine, Daniel Kolitz recently wrote about communities of men who meet online and masturbate together. The details are shocking and disgusting, but there is no hint that there are any obstacles in the way of the subjects fulfilling any fantasy they have, as long as it comes in virtual form. There was a time not that long ago when states tried to ban sex toys. Ted Cruz in 2007 defended one such law as solicitor general of Texas, but by 2016 this had turned him into a laughing stock. Legal restrictions on adult self-pleasure are practically unthinkable.

Perhaps 2% of American women between the ages of 18 and 45 are or have been OnlyFans creators.1 I looked for data expecting to see evidence for an increase in pornography consumption, but at least one survey shows that the number of American men and women who report having “seen an X-rated movie in the last year” was stable between the mid-1980s and 2012. Nonetheless, there is relatively little moral panic about porn, in the US at least, even as there are increasing worries about what social media does to minors. The attitude is generally live and let live.

Meanwhile, sex has been increasingly made problematic. Age gaps between consenting adults are stigmatized. The desire to protect minors reaches the height of absurdity when we lock up adult women for having sexual relations with teenage boys. We did this in the 1990s too, but there was a cultural understanding that the only reason this happened was because the law needed to treat the sexes equally as a matter of principle. Now it is not that unusual to see these lucky young lads referred to as victims of “pedophilia.”
