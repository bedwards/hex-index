Chartbook 439 Electrostates v. petrostates. Clarifying a tricky distinction.

Adam Tooze · Chartbook ·Apr 2, 2026 ·11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Adam Tooze dismantles a trendy geopolitical binary that has captivated headlines: the idea that the world is splitting into "electrostates" and "petrostates." While the narrative suggests China is racing toward an electric future while the United States is stagnating in fossil fuel dependency, Tooze argues this is a dangerous oversimplification that ignores economic reality. He posits that the US is not a petrostate by definition, but rather a sophisticated economy making a political choice to potentially freeze its energy model in place—a move that would be a unique historical anomaly rather than a natural economic outcome.

Redefining the Electrostate

Tooze begins by clarifying the technical definition that drives the entire debate. "We call a country an electrostate, if it draws a large and increasing share of its total final energy consumption in the form of electricity," he writes. This distinction matters because the global clean energy transition relies on the mantra to "electrify everything." If a society burns oil or gas directly in cars and factories, it is locked into a carbon-intensive path. If it runs on electricity, the door to decarbonization is open.

The author correctly identifies China as the archetype of this new model, noting how the nation has expanded its electricity share while renewables have become the fastest-growing source of generation. However, Tooze pushes back against the idea that this is an inherent advantage of China's political system. He reminds readers that the Soviet Union was once an "electrical engineering powerhouse," and that the State Grid Corporation of China's dominance is built on decades of infrastructure investment, not just ideology. The real shift, he argues, is technological: "The advent of LNG and the arrival of renewables and batteries mean that electrification is now an option for everyone, everywhere."

This reframing is crucial. It suggests that being an electrostate is not about geography or resource endowment, but about "economic and governmental rationality." The ability to build a modern grid is a measure of state capacity. Critics might note that Tooze underestimates the sheer inertia of China's coal-heavy industrial base, but his core point holds: the technology is universally available. The question is whether a government has the will to deploy it.

The American Paradox

The most provocative section of the commentary tackles the labeling of the United States. Tooze argues that calling the US a "petrostate" is a "discursive construction" rather than an economic fact. In the classic sense, a petrostate is an economy dependent on oil rents for survival, like Angola or Russia. The US, by contrast, has a diversified economy where the oil and gas sector accounts for only 7-8 percent of GDP.

"The designation of petrostate applies to an economy like that of Angola," Tooze writes, contrasting it with the American reality where Texas, the leading oil producer, is also the leading generator of renewable electricity. This paradox exists because, as he notes, "Having a dynamic sector, like oil and gas, in a regional economy in fact tends to raise costs for other sectors... making them more, not less interested in other ways of saving money."

The US is not not an electrostate. It is an electrostate, just one that is slouching rather than racing towards the new era of ultra-low cost electric power.

This observation cuts through the noise of political rhetoric. The US grid is indeed broken, with hyperscaling AI companies desperate to build out capacity but blocked by interconnection delays. Yet, the barrier is not a lack of resources. The US produces over 13 million barrels of oil a day and consumes nearly 20 million. This massive demand creates a unique vulnerability. While China and the EU have a compelling economic logic to replace imported oil with domestic electricity, the US has the luxury of self-sufficiency in hydrocarbons.

The Choice to Freeze

Tooze identifies the true danger: the US is large and rich enough to ignore global trends. "In an inversion of familiar logic, the US is large enough, rich enough and sophisticated enough to simply freeze in place the late 20th century model for the foreseeable future," he writes. This is not a natural evolution of a resource-rich nation; it is a deliberate political decision to prioritize the internal combustion engine and gas-based electrification over the global shift to renewables.

He warns that this path would create a "closed petrostate," a novel form of state that uses its economic size to resist the division of labor that drives global efficiency. The administration's opposition to a mixed model of fossil and electrotech development is described as a "perverse, political choice." Unlike Saudi Arabia, which must diversify because its oil has no domestic market, the US can theoretically lock in a hydrocarbon model indefinitely.

However, the author acknowledges the practical hurdles. Even if the political will existed to revert to a gas-based grid, the US lacks the capacity to produce enough gas turbines or connect new power plants efficiently. The infrastructure bottleneck is real, regardless of who sits in the White House. A counterargument worth considering is that the market forces Tooze describes—cheap solar and wind—are already winning in Texas and California, suggesting that the "frozen" model may be harder to maintain than he predicts.

Bottom Line

Tooze's strongest contribution is exposing the "petrostate" label as a political weapon rather than an economic diagnosis, revealing that the US's fossil fuel reliance is a choice, not a destiny. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in underestimating how quickly grid modernization could be accelerated if the political will aligned, potentially rendering the "frozen" scenario obsolete before it begins. The reader should watch for whether the US grid's physical bottlenecks will force a transition regardless of political ideology, or if the country truly becomes the world's first "closed petrostate."

If the United States chooses to use its endowment of fossil fuels and the size of its economy to freeze in place the early 20th-century status quo and thus to fall more and more out of step with global electrotech development, that will not be a natural fact, but a matter of political choice.

Some time last year a distinction began to be drawn between electrostates and petrostates. China was the key instance of an electrostate. The US under Donald Trump has made a spectacle out of its commitment to fossil fuels. The result have been a spate of headlines and articles pitting electrostates v. petrostates. I made my own contributed to the genre with the title of my LRB talk in the fall.

Since then I’ve become increasingly worried about the proliferating uses of the contrast. Because, as compelling as it may be for leader writers, it is a distinction that needs to be handled with care.

This is opposed to a society that relies heavily on burning gas, oil or fuel directly, whether in industrial or domestic uses or in cars, trucks, ships and airplanes. How you get your energy delivered matters, because in the clean energy transition, the key mantra is “electrify everything”. Electricity is one form of power that we know how to produce by clean means. In most places that is still far from true for much of electricity generation, but it is the prospect of the energy transition that gives significance to the question of electrification and supercharges the idea of an “electrostate”.

China has been singled out as the archetypal electrostate. It has been expanding the share of its final energy use which is delivered in the form of electricity. Added to which, in the last five years, renewables have taken over as the most rapidly expanding form of electricity generation. Furthermore, Chinese manufacturers dominate the EV business, bringing the prospect into view that the internal combustion engine will be rapidly displaced. Ultra high-speed (electrified) railways compete with inter-regional flying inside China. And China, of course, is very much a mixed economy with the state playing a key role. So China ticks all the boxes of an ElectroState.
