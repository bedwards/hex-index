Antarctica : What happens if the 'Doomsday' Glacier collapses?

Dave Borlace · Just Have a Think ·Mar 15, 2020 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Borlace doesn't just describe a melting glacier; he reveals a hidden engine of climate catastrophe operating deep beneath the Antarctic ice, where warm ocean water is dissolving the continent's foundation from the bottom up. While the surface of Antarctica remains a frozen wasteland, the real story is happening 600 meters down, where a temperature probe confirmed waters are two degrees above freezing—a seemingly small shift with planetary consequences. This is not a distant theoretical risk; it is a measurable acceleration of the 'Doomsday Glacier' that could reshape global coastlines within a single human lifetime.

The Hidden Heat Beneath the Ice

Borlace begins by dismantling the assumption that Antarctica is a static, frozen monolith. He notes that while the continent is "about 30% bigger than Europe overall" and holds "more than 70% of all the fresh water that's available on the planet," the climate down there is "changing fast, much faster than the rest of the planet." The author contrasts the stable, ancient ice of East Antarctica with the precarious situation in the west, where Thwaites Glacier sits on islands that "dip well below sea level." This distinction is crucial. It explains why the western sector is the weak link, vulnerable to oceanic intrusion rather than just atmospheric warming.

The core of Borlace's argument rests on a groundbreaking expedition. He describes how researchers deployed a probe called "Ice Fin" to the glacier's base, where it met the ocean. The finding was stark: "unlike the waters at the surface which are about minus 2 degrees Celsius... the temperature of the ocean water now washing against the base of the glacier is 2 degrees Celsius above freezing." This detail is the piece's smoking gun. A mere four-degree differential between surface and bottom water is enough to trigger catastrophic melting because the scale of the glacier is so vast. Borlace writes, "the difference in temperature is only 2 or 3 degrees Celsius but like so many other aspects of climate change because the scale is so vast the effect can be very dramatic." This framing effectively translates abstract thermodynamics into a tangible threat.

Critics might note that the article simplifies the complex mechanics of the thermohaline circulation, often called the "great ocean conveyor belt," to explain how warm Pacific waters reach the Antarctic shelf. However, the simplification serves the narrative well, focusing the reader on the result—warmer water at the grounding line—rather than getting bogged down in oceanographic modeling.

The Feedback Loop of Collapse

The most alarming section of the coverage details the self-reinforcing nature of the melt. Borlace explains that as the glacier erodes at its base, larger sections of the ice shelf break off. This removal of the "giant plug" at the front of the West Antarctic Ice Sheet allows the remaining ice to flow faster into the ocean. He quotes Professor David Holland, who warns that "warmer water can increase melt rates by as much as a hundred fold." This exponential increase is the definition of a feedback loop. As the ice flows faster, it thins, making it even more susceptible to the warm water, which in turn accelerates the flow further.

Borlace captures the urgency of this mechanism by stating, "the more the glacier melts... the faster the ice within the glacier is likely to flow out and lo and behold we've got ourselves yet another of those pesky feedback loops." The use of "pesky" here is a masterful touch of understatement, highlighting how a minor-sounding phenomenon can trigger a chain reaction that defies linear prediction. The author reinforces this by noting that satellite data shows the rate of ice loss has already "doubled" over the last couple of decades. This evidence suggests the process is not just theoretical but already in motion.

The more the glacier melts, the faster the ice within the glacier is likely to flow out, creating a feedback loop that accelerates the very process causing it.

From Meters to Millions

The article pivots from the science of the ice to the reality of human impact. Borlace translates the potential collapse of Thwaites into concrete numbers: enough fresh water to raise global sea levels by 50 centimeters on its own, or up to a meter if the entire West Antarctic Ice Sheet follows suit. He cites Professor David Vaughan, who suggests that a 50-centimeter rise would mean a "thousand-year storm arriving every 100 years or so," while a one-meter rise would make such a storm a "once a decade" event. This reframing of extreme weather as a new normal is the piece's most potent warning.

The human cost is quantified with chilling precision. Borlace references a European Commission paper suggesting that "300 million homes will be affected by coastal flooding in the next 30 years and 630 million by the year 2100." These figures move the story from a scientific curiosity to a global humanitarian crisis. The author concludes by linking this physical reality to the political imperative of the COP26 conference, arguing that avoiding this future requires "an absolutely unanimous commitment" to radical emission reductions and massive reforestation. He frames the necessary response as "the biggest coordinated global effort since the Second World War," a comparison that underscores the scale of the challenge.

A counterargument worth considering is that the timeline of "gone completely within a century" might be overly pessimistic or optimistic depending on future carbon trajectories. Some glaciologists argue that the collapse could take centuries, not decades. However, Borlace's reliance on the "current business as usual trajectory" provides a necessary baseline for worst-case scenario planning.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage is strongest in its ability to visualize the invisible: the warm water eating away at the glacier's foundation from below. By anchoring the narrative in the specific data from the "Ice Fin" probe, he transforms a distant, abstract threat into an immediate, measurable reality. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its brevity; the complex interplay of ocean currents and wind patterns is necessarily simplified, which could leave readers with an incomplete picture of the mitigation levers available. However, the verdict is clear: the feedback loops are active, the data is accelerating, and the window for preventing a meter of sea-level rise is closing faster than the ice itself.
