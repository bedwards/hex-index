Does manufacturing matter?

Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk ·Dec 29, 2025 ·11 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Jordan Schneider cuts through the noise of America's renewed manufacturing fervor with a brutally pragmatic question: does making things actually make us richer, or just safer? While the administration has fully embraced muscular intervention in private markets—shifting from the free-market orthodoxy of a decade ago to a strategy of equity stakes and golden shares—Schneider argues we are rushing to spend without a clear map of what we are buying. This is not a Luddite rant against industry, but a demand for precision in a policy landscape currently defined by broad strokes and historical reflexes.

The Jobs and Productivity Myth

Schneider immediately dismantles the most common political selling point for industrial policy: job creation. He writes, "I find completely unconvincing the theory, commonly hawked by politicians of both parties, that manufacturing is a good source of 'jobs.'" His logic is stark: manufacturing represents only about 10% of U.S. employment, so even a massive 20% boost in that sector would be negligible for the economy as a whole. Furthermore, he notes that cost-effective domestic manufacturing now requires aggressive automation, meaning more factories do not necessarily mean more human workers.

The author extends this skepticism to the idea that a large manufacturing base is required to drive productivity growth. "If that were true, Germany would be richer and America poorer," Schneider observes, pointing out that the U.S. has deindustrialized by GDP share while outperforming its peers. He suggests that for a mature economy like the United States, the link between physical production and economic dynamism is far weaker than the historical narrative suggests. Critics might note that this view underestimates the multiplier effects of high-wage industrial jobs on local communities, but the macroeconomic data on aggregate employment does support his caution.

"The smartphone company furthest from the manufacturing has made the most money, now for nearly two decades."

Schneider uses the smartphone industry as a case study to challenge the assumption that proximity to the factory floor equals value capture. He highlights that while China dominates the assembly of billions of phones, the vast majority of profits flow to Apple, a firm that outsources almost all physical production. "Around a quarter of the bill of materials of an iPhone accrues to China-based suppliers, but generally for the lower margin components," he writes. This distinction is crucial; it suggests that in the modern economy, design, software, and brand equity often trump the act of assembly itself. The implication is that a blanket policy to "bring back manufacturing" might be chasing the wrong part of the value chain.

The Geopolitical Calculus

Where Schneider concedes the most ground is on national security. He acknowledges that the argument for deindustrialization collapses when viewed through the lens of defense, citing Sander Tordoir's sharp retort: "Europe will need drones and tanks, not just consultants." The ability to produce physical goods has undeniable military utility, a lesson reinforced by the "Arsenal of Democracy" era when American industry pivoted to produce tanks and planes at a scale that won the war. However, Schneider pushes back against the idea that this requires a return to broad-based industrialization.

He argues that the policy question should not be "is manufacturing useful?" but rather "how much should we spend to boost our capabilities given resource constraints?" The administration has signaled a preference for high-tech goods over consumer basics, with one official noting, "I'm not looking to make t-shirts, to be honest... We are looking to do chips and computers and lots of other things, and tanks and ships." Schneider agrees with this prioritization but warns that the middle ground—cars, complex machinery, and chemicals—is where the real trade-offs lie. He points out that even if the U.S. eliminated all imports of textiles and toys, it would still be importing a massive volume of complex manufactured goods that pose strategic risks.

"If you gave me money and tasked me with mitigating risks to U.S. security, I would buy a lot of anti-ship missiles and place them near the Taiwan Straits. Then some more missile and air defense systems."

This is perhaps the most provocative part of the commentary. Schneider forces the reader to consider the opportunity cost of every dollar spent on building a factory versus buying a weapon or securing a supply line. He suggests that in a world of scarce labor and capital, subsidizing a broad manufacturing base might be an inefficient use of funds compared to direct defense procurement. "We're constrained by labor, electricity, capital, etc.," he writes, reminding us that building a factory is not free and competes with other security needs. A counterargument worth considering is that a robust industrial base provides the option to scale production in a crisis, a flexibility that buying off-the-shelf missiles does not. Yet, Schneider's point stands: we need to know exactly what we are buying before we write the check.

The Ecosystem Dilemma

The piece concludes by examining the unique advantage of the current U.S. labor market. Schneider highlights a conversation with Christian Brose of Anduril, who noted that the decline of the auto industry has left a pool of skilled workers in places like Ohio who understand the discipline of high-volume manufacturing. "These are people who have spent decades perfecting the art of taking complex designs and turning into physical products," Brose said. Schneider finds a grim irony here: the shrinking of the civilian auto sector has inadvertently created a ready-made workforce for the defense industrial base.

However, he warns that this is a fragile solution. If the auto industry were to recover and grow, that talent would be reabsorbed, potentially leaving the defense sector scrambling again. The core challenge remains: we need better analytics on monopolization risks, the ability to shift capacity in a crisis, and the true strength of ecosystem effects. "For everything in between—cars, computers, chemicals, industrial equipment, etc—we need better analytics," Schneider insists. He calls for a move away from ideological reflexes toward a nuanced understanding of where manufacturing truly matters.

"We need to prioritize when allocating people, dollars, and other scarce resources. So whether and when does manufacturing matter?"

Bottom Line

Schneider's strongest contribution is his refusal to accept the romanticized view of manufacturing as a panacea for either economic prosperity or national security. His argument is vulnerable only if one assumes that the value of an industrial ecosystem lies entirely in its ability to pivot during a total war, a scenario that may not justify peacetime subsidies for every sector. The reader should watch for whether the administration can move beyond the broad rhetoric of "reindustrialization" to the granular, difficult decisions about which specific supply chains are worth the cost of domestic production.

Does manufacturing matter?

by Jordan Schneider · ChinaTalk · Read full article

While 2025 was, in USTR Jameison Greer’s phrasing, “the year of the tariff”, industrial policy served as a strong leitmotif. From the US-China rare earths saga to equity stakes, golden shares and prepurchase agreements, Trump 2.0 has wholly embraced the sort of muscular intervention into private markets that would have made the GOP of just a decade ago cry bloody murder.1 Looking past this administration, JD Vance and Rubio, the two 2028 nominee frontrunners, both have Senate track records filled with bill proposals around industrial banks and domestic manufacturing promotion.

But for all the motion around creative applications of industrial policy in America, it’s been surprising to me how little thought has been applied to the big questions around these swings. Key questions I see unanswered include:

What should the long term goals of national industrial policy be?

What does it mean to be an economically secure nation? Where should marginal dollars be spent to promote economic security?

Just how important is manufacturing relative to services?

What are the tradeoffs involved in furthering these aims?
