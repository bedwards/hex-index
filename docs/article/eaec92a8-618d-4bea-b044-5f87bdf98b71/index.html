Heather Cox Richardson delivers a harrowing yet meticulously sourced chronicle of an administration that attempted to operate outside the constitutional framework, only to be halted by the very legal and civic structures it sought to dismantle. This is not a standard political recap; it is a forensic accounting of how the executive branch weaponized emergency powers and private wealth to bypass Congress, and how the rule of law eventually reasserted itself through the courts and the ballot box.

The Architecture of Exception

Richardson begins by framing the administration's first year not as a series of policy disagreements, but as a fundamental assault on the separation of powers. She writes, "The hallmark of the first year of President Donald J. Trump’s second term has been the attempt of the president and his cronies to dismantle the constitutional system set up by the framers of that document." This is a bold claim, but she backs it with the specific actions of billionaire Elon Musk, who was appointed to lead a new "Department of Government Efficiency." Richardson notes that Musk vowed to cut at least $2 trillion from the federal budget, a goal that led to operatives gaining access to government systems and eliminating jobs.

The human cost of this efficiency drive is stark. Richardson points out that the dismantling of the U.S. Agency for International Development (USAID) has "caused the deaths of hundreds of thousands of people." This is a critical detail often lost in discussions of budget cuts; the author forces the reader to confront the mortality behind the spreadsheet. The Cato Institute's finding that the department cut almost 10% of federal employees without actually reducing spending underscores the chaos rather than the efficiency. Richardson argues that this was less about fiscal responsibility and more about a philosophical shift: "Musk and DOGE also established the idea that the unelected officials in the Trump administration could do whatever they wished, without regard to the laws or the Constitution."

This reliance on unchecked executive power draws a direct line to the political theories of Carl Schmitt, a Nazi-era theorist whose ideas have resurfaced in right-wing circles. Richardson explains that Schmitt argued power belongs to the leader who can exploit emergencies to create exceptions to the constitutional order. She quotes the administration's own rhetoric to illustrate this: Trump asserted on August 26, "the right to do anything I want to do... If I think our country’s in danger... I can do it." This framing is chilling because it reveals a worldview where the law is an obstacle to be removed, not a boundary to be respected. As now-Vice President J.D. Vance described Schmitt's ideas in 2024, "There’s no law, there’s just power."

There’s no law, there’s just power.

Critics might argue that the administration was simply acting on a mandate to cut waste, but Richardson effectively counters this by highlighting the constitutional reality that the power of the purse belongs to Congress. The administration's declaration of nine "emergencies" to bypass legislative oversight was a direct violation of the 1974 Impoundment Control Act, a law passed specifically to prevent presidents from withholding funds Congress had appropriated. By ignoring this, the administration wasn't just breaking a statute; it was rejecting a decades-long bipartisan effort to restore congressional authority.

The Collision of Law and Power

The narrative shifts from theoretical overreach to concrete legal battles, where the administration's actions met the resistance of the judiciary and the public. Richardson details how the administration launched a tariff war in April, a power the Constitution reserves exclusively for Congress. When lawmakers tried to challenge this, House Republicans, led by Speaker Mike Johnson, manipulated the legislative calendar to prevent a vote, effectively freezing the opposition. The resulting "One Big Beautiful Bill" extended tax cuts for the wealthy while slashing Medicaid and food assistance, passed through budget reconciliation to avoid a filibuster.

However, the administration's attempt to operate above the law began to fracture under the weight of its own contradictions. Richardson highlights the case of Kilmar Ábrego García, a Maryland man with legal status who was rendered to a notorious terrorist prison in El Salvador despite a federal judge's order to the contrary. "The administration’s consistent refusal to bring Ábrego García back... helped to focus anger at the administration," she writes. This specific instance of judicial defiance became a rallying point, illustrating that the administration was willing to ignore court orders even when the Supreme Court was involved.

The public response was swift and massive. Richardson describes how protests evolved from "Tesla Takedowns" targeting Musk to "Hands Off" rallies involving millions. The administration's deployment of National Guard troops to cities like Los Angeles, Portland, and Chicago, often against the wishes of local governors, further eroded support. Richardson notes that Trump's 79th birthday coincided with the 250th anniversary of the U.S. Army, a moment he tried to co-opt with a military parade, only to be met with "No Kings" protests. The administration's attempt to frame these events as necessary security measures failed to mask the reality: "Masked agents from Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) and the U.S. Border Patrol were arresting foreign students... and all the undocumented immigrants they could find."

The human toll of these policies extended beyond domestic unrest. Richardson adds a crucial, somber update regarding the administration's military actions: "In September, the administration began the bombing of small boats apparently coming from Venezuela... killing more than 100 individuals to date on the president’s authority alone." This action, taken in violation of the 1973 War Powers Act, underscores the administration's willingness to engage in lethal force without congressional input. The lack of due process and the disregard for international law here are not minor procedural errors; they represent a fundamental break from American tradition.

The Reckoning

As the year closed, the momentum shifted decisively. Richardson observes that the administration's failure to meet legal deadlines, such as the release of the Epstein files mandated by Congress, exposed its fragility. The overwhelming bipartisan vote to force the release of these files, followed by the administration's failure to comply, signaled a break in the Republican coalition. "Many MAGA Republicans had backed Trump in 2024 because of what they thought was a promise to release those files, and yet House speaker Johnson refused to allow the House to vote," she notes. The subsequent revelation that Trump's Mar-a-Lago spa was involved in recruiting young women for Epstein further damaged the administration's credibility.

The legal system finally caught up. Richardson writes, "On December 22 a federal judge ordered the Trump administration to file a plan to return the men it sent to CECOT... On December 23 the Supreme Court issued a preliminary rejection of Trump’s justification for deploying National Guard troops in Illinois." These were not just legal victories; they were affirmations that the Constitution still held power. The electoral results in November, with Democrats winning governorships in Virginia and New Jersey by double-digit margins and flipping state senate seats, confirmed that the American public had rejected the administration's authoritarian drift.

Richardson concludes with a powerful historical parallel. She connects the events of 2025 to the 250th anniversary of the Declaration of Independence, noting that the founders rejected the idea of kings to launch an experiment in government based on the rule of law. "It appears that as we approach the 250th anniversary... the American people are reclaiming that history," she writes. This framing elevates the piece from a political critique to a meditation on the resilience of democratic institutions. The administration's attempt to create a "New World Order" was ultimately thwarted by the very system it tried to destroy.

It appears that as we approach the 250th anniversary of the Declaration of Independence... the American people are reclaiming that history.

Bottom Line

Richardson's most compelling argument is that the administration's greatest vulnerability was its own arrogance; by acting as if the law did not exist, it provoked a unified response from the courts, Congress, and the electorate that it could not anticipate. The piece's greatest strength lies in its refusal to treat the erosion of norms as abstract politics, instead grounding every claim in specific human costs and legal precedents. Readers should watch for how the courts continue to enforce these boundaries in 2026, as the administration's initial momentum has clearly stalled against the bedrock of the rule of law.

The hallmark of the first year of President Donald J. Trump’s second term has been the attempt of the president and his cronies to dismantle the constitutional system set up by the framers of that document when they established the United States of America. It’s not simply that they have broken the laws. They have acted as if the laws, and the Constitution that underpins them, don’t exist.

As soon as the 2024 election results were clear, billionaire Elon Musk, who had supported Trump’s campaign both through his purchase of Twitter—now X—and with $290 million in cash, posted on social media: “Novus Ordo Seclorum,” Latin for “New World Order.” Although he won with less than 50% of the vote, Trump announced that he had an “unprecedented and powerful mandate.” Musk would head a new “Department of Government Efficiency” that Musk vowed would cut at least $2 trillion from the federal budget.

Musk and his operatives muscled their way into government offices and gained access to computer systems. With strokes of a keyboard they eliminated jobs and programs, including, as Musk put it, feeding “into the wood chipper” most of the U.S. Agency for International Development, the government agency aimed at combating disease and malnutrition around the globe. That dismantling has caused the deaths of hundreds of thousands of people.

The Cato Institute, a libertarian think tank, recently concluded that while the Department of Government Efficiency did not actually reduce spending, it did cut almost 10% of federal employees, a key goal of Office of Management and Budget director Russell Vought, a key author of Project 2025.

And, crucially, it put operatives in virtually all government departments and agencies, where they gained access to privileged information about Americans, including citizens, legal residents, and undocumented immigrants.

Musk and DOGE also established the idea that the unelected officials in the Trump administration could do whatever they wished, without regard to the laws or the Constitution. The Constitution, judicial precedent, and the 1974 Impoundment Control Act all make it very clear that the power of the purse belongs to Congress. As the elected representatives of the American people, only members of the House of Representatives and the Senate can determine how the nation’s money is spent. Then the president must “take Care that the laws be faithfully executed.”
