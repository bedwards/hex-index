China's Catl Group is Winning the EV Battery Industry

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Jun 20, 2021 · 17 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Asianometry reframes the global electric vehicle race not as a contest of car design, but as a battle for chemical dominance, revealing how a single Chinese firm, Contemporary Amperex Technology Co. Ltd (CATL), leveraged state policy and engineering grit to corner the market. The author's most striking claim is that CATL's rise was not inevitable but the result of a calculated spin-off from a Japanese-owned consumer electronics giant, timed perfectly to exploit a government mandate that excluded foreign competitors. This narrative matters now because as the world's largest battery manufacturer, CATL's decisions on cathode chemistry and supply chains will dictate the pace and cost of the global energy transition for decades.

The Architect of a Spin-Off

The piece begins by dismantling the myth of the overnight success story, tracing CATL's lineage to Robin Zheng, a physicist who rose from a state-owned enterprise in a poverty-stricken coastal city to become a technical director in Hong Kong. Asianometry writes, "Born in the impoverished city of Ningde in the Chinese coastal province of Fujian, he had been assigned to work at a state-owned enterprise there after university but he was not interested in making 30 USD a month and left." This biographical detail is not mere color; it establishes the founder's drive and the specific regional context that would later become a hub of industrial policy.

The author meticulously details Zheng's tenure at Amperex Technology Limited (ATL), a company that mastered lithium-ion polymer batteries for consumer electronics like iPods and iPhones. The core argument here is that ATL's success in solving the "swelling" issue in batteries provided the technical foundation for CATL, but the political landscape forced a schism. As Asianometry puts it, "ATL, 100 percent owned by Japan-based TDK, could not participate [in the EV sector] thus in 2011 the company began a spin-off to cater to the EV battery industry." This pivot was a high-stakes gamble: Zheng left a stable, profitable consumer electronics business to build a company in a market with only a handful of electric vehicles on the road.

Critics might note that attributing this success solely to founder vision overlooks the sheer weight of state capital and infrastructure support that made the gamble viable. However, the author effectively argues that without the technical competence of the spin-off team, the subsidies would have been wasted. The narrative highlights a crucial distinction: while the Chinese government closed the door to foreign-owned battery makers, it left a window open for a domestic entity with the right pedigree.

The Japanese invented the lithium battery, the Koreans made it bigger, and the Chinese made it the number one in the world. If we are not the number one in the world, we have no value for existence.

This quote from Zheng, cited by Asianometry, captures the existential urgency driving the company's strategy. It is a stark reminder that in the eyes of Chinese industrial champions, global dominance is not a goal but a prerequisite for survival. The author uses this to explain why CATL's growth trajectory is so aggressive, mirroring the "Huawei model" of intense research and development and relentless work culture.

The Chemistry of Dominance

The commentary then shifts to the technical differentiators, explaining that while battery chemistry advances slower than semiconductors, CATL has engineered around physical limitations. Asianometry notes, "Differences in capacity and prices between battery types are primarily due to how the cathode is made such materials like cobalt and nickel are what make it the priciest and most controversial part of the battery." The author contrasts two main battery types: the safer, cheaper, but lower-range lithium iron phosphate (LFP) batteries favored by BYD, and the higher-energy-density ternary lithium batteries that CATL champions.

The author argues that CATL's genius lies in its ability to innovate within these chemical constraints. "CATL has created some interesting innovations to directly integrate lithium-ion phosphate batteries into their packs so to increase an energy density and increase an EV battery's range," Asianometry writes. This "cell-to-pack" approach is presented as the key factor that convinced Tesla to sign a deal, despite Tesla's previous reliance on Panasonic. This evidence holds up well; it demonstrates that CATL is not just a low-cost manufacturer but a technological leader capable of solving the "range anxiety" problem that plagues the industry.

However, the piece briefly touches on the fragility of this success. The author points out that the Chinese government's decision to phase out subsidies and open the market to foreign suppliers in 2020 presented a "first test of the country's EV industry." Asianometry observes, "While the phase-out has been delayed, it has not been cancelled... this would present the first test of the country's EV industry whether or not they are globally competitive without the government's financial tailwinds." This is a critical vulnerability in the argument. While CATL has dominated the protected domestic market, its ability to compete on a level playing field against established Korean and Japanese rivals remains an open question.

The Lock-In Strategy

Perhaps the most compelling part of the analysis is the discussion of switching costs. Asianometry explains that unlike consumer electronics, where a new battery can be swapped easily, EV batteries are deeply integrated into the vehicle's architecture. "Once an EV manufacturer chooses to work with CATL, getting them to switch away can be exceptionally difficult," the author writes. "Either they need to redesign their EV system or the new supplier would have to make something totally custom to fit their needs."

This creates a powerful moat for CATL. The author illustrates this by noting that while LG made a custom battery for Tesla, it is rare. Most automakers prefer to stick with a supplier that has already proven its integration capabilities. This strategic insight explains why CATL's market share remains so high despite the entry of new competitors. The author effectively connects this to the broader "Huawei model" of deep collaboration, where suppliers and manufacturers co-develop products, making the relationship symbiotic and sticky.

There is no well-defined battery pack standard, thus they each have different dimensions and tolerances and though batteries from different suppliers might have similar specs on paper, in practice the different ways manufacturers make their packs mean that the only real way to find out how their battery pack is going to perform in your EV is to test it and to test it for a while.

This observation underscores the practical reality of the industry: theoretical specs matter less than real-world integration. The author's coverage here is particularly strong because it moves beyond the hype of "green energy" to the gritty engineering realities that determine market winners. It suggests that CATL's dominance is not just a result of government policy but of a structural advantage that is difficult to replicate.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's coverage succeeds in demystifying CATL's rise, moving beyond simple nationalism to reveal a complex interplay of founder ambition, technical innovation, and strategic timing. The strongest part of the argument is the detailed explanation of how the "cell-to-pack" technology and the high switching costs for automakers create a durable competitive advantage that transcends government subsidies. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its optimism regarding the post-subsidy era; while CATL is a titan today, the author acknowledges that the coming years will test its ability to compete globally without the protective shield of the Chinese state. Readers should watch closely to see if CATL can maintain its technological lead as the global market opens up and competitors like LG and Panasonic ramp up their own innovations.
