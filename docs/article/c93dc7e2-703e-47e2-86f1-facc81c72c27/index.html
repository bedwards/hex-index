According to a recent BBC poll, between 2013 and 2019, the number of people across the Arab world who identified as not religious rose from 8% to 13%. Among those under 30, that number reached as high as 18%. In fact, in every country surveyed except for Yemen, non-religious identification increased. The Egyptian journalist Kala Diyab described it as a tsunami of atheism.

Now, whether it's really a tsunami or just a ripple is up for debate, but one thing is clear. Something is changing in the religious landscape of Arab countries. Non-religious views are becoming more visible and harder to ignore. And for a long time, scholars of religion did ignore non-religion in Arab countries.

We can speculate a few reasons for this blind spot. The Moroccan journalist Ahmed Benchmpsey thought it was a lack of imagination among many Western researchers who couldn't even fathom an Arab atheist. Back in 1991, the scholar Ernest Geller claimed that secularization simply hadn't happened in the Islamic world and maybe couldn't, declaring, I think it is fair to say that no secularization has taken place in the world of Islam and that Islam is as strong now than it was 100 years ago. Apparently, he was one of many scholars who were not noticing the development going on under the surface.

Another reason for this blind spot is that studying Arab non-religion poses several serious challenges. As in, it's not just overlooked, it's hard to access. Researchers face limited data, hidden communities, and real risks for both participants and the scholars. As we'll see later in this video, atheism is stigmatized and sometimes even criminalized in many Arab majority countries.

So, non-religious views tend to stay underground, shared only in private groups. Many official government statistics are also unreliable, claiming a near totally religious population, and much of mainstream media in the Arab world either ignores or actively censors discussions about atheism and secularism. That means fewer public discussions, fewer visible movements, and until recently, less academic research. Also, as we've seen in other videos in the series on non-religion, the terminology can get really, really tricky.

Not everyone who steps away from religion automatically calls themselves an atheist. Some identify as agnostic, secular, spiritual but not religious or skeptical. And that's just in English. How do we talk about non-religion in Arabic and in a Muslim majority context?
