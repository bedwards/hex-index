AI Literacy - Lecture 10.1: The Past - and Future? - of Exponential Economic Growth

Kenny Easwaran · Kenny Easwaran ·Sep 16, 2024 · 20 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Kenny Easwaran reframes the AI anxiety cycle not as a novel crisis, but as a historical anomaly in the making. While most commentators fixate on immediate job displacement or utopian abundance, Easwaran argues that the real story is the exponential growth rate itself—a trajectory that has held steady for two centuries despite wars, plagues, and political upheavals. This context is vital for busy readers because it suggests that the future of AI isn't about breaking the rules of economics, but about whether it can replicate the singular energy transition that launched the modern age.

The Great Acceleration

Easwaran begins by dismantling the common misconception that Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is merely a measure of stock markets or financial intermediaries. He insists that "the actual measure is attempting to measure the value of the goods and services that are being provided not the financial intermediaries." This distinction is crucial; it shifts the focus from abstract wealth to tangible human well-being. By adjusting for purchasing power parity using "international dollars at 2017 prices," the data reveals a massive reduction in severe poverty across China, India, and Africa, a trend that often gets lost in Western-centric economic debates.

"Even though things were more expensive in the past or are more expensive in other places, that's not going to be a contributing factor; it's going to try to measure how much of given goods and services are being produced."

The author then addresses the "population dilution" counterargument. Critics often suggest that rising global output is just a function of more people. Easwaran counters this by showing that while the population grew tenfold, total economic output grew 150-fold. Even when adjusted per person, the growth is a fifteenfold increase. He visualizes this through a logarithmic chart, noting that "economic output per person doubles roughly every 50 to 60 years during this period." This consistency is the piece's most compelling insight: the rate of progress has been remarkably stable, acting as a "human default" driven by technological accumulation.

"It feels like a reasonable default to assume that this same rate of exponential growth is the human default due to technological progress as long as we keep developing new economic technologies at this same exponentially accelerating rate that we have been right."

Critics might argue that this "default" is actually a statistical artifact of the Industrial Revolution's unique starting point, ignoring the stagnation of the preceding millennium. Easwaran anticipates this by zooming out to a 1,000-year view. He points out that from 1000 to 1800 AD, despite the Black Death, Mongol conquests, and colonization, "there was not growth in goods and services anything like the following 200 years." The Industrial Revolution was not a continuation of a trend; it was a structural break in history.

The Energy Bottleneck

The core of Easwaran's argument shifts from economics to physics. He posits that the Industrial Revolution's acceleration was fundamentally a "revolution in energy use." He traces the shift from biomass (wood and crops) to fossil fuels, noting a lag where "coal didn't actually catch up to traditional biomass until nearly a century into the industrial revolution." This historical nuance suggests that technological adoption is often slower than we assume, and that the current exponential curve is inextricably linked to the availability of dense, cheap energy.

"If energy use is the core of global economic growth, then an energy transition where we lose these sources of most of the world's energy... perhaps a decrease in energy use might put just as sudden an end to the current exponential age as its sudden beginning."

This is where the commentary on AI becomes most provocative. If AI is to be the next great accelerator, it must solve the energy constraint just as the steam engine did. Easwaran references physicist Tom Murphy's stark visualization of energy use, which shows a massive spike in the last two centuries that must inevitably flatten or crash unless a new energy source replaces fossil fuels.

"Over the next few centuries that will stop and either we will have some new energy source that makes up for that or maybe even continues the growth or we will be going back to the energy use of that path."

The implication is sobering: AI might not be able to drive exponential economic growth if the energy required to run it cannot be scaled sustainably. The argument holds up well against the "AI will solve everything" hype, grounding the discussion in the physical limits of the planet. However, it perhaps underestimates the potential for AI to drastically improve energy efficiency or discover new fusion pathways, which could decouple growth from traditional energy constraints.

"The Industrial Revolution really was a bigger change in the global trajectory of quality of life than anything that came in those centuries before."

Bottom Line

Easwaran's strongest contribution is his refusal to treat AI as an isolated variable, instead embedding it within the long arc of energy-driven economic history. His argument is vulnerable only in its assumption that the "exponential default" is a law of nature rather than a temporary condition of fossil fuel abundance. The reader should watch for whether AI development can actually solve the energy bottleneck, or if it will simply accelerate the approach to the cliff that Murphy predicts.
