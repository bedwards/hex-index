A fictional scenario based on two very real dissidents

Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

This piece from Natural Selections does something rare for pandemic-era narratives: it bypasses the shouting matches to expose the profound isolation that fueled them. Rather than focusing on policy mandates or viral transmission rates, the editors present a fictionalized script where two self-described "dissidents" and a weary bus driver find unexpected common ground through shared loneliness. The most striking claim isn't about virology; it's the argument that the true casualty of the lockdown era was the human capacity for nuance, replaced by a binary of safety versus danger that severed families and communities alike.

The Architecture of Alienation

The piece opens not with data, but with a character study of a bus driver who represents the average citizen saturated by fear-based news cycles. Natural Selections describes him as someone who "spends each working day sitting on a bus just to go home and sit in front of the television watching mainstream news, worrying about nearly everything he's ingesting from it." This framing immediately shifts the conflict from a medical debate to an information crisis. The driver stops the bus not because of a calculated risk assessment, but because he feels "unsafe," highlighting how perceived threat often overrides protocol when anxiety is high.

The narrative then introduces Andrew and Sven, two men who view themselves as outliers. When Andrew explains his refusal to wear a mask, he claims, "I am exempt." The driver's retort—"You know, I could kick you off the bus right now"—escalates the tension until Sven intervenes. Natural Selections uses Sven to voice a specific brand of skepticism that gained traction globally: the belief that the response was disproportionate and politically motivated. Sven argues, "The whole thing is a ruse," and draws a chilling parallel between modern public health measures and historical totalitarianism, asking, "So… is this how the Third Reich started?"

"In barely two years, you could watch, in real time, how easily a population can be captured."

This comparison is the piece's most provocative element. While critics might note that equating mask mandates with the atrocities of the Third Reich risks trivializing historical genocide and lacks proportional gravity, the script uses this hyperbole to illustrate the psychological state of those who feel stripped of agency. The argument isn't necessarily about the efficacy of masks in a vacuum, but rather the feeling of being "reduced to less than human" when public health directives clash with personal liberty.

The Weaponization of Information and the Raoult Case

The dialogue deepens as Sven recounts his experience following French scientist Didier Raoult, who promoted hydroxychloroquine early in the pandemic. Natural Selections details how the media ecosystem moved "in unison to destroy his credibility," labeling him a fraud overnight. Sven's realization is pivotal: "If everyone wants to silence him, then he is exactly the man I should listen to." This captures the essence of the contrarian mindset that formed during the crisis—a distrust of institutions so deep that any unified front was viewed as proof of deception.

The piece notes that Raoult had a "142-page curriculum vitae" and managed real epidemics, yet was portrayed as a liar. Natural Selections suggests that the media's aggressive tactics—interrupting, derailing, and confusing—were designed to manufacture doubt where there was expertise. This mirrors historical moments where scientific consensus was fractured by political pressure, though the specific dynamics of the 2019-2023 period were unique in their speed and global reach.

Critics might argue that this narrative oversimplifies a complex scientific debate regarding hydroxychloroquine's efficacy, ignoring the substantial data that eventually led major health organizations to advise against its use for COVID-19 treatment. However, the piece is less interested in the clinical facts than in the process of how dissenting voices were marginalized. The script posits that when a scientist is "isolated on a hostile set," the public loses access to alternative viewpoints, creating an echo chamber where only one narrative survives.

The Fringe and the Heart: A Shared Loneliness

The turning point of the story arrives not through a debate about virology, but through a confession of socioeconomic reality. Andrew reveals that while others mourned lost travel and dining, he was already living on the margins: "Most stores—I couldn't afford anything in them, so their doors may as well have been locked." He offers a powerful metaphor for the lockdown experience:

"Imagine society is a city... Most people live in the heart of that city which is society. What society experienced during lockdown was a bit like what it's like to live on the fringe."

This reframing is the piece's most successful move. It suggests that the anger and fear driving the conflict were often rooted in a sudden, universal experience of deprivation that privileged populations had never felt before. The bus driver, initially hostile, softens as he realizes these men are not "menaces" but fellow humans grappling with isolation. He admits, "I was afraid. And angry. And foolish."

The resolution is deeply personal rather than political. The driver calls his daughter to apologize for letting fear drive a wedge between them: "This fight over who is vaccinated or not is superseding the love I have for you both." Natural Selections ends with the driver realizing that in trying to protect himself, he was "harming not only myself, but those I love."

"I thought you were risking harming me. Now I can see I was harming not only myself, but those I love."

This emotional arc echoes the lessons of the SARS-CoV-1 outbreak in 2003, where the initial panic often gave way to a realization that community cohesion was the most effective defense against the virus's spread. Just as the 2003 crisis taught us that fear can be as contagious as the pathogen itself, this script argues that reconnection is the only antidote to the polarization of the last few years.

Bottom Line

Natural Selections succeeds in humanizing a polarized era by shifting the focus from policy disputes to the psychological toll of isolation and misinformation. Its strongest argument is that the "dissident" label often masks a desperate need for connection and agency, while its greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on hyperbolic historical comparisons that may alienate readers seeking nuance. The piece ultimately suggests that the path forward requires acknowledging shared loneliness over debating individual mandates.

Note from Heather: And now for something completely different. I have received so many remarkable stories for the Covid Era Stories project. Sven (a pseudonym) and Andrew (his real name) are real people who wrote in with real stories. With help from Zirrus, we produced a mash-up of their stories, in script form. It reflects reality, as the best fiction always does, but this has far more truth than fiction.

Cast:

Sven: French, late-thirties, intelligent, clever and spunky Covid Era dissident.

Andrew: Canadian, late-thirties, shy, bookish and resolute Covid Era dissident.

Bus driver: late-fifties, spends each working day sitting on a bus just to go home and sit in front of the television watching mainstream news, worrying about nearly everything he’s ingesting from it.

All are loners at heart.

Setting: Public transportation in Toronto, well after full-on lockdowns, but still in the madness era. Midsummer.

Most of the other passengers have gotten off at various stops along the way, and Andrew can see the bus driver eyeing him suspiciously. His eyes are what is mostly visible, not because the mirror is small, but because the driver is fully masked. Andrew is not, and the guy all the way at the back of the bus isn’t either, but he has his face securely buried in his laptop. The angry driver finally speaks to Andrew.

Bus driver: Where is your mask, son?

Andrew: I’m sorry, what? Your mask makes it hard for me to understand you.

Bus driver: Your mask! Where’s your mask?!

Andrew: I don’t need one.

Bus driver: You don’t seem to have a breathing issue...

Andrew: I just don’t need one.

Bus driver: You trying to be a menace?

Andrew: No, I just don’t need to be wearing a mask. I’m exempt.

Bus driver: What kind of crap is that? You know, I could kick you off the bus right now.

Andrew: I am trying to get to the library.

Bus driver: I have every right to just stop this bus, it’s in the handbook! If I’m feeling unsafe, I am free to stop the bus and not budge until the danger is removed!

Andrew: Really, sir, I’d just like to get to the library.
