Our Consumer Society

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Jun 9, 2022 · 48 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now dismantles the lazy stereotype of the shallow shopper by tracing a 500-year arc from Renaissance moral panic to the modern shopping mall, revealing that our obsession with "stuff" is less a character flaw and more a engineered cultural revolution. The piece is notable for its refusal to settle on a single definition of consumerism, instead presenting it as a shifting landscape where ancient philosophy, slave labor, and the invention of the modern salesman collide. For the busy listener trying to navigate a world of one-day delivery and targeted ads, this historical grounding offers a rare clarity: we are not just buying products; we are participating in a centuries-old experiment on desire itself.

The Ancient Roots of Modern Spending

The author begins by challenging the assumption that consumerism is a purely modern invention, arguing instead that it has deep roots in the moral economies of the past. Then & Now writes, "the cardinal features of this culture were acquisition and consumption as the means of achieving happiness, the cult of the new, the democratization of desire and money value as the predominant measure of all value in society." This quote, drawn from historian William Leach, effectively frames the stakes: consumerism isn't just about shopping; it's a totalizing worldview where money becomes the only metric of worth. The commentary here is sharp, contrasting this modern "cult of the new" with the ancient Greek and Christian skepticism toward luxury.

The text details how Renaissance Italy was a battleground between new merchant wealth and old moral codes. Then & Now notes that "aristotle... argued that the displaying of wealth should be done virtuously," while the Stoics warned that attachment to material things leads to frustration. This historical context is crucial because it shows that the anxiety we feel today about overconsumption is not new; it is a recurring tension in human history. The author points out that in 1497, a friar in Florence famously organized a "bonfire of vanities," burning cosmetics, clothes, and art to purge society of temptation. This vivid imagery serves as a powerful counter-narrative to the idea that humans have always been naturally acquisitive.

We have a wider variety of goods, shops and services, we have more pervasive marketing, everything is commercialized and exchanged.

Critics might argue that focusing on the moral debates of 15th-century Italy distracts from the sheer scale of modern industrial production, which is qualitatively different from the artisanal trade of the Renaissance. However, the author's point stands: the psychological struggle between desire and virtue remains the same, even if the goods have changed from tapestries to smartphones.

The Engine of Desire

The commentary shifts to the 18th century, identifying the true birth of modern consumerism not in the factory, but in the mind of the marketer. Then & Now highlights the work of Josiah Wedgwood, an English pottery maker who "invented the modern salesman" by employing tactics like "inertia selling campaigns, product differentiation, market segmentation, detailed market research." This is a pivotal moment in the narrative. The author argues that before this, people bought what they needed; after Wedgwood, they were taught to want what they didn't need. The text suggests that this was a deliberate engineering of human psychology, not an organic evolution of taste.

The piece connects this shift to the broader economic changes of the era, noting that "a taste for new foods and drinks made cheaper and more abundant by the atlantic slave trade increased." This is a sobering reminder that the expansion of consumer choice was often built on the backs of enslaved people. Then & Now writes, "cotton from plants instead of animals again made cheaper with slave labor became more affordable, took dyes better and suddenly clothes became more colourful, more varied, more abundant." This sentence forces the reader to confront the dark underbelly of the "democratization of desire." The author's integration of this historical fact adds necessary moral weight to the discussion of consumer culture.

As the narrative moves into the 20th century, the focus turns to the physical architecture of consumption. The author describes the shopping mall not just as a place to shop, but as a "self-contained city" designed by architect Victor Gruen. Then & Now writes, "Gruen believed that these new developments could be the hearts of the new suburban communities... later in life though he became appalled at how they turned out." This tragic arc—from utopian vision to commercial dystopia—is one of the piece's most compelling elements. It illustrates how the very structures built to foster community ended up isolating people in a landscape of pure commerce.

Glass democratized desire even as it democratized access to goods.

The author uses the invention of the glass shop window as a metaphor for this shift. By placing goods behind glass, merchants created a barrier that turned desire into a visual spectacle. This is a sophisticated argument that moves beyond simple economics to explore the sociology of display. The commentary suggests that the "window" is where the modern consumer identity was forged, separating the buyer from the object until the moment of purchase.

The Psychology of the New

In the final section, the author tackles the psychological drivers of consumerism, specifically the "production of new desires" and "social mimicry." Then & Now cites sociologist Colin Campbell, who argues that the consumer "withdraws from reality as fast as he encounters it, ever casting his daydreams forward in time, attaching them to objects of desire." This is a profound insight into the nature of modern dissatisfaction. The author suggests that we don't buy things to satisfy a need; we buy them to sustain a fantasy of a better self or a better life.

The text explores how the Romantic movement of the 19th century fueled this shift, emphasizing feelings and novel experiences over tradition. Then & Now writes, "we seem to have a desire for the new but really understanding what that means... has only developed over the past few hundred years." This reframes the constant churn of new products not as a sign of progress, but as a cultural construct designed to keep us in a state of perpetual longing. The author also touches on the "flaneur," the aimless stroller of Paris, who became a symbol of this new, experience-driven identity.

However, the author is careful not to paint all consumption as negative. Then & Now writes, "we consume books and audio books and online lectures, we consume healthy foods... we don't necessarily enjoy sometimes." This nuance is vital. It acknowledges that the category of "consumerism" is too broad to be dismissed entirely. We are consumers of art, travel, and education, not just gadgets and fast fashion. The piece argues that the problem lies not in consumption itself, but in the type of desire that is being cultivated.

The modern world created adventurers, explorers, dandies and flaneurs moving through the streets... just to collate and to understand the new.

A counterargument worth considering is that the author may overstate the role of marketing in creating desire, underestimating the genuine human drive for improvement and novelty that exists independently of commercial manipulation. While the article brilliantly traces the history of how desire was harnessed, it leaves open the question of how much of it is manufactured versus how much is innate. Nevertheless, the evidence presented regarding the deliberate strategies of early marketers like Wedgwood is compelling.

Bottom Line

Then & Now delivers a masterful historical excavation that reframes our relationship with goods from a moral failing to a complex cultural evolution. The piece's greatest strength is its ability to connect the dots between Renaissance philosophy, the brutality of the slave trade, and the design of the modern shopping mall, showing how they all feed into the same engine of desire. Its biggest vulnerability is the lack of a clear path forward; while it diagnoses the problem of "consumer power" with precision, it offers little on how to reclaim agency in a world where desire is so thoroughly commodified. The reader is left with a deeper understanding of the landscape, but perhaps a more urgent need to find their own way out of it.
