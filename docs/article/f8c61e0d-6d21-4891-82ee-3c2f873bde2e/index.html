The Nature of Space and Time AMA

Matt O'Dowd · PBS Space Time ·Feb 10, 2022 · 26 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Matt O'Dowd opens a door to the universe's most fundamental mystery with a confession that defies the usual confidence of scientific exposition: no one actually knows what space and time are. In this "Ask Me Almost Everything" session, the PBS Space Time host dismantles the comforting notion that physics has solved the puzzle of reality, revealing instead a landscape where our best theories collapse and our most intuitive metaphors fail. For the busy mind seeking clarity, O'Dowd offers not a textbook definition, but a rigorous tour of the intellectual frontier where the smooth fabric of the cosmos meets the jagged edge of quantum uncertainty.

The Stage That Isn't a Stage

O'Dowd begins by tracing the evolution of our understanding, moving from the rigid certainty of Isaac Newton to the fluid reality of Albert Einstein. He describes the Newtonian view where space and time are merely "the dimensions that form the stage on which the play of the universe takes place." This static grid, however, was shattered by relativity. As O'Dowd explains, "Einstein came and changed all of it. Space and time became space-time due to the fact that these two dimensions have the tendency to morph together depending on reference frame."

This shift is crucial because it destroys the idea of a universal "now." O'Dowd notes that the "relativity of simultaneity showed us that my perception of now could be some other observer's future and yet another observer's past." This leads to the concept of the "block universe," where all of history exists simultaneously. Yet, even this elegant model has cracks. The mathematics of relativity suggest that space and time are continuous, meaning they can be divided infinitely. O'Dowd points out that "most physicists now think that we need more than this" because the smoothness breaks down at the smallest scales.

"We know that space and time simply can't be smooth and continuous indefinitely all the way down."

This admission is the pivot point of the entire discussion. The conflict between General Relativity and Quantum Mechanics forces us to confront the Planck length and Planck time—the smallest possible units of reality. Below this threshold, the physics simply stops working. O'Dowd frames this not as a failure, but as a necessary boundary that demands new theories of quantum gravity, such as string theory or loop quantum gravity, where space and time might not be fundamental at all, but rather emergent properties of something deeper.

The Stretching of Nothing

One of the most counterintuitive concepts O'Dowd tackles is the expansion of the universe. Listeners often ask if space is stretching, being created, or if galaxies are just moving apart. O'Dowd clarifies that mathematically, these are indistinguishable descriptions of the same phenomenon. He describes the expansion as the growth of a "scale factor," which is essentially a "multiplicative factor that... increases the size of the grid of space over time."

The implications of this are staggering. If space is a grid that stretches, and we can draw a new grid between the expanded points, then we are effectively creating new space. O'Dowd highlights the bizarre consequence of this: "It's like you're adding more dark energy every time you add a new grid inside." This means the total amount of dark energy in the universe grows as the universe expands, a fact that drives the accelerating expansion of the cosmos.

Critics might note that the distinction between "moving through space" and "space expanding" is often a semantic debate with no observational difference, as O'Dowd himself admits. However, the conceptual shift is vital for understanding why the universe doesn't need to expand "into" anything. The universe is not a balloon inflating in a room; the room itself is growing.

"In a sense, that's a pretty valid way to think about [creating new space], because when we stretch the grid, we can borrow new grid inside it."

The Rubber Sheet Trap

Perhaps the most valuable part of O'Dowd's commentary is his deconstruction of the most famous analogy in physics: the rubber sheet. We are all taught to visualize gravity as a heavy ball depressing a stretched sheet, causing other objects to roll toward it. O'Dowd dismantles this image, pointing out that it relies on a fourth dimension to explain the depression, which is not how our three-dimensional space works.

He argues that the analogy fails because "the stretching of space in a gravitational field is only a small part of the source of gravity. The more important part is the stretching of time." In a gravitational field, space is actually stretched to contain more volume than expected, while time slows down. O'Dowd suggests a better visualization: "Maybe a better way to do the rubber sheet is to pinch it inwards... it represents something like the space-time grid being compacted into a higher density."

This correction is essential. It moves the listener from a 2D cartoon of gravity to a 4D reality where the curvature of time is just as significant as the curvature of space. O'Dowd emphasizes that these descriptions are "stories" and "ways of visualizing the math," not the math itself. He warns against taking the metaphor too literally, noting that in four dimensions, the "degree of freedom" is the curvature of the space itself, not a dip into a higher dimension.

"All of these descriptions are a kind of stories. There are ways of visualizing the math, and one story isn't typically the unique and only way to do it."

Bottom Line

Matt O'Dowd's greatest strength here is his intellectual honesty; he refuses to pretend that modern physics has a complete answer for the nature of reality. The strongest part of his argument is the clear delineation of where our current theories succeed and where they catastrophically fail, specifically at the Planck scale. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the inherent difficulty of explaining emergent space-time without resorting to the very metaphors he critiques. Readers should watch for how future theories of quantum gravity attempt to resolve the tension between the smooth geometry of Einstein and the discrete, granular nature of the quantum world.

"We don't know. Some people are making some very brave efforts to figure it out."

This piece is a masterclass in navigating the unknown, proving that in science, admitting what we don't know is often more illuminating than pretending we do.
