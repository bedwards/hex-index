The Best Way to Tie Your Shoelaces: A 6-Year Clinical Trial

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Mar 31, 2020 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In an era of medical misinformation, Rohin Francis delivers a masterclass in critical thinking by turning his own feet into a six-year clinical trial. He doesn't just debunk a viral shoelace hack; he uses the absurdity of a "bum" (an undone shoelace) to expose the rigorous standards required to prove that any treatment actually works. For busy professionals navigating a sea of health claims, this piece is a vital reminder that anecdote is not data, no matter how compelling the story.

The Hierarchy of Evidence

Francis begins by dismantling the weakest forms of scientific inquiry, starting with the case series. He writes, "They collect a bunch of people who report a benefit from some ineffective therapy... and say this proves that the therapy works. No, it doesn't. It proves nothing." This blunt dismissal is necessary because so much of modern wellness marketing relies on exactly this flawed logic. By placing case reports at the bottom of the evidence pyramid, Francis sets a high bar for what constitutes proof.

He then walks the reader through observational studies, explaining why they cannot establish causality. He illustrates this with a hypothetical scenario about diabetics and running, noting that "it's tempting to say that's the cause of their diabetes... but you can't establish causality from an observational study." This distinction is crucial for readers who often confuse correlation with cause in nutritional science or lifestyle advice. The author's ability to translate complex epidemiological concepts into the mundane reality of shoe-tying makes the lesson stick.

"I randomized one of my feet to be the intervention arm of the study and the other foot to be the control arm. That's probably a sentence that's never been said before."

The Gold Standard in Action

Moving beyond observation, Francis details his own randomized controlled trial (RCT), the gold standard of medical research. He explains the mechanics of his experiment: one foot tied with the new technique, the other with the old, with a crossover design every year to account for individual foot differences. He admits the limitations candidly, noting, "I didn't feel it was possible to achieve this without hiring a full-time shoelace administrator." This self-deprecating humor underscores the immense effort usually required to generate reliable data, a stark contrast to the instant "cures" often sold online.

Critics might argue that a sample size of two feet is statistically irrelevant, but Francis anticipates this. He uses his own data to demonstrate why small sample sizes fail to yield definitive answers. "The new Ted technique resulted in fewer bums overall but this is not a statistically significant result," he reports, adding that the difference could have occurred "about 20% of the time just due to chance." This admission is the piece's intellectual core: it shows that even with a perfect study design, the data must be robust enough to rule out luck.

The Verdict on the "Better" Knot

Ultimately, Francis refuses to hype a result that doesn't exist. Despite the new knot looking "nicer" and sitting horizontally rather than vertically, he concludes, "I can't recommend that you adopt the new technique... because the statistics in an admittedly extremely small sample size do not back that up." This restraint is rare in an attention economy that rewards certainty over nuance. He challenges readers to apply this same skepticism to serious health claims, warning against "breathless promotion of some wacky treatment" that has only been tested on mice or via anecdotes.

"If they give you a bunch of anecdotes you know that this isn't a proven therapy. I'm not saying you shouldn't do it... but just know where you stand."

Francis's argument holds up because it prioritizes the process of inquiry over the outcome. He acknowledges that while his specific trial was overkill for shoelaces, the methodology is essential for anything that enters the human body. The piece effectively argues that the burden of proof lies with the claimant, not the skeptic.

Bottom Line

Francis's strongest move is using a trivial problem to teach a profound lesson about evidence, making the abstract concept of a "randomized controlled trial" tangible and memorable. The argument's only vulnerability is its reliance on the reader's willingness to accept that a six-year study on shoes is a valid metaphor for medical research, but the clarity of his logic bridges that gap effortlessly. Readers should watch for this same rigor when evaluating the next viral health trend: if the evidence looks like a case series, it's probably just a story.
