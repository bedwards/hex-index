Sidgwick defended

Richard Y Chappell · Good Thoughts ·Jan 16, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Richard Y Chappell tackles a quiet but explosive fault line in modern ethics: the idea that we might be morally obligated to bring more people into existence, even if it complicates our current world. While critics dismiss this as a mathematical quirk, Chappell argues it is actually a test of how deeply we value human life itself. For busy readers navigating complex policy debates, this piece offers a rare clarity on why the "total view" of population ethics isn't just a philosopher's game, but a fundamental stance on the unconditional worth of future generations.

The Cost of Indifference

Chappell begins by addressing the skepticism of economist John Quiggin, who blames the late philosopher Henry Sidgwick for distorting utilitarian thought. Chappell suggests Quiggin is missing the forest for the trees, arguing that the real error lies in thinking that ignoring difficult questions is a virtue. "People often make the mistake of thinking that neglecting a puzzling problem puts one in a philosophically better position," Chappell writes, "but when every complete and precise view has significant costs, we should appreciate that a noncommittal disjunction of costly commitments cannot be better than its least-costly disjunct."

This framing is sharp. It forces the reader to confront the idea that avoiding the hard math of population growth doesn't make a moral theory cleaner; it just makes it evasive. Chappell notes that classical utilitarians like Bentham and Mill didn't necessarily exclude future people from their calculations; they simply hadn't fully articulated the problem. He points out that it is "hard to imagine them approving of voluntary human extinction, for example, even if the final generation got a slight utility boost from increased material consumption." This historical nuance is crucial. It connects back to the deep work of Derek Parfit, who later formalized these population puzzles, showing that the tension between "total" and "average" utility has been a simmering issue since the 19th century, not a modern invention.

The dispute ultimately comes down to whether you value people (and their getting to live good lives) conditionally or unconditionally.

Chappell's central thesis is that rejecting the idea of creating new happy lives is a form of "thin, conditional humanism." He asks a piercing question: "When you see a happy child, should you merely feel glad that they're happy (rather than unhappy), while being coldly indifferent to whether they exist at all? Or should we rather recognize that it's a good thing that they exist?" This rhetorical move shifts the debate from abstract numbers to moral sentiment. It suggests that a philosophy which treats potential lives as irrelevant is less robust, not more prudent. Critics might argue that this "warm welcome" for potential lives ignores the resource constraints that make bringing people into existence a zero-sum game for the living. Chappell anticipates this, noting that affirming the value of new lives doesn't automatically lead to the "repugnant conclusion" of a world full of barely tolerable lives, but rather invites a more nuanced discussion on quantity versus quality.

The Public vs. The Individual

The piece then pivots to the scope of utilitarianism. Quiggin argues that utilitarianism works for public policy but fails as a guide for individual action because it is "impossibly demanding." Chappell pushes back, insisting that the original vision of the philosophy was always meant to cover both. He cites John Stuart Mill directly: "actions"—not just policies!—"are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness."

Chappell acknowledges that the "maximizing frame" can be misleading, but he defends the idea that moral ideals are supposed to be demanding. "It should hardly be surprising that ideal actions may be 'impossibly demanding'!" he notes. This is a vital distinction for the modern reader, who often conflates what is legally or socially acceptable with what is morally ideal. Chappell argues that the "counterintuitive" results of utilitarianism, like the famous Trolley Problem, are often artifacts of bad philosophy rather than flaws in the theory itself. He suggests that "moral intuitions track virtue signals," warning readers to be careful about relying on gut feelings when they are actually just social posturing.

Moral intuitions track virtue signals; one must be careful in how you rely on them.

This section is particularly effective because it strips away the fear that utilitarianism requires everyone to be a saint 24/7. Chappell clarifies that Sidgwick was distinguishing between a moral ideal and the bare minimum for social acceptability. The argument holds up well against the charge of absurdity, provided one accepts that a moral theory can describe a perfect world even if we fall short of it in practice. However, the dismissal of the "impossible demands" objection might feel unsatisfying to those who struggle with the sheer weight of global suffering. Chappell's solution is to reframe the objection as a failure to distinguish between ideal and non-ideal guidance, a move that is intellectually consistent but emotionally heavy.

The Reality of Outcomes

Finally, Chappell addresses the tension between what we expect to happen and what actually happens. Quiggin favors "expected utility," arguing that we should judge actions by their probabilities. Chappell, however, sides with Sidgwick's view that the "ultimate standard of rightness must be the amount of good actually produced." He interprets this not as a call for recklessness, but as a recognition that we should desire the best actual outcome, even if we must use probabilities to get there.

"We should presumably prefer that the actually-best outcome be realized, and so will be glad that the doctor did as they 'objectively ought', even if they acted subjectively wrongly," Chappell writes. This distinction between "objective rightness" (what a fully informed spectator would want) and "subjective rightness" (what we can reasonably decide under uncertainty) resolves a long-standing debate. It allows for a system where we can praise a doctor for making the right choice with the information they had, while still acknowledging that the outcome was tragic. This synthesis is the piece's strongest analytical contribution, showing that the two views are not rivals but complementary tools for different parts of the moral equation.

Objective utilitarianism tells us which choices are objectively preferable, and expectational utilitarianism tells us how to make rational moral decisions under conditions of uncertainty.

Chappell concludes by challenging the status quo of modern social welfare theory, which often defaults to "average" utility to avoid the complexities of population growth. He argues that this is a strategic retreat, not a philosophical victory. "If I currently have a mistaken view on that question, I'd welcome discovery of a better view," he admits, but he remains convinced that the "dialectic frankly seems about as rationally lopsided as debates in ethics ever get." This confidence is refreshing, even if it leaves the reader with more questions about how to balance the lives of the many against the needs of the few.

Bottom Line

Richard Y Chappell's defense of Sidgwick is a masterclass in reframing a technical philosophical dispute as a matter of moral courage. The strongest part of the argument is its insistence that ignoring potential lives is a form of moral indifference, not prudence. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the practical application: how do we actually implement a "total view" without triggering the resource crises that critics fear? Readers should watch for how this debate evolves in policy circles, particularly as demographic shifts make the question of population growth increasingly urgent.

by Richard Y Chappell · Good Thoughts · Read full article

John Quiggin is not a fan of contemporary utilitarianism, and he blames Sidgwick. I think he’s right that Sidgwick has had significant influence (especially via Parfit); but I think more by introducing previously-unasked questions than by turning away from any clearly contrary commitments in Bentham and Mill. As I explained in Puzzles for Everyone, people often make the mistake of thinking that neglecting a puzzling problem puts one in a philosophically better position (since it allows one to refrain from committing to any particular set of costs), but when every complete and precise view has significant costs, we should appreciate that a noncommittal disjunction of costly commitments cannot be better than its least-costly disjunct. My sense is that a mistake along these lines—not appreciating the costs of the alternatives—may underlie Quiggin’s objections to Sidgwick’s views.

Let’s consider the three objections in turn.

Valuing potential lives.

The classical utilitarians argued for public policies which promoted the welfare of the community to which they applied, on the basis of “each to count for one, and none for more than one”. This applied both to the current population and to the children who would actually be born as a result of their choices, but not to hypothetical additional people who might raise the sum of total utility.

By contrast, contemporary utilitarian philosophy yields bizarre spectacles like “longtermism” which implies that our primary goal should be to produce as many descendants as possible provided that the result is an increase in aggregate utility.

Firstly, that’s not an accurate statement of longtermism (which is more ecumenical than the total view of population ethics). But more to the point, it seems anachronistic to read Bentham and Mill as excluding “additional people” from counting, as opposed to simply failing to consider the relevant question. It’s hard to imagine them approving of voluntary human extinction, for example, even if the final generation got a slight utility boost from increased material consumption. Such a narrow focus on guaranteed existents fits ill with Mill’s deep concern for “the permanent interests of man as a progressive being,” for example.

Quiggin quotes Sidgwick as urging total over average utilitarianism. I find it strange to call this an “error” when the average view is subject to far more decisive counterexamples.
