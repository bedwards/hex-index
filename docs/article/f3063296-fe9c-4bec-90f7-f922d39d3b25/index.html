Sound Dramatically Helps Plants Grow

Benn Jordan · Benn Jordan ·Jan 19, 2025 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Benn Jordan doesn't just ask if music makes plants grow; he dismantles the very idea of a binary "yes" or "no" answer, replacing pop-science folklore with a rigorous, self-correcting experiment that exposes the fragility of human bias. While most coverage of this topic leans on anecdotal evidence or the whimsical claims of 1970s mysticism, Jordan brings a unique combination of acoustic engineering expertise and a deep philosophical critique of the scientific method itself. For the busy listener, this is not merely a story about talking to wheatgrass; it is a masterclass in how to think when the data refuses to be simple.

The Ghost in the Garden

Jordan begins by stripping away the romanticized notion that plants have feelings or preferences. He addresses the historical context, noting that while a 1962 study claimed a 20% growth increase in holy basil from classical music, the field later descended into the "kooky" territory of Dorothy Retallack's 1973 experiments. Retallack famously claimed plants grew toward classical music and away from Led Zeppelin, a finding that Jordan dismantles with surgical precision. He writes, "My interpretation of the book was not that Dorothy was interested in increasing plant yield as much as she was interested in attempting to prove that there was a positive music and a negative music." This observation is crucial; it reframes the entire history of the topic not as a failed scientific pursuit, but as a projection of human desire onto non-human entities.

The author's critique of anthropomorphism lands hard. He argues that attributing emotions to plants is a fundamental error in reasoning. "Plants don't have feelings or emotions much less do they enjoy or dislike particular genres of music," he states. This distinction is the bedrock of his own experiment. By refusing to ask if the plants "liked" the sound, Jordan shifts the inquiry to a measurable physical interaction. Critics might argue that this reductionist approach misses the holistic complexity of plant biology, but Jordan's methodical exclusion of emotional variables is exactly what allows the physical data to emerge clearly.

The Architecture of Bias

The core of Jordan's argument extends beyond botany into the philosophy of science. He posits that the scientific method exists primarily to "reduce human bias," a point he illustrates with a vivid hypothetical about a researcher who has invested their life savings in a sound-fertilizer device. "If you put your life savings into developing a speaker device that you believe will one day help plants grow you'll naturally be more receptive to information supporting the idea," he writes. This is a powerful reminder that objectivity is not a natural state but a disciplined practice.

Jordan's experiment was designed to be a fortress against this bias. He details the painstaking process of controlling variables, from filtering and boiling water to weighing seeds and calibrating scales. He notes, "Semantics and painfully annoying detail are everything." This dedication to the mundane is where the real science happens. He contrasts his approach with the vague methodologies of the past, where inadequate definitions of plant chambers and undefined musical genres led to irreproducible results. As he puts it, "The replication crisis is more about researchers not being funded to reproduce their own or other studies... thus creating an overabundance of unverified claims." This contextualization is vital; it suggests that the "crisis" is often a failure of resources and incentives rather than a failure of the scientific method itself.

There is no such thing as a definite truth or a definite answer or a binary. Every single thing in science and as far as we know in reality exists on a spectrum somewhere between highly improbable and highly probable.

The Spectrum of Truth

The most striking part of Jordan's commentary is his integration of Kurt Gödel's incompleteness theorems into a discussion about plant growth. He uses this mathematical concept to argue that absolute certainty is impossible. "What Gödel's incompleteness theorem tells us is that the only true thing about mathematics is that there is no such thing as true," he explains. While this might seem like a philosophical tangent, it serves a practical purpose: it prepares the reader for the nuance of his results. He acknowledges that his findings—specifically that wheatgrass exposed to a 4,000 Hz sine wave grew 28% more in biomass—are not a universal law, but a specific data point on a spectrum.

Jordan offers three theories for why the sound worked: a survival signal, increased photosynthetic efficiency via acoustic pressure, or acoustic levitation assisting the plant's structural integrity. He admits the last one sounds silly but notes his personal patent in acoustic levitation makes it worth investigating. This willingness to entertain improbable ideas while maintaining rigorous testing is the hallmark of a healthy scientific mindset. He concludes that the lack of reproducibility in other fields often stems from a lack of funding to challenge established narratives, particularly when those narratives serve corporate interests. "A fracking company is not going to be interested in spending money on a do-over for a published study of the safety of a new chemical," he observes, highlighting the economic forces that distort scientific truth.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's piece is a triumph of intellectual honesty, successfully using a simple plant experiment to expose the complex machinery of bias, funding, and human perception that underpins all scientific inquiry. Its greatest strength is the refusal to offer a neat conclusion, instead inviting the reader to accept uncertainty as the only reliable path to truth. The biggest vulnerability lies in the sheer complexity of the experiment, which may be difficult for others to replicate without similar resources, but the methodological rigor he demonstrates provides a clear roadmap for doing so. Readers should watch for how this spectrum-based thinking applies to other contested scientific claims, from climate models to medical treatments, where the binary of "proven" or "debunked" often obscures the reality.
