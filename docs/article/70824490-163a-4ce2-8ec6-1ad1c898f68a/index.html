The housing market isn’t for single people

The Walrus · ·Jan 6, 2026 ·15 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Renée Sylvestre-Williams dismantles a comforting myth: that the current housing crisis is merely a temporary market fluctuation. Instead, The Walrus argues that the system is structurally designed to exclude single people, turning a demographic reality into a financial catastrophe. This isn't just about high prices; it's about a century of policy that assumed everyone would marry, have children, and die in a specific sequence, leaving modern singles with no roadmap to security.

The Arbitrary Math of Affordability

The piece opens by exposing the fragility of the "30 percent rule" for housing costs. The Walrus writes, "It's both arbitrary and standard," quoting expert Carolyn Whitzman to reveal that this benchmark is a relic, not a law of economics. The author traces the line back to the 1944 Curtis Report on postwar reconstruction, which originally set the standard at 20 percent of income, reflecting a different era of labor rights and shorter life expectancies. That figure slowly crept up to 25, then 30 percent, yet our housing stock has not evolved to match the reality that single-person households are now the norm.

This historical context is crucial. The Walrus notes that the Curtis Report recommended a balanced mix of nonprofit, regulated, and private housing, but the federal government chose to let the market decide, prioritizing home ownership as a wealth-building tool over shelter as a right. The result is a system where the median income for a single person in Canada is roughly $45,000, yet the average rent for a one-bedroom apartment has ballooned to $2,109—nearly double the affordable guideline. As The Walrus puts it, "Overburdened is starting to look like the new normal."

Critics might argue that market forces simply reflect supply and demand, and that government intervention distorts prices. However, the article effectively counters this by showing how policy decisions actively shaped the market to favor dual-income families and investors, leaving singles to absorb the volatility alone.

Housing policy is still locked in assumptions from another century when people were expected to get married and have children in their twenties.

The Single Income Trap

The commentary shifts to the human cost of these structural failures, illustrating how the "pathway to wealth" narrative has collapsed for those without a partner's income to share the load. The Walrus shares a personal anecdote of buying a condo in 2009, a window of opportunity that has since slammed shut. The author highlights the absurdity of current requirements: to buy a detached home in Vancouver, one needs an income of nearly $215,000, while the national average sits at $57,100. The math simply doesn't work for the average single earner.

The piece powerfully illustrates the precarity of renting for singles. Jo Pavlov, an education worker, describes how the departure of a roommate pushed their housing costs to over 60 percent of their net income. "When she left, all responsibilities fell to me, and it's more than I can afford," Pavlov told the author. This isn't just a budgeting issue; it's a systemic vulnerability. The Walrus points out that while dual-income couples can split costs, singles bear the full weight of utilities, maintenance, and insurance, making them the first to be priced out during inflationary spikes.

Furthermore, the article exposes the lack of legal protections in many jurisdictions. While Canada maintains some form of rent control, the piece notes that thirty-seven states in the US outright prohibit it, leaving tenants in those regions entirely at the mercy of landlords. This disparity highlights how policy choices, rather than market inevitability, determine who gets to stay in their home.

Beyond the Nuclear Family Model

Perhaps the most innovative part of the coverage is its exploration of alternative living arrangements that challenge the nuclear family model. The Walrus details the story of "Clarens Commons," where six friends pooled resources to buy a $1.3 million home in Toronto, securing a co-ownership mortgage that defies traditional banking norms. Valery Navarrete, one of the owners, explains their motivation: "We're very much coming from the housing-as-a-human-right kind of perspective." They weren't looking for an investment; they were looking for stability in a market defined by "renovictions."

The author describes the rigorous process these friends undertook, which they dubbed "financial nudity." They had to confront not just their bank accounts but their deepest beliefs about money and family. "It was equal parts 'What's your income and what are your expenditures?' and 'How did your family treat money?'" The Walrus uses this example to argue that the solution isn't just building more units, but reimagining who is allowed to own them. The barrier isn't just capital; it's the rigid structure of mortgages and insurance that assumes a single borrower or a married couple.

A counterargument worth considering is whether these co-ownership models are scalable. Relying on deep friendships and intense legal due diligence may work for a select group, but it doesn't solve the crisis for the millions of lonely or isolated individuals who cannot find a community to join. The Walrus acknowledges this tension, noting that while roommates are an option, many people at certain life stages simply want independence without the risk of a stranger in their home.

Needing a roommate should not be the default for owning or renting.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its refusal to treat the housing crisis as a simple supply problem; it correctly identifies the mismatch between 20th-century policy frameworks and 21st-century demographics. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in the scalability of its proposed solutions—co-ownership and community living are powerful but difficult to mandate or replicate at a national level. Readers should watch for how policymakers respond to the growing political pressure from the single demographic, as the current trajectory promises to deepen inequality unless the definition of "affordable housing" is fundamentally rewritten.

The housing market isn’t for single people

By Renée Sylvestre-Williams

There’s a running joke in one of my friend circles that, if we ever pooled our resources and lived together, we’d need a cat room. We daydream about what our space would be like beyond feline accommodations: Everyone would have their own bedroom with en suite bathroom, naturally. We’d have a communal kitchen and living space, so we could hang out and get some friend time in. We’d have a housekeeper come in once a week, and as we get older, maybe a nurse to check in on us. It’s a great dream, and I think we’re half serious about living together, especially because half of us still rent. As my friend Diane once said, “As soon as I think I’ve saved enough for a down payment, prices jump.”

Housing is undeniably one of the major questions of our time. In Canada, not only is home ownership out of reach for many people, but even paying rent is getting harder. A 2024 Abacus survey showed nearly three in five Canadians are somewhat or very concerned about losing their home or rental because of financial issues. And that precarity is even more pronounced for single people.

The old rule of thumb was that housing should be 30 percent of your net income. At 40 percent, the Organisation of Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) considers a household “overburdened.” Overburdened is starting to look like the new normal.

In 2025, when I wrote this, surveys found that some Canadians were spending way more than 30 percent of their income on a place to live. The median gross income for single people was $45,069, and 30 percent of that is $13,521, or $1,125 per month. The average rent for a one-bedroom apartment in Canada, in January 2025, was $2,109, almost double the 30 percent guideline for a median income Canadian. The average monthly mortgage payment in 2024, according to the Canadian Mortgage and Housing Corporation, ranged between $1,337 in New Brunswick and $2,836 in British Columbia. In the United States, the average monthly mortgage payment in 2024 was $2,209 (US). California has the highest monthly payments at $2,500 (US), compared to West Virginia’s $960 (US).
