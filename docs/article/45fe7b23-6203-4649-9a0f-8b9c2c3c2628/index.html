The permanent emergency

Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Scott Alexander transforms the mundane chaos of raising toddlers into a profound anthropological case study, arguing that the modern parenting experience feels like a "permanent emergency" not because of poor planning, but because our children are operating on outdated evolutionary software. This piece stands out by refusing to offer parenting tips; instead, it dissects the friction between the ancestral environment our brains expect and the digital, safety-regulated world we inhabit. For the busy professional seeking to understand the hidden logic behind the screaming, Alexander provides a framework that turns frustration into a window on human nature.

The Ancestral Mismatch

Alexander begins with a personal anecdote about a 6 a.m. police visit triggered by a toddler's demand for a specific version of a song, using it to illustrate the high-stakes volatility of early childhood. "Everything about having toddlers feels like a permanent emergency," he writes, grounding the abstract concept of developmental psychology in the visceral reality of a sleep-deprived parent. He suggests that this chaos isn't random noise but a signal of deep-seated biological drives. The author posits that toddlers are not merely difficult; they are running on a specific operating system designed for a different era. "Toddlers, much more than adults, are still running off evolved instincts that expect the ancestral environment and a hunter-gatherer lifestyle," Alexander observes, noting that their primary programming is to identify and name every animal in the vicinity.

This framing is particularly effective because it reframes parental exhaustion as a clash of timelines. The child's obsession with naming vehicles as if they were megafauna—shouting "MUZZKA!" until the parent correctly identifies a motorcycle—is not a tantrum but a rigorous data-gathering exercise. Alexander humorously speculates that our forebears might have used toddlers as lookouts, scanning the savanna to announce "ANTELOPE! ANTELOPE! ANTELOPE!" until the tribe acknowledged the sighting. While anthropologists might argue that modern hunter-gatherers are not perfect proxies for hominid ancestors, the metaphor holds weight: the child's brain is prioritizing survival-relevant data (animals, large moving objects) over safety lessons like "pills aren't food." Critics might note that this evolutionary psychology lens can sometimes overstate the rigidity of these instincts, ignoring the role of cultural conditioning in how children learn to categorize the world. Yet, the core insight remains: the "emergency" is the sound of a brain trying to process a modern world through a Stone Age filter.

The algorithm knows we want children's songs related to a certain concept, and can usually find one. We've learned songs we could never previously have imagined.

The Digital Interface and the Cottage Industry

The piece takes a sharp turn into the modern economy of attention, revealing how the toddler's evolutionary drive to name things has been monetized by a "cottage industry among mildly scummy musicians." Alexander describes how children, unable to pronounce complex titles, have developed their own monikers like "sun song" or "die die die song" (for Dayenu), creating a perfect storm for algorithmic exploitation. "Every time the children learn a new word, they test whether it's a song," he notes, explaining how the market has responded with tracks like "The Poop Song" designed to capture the $0.001 per impression from tech giants. This observation highlights a strange feedback loop where the child's biological imperative to categorize the world is met by a digital infrastructure optimized to feed that impulse with low-effort content.

Alexander's analysis of the "Jonah song" incident—where a request for a babysitter's name yields a Christian children's song about a whale—further illustrates the gap between human intent and algorithmic interpretation. The child's confusion, "Whale swallow Jonah?", underscores the absurdity of a system that can instantly retrieve a song about a biblical figure but cannot distinguish between a person and a story. This section serves as a subtle critique of the "smart home" ecosystem, suggesting that while the technology is impressive, it often fails to grasp the nuance of human context, leaving parents to bridge the gap between the child's raw demand and the machine's literal response.

The Battle of the Best Page

The commentary shifts to the dynamics of sibling rivalry and the specific ways children engage with narrative, contrasting the twins' approaches to reading. While one child reads sequentially, the other, Kai, engages in a form of "twin sabotage," flipping books to the "best page"—usually featuring the moon, dogs, or trains—and defending it with his life. Alexander connects this behavior to his previous work on twin studies, noting how a theoretical "gene for sabotage" he once mocked has manifested in his own home. "God punished me for my mockery by sending me a son obsessed with sabotaging his twin sister's educational prospects," he writes with self-deprecating humor.

This anecdote serves as a microcosm for the broader debate on gender and education. Alexander uses the twins' dynamic to challenge the notion that girls outperform boys due to a "Feminized Longhouse Matriarchy," suggesting instead that the difference may lie in individual temperament and the specific ways boys and girls engage with the world. "I now think we'll discover their long-sought 'boy-friendly teaching methods' around the same time we finally eliminate the 'bro culture' that prevents women from winning exactly 50% of physics Nobels," he concludes, turning a domestic squabble into a pointed critique of systemic educational theories. The argument is compelling because it refuses to generalize, instead using the specific, chaotic reality of his household to question broad sociological claims.

The Bedtime Impasse

The final section addresses the ultimate test of parental endurance: bedtime. Alexander describes the ritual not as a routine but as a negotiation where the child has read the "How To Be A Toddler" books and knows exactly how to exploit parental soft spots. The cycle of fake drinking, toothpaste demands, and the "NO DIAPER CHANGE" paradox illustrates a child who has mastered the art of delay. "Kai has been reading the How To Be A Toddler books, and they all say that you should never, under any circumstances, stop crying, because then your parents will think they won," Alexander writes, highlighting the strategic nature of the child's behavior.

This section reveals the futility of standard parenting advice when faced with a child who understands the game better than the parent. The "impasse" described is not a failure of discipline but a recognition that the child's goal (staying awake) and the parent's goal (sleep) are fundamentally opposed, and the child is using every tool at their disposal to win. Alexander's observation that the child drinks milk immediately but pretends to sip it to buy time is a perfect example of the "permanent emergency"—a situation where the rules of the game are constantly shifting, and the only constant is the child's determination to control the outcome.

Bottom Line

Scott Alexander's "The permanent emergency" succeeds by reframing the chaos of toddlerhood not as a parenting failure, but as a collision between ancient evolutionary drives and a modern, algorithmic world. The piece's greatest strength is its ability to find deep anthropological meaning in the mundane, turning a 6 a.m. police visit into a lesson on human nature. Its vulnerability lies in its reliance on a specific, hyper-verbal family dynamic, which may not resonate with parents whose children express their needs differently. However, for anyone navigating the surreal landscape of early childhood, this commentary offers a vital perspective: the screaming is not noise, it is data, and the emergency is the sound of a new mind trying to map a world it was never built to understand.

The permanent emergency

by Scott Alexander · Astral Codex Ten · Read full article

One morning around 6, the police banged on our door. “OPEN UP!” they shouted, the way police shout when they definitely have an alternative in mind for if you won’t.

I was awake at the time, because the kids were up early and I was on shift. I opened the door. The cops seemed mollified by the fact that I was carrying twin toddlers and looked too frazzled to commit any difficult crimes. They said they’d gotten a 9-1-1 call from my house with plenty of screaming. Had there been any murders in the past hour or so?

I never did figure out how the police got called. My first guess was that one of the twins had gotten their hands on a phone and dialed random things, but neither my nor my wife’s call history showed anything incriminating. My second guess was that they’d screamed at Alexa so hard that it called emergency services, but the documentation says Alexa doesn’t have that function. Maybe a neighbor called and the police got the location wrong, I don’t know.

I do have a pretty good idea about the screaming, though. When Kai demanded “the sun song”, I had accidentally told Alexa to play Raffi’s version of Mister Golden Sun instead of SuperSimpleSongs’ version. Kai did not consider this a sufficiently faithful rendition, and made his displeasure clear to everyone in the neighborhood at six in the morning. Then Lyra didn’t like that Kai was screaming, and started screaming too. By the time I realized the song mishap, I couldn’t rectify my mistake, because they were screaming too loud for Alexa to hear my commands (and too loud for them to notice if the song changed anyway).

Again, I don’t know if this was why the police got called - maybe in a few weeks I’ll learn one of our neighbors got murdered within the GPS margin-of-error of our house. But I like to think that it was. My toddlers jointly calling 911 because I played a slightly different version of their favorite song is too perfect a metaphor to lose. Everything about having toddlers feels like a permanent emergency.
