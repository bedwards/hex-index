How To Build The JHS Pedals 3-Series Fuzz - Short Circuit Episode: 21

Josh Scott · JHS Pedals ·Feb 3, 2025 · 56 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Josh Scott doesn't just teach you how to solder a circuit; he argues that the most iconic sound in rock history is actually a flawed design that needs fixing to be truly usable. In this twenty-first episode of his "Short Circuit" series, Scott dismantles the myth of the vintage fuzz face, revealing that its legendary tone comes with a hidden cost: a lack of volume and a temperamental gain structure that forces players to keep knobs at maximum. He presents a radical solution—a "synthesis" of decades of modifications—that transforms a fragile, quiet circuit into a robust, studio-ready tool without losing its soul.

The Anatomy of a Fix

Scott begins by grounding the viewer in the history of the circuit, acknowledging the debate over its origins while quickly pivoting to the practical reality of building it. He describes the 3-Series Fuzz not as an invention, but as a deliberate curation of existing ideas. "It is an amalgamation... of just a lot of great Buzz face mods nothing that like I or we at JHS invented but it is something that we synthesized into something that I think is unique," Scott explains. This framing is crucial; it positions the builder not as a passive follower of instructions, but as an engineer making informed choices about circuit topology.

The core of his argument rests on three specific modifications that address the classic fuzz face's most glaring weaknesses. First, he tackles the volume issue. Vintage circuits often suffer from a massive signal drop, requiring the player to crank the volume knob to the max just to be heard. Scott introduces a "clean boost" stage at the end of the circuit, a move he describes as "textbook like literally out of a textbook." By adding a common emitter gain circuit, he ensures the pedal can drive an amp even when the fuzz control is dialed back. This is a pragmatic engineering decision that prioritizes stage utility over vintage purism.

"You just need if you have a classic fuzz face you need to turn it up and never turn it down because it's just that's how it should I mean it just doesn't sound good when you turn it down."

This observation cuts to the heart of why many guitarists struggle with vintage-style pedals. Scott's solution isn't to change the tone, but to change the gain structure. He replaces the traditional fuzz control with a fixed 1K resistor, effectively locking the fuzz stage to maximum saturation, and moves the volume control to a pre-gain stage. This allows the player to control the amount of fuzz entering the circuit without killing the output volume. It is a subtle but profound shift in how the instrument interacts with the player's dynamics.

Critics might argue that fixing the volume issue strips the pedal of its authentic, unpredictable character. However, Scott's approach suggests that the "character" of a fuzz face is often just the result of its limitations. By stabilizing the gain, he allows the player to explore the circuit's tonal range rather than fighting its physics.

The Bias and the Fat Switch

Beyond the volume fix, Scott introduces two other critical controls that redefine the pedal's versatility. The first is an external bias control, a feature that allows the builder to fine-tune the operating point of the transistors. "That bias control when you turn it it's an external control for the ladder of potential gain on Q2," he notes. This is a nod to the deep dives on tone control circuits found in his broader library, where he has previously explained how biasing affects the harmonic content of the signal. By making this external, he invites the builder to experiment with the "squeal" and "cut" of the fuzz, turning a static component into a dynamic performance tool.

The second addition is a "fat toggle," which Scott describes as a simple capacitor swap. "It's simply changing the coupling input capacitor value it's adding a capacitor in parallel that's all it's doing," he says. This modification shifts the frequency response, adding low-end weight to the circuit. It is a small change with a massive impact, bridging the gap between the thin, nasal sound of early fuzzes and the thicker, more modern distortion tones. This connects back to his discussions on input impedance, where he has shown how even minor component changes can drastically alter how a pedal interacts with a guitar's pickups.

"Transistor circuits are transistor circuits if they're a boost or a fuzz they're still going to have the same mechanic like the same principles in the circuit topology."

This statement serves as the philosophical anchor of the entire episode. Scott is teaching a language, not just a recipe. He wants the audience to understand that the principles of a fuzz face are universal, whether they are using silicon or germanium transistors. By breaking down the circuit into its fundamental components—gain stages, feedback loops, and coupling capacitors—he empowers the listener to modify any pedal they own. The lesson extends far beyond the 3-Series Fuzz; it is a masterclass in analog electronics.

The Human Element of Engineering

What sets Scott's commentary apart is his refusal to hide the messiness of the process. He admits to the challenges of live streaming, the quirks of the equipment, and the sheer volume of knowledge required to build these circuits. He acknowledges that the community is the driving force behind his work, noting that the "Short Circuit Community" is his "favorite YouTube community." This humanizes the technical content, reminding the reader that engineering is a collaborative, iterative process.

He also touches on the commercial reality of his work, explaining that the YouTube membership model allows him to produce deeper content without compromising the free tutorials. "We're not Mr Beast YouTube Mogul over here," he admits, grounding his ambitious project in a relatable reality. This transparency builds trust, making the technical advice feel more like a conversation between peers than a lecture from an expert.

"I would say that this would be My ultimate the devised fuzz face from JHS so after all the years of messing with it I'm really proud of this one."

This declaration of pride is significant. It signals that the 3-Series Fuzz is not just another product, but the culmination of years of experimentation and refinement. It represents a point where the theoretical meets the practical, where the ideal circuit finally matches the real-world needs of the player.

Bottom Line

Josh Scott's commentary succeeds because it treats the listener as an intelligent collaborator rather than a passive consumer. By deconstructing the fuzz face into its fundamental parts and explaining the "why" behind every modification, he transforms a simple build guide into a lesson in circuit design philosophy. The strongest part of his argument is the insistence that vintage fidelity should not come at the cost of playability; his solution to the volume and gain issues is both elegant and essential. The biggest vulnerability, perhaps, is the sheer density of information, which may overwhelm a complete beginner, but for the smart, busy listener, it offers a clear, actionable path to mastering one of the most complex and rewarding circuits in guitar history.
