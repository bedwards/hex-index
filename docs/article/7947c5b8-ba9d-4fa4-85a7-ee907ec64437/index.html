Goodfellas: The Peak of Gangster Cinema?

Tom van der Linden · Like Stories of Old ·Aug 24, 2023 · 54 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Tom van der Linden doesn't just analyze Goodfellas; he dissects why the film feels less like a period piece and more like a cultural mirror for the modern viewer. While many critics focus on the violence or the acting, van der Linden zeroes in on a startling, often uncomfortable question: is the film's seductive power actually a fantasy designed for women as much as men? This isn't a standard film review; it's a deep dive into the psychology of the genre that challenges the listener to reconsider who the gangster movie is really for.

The Myth of the Gangster

The conversation begins with a personal reflection on how the film lands differently depending on when you watch it. Van der Linden notes that while The Godfather feels slow to the uninitiated, Goodfellas delivers the "gangster movie" punch immediately. He argues that the film exists in a unique space where the audience already knows the rules. "Goodfellas is a movie that comes into the genre with it as an established contemporary genre," he observes. This is a crucial distinction. Unlike earlier films that had to explain the allure of crime, Goodfellas assumes the viewer is already hooked by the mythology of the mob.

The author suggests that Martin Scorsese's genius lies in how explicitly he lays out the protagonist's motivation. In The Godfather, we must infer Michael Corleone's descent; in Goodfellas, the narrator, Henry Hill, spells it out. "He just comes out in the voiceover and says it made me feel important," van der Linden explains. This directness changes the film's texture. It transforms the story from a tragedy of circumstance into a confession of vanity. The film doesn't ask us to guess why Henry wants to be a gangster; it tells us he wanted to be "somebody" in a world where being a "schmuck" was the only other option.

"The movie confirms that later on... when we show exactly the life that we assume is part of the life of the gangster."

This approach creates a feedback loop between the film and the audience. The movie knows we've seen Scarface and The Godfather, so it leans into those tropes with a self-aware energy. Critics might note that this meta-commentary risks making the film feel like a parody of itself, but van der Linden argues it actually grounds the fantasy in a recognizable reality. The audience isn't discovering the gangster life; they are recognizing it.

The Gendered Fantasy

Perhaps the most provocative thread in the discussion is the question of gender. Van der Linden pauses to ask, "Is there some kind of element of a lore to some extent?" when considering why women in the film are so drawn to these men. He challenges the assumption that the film's appeal is purely masculine. "Whenever you have stories that are being told by men about men and then you have women in those stories who are very attracted to the men in that, it's still very much 'oh they're so powerful,'" he notes.

This observation cuts to the heart of the genre's emotional core. The power dynamic isn't just about money or violence; it's about the perception of status. The women in the film aren't just victims; they are participants in the fantasy of power. Van der Linden suggests that the film's enduring popularity might stem from this dual appeal. It offers a power fantasy for men who want to be the ones holding the gun, and a status fantasy for women who want to be the ones standing next to the man holding it.

"My question is how much of that is an accurate representation of why those women are drawn into those situations."

This line of questioning is where the commentary shines. It moves beyond the surface-level excitement of the heists and the shootouts to ask what the film says about human desire. The answer, as van der Linden implies, is that the gangster lifestyle is a performance of importance that transcends gender. The tragedy isn't just that the men die; it's that the women buy into a lie that promises power but delivers only danger.

The Scorsese Signature

The discussion also touches on how Goodfellas fits into the broader arc of Scorsese's career. Van der Linden points out that while the film is often criticized for glorifying crime, it sits within a larger body of work obsessed with redemption and sin. "Scorsese has always been a filmmaker that has this strange balance between showing the life of sin and showing the life of the sacred," he writes.

This context is vital. Without it, Goodfellas could be mistaken for a simple celebration of violence. But van der Linden reminds us that the film is also a study of spiritual emptiness. The high-octane pacing and the upbeat music mask a deeper, darker theme: the inability of these men to find true redemption. The film's energy is a distraction from the inevitable fall.

"There's a preoccupation a little bit with like redemption and religious themes to a degree but it's definitely much more implicit."

This implicit nature is what makes the film so enduring. It doesn't preach; it shows. The violence is sudden, the betrayals are casual, and the end is bleak. The film's structure mirrors the life of the gangster: a high that never lasts, a rush that eventually fades into nothing. Van der Linden's analysis of this structure helps explain why the film feels so different from its contemporaries. It's not just a crime story; it's a moral fable wrapped in a rock-and-roll package.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's commentary succeeds because it refuses to treat Goodfellas as a static classic, instead treating it as a living conversation about power, gender, and the stories we tell ourselves. The strongest part of the argument is the reframing of the film's appeal as a shared fantasy for both men and women, a nuance often overlooked in standard critiques. However, the piece occasionally struggles to fully unpack the "lore" aspect of the gangster myth, leaving the reader wanting more on how this specific cultural code operates. For the busy listener, this is a reminder that the best gangster movies aren't about the crime; they are about the seduction of the lie.
