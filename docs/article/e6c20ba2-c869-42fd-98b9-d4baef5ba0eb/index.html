Halfway through 2026, Jordan Schneider offers a sobering counter-narrative to the usual techno-optimism surrounding China's rise: the nation's most aggressive moves are often born of internal fragility and strategic miscalculation rather than confident dominance. This compilation of twenty insights strips away the veneer of invincibility, revealing a leadership that is purging its own military establishment and an AI sector grappling with the stark reality that hardware quantity cannot substitute for quality. For the busy observer trying to cut through the noise of geopolitical posturing, Schneider's work provides a rare, granular look at the cracks forming beneath the surface of a superpower in transition.

The Military Paradox: Purging Experience

Schneider opens with a startling observation about the internal dynamics of the Chinese military. He notes that President Xi Jinping has demonstrated a willingness to "purge trusted allies, not just rivals," citing the January removal of Zhang Youxia as a prime example. This is not merely a personnel shuffle; it signals a fundamental restructuring where the People's Liberation Army command structure now possesses less actual warfare experience than at any point in its history.

"Xi's willingness to totally clean house — renovate the military, strip the high command down to its studs — shows he feels pretty comfortable about the external environment and the cross-strait environment in particular."

Schneider attributes this boldness to a specific convergence of factors: the perceived lack of personal investment from the US administration regarding Taiwan, and political instability within Taiwan's own leadership following a failed recall campaign. He argues that Beijing sees a window of opportunity leading up to 2028, when a new opposition leadership in Taipei may offer favorable terms. This framing is compelling because it shifts the focus from inevitable conflict to calculated timing based on perceived Western disengagement. However, this analysis risks underestimating the volatility introduced by removing experienced commanders during a period of rapid technological change in warfare. The human cost of such internal purges extends beyond political maneuvering; it erodes institutional memory that could be vital if miscalculations occur.

"The PLA's command structure has never had less experience with actual warfare than it does right now."

Industrial Legacies and the Drone Pivot

Moving from military strategy to industrial capacity, Schneider draws a powerful parallel between Ukraine's wartime adaptation and the potential for legacy manufacturing to resurface in unexpected ways. He highlights how Ukraine's historical engineering talent, once dormant or diverted to software, was rapidly repurposed when traditional naval assets proved unusable.

"Civilian talent is portable, and having a historically strong manufacturing base can be an asset even if the capabilities have been dormant for decades."

The argument here is that while legacy systems may be in disrepair, the underlying human capital remains a strategic reserve. Schneider points out that Ukraine's shift to unmanned surface vessels (USVs) was not just a technological pivot but a necessity driven by the "complete shambles" of its traditional fleet. This insight challenges the notion that modern warfare requires entirely new industrial bases; instead, it suggests that the ability to retool existing talent is a decisive factor. Critics might argue that this view romanticizes improvisation in the face of overwhelming firepower, but the evidence from the ground supports the idea that adaptability often trumps static superiority.

The AI Illusion: Quantity vs. Quality

Perhaps the most critical section for investors and strategists alike is Schneider's dissection of China's artificial intelligence capabilities. Despite holding roughly one-eighth of global compute power, the narrative of imminent parity with US models is crumbling under scrutiny. Schneider cites a former tech lead from Qwen who estimates only a "20% chance" that a Chinese model will lead globally within three to five years.

"With AI chips, quantity is not a substitute for quality. Export controls are working."

The piece argues that networking limitations prevent China from simply stacking more inferior chips to match US performance. Schneider further complicates the picture by noting that "national champion" status has actually stifled innovation at companies like DeepSeek, causing talent drain and forcing reliance on outdated architectures. The data suggests a dollar buys significantly more computational power in the United States than in China, rendering the sheer volume of Chinese investment less effective than hoped.

"DeepSeek missed out on a golden period of market development inside China... Between V3 and V4, ByteDance's Doubao became China's most-downloaded chatbot."

This section effectively dismantles the fear that China is on an unstoppable trajectory in AI. The argument holds weight because it relies on hard metrics regarding chip efficiency and market dynamics rather than speculative geopolitical posturing. A counterargument worth considering is whether China's state-directed approach could eventually overcome these bottlenecks through sheer resource allocation, but current trends suggest the gap is widening, not closing.

Social Technologies and the Cost of Control

Schneider also explores the divergent paths of social control, contrasting Egypt's massive investment in a "revolution-proof" capital city with Taiwan's use of soft power to rebuild civic trust. The comparison highlights how different regimes attempt to solve the problem of legitimacy.

"Egypt spent ~US$58 billion building a revolution-proof capital city that no one wants to live in, while Taiwan rebuilt civic trust after decades of martial law with cute mascots."

This observation underscores a broader theme: authoritarian efficiency in construction does not guarantee social stability or human satisfaction. Schneider notes the irony that countries without entrenched rule of law can build cities for millions that remain empty, while democracies struggle to house existing populations due to regulatory hurdles. The divide is not merely between autocracy and democracy, but between systems that prioritize image over utility and those that are constrained by process.

"The actual dividing line, therefore, might not be China versus everybody else, but between countries with entrenched rule of law and weak political consolidation on one side, and countries without either constraint on the other."

This framing is particularly sharp because it moves beyond the binary of "good vs. bad" governance to analyze the specific mechanics of how different systems fail or succeed in meeting human needs. It suggests that the "algorithm for how civilizations grow or decay" may be rooted in these structural differences rather than ideological purity.

The Human Toll of Technological Disruption

Finally, Schneider addresses the societal anxiety driving China's AI adoption. Contrary to the image of a techno-optimistic society, he argues that Chinese citizens embrace AI out of fear and a "last bus" mentality, driven by high youth unemployment and job insecurity.

"The answer is not a narrative about AI, but about an earlier transformation also perceived as inevitable... It is a story about how Chinese society has learned, through repeated upheaval, what it believes to be the only permissible response to disruption."

This insight reframes the high adoption rates of AI in China not as enthusiasm for innovation, but as a desperate coping mechanism. The government's inability to cushion this displacement, due to collapsing local budgets, adds a layer of fragility to the system. Schneider points out that while courts have ruled against firing workers made obsolete by AI, the state lacks the resources to enforce these protections effectively.

"Accurately interpreting that response — which is often misleadingly called 'enthusiasm' — is essential to understanding that worried Americans watching China's AI frenzy might not be looking at a rival but into a mirror."

This is a profound observation: the fear of technological displacement is universal, but the political and economic contexts in which it plays out differ drastically. The human cost here is significant, as workers are forced to adapt to automation without a safety net, creating a volatile social environment that could destabilize even the most rigid regimes.

"China holds roughly one-eighth of global AI compute."

Bottom Line

Jordan Schneider's compilation succeeds in replacing geopolitical hype with hard-nosed analysis, revealing that China's strategic moves are often reactive and constrained by internal limitations rather than driven by overwhelming strength. The strongest part of the argument is the data-driven debunking of China's AI parity myth, which effectively counters the prevailing narrative of inevitable competition. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on current trends to predict future outcomes; it may underestimate the capacity for rapid, state-directed pivots that have historically surprised observers. The reader should watch for how the Chinese administration navigates the tension between its aggressive military posturing and its crumbling internal economic realities in the lead-up to 2028.
