AI and the Myth of the Machine

Various · Compact Magazine ·Mar 19, 2026 ·10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

While Silicon Valley obsesses over the "eye of the needle"—the narrow path between artificial general intelligence and human extinction—a more unsettling diagnosis lies just beneath the surface. This piece argues that the frantic debate between "doomers" and "boomers" is a distraction from a deeper cultural sickness: a secular faith that treats technological acceleration as an inherent moral good, regardless of its cost to human flourishing. It forces a necessary pause for busy readers who assume that faster, smarter machines automatically mean a better life.

The Theology of Efficiency

The author, writing under the collective byline "Various," begins by dismantling the prevailing narrative that AI progress is a neutral, rational extrapolation of trends. Instead, they frame the current discourse as a religious phenomenon. "The work of Lewis Mumford... offers an alternative perspective: that such assumptions are rooted in a secular faith that lies behind the modern embrace of technological development." This reframing is crucial because it shifts the conversation from technical safety to philosophical critique. The author notes that even those who fear extinction, the "doomers," often share the same underlying premise as the optimists: that the only question is whether we can survive the transition to a higher state of being.

The text draws a sharp parallel between modern tech theology and historical religious concepts. "The myth of the machine offers a secular equivalent [to theodicy]. It allows us to view beneficial outcomes as inherent to technological development itself, while understanding harms as incidental or as unintended byproducts." This is a devastatingly accurate observation of current policy debates. When negative outcomes like cognitive offloading in schools or worker displacement occur, the immediate reaction is rarely to question the technology itself. Instead, the burden is placed on humans to adapt. "Workers who fear being replaced by AI are told they are inadequately prepared for the evolution of the workplace." The author suggests this is not a bug in the system, but a feature of the myth: it protects the technology from moral scrutiny by blaming human incompetence.

The myth of the machine is dehumanizing because it subordinates human values to machine values: speed and efficiency.

This argument gains historical weight by referencing the "cultural preparation" that preceded the Industrial Revolution. The author points out that the drive for technological dominance didn't start with steam engines but with the "ordering of daily life that first appeared in the medieval monastery" and the "megamachines" of ancient Egypt. This connects to the broader Technocracy movement of the early 20th century, where figures like Thorstein Veblen argued that engineers, not politicians, should run society. The article suggests we are not breaking new ground but merely accelerating an old impulse: "it was only after humans had already come to view the world instrumentally that we could create a system of technological production geared towards its own acceleration."

The Transvaluation of Human Life

The piece then pivots to the tangible consequences of this worldview, arguing that we have undergone a "transvaluation" where efficiency has replaced human well-being as the supreme value. The author marshals a sobering list of social indicators to prove that technological abundance has not led to a better life. "The members of these societies are less likely to get married, are having fewer children, have fewer close friends, have weaker social ties, take part in fewer communal activities, and worship less." This data serves as a powerful counter-narrative to the idea that connectivity tools foster community. The author is careful to avoid simplistic causality, noting, "The point here is not that there is a clear causal link between social media or e-commerce platforms and these trends. It is rather that when we adopted these technologies, novelty and market success took priority over any consideration of whether they would promote older ideas of the good life."

Critics might argue that this view is overly nostalgic, ignoring the genuine liberation technology has brought to marginalized groups or the sheer convenience that improves daily life for millions. However, the author's point is not to reject tools but to challenge the automatic assumption that "if we just keep upgrading our tech, human life will keep getting better." The text highlights how this faith blinds us to the trade-offs. "That something saves time is seen as a good in itself, regardless of its effect on other measures of human flourishing." This creates a paradox where we use technology to save time, only to fill that saved time with more work, effectively becoming more machine-like.

The author warns that this mindset has made the "myth of the machine" so pervasive that even the skeptics are trapped within it. "Those branded as skeptics or doomers also still accept the premises of the myth of the machine." By focusing on "mitigating risks" to "realize the benefits," they implicitly concede that the benefits are inevitable and desirable. The author suggests this is a form of misanthropy, a belief that "humans... need machinic augmentation" because they are flawed. This echoes the Technocratic belief that human irrationality is the primary obstacle to progress, a view that has historically led to authoritarian solutions.

For those of us who have thrown off the myth of the machine, the next move is ours: for the gates of the technocratic prison will open automatically, despite their rusty ancient hinges, as soon as we choose to walk out.

The conclusion of the article is a call to abandon the race for a "promised land" of superintelligence. Instead, the author posits that "technological doom is not something that lies ahead of us. It has already arrived." This is a provocative claim, suggesting that the "gradual disempowerment" of human agency is the true catastrophe, not a future apocalypse. The argument implies that policy changes alone are insufficient because they operate within the same belief system that created the problem. "Background systems of belief... condition what we take for granted in our relationship to technology and delimit the possible in policy."

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this commentary is its ability to reframe the AI safety debate from a technical engineering problem to a philosophical crisis of values, effectively using Mumford's historical lens to expose the religious underpinnings of Silicon Valley's optimism. Its greatest vulnerability lies in its potential to sound like a Luddite rejection of progress, potentially alienating readers who see genuine utility in AI tools for accessibility and productivity. Readers should watch for how this critique of "machine values" influences the next generation of AI governance, specifically whether policymakers will begin to prioritize human flourishing metrics over raw efficiency gains.

AI and the Myth of the Machine

by Various · Compact Magazine · Read full article

Last April, 600 people gathered for a technology policy conference in downtown Washington, DC. The main speaker, former Google CEO Eric Schmidt, laid out what he called the “San Francisco consensus”: the view that “within three to five years, we’ll have what is called artificial general intelligence,” which will be able to extend its own capabilities without needing input from human beings. This development, according to the consensus, could bring considerable benefits, but also risks bringing about human extinction. The challenge of getting to one by avoiding the other, Schmidt explained, “is called the ‘eye of the needle’ problem. You need to get through this eye of the needle without killing yourself and killing everybody else, to get to this promised land of AI.” 

In this statement, Schmidt summed up a common way of understanding AI progress. We are developing more and more powerful AI systems that pose extreme risks, but also promise great benefits. The AI community is colloquially divided between “doomers” and “boomers,” but the two groups don’t differ on the promised land so much as in their confidence in our ability to make it through the eye of the needle. 

Regardless of where they fall on this spectrum, those who regard the AGI future as a potential “promised land” tend to argue that their predictions have an entirely rational basis. They are, they tell us, merely making logical extrapolations from observable trends. The work of Lewis Mumford, one of the twentieth century’s most caustic critics of technology, offers an alternative perspective: that such assumptions are rooted in a secular faith that lies behind the modern embrace of technological development. Mumford’s oeuvre covered a broad range, including urban design and literary criticism as well as the history and philosophy of technology. He laid out his case against uncritical belief in the benevolence of technology in a series of books and articles, notably Technics and Civilization (1934) and The Pentagon of Power (1970).

“If we just keep upgrading our tech, human life will keep getting better.”

Mumford’s work suggests that technologists’ use of religious language—as with Schmidt’s “promised land”—reveals their faith in the “myth of the machine,” which he calls the “ultimate religion of our seemingly rational age.” At the core of the myth of the machine, he argued, is the idea that the development of technology is inherently connected to the improvement of the human condition.
