Why Every 3nm Process Node is Different

Asianometry · Asianometry ·Sep 29, 2022 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Asianometry reveals a counterintuitive truth about the semiconductor industry's cutting edge: the era of a single, universal chip manufacturing process is dead. The author argues that what we casually call the "3nm node" is actually a fractured multiverse of variants, each requiring a bespoke marriage between chip design and factory physics. For busy executives and engineers, this is not just technical trivia; it is a strategic pivot point where the old rules of Moore's Law no longer apply, and the cost of getting the partnership wrong is catastrophic.

The End of Simple Scaling

The piece begins by dismantling the nostalgia for the "glory days" of Moore's Law, a period from 1980 to 2005 when chip design was largely independent of manufacturing constraints. Asianometry writes, "during the glory days of moore's law... people designed chips without much thought about how those designs might be fabricated." The author explains that for decades, shrinking a chip was as simple as scaling down both dimensions, a process that happened automatically like "water from a tap." This framing is effective because it establishes a stark contrast between the predictable past and the chaotic present, immediately justifying why the industry has become so complex.

However, the narrative correctly identifies 2005 as the turning point when fundamental physical limits, specifically in lithography, halted this easy progress. As Asianometry puts it, "the foundry started hitting fundamental limitations which would have long lasting effects on the entire industry." The author details how the industry was forced to introduce "resolution enhancement techniques" and "guard rails" to compensate for the inability of light to print features smaller than its own wavelength. This shift from simple scaling to complex rule-making is the first major fracture in the industry's unity.

Critics might note that the author slightly oversimplifies the timeline of lithography struggles, as challenges existed even before 2005, but the core thesis—that the separation between design and manufacturing has collapsed—remains robust. The introduction of Design for Manufacturability (DFM) rules at the 90nm node marked the beginning of a bureaucratic nightmare where design rules multiplied fourfold in just two generations.

The Rise of Co-Optimization

The commentary then pivots to the modern solution: Design Technology Co-Optimization, or DTCO. This is where the author's analysis shines, moving beyond the problem to explain the new operational reality. Asianometry writes, "roughly about the time of the 28 nanometer process note the foundries and the chip designers decided that a more tightly bound relationship was necessary." The author describes this new relationship not as a transaction, but as a negotiation, likening it to "haggling at a taiwan night market" where trade-offs are made across power, performance, area, and cost.

The explanation of how this works is particularly lucid. Instead of the foundry dictating rules, the two parties now jointly define the physical parameters of the transistor, such as the height and width of the "fin" in a FinFET structure. Asianometry notes, "the chip designer works directly with the foundries process engineers to end up with a process node capable of meeting the project's desired targets." This is a crucial distinction: the process is no longer a commodity but a custom product.

It truly takes a village to make a new leading edge process node.

The author illustrates this with a concrete example of scaling from the 14nm to 10nm node, where the final area reduction was a mathematical product of both design changes (reducing track heights) and manufacturing advances (triple patterning). This evidence holds up well, demonstrating that the "node" is no longer a single number but a composite of multiple engineering decisions. A counterargument worth considering is whether this level of customization creates too much friction for smaller players who cannot afford the deep collaboration required, potentially cementing the dominance of only the largest chipmakers.

The Future of System Integration

Looking forward, Asianometry suggests that DTCO may eventually evolve into System Technology Co-Optimization (STCO), which would integrate advanced packaging and chiplet architectures into the design loop. The author writes, "stco would extend the chiplet approach that amd has popularized... to help segregate certain parts that cannot shrink." This forward-looking perspective adds significant value, positioning the current 3nm fragmentation not as a dead end, but as a stepping stone toward a more modular future.

The piece concludes with a fascinating observation about the industry's structural evolution. Despite decades of academic discourse predicting a split between design and manufacturing, the leading edge has forced a reunion. Asianometry writes, "the two sides are working together again at the leading edge closer than ever before." This irony is the piece's strongest thematic element, highlighting how physical constraints have driven economic convergence.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's strongest argument is the definitive end of the "one-size-fits-all" process node, replaced by a complex, co-developed ecosystem where trust and data sharing are as valuable as silicon. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its brief treatment of the economic barriers this new model creates for smaller firms, which could accelerate industry consolidation. Readers should watch for how this co-optimization trend reshapes the competitive landscape between foundries like TSMC and their biggest customers, as the line between designer and manufacturer blurs further.
