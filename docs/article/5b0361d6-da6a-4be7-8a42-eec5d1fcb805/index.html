This piece from Works in Progress, drawing on Richard Rhodes' historical account, delivers a startling correction to the popular narrative of American technological supremacy: the United States, despite inventing the atomic age, was actually the third nation to generate commercial nuclear power. The author argues that this delay wasn't a failure of engineering, but a deliberate consequence of the Cold War's "balance of terror," where every gram of fissile material was hoarded for bombs rather than burned for electricity.

The War That Stole the Peace

The author establishes a stark timeline, noting that while the first man-made reactor went critical in a squash court in 1942, it took fifteen years for the U.S. to generate nuclear electricity in any quantity. "Fifteen years would pass before nuclear electricity was generated in any quantity in the United States. That is rapid development or surprising delay, depending upon one's perspective," the text observes. This framing is crucial; it forces the reader to abandon the assumption that scientific breakthroughs automatically translate to industrial application. The delay was structural, not accidental.

The piece highlights a chilling prioritization of weaponry over utility. The Tolman Committee, formed in 1944 to plan for the postwar world, explicitly dismissed commercial power. "'The development of fission piles solely for the production of power for ordinary commercial use,' it found, 'does not appear economically sound nor advisable from the point of view of preserving national resources,'" the author writes. Here, "national resources" was a euphemism for uranium needed for the bomb. The argument lands hard because it reveals that the hesitation to build power plants was a strategic choice to maintain a nuclear arsenal, not a lack of technical capability.

The 'national resources' that the Tolman Committee sought to preserve were the bomb materials that a mere power reactor would burn.

Critics might argue that the committee's economic assessment was actually sound given the nascent state of nuclear technology and the high cost of enrichment at the time. However, the author's evidence suggests the primary driver was the scarcity of fissile material for the arms race, a point that reframes the entire early history of the industry as a byproduct of military necessity.

The Totalitarian Monopoly

The coverage shifts to the legal architecture that cemented this military-first approach. The Atomic Energy Act of 1946 is described not as a regulatory framework, but as a mechanism of absolute state control. "That remarkable act made atomic energy in all its manifestations an absolute monopoly of the US government," the author notes. This was a radical departure from the American tradition of private enterprise, creating what one historian called "the most totalitarian governmental commission in the history of the country."

The text explains that this monopoly was born from a fear of both military control and public ownership. "All discoveries concerning atomic energy were to be considered 'born' secret... and the penalty for divulging atomic secrets was life imprisonment or death," Various writes. This legal stranglehold meant that private companies could not innovate or build reactors without government contracts. The author effectively uses this legal history to explain why the U.S. lagged behind Britain and the Soviet Union, whose state-directed models might have been more agile in this specific, highly controlled domain.

The narrative then introduces Admiral Hyman Rickover as the singular force who broke this deadlock. While the government was paralyzed by secrecy and the military was focused on weapons, Rickover pushed for a submarine reactor. "If any single person has contributed more than any other to the uneasy nuclear standoff between the United States and the Soviet Union that passes for nuclear truce, that person is the American engineer and naval officer Hyman Rickover," the text asserts. This is a bold claim, but the evidence of his relentless prodding of the Atomic Energy Commission supports it. Rickover didn't just build a reactor; he forced the bureaucracy to prioritize propulsion, which eventually paved the way for civilian power.

The Physics of Priority

The author also dives into the technical divergence that shaped the industry. While Britain and Canada pursued natural uranium reactors, the U.S. chose enriched uranium and light water. "The enriched uranium was far more efficient for chain reaction than natural uranium – making the reactor far more compact – and enriched uranium would be thereafter the material of choice for US reactors of every kind," the piece explains. This choice was driven by the need for compact reactors for submarines, a military requirement that dictated the civilian path.

The text contrasts the modest, almost primitive nature of Fermi's first "atomic pile" with the massive, heat-wasting reactors built at Hanford for plutonium production. "They wasted copious quantities of heat, but their temperature was inefficiently low," the author notes regarding the Hanford reactors. This technical detail underscores the central thesis: the early infrastructure was designed for bombs, not efficiency. The transition to power generation required a fundamental rethinking of the technology, a shift that only occurred when the military need for submarines aligned with the political desire for cheap electricity.

'There are overtones in this development,' wrote the physicist and statesman J Robert Oppenheimer in 1957, 'that have been absent in power developments in other respects not wholly beyond comparison, such as the diesel engine and the gas turbine: overtones of pride and terror, of mystery and hope.'

This quote serves as the emotional anchor of the piece. It captures the unique psychological weight of nuclear power, distinguishing it from other industrial revolutions. The author uses Oppenheimer's words to suggest that the "terror" of the bomb and the "hope" of clean energy were inextricably linked, a duality that continues to define the industry today.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its unflinching exposure of how the Cold War's security imperatives actively suppressed the commercial potential of nuclear energy for a decade. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a "great man" theory of history regarding Rickover, potentially underplaying the broader economic and political shifts that eventually forced the government's hand. Readers should watch for how this historical tension between military secrecy and commercial openness continues to shape the current debate over nuclear renaissance.

Excerpt from the original article on Works in Progress

Richard Rhodes writes on how Admiral Hyman Rickover built Shippingport, the first full-scale American civilian reactor.

From the beginning, it was clear – in this case the beginning was 2nd December 1942, the day the first man-made nuclear reactor was nudged to criticality in a squash court beneath the west stands of the University of Chicago’s Stagg Field and, incidentally, the first day of wartime gasoline rationing – that the fissioning atom radiated heat energy and that such energy might, in the fullness of time, be applied to make electricity for power. Fifteen years would pass before nuclear electricity was generated in any quantity in the United States. That is rapid development or surprising delay, depending upon one’s perspective, but the fact is that, despite its imposing technical lead in nuclear matters, the United States did not arrive first at the production of commercial nuclear power. Great Britain did. On a smaller scale, even the Soviet Union preceded us. The reasons are intriguing. How the United States contrived to get back into the nuclear power business is instructive. ‘There are overtones in this development’, wrote the physicist and statesman J Robert Oppenheimer in 1957, ‘that have been absent in power developments in other respects not wholly beyond comparison, such as the diesel engine and the gas turbine: overtones of pride and terror, of mystery and hope’. There are still such overtones today.

Atoms in war and peace

Enrico Fermi’s first ‘atomic pile’, literally a flattened spherical pile of graphite blocks plugged with cylinders of purified natural uranium, radiated heat equivalent to some two hundred watts of electricity, no more. The dark, dirty mechanism, its emergency quenching system three young men crouched on top of the last layer of graphite, up under the ceiling, balancing buckets of cadmium solution, was designed to prove that a reactor would work, and it barely did – so barely that it required no cooling system and no shielding. It was simplicity itself. The graphite served as a sort of physical catalyst; the uranium did the work.
