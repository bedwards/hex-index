Anchor Yourself in Truth & Reality: The Supreme Court 14 1/2 Months Late to the Birthright-Citizenship Party

Brad DeLong · DeLong's Grasping Reality ·Apr 1, 2026 · 14 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Brad DeLong does not merely analyze a legal dispute; he diagnoses a deliberate, fourteenth-month strategy to weaponize judicial delay against the Constitution itself. In a piece that reads less like a legal brief and more like an indictment of a "neofascist" court, DeLong argues that the Supreme Court's procrastination was not an accident of procedure but a calculated move to let facts on the ground harden before a ruling. For the busy listener, this is not just about immigration policy; it is about whether the highest court in the land is actively collaborating to dismantle a century of settled law to create a permanent underclass.

The Architecture of Delay

DeLong's central claim is that the Court's conservative bloc has been "slow-walking the most nakedly unconstitutional move of Trump's presidency" for over a year. He asserts that a standard, uncorrupt court would have immediately dismissed the challenge to the executive order, but instead, the justices "paused for five months" before issuing a partial stay. This delay, DeLong suggests, was tactical. By refusing to summarily affirm the nationwide injunction, the Court forced lower courts to narrow their rulings, creating a procedural mess that the Trump administration could exploit.

The author's most biting observation concerns the Court's decision to grant "cert-before-judgment." DeLong writes, "That keeps the written appellate record 'clean': no precedential First Circuit opinion fortifying Wong Kim Ark, birthright citizenship, and tying it tightly to modern practice." By skipping the appellate court, the Supreme Court avoided a robust, well-reasoned defense of the 1898 United States v. Wong Kim Ark precedent, which established that children born in the U.S. are citizens regardless of their parents' status. Instead, the Court handed Trump's legal team a "friendlier canvas" to paint over with district-court analysis that could be easily distinguished or buried.

The Court's choice to indulge this nonsense—rather than kill it on day one—is a real constitutional crisis.

This framing is powerful because it shifts the narrative from a debate over legal interpretation to one of institutional sabotage. DeLong argues that the Court's actions were designed to give Trump a "chance to roll the dice on overturning birthright citizenship before the midterm elections." While critics might argue that the Court simply followed its standard docket management protocols, DeLong's evidence of the specific timing—waiting until a class action could be certified but before a strong appellate opinion could solidify—suggests a coordinated effort to weaken the legal defense of citizenship.

The Economic Engine of Statelessness

Moving beyond the courtroom, DeLong connects the legal maneuvering to a broader economic strategy. He dismisses the idea that the push to end birthright citizenship is about "safeguarding 'native' jobs" or "public finances." Instead, he posits that the goal is to "build a serf class of people who need to be quiet and accept lowered pay or be harassed by ICE."

DeLong draws a sharp line between the rhetoric of sovereignty and the reality of labor markets. He notes that "decades of workplace immigration enforcement... show a consistent pattern: the state periodically descends on workplaces, selectively terrorizes unauthorized workers, and then leaves the underlying production structure intact." This creates a workforce that is "deportation-proof" not because they are safe, but because their fear makes them easier to exploit. He points to the 2006 Swift meatpacking raids as a historical parallel, where the industry simply rebuilt around "ever more vulnerable refugee labor."

As Brad DeLong puts it, "The net macroeconomic effect is not an enduring increase in wages or employment for citizens; it is a shift in bargaining power away from labor and toward employers." This argument is compelling because it strips away the moral panic often associated with immigration debates and replaces it with a cold, economic calculation. The "pseudo-originalist" arguments made by legal scholars like Randy Barnett and Ilan Wurman, which claim that illegal immigrants are not "subject to the jurisdiction" of the U.S. because they did not enter in "amity," are dismissed by DeLong as "frantically inventing... just-so stories."

The revisionist case rests less on new evidence than it does on Trump's claim to embody the nation and its desires.

DeLong highlights the hypocrisy of these scholars, noting that Wurman and Barnett had previously held the traditional view of the 14th Amendment before pivoting to support the executive order. He cites Jamelle Bouie's observation that the drafters of the 14th Amendment wrote the citizenship clause specifically to repudiate the Dred Scott decision, which had denied citizenship to Black people. The current legal challenge, DeLong argues, is a return to that same exclusionary logic, disguised as originalism.

The Court's Revealed Preferences

The most provocative section of DeLong's commentary is his speculation on why the Supreme Court delayed so long. He rejects the "generous interpretation" that the justices were trying to avoid a political explosion. Instead, he leans toward the "ungenerous interpretation": "they were deliberately letting the clock tick to assist Trump."

DeLong suggests that the Court has a history of "dragging things out so that Trump can create facts on the ground that alter realities." He points to the behavior of specific justices during oral arguments as evidence of their bias. He mocks Justice Alito's hypothetical questions about "Iranian sleeper agents" and Justice Thomas's suggestion that the 14th Amendment might only apply to freed slaves. DeLong writes, "Thomas will vote for Trump because Trump is a king who can legislate by executive order and the 14th Amendment applies only to ex-slaves."

He also notes the absurdity of the "allegiance" argument raised by the Trump administration, which claims that parents who enter illegally cannot owe allegiance to the U.S. DeLong counters this by pointing out that "captured slaves... had no 'allegiance' to the US, and arguably wanted to escape. But their childrens were citizens." This historical fact, he argues, completely undermines the legal theory being advanced by the administration.

These are crazy, corrupt people who have little fear for God or man.

While some might argue that DeLong's tone is too partisan to be taken seriously, his analysis of the Court's procedural choices offers a chilling alternative explanation for the delay. If the Court intended to uphold the law, the path of least resistance would have been to dismiss the case immediately. By choosing the path of maximum delay and procedural complexity, the Court has arguably signaled its intent to allow the executive branch to test the limits of its power.

Bottom Line

DeLong's strongest argument is the connection between judicial delay and the creation of a vulnerable labor class, a link that transforms a legal technicality into a profound economic and moral threat. His biggest vulnerability is his reliance on the "ungenerous interpretation" of the Court's motives, which, while supported by the timing of events, remains speculative. However, the piece serves as a vital warning: the battle for birthright citizenship is no longer just about text and history, but about whether the Supreme Court will allow the Constitution to be rewritten by executive decree and judicial inaction.

For fourteen months, the Supreme Court’s conservative bloc has slow-walked the most nakedly unconstitutional move of Trump’s presidency: his attempt to erase birthright citizenship by decree. We all know that an un-corrupt Supreme Court would have waived through the nationwide injunction of Trump’s “no birthright citizenship” January 20, 2025 executive order. That’s not what the Supreme Court did. It paused for five months. Then the corrupt neofascist six issued a partial stay, ordering lower courts to narrow their individual injunctions so that they provided relief only to “each plaintiff with standing”. The ACLU was ready: by July 10, 2025 there was a certiified class of “born and unborn babies who would be deprived of their citizenship” under the EO, and an injunction against its application to them issued by Judge LaPlante to apply nationwide. The Trump administration then asks for cert-before-judgment, and the majority bites. That keeps the written appellate record “clean”: no precedential First Circuit opinion fortifying Wong Kim Ark, birthright citizenship, and tying it tightly to modern practice. That gve the Trump administration the chance to get the Supreme Court to write on a friendlier canvas, with only district‑court analysis to distinguish or bury rather than a long, well-written 1st Circuit opinion. It also gave Trump the opportunity to roll the dice on overturning birthright citizenship before the midterm elections likely show strongly how unpopular Donald Trump now is…

To sum up: the corrupt neofascist majority on the Supreme Court declines to summarily bless the nationwide injunction against the EO when it could, spends a term remaking the law of remedies in Trump’s favor in CASA, and then, when the ACLU exploits the Kavanaugh-crated doctrinal gap with a class action, reaches down and pulls that case up before the only hostile appellate court in the chain can strengthen it. Meanwhile, Trump’s allies in the legal academy have been reversing their previous intellectual commitmenst and spent time frantically inventing “pseudo-originalist” just-so stories to justify making American-born children stateless. The Court’s choice to indulge this nonsense—rather than kill it on day one—is a real constitutional crisis.

And thus it is today, April Fools Day, that is Supreme Court birthright-citizenship Trump v. Barbara Supreme Court oral argument day.
