Weekend Update #175: The Week In Which Ukraine Stood Up To Help The USA And The USA Stood Up To Help Russia

Phillips P. O'Brien · Phillips P. O'Brien ·Mar 8, 2026 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Phillips P. O'Brien presents a jarring inversion of the current geopolitical narrative: while the United States government is depicted as smug and unprepared, Ukraine emerges as the strategic savior, simultaneously the US is inadvertently bolstering Russia's war machine. This is not a standard recap of battlefield movements but a sharp critique of American strategic rot, arguing that Washington's incompetence has forced it to beg for Ukrainian expertise in drone defense while its own actions in the Middle East have handed Moscow a massive economic and diplomatic windfall.

The Reversal of Roles

The core of O'Brien's argument rests on a stark contradiction he identifies in recent US foreign policy. He writes, "The USA was smug, domineering and did not understand what it was doing," leading to a situation where "the USA was left unprepared for its war and has had to ask for Ukrainian help for itself and its allies." This framing is effective because it strips away the usual rhetoric of American benevolence to reveal a dependency that the current administration is desperate to hide. The author suggests that the Trump administration's dismissal of Ukraine was not just political posturing but a symptom of a deeper failure to grasp modern warfare realities.

"The most incompetent military/industrial preparations for war In US history."

O'Brien supports this hyperbolic claim with specific evidence regarding the US failure to anticipate the threat of low-cost unmanned aerial vehicles. He notes that while the US military burned through expensive anti-air armaments against cheap Iranian drones, Ukraine had spent years perfecting a cost-effective interception strategy. The author points out that "the Ukrainians have developed a range of systems which costs starting as low as $1000" to destroy targets like the Shahed drone, which costs around $35,000. This cost-benefit ratio, he argues, highlights a critical gap in American readiness that the US is now forced to bridge by appealing to the very nation it has been insulting. Critics might argue that comparing a global superpower's entire defense industrial base to a specific tactical niche is an overreach, yet the specific failure to intercept cheap drones in a high-intensity conflict is a documented vulnerability that undermines the US's claim of military supremacy.

The Paradox of Aid

The commentary takes a darker turn when examining the US reaction to Russia's involvement in the Middle East. O'Brien describes a scenario where the US government actively defends Russian actions that endanger American lives. He quotes White House spokesperson Katherine Leavitt, who dismissed the threat of Russian collaboration with Iran by stating, "Whether or not this happened frankly it does not really matter because President Trump and the United States military are absolutely decimating the rogue Iranian terrorist regime." This quote is chilling in its casual disregard for the safety of US personnel, suggesting a prioritization of political narrative over strategic reality.

The author argues that this defense of Russia is not an isolated incident but part of a broader pattern where US actions inadvertently serve Moscow's interests. O'Brien writes, "The US is burning through its military stocks of anti-air equipment at a sobering rate," noting that the US has fired over 800 Patriot missiles in a short period, depleting stocks that would otherwise be available for Ukraine. Furthermore, the conflict has driven oil prices up, providing a "major and much needed boon to Russian revenues." The logic here is that by destabilizing the region, the US has created a perfect storm of economic and military benefit for its adversary. A counterargument worth considering is that the rise in oil prices is a global market reaction to supply disruption, not necessarily a direct result of US policy intent, but O'Brien's point about the effect on Russian coffers remains valid regardless of intent.

"This was the week in which Ukraine stood up to help the USA and the USA stood up to help Russia."

This sentence serves as the piece's thesis, encapsulating the absurdity of the current geopolitical moment. O'Brien uses it to underscore the irony that while Washington insults Kyiv, it is Kyiv's expertise that is saving American lives, and while Washington claims to oppose Russia, its actions are financially and strategically aiding the Kremlin. The author suggests that the Trump administration's "pathological" need to criticize Ukraine blinds them to the fact that they are now dependent on Ukrainian innovation.

Battlefield Realities

Beyond the geopolitical maneuvering, O'Brien touches on the ground war, challenging the narrative of Russian inevitability. He cites data from the Institute for the Study of War to show that "Ukraine has liberated more territory in the last few weeks than it lost." This data point is crucial because it counters the "inexorable march to victory" often portrayed in Western media. The author argues that Russian advances have been "historically tiny and achieved at great cost," suggesting that the Russian military is stuck in a "strategic quagmire."

He notes that "the silence in the press and analytical community means they might be right" about the effectiveness of Ukraine's strategy. This observation highlights a disconnect between the official narrative and the on-the-ground reality. By focusing on the net gain of territory and the high Russian casualty rates, O'Brien paints a picture of a war that is far from decided, despite the overwhelming resources Russia brings to bear. The author's reliance on specific territorial data adds weight to his argument, moving the discussion from abstract geopolitical posturing to concrete military outcomes.

Bottom Line

Phillips P. O'Brien's most compelling contribution is his unflinching exposure of the US administration's strategic incoherence, where incompetence in drone defense and reckless escalation in the Middle East have combined to aid Russia while forcing a reliance on Ukraine. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its attribution of intent, assuming that the benefits to Russia were a calculated "bonus" rather than a chaotic byproduct of poor planning, yet the evidence of US actions aligning with Russian interests is difficult to ignore. Readers should watch for whether the US can actually secure the drone interception technology it needs from Ukraine before its own stockpiles are critically depleted.
