Why smart people can’t agree on whether AI is a revolution or a toy

The Experience Gap

Alberto Romero's piece cuts through the noise of AI debate by refusing the easy answer that one side is simply wrong. Instead, he argues that enthusiasts and skeptics are both telling their truth—truths shaped by invisible differences in how they actually use the technology.

Why the Debate Breaks Down

Romero opens with a metaphor: imagine a friend hands you a magic wand that promises to finish your work, organize your life, predict the future. You try it. The result is mediocre—like a college student skimmed Wikipedia for your profession. You hand it back. "Fun toy, though." Your friend insists you're doing it wrong. The friendship strains.

Alberto Romero writes, "only the experience of the power users—those who use AI 10x or even 100x more than the rest—matches the extreme hype everyone witnessed." This is the core insight: the disagreement isn't about facts. It's about lived experience.

As Alberto Romero puts it, "Skeptics are not a different species, but the same species under a different life experience." The enthusiast who spends hours daily refining prompts, building workflows, integrating AI into their profession sees transformative value. The skeptic who tries it once on a vague task and gets generic output sees a defective wand. Both are rational. Both are responding to what the tool actually did for them.

Four Invisible Variables

Romero identifies four factors that determine your AI experience—and none of them are about the technology itself.

Your job matters. A lawyer using AI to draft briefs before applying twenty years of judgment gets enormous value. A lawyer testing it on subtle legal reasoning confirms it's overhyped. The same tool, different tasks, opposite conclusions.

Your disposition matters. Some hear "AI can do X" and think "let me try." Others think "prove it." The first group accumulates experience that makes the tool better for them. The second runs one test and walks away.

Your background matters. If you've spent time around software, stochastic failure doesn't sound like an insult. You rephrase, decompose, iterate. If you haven't—which is most people—the first failure may be the last attempt.

Your geography matters. In San Francisco, AI is ambient. Everyone uses it, talks about it, pressures you to try it. In Madrid, or most places, AI is headlines your friends haven't heard about. Being first is harder than being last.

Alberto Romero writes, "The experience you have with AI is not determined by AI...It's determined by: what you do, where you are, how you're wired, what you've tried, when you tried it."

The world is built on bridges, and undone by gaps.

What This Framework Doesn't Address

Critics might note that explaining disagreement doesn't resolve which position is more accurate. A framework for why skeptics exist doesn't tell us whether skepticism is warranted. The power user's experience might itself be selective—what if the benefits they see don't generalize to broader populations?

Romero also doesn't address whether the gap should be bridged at all. Some skepticism may be healthy. Not every technology deserves universal adoption. The piece assumes bridging is the goal, but doesn't argue why.

The Forum Problem

Here's where Romero gets sharp: public debate happens on shared forums—social media, X, comment threads—but private experience is wildly divergent. You can only give people arguments, which they evaluate through the lens of experience they already have. That experience confirms what they believe. The circles drift further apart.

As Alberto Romero puts it, "we share the forum where we debate our lives (social media, etc.), but the lives being debated are hardly shared. This gap between ones and others is widening extremely fast."

This explains why Derek Thompson's question—"What would change your mind about AI?"—has no clean answer. You don't change minds with arguments. Minds are products of life, not the other way around.

Bottom Line

Romero's framework is valuable because it treats both sides as honest. The AI debate isn't broken because people are dumb or dishonest. It's broken because the technology's value is contingent on invisible variables—job, disposition, background, geography—that shape experience before argument ever starts. The gap widens not from malice but from mismatched lives in a shared forum. Bridging it requires goodwill, communication, and recognition that both truths are real.
