I Visited the Busiest Crossing in the World

Dave Amos · City Beautiful ·Aug 23, 2024 · 15 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Dave Amos doesn't just visit the world's busiest intersection; he dismantles the myth that it earned that title through sheer traffic volume alone. By tracing the lineage of a loyal dog, a post-war Olympic village, and a private railway empire that treated transit as real estate, Amos reveals that Shibuya Crossing is less a natural phenomenon and more a carefully engineered cultural feedback loop.

The Myth of the "Busiest" Title

Amos begins with a necessary reality check, challenging the very premise of his own video topic. He notes that "no such organization exists" to certify the world's busiest intersection, and he is "pretty sure that nobody's done a study of all possible busiest intersections in the world." This skepticism is refreshing in an era of clickbait superlatives. Instead of accepting the label, he visits the site during a random Tuesday morning and finds it "surprisingly not that busy," observing that the crowds are "certainly not at Tokyo Drift levels." This grounding in empirical observation before diving into history prevents the piece from becoming a mere travelogue.

Critics might note that dismissing the "busiest" title ignores the statistical reality of peak-hour density, which is indeed unmatched. However, Amos's point stands: the label is as much about perception as it is about data.

"Shibuya Crossing is unique in that it's a participatory tourist attraction. It's not like a museum or a statue or some other static landmark."

The Architecture of Loyalty and Commerce

The core of Amos's argument lies in the symbiotic relationship between Tokyo's private rail operators and urban development. He explains that while Western rail companies often struggled, the Tokyu Corporation "continued to expand and diversify its business along its Rail lines," creating what researchers now call "Rail Integrated Communities." This isn't just transit-oriented development; it is a holistic ecosystem where housing, shopping, and work are inextricably linked to the train station.

Amos highlights a peculiar but pivotal factor: the story of Hachiko. He writes, "Hachiko waited for him every day at Shibuya station for the next 9 years, 9 months and 15 days." This narrative transformed a simple exit into a national symbol of loyalty. The author argues that this emotional anchor, combined with the station's role as a hub for youth culture, created a "recipe for a pretty busy pedestrian environment." The argument is compelling because it humanizes urban planning, showing how a dog's devotion helped elevate a transit hub into a global landmark.

"When a huge percentage of the largest urban area in the world takes trains because everything they need... are located near the stations, you're going to get a lot of riders."

The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy of Crowds

As the piece progresses, Amos identifies a fascinating paradox: the crossing is busy because it is famous. He describes the phenomenon as a "self-fulfilling prophecy at this point," where the reputation of the intersection drives the very crowds that validate the reputation. He notes that tourists visit specifically to "take part in the ritual by crossing the street," effectively turning themselves into the spectacle.

This observation is crucial for understanding modern urban tourism. The crossing isn't just a place people go; it is an event people perform. Amos admits, "I don't know how I feel about being a tourist attraction and visiting the tourist attraction at the same time," a moment of self-awareness that adds depth to his analysis. He suggests that the "influx of foreign tourists" is now a primary driver of the congestion, overshadowing the original commuter traffic that built the infrastructure.

"It's like a self-fulfilling prophecy at this point: it's the busiest, therefore people want to see it, which increases its crossing numbers, which means it's the busiest."

Bottom Line

Dave Amos's coverage succeeds by peeling back the layers of a global icon to reveal a complex web of private enterprise, historical accident, and cultural feedback loops. His strongest move is reframing the "busiest intersection" not as a traffic statistic, but as a participatory performance driven by the very people who watch it. The piece's only vulnerability is its reliance on the "self-fulfilling prophecy" theory to explain peak density, potentially underestimating the sheer efficiency of Tokyo's commuter rail network that still feeds the crossing long after the tourists leave.
