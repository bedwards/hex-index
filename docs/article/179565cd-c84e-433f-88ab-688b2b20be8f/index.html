Bill Clinton & the Day Physics Died

BobbyBroccoli · BobbyBroccoli ·Jun 3, 2022 · 38 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

BobbyBroccoli dismantles a persistent myth about the end of physics while simultaneously exposing how political pragmatism, rather than scientific merit, killed America's most ambitious particle accelerator. The piece is notable not just for debunking the Lord Kelvin urban legend, but for weaving a narrative where a single careless quote by a project director and a president's focus on trade wars conspire to bury the Superconducting Super Collider. This is a history lesson that reframes the collapse of the SSC not as a failure of science, but as a failure of political storytelling.

The Myth of the End

The author begins by correcting a historical record that has been repeated for over a century. BobbyBroccoli writes, "there is nothing new to be discovered in physics now all that remains is more and more precise measurement." The author immediately pivots to reveal the irony: "kelvin never said anything like this." Instead of predicting the end of physics, Kelvin identified two specific "clouds" that would eventually give rise to quantum mechanics and relativity. This framing is effective because it challenges the reader's assumption that great scientists ever truly believed they had solved the universe.

The commentary suggests that the belief in a "final theory" is a recurring human sentiment. As BobbyBroccoli puts it, "it's a recurring sentiment in human history where we feel close to the end close to figuring out the final mysteries of the universe only to tug at a thread that unravels a dozen new questions." The author uses Ernest Rutherford's discovery of the atomic nucleus to illustrate this, noting Rutherford's own shock at the results: "it was quite the most incredible event that has ever happened to me in my life." This historical context sets a tragic stage for the modern era, where the hubris of thinking we know enough leads to the abandonment of the very tools needed to find out more.

The Political Collision

The narrative shifts from historical myth to modern political reality with the rise of the Superconducting Super Collider (SSC). The author argues that the project's undoing began with a lapse in judgment by Roy Schwitters, the project director. BobbyBroccoli writes, "we should be devoting ourselves to completing the machine as rapidly and cheaply as possible and get on with real science instead our time and energy are being sapped by bureaucrats and politicians." This quote is the turning point; it paints the scientists as out of touch with the political reality of the 1990s. The author notes that Schwitters became the "cliche of the arrogant and pretentious physicist who felt entitled to billions of taxpayer dollars."

Critics might note that the author places too much blame on Schwitters' rhetoric, ignoring the genuine frustration scientists felt when their work was hampered by political interference. However, the piece effectively demonstrates how that rhetoric played into the hands of politicians looking for a reason to cut spending. The Clinton administration, focused on the economy and trade, viewed the SSC with indifference. BobbyBroccoli describes President Clinton's support as "the kind of half-hearted conditional support you give to projects that aren't really the focus of your political platform." The author captures the tone perfectly: "that is the voice of a man who could not care less about this project."

The ssc's fate was never in their hands but i'm sure it felt like it was.

The author contrasts the SSC's struggle with the International Space Station (ISS), which survived despite similar financial hemorrhaging. The key difference, as BobbyBroccoli points out, was international political fallout. "if the u.s gave up on the iss it would have meant a huge political fallout from several angry nations who were committed to funding it as well as nearly 75 000 american jobs disappearing." In contrast, the SSC was seen as a domestic luxury. The author highlights the absurdity of the situation where the SSC was fighting for survival while the ISS was saved by diplomatic necessity, not scientific superiority.

The Final Vote

The climax of the piece describes the House vote on June 23rd, where the SSC survived by a single vote. The author builds tension, noting that "both al gore and bill clinton had spent the previous night making desperate one-on-one phone calls." Yet, the author pulls a rug out from under the reader: "this wasn't the vote count for the ssc it was the vote count for the iss." This narrative twist serves to highlight the precariousness of big science projects. The author then reveals the grim reality for the SSC: "on june 24th the very next day when the ssc came up to a vote the result was exactly what [the reader feared]."

The commentary suggests that the SSC was a victim of a shifting political landscape where "business as usual would not play well in this new political era." The author notes that the new administration prioritized "renewable energy climate change and environmental research" and "anything with a short term economic payoff." This focus came "at the expense of basic physics research." The author's argument is that the SSC was not killed by a lack of scientific value, but by a lack of political utility in a post-Cold War world.

Bottom Line

BobbyBroccoli's strongest argument is the juxtaposition of the Kelvin myth with the SSC's reality: the belief that we have discovered everything is not just scientifically wrong, but politically dangerous. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its heavy reliance on the narrative of the "arrogant scientist," which may oversimplify the complex budgetary and geopolitical factors at play. Readers should watch for how this pattern of cutting basic science in favor of immediate economic or political gains repeats in current debates over funding for fusion energy and space exploration.
