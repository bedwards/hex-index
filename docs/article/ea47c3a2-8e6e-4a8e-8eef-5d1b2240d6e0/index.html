We Investigated The Shady Business Behind The UFC

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Mar 6, 2026 · 17 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

More Perfect Union doesn't just report on the UFC; it exposes a decades-long strategy of monopolization that has turned a brutal sport into a luxury product for the elite while leaving the athletes who risk their lives in financial ruin. The author's most startling claim is that Dana White's empire was built not on athletic merit, but on a deliberate legal architecture designed to suppress wages and eliminate competition, a structure now being exported to boxing. This is essential listening for anyone who thinks the recent $7.7 billion media deal is a sign of health rather than a symptom of a market with no competition.

The Architecture of a Monopoly

The piece argues that the UFC's dominance was never accidental. More Perfect Union writes, "In 2001, he connected with his childhood friends, casino mogul Frank and Lorenzo Fertitta... If we can sign enough athletes to an exclusive contract and keep sanctioning bodies out of the game, we can own the sport." This historical pivot is crucial. Unlike boxing, which fractured under the weight of multiple sanctioning bodies and the 1998 Muhammad Ali Boxing Reform Act that prohibited promoters from controlling rankings, the UFC engineered a closed loop. By banning rival promotions and locking fighters into exclusive, long-term contracts, they created a marketplace with no alternative for talent.

The author effectively illustrates the human cost of this strategy through the lens of fighter compensation. As More Perfect Union puts it, "Fighters were able to get all their own sponsors... But in 2015, the UFC signed an exclusive $70 million deal with Reebok and banned all personal sponsorships during fights." This move stripped the average fighter of their primary income source, a fact that becomes even more damning when paired with the revelation that fighters are classified as independent contractors. The author notes that this classification means "no comprehensive health insurance, no pension, no collective bargaining rights." This framing is powerful because it contrasts the league's $1.4 billion revenue with the reality that "fighters see roughly 17% of that," a stark disparity compared to the NFL's guaranteed 50%.

"You just got to look at the structure and realize it's designed to screw you and exploit the athlete."

Critics might argue that the UFC's model is simply efficient business, creating a single global brand that elevates the sport's profile. However, the evidence presented regarding the 4,000% rise in ticket prices and the exodus of elite talent like Francis Ngannou suggests the model prioritizes extraction over sustainability. The author highlights that when Ngannou requested basic protections like health insurance and the ability to negotiate with sponsors, Dana White's response was dismissive: "He's full of... Believe me, I have no sleepless nights over Francis leaving."

The Expansion to Boxing

The commentary shifts to the UFC's current ambition: absorbing boxing. More Perfect Union writes, "With backing from powerful allies in DC and Saudi Arabia, UFC President Dana White is coming for boxing." The author details White's formation of Zufa Boxing and his partnership with figures like Ary Emanuel, aiming to "rewrite the laws that govern the sport." This is a direct threat to the regulatory gains made by the Muhammad Ali Boxing Reform Act, which specifically banned the type of coercive, multi-fight contracts the UFC perfected. The author warns that White's goal is not to compete, but to dominate: "I don't want to say we'll be a viable competitor. We're going to blow them all out of the water is what we're going to do."

The piece draws a chilling parallel between the UFC's current trajectory and boxing's dark past, where promoters like Don King exploited fighters until the Ali Act intervened. By attempting to dismantle those protections, the UFC risks repeating the very abuses that led to boxing's decline in popularity. The author notes that Dana White's hostility toward critics is palpable, with White stating, "People have called us a monopoly and all this stuff for years. A monopoly. We're just the best." This deflection is presented not as confidence, but as a tactic to avoid the antitrust scrutiny that the 2014 class-action lawsuit, settled for $375 million, only temporarily paused.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union delivers a damning indictment of the UFC's business model, successfully arguing that its massive growth is built on the systematic exploitation of fighters and the elimination of market competition. The strongest part of the argument is the detailed breakdown of how exclusive contracts and the independent contractor status create a trap that keeps athletes impoverished despite the league's record-breaking revenue. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that regulatory intervention is the only solution, given the political entanglements with figures like Donald Trump and the influence of Saudi capital, which may make legal reform nearly impossible in the current climate.
