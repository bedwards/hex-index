Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill deliver a harrowing dispatch that refuses to let the world look away from the machinery of erasure currently operating in Sudan and Gaza. While mainstream headlines chase the spectacle of political maneuvering in Washington, this piece forces a confrontation with the cold, hard data of mass death, using satellite imagery to document what the administration prefers to keep in the shadows.

The Architecture of Erasure

The authors anchor their argument in a devastating visual reality: the systematic attempt to hide the scale of atrocities in El Fasher. Citing data from the Yale Humanitarian Lab, Grim and Scahill write, "Activity observed... between October 27 and November 28, 2025" reveals a pattern of burning and burying thousands of bodies to obscure mass killings. This is not merely a report on casualties; it is an indictment of a strategy designed to make genocide unprovable. The framing is effective because it shifts the burden of proof from the victims to the perpetrators, showing that the very act of hiding the evidence is now the primary evidence of the crime.

The commentary on Sudan draws a chilling parallel to the institutional dismantling seen elsewhere. Just as the executive branch has moved to sever the lifelines of the United Nations Relief and Works Agency (UNRWA) in the West Bank—advancing legislation to cut water and electricity to its properties—the same logic of erasure is applied to the displaced populations in Darfur. Grim and Scahill note that the Sudanese army is specifically "targeting non-Arab farming communities," a detail that underscores the ethnic dimensions of the violence often glossed over in diplomatic briefings.

The body of one Palestinian arrived at a hospital, while one Palestinian was injured... The total recorded death toll since October 7, 2023 is now 70,668 killed.

The sheer volume of these numbers, presented without sensationalism, creates a rhythm of horror that is difficult to ignore. The authors highlight that since the ceasefire began, Israel has killed at least 394 Palestinians, with 95% of them civilians. This statistic challenges the official narrative of a "calibrated" response, suggesting instead a continuation of the same lethal trajectory. Critics might argue that ceasefire violations are mutual, yet the data presented here—90% of shelters flooded, 17 buildings collapsed in a single winter storm—points to a catastrophic failure of protection for the most vulnerable.

The Expansion of Exclusion

Shifting focus to the United States, the piece exposes how the administration's foreign policy is inextricably linked to a domestic agenda of exclusion. Grim and Scahill detail the expansion of travel restrictions, noting that the new order "revives and widens the framework of his 'Muslim Ban,'" now targeting nationals from Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger, South Sudan, and Syria. This is not a security measure in the traditional sense; it is a geopolitical realignment that isolates the Global South.

The authors connect this to a broader pattern of resource extraction and control, particularly regarding Venezuela. They write, "Venezuela's vast oil reserves sit at the center of President Donald Trump's campaign against Nicolás Maduro," framing the military buildup not as a defense of democracy but as a bid for energy dominance. The claim that the U.S. is assembling the "largest Armada ever assembled in the history of South America" to blockade a sovereign nation is presented with a gravity that strips away the rhetoric of "drug interdiction."

This aggressive posture extends to the treatment of migrants within U.S. borders. The transfer of Cuban migrants to the Guantánamo Bay naval base is described as "repopulating the detention site for the first time in two months," a move that echoes the legal limbo of the post-9/11 era. Grim and Scahill point out that this occurred despite a federal judge ruling that the administration lacked the authority to detain migrants there, highlighting a disregard for judicial oversight.

"This is a policy choice by the Trump administration to make it worse for everybody," said Mike Pierce of Protect Borrowers.

The domestic fallout is equally stark. The termination of the student loan repayment program is framed not as a budgetary adjustment but as a deliberate policy choice to deepen financial strain on millions. The authors note that unemployment has risen to a four-year high, yet the administration's response is to cut support for borrowers and expand detention facilities. This juxtaposition reveals a governing philosophy that prioritizes enforcement over economic stability.

The Erosion of Accountability

Perhaps the most disturbing aspect of the coverage is the administration's refusal to be transparent about its own military actions. The authors highlight Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's decision to withhold video of a second strike on a Venezuelan boat, calling the footage "top-secret" despite mounting calls for its release. Grim and Scahill write that Democrats and legal experts have described the strike—carried out when survivors were clinging to an overturned boat—as a "potential war crime."

This opacity is mirrored in the domestic sphere, where the administration consults on the legality of a third term. The authors recount a conversation where billionaire donor Miriam Adelson told attorney Alan Dershowitz, "We can do it... think about it. I will give you another $250 million." This moment crystallizes the piece's central warning: the boundaries of constitutional governance are being tested not by abstract theory, but by the convergence of wealth, power, and ambition.

The coverage also touches on the global ripple effects of these policies, from the pressure on Pakistan to contribute troops to a Gaza "stabilization force" to the crackdown on protests in Australia following the Bondi Beach shooting. In each case, the authors show how the U.S. model of militarized response is being exported, often with disastrous consequences for local stability.

"Oh, hell no. It was a joke," Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez said, adding that it was "not a serious intelligence briefing" but "the communication of an opinion."

This quote from Ocasio-Cortez serves as a damning assessment of the administration's approach to oversight. It suggests that the checks and balances designed to prevent overreach are being reduced to performative briefings that offer no real accountability.

Bottom Line

Grim and Scahill have constructed a narrative that connects the dots between the erasure of bodies in Sudan, the expansion of the Muslim Ban, and the militarization of Venezuela, revealing a coherent strategy of dehumanization and control. The piece's greatest strength is its reliance on hard data and satellite imagery to bypass political spin, forcing the reader to confront the human cost of these policies. However, the sheer scale of the tragedy documented risks overwhelming the reader, potentially leading to a sense of fatalism rather than a call to action. The most critical takeaway is that the mechanisms of accountability are being systematically dismantled, leaving the world vulnerable to unchecked power.
