Sports Betting: What DraftKings & FanDuel Don’t Want You To Know

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Feb 27, 2025 · 26 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union exposes a disturbing inversion at the heart of modern sports betting: unlike almost every other industry, the business model of sportsbooks requires the customer to lose. While the public narrative is built on the promise of easy wins and "risk-free investments," the author argues that the digital explosion of betting has created a predatory ecosystem where the house doesn't just have an edge—it actively hunts for winners to ban. This isn't just about gambling addiction; it's a structural conflict of interest that has turned the nation's favorite pastime into a financial trap for the very fans who fuel it.

The Death of the Loss Leader

The piece begins by dismantling the nostalgia of the Las Vegas sportsbook, where betting was a minor amenity designed to keep gamblers in the casino. More Perfect Union writes, "Today a lot of the sports books that we bet with in America don't have casinos bankrolling them. That puts a lot of pressure on the sports book to make money through sports bets." This shift is critical because it changes the fundamental math of the industry. In the old days, a sportsbook could afford to let customers win occasionally because the real money was made on slot machines and table games. Now, publicly traded companies like DraftKings and FanDuel face shareholder pressure to generate profit directly from the wagers.

The author highlights the absurdity of this dynamic when a CEO admits to a "customer friendly stretch of NFL outcomes," which is corporate speak for "our customers won too much." More Perfect Union notes, "Unlike a lot of businesses in America, the interests of the business and the business's customers here are incredibly diametrically opposed." This is the core of the argument: in a standard market, a business wants you to be happy so you return. In sports betting, your happiness is their bankruptcy. The author effectively uses this contradiction to show why the "fairness" of the game is a myth.

"The relationship between the customer and the provider is a lot different than what the relationship between the better and the bookie used to be."

Critics might argue that the "vig" or commission has always existed and that winning consistently is simply a matter of skill, not a rigged system. However, the author counters this by pointing out that even skilled bettors are not allowed to play. More Perfect Union writes, "If you show any real ability to effectively handicap a game and beat the sports book, they will limit your bets and essentially kick you out of the sports book." This admission—that the industry actively punishes success—is a damning indictment of the current market structure.

The Same Game Parlay Trap

The commentary then pivots to the most dangerous innovation in the industry: the "same game parlay." These bets allow users to combine multiple outcomes from a single match, such as a team winning, a specific player scoring, and a total points line being hit. More Perfect Union describes this as the "credit default swap of sports betting," comparing the complexity to the financial instruments that caused the 2008 crash. The author explains that while a traditional bet might offer the house a 5 to 6% return, a single game parlay can return up to 30% to the sportsbook.

The allure is the massive payout for a small risk, but the math is deliberately opaque. More Perfect Union writes, "Hardly any of us can do it, so the sports book can then bake into those bets a very very big margin." The author argues that these products are designed to exploit the cognitive bias that a fan's knowledge of the sport translates to betting expertise. As the text puts it, "If you know about sports, you know about sports betting... which is why so many people lose money." This framing is particularly effective because it targets the specific demographic of sports fans who feel they have an edge, only to find they are playing a rigged game.

The marketing machine reinforces this delusion. The author notes that leagues and athletes have signed deals to promote these products, blurring the line between entertainment and gambling. More Perfect Union writes, "People are so upset at sports betting now they're being forced... to watch Charles Barkley tell them about his parlay... I think what is is risk-free investment." The irony is palpable: the very people who should be protecting the integrity of the sport are now its biggest salesmen.

The Regulatory Faustian Bargain

The piece concludes by examining how governments, desperate for revenue, have accelerated this trend. The author traces the history of gambling from colonial lotteries funding the American Revolution to modern state budgets relying on betting taxes. More Perfect Union writes, "They essentially went in and said pass this bill, we'll open up in your state and we'll give you all the money you need to do it." This creates a political incentive to expand betting regardless of the social cost.

The shift in professional sports leagues is perhaps the most striking element. Once vehemently opposed to gambling, leagues now demand a cut of the profits. More Perfect Union writes, "The sports leagues immediately got involved and got into bed with the sports books right away. There was no hesitation at all." This reversal suggests that the integrity of the game is now secondary to the revenue stream. The author argues that this infiltration has ruined the innocent enjoyment of sports for many fans.

"Fundamentally people should want sports books and betting on sports to be fair, and I don't know that it is fair right now."

A counterargument worth considering is that the availability of betting apps has actually increased transparency and reduced the black market. However, the author's evidence regarding the banning of winning customers and the complexity of parlays suggests that the "regulated" market is simply a more efficient version of the old underground bookie, but with a much wider net.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union delivers a compelling, if unsettling, case that the modern sports betting industry is structurally designed to fail its customers. The strongest part of the argument is the exposure of the "diametrically opposed" interests between the sportsbook and the bettor, a reality that marketing campaigns actively obscure. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its lack of specific data on the exact scale of losses for the average user, relying heavily on structural analysis rather than individual case studies. Readers should watch for how state legislatures respond to the inevitable wave of problem gambling as these "same game parlays" become even more ubiquitous.
