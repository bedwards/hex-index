How Poland Is Building the Biggest Army in Its History

Good Times Bad Times · Good Times Bad Times ·Mar 5, 2026 · 23 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original video →

Good Times Bad Times presents a startling proposition: Poland is not merely reacting to the war in Ukraine, but is actively engineering the largest conventional land army in European history, with a price tag that could consume half of its annual GDP by 2035. The author moves beyond standard geopolitical fear-mongering to dissect the granular reality of a military transformation that is already reshaping the continent's balance of power. This is not a story about potential; it is a detailed inventory of a nation betting its entire economic future on the assumption that deterrence by denial is no longer enough.

The Scale of Ambition

The core of the argument rests on the sheer velocity of Poland's expansion. Good Times Bad Times writes, "By 2035, the Polish armed forces aim to operate across all domains, land, air, sea, cyber, and space with the ability to strike deep behind enemy lines." This is a radical departure from the traditional Polish doctrine of simply holding the line. The author notes that the force structure is exploding from roughly 100,000 active personnel a few years ago to a planned 500,000, including a massive reserve component.

This ambition is anchored in a long-term technical modernization plan for 2025 to 2039 that, notably, "remains largely outside day-to-day political disputes." This political insulation is as remarkable as the military build-up itself, suggesting a national consensus that transcends the usual parliamentary squabbles. The author details how the backbone of this force consists of established divisions like the 11th Armoured Cavalry Division, which historically guarded the western flank, now being augmented by new formations like the "Legends Division" and the "Eighth Home Army Division." The goal is a structure where each division fields four brigades, and each brigade four battalions, creating a dense, layered defense.

"Traditionally, Poland relied on deterrence by denial... The new goal moves a step further. Deterrence by punishment, the capability to impose significant cost deep inside an adversaries territory."

Critics might argue that such a rapid expansion risks creating a hollow force, where the numbers look impressive on paper but lack the logistical depth to sustain a prolonged conflict. However, the author counters this by highlighting the specific focus on deep-strike capabilities, including a planned special-purpose division designed to combine mass drone operations with cruise and ballistic missiles capable of reaching over 1,000 kilometers. This shift from a defensive shield to a striking sword is the piece's most compelling strategic insight.

The Tank Paradox and the Infantry Gap

The coverage of Poland's armored forces reveals a fascinating paradox: the nation is simultaneously giving away its old fleet to Ukraine and building a new, world-class one for itself. Good Times Bad Times writes, "Most of the T72 and PT91 fleet around 400 vehicles was transferred to Ukraine just after refurbishment... It was a major contribution to Ukraine's war effort, but it also created an urgent capability gap at home."

The author meticulously tracks the replacement strategy, noting the arrival of 116 M1 A1 Abrams tanks and the impending delivery of 250 advanced M1 A2 SEP V3 variants, which are among the most capable tanks in the world. Simultaneously, Poland is integrating South Korea's K2 Black Panther tanks, with plans for local production of up to 800 units. This creates a hybrid fleet of American and South Korean engineering that is unprecedented in scale. The author points out that Poland already operates around 522 modern main battle tanks but aims to triple that number.

However, the narrative takes a sober turn when addressing mechanized infantry. The author highlights a stark disparity: while tanks are rolling off the line, the infantry fighting vehicle (IFV) situation is dire. "As one joke circulating among Polish soldiers puts it, the most common infantry fighting vehicle in the Polish army today is a Jel truck." This vivid detail underscores the reality that hundreds of aging BMP-1s were sent to Ukraine, leaving a gap that the new domestic Borsuk IFV program cannot fill quickly enough.

"The total number of armored vehicles lost as a result of support for Ukraine may have reached as many as 800 vehicles. That is equivalent of the equipment of roughly 14 mechanized battalions."

This section effectively illustrates the central tension of the modernization effort: the gap between ambitious plans and current reality. While the tank fleet is becoming a juggernaut, the infantry remains vulnerable, relying on wheeled APCs and upgrades to older platforms until the next generation of tracked vehicles comes online in the 2030s.

Artillery and the Long Arm of Reach

Perhaps the most transformative element described is the overhaul of Poland's missile and artillery forces. The author details a controversial but decisive shift toward South Korean systems to replace aging Soviet-era Dana and Gromad howitzers transferred to Ukraine. "Poland continued purchasing crap howitzers, but also ordered nearly 400 Korean K9s," the text notes, though the transcription likely meant "Crab" (the Polish domestic name for the K9 variant). The result is a fleet of nearly 600 modern self-propelled howitzers, integrated with the advanced Topas fire control system.

The modernization extends to rocket artillery as well. Under the Homar program, Poland is fielding both the American HIMARS and the South Korean K239 Chunma. These systems, capable of striking targets up to 500 kilometers away, are the physical manifestation of the "deterrence by punishment" doctrine. The author emphasizes that this is supported by massive stockpiles of ammunition and a growing domestic production capacity, with NATO allies expected to reach an annual output of 3 million 155mm shells by 2027.

"Poland has joined a European program aimed at developing a land-based equivalent of the Tomahawk designed for large scale production."

This move toward long-range precision strike capabilities, including potential cooperation with Ukraine on missile technologies, signals a profound doctrinal change. It suggests Poland is preparing not just to defend its soil, but to project power deep into the rear areas of any potential aggressor. While the timeline for some of these projects extends into the 2030s, the commitment to building a sovereign missile industry is a clear signal of long-term strategic independence.

Bottom Line

Good Times Bad Times delivers a masterful dissection of Poland's military metamorphosis, successfully arguing that the nation is building a force designed to punish rather than just deny. The piece's greatest strength lies in its granular inventory of equipment and its honest admission of the logistical gaps, particularly in infantry fighting vehicles. The biggest vulnerability in the argument is the reliance on a 2035 timeline; in a volatile security environment, the decade-long wait for full readiness could prove fatal if a conflict erupts sooner. Readers should watch closely to see if Poland can maintain this spending momentum without triggering an economic crisis, as the cost of this ambition is nothing short of half the nation's GDP.
