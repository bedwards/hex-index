Generative AI Comes for Music: Two Views from the Frontlines

Multiple sources: Kenny Easwaran Kenny Easwaranand 1 other
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

Two recent video essays approach the same unsettled terrain — generative AI's collision with music and the broader arts — from strikingly different vantage points. Kenny Easwaran, lecturing as part of an AI literacy course, takes the long view of a philosopher cataloguing the arts and asking where machine generation fits into a taxonomy that already strains to accommodate fiber arts, video games, and cooking. Benn Jordan, a working musician, takes the trench view: which streaming service actually pays artists, who is laundering AI-generated tracks onto Spotify, and whether a model can be trained to catch them.

Easwaran frames his discussion with characteristic academic humility. This lecture will focus on general background issues for generative AI for video and music based on what I'm aware of in August 2024, he tells viewers, which of course is only a fraction of what professionals in these fields are aware of right now, which is itself likely only a fraction of what exists on whatever date it is that you're watching this video in the future. The disclaimer is more than throat-clearing. It is an admission that the academic study of generative art is structurally lagging the technology, and that any taxonomy offered today is provisional.

The academic is still drawing the map. The musician is already being robbed on the territory.

Jordan has no such patience for taxonomy. His concern is material. The most common question that I've gotten in 2024 by far is what streaming service should I use if I want to support my favorite artists, he says, promising loads of hard data and an answer that not one of the viewers of this video will have guessed correctly. From there he pivots to what has clearly become an obsession: I've sussed out a way to catch and identify musicians and artists who download music off of AI music generator sites, claim it as their own, and then put it on Spotify and other streaming services to siphon royalties away from actual musicians writing their own music. He then escalates, describing an AI model that so far has been able to detect if a song has been generated by AI with 100% accuracy.

The contrast is instructive. Easwaran's instinct is to situate generative music within a centuries-old conversation about what counts as art — a conversation that, as he notes, philosophers like Barbara Gail Montero and C. Thi Nguyen have recently extended to cooking, games, and martial arts. Jordan's instinct is to build a classifier and start naming names. Both responses are legitimate, and arguably both are necessary. But they reveal a widening gap between how the academy and the working practitioner are metabolizing the same technological shock.

What Neither Source Quite Says

Both videos sidestep an awkward question: detection is a temporary advantage. Jordan's 100% accuracy figure is impressive, but it describes the current generation of generative models against the current generation of detectors. The history of adversarial machine learning suggests this equilibrium will not hold. Every published detector becomes training signal for the next generator. A royalty-enforcement regime built on classifier accuracy is building on sand.

Easwaran, for his part, does not press on the economic question at all in the excerpt available. Taxonomies of art are interesting, but the more urgent question for working musicians is not whether AI-generated music counts as art — it is whether the platforms that distribute it will continue paying humans once the marginal cost of a plausible track approaches zero. That is a question philosophers are well-equipped to address, and it is conspicuous when they don't.

Every published detector becomes training data for the next generator.

The Streaming Question

Jordan's tease about which streaming service pays artists the most is the kind of empirical claim the broader discourse desperately needs. Anecdote and grievance dominate the conversation; hard per-stream payout data, broken out by service and tier, is rarer than it should be. Whatever his answer turns out to be, the methodology matters more than the punchline — and it is the sort of work that academic researchers, with institutional access and peer review, ought to be doing in parallel rather than ceding entirely to YouTubers with spreadsheets.

Bottom Line

Easwaran and Jordan are looking at the same elephant from opposite ends. The philosopher is asking whether the elephant fits in the existing zoo. The musician is asking who is going to pay for the damage it is doing to the garden. Both questions deserve answers, but the asymmetry in urgency is real. Detection tools and taxonomic debates are useful, but neither addresses the structural problem: the economics of recorded music were already brittle before generative models arrived, and no classifier — however accurate today — substitutes for platform policy, licensing reform, and a clearer legal regime around training data. The most honest thing either source says is Easwaran's admission that whatever they know now is a fraction of what exists already. The rest of the conversation should proceed from that humility.
