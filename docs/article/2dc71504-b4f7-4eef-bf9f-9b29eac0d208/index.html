This video is sponsored by my Nebula original documentary, 17 pages, which is out right now. Back in March, I spoke at a government conference in Washington DC. And if that's surprising to you, well, it was surprising to me because for a 24-hour period, right before I was supposed to fly down, I didn't know if I was going to be allowed to give the talk I wanted to give. So, for some context, the conference I was going to is the annual summit for ARPA.

And ARPA is like the federal R&D wing for energy projects run by the US government. Now, normally the summit sees a few thousand attendees and there are some pretty big names that appear and give talks like the Secretary of Energy. That was a talk I got to see. So, that was pretty surreal.

But the talk I was invited to give was a lot more low-key. It was going to be to the student section which had an audience of around a hundred or so people. Now, on the one hand, I love to give talks. I like talking about science communication, everything related to that.

I've given talks at high schools, at universities. If you want to invite me for a talk somewhere, please feel free to reach out. but I've never been invited to give a talk at a US government conference like this. Like, this is a big deal.

It's a big opportunity, and I obviously want to take advantage of that. But on the other hand, there were a few things that gave me pause about accepting this offer. The first one, if you've seen the news, it's a little bit of a weird time for Canadians to be traveling to the US. secondly, I would have had to pay for my plane ticket and my hotel.

They were really only going to cover my entrance fee to the conference. And thirdly, and most importantly, I needed to choose something to actually talk about, like what topic. given the focus on energy at the conference, it felt natural to maybe give an a bridge summary of my Cold Fusion documentary.
