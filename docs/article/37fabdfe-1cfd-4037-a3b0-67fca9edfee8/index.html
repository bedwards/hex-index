The corollary: A q&a with gabriel aguirre about Venezuela

Todd Miller · ·Jan 8, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Todd Miller's interview with Gabriel Aguirre cuts through the fog of official justifications to expose a stark reality: the United States has returned to 19th-century imperial tactics under a 21st-century banner. While mainstream coverage focuses on the legal charges against Venezuelan leadership, this piece forces a confrontation with the human cost of a military operation that killed 125 people and targeted civilian infrastructure. It is a rare, unflinching look at how the "Trump Corollary" is not just rhetoric, but a lethal policy of resource seizure disguised as counter-narcotics enforcement.

The Resource Imperative

Miller frames the conversation by centering the geopolitical and economic drivers behind the aggression, rather than the personality of the actors involved. Aguirre, the Latin American organizer for World beyond War, immediately dismantles the idea that this is a sudden rupture in US-Venezuela relations. He points to a decades-long history of friction, noting that the relationship has been "marked by the contradictions of a country seeking to reaffirm its sovereignty and independence against the hegemonic pretensions of a military and imperialist power."

This context is crucial. The interview reminds readers that the US has long sought to control the Orinoco Oil Belt, a region holding some of the world's largest reserves. Aguirre explains that even after the 1976 nationalization of oil, US companies retained access through technical assistance, debunking the myth that recent administrations are the first to face resistance. The current escalation, he argues, is a direct response to the region's refusal to submit to US corporate dominance.

"The United States has a long history of lies that it used to justify previous military invasions... This time, the United States constructed several narratives simultaneously, aiming to observe which would be most convincing to the American people and the world."

The argument here is that the "narcotrafficking" narrative is a convenient smokescreen. Aguirre cites 15 United Nations reports indicating that Venezuela is free of illicit crop production and that 95 percent of regional drug trafficking moves through the Pacific, not the Caribbean. This evidence suggests the military deployment was never about stopping drugs, but about securing a strategic foothold. Critics might argue that intelligence on cartel ties is often classified and not fully public, but the sheer weight of the UN data Aguirre cites makes the official justification difficult to sustain.

The Human Cost of the Corollary

The piece shifts from geopolitical strategy to the grim reality on the ground, refusing to let the reader look away from the violence. The "Trump Corollary" is described not as a policy paper, but as a license for aggression that has resulted in the "illegal and unilateral bombing of the country and the kidnapping of a sitting president along with his wife." Miller highlights the specific toll: 125 dead, with residential areas, health centers, and schools in Caracas bearing the brunt of the strikes.

Aguirre draws a chilling parallel between this event and the expansion of the US border into the Western Hemisphere. The strategy to "enlist and expand" control over migration and security has effectively turned the Caribbean into a militarized zone. He describes the US foreign policy as behaving like a "Mafia," citing the theft of Venezuelan oil tankers and threats against Panama as evidence of a gangster-like approach to international relations.

"We are facing a power that behaves like the Mafia. Today, the foreign policy of the United States is a gangster policy."

This framing is aggressive, but it is grounded in the specific actions Aguirre lists: the establishment of new military bases, the deployment of troops, and the use of force to block agreements with China. The interview posits that the US is acting as a rogue state, detached from international law. While some may view this language as hyperbolic, the specific mention of the 20 bombings of boats and the targeting of non-military zones lends a terrifying credibility to the claim that the rules-based order is being dismantled.

A Call for Global Solidarity

The final section of the interview moves from analysis to a direct call to action. Aguirre emphasizes the resilience of the Venezuelan people, who continue to work and attend school despite the trauma of recent bombings. He argues that the attack has paradoxically fostered a deeper sense of Latin American unity against what he terms "barbarism, death, and fear."

Miller ensures the reader understands that the proposed response is not just diplomatic but requires mass mobilization. The demands are explicit: end hostilities, cut funding, free the detained leadership, and renounce the Monroe Doctrine entirely. Aguirre warns that the US has opened a "Pandora's box" that could lead to a new global confrontation, urging the American public to question whether they will "passively watch the unfolding of a new, endless war."

"The United States has opened a Pandora's box that it may not know how to close, and we cannot predict the near future."

The interview concludes by framing the struggle as a choice between the defense of sovereignty and a descent into chaos. It challenges the reader to consider the long-term consequences of a foreign policy that prioritizes resource control over human life.

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this piece is its refusal to accept the "narcotrafficking" narrative at face value, instead grounding the analysis in the historical struggle for control over Venezuela's oil reserves and the specific data contradicting the drug trafficking claims. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its absolute dismissal of any internal Venezuelan instability, focusing entirely on external aggression. Readers should watch for how the international community responds to the detention of the Venezuelan leadership, as this will test whether the "rules-based order" can withstand the executive branch's new, unilateral approach to the Western Hemisphere.

The corollary: A q&a with gabriel aguirre about Venezuela

by Todd Miller · · Read full article

In November the Trump administration released its National Security Strategy in which it announced the “Trump Corollary,” according to which the “United States will reassert and enforce the Monroe Doctrine to restore American preeminence in the Western Hemisphere.” This is a 21st-century version of 19th-century U.S. imperialism in which, according to the strategy paper, the U.S. will “enlist and expand” countries in the Western Hemisphere to “control migration, stop drug flows, and strengthen stability and security on land and sea.”

It didn’t take long for the world to see the new strategy in effect. On January 3, the United States attacked Venezuela and abducted its president, Nicolás Maduro, and his wife, Cilia Flores, who are now both facing drug-trafficking charges in the United States. Venezuelan Gabriel Aguirre, the Latin American organizer for World beyond War, joined The Border Chronicle to answer questions about the situation, its background, and key points that people might not know about Venezuela and its relationship with the United States (including those turbulent issues of natural resources and oil). He also discusses how he thinks the situation will proceed from here and what people should do about it.

What are some important points about Venezuela and the background to this attack that people in the United States might not know?.

I must begin by mentioning that Venezuela is a small country located on the South American continent, with a population of 30 million and unimaginable natural wealth. It has the world’s largest oil reserves, at 300 billion barrels; the seventh-largest gas reserves; the largest gold reserves in Latin America; and abundant mineral resources, water sources, and great biodiversity.

The country’s location gives it geostrategic importance as the gateway to South America. Its economic development has always been closely tied to its dependence on the U.S. economy, a dependence that intensified with the discovery of vast oil reserves. For many years, most U.S. companies had access to Venezuelan oil, which translated into enormous wealth for these corporations, but not for the economic development of Venezuela, and even less so for its people.

In 1976 the Venezuelan state decided to nationalize oil exploration, production, and commercialization. This law, enacted during the government of Carlos Andrés Pérez, still allowed for the participation of U.S. companies under technical assistance schemes, thus debunking the myth that it was only in recent years, during Nicolás Maduro’s presidency, that U.S. companies were excluded ...
