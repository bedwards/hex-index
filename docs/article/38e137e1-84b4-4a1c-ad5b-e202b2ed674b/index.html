Why Young Men Don’t Like The Democrats

More Perfect Union · More Perfect Union ·Jun 6, 2025 · 28 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

More Perfect Union cuts through the noise of the "manosphere" to reveal a painful truth: the Democratic Party isn't just losing young men; it has actively alienated them by dismissing their economic despair as mere cultural pathology. While pundits obsess over podcasters and testosterone, this piece grounds the crisis in the brutal reality of a working-class man who walks into a courtroom with blood on his arm, only to be laughed at by a judge. It is a rare, unvarnished look at why nearly half of young men have abandoned the left, not because they love Trump, but because they feel the system has no script for their lives anymore.

The Economic Script That Broke

The author, More Perfect Union, anchors the piece in hard data before diving into the human element, noting that while the top 20% of earners are flourishing, the trajectories for men outside that bracket have diverged sharply from women over the last fifty years. The commentary highlights a stark shift: women are working more and earning better, while men are working less, often for stagnant or declining real wages. This isn't just about gender; it's about the collapse of the traditional male provider role without a replacement.

"Men, on the other hand, are working less, often for worse pay. Real wage levels for many men are trending down," More Perfect Union writes, setting the stage for the interviews that follow. The piece argues that the loss of manufacturing jobs—a sector historically dominated by men—has left a vacuum that the modern economy has failed to fill. This framing is effective because it moves the conversation away from abstract culture wars and toward the tangible loss of opportunity that drives political radicalization.

Critics might note that the piece briefly glosses over how automation and globalization have also devastated working-class women, potentially oversimplifying the gendered nature of the decline. However, the author correctly identifies that the cultural expectation for men to be providers has not adjusted to this new economic reality.

"It's almost like they're being cast in a modern-day HBO drama with a screenplay from Leave It to Beaver."

This metaphor perfectly captures the dissonance young men feel: they are expected to act like 1950s fathers in an economy that no longer supports that role. The author interviews five young men who articulate this tension, with one noting that while he wouldn't mind if his future wife earned more, the social pressure to "pay on the first date" remains a rigid, unspoken rule. The disconnect between economic capability and social expectation creates a profound sense of inferiority.

The Failure of Institutions

The most harrowing section of the piece involves a young man named Darren, who recounts a domestic violence incident where he was the victim. His story illustrates a systemic failure that goes beyond politics; it is a failure of the legal system to recognize male vulnerability. More Perfect Union uses this anecdote to argue that liberals often dismiss male struggles as individual failings or symptoms of "toxic masculinity," while conservatives exploit these grievances to push for a return to traditional gender roles.

"Liberals refuse to accept that gender gaps can now run both ways, and they're too quick to label male problems as individual failings, symptoms of toxic masculinity," More Perfect Union writes. This is the piece's most provocative claim: that the left's refusal to acknowledge male suffering is driving men toward the right, not because they agree with the right's economics, but because the right at least acknowledges their pain.

The author captures the frustration of these men when one interviewee states, "The Democratic party... has abandoned men. You're telling men not to be men." This sentiment is powerful because it comes from a man who is not a conservative ideologue but a working-class individual who feels invisible. The piece suggests that the left's focus on identity politics has inadvertently created a blind spot for the specific struggles of working-class men.

The Class Divide vs. The Culture War

The interviews reveal a surprising consensus among the young men: the real divide in America is not between men and women, or between races, but between the working class and the elite. One participant, a union organizer, points out that the culture war is a distraction from the real exploiters. "Trans people are not hiking my credit card interest rates. They did not keep my wages stagnant. You're focusing on the wrong 1%," he argues.

More Perfect Union synthesizes this by noting that both sides of the political spectrum are failing these men. "The left turns its back on boys and men, and the right turns back the clock on women and girls," the author concludes. This dual critique is the piece's strongest asset, as it refuses to let either party off the hook. It suggests that the solution lies not in choosing a side in the culture war, but in rebuilding a class-based coalition that addresses the economic roots of the crisis.

Critics might argue that this class-based analysis risks downplaying the very real harms of misogyny and the need for continued gender equity reforms. The piece walks a fine line here, acknowledging that while men are struggling, the solution cannot be a regression to traditional gender roles. However, the author's reliance on the interviewees' own words to make this point lends it significant credibility.

"The divide in America really should be class. You know, work versus... the elite."

This quote encapsulates the central thesis: the political realignment of young men is a symptom of a broken economic contract, not a moral failing. The author effectively uses the voices of these young men to show that their turn toward the right is often a reaction to feeling unheard rather than an embrace of conservative ideology.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union delivers a vital corrective to the mainstream narrative on young men, proving that their political drift is rooted in economic despair and institutional neglect rather than mere cultural resentment. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to demonize these men, instead listening to their stories with empathy and connecting their personal struggles to broader structural failures. Its biggest vulnerability is the difficulty of translating this empathy into a political platform that can actually win these voters back without compromising on gender equity. The next step for progressives is clear: they must address the economic script that broke for these men before they can hope to rewrite the cultural one.
