Covid: Where Science Goes To Die

Rohin Francis · Medlife Crisis ·Aug 23, 2020 · 21 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Rohin Francis does not merely critique the noise of the pandemic; he indicts the very architects of scientific consensus for turning uncertainty into dogma. His most startling claim is not that politicians ignored science, but that scientists themselves have been the primary agents of confusion, publishing "80 to complete trash" studies that have been weaponized by the public and media alike. For busy listeners navigating a world of conflicting health advice, this piece offers a rare, unflinching look at how the scientific method collapsed under the pressure of a global crisis.

The Illusion of Certainty

Francis opens by dismantling the comforting but false narrative that governments simply "followed the science." He argues that this phrase implies science is a machine that produces clear answers, when in reality, it is a process defined by ambiguity. "The statement pisses me off because it implies that science is like deep thought a big computer that spits out solutions," Francis writes. This is a crucial distinction for any listener trying to understand why policy shifted so violently over the last year. The author suggests that when leaders claim to follow a monolithic "science," they are hiding the messy, iterative, and often contradictory nature of actual research.

The core of his argument rests on the explosion of pre-print servers, where papers are published without peer review. Francis notes that while the intention was to share data quickly, the result was a flood of unvetted information. "These were not read by anyone outside the research team before going online," he explains, yet millions of people treated them as gospel. This shift is dangerous because it bypasses the very mechanism—peer review—that ensures reliability. Critics might argue that in a pandemic, speed is more valuable than perfection, but Francis contends that the lack of quality control created a "lifeless carcass" of science that was beaten into a pulp by bad actors.

"I can pinpoint the exact moment that I realized science had been killed and over the last five months we've just been beating its lifeless carcass into a pulp."

The Anatomy of Bad Data

Francis moves from the general to the specific, dissecting several high-profile failures where statistical errors were mistaken for breakthroughs. He highlights the initial panic over ibuprofen, which stemmed from a single theoretical line in a paper with no data, and the bizarre claim that smoking protects against the virus. The latter, he explains, was a classic case of "collider bias," a statistical trap where correcting for certain variables creates a false correlation. "You might end up with stats that suggest smoking is beneficial due to erroneous correction," Francis writes. This is a vital lesson for the public: a headline claiming a correlation does not mean a causal relationship exists, yet the media and even some scientists failed to make this distinction.

He also tears into a study on proton pump inhibitors (acid reflux medication), pointing out that the data was so flawed it was essentially fiction. The study claimed to have tens of thousands of respondents, yet the demographics were wildly skewed: 70% were Latino, 63% earned over $200,000, and 73% were smokers. "None of this makes any sense until you realize that the researchers offered a financial incentive for filling in the forms," Francis observes. The fact that the lead author was also the journal's editor-in-chief adds a layer of institutional failure that goes beyond simple error. This section is particularly effective because it shows how easily a flawed study can influence public behavior if the underlying data isn't scrutinized.

The narrative takes a darker turn with the Santa Clara study by John Ioannidis, a former hero of the scientific community who Francis claims "drowned his reputation in hand sanitizer." The study suggested the virus was no worse than the flu, a claim that was eagerly adopted by politicians to justify reopening. Francis notes that the study suffered from "selection bias, erroneous assumptions, incorrect extrapolation and ignoring test inaccuracy." Despite being debunked, it remains a touchstone for those opposing lockdowns. This illustrates a recurring theme: once a convenient narrative is released, it is incredibly difficult to kill, regardless of the evidence.

The Collapse of Trust

Perhaps the most damaging section of the commentary focuses on the messaging around masks. Francis argues that the initial refusal to recommend masks, followed by a sudden reversal, destroyed public trust. "The retrospective explanation is that governments didn't want to create a run on PPE... but this never made any sense because you're telling people they don't work," he writes. The public, he suggests, saw through the logic gap immediately. By demanding randomized controlled trials for a low-risk intervention like mask-wearing, scientists missed the forest for the trees.

Francis posits that the solution was never about perfect evidence but about precaution. "It's okay to say we're not sure but let's err on the side of caution," he argues. The failure to communicate this nuance turned a simple public health measure into a partisan battleground. He contrasts this with Asian countries, where the memory of SARS made mask-wearing a normalized, non-political act. "If we had senior politicians and public figures wearing masks early on normalizing it maybe it wouldn't have become this insane partisan issue," he suggests. This is a poignant critique of how scientific hesitation can be interpreted as incompetence or deceit.

"The people who demand randomized controlled trials are wrong. The people who say that masks are categorically proven to work are wrong. It's okay to say we're not sure but let's err on the side of caution."

Bottom Line

Francis's most compelling contribution is his refusal to let scientists off the hook for the pandemic's misinformation crisis, shifting the blame from political actors to the researchers who failed to communicate uncertainty. His argument is strongest when exposing the statistical fallacies that drove public policy, but it occasionally risks painting the entire scientific community with too broad a brush, ignoring the many who worked tirelessly to correct errors in real-time. As we move forward, the lesson is clear: science is not a set of answers, but a process of questioning, and pretending otherwise is the most dangerous lie of all.
