Welcome back

In a media landscape obsessed with polished perfection and relentless productivity, Alison Roman offers a radical act of resistance: the admission that she is exhausted, raw, and done pretending. Her return to writing isn't marked by a grand strategic pivot or a curated list of gourmet triumphs, but by a stark, vulnerable confession about the physical and emotional reality of early motherhood. This is not a story about recipes; it is a story about the collision between the public persona of a food writer and the private, messy truth of a new parent.

The Cost of Authenticity

Roman begins by dismantling the expectation that a return to work requires a grand explanation. She writes, "I've spent too much time agonizing over how to 'come back' after an unexpectedly early, abrupt departure." Instead of offering a polished narrative, she strips away the veneer of the professional writer to reveal the human behind the byline. The core of her argument is that the pressure to perform normalcy is exhausting and ultimately counterproductive. She notes that while others share their postpartum journeys with inspiration, she feels the opposite: "Nothing has ever seemed more sacred, more worthy of protection."

This framing is effective because it rejects the commodification of personal struggle. Roman admits to a state of hyper-sensitivity, describing herself as "susceptible to bursting into tears because someone thought I was going to poison my baby with a cactus." This hyperbole serves a serious purpose: it illustrates the fragility of the postpartum mind. Critics might argue that such vulnerability alienates readers who tune in for escapism or culinary instruction, but Roman anticipates this. She acknowledges the risk of alienating those without children or those who find the phrase "milk-heavy" unsettling in a newsletter about dinner, yet she chooses authenticity over broad appeal.

I personally have never felt more raw or sensitive, more susceptible to bursting into tears because someone thought I was going to poison my baby with a cactus.

Redefining the Kitchen

The piece takes a sharp turn when Roman addresses the practical implications of her new life on her craft. She admits that her recent culinary output has been far from the elaborate dishes her audience expects. "When the job is to write recipes but 'recipes' mean broth out of a mug, toasted english muffins with too much butter... there's not much to write about." This is a crucial moment of honesty. She exposes the gap between the idealized version of food culture and the reality of survival cooking.

Roman describes her lifestyle as "very breast-focused, milk-heavy and infant sleep cycle-obsessed," a stark contrast to the leisurely, ingredient-focused narratives often found in food media. She details the mundane yet monumental tasks of her new existence: washing bottles, unboxing forgotten orders, and finding an "inner-glow" from a simple trip to the pharmacy. By elevating these moments, she challenges the notion that only high-effort cooking is worthy of documentation. The argument here is that the act of feeding oneself and a child, even with burnt pizza or mug broth, is a profound act of care.

She also touches on the psychological toll of trying to separate her personal life from her professional output. For most of her pregnancy, she attempted to "sideline that major plot-point," a strategy that left her feeling "unfulfilled as a writer and nervous that it was obvious I was faking." This admission resonates deeply in an era where personal branding often demands a seamless, curated existence. Roman's decision to stop faking it is a rejection of the performative nature of modern content creation.

The Return

Ultimately, Roman's return is framed not as a triumph of willpower, but as a surrender to her new reality. She writes, "Coming back from a little break with a renewed sense of 'I don't have the energy to care what anyone thinks,' seems like a good time to remind myself of that." This shift in perspective is the piece's emotional anchor. It suggests that the most authentic connection with an audience comes not from perfection, but from shared humanity.

She promises to continue writing, even if the content includes "milk-heavy" references or grammatical errors. "Tomorrow, the newsletter will be back with as much regularity as possible because I love writing it and honestly, I miss writing it." The underlying message is that the desire to connect and create persists, even when the conditions are far from ideal. She reframes the newsletter not as a product to be consumed, but as a way of "letting someone cook for you, a person with annoyingly particular taste, in your time of need."

It's about letting someone cook for you, a person with annoyingly particular taste, in your time of need.

Critics might note that this level of personal exposure is a double-edged sword; it builds deep loyalty but risks burnout if the writer feels compelled to constantly share their private struggles. However, Roman's tone suggests she is setting boundaries, not just opening a floodgate. She asks readers to forgive typos and keep them to themselves, a small but significant request for grace that mirrors the grace she is seeking for herself.

Bottom Line

Alison Roman's most powerful move is her refusal to separate the writer from the human, challenging the food media industry's obsession with curated perfection. While the piece risks alienating readers seeking only instruction, its raw honesty creates a more durable and meaningful connection with the audience. The strongest part of this argument is the realization that survival cooking is just as worthy of celebration as gourmet feasts, a perspective that feels urgently necessary in a culture of constant performance.

Hello and welcome to A Newsletter! If you’ve found your way over by some miracle but are not yet subscribed, here, let me help you with that:

Hello and welcome back (all of us) to this newsletter. I’ve spent too much time agonizing over how to “come back” after an unexpectedly early, abrupt departure. How much to share? How much to explain? The short of it: I had a baby, he was one month early, and though his entry into the world was dramatic and unexpected, we are healthy and he is devastatingly cute.

I’m impressed by people who feel inspired to share more during their postpartum time, but the whole experience has made me feel the opposite. Nothing has ever seemed more sacred, more worthy of protection. I personally have never felt more raw or sensitive, more susceptible to bursting into tears because someone thought I was going to poison my baby with a cactus (just kidding, you can’t get to me!). Simply put: I’m tired and sensitive (though these days, who among us, etc.).

That said, I want to keep writing, and the only thing I know, is to write about what I know. Of course, a commitment to authenticity is a blessing and a curse blah blah. When the job is to write recipes but “recipes” mean broth out of a mug, toasted english muffins with too much butter, pots of beans with miscellaneous old leftovers mixed in and crisping up your just-delivered pizza in a 450°F oven before eating it because no pizza delivery is ever crispy enough (good tip), there’s not much to write about. Though, if you want a recipe for broth out of a mug, I can help.

In addition to feeling a little, let’s call it… “unfocused” in the kitchen, my general lifestyle has recently been very breast-focused, milk-heavy and infant sleep cycle-obsessed. I spend a lot of time washing bottles and pump parts, unboxing packages full of things I forgot I ordered but swore I needed and staring at my baby’s perfect face then crying. I wear the same cardigan 6 days in a row (on the seventh day…we wash it). Taking a quick shower feels like a trip to the Wall Street Baths and going on a walk around the block to pick something up at CVS fills me with an inner-glow only previously achieved by an Italian vacation.
