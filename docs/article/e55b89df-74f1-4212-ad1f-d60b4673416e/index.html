Artist Resources We Love, Vol. 5

Various · Animation Obsessive ·Mar 15, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original article →

In an era where creative tools are often locked behind paywalls or corporate silos, this collection makes a radical claim: the most vital education for modern animators lies in rediscovering the past. The author argues that true innovation isn't about chasing the latest software update, but about mastering the timeless principles of movement, light, and public domain storytelling. This is not merely a list of links; it is a manifesto for artistic independence in a culture dominated by copyright hoarding.

The Anatomy of Movement and Style

The piece opens by spotlighting James Baxter, a figure whose technical prowess has defined a generation of animation. Various writes, "Baxter breaks down walks in three styles: realistic, quasi-realistic and cartoony." The author uses this to illustrate how a legend can democratize high-level craft, noting that "If you listen closely, you can hear the cheers of thousands of animation students from across the world as this video was posted." This observation holds weight because it highlights a shift in the industry: the gatekeepers of technique are no longer the only ones teaching it. By breaking down the mechanics of a simple walk, Baxter proves that "limbs open outward kind of like an umbrella," a metaphor that simplifies complex physics into actionable advice.

The commentary then pivots to the historical context of style, specifically the mid-century shift away from the Disney model. The author points to Amid Amidi's Cartoon Modern as the definitive guide to this era, where artists sought to "move beyond the slapstick routines and 'hurt gags' that had been the stock-in-trade of American animated filmmakers." This reframing is crucial; it suggests that the "modernist animation" of the 1940s and 50s wasn't just a stylistic choice, but a philosophical rebellion. The text notes that this movement "assimilating and adapting the principles of cubism, surrealism and expressionism" to convey contemporary ideas. This connects directly to the broader history of the "Black Square" movement in art, where the rejection of realistic representation opened the door for the abstract visual language seen in later works like Samurai Jack.

"Animation artists conceived a bold visual style that was derived from the modern arts, assimilating and adapting the principles of cubism, surrealism and expressionism."

Critics might argue that focusing on mid-century modernism ignores the rich diversity of global animation styles that existed simultaneously, particularly in Eastern Europe or Japan. However, the author's focus here is on the specific lineage that influenced Western indie animation, making the scope appropriate for the argument.

Reclaiming Academic Rigor

The article takes a surprising turn by defending 19th-century academic painting techniques, which are often dismissed as outdated or overly rigid. The author introduces Jake Taplin, a contemporary artist challenging the notion that "learning to draw mean[s] we have to suffer and even learn to dread drawing." Taplin's approach is refreshing because he treats these methods not as grueling tests of perfection, but as tools for understanding. The text highlights a critical distinction in how these techniques are taught today versus historically. As the author explains regarding the famous Bargue plates, "Students nowadays line up the drawing in scale, side by side with the plate, and spend dozens and sometimes even a hundred-plus hours copying it."

In contrast, the original curriculum at the École des Beaux-Arts operated under "rigorous time limits," where a massive figure drawing was completed in just 12 hours. The author notes that "if you spent too long drawing these plates, your work would become stiff and overly flat." This insight is vital for busy professionals who might feel intimidated by the idea of spending months on a single study. It suggests that the goal is speed and understanding, not just replication. This historical nuance echoes the spirit of Charles Bargue's own work, which was designed to train the eye to see structure quickly, a skill that remains relevant for animators needing to visualize form rapidly.

The piece also touches on J.M.W. Turner, whose sketchbooks at the Tate reveal a master who "paid more attention to light than to firm shapes." This serves as a reminder that technical training should ultimately serve creativity, not stifle it. As director Frédéric Back is quoted, "All too often, young people limit themselves to technical training without first (or at the same time) acquiring the kind of artistic education that opens their eyes and minds."

The Rebellion of the Public Domain

The final and perhaps most potent argument concerns the public domain. The author frames access to these resources not just as a convenience, but as an act of defiance against a culture "controlled by a handful of copyright-hoarding corporations." The text lists a staggering array of newly available works, from Nancy Drew to Philip K. Dick, noting that "some of the best animated films of all time — like The Tell-Tale Heart (1953) and the Soviet Winnie-the-Pooh (1969) — were adaptations of stories that are currently open to all."

This section argues that the public domain is the "ultimate resource for artists," allowing for "lateral thinking" to conjure new work from old materials. The author points out that while early versions of characters like Mickey Mouse are now free in America, the landscape is complex, requiring "care in some cases." Yet, the potential remains immense. By utilizing these free resources, artists can bypass the expensive licensing fees that often stifle independent creators. The text suggests that "taking a piece of the public domain for yourself is almost rebellious," a sentiment that resonates deeply in an industry where intellectual property is often weaponized to block new voices.

"At a time when so much of culture is controlled by a handful of copyright-hoarding corporations, taking a piece of the public domain for yourself is almost rebellious."

A counterargument worth considering is that the sheer volume of public domain material can be overwhelming, and navigating the legal nuances of international copyright can be a trap for the unwary. The author acknowledges this complexity but maintains that the risk is worth the reward of creative freedom.

Bottom Line

This commentary succeeds by reframing "resources" as a pathway to artistic autonomy, proving that the past holds the keys to future innovation. Its strongest element is the historical grounding of modern techniques, showing that today's "new" styles are often rediscovered wisdom. The biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that all artists have the time to dig through archives, a luxury not everyone possesses in a fast-paced industry. Readers should watch for how the legal landscape of the public domain evolves, as it will likely define the next wave of independent animation.
