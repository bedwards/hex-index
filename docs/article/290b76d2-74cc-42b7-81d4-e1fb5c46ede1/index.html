OceanGate Is Getting Majorly Sued

Devin Stone · LegalEagle ·Jul 15, 2023 · 26 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Devin Stone doesn't just recap the OceanGate tragedy; he dismantles the illusion that a signed piece of paper can absolve a company of reckless behavior. While the public fixates on the implosion, Stone zeroes in on the legal battlefield where the real drama is unfolding: the collision between a specific liability waiver and the concept of gross negligence. This isn't a standard wrongful death analysis; it is a forensic audit of a CEO who treated deep-sea exploration like a camping trip.

The MacGyver Submersible

Stone immediately strips away the corporate veneer surrounding the Titan. He notes that CEO Stockton Rush was "bullish on the submersible that he built to visit the Titanic," yet he "scoffed at the complex navigation systems used by other commercial Subs." Instead, Rush championed a design that was essentially a gamble, telling the world, "We only have one button that's it this is to other submersibles what the iPhone was to the Blackbird." The comparison is jarring because it highlights a fundamental misunderstanding of engineering safety. Rush wasn't innovating; he was cutting corners.

The author points out that Rush "patched together off-the-shelf items from Camping World and charged people 250,000 a piece to travel two miles under the sea." Stone's commentary here is sharp: he asks, "does that not raise anybody's eyebrows in the industry?" and answers with a resounding, "oh yeah." This framing is crucial because it establishes that the danger wasn't a hidden secret; it was an open secret that the passengers were willing to ignore, or perhaps, were misled into ignoring.

"The hallmark of gross negligence is that the defendant showed a total lack of care and disregard for the safety of others and here that is definitely a distinct possibility."

The Illusion of Consent

The core of Stone's argument rests on the legal distinction between ordinary negligence and gross negligence. He explains that while passengers signed waivers acknowledging the risks, including the fact that the vessel "has not been approved or certified by any regulatory body," these documents have limits. Stone clarifies that "in the U.S liability waivers only cover ordinary negligence if a company's act constitutes gross negligence or intentional harm the liability waiver just simply doesn't apply."

This is the pivot point of the entire case. Stone argues that for gross negligence to be proven, a defendant must be "more than careless that they were Reckless and engaged in behavior that might even appear to be deliberate." He marshals evidence that Rush was warned repeatedly. Deep sea exploration specialist Rob McCollum told Rush he was "mirroring that famous catch cry she is Unsinkable," to which Rush replied, "we have heard the baseless cries of you are going to kill someone way too often I take this as a serious personal insult." Stone uses this exchange to show that Rush's disregard for safety was not accidental; it was a choice.

Critics might argue that the passengers were adults who signed a contract, fully aware of the risks, and that the waiver should hold. However, Stone counters this by highlighting the disparity in information. The waiver stated the risks could not be eliminated, yet Rush was telling potential passengers the sub was "way safer than flying in a helicopter or even scuba diving or even crossing the street." This contradiction suggests the consent was not fully informed, undermining the fairness of the agreement.

A Trail of Red Flags

Stone builds a damning timeline of ignored warnings that transforms the narrative from a tragic accident to a preventable catastrophe. He details how the Titan was "severely damaged after it was struck by lightning in 2018," an event that forced a delay in testing. Then, sub expert Carl Stanley reported "cracking sounds in the hull" during a 2019 trip, warning that the carbon fiber might not withstand the pressure. Stone notes that Stanley wrote it "sounded like a flaw defect in one area being acted on by the tremendous pressures and being crushed slash damaged."

Perhaps the most chilling detail Stone uncovers is Rush's confidence in the window. Rush told a YouTuber that the acrylic window was safe because "before it cracks or fails it starts to crackle so you get a huge warning if it's going to fail." Stone dismantles this logic immediately, comparing it to "setting up a camera to warn you thunder is coming you will see the lightning before the Thunder but the time between them is minimal." If the window cracks, the implosion is instantaneous; there is no time to surface. This specific critique of Rush's engineering logic is where Stone's legal analysis meets hard physics, making the argument for gross negligence even stronger.

"If the window couldn't withstand the pressure and the people in the sub could hear the window cracking it's probably too late the entire submersible was at risk of imploding before it could surface."

The Legal Tightrope

The author then navigates the complex jurisdictional issues. The waiver mandated that disputes be governed by the laws of the Bahamas, a system based on English common law. Stone explains that courts generally uphold waivers for "risky recreational activities like skydiving or scuba diving" because they are not "public necessities or essential services." However, he introduces a critical counterpoint: the "take it or leave it" nature of the contract and the unequal bargaining power.

Stone draws a parallel to a Montana case involving a diving accident where a judge ruled that a waiver was unenforceable due to gross negligence, despite the signed release. He notes that in that case, the dive shop "pressed forward with the dive without doing a gear check" and used "unsuitable" equipment. By comparing OceanGate to this precedent, Stone suggests that even if the Bahamas law is strict, the sheer magnitude of the recklessness might override the waiver. He highlights that "courts in many jurisdictions tend to uphold liability waivers if they are specific," but also that "damage was the result of willful or wanton conduct" is a common exception.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's most compelling contribution is his ability to translate technical engineering failures into a clear legal narrative of gross negligence, proving that a waiver cannot protect a company that ignores the laws of physics. The argument's greatest strength lies in the specific evidence of ignored warnings, which transforms the tragedy from an act of God into an act of recklessness. However, the outcome remains uncertain as courts in different jurisdictions weigh the enforceability of the Bahamas choice-of-law clause against the public policy of holding companies accountable for willful misconduct. Readers should watch for how the courts define the threshold between "ordinary negligence" and "recklessness" in this unprecedented deep-sea context.
