Packy McCormick doesn't just track the news; he identifies the moment the future shifts from science fiction to industrial reality. In this 185th dose of optimism, he argues that the era of abstract software dominance is ending, replaced by a brutal, capital-intensive race to digitize the physical world. The evidence he brings isn't just about stock prices, but about a fundamental convergence of robotics, manufacturing, and biology that suggests we are entering a new industrial age.

The Return of the Physical World

The narrative begins with a dramatic comeback story. Travis Kalanick, the founder who was once ousted from Uber in a blaze of controversy, has spent eight years in what McCormick calls "the most extreme version of stealth mode any modern founder has pulled off." While the world watched the rise of humanoid robots, Kalanick was quietly building a "full-stack food infrastructure business" and buying real estate assets under the name City Storage Systems. McCormick notes that this was merely "a little amuse-bouche before the main course." The main course is Atoms, a robotics company explicitly designed to be anti-humanoid.

McCormick writes, "The recent humanoid Olympics in Beijing highlighted many advances in humanoid development... I watched the half-marathon and couldn't help but think how much better it would be if they just had wheels." This is a sharp, pragmatic critique of the current hype cycle. While competitors are obsessed with bipedal machines that struggle to walk, Kalanick is betting on specialized, wheeled automation for mining and food. It is a bet on utility over novelty. The connection to Anthony Levandowski, the Waymo founder who started Pronto AI (now part of Atoms), adds a layer of historical depth; it shows how the autonomous vehicle talent pool is migrating from roads to mines and factories.

"Uber was Kalanick's first attempt to digitize the physical world. Atoms seems to be the rest of it."

The argument holds up because it aligns with the hard constraints of physics. Humanoids are incredibly difficult to engineer for reliability in chaotic environments. By focusing on "a wheelbase for robots," Atoms sidesteps the balance and locomotion problems that plague humanoid startups. Critics might note that this approach limits the versatility of the robots to specific, structured environments, but in an industrial context, that specialization is often a feature, not a bug.

The Industrial Renaissance

The scale of ambition expands when McCormick turns to Jeff Bezos. The author describes a potential $100 billion fund aimed at buying existing manufacturing companies in chipmaking, defense, and aerospace. This isn't just about investing; it's about "AI rollup funds targeting accounting firms as new Jeff Bezos is to scrawny old Jeff Bezos." The plan involves using "Project Prometheus" to simulate and optimize the physical world, effectively applying the software playbook to hardware.

McCormick observes, "If you told me a couple of years ago, in the depths of the bear market, when everyone said that America couldn't build things anymore, that Jeff Bezos, Travis Kalanick, and Elon Musk were going to be competing to industrialize America harder... I would have told you... welcome to The American Millennium." This framing is powerful because it counters the prevailing narrative of American deindustrialization. The convergence of these three titans suggests a coordinated, albeit competitive, effort to rebuild the supply chain.

The article also highlights Mariana Minerals' "Copper One" project, an autonomy-first mine in Utah. McCormick praises their plan to "Deploy PlantOS at scale to maximize copper recovery" and integrate scrap processing. This is critical context because the entire robotics revolution depends on the raw materials to build the robots. The industry is structurally "not winner-take-all," meaning multiple companies can succeed by simply being efficient.

"We're going to need to find a lot of metals and minerals, we're going to need to get them out of the ground and refine them more efficiently, quickly, and cleanly."

This section is the most grounded in the article. While the Bezos fund is still in the rumor phase, the copper mine is a tangible, immediate example of autonomy being applied to resource extraction. The risk here is execution; scaling autonomous mining is fraught with regulatory and geological hurdles that software companies often underestimate.

Biology and the Mind

The final pivot moves from steel and silicon to biology and neuroscience. McCormick details a breakthrough from the University of California San Francisco where scientists created cancer-fighting immune cells directly inside the body. This bypasses the traditional, expensive CAR-T therapy process which involves extracting cells, editing them in a lab, and reinfusing them.

"The whole thing is designed so only T cells get edited, and only at a specific, safe genomic location, avoiding the random integration that can, in rare cases, cause secondary cancers," McCormick explains. The result in mice was a single injection that cleared leukemia in nearly all animals within two weeks. This could transform a $400,000, last-resort treatment into something "closer to a vaccine."

The commentary then shifts to the mind, referencing Shinzen Young's prediction that the "next Buddha" will be a collective of scientists and technologists. McCormick describes a study where transcranial focused ultrasound was used to decouple the brain's default mode network, a pattern that usually takes experienced meditators hundreds of hours to achieve.

"The ultrasound made the practice better and gave novices experienced meditator brains."

This is the most speculative yet compelling part of the piece. It suggests that technology can shortcut the years of discipline required for mental well-being. However, the counterpoint is significant: the study was small, a preprint, and conducted on mice or novices. The leap from "decoupled network patterns" to genuine, lasting human happiness is not yet proven. Critics might argue that relying on technology to solve internal human struggles could undermine the value of the struggle itself.

"I love this because I've written that the worst outcome would be for us to get all of the technological wonders we could have asked for and still be unhappy, and I think meditation, and the associated ability to pay attention, can help there."

Bottom Line

McCormick's strongest argument is the synthesis of these disparate threads into a single thesis: the next decade belongs to those who can apply software logic to physical constraints, from mining copper to editing genes. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its optimism bias; it assumes that capital and engineering will inevitably solve these complex problems without addressing the regulatory, ethical, and social friction that often stalls such breakthroughs. Readers should watch for the transition of these concepts from pilot programs to scalable, profitable realities.
