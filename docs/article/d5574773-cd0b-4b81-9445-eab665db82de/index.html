The Delayed Choice Quantum Eraser, Debunked

Sabine Hossenfelder · Sabine Hossenfelder ·Oct 30, 2021 · 10 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sabine Hossenfelder delivers a rare, necessary correction to a popular quantum myth: the idea that the delayed choice quantum eraser experiment proves we can rewrite the past. While many science communicators lean into the mystery, Hossenfelder strips away the drama to reveal a mundane statistical trick. She argues that the "weirdness" is an illusion created by how data is sorted, not by any backward-in-time causality.

The Captain and the Confusion

Hossenfelder begins not with physics, but with a riddle about a ship captain named Peter to illustrate how easily we get distracted by irrelevant details. "The quantum eraser is a puzzle just like this," she writes. "Everything I told you after this is completely unnecessary and just there to confuse you." This analogy is a masterstroke of clarity. By framing the complex experiment as a simple distraction, she prepares the reader to ignore the sensationalist narrative that usually accompanies it. The core of her argument is that the experiment's reputation for rewriting history relies on the audience forgetting the initial conditions.

She dismantles the standard explanation of the double-slit experiment, noting that popular videos often oversimplify the results. "Just because you know which slit the wave function goes through doesn't mean it stops being a wave function," Hossenfelder explains. "It's just no longer a wave function going through two slits; it's now a wave function going through only one slit." This distinction is crucial. She points out that the resulting pattern isn't two clean blobs as often depicted, but rather a "fuzzy" diffraction pattern. This technical correction matters because it sets the stage for understanding why the "erasure" doesn't actually change the past behavior of the particles.

The quantum eraser isn't remotely as weird as you think, doesn't actually erase anything, and certainly doesn't rewrite the past.

The Illusion of Erasure

The heart of the experiment involves entangled photons, where measuring one particle seems to dictate the behavior of its partner. Hossenfelder describes how researchers can choose to measure "which-way" information or erase it by combining paths. The popular conclusion is that this choice, made after the first particle hits the screen, retroactively determines whether the first particle behaved as a wave or a particle. Hossenfelder rejects this entirely. "Because of this which-way information, the photons on the screen can't create an interference pattern," she states. "Everything I told you after this is completely irrelevant."

Her argument hinges on the fact that the interference patterns only appear when you selectively filter the data. "What you actually do in the eraser experiment is that you sample the photon path in two groups and you do that in two different ways," she paraphrases the mechanism. When you look at the total distribution of all photons on the screen, the interference pattern is never there. It only emerges when you correlate the screen hits with specific detectors on the other side. "This means that the interference pattern really comes from selectively disregarding some of the particles," she notes. "This has nothing to do with quantum mechanics."

Critics might argue that the correlation itself, regardless of how the data is sorted, remains a profound mystery that challenges our understanding of locality. However, Hossenfelder's point stands: the retrocausality claim is a misinterpretation of post-selection. The pattern doesn't change; our ability to see a pattern within the noise changes based on how we group the data.

The Verdict on Weirdness

Hossenfelder acknowledges that quantum mechanics is still strange, but she insists the delay choice experiment is not the source of the strangeness. "The result must be correlated throughout space and fit to the measurement setting," she writes, attributing the true oddity to the standard collapse of the wave function rather than time travel. She even suggests that the "bomb experiment" is far weirder, pushing readers to look elsewhere for genuine paradoxes.

By citing Sean Carroll as one of the few others to explain this correctly, Hossenfelder positions her work as a correction to a widespread failure in science communication. "I actually think the bomb experiment is far weirder than the quantum eraser," she concludes, redirecting the audience's curiosity toward more substantive puzzles. Her approach is refreshing because it prioritizes mathematical rigor over clickbait narratives.

It seems you've managed to get rid of the which-way information by combining those paths and that restores the interference... but this is clearly nonsense.

Bottom Line

Hossenfelder's strongest asset is her refusal to treat quantum mechanics as magic; she demonstrates that the "rewriting of the past" is a statistical artifact of data sorting, not a physical phenomenon. Her argument's only vulnerability is that it may disappoint an audience seeking the thrill of time-bending paradoxes, but for those seeking accuracy, it is an essential correction to the record.
