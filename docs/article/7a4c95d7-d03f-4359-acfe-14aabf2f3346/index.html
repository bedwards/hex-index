How F1 Exploded

Sam Denby · Wendover Productions ·Feb 7, 2025 · 19 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Sam Denby doesn't just recount the history of Formula 1; he exposes a terrifying financial paradox where the sport's most inefficient, one-off street circuits are actually its most profitable engines. While most analysis focuses on the cars or the drivers, Denby zeroes in on the absurd economics of spending half a billion dollars to build a track that exists for only ninety minutes a year, arguing that this reckless spending is the very symptom of a sport that successfully reinvented itself from a niche engineering contest into a global entertainment juggernaut.

The Economics of Inefficiency

Denby opens with a staggering comparison to ground the reader in the sheer scale of F1's ambition. He notes that for the Las Vegas Grand Prix, "F1 spent more than a small nation's GDP to build the facilities for one annual 90-minute race." This isn't just a fun fact; it's the central thesis of the piece. The author details how the paddock alone cost $240 million for a tiny plot of land, with construction adding another $100 million, all while disrupting a city known for its congestion. The logic here is counterintuitive: in almost any other industry, building infrastructure that sits idle for 364 days a year would be a bankruptcy guarantee.

"Despite this terrible inefficiency, plenty of these races actually turn a profit for the circuits and promoters, not all but enough that F1 now has far more venues to host a race than they can reasonably accommodate in an annual calendar."

This observation is the piece's most compelling insight. Denby argues that the demand has outstripped the supply of race weekends, forcing the sport to schedule some events biennially just to cope. He contrasts this with the 2010s, a decade defined by decline where "viewership figures were shrinking year after year and by 2015 this summed to a third of its audience gone in just 7 years." The shift from a dying sport to one that can afford to burn cash on temporary street circuits in Monaco, Singapore, and Las Vegas is the result of a fundamental strategic pivot, not just better cars.

The Boredom Problem

To understand the solution, Denby forces us to confront the problem: the sport was objectively dull. He dissects the 2015 season, where Lewis Hamilton dominated so thoroughly that "Hamilton never left first place in the driver championship from the audience perspective there was no struggle no uncertainty it was just pure dominance." The author explains that the "formula" in Formula 1 refers to the open-ended engineering rules, which allowed wealthy teams like Mercedes to build cars so superior that driver skill became irrelevant. "The top three drivers combined led 97%" of the laps, turning races into processions.

"The explanation for why Formula 1 can be boring is right in the name it's the fault of the formula... the secretive development happening behind the scenes clearly does not translate into very exciting television."

Denby correctly identifies that the old leadership, specifically former CEO Bernie Ecclestone, doubled down on this elitism. He cites a damning interview where Ecclestone argued against social media engagement because "young people won't have the money required to interact with the sports sponsors like Rolex or UBS." This framing of the audience as a demographic to be ignored rather than cultivated was the fatal flaw that nearly killed the brand. Critics might note that Ecclestone's focus on high-value sponsors was a rational business choice for a niche product, but Denby effectively argues that it failed to account for the long-term erosion of the fanbase.

The Humanization Strategy

The turnaround began not with a change in the cars, but with a change in the narrative. When Liberty Media purchased the sport in 2016, they realized the solution was to "make people care less about who was going to win the race" and more about the people involved. Denby highlights the lifting of social media restrictions in 2017, which allowed teams to show "what he eats for breakfast and with whom how he stretches and prepares for the testing." This shift turned drivers from anonymous helmets into relatable characters.

"Fans it turned out wanted to know more about who was under the helmet and F1 obliged."

The author illustrates this with the rise of Daniel Ricciardo, who, despite his race results dipping, became "as famous as he is now as an ever available equally fun-loving and emotionally open athlete that the most casual F1 fan recognizes." The strategy extended to the broadcast itself, introducing live microphones to capture "strategic communication the high stakes high stress decision-making and the occasional argument over rules." By focusing on the human drama—the arguments, the fatigue, the personalities—the sport made the racing itself secondary to the story.

"The transformation was complete over 20 posts all of it content for the fan to consume without leaving the app."

This shift in content strategy was as radical as the financial spending on the tracks. Denby points out that the official F1 Twitter account went from three posts a day in 2015 to over twenty in 2025, with the content shifting from dry press releases to immersive storytelling. The argument holds up because the data supports it: viewership has exploded, and the sport is now expanding into new markets like the US, where the Vegas race is a massive success.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's analysis succeeds by reframing Formula 1's success not as a triumph of engineering, but as a masterclass in content marketing and brand humanization. The strongest part of the argument is the juxtaposition of the sport's financial recklessness with its strategic brilliance in understanding its audience. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the assumption that this humanization can permanently override the fundamental boredom of one-sided racing; if the cars become too dominant again, the personalities may not be enough to save the product. Readers should watch for whether the sport can maintain this delicate balance between technical competition and entertainment spectacle as it continues to expand globally.
