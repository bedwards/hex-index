The Job Market Doesn’t Care If You Don't Believe in AI

Alberto Romero · The Algorithmic Bridge ·Feb 19, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Alberto Romero cuts through the endless debate on whether artificial intelligence is a miracle or a mirage with a stark, career-defining truth: the labor market has already decided, and your personal skepticism is now a liability. While economists argue over productivity stats, Romero presents a chilling reality where job postings have shifted sevenfold in two years, creating a "binding" reality for workers regardless of technological truth.

The Numbers on the Wall

Romero's opening move is to dismiss the philosophical debate entirely, focusing instead on the brutal arithmetic of hiring. He writes, "The hiring manager's opinion is the one that pays your rent, and I think it's pretty clear what the average recruiter thinks when you look at job postings." This framing is effective because it strips away the comfort of intellectual superiority; you can be right about the technology being overhyped, but if you cannot speak the language of the hiring manager, you are unemployed.

The data he marshals is aggressive. He notes that "the number of workers in occupations where AI fluency is explicitly required has grown sevenfold in two years—from roughly 1 million in 2023 to around 7 million in 2025." This surge isn't limited to coders; it has bled into marketing, sales, and even chemistry. Romero argues that the premium for these skills is now "56%, up from 25% last year," a figure that suggests a massive arbitrage opportunity for those who adapt quickly.

"You can be right about it all and still be unemployable, because the labor market doesn't run on truth but on supply and demand."

Critics might argue that these job descriptions are merely "aspirational documents" or corporate theater, a point Romero acknowledges but ultimately dismisses as irrelevant to the individual job seeker. He correctly identifies that even if the requirements are performative, the filter remains real.

The Skeptic's Trap

The article's most compelling section addresses the "Skeptic's Counter," where Romero dismantles the argument that AI adoption is shallow. He cites the famous observation by Nobel laureate Robert Solow regarding the computer age: "You can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics." Romero uses this historical parallel to validate the skeptic's view that current AI usage is low and impact is unproven.

However, he pivots sharply to argue that this macroeconomic reality is a trap for the individual. He writes, "It doesn't matter that the postings are mostly fake... The answer is a resounding NO." His reasoning is that while the technology may not yet be delivering massive efficiency gains, the expectation of it has reshaped the hiring landscape. He points out that "45% of hiring managers in tech say employees with outdated skills could be laid off," turning the debate into a binary choice between adaptation and obsolescence.

This is where the argument gains its teeth. Romero suggests that the "enthusiast" who has spent two years tinkering with tools is more valuable than the "skeptic" who has correctly identified the flaws. He notes, "The skeptic... may be analytically right. But they cannot speak the language; they haven't built the muscle memory." The danger here is that the market rewards the narrative of innovation, not necessarily the reality of it.

"Your fate depends on someone who's already made up his mind."

The Minimum Viable Investment

Romero shifts from diagnosis to prescription, offering a pragmatic path forward that avoids the trap of needing to become a full-time engineer. He argues that the "minimum viable investment is smaller than you think." Instead of mastering complex architectures, he suggests candidates simply need to "describe, honestly and specifically, how you've used AI tools in your actual work."

He demystifies the jargon, explaining that "Experience with LLM-based workflows" often just means "you've used ChatGPT for something." This is a crucial distinction that lowers the barrier to entry. He also highlights a hidden advantage for the skeptic: "The person who understands AI's limitations... is, in a way, more valuable than the uncritical enthusiast." This aligns with the concept of Goodhart's law, where a measure (AI proficiency) becomes a target and is corrupted; the best workers are those who can navigate the tool without falling for its hype.

"The language matters more than the skill."

He warns that the window is closing, noting that the "56% wage premium" will compress as more people skill up. The urgency is real, but the solution is surprisingly accessible: one concrete example of a problem solved with AI, and one of where it failed.

Bottom Line

Romero's strongest contribution is his refusal to let the reader hide behind the "AI is overhyped" narrative; he forces a confrontation with the market's current reality where perception is the only currency that matters. His argument is vulnerable only if the current wave of AI adoption proves to be a complete bubble that bursts before skills become standardized, but given the $650 billion in infrastructure spending by tech giants, that risk appears low. The reader must act now, not because the technology is perfect, but because the job market has already decided it is essential.

I. THE NUMBERS ON THE WALL

The number of workers in occupations where AI fluency is explicitly required has grown sevenfold in two years—from roughly 1 million in 2023 to around 7 million in 2025, according to McKinsey. Job postings requiring AI skills surged 73% from 2023 to 2024 and then another 109% from 2024 to 2025. In IT specifically, 78% of job postings now mention AI-related skills, according to a Cisco report. AI-related job postings grew 7.5% last year, per PwC, even as total job postings fell 11.3%.

The overall job market is contracting while the AI-skilled slice of it is expanding. If you’re inside that slice, the odds are tilting in your favor. If you’re outside it… Well, you really should not be outside it.
