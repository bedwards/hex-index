Empires of Modernity

Then & Now · Then & Now ·Oct 29, 2020 · 21 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Then & Now delivers a chilling revelation: the British Empire wasn't just a political conquest, but the physical manifestation of a radical philosophical shift where human nature was redefined as an insatiable machine for maximizing pleasure and profit. By tracing the lineage from 17th-century swimmers to 18th-century merchants, the author argues that modernity's "improvement" was merely a euphemism for the systematic dismantling of a richer, more stable civilization in India. This is not a dry history lesson; it is an autopsy of the mindset that turned a joint-stock company into a sovereign power capable of starving millions.

The Calculus of Self-Interest

The piece opens with a jarring juxtaposition of a hymn about salvation and the cold reality of imperial expansion. Then & Now writes, "Modernity is many things: urbanization, industrialization, technologization. At its simplest though, it's a project of supposed improvement." The author immediately challenges this narrative, suggesting that the drive for "improvement" was actually a drive for expansion fueled by a new, selfish psychology. The text identifies a pivotal moment in 1658 when William Percy, in The Complete Swimmer, argued that the only true inducements for human action were "pleasure and profit." This was a departure from centuries of thought that valued honor, virtue, or the common good.

The author effectively uses this shift to explain the explosion of European exploration. As Then & Now puts it, "For Percy, even when it came to swimming, the motivations were simple: not honor, virtue, achievement, sacrifice, the common good, but pleasure and profit." This reframing is crucial because it strips away the romantic veneer of exploration. It suggests that the "modern" world was built on a fundamental reordering of human motivation toward the self. The argument gains weight when connecting Percy to Thomas Hobbes and Adam Smith, showing a clear intellectual arc where "interest" evolved from a neutral term to a justification for unlimited accumulation.

"Interest and interests have no limit; they can be pursued without hitting a ceiling."

This observation is the piece's intellectual anchor. It explains why the British could justify actions that would have been unthinkable in a virtue-based society. Critics might note that this view of human nature is somewhat reductive, ignoring the complex mix of religious zeal, national pride, and genuine curiosity that also drove the era. However, the author's focus on the dominant economic philosophy provides a necessary corrective to the idea that empire was solely about spreading civilization.

The Company as Sovereign

The narrative then pivots to the East India Company, describing it not as a business, but as a "ragtag group of merchants" who eventually became the rulers of a subcontinent. Then & Now highlights the sheer scale of the Mughal Empire at the time, noting that "India had a fifth of the world's population and was producing a quarter of the globe's manufacturing output," while England was producing just three percent. The contrast is stark: a wealthy, stable, and sophisticated civilization versus a hungry, emerging power.

The author details how the Company exploited the internal fractures of the Mughal Empire, using superior weaponry and the chaos of succession disputes to seize power. The character of Robert Clive is presented as the embodiment of the new modern man: violent, self-interested, and devoid of cultural empathy. "Clive despised India," the text states, "he had no interest in the culture, in the languages, in the religion, or the philosophy." This personal disdain mirrors the institutional attitude of the Company. The victory at the Battle of Plassey in 1757 is described as a watershed moment: "It was the first time in world history that a stock company had declared war on a prince."

The financial machinery behind this conquest is laid bare. The author explains that the Bank of England and the stock exchange were not just economic tools but the "governor of the imperial engine." As the economist J.A. Hobson is quoted, "Finance is the governor of the imperial engine." This connection between domestic financial stability and overseas plunder is a sophisticated point often missed in standard history. The British government could borrow vast sums to protect merchants because the profits from those merchants were guaranteed by the Company's monopoly. The system was self-reinforcing: debt funded the navy, which secured the trade, which paid the debt.

The Human Cost of Maximization

The most devastating section of the commentary details the aftermath of this "maximization." Once in power, the Company did not build; it looted. Then & Now writes, "The company was granted the rights to tax the 20 million people of Bengal... and now India's richest province would be run from an office in London." The result was catastrophic. The author cites contemporary observers who described the moral decay of the British presence: "The morals of this nation otherwise so worthy of respect have here become prodigiously depraved."

The text does not shy away from the brutality. It recounts how British soldiers and traders "beat to death unfortunate Indians to extract money not owed to them." The author uses the words of Mir Kasim, a local leader, to illustrate the extraction: "The English forcibly take away the goods and commodities of the merchants for a fourth part of their value." This was not trade; it was theft disguised as commerce. The ultimate consequence of this philosophy was the Great Bengal Famine of 1770. While drought was the trigger, the author argues that the Company's refusal to provide relief and its continued demand for taxes turned a disaster into a holocaust.

"Dogs, jackals, and vultures and every bird and beast of prey grew fat and unwieldy on the flesh of man."

This quote is the emotional core of the piece. It forces the reader to confront the reality of a system where human life was a variable in a profit equation. The author notes that while traditional rulers had grain reserves and relief measures, the Company officials "cared little and continued to enforce tax collection." One million two hundred thousand people died. The author's argument here is that the famine was not an accident of nature, but a direct result of the "modern" philosophy of maximizing utility and profit at the expense of all else. A counterargument worth considering is that the British were also victims of the same logistical limitations and information gaps that plagued other empires, but the author's evidence of active tax collection during a famine undermines the idea of mere incompetence.

Bottom Line

Then & Now succeeds in exposing the dark underbelly of the "modern" project, proving that the pursuit of unlimited profit was the engine of imperial catastrophe. The strongest part of the argument is the seamless connection between abstract philosophical shifts in Europe and the concrete, bloody reality in Bengal. The biggest vulnerability is the slight tendency to treat "modernity" as a monolith, potentially overlooking the internal dissent and moral conflicts that did exist within the empire. Readers should watch for how this framework applies to modern corporate power, where the same logic of unlimited growth often overrides human and environmental costs.
