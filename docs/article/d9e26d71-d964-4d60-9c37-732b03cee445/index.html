Live IN Taiwan 4: Election Coverage & Domino's Boba Pizza

Chris Chappell · China Uncensored ·Jan 14, 2020 · 38 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Chris Chappell and Shelley Jeong do not merely report on the 2020 Taiwan election; they weaponize culinary absurdity to illustrate the depth of cultural identity at stake. By serving a Domino's "Boba Pizza"—a grotesque fusion of tapioca pearls, brown sugar syrup, and cheese—during a live broadcast about a high-stakes democratic vote, the authors argue that the election was less about policy and more about a fundamental rejection of Chinese Communist Party influence. This is not a standard political recap; it is a sensory experience where the overwhelming sweetness of the pizza mirrors the overwhelming, and often suffocating, pressure of Beijing's disinformation campaigns.

The Apology Tour and the Sugar Rush

The segment opens with a self-deprecating admission of previous technical failures, but Chappell quickly pivots to the pizza as a metaphorical "apology tour" for their last election coverage. "I thought you know this could be our apology tour the quality of our last election live stream," Chappell explains, framing the culinary disaster as a penance for bad internet. The visual of the hosts struggling to eat a pizza that smells like "sweet boba tea" but tastes like a "salty pizza" sets the tone for the entire analysis: the situation in Taiwan is a confusing, jarring mix of the familiar and the alien.

The hosts describe the pizza as a "perfectly good cheese pizza" that has been smothered in syrup and tapioca balls, noting that the sweetness is "so overpowering" it mitigates the cheese. This description serves as a clever, if bizarre, analogy for the political landscape. Just as the sugar overwhelms the savory elements of the pizza, Chappell suggests that the sheer volume of Chinese influence campaigns threatens to overwhelm the authentic political discourse of the island. The absurdity of the food choice underscores the absurdity of the geopolitical pressure Taiwan faces.

"If they can take the KMT party the want the party that fought a civil war against China and got kicked out of the country if they can turn those guys into CCP supporters that just shows you how well the Communist Party is able to subvert an institution from the inside."

This moment is the intellectual anchor of the broadcast. Chappell moves past the food to address the core political shift: the transformation of the Kuomintang (KMT), the party that historically fought the Chinese Civil War, into a vehicle for closer ties with the People's Republic of China. He argues that this historical reversal is the ultimate proof of the CCP's effectiveness in subverting institutions. Critics might note that the KMT's shift is also driven by pragmatic economic concerns and the fear of conflict, not solely by subversion, but Chappell's framing highlights the psychological impact of Beijing's long-term strategy.

The Sunflower Movement and the Rejection of "One Blood"

The commentary then delves into the historical context of the "Sunflower Movement," where students occupied the legislature to protest a trade agreement with China. Chappell contrasts the rhetoric of former President Ma Ying-jeou, who claimed "blood is thicker than water" and that "we're all one," with the modern Taiwanese rejection of this narrative. The hosts point out that the 74% voter turnout was a "super huge" demonstration of this rejection, signaling that the population no longer accepts the premise of shared blood as a justification for political unification.

The hosts describe the election results as a blow to the idea of closer ties, noting that the incumbent, Tsai Ing-wen, won decisively. Chappell emphasizes that this was not just a political victory but a cultural assertion. "Taiwanese people standing up and saying that we don't want really a lot to do with the Chinese Communist Party," he says, linking the election directly to the broader resistance seen in Hong Kong. The narrative suggests that the election was a referendum on identity, where the "sweetness" of Chinese economic promises was rejected in favor of the "salty" reality of democratic autonomy.

"The entire country was so wrapped up in the election... we saw a lot of the people who are probably most I guess when you go to a political rally you will get the people who are interested in politics but yeah Taiwan is a super huge voter turnout 74%."

This statistic is presented not just as data, but as evidence of a society fully engaged in its own destiny. The high turnout validates the hosts' argument that the election was a defining moment for Taiwan's future. The juxtaposition of this serious political engagement with the hosts' struggle to eat the boba pizza creates a unique tonal dissonance that keeps the audience alert. It suggests that while the stakes are life-and-death, the cultural expression of that struggle can be as strange and specific as a pizza topped with bubble tea pearls.

The Limits of the Metaphor

While the boba pizza serves as a memorable hook, the argument occasionally risks letting the spectacle overshadow the substance. The hosts spend significant time debating the texture of the tapioca pearls and the "shiny" coating of the pizza, which, while entertaining, can distract from the gravity of the disinformation campaigns they are discussing. A counterargument worth considering is that the focus on such a gimmick might trivialize the serious nature of the CCP's influence operations, reducing a complex geopolitical struggle to a matter of taste preferences. However, Chappell and Jeong manage to pivot back to the serious implications of the election, using the pizza as a springboard rather than a destination.

The discussion of the "General Tso's chicken" debate further illustrates the cultural nuances at play. The hosts note that the "original" version in Taiwan is actually "good" and "crispy," unlike the "greasy" version found in American Chinese restaurants. This distinction mirrors the political argument: the authentic Taiwanese identity is being reclaimed and refined, separate from the distorted versions often presented by external powers. The hosts' preference for the "original" General Tso's over the Americanized version reinforces their broader point about the importance of preserving local culture against homogenizing forces.

"I'm really questioning my life decisions they have led me here."

This moment of personal regret from Chappell, uttered while chewing the sugary pizza, humanizes the broadcast. It reminds the audience that the hosts are not just detached analysts but participants in the culture they are discussing. Their willingness to endure the "disgusting" pizza for the sake of the show mirrors the resilience of the Taiwanese people in the face of external pressure. It is a small, humorous detail that adds a layer of authenticity to the political commentary.

Bottom Line

Chris Chappell and Shelley Jeong succeed in making a complex geopolitical narrative accessible and memorable by anchoring it in the visceral experience of eating a bizarre pizza. Their strongest argument lies in connecting the historical shift of the KMT party to the broader, insidious nature of CCP influence, using the election results as definitive proof of Taiwan's rejection of Chinese hegemony. However, the reliance on the boba pizza metaphor, while effective for engagement, occasionally threatens to overshadow the serious implications of the disinformation campaigns they describe. The takeaway is clear: Taiwan's democracy is vibrant and resilient, but it faces a sophisticated enemy that seeks to reshape its identity from the inside out.
