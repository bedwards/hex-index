Transcript excerpt on Religion For Breakfast

25 years ago, the God helmet captivated America. The God helmet was an experimental device invented by professors Stanley Corin and Michael Persinger that was designed to induce religious experiences. It was a humble ski helmet wired with electromagnets that could be aimed at specific parts of the brain. Dr.

Persinger, a neuroscientist, hypothesized that by aiming at the temporal loes, he could use transcranial magnetic stimulation to disrupt the communication between the hemispheres of the brain and induced the feeling of a non-physical presence, a synthetic ghost. His research subjects wore the helmet for 35 minutes in a sensory deprivation room. While Persinger sent different electromagnetic patterns through the brain, he claimed to have discovered that one pattern, which he called burst X, triggered a feeling of presence. In fact, many of Persinger's subjects reported mystical experiences and altered states while wearing the helmet.

And he claimed that at least 80% of his subjects have felt a sense of presence of someone else in the room with them. He speculated that he had discovered a method for triggering religious experiences and that he'd even discovered the origin of religion in the brain. In 1999, Wired magazine published a fulllength story about Persinger's helmet in which the article's writer, the popular author Jack Hit, tried the helmet for himself. Hit was skeptical and he avoided admitting he'd experienced the presence of God like others had.

But he did confess that something took place, even if that something was just a fairly convincing out-of- body experience. Turns out though that physically the God helmet wasn't doing anything. The magnets were not powerful enough to have influenced people's brains, just the surface of their skin, equivalent to the magnetic field of a phone or a haird dryer. Subsequent studies have tried to confirm the efficacy of the God helmet in producing religious experiences, but have come to dubious conclusions.

While a 2014 Brazilian study claims to have replicated his results, other scientists have criticized it for being poorly designed with a low sample size and the participants being tested under different experimental conditions. Earlier studies in 2005, 2009, and 2012 could not replicate Persinger's findings. The 2005 study concluded that the helmet was ineffective and that Persinger had achieved his results not through electromagnetism but through the power of suggestion.
