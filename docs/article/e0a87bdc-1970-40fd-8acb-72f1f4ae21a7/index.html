Decolonization gone wrong

Matt Yglesias · Slow Boring ·Mar 29, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Matt Yglesias cuts through the noise of geopolitical posturing to reveal a startling truth: the most vocal critics of the Chagos Islands handover aren't just hawkish strategists, but the very people the deal claims to liberate. While Donald Trump's "DO NOT GIVE AWAY DIEGO GARCIA!" headline grabs attention, Yglesias exposes a deeper, more uncomfortable reality where a well-intentioned decolonization effort risks becoming a second displacement for the Chagossian people.

The Human Cost of Geopolitics

Yglesias anchors his argument in the brutal history of the 1966 agreement between the U.S. and Britain, which mandated the islands be uninhabited. He describes the forced removal of the Chagossian population with a gravity often missing from policy debates. "Residents were ordered to leave their homes with only what they could carry and transported to Mauritius and the Seychelles," Yglesias writes, noting that some recall arriving to see their dogs and livestock killed before being pushed onto ships. This visceral detail transforms abstract diplomatic maneuvering into a human tragedy, grounding the political dispute in moral urgency.

The author effectively dismantles the notion that transferring sovereignty to Mauritius is a simple act of justice. He points out that while the International Court of Justice supports the legal claim, the human reality is far more fractured. "Many members of our community have experienced discrimination in Mauritius and have often felt treated as second-class citizens," he quotes Frankie Bontemps of Chagossian Voices. This is a crucial pivot; Yglesias shows that the "righting of wrongs" is complicated by the fact that the proposed new ruler is often viewed with deep suspicion by the displaced.

For many Chagossians, there is also deep concern that the transfer of sovereignty could feel like moving from one form of colonial rule to another.

Critics might argue that the legal precedent set by the 2019 advisory opinion should supersede local sentiment, but Yglesias rightly suggests that ignoring the community's voice undermines the very purpose of decolonization. The article highlights that the U.K. government admitted it "did not consult the Chagossian community about the terms of the U.K./Mauritius agreement," a failure that turns a potential victory for self-determination into another top-down decree.

The Strategic Gamble

Beyond the moral imperative, Yglesias tackles the hard-nosed security concerns that dominate the right-wing discourse. He acknowledges the unique strategic value of Diego Garcia, noting that its position allows long-range aircraft to reach the Middle East without aerial refueling. However, he challenges the assumption that the base's security is guaranteed simply because it remains leased. "In the same way European or Gulf nations often restrict the kinds of operations U.S. forces can conduct from their bases," he quotes defense analyst Bryan Clark, "the Mauritian government could impose restrictions on the actions forces can conduct from Chagos."

This section is particularly sharp because it moves beyond the binary of "U.S. vs. China" to examine the nuances of alliance reliability. Yglesias notes that Mauritius "supports China on virtually every geopolitical issue," creating a potential friction point that the current deal glosses over. He references the January confirmation that the base would be prohibited from storing nuclear weapons under the new sovereignty, a detail that signals a shift in operational freedom.

Yet, the argument is not without its counterpoints. Legal scholar Marko Milanovic suggests that critics overstate the risk, arguing that "nothing would, as a practical matter, change regarding Diego Garcia when compared to the status quo." Yglesias presents this view fairly but implies that the erosion of trust between the U.S. and a China-aligned host is a long-term vulnerability that outweighs short-term diplomatic gains.

A Missed Opportunity for Agency

The piece concludes by identifying a third path that was ignored: one where the Chagossians themselves lead the solution. Yglesias argues that the U.K. missed a chance to craft a policy that satisfied all three goals: repairing reputation, aiding the displaced, and securing national security. "If the U.K. had engaged the Chagossian community as partners from the beginning, it is plausible that together they could have come up with an approach that would accomplish all three goals," he posits. This is the article's most compelling insight—that the failure lies not in the desire for justice, but in the exclusion of the beneficiaries from the process.

The author notes that the Maldives has even raised objections to the Mauritius deal, suggesting alternative partners with stronger U.S. ties, though many Chagossians reject the idea of choosing between sovereigns altogether. Instead, they demand a guaranteed right to return and a seat at the table. "A settlement that fails to secure the support of the people most directly affected... risks solving neither problem particularly well," Yglesias writes, delivering a final verdict on the current trajectory.

Bottom Line

Yglesias's strongest contribution is his refusal to let the narrative be hijacked by either imperial nostalgia or simplistic anti-China rhetoric, instead centering the silenced voices of the Chagossians. The argument's greatest vulnerability is the lack of a concrete, alternative roadmap for how the U.K. could have achieved its goals without ceding sovereignty, leaving the reader with a clear diagnosis of failure but an uncertain prescription for success. The next move for observers is to watch whether the House of Lords ratifies the deal despite the glaring lack of consent from the people it claims to help.

Excerpt from the original article on Slow Boring

“DO NOT GIVE AWAY DIEGO GARCIA!” Donald Trump wrote on Truth Social last month, in what became most Americans’ first time hearing about the joint U.K.-U.S. military base strategically located in the central Indian Ocean.

What he’s referring to is an agreement between Britain and its former colony Mauritius to transfer sovereignty of the Chagos Islands, another British colony, to the latter. Conceived as a way to right some of the wrongs of Britain’s imperial past, the agreement has come under criticism mostly because critics argue it would give an ally of China a say over Diego Garcia, one of America and Britain’s most important bases. Many former residents of the islands and their descendants also argue against the transfer.

While the administration has been mercurial on who exactly should own the base — Marco Rubio’s State Department initially supported the U.K.’s handover of the islands — opposition to the deal has generally been “a thing” in the right-wing discourse spaces on both sides of the Atlantic. Priti Patel, the Conservative Party’s foreign policy critic, has called it a “surrender deal.” Right-wing British commentator Rod Liddle has said protestors opposing the deal are “up against idiocy.” On our side of the pond, John Kennedy of Louisiana has taken to the Senate floor to rail against the handover.

But is that right? Democrats don’t want to give China a major advantage in wartime any more than Republicans do, and there are serious decolonization criticisms of the deal that come from perspectives typically associated with the political left. Right now, Chagossians themselves are making the case that the deal does not right the wrong done to them and was not crafted with them in mind or with their consultation.

There’s a real case to be made that the dispute transcends the left-right divide. But to understand why, you have to start with how the base came to exist in the first place.

How did we get here?

It was in 1966 when the U.S. and Britain signed an agreement to build a military base on the Chagos Islands, a territory the latter has controlled since 1814. The countries stipulated that the islands be uninhabited, so the British government, with support from the U.S., forcibly removed the Chagossian population.
