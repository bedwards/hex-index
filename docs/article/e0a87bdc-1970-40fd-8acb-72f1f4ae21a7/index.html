Decolonization gone wrong

Matt Yglesias · Slow Boring ·Mar 29, 2026 ·12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Matt Yglesias cuts through the geopolitical noise surrounding the Chagos Islands to reveal a troubling paradox: a deal designed to correct a historical injustice may end up perpetuating it while simultaneously jeopardizing a critical strategic asset. The piece is notable not for its policy prescriptions, but for its refusal to let the debate be hijacked solely by great-power competition or partisan posturing, instead centering the voices of the displaced Chagossians who have been sidelined in their own story.

The Geometry of Displacement

Yglesias begins by dismantling the simplistic narrative that this is merely a dispute between Britain and Mauritius. He reminds readers that the current crisis stems from a deliberate 1966 agreement between the United States and the United Kingdom to build a military base on the Chagos Islands, stipulating that the territory be "uninhabited." To meet this requirement, the British government forcibly removed the entire Chagossian population, a move Yglesias describes with chilling clarity: "Residents were ordered to leave their homes with only what they could carry and transported to Mauritius and the Seychelles." The emotional weight of this historical erasure is captured in the testimony of one islander who recalled it as "the day the world went dark."

The author argues that the proposed solution—transferring sovereignty to Mauritius while leasing the base back—is a legalistic fix that ignores the human reality. While the International Court of Justice has supported Mauritius's claim, Yglesias points out that the islands were administratively separated from Mauritius in 1965 specifically to host the base, a move that severed the historical tie just before Mauritian independence. This context is crucial; as the article notes, the relationship is less about ancient borders and more about Cold War maneuvering. The author effectively highlights that the "righting of wrongs" is being attempted without the consent of the people most wronged.

For many Chagossians, there is also deep concern that the transfer of sovereignty could feel like moving from one form of colonial rule to another.

This observation is the piece's moral anchor. It challenges the assumption that sovereignty automatically equals justice. Yglesias notes that the Chagossian community, now largely living in the diaspora, faces discrimination in Mauritius and feels treated as "second-class citizens." The deal, therefore, risks trading British colonial oversight for a form of neglect under a new administration that may not share the same historical obligations. Critics might argue that international law demands the restoration of territorial integrity to Mauritius, regardless of the demographic shifts, but Yglesias suggests that ignoring the specific grievances of the Chagossians undermines the very legitimacy the deal seeks to restore.

The Strategic Gamble

Beyond the human rights dimension, Yglesias pivots to the hard realities of national security, a topic often dominated by the rhetoric of the "special relationship" between the U.S. and the U.K. The Diego Garcia base is not just a symbol; it is a linchpin for power projection across the Middle East, East Africa, and Southeast Asia. The author cites Bryan Clark of the Hudson Institute to explain the base's unique utility: it allows long-range aircraft to reach key theaters "without aerial refueling and without flying over countries that may not support U.S. or British operations."

The crux of the strategic argument is that while the deal promises to maintain the lease, it introduces a new variable: a host government with different geopolitical alignments. Yglesias writes, "The trust that exists between the U.S. and the U.K. cannot be compared to the U.S.-Mauritius relationship." This distinction is vital. Unlike the Five Eyes alliance, which binds the U.S. and U.K. in deep intelligence sharing, Mauritius has increasingly aligned with China on global issues. The author highlights a concrete risk: "In the same way European or Gulf nations often restrict the kinds of operations U.S. forces can conduct from their bases, the Mauritian government could impose restrictions on the actions forces can conduct from Chagos."

The article further complicates the picture by noting that China has been actively courting small island nations to challenge Western influence, and there is evidence that Russia supported Mauritius's legal claim. This suggests that the transfer could open the door to espionage or political pressure that the current British custodianship effectively blocks. Yglesias balances this by acknowledging the counterargument from international law professor Marko Milanovic, who contends that "Nothing would, as a practical matter, change regarding Diego Garcia when compared to the status quo." However, the author remains skeptical, pointing out that the U.S. and U.K. would no longer be in a position to "unilaterally assert control over Diego Garcia" if the host nation decided to restrict operations.

A Missed Opportunity for Agency

The most compelling part of Yglesias's analysis is his critique of the process itself. The deal was crafted without the input of the very people it claims to help. Yglesias cites a House of Lords committee finding that the U.K. government "did not consult the Chagossian community about the terms of the U.K./Mauritius agreement." This procedural failure has led to a situation where the Chagossians feel "sidelined and wish greater involvement in future decision-making."

The author suggests that the U.K. had other options, such as engaging the Chagossians as partners to design a solution that satisfied all three goals: reputational repair, justice for the displaced, and security. Instead, the U.K. appears to have taken a "backseat in advocating for their national interests," settling for a quick diplomatic win at the expense of a sustainable solution. The article notes that the Maldives has also raised objections, suggesting that a different sovereign partner might have been strategically preferable, but Yglesias argues that the real issue is the exclusion of the Chagossians themselves. As he puts it, "Many Chagossians reject framing the issue as a choice between potential sovereigns altogether, saying that the community itself should play the central role in determining the islands' future."

A settlement that fails to secure the support of the people most directly affected — while introducing new uncertainty around one of America's most strategically valuable military bases — risks solving neither problem particularly well.

This conclusion ties the moral and strategic threads together. The author argues that the current path is a "missed opportunity" that could affirm a deal that is neither just nor secure. The piece ends on a note of cautious hope, noting that the agreement has not yet been ratified in the House of Lords, leaving room for a course correction that centers the agency of the Chagossian people.

Bottom Line

Yglesias's strongest contribution is his refusal to let the Chagos dispute be reduced to a binary choice between imperial nostalgia and geopolitical pragmatism; he successfully argues that true justice requires the active participation of the displaced community, not just their passive inclusion in a legal settlement. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that a Chagossian-led solution is politically feasible given the entrenched positions of the U.K. and Mauritius, but the piece effectively demonstrates that the current trajectory risks creating a new form of instability in a critical region. Readers should watch for the final ratification vote in the House of Lords, where the tension between diplomatic expediency and human rights will be finally tested.

Decolonization gone wrong

by Matt Yglesias · Slow Boring · Read full article

“DO NOT GIVE AWAY DIEGO GARCIA!” Donald Trump wrote on Truth Social last month, in what became most Americans’ first time hearing about the joint U.K.-U.S. military base strategically located in the central Indian Ocean.

What he’s referring to is an agreement between Britain and its former colony Mauritius to transfer sovereignty of the Chagos Islands, another British colony, to the latter. Conceived as a way to right some of the wrongs of Britain’s imperial past, the agreement has come under criticism mostly because critics argue it would give an ally of China a say over Diego Garcia, one of America and Britain’s most important bases. Many former residents of the islands and their descendants also argue against the transfer.

While the administration has been mercurial on who exactly should own the base — Marco Rubio’s State Department initially supported the U.K.’s handover of the islands — opposition to the deal has generally been “a thing” in the right-wing discourse spaces on both sides of the Atlantic. Priti Patel, the Conservative Party’s foreign policy critic, has called it a “surrender deal.” Right-wing British commentator Rod Liddle has said protestors opposing the deal are “up against idiocy.” On our side of the pond, John Kennedy of Louisiana has taken to the Senate floor to rail against the handover.

But is that right? Democrats don’t want to give China a major advantage in wartime any more than Republicans do, and there are serious decolonization criticisms of the deal that come from perspectives typically associated with the political left. Right now, Chagossians themselves are making the case that the deal does not right the wrong done to them and was not crafted with them in mind or with their consultation.

There’s a real case to be made that the dispute transcends the left-right divide. But to understand why, you have to start with how the base came to exist in the first place.

How did we get here?.
