The DUNA: What it is and why it matters now

a16z crypto · web3 with a16z ·Apr 4, 2026 · 12 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Miles Jennings and Aiden Slavin of a16z crypto make a startling claim: the path to saving the internet from corporate monopolies isn't more regulation, but a specific, obscure legal structure called the DUNA. They argue that for the first time, decentralized communities can achieve limited liability without sacrificing their core ethos, a pivot that transforms decentralization from a theoretical ideal into a legally enforceable reality.

The Gravity of Centralization

The authors frame the current crypto landscape as a battle against a natural force. "Much like gravity, centralization is a force that's hard to resist," they write, noting that it allows companies to coordinate resources efficiently while users remain powerless. This analogy is effective because it acknowledges why big tech dominates: it works. Centralized entities move fast, make unilateral decisions, and reap rewards, creating a "monopoly-like power" that extracts value from the very users who generate it.

However, Jennings and Slavin argue that decentralization is the rocket ship capable of breaking this gravity, provided it gets enough thrust. "To achieve lift-off, decentralization requires immense energy, effort, and engineering to overcome the natural order," they explain. The DUNA, they posit, is that boost. It provides the legal "escape velocity" needed for distributed groups to compete with centralized incumbents. This framing is compelling because it moves beyond ideology to practical engineering; it treats legal structure as a necessary component of network architecture.

Critics might note that equating legal personhood with network success overlooks the human coordination challenges that have plagued decentralized projects for years. A law cannot fix poor governance or lack of user engagement. Yet, the authors are right to identify the legal vacuum as a primary bottleneck.

Beyond the Foundation Fiction

For years, the crypto industry relied on offshore foundations as a workaround. Jennings and Slavin are blunt about this era: "Foundations became convoluted workarounds to appear decentralized — something we've also called 'decentralization theater' — rather than mechanisms for actually achieving decentralization." They argue that these entities often introduced opacity and misaligned incentives, forcing legitimate builders to operate outside the United States due to prohibitive costs and independence mandates.

The article draws a sharp line between these old models and the new DUNA. "The separation and fiction of foundations is no longer necessary," they assert. Instead of a neutral steward hiding behind a veil of non-profit status, the DUNA is a legal representation of token-based governance. It is not designed to "run" an ecosystem but to execute the discrete decisions made by token holders. This distinction is crucial. As the authors put it, "A DUNA is only responsible for the discrete actions that token holders are empowered to take via token-based governance, and it only acts when token holders act."

This shift mirrors the historical evolution of business structures. Just as the Limited Liability Company (LLC) provided flexibility for business owners in Wyoming two decades ago, the DUNA is described as a "digital UNA optimized for the decentralized organizations building the future of the internet." It allows communities to contract, pay taxes, and appear in court without forcing them into the top-down hierarchies of traditional corporations. The argument lands because it solves the "liability trap" where DAO members were previously exposed to unlimited personal liability, effectively treating them as general partnerships.

Paying taxes is a feature, not a bug.

The Strategic Imperative

The urgency of the piece comes from the intersection of state legislation and federal policy. With Wyoming, Alabama, and West Virginia already enacting DUNA laws, the authors warn that waiting is a strategic error. "Founders who adopt the DUNA today, rather than waiting, will have a demonstrated, defensible governance record when that legislation passes," they write. This is a pragmatic call to action. They suggest that federal market structure legislation, such as the CLARITY Act, is moving toward recognizing decentralized governance, and the DUNA is the only structure currently recognized for this purpose in draft bills.

The authors emphasize that this is not just about crypto. "Decentralization is at the heart of many issues dominating our conversations," they note, ranging from social media control to AI dominance. By providing a viable path for decentralized networks, the DUNA enables "community-owned-and-operated networks and services that benefit users, including beyond crypto." This broader context elevates the piece from a legal update to a manifesto for the future of digital ownership.

However, a counterargument worth considering is whether the "nonprofit" label in DUNA might still create friction for commercial ventures, despite the authors' assurance that they can engage in for-profit activities. The market will need to see real-world examples of DUNAs successfully balancing commercial viability with decentralized governance before this model is universally adopted.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its reframing of legal compliance not as a constraint, but as the essential fuel for decentralized scale. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that legal clarity alone will solve the coordination problems that have historically stalled decentralized projects. Readers should watch for the first wave of DUNA-adoption cases to see if this structure delivers on its promise of liability protection without stifling innovation.

by Miles Jennings and Aiden Slavin

As of this week, three states have officially enacted the relatively new “decentralized unincorporated nonprofit association” act — aka the DUNA. The DUNA provides legal status for decentralized organizations and limited liability protections for their members and administrators. By doing so, the DUNA gives these communities and builders the legal and structural certainty to build, govern, contract, and scale in the real world.

Wyoming was the first state to adopt the DUNA two years ago. [Notably, it was also the first state to adopt the unincorporated nonprofit association (UNA) and the first to adopt the limited liability company (LLC), which provided key protections and flexibility for business owners so they could unlock innovation. Think of the DUNA as a digital UNA optimized for the decentralized organizations building the future of the internet.]

Alabama signed the DUNA Act yesterday. And West Virginia just did too, with more states on the way. States have moved to the center of crypto policymaking, and this is forward-looking policymaking at its best: It embraces innovation, protects users, and empowers internet-native communities to compete with big tech incumbents. And it comes at a pivotal moment in the effort to make the U.S. the crypto capital of the world. As federal crypto market structure legislation moves closer to becoming law, builders need effective domestic legal structures.

Several prominent crypto organizations have also adopted the DUNA — including Uniswap Governance, Nouns DAO, Syndicate Network Collective, and more, with others coming.

But first, why does this matter?

Decentralized governance is essential to crypto’s future, and the DUNA provides a legal structure that fits decentralized organizations.

More specifically, DUNAs are legal entities that provide limited liability and other key protections to participants in a blockchain network, while allowing them to remain decentralized. Fundamentally, the DUNA enables blockchain networks to remain decentralized while complying with the law. It not only grants decentralized organizations like DAOs legal existence, but also allows them to:

  • contract with regular businesses and appear in court;

  • allows them to pay taxes; and

  • equips them with key protections.

In short, the DUNA puts decentralized organizations on equal footing with other entity forms like corporations and the LLC.

Importantly, the DUNA allows decentralized organizations to innovate as blockchain networks, without forcing them into the top-down, centralized control structures of companies that depend on centralized management or hierarchies ...
