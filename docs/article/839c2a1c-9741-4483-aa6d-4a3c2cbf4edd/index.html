Let's Read! John Hardwig, 1985, "Epistemic Dependence"

Commentary by Brian Edwards

Kenny Easwaran tackles a provocative paradox: that true rationality sometimes demands we stop thinking for ourselves. In his analysis of John Hardwig's 1985 paper, Easwaran dismantles the modern obsession with individual verification, arguing that our intellectual survival depends on trusting experts even when we lack the evidence to verify their claims ourselves.

The Distinction Between Evidence and Good Reason

Easwaran begins by clarifying a crucial terminological split that most of us miss. He notes that Hardwig distinguishes between "evidence," which must be possessed by someone to establish truth, and "good reason," which can exist simply because a trusted authority has the evidence. Easwaran explains that "the mere fact that someone claims that something is true could never count as evidence in his sense but the fact that someone claims something is true could provide me with good even though it wouldn't be evidence." This distinction is vital because it allows us to justify beliefs without personally holding the proof. The argument lands effectively because it mirrors our daily reality; we cannot possibly verify the safety of our cars, the efficacy of our medicines, or the stability of our power grids.

"I believe too much there is too much relevant evidence much of it available only after extensive specialized training intellect is too small and life is too short."

By quoting Hardwig's admission that the list of unverified beliefs is "virtually endless," Easwaran highlights the absurdity of demanding personal evidence for everything. Critics might argue that this opens the door to blind conformity, but Easwaran counters that the alternative is intellectual paralysis. The core of the argument is that we are not irrational for deferring to others; we are rational because we recognize the limits of our own capacity.

The Rationality of Refusing to Think for Yourself

The most striking claim Easwaran explores is the idea that "rationality consists in refusing to think for yourself." He contextualizes this by pointing to recent public failures where individuals insisted on verifying complex topics like vaccines or epidemiology, only to "get themselves into all sorts of trouble." Easwaran writes that "if there really are good experts on something available then insisting on thinking for yourself rather than deferring to the expert will often constitute some sort of irrationality." This is a bold inversion of the liberal education ideal, yet it holds up under scrutiny when the cost of error is high. The argument suggests that the virtue of independent thought is often a vice when applied to domains requiring specialized training.

"Believing things without evidence and refusing to think for yourself... perhaps some of this might become a bit more plausible once we turn to his terminology."

Easwaran acknowledges the tension this creates with our educational systems, which emphasize finding evidence and thinking independently. He suggests this is a necessary training ground, not for daily life, but to help us identify who the actual experts are. "By working through these things in your education you're going to get some sense of what is involved in expertise so that you might get better at identifying experts to defer to." This reframing is persuasive: education teaches us how to know, so we can later trust those who do know. A counterargument worth considering is that this reliance creates a vulnerability to bad actors posing as experts, a risk Hardwig's framework struggles to fully mitigate without robust institutional checks.

Reconceptualizing Knowledge

Finally, Easwaran guides us through Hardwig's conclusion that we must rethink the very nature of knowledge. If we accept that we can know things without personal evidence, then "knowledge is something that a community has rather than an individual." Easwaran notes that Hardwig argues "the expert layman relationship is essential to the scientific and scholarly pursuit." This shifts the locus of knowledge from the solitary thinker to the collaborative network. It challenges the "epistemic individualism" that dominates much of Western philosophy.

"The epistemic superiority of the expert to the layman implies rational authority over the layman undermining the intellectual autonomy of the individual and forcing a re-examination of our notion of rationality."

This is the piece's most profound implication. It suggests that intellectual autonomy is not the highest good; rather, the health of the epistemic community is. While this may feel uncomfortable to those who prize self-reliance, Easwaran makes a compelling case that our modern world is too complex for any single mind to hold all the evidence.

"The epistemic individualism implicit in many of our epistemologies is thus called into question with important implications for how we understand knowledge and the knower."

Bottom Line

Easwaran's commentary on Hardwig offers a necessary corrective to the cult of individual verification, proving that trusting experts is not a surrender of reason but an exercise of it. The argument's greatest strength is its pragmatic grounding in the limits of human cognition, though it leaves the difficult task of identifying trustworthy experts somewhat underspecified. Readers should watch for how this framework applies to the current crisis of expertise, where the line between legitimate authority and charlatanism is increasingly blurred.
