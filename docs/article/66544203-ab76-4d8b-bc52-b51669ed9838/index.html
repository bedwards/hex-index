Timothy Snyder: The Making of Modern Ukraine. Class 7. Rise of Muscovite Power

Yale University · Yale Courses ·Sep 29, 2022 · 46 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

Timothy Snyder, a Yale University professor, delivers a lecture that defies the standard historical narrative by arguing that modern Russia's identity is not a pure, ancient lineage but a messy, violent transformation born from unexpected encounters. In a classroom interrupted by an ongoing war, he connects the dots between 11th-century Vikings, 13th-century Mongols, and the Buddhist soldiers dying in Ukraine today, suggesting that the very concept of "Russia" is a product of geopolitical collision rather than organic evolution. This is not just a history lesson; it is a direct rebuttal to the Kremlin's justification for invasion, which relies on the myth of a thousand-year, unbroken continuity from Kiev to Moscow.

The Myth of Pure Origins

Snyder begins by dismantling the seductive but false idea that nations emerge from a state of innocence. "Origins are always tricky because it would be nice if we could have something from nothing," he observes, noting that national stories often claim a pure beginning corrupted by outsiders. He rejects this in favor of a more chaotic reality, comparing historical formation to Ovid's Metamorphoses, where "unexpected things arise from the encounters of things." This framing is crucial because it shifts the reader's perspective from looking for a static "Russian essence" to understanding a dynamic process of synthesis and violence.

The professor argues that the current war is fueled by a specific origin story: "the justification for the war... has to do with the continuation of some kind of permanent identity from Kiev a thousand years ago into Russia Today." By exposing this as a fabrication, Snyder undermines the ideological bedrock of the conflict. He suggests that history is not a straight line but a series of transformations where "you're getting something from something else," a point that feels particularly urgent given the current bloodshed.

"History is more like that [Ovid's Metamorphoses]... it's an unexpected things arise from the Encounters of things and then later on you tell a story about how it had to be that way but the time it's all very messy and confusing."

The Post-Viking World and the Language of Power

To illustrate how states are built on contact rather than isolation, Snyder traces the arc of the "post-Viking" world, linking England, Scandinavia, and the East Slavic lands. He points out that the English language itself is a testament to this synthesis, noting that "it would be very hard for me to create a sentence in English which doesn't involve a word which came from French." This linguistic reality stems from the Norman Conquest, a "post-Viking" event where "the Normans are the ones who win" while the Norwegians lose.

Snyder uses the life of Harold Hardrada to weave these disparate geographies together. He describes a figure who "starts out in Norway he goes to Kiev he serves Yaroslav the wise... then goes on to Byzantium... then goes back to Norway... invades England but gets killed right away." This narrative demonstrates that the East Slavic world was never isolated; it was part of a vast network stretching from England to Byzantium. The argument holds weight because it uses concrete historical figures to prove that the "Russian" world was always defined by its connections to the West and South, not by a closed, internal purity.

Critics might argue that focusing on the Viking influence risks downplaying the distinct Slavic cultural developments that occurred independently. However, Snyder's point is not to erase Slavic identity but to show that it was forged in the fires of external pressure and alliance, much like the later rise of Lithuania.

The Mongol Shock and the Birth of Muscovy

The lecture pivots to the catastrophic impact of the Mongol invasion, which Snyder identifies as the catalyst for the rise of Muscovy. He explains that the rise of Lithuania as a great power "only makes sense if you understand Teutonic Knights pushing from the West Mongols have come in from the East and destroyed Roos." Without the destruction of Kievan Rus', the political vacuum that allowed Muscovy to consolidate would not have existed.

Snyder highlights the irony of the current war by asking why "indigenous people from deep into Asia... are disproportionately fighting and killing and dying in Ukraine." He connects this modern tragedy to the historical expansion of the Russian state, which absorbed these Asian peoples. "Why are these Buriats... doing so much of the fighting and dying in Ukraine?" he asks, forcing the audience to confront the multi-ethnic, imperial nature of the Russian military machine. The answer lies in the historical transformation of the state into an empire that consumed its neighbors, a process that began with the Mongol conquests.

"The justification for the war... has to do with the continuation of some kind of permanent identity from Kiev a thousand years ago into Russia Today... but deep down in the beginning it was all folk songs and and Harmony before the other people came and messed everything up."

Bottom Line

Snyder's most powerful contribution is his refusal to let the myth of a "pure" Russian origin stand unchallenged, replacing it with a rigorous, evidence-based account of violent synthesis and imperial expansion. While the lecture relies heavily on the "contact" theory of state-building, which some might find reductive of internal cultural dynamics, its application to the current war provides an indispensable context for understanding why the conflict feels so existential to both sides. The reader must watch for how this historical framing influences future diplomatic narratives, as the deconstruction of the "thousand-year identity" myth is a direct strike at the war's ideological core.
