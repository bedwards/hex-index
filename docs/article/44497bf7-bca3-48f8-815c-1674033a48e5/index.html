A Strategic Learning Deficit: Western Military Institutions Ignored Ukraine War Lessons. The Big Five, 8 March edition

Commentary by Brian Edwards
Deep dives· Original article →

Mick Ryan delivers a stinging indictment of Western military complacency, arguing that the very institutions tasked with defending the free world have failed to learn the most basic lessons from the war in Ukraine. In a timeline set in March 2026, Ryan exposes a humiliating irony: the United States and its allies are now begging Kyiv for help against Iranian drones, a threat Ukraine has been neutralizing for years with low-cost, homegrown solutions. This is not just a status report; it is a warning that ignoring the realities of modern warfare comes at the cost of American lives and strategic credibility.

The Cost of Ignorance

Ryan's central thesis is that Western military organizations suffer from a "systemic learning deficit," a failure of humility that has left them dangerously unprepared. He highlights a pivotal moment where the U.S. formally requested Ukrainian assistance to counter Iranian "Shahed" drones after a barrage of 2,000 unmanned systems and 500 ballistic missiles overwhelmed American defenses. "The irony is huge," Ryan writes, noting that Ukraine has spent four years building a "layered, cost-effective counter-drone architecture" that the U.S. lacks. "It is exactly what the United States and some of its partners lack in the Gulf."

The author's evidence is damning. While American air defenses, designed for a bygone era, struggled against cheap, slow-moving drones, Ukraine deployed low-cost interceptors that are an "order of magnitude cheaper" than the threats they face. Ryan points out that the U.S. eventually deployed its own "Merops" system, citing lessons learned from the Ukrainian theater, but only after a strike in Kuwait killed six American servicemembers. "A highly predictable threat from Iran which was not paid sufficient respect by the Americans and others, and as a result, they were unprepared," he argues.

Ignoring lessons of modern wars - in an era when war has never been more visible - will hurt you at some point.

Critics might argue that the technological gap between the U.S. and Ukraine is a matter of budget and industrial capacity rather than a failure of will. However, Ryan counters this by pointing to a British politician who tweeted from an undeclared maintenance site, exposing it to Russian targeting via simple social media geolocation. "How had they not learned this?" Ryan asks. "For four years, both Ukraine and Russia have employed simple and widely available geolocation from social media posts... to prime their targeting process." This suggests the issue is not just resources, but a fundamental cultural blindness to the lessons of the battlefield.

Ukraine's Strategic Maturity

While Western institutions stumble, Ryan details how Ukraine has evolved into a model of adaptive warfare. The Ukrainian Armed Forces have transitioned from "chaotic developments to a holistic state system," prioritizing feedback loops and rapid innovation. Ryan notes that in 2025, the bulk of research funding went to unmanned aerial vehicles (28%) and robotic systems (15%), reflecting a clear strategic pivot. "The key principle is feedback," he writes, describing a military that scales research activities and integrates artificial intelligence across all branches.

This strategic focus is yielding tangible results on the ground. Ryan reports that Ukrainian forces captured more territory in February 2026 than Russia did in the same period, breaking the pattern of Russian winter advances. "This represents the best relative territorial performance for Ukraine's ground force since the Kursk operation in August 2024," he observes. The data supports this: Russian monthly gains collapsed from their late-2025 peak, with Ukraine achieving a net gain of nearly 130 square kilometers in the south alone.

The most striking example of this maturity is the long-range strike on Novorossiysk. Ryan describes a complex operation involving 200 aerial and maritime drones coordinated across multiple agencies to hit the Russian Black Sea Fleet's primary base. "The ship sustained critical damage that significantly limits its ability to use Kalibr cruise missiles," he quotes the Security Service of Ukraine regarding the frigate Admiral Essen. This is not just a tactical victory; it is a demonstration of a "multi-domain drone swarm" capability that other nations are only beginning to understand.

Diplomatic Fractures

The article also captures the fragility of the diplomatic front. With the U.S. absorbed in a new war against Iran, trilateral talks between Ukraine, Russia, and the U.S. have been postponed indefinitely. Ryan notes that the Trump administration's attention has shifted, leaving Ukraine vulnerable. "Zelenskyy was direct about the risk," Ryan writes, warning that a prolonged U.S.-Iran war could deprive Ukraine of critical air defense systems as Washington prioritizes the Middle East.

The Kremlin is already exploiting this distraction. Ryan points out that Russian officials are "using the escalation in the Middle East to set conditions to blame the United States for any future failures in negotiations for a peace in Ukraine." The geopolitical landscape has shifted so rapidly that the venue for talks in the UAE is now compromised by active missile strikes. "Peace in 2026 is looking even more unlikely," Ryan concludes, noting that the war in Ukraine continues regardless of diplomatic paralysis.

Ukraine has been defending itself against Shahed drones since 2022. For at least a year, Ukraine has employed low-cost drone interceptors, which are an order of magnitude cheaper than Shaheds, as part of this air defence system.

Bottom Line

Ryan's most compelling argument is that the West's failure to learn from Ukraine is not a minor oversight but a strategic catastrophe that is already costing lives. The piece's greatest strength is its ability to juxtapose the gritty, adaptive reality of the Ukrainian military against the rigid, outdated doctrines of Western powers. However, the argument's vulnerability lies in its assumption that Western institutions can simply "adopt" Ukrainian tactics without addressing the deeper bureaucratic and industrial inertia that caused the deficit in the first place. Readers should watch whether the U.S. can rapidly integrate these low-cost, high-volume drone strategies before the next escalation renders current defenses obsolete.
