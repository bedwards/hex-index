Digital security Part 2 - how to protect yourself on the Internet

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Jan 9, 2020 · 87 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a digital landscape where deletion is often mistaken for erasure, The Hated One delivers a sobering reality check: if you haven't encrypted it, you haven't protected it. This piece cuts through the noise of complex threat models to reveal a startling truth about data recovery—what you delete is rarely gone, but what you encrypt is effectively invisible. For the busy professional assuming a quick format wipes their slate clean, this is the wake-up call they need before the next security audit or physical seizure.

The Illusion of Deletion

The Hated One begins by dismantling a common misconception with brutal efficiency. Most users believe that hitting "delete" removes a file, but the author clarifies that "deleting stuff in your operating system doesn't actually delete anything from from the actual hard drive. It just tells the file system that the space that was used by that file is now available again." This distinction is critical because it means that anyone with a basic Linux utility or a data recovery service can resurrect your "deleted" photos, documents, and secrets for free.

The author illustrates this with a cautionary tale of a colleague who was forced to delete incriminating photos by a security guard. The colleague simply swapped the memory card, took it home, and recovered the images instantly. "That that that's again operational security," The Hated One notes, highlighting how a lack of technical knowledge can render physical enforcement useless. However, this anecdote also serves as a warning: relying on the ignorance of an adversary is a fragile strategy. If the guard had known how to physically remove the storage medium and run a forensic scan, the trick would have failed.

"The only thing that that um like well what you can do is is if you if you delete something um there's there are tools that kind of like really delete your the the free space... override that with random data to have it actually deleted."

While overwriting free space is a valid technique, The Hated One points out its limitations, particularly with modern Solid State Drives (SSDs) and SD cards. These devices use wear leveling, where the controller automatically moves data to different cells to extend the drive's life. This means that even if you overwrite a file, the original data might linger in a "spare cell" that the operating system can't access. Recovering it would require "solder out the cells and extra manually extract the data," a process reserved for high-level threat actors with massive budgets. For the average user, this is overkill, but it underscores why simple deletion is a gamble.

The Encryption Imperative

The commentary pivots to the only reliable solution: full disk encryption. The Hated One argues that "it's really way easier to just throw away the encryption key than to delete all the data." This is a profound shift in mindset. Instead of trying to hunt down every stray bit of data on a drive, you simply destroy the key that unlocks it. Once the key is gone, the data becomes mathematically unreadable gibberish, regardless of whether it was overwritten or not.

A common point of confusion arises regarding reformatting drives. The author clarifies that setting up encryption does not automatically wipe existing data; it only encrypts new files as they are written. "If you only format it, the the old encrypted data will not be erased," The Hated One explains. To truly secure a drive, one must wipe it first, then encrypt. This nuance is vital for users who might think enabling a "BitLocker" or "FileVault" toggle instantly sanitizes their history.

"Don't don't do wiping, use encryption. It's much better. And um wiping is hard. Encryption is easy at least to some extent."

The author also addresses the myth of the "seven-pass wipe," a legacy recommendation from the US intelligence community. The Hated One dismisses this as unnecessary for most scenarios, stating that "for levels for fat model one and two once is enough." Even for high-threat models, two passes of random data are sufficient to make recovery practically impossible. The real takeaway is that encryption renders the number of wipes irrelevant; if the data is encrypted, the attacker cannot distinguish between a wiped drive and one filled with random noise.

The Limits of Protection

Despite the strength of encryption, The Hated One does not promise invincibility. The discussion turns to the danger of data in memory. Even if a messaging app like Signal deletes a message from the database, "memory is still unencrypted and if you root you can just read every every memory um of every app that's running." This is a sobering admission: if an adversary gains root access or physical control of a powered-on device, they can extract keys and messages directly from RAM before they are ever written to disk.

Critics might note that this leaves a significant gap in security for users who are targeted by sophisticated state actors capable of exploiting zero-day vulnerabilities. While full disk encryption protects data at rest, it offers no shield against an active, compromised system. The Hated One acknowledges this, noting that "if you really own the device then well yeah security is depressing." Yet, the author maintains that basic principles still provide a formidable barrier against the vast majority of threats.

"If the Chinese Muslims that were targeted by the iOS exploits, if they had this option enabled that would automatically delete their, you know, messages that were at rest. then the the adversaries would not have had not have had access to those the the the data."

This observation highlights the importance of self-destructing messages and automated deletion features. While not a silver bullet, they reduce the window of opportunity for an attacker to extract data from a seized device. The Hated One suggests that for most users, the combination of full disk encryption and aggressive data retention policies is the most effective defense available.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's argument is a masterclass in prioritizing the right security controls: encryption is superior to deletion, and prevention is better than recovery. The piece's greatest strength is its ability to demystify complex storage technologies, showing that for 99% of users, the fear of data recovery is misplaced if encryption is enabled. However, the argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the user's ability to maintain operational security—specifically, never plugging in unknown devices and ensuring the device is powered off when not in use. For the busy professional, the verdict is clear: enable full disk encryption today, and stop worrying about how many times you need to wipe your drive.
