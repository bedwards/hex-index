Against doom

Emma Collins · ·Jan 2, 2026 ·19 min read
Commentary by Hex Index staff
Deep dives· Original article →

Emma Collins delivers a scathing, necessary rebuke to a growing literary trend that conflates spiritual depth with nihilistic despair. While many contemporary voices warn of civilizational collapse, Collins argues that Paul Kingsnorth's Against the Machine crosses a line from critique into a dangerous, fundamentalist fantasy that rejects the very foundations of human hope and freedom. This is not a standard book review; it is a cultural diagnosis of why the well-educated elite are increasingly drawn to a worldview that celebrates the end of the world.

The Architecture of Despair

Collins begins by dismantling the romanticized image of the modern religious convert, using an encounter with an Orthodox enthusiast to highlight a troubling pattern: the use of faith to justify reactionary politics while ignoring the Enlightenment principles that allow for that freedom in the first place. She writes, "The manosphere beard should have been a dead giveaway," a sharp observation that sets the stage for her critique of Kingsnorth's own brand of performative piety. Collins notes that Kingsnorth, a former environmental activist turned rural homesteader, has transformed his spiritual journey into a "screed not only against technology, but against the forces of human ambition, empire, infrastructure, modern capitalism, and accumulated power."

The author's central charge is that Kingsnorth has forgotten the biblical definition of prophecy. Citing 1 Corinthians 14:3, she argues that true prophecy is meant for "strengthening, encouraging, and comfort," whereas Kingsnorth's work offers only a "mass of brown sludge." She describes the reading experience as "falling down a very deep abyss and plunging closer and closer to utter hopelessness." This framing is effective because it shifts the debate from the merits of technology to the moral health of the reader's soul. Collins suggests that a book claiming to be spiritual while exuding such darkness is inherently suspect.

"Only God can know the contours of someone's heart, but when a book claims to promote faith and instead exhales darkness, its nature should be questioned."

The Violence of the Raw Ascetic

The commentary takes a darker turn as Collins exposes the violent undercurrents of Kingsnorth's philosophy. She identifies the "Machine" not just as a metaphor for capitalism, but as a Satanic force that must be destroyed. Collins highlights a particularly disturbing passage where Kingsnorth describes the mindset of the "raw ascetic": "Bombing the data centers: this is the mindset of the raw tech-ascetic." He justifies this by claiming, "The raw ascetic understands that he or she is fighting a spiritual war."

This is where Collins' critique becomes most potent. She argues that such rhetoric reveals a fundamentalist mind that "cannot stand freedom" and is "consumed with Satan, whom he loves as he loves death." By invoking the Old Testament deity Moloch and describing the internet as a "digital noose," Kingsnorth creates a worldview where the only solution is annihilation. Collins points out the irony that this extremism is embraced by "bien-pensant upper-class liberals" who might otherwise reject violence. She notes that Kingsnorth's hatred is so all-consuming that he admits, "When I see people taking selfies on mountaintops, I want to push them off." This admission, she argues, strips him of any moral authority to speak on faith or community.

Critics might argue that Kingsnorth's hyperbole is merely rhetorical flair intended to shock readers out of complacency, but Collins rightly counters that the sheer volume of such language makes it impossible to separate the message from the method. The call to destroy infrastructure, even metaphorically, resonates dangerously in an era where political violence is on the rise.

The Hypocrisy of the Controlling Hippie

Collins then pivots to a cultural critique of the "Controlling Hippie" archetype, a figure she sees embodied by Kingsnorth. She describes this persona as an aficionado of hypocrisy who lives in the suburbs but pretends to be a rural sage, preaching asceticism while enjoying the benefits of the very system he condemns. "The Controlling Hippie makes his living on the internet while telling everyone else to get off it," she observes. This section is particularly biting because it addresses the classism inherent in Kingsnorth's work. Collins, drawing on her own background, notes that real rural people are too practical to embrace such "fulminations," preferring to "eat hamburger soup from the crockpot while watching the local news" rather than churning artisanal butter.

She also touches on the anti-American sentiment permeating the text, noting Kingsnorth's desire for the "end of the demonic 'american' empire." This aligns with a broader trend in post-liberal thought, which Collins compares to former Marxists who became "hectoring pessimists" carrying "apocalyptic baggage" as they shifted political camps. The reference to Paul Kingsnorth's conversion in 2021 and his subsequent retreat to Ireland serves as a backdrop for this critique, suggesting that his rejection of the West is less about spiritual purity and more about a retreat from the complexities of modern life.

"We built the Machine to run the world for us. Now the Machine runs us."

Bottom Line

Emma Collins' review is a vital corrective to the doom-laden literature currently saturating the cultural conversation. Her strongest argument is that a faith that offers no hope is not faith at all, but a mask for fundamentalist rage. However, her dismissal of the genuine anxieties about technology and environmental collapse as mere "pathetic fantasies" risks oversimplifying the very real crises that drive readers to Kingsnorth's work. The piece succeeds not by offering a new solution, but by exposing the moral bankruptcy of a worldview that finds satisfaction in the end of the world.

A few years ago, I attended a Christmastime service at an Orthodox church in Washington, D.C. The liturgy was beautiful and the clergy welcoming and gracious. The icons decorating the church’s interior were indeed evocative, and the long, slender candles parishioners placed in sand added to the atmosphere. As I was walking back home I encountered a young man who had been there too, and explained that he was a convert. “In Orthodox Christianity,” he gushed, his eyes widening, “it’s like the Enlightenment never existed.”

Cool story, bro. While I know there are sincere Orthodox converts who are gentle, believing people (a girlfriend who cuts my hair is one of them), there does seem to be a contingent of dudes who find their way into this denomination in order to justify reactionary politics. What’s ironic about these types, especially in America, is that it never occurs to them to pay homage to the First Amendment with its guarantee of religious freedom (a product of the Enlightenment) — the very condition on which their license to worship rests.

The manosphere beard should have been a dead giveaway. I parted ways with the young pilgrim and his unkempt facial hair and happily returned the next day to my own Catholic church. I told a woman there about my conversation and she smiled knowingly.

Most of us are familiar with people who use belief in God as a tool of dominion over others, or try to. Maybe they have an utterly negative, dystopian view of the world, and in a backwards way, they tell a conversion story to make it all “make sense.” They then write from a spiritual perspective, purporting to carry God’s message, while in fact doing the opposite.
