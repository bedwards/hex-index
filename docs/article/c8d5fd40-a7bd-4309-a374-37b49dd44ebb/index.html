You Should Make A Digital Bugout Bag

The Hated One · The Hated One ·Mar 18, 2022 · 11 min read
Commentary by Brian Edwards
Original video →

In a world where paper records burn and paper trails vanish, The Hated One makes a startlingly pragmatic claim: your most vital survival tool might not be a can of beans, but a microscopic, encrypted memory card. While standard emergency advice focuses on physical supplies, this piece argues that the true bottleneck in a crisis is identity recovery, and that a digital bugout bag offers a layer of security paper simply cannot match.

The Case for Digital Resilience

The core of the argument rests on the fragility of traditional documentation. The Hated One writes, "paper documents can easily get destroyed by floods fire and collapse they can be lost or stolen and in case of theft your paper documents are not encrypted and they could be easily abused for fraud." This is a compelling reframing of preparedness; it shifts the focus from mere survival to the preservation of one's legal and digital identity. The author suggests that while physical copies are necessary, they are insufficient without a secure, portable digital fail-safe.

The proposed solution is surprisingly low-tech in its hardware but high-tech in its execution. The Hated One advocates for a micro SD card over a USB drive, noting that "sd cards generally provide larger capacity much cheaper than usb flash drives" and are easier to conceal. The logic here is sound for the paranoid or the stealth-conscious: a card can be hidden inside a coin, a toy, or a piece of clothing, whereas a USB drive is often conspicuous. Critics might note that micro SD cards are physically more fragile than ruggedized USB drives and require an adapter for many computers, potentially complicating access in a chaotic environment. However, the trade-off for concealment and capacity seems to be the author's priority.

A secure digital backup gives you the flexibility you need to adapt it to your particular threat model depending on your situation geography or country.

The Encryption Imperative

The commentary then pivots to the software, where the author's expertise becomes most apparent. The choice of VeraCrypt, a free and open-source tool, is presented not just as a convenience but as a necessity for plausible deniability. The Hated One explains that the software allows for "hidden volumes and hidden operating systems with a decoy volume if you need plausible deniability for when you are forced to unlock your data." This is a critical distinction. In a worst-case scenario where an adversary has physical access to the device, the ability to present a decoy password that unlocks a harmless volume while keeping the real data hidden is a powerful defense.

The author is refreshingly honest about the human element of security. They admit, "I'm going to choose to encrypt my entire card because i don't have a trusted friend because i have no friends and i don't know if i can keep doing this any longer." This raw vulnerability underscores the importance of self-reliance in their framework. The advice to use a long, memorable passphrase rather than a complex string of symbols is practical. "A phrase like splinter it into a thousand pieces and scattered into the winds is 53 characters long easily memorable and most definitely uncrackable," The Hated One suggests. This approach balances the mathematical strength of encryption with the cognitive load on the user during a crisis.

Beyond the Backup: The Full Kit

The piece extends beyond simple file storage to a holistic digital survival kit. The Hated One recommends specific offline applications to replace internet-dependent services. "osmn this is a free and open source privacy respective map and navigation app," is highlighted as essential, alongside offline weather forecasts and a massive database of Wikipedia articles. The inclusion of a plant identification app for foraging demonstrates a deep understanding of what survival actually entails: finding food and navigating without infrastructure.

The author also stresses the importance of backing up the backup. "Don't just copy your veracrypt volume file into a new location instead create a new vertrip volume following the same steps," they instruct. This redundancy is crucial, as hardware failure is a constant risk. The recommendation to store these items in a waterproof Faraday bag to protect against electromagnetic pulses or electronic theft adds a layer of tactical sophistication often missing from standard prepper lists.

Protecting physical security of your backups is just as important as the encryption itself.

Critics might argue that the reliance on a single micro SD card creates a single point of failure, and that the complexity of managing encrypted volumes could overwhelm a non-technical user in a high-stress situation. Furthermore, the suggestion to hide the card in "low value items of no particular interest" relies heavily on the adversary not finding the hiding spot, which is a gamble against determined searchers. Yet, the author's emphasis on "out of sight is out of mind" remains a valid operational security principle.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its integration of digital security with physical survival, treating identity as a critical resource that must be protected with the same rigor as food or water. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that the user can maintain the discipline to keep these digital tools updated and the passwords memorized without a support network. The reader should watch for the practical application of these tools, as the theoretical security of VeraCrypt means nothing if the passphrase is forgotten in the moment of evacuation.
