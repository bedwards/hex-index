Six weeks ago I published my first book: Shattered Lands: Five Partitions and the Making of Modern Asia.

This came at a particularly charged moment. Tensions between India and Pakistan have once again flared, reminding us that Partition is not some closed chapter of the past. And yet, despite all the books, documentaries, and films that have covered the Partition of 1947, the story that we usually tell is somewhat incomplete.

In the 1920s, the Raj was far bigger than we tend to imagine today, stretching from Yemen in the west to Burma in the east.

“Partition” was not one rupture, but a series of five imperial breakages that created twelve modern nation states.

We live in a world still shaped by its aftershocks, from the Rohingya crisis to the nuclearization of South Asian, from crackdowns in Kashmir to the origins of the Gulf War. There’s never been a more urgent time to understand how these borders were drawn, and what was broken in the process.

The First Demands for Partition

Today if you wander around Delhi or Dhaka, you wont hear many people talking about the partition of Burma in 1937. Its an almost forgotten event. Burma is always sort of there on the maps of the Raj and yet no one ever really questions it.

And yet Burma’s partition from India is arguably one of the most important events of the twentieth century, setting into motion a series of dominoes that would lead to the Bengal Famine, the Long March, the creation of Pakistan, the Rohingya Genocide, insurgencies in Northeast India and the longest ongoing civil war in the world. It’s a HUGELY important story, and one that is almost entirely forgotten.

It also has surprising lessons for post Brexit Britain. In the 1920s, Burma was arguably the economic centre of Asia. In the 1920s, the economy was booming, attracting Armenian, Tamil, Bengali, Chinese and Japanese traders, and in the 1920s more migrant workers set sail for Burma than across the Atlantic Ocean. The Burmese Dream was rapidly outpacing the American one and as one British labour official wrote shortly after the Simon Commission’s visit:

“[Rangoon] was till recently second only to New York in importance as an immigration port. It now occupies pride of place as the first immigration and emigration port of the world.

But in the wake of a massive recession, an anti-immigration lobby in Burma would gain massive power, pushing for independence from India as much as from Britain. Burmese elites began to paint Indians as a parasitic burden and championed the idea of “taking back control”.

At the time, however, there was a huge amount of opposition to separating Burma. Many Burmese worried that separation would eviscerate their thriving economy, and Burma’s most prominent politician at the time, U Ottama, argued that Burma was an integral part of the Indian nation.

Just like the movement for Brexit, the crucial thing that changed was a global recession. Most of the bankers in rural Burma were Chettiar Tamils and during the Great Depression, hundreds of thousands of Burmese farmers began defaulting on their debts.

In a matter of years, the Tamil Chettiars would seize a quarter of all Burmese agricultural land, triggering immense racial tension.

Then, in the months that followed, a massive rebellion would sweep over Burma - the largest rebellion that the Raj had ever seen.

The Saya San Rebellion

The Saya San rebellion that broke out in Burma in 1930 remains one of the most fascinating and overlooked events in the history of the Raj. Its origins remain obscure to this day, but according to the most popular narrative, the rebellion began at Myasein Taungyo Pagoda - the Pagoda of the Emerald Green Mountain.

Wearing gem-studded slippers and surrounded by an entourage carrying white umbrellas, a Buddhist monk named Saya San was said to have crowned himself king at precisely 11:33pm on 28 October 1930.

Seventeen days later, he reportedly imbibed a series of invincibility potions made of oil, lime and betel nuts, and then, entering a bamboo ‘palace’, he settled down for a royal breakfast with his five queens and four new ministers.

Seated upon a lion throne carved from banyan-wood with ruby eyes, Saya San finally declared war on the British Empire: “I, Thupannaka Galon Raja, declare war on the heathen British who have enslaved us.”

Tharrawaddy

On a pale January morning at the start of this year, I set out for Tharrawaddy, the township where it all began. The drive from Yangon took us past plains punctuated by universities, many relocated here by the junta to smother campus protests.

Towns of teak and crumbling colonial mansions passed by, their pipal groves tangled with bougainvillea. Villages of bamboo and thatch dotted the roadside, not unlike those in Bengal or Assam.

Tharrawaddy was hot, quiet and industrious. The British Gazetteers once claimed that this was a land of leopards, tigers, even rhinoceros. Today, however, you’d be lucky to see a jackal. An old teak train station stood unaltered since the 1920s, and a hulking sugar mill churned on in the background. There, beneath a sagging awning, an immigration officer and a man from Special Branch appeared, demanding papers, snapping photographs of our passports upside down.

We left the town behind, the road giving way to fields of green beans and paddy. Eventually, we arrived at a small nunnery surrounded by cane fields. In its heart stood a golden stupa—erected by rebels one year after Saya San’s execution. Here, they said, the rebellion had been declared.

U Khyaw Soe, whose grandfather had once fought with Saya San, met us in a green cap and striped shirt. Behind him, a nun rocked gently on a swing, orphans sleeping in the shade beside her.

“Even fifteen, twenty years ago there were still people alive who met Saya San,” he told us. “They’re all gone now. But we heard their stories.”

According to his grandfather, the key motivation to rebel had been the weight of rice taxes and the cruel grasp of Tamil Chettiar moneylenders. “They came in dhotis, shirtless,” he said. “If you couldn’t pay, they took your buffalo. If you still couldn’t, they took your land.” His family eventually lost everything.

There is a line in a local folksong: "Our land was taken by the Chettiars; our wives and daughters were taken by the Chettiars."

Before arriving here, Saya San had met dignitaries in Inywa, drawn up plans in secret. His ‘palace’ was just a bamboo shack, but people believed in him. “He crowned himself king,” I asked. “What did villagers think of that?”

Before coming to the village, Saya San spent a few weeks planning the rebellions with local dignitaries in Inywa. Saya San’s ‘palace’ was actually just a bamboo structure, U Khyaw Sow explains. “He had a big bamboo chair decorated with banana leaves.” I asked him what locals thought about him crowning himself king, but he brushes off the question.

"People trusted him” Khyaw Soe said plainly. “We had nothing to suspect about his dignity".

The Rebellion Spreads

Three days before Christmas in 1930, the police in Burma’s Tharrawaddy district received an anonymous tip-off that “trouble was brewing” in the region.

Tharrawaddy had been one of the worse hit areas by the Great Depression, and outrage was simmering. It had been an unusually stormy December, and as two police officers set off around the district, the undergrowth shone an emerald green.

The roads were still waterlogged, but the atmosphere was calm, and when they returned to the police station a few hours later, they were happy to report that nothing seemed amiss

That very night a mysterious group of rebels killed two headmen and a Deputy Forest Ranger and on the second night, “a Chinaman and two Indian shopkeepers” were killed.

News of these attacks first reached Rangoon, in the form of whispered rumours. Amidst Christmas preparations, there was talk of Indian troops being picked off in the Burmese jungle, one by one. “For the first hectic weeks of the outbreak” recalled a Rangoon-based British lawyer,

“the white community in the country wondered just how serious matters were, and if we were to be faced with a general rising. With the usual British disregard for grim possibilities, we carried on with a seeming contempt for events happening withing fifty miles or so of the capital. Police officers would possibly identify a score of dead rebels one morning, then dash into Rangoon to back their fancies on the race-course that same afternoon.”

But soon it was no longer possible to be complacent. Revolts began erupting across the Irrawaddy basin and within days the Indian Army was sent in to quell the rebellion.

Yet the rebellion only grew, and reports took on ever more fantastical tones. Mr Fields-Clarke of the Imperial Forest Service was said to have been murdered “so that his spirit would fight… against his former employers” and one report claimed that the rebel’s tattoos “make them invulnerable.”

All the while, the cause of the rebellion remained a mystery to officials. “Information was obtained of a mysterious leader called Saya San” read one report, “and of a headquarters, considered by the rebels as their king’s palace, in the forests ten or fifteen miles east of Tharrawaddy.” The Indian Army moved quickly, and on Christmas eve, Saya San’s ‘palace’ was raided and burnt to the ground.

Nonetheless, Saya San remained at large, and descriptions of him continued to vary wildly. The Times described an “eccentric” middle-aged man of 43 with a large scar carved into his forehead whereas The New York Times reported a “tall, muscular blond body, tattooed head to foot” “duplicating the tactics of Lawrence of Arabia.” A premature obituary announcing that “Burma’s Rebel Chief, King Golden Crow, Is Dead” only contributed to a growing myth of Saya San’s indestructibility, and soon the British press had labelled him an alchemist and practitioner of black magic.

But whatever else he was, the rebellion he sparked was real, and it terrified the colonial state. In the following months, Saya San’s revoly would evolve into the largest and most sustained rebellion in the history of the post-1857 British Raj. Almost half the districts in Burma took up arms, and Bengali, Shan and Naga revolutionaries fought alongside Burmese. Almost 7,000 Indian army troops would be deployed to suppress it.

In Prome, during the wet season of 1931, five hundred rebels ambushed British and Indian troops. In the aftermath, colonial forces responded with cruelty so gruesome it entered nationalist memory: sixteen rebel heads were cut off, wrapped in cotton blankets, and carried on foot to the police station for identification.

Durga, an amateur photographer from the city, was one of those present at the police station that morning, and he later recalled “two or three sahibs” sitting around a table, upon which sat sixteen human heads. “I had never heard or seen such a thing before” Durga recalled later. His photograph of the scene was published in the Thuriya newspaper, and overnight it became a national sensation. People across the country were outraged, and nationalist politician U Saw wrote a horrified letter to the Secretary of State.

In the wake of the beheadings, over a thousand rebels surrendered in Prome alone and shortly afterwards Saya San’s men betrayed him on the edge of the Nawngkwan jungle.

After a year of fighting, Saya San was put on trial. To everyone’s great surprise he looked nothing like the scarred “blond body” reported in the newspapers, and instead was merely “a thin, small man of medium size” with a “strong determined face,” and “glowing eyes.” Nonetheless, his trial was a sensation. Saya San’s prosecution demanded “that all those who were tattooed were rebels and should therefore be convicted, even though there was no evidence against them.” Tattoo artistry was a deep-rooted cultural obligation for many in Burma, yet the decision might have gone ahead if it wasn’t for the fact that one of the Burmese judges was himself covered in tattoos.

A month later, the British courts condemned Saya San to death. He “went up the scaffold with his head erect” wrote the judge Ba U years later. “Saya San, U Aung Hla and other rebel leaders were hanged, but the nationalism that they had helped to further refused to be buried with their bodies”.

The Legacy

The British thought the rebellion a result of Burmese ignorance and superstition. Later Burmese historians cast Saya San as a proto-nationalist hero, a martyr.

Perhaps the greatest legacy of the rebellion, however, was to kickstart a sense of Burmese nationalism independent from India.
