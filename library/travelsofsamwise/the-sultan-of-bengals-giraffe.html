In the year 1414, the Ming court stood stunned as a Bengali delegation presented tribute to the Yongle Emperor of China.

Standing before the Yongle Emperor was a towering, spotted beast with an enormously long neck. “The ministers and the people all gathered to gaze at it” wrote the court painter Shen Du, “and their joy knows no end.”

Shen Du, subsequently captured the moment in a famous silk scroll, identifying the animal as a qilin, a mythical beast whose appearance heralded a golden age.

To the modern eye, the painting is clearly a giraffe. But what is extraordinary is that the animal had not been sent from Africa, but as a diplomatic gift from the Sultan of Sonargaon, a city in the heart of the Bengal Delta. Bengal was such a globalised region at the time, that giraffes from East Africa were available to the Sultan there.

Today, Sonargaon is a quiet suburb of Dhaka, scattered with moss-covered ruins. It was capital of the Bengal Sultanate when Bengali first received recognition as an official language, and the sultanate’s subsequent capital of Gaur was briefly the fifth-most populous city in the world. Sonargaon today is a city that’s little known outside of Bangladesh. Yet it was once one of the great trading emporiums of the world.

Out of all the places that have served as capitals of independent states on the Bengal Delta, Sonargaon also holds the distinction of being the only one that is virtually in the same place as the current capital of the independent state of Bangladesh. It is also where the dying ember of the last independent state, the Sultanate of Bangala, was put out in the late 1500s by an expanding Mughal Empire, when Akbar’s famous general Man Singh defeated Isa Khan, the leader of a rebel alliance called the Baro Bhuiyan (the Twelve Chiefs), in a dramatic hand to hand duel of swords.

But Sonargaon is much older than Akbar and Isa Khan.

Ancient Sonargaon

The name Sonargaon literally means ‘Golden Village,’ and its pedigree is ancient. Situated at the confluence of the Meghna, Brahmaputra and Lakha rivers, the oldest mention of the city may come from Pliny the Elder and Ptolemy, who both spoke of a bustling trading emporium in the Bay of Bengal called Sonagoura.

The identification of the ancient port with modern Sonargaon, however, remains a point of scholarly intrigue. Dr. Sufi Mustafiz, a leading Bangladeshi archaeologist, argues that the Roman “Sonagoura” is actually Wari-Bhateswar, an iron-age site 70km away from modern Sonargaon.

After this briefest of European introductions, Sonargaon all but disappears from the historical record for the next 900 years, during which all of Bengal’s capitals - from Shoshanko’s one in Kornoshuborno, to the Pala and Sena ones at Gaur - are built further west, on higher ground, in the geological region known as Borendro, or the Barind Tract. In the central delta, the region known as Bonga (from which the names “Bangala’ and ‘Bangla’ are derived) minor kingdoms continued to use Sonargaon as a provincial capital. Then, everything changed with the arrival of the Muslim sultanates.

The Sultanate of Sonargaon

By the 14th century, Sonargaon reemerged as a vital “mint town” for the Delhi Sultanate. Its possible, of course, that Sonargaon was a new city, claiming the legacy of the older settlement to the north.

Either way, the city’s true Golden Age began in 1338. Bengal was at the time a restive province of the Delhi Sultanate, but Delhi’s grip was tenuous. A soldier named Fakhruddin Mubarak Shah seized the moment, declared independence, and turned Sonargaon into the capital of a sovereign state.

Delhi loyalists were sent to crush Fakhruddin, but they were killed in battle, and Delhi’s grip on Bengal collapsed. By the end of the year, there were three independent kingdoms in the erstwhile Delhi Sultanate province of Bengal: Sonargaon (ruled by Fakhruddin), along with Laknauti and Satgaon.

The ensuing decade was a bloody game of thrones. In 1342, the ambitious Shamsuddin Ilyas Shah united the western two kingdoms, so that only Sonargaon, shielded by its vast rivers, remained out of reach.​ But in East Bengal - modern Bangladesh - Fakhruddin continued to reign and expand, taking Chittagong and parts of Tripura into his realm.

The inevitable showdown - a brutal ten-year war - concluded in 1352. Ilyas Shah finally defeated Fakhruddin’s son, uniting the entire delta under a single banner. He chose a name for his new unified realm based on the eastern heartland he had just conquered: the Sultanate of Bangala.

An Emporium of the World

Under the Ilyas Shahi dynasty, Sonargaon transformed into a global metropolis. ​When the Moroccan traveller Ibn Battuta arrived in the city, he described Sonargaon as a river port with connections to China and the Arabia Sea, and where the price of goods was surprisingly low. A Chinese traveller, meanwhile, described the city as a “walled city with broad temples, tanks and bazaar. All goods are collected and distributed here”.

The city became the eastern terminus of the Grand Trunk Road, feeding goods and cargo all the way up to Afghanistan, and Sufis and scholars - including the brilliant Alaul Haq - migrated there en-masse, writing several influential books on jurisprudence and mysticism. The Five Auliya Mazar, in the present-day town of Shonargaon, along with the Goaldi Mosque, are legacies of this period.

Then, under Ghiyasuddin Azam Shah, Sonargaon once again became Bengal’s de-facto capital. Azam Shah sponsored construction projects as far away as Mecca, and sent an unfinished poem to Hafiz of Shiraz, inviting him to the city. Hafiz declined the offer on account of the turbulent journey from Persia to Bengal, but he completed the Sultan’s poem, and today it forms part of the Divan-e-Hafiz to this day!

‎ساقی حدیث سرو و گل و لاله می‌رود

‎وین بحث با ثلاثه غساله می‌رود

‎شکرشکن شوند همه طوطیان هند

‎زین قند پارسی که به بنگاله می‌رود

‎حافظ ز شوق مجلس سلطان غیاث دین

‎غافل مشو که کار تو از ناله می‌رود

‘O Saqi (cup-bearer)! The tale of the cypress, the rose and the tulip is going on

And with the three washers (of cups), this dispute is going on

All the parrots [poets] of Hind have become sugar-shattering [excited]

That this Persian candy [ode], to Bengal is going [on]

Of love for the assembly of the Sultan Ghiyasu-d-Din, oh Hafiz

Be not silent. For, from lamenting, your work is going on’

In addition to this, Ghiyasuddin Azam Shah was a patron of scholars and poets. His poet laureate, Shah Muhammad Sagir, wrote the famous Yusuf Zulekha at the Sultan’s request.

Meanwhile, the Hindu poet, Krittibas Ojha translated the Ramayana into Bengali for the first time. His Krittivasi Ramayan may have been the single most popular single book in early modern Bengal.

Sultan Ghiyassudin Azam Shah also seems to have been the first Sultan of Bengal to send envoys to the Ming Court of China. This early Bengali delegation seems to have departed on private merchant ships some time between 1404-8 and arrived in the Ming court laden with silks.

There were no giraffes recorded on this journey, yet a carving on the Konarak Sun Temple in Orissa from decades earlier shows that the animal was already a familiar sight in the rapidly globalising region.

Whatever the case, over the following decade, half a dozen Bengali delegations would set sail for China. The largest, which arrived in Nanjing on 17 February 1409, saw no less than 230 Bengali diplomats pay tribute to the Emperor. Then, in 1414, the Sultan of Bengal’s giraffe arrived in Nanjing.

It would be the last delegation to China in six years, however, for the Bengal Sultanate would soon descend into civil war instigated by an upstart called Raja Ganesh

Raja Ganesh

Raja Ganesh’s rise would unwittingly begin Bengal’s transformation into a Muslim-majority region. Indeed today, Bengalis are the second-largest Muslim ethnic group in the world after Arabs. Yet when Turkic rulers first brought Islam to the Bengal Delta, they came as a foreign colonial elite, living out of cantonment towns. To the local population, the Ilyas Shahis and other Muslim elites were simply Turuskas (Turks), not fellow countrymen.

So it was that, in 1415, a Hindu courtier named Raja Ganesh staged a coup against his Turkic overlords. However, facing an imminent invasion from the neighbouring Sultanate of Jaunpur, Ganesh was forced into a desperate diplomatic gamble. To pacify his rivals and gain religious legitimacy, he struck a deal with a powerful Sufi saint: Ganesh would remain the power behind the throne, but his son, Jadu, would convert to Islam and rule as Sultan.

Soon after taking over Bengal, however, the neighbouring Sultans of Jaunpur threatened to invade. To stop the invasion, he hatched a plot. Pretend to convert his son to Islam, rule over the Sultanate as regent, and then take his son back into the Hindu fold afterwards.

Jadu took the name Jalaluddin Muhammad Shah, becoming the first native Bengali Muslim to rule the delta.

Coins struck under his reign display a syncretic identity: on one side his proclaims himself the ‘Caliph of Allah” (a bold claim even his Turkic predecessors would have hesitated to make). On the other side is an image of a lion - the vehicle of his ancestral Goddess Devi.

Jallaludin stopped patronising Turkic architects, funding indigenous ones instead. His mosques took on a distinctively Bengali idiom - with sloping terracotta rooves, and minarets directly inspired by the temple architecture he grew up with.

Perhaps most importantly, he elevated the Bengali language. Previous Hindu and Buddhist kings had used Sanskrit or Pali for liturgy, whilst Muslim ones used Arabic and Persian. Now Jalaluddin encouraged Bengali to become the language of the mosque. By translating the faith into the local idiom, he made Islam feel indigenous rather than imposed.

Over the following centuries, this more vernacular form of Islam spread through the delta’s villages. It was this unique cultural shift (along with all sorts of later events) that eventually led to the Muslim-majority identity of East Bengal, and thus the the birth of modern Bangladesh.

A Second Giraffe

During all this upheaval, Bengal’s links to China began to fray. When Jalaluddin sent his own envoy to the Ming court in 1420, the Emperor (perhaps wary of the political instability) refused to recognize him as a “King,” dismissing him as a mere “chieftain.”

The diplomatic silence lasted for nearly two decades, until the old Ilyas Shahi dynasty was restored under Nasir al-Din Mahmud in 1434. To signal that Sonargaon was once again stable and open for business, the Sultan performed a spectacular diplomatic encore.

Thus, on 19 October 1438, a second Bengali giraffe arrived at the Ming court, accompanied by white parrots and exotic doves. The Ming chroniclers recorded a grand banquet held in the emissaries’ honour, where they were draped in gold-embroidered robes.

Sonargaon’s Decline and the Birth of Dhaka

It is difficult to tell today just how central to the global economy Sonargaon once was. Much of the city seems to have been razed to the ground by the Mughals after Sonargaon became home to Bengal’s anti-Mughal resistance – the Baro Bhaiyan.

In 1610, the Mughals then moved the capital to Dhaka. The shifting rivers eventually silted up Sonargaon’s port, making it impossible for the great merchant ships to dock.

Sonargaon continued to be the centre of the muslin trade however and this allowed a brief revival in the towns fortunes in the 19th century. Hindu merchants involved in the cotton and muslin trade settled there and built a sophisticated township over the older, ruined settlements.

Panam Nagar, also known as Panam City, is a well-preserved stretch of approximately 52 dilapidated brick mansions lining a single street. Most of these houses were Hindu, and several temples survive in the area. Built in the chala style, they are all located on the second floor of their respective houses.

This may be because of flooding, but may also be related to the 17th century trend for temples on the second floor of a bazaar. This can be found in Delhi, Lahore and several other Mughal cities.

The greatest house of all is the Bara Sardar Bari, built in 1901 by the zamindar. This colonial city’s was abruptly abandoned during the 1947 Partition of India. Later, communal riots in 1964 pushed the last remaining Hindu merchants out of town.
