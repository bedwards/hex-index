The Great Heist of Santa Claus

This is one of those stories that you just can’t make up. Long before Coca Cola advertising gave him a nice red and white hat, Father Christmas was actually a real Christian Saint - St Nicholas, or ‘Sintaklaas’ in Dutch. He was renowned for his generosity and gift giving, and after he died, his tomb in Myra (Modern Turkey) became a site for Christian pilgrimage.

By around 1000AD, a rogue group of Puglian sailors decided to stage a daring heist: to steal the mortal remains of Santa Claus.

The fishermen succeeded, much to the upset of the shrine’s Greek priests, and managed to bring back his relics all the way back home to Italy. The Pope was quite impressed and built a massive basillica in Bari to house the relics.

This is just one of many fascinating stories behind the man we now call Santa Claus. His story is far FAR more interesting than I think we all realise. And to really get a sense of the man, we need to go back to Ancient Lycia.

Greeks and Persians in Ancient Lycia

Santa - or St Nicholas - was born around 270AD in Lycia, on the southern coast of modern Turkey. This ancient region is first recorded by the Hittites and Egyptians in 1500BC as Lykka or Lukka, and was populated by speakers of the Luwic languages. Their own name for their region was Trm̃mis.

Like anywhere on the Mediterranean, this was always a crossroads of cultures of sorts, and his hometown's history is utterly fascinating.

In 546BC, Lycia was conquered by the Achaemenid Persian Empire, just before the more famous Achaemenid assault on Greece, immortalised in movies like 300.

Yet Lycias integration into the Achaemenid Empire would have an extraordinary impact on the region. The cities of Lycia, including Xanthos, Myra, and Pinara, quickly became wealthy, as demonstrated by the elaborate tombs that emerged around the 5th century BC. Indeed Lycians soon began translating their wooden architecture into stone, cutting into the soft sandstone.

Early Indian rock cut architecture, like the Ajivika cave monastery at Barabar, bears fascinating similarities to the Lycian rock cut tombs. Indeed so striking are the similarities, that many scholars have attempted to identify potential links between the two.

One such potential link is through the now-lost timber architecture of Achaemenid Punjab, Sindh and Afghanistan. There is a huge amount of scholarship on the influence of Achaemenid Persian sculpture and architecture on the Mauryans: the Ashoka pillars seem to echo the capitals of Persepolis, the starving Buddha of Lahore has fire worship imagery on its base, and the Bhaja and Karla caves have Achaemenid bull rider imagery. When the Mauryans conquered the Indus Valley and Afghanistan, it had been under Achaemenid rule for centuries. The Persian and Indic worlds have always had a much closer proximity than modern borders suggest.

Potentially, therefore, we are witnessing Achaemenid influence heading in both directions - east to India and west to Lycia.

The emergence of the Achaemenid Empire forced Lycia’s ruling elite to seek new syncretic forms of representation to assert their power.

One of the earliest and most celebrated examples of this synthesis is the Harpy Tomb at Xanthos. The tomb appears influenced by Zoroastrian architecture but the sculptural reliefs adorning the tomb, now housed in the British Museum, are executed in the Late Archaic Greek style.

The iconography is centered around winged figures, which art historians now largely identify as Sirens - beings believed to carry the souls of the dead to the Underworld - rather than the mythological Harpies.

Most archeologists who visit Lycia today focus on the Greek Hellenistic elements of its art. Yet we should really see it as one of the great epicentres of Achaemenid Persian art.

Outside of Iran, there is probably no greater density of monuments reflecting the world of Darius and Cyrus, and certainly no better example of Achaemenid sub-imperial art in existence.

The Nereid Monument, constructed for the Lycian dynast Arbinas around 390BC, is the largest and most elaborate surviving Lycian tomb from Xanthos. Its structure revolutionized Lycian funerary architecture by synthesizing the local style with an imported Greek Temple form.

Its frieze, however, shows a Persian official, potentially a Persian satrap from Persepolis, receiving an embassy, with a young servant holding a parasol, of royalty or high authority in the Indo-Persian tradition. Fascinatingly, the monument may have inspired the construction of the later Mausoleum of Halicarnassus, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World.

Another fascinating tomb is the tomb of Payava from about 375BC. Payava identifies himself as “by race a Lycian”, but one in the employment of the Persians.

One side of the tomb overtly displays Persian allegiance, depicting a dignified seated figure in full Persian dress - likely the satrap Autophradates, who is named in the inscription - receiving a delegation. This scene publicly acknowledges Payava’s integration into the Achaemenid imperial hierarchy. Simultaneously, the other sides of the tomb feature scenes rooted in Hellenic culture, such as an athlete being crowned and complex battle scenes involving cavalry and foot soldiers.

So often we imagine the battle of Marathon as a clash of civilisations. But in Lycia, we see a fascinating Greco-Persian syncretism, a syncretism that the myth of St Nicholas (Santa) was heavily influenced by.

The defeat of the Achaemenid Empire by Alexander the Great in 334 BCE ushered in a new era of extensive Greek cultural penetration. Due to significant Greek migration, Lycia underwent rapid Hellenization under Macedonian hegemony, though it retained periods of freedom.

The establishment of the Lycian League in the 2nd century BCE, a federation of autonomous city-states - including Xanthos, Pinara, and Myra - is recognized by scholars as the first known democratic union in history. Later, Lycia was transformed into a Roman province, and an enormous amphitheatre was constructed in Myra, with a capacity of 11,000 spectators.

A Saint Called Nikolaos

Myra is nestled between the Turquoise Coast and the Bey mountains, at the westernmost extremity of the Taurus mountains. The landscape around is full of pine trees, olive groves, the call of cicadas, and the hills around are dotted with extraordinary Lycian rock-cut tombs.

The scruffy modern town of Demre has grown around the old archeological site, and is full of concrete blocks, shopping malls and the occasional mosque.

It thus seems rather incongruous when you come across a series of statues of Santa Claus.

St Nicholas, or Nikolaos as he would have pronounced his name, probably lived here from 280-352AD. A local priest, Jeremy Seal writes that he “proved pre-naturally miraculous, leaving his mother sterile from the moment of his conception as if nature, in Symeons words, having lavished such bounty, could offer no more.”

He is said to have been imprisoned under Diocletian for being a Christian. Yet he got away lightly, living to see the legalisation of Christianity under Constantine, and dying in his own bed.

He died on 6 December and was placed in a sarcophagus in Byzantine Lycia around 352AD (there are a few traditions that dispute the year, although none dispute the day of his death.) Soon after his death, a church was built in his honour.

The most famous story tells of him saving the daughters of a destitute nobleman in Patara from prostitution. Each night, before the nobleman is about to sell one of his daughters into prostitution, St Nicholas creeps up and throws a bag of gold into the house at midnight. This happens three times, with three bags of St Nicholas’ gold being used to save three daughters from prostitution. This story sets the basis for much of the later Santa legend: “an arrival at night, bearing secret gifts to the young.”

The Church of St Nicholas

The Basilica of St. Nicholas is actually one of the best preserved Early Byzantine churches in existence. The initial structure was an ancient East Roman basilica, constructed around AD 520, funded or supported by Emperor Justinian I. It was purposefully built on the foundations of an older Christian church where St. Nicholas had served, and directly over his original burial site.

The overall style adheres to the continuity between Late Roman and Early Byzantine architecture, utilizing arches, vaults, and domes on a grand scale. Major renovations and redecoration occurred during the 11th century, when the church functioned as an important pilgrimage center.

Myra was earlier a centre for Poseidon worship, and a temple of Poseidon survives at Andriake. Interestingly, St Nicholas would also take on many of the attributes of Poseidon, becoming a patron saint for sailors and the “deliverer of pilgrims to the Holy Land by Sea.” His feast day, December 6th, is also speculated to have coincided with ancient pre-Christian festivals honoring Poseidon.

Early testimonies of Nick don’t mention any sailing experience, so its safe to say that his reverence as a protector of sailors is the result of Christian-Pagan syncretism. The Greek Reporter notes,

“A further interesting connection between St Nicholas and Poseidon lies written in the Books XI and XXIII of Homer’s Odyssey. In the epic, Odysseus is instructed by the blind prophet Tiresias to take an oar (Winnowing Oar) from his ship and to walk inland until he finds a “land that knows nothing of the sea”, where the oar would be mistaken for a winnowing fan. At this point, he is to offer a sacrifice to Poseidon, and then at last his journeys would be over. Ιn the Greek folkore stories, St Nicholas was a professional sailor who grew tired of the sea and its plagues and decided to live away from it. He then took an oar and started walking inland looking for a place where its people would not know what an oar is. Finally he reached a place where people described the oar as plain wood, and upon this, Nicholas decided to stay there and become a hermit. The cathedral of St Nicholas in Didyma of Argolis is built in the same place where geographer Pausanias located the once temple dedicated to Poseidon. In fact, one of the cathedral’s walls is the ancient one surviving time and erosion to date. Recently, archaeologists unearthed in Sozopol, Bulgaria, the well-preserved remains of an ancient altar possibly belonging to Poseidon’s temple. The ancient ruins are located only a short distance away from the Christian church of St. Nicholas.”

To an extraordinary extent then, the cult of St Nicholas - today known as Santa Claus - is partially a continuity of the Cult of Poseidon. As we shall see, however, modern Santa would also adopt aspects of the Norse cult of Odin.

Santa Goes Global

Soon there were Churches to St Nicholas in both Rome and Constantinople. A fresco of the Three Daughters legend survives in Rome's 8th century Santa Maria Antiqua church.

From there, the cult of St Nicholas spread up the rivers. By 882 there was a church to St Nick in Kiev, on the Dnieper River, a church “intended as the mother church of the Russian State”
