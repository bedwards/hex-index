Some Guy cuts through the noise of modern outrage by arguing that the true crisis facing the nation isn't a lack of anger, but a collapse of the specific courage required to remain calm while facing pain and death. In an era where social media algorithms reward panic, the author suggests that the willingness to endure discomfort without losing one's mind is the missing lubricant that keeps the gears of society from grinding to a halt.

The Anatomy of Calm

The piece opens with a visceral recounting of Navy SEAL drown-proofing, a training exercise where candidates must repair scuba gear while being forced underwater until they nearly black out. Some Guy uses this extreme scenario to illustrate a paradoxical truth: "The only way he's coming out of that swimming pool is as a Navy Seal or a corpse." The author argues that the candidate's success came only after he accepted the possibility of death, which paradoxically removed the fear that caused his earlier failures. This framing is striking because it inverts the standard self-help narrative; instead of fighting fear, the author suggests that surrendering to the reality of mortality is the only path to mastery.

"Paradoxically, this willingness to die leads him to have a much easier time completing the course. It's barely even hard now that his fear is gone."

The commentary draws a sharp line between this stoic resolve and the current American temperament. Some Guy observes that if society had a gauge for this specific type of courage, it would be flashing emergency lights. The author admits to their own struggle, confessing that while they have no fear of pain, the "calm part escapes me," leading to "furious reveries" when witnessing injustice. This vulnerability grounds the high-concept argument in human reality, acknowledging that the ideal of stoicism is often at war with the biological instinct to rage.

Critics might note that equating the controlled stress of military selection with the chaotic violence of civilian life risks oversimplifying the psychological toll of trauma. Not everyone can access a "switch" to turn off fear, and for many, the trauma of systemic violence is not a test to be passed but a wound to be healed. Yet, the author's point remains potent: without a collective capacity to remain steady, the machinery of democracy becomes brittle.

The Cost of Leadership Vacuums

The argument shifts from internal discipline to external responsibility, specifically targeting the failure of public figures to model restraint. Some Guy writes, "America drowns in shitty demure 'I'm just some guy' and 'I have problems too' leadership." The author contends that when leaders claim to speak for a community, they inherit a duty to guide that community toward good, rather than chasing social media engagement or political points.

This section addresses the recent killing of Renee Good, a mother who was shot during a confrontation involving Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agents. The author expresses deep sorrow for the loss of life, noting, "Whatever else she believed, whatever else she did in her life, she was doing what she thought was right and she was someone's mother." The commentary refuses to reduce Good to a political pawn, emphasizing instead the human tragedy of a mother's death and the disturbing comfort social media users have found in dissecting her corpse for ideological scores.

"It's making us sick. This is the same darkness people indulged in the middle ages at public executions. Except now it's public executions every day, as many as you can see on your social media scroll."

The author connects this modern spectacle to historical precedents, noting that the public's appetite for violence mirrors the grim entertainment of medieval executions. This comparison is effective because it strips away the illusion of progress; the medium has changed from the gallows to the smartphone, but the underlying human impulse to consume suffering remains. The piece suggests that the solution lies not in more outrage, but in a return to "clear-headed discernment," a concept deeply rooted in Stoic philosophy where one must separate what can be controlled from what cannot.

The Paradox of American Abundance

Some Guy then pivots to a defense of American assertiveness, arguing that the nation's abundance is a direct result of its citizens' willingness to stand their ground. "We are the abundant nation of the Denny's Grand Slam and the Big Gulp Slurpee because we are the land of the free and the home of the brave," the author writes. The core claim is that the American capacity to protest and resist tyranny is what prevents the slide into authoritarianism, even if the methods are sometimes messy or inarticulate.

The author acknowledges the complexity of immigration enforcement, admitting that while they do not want ICE agents killed, the agency's actions—such as abducting a minor at a Target and failing to return him—invite a "social cost." The argument is that when an organization acts with maximum invasiveness, it must expect pushback. "Nobody gets to do that with no pushback," Some Guy asserts, framing resistance not as lawlessness but as a necessary check on power.

"The capacity of Americans to protest almost anything is a constant and powerful message to leaders in our country and around the world to say, 'hey, we will not tolerate this.'"

However, the author distinguishes between the right to resist and the need for tactical discipline. The piece suggests that while the impulse to fight is vital, the execution often lacks the "calm" discussed earlier. The author proposes that leaders from across the political spectrum should have urged greater caution during confrontations, such as instructing protesters to comply with arrest orders while maintaining their dissent. This creates a tension: how does one maintain the fierce spirit of resistance without succumbing to the panic that leads to tragedy?

The Path Forward

In the conclusion, Some Guy calls for a personal reset, urging readers to find fault in allies and virtue in opponents more often than they prefer. The author admits to personal struggles, including the weight of their son's autism diagnosis, and uses this to frame a broader moral imperative: "those with the best excuse have the power to set the greatest example." The piece ends on a note of quiet determination, suggesting that the way through the current darkness is to "speak true things whether or not they make anyone happy."

"The way through that isn't to panic or to give into fear. It's to be very calm and reset ourselves and the world around us, moving steadily even as consequences loom larger and larger."

This final appeal to personal responsibility is the piece's strongest element. It avoids the trap of blaming a single administration or political party, instead focusing on the institutional dynamics and individual behaviors that sustain a healthy society. By weaving together the discipline of military training, the tragedy of recent violence, and the philosophy of Stoicism, the author offers a roadmap that is both demanding and deeply humane.

Bottom Line

Some Guy's most compelling argument is that the nation's fragility stems not from a lack of passion, but from a deficit of the specific courage required to remain calm in the face of mortality. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on individual stoicism to solve systemic failures, potentially underestimating the structural forces that drive panic and violence. Readers should watch for how this call for "clear-headed discernment" translates into concrete policy changes or community actions in the coming months.
