This piece from Laura Rozen for Diplomatic delivers a jarring but vital correction to the prevailing narrative: the public credit for ending the hostage crisis belongs not to the Israeli Prime Minister, but to a former US president whose personal intervention reportedly broke a two-year deadlock. Rozen synthesizes on-the-ground reporting from Tel Aviv and Haaretz to argue that the emotional weight of the moment has shifted the political landscape in Israel, creating a fragile new reality where the war's end is declared by Washington, not Jerusalem.

The Weight of the Morning

Rozen anchors her analysis in the visceral experience of the release, drawing on Marc Schulman's account of Hostage Square. The scene described is not one of cautious relief, but of overwhelming, almost disbelieving joy. "I woke up an hour before my alarm, and decided to take Milo out for a walk through Hostage Square at 6:00 in the morning," Schulman writes. "What I saw was unlike anything I had witnessed there before. The square was full, completely packed, at that early hour." Rozen uses this imagery to establish the stakes: this was a moment where the nation collectively held its breath, only to exhale in unison.

The physical condition of the returned hostages is a critical detail often glossed over in high-level policy summaries. Rozen highlights that "overall, the released hostages appeared to be in better physical condition than many had feared," noting that "all were able to walk unassisted." This observation matters because it counters the grim expectations that had built up over two years. However, the author is careful not to let the celebration obscure the ongoing tragedy. As Schulman adds, "The recovery of the remains of the deceased hostages is still underway, a painful process that will likely continue for some time." This duality—the joy of the living and the grief for the dead—is the emotional core of the coverage, and Rozen handles it with the necessary gravity.

"For the first time, we can say that no living hostages remain in the hands of Hamas."

A Shift in Political Credit

The most provocative element of Rozen's piece is her focus on the attribution of success. While the Israeli government orchestrated the final logistics, the public sentiment described by Amir Tibon suggests a different architect of the deal. Rozen writes, "The Israeli public largely credits him –not Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu – with the achievement of bringing back the hostages." This is a significant departure from standard diplomatic reporting, which usually centers the sitting administration. Rozen argues that the personal insistence of the former US leader was the catalyst that finally moved the needle after months of stalled talks.

This framing challenges the conventional wisdom that the current Israeli leadership is the sole driver of national security outcomes. Rozen notes that the former president "will need to ensure that Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu does not try to return to war," highlighting a potential fracture in the alliance. The tension is palpable: while the public celebrates a definitive end, the political leadership in Jerusalem has offered contradictory signals. Rozen points out that "so far, Trump and Netanyahu have offered contradicting answers" regarding whether the war is truly over.

The Crucial Question

The piece turns on a specific, unscripted moment that Rozen identifies as more significant than any formal speech. When asked by reporters if the war was finally over, the former president's response was immediate and absolute. "Trump said before taking off to Israel that the agreement signed by Israel and Hamas represented the end of the war," Rozen explains. But the real test came in the hallway of the Knesset. "When Trump, walking next to Netanyahu, was presented with this question, his answer was a short and decisive yes." Rozen argues that this off-the-cuff declaration carries more weight than the formal terms of the deal because it represents a direct challenge to the Prime Minister's stated intentions.

Critics might note that relying on the personal assurances of a former leader, rather than binding legal mechanisms or the current administration's policy, introduces a high degree of uncertainty. If the executive branch in Washington changes or if the former president's influence wanes, the guarantee of peace could evaporate. Furthermore, the article focuses heavily on the political credit, potentially underplaying the complex humanitarian conditions on the ground that will determine whether the ceasefire holds.

"This matters more than anything in his written remarks – as long as he actually means it."

Bottom Line

Rozen's strongest contribution is her ability to capture the immediate, human shift in Israeli public sentiment, where the credit for peace has been reassigned from the sitting Prime Minister to a former US president. The piece's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the personal will of that former leader to prevent a return to conflict, a strategy that lacks institutional safeguards. Readers should watch closely to see if the "decisive yes" given in the Knesset hallways translates into a sustained policy of non-intervention, or if the contradictions between the two leaders will reignite the war.
