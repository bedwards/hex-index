In a digital landscape where public figures usually curate their image through polished press releases, Bentham's Bulldog offers something far more radical: a raw, unvarnished audit of their own intellectual blind spots. This piece is not a defensive maneuver but a strategic dissection of why the author's most controversial claims—from the ethics of insect welfare to the probability of miracles—resonate with some and repel others. For the busy reader, this is a masterclass in intellectual honesty, revealing how a philosopher navigates the friction between rigorous utilitarian calculus and the messy, emotional realities of human morality.

The Calculus of Criticism

Bentham's Bulldog begins by dismantling the traditional Machiavellian choice between being loved or feared, opting instead for a utilitarian metric of being "universally loved" while acknowledging the failure of that project. The author's investigative method is refreshingly direct: rather than speculating, they asked detractors for their grievances. The responses reveal a deep tension between the author's high-velocity output and the community's desire for depth. One critic notes, "I like your blog a lot, but if I had to explain why people don't like you…" before pivoting to complaints about the lack of theoretical grounding in charitable distribution and the production of posts that merely reinforce existing beliefs.

This admission of imperfection is the piece's strongest asset. Bentham's Bulldog concedes, "I'm not a perfect utilitarian robot, nor are most people. I don't always do what's best." This humility is crucial; it prevents the piece from becoming a lecture and turns it into a dialogue. However, the author's defense of their political writing—admitting it is "the least careful" yet most popular—raises a question about the trade-off between engagement and rigor. Critics might argue that prioritizing popularity in political discourse undermines the very intellectual integrity the author claims to value.

"I don't think you can expect every article to radically upend a person's worldview. I write a blog post every day—they can't be expected all to change people's lives."

The Weight of Confidence

The commentary then shifts to the author's controversial confidence in specific philosophical frameworks, particularly the Self-Indication Assumption (SIA) in anthropics and the ethics of wild animal suffering. Bentham's Bulldog addresses the criticism of being "far too confident" by pointing to a lack of substantive counter-arguments from critics. "Basically all of the pieces that criticize the SIA just give that one objection," the author argues, suggesting that the criticism is stale rather than the theory is flawed. This framing is effective in shifting the burden of proof back onto the skeptics, yet it risks appearing dismissive of legitimate philosophical dissent.

The discussion on wild animal suffering is particularly provocative. Bentham's Bulldog holds a 70% confidence that wild animals live net-negative lives, a view that clashes with the "plurality opinion" of other effective altruists. The author defends this stance by noting a "clear bias towards normality" in the field, arguing that the psychological discomfort of reducing nature leads to a middling, unconvincing consensus. This echoes the historical tension found in The Prince, where Machiavelli often had to advocate for actions that were morally repugnant to the public but necessary for a specific outcome. Here, the "necessary" outcome is the reduction of suffering, even if it means dismantling the natural order.

Critics might note that a 70% confidence level in such a complex, data-sparse field could be seen as overreach, especially when the proposed solution involves "reducing nature at the margins." The author's willingness to entertain irreversible actions, even while claiming they are not irreversible, highlights the high stakes of effective altruism when applied to the natural world.

The Style of Engagement

Perhaps the most revealing section addresses the author's writing style, which critics describe as "glib," "sarcastic," and occasionally "playing to the crowd." Bentham's Bulldog acknowledges this aesthetic friction, comparing their style to Michael Huemer's "breezy and confident" approach. "I agree I'm sometimes a bit glib and sarcastic and that some people dislike this style aesthetically," the author admits. This self-awareness is vital; it suggests the tone is a deliberate choice to cut through noise, even if it alienates those seeking a more traditional academic demeanor.

The author also tackles the accusation of inconsistency, specifically regarding the belief in a God who creates a multiverse while allowing suffering. Bentham's Bulldog reframes this not as a contradiction but as a complex theological strategy: "If things get screwed up, it might be bad to create more sentient beings because their lives aren't sufficiently good." This attempt to reconcile seemingly opposing views demonstrates a sophisticated, if controversial, mental model. However, the reliance on such complex resolutions can feel like "ad hoc" reasoning to those who prefer more straightforward ethical frameworks.

"I could have written 'because I think I am correct,' and it would have had the same meaning, while adding extra words and seeming less confident."

Bottom Line

Bentham's Bulldog succeeds in transforming a potential PR crisis into a compelling defense of a rigorous, if uncomfortable, philosophical worldview. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to retreat into vague platitudes, instead engaging directly with the specific, often harsh, critiques of its audience. Its biggest vulnerability remains the tension between the author's high-velocity, confident style and the community's demand for nuance and slower, more deliberate engagement. For the reader, the takeaway is clear: in the pursuit of truth, the cost of being universally loved is often the loss of being universally understood.
