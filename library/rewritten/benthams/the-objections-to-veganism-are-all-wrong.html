Most ethical debates about food stall at the question of taste or tradition, but this piece from Bentham's Bulldog refuses to accept those boundaries, arguing instead that the standard objections to veganism are not just weak, but logically incoherent. The author strips away the comforting narratives we tell ourselves about "natural" diets to reveal a stark arithmetic of suffering that demands immediate attention from anyone who values consistency in their moral reasoning.

The Arithmetic of Suffering

Bentham's Bulldog anchors the entire argument in a single, brutal statistic: the overwhelming majority of animals consumed are not raised in idyllic pastures but in industrial facilities designed for maximum efficiency and minimum cost. The author writes, "Almost all animals are factory farmed," and proceeds to detail the reality of that system with unflinching clarity. They describe animals "locked in a cage where they can't move, chronically sleep deprived, mutilated by debeaking and dehorning... and killed by being dragged upside down by one foot, stunned, and then sliced in the throat."

This framing is effective because it bypasses the abstract debate about animal rights and focuses on the concrete reality of pain. The author posits a simple ethical principle: "You shouldn't cause others lots of pain and suffering for the sake of comparatively minor benefits to yourself." This lands with force because it mirrors the way we treat other sentient beings, like dogs, in our own homes. If we would never burn a dog for the slight benefit of a better smell, the author argues, we cannot justify doing the same to a pig for the sake of a burger. The comparison is jarring, but the logic holds: "There is not a morally relevant difference between smell and taste."

Critics might argue that this utilitarian calculus ignores the complexity of human biology or the cultural significance of meat, but the author anticipates this by noting that intelligence is not a prerequisite for moral protection. "The badness of pain doesn't come from one's intelligence," Bentham's Bulldog asserts, drawing a parallel to how we treat human infants and those with severe cognitive disabilities. Just as we do not torture a baby because they cannot understand morality, we should not torture a pig because it lacks human-level cognition.

It is wrong, for the same reason, to hurt animals a lot so that we can enjoy the taste of their flesh.

Deconstructing the Excuses

The piece then systematically dismantles the most common defenses of meat consumption, treating them not as valid counter-arguments but as logical fallacies. When addressing the claim that "it's natural to eat meat," the author points out the absurdity of using nature as a moral guide. "It is natural to die of disease at a young age, but that is a bad thing," they write, noting that we also reject other natural behaviors like tribalism and violence because we have evolved to be better than our instincts.

Similarly, the argument that "other animals eat meat" is dismissed as a category error. Bentham's Bulldog notes that while a lion killing a gazelle is not immoral because the lion lacks moral agency, humans are different. "We know better," the author writes. "Lions are also obligate carnivores—they have to eat meat to survive. We don't, so you shouldn't straightforwardly extrapolate from how lions should behave to how we should do."

The environmental argument receives similar scrutiny. Many believe that plant-based diets are worse for the planet because of shipping emissions, but the author counters that "emissions from transporting food are dwarfed by the emissions from the food itself." In fact, the piece highlights a startling fact: "Animal agriculture produces a similar amount of greenhouse gasses as all transport combined." This reframes the environmental debate, shifting the focus from the distance food travels to the sheer inefficiency of feeding crops to animals only to eat the animals.

The Scale of the Crisis

Perhaps the most chilling section of the piece is the quantification of the problem. Bentham's Bulldog moves beyond the individual act of eating to the systemic scale of the industry. They note that "we torture and kill around 2 billion animals per week." This number is so large it becomes almost abstract, yet the author insists on its weight: "We inflict more suffering on animals, in a few years of factory farming, than in humans throughout all of history."

The author also tackles the "no effect" argument—the idea that an individual's purchase doesn't change production levels. They argue that this is a dangerous gamble. "For this to justify eating meat, you need to be extremely confident that it's right," Bentham's Bulldog writes. "You don't get to seriously risk torturing others for a hamburger." Even if the probability of one meal causing one animal to be born and killed is low, the expected value of that risk is too high to ignore.

We inflict more suffering on animals, in a few years of factory farming, than in humans throughout all of history.

The piece also touches on the historical context of intensive animal farming, noting that this is a relatively recent phenomenon. While humans have hunted for millennia, the industrialization of slaughter is a modern invention that has severed the connection between the eater and the killed, allowing for a scale of suffering that would have been unimaginable in previous eras. This context strengthens the argument that the current system is not an inevitable part of the "circle of life" but a specific, avoidable policy choice.

Bottom Line

Bentham's Bulldog's strongest move is refusing to let the reader hide behind "edge cases" like happy cows or indigenous hunting; the argument is that the overwhelming reality of factory farming makes the default position of eating meat ethically indefensible. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a strict utilitarian framework that some may find too rigid, particularly regarding the "no effect" objection, but the sheer volume of evidence regarding animal suffering makes the conclusion difficult to escape. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: the debate is not about whether animals feel pain, but whether we have the courage to align our daily habits with the moral truth that pain is bad, regardless of the species feeling it.
