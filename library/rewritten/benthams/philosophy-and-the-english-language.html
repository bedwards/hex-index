In a landscape often dominated by ideological shouting matches, Bentham's Bulldog makes a startlingly simple claim: the most effective way to obscure moral horror is not through lies, but through vague, abstract language. This piece argues that when we stop naming specific acts of violence—like suffocating shrimp or gassing pigs—and start using euphemisms like "culling" or "welfare," we disarm our own moral intuitions. For the busy reader, this is not just a lesson in writing style; it is a diagnostic tool for spotting how power protects itself by making the concrete invisible.

The Architecture of Euphemism

Bentham's Bulldog opens by channeling George Orwell, noting that "prose consists less and less of words chosen for the sake of their meaning, and more and less of phrases tacked together like the sections of a prefabricated hen-house." The author uses this to frame a broader critique of how language functions in defense of the status quo. The argument posits that nebulous writing is a shield; it allows society to discuss widespread, entrenched practices without confronting the specific, ugly mechanics of those practices.

The piece offers a stark example: the meat industry. Bentham's Bulldog writes, "It is very easy to defend pork. It is a lot harder to defend gassing pigs to death." By swapping the concrete reality of death for the abstract noun "pork," the moral weight of the act evaporates. This linguistic sleight of hand is not accidental. As the author notes, "Those phrases maintain a level of lofty abstraction that does not require one to grapple with the specific wrongs of the practice." This is a powerful observation that connects directly to the history of political rhetoric, echoing the themes in Orwell's "Politics and the English Language," where inflated style is described as a way to make murder look respectable.

"A mass of Latin words falls upon the facts like soft snow, blurring the outlines and covering up all the details."

The author applies this lens to the debate over animal welfare, specifically targeting the dismissal of shrimp suffering. Critics often mock the idea of caring for crustaceans, but Bentham's Bulldog argues this mockery relies on abstraction. "Shrimp welfare is an abstraction," they write. "When one hears the phrase, nothing very concrete comes to mind other than the silliness of those bleeding hearts who care about shrimp." The author contends that if critics were forced to visualize the specific act—"spending a single dollar to prevent over 10,000 shrimp from slowly suffocating to death"—the dismissal would be much harder to sustain.

The Failure of Intuition at Scale

The commentary then shifts to a more complex philosophical problem: why human intuition fails when dealing with large numbers. Bentham's Bulldog argues that our moral compass is calibrated for individual stories, not statistical aggregates. We can easily feel the horror of a single person being stabbed, but we cannot intuitively grasp the scale of a million deaths. This cognitive gap is exploited by those who wish to defend harmful systems.

The author illustrates this with a thought experiment involving remotes and buttons. If pressing a "human" button extends a life by one day, and a "chicken" button frees a bird for a year, most people choose the chicken. Yet, if you repeat this 14,000 times, you have traded a human life for chicken freedom. "Our intuitions about big numbers are hazy," Bentham's Bulldog writes. "We know that it's very bad for people to die; because we underestimate the badness of chickens being trapped for 14,000 years, we don't see that it is worse." This insight is crucial for understanding why movements like Effective Altruism (EA) often face irrational hostility; they are asking people to trust math over gut feeling, a difficult sell when the gut cannot visualize the magnitude of the problem.

Critics might note that relying on cold calculation can sometimes strip morality of its emotional resonance, potentially alienating the very people needed to drive change. However, Bentham's Bulldog suggests that the alternative—ignoring the numbers—is a far greater moral failure. The author warns that "ethical intuitions fail us when we try to intuitively grasp global problems where large numbers are involved," making it essential to rely on "arguments from simple principles" rather than vague feelings.

The Weaponization of Abstraction

Finally, the piece turns its gaze to the critics of Effective Altruism, accusing them of using the same abstract tactics they claim to oppose. Bentham's Bulldog points out that detractors often ignore the concrete good EA does—saving children from dying—and instead attack "lofty and relatively formless ideological pronouncements." The author cites Emile Torres, who grouped EA with six other disparate movements under the "TESCREAL" bundle (transhumanism, extropianism, singularitarianism, cosmism, rationalism, effective altruism, and longtermism). This grouping, the author argues, is a rhetorical trick to create a straw man.

"It is easier to criticize a movement that does lots of good things if one ignores what is done and instead sticks to lofty and relatively formless ideological pronouncements," Bentham's Bulldog asserts. The author compares this to a polemic against a political movement that focuses entirely on the philosophy of Edmund Burke while ignoring the actual policies of the administration in power. The critique is sharp: "If one makes an hour-long video arguing that EA is a destructive bourgeois movement that greases the wheels of neoliberalism, it seems worth mentioning, at least once, some of the things EAs do."

This section highlights a dangerous trend in public discourse where the "concrete must become abstract" to avoid scrutiny. The author warns that when we stop naming things, we stop seeing them. "Plain description is not always adequate," they admit, but it is the only way to pierce the "soft snow" of euphemism that covers up reality.

"The concrete must become abstract. But mere concreteness is not adequate when our intuitions about a subject are not, themselves, concrete."

Bottom Line

Bentham's Bulldog delivers a compelling case that clarity is a moral imperative, arguing that the fog of abstraction is the primary tool used to defend systemic cruelty. The piece's greatest strength is its ability to expose how language shapes our ethical blind spots, from the meat industry to global existential risks. However, its biggest vulnerability lies in assuming that if people simply saw the concrete facts, they would inevitably agree with the author's conclusions, potentially underestimating the deep-seated ideological barriers that prevent such a shift. Readers should watch for how this framework applies to other debates where "status quo" practices are defended by vague, high-minded principles rather than specific, defensible outcomes.
