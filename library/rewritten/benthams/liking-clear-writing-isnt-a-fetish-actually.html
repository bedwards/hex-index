In a digital landscape where obscurity is often mistaken for depth, Bentham's Bulldog mounts a surprisingly vigorous defense of plain English against the high priests of academic jargon. This is not merely a stylistic preference; the author argues that clarity is the primary mechanism for detecting logical errors, and that the deliberate obfuscation found in much of continental philosophy actively undermines reasoned thought.

The Cost of Confusion

Bentham's Bulldog opens by recounting a previous critique of continental philosophy that sparked a firestorm, noting that the backlash often devolved into personal attacks rather than substantive engagement. The core thesis is stark: "If one writes unclearly about a subject, one will think unclearly about a subject, and then think bad arguments are good and good arguments are bad." This framing is effective because it shifts the debate from aesthetics to epistemology. It suggests that the inability to parse a sentence is not a failure of the reader, but a failure of the writer to have done the hard work of thinking clearly.

The author dismantles the idea that complexity equals profundity by highlighting how vague language masks weak reasoning. "Because mental confusion is one of the chief ways people go wrong, anything that masks confusion seriously undermines reasoned thoughts," they write. This is a crucial intervention. In an era where AI and text-to-speech tools are becoming ubiquitous, the demand for clarity is no longer just an academic luxury; it is a functional necessity for information processing.

Critics might argue that some philosophical concepts are inherently resistant to simple articulation, but Bentham's Bulldog counters that one can reject complex ideas clearly. "One can rebel against any idea clearly—as Daniel Munoz points out, they can simply write 'this idea is false.'" This distinction separates the difficulty of the subject matter from the difficulty of the expression.

The Fetish of Fuzziness

The piece turns its attention to a specific rebuttal by philosophy professor Ellie Anderson, titled "Continental philosophy and the fetish for clarity." Bentham's Bulldog acknowledges the respectfulness of Anderson's tone but rejects her central premise. The author points out that Anderson's critique relies on a series of logical leaps that do not hold up under scrutiny. For instance, Anderson claims that valuing clarity commits one to the correspondence theory of truth—the idea that a belief is true if it corresponds to an actual state of affairs. Bentham's Bulldog finds this connection baffling.

"Writing clearly does not commit one to the correspondence theory of truth," the author asserts, noting that philosophers like Derek Parfit wrote with crystal clarity while explicitly denying that theory. This exposes a fundamental flaw in Anderson's argument: it conflates a stylistic norm with a specific metaphysical commitment. The author notes, "This connection might be alright as an item of continental philosophy slam poetry word association, but as an argument, it is considerably weaker."

The commentary also addresses the historical context of this divide. Bentham's Bulldog references the Sokal affair, where physicist Alan Sokal submitted a nonsense paper to a cultural studies journal to expose the lack of rigor in postmodern scholarship. The author notes, "It is no surprise that in his hoax paper accepted by a prestigious continental philosophy journal, Sokal seemed to deny that there was any objective truth." This historical touchstone adds weight to the argument that obscurity can be a shield for intellectual dishonesty.

If an argument is clothed in elaborate garb, it is often difficult to gauge its force or the plausibility of its premises.

The author further critiques the reliance on authority figures within the continental tradition. When Anderson cites Theodor Adorno to suggest that clarity is a dogma inherited from Descartes, Bentham's Bulldog pushes back hard. "I could not care less what Adorno had to say about clarity; I do not think that Adorno's writings are inerrant, or even inspired," they write. This is a refreshing rejection of the "appeal to authority" fallacy. The argument is that the validity of clarity does not depend on the approval of a specific philosopher, but on its utility in communication.

The Reality of Subjectivity

A significant portion of the piece tackles the claim that continental philosophers reject objective reality. Bentham's Bulldog cites Michel Foucault's assertion that "Truth is a thing of this world: it is produced only by virtue of multiple forms of power." While acknowledging that such ideas are widespread, the author argues that proponents of this view are often unable to articulate exactly what they are denying without resorting to vagueness. "They can always suggest you misunderstood them," the author observes, highlighting the defensive posture that often accompanies obscure writing.

The piece also addresses the historical formation of the analytic-continental split, noting that the distinction solidified during the McCarthy Era due to fears of "decadent and/or commie European philosophers." However, Bentham's Bulldog correctly points out that the political origins of a distinction do not invalidate the distinction itself. "The mere existence of fuzzy boundaries doesn't preclude one from speaking meaningfully about a subject," they argue, drawing a parallel to the fuzzy boundaries of concepts like fascism, which does not stop us from condemning it.

Critics might note that the demand for absolute clarity can sometimes strip nuance from complex social realities. However, the author's defense is that clarity allows for the discussion of nuance, whereas obscurity prevents it entirely. "Providing some broad historical analysis of how an idea came to be and noting that some of your favorite thinkers don't like that idea does nothing to discredit it," Bentham's Bulldog concludes.

Bottom Line

Bentham's Bulldog delivers a compelling, no-nonsense defense of intellectual transparency, successfully decoupling the value of clarity from specific metaphysical commitments. The piece's greatest strength is its insistence that obscurity is often a crutch for weak arguments, not a badge of deep thought. Its biggest vulnerability lies in potentially underestimating the genuine difficulty of articulating certain non-linear philosophical concepts, though the author's rebuttal—that one can at least state the rejection of a concept clearly—remains a powerful counterweight. For busy readers, this is a reminder that if you can't explain it simply, you likely haven't thought it through.
