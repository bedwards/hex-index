In a year dominated by noise, Bentham's Bulldog offers a startlingly clear signal: the most urgent moral imperative of our time is not the immediate relief of human suffering, but the prevention of infinite future catastrophe. This is not a standard year-in-review; it is a manifesto for a worldview where the welfare of shrimp and the trajectory of artificial intelligence outweigh the comfort of the living. The author's journey from animal welfare to a hardline Longtermist stance provides a rare, data-driven window into how effective altruism is evolving from a charitable movement into an existential defense strategy.

The Math of Infinite Stakes

The piece opens with a playful calculation that quickly pivots to a sobering reality. Bentham's Bulldog notes the publication's growth from 4,200 to 9,903 readers, then extrapolates this trend with a chilling precision: "So if doubling trends continue, in 2095, I will have around 1.1805916 x 10^21 blog subscribers. This strikes me as a conservative estimate, because such things tend to speed up." This exponential framing is not merely a boast about readership; it serves as a metaphor for the author's new conviction regarding technological acceleration. The core argument is that the next decade or two will witness "growth rates much faster than any that have previously been seen in human history," driven by artificial intelligence.

This shift in perspective has fundamentally altered the author's charitable calculus. Bentham's Bulldog admits that while they previously accepted Longtermism intellectually, they did not act on it, preferring to fund animal welfare. Now, the logic has flipped. "I've now become convinced that Longtermist charities are better, and I give them a decent slice of my money." The reasoning is stark: the potential scale of future suffering or flourishing is so vast that it dwarfs current concerns. This aligns with the historical roots of Longtermism, which gained significant traction in the early 2010s through the work of philosophers like Toby Ord, who argued that the long-term future holds more value than the present. Bentham's Bulldog takes this a step further, suggesting that the "infinite evil" of a future dominated by a hostile intelligence justifies any sacrifice in the present.

"A terrible enemy is poised to rule both worlds with irrevocable power, forever. It would never, never end. Do you understand me? … To keep that enemy from achieving that kind of mastery, that infinite evil, I would let any of you die. I would die myself, if by dying I could be sure of stopping him."

The author cites this line from Orson Scott Card's Mither Mages series as the perfect articulation of their new worldview. It is a powerful rhetorical device, borrowing the gravity of fiction to ground abstract philosophical stakes. However, critics might note that this framing risks moral hazard; prioritizing the hypothetical future to the point of indifference toward current lives can lead to dangerous policy blind spots. The author acknowledges the tension but remains convinced that the math demands this prioritization.

The Continental Wars and the Cult of Clarity

Beyond the macro-stakes of AI, the piece offers a sharp, often hilarious dissection of the intellectual culture wars currently fracturing the philosophy community. Bentham's Bulldog details the "continental brouhaha," a saga sparked by a critique of continental philosophy's lack of clarity. The conflict escalated quickly, with figures like Daniel Munoz and J Edward Hackett trading barbs over academic credentials and the definition of truth itself. The author highlights the absurdity of the exchange, particularly a comment thread where a critic questioned Munoz's MIT graduation based on a "critical-intellectual genealogy" and the claim that "everybody knows that an MIT graduate would never do [read Adorno]."

The author's defense of clarity is not just stylistic; it is ethical. They reference George Orwell's Politics and the English Language to argue that "nebulous language can be used to obscure wickedness." This connects the blogosphere squabbles to a broader historical concern about how language shapes reality. Bentham's Bulldog writes, "Joe Rogan asking about quantum-proof encryption for 20 minutes... Not letting the guy finish, just kinda vaguely going: 'dude, what if we never have encryption again because the quantum computers are so fast'." This anecdote serves as a microcosm for the failure of public discourse when technical nuance is abandoned for sensationalism.

The commentary on the "continental wars" is effective because it exposes how intellectual posturing can derail genuine inquiry. The author points out the irony of a critic claiming that "1+1=2 is true which, they claimed, it isn't because of Godel's theorem—which, as anyone familiar with Godel's theorem will tell you, is a profound butchering." This moment of clarity amidst the chaos reinforces the piece's central thesis: rigorous thinking is a prerequisite for navigating the existential risks ahead.

"If it were not a law of the universe that everything must come from something, you might conjecture that he does not have any. It is proof of his happy upbringing that he—after addressing the prosaic tragedies of human life: falling in love with hot, unattainable people and feeling odd and misshapen and wretched—chose to write about tuberculosis."

In a brief digression on John Green, the author uses literary analysis to illustrate a psychological point about happiness and trauma. This stylistic choice adds depth to the piece, showing that the author's commitment to clarity extends to the emotional and narrative dimensions of human experience. It suggests that the same rigor applied to AI safety is necessary for understanding the human condition.

The Bottom Line

Bentham's Bulldog has successfully articulated a worldview where the stakes are not just high, but infinite, and where the only rational response is a radical reordering of priorities. The strongest part of this argument is its unflinching commitment to following the evidence, even when it leads to uncomfortable conclusions about the value of current lives versus future ones. However, the biggest vulnerability lies in the potential for this utilitarian calculus to become a self-fulfilling prophecy of detachment, where the abstract future consumes the concrete present. As the author graduates into the "real world," the challenge will be to apply this high-stakes Longtermism without losing sight of the human cost of the transition. The reader should watch for how this philosophy translates from blog posts to policy, as the "intelligence explosion" approaches.

"Potentially billions fewer animals will suffer because of your actions this year."

This final statistic encapsulates the author's ultimate goal: a world where the suffering of sentient beings, whether shrimp or humans, is minimized through rigorous, long-term thinking. It is a call to action that is as terrifying as it is necessary.
