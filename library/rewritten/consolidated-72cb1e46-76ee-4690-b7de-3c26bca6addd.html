Two analysts looking at the same September sky over Poland and Estonia reach overlapping but distinct conclusions. Good Times Bad Times sees a cost-asymmetry trap being sprung on a high-tech alliance. Perun sees a psychological operation aimed at the political will of NATO publics. Read together, they describe a confrontation in which cheap hardware and ambiguous intent are doing work that tanks and missiles cannot.

Two Frames for One Incident

Good Times Bad Times treats the incursion of more than twenty Russian drones into Polish airspace as a deliberate act, not a navigational accident. "When more than 20 drones are involved, some of them entering from Belarusian territory, the idea of an accident can be ruled out. It was deliberate," the author writes. The framing is economic: Moscow forced Warsaw to shut four major airports and scramble F-16s, F-35s, Patriot batteries, and Italian tankers to neutralize what amounted to styrofoam decoys. Former NATO commander General Ben Hodges is cited as saying the alliance "is not adequately prepared and lacks mechanisms for dealing with such threats."

Perun, by contrast, situates the same night within a broader "gray zone" theory of conflict. For Perun, the drones over Poland and the three armed MiG-31s that loitered for twelve minutes in Estonian airspace are part of a single political project. "Hybrid methods are used to blur the lines between war and peace, an attempt to sew doubt in the minds of target populations. They aim to destabilize and undermine societies," he writes. Where Good Times Bad Times measures the price tag of interception, Perun measures the erosion of nerve.

"The mere fact that NATO had to scramble F-16s, F-35s, Patriot batteries, and other systems to neutralize drones worth about as much as a mid-range car shows that the alliance is not adequately prepared."

The MiG That Could Not Be Mistaken

Perun's most pointed contribution is his insistence that the Estonian incursion cannot be waved away as a glitch. A drone might drift; a manned interceptor with its transponder off, ignoring radio calls for twelve minutes, cannot. "When you see a B1B Lancer bombing someone, no one's going to blame Argentina. Russia is currently the only country flying the big 31," he notes, adding dryly that "with a manned aircraft, you do have a backup if the electronic systems go haywire. It's called the pilot navigating."

This is where the two writers complement each other most usefully. Good Times Bad Times explains why the Polish episode is expensive to defend against. Perun explains why the Estonian episode is impossible to deny. Together they describe a Russian playbook that exploits both the wallet and the nerve of the alliance simultaneously.

The Question of Response

The two voices diverge sharply on what to do next. Good Times Bad Times floats a controversial symmetric option: "After 19 or more drones were launched into Poland, an identical number should be launched by the alliance against Russian territory or against Russian systems stationed in Belarus." The author cites the India-Pakistan dynamic as evidence that proportional retaliation can paradoxically de-escalate. Perun never endorses anything so kinetic. His prescription is closer to patient hardening — better detection, faster scrambles, tighter cohesion — on the theory that Moscow's "escalation inferiority" means it cannot afford to be answered in kind.

"Hybrid methods are used to blur the lines between war and peace, an attempt to sew doubt in the minds of target populations."

Both arguments have soft spots. The symmetric-retaliation thesis assumes that a kinetic exchange between nuclear powers can be cleanly bounded — a confidence history does not particularly reward. Perun's gray-zone thesis, meanwhile, leans on the assumption that the Russian leadership is acting with cold rationality; if Moscow miscalculates the alliance's threshold, the same "probe" that was meant to test resolve could trigger the war it was designed to avoid.

Counterpoints Neither Author Pressed

Neither writer dwells on a third possibility: that the incursions are aimed less at NATO publics than at Russia's own. A leadership facing a grinding war of attrition has obvious domestic reasons to demonstrate that it can still reach into European airspace at will, regardless of whether the alliance flinches. Nor does either piece engage seriously with the industrial response already underway in Kyiv and Warsaw. Good Times Bad Times mentions the EU's SAFE program and the roughly 44 billion euros earmarked for Poland, noting that "Ukraine fighting for its very survival has developed an extensive and above all cheap domestic drone industry," but neither author fully reckons with how quickly a Polish-Ukrainian drone manufacturing axis could collapse the cost asymmetry that makes the current Russian playbook attractive.

There is also the matter of the executive branch in Washington. Good Times Bad Times is skeptical that the political will exists for a hard sanctions regime against buyers of Russian oil, citing "the hesitation and lethargy of Europe's leaders" alongside what the author calls a naive posture from the White House. Whether the administration is prepared to lean on China, India, and Turkey to choke off the roughly 96 percent of Russia's export market that such an embargo would touch is the unanswered political question hanging over both analyses.

Bottom Line

Good Times Bad Times and Perun are looking at the same flashpoints through different lenses — one economic, one psychological — and both lenses reveal something real. The drones over Poland exposed how expensive it is for a high-end alliance to swat cheap decoys. The MiGs over Estonia exposed how little plausible deniability remains when manned interceptors loiter in sovereign airspace. The open question is whether NATO's answer will be confined to faster scrambles and sterner statements, or whether it will move toward the industrial integration with Ukraine that would actually neutralize the cost trap. Until that shift happens, the gray zone is not closing. It is being mapped, by Moscow, one probe at a time.
