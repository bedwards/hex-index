The Hated One delivers a stark warning that cuts through the comforting myths of digital safety: your privacy is not just eroding, it is being systematically dismantled by the very tools you trust to protect it. The author's most provocative claim is that Virtual Private Networks (VPNs) offer a false sense of security, providing anonymity only in name while leaving your identity fully exposed to sophisticated metadata analysis. In an era where even private browsing modes fail to hide you, this piece argues that true anonymity requires a complete architectural overhaul of how you connect to the internet, not just a simple setting toggle.

The Myth of the Safe Tunnel

The core of the argument dismantles the popular belief that a VPN is a silver bullet for privacy. The Hated One writes, "VPNs only change your ip address and make it difficult for your isp to see and control your traffic... but they don't give you any anonymity whatsoever." This distinction is crucial. While a VPN masks your location from your Internet Service Provider, the VPN provider itself becomes the new point of surveillance, knowing exactly who you are and what you are doing. The author effectively reframes the conversation from "hiding your location" to "hiding your identity," a much harder task.

The piece leans heavily on the concept of metadata as the ultimate tracking tool, surpassing the importance of encrypted content. As The Hated One puts it, "The former nsa chief once had something to say about this... okay we kill people based on metadata." By defining metadata as "who you talk to when where for how long how often on what device and software," the author illustrates that even if your messages are unreadable, the pattern of your communication reveals your entire life. This framing is effective because it shifts the focus from the content of your data to the context of your existence, a nuance often missed in mainstream privacy discussions.

Encryption only protects the content but in modern surveillance it's the metadata that's most valuable.

Critics might note that the author's reliance on the NSA quote, while rhetorically powerful, conflates state-level surveillance capabilities with the commercial tracking faced by average users. However, the underlying principle remains sound: the data patterns generated by your digital footprint are often more revealing than the data itself.

The Tor Network and Behavioral Hygiene

Moving from theory to practice, the author introduces the Tor network as the only viable path to verifiable anonymity. The Hated One explains that Tor achieves this by "encrypting and rerouting your traffic through three independent tor relays or hops none of the relays knows the full length of the tor traffic." This technical explanation is paired with a critical warning about user behavior. The author argues that technical tools are useless if the user compromises them through poor habits, stating, "You are not anonymous just for being on the tor network if you give someone your name it doesn't matter how much you are blocking trackers and hiding your ip address."

The commentary on behavioral hygiene is perhaps the most practical section of the piece. The Hated One advises users to "never sign in to online accounts on tor" and to avoid changing default settings, noting that "if you make changes to how you look you're making yourself stand out." This emphasis on blending in rather than standing out is a counter-intuitive but vital strategy. The author also highlights the danger of writing style, warning that "less than 500 words of a text written by you can lead to your identity." This suggests that anonymity is as much about psychology and linguistics as it is about cryptography.

Building an Impenetrable Wall

The final layer of the argument addresses the operating system itself, positing that running Tor on a standard Windows or Mac machine is a fundamental flaw. The Hated One writes, "Tor browser is only as secure as the operating system you are running it on which is not very secure." To solve this, the author advocates for Whonix, a Linux-based system designed to run inside a virtual machine, creating a "wall around your browser that eliminates the attack surface to an absolute minimum."

The author's push toward Linux is not just about security features but about control. "Using windows and macos is not secure because these platforms are not transparent about what they can and can't do with your data," The Hated One argues, adding that major tech companies have "built in backdoors into their systems for undisclosed amount of government agencies." While the claim that Apple and Microsoft are secret NSA partners is a common conspiracy theory, the broader point about the lack of transparency in proprietary software holds significant weight in the privacy community. The recommendation to migrate to open-source distributions like PureOS or Linux Mint is presented as a necessary step for anyone serious about reclaiming their digital sovereignty.

Critics might argue that the barrier to entry for setting up virtual machines and Linux distributions is too high for the average user, potentially limiting the advice to a niche of tech-savvy individuals. Yet, the author insists that "linux is something that will be in your way for a while but it is a habit and just like with anything it will become seamless after a while," suggesting that the initial friction is a worthy trade-off for long-term security.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest contribution is the relentless dismantling of the "privacy theater" offered by standard tools like VPNs and private browsing modes, replacing them with a rigorous, multi-layered approach to anonymity. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its high technical barrier, which may alienate the very users most vulnerable to surveillance, but its core thesis remains undeniable: true anonymity requires a fundamental change in both technology and behavior. Readers should watch for the coming implementation of social scoring systems in the West, as the author predicts, and consider whether their current digital habits are sufficient to withstand such a future.
