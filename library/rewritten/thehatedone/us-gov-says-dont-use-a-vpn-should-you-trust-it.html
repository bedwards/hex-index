When the U.S. government suddenly advises citizens to stop using personal Virtual Private Networks, the immediate reaction should be suspicion, not compliance. The Hated One cuts through the bureaucratic noise to reveal a startling contradiction: the very agency warning against VPNs is the same one that helped create the vulnerabilities they now claim to fear. This piece matters because it exposes how a national security crisis, born from Chinese hackers exploiting law enforcement backdoors, has forced the government into a corner where it must finally admit what privacy advocates have said for decades.

The Backdoor Paradox

The core of the argument rests on a historical irony. The Hated One notes that "when the US intelligence tells you anything there is a 50/50 chance the opposite might be correct." The author argues that the government is seeking moral redemption after decades of pushing for encryption backdoors that ultimately backfired spectacularly. The evidence is stark: Chinese hackers accessed U.S. telecommunications by abusing the very "wired tab" or backdoor mechanisms that U.S. law enforcement lobbied for, proving that "one man's back door is another man's vulnerability."

This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from user error to systemic failure. The author points out that while the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) now endorses end-to-end encryption, the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) still shields for backdoors. As The Hated One writes, "encryption back door that only the good guys can use does not exist." This is the piece's strongest move: dismantling the fantasy that law enforcement can have a secret key without malicious actors eventually stealing it. Critics might note that the FBI's stance is driven by legitimate concerns over terrorist communications, but the author's evidence of the telecom breach suggests the risk of a universal backdoor far outweighs the benefit of a single point of access.

One man's back door is another man's vulnerability.

The VPN Trust Deficit

The commentary then pivots to the specific warning against personal VPNs. The Hated One dissects CISA's claim that "personal vpns simply shift residual risks from your internet service provider to the VPN provider often increasing the attack surface." The author agrees with the technical assessment but challenges the strategic conclusion. The argument is that while a VPN hides traffic from an Internet Service Provider (ISP), it merely places that same trust in a private company that may have even worse privacy policies.

The Hated One writes, "vpns are all about trust and that's a fundamental flaw of a technology that quite frankly was never even designed for privacy at all." This is a crucial distinction often missed in mainstream tech coverage. The author highlights that many commercial VPNs are owned by a handful of parent companies, some with ties to intelligence communities, and that "many free and Commercial VPN providers have questionable security and privacy policies." The argument holds up well here, as it exposes the illusion of anonymity sold by sponsored content creators who often fail to mention that a VPN provider can see everything the ISP could, plus the user's real IP address.

However, the author acknowledges a nuance that many critics overlook: the original design purpose of VPNs. As The Hated One puts it, "if your organization requires a VPN client to access its data that is a different use case." VPNs were built for corporate security, not personal privacy. This distinction is vital. The author argues that for personal use, the risk is high, but for corporate data, the trade-off is acceptable because the company benefits from monitoring employees. This contextualizes the government's warning: they are technically correct about the risks, but they are ignoring the fact that the alternative—trusting an ISP that sells data to brokers—is often worse.

The Path to True Privacy

So, if VPNs are flawed and ISPs are predatory, what is the solution? The Hated One argues that true privacy requires a holistic approach, not just a toggle switch. The author states, "in order to be truly private on the internet you need to take so many steps first before you even start thinking about your IP address." This involves minimizing data footprints by switching to privacy-respecting services like Proton Mail, Signal, and DuckDuckGo, and compartmentalizing identities.

The author's personal recommendation is to only use a VPN after these foundational steps are taken, and even then, to choose providers with strict no-log policies and independent verification. The Hated One writes, "the gold standard is mullvad which is based in Sweden and uses Ram only VPN infrastructure." This specific, actionable advice elevates the piece from a critique to a guide. It acknowledges that while no tool is perfect, a carefully selected VPN can still offer protection against ISP tracking and geo-blocking when used correctly.

Critics might argue that the average user cannot be expected to master this level of digital hygiene, making the government's blanket "don't use a VPN" advice a pragmatic, if cynical, default. Yet, the author's insistence that "trusting the default option is really bad" serves as a necessary call to action for a more informed citizenry.

Bottom Line

The Hated One delivers a compelling critique that validates the government's technical warnings while exposing the hypocrisy of their motives. The strongest part of the argument is the demonstration that backdoors inevitably become vulnerabilities for everyone, not just the intended targets. The biggest vulnerability remains the difficulty of implementing true privacy in a surveillance economy, but the author's roadmap offers a realistic, if demanding, path forward for those willing to take it.
