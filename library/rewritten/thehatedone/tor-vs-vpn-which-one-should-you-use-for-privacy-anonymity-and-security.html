In an internet saturated with sponsored VPN ads promising privacy, The Hated One delivers a scathing, technically grounded rebuttal that cuts through the marketing noise. The author's most provocative claim is not that Virtual Private Networks are useless, but that they fundamentally fail at their primary promise: true anonymity. By contrasting the centralized business model of VPNs with the decentralized, trustless architecture of The Onion Router, the piece argues that users are not enhancing their security when they pay for a VPN; they are simply delegating their trust to a different entity that has both the means and the motive to sell their data.

The Illusion of the Single Hop

The Hated One dismantles the standard marketing pitch for consumer-grade Virtual Private Networks by exposing their structural weakness. "VPNs do this by rerouting your connection through a single relay also known as hop," the author explains, noting that this single point of failure is where the anonymity collapses. The core of the argument rests on the fact that the VPN provider acts as both the entry and exit node, meaning they possess a complete map of the user's activity. "Your VPN provider acts as your entry and exit node; they know your true IP address and they know what websites you visit," The Hated One writes. This is a critical distinction that many consumers miss, assuming that encryption from their device to the server is enough to hide their identity from the world.

The author points out that this trust is fragile because of the money trail. Even if a provider claims a "no-logs" policy, the financial transaction of signing up ties the user's real identity to their browsing history. "If they are required by law enforcement they can hand over all your browsing data to them because many VPN services are paid and they have a money trail that leads straight to your bank account," The Hated One argues. This framing effectively shifts the conversation from technical encryption to the sociology of trust. Critics might note that reputable, audited VPN providers exist that operate in privacy-friendly jurisdictions, but the author's point remains that the system of a single-hop, paid service is inherently vulnerable to coercion or corruption.

The only trustworthy system is the one you don't have to trust at all.

The Architecture of True Anonymity

In contrast to the centralized VPN model, the commentary highlights how The Onion Router achieves anonymity through decentralization and randomness. The Hated One explains that Tor routes traffic through three independent relays, chosen at random, ensuring that no single operator knows the full path of the data. "No single relay knows the full traffic; they only know the nearest entry and exit points," the author states. This design makes mass surveillance economically unviable for most adversaries. The piece emphasizes that this is not just a technical preference but a philosophical one: "Tor is a clear winner of this category... you are not required to trust any single provider."

The argument gains strength when discussing the "trustless design." Because anyone can run a relay, the network becomes more robust as more people join, rather than relying on the integrity of a single corporation. The Hated One notes that the only way to compromise a Tor user is through a highly resource-intensive correlation attack, which is beyond the reach of most entities. "This can be done on mass with current technology and only global adversaries such as the NSA or GCHQ have the capacity to successfully target Tor users," the author concedes, adding necessary nuance. This admission prevents the piece from sounding like a cult manifesto and grounds the security claims in reality: Tor is not magic, but it is mathematically superior for hiding identity compared to a single-hop tunnel.

The Trade-Off: Usability and Speed

The commentary does not shy away from the significant downsides of Tor, specifically regarding user experience and performance. The Hated One acknowledges that while Tor wins on security, it loses on convenience. "Tor is not difficult to learn but it does require some learning," the author admits, contrasting the polished, "big button" interfaces of VPN apps with the more complex configuration required for system-wide Tor usage. The piece suggests that for the average user, the friction of using Tor—such as needing to boot into a specialized operating system like Tails for maximum security—is a high barrier to entry.

Furthermore, the speed penalty is unavoidable due to the three-hop architecture. "There is no question three hops are always going to be slower than a single hop," The Hated One writes, explaining why Virtual Private Networks remain the superior choice for streaming or accessing geo-restricted content. This section is vital because it prevents the reader from walking away with the idea that Tor is a drop-in replacement for a VPN in every scenario. The author concludes that while Tor is the only solution for strong anonymity and censorship resistance, "VPNs are useful for accessing geolocation restricted content and they're generally faster than Tor simply due to them sacrificing stronger anonymity and security."

Bottom Line

The Hated One's analysis is a necessary corrective to a market flooded with privacy theater, successfully arguing that paying for a VPN often just shifts the surveillance burden rather than eliminating it. The strongest part of the argument is the rigorous distinction between privacy (hiding activity from an ISP) and anonymity (hiding identity from the world), a nuance most commercial providers blur to sell subscriptions. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability is its assumption that the average user is willing or able to navigate the steep learning curve of Tor to achieve this level of security. For the busy professional, the verdict is clear: if you need to hide who you are, use Tor; if you just need to hide what you are doing from your ISP, a VPN suffices, but never mistake the latter for the former.
