The Hated One delivers a scathing indictment of Signal, arguing that the privacy champion has abandoned its open-source roots for a centralized, opaque cryptocurrency scheme that threatens the very trust users placed in it. This isn't just a critique of a new feature; it is a warning that Signal's leadership may be trading its moral authority for financial survival in a way that fundamentally breaks the social contract of the free and open-source software movement.

The Open Source Imperative

The piece begins by establishing the non-negotiable nature of open-source code for privacy tools. The author writes, "if you want real guarantees for protecting our privacy free and open source is the only way." This framing is crucial because it sets the stakes: without transparency, there is no verification, and without verification, there is no privacy. The Hated One correctly identifies that while proprietary software can be secure, only open code allows the community to audit for backdoors, a necessity highlighted by the need for whistleblowers like Edward Snowden.

However, the author acknowledges the economic reality that makes this difficult. "Open source has struggled to come up with sustainable business models," they note, pointing out that donations often fail to cover the massive bandwidth costs of modern messaging. This is a fair assessment. The text contrasts Signal with competitors like Threema, which charges a flat fee, and ProtonMail, which uses a freemium model. The Hated One argues that Signal's initial reliance on donations was a "simple and clean" approach that worked for text, but the addition of high-bandwidth features like video calling and group chats created a financial hole that donations alone could not fill.

Critics might note that the author slightly oversimplifies the success of donation models, as many large open-source projects thrive on corporate grants and foundation support without resorting to controversial crypto ventures. Yet, the core tension remains valid: how does a non-profit scale expensive infrastructure without compromising its principles?

The MobileCoin Betrayal

The commentary shifts to the crux of the controversy: Signal's pivot to MobileCoin. The Hated One writes, "MobileCoin is a direct opposite of what Bitcoin or Monero are trying to achieve." This is the article's most damning claim. The author explains that while MobileCoin promises speed, it achieves this by relying on centralized validators using proprietary Intel SGX chips, rather than the permissionless, decentralized mining of Bitcoin. "With MobileCoin you cannot mine it and you cannot run your node," the author states, highlighting a fundamental departure from the ethos of decentralization.

The piece details how this centralization creates a single point of failure and trust. "MobileCoin validators will be able to see and censor transactions if they so choose," The Hated One warns, noting that this exposes users to potential government pressure and regulatory crackdowns. This is a significant vulnerability in the argument for a privacy tool; if the network can be censored, it is not truly private.

"Signal had a simple and clean app that was easy to audit and didn't attract too much unwanted attention. Now the app will be investigated by every three-letter government agency on the planet."

The author also scrutinizes the tokenomics, pointing out that the entire supply was pre-mined and that a portion was sold to private investors in a manner that resembles a "pump and dump" scheme. The text notes, "nobody knows how many MobileCoins there are in circulation," which destroys the transparency required for a trustworthy financial layer. The Hated One questions the relationship between Signal founder Moxie Marlinspike and MobileCoin, citing conflicting whitepapers and an evasive "Ask Me Anything" session by MobileCoin's CEO. "We don't really know what the true scale of their relationship is," the author concludes, suggesting a conflict of interest that undermines Signal's claim of independence.

The Final Nail

The argument culminates with the most alarming development: the potential closure of Signal's server code. The Hated One writes, "Signal is no longer open source," noting that the server code has not been updated in the same way as the client. This is the "final nail in the coffin" because it removes the community's ability to verify the server-side logic that handles encryption keys and message routing. The author argues that this move transforms Signal from a verifiable tool into a black box, where users must simply trust the organization's word over mathematical proof.

Critics might argue that the author is being overly alarmist about the timeline of these changes or the actual implementation of the server code restrictions. However, the pattern of opacity described—from the MobileCoin whitepaper discrepancies to the lack of market cap data—supports a narrative of declining transparency that is hard to dismiss.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest argument is that Signal's pursuit of a sustainable business model has led it to adopt a centralized, non-transparent financial layer that contradicts its core mission of user sovereignty. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that MobileCoin's centralization will inevitably lead to censorship, a scenario that has not yet fully materialized. Readers should watch closely to see if Signal's server code remains open and whether the MobileCoin integration actually proceeds, as these factors will determine if Signal remains a privacy tool or becomes just another tech platform with a privacy brand.
