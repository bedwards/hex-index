The Hated One reframes the smartphone not as a gadget, but as a digital extension of the human mind, arguing that securing it is a matter of existential necessity rather than mere convenience. The author's most striking claim is that true security isn't about creating an impenetrable fortress, but rather about exhausting an adversary's resources until they simply give up. This perspective shifts the burden from the user achieving perfection to the user achieving sufficient friction.

The Cyborg Reality

The piece opens with a provocative assertion that redefines our relationship with technology. "Your phone is an inseparable part of you... axes into your phone is an access into your mind," The Hated One writes, establishing that the stakes are personal and psychological. This framing is effective because it moves the conversation beyond technical specifications to the core of identity. The author argues that because we are effectively "cyborgs," the vulnerabilities in our devices are vulnerabilities in our own cognition.

However, the author quickly grounds this philosophical stance in a pragmatic threat model. The text distinguishes between common scams and the terrifying reality of state-sponsored actors. "The purpose of security isn't make an impenetrable device that's impossible," The Hated One states, clarifying that the goal is resource exhaustion. This is a crucial distinction often missed in consumer advice. Critics might note that for the average user, the threat of a state-sponsored advanced persistent threat is statistically negligible compared to a simple phishing link, but the author's inclusion of this extreme scenario serves to illustrate the upper limits of what is possible.

You're a cyborg. Your phone isn't just a gadget; your phone is you.

The Mechanics of Defense

Moving from theory to practice, the commentary focuses on the specific, actionable steps required to harden a device. The author emphasizes that encryption is the baseline, yet warns against overconfidence. "Encrypting your phone does not protect you from malware or remote exploitation," The Hated One writes, a blunt reminder that physical theft is only one vector of attack. This is a vital correction to the common misconception that a passcode alone makes a device safe.

The guide then pivots to the importance of account security, noting that the phone is only as strong as the Google or Apple ID tethered to it. The recommendation to use hardware tokens like YubiKey or Nitro Key over software-based authenticators is presented as a non-negotiable upgrade for serious users. "The best option is to go for a USB security token," The Hated One advises, acknowledging the cost but emphasizing the value. This advice holds up well against modern credential stuffing attacks, though it does introduce a layer of complexity that might frustrate less tech-savvy users.

Furthermore, the author tackles the issue of software hygiene with a focus on minimalism. "The more apps you have, the more exposed to software vulnerabilities you are," The Hated One argues, advocating for a drastic reduction in installed applications. This aligns with the principle of reducing the attack surface. The suggestion to disable JavaScript entirely in browsers is particularly aggressive. "JavaScript is evil so disable javascript entirely," The Hated One declares. While this significantly reduces the risk of drive-by downloads, a counterargument worth considering is that it renders a vast portion of the modern web unusable, potentially isolating the user from essential services.

The Network and The Stalker

The final section of the guide addresses connectivity and the specific threat of "stalkerware"—commercial spyware often installed by acquaintances. The author suggests that if one suspects they are being followed, the only certain solution is a factory reset. "The fastest and most certain option is to factory reset your device," The Hated One writes, before delivering a darkly humorous, hyperbolic conclusion about dealing with the stalker themselves. This shift in tone from technical to visceral highlights the emotional toll of surveillance.

On the topic of networks, the advice is equally stark. "Public Wi-Fi are evil," The Hated One states, recommending the Tor network or specific, paid VPNs like Proton VPN to mask traffic. The author admits that most free VPNs are themselves the product, a sentiment that resonates in an era of data monetization. The recommendation to use Nextcloud for self-hosted backups further reinforces the theme of taking control away from big tech. "If you don't pay for the service you are the product," The Hated One summarizes, urging users to own their encryption keys.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's guide succeeds by stripping away the illusion of safety that comes with default settings, replacing it with a philosophy of active, friction-heavy defense. Its greatest strength is the realistic admission that perfection is impossible, yet the effort to exhaust an attacker is always worth it. The biggest vulnerability in the argument is the sheer intensity of the recommended lifestyle changes, which may lead to user fatigue and abandonment of security practices entirely. Readers should treat this not as a one-time checklist, but as a mindset shift toward constant vigilance.

If you don't pay for the service you are the product. Only you should be able to decrypt your data.
