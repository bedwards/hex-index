The Hated One delivers a blistering indictment of Jeff Bezos's business model, arguing that Amazon's profitability is a mirage constructed on predatory pricing and regulatory loopholes. This isn't just another critique of wealth inequality; it is a forensic breakdown of how a company can lose billions annually while simultaneously building a monopoly that dictates what we buy, how we ship it, and who gets to sell it. For busy readers tracking the future of the economy, the author's central claim—that Amazon's strategy is not accidental but a deliberate, shareholder-supremacy-driven plan to eliminate competition entirely—demands immediate attention.

The Illusion of Profit

The Hated One opens by dismantling the conventional wisdom that Amazon is a profit machine. The author points out that despite revenue peaking at $232 billion, the company "has been regularly operating at a loss especially on international markets." This observation is crucial because it reframes Amazon's growth not as organic success, but as a calculated burn rate designed to suffocate rivals. The core of the argument rests on the idea that Bezos prioritizes market share over immediate returns, a strategy that confuses traditional investors but delights those focused on long-term dominance.

"Bezos believes in shareholder supremacy which means everything is justified as long as the share value is growing."

This framing is effective because it exposes the psychological contract between Bezos and his investors: short-term pain for long-term control. The author notes that it wasn't until the rise of Amazon Web Services, the cloud computing arm, that the company began reporting consistent profits. This suggests that the retail arm was never meant to be the cash cow, but rather the Trojan horse for total market saturation. Critics might argue that this aggressive expansion created efficiencies that lowered prices for everyone, but the author counters that these "savings" were merely the bait for a much larger trap.

The Prime Lock-In

The commentary then shifts to the mechanics of customer retention, specifically the Prime membership program. The Hated One reveals a startling detail: for years, Amazon was losing money on every single Prime subscription. The author writes, "by 2011 estimates showed that the average annual cost of each Prime membership ranked up to fifty five dollars in shipping and $35 in streaming."

"It was never about the $79 it was really about changing people's mentality so they wouldn't shop anywhere else."

This is the piece's most chilling insight. The financial loss on Prime was an investment in behavioral psychology, not logistics. By absorbing these costs, Amazon successfully isolated its users from the rest of the market. The author cites data showing that less than 1% of Prime customers would consider competitive retail sites during a shopping session. This creates a feedback loop where Amazon's dominance becomes self-reinforcing. The argument holds up well here, as the data on user loyalty is concrete and the psychological shift is undeniable. However, the author briefly overlooks the genuine convenience factor that many users cite as their primary motivation, focusing almost exclusively on the manipulative aspects of the program.

Predatory Tactics and Vertical Integration

The narrative deepens as the author details how Amazon weaponizes its infrastructure against competitors. The text describes the acquisition of Quidsi, the parent company of Diapers.com, as a textbook case of predatory monopolization. The Hated One explains that when Quidsi refused to sell, Amazon "began tracking diapers.com and cut their own prices for baby products by up to 30%."

"Unlike Amazon quit see it was a new venture and didn't have investors backing their losses while they competed with Amazon's monopolistic ambitions."

This section is particularly strong because it illustrates the asymmetry of the battlefield. Amazon could afford to bleed money for quarters to drive a competitor out of business, a luxury no other retailer possessed. The author notes that once Quidsi was acquired, the generous discounts vanished, and prices rose. This confirms the author's thesis that the low prices were a temporary tactic, not a permanent benefit to consumers. The Hated One also highlights how Amazon uses its logistics network to disadvantage third-party sellers, forcing them to use "Fulfillment by Amazon" because shipping directly via UPS or FedEx became prohibitively expensive due to Amazon's leverage.

"Amazon is using these data to study purchasing patterns and trends to undercut third-party merchants on price or give their own products featured placement."

This revelation about data usage is a critical addition to the argument. It suggests that Amazon is not just a marketplace but a surveillance engine that uses its competitors' own data to destroy them. A counterargument worth considering is that third-party sellers choose to use Amazon's services because the volume of traffic they generate is unmatched, implying a voluntary partnership rather than coercion. Yet, the author's point about the lack of viable alternatives remains potent.

The Cloud as a Weapon

Perhaps the most underappreciated aspect of Amazon's power, according to the author, is Amazon Web Services (AWS). The Hated One argues that the cloud division serves as an "ultimate tool of industrial espionage." By hosting the infrastructure for startups and competitors, Amazon gains early visibility into emerging threats.

"It gives Amazon control over data none of its competitors have and thus Amazon can enter new markets much more quickly and effectively than any other retailer out there."

This creates a paradox where the platform that helps a business grow is also the entity most capable of crushing it. The author's coverage here is comprehensive, linking the cloud's profitability back to the retail losses, creating a holistic view of the Bezos empire. The argument is compelling because it shows how different parts of the business reinforce each other in ways that are invisible to the average consumer.

"Monopolies destroy free markets and with them the freedom to choose not just as a consumer but as a small business owner a worker an internet user and a citizen."

This statement encapsulates the author's broader concern: that the centralization of economic power erodes democratic freedoms. The Hated One concludes by urging readers to support small businesses directly, framing every purchase as a vote for the kind of economy they want. While this call to action is idealistic, it underscores the urgency of the situation.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest asset is the ability to connect disparate business tactics—from Prime membership losses to cloud computing data mining—into a single, coherent strategy of monopolization. The argument's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that regulators will never intervene, a premise that is currently being tested by ongoing antitrust investigations. Readers should watch for how the legal system responds to the specific claim that operating at a loss is not inherently anti-competitive, as this could define the future of the entire tech sector.
