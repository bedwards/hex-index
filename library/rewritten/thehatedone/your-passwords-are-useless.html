In an era where computing power has exploded a thousand-fold since the 1990s, The Hated One delivers a blunt truth: the password is not just broken; it is obsolete. The author argues that relying on a system where "half of your password security depends on making sure you do everything right" is a fundamental design flaw that no amount of user education can fix. This is not a plea for better habits, but a call to abandon the very mechanism that secures our digital lives.

The Math Doesn't Lie

The Hated One opens by dismantling the illusion of security, noting that "passwords have been the default authentication method for decades and not much has changed about that except we went from counting computing power in megahertz to gigahertz." The author points out that while hardware has evolved, the vulnerability remains static. With modern adversaries capable of "trillions of guesses per second," the time required to crack a password has collapsed from years to mere seconds. The argument here is compelling because it shifts the blame from the user to the technology itself. It is a stark reminder that the tools we use are no longer up to the task of defending against the machines we built.

"The technology is far surpass the level of protection passwords can offer but we are still using them only with the non-binding recommendation of just try to make them more complicated."

The author correctly identifies that the burden of security has been unfairly placed on individuals. The expectation that a user must "create and manage complicated but unique passwords for each of your accounts" that "periodically change every few months" is, as The Hated One puts it, a recipe for failure. The commentary highlights how this complexity leads to fatigue, causing users to reuse credentials or fall for phishing. Critics might argue that strong password hygiene, combined with password managers, still offers adequate protection for the average person. However, the sheer volume of phishing sites—reaching "2.11 million" in 2020 alone—suggests that human error is an inevitable variable in the security equation.

The Centralization Trap

The piece then pivots to the structural weakness of the current model: centralization. The Hated One explains that because "passwords are centralized meaning the only way they can work is if the companies have access to them," corporate databases become high-value targets. The author cites the Facebook scandal, where "half a billion users" had their passwords stored in plain text, to illustrate that even tech giants fail to meet basic standards. The core of this argument is that hashing, often touted as a solution, "doesn't address the issue of password centralization at all." This is a crucial distinction that many security discussions gloss over. Even if a password is hashed, the existence of a centralized database creates a single point of failure that hackers will relentlessly target.

"The most a company with a centralized password database can offer you is to hash them... but hashing doesn't address the issue of password centralization at all."

The author's critique of password recovery mechanisms is equally sharp, dismissing security questions as a "complete joke" in an age of open-source intelligence. The argument holds weight because it exposes how "social engineering" exploits the very human tendency to share personal details. The Hated One notes that "65 of people still reuse passwords," a statistic that suggests the current model is fundamentally at odds with human behavior. The solution, the author asserts, is not to try harder but to change the architecture entirely.

The Passwordless Future

The proposed alternative is a shift to public key cryptography via the FIDO (Fast Identity Online) protocol. The Hated One describes this as a system where "the online service verifies that you hold the authentication on your device and it is you who is requesting that the website is legitimate." Unlike passwords, which are sent to a server, the private key in this system "never leaves your device ever." This mechanism effectively neutralizes phishing, as the cryptographic signature is tied to the specific domain. If a user visits a clone site, "the key pairs are unique for each online service" and the token will simply refuse to authenticate.

"The best way to avoid getting hacked is to have nothing to hack."

The author details how a FIDO token works: the user presents a physical key or uses a biometric sensor, and the device signs a challenge from the website. This process is described as "mathematically robust" and impossible to crack with current technology. The Hated One emphasizes that this is not a theoretical future but a present reality, supported by major browsers and operating systems. While the author acknowledges that hardware tokens like YubiKey or NitroKey are the gold standard, they also note that native implementations on phones and laptops are closing the gap. A counterargument worth considering is the risk of physical loss; if a user loses their token, recovery can be difficult without backup codes. The Hated One addresses this by suggesting that users must "remove it from your accounts" if lost, but the friction of recovery remains a valid concern for mass adoption.

"Fido protocols are resistant to phishing attacks because key pairs are unique for each online service and associated account."

The commentary concludes that the transition to passwordless authentication is not just an upgrade but a necessity. The author's call to action is clear: "we kill the password." The argument is strengthened by the fact that this protocol is open and owned by no single company, ensuring that it remains a public good rather than a proprietary lock-in. The Hated One's insistence that "sites that do not support it are lagging behind" serves as a challenge to both consumers and corporations to demand better security standards.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest argument is the mathematical inevitability of password failure in the face of exponential computing growth; the technology simply cannot keep up. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in the practical friction of transitioning billions of users to a new authentication model, a hurdle that requires more than just better technology to overcome. Readers should watch for the rapid adoption of FIDO standards by major platforms, as this will likely mark the true beginning of the end for the traditional password.
