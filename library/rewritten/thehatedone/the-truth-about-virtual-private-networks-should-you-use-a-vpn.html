In an era where privacy is sold as a subscription service, The Hated One delivers a jarring reality check: the very tools marketed to make you invisible are often the single point of failure in your digital defense. This piece dismantles the myth that a Virtual Private Network is a magic shield against surveillance, arguing instead that it offers privacy by policy rather than privacy by design. For the busy professional relying on a monthly fee to sleep soundly at night, this is not just technical nitpicking; it is a critical correction of a dangerous misconception.

The Illusion of Anonymity

The Hated One begins by exposing the fundamental flaw in the VPN business model. While these services encrypt traffic between your device and their server, they cannot hide your identity from the provider itself. "Your VPN provider can still see everything your internet service provider can see all your traffic logs devices browser history," the author notes, pointing out that the only thing stopping them from abusing this data is a promise, not technology. This is a devastatingly simple truth that cuts through the marketing noise. The author argues that we are trusting strangers with our entire digital footprint based on a handshake agreement.

The commentary here is sharp because it shifts the blame from the user's lack of knowledge to the industry's deliberate obfuscation. The Hated One writes, "Virtual private networks offer privacy by policy not privacy by design that is nothing technologically prevents your VPN from monitoring your traffic and do with that whatever they want sell it throttle it or censor it." This reframing forces the reader to question the very nature of the product they are buying. If the provider holds the keys to the kingdom, the "tunnel" is merely a hallway with glass walls.

The Fingerprint You Cannot Hide

Even if a provider is honest, The Hated One argues that the technology of the internet itself makes true anonymity nearly impossible. Hiding an IP address is like hiding your face in a crowd while wearing a neon sign that says "I am here." The author explains that modern trackers collect thousands of data points beyond just your location. "Anything from snippets of your browsing history cache cookies browser configuration or device configuration along with your operating system battery life and screen resolution can be uniquely identifying," The Hated One states.

This section is particularly effective because it moves the conversation from network security to behavioral profiling. The author points out that even if you log off your Google account, the trackers embedded in the websites you visit, or even the VPN provider's own site, can stitch together a profile that identifies you. "Hiding your IP address doesn't do anything to block these trackers on mobile devices it becomes completely useless as you're often connected to GPS or cellular networks that also reveal your location." Critics might argue that for the average user, this level of tracking is an acceptable trade-off for convenience, but The Hated One insists that the expectation of anonymity is a lie sold to the non-technical public.

"Most of them require your email address upon signup and most payment methods will reveal your identity directly unless they accept cryptocurrency which is only pseudonymous or cash which can be truly anonymous but cash is very rarely accepted."

The Encryption Trap and Government Leverage

Perhaps the most unsettling argument concerns the limits of encryption and the power of the state. The Hated One clarifies that while VPNs encrypt traffic from the user to the server, the data is decrypted the moment it leaves the VPN provider to reach the destination website. "Technically none of these are lying they are encrypting your traffic but only from your device to their servers the traffic is only encrypted for your own network," the author explains. This distinction is vital; it means the VPN is not a hidden overlay network like Tor, but a relay point in the clear internet.

Furthermore, the author highlights the vulnerability of VPN providers to government pressure. "VPNs are the easiest thing for the government to monitor oftentimes they just go to a VPN provider directly and force them to reveal all user data or even started logging them if they didn't do it until then." This is a sobering reminder that in many jurisdictions, the provider has no legal incentive to fight for your privacy. The argument that a VPN makes you "NSA proof" is dismissed as a dangerous fantasy, with the author noting that global adversaries can easily correlate traffic timings to de-anonymize users.

When a VPN Actually Makes Sense

Despite the scathing critique, The Hated One does not dismiss VPNs entirely. The author concedes that these tools have legitimate uses, provided the user lowers their expectations regarding anonymity. "VPNs are useful at bypassing censorship and geo blocking they might be helpful at preventing throttling of your internet traffic by your ISP," the author writes. They are also valuable for securing connections on public Wi-Fi, such as in a coffee shop, where the local network might be compromised.

However, the author insists that a VPN must be part of a broader strategy, not the entire strategy. "Paying for a VPN service is a waste of money if you don't take those steps first," The Hated One argues, listing essential tools like ad blockers, cookie managers, and private browsing modes. The core message is that privacy is a practice, not a product. A counterargument worth considering is that for many non-technical users, the complexity of managing multiple privacy tools is a barrier to entry, making the "set it and forget it" promise of a VPN appealing despite its flaws. Yet, The Hated One maintains that this convenience comes at the cost of a false sense of security.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest contribution is the exposure of the "privacy by policy" fallacy, forcing readers to realize that a VPN is a tool for obfuscation, not invisibility. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its high bar for true privacy, which may feel unattainable to the average user, but this rigor is necessary to break the cycle of misinformation. The reader should walk away understanding that if they want real privacy, they must stop looking for a silver bullet and start building a layered defense.
