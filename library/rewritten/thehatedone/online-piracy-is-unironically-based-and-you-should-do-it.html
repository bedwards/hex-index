The Hated One makes a provocative claim that piracy is not merely a victimless crime, but a moral imperative against a legal system designed to strip individuals of ownership. This piece stands out because it moves beyond the tired debate of "stealing vs. sharing" to argue that modern copyright law has become a tool for corporate monopolies to control everything from your car's acceleration to your right to repair your own tractor. For anyone who has ever felt frustrated by a software lock or a subscription model, this analysis offers a radical reframing of their daily digital struggles.

The Myth of Theft

The author immediately dismantles the standard moral objection to piracy. The Hated One writes, "Piracy is not theft and it will never be theft. Theft is a zero sum game. You steal from somebody and they no longer have it." This distinction is crucial to the entire essay; by defining theft as the removal of a physical object, the author argues that copying an idea leaves the original owner with nothing lost. The core of the argument is that copyright infringement is a legal technicality, not a moral failing, especially when the "victim" is a conglomerate with billions in profits. This framing is effective because it shifts the burden of guilt from the individual user to the legal framework itself.

However, critics might note that while digital copying isn't theft in the traditional sense, it still deprives creators of potential revenue in a market where that revenue is the primary incentive for production. The Hated One dismisses this by pointing to the scale of corporate wealth, but the economic impact on independent artists remains a valid counterpoint.

The Erosion of Ownership

The commentary then pivots to the concept of ownership in the digital age, arguing that purchasing a product no longer guarantees possession. The Hated One writes, "When you buy a copyrighted work, you don't own it like you would own anything else you could buy. You technically only own the paper and ink the book is made of." This is a stark reminder that what we buy is often just a license to use, not the item itself. The author extends this logic to physical goods, noting that software locks now prevent owners from repairing their own devices. The argument here is that copyright law has been weaponized to create artificial scarcity and dependency, turning consumers into renters of their own property.

Copyright is literally the biggest conspiracy in the entire world and the neighboring galaxy.

The Hated One illustrates this with the example of John Deere tractors, where farmers are legally barred from fixing their own equipment, forcing them to rely on expensive, authorized technicians. This section lands hard because it connects abstract legal theory to tangible, life-or-death consequences for farmers who cannot afford to wait for a repair. The author suggests that bypassing these locks is not just a technical act, but a necessary rebellion against a system that prioritizes profit over functionality.

Silencing Speech and Surveillance

Beyond ownership, the piece argues that copyright is a primary tool for censorship and surveillance. The Hated One writes, "If you want to protect free speech by spreading these copyright materials, you are committing piracy." The author provides examples ranging from the Turkish government using copyright strikes to silence political dissent to police officers blasting music to prevent the recording of brutality. The argument is that automated copyright systems allow powerful entities to bypass due process and silence critics instantly. Furthermore, the author posits that piracy is a superior privacy strategy compared to legal streaming services. The Hated One writes, "When you pirate, you still need to be careful where you get your files from. But in most cases, you have more privacy by not signing up to a platform with your personally identifiable information." This reframes piracy from a crime of greed to an act of digital self-defense against data harvesting.

Critics might argue that relying on piracy for privacy is a fragile solution that leaves users vulnerable to malware and lacks the stability of legitimate services. Yet, the author's point about the invasive nature of anti-cheat software and data brokers remains a strong indictment of the current digital ecosystem.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its ability to connect disparate issues—repair rights, free speech, and data privacy—under a single umbrella of copyright overreach. The Hated One's biggest vulnerability is the lack of a concrete, scalable alternative to the current system, relying instead on piracy as a temporary stopgap. Readers should watch for how this argument influences the growing "Right to Repair" movement and whether it can successfully shift public perception of intellectual property laws.
