The Hated One delivers a scorching indictment of Sam Altman, framing the OpenAI CEO not as a visionary, but as a monopolist who weaponized the promise of open-source technology to build a closed, profit-driven empire. This piece stands out for its willingness to connect the dots between Altman's public safety rhetoric, his internal boardroom coups, and the grim reality of the low-wage labor powering his models. It forces a re-evaluation of whether the current AI boom is a revolution or a carefully orchestrated consolidation of power.

The Illusion of Openness

The core of the argument rests on the betrayal of OpenAI's founding mission. The Hated One writes, "The Duality of a tech bro a benign utopian with a dream to change the world and a Cutthroat CEO of a big Tech startup looking to monopolize a new market all for himself that is the story of any Silicon Valley Idol in the past 40 years and that is now the story of Sam Altman." This framing is effective because it strips away the novelty of the AI narrative, placing Altman squarely in a lineage of tech giants who promised disruption while delivering control. The author argues that the shift from a nonprofit to a for-profit entity was not a necessary evolution for safety, but a calculated move to secure capital and lock out competitors.

As The Hated One puts it, "Sam Altman is locking it down instead of Open Access he is creating a blackbox to get to this point." The commentary here is sharp: by refusing to share model weights or training data, Altman prevents independent verification and stifles the collaborative progress that defined the early days of the field. Critics might note that proprietary models can sometimes accelerate commercial adoption, but the author effectively counters that this speed comes at the cost of transparency and democratic access. The piece suggests that the "safety" argument is merely a shield for monopoly, noting that Altman has lobbied the government to regulate out open-source competitors while his own company operates with impunity.

The Billion-Dollar Lifeline

The narrative then shifts to the financial precariousness of OpenAI and its desperate reliance on government and corporate alliances. The Hated One highlights a massive joint venture involving the United States government, Oracle, and SoftBank, stating, "The Venture had already committed $100 billion and this number will rise to 500 billion over four years." This section is crucial because it challenges the popular perception of AI as a self-sustaining miracle. The author argues that despite the hype, the technology is hitting a wall of diminishing returns, with quality data running dry and training costs skyrocketing.

"Sam Altman was probably going to run out of money very soon... The half a trillion dollar joint venture with Donald Trump is a Divine salvation for him."

The author's assertion that this venture is a "divine salvation" for a company that is "losing money on their premium subscriptions" paints a picture of an industry propped up by state capital rather than genuine product-market fit. The commentary suggests that the push for nuclear-powered data centers is less about solving an energy crisis and more about creating a barrier to entry that only the most well-funded entities can cross. This creates a scenario where the government effectively subsidizes a private monopoly, a point that resonates deeply in an era of growing skepticism toward big tech.

The Human Cost of the Black Box

Perhaps the most disturbing revelation in the text concerns the human labor hidden behind the polished interface of ChatGPT. The Hated One writes, "One of Altman's best kept secret is hidden behind the line that ChatGPT is a testament of computer thinking... what isn't told often is that ChatGPT was built on top of cheap labor in poor countries to make ChatGPT less toxic." The author details how OpenAI outsourced the task of filtering harmful content to workers in Kenya, who were paid as little as $1.32 per hour to view millions of hours of graphic violence and abuse.

This evidence is presented not just as an ethical failing, but as a structural necessity for the current business model. The Hated One argues, "Building such a detector is mostly done by hand which means thousands of people have to go through millions of hours of the most horrible content you can imagine." The commentary here is devastating: the "safety" of the AI is purchased with the psychological trauma of underpaid workers. The author notes that the company's reliance on this labor force collapsed quickly due to the horrific nature of the work, yet the system continues. This undermines the narrative of AI as an autonomous, benevolent force, revealing it instead as a machine built on human suffering.

The Boardroom Coup

The piece also reconstructs the dramatic events of Altman's brief firing in 2023, portraying it as a failed attempt by the board to stop his authoritarian drift. The Hated One states, "The original board members were the people that feared powerful AI... but they were also concerned with such a powerful AI falling into the wrong hands... for the longest time the effective Ultra on the board thought the public were the wrong hands but Altman made them realize that they could also belong to one of their own." This reframing of the board's motivation—from abstract safety to immediate corporate governance—is a compelling angle.

As The Hated One puts it, "Microsoft threatened the board that if they don't reinstate Altman as CEO they would move him and all of his loyal employees over to Microsoft." The author uses this incident to illustrate that Altman's power is not just in his ideas, but in his ability to leverage Microsoft's resources to neutralize internal dissent. The narrative suggests that the board's capitulation was a surrender of their fiduciary duty, leaving OpenAI as a "subsidiary of Microsoft" with no effective oversight. Critics might argue that the board's initial decision to fire Altman was chaotic and poorly executed, but the author's focus on the subsequent threat of mass defection highlights the structural imbalance of power that now defines the company.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest asset is the synthesis of Altman's public persona with the gritty, often unethical realities of his company's operations, from the exploitation of Kenyan workers to the political maneuvering that secured a half-trillion-dollar bailout. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on a few unverified claims regarding specific financial losses and the exact nature of the "distillation" accusations against competitors, which could be contested by industry insiders. However, the piece succeeds in forcing readers to look past the hype and ask who truly benefits from the concentration of AI power, making it an essential read for anyone trying to understand the current trajectory of the technology.
