The Hated One delivers a chilling premise that reframes the internet not as a natural phenomenon, but as a fragile agreement that is actively unraveling. The most startling claim isn't that the internet will die, but that it is already being replaced by a "multiverse of parallel internets" where the simple act of reaching someone across a border becomes impossible. This is not science fiction; it is a technical reality being engineered by governments and corporations right now.

The Fragility of Consensus

The piece begins by dismantling the myth of the internet's permanence. The author argues that what we call the internet is merely "a global network of all networks formed by consensus of voluntary participants." This is a crucial distinction often missed in mainstream coverage: the internet exists only because everyone agrees to use the same rules for addressing devices. The Hated One explains that without the Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (ICANN) to coordinate unique identifiers, "there wouldn't be one unified global internet." Instead, we would face a scenario where "users would be locked in to access only those addresses governed by their national authority."

This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from abstract "censorship" to concrete technical fragmentation. The author notes that while ICANN operates on voluntary consensus, "there is no rule that says countries have to be a part of it." This creates a vulnerability where nations can opt out, creating what experts call the "splinternet." The argument holds up well when examining recent events, such as Ukraine's request to ICANN to remove Russian IP addresses. While ICANN rejected this as technically unfeasible, the mere attempt highlights the tension. As The Hated One writes, "The only actor that can effectively shut down Russia's internet is Kremlin itself." This is a sobering reminder that the global network relies on the self-restraint of sovereign states.

If more countries or blocks follow suit, it could start an information cold war.

The Rise of the Sovereign Net

The commentary moves to the active construction of these walled gardens, focusing on Russia's "Runet." The author details how Moscow launched a national domain name system to function independently if cut off from the global consensus. The Hated One notes that tests in 2021 showed Russian websites could still run even when disconnected from the world, a move designed to create a "sovereign splinter net." This isn't just about resilience; it's about control. The author warns that if more nations follow this path, "nations could start feeling encouraged to launch cyber attacks to the others splinter nets because there wouldn't be the risk of backfire and collateral damage."

Critics might argue that national firewalls are primarily defensive measures against foreign interference rather than offensive tools for an arms race. However, the author's point about the "information cold war" gains weight when considering how these isolated networks could be weaponized without the usual global interdependencies. The piece effectively illustrates that the splinternet is not a single event but a gradual process of "cyber vulcanization" where the global web hardens into separate, incompatible blocks.

Corporate Gatekeepers and Algorithmic Walls

Beyond state actors, The Hated One identifies a second, perhaps more insidious driver of fragmentation: tech giants. The author argues that "internet edge providers such as social media and search engines have become central gatekeepers of much of the content visible to most users." When companies like Apple or Amazon make business decisions, they can "curate a splinternet" for their users. The example of Apple removing news apps in China or Amazon curtailing operations in Russia demonstrates how private policy can enforce public isolation. "When Apple decided to remove news media apps like Quartz or The New York Times from the Chinese app store it meant that no iPhone user would be able to use these apps and there was no way to work around it."

This section is particularly strong because it highlights the "quasi infrastructure status" of these corporations. The author points out that Google's algorithms already create "filter bubbles" where "most content users subscribed to will never appear on their feeds." This algorithmic bias acts as a soft firewall, splintering the user experience even without government intervention. As The Hated One puts it, "Fulfilling the demands for content censorship on social media and search engines is frighteningly easy." The danger here is that the fragmentation is automated and personalized, making it harder to detect than a national firewall.

The Chinese Model and the New Protocol

The piece culminates in a deep dive into China's "Great Firewall" and its ambition to build a new internet protocol. The Hated One describes a system where "internet access will be permissioned and administrators would have the power to operate a whitelist mode." Unlike the current global internet, which is "fundamentally leaky," the proposed Chinese model would "hard code access controls into the protocol." This represents a fundamental shift from a network of equals to a top-down hierarchy. The author warns that if China succeeds, they could "start offering it as a service to developing nations, expanding their control on China's internet abroad."

This is the most alarming part of the argument. The author suggests that the current internet allows anyone to host a website with just a computer and access, but the new model would require a license. "The differences with the current internet protocol are stark." While the global internet is built on openness, this new framework is designed for surveillance and control, sharing all data with the government to serve AI and big data applications. A counterargument worth considering is whether such a rigid system can truly compete with the innovation of the open web, but the author's focus is on the geopolitical spread of this model, which poses a genuine threat to the global consensus.

The Path to a Permissionless Future

In the final analysis, The Hated One offers a glimmer of hope through peer-to-peer technology. The author argues that the solution lies in "mesh networks of peer-to-peer connections" that do not rely on traditional infrastructure. "If we can create our own mesh network... we wouldn't have to rely on ground wires as much." Projects like Briar and Cushe are highlighted as early attempts to build networks that work without the internet, using Bluetooth and Wi-Fi to bypass central authorities. The Hated One concludes that "the first peer-to-peer network that begins offering mesh routing would be our best chance to create a truly permissionless neutral and consent based internet."

This optimistic turn feels earned because it addresses the root cause of the splinternet: reliance on centralized pipes and servers. By shifting to a model where devices communicate directly, the power to disconnect is removed from the state and the corporation. The author's vision of a "new uncontrollable internet from scratch" is a bold counter-narrative to the doom of the splinternet.

Bottom Line

The Hated One's strongest argument is the technical deconstruction of the internet as a voluntary consensus, making the case that its fragmentation is an inevitable consequence of rising digital sovereignty. The piece's biggest vulnerability is the assumption that peer-to-peer mesh networks can scale quickly enough to counter the momentum of state-sponsored splinternets. Readers should watch for the next phase of this conflict: whether China's new protocol gains traction in the Global South, effectively creating a second, incompatible internet that runs parallel to the one we know.

The trust in the telecommunication providers is the core of this issue.

As governments and corporations carve up the digital landscape, the only way to preserve a truly global connection may be to stop using the internet as it was designed and start building our own.
