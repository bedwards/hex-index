Sam Denby delivers a masterclass in geopolitical anomaly by exposing the Vatican not as a traditional nation, but as a sovereign state that defies every standard definition of statehood. While most analyses of international relations focus on borders and armies, Denby zeroes in on the bizarre reality that the world's smallest country has no permanent population, no economy, and no demonym, yet commands more diplomatic respect than most superpowers. This is essential listening for anyone trying to understand how moral authority can supersede physical territory in the modern world.

The Paradox of Non-Existence

Denby begins by dismantling the conventional checklist for what makes a country. He points out that while nations like France or the United States fit the mold, the Vatican sits in a category of its own. "Perhaps most damningly the Vatican has no real permanent population," Denby writes, noting that everyone there is merely a temporary worker. He highlights the linguistic void this creates: "there isn't even a universally recognized term to refer to someone from the Vatican because nobody's from the Vatican." This observation is striking because it forces the listener to confront the idea that citizenship can exist without a homeland or a birthright.

The author further argues that the Vatican lacks the functional machinery of a state. It has no independent legal system, relying on Italian courts and prisons, and no healthcare system of its own. "When you look at it objectively the Vatican is hardly a nation and yet it enjoys complete near universally recognized Sovereign statehood," Denby asserts. This contradiction is the engine of the piece. It suggests that in international law, recognition is more powerful than reality. Critics might argue that this distinction is merely semantic, but Denby's evidence that the Vatican is the only entity in the CIA World Factbook without a listed demonym proves the anomaly is structural, not just theoretical.

The Vatican is the only country in the CIA World Factbook that just doesn't have a demonym listed because everyone who holds Vatican citizenship was born into another citizenship.

The Evolution of Sovereignty

The commentary then shifts to the historical mechanics of how this unique status was achieved. Denby explains that the Pope's refusal to accept a non-sovereign offer from Italy in 1870 was a strategic gamble that eventually paid off. "The pope quickly rejected Italy's offer since doing so would effectively act as recognition of their power over the conquered territory," Denby explains. This refusal led to the "Roman Question," a sixty-year standoff where Popes considered themselves prisoners in their own city. It wasn't until Mussolini's rise that the 1929 Lateran Treaty resolved the conflict, creating the modern state.

Denby describes the resulting government as a "non-hereditary elected absolute monarchy," a phrase that perfectly captures the absurdity of the system. The Pope holds unchecked authority, yet he is elected by cardinals rather than inheriting the throne. "The Holy See is both the governing body of the Vatican State and the Roman Catholic Church that's to say there's zero distinction between the state and the church they are quite literally the same thing," he notes. This fusion is the key to understanding why the Vatican can operate globally. Unlike a standard government, the Holy See is a non-territorial sovereign entity that maintains diplomatic ties even without a population to represent in the traditional sense.

Diplomacy Without a Vote

The most compelling part of Denby's coverage is the analysis of the Holy See's role at the United Nations. He details how the entity secured permanent observer status, allowing it to speak on global issues without the power to vote. "The Holy See could observe comment and maintain seats at the UN's general assembly but it couldn't vote," Denby writes. This arrangement was born from a desire for neutrality; the Vatican feared that voting would compromise its moral authority. "Such permanent neutrality would be undercut by a vote to enact sanctions against a nation for example," he argues.

Denby illustrates this influence through the case of human cloning in the early 2000s. While many nations wanted a simple ban on reproductive cloning, the Holy See pushed for a broader prohibition that included therapeutic cloning, arguing that using embryos for research was destroying human life. "The Holy See was undeterred and continued the moral lobbying on the part of the church across the following vote," Denby recounts. This persistence helped shape a global ban that was far more comprehensive than the initial proposals. The argument here is that the Vatican's lack of voting power is actually a strength, allowing it to act as a purely moral arbiter rather than a political player.

The Holy See is the only instance of an absolute monarchy where the ruler is elected rather than pulled from a line of succession.

Critics might note that this moral high ground is not always universally accepted, particularly when the Vatican's stance on issues like reproductive rights or gender identity clashes with modern human rights frameworks. However, Denby's point remains valid: the Vatican's ability to influence global policy without the baggage of a national agenda is a unique diplomatic asset that no other entity possesses.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's piece succeeds by treating the Vatican not as a religious relic, but as a sophisticated geopolitical entity that has mastered the art of soft power. The strongest part of the argument is the demonstration that sovereignty is a social construct sustained by mutual recognition rather than physical attributes. The biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that this model is sustainable indefinitely, as the world's secularization may eventually erode the moral authority that underpins the Holy See's unique status.
