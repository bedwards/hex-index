Sam Denby doesn't just explain how aircraft carriers work; he reframes them as the ultimate paradox of modern warfare: floating cities so massive they are both the most powerful weapons on Earth and the most vulnerable targets imaginable. While most coverage fixates on the sheer tonnage of these ships, Denby's analysis of the logistical ballet required to keep them running—specifically the high-stakes choreography of resupply at sea and the color-coded chaos of the flight deck—reveals a level of operational fragility that contradicts their image of invincibility.

The Floating City Paradox

Denby opens by establishing the sheer scale of these assets, noting that "a single aircraft carrier is enough to markedly change the level of a nation's military might." He grounds this abstract power in geography, pointing out that under international law, these ships can legally position themselves "up to 14 miles or 22 kilometres from any country's coast." This proximity is the source of their strategic value, allowing a nation to project air power directly into the heart of a region where "80% of the world's population lives within 60 miles or 100 kilometers from the ocean."

The author's most striking observation, however, is the comparison of operational tempo. He writes that the French Navy's Charles de Gaulle "is capable of launching an aircraft every 30 seconds," which means that at peak efficiency, "the aircraft carrier Charles de Gaulle becomes busier than Charles de Gaulle Airport in Paris." This analogy effectively strips away the military mystique and replaces it with a relatable metric of industrial throughput. It forces the reader to visualize a moving platform performing the work of a major international hub, a feat Denby rightly identifies as "no easy feat."

The operation of these vessels gives militaries enormous strategic advantage, but they also represent one of their greatest operational challenges.

The Logistics of Survival

The core of Denby's argument shifts from offense to the delicate ecosystem of survival. He clarifies that while carriers are offensive powerhouses, they are "big targets" that "never travel alone." He details the composition of a Carrier Strike Group, which includes destroyers, cruisers, and submarines, but focuses heavily on the unsung hero: the supply ship. Denby explains that while nuclear-powered carriers can sail for decades without refueling, they cannot carry enough food or aviation fuel for long-term combat. "It would be inefficient and place the carriers in a position of vulnerability to have to visit a port every few weeks," he argues, making the ability to restock while moving critical.

He describes the resupply process with vivid detail, noting that ships "match speed and maneuver alongside the carrier" to transfer goods. The methods range from the mechanical "zip line" of pallets on dollies to the "simpler yet more dangerous" method of using helicopters to ferry cargo. This section highlights the fragility of the system; a single error in speed matching or weather could halt the flow of essentials to thousands of sailors.

Critics might note that Denby underplays the vulnerability of these supply lines in an era of long-range anti-ship missiles. If an enemy can strike the supply ship before it reaches the carrier, the entire strike group's endurance is compromised. However, the author's focus remains on the ingenuity of the current system rather than its potential failure points.

The Choreography of Chaos

Perhaps the most humanizing element of Denby's coverage is his breakdown of the flight deck's "carefully choreographed chaos." He explains that with only 1,106 feet of runway, aircraft cannot simply taxi to takeoff speed. Instead, "all US and French carriers use a system of catapults to get aircraft up to takeoff speed within three to four seconds." This mechanical necessity dictates the human organization on deck, where every role is visually distinct.

Denby writes that "everyone's job is easily identifiable by the color shirt they wear," listing the specific duties of yellow shirts (navigation), red shirts (ammunition), and brown shirts (plane captains). This color-coding is not just aesthetic; it is a life-or-death communication system in an environment where noise and confusion are constant. He notes that the flight deck is so small that not all aircraft can fit on it, requiring massive elevators to shuttle planes between the deck and the hangar.

The flight deck is a dangerous place given its small size it's so small that all the carrier's aircraft can't fit on it.

The author also touches on the human cost of this environment, revealing that 6,000 people live on board, with enlisted personnel often sharing bunks in rooms with over a hundred others. "Many of those on board can go weeks without seeing sunlight," a stark reminder that these "cities" are also prisons of sorts, where the mission takes precedence over personal space.

The Question of Obsolescence

Denby concludes by addressing the elephant in the room: are aircraft carriers becoming obsolete? He acknowledges that "the sinking of a single US aircraft carrier could result in more American military deaths than the entire Iraq war." He cites war games where Swedish and French submarines successfully "approach close enough to the carrier's where they could have... launch torpedoes and potentially sank the carriers." This evidence suggests that despite their defensive screens, carriers are not unsinkable.

Yet, Denby argues that the strategic value of power projection outweighs these risks. He points out that the US maintains a near-constant presence, with carriers capable of reaching the Middle East from Norfolk in a week or North Korea from Japan in 29 hours. "American carriers spend plenty of time just cruising around the world's oceans reminding other countries of the US military power," he writes. Even if they are vulnerable, their mere presence serves as a deterrent.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's coverage succeeds by balancing the awe-inspiring scale of aircraft carriers with the gritty, dangerous reality of their operation. His strongest argument is that these ships are not just weapons, but complex logistical ecosystems that require a level of coordination bordering on the miraculous. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its slight hesitation to fully grapple with the existential threat posed by modern hypersonic missiles and drone swarms, which could render the carrier's massive size a liability rather than an asset. Nevertheless, as long as nations believe in the power of presence, these floating cities will remain central to global strategy.
