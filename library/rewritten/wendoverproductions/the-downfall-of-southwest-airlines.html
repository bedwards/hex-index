Sam Denby doesn't just tell the story of an airline's collapse; he exposes how a beloved American institution was dismantled not by market forces, but by a specific, preventable failure of technology and hubris. While most analyses blame the pandemic or general inflation, Denby argues that Southwest's downfall was a self-inflicted wound caused by a hostile force: the very cost-cutting culture that once made it great. This is a cautionary tale for any leader who believes efficiency can replace resilience.

The Golden Age of Egalitarianism

Denby begins by establishing the high water mark of Southwest's reputation, painting a picture of an airline that seemed to operate on a different moral plane than its competitors. He writes, "Southwest used to be the stuff of legend. An audacious upstart turned industry giant." The author meticulously details how the airline maintained a 20% profit margin in an industry where 5% is considered a success, all while offering perks like free checked bags and open seating. This framing is crucial because it sets the stakes: this wasn't a struggling company trying to survive; it was a dominant player that chose to erode its own brand.

The author traces this success back to a unique regulatory loophole in Texas that allowed Southwest to set its own fares, free from the Civil Aeronautics Board's oversight. Denby notes that this allowed the company to "tap into a price sensitive market that other airlines couldn't." He argues that the company's internal culture was just as vital as its legal status. "Southwest argued that taking good care of their staff allowed them to take good care of their customers," Denby writes, highlighting a profit-sharing model and a near-zero layoff policy that created a loyal workforce. This section effectively reframes Southwest not just as a business, but as a social experiment that worked for decades.

Critics might argue that this "golden age" was an anomaly driven by specific regulatory conditions that no longer exist, making the comparison to today's environment unfair. However, Denby's point is that the company had the capital and the culture to adapt, yet chose to double down on short-term gains instead.

For decades upon decades, it stayed egalitarian, ethical, and just simply excellent. Until now.

The Infiltration of Finance

The narrative shifts as Denby identifies the turning point: the moment the company's core principles were sacrificed for shareholder value. He describes a "hostile force" that infiltrated the company, forcing it to abandon its identity. "In one quick moment, Southwest eroded five decades of industry-leading brand image in favor of squeezing the customers for more," Denby observes. The evidence is stark: the introduction of baggage fees, expiring flight credits, and the reduction of legroom to create a premium section. This is not subtle commentary; it is a direct indictment of a strategic pivot that alienated the very customers who built the brand.

The author suggests that the leadership, specifically CEO Bob Jordan, was a product of the old system but failed to protect it. Denby points out that Jordan had been with the company for 37 years, rising from a programmer to the top job. "The fact that the decision makers from the airline didn't come from other airlines who did things differently was almost certainly why the airline stayed so unique for so long," he argues. The implication is that once the company grew too large to ignore Wall Street's demands, the internal guardians of the culture were powerless to stop the slide toward standardization and greed.

The Elliot Meltdown: A Technological Failure

The climax of Denby's argument arrives with the 2022 winter storm Elliot. He moves beyond the weather to dissect the operational fragility that the storm exposed. The author explains that while other airlines relied on third-party contractors with enclosed deicing rigs, Southwest's cost-cutting led them to use open-basket rigs that were efficient in normal weather but disastrous in extreme cold. "The exposure meant the subset of staff trained in deicing operations had to be swapped out frequently to prevent frostbite and hypothermia," Denby writes. This detail is vital; it shows how a decision made to save money on equipment directly compromised human safety and operational capacity.

However, the true catastrophe was software. Denby introduces "SkySolver," the proprietary system meant to manage the airline's complex point-to-point network. He describes it as a system that "wasn't very good at that." The software took 25 minutes to calculate a solution, a timeframe that was useless in a rapidly evolving crisis. "SkySolver could only solve future problems, not existing ones," Denby notes, explaining that once a flight was late, the system became blind to the issue. This is the core of his argument: Southwest's reliance on an antiquated, brittle system to manage a massive, interconnected network was a recipe for disaster.

The human cost of this technological failure was immense. Denby describes how the airline "quite literally lost track of where all its crew were." With the automated system failing, humans had to manually reassign thousands of flights, but the network operation center was understaffed and overwhelmed. "Flight crews waited for hours on hold," Denby writes, noting that during this wait, they were not accumulating the rest hours required by the Federal Aviation Administration to fly the next day. This created a vicious cycle where the solution to the delay caused more delays.

When something went wrong in Southwest's network, it was up to its proprietary SkySolver software to fix it. The only problem was it wasn't very good at that.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's strongest argument is that Southwest's collapse was not an act of God, but a failure of governance where cost-cutting on technology and human resources destroyed operational resilience. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its somewhat deterministic view of corporate finance, implying that the "hostile force" of Wall Street is the sole villain, which may overlook the internal complacency of a leadership team that failed to modernize its systems for decades. Readers should watch for whether Southwest's new management can actually fix the software and culture, or if the brand has been permanently damaged beyond repair.
