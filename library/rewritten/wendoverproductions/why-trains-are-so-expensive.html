Sam Denby dismantles the common assumption that trains are expensive because they are inefficient, revealing instead that they are expensive because they are overstaffed, overregulated, and socially obligated to serve routes no airline would touch. While most analyses stop at comparing ticket prices to flights, Denby goes deeper, exposing how the very structure of rail travel—its slow speed, its unionized workforce, and its political mandate to connect rural America—creates a cost structure that defies simple market logic.

The Human Cost of Slowness

Denby's most striking revelation is that the primary driver of train fares isn't the steel or the electricity, but the sheer number of hours people are paid to move them. "Trains require a lot of people to operate," he notes, pointing out that Amtrak employs 20,000 people for just 85,000 daily passengers. This ratio—one employee for every four passengers—stands in stark contrast to the efficiency of aviation. As Denby puts it, "an airline would only have to pay employees for an hour of work while Amtrak has to pay their employees for 3 and a half hours of work." The math is brutal: on the Chicago to Los Angeles route, an airline pays for four hours of labor, while Amtrak pays for 44. This isn't just inefficiency; it is the direct financial consequence of speed. Critics might argue that automation could solve this, but Denby correctly identifies that the physical nature of rail and the density of stops make the current staffing model a structural necessity rather than a management failure.

"The single largest expense for Amtrak is Staffing... these salary costs are so high that they account for over a third of the ticket price."

The argument holds up because it reframes the "expensive train" narrative. We often blame the infrastructure, but Denby shows that the human element is the heaviest burden. The high wages are justified by the specialized, unionized nature of the work, yet the low speed of the train means those wages are amortized over a much longer period than in the sky.

The Hidden Subsidy: Serving the Unprofitable

Perhaps the most compelling part of Denby's coverage is his explanation of why profitable routes exist at all. He details how the Northeast Regional, the only consistently profitable Amtrak line, effectively subsidizes the entire network. "Some routes like the Sunset Limited between New Orleans and Los Angeles lose as many as 21.7 cents per passenger per mile," he writes. This loss is not an accident; it is a feature of the system. Denby argues that these unprofitable routes are essential because "trains are less expensive than planes to service small communities." He illustrates this with a stark comparison: it is cheaper to fly from Havre, Montana, to Chicago than to fly from Havre to London, yet Amtrak can bring Havre residents to Chicago for a few dollars.

This is where the political reality clashes with the economic one. Denby suggests that the high cost of the profitable routes is the price we pay for social mobility. "Research has shown that ease of access to transport has a stronger influence on whether someone will earn more than their parents did than the level of crime in their area." By keeping rural lines open, the system ensures that geography doesn't dictate destiny. A counterargument worth considering is whether this cross-subsidization is the most efficient way to achieve social equity, or if direct grants to rural communities would be more effective. However, Denby's point stands: the train is not just a vehicle; it is a lifeline that the market would otherwise sever.

The Psychology of Pricing

Denby also tackles the confusing, often frustrating nature of train ticket pricing, explaining it not as greed, but as a sophisticated attempt to capture value from different types of travelers. He describes how operators sell three types of tickets—Advance, Off-Peak, and Anytime—to segment the market. "The operator always wants to have as many seats as possible filled even if that means selling cheap tickets," he explains. The system is designed to extract the maximum willingness to pay from business travelers who need flexibility, while still offering a seat to the budget-conscious tourist who books months in advance. "What they're really paying for is convenience," Denby concludes, noting that a person paying £20 might sit next to someone paying £200 for the same physical journey.

"In a perfect world for the operator they could ask every potential passenger what the maximum amount they'd be willing to pay for the journey is... however in practice nobody would ever answer the question truthfully."

This analysis of dynamic pricing is particularly sharp because it demystifies the anger many feel when ticket prices surge. It is not price gouging; it is economic survival in a high-fixed-cost industry. The complexity of the pricing model is a direct result of the need to balance the high costs of staffing and infrastructure with the need to fill seats.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's analysis succeeds because it refuses to treat train travel as a simple commodity; instead, he reveals it as a complex intersection of labor economics, political mandate, and social engineering. The strongest part of his argument is the realization that the high cost of a ticket in New York is the direct price of keeping a train running in rural Montana. The biggest vulnerability in the piece is a slight underestimation of how much technological innovation in automation could eventually reduce the massive staffing costs that currently dominate the fare structure. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: when you pay a premium for a train ticket, you aren't just paying for a seat; you are paying for the social contract that keeps the entire network alive.
