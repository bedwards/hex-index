Sam Denby transforms a geography lesson into a global scavenger hunt, proving that the most mundane borders often hide the world's strangest stories. Rather than simply listing nations, Denby argues that the quirks of sovereignty—from double enclaves to flags that defy color theory—reveal a world far more chaotic and interconnected than any textbook suggests.

The Architecture of Absurdity

Denby's approach is to let the map speak for itself, using specific, often ridiculous anomalies to anchor his narrative. He doesn't just state facts; he highlights the sheer improbability of them. "Namibia has this long protusion which gives them access to the zambesi river although there are other countries that speak a cre version of Dutch like Namibia which speaks Africans which is just close enough to Dutch that a Dutch and namibian could hold a conversation." This observation about language and geography colliding sets the tone: the world is a patchwork of historical accidents.

The commentary shines when Denby connects disparate locations through these oddities. He notes that "Libya is so large that its Northwest point is closer to Luxembourg than to its Southeast Point," a fact that instantly reframes our understanding of scale and proximity. By juxtaposing the world's largest countries with its smallest, he creates a sense of global intimacy. "If you dig a hole straight down and through the Earth you'd pop up just off the coast of uguay," he writes regarding the Korean Demilitarized Zone. This playful use of antipodes serves as a recurring motif, reminding the reader that every point on Earth has a hidden counterpart.

If you dig a hole straight down through the Earth from the Marshall Islands you arrive just off the coast of Liberia.

Critics might argue that focusing on trivia distracts from the serious geopolitical tensions that define these borders. However, Denby's inclusion of the "United Nations disengagement Observer Force Zone" and the "buffer between Israeli controlled Syria and Syria" grounds the absurdity in reality. The humor doesn't erase the conflict; it makes the complexity of sovereignty more digestible for a busy audience.

The Human Element in Data

Beyond geography, Denby weaves in surprising social statistics that challenge stereotypes. He contrasts the political progress of one nation with the stagnation of another: "Uganda is a surprisingly Progressive Nation with the highest representation of women in Parliament in the world at 63%" while "the Solomon Islands on the other hand only has one women in their Parliament or 2% representation." This direct comparison forces the reader to confront the uneven distribution of progress across the globe.

He also tackles the economics of tourism and isolation with a sharp eye. "Bahrain on the other hand receives 7.7 tourist visits per citizen per year," a stark contrast to "Bangladesh is the number one least touristy country since for each yearly tourist visit there are 1,273 Bangladeshi citizens." These numbers are not just data points; they are narratives about openness and isolation. Denby's observation that "Macedonia will almost always have fyrom under its name" due to a naming dispute with Greece illustrates how political friction can literally rewrite a country's identity on a map.

The solution was to call the country the Former Yugoslav Republic of Macedonia in all official capacities while colloquially it's still known as Macedonia.

The piece also touches on the bizarre nature of modern statehood. He points out that "Oman is an exclave of the United Arab Emirates except this part of Oman is actually an enclave inside the UAE which makes this the only double Enclave in the Middle East." This geometric impossibility highlights the messy reality of colonial borders and modern treaties. While some might find the rapid-fire delivery overwhelming, the sheer density of these facts creates a compelling rhythm that keeps the listener engaged.

The Global Tapestry

Denby concludes by circling back to the sheer diversity of human organization. He notes that "Kuwait has the highest value currency in the world at $3.3 to each Kuwaiti," while "saom and prce doora at 23,000 doora to the dollar" represents the opposite extreme. This economic spectrum, paired with cultural tidbits like "Antigua and Barbuda whose native language too is the origin of English words like canoe barbecue hammock and hurricane," paints a picture of a world that is simultaneously vast and intimately connected.

His final observation about the "teranes"—a tiny section of border between Slovenia and Croatia that is claimed by neither—serves as a perfect metaphor for the entire piece. "There are only three African countries left... Atria which has neither an official or majority spoken language." These edge cases are not footnotes; they are the defining features of a complex global system. Denby's ability to move from the highest mountain to the smallest currency without losing momentum is a testament to his skill as a storyteller.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's "Every Country in the World" succeeds because it treats geography not as a static list, but as a dynamic story of human conflict, cooperation, and confusion. The strongest part of the argument is its refusal to separate the trivial from the significant, showing how a border dispute in Macedonia or a double enclave in Oman reflects the broader chaos of international relations. Its biggest vulnerability is the sheer volume of information, which risks overwhelming the listener, but the engaging delivery and surprising connections make the effort worthwhile.

The world is not a puzzle to be solved, but a collection of stories waiting to be told, one border at a time.
