Sam Denby dismantles the notion that buying Greenland is a strategic masterstroke by revealing a stark military reality: the United States already dominates the Arctic, while Russia is aggressively fortifying its position. This isn't a story about real estate; it's a geopolitical audit that exposes why a simple cash transaction ignores the brutal physics of ice, distance, and nuclear deterrence. For busy leaders tracking the shifting balance of power, Denby's breakdown of why Denmark's minimal presence is actually a feature, not a bug, is essential listening.

The Illusion of the Purchase

Denby immediately grounds the reader in the absurdity of the proposal by contrasting the grandeur of the idea with the meager reality of Danish defense. He describes Station Nord, the northernmost Danish base, noting that "militarily it doesn't have tremendous purpose Beyond existing." The author argues that the Danish military presence is essentially a handful of soldiers and dog sled patrols, which "do little Beyond assert sovereignty." This framing is crucial because it strips away the romanticized notion of a vast, undefended territory waiting for a buyer. Instead, Denby suggests the territory is already effectively managed through a unique economic arrangement where "Denmark pays for about 2/3 of Greenland's government budget" while receiving no taxes in return.

The core of Denby's argument is that the proposal is rooted in a colonial mindset that ignores the actual strategic landscape. He writes, "large land purchases by one country from another have not meaningfully happened since the era of colonialism and the Very appeal of the idea is rooted in the same manner of exploitation of a territory's resources." This lands hard because it reframes the Trump administration's interest not as a strategic necessity, but as a relic of a bygone era. A counterargument worth considering is that the US might simply want to preempt Russian influence, regardless of the cost, but Denby's evidence suggests the US already has the infrastructure to do that without buying a sovereign nation.

The Real Arctic Power Balance

The commentary shifts decisively when Denby introduces the true heavyweight in the region: the United States' own Pituffik Space Base. He describes it as "colossal" compared to Danish facilities, with a "10,000 ft 3,000 M Runway" and a population that once peaked at over 10,000. Denby points out that this base is a "3 to 4 hour flight to all of Russia's northern coast," making the idea that the US lacks a foothold in Greenland demonstrably false. The author's analysis of the broader North American presence is equally revealing, noting that Canada's facilities are sparse and often reliant on complex microwave networks just to maintain satellite contact due to the curvature of the earth.

"Russia has been far more concerned than pretty much any country in building up its military presence in the far north."

This observation is the pivot point of the entire piece. Denby contrasts the Western approach with Russia's aggressive modernization, citing the upgrade of gravel strips to "6,900 ft 2100 met paved Runway" capable of hosting fighter jets year-round. He notes that Russia's northernmost base now boasts an "11,500 Ft" runway, allowing it to project power in ways the West cannot match. The evidence here is compelling because it moves beyond base counts to actual capability, showing that Russia is treating the Arctic as a vital defense corridor rather than a frontier to be explored.

The Icebreaker Gap and Nuclear Strategy

Perhaps the most damning evidence Denby presents is the disparity in icebreaking capabilities, which dictates who can actually operate in the Arctic. He states bluntly that "no other us ships have hardened holes capable of cutting through sea ice," leaving the US reliant on just three Coast Guard icebreakers. In stark contrast, "Russia meanwhile has 41 of them," including nuclear-powered vessels that are "more powerful and longer ranged than any other in the world." This imbalance is not just a logistical inconvenience; it is a strategic choke point that Denby argues makes the US unable to project power by sea for most of the year.

The author connects this hardware gap to a deeper strategic doctrine, explaining that Russia's buildup is part of a "Bastion defense" strategy designed to protect its nuclear deterrent. Denby writes that the region "Harbors what's believed to be about 2/3 of Russia's Nuclear Strike capabilities in its ballistic missile submarines." This context is vital; it explains why Russia is so paranoid and why the Arctic is not just a resource grab, but a matter of national survival for Moscow. Critics might argue that NATO's expansion with Finland and Sweden joining the alliance shifts the balance, but Denby suggests this has only deepened Russia's sense of encirclement, making them more, not less, aggressive in the north.

Bottom Line

Denby's strongest contribution is his refusal to let the "buy Greenland" narrative exist in a vacuum, forcing readers to confront the massive, existing US infrastructure and the terrifying Russian buildup that dwarfs it. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its heavy reliance on open-source intelligence regarding Russian capabilities, which are notoriously opaque, but the trend lines he identifies are undeniable. The real story isn't about a transaction; it's about a race the US is currently losing in the one place where geography favors the defender with the most icebreakers.
