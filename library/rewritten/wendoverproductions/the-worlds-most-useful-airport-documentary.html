Sam Denby doesn't just tell the story of an airport; he chronicles the violent collision between modern aviation physics and a 500-year-old island isolation. While most travel documentaries celebrate infrastructure as an unalloyed good, Denby reveals how a single meteorological quirk nearly turned St. Helena's new lifeline into the world's most expensive white elephant. This is not a tale of triumph, but a nuanced examination of how geography can defeat even the best-laid engineering plans.

The Weight of Isolation

Denby begins by grounding the reader in the profound loneliness of the location. "It's not a place that one hears a whole lot about," he admits, noting that for many, the island's only claim to fame is the exile of Napoleon. He paints a vivid picture of a society where "the thing they need most of all... is change," driven by an economy that has struggled for decades. The island's GDP per capita hovers around $12,000, a stark contrast to the $40,000 in the UK, largely because "getting people and things to and from the middle of nowhere costs a great deal."

The author effectively details the island's historical reliance on the RMS St. Helena, a ship that served as both cargo carrier and luxury liner. When that vessel finally retired, the government faced a choice: spend $100 million on a new ship or build an airport. Denby argues that the decision to build was logical, given the global shift away from ocean liners. He writes, "after decades of feasibility studies discussions debates false starts delays and bidding a contract was produced and with a few quick signatures life on st. Helena would change forever." This framing is crucial; it establishes that the airport was not a whim, but the culmination of a desperate, decades-long strategy to break economic stagnation.

The Wobble That Changed Everything

The narrative pivot occurs during the 2016 implementation flight. Denby describes the moment with cinematic precision: "just seconds away from touchdown there was a sort of wobble then another one then once its wheels were just feet above the runway the pilots elected to abort the landing." This was not a mechanical failure, but a demonstration of wind shear, a phenomenon where wind direction shifts violently due to the island's northern cliffs.

The author's coverage here is particularly sharp because he avoids the sensationalism that plagued the actual media response. He explains that while the media called it a "farcical vanity project," the reality was more complex. The solution required landing with a tailwind, which meant planes had to be so light they carried few passengers. "The 737 would have to be quite light... it wouldn't be able to take its full capacity of passengers or cargo," Denby notes, explaining why the airline Comair could not make the route profitable. This is the core of the tragedy: the airport was certified and functional, but the physics of the site made commercial viability impossible.

The world's most useless airport. That was the one that stuck, and I think a lot of the sort of initial label given by the popular media has not helped the island.

Critics might argue that Denby is too sympathetic to the British government's decision, given the massive financial waste. However, his point is that the failure wasn't in the ambition, but in the inability to predict the specific wind patterns that would render the runway commercially useless. He highlights the media's distortion, noting that tabloids claimed it was "impossible to land" when, in fact, private and medical flights were landing without issue. The problem was strictly economic, not operational.

The Human Cost of a Label

Denby weaves in the voices of the locals to show the emotional toll of the "useless airport" label. He interviews residents who felt a sense of historic pride during the first landing, only to watch that pride curdle into embarrassment as the news cycle turned. One local, Susan, expresses the initial hope: "now we've got the Gateway to get people here... this means we can have a steady stream." The subsequent cancellation of commercial flights left that hope unfulfilled, reinforcing the island's isolation rather than ending it.

The author's commentary on the media's role is biting. He suggests that the nickname "The World's Most Useless Airport" did more damage than the wind shear itself. "It became a lot about how bad the British government had," he observes, implying that the political fallout overshadowed the genuine needs of the islanders. The documentary serves as a corrective to the headlines, reminding us that a project can be technically successful yet economically doomed, and that the narrative of failure often ignores the human cost.

Bottom Line

Sam Denby's coverage is a masterclass in separating technical reality from public perception, proving that the "useless airport" was actually a victim of bad timing and bad weather, not bad planning. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to simplify the tragedy into a story of incompetence, instead offering a sober look at how geography can defeat even the most well-funded ambitions. Readers should watch for the long-term impact of this narrative on St. Helena's ability to attract future investment, as the shadow of the "useless" label may linger long after the wind shear is solved.
