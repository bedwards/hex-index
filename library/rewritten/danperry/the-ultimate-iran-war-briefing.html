Dan Perry cuts through the noise of the "ultimate war briefing" to expose a terrifying strategic disconnect: the United States is fighting on a rational clock while its adversary operates on a timeline of ideological endurance. Perry argues that the real contest is not about who wins a battle, but who breaks first under the economic strangulation of the Strait of Hormuz.

The Three Trajectories and the Clock of Fanatics

Perry begins by mapping the battlefield not with troop movements, but with political outcomes. He identifies three plausible paths: the collapse of the Iranian regime, a chastened survival, or the most likely scenario—a costly stalemate where the U.S. retreats after claiming a hollow victory. "The first is the fall of the Iranian regime... If there's no resulting chaos, this would be greeted with cheers across much of the world." This optimistic opening is quickly tempered by the author's sobering assessment of the second option, where the regime survives but is forced to renounce nuclear ambitions and proxy support.

The core of Perry's argument, however, lies in his analysis of time. He posits that the United States and its partners are "operating on one clock – that of rational people," whereas the Iranian leadership is driven by a different logic. "The Iranian regime is operating on a different clock – fanatics who do not care about their people, their views, their preferences and their suffering." This distinction is crucial. It suggests that traditional metrics of military success—territory gained or leaders eliminated—may not translate to strategic victory if the adversary is willing to absorb catastrophic pain to inflict economic damage.

The real contest is whether Iran can sustain enough disruption to the world economy that it basically outlasts its foes, despite the vast military advantage.

Critics might argue that labeling the entire regime as "fanatics" ignores the intense internal debates Perry acknowledges are happening following high-level assassinations. However, Perry's point remains that the regime's calculated bet is that global economic pain will fracture the coalition before internal dissent fractures the government.

Failing to Foresee the Obvious

The commentary shifts sharply to a critique of American strategy, specifically the failure to anticipate Iran's asymmetric response. Perry describes the decision to ignore warnings about the Strait of Hormuz not as a simple error, but as a fundamental breakdown in strategic thinking. "According to detailed reporting in The Wall Street Journal, [the administration] was explicitly warned, repeatedly, that Iran's most powerful response to an attack would be to disrupt or close the Strait of Hormuz."

Perry argues that believing Iran would simply absorb strikes without using its most potent lever was "basically absurd." He characterizes this oversight as "malpractice," noting that every serious analyst understood that Hormuz was the regime's ultimate shield. "If air and naval power prove insufficient to secure the strait, the logic of escalation becomes difficult to avoid." The author warns that securing the waterway requires more than just destroying targets; it demands sustained control of a contested maritime environment, potentially forcing the U.S. into a ground war it did not plan for.

Reopening Hormuz is not simply a matter of destroying targets, but requires sustained control of a dense, contested maritime environment against an adversary that only needs to succeed intermittently to impose strategic costs.

This section draws a chilling parallel to historical naval conflicts, such as Operation Morvarid, where Iran demonstrated its ability to strike back decisively against superior naval forces in the Persian Gulf. Perry implies that history is repeating itself, but with higher stakes for the global economy. The failure to plan for this scenario has left the U.S. playing defense in the world's most critical choke point.

The Diplomatic Isolation

The piece then turns to the fractured state of the Western alliance. Perry paints a "tragicomic" picture of a U.S. administration that undermined its own alliances and now expects them to bail out a crisis of its own making. He notes that Trump has spent years "deriding NATO as obsolete" and treating alliances as "transactional burdens." Now, when the U.S. needs help securing the strait, the allies are absent. "Every country in the world, basically, has a massive interest in the Hormuz being reopened. None heeded Trump's call for assistance."

Perry suggests that the European reaction is driven by a mix of self-preservation and "schadenfreude," as they watch the U.S. twist in the wind after years of diplomatic disrespect. He points out the irony that Article Five of the North Atlantic Treaty cannot be invoked because no member nation was attacked, leaving the U.S. isolated. "The United States owes its allies an apology." This isolation is compounded by the internal political fallout, with figures like Joe Kent resigning in protest and claiming the war was driven by Israeli pressure rather than an imminent threat to the U.S.

When this is over, the real diplomatic repair job is within NATO. The United States owes its allies an apology.

A counterargument worth considering is that the lack of NATO involvement might be a calculated move by European powers to avoid direct entanglement in a Middle Eastern conflict that does not directly threaten their borders, rather than a simple rejection of U.S. leadership. However, Perry's framing highlights a deeper crisis of trust that will have long-term consequences for the alliance.

The Global Chessboard

Finally, Perry expands the scope to the broader geopolitical implications, noting how the conflict is reshaping the roles of Russia, China, and Ukraine. He observes that Russia is benefiting from the chaos, sharing drone technology with Iran to distract the U.S. and boost oil prices. "Moscow has every reason to do so: a prolonged Gulf conflict raises oil prices, strains US military inventories, distracts Washington from Europe."

Conversely, China is positioned to weather the storm due to its strategic petroleum reserves, potentially allowing it to stay cautious while the West absorbs the humiliation. "The estimate is that China could withstand a total cutoff of Middle Eastern crude for roughly six months." Perry warns that the U.S. approach is eroding the post-war order, normalizing unilateral force and inviting China to justify its own aggressive moves, particularly regarding Taiwan.

It helps if there's an argument – yes, the regimes in Iran, Cuba and Venezuela are all horrendous – but this is nonetheless a departure from the postwar order.

The author also notes the unexpected alignment between the U.S. and Ukraine, as Kyiv offers drone expertise that is now being deployed in the Middle East. This irony is not lost on Perry: the nation Trump has treated as a burden is now a security exporter helping the U.S. against a common threat.

The map points in one direction: the Iranian coastline adjacent to Hormuz. Securing it would mean boots on the ground – limited, perhaps at first, but with all the risks of expansion, entanglement, and time.

Bottom Line

Dan Perry's most compelling argument is that the U.S. has entered a conflict without a viable exit strategy, trapped by its own failure to anticipate Iran's asymmetric leverage in the Strait of Hormuz. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its heavy reliance on a specific political narrative regarding the U.S. administration's incompetence, which may obscure the complex, multi-faceted nature of the Iranian regime's decision-making. Readers should watch for whether the economic pressure on the global market forces a diplomatic breakthrough or accelerates the slide into a prolonged, open-ended quagmire.
