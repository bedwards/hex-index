In a landscape where official data from authoritarian regimes is often treated as fiction, Joeri Schasfoort presents a forensic investigation into the Russian economy that refuses to rely on the Kremlin's word. By triangulating fifteen disparate data points—from energy grid frequency to foreign car sales—Schasfoort reveals a stark divergence between Moscow's optimistic GDP figures and the reality of a war-torn consumer economy. This is not merely an academic exercise; it is a vital tool for understanding how sanctions actually bite when the official statistics go silent.

The Trust Deficit

The conversation begins with a fundamental question that has haunted economists since the invasion of Ukraine: can we trust Rosstat, Russia's statistical agency? Schasfoort notes that before the war, the consensus among thousands of professional economists was that Russian GDP data was surprisingly reliable. "If you know all of these people that work with this data professionally see no red flags, then yes, you know, even if you don't trust the Russian government, it's a pretty safe assumption that before the war at least, uh these statistics can be trusted," the host observes, setting the stage for Schasfoort's analysis.

However, the invasion shattered this trust. Schasfoort explains that the Russian government immediately began altering methodologies, making historical comparisons impossible. "The Russian statistics office changed the methodology immediately in February 2022 so that it's very difficult to compare the same indicators in historical perspective," Schasfoort writes. This sudden opacity is a classic signal of data manipulation. When a government stops publishing granular data and shifts to aggregate figures, it is often because the detailed numbers tell a story they wish to hide.

Critics might argue that assuming pre-war data was perfect is naive, given the history of statistical inflation in authoritarian states. Yet, the sheer volume of independent scrutiny Russia's data faced prior to 2022 lends credibility to the baseline assumption. The real story isn't that the data was always fake, but that the moment the war turned unfavorable, the facade crumbled.

"We never had any contact... with any not just Rostat but also Russian central bank... all the data that we source for our tracker is all publicly available on the internet with zero contact."

The Energy Blackout

The most chilling evidence of data suppression comes from the energy sector. For decades, the Russian energy operator published daily generation and consumption figures, a reliable proxy for industrial activity. Schasfoort describes a moment of realization when this data stream simply vanished. "I started in October but in November one day I opened the website and I realized that they stopped publishing that data," Schasfoort recounts. The site was replaced with vague metrics like "frequency in the grids," which offer no insight into actual economic output.

This disappearance is not a technical glitch; it is a strategic blackout. Schasfoort posits that the Kremlin likely noticed the research team from the European Central Bank scraping the data and cut the feed to prevent outsiders from seeing the true depth of the economic contraction. "For me it was a very big clue that okay maybe they actually noticed that there was someone with Frankfurt IP trying to access their website in a regular basis and they just disclose the information," Schasfoort writes. This suggests that the official narrative is being actively curated in real-time, not just retrospectively altered.

The Alternative Tracker

With official channels closed, Schasfoort and her team constructed a "domestic activity tracker" using fifteen alternative indicators. These range from traditional metrics like imports sourced from 38 trade partners to unconventional proxies like the sales of foreign-branded motor vehicles. The goal was to isolate domestic consumption from the massive surge in military spending, which artificially inflates GDP. "We sort of wanted to also understand how the economy is performing net of that increase in military production because obviously Russia is investing now billions in producing ammunition, producing tanks, missiles and so on," Schasfoort explains.

The results are telling. While Rosstat paints a picture of resilience, Schasfoort's tracker reveals a sharper, deeper contraction. "Our tracker basically points to the sharp contraction but then this contraction goes a little bit deeper than the official statistics or at least it provides some kind of early indicator that there will be further decline," Schasfoort writes. The divergence is significant. It suggests that while the war machine is churning, the civilian economy is far more fragile than Moscow admits.

One counterargument to consider is the reliability of these alternative proxies. For instance, tracking foreign car sales might miss the explosion of Chinese imports, potentially underestimating consumer resilience. However, the convergence of multiple independent data points—energy, trade partners, and business surveys—creates a robust picture that is harder to dismiss than any single metric.

"We assumed that anything that happened even after Russia annexed Crimea and invaded Donbas... we still sort of assumed that okay even in that period we could still trust the statistics."

Bottom Line

Schasfoort's work provides a critical reality check, proving that in the absence of transparency, the only way to see the truth is to look at the shadows cast by the economy's machinery. The strongest element of this analysis is the methodological rigor of using non-Russian data sources to bypass state censorship, while its greatest vulnerability lies in the potential blind spots of those very proxies, such as the unmeasured rise of Chinese trade. As the war continues, the gap between official statistics and alternative data will likely widen, making these independent trackers the only reliable compass for policymakers and investors navigating the Russian economic landscape.
