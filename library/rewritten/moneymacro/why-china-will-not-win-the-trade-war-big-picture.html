Joeri Schasfoort challenges the prevailing doom-mongering about China's inevitable rise by arguing that the historical playbook for empire-building no longer applies in a post-gold standard world. While many investors see a repeating cycle of rising powers displacing old hegemons, Schasfoort contends that the United States holds a unique, non-replicable advantage: the dollar's status as a global reserve currency is no longer tethered to physical gold, but to trust, safety, and access to the American consumer. This distinction is critical for anyone trying to understand why the US might not lose the current trade war, even if its industrial base lags behind China's.

The Gold Standard Trap

The author begins by dismantling the popular narrative championed by macro investor Ray Dalio, which suggests that China is simply following the historical path of the Netherlands, Britain, and the US. Schasfoort writes, "In a gold standard world I think Dalio's analysis holds up pretty well actually." He explains that historically, rising powers had to become industrial exporters first to accumulate gold, which eventually allowed them to issue the world's reserve currency once the old hegemon ran out of bullion. This logic dictated that Britain overtook the Netherlands, and the US overtook Britain, only after a long lag of industrial dominance followed by financial supremacy.

However, Schasfoort points out a fatal flaw in applying this model to the 21st century. "After all, the US did already run out of gold in the 1970s, and rather than perishing like the UK pound and Dutch Gilder did before it, the US dollar became more valuable than ever." The author argues that the mechanism of empire has fundamentally shifted. Without the constraint of gold, a nation can run massive trade deficits and still maintain its currency's dominance, provided the rest of the world trusts the system. This reframes the trade war not as a battle for industrial output, but as a battle for the psychological and structural dominance of the dollar.

"The dollar's reserve currency status has given the US a tremendous advantage. It can now keep a military presence all over the world. Even though it does not have the industrial might to really back that up."

The Real Stakes of the Trade War

If the gold standard is dead, what keeps the dollar king? Schasfoort identifies two pillars: safety and liquidity. Central banks hold dollars because they fear confiscation and volatility elsewhere, and because they need access to deep, liquid markets. The author notes that the US has essentially created a system where developing nations are incentivized to export to the American consumer to earn dollars, locking them into the US financial orbit. "By making this easy, the US has essentially given developing countries an extremely strong incentive to plug into the dollar system."

The trade war, therefore, is a desperate attempt by the US to reclaim the industrial crown that it has lost, rather than a fight to maintain the currency itself. Schasfoort writes, "I would argue that winning the trade war for the US means taking back the industrial crown from rising power China." Conversely, for China to win, it must do the impossible: displace the dollar as the global reserve asset. The author suggests that China's current trajectory mirrors Japan and the EU, whose GDPs in dollar terms stagnated despite superior export performance, because they could not break the dollar's grip.

Critics might note that this analysis underestimates the fragility of a fiat currency system that relies entirely on political stability. If the US political environment becomes too chaotic, the "safety" premium of the dollar could evaporate faster than the author anticipates.

The Reforms China Must Make (But Won't)

The most provocative section of the commentary outlines exactly what China would need to do to replace the dollar. Schasfoort argues that for the renminbi to become a true alternative, Beijing would need to embrace radical transparency and openness. "First, the Chinese recognize that foreign central bankers value safety and predictability... China's leadership moves to make its central bank completely independent." The author details a three-step plan: guaranteeing that foreign assets cannot be confiscated, opening the consumer market to developing nations, and removing subsidies to allow wages and consumption to rise naturally.

"If, for example, you speak out against China, this instantly gives a renminbi edge over the dollar for relatively developed countries with lots of reserves like the Gulf countries, South Korea, and Japan."

Yet, Schasfoort concludes that this scenario is highly unlikely because it contradicts the core ideology and power structure of the Chinese Communist Party. He writes, "This entire plan involves seating a lot of government power to independent institutions that protect foreign money in China. So far, Chinese leadership has done exactly the opposite, seeking more power rather than less." The author posits that President Xi Jinping's passion for exports and state control makes the necessary reforms politically impossible. Consequently, China is unlikely to win the trade war because it cannot win the currency war without dismantling its own system of governance.

The American Paradox

The commentary ends with a sharp critique of the current US approach. While Schasfoort believes China is unlikely to win, he is skeptical that the current US administration is well-positioned to win either. He argues that the chaotic, unpredictable nature of the current trade war threatens the very trust that underpins the dollar. "As a consequence, the dollar has been really volatile lately, and the development model of many nations is now in danger, both weakening the attractiveness of the dollar as a reserve currency."

The author suggests that for the US to truly secure its position, it would need to reverse course: implement moderate, predictable tariffs, and restore faith in its institutions. Instead, the current strategy risks accelerating the very de-dollarization it seeks to prevent. The irony is palpable: the US might lose the trade war not because China is too strong, but because the US is too chaotic.

Bottom Line

Schasfoort's strongest argument is the decoupling of industrial might from currency dominance, a nuance often missed in the "China rising" narrative. However, his conclusion relies heavily on the assumption that China's political leadership is incapable of the self-reform required to challenge the dollar, a prediction that hinges on internal political dynamics that are notoriously difficult to forecast. The reader should watch not just for tariff announcements, but for any signs of Chinese financial liberalization or US institutional instability, as these are the true indicators of who will hold the global hegemon's crown next.
