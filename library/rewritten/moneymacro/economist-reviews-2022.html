Joeri Schasfoort does not merely recount the economic headlines of 2022; he subjects his own previous predictions to a brutal, real-time audit, revealing how easily even seasoned analysts miss the mark when geopolitics collides with macroeconomics. His most striking admission is that the "transitory" inflation narrative he backed in late 2021 collapsed not because of a failure in economic theory, but because of the unpredictable whims of a single world leader. This is not a standard year-end review; it is a masterclass in intellectual humility that forces listeners to confront the limits of forecasting in a fractured world.

The Inflation Miscalculation

Schasfoort begins by dismantling the consensus that formed at the start of the year. He recalls a massive video he produced in late November 2021, where he sided with the "transitory" camp against prominent voices like Larry Summers and Peter Schiff. "I backed team transitory has the best arguments in our opinion," Schasfoort writes, noting the critical condition that the pandemic would effectively be over. That condition, of course, was shattered by the Delta variant and subsequent geopolitical shocks. The core of his argument here is that while economic models can account for fiscal stimulus or supply chain bottlenecks, they often fail to price in the irrational decisions of autocrats.

This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from bad data to bad luck, specifically the invasion of Ukraine. Schasfoort admits, "Nobody saw that coming... Putin had already turned off the gas supply to a certain extent to Europe and that reverberated already to many markets." He uses this to illustrate a painful lesson: "As a microeconomist you can make these predictions but you are dependent on so many factors including the whims of individual people." Critics might argue that a robust macroeconomic model should include geopolitical risk premiums, but Schasfoort's candidness about the sheer unpredictability of Vladimir Putin's decisions offers a more honest assessment of why the "transitory" thesis failed.

"If you want to be the best macroeconomic forecaster you should also be able to forecast what individuals that are super influential like the chairman of the Fed maybe somewhat easier to do but also these world leaders like she if she is going to stimulate the economy like crazy China's economy is not going to collapse if Russia invades Ukraine there's going to be a huge extra bout of energy inflation."

The Sanctions Paradox

The commentary then pivots to the economic war with Russia, where Schasfoort's analysis of Putin's "war chest" of foreign reserves aged in a peculiar way. Initially, he argued that Russia's massive reserves would make them "immune to sanctions," a claim he later softened to "shielded against sanctions." The reality proved more complex: the ruble collapsed initially, only to recover because Western policymakers froze the reserves, an "absolutely unheard of" move that forced Russia to rely on capital controls. Schasfoort notes that the financial sanctions, which dominated the news cycle, ultimately did less damage than the trade restrictions.

He argues that the true pain came from the inability to import goods, not the inability to move money. "The export import sanctions of real goods seem to be hurting way more than the financial sanctions," Schasfoort writes, pointing to Russian industry struggling with inferior technology and tanks flying with cheap consumer-grade GPS units. This distinction is crucial. While financial sanctions are dramatic, the long-term degradation of a nation's industrial base happens quietly through export controls. A counterargument worth considering is that the financial freeze did prevent Russia from fully leveraging its reserves to stabilize the currency in the short term, even if the trade sanctions delivered the heavier blow later.

"Export import sanctions of real goods seem to be hurting way more than the financial sanctions... Seemingly didn't do so much right? But you saw all of these tanks and and airplanes flying with cheap consumer grade GPS's."

The Global Slowdown and Housing Bubbles

Beyond the war, Schasfoort touches on the broader economic indicators, noting that while real GDP held up surprisingly well in many places, inflation was "a terrible year" and financial markets were equally dire. He highlights the housing markets in Canada, New Zealand, and Australia as particularly vulnerable, citing an IMF report that suggests these nations are at risk of a proper bust because they "never had a housing bust properly." He also addresses the Chinese property slowdown, acknowledging the "huge ghost cities" and the government's attempt to deflate a bubble where prices reached Western levels despite local incomes remaining low.

His assessment of China is cautious, noting the difficulty in getting accurate data from inside the country. "It's quite difficult to get information accurate information from inside China about this," Schasfoort admits, but he agrees with the consensus that the property sector was "super big" and unsustainable. This section serves as a warning that while the war in Europe dominated the news, structural cracks in Asian and North American housing markets were widening simultaneously.

"Canada, New Zealand, Australia, these countries that never had housing bust properly are really really at risk."

Bottom Line

Schasfoort's review succeeds because it prioritizes intellectual honesty over the illusion of certainty, demonstrating that the biggest economic shocks of 2022 were driven by human volatility rather than market mechanics. His strongest insight lies in distinguishing between the noise of financial sanctions and the silent, grinding impact of trade embargoes on industrial capacity. However, the piece's reliance on "whims" as an explanatory variable leaves readers with a sobering realization: in an era of geopolitical fragmentation, traditional forecasting models may be increasingly obsolete.
