Joeri Schasfoort challenges a comforting narrative with a jarring contradiction: if Spain's economy is technically the "best" in the rich world, why do its citizens feel so miserable? By dissecting the methodology behind a prestigious ranking, he exposes how macroeconomic aggregates can mask deep-seated structural decay, offering a masterclass in why "growth" is not synonymous with "prosperity."

The Illusion of Aggregate Growth

Schasfoort begins by interrogating The Economist's claim that Spain was the top-performing economy in 2024. The ranking relied on five metrics: GDP change, stock price growth, inflation, unemployment shifts, and government spending versus tax revenue. "The Economist writes that they chose this measure because it is widely regarded as the most reliable measure of an economy's overall health," Schasfoort notes regarding GDP. However, he immediately pivots to a crucial distinction that the original ranking glossed over: the difference between total GDP and GDP per person.

The core of the argument is that total GDP can be inflated by population growth rather than genuine wealth creation. Schasfoort points out that Spain's 3% growth was driven partly by a record influx of migrants, which mechanically lifts output without necessarily improving individual living standards. "This could explain why Spain's economy looks good on paper but feels bad to the people in it," he observes. This is a vital correction. It highlights a common flaw in economic reporting where the size of the pie grows, but the slices for existing residents shrink or stagnate.

"More people means a bigger economy, simple as that, but it does not necessarily translate to people feeling good about the economy."

Critics might argue that immigration is a long-term economic asset that eventually boosts per-capita wealth, but Schasfoort's point stands: the immediate sentiment of the population cannot be ignored when defining a "successful" economy. If the metric is "best economy," it must account for the experience of the median citizen, not just the aggregate output.

The Disconnect Between Markets and Main Street

Moving beyond GDP, Schasfoort scrutinizes the inclusion of stock market performance as a proxy for economic health. He acknowledges that rising stock prices often signal optimism about future corporate profits, yet he questions their relevance to the average worker. "The Economist does not explain in this article why they think stock market prices say something about an economy," he writes, suggesting the link is tenuous for a population struggling with cost-of-living pressures.

The author then tackles the issue of inequality, noting that while unemployment rates have improved, they remain a blunt instrument for measuring shared prosperity. He investigates the Gini coefficient to see if wealth disparity explains the public's gloom, finding that while inequality is a factor, it doesn't fully account for the depth of Spanish dissatisfaction. Instead, he turns to the concept of sustainability, questioning whether growth fueled by debt is truly "good." "Economic growth can also be unsustainable if it is powered by a lot of private borrowing, not government borrowing," Schasfoort argues, drawing a parallel to the pre-2008 crisis era. This adds necessary nuance, reminding readers that a booming stock market or low unemployment today can be built on fragile foundations if private debt is spiraling.

The Long-Term View vs. Year-Over-Year Gains

The most compelling part of Schasfoort's analysis comes when he shifts the lens from short-term improvements to long-term stagnation. He compares Spain to the Netherlands, noting that while Spain showed impressive year-over-year gains in 2024, its baseline remains significantly lower. "Ever since 2013 there is a clear trend that Spaniards are getting less negative about their economy," he observes, but he cautions that this is a recovery from a devastating crash, not a return to normalcy.

Schasfoort uses a sports analogy to drive the point home: ranking a team as the "best" simply because they improved their position more than a rival, even if the rival is still vastly superior in total strength. "It would be a little bit like calling Dutch Football Club fan the best club of 2024 because it's improved its position quite a lot more than Spain's Real Madrid," he quips. This reframing is powerful because it challenges the reader's intuition about what "progress" actually means. A country can be the fastest runner in a race while still being far behind the finish line.

"People mostly care about the overall state of the economy rather than just the improvement last year."

This insight bridges the gap between the data and the sentiment. The Spanish public isn't reacting to the 2024 growth spurt; they are reacting to a decade of lost wages, high unemployment relative to peers, and a stock market that remains small compared to their northern neighbors. The survey data showing 63% of Spaniards viewing their economy as "bad" is not a cultural quirk or a failure of optimism; it is a rational response to a long-term deficit.

Bottom Line

Schasfoort's deconstruction of the "best economy" label is a necessary corrective to the seductive simplicity of headline-grabbing rankings. His strongest contribution is demonstrating that year-over-year growth metrics are insufficient to capture the lived reality of a population still recovering from a historic crisis. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that "best" should be defined by median welfare rather than aggregate momentum, a distinction that policymakers often prefer to blur. Ultimately, the piece serves as a reminder that economic health is not a single number, but a complex tapestry of debt, inequality, and long-term stability.
