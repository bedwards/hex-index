Most people assume the global financial system runs on cash moving across borders or on the Federal Reserve printing money. Joeri Schasfoort, hosting a conversation with expert Jeff Snyder, dismantles that intuition by revealing that the true engine of global commerce is an invisible, offshore ledger system that operates entirely outside the United States. This is not a niche academic curiosity; it is the functional reserve currency that funds everything from building factories in Malaysia to paying freelancers in Pakistan, yet it remains almost entirely absent from mainstream economics education.

The Hidden Plumbing of Global Finance

Schasfoort guides the listener through a revelation that upends standard monetary theory: the "Eurodollar" has nothing to do with the European Union. Instead, it refers to US dollars held in banks outside the US, creating a massive shadow banking system. "The euro dollar term is a bit older than that and it basically refers to Offshore dollars," Schasfoort notes, setting the stage for a discussion that traces the system's origins to the 1950s. The core of the argument is that this system evolved because physical cash is inefficient. As Snyder explains, "it's easier just to transact in claims on US Dollars... we've removed ourselves from the physical currency entirely to just transact in claims on currency."

This framing is powerful because it shifts the focus from central bank policy to private banking mechanics. The system allows banks to intermediate transactions globally without ever moving a single physical bill. "Rather than me stick this five dollar bill in an envelope and mail it to you... we can just use these claims on physical currency as the actual currency," Snyder argues. This efficiency is the system's greatest strength, but it also creates a fragile architecture where trust in the ledger replaces the security of tangible assets. Critics might note that this reliance on private ledgers creates systemic risks, as seen in past banking crises, which are often harder to regulate than traditional central bank operations.

"The euro dollar system is the real Reserve currency and the way it operates actually means how much money is floating around the world."

Why the Offshore System Matters to Everyone

The conversation moves beyond technical definitions to the real-world impact on global trade and investment. Schasfoort highlights a personal anecdote about receiving payments in dollars from sponsors worldwide, illustrating the system's ubiquity. "A lot of people especially in the United States tend to think of a reserve currency as oh we get to price oil in US Dollars that's not it at all," Schasfoort observes, quoting Snyder's correction. The true function of a reserve currency is to serve as a "vehicle currency," a common language for disparate economies to trade.

Snyder emphasizes that this system is the only way for non-US entities to finance large-scale projects. "If you're over there in Europe and you want to invest... in Malaysia you got to have access to the euro dollar system," he states. This point is crucial for macroeconomists because it explains why countries outside the US are so dependent on dollar liquidity. The argument holds up well against the backdrop of recent global financial stress, where shortages of offshore dollars have caused panic even in nations with strong domestic currencies. The system's ability to "intermediate between various different places around the world" means that a disruption in London or the Cayman Islands can freeze trade in Southeast Asia.

The Illusion of Control

The most striking aspect of the coverage is the admission that this system operates largely in the dark. "What really bothered me the most was as a young man in this profession was nobody wanted to know it," Snyder recalls of his early research. The system's opacity is not accidental; it is a feature that allows for rapid expansion and innovation, but it also means that policymakers often lack a full understanding of the risks they are managing. "It's not just a market for US dollars in London it is the functional means by which all the all the all the necessary requirements of a global Reserve currency are done," Schasfoort summarizes.

This lack of transparency is a double-edged sword. While it allows for frictionless global commerce, it also means that the true size and leverage of the global dollar system are difficult to measure. A counterargument worth considering is whether this system is sustainable in the long term, or if it is simply a bubble waiting to burst under the weight of its own complexity. Yet, as long as the world needs a common medium for trade, the offshore dollar system will likely remain the dominant force.

Bottom Line

Schasfoort and Snyder deliver a compelling case that the global economy runs on a private, offshore ledger system that is far more significant than the Federal Reserve's balance sheet. The strongest part of their argument is the demonstration of how this system functions as the true vehicle currency for global trade, bypassing physical cash entirely. However, the biggest vulnerability remains the system's opacity and the lack of regulatory oversight, which could lead to unforeseen crises. Readers should watch for how central banks attempt to manage liquidity in this shadow system, as it will likely define the next major financial cycle.
