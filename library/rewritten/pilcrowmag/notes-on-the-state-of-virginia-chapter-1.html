PILCROW does not merely recount a crime; they reconstruct the architecture of a life through a literary experiment that mirrors the Enlightenment's most famous inquiry into statehood. By framing a modern tragedy in North Carolina through the structural lens of Thomas Jefferson's Notes on the State of Virginia, the author transforms a standard true-crime narrative into a philosophical excavation of morality, privacy, and the limits of truth. This is not a story about a murder; it is a story about how we choose to remember the dead when the facts are insufficient to carry the weight of their humanity.

The Jeffersonian Framework

The piece's most distinctive move is its structural ambition. Instead of a linear chronology, PILCROW adopts the "Query" format from Jefferson's 1781 work, a text originally written to answer specific questions from the French government about the American colonies. "In 1781, François Barbé-Marbois, Secretary of the French Legation in Philadelphia, asked Jefferson for information about his state of Virginia, posing specific questions to him," PILCROW notes, establishing the historical parallel immediately. The author then pivots to the modern subject, Virginia Statusen, asking why not apply this same rigorous, question-driven methodology to a single human life? "Twenty-three Queries to develop a person and a story! That should be quite enough," they write, suggesting that the complexity of a human soul requires as much taxonomic effort as the mapping of a nation.

This framing is effective because it elevates the subject from a victim of violence to a subject of study, demanding the same intellectual respect one might afford a geopolitical analysis. However, critics might note that applying a document designed for territorial inventory to a personal tragedy risks reducing the chaotic nature of grief to a sterile checklist. Yet, PILCROW anticipates this by acknowledging the inherent tension in their method: "I reaffirmed my faithfulness to truth, but argued for license to creatively bridge gaps that might lie between the facts reliably offered by witnesses and documents."

My task would not be precise replication, even if that were possible, but the realization of more refined textures and greater expression.

The author admits that this approach requires a delicate negotiation with the subject herself. Virginia Statusen, initially resistant, eventually agreed to provide "extensive written responses to numerous questions that I posed to her; and (2) redacted portions of voluminous personal journals germane to the events at issue." The condition was stark: the documents were to be viewed only by the author and "destroyed by fire upon publication of this book." This act of destruction serves as a powerful metaphor for the novel's central theme: that some truths are too volatile to be preserved in their raw form, requiring the alchemy of fiction to become bearable art.

The Classroom as Microcosm

The narrative shifts to a specific moment in October 2017 at Cornell University, grounding the philosophical abstraction in a tangible, flawed reality. PILCROW introduces Professor Robert Brassting, a man whose personal disintegration mirrors the intellectual crisis of his philosophy class. "Professor Brassting, despondent, was in the near aftermath of divorce from wife number two over acts of moral turpitude (his) and financial ineptitude (hers)," the author writes, immediately establishing a character who is ill-equipped to teach about virtue.

The class discussion centers on Plato's Euthyphro, a dialogue concerning the nature of piety and whether something is good because the gods love it, or loved by the gods because it is good. This is not merely academic posturing; it is the thematic engine of the entire novel. As PILCROW describes the scene, "Socrates wants to talk to Euthyphro. Why? ... Socrates is impressed that Euthyphro seems certain in thinking that what he is doing is right, that is, that it's pious or pleasing to the gods." The author uses this ancient debate to foreshadow the moral certainties and failures that will define the events of August 11, 2018, the date of the tragedy in North Carolina.

The portrayal of the classroom is sharp and unflinching. The students are not idealized; they are distracted, self-interested, or physically uncomfortable. "His rapid marital unraveling was searing and anathema to his desired presentation of a man and woman in union," PILCROW observes of the professor, highlighting the dissonance between the high-minded topics discussed and the messy reality of the participants. This grounding in human frailty makes the eventual violence feel more inevitable, not less. The professor's mind drifts to a student named Céline, imagining "deep, narcotic kisses" while discussing the nature of the soul, a detail that underscores the failure of philosophy to contain human desire.

The Ethics of the Non-Fiction Novel

The most provocative element of PILCROW's commentary is the admission of their own ethical compromise. The author confesses to betraying the trust of Virginia Statusen "in a moment of weakness when the day of our meeting finally arrived." This confession is crucial. It prevents the narrative from becoming a detached, clinical observation and forces the reader to confront the messy reality of the writer-subject relationship. "This, I told her, was a most challenging task—one certainly risking rebuke from either proponents of the purest truth or, most importantly, the main actor and witness herself," PILCROW writes, acknowledging the precariousness of the project.

The author draws a direct line to Truman Capote's In Cold Blood, another work that blurred the lines between journalism and fiction, but with a distinct difference in intent. While Capote sought to capture the "non-fiction novel" in its purest form, PILCROW seems to be arguing for a more fluid approach. "Think of me, I wrote, as a painter and she the subject," they explain, prioritizing emotional truth over forensic accuracy. This choice is risky; it invites accusations of fabrication. However, the author's willingness to expose their own vulnerability and the subject's demand for privacy creates a unique contract with the reader. We are not being sold a verified report; we are being invited into a shared act of imagination.

She preferred not to. But after two weeks and one day, I received an unexpected letter from her, which gave me hope.

This moment of hesitation and eventual cooperation highlights the central tension of the piece: the conflict between the public's right to know and the individual's right to be left alone. The fact that Statusen communicated only in writing, using a "black ink fountain pen (a Mont Blanc, I later learned) on classic ivory linen paper," adds a layer of deliberate distance that the author must navigate. It is a refusal to be consumed, a demand to be seen only on her own terms.

Bottom Line

PILCROW's Notes on the State of Virginia succeeds by refusing to treat the tragedy of Virginia Statusen as a mere plot point, instead using the scaffolding of Jefferson's political philosophy to explore the nature of moral certainty in a fractured world. The piece's greatest strength is its structural ingenuity, which forces the reader to engage with the subject as a complex system rather than a victim. Its vulnerability lies in the inherent ambiguity of the author's admission to betraying trust, leaving the reader to question where the facts end and the fiction begins. As the narrative moves toward the events of August 11, 2018, the reader is left with a singular, haunting question: can art ever truly capture the truth of a life, or is it merely a beautiful, necessary lie?
