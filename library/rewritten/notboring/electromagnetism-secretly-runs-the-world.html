Packy McCormick makes a startling claim: the invisible force powering our modern world is also the one we understand the least, creating a critical bottleneck for the next century of innovation. While most discussions on technology focus on software or artificial intelligence as the primary drivers of change, McCormick argues that the physical manipulation of electromagnetic fields is the true frontier, one where human intuition has hit a hard ceiling. This is not just a physics lesson; it is a warning that our ability to electrify everything from cars to aircraft is being held back by a scarcity of human minds capable of "thinking like an electron."

The Invisible Bottleneck

McCormick begins by dismantling the assumption that we have mastered the physical world. He points out that while electromagnetism runs everything from GPS to microwave ovens, it remains largely opaque to the human brain. "Electromagnetism secretly runs our world," he writes, "because only a few people on this planet can intuit how it works." This framing is effective because it reframes a complex engineering challenge as a fundamental biological limitation. We evolved to see visible light and feel gravity, but the rest of the electromagnetic spectrum is invisible to us, leaving us dependent on a tiny elite of engineers who can visualize these fields.

The author illustrates this scarcity with a vivid anecdote about a peer in his physics program. "This guy thinks like an electron," McCormick recalls his professor saying. "Electrons, if they were sentient, would feel all of these different fields pulling at them." This comparison highlights the gap between human intuition and physical reality. For the vast majority, electromagnetism is "black magic," a domain where standard circuit logic fails and wave-like behaviors dominate. Critics might argue that simulation tools have already democratized this knowledge, but McCormick counters that true intuition—the ability to instantly grasp how a shape will manipulate a field—is still a rare, almost mystical trait possessed by only a handful of people globally.

Humans can see a small part of the electromagnetic spectrum, but we haven't needed to see beyond the visible spectrum in order to survive. Until electromagnetism came to run the world.

The Economic Imperative

The stakes of this bottleneck are rising exponentially as the economy shifts from mechanical to electric. McCormick provides stark data to support his urgency: in 1970, electronics accounted for just five percent of a new car's cost; by 2020, that figure hit forty percent, and by 2030, it is projected to reach fifty percent. "The electrical and electromagnetic components are the 'nervous system' of modern hardware and contribute to 40-50% of failures," he notes, quoting Arena Physica CEO Pratap Ranade. This statistic is crucial; it suggests that as we electrify, we are not just adding features, we are introducing the primary source of fragility into our systems.

The argument extends to defense and aerospace, where the complexity is even higher. McCormick notes that in the F-35 Lightning II, electronics comprise 35% of the cost, surpassing the engine itself. This trend implies that the next generation of hardware, such as the projected F-47, will be even more dependent on these difficult-to-master systems. The historical context here is vital. Just as the Chain Home radar system in the 1930s required a leap in understanding radio waves to win the Battle of Britain, today's progress in autonomous vehicles and AI infrastructure depends on a similar leap in our ability to manipulate electromagnetic fields. Without a new way to design these systems, the electrification of the economy could stall.

The AI Solution: A Large Field Model

McCormick's proposed solution is as bold as the problem is dire: if humans cannot intuit electromagnetism, we must teach machines to do it. He introduces the concept of a "Large Field Model" (LFM), analogous to the Large Language Models (LLMs) that have revolutionized software, but designed to generalize across electromagnetic physics. "Fortunately, AI doesn't share our blind spots," he argues. "It is particularly good at seeing patterns, at making connections and understanding dependencies that are not necessarily intuitive to humans."

This is where the argument shifts from diagnosis to prescription. McCormick suggests that while humans struggle with the counterintuitive nature of wave interference and phase shifts, an AI trained on the vast data of electromagnetic interactions could navigate these complexities with ease. He draws a parallel to the evolution of computing itself. Early computers relied on mechanical relays that were slow and prone to arcing, until the invention of the semiconductor allowed for the rapid, reliable switching of electrons. "The great breakthrough was the semiconductor," he writes, noting how it enabled the transition from mechanical to digital. Now, he posits, the next breakthrough will be using AI to design the electromagnetic structures that will power the future.

We should be able to build a Large Field Model — like an LLM that generalizes across language, except ours generalizes across EM.

A counterargument worth considering is whether the physical world can be modeled with the same statistical confidence as language. Language has patterns and rules, but physics is governed by immutable laws where a single miscalculation can lead to catastrophic failure. However, McCormick's confidence stems from the fact that electromagnetism is described by quantum electrodynamics, the most precisely tested theory in science. The challenge is not a lack of mathematical precision, but a lack of human intuition to apply it efficiently. If AI can bridge that gap, the implications for hardware design are profound.

Bottom Line

McCormick's strongest argument is the identification of a biological ceiling on technological progress: we cannot build the future with minds that cannot see the forces powering it. His biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that an AI model can be trained on data that is currently too sparse or too expensive to generate, given the decline in national testing capabilities he mentions. The reader should watch closely to see if "Electromagnetic Superintelligence" can move from a theoretical concept to a practical tool that actually accelerates the design of the next generation of electric hardware.
