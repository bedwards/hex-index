Packy McCormick delivers a rare weekend dispatch that bypasses the usual noise to spotlight a quiet revolution: the moment a nation admits its most entrenched institutional errors are fixable. While most analysis fixates on political gridlock, this piece argues that the very act of correcting a decades-old dietary myth signals a broader capacity for institutional self-correction.

The Food Pyramid Correction

McCormick opens with a historical autopsy of the American food pyramid, tracing its origins not to science, but to a "pyramid scheme" built on cherry-picked data and sugar industry lobbying. He details how Ancel Keys' flawed Seven Countries Study and payments from the sugar industry to Harvard scientists created a consensus that demonized fat and elevated carbohydrates. "In 1977, Senator George McGovern's committee translated the fat hypothesis into the first federal dietary guidelines, drafted by staffers with no scientific background over the objections of researchers who said the evidence wasn't there yet." This bureaucratic inertia persisted for decades, leading to a food environment where Americans dutifully consumed low-fat, high-sugar products, only to see obesity rates triple.

The core of McCormick's optimism lies not in the science itself, which has been settled for years, but in the recent action by the National Design Studio. He notes that while the scientific consensus cracked in the 2010s, the official guidelines remained frozen due to "institutional sclerosis." The breakthrough is that a government entity finally published a new, more accurate food pyramid. "What makes me most optimistic though is that there was this obviously dumb and wrong thing that everyone agreed was dumb and wrong but did nothing about that we now, as a nation, have done something about." This framing is powerful because it shifts the narrative from inevitable stagnation to actionable correction.

What makes me most optimistic though is that there was this obviously dumb and wrong thing that everyone agreed was dumb and wrong but did nothing about that we now, as a nation, have done something about.

Critics might argue that changing a graphic on a website does little to alter the deep-seated habits of the population or the lobbying power of the food industry. However, McCormick's point is symbolic: the mechanism for correction exists and has been activated.

The Grey Market and Biological Enhancement

The commentary then pivots to the unregulated frontier of human enhancement, specifically the surge in "Chinese peptides." McCormick highlights the stark disparity between the slow, expensive path of FDA approval and the agile, grey-market alternatives. He cites a Substack essay noting that while Eli Lilly's retatrutide achieves significant weight loss in trials, Chinese suppliers are already shipping enough semaglutide API to produce over a billion doses. The price differential is staggering: grey market semaglutide costs roughly $50 a month compared to $24,000 for brand-name versions.

McCormick describes these next-generation drugs as "the holy grail of weight loss medications," noting that Gen3 GLP-1/GIP/glucagon agonists work "astonishingly well." The argument here is that the market is moving faster than the regulatory framework. He suggests a future where "everyone is going to be skinny and jacked" as oral GLP-1s democratize access and gene therapy moves from self-experimenters to the mainstream. The piece acknowledges the risks, noting that many peptides used have "thin medical backing" and the grey market supply chains may not respect safety protocols.

Everyone is going to be skinny and jacked.

This trajectory presents a complex ethical challenge. While the democratization of health technology is appealing, the lack of oversight in the grey market poses significant public health risks that the author touches on but does not fully resolve. The tension between rapid innovation and patient safety remains the critical variable to watch.

Open Source Biology and Nuclear Renaissance

McCormick identifies a third pillar of optimism in the democratization of scientific tools, specifically through Boltz Lab. He contrasts the proprietary nature of Google DeepMind's AlphaFold with the open-source approach of the Boltz team, who built a state-of-the-art model with resources for only "one training run." "Boltz is a David and Goliath story," McCormick writes, noting that the open model is now used by every top 20 pharma company and over 100,000 scientists worldwide. This section argues that open-sourcing complex models gives scientists the "superpowers" to program biology, accelerating discovery without the gatekeeping of massive tech monopolies.

Simultaneously, the piece highlights a dramatic shift in energy policy, moving from obstruction to aggressive funding of domestic nuclear capacity. The administration has awarded a $900 million task order to General Matter to create domestic high-assay low-enriched uranium (HALEU) enrichment capacity, ending reliance on Russian fuel. McCormick observes that "in just a couple of years, we've gone from the government blocking nuclear to funding it aggressively." This shift is driven by the dual pressures of data center energy demands and the emergence of viable entrepreneurs like Scott Nolan.

Biology is hard, so I have a very complicated process to try to understand whether something new in bio is legit: I text Elliot.

The convergence of open-source biology and domestic nuclear energy suggests a broader trend: the removal of bottlenecks that have historically slowed technological progress. Whether it is code or fuel, the barrier to entry is lowering.

The Human-Machine Flywheel

Finally, McCormick turns to the philosophical implications of automation through the lens of Jacob Rintamaki's writing on nanosystems and robotics. He describes a "flywheel" where robots build AI infrastructure, which leads to better models, which in turn create smarter robots. But the most compelling part of the argument is the shift from economic utility to human meaning. McCormick agrees with Rintamaki's optimistic conclusion that "the robots are going to make us more human."

The robots are going to make us more human.

This is a bold claim in an era often dominated by fears of displacement. It reframes automation not as a threat to human purpose, but as a tool that liberates humans from drudgery, allowing for a redefinition of what it means to be productive and meaningful.

Bottom Line

McCormick's strongest argument is the demonstration that institutional inertia can be broken, as seen in the correction of the food pyramid and the pivot to domestic nuclear energy. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its optimism regarding the grey market for peptides, where the lack of oversight could lead to significant harm before the benefits of democratization are realized. Readers should watch for how the administration balances the rapid deployment of these new technologies with the necessary safety frameworks.
