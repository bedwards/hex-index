Packy McCormick delivers a startling diagnosis for a job market that feels broken despite strong economic numbers: the problem isn't a lack of work, but an abundance of costless effort. By weaving together ancient theology, modern hiring data, and the rise of generative AI, he argues that when the barrier to entry for "good enough" work vanishes, the signal-to-noise ratio collapses, leaving the truly dedicated with nowhere to stand. This is not just a complaint about spam; it is a structural warning that we are entering an era where the value of human sacrifice has been arbitraged away.

The Theology of Effort

McCormick anchors his argument in an unexpected place: the Old Testament. He recounts the story of King David, who, after a plague, is instructed to build an altar. When the landowner Araunah offers the materials for free, David refuses, insisting on paying because "I will not sacrifice to the Lord my God burnt offerings that cost me nothing." McCormick uses this to frame a modern economic crisis: Costless sacrifice is not sacrifice.

"The burnt offerings that cost nothing and the burnt offerings that cost everything smell the same, because we make our offerings not to an omniscient God, but to fallible, overwhelmed humans."

The author's move here is brilliant because it shifts the metric of value from the output to the input. In a world where Large Language Models can generate a custom cover letter in seconds, the "cost" of applying to a job has plummeted. McCormick cites data showing that the applications-to-recruiter ratio has ballooned to 500-to-1, a fourfold increase in just four years. The result is a "Red Queen's Race" where everyone must run faster just to stay in the same place.

"Here's the hard thing about easy things: if everyone can do something, there's no advantage to doing it, but you still have to do it anyway just to keep up."

This dynamic creates a perverse incentive structure. If a candidate spends twenty hours crafting a heartfelt application, that effort is drowned out by a thousand AI-generated applications that look identical on a recruiter's screen. The system no longer rewards the "sacrifice" of time and emotional labor. Critics might argue that this is simply a signal-processing problem that better AI filters will eventually solve, but McCormick suggests the problem is deeper: the signal itself has been corrupted by the sheer volume of noise.

The Asymmetry of the Laggard

The piece expands beyond hiring to the broader creative economy, arguing that AI empowers "laggards" to degrade the experience for "leaders." McCormick illustrates this with the metaphor of a craftsman hand-polishing a widget while competitors mass-produce inferior copies and stamp them with "Handcrafted with Love."

"When every rebel is armed, none really is. It's like when you played GoldenEye 007 as a kid. Getting the Golden Gun the hard way was dope. Everyone getting the Golden Gun with a cheat code made the game suck."

This framing effectively captures the frustration of high-effort creators. The danger isn't that AI produces bad work; it's that it produces plausible work at a scale that makes human effort seem irrational. McCormick admits that he sometimes abandons his own deep-dive essays because he sees "pretty good" versions pop up instantly online.

"The pretty good is the enemy of the potentially great."

This is the essay's most uncomfortable truth. The market for "feeling productive" is now larger than the market for "being productive." We are seeing a "Crisis of Control" where information production has outpaced the ability to process it, much like the Industrial Revolution outpaced logistics before the telegraph and standardized clocks arrived.

"We can mindlessly and costlessly print information with no reference to the underlying stuff about which it was meant to inform."

A counterargument worth considering is that this "noise" is merely a transitional phase. Historically, new technologies that lower production costs eventually lead to new standards of quality and new ways to signal trust. However, McCormick's point is that the transition itself is where the damage occurs: the dream job goes to the person who applied to 347 random openings via AI, while the person who truly wanted that specific role is lost in the pile.

The Future of the Signal

McCormick concludes by looking at the trajectory of code generation, noting that AI is already responsible for 4% of GitHub commits and could reach 20% by 2026. He positions his investment thesis not on the "Singularity," but on the explosion of tokens.

"The market for feeling productive is orders of magnitude larger than the market for being productive."

The author's insight is that we are not just automating work; we are automating the appearance of work. The "numbers going up" become the product, regardless of their utility. This creates a world where the "costless sacrifice" of generating content becomes the default, leaving the few who still choose to pay the "fifty shekels of silver" in a market that no longer recognizes the currency.

"If they cost nothing, they signify nothing."

Bottom Line

McCormick's strongest contribution is reframing the AI disruption not as a replacement of human labor, but as a devaluation of human signal. The argument's greatest vulnerability is its assumption that the market will not eventually develop new, high-cost signals of trust to replace the broken ones of the past. However, his warning is immediate: until we find a way to value the "cost" of effort again, we are doomed to a future of hollow, costless abundance.
