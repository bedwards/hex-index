Packy McCormick and Evan Beard challenge the Silicon Valley obsession with a single, magical breakthrough in robotics, arguing instead that the path to automation lies in a relentless, unglamorous climb up the "gradient of variability." While the industry chases the dream of a general-purpose robot that can walk into any room and work, this co-written piece offers a grounded counter-narrative: real economic value is being unlocked today by solving specific, messy problems one by one. For investors and operators watching the sector, the distinction between a "giant leap" and "small steps" isn't just semantic; it dictates where capital flows and which companies will survive the transition from lab demos to factory floors.

The Myth of the Threshold

The piece begins by dismantling the prevailing "Giant Leap" view, which posits that robotics is currently useless until a critical mass of compute and data triggers a sudden explosion of capability. McCormick writes, "There is a belief in my industry that the value in robotics will be unlocked in giant leaps... throw enough GPUs, models, data, and PhDs at the problem, and you'll cross some threshold on the other side of which you will meet robots that can walk into any room and do whatever they're told." This framing is effective because it exposes the allure of the "moonshot" strategy, which promises a multi-trillion dollar market but often results in companies burning cash on research that has no immediate commercial application.

The authors argue that this approach ignores the reality of the labor market. Most human jobs are not fully repetitive like a machine, nor are they fully open-ended like a creative director; they exist in a complex middle ground of structure and variation. McCormick notes, "Different parts, slightly bent boxes, inconsistent lighting, worn fixtures, humans nearby doing unpredictable things. It's the same for robots." By focusing on this middle ground, the authors reframe the challenge not as a lack of intelligence, but as a lack of adaptability to real-world chaos.

"There is no single fundamental innovation that will suddenly automate the world. We will eventually automate the world. But my thesis is that progress will happen by climbing the gradient of variability."

This argument lands because it aligns with how software and AI have actually evolved: through iterative improvement on specific tasks rather than sudden, totalizing generalization. Critics might note that this "small step" approach risks creating a fragmented ecosystem of narrow bots that never truly achieve the general autonomy promised by the "Giant Leap" proponents. However, the authors counter that the market is already paying for these narrow solutions, suggesting that the path to general intelligence is paved with profitable, specific deployments.

The Economics of Deployment

The core of the strategy presented is that deployment itself is the data engine. Unlike the "Giant Leap" model, which keeps teams locked in labs, the "Small Step" strategy relies on getting robots into the field to learn from failure. McCormick explains, "Success shows you what's stable, failure shows you where the model breaks, and both tell you exactly what to work on fixing next." This is a crucial insight for the industry: the most valuable data isn't synthetic or scraped from the internet; it is the "intervention data" generated when a robot encounters an edge case in a real factory.

The authors highlight the inefficiency of current industrial automation, where robots are programmed with brittle motion sequences that break the moment the environment changes. They point out that even with these limitations, companies like FANUC and ABB generate billions in revenue because the value of automation is high enough to justify the friction. McCormick writes, "Despite all that friction, customers keep buying these robots! That's the market talking." This observation is powerful because it validates the economic viability of imperfect automation, suggesting that the barrier to entry isn't technological perfection, but rather the cost of integration and adaptation.

"We expect our robots to do everything one day, too. We just believe that: 'Everything' is made up of a continuous spectrum of small 'somethings.'"

The piece argues that Standard Bots' vertically integrated approach—controlling the data, models, and hardware—allows them to iterate faster than competitors who rely on third-party components. By "eating the spectrum" one use case at a time, they claim to be building a moat of proprietary data that is impossible for a lab-based competitor to replicate. A counterargument worth considering is that this vertical integration could slow down the ability to adopt breakthroughs in foundational models developed by larger AI labs, potentially ceding the "general intelligence" race to those who can leverage massive, pre-trained networks.

From Lab to Factory Floor

The authors illustrate their point with the example of screwdriving, a task that seems simple but requires a robot to "wiggle" and feel its way into a slot rather than following a rigid path. McCormick states, "Search and feedback is cheap for learning systems. Our robot can move the screwdriver around until it feels that it's in the right place." This shift from pre-programmed precision to adaptive feedback loops is the key differentiator. It moves robotics from a hardware problem to a software and data problem.

The piece concludes by emphasizing that the "Giant Leap" view is a distraction. The real opportunity lies in the thousands of jobs that are too variable for current automation but too structured to require a human. McCormick writes, "Between automation and a McDonald's robot that can fully replace a worker is an incredible number of jobs. It's my belief that these small steps across this spectrum are where we'll unlock major economic value today." This is a pragmatic, if unsexy, roadmap that prioritizes revenue and data collection over hype.

"We are building a real business today because we believe that it's the most likely to get us to that abundance-inducing end state first."

The authors' confidence is bolstered by their own traction, citing hundreds of millions in sales pipeline and deployments at major firms like NASA and Lockheed Martin. This evidence suggests that the "Small Step" strategy is not just a theoretical alternative, but a currently profitable business model. While the "Giant Leap" approach captures the imagination of venture capital, the "Small Step" approach captures the cash flow of the industrial economy.

Bottom Line

McCormick and Beard make a compelling case that the robotics industry's obsession with a singular breakthrough is a strategic error that ignores the economic reality of the labor market. Their strongest argument is that data quality and diversity are best achieved through real-world deployment, not lab simulations. The biggest vulnerability in their thesis is the potential for vertical integration to become a bottleneck if the industry shifts toward a model where generalist AI models can be applied to any hardware platform. Readers should watch whether the "small steps" approach can scale fast enough to compete with the massive capital reserves of companies betting on the "giant leap."
