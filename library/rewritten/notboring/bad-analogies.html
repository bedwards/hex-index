Packy McCormick cuts through the noise of the current artificial intelligence boom with a warning that feels both timely and dangerously specific: just because a company is burning cash doesn't mean it is the next Amazon. While the market often reflexively defends massive losses in frontier AI labs by citing the early days of tech giants, McCormick argues that this analogy is a dangerous shortcut that ignores the fundamental mechanics of value creation. He brings a unique perspective by contrasting the strategic clarity of Jeff Bezos with the economic delusions of WeWork, forcing readers to distinguish between calculated investment and reckless spending.

The Amazon Trap

McCormick identifies a pervasive pattern in investor psychology where losses are automatically rebranded as strategic investments. He notes that when critics point out that OpenAI has no profitable business lines, defenders immediately retort, "You could have said the same thing about Amazon!" This reflexive comparison, McCormick suggests, does more harm than good by obscuring the specific conditions that allowed Amazon to succeed. He writes, "Amazon's success has done a great deal of harm to a great number of companies," highlighting how the narrative of "losing money to win" has been weaponized to justify bad unit economics.

The author grounds his argument in the historical record of Jeff Bezos, specifically the 1997 Letter to Shareholders. McCormick points out that Bezos was not blindly spending; he was making a calculated trade-off. He quotes Bezos directly: "When forced to choose between optimizing the appearance of our GAAP accounting and maximizing the present value of future cash flows, we'll take the cash flows." This distinction is crucial. Bezos understood that earnings do not equal value; rather, shares are worth the present value of future cash flows. McCormick emphasizes that this strategy worked because of a specific engine: "negative working capital," where growth generated cash that funded more infrastructure, which in turn drove lower prices and faster delivery.

Amazon's success has done a great deal of harm to a great number of companies.

Critics might argue that in the early stages of a paradigm shift like AI, traditional metrics like working capital are less relevant. However, McCormick's point stands that the direction of the cash flow matters. In Amazon's case, the burn was an investment in a defensible moat. In other cases, it was simply a hole in the bottom of the boat.

The WeWork Warning

To illustrate the danger of misapplying the Amazon analogy, McCormick turns to the collapse of WeWork. He recalls his own time in the industry, noting that investors justified WeWork's massive losses by comparing them to Amazon's early years. "If you had justified a WeWork investment to yourself by analogizing to Amazon and repeating 'It takes money to make money,' you would have lost it all in the 2023 bankruptcy," he writes. The difference lay in the unit economics. While Amazon's model improved with scale, WeWork's model was broken; no matter how optimistic the revenue projections, the cost of acquiring a customer and the build-out costs meant they could never make money on a single space.

McCormick highlights that even Adam Neumann eventually admitted, "he let the economics get away from him in the halcyon days of 2018 and 2019." The analogy failed because it ignored the specific mechanics of the business. Amazon's strategy was to build a distribution network that became cheaper to operate per unit as it grew. WeWork's strategy was to sign long-term leases and furnish spaces, a model that became more expensive and rigid as it scaled. The author argues that "bad analogies" allow investors to ignore these fatal flaws by hiding behind the halo of a successful predecessor.

When the Analogy Actually Works

McCormick does not dismiss analogies entirely; he argues they must be used as a starting point for deeper investigation, not as a conclusion. He points to Uber as a case where the "Amazon analogy" held water because the underlying mechanics were similar. Uber burned $9.1 billion in 2022, far more than WeWork, yet it succeeded because it was investing in a network effect that improved the product for every user. "You could have made a smart investment," McCormick suggests, if you looked past the losses and saw that Uber was "burning money in actually kind of the same way that Amazon was."

The key differentiator, according to McCormick, is whether the spending creates a durable competitive advantage. He cites DoorDash as the true success story of the "Uber-for-X" model, not because they copied Uber blindly, but because they "tuned it to fit the situation at hand." DoorDash targeted underserved suburbs where competition was lower and execution was relentless. "They made deliveries just a little faster and more reliable every week," McCormick writes, noting that this operational discipline, not just the analogy to Uber, drove their success.

If you had justified a WeWork investment to yourself by analogizing to Amazon and repeating "It takes money to make money," you would have lost it all in the 2023 bankruptcy.

The AI Frontier and the Altar of Scaling

Applying this framework to the current AI landscape, McCormick expresses skepticism about the "lines of code" mentality that has migrated to "tokenmaxxxxxing." He warns that just as lines of code were once mistaken for the goal of software development rather than the means, AI labs might be confusing token consumption with value creation. "The lines of code themselves were not the valuable thing; they were valuable insofar as they were used to solve problems the company needed to solve," he observes, drawing a parallel to Goodhart's Law.

The author questions whether the current AI strategy offers the same strategic uniqueness as Amazon's logistics network or Uber's ride-hailing dominance. He notes that while Amazon had no real competition in its strategy, the AI labs are "fighting with basically the same weapons." Every major lab is "praying at the same altar, the Altar of Scaling Laws." McCormick writes, "Could you say the same for any of the AI labs? We don't have to spend a lot of time answering this. There is a lot of direct competition." The strategies are homogenous, and the competition is "multidenominational," with everyone trying to build the "One True God" through sheer capital expenditure.

He acknowledges the rapid revenue growth of companies like Anthropic and OpenAI, noting that Anthropic grew from a $9 billion run rate to $19 billion in a few months. However, he cautions that dismissing the cash burn as "just like Amazon" ignores the variable cost structure of AI. "Each time you ask Claude to build you an app... it costs Anthropic money," he points out, contrasting this with Amazon's model where serving more customers became cheaper. While margins are improving, the fundamental cost of inference remains a variable that must be managed, not just a strategic investment in a moat.

Bottom Line

McCormick's strongest contribution is his refusal to let the "Amazon analogy" serve as a get-out-of-jail-free card for poor unit economics in the AI sector. By dissecting the specific mechanics of Bezos's strategy versus the structural failures of WeWork, he provides a rigorous framework for evaluating capital-intensive startups. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in the unique nature of AI, where the "product" is still being defined and the potential for a "fast takeoff" could theoretically render current cost models obsolete. Readers should watch for whether AI labs can transition from a "tokenmaxxxxxing" strategy to a model where every dollar spent builds a defensible, non-replicable advantage, rather than just fueling a race to the bottom.
