Kathy Fish cuts through the noise of literary theory to make a startlingly simple claim: flash fiction is not defined by its brevity, but by its velocity. While many writers mistake the form for a snapshot or a static image, Fish argues that without the engine of movement and change, a piece collapses into prose poetry. This distinction matters right now because it offers a practical lifeline to writers struggling to make short work feel complete; she provides not just a definition, but a mechanical blueprint for how time functions as the primary plot device in micro-fiction.

The Engine of Change

Fish begins by dismantling the assumption that flash fiction requires a traditional plot structure. Instead, she posits that "All stories require the engine of time, movement, change." This is her central thesis: the genre's defining characteristic is not length, but the necessity of progression. She writes, "A prose poem may convey a sense of story. A flash fiction must convey it." This distinction is crucial for busy writers who often conflate the two forms; Fish clarifies that while a prose poem can linger in a mood or an image, flash fiction demands a trajectory.

The author identifies her own "three essentials" as Emotion, Movement, and Resonance, noting that "Movement is my stand-in for plot." This reframing is effective because it lowers the barrier to entry for writers intimidated by complex plotting while raising the stakes for emotional impact. She argues that flash can work without a strict narrative arc, but there "absolutely must be movement," which she defines as "Some significant change."

All stories require the engine of time, movement, change. It's what distinguishes flash from prose poetry.

Fish supports this by analyzing Ada Limón's poem "After You Toss Around the Ashes," demonstrating how a piece can function as flash fiction if it contains "meaningful change or movement." She points specifically to the text's use of time markers—phrases like "When she was dying," "Months after that," and "Once"—to show how Limón creates a timeline where the narrator shifts from grief to a sudden, jarring realization: "I am beautiful. I am full of love. I am dying." The argument here is that these temporal anchors are not just decorative; they are structural pillars that force the reader forward through the emotional arc.

Critics might note that this rigid distinction between prose poetry and flash fiction could feel prescriptive to experimental writers who intentionally blur these lines. However, Fish's point remains practical: if a piece lacks that internal shift or external movement, it risks stagnation regardless of its label.

Segmentation as Time Travel

Moving from single narratives to segmented works, Fish examines DeMisty Bellinger's "Black Girl's Magic" to illustrate how structure can manipulate the perception of time. She praises Bellinger's attention to sound but focuses on how the piece uses subtitles and shifts in relationship to convey a "progression of the story as a whole." The segments themselves act as distinct moments in time, forcing the reader to bridge the gaps between them.

In analyzing the transition from the "Voodoo Doll" section to "Zombied," Fish highlights how Bellinger creates a sense of disorientation that mirrors the character's internal state. The text moves from physical pain ("sticks a sewing needle in the fattest part of my left breast") to a surreal dissociation where the narrator sees their skin as "discolored with white powder." This is not just imagery; it is movement through a psychological landscape. Fish observes that things happen "vividly, compellingly, in each segment," proving that segmentation can accelerate time or freeze it at will.

Things happen, vividly, compellingly, in each segment to convey the progression of the story as a whole.

The commentary on Bellinger's work is particularly strong because Fish connects the structural choice (segmentation) directly to the thematic content: the "black girl magic" that exists between the lines of Western expectation and ancestral practice. The movement here is cultural as well as temporal, shifting from a ritualistic space to a mundane reality ("Sunday Morning") where the narrator must reconcile two worlds. This aligns with the historical concept of pareidolia—seeing patterns in chaos—which serves as a metaphor for how readers construct meaning from fragmented narratives.

Repetition as Rhythm and Motion

Perhaps the most counterintuitive part of Fish's argument is her analysis of Lydia Davis, where she suggests that "relentless repetition creates movement within this story." In analyzing Davis's flash "Agreement," Fish shows how the cyclical nature of a couple arguing about who left first actually propels the narrative forward. The text loops: "First she was out... Then while he was out, she walked out on him... Later, he said she had walked out on her."

Fish argues that this repetition functions as a drumbeat that increases tension rather than stalling it. She writes, "The audience of this form of text is also thereby more likely to remember the information conveyed because having three entities combines both brevity and rhythm with having the smallest amount of information to create a pattern." This connects to the literary principle known as the rule of three, which she notes makes events more satisfying or effective. By repeating the action of "walking out," Davis forces the reader to feel the exhaustion and inevitability of the relationship's breakdown.

The rule of three... is a writing principle that suggests that a trio of entities such as events or characters is more humorous, satisfying, or effective than other numbers.

Fish uses this to transition into practical advice for writers, suggesting they use repetition to "build and create movement with your repetition." She encourages an "incantation" style where the rhythm itself becomes the engine of change. This is a sophisticated take on craft; it suggests that in flash fiction, the sound of the story can drive its sense.

Adding to #5 above, creating a distinctive pattern, then breaking it, is another great way to convey movement in your story.

A counterargument worth considering is whether this reliance on repetition might alienate readers seeking traditional narrative resolution. However, Fish's examples demonstrate that when done well, the "break" in the pattern provides the necessary emotional release or epiphany that defines the genre's success.

Bottom Line

Kathy Fish delivers a masterclass in reframing flash fiction not as a miniature novel, but as a kinetic experience where time and change are the only true metrics of success. Her strongest contribution is the demonstration that structural devices like segmentation and repetition are not stylistic flourishes, but essential mechanisms for generating movement. The biggest vulnerability in her approach is the potential rigidity of her definitions, which might discourage writers who wish to experiment with static, image-heavy forms; however, as a guide for crafting emotionally resonant short stories, her framework is indispensable.
