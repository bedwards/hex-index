Freddie deBoer delivers a stinging critique of the 2020 radical moment, arguing that the failure of the "defund the police" movement was not a betrayal by moderates, but a collapse rooted in historical illiteracy and the hollow nature of internet-driven activism. He posits that the slogan was a strategic disaster because it lacked a coherent theory of politics, alienated the very communities it claimed to serve, and ignored centuries of leftist history that actually supported the maintenance of order. For a reader trying to understand why the energy of 2020 dissipated so quickly, deBoer offers a necessary, if uncomfortable, diagnosis: we tried to skip the hard work of building a movement because we were addicted to the speed of the feed.

The Trap of Viral Politics

deBoer begins by dismantling the narrative that the movement failed because it wasn't radical enough. Instead, he suggests the problem was the source of its radicalism. He writes, "Large numbers of people encountered radical left politics primarily through online networks like Twitter or Tumblr, spaces optimized for speed, virality, and tribal signaling rather than sustained study or collective discipline." This observation cuts deep. It suggests that the movement's foundation was built on a platform designed for performance, not power. The result was a surge of converts who lacked the intellectual grounding to withstand the inevitable counter-attacks from institutions.

The author argues that this environment created a dangerous gap between moral urgency and strategic capability. "The movement gained visibility and numbers but lacked the shared intellectual and historical foundation needed to turn moral urgency into durable power," deBoer notes. This is a crucial distinction. It implies that the sheer volume of support in 2020 was an illusion of strength, masking a fragility that would be exposed the moment the cameras stopped rolling. Critics might argue that online organizing is the only viable path for modern movements, but deBoer's point is that without a shared history, that online energy evaporates as quickly as it appears.

"Movements must open themselves up to newcomers if they are to grow, but growth without standards risks hollowing them out."

A Historical Blind Spot

Perhaps the most provocative section of the piece challenges the assumption that police abolition is a traditional left-wing goal. deBoer writes, "The widespread belief that abolishing or defunding the police has historically been a central aim of far-left politics is ahistorical, a matter of taking relatively recent framing and retroactively applying it to our history." He traces this back to the foundational texts of Marxism, noting that while Marx and Engels critiqued the state's violent apparatus, they never called for the immediate abolition of law enforcement in pre-revolutionary societies. Instead, they envisioned a "withering away" of the state only after a classless society was fully realized.

To illustrate the danger of skipping these historical steps, deBoer points to the Bolsheviks in Russia. He notes that the Bolsheviks consolidated power not by dismantling order, but by promising to restore it after the chaos of the February Revolution. This historical parallel is stark: the nascent Soviet Union established the militsiya, a regular police force, to enforce the new order, while the secret police (the Cheka) were expanded to suppress counter-revolution. "The idea that far-left politics necessarily entail anti-police animus... was and is simply ahistorical," deBoer asserts. This reframing is essential. It suggests that the 2020 movement was trying to implement a post-revolutionary goal in a pre-revolutionary context, a tactical error that history has repeatedly punished.

Furthermore, deBoer highlights that even the Black Panthers, often cited as the archetype of anti-police radicalism, did not call for abolition. "Their goal was to shift accountability to local communities so that police would protect Black and working-class neighborhoods rather than oppress them," he explains. This distinction between community control and total abolition is often lost in modern discourse, yet it represents a more pragmatic, historically grounded approach to safety.

The Disconnect with the Base

The final pillar of deBoer's argument addresses the most painful irony of the movement: it pushed a demand that the communities it claimed to represent actively opposed. He cites polling data showing that Black Americans, who are disproportionately victims of violent crime, have consistently favored maintaining or increasing police presence. "Pew Research, for example, found that in June 2020... 55 percent of Black respondents wanted police spending in their area to stay the same or to grow; by September 2021, that number had grown to 76 percent," deBoer writes.

This data creates a profound tension. The movement was driven by a coalition of white liberals and academic activists who projected their own ideological desires onto Black communities, ignoring the lived reality of those communities. deBoer argues that this reflects a "lack of clear class politics" within the social justice coalition. He writes, "If you're more likely to be the victim of violent crime... then you are understandably more sensitive to policing of violent crime and property crime." The failure to listen to the very people the movement was supposed to empower rendered the slogan politically toxic and strategically useless.

The author also points out the incoherence of the slogan itself. "No one seems to agree on what defunding the police actually means, even now, which is terrible politics," he states. For some, it meant abolition; for others, it was merely "unbundling" police duties to social workers. This ambiguity allowed the movement to be co-opted by those who wanted to appear radical without committing to the actual policy changes, a process deBoer calls "sanewashing."

"The call to defund the police was inescapable on left-leaning social media... but the concept of police abolition was too radical for them to express."

Bottom Line

Freddie deBoer's strongest contribution here is the insistence that political movements cannot survive on moral outrage alone; they require a rigorous grounding in history and a genuine alignment with the material needs of their base. The argument's greatest vulnerability is its potential to be read as a dismissal of the urgency of the 2020 moment, but deBoer's core thesis—that the movement failed because it was built on a foundation of historical amnesia and performative online signaling—offers a necessary corrective for future organizing. The lesson is clear: without a shared intellectual framework and a strategy that respects the actual desires of the people you claim to lead, even the most righteous cause will crumble under pressure.
