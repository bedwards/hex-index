Freddie deBoer delivers a searing indictment of the American foreign policy establishment's moral blindness, arguing that the United States has never been a liberator of oppressed peoples and is currently preparing to repeat its historical failures in Iran. What makes this piece essential reading is its refusal to offer the comforting illusion that American intervention can ever result in genuine democracy, instead forcing a confrontation with the brutal continuity of U.S. actions from the 1953 coup to the present day.

The Myth of American Benevolence

deBoer opens by dismantling the prevailing narrative that the U.S. acts as a force for freedom, pointing to recent events in Venezuela as a stark example of raw imperial ambition. "We literally JUST kidnapped Nicolas Maduro and, rather than instituting elections or even making a wave towards democracy, we hand-picked a successor from within his own regime and explicitly told the world we were going to rule the country for as long as we wanted in order to access its oil," he writes. This framing is jarring because it strips away the diplomatic euphemisms usually surrounding such interventions, revealing a transactional logic that prioritizes resource extraction over self-determination.

The author argues that the administration's honesty about its motives in Venezuela—"we don't care about Venezuelan democracy, we're going to run the country as long as we want"—should serve as a warning bell for those hoping for a different outcome in Iran. The core of deBoer's argument is that the American media and political class suffer from a collective amnesia, ignoring the fact that the current Iranian regime is a direct product of American interference. He traces this lineage back to the 1953 coup, where the CIA deposed the democratically elected Prime Minister Mohammed Mossadegh to protect British oil interests. "Mossadegh immediately moved to end British exploitation of Iranian oil, and for good reason: the status quo was, simply, a terrible ripoff, exploitative by any definition and a legacy of British colonialism," deBoer notes. By restoring the Shah, the U.S. installed a dictator whose brutality eventually fueled the very theocratic revolution that Americans now claim to oppose.

The United States does not liberate oppressed peoples. That is not what this country does and that is not who we are.

Critics might argue that deBoer's historical determinism ignores the agency of modern Iranian protesters who genuinely seek regime change, regardless of U.S. involvement. However, the author counters this by highlighting that many of these protesters also harbor deep anti-American sentiments due to this history, a nuance often erased by Western media narratives that seek to paint the U.S. as the sole champion of freedom.

The Danger of Proxy Regimes

The commentary shifts to the specific danger of the administration backing Reza Pahlavi, the son of the deposed Shah, as a potential successor to the current theocracy. deBoer points out the irony that the preferred candidate for the American security state is a monarchist who has never renounced his father's autocratic legacy. "He has been repeatedly accused of misrepresenting his level of support in the country, and his cadre has often been accused of harassment and violence by other dissidents," he writes. This section exposes the hypocrisy of advocating for "democracy" while supporting a figure who represents a return to monarchy, a system fundamentally incompatible with democratic self-governance.

The author warns that the administration's strategy involves installing a pliable government that aligns with American and Israeli military preferences, rather than allowing Iranians to determine their own future. "Tehran is full of Israeli and American operatives who will be working to ensure that any post-Islamist Iranian regime is a pliable one that comports to American and Israeli military and economic preferences," deBoer asserts. This argument challenges the reader to consider whether the goal is truly regime change or merely regime replacement with a more compliant puppet.

The Iraqi Precedent and the Cost of Intervention

To illustrate the likely outcome of such interventions, deBoer turns to the post-Saddam Iraqi government, a project that consumed trillions of dollars and thousands of lives yet resulted in a state rife with corruption and authoritarianism. He cites the Transparency International 2024 Corruption Perceptions Index, which ranks Iraq 140th out of 180 countries, as evidence that American intervention has failed to deliver on its promises of freedom and prosperity. "Almost 23 years after the American invasion, the current Iraqi government remains mired in systemic corruption that has effectively hollowed out the state's ability to provide for its citizens," he observes.

The piece details how the "muhasasa" system, a sectarian power-sharing arrangement, has institutionalized corruption, turning ministries into personal fiefdoms for political elites. deBoer highlights the 2022 "Heist of the Century," where $2.5 billion was stolen from tax deposits, as a testament to the systemic rot that follows foreign-imposed governance. "This is a kind of 21st century, postmodern authoritarianism: the government creates structures that are formally legal within the system but which are clearly antithetical to real personal liberty and self-governance by the people," he writes. This evidence serves as a grim forecast for what might happen in Iran if the U.S. attempts to engineer a political outcome.

You think freedom spreads from the United States like a fart in a crowded elevator. You don't want to hear about complications.

The author's use of this crude metaphor effectively captures the absurdity of believing that complex geopolitical transformations can be achieved through military force and external manipulation. It underscores the disconnect between the idealistic rhetoric of American policymakers and the messy, often disastrous realities on the ground.

Bottom Line

Freddie deBoer's most compelling contribution is his unflinching refusal to separate the U.S. from the historical consequences of its interventions, forcing a reckoning with the idea that American power has consistently undermined democracy in the Middle East. While his argument risks oversimplifying the diverse motivations of Iranian protesters, the historical evidence he marshals regarding the 1953 coup and the failures in Iraq provides a necessary corrective to the current optimism about regime change. The reader must watch for whether the administration's stated intentions in Iran will indeed align with its actions in Venezuela, or if the cycle of intervention and instability will continue unabated.
