PolyMatter delivers a chilling diagnosis of China's education crisis, arguing that the government's massive crackdown on private tutoring was less about protecting children and more about preserving the fragile illusion of social fairness. The piece stands out not for its description of the crackdown itself, but for its assertion that the policy was a desperate, heavy-handed attempt to fix a broken meritocracy that the tutoring industry had accidentally exposed.

The Anatomy of a Rat Race

PolyMatter opens by painting a visceral picture of the stakes involved for Chinese students, describing a childhood where "you do this because if you don't you won't get tracked into one of your school's advanced classes." The author effectively illustrates the domino effect of failure: a bad exam score leads to a bad high school, which precludes a university degree, a white-collar job, and eventually a house and marriage. This framing is crucial because it explains the irrational intensity of the parental investment. The author notes that the only true beneficiaries of this "soul crushing existence" were the tutoring companies, which saw their market quadruple between 2012 and 2018.

The narrative then pivots to the government's reaction. In 2021, Beijing moved with unprecedented speed to dismantle a $120 billion industry. PolyMatter writes, "with the stroke of a pen the entire $120 billion a year industry was decimated virtually overnight." The scale of this action is staggering, with 120,000 centers closing and 60,000 people laid off by a single company alone. This evidence is compelling because it highlights the government's willingness to sacrifice short-term economic stability and youth employment to address a deeper social rot.

"The industry was distorting the entire Chinese economy draining it of money that could be productive elsewhere acting effectively as a tax in party speak contributing to the disorderly expansion of capital."

This observation is particularly sharp. It reframes the tutoring boom not just as an educational issue, but as a macroeconomic drain that was siphoning resources from productive sectors. The author argues that the real driver for the ban was the threat to social harmony. China is a country of extreme inequality, where the richest 10% earn 41% of the income while the bottom 50% earn just 15%. The only thing holding this disparate society together is the belief in the gaokao, the national college entrance exam, as a fair arbiter of success.

The Illusion of Meritocracy

The core of PolyMatter's argument is that the tutoring industry didn't just raise costs; it shattered the myth of meritocracy. By selling "exam shortcuts strategies tips and tricks," companies convinced parents that the gaokao was a game that could be hacked. This commercialization of the exam threatened the state's legitimacy. As PolyMatter puts it, the aggressive marketing "forced the government's hand and it had to crack down to restore faith and meritocracy."

The author points out a tragic irony: the crackdown was designed to level the playing field, but it likely made it steeper. Once the supply of tutors was restricted, the remaining ones could charge higher fees, pricing out low and middle-income families. PolyMatter notes that "low and middle income parents have been priced out of the market but they also lack the time to find vet and manage individual tutors." This suggests the policy failed to address the root cause: the intense demand driven by a high-stakes system.

Critics might note that the author downplays the genuine health and developmental benefits of reducing the workload on children, focusing almost exclusively on the economic and political motives. However, the data on plummeting birth rates and the financial strain on families supports the idea that the system was unsustainable regardless of the fairness argument.

The Universal Trap of Reform

The piece broadens its scope to argue that this is not a uniquely Chinese problem. PolyMatter draws a parallel to the United States, where well-intentioned reforms like charter schools or holistic admissions often end up widening inequality. The author writes, "inequality disguised as equality in fact most ironic of all it was precisely China's earlier attempts at reducing the burdens placed on children that led to the rapid rise in private tutoring in the first place."

This is a powerful critique of the "Band-Aid" approach to policy. The government tried to solve the symptom (tutoring) rather than the disease (the high-stakes nature of the exam and the lack of social mobility). PolyMatter concludes that "the only thing unique about this latest Crackdown is its incredible speed and scale," but the fundamental failure is the same as in any other country: "the Chinese government reduced the supply of tutors the symptom but it failed to address demand the root cause."

"The simple unfortunate reality is that everything favors the rich there's no test no process no criteria or metric that doesn't give the wealthy an advantage."

This line cuts to the heart of the issue. The author suggests that the gaokao and the SAT are actually the "absolute best we can do" precisely because they are arbitrary and standardized, yet even they are not enough to overcome the advantages of wealth. The real solution, PolyMatter argues, would require addressing the inputs—housing prices, work hours, and youth unemployment—rather than just the educational outputs.

Bottom Line

PolyMatter's strongest argument is the identification of the crackdown as a political maneuver to preserve the illusion of fairness in a deeply unequal society, rather than a genuine educational reform. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its somewhat fatalistic conclusion that no metric can ever truly be fair, potentially discouraging the pursuit of incremental improvements. Readers should watch for whether China's government attempts to address the underlying economic pressures that drive this educational arms race, or if this ban remains a temporary, heavy-handed fix for a systemic disease.
