A landmark jury verdict against Meta and YouTube has ignited a familiar argument about whether social media is poisoning a generation. Two very different commentators — child psychologist Jacqueline Nesi of Techno Sapiens and the video essayist behind Then & Now — arrive at the same destination from opposite directions. One urges restraint and clinical precision; the other marshals epidemiological alarm. Read together, they sketch a more honest picture than either offers alone: the harm is real, the mechanism is the product, and the legal theory that finally stuck has almost nothing to do with what kids are saying to each other online.

Two framings, one defendant

Nesi's contribution is to insist on what the verdict actually said. The plaintiff did not win because a jury declared the internet toxic. She won because her lawyers reframed the case as personal injury from a defective product, sidestepping the Section 230 shield that has buried nearly every prior suit. As Nesi puts it, the companies "failed to do so by designing features they knew were harmful (e.g., infinite scroll, autoplay, beauty filters) and neglecting to warn people about them." The jury found it "more likely than not" that the defendants were negligent — a finding about engineering, not expression.

Then & Now is less interested in legal theory and more interested in the body count. The essayist points to a now-familiar graph: teenage depression, particularly in girls, climbing sharply from around 2010, with "reports of mental health problems depression anxiety self harm suicide and so on" nearly tripling across multiple countries. The timing, the author argues, is not coincidental. "Instagram was found it in 2010 just before that uptick and the iPhone 4 was released at the same time the first phone with a front facing camera."

"It is one thing to say that for some kids, social media is one factor contributing to mental health symptoms. It is another entirely to say that social media is causing mental health problems on a large scale."

Where the two voices collide

The tension between these commentators is the most useful thing about reading them side by side. Nesi, drawing on her training as a child psychologist, warns against the seductive simplicity of a single-cause story. She invokes the developmental concepts of multifinality and equifinality — "many different mental health outcomes can result from the same experience... many different experiences can lead to the same outcome" — to argue that for most teenagers, an Instagram account is not going to manufacture a mental illness out of thin air. Poverty, bullying, family dysfunction, and a dozen other stressors are still doing most of the work.

Then & Now will have none of that hedging. The author explicitly rejects the "correlation is not causation" reflex as an excuse for paralysis, comparing the strength of the social media correlation to childhood lead exposure. The essayist's most damning evidence is not survey data at all but the algorithmic record of a single child: "out of the 16,300 pieces of content that Molly interacted with on Instagram in the 6 months before she died 2,100 were related to" self-harm and depression. A Swansea University study cited in the piece found that 83% of respondents with a history of self-harm had been recommended such content without ever searching for it.

This is where the two arguments actually converge, even if their authors would not put it this way. Nesi's narrow legal frame — features, not feeds — and Then & Now's broad public-health alarm both indict the same thing: the machinery of recommendation and engagement, not the speech of users. Nesi keeps the focus there deliberately. "If a child is faced with infinite scroll," she writes, "they are going to spend more time on a platform than they would if those features did not exist."

The addiction question

Nesi pushes back hardest on the word "addiction," which has become legislative shorthand for any unease about screen time. "For the vast majority of people (and kids) using social media, though, I do not think addiction is the right word to use," she insists. Pathologizing ordinary behavior, in her view, lets the product designers off the hook by relocating the problem inside the child.

"Social media is popularity Quantified for everyone in the schoolyard to see."

Then & Now would likely agree with the diagnosis even while preferring stronger language. The essayist's description of platforms as "popularity Quantified for everyone in the schoolyard to see" is structural, not clinical. It points at the same place Nesi points: a product whose core feature is the public scoring of adolescent worth, refreshed continuously, with no off switch the user can find.

Counterpoints neither source fully addresses

Both commentaries leave gaps worth naming. Neither grapples seriously with the substantial minority of teenagers — particularly LGBTQ youth, kids in isolated rural areas, and those with rare medical conditions — for whom online communities are demonstrably protective. A regulatory regime that strips out recommendation algorithms wholesale could sever lifelines along with hazards. Nesi gestures at this complexity but does not work through it; Then & Now barely acknowledges it.

Nor does either author confront the international enforcement problem. A jury verdict in one American courtroom changes the calculus for Meta only if regulators elsewhere follow. The European Union's Digital Services Act is the more consequential lever, and neither piece situates the verdict inside that broader regulatory tide. There is also the question of what "safer design" actually looks like in practice — is it chronological feeds, mandatory time caps, age verification, or something more radical? The product-liability framing wins the case but does not specify the remedy, and the companies will spend the appeals process arguing that no specification is possible.

Bottom Line

The honest synthesis is this: Then & Now is right that something specific and severe happened to adolescent mental health starting around 2010, and that the algorithmic amplification of self-harm content is not a hypothetical harm but a documented one. Nesi is right that the legal victory matters precisely because it is narrow — it targets engineering choices rather than user speech, which is the only path to accountability that does not collapse into censorship. The verdict is not a vindication of moral panic. It is a vindication of a much more interesting claim: that the people who built infinite scroll, autoplay, and beauty filters knew what those features did to children and shipped them anyway. If the ruling survives appeal, the era in which product design for minors faced no liability is ending. What replaces it will be decided not in jury rooms but in the rule-making proceedings now beginning at federal agencies and in statehouses watching this case very closely.
