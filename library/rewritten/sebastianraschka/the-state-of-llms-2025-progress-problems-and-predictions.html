This piece cuts through the noise of annual AI hype by identifying a specific, algorithmic pivot that is reshaping the entire industry's trajectory. Sebastian Raschka argues that the era of simply throwing more compute at pre-training is yielding diminishing returns, replaced by a new focus on "reasoning" models trained via Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards. For the busy professional tracking the economic and technical undercurrents of the sector, this is the definitive map of where the ground is shifting beneath the models we rely on.

The DeepSeek Moment and the Cost of Intelligence

Raschka anchors his 2025 review in a single, disruptive event: the release of the DeepSeek R1 model. He notes that while scaling laws continued to hold, they "didn't really change how LLMs behaved or felt in practice" until this specific breakthrough. The significance lies not just in the model's performance, but in its economic implications. Raschka writes, "The DeepSeek R1 paper prompted many people, especially investors and journalists, to revisit the earlier DeepSeek V3 paper... which then led to a revised conclusion that while training state-of-the-art models is still expensive, it may be an order of magnitude cheaper than previously assumed."

This is a crucial reframing. The industry narrative had settled on a "billion-dollar moat" for top-tier models, but Raschka highlights data suggesting training costs could be closer to five million dollars rather than the previously assumed fifty or five hundred million. "The DeepSeek R1 supplementary materials estimate that training the DeepSeek R1 model on top of DeepSeek V3 costs another $294,000," he points out. This suggests a democratization of high-end capability that could upend the current consolidation of power among a few massive tech giants.

However, Raschka is careful to temper this optimism with necessary caveats. He acknowledges that these figures capture only the compute credit cost, excluding the "researchers' salaries and other development costs associated with hyperparameter tuning." A counterargument worth considering is that while the training cost may be lower, the inference cost for these complex reasoning models could be significantly higher, potentially shifting the economic bottleneck rather than removing it.

The Shift from Human Feedback to Verifiable Rewards

The core technical argument of the piece is the transition from Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) to Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). Raschka explains that previous methods were "bottlenecked by requiring expensive written responses or preference labels," creating a "chicken-egg problem" where you need a smart model to label data for an even smarter model.

In contrast, RLVR utilizes deterministic correctness labels, primarily in math and code. "The V in RLVR stands for 'verifiable,' which means we can use deterministic approaches to assign correctness labels, and these labels are sufficient for the LLM to learn complex problem-solving," Raschka writes. This approach, powered by the GRPO algorithm, allows for scaling during the post-training phase in a way that was previously impossible. The result has been a rapid industry-wide shift: "Essentially, every major open-weight or proprietary LLM developer has released a reasoning (often called 'thinking') variant of their model following DeepSeek R1."

This pivot echoes the historical significance of the 2022 introduction of RLHF via the PPO algorithm, which launched the modern chatbot era. Yet, Raschka suggests we are now in a new phase where the "Year of Reasoning" is defined by mathematical verifiability rather than subjective human preference. The limitation here is clear: this method works brilliantly for math and code, but the path to applying it to open-ended creative or social domains remains murky.

Architecture and the Future of Inference

Beyond the training algorithms, Raschka observes a convergence in model architecture. While the decoder-style transformer remains dominant, the industry is aggressively adopting "mixture-of-experts" (MoE) layers and efficiency-tweaked attention mechanisms to manage the rising costs of serving these models. He notes that "at the scale at which LLMs are trained, deployed, and used, it just makes sense from a financial perspective for these companies, which are still burning a lot of money on serving LLMs."

Looking ahead, Raschka predicts a move toward "inference-time scaling," where the model spends more computational resources during the generation of an answer to improve accuracy. "It's a trade-off between latency, cost, and response accuracy," he explains, but for high-stakes applications, "extreme inference-scaling can totally be worth it." This is a profound shift from the current paradigm of optimizing for speed at all costs.

Critics might argue that relying on "inference-time scaling" is merely a band-aid for models that haven't truly internalized reasoning capabilities, essentially outsourcing the thinking process to the moment of interaction rather than the training phase. Furthermore, while Raschka mentions "continual learning" as a hot topic for 2027, he admits there has been "no new or substantial breakthrough" yet, noting that the challenge of "catastrophic forgetting" remains a significant hurdle.

"The next logical step is to not only use the final answer's correctness as a reward signal but also judge the LLM's explanations during RLVR training."

Bottom Line

Sebastian Raschka's analysis is strongest in its identification of the economic and algorithmic pivot toward verifiable rewards, effectively dismantling the myth that only the largest entities can build top-tier reasoning models. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its optimistic projection of "continual learning" as an imminent breakthrough, given the persistent technical barriers of catastrophic forgetting. For the strategic reader, the takeaway is clear: the next competitive advantage will not be found in who has the biggest pre-training dataset, but in who can most efficiently scale reasoning during inference.
