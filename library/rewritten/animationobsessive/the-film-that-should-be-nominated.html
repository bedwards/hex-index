In a year dominated by record-breaking box office giants, the author makes a startling claim: the most significant animated film of 2025 is not the one breaking records, but the one the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences has effectively erased. This piece does not merely lament a snub; it exposes how rigid qualification rules and provincial tastes are blinding the global industry to a quiet revolution in Chinese storytelling that prioritizes "mythological realism" over spectacle.

The Provincial Blind Spot

The author, writing as Various, immediately dismantles the assumption that the Oscar shortlist represents the pinnacle of the art form. They note that while Zootopia 2 and KPop Demon Hunters secured their spots, the actual highest-grossing film of the year, Nezha 2, and the most emotionally resonant indie hit, Nobody, are absent. Various writes, "The largest animated feature of 2025 won't be there, nor will (arguably) the finest. And it's a shame — because Nobody, especially, feels like a sign of animation's future." This framing is crucial because it shifts the conversation from a simple complaint about exclusion to a critique of the industry's inability to recognize innovation that doesn't fit a Hollywood mold.

The article argues that the omission of Nobody is not an oversight but a structural failure. The film missed the longlist because its distribution in American theaters was not "tuned to get it qualified," a bureaucratic hurdle that often sidelines international art-house cinema. As Various puts it, "This isn't simply an oversight by the Academy. Yet it still makes this year's Oscars feel a bit provincial." This observation lands hard because it highlights a paradox: the institution tasked with honoring global cinema is increasingly blind to the very markets producing its most vital work. Critics might note that the Academy has historically struggled with non-English language submissions regardless of distribution, but the author's point remains valid: the rules favor commercial machinery over artistic merit.

"Most of the world consists of 'nobodies.' That's why their stories resonate so powerfully."

The Rise of Mythological Realism

The commentary then pivots to the film itself, offering a masterclass in how to analyze visual language without getting bogged down in jargon. The author introduces director Yu Shui, a former university teacher who spent two decades in the industry before his breakthrough. The piece details how Yu's team rejected the slick, elastic animation style of Western blockbusters in favor of something more grounded. Various writes, "If the little pig monster came out like Mickey Mouse, with elastic, smoothly flowing deformation and stretching, it would just be an American pig monster. But we want precisely the opposite: first, it needs a feeling of lived-in texture, a little bent and clumsy."

This distinction is the heart of the article's argument. The film draws heavily on the legacy of the Shanghai Animation Film Studio, specifically the ink-wash techniques of the mid-20th century, yet it updates them for a modern cinematic language. The author notes that the team studied Buddhist statues in Shanxi Province to capture a sense of "lived-in texture," blending traditional 2D aesthetics with Western concepts of light and shadow. As Chen Liaoyu, the art director, explains, "We had to figure out how to integrate the effects of light and shadow within ink-wash painting... This approach allows the audience to appreciate the beauty of Chinese aesthetics while experiencing the intuitive realism of cinema."

The author effectively uses this technical detail to make a broader point about the state of the industry. While many modern Chinese productions chase "over-the-top" visual effects, Nobody proves that restraint and narrative depth can outperform flash. The piece references the studio's 1980 classic Three Monks to illustrate this continuity, noting that "every facet was intentional" in the old days, and that same rigor is present here. This historical context adds significant weight, suggesting that the film is not an anomaly but a return to a golden standard of craftsmanship.

The Universal Appeal of the Underdog

Beyond the visuals, the article champions the film's thematic core: the story of outcasts impersonating heroes. The plot follows a ragtag group of animal monsters who hire a concept artist to design their look because they don't know what the legendary Sun Wukong actually looks like. This meta-narrative allows the film to explore identity without requiring deep knowledge of Journey to the West. Various writes, "The frauds — a boar, toad, weasel and gorilla — grow as they try to live up to their pretend roles. What begins as farce turns into halting, absurd, small-scale but real heroism."

The author argues that this universal arc is why the film resonates so deeply, transcending cultural barriers. Yu Shui's own philosophy is quoted directly: "Most people, myself included, are just ordinary individuals. But how do ordinary people establish themselves and navigate life? So many things are beyond our control... Essentially, numerous external forces collectively shape our lives." This quote elevates the commentary from a film review to a meditation on the human condition. The film's success, despite its low budget and lack of marketing, proves that there is a hungry audience for stories that acknowledge the struggles of the "nobodies."

The piece concludes by noting that while Nobody will not compete for an Oscar, the trend it represents is undeniable. The author points to other international successes like Flow to show that small, risky animation is gaining ground globally. As Various concludes, "With or without the Oscars, though, the rise of small, thoughtful and risky animation is happening. There's an audience for it. The untold stories are coming to light."

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its refusal to accept the Oscar shortlist as the definitive measure of quality, instead using Nobody to expose the limitations of a system that prioritizes distribution logistics over artistic innovation. Its biggest vulnerability is the assumption that the Academy's exclusion is purely procedural, potentially underestimating the cultural biases that also play a role. Readers should watch for how this film influences the next wave of international co-productions, as the demand for "mythological realism" appears to be a growing global trend.

"It's not that you can't be cool, but don't be cool for the sake of being cool, or show off for the sake of showing off; above all, you need to tell a good story."
