The Animation Obsessive staff makes an unusual argument about Hayao Miyazaki: that the real engine of his six-decade career isn't his finished films but a shadow archive of loose sketches he has kept since the 1960s. These so-called image boards, the essay contends, are where Ghibli's most iconic moments actually lived first, sometimes decades before reaching screen. It is a quiet claim with radical implications for how animation history gets written.

A Practice, Not A Pipeline

The piece opens by collapsing a common misconception. An image board, in Miyazaki's own phrasing, is "something drawn to prepare for a work"—closer to concept art than to storyboarding. The staff is careful to distinguish this from the rigid pre-production flowcharts of American studios. Miyazaki's method is looser, messier, and almost confessional. "I drew quickly and simply with a pencil and glossed it over with a single color," he recalls of his earliest sessions. The speed is the point. "The idea is not to spend an infinite amount of time creating these things," he says, "but to do them as quickly as possible."

That velocity turned the practice into a form of thinking aloud. On Horus: Prince of the Sun, the young Miyazaki tacked his sketches to studio walls where, in his words, "people who were participating in the preparation and those who weren't could freely take a look." The essay frames this as a democratizing gesture, and it is, but it is also something stranger—a director externalizing his subconscious so that colleagues could wander through it.

The Frustrated Decade

The most affecting stretch of the essay covers the 1970s, when Miyazaki was grinding through television work under Isao Takahata. "After Heidi, I didn't have my whole heart in my work," he admits, and the Animation Obsessive staff treats this confession as load-bearing. The image boards from that period are, by their reading, a kind of secret second career—fantasy concepts sketched in private while the day job demanded naturalism. The famous Totoro bus-stop scene, they note, existed on paper years before the film that made it famous.

"The drawings Miyazaki accumulated between 1980 and 1982 formed the basis of all his work, from Nausicaä onward."

The staff leans hard on this claim, and it is the essay's boldest move. If true, it reframes Miyazaki's breakthrough not as a burst of inspiration but as a slow harvest from a dormant field. His own assessment of his earlier, more derivative work is brutal. Of The Castle of Cagliostro's recycled approach, he says simply, "I realized I should never do this again."

What The Essay Leaves Out

For all its reverence, the piece has blind spots worth naming. It celebrates Miyazaki's absorption of Mœbius, Richard Corben, and Frédéric Back in the early 1980s without seriously asking whether the line between influence and appropriation holds up under scrutiny. Yasuo Otsuka's wry admission that "no matter how much I drew, my drawing skill couldn't match Miyazaki-san's" is quoted admiringly, but the essay doesn't interrogate how much of Ghibli's visual identity came from colleagues like Yoichi Kotabe whose sketches hung on the same walls.

There is also a survivorship problem the staff never addresses. The image boards matter to us because the films they seeded became canonical. Thousands of equally casual sketches by equally talented directors exist in archives nobody reads. The method is being credited with an outcome that depended at least partly on studio funding, producer patience, and audience reception—contingencies the retrospective framing obscures.

"No matter how much I drew, my drawing skill couldn't match Miyazaki-san's."

A sharper counterpoint: the essay treats pencil and watercolor as essential to the method rather than incidental. But the practice being described—rapid, intuitive, accumulative visual thinking—isn't bound to any particular tool. A younger Miyazaki with a tablet might have produced the same archive faster. The fetishization of the physical medium risks mistaking the scaffolding for the building.

Bottom Line

The Animation Obsessive staff has written something valuable: a reminder that auteurship is often slower, sloppier, and more collaborative than the finished product suggests. Miyazaki's image boards are genuinely revelatory as a window into how ideas incubate across decades rather than arriving fully formed. The essay's weakness is its refusal to sit with the uncomfortable questions—about collaborators, about luck, about the difference between a method that works and a method that worked for one extraordinary person. Readers should take the central insight and leave the hagiography. The lesson isn't that everyone should sketch like Miyazaki. It is that finished work rests on hidden labor, and that labor deserves its own accounting.
