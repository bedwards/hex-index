Ryan Grim & Jeremy Scahill deliver a forensic account of how a decade of Emirati statecraft in Yemen evaporated in less than two days, revealing that the region's most powerful Gulf states are now locked in a zero-sum struggle where Yemeni sovereignty is merely collateral damage. This is not just a story about shifting battle lines; it is a chilling demonstration of how quickly proxy armies can be dismantled when their patrons turn on one another, leaving civilians to face the fallout of a geopolitical chess game played with human lives.

The 48-Hour Collapse

The authors frame the recent events not as a military victory, but as a sudden political unraveling that exposes the fragility of the anti-Houthi coalition. Grim & Scahill write, "Their decade-long project in Yemen officially ended in 48 hours." This stark timeline underscores the precarious nature of the Southern Transitional Council (STC), a group that had seemingly secured control over southern Yemen's strategic territories only weeks prior. The narrative power here lies in the speed of the reversal: the STC forces, once flush with Emirati equipment and confidence, were routed so thoroughly that fighters admitted, "we went to Hadhramaut and Al-Mahrah with a large force and high-end equipment…and returned on buses."

The commentary effectively highlights the human cost of this rapid shift, noting that "any prolonged political crisis will severely impact Yemeni civilians already struggling to recover from a devastating humanitarian crisis." The authors remind us that this is happening against a backdrop where "half of the population in anti-Houthi territory is food insecure, with 15% classified as one step from the international classification of famine." By anchoring the geopolitical maneuvering in these statistics, the piece avoids the trap of treating the conflict as an abstract power struggle.

The Southern Transitional Council is not a company to be dissolved by a statement.

This quote from an STC official in Aden serves as a defiant rebuttal to the Saudi administration's unilateral declaration dissolving the group. Grim & Scahill use this to illustrate the disconnect between the coercive tactics of the executive branch in Riyadh and the reality on the ground in Yemen. The argument suggests that while Saudi Arabia can issue orders from a distance, the legitimacy of the separatist movement cannot be erased by a press release. However, the authors also acknowledge the brutal reality of the Saudi response: the delegation of STC leaders who flew to Riyadh for talks were effectively held incommunicado, mirroring historical precedents of coercion.

A History of Coercion

The piece draws a sharp parallel between the current detention of Yemeni leaders and the 2017 Ritz-Carlton purge in Saudi Arabia, where the kingdom's elite were forced to sign over assets. Grim & Scahill note that "in 2017, Saad Hariri, Lebanon's then Prime Minister, travelled to Riyadh... where he made a surprise resignation speech on Saudi television," only to retract it later. This historical context is crucial; it reframes the current detention of the STC delegation not as a diplomatic disagreement, but as a continuation of a specific playbook of political control.

The authors argue that the UAE's withdrawal was not a voluntary exit but a forced retreat following a direct ultimatum from Riyadh. They write, "The kingdom followed up with an ultimatum, giving the UAE 24 hours to leave and end its 'military or financial support to any party within Yemen.'" This detail shifts the blame for the collapse from the Yemeni fighters to the regional powers. The UAE, which had spent years building a network of militias to counter political Islamists and secure maritime routes, found its assets suddenly vulnerable when the Saudi administration decided to prioritize its own dominance over the coalition's unity.

Critics might note that the article focuses heavily on the Saudi-UAE rivalry, potentially underplaying the agency of Yemeni factions who have long sought independence regardless of foreign patronage. Yet, the evidence presented regarding the sudden cutoff of salaries and the flight of STC leader al-Zubaydi to the UAE suggests that without foreign sponsorship, the movement's operational capacity is severely compromised.

The Human Cost of Proxy Wars

Perhaps the most sobering section of the coverage is the reminder that while the Gulf states fight over influence, the people of Yemen are left in a vacuum of security and aid. Grim & Scahill write, "Price hikes, extreme floods, and aid cuts have all contributed to worsening conditions in 2025." This connects the current military escalation to the broader humanitarian disaster that has persisted since the conflict began in 2014. The authors do not shy away from the fact that the "Saudi-backed advance" has resulted in the reclamation of territory, but they frame it as a victory that comes at the expense of stability for the local population.

The piece also touches on the broader regional implications, linking the Yemen conflict to the war in Sudan. The authors suggest that the rift between the two Gulf powers was precipitated by disagreements over Sudan, noting that the standoff there "precipitated the battle for Hadhramaut." This expands the scope of the analysis, showing how local conflicts in Yemen are inextricably linked to the wider struggle for regional hegemony.

An Emirati 'exit' in uniform does not necessarily mean an exit in influence.

Andreas Krieg's observation, cited by Grim & Scahill, serves as a critical counterpoint to the narrative of a total Saudi victory. It suggests that the UAE may simply be shifting its strategy from direct military involvement to financial and logistical support through proxies. This nuance is vital for readers trying to understand the long-term trajectory of the conflict. The authors' framing implies that the current silence on the ground may be deceptive, and that the competition between Riyadh and Abu Dhabi is far from over.

Bottom Line

Ryan Grim & Jeremy Scahill's strongest asset is their ability to strip away the diplomatic veneer of the Saudi-UAE rivalry to reveal the raw coercion and human suffering underneath. The piece's greatest vulnerability is the inherent opacity of the situation; with key leaders detained and communication cut, the full extent of the Saudi administration's control remains speculative. Readers should watch closely for whether the UAE's influence truly recedes or merely goes underground, as the next phase of this conflict will likely be defined by shadowy financial flows rather than open battlefield maneuvers.
