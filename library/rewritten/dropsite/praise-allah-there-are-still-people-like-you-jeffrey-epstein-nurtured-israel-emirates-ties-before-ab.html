Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill have unearthed a chilling thread in the geopolitical tapestry of the Middle East: the idea that Jeffrey Epstein was not merely a predator, but an active, informal architect of the strategic alliance between Israel and the United Arab Emirates. This piece moves beyond the tabloid horror of Epstein's crimes to reveal a disturbing reality where his network served as a backchannel for statecraft, weaving together logistics, intelligence, and elite diplomacy long before the Abraham Accords made the relationship official. For listeners tracking how global power is actually consolidated, the evidence that a convicted sex offender was facilitating high-level meetings between Emirati princes and Israeli security chiefs is not just salacious—it is a fundamental shift in how we understand the origins of the current regional order.

The Logistics of Influence

The authors anchor their investigation in the commercial empire of Sultan Ahmed bin Sulayem, the head of DP World, a logistics giant that controls critical ports from Dubai to Djibouti. Grim and Scahill write, "The story of how a diminutive Gulf state came to sit so close to the center of global power has never been fully told. And Jeffrey Epstein played a part in coaching Emirati elites and building the relationship between Israel and the UAE." This framing is crucial because it reframes the Abraham Accords not as a sudden diplomatic breakthrough, but as the culmination of a decade-long, shadowy courtship facilitated by a man with access to everyone.

The correspondence reveals a relationship that was deeply personal and transactional. After the United States blocked DP World from acquiring American seaports in 2006, Sulayem turned to Epstein to repair the relationship with Washington. Grim and Scahill highlight a disturbing email exchange from 2007 where Sulayem boasts about his motivations for meeting a mutual acquaintance, writing, "Yes after several attemps (sic) for several months we managed to meet in NY. There is a missunderstanding (sic) she she wanted some BUSINESS! while i only wanted some PUSSYNESS!" Epstein's response—"praise Allah , there are still people like you"—is not just a joke; it is a signal of shared values that transcended business, binding two men in a web of mutual exploitation and privilege.

"The only person I have met who is as crazy as me."

This quote, attributed by Grim and Scahill to an email from 2007, captures the bizarre intimacy of the bond. The authors argue that this personal connection was the lubricant for state-level deals. While the public focus was on trade tariffs and port security, the private reality involved vacations on Epstein's private island and the exchange of sensitive political intelligence. The authors note that Epstein even offered literary advice on a book by Dubai's ruler, suggesting the title "My Country's POEM" to ensure the translation conveyed "sophistication" rather than "crude" words. This level of access suggests that Epstein was viewed not as a pariah, but as a trusted confidant within the highest echelons of the Emirati elite.

Bridging the Divide

The most significant contribution of this reporting is the timeline it establishes for the Israel-UAE relationship. Grim and Scahill detail how Epstein actively arranged meetings between Sulayem and former Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak, often in third countries like Russia, to bypass formal diplomatic channels. In 2013, Epstein wrote to Barak, "I tnk [sic] you should meet," referring to Sulayem as "the right hand of maktoum." The authors point out that these meetings were not idle social calls; they were attempts to integrate Israeli cybersecurity firms like Carbyne into Emirati infrastructure.

The evidence suggests that the commercial and security integration between the two nations began years before the 2020 normalization. Grim and Scahill write, "In the decades before the Abraham Accords 'normalization' in 2020, Israel and the UAE quietly built extensive intelligence and commercial ties." They trace the flow of capital from UAE funds into Israeli defense and AI startups, noting that Emirati investors became major backers of Carbyne shortly after the Accords were signed. This raises a critical question: did the public announcement of peace simply formalize a relationship that had already been operationalized through private networks?

Critics might note that attributing the entire geopolitical shift to Epstein risks oversimplifying the complex strategic calculations of sovereign states. The Abraham Accords were driven by shared concerns over Iran and a desire for economic diversification, not just the whims of a financier. However, the authors effectively argue that while the motivation was strategic, the mechanism for building trust was facilitated by Epstein's unique position as a bridge between the two elites. The evidence of direct investment in Israeli security tech by UAE-linked funds, such as TALC Investment and KAAF Investments, supports the claim that these ties were deep and financial, not merely diplomatic.

The Human Cost of Strategic Gambits

The commentary must also address the darker implications of this alliance. Grim and Scahill connect the deepening ties between Tel Aviv and Abu Dhabi to aggressive interventions in the Horn of Africa. They note that the UAE's push for a separatist state in Somaliland, which Israel recently recognized, has drawn furious objections from the Arab League and the African Union. The authors write, "The strategic gambit in Somaliland marks another chapter in the deepening relationship between Tel Aviv and Abu Dhabi," linking this diplomatic maneuver to the UAE's support for the Rapid Support Forces in Sudan, a group accused of genocide.

This is where the tone of the piece must shift from geopolitical analysis to a sober assessment of human suffering. The authors do not shy away from the consequences of these alliances. They highlight how the UAE's interventions have "sharpened tensions" with neighbors and fueled conflicts that have devastated civilian populations. The recognition of Somaliland by Israel is framed not as a diplomatic victory, but as a move that "inflamed the government of Somalia" and strengthened a logistics hub that protects Red Sea shipping interests while ignoring the stability of the region. The human cost of these "strategic gambits" is often invisible in the boardroom deals facilitated by figures like Epstein, but it is the reality for the people living in the conflict zones.

"The story helps reveal the hidden backstory of the Abraham Accords, which were signed one year after Epstein's death."

This observation by Grim and Scahill serves as a stark reminder that the public face of diplomacy often masks a shadowy history of backroom deals. The authors argue that the Abraham Accords were not a sudden miracle of peace, but the public unveiling of a relationship that had been nurtured in the dark, with Epstein as a key gardener. The fact that these ties survived Epstein's death and legal troubles suggests that the institutions and elites involved were deeply invested in maintaining the connection, regardless of the moral cost.

Bottom Line

Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill have delivered a compelling, if unsettling, narrative that recontextualizes the Abraham Accords as the culmination of a long-term, privately facilitated alliance between Israel and the UAE. The strongest part of their argument is the use of direct correspondence to prove that Epstein was not a peripheral figure but an active participant in high-level diplomatic and commercial networking. The biggest vulnerability of this framing is the potential to overstate Epstein's agency in the face of massive state interests, yet the evidence of his role in facilitating specific meetings and investments is undeniable. Listeners should watch for how these private networks continue to influence regional conflicts, particularly in the Horn of Africa, where the human cost of these strategic maneuvers is being paid in full.
