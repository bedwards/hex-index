Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill have unearthed a chilling thread connecting the darkest corners of the Jeffrey Epstein network to the highest levels of geopolitical energy strategy. This is not merely a story about a disgraced financier; it is an exposé on how the Leviathan gas field, a resource that could reshape the Eastern Mediterranean, was shepherded through its most fragile years by a shadowy alliance of intelligence operatives, bank executives, and a convicted sex trafficker. For readers tracking the intersection of capital and statecraft, the revelation that Epstein acted as a critical broker for the Netanyahu government and JPMorgan Chase is a necessary correction to the sanitized narrative of energy diplomacy.

The Broker of Leviathan

The authors challenge the prevailing view of Epstein as a mere social climber, arguing instead that he functioned as a sophisticated dealmaker for elite institutions. "Epstein's role in Israel's gas politics contradicts the image, advanced in a recent New York Times profile, of Epstein as a confidence man who was viewed with skepticism by financial and political elites," Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill write. This framing is crucial because it forces a re-evaluation of the networks that enabled Epstein's operations for decades. If the world's most powerful banks and governments utilized his services for high-stakes negotiations, the question shifts from "how did he get away with it?" to "why was he so useful to them?"

The evidence presented relies heavily on hacked emails from former Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak, which reveal a deep, ongoing collaboration. Grim and Scahill detail how Barak, while searching for international backers, leaned on Epstein to navigate complex regulatory hurdles. "During that period, the development of Israel's natural gas resources was transforming into an urgent political priority," the authors note, placing the story against the backdrop of the 2011 Arab Spring and the disruption of the Arab Gas Pipeline. This historical context is vital; the region was unstable, and the discovery of the Leviathan field offered a potential lifeline, making the need for a trusted, albeit illicit, intermediary even more pressing.

The authors highlight a specific, suspicious coincidence: the timing of a meeting between Netanyahu and JPMorgan leadership. "Whether by chance or by design, Netanyahu agreed to the meeting on March 23, 2011—the same day the Knesset Finance Committee voted on a major tax increase on natural gas exports," Grim and Scahill observe. The implication is that Epstein's access was not incidental but instrumental in securing favorable policy outcomes for the gas consortium. Critics might argue that correlation does not equal causation, suggesting the meeting could have been scheduled independently of the legislative vote. However, the internal JPMorgan emails cited by the authors suggest a level of coordination that makes such a coincidence highly improbable.

The Bank's Complicity

Perhaps the most damning section of the piece focuses on JPMorgan Chase's internal knowledge of Epstein's utility. The authors dissect the bank's internal probe, "Project Jeep," which was launched after Epstein's 2019 arrest. Despite the bank's public denials, the internal documents tell a different story. "The bank's statement appears to contradict its own analysis in 'Project Jeep,' which includes several examples of Epstein introducing senior bank leadership to government leaders in the UK, Africa, and the Persian Gulf," Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill write. This contradiction exposes a pattern of institutional hypocrisy, where public morality clashes with private utility.

The authors zero in on an email exchange between JPMorgan executive Jes Staley and Epstein following the successful Netanyahu meeting. Staley forwarded the confirmation to Epstein, who replied with a chillingly casual note: "surprisee suprise." This moment, captured in the source text, encapsulates the casual arrogance of the network. It suggests that for these elites, access to a sitting Prime Minister was a commodity that could be traded on a whim. The authors effectively use this exchange to strip away the veneer of legitimacy surrounding the bank's operations.

Furthermore, the piece details how Epstein and Barak worked to circumvent antitrust regulations that threatened to break up the gas monopoly. "Epstein coached Barak on credibly presenting himself as an expert on energy, as Barak searched for friendly partners in Europe, Russia, and the U.S. to help Tshuva's group become compliant with Israel's anti-monopoly law," the authors explain. This reveals a disturbing dynamic where a convicted criminal was effectively acting as a lobbyist for a national energy strategy, blurring the lines between state interests and private corruption.

The Human Cost of Geopolitics

While the article focuses on the mechanics of the deal, the authors do not lose sight of the broader context. They note that the deal is expected to "reinforce the Camp David peace framework between Egypt and Israel—an arrangement strained by the Gaza genocide." This phrasing is deliberate and heavy. It acknowledges that the economic stability of the region is being purchased with the blood of civilians in Gaza. The authors do not shy away from the moral weight of cementing energy ties while humanitarian crises unfold.

The narrative also touches on the corruption that permeated the process, citing a recording of Yair Netanyahu, the Prime Minister's son, bragging about the deal. "My dad made an awesome deal for your dad, bro. He fought, fought in the Knesset for this," the son is quoted as saying, while asking for cash to pay a stripper. Grim and Scahill use this anecdote not just for sensationalism, but to illustrate the casual entitlement that allowed the Leviathan project to proceed despite public outcry and legal challenges. The juxtaposition of a $35 billion national asset with a drunken confession of corruption is stark.

The authors also explore the geopolitical maneuvering involving Russia. They point out that while Gazprom was rebuffed from owning Leviathan, they were allowed to market gas from the smaller Tamar field in Asia. "With U.S.-Russia competition in the background, Epstein emailed Barak on August 1, 2013 to advise him about challenges to American investment in the Leviathan field's development," the authors write. This detail underscores how Epstein's network was a conduit for global power struggles, with the Middle East serving as the chessboard.

Bottom Line

Ryan Grim and Jeremy Scahill have constructed a compelling case that the Leviathan gas field was not just an economic project, but a political operation facilitated by a criminal network. The strongest part of their argument is the use of primary source documents—hacked emails and internal bank memos—to prove that Epstein was a trusted advisor to the highest levels of power, not an outlier. The piece's vulnerability lies in its reliance on the interpretation of redacted court documents and the inherent difficulty of proving direct causation in complex financial negotiations. However, the cumulative weight of the evidence suggests that the lines between statecraft, banking, and criminality were far more blurred than the public was led to believe. As the region continues to grapple with the consequences of these deals, the question remains: who truly owns the gas, and at what cost to the people living in its shadow?
