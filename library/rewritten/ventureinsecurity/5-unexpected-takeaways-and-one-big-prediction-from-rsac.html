Ross Haleliuk cuts through the noise of the annual RSA Conference with a counter-intuitive observation: the industry's biggest fear isn't an AI apocalypse, but a return to the "boring" basics of security. While the hype cycle suggests a future dominated by deepfakes and autonomous attacks, Haleliuk argues that the most valuable conversations happening on the expo floor are actually about fixing asset visibility and tightening identity controls. For the busy executive trying to separate signal from noise, this piece offers a rare, grounded perspective from someone who just transitioned from industry insider to startup founder, revealing that the real competitive advantage lies not in buying new tools, but in finally using the ones you have correctly.

The Reality Check on AI Hype

Haleliuk's most striking contribution is his dismissal of the "AI armageddon" narrative that has dominated security discourse for the last two years. He notes that despite predictions that deepfakes would be the number one concern by 2025, the reality on the ground is far more pragmatic. "Every security leader I talked to at RSAC shared security concerns that are grounded, realistic, and pragmatic," he writes. This is a crucial correction to the market's fever dream. Instead of fearing that AI will replace human judgment entirely, Haleliuk observed CISOs worrying about "shadow AI"—the uncontrolled proliferation of AI tools by employees that obscures where corporate data is actually going.

The author argues that the industry is finally moving past the initial shock of generative AI and into a phase of practical application. He points out that the excitement isn't about replacing engineers, but about solving problems that have been "stuck for decades." He cites the example of Jazz Security, a winner in the CrowdStrike, AWS, and NVIDIA accelerator, which is using AI to tackle data loss prevention (DLP), a category the industry has historically struggled to get right. "Take my experience at the CrowdStrike, AWS, and NVIDIA accelerator, where I presented as one of the finalists," Haleliuk notes, highlighting how AI is finally providing a shot at solving unsolvable problems.

"AI is becoming a way to finally execute on the fundamentals at scale."

This shift in tone from dread to possibility is significant. It suggests that the market is maturing enough to stop treating AI as a magic wand or a monster, and start viewing it as a force multiplier for existing workflows. However, critics might note that this optimism could be premature; while CISOs are focused on fundamentals today, the speed at which attackers adopt these same AI tools for reconnaissance at scale could still outpace defensive capabilities, potentially reigniting the "doomsday" fears Haleliuk claims have subsided.

The Messaging Renaissance and the Buyer's Dilemma

One of the most unexpected observations Haleliuk makes is that security startups are currently outperforming large enterprises in clarity of communication. He describes a moment on the expo floor where he realized he could finally understand what most companies were selling. "I was able to understand what most of the companies were actually doing!" he writes. He attributes this to the efficiency of large language models (LLMs) helping technical founders articulate their value propositions without the bureaucratic dilution that plagues larger firms.

The author contrasts this with the "watered-down" messaging of big vendors, where a clear promise like "we back up your data so you can recover from ransomware" gets mangled into abstract slogans like "resiliency at the speed of light." This observation is particularly poignant given the recent consolidation trends in the industry, where large acquisitions often lead to confused product portfolios. For context, this mirrors the challenges seen in the governance, risk, and compliance (GRC) space, where the sheer volume of regulations and fragmented ownership has historically made clear communication difficult, a problem Haleliuk notes keeps 46% of security leaders up at night.

"The real competition is not other vendors, it's doing nothing."

Haleliuk identifies a critical bottleneck for startups: it is not budget, but buyer attention. He argues that most security teams are not running endless proofs of concept (POCs) with ten different vendors; instead, they are deciding whether to prioritize a problem at all. This is a sobering reality for the startup ecosystem. While the "build vs. buy" debate rages on social media, Haleliuk's on-the-ground experience suggests that for many CISOs, the answer is simply to do nothing because the problem isn't a priority. This challenges the assumption that the market is always hungry for the next new solution.

The Return to Fundamentals

The core of Haleliuk's argument is that the industry is pivoting back to the basics. After years of chasing novel zero-day threats and complex attack chains, the consensus is that breaches are still caused by simple failures. "My biggest takeaway from RSAC 2026 is that security leaders are going back to fundamentals," he states. He lists the specific areas where teams are doubling down: asset visibility, identity controls, and least privilege.

This return to fundamentals is not just a reaction to fatigue, but a recognition that "what gets companies breached aren't some novel zero days or AI-driven threats, but weak fundamentals." The author suggests that the question has shifted from "what new tool should we buy?" to "are we actually using what we already have the right way?" This aligns with the broader trend of simplification and consolidation that has been gaining traction since the massive CrowdStrike outage in 2024, which exposed the fragility of over-reliance on complex, interconnected toolchains.

"The rest is just marketing."

Haleliuk's prediction is that once the hype settles, this trend toward fundamentals will accelerate. He believes that the industry will realize that most breaches come from "default credentials that were never changed" or "assets that no one even knew were still out there." This is a powerful, if somewhat cynical, reminder that the most sophisticated technology cannot fix a lack of operational rigor.

Bottom Line

Ross Haleliuk's piece is a necessary corrective to the industry's obsession with the next big thing, offering a clear-eyed view that the most effective security strategy is often the most boring one. The argument's strength lies in its grounding in direct observation from the expo floor, yet it risks underestimating how quickly the threat landscape could evolve to exploit the very "fundamentals" it champions. Readers should watch to see if this focus on basics translates into actual budget shifts, or if it remains a rhetorical stance while the pressure to adopt AI-driven tools continues to mount.
