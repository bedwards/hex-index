In an industry obsessed with acquiring the latest detection tools, Ross Haleliuk makes a startling claim: the future of cybersecurity isn't about finding better sensors, but about owning the place where work actually happens. While competitors race to bundle more security features, Haleliuk argues that ServiceNow is quietly executing a strategy to become the "system of action" for the entire enterprise, turning the messy reality of organizational workflows into its greatest defensive asset.

The Gravity of Work

Haleliuk introduces a crucial distinction that reframes the entire competitive landscape. He writes, "While Splunk has successfully centralized enterprise data, ServiceNow has effectively won the title of becoming an operating system for enterprise IT." This is not merely a semantic difference; it is a strategic pivot from data gravity, where systems attract information, to "workflow gravity," where systems attract action. As Haleliuk explains, "Workflow gravity, on the other hand, is when a system becomes the system of action where work happens, and then uses this position to pull other work into the platform."

The author's reasoning is that once a platform owns the workflow, it owns the decision-making process. This creates a powerful flywheel effect. "The more this system is used, the more context it accumulates," Haleliuk notes, pointing out that ServiceNow holds a "treasure trove of historical data about everything from who requested what to why a certain person requested access to a new application three years ago." This context allows for automation that point-in-time security tools simply cannot match. Critics might note that assuming every enterprise has fully standardized their workflows on a single platform is optimistic; many large organizations still suffer from fragmented legacy systems that resist such centralization.

Data may be the new oil, but it's workflows that get redefined with AI agents.

Why Security is the Perfect Target

Haleliuk identifies three specific characteristics that make cybersecurity uniquely susceptible to this strategy: it is cross-functional, continuous, and proof-driven. He argues that security is not a siloed technical function but a business process. "Nearly every security function is a part of some business process: cloud security is a function that impacts cloud engineering, application security impacts software engineering, identity security impacts identity and IT, and so on," he writes.

This framing is compelling because it shifts the focus from technical detection to organizational coordination. The author highlights that security is "a proof-driven discipline" where every change must be documented for audit. Since ServiceNow is already the repository for this evidence, it is naturally positioned to absorb security operations. Haleliuk asserts, "The fact that ServiceNow is where workflows live makes it the place where audit evidence lives." This suggests that the path to security dominance isn't through better algorithms, but through better governance of the processes that create risk.

The Acquisition Strategy: Seeing, Deciding, Acting

The piece details how recent massive acquisitions, specifically Veza and Armis, fit into a three-layer stack: "see" (visibility), "decide" (prioritization), and "act" (workflow). Haleliuk writes, "Put simply, Veza helps ServiceNow own 'who/what can access,' and Armis helps it own 'what's out there and how risky it is.'" These are not just new products; they are upstream decision points that feed directly into the workflow engine.

The author contrasts this with competitors like Palo Alto Networks and Cisco, who are building broad portfolios but often lack the unified workflow layer. "ServiceNow stands out as a player with a distinctively unique set of advantages," Haleliuk argues, noting that its core differentiator is the ability to "turn messy cross-team work into structured workflows that can be measured, governed, automated, and secured at scale." The recent acquisitions of Veza and Armis are described as aggressive moves to "acquire control points in security where 'visibility + prioritization' decisions start."

The Human Cost of Complexity

Perhaps the most profound insight in the piece is the argument that the root cause of most breaches is not technical failure, but organizational complexity. Haleliuk writes, "The majority of risks aren't an outcome of poorly designed products; they are an outcome of organizational complexity." He points out that "over 99% of all breaches will be the result of misconfigurations," a statistic that underscores the difficulty of managing identity and access at scale without a unified system.

This is where the argument for "workflow gravity" becomes a human imperative. If security is embedded into the daily workflows of employees, IT, and facilities teams, the friction that leads to misconfigurations is reduced. Haleliuk concludes that "we need to find a way to embed security into the enterprise workflows," and that ServiceNow is uniquely positioned to do so. While the author is optimistic about the potential for AI agents to automate these workflows, a counterargument worth considering is whether centralizing all security decisions in one platform creates a single point of failure or a massive target for attackers.

If we want to solve the problems of enterprise security, we need to find a way to embed security into the enterprise workflows.

Bottom Line

Ross Haleliuk's analysis offers a sophisticated look at how ServiceNow is leveraging its dominance in enterprise workflows to disrupt the cybersecurity market, moving beyond simple tool aggregation to owning the decision-making layer. The argument's greatest strength is its focus on organizational complexity as the primary driver of risk, but its biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that enterprises will willingly consolidate their entire security posture into a single vendor's ecosystem. Readers should watch to see if ServiceNow can successfully integrate these new acquisitions without creating the very bottlenecks they aim to solve.
