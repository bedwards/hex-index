Dylan Patel reveals a hidden engine driving the next wave of artificial intelligence: the industrial-scale construction of digital playgrounds where models practice real-world jobs. While the public fixates on model size, Patel argues the true bottleneck has shifted to the creation of specialized, high-fidelity environments where AI agents can learn to file taxes, write code, and navigate software suites. This is not merely a technical update; it is a fundamental restructuring of how intelligence is manufactured, moving from passive internet scraping to active, labor-intensive simulation.

The Shift from Pre-Training to Reinforcement Learning

Patel's central thesis is that the era of scaling pre-training on raw internet data is plateauing, giving way to a new critical path: reinforcement learning (RL). He points to OpenAI's recent trajectory as the definitive proof of concept. "The company has used the same base model, GPT-4o, for all their recent flagship models: o1, o3, and GPT-5 series," Patel writes, noting that "gains in the performance of OpenAI's models for 18 months were being driven by post-training and scaling up RL compute alone." This observation reframes the narrative of AI progress; it suggests that the raw power of the base model matters less than the quality of the curriculum it is forced to master.

The author correctly identifies that this shift creates a massive data scarcity problem. Unlike pre-training, which could scrape the entire internet, RL requires tasks that are graded, specific, and often non-existent in the public domain. "Scaling up RL is difficult as it requires a steady stream of tasks the model needs to solve and learn from," Patel explains. The solution, he argues, is not just more compute, but the deliberate engineering of "environments" where models can fail, learn, and retry. This moves AI development from a science of discovery to a science of curriculum design.

The Rise of the Data Foundry

The most striking insight in the piece is the emergence of a new industrial sector dedicated solely to building these training grounds. Patel describes a "Gold Rush" where the most valuable asset is no longer the pickaxe (the model) but the shovel (the environment). "In an RL Scaling Boom, sell RL environments," he posits, highlighting a market where over 35 startups are now cloning websites and software interfaces to serve as training simulators. These "UI gyms" replicate the digital world—cloning the interface of DoorDash, Slack, or AWS terminals—so agents can practice navigation without risking real-world damage.

This industrialization of training data has profound implications for the labor market and the nature of AI capabilities. The process is incredibly manual and expensive, often requiring human experts to design tasks and grade outputs. "Finance professionals help define financial tasks, doctors for medicine, lawyers for law, etc.," Patel notes, describing a new class of contractors who are essentially writing the homework for the next generation of AI. The scale is staggering, with firms like Surge and Mercor acting as intermediaries to connect labs with these domain experts. The result is a system where AI utility is directly tied to the breadth of these curated, human-verified scenarios.

In an RL Scaling Boom, sell RL environments.

Critics might argue that this heavy reliance on human-constructed environments creates a ceiling for generalization. If models are trained only on specific, curated tasks like filing a tax return or navigating a specific version of a website, they may struggle to adapt to novel, unstructured real-world situations. However, Patel suggests that the combination of real-world data mining and synthetic bug generation is creating a robust enough dataset to overcome these limitations, at least for high-value professional tasks.

From Abstract Benchmarks to Economic Utility

The piece also marks a significant shift in how success is measured. The industry is moving away from abstract intelligence tests toward evaluations that mirror actual economic output. Patel introduces the GDPval benchmark, a metric designed to measure how well models perform tasks across 44 occupations. "Many of these tasks are digital but require several hours to complete for a human," he writes, citing examples like creating a client advisor presentation or filing a tax return. The goal is no longer just to answer a multiple-choice question correctly, but to produce work that a human expert would prefer.

This reorientation signals that the endgame for AI is not just conversation, but execution. The current leader, GPT-5.2, scores around 71% on these utility tests, meaning its output is preferred over a human's 71% of the time. "This stands in contrast to most of the previous model evaluations, which focused on things like mathematical knowledge or PhD level scientific questions graded via multiple choice," Patel observes. The implication is clear: the race is now about who can build the most effective agent for the economy, not just the smartest chatbot.

The Bottom Line

Patel's analysis effectively dismantles the myth that AI progress is solely a function of raw compute power, revealing instead a complex, labor-intensive infrastructure of data foundries and simulated environments. The strongest part of the argument is the identification of the "shovel sellers"—the startups and contractors building the digital training grounds—as the new bottleneck and opportunity in the AI stack. The biggest vulnerability remains the scalability of human-in-the-loop grading; if the demand for expert-verified tasks outpaces the supply of qualified humans, the entire reinforcement learning model could stall. Readers should watch for how quickly these specialized environments can be automated, as that will determine whether the next leap in AI is a gradual climb or an explosive jump.
