Dylan Patel delivers a jarring reality check to the AI industry: the era of cheap, abundant compute is over, replaced by a frantic, high-stakes scramble where availability matters more than price. While many analysts predicted a crash in rental rates as new chips arrived, Patel presents irrefutable data showing that demand has outpaced supply so severely that renting a GPU cluster now resembles a black market transaction more than a standard commercial service. This is not just a market fluctuation; it is a structural shift that threatens to bottleneck the very innovation the industry claims to be driving.

The Great Scramble

Patel opens with a stark observation about the current market psychology. "Trying to find GPU compute in early 2026 has been like trying to book airplane tickets on the last flight out, high prices, and almost no availability." He quickly escalates the analogy, noting that the reality is even more extreme: "That's the PC analogy, but the more accurate analogy is that trying to rent a cluster is actually like trying to buy drugs." This hyperbolic comparison serves a crucial purpose; it strips away the veneer of corporate procurement and reveals the desperation of buyers who cannot access the hardware needed to run their models.

The data backing this claim is as surprising as it is alarming. Despite the anticipation that newer chips would lower costs, Patel reports that "H100 1-year GPU rental contract pricing has shot up almost 40% from a low of $1.70/hr/GPU in October 2025 to $2.35/hr/GPU by March 2026." This surge defies the standard technology adoption curve where prices typically plummet as supply ramps up. The author explains that this is driven by a perfect storm of surging demand from major labs like Anthropic and OpenAI, combined with a supply chain that is choking on its own success.

"The debate on the true return of using AI is now a settled question — the use of AI tools can deliver value an order of magnitude greater than the cost of using the tools."

Patel argues that this high return on investment creates an inelastic demand curve. Because companies are saving massive amounts of time and money using tools like Claude Code, they are willing to pay exorbitant rates for compute. "If the return on investment from using AI tools is 5-10x, then there is clearly a long way to go in GPU rental pricing before prices rise enough to curtail demand." This logic is compelling, but it overlooks a critical vulnerability: at some point, the cost of the infrastructure will inevitably eat into the margins of the applications themselves, forcing a reckoning that the current frenzy ignores.

The Blackwell Paradox and Supply Chain Friction

One of the most insightful parts of Patel's coverage is how he dismantles the expectation that newer hardware would solve the shortage. The industry expected the transition to Blackwell microarchitecture to lower costs, but the opposite occurred. "Before late 2025, the prevailing expectation across the ecosystem was that Hopper (i.e. H100 and H200) rental prices would drop considerably as Blackwell deployments ramped given the latter's much lower cost of compute. Instead, the opposite happened in late 2025: demand for H100s was holding firm, and in many cases, strengthening."

This counterintuitive trend is driven by the specific needs of the market. While the new Blackwell chips are powerful, the massive clusters required for training and inference are still being built, and the older Hopper chips remain the workhorse for many tasks. Patel notes that "Customers are fighting to pay $14/hr/GPU for p6-b200 spot instances in AWS," highlighting that even expensive, older hardware is in short supply. The situation is exacerbated by memory costs; "January was the next inflection point for compute when memory pricing, across both DRAM and NAND pricing, went from rising aggressively for several quarters, to going completely parabolic."

This memory bottleneck is a crucial detail often missed in high-level summaries. The cost of the chips themselves is only part of the equation; the supporting infrastructure is becoming prohibitively expensive. "OEMs began repricing AI servers at levels that significantly exceeded the underlying increase in component costs," Patel writes. This pricing behavior has forced some operators to "slow-roll or abandon deployments," effectively withholding supply from the market and tightening the rental environment further. Critics might argue that this is a temporary supply shock that will resolve as manufacturing scales, but the lead times Patel cites suggest a longer-term structural deficit.

The Rise of the Neocloud Slumlord

Perhaps the most disturbing trend Patel identifies is the shift in power dynamics between buyers and sellers. The market has flipped from a buyer's market to a seller's market so drastically that rental providers are acting less like service companies and more like monopolists. "Neoclouds and Hyperscalers are now in the driver's seat — they can now negotiate for more favorable terms such as higher prepay, better pricing, longer contract lengths and can even pick and choose the contract start and end dates to match their inventory availability."

This power shift has led to bizarre market behaviors. Patel describes a scenario where "renters of compute subdividing their clusters and subletting the compute just like an apartment during the Monaco Grand Prix." He provocatively asks, "Coming soon — the rise of Neocloud slumlords?" This imagery is effective because it humanizes the abstract concept of "capacity allocation." It suggests that the market is becoming inefficient and opaque, with capacity being hoarded and resold at premiums rather than distributed based on need.

The author also highlights the opacity of the market, noting that "Most large Neoclouds have a preference to rent out capacity on at least a 1-year term with 2 or 3 year terms preferred." This preference for long-term contracts locks up capacity that could otherwise be used by smaller players, further entrenching the dominance of the biggest labs. "There is no guarantee that buyers of compute are actually transacting at prices publicly posted by Hyperscalers and Neoclouds," Patel warns. The public prices are often just a lagging indicator, while the real action happens in private negotiations that are invisible to the broader market.

"We have even heard of renters of compute subdividing their clusters and subletting the compute just like an apartment during the Monaco Grand Prix. Coming soon – the rise of Neocloud slumlords?"

Patel's introduction of the H100 1-year rental price index is a significant contribution to the field. By moving away from spot prices and focusing on contract data, he provides a more accurate picture of the "real" cost of compute. "Our index is constructed from direct survey data across a pool of 100+ market participants including Neocloud providers, buyers and sellers of compute that is captured every month to determine a representative range." This methodology is superior to relying on public listings, which often fail to reflect the true scarcity of the market.

Bottom Line

Patel's argument is a powerful corrective to the naive optimism that has pervaded the AI sector, demonstrating that the bottleneck is not just technical but economic and structural. The strongest part of his coverage is the data-driven revelation that demand is inelastic and supply is being artificially constrained by pricing strategies and memory shortages. However, the biggest vulnerability in this narrative is the assumption that the current pricing power of Neoclouds is sustainable; if the cost of compute continues to rise without a corresponding increase in application revenue, the bubble could burst. Readers should watch for the moment when the "slumlords" of the GPU market meet their first serious challenger or when the cost of inference finally exceeds the value it generates.
