Dylan Patel delivers a rare, granular autopsy of Nvidia's Blackwell architecture, bypassing marketing fluff to expose the raw assembly instructions that actually drive AI performance. While the industry fixates on theoretical peak speeds, Patel argues that "no public datacenter Blackwell architecture microbenchmarking study on PTX and SASS instructions" existed until his team filled the void with months of engineering labor. This is not a press release; it is a forensic investigation into how the world's most expensive chips actually behave under load.

The Architecture Shift

Patel's central thesis is that Blackwell represents a fundamental shift in how data moves, not just how it is calculated. He writes, "The introduction of tensor memory (TMEM) to hold MMA accumulators" marks a departure from previous generations where threads implicitly owned results. This change forces software to explicitly manage memory scope, a subtle but critical pivot that Patel notes is reflected in how "tcgen05 operations are now issued by a single thread on behalf of the entire CTA."

This move toward explicit management is significant because it mirrors the historical evolution of systolic arrays, where the WARP concept once defined how data flowed through a grid of processing elements. Patel suggests that by centralizing instruction issuance, Nvidia is attempting to reduce the overhead that previously plagued large-scale matrix multiplications. However, the author admits this complexity creates a steep learning curve: "Threads no longer implicitly own the results of MMA operations and instead, TMEM is explicitly managed at the MMA scope from software."

Critics might argue that this increased software burden could stifle adoption among developers who prefer the abstraction layers of previous generations. Yet, Patel's data suggests the trade-off is necessary for the massive gains in operational intensity that Blackwell promises.

"We do this to discover unit- and instruction-level hardware throughput and latency limits, providing a useful characterization from an ML systems and kernel development perspective."

The Yielding Reality

Perhaps the most surprising revelation in Patel's coverage is the chaotic reality of chip manufacturing. He dismantles the illusion of uniformity, explaining that "the number of yielded SMs per GPCs is not fixed, not the same between GPCs on the same chip, and may not even be symmetrical between dies in the same package." This "floorsweeping" phenomenon means that two Blackwell chips with identical logical configurations can have vastly different physical layouts.

Patel details how his team reverse-engineered these hidden topographies by measuring latency across the chip. "We can see two clear sets of SMs, separated by >300 cycles average distance to L2; this must be the die-to-die crossing," he observes. This finding is crucial for kernel developers who might naively assume that launching a persistent CTA on every available SM will result in parallel execution. Instead, they risk serialization if the cluster size does not align with the physical, defect-ridden reality of the silicon.

This section effectively bridges the gap between theoretical computer science and the messy physics of semiconductor production. It reminds us that while the register file and coalescing logic are designed with mathematical precision, the physical substrate is a landscape of compromises.

Memory Subsystem Showdown

The article's most actionable insights come from its comparison of memory copy mechanisms. Patel contrasts the older asynchronous copy (LDGSTS) with the newer Tensor Memory Accelerator (TMA). He notes that "TMA is good for large loads with regular access patterns but has higher latency, while async copy can handle irregular memory access patterns but has size limits."

The data reveals a nuanced trade-off: "Async copy slightly outperforms TMA at less than 32 bytes in flight, but TMA catches up after that and can continue scaling to 128 KiB." This distinction is vital for optimizing libraries like FlashInfer, which must choose between speed for small, dynamic loads and efficiency for massive, static tensors. Patel points out that "Blackwell MLA kernels use async copy for dynamically loading pages, while its MHA kernels use only TMA," a decision driven by the specific memory access patterns of those algorithms.

Patel also highlights the potential of TMA multicast, where "a single load copies data to the shared memory of multiple SMs." This feature, he argues, significantly reduces HBM loads and L2 traffic by coalescing requests. However, he expresses frustration at the opacity of current implementations: "To understand the exact mechanics of the kernels, we urge NVIDIA to open source the FlashInfer TRT-LLM kernels for the benefit of the community."

"The unit responsible for serving TMA multicast requests is called the L2 Request Coalescer (LRC): It processes incoming requests for L2 and tries to coalesce read requests before forwarding them to the L2 cache."

Bottom Line

Patel's piece is a masterclass in demystifying the "black box" of modern AI hardware, proving that theoretical peaks are often meaningless without understanding the latency and throughput of specific instructions. Its greatest strength lies in exposing the non-deterministic nature of chip yields, a factor that could derail performance for unprepared developers. The biggest vulnerability remains the lack of official documentation, forcing the community to reverse-engineer the very chips they rely on, a situation that Patel rightly identifies as a barrier to optimal kernel development.
