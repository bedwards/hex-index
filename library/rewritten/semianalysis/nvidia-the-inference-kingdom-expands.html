Dylan Patel delivers a startling revelation: Nvidia didn't just buy a competitor; it executed a regulatory end-run to acquire Groq's intellectual property and talent for $20 billion without triggering an antitrust review. This isn't a standard industry consolidation story; it is a masterclass in navigating legal constraints to secure a specific architectural advantage for the next decade of AI inference. For the busy executive tracking the hardware bottleneck, the core insight is that the future of AI isn't just about bigger GPUs, but about a hybrid system where speed and capacity are physically separated.

The Regulatory Workaround

Patel's most provocative claim concerns the structure of the deal. He writes, "Strictly speaking, Nvidia paid Groq $20B to license their IP and hire most the team. This functions almost as an acquisition, though its structure technically falls short of it being legally considered as one, thereby simplifying or obviating the need for regulatory approvals." This distinction is crucial. By avoiding a formal merger, Nvidia bypassed the likely death sentence of an antitrust block, given its dominant market share.

The author argues that this maneuver allowed for immediate integration, noting, "Given Nvidia's market share, if this transaction were structured as a full acquisition and were put to anti-trust review, such a transaction would likely not go through." The speed of execution is the real story here; less than four months after the announcement, the technology is already being folded into Nvidia's Vera Rubin inference stack. Critics might note that licensing agreements can be fragile and that retaining talent without equity incentives is historically difficult, but Patel suggests the momentum is already too strong to reverse.

"Nvidia got instant access to Groq's IP and people. This is why, less than four months after the deal was announced, Nvidia already has a system concept that is being integrated into the Vera Rubin inference stack."

The Architecture of Determinism

The commentary then pivots to the technical "why." Patel explains that Groq's Linear Processing Unit (LPU) is fundamentally different from the GPU's General Purpose architecture. He describes the design: "Groq re-organized the architecture into groups of single-purpose units connecting to other groups of different purposes, and they named the groups 'slices.'" Unlike traditional chips that rely on complex memory hierarchies, Groq uses a single-level scratchpad SRAM to ensure the hardware execution is deterministic.

This is where the historical context of memory architecture becomes vital. While modern GPUs rely heavily on High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) which acts as a massive, fast cache, Groq's approach mirrors the "scratchpad memory" concepts discussed in earlier deep dives, where data is explicitly managed rather than cached. Patel writes, "Groq opted for single-level scratchpad SRAM instead of multi-level memory hierarchy to make the hardware execution deterministic." This allows the compiler to aggressively schedule instructions, hiding latency that would otherwise stall a GPU.

However, the author is candid about the hardware's limitations. "The standalone Groq LPU system is not economical for serving tokens at scale, but it can serve tokens very quickly which can demand a large market premium." The chip is fast but expensive per token because its massive on-chip memory fills up quickly with model weights, leaving little room for the "KV cache" needed to handle multiple users simultaneously.

The Hybrid Solution: AFD

The most significant strategic shift Patel outlines is the move toward "Attention FFN Disaggregation (AFD)." This is not just a software tweak; it is a fundamental re-architecting of how inference runs. The author explains that Large Language Model (LLM) inference has two distinct phases: the "prefill" (compute-heavy) and the "decode" (memory-heavy).

Patel writes, "We map attention computations to GPUs, which handle dynamic workloads well. For FFNs, we map them to LPUs, since LPU architecture is inherently deterministic and benefits from static compute workloads." This separation allows the GPU to focus on the stateful, memory-intensive attention mechanisms, while the LPU handles the stateless, repetitive Feed Forward Network calculations with blistering speed.

This approach addresses a critical inefficiency in current systems. As models grow more sparse, with tokens choosing from larger pools of "experts," GPU utilization drops. Patel notes, "During decode phase, the GPU utilization of attention barely improves when scaling batch size due to being bounded by loading KV cache." By offloading the FFN work to the LPU, the GPU can dedicate its entire memory capacity to the KV cache, effectively increasing the number of users a single rack can serve.

"Nvidia has decided to combine these architectures to get the best of both worlds: accelerate parts of decode that are more latency sensitive and are not as memory heavy on a low-latency SRAM-heavy chip like the LPU, while memory hungry attention is performed on GPUs that come with a lot of fast (but not SRAM fast) memory capacity."

Manufacturing and Supply Chain Realities

Patel also dissects the manufacturing strategy, which is as much about geopolitics as it is about silicon. The original Groq chips were built on a mature 14nm process, but the new LP30 chip is being fabricated on Samsung's SF4X node in Austin, Texas. This is a deliberate move to bypass the bottlenecks plaguing TSMC.

He writes, "One of the benefits of relying on SF4 is that it isn't constrained like TSMC's N3, which is putting a cap on accelerator production and is a key reason why the industry remains compute constrained." By utilizing Samsung's capacity, Nvidia can ramp up LPU production without eating into its precious TSMC allocation for its own GPUs. This is a clever supply chain hedge.

However, the author acknowledges the technical hurdles that nearly derailed the project. The second-generation Groq chip failed because "The C2C SerDes on the chip couldn't hit the advertised 112G speed which caused the design to malfunction." The fact that Nvidia has now resolved these issues and is moving the next generation (LP40) to TSMC's advanced N3P process suggests a maturation of the technology that was previously stalled.

Bottom Line

Patel's strongest argument is that the era of the monolithic AI accelerator is ending; the future belongs to disaggregated systems where different hardware types specialize in different parts of the inference pipeline. The biggest vulnerability in this thesis is the complexity of the software stack required to manage the "ping pong" data flow between GPUs and LPUs without introducing new latency bottlenecks. Readers should watch closely to see if the promised "Attention FFN Disaggregation" can be implemented at scale without the communication overhead negating the speed gains.
