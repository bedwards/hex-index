In an era where datacenter water usage is often framed as an existential threat, this piece delivers a jarring reality check by pitting a gigawatt-scale AI facility against a fast-food staple. Dylan Patel doesn't just crunch numbers; he dismantles the prevailing narrative that equates digital infrastructure with environmental collapse, offering a metric so absurd it forces a re-evaluation of the entire debate.

The Flawed Baseline

Patel opens by challenging the alarmism surrounding datacenter water consumption, noting that "the debate is overstated, as the numbers are often misleading and key variables are frequently ignored." He correctly identifies that headlines often strip away context like cooling architecture, power sources, and local water scarcity, leaving readers with a distorted view of the problem. By refusing to accept "datacenters are choking the world" as a settled fact, Patel forces the reader to confront the nuance of water accounting. The argument is compelling because it exposes how easily statistics can be weaponized when the methodology is opaque.

"A lot of nuances that makes the 'datacenters are choking the world' headline hard to assess."

The author breaks down the water footprint of xAI's Colossus 2 facility in Memphis, distinguishing between direct usage (cooling) and indirect usage (chip manufacturing). He notes that the facility employs a hybrid system of dry and adiabatic cooling, which significantly reduces evaporation compared to traditional wet systems. This technical specificity is crucial; without it, any comparison to other industries is meaningless. Critics might note that focusing on a single, state-of-the-art facility ignores the water intensity of older, less efficient datacenters that still make up a large portion of the global fleet. However, Patel's point stands: the future of AI infrastructure is being built with water efficiency in mind, not against it.

The Burger Benchmark

The piece's most distinctive move is the decision to compare the datacenter to an In-N-Out Burger store. Patel calculates the "blue water" footprint—surface and groundwater used in production—rather than including rainfall, ensuring a fair comparison. He finds that a single Double-Double burger requires 245 gallons of water, mostly for cattle feed. When scaled to an average store selling 600,000 burgers a year, the footprint balloons to 147 million gallons.

"The only rational argument is that people clammering for slowing down datacenters because of water consumption are looking at the wrong problem."

This comparison is not just a gimmick; it serves to contextualize the sheer scale of agricultural water usage, which is often invisible to the average consumer. Patel writes, "one the largest datacenters in the world only consumes as much water as 2.5 In-N-Out's." This reframing is powerful because it shifts the moral weight from the digital to the physical, suggesting that our dietary choices may have a far greater environmental cost than our digital ones. The argument holds up well, provided one accepts the premise that the datacenter's efficiency will continue to improve as it scales.

The Value of a Token

Beyond the raw volume of water, Patel introduces a new metric: the economic and intellectual value generated per gallon. He estimates that the Colossus 2 facility could generate 3.9 quadrillion output tokens annually, translating to "8.9 million tokens per gallon of footprint." This leads to the piece's most striking calculation: a single burger's water footprint equals using an AI model for 668 years at a rate of 30 queries per day.

"Even more, if we assume a daily request number of 30 queries per day and an average output length of 375 tokens, we get to the conclusion that a single burger's water footprint equals using Grok for 668 years, 30 times a day, every single day."

This is where the commentary transcends simple comparison and enters the realm of policy. Patel argues that we must assign an economic value to intelligence, suggesting that the return on investment for water used in AI is orders of magnitude higher than for beef production. A counterargument worth considering is that this assumes a linear relationship between water use and AI capability, ignoring potential diminishing returns or the environmental impact of the energy required to generate those tokens. Nevertheless, the framing forces a difficult question: are we willing to sacrifice the potential for accelerated scientific discovery to save a few gallons of water that could have grown a burger?

The Net-Zero Pivot

The piece concludes by revealing a critical detail often omitted from environmental impact reports: the datacenter is building a water recycling plant to treat municipal wastewater. Patel notes that this facility is expected to "more than exceed the cooling water needs," potentially making the site a net-zero water datacenter. This detail fundamentally alters the debate, moving it from a discussion of consumption to one of circular economy and infrastructure innovation.

"This was nothing more than our lighthearted, researched take on an important issue. Water usage matter, but we think the debate is often overstated and needs to be put into context."

By ending on this note, Patel suggests that the solution to water scarcity isn't to halt progress, but to engineer smarter systems. The argument is effective because it acknowledges the problem while presenting a viable, forward-looking solution, rather than simply denying the issue exists.

Bottom Line

Patel's strongest asset is his ability to use absurdity as a tool for clarity, forcing readers to see the relative scale of water usage in a new light. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on idealized efficiency metrics that may not apply to the broader, less regulated datacenter industry. Readers should watch for how this "token per burger" metric is adopted by policymakers as a standard for evaluating infrastructure projects, potentially shifting the burden of proof onto critics of AI expansion.

"Next time you hear about datacenter water consumption, think about that last hamburger."

The core takeaway is that environmental debates often suffer from a lack of context, and without rigorous, apples-to-apples comparisons, we risk making decisions based on fear rather than fact. Patel's work serves as a necessary corrective, reminding us that the path to a sustainable future may require embracing technology, not rejecting it.
