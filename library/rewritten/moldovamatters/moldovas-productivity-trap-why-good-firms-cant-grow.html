Mihnea Constantinescu, Deputy Governor of the National Bank of Moldova, dismantles the standard narrative that a lack of ambition is holding back the country's economy. Instead, he presents a chilling structural diagnosis: the financial system is not broken by accident, but by a rational response to a broken information architecture. This is not a story about bad loans; it is a story about how the absence of reliable data forces good firms to stay small, trapping a nation in a low-productivity equilibrium. For investors and policymakers watching Eastern Europe, the implication is stark: pouring more capital into the system without fixing the underlying data plumbing will only deepen the debt trap, not the growth story.

The Information Black Hole

Constantinescu argues that the core constraint is not a lack of capital, but a fundamental inability to verify creditworthiness. In developed markets, credit bureaus and transparent financial statements act as the bridge between borrower and lender. In Moldova, that bridge is missing. "In Moldova, these information channels are either broken or non-existent," he writes, noting that banks are forced to rely on what they can physically seize rather than what a business can actually earn. This forces a bifurcation of the market where banks demand real estate collateral and audited accounts, while non-bank credit organizations (OCNs) resort to betting on the character of the business owner.

The result is a system where 90% of small firms rely exclusively on internal financing, growing only as fast as they can save. "The 35-percentage-point gap between medium firms' bank usage and small firms' usage represents one of Eastern Europe's widest disparities," Constantinescu notes, highlighting a structural failure that stifles the "missing middle" of the economy. This framing is powerful because it shifts the blame from the entrepreneur to the ecosystem. However, critics might argue that the author underplays the role of regulatory compliance costs in driving firms toward informality, suggesting that the information gap is partly a symptom of a tax system that penalizes formalization.

The problem is not lack of ambition, it's that firms enter small and stay small, do not innovate and only rarely are able to cover the fixed costs of internationalization.

The Vicious Circle of Informality

The commentary deepens as Constantinescu explores the paradox of formalization. Firms know they need to be formal to access better credit, but the immediate costs of taxes and compliance outweigh the uncertain benefits of a loan that might not materialize. "Formalization brings immediate costs—taxes, compliance, fees—for uncertain benefits," he explains. This creates a rational trap where businesses stay in the shadows because the institutional environment—slow courts, uncertain property rights, and fragile contract enforcement—makes long-term planning impossible. Banks, in turn, refuse to lend long-term because they cannot enforce contracts, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy of short-termism.

The author points out that credit maturities rarely exceed 18 months, a timeframe that makes it impossible for firms to invest in the productivity upgrades needed to grow. "Each actor behaves rationally given their constraints, the outcome is systematically bad for everyone," Constantinescu writes. This is a crucial insight: the stagnation is not a failure of will, but a failure of coordination. The legal system's inability to enforce collateral rights means that even firms with valuable assets cannot use them as leverage. "When recovering collateral takes months or years with unpredictable outcomes, secured lending becomes fiction," he asserts, forcing banks to stick to real estate, the only asset where seizure is somewhat predictable.

Beyond Finance: The Innovation Gap

Even if the credit market were fixed, Constantinescu warns that capital alone cannot solve the productivity puzzle. He contrasts Moldova's export diversification with neighbors like Romania and Lithuania, noting that Moldova introduced merely 12 new export product categories between 2008 and 2023, contributing just $40 per capita. "Providing these firms with more credit will not automatically solve their innovation deficit," he argues, warning that it may simply finance continued production using outdated methods. The real bottleneck is a lack of technical expertise and the organizational capabilities required to enter global value chains.

This is where the piece pivots from diagnosis to a specific industrial strategy. Constantinescu suggests that Moldova should leverage its existing strengths in agriculture and fermentation to build a biotech sector. "These quaint traditions, with the right tech mix can turn into industrial capabilities with direct biotech applications," he writes. The argument is that wine production expertise can be repurposed for biofertilizers and probiotics, allowing firms to learn by doing without the massive upfront costs of sectors like semiconductors. This approach avoids the "lumpy" investment risks that scare off capital in other industries.

The Anchor Firm Solution

The most distinctive part of Constantinescu's proposal is the emphasis on "anchor firms"—large multinational corporations that can act as templates for domestic suppliers. He argues that clusters of firms cannot form spontaneously; they need a large player to stabilize costs and demand. "An anchor firm, typically a multinational with established networks, brings what domestic SMEs cannot build independently: proven processes, quality standards, market access, and credible knowledge about what works," he explains. By meeting the strict standards of a multinational, local suppliers are forced to upgrade their quality control and management systems, effectively outsourcing their learning curve.

The author draws a parallel to Chile's salmon farming industry, which evolved from low-tier processing to global leadership by leveraging foreign collaboration. "The anchor relationship provides stability and knowledge transfer making this progression possible," Constantinescu writes, suggesting that Moldova can follow a similar path from dependence to autonomy. This is a pragmatic, if somewhat optimistic, view of global value chains. A counterargument worth considering is that this model risks creating a form of neo-colonial dependence, where local firms remain forever locked in as low-margin suppliers to foreign giants, never achieving the brand equity or market power to compete independently.

The missing middle is not missing because entrepreneurs lack ambition, it's missing because the past legal and infrastructure setup led to an information architecture that prevented growth.

Bottom Line

Constantinescu's analysis is a masterclass in identifying structural bottlenecks rather than blaming individual actors. His strongest move is reframing the "productivity trap" as a failure of information architecture and legal enforcement, rather than a lack of capital. The biggest vulnerability in his argument, however, is the assumption that attracting anchor firms and building clusters is politically and economically feasible in a fragile institutional environment. The path forward he outlines is clear, but the execution requires a level of institutional stability that Moldova has yet to secure.
