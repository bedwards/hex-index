David Smith exposes a critical flaw in Moscow's latest playbook: the Kremlin's attempt to fuse religious conservatism with Soviet nostalgia is a house of cards built on contradictions that its own allies cannot ignore. By dissecting the 2024–2025 Moldovan election cycle, Smith reveals how Russian influence operations are clumsily stitching together "traditional values" rhetoric with a geopolitical agenda that alienates the very right-wing groups they seek to recruit. This is not just about fake news; it is about a strategic dissonance that offers a roadmap for defenders of democracy to dismantle the narrative from the inside.

The Ideological Mismatch

Smith argues that the Kremlin's strategy relies on a "tactical effort at political manipulation" that is increasingly at odds with the ideological DNA of its Western partners. He points out that figures like Brian Brown of the World Congress of Families are being pulled into a Russian orbit, yet the underlying Russian agenda remains fundamentally incompatible with the American and Romanian religious right. "Putin's Russia did not begin a conversation about 'traditional values' vs 'globalists' / 'Sorosists.' This is language that they have adopted and adapted from existing western discourse," Smith writes. The author's insight here is vital: it reframes Russian aggression not as an export of a unique ideology, but as a cynical hijacking of existing Western culture wars.

The core of the argument rests on the incompatibility between the religious right's deep aversion to socialism and the Kremlin's continued reliance on Soviet-legacy parties in Moldova. Smith notes that while the Moldovan Socialist party has tried to rebrand as "sovereigntists" to align with figures like George Simion, their history creates a fatal friction. "In 2025 this ambiguity became a liability and Socialist leader Igor Dodon openly floated the idea of renaming the party the 'Party of Sovereigntists of Moldova,'" Smith observes. This move, he explains, was a desperate attempt to bridge the gap between Soviet nostalgia and modern right-wing populism, but it ultimately exposed the coalition's fragility.

The false inclusion of 'Antifa' in this mix only underlines how much electoral messaging is being framed in MAGA terms globally.

This observation is particularly sharp. Smith highlights how the Russian narrative becomes so distorted in translation that it loses credibility even among its intended audience. When Moldovan Ambassador Vlad Kulminski told Fox News that Russia was backing a coalition including "Antifa," Smith notes, "This analysis about the Communists and Socialists made little sense in Moldova, but drove a wedge into arguments being made in these FIMI campaigns." The error is not just a slip of the tongue; it is a symptom of a disinformation strategy that prioritizes global resonance over local truth, creating a vulnerability that can be exploited.

The Orthodox Paradox

The most damning evidence Smith presents is the contradiction between the Kremlin's claims of religious persecution in Moldova and the reality on the ground in Russian-controlled territories. The narrative that the Orthodox Church is under assault by a "satanic" government in Chisinau falls apart when contrasted with the situation in Transnistria, where Soviet statues stand alongside new imperial monuments. Smith recounts a conversation with Conrad Franz, who visited Transnistria and found a "perfect religious freedom" claim that clashed with the visible presence of Lenin and Marx statues.

Smith quotes Franz's interview with Transnistrian leader Vadim Krasnoselsky to illustrate the absurdity of the situation: "People accuse us of being a sliver of the Soviet Union. I say no. I say we're actually a sliver of the Russian Empire." Smith uses this to demonstrate that the region is trying to have it both ways, claiming a return to imperial Orthodoxy while maintaining the physical infrastructure of Soviet atheism. This duality is a "major contradiction" that undermines the entire FIMI campaign.

The author then pivots to the actual state of religious freedom, citing interviews with Evangelical missionaries who describe a "New Iron Curtain" where Russia is the most oppressive, followed by Belarus and Transnistria. Smith writes, "The Russian Federation - The 2016 'Yarovaya' law and its subsequent implementation has effectively banned religious freedoms." This historical context is crucial; the Yarovaya law, which equates unregistered religious activity with terrorism, stands in stark contrast to the "persecution" narrative being sold to the West. Smith notes that in Moldova, the situation is vastly different: "Ukraine has the most religious freedom in Europe, after Ukraine is Moldova."

Asked about assertions by the bloggers and podcasters from this report about how Moldova is more oppressive of religious freedom than the Soviet Union he became apoplectic and did not feel that this statement even deserved response.

This reaction from the missionary community is a powerful rebuttal to the Kremlin's propaganda. Smith emphasizes that the only persecution these groups have witnessed in recent years came from local Orthodox priests, not the state. "At least once every 2 weeks someone at our church will pray and give thanks for their freedom," one missionary told him. The author's use of this testimony effectively dismantles the claim that Moldova is a theocratic nightmare, replacing it with a nuanced reality where religious minorities are thriving compared to their counterparts in the Russian sphere.

Critics might argue that the Evangelical community's support for EU integration is driven by political convenience rather than genuine religious freedom, or that the "traditional values" rhetoric still holds sway among the Orthodox majority. However, Smith's evidence suggests that even within the Orthodox community, the narrative of state persecution is failing to gain traction because the facts do not support it. The "narratives a mile wide and an inch deep" strategy is crumbling under the weight of its own inconsistencies.

The Strategic Failure

In the conclusion, Smith synthesizes these contradictions to show why the Kremlin's long-term strategy is flawed. The attempt to align with American figures like JD Vance or the World Congress of Families is hampered by the need to ignore the "tougher questions of religious freedom" in Russia itself. "Much of the content produced in this campaign took place in the months after the elections with the most recent content about the trip coming in December 2025," Smith notes, highlighting the reactive and desperate nature of the campaign.

The author argues that the "Shor-network actors" have built a sophisticated infrastructure of paid activists and AI-generated content, but the message itself is hollow. "Strategically, religious messaging is one component of a larger Russian strategy of ideological alignment across right wing groups in the US and Europe," Smith writes. Yet, this alignment is superficial. The "fertile common ground" of culture war issues is being used to mask a geopolitical agenda that the religious right fundamentally opposes.

Looking forward, these networks - inside and outside Moldova - are likely to play an ever more key role in Kremlin FIMI.

Smith's warning is clear: the networks are adapting, and the "hybrid war of disinformation" will continue. However, the contradictions he has laid out provide a "fertile soil for counter narratives." The author suggests that the best defense is not just to debunk the lies, but to "actively exploit the weaknesses of their narratives." By highlighting the gap between the Kremlin's rhetoric and the reality of Soviet statues in Transnistria or the Yarovaya law in Russia, defenders can turn the disinformation campaign against itself.

Bottom Line

David Smith's most compelling contribution is his ability to expose the internal logic of Russian disinformation as it fractures under the weight of its own contradictions. While the Kremlin's infrastructure for spreading FIMI is sophisticated and evolving, its reliance on a narrative that conflates Soviet nostalgia with Orthodox revival creates an unbridgeable gap with its Western allies. The biggest vulnerability in this strategy is its inability to withstand scrutiny from the very religious communities it claims to protect, a weakness that savvy counter-messaging can and should exploit.
