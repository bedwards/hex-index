Ethan Mollick doesn't just report on a new software update; he documents the moment the barrier between human intent and digital execution effectively dissolves. By demonstrating an AI that can conceive, build, and deploy a functional business in under ninety minutes, the piece forces a reckoning with the idea that "coding" is no longer a prerequisite for "building."

The End of the "Vibe Coding" Era

Mollick opens with a startling experiment: commanding an AI to generate a profitable startup without any human intervention beyond a single prompt. "Claude Code does not have a friendly interface, but you can see how I made a single request, the AI interviewed me about it, it worked for over an hour independently, and then gave me exactly what it asked for, without any discernible errors." This isn't a chatbot guessing at an answer; it is an autonomous agent executing a complex workflow. The author's choice to let the AI generate a "sketchy" site with fake marketing claims serves a specific rhetorical purpose: it proves the tool's technical competence is already ahead of its ethical guardrails.

The core of the argument rests on two specific technological shifts. First, the models have gained the ability to self-correct during long tasks. Second, they are equipped with an "agentic harness"—a suite of tools that allows them to interact with the real world. Mollick writes, "The result of these two factors has led to big leaps in the latest AI tools made by the big AI companies." He backs this with data from METR, an independent tracking organization, noting that the length of tasks AI can complete autonomously is increasing exponentially. This evidence is crucial because it moves the conversation from hype to measurable capability.

"If you are doing real work, however, having the AI forget some of your code as it reads new code becomes a big problem."

Mollick explains that the primary limitation of previous AI models was the "context window"—the amount of information the system can hold in its active memory. To solve this, the new tools employ a "compaction" strategy. When memory fills up, the AI summarizes its progress, clears the window, and loads the summary to continue. Mollick draws a sharp historical parallel here, comparing the process to the amnesiac protagonist in the film Memento, who relies on tattoos to reconstruct his identity and mission upon waking. Just as the character uses physical notes to bridge gaps in memory, the AI uses interim reports to maintain continuity over hours of work. This analogy effectively demystifies a complex technical constraint for a non-technical audience.

Critics might note that relying on "compaction" risks the loss of nuance, as the AI must compress thousands of words of code and conversation into a summary, potentially discarding critical context. However, Mollick suggests that for the vast majority of tasks, this trade-off is the price of sustained autonomy.

The "Skills" Revolution and the New Interface

The article shifts from what the AI can do to how it does it. Mollick introduces the concept of "Skills," which are essentially pre-packaged sets of instructions and tools that the AI can load on demand. He likens this to the scene in The Matrix where the character Neo instantly acquires martial arts proficiency. "Skills solve this problem," Mollick writes. "They are instructions that the AI decides when to use, and they contain not just prompts, but also the sets of tools the AI needs to accomplish a task."

This is a profound shift in the user experience. Instead of a human constantly prompting the AI with new instructions, the AI dynamically selects the right "skill" for the job. Mollick illustrates this with a list of skills released by developer Jesse Vincent, which guide the AI through a full software development lifecycle from brainstorming to testing. The author emphasizes that these skills are written in plain language, meaning non-programmers can theoretically create or modify them.

"It is like when Neo in the Matrix gets martial arts instructions uploaded to his head and acquires a new skill: 'I know kung fu.'"

The piece also highlights the "Model Context Protocol" (MCP), a standard that allows AI agents to access external data sources like scientific papers or financial records. This turns the AI from a text generator into a researcher with direct access to the tools of the trade. Mollick notes, "The result is a very flexible system where a smart generalist AI like Claude Opus 4.5 can apply specialized skills on the fly, use tools as needed, and keep track of what it is doing."

However, the author is careful to ground this excitement in reality. He acknowledges that these tools are currently "built for programmers" and feature interfaces that resemble "something from a 1980s computer lab." This is a critical admission. The power is there, but the accessibility is still gated by technical literacy. Mollick argues that this is a temporary state, suggesting that the "awkwardness" of the current interface will soon give way to more intuitive harnesses for general knowledge workers.

The Human Cost of Automation

The most unsettling part of the commentary is the discussion of risk. Mollick is blunt about the dangers of giving an AI full access to a computer's file system and web browser. "The AI might delete files it shouldn't, execute code with unintended consequences, or access sensitive data in your browser," he warns. He advises users to make backups and use dedicated folders, treating the AI not as a helpful assistant but as an independent actor with the power to cause real damage.

This section serves as a necessary counterweight to the "magic trick" narrative. While the AI can build a game with its own tectonic plates and weather systems, it can also wipe a hard drive. Mollick writes, "Despite these warnings, I am going to give you a very quick intro to Claude Code, but make backups, use a dedicated folder, and don't give it access to anything you can't afford to lose."

The article concludes by quoting Andrej Karpathy, a prominent figure in the AI field, who admits to feeling "decidedly like skill issue" in the face of these new tools. Mollick uses this to frame the coming changes not as a replacement of humans, but as a dramatic refactoring of the profession. "The profession is being dramatically refactored as the bits contributed by the programmer are increasingly sparse and between," Karpathy is quoted as saying. Mollick's analysis suggests that the future belongs to those who can "properly string together" these new capabilities.

"Don't let the awkwardness of the current Claude Code or its specialization for coding fool you. New harnesses that make AI work for other knowledge tasks are coming in the near future, and so are the changes that they will bring."

Critics might argue that this rapid acceleration will lead to a period of significant economic disruption before new roles emerge, a transition that could leave many workers behind. Mollick acknowledges the speed of change but remains optimistic about the "deeper point": that with the right tools, AI is capable of "real, sustained work that actually matters."

Bottom Line

Mollick's piece is a vital early warning and invitation, successfully arguing that the era of AI as a passive chatbot is over, replaced by an era of autonomous agents capable of sustained, complex work. The strongest part of the argument is the concrete demonstration of the "compaction" and "skills" mechanisms, which explain how the leap in capability was achieved rather than just asserting that it happened. The biggest vulnerability remains the current user interface, which limits adoption to the technically savvy, but the trajectory Mollick outlines suggests this barrier is temporary. Readers should watch for the rapid development of non-coding interfaces that will bring these "agentic" capabilities to the general workforce.
