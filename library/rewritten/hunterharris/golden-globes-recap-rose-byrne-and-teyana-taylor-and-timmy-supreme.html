Hunter Harris doesn't just recap the 83rd Golden Globes; they dismantle the ceremony's recent attempt at polished dignity, arguing that the event's chaotic, boozy history is actually its greatest strength. While the awards season often drifts toward sterile reverence, Harris makes the provocative claim that the night's most memorable moments came from its refusal to be serious, celebrating a specific kind of messy, unfiltered Hollywood energy that has largely vanished from modern award shows.

The Triumph of the "Deadbeat" and the Unconventional

Harris opens with a celebration of the night's most surprising victories, focusing on performances that defy the industry's usual preference for tidy, redemptive arcs. They highlight the wins for Rose Byrne and Teyana Taylor, noting that "No one usually votes for the deadbeat moms, but a deadbeat dad can take home every trophy." This observation cuts to the heart of the night's narrative: a rejection of the sanitized, morally upright protagonist in favor of complex, flawed humanity. Harris writes that Byrne's role in If I Had Legs I'd Kick You is "the performance of the year to me, up there with Cate Blanchett in Tár or Frances McDormand in Nomadland," positioning these choices as a corrective to years of overlooking difficult women.

The author's enthusiasm is palpable when describing the wins for Paul Thomas Anderson and the film Hamnet (referenced in the text as Boring Shakespeare In Love before its working title is clarified). Harris notes that Anderson swept the musical or comedy categories, a rare feat that underscores the film's unique tonal balance. This aligns with the historical weight of the Golden Globes, which has often served as a barometer for films that blend drama with a distinct, sometimes subversive, comedic edge, much like the Academy's own shifting tastes over the decades. However, a counterargument worth considering is whether these wins signal a genuine shift in voter demographics or merely a reaction to a particularly weak field in the traditional categories.

"The Golden Globes are not a place to dress old Hollywood!"

The Host and the Return of Chaos

A significant portion of Harris's analysis is dedicated to host Nikki Glaser, whom the author credits with saving the broadcast from its own self-importance. Harris argues that Glaser succeeded because she "actually likes movies and TV as much as she likes to rib them," creating a dynamic where celebrities and corporations are "equal-opportunity joke fodder." This approach stands in stark contrast to the recent trend of hosts who tiptoe around the industry's power structures. Harris points out that Glaser's joke about Leonardo DiCaprio's dating habits was effective precisely because "it is the only thing we know about him," a sharp critique of how the media often reduces complex figures to a single, repetitive narrative.

The author also takes aim at the broadcast's commentary team, specifically criticizing the "constant commentary of Variety's Marc Malkin and Entertainment Tonight host Kevin Frazier." Harris describes their interjections as "a long distraction" that undermined the show's energy. The argument here is that the Globes should embrace its reputation as an "unserious organization," rather than trying to mimic the gravity of the Oscars. Harris writes, "Keep the Golden Globes messy, I say, and keep them boozy and fun," citing images of Sean Penn smoking and Bill Maher frowning as the kind of authentic moments that define the event's charm. This preference for chaos over polish is a bold stance, especially as the industry increasingly tries to sanitize its public image in the wake of various scandals.

The Red Carpet and the Cultural Moment

Harris's critique extends to the visual presentation of the night, specifically the "west coast Met Gala energy of the carpet." The author argues that the event has become too obsessed with high-fashion seriousness, losing the playful spirit that once defined it. Instead, Harris champions the looks of Tessa Thompson, Renate Reinsve, and Teyana Taylor, suggesting that their style choices reflected the night's overall theme of authenticity over aspiration. The author also notes the surprise win for Rhea Seehorn in Pluribus and the continued dominance of Severance in the television categories, though they lament that HBO's Succession and The Pitt have managed to dethrone the Apple TV+ hit in previous years, showing the fluidity of television's power dynamics.

"The Golden Globes are an unserious organization, despite Jay Penske's best efforts."

Bottom Line

Hunter Harris delivers a compelling defense of the Golden Globes' inherent messiness, arguing that the ceremony's attempt to become a polished, serious institution is a fundamental misunderstanding of its brand. The strongest part of the argument is the identification of how the night's most genuine moments came from its refusal to adhere to traditional award show etiquette. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its romanticization of "messiness," which can sometimes mask the very real issues of bias and exclusion that plague the industry. As the Oscars approach, the question remains whether the Globes can sustain this chaotic energy or if the pressure to be taken seriously will inevitably win out.
