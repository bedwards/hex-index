Matt Taibbi's latest piece cuts through the noise of routine political appointments to expose a deeper, more unsettling ideological shift within urban governance and academia. While the surface story involves a controversial New York City housing official, Taibbi argues the real crisis is the institutionalization of a specific racial theory that treats whiteness as a form of property to be dismantled, rather than a historical wrong to be corrected. This is not merely a debate about housing policy; it is an examination of how academic jargon is being weaponized to justify the expropriation of private assets from specific demographic groups.

The Appointment and the Backlash

The narrative begins with the appointment of Cea Weaver by New York City Mayor Zohran Mamdani to lead a new "Office to Protect Tenants." Weaver, a longtime tenant advocate, faced immediate scrutiny after her social media history resurfaced, revealing statements that went far beyond standard tenant rights advocacy. Matt Taibbi writes, "There aren't many things more obnoxious than a housing official threatening to take your home for the crime of owning it." This stark observation frames the controversy not as a policy disagreement, but as a fundamental clash with the concept of private property.

The backlash was swift, driven by citizen journalists who archived Weaver's past comments. Taibbi notes that one critic, Michelle Tandler, compared Weaver to former San Francisco District Attorney Chesa Boudin, calling her "Chesa Boudin times a thousand." The core of the issue lies in Weaver's rhetoric regarding the nature of ownership. As Taibbi paraphrases her viral video comments, she argued for shifting home ownership from an "individualized good" to a "collective good," suggesting that white families, in particular, must accept a "different relationship to property." The administration's response was to double down, with Mayor Mamdani stating, "We made the decision to have Cea Weaver serve as our executive director... to build on the work that she has done to protect tenants across the city."

While some establishment figures like former Deputy Mayor Vicki Been described Weaver as "very savvy, strategic," Taibbi suggests this defense misses the forest for the trees. The argument here is that Weaver's views are not fringe outliers but are representative of a broader, sanctioned intellectual movement. Critics might note that tenant advocates have long argued for stronger rent controls and anti-eviction measures, but Taibbi distinguishes between policy advocacy and the ideological rejection of ownership itself.

The Academic Roots of "Whiteness as Property"

Taibbi traces the lineage of these ideas back to a specific academic text: the 1993 Harvard Law Review essay "Whiteness as Property" by Cheryl Harris. He argues that this essay has become a "sacred screed" in certain university circles, providing the theoretical justification for the views expressed by Weaver and Mamdani. The piece posits that the concept of property rights in America is inextricably linked to the construction of white identity. Matt Taibbi writes, "The academic-sounding rhetorical device is that whiteness is synonymous with property. The simpler translation is that 'whiteness' is just a stand-in for 'white.'"

This framing, Taibbi contends, creates a theology of collective guilt. He explains that the essay argues against the idea of requiring proof of present-day discrimination, suggesting instead that the "property interest in whiteness" implies an inherent guilt for all white people. Taibbi paraphrases the essay's logic: "The inability to see affirmative action as more than a search for the 'blameworthy' among 'innocent' individuals is tied to the inability to see the property interest in whiteness." This shift from individual accountability to collective racial determinism is what Taibbi identifies as the dangerous pivot point.

When Taibbi reached out to Cheryl Harris for clarification, she declined to engage with the historical timeline of slavery and property rights globally, stating she was "not making a global, trans-historic claim about race and property; I was writing about its formation in the context of US history and American law." Taibbi uses this to highlight the insularity of the theory, arguing that by excluding the broader history of property and slavery, the theory creates a self-referential loop that justifies radical action against specific groups.

The Institutional Consequences

The article moves from theory to practice, highlighting how these academic concepts are now influencing federal and local policy. Taibbi points to the involvement of Assistant Attorney General for Civil Rights Harmeet Dhillon, who has flagged these ideas as a federal concern. "We are attacking it where we find it and it's federally funded," Dhillon told the publication. The implication is that taxpayer money is supporting the propagation of ideas that some view as legally and morally untenable.

Taibbi contrasts this modern academic framing with the original intent of the Founders, specifically James Madison, who viewed "conscience" as the "most sacred of all property." He argues that the current movement fundamentally misunderstands the role of government, which was designed to protect private rights rather than dismantle them based on racial identity. As Taibbi puts it, "I understand the tragic fact that so many Americans were long denied such protections, but what sane person sees taking them away now as a solution?"

The piece also touches on the broader cultural impact, noting how these theories have permeated discussions on "white supremacy culture," listing traits like punctuality and individualism as negative. Taibbi suggests this leads to a caricature of non-white people as "half-literate, biased communitarians," a reversal of the very racism these theories claim to fight. A counterargument worth considering is whether Taibbi's dismissal of "Whiteness Studies" overlooks the genuine historical analysis of how laws were constructed to benefit specific racial groups, even if the current application of the theory has become extreme.

The Harris essay in arguing against the idea of requiring proof of present-day discrimination even puts the term "innocent" in quotes, saying: "The inability to see affirmative action as more than a search for the 'blameworthy' among 'innocent' individuals is tied to the inability to see the property interest in whiteness."

Bottom Line

Matt Taibbi's strongest argument is his ability to connect obscure academic theory to tangible, high-stakes political appointments, revealing how "Whiteness as Property" has moved from law review journals to city hall. The piece's greatest vulnerability, however, lies in its potential to oversimplify the motivations of tenant advocates who may seek radical housing reform without necessarily subscribing to the full theological weight of Harris's essay. Readers should watch for how federal authorities, specifically the Department of Justice, proceed with their investigations into these federally funded programs, as the outcome could redefine the boundaries of acceptable discourse in public policy.
