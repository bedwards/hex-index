Matt Taibbi delivers a blistering takedown of how modern media ecosystems reward absurdity, focusing on podcaster Candace Owens and her ability to weaponize conspiracy theories into political reality. The piece is notable not just for its scathing critique of Owens, but for its diagnosis of a media vacuum where belief is scarce, making the public vulnerable to voices that project conviction without evidence. In an era where trust in institutions has collapsed, Taibbi argues that the most dangerous figures are not those with the most facts, but those with the most audacity.

The Architecture of Belief

Taibbi begins by observing the fractured nature of the current information landscape, noting that "No one believes anything, a problem because it's in the nature of people to need to believe something." He suggests that this vacuum has created a prime hunting ground for voices that sell stories tailored to audience fears. The author posits that Owens has mastered this dynamic, becoming a "force of nature" whose influence extends far beyond typical punditry. "No one tops podcaster Candace Owens at this game," Taibbi writes, highlighting her unique capacity to trigger lasting change within the Republican Party.

The commentary draws a sharp parallel to historical precedents of media manipulation. Taibbi invokes the spirit of P.T. Barnum, suggesting that Owens's ability to impact world events with "sheer balls and 100% Grade-A All-American Bullshit" will eventually secure her a place among national legends. This framing is effective because it strips away the veneer of political seriousness and exposes the performance art at the core of her strategy. By comparing her to figures like Colonel Tom Parker, Taibbi underscores that her power lies in spectacle, not substance.

Critics might argue that dismissing a political figure's influence as mere "bullshit" underestimates the genuine grievances of their supporters. However, Taibbi's point is precisely that the mechanism of belief has been hijacked, rendering the content of the grievance secondary to the intensity of the delivery.

The Mechanics of the Conspiracy

The piece dissects specific, outlandish claims made by Owens, such as her theory that the assassination of conservative influencer Charlie Kirk was orchestrated by "bee people." Taibbi notes the absurdity of her evidence: "Owens showed the text messages she sent to friends about an 'underground related to bees' with a September 1st time stamp, to prove she had concerns about 'imminent danger' involving schools nine days before Charlie Kirk was killed at a school." He contrasts this with the reality of the situation, where mundane explanations were available but ignored in favor of a narrative involving "cops, Egyptians, FBI personnel, the French Foreign Legion, mysterious rich people in Orem, an airline services company a few miles from my house in New Jersey, Bibi Netanyahu, and Erica Kirk."

Taibbi highlights the absurdity of Owens's deadpan delivery, stating, "If you gave me a thousand takes I couldn't deliver that theory with a straight face, but Owens is the LeBron James of deadpan." This observation is crucial; it suggests that the success of such theories relies entirely on the performer's confidence, not the plausibility of the claim. The author points out that this certitude is "blowing a hole in the hull of the Republican Party," indicating that the damage is internal and structural.

That ability to impact world events with sheer balls and 100% Grade-A All-American Bullshit will someday give her a place among national legends like P.T. Barnum and Colonel Tom Parker.

The article also touches on Owens's claim that French President Emmanuel Macron and his wife put a "hit" out on her. Taibbi notes that this story has been viewed 45 million times, yet she admits there is "no concrete evidence" behind it. He critiques the media's role in amplifying these claims, noting that "bystanders like Duncan Aviation... was more than happy to finally get a chance to comment," only to reveal that the cars Owens suspected were for "aircraft crew" performing maintenance. This section effectively illustrates how a simple logistical explanation is often discarded for a more dramatic, conspiracy-laden narrative.

The Strategy of Humiliation

Taibbi identifies a recurring tactic in Owens's playbook: luring targets into a humiliating self-defense. He references the political gamesmanship of Lyndon B. Johnson, who allegedly instructed an aide to spread a rumor about an opponent having sex with pigs, knowing that "let's make the sonofabitch deny it" was the goal. Taibbi writes, "Another old media trick is luring a high-profile target into a humiliating self-defense, which she's done by claiming Brigitte Macron is a man." By forcing the Macrons to engage in a "Transvestigation," Owens secures headlines and maintains her relevance, regardless of the truth.

The author also critiques Owens's use of vague sourcing, such as her claim that she is "sensing" that Ben Shapiro is part of a murderous plot. Taibbi notes, "'I sense' is a thing with her, she used it in her 'underground related to bees' speech," and compares it to the telepathic tropes of 1970s entertainment. This reliance on intuition rather than evidence allows her to pivot quickly and avoid accountability. "I believe Charlie Kirk was betrayed by the leadership of Turning Point USA and some of the very people who eulogized him on stage," Taibbi quotes, calling it a "smear, not a question."

Critics might suggest that Owens's tactics are merely a reflection of a broader trend in political discourse where hyperbole is the norm. Yet, Taibbi's analysis suggests that her specific brand of conspiracy-mongering is uniquely damaging because it blurs the line between satire and reality, making it difficult for the public to distinguish between legitimate criticism and fabricated plots.

The Complicity of the Ecosystem

The piece concludes by examining why Owens faces little mainstream condemnation. Taibbi argues that her anti-Israel rhetoric finds support among both the far-right and some left-leaning commentators who are critical of Israel's policies. "She's always been giddy on this topic... but now finding Israel under every manhole is eminently retweetable, and so is she," he writes. This observation highlights a dangerous convergence where legitimate political criticism is co-opted by conspiracy theories, making it harder to address the underlying issues.

Taibbi expresses a desire for a return to sanity, noting, "Simultaneously bonkers and one of the most-followed voices on earth! May we find our way back to duller times." This sentiment underscores the exhaustion many feel in the face of relentless, baseless accusations. The author's frustration is palpable, as he watches the "fig leaf terminology about Zionists" being discarded in favor of direct attacks on Jewish people.

Every media person knows what this is — in every mania there's always a person whose willingness to spread the unconfirmable theories is silently embraced on the fringes — but it usually comes with mainstream condemnation. That's not happening in this case.

Bottom Line

Taibbi's strongest argument is that the current media environment rewards the most audacious liars, creating a feedback loop where truth is irrelevant and spectacle is king. His biggest vulnerability is the assumption that the public can simply "find our way back to duller times," ignoring the structural incentives that keep the noise loud. Readers should watch for how these conspiracy theories continue to reshape political alliances and erode trust in institutions, as the line between satire and reality becomes increasingly blurred.
