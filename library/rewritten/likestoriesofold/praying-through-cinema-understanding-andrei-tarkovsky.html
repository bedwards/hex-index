Tom van der Linden makes a startling claim: that the greatest films are not stories to be solved, but prayers to be felt. In a media landscape obsessed with plot points and algorithmic pacing, he argues that Andrei Tarkovsky used cinema to bypass logic entirely, sculpting time to reveal the spiritual architecture of the human soul. This is not a standard film critique; it is a meditation on why we watch, suggesting that the ultimate function of art is to prepare us for death by making our lives feel significantly longer.

The Architecture of a Spiritual Mirror

Van der Linden begins by dismantling the idea that Tarkovsky was merely a storyteller. Instead, he positions the director as a seeker. "Tarkovsky used his artistic medium in a similar way he wasn't just making films he was exploring a world in search for understanding for a meaning to our existence." This reframing is crucial. It shifts the viewer's burden from intellectual analysis to emotional receptivity. The author argues that Tarkovsky believed art exists to capture an "intangible quality to being human" that logic cannot grasp.

The commentary suggests that Tarkovsky's films, from Ivan's Childhood to Solaris, function as mirrors reflecting our subjective interiority rather than objective reality. "If we had a mirror reflecting our deepest self what would it reveal it was clear to takashi that we wouldn't see our experiences as a linear sequence of events captured objectively and logically into what could be called a plot." This insight lands with force because it explains why Tarkovsky's work feels so disorienting yet deeply familiar. He forces the audience to abandon the comfort of a linear narrative and inhabit the chaotic, non-linear flow of memory and emotion.

"The present slips and vanishes like sand between fingers acquiring material rate only in its recollection."

Critics might note that this intense focus on the subjective risks alienating audiences who crave narrative clarity or social realism. However, Van der Linden counters this by showing how Tarkovsky's "subjective" approach actually creates a more universal connection. By stripping away the artificial constraints of genre, particularly in Solaris, the director reveals that our internal landscapes are far more alien and profound than any sci-fi monster.

Sculpting Time and the Instinct of the Heart

The piece moves into the mechanics of how Tarkovsky achieved this, arguing that the director must trust his own heart above all else. "To succeed in the seemingly impossible task directors must work with striking imagery with that which signifies the distinctively human." Van der Linden illustrates this with specific, haunting examples: a woman watching a burning house while sitting on a well, or a man reaching for a light switch on the wrong side of a wall. These are not just visual tricks; they are emotional truths.

The author posits that the director acts less like a writer and more like a composer, organizing images into a symphony that transcends words. "The director becomes like a composer driven by emotional intuition and using an internal compass to organize sound into music into a symphony that transcends the boundaries of noise." This metaphor is effective because it explains the rhythmic, almost hypnotic quality of Tarkovsky's pacing. It suggests that the "boredom" some critics find in his long takes is actually a necessary space for the audience to feel the weight of time passing.

Yet, this approach requires a leap of faith from both the artist and the viewer. "When art is used to reach some higher poetic truth or hidden beauty one has to believe such a thing exists in the first place." Van der Linden acknowledges the difficulty of this in a modern world that prioritizes material progress over spiritual depth. He points to Stalker as a warning against a society that seeks to "conquer the infinite with the finite," arguing that our technological arrogance has stripped the world of its mystery.

The Ultimate Purpose: Preparing for Death

The argument culminates in a profound statement on the utility of cinema. Van der Linden rejects the notion that art is for propaganda or education. Instead, he quotes Tarkovsky's most radical idea: "The aim of art is to prepare a person for death to plow and Harrow his soul rendering it capable of turning to good."

This is the piece's most striking claim. It suggests that the value of cinema is not in the information it imparts, but in the way it expands our capacity to experience life. "Cinema like no other art has the power to widen enhance and concentrate a person's experience to make it longer significantly longer." By allowing us to live through the experiences of others and to feel the weight of time, art makes us more humble, compassionate, and ultimately more alive.

"The allotted function of art is not as is often assumed to put across ideas to propagate thoughts to serve as example."

A counterargument worth considering is whether this spiritual framing ignores the political and social dimensions of Tarkovsky's work. While Van der Linden touches on the suffering in Andrei Rublev, the focus remains heavily on the internal, spiritual struggle. Some might argue that Tarkovsky's films are also sharp critiques of Soviet oppression, a dimension that gets smoothed over in this purely metaphysical reading. Nevertheless, the author's focus on the universal human condition remains a powerful lens.

Bottom Line

Van der Linden's coverage succeeds because it refuses to treat Tarkovsky as a historical artifact, instead presenting his work as a living, breathing spiritual practice. The strongest part of the argument is the redefinition of cinema as a tool for expanding time rather than telling stories. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its heavy reliance on the viewer's willingness to abandon logic for intuition, which may feel inaccessible to those seeking traditional narrative engagement. For the busy reader, this piece offers a vital reminder: the most valuable use of time is not efficiency, but depth.
