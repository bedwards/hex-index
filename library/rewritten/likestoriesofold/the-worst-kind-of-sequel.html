Tom van der Linden does not simply ask why Gladiator 2 feels like a disappointment; he argues that the film's greatest failure is its refusal to commit to the radical, necessary deconstruction of the original's heroism. While most critics dismiss the sequel as a cash grab, van der Linden identifies a fascinating, albeit botched, ambition: to show that in our current fractured times, righteous rage and righteous action are no longer the same thing.

The Fractured Hero

The core of van der Linden's analysis rests on a bold structural observation. He posits that the sequel attempts to "break Maximus in two," separating the personal vendetta from the political redemption that defined the first film. "Unlike the first movie where Maximus resolving his own Vendetta was perfectly aligned with the Redemption of Rome as a whole in Gladiator 2 it is quickly revealed that these two elements do not go hand in hand," he writes. This is a compelling lens. It suggests the film is trying to tell a story where the hero's internal drive actually harms the very society he seeks to save.

This framing is effective because it moves beyond surface-level plot complaints to examine the thematic DNA of the franchise. Van der Linden argues that the original allowed for "uncritical investment" in Maximus, a trust the sequel deliberately dismantles. "Gladiator 2 however does not allow for that same kind of uncritical investment in fact it is designed to make you actively question those same motivations and actions you were able to take for granted in the first movie," he notes. This is a brave narrative choice, one that reflects a modern skepticism toward traditional heroism. However, critics might argue that by forcing the audience to question the hero's every move without providing a clear alternative moral compass, the film risks alienating viewers rather than challenging them.

The Shadow of Power

Van der Linden extends his psychological analysis to the villains, offering a fresh take on the antagonist's role. He suggests that the twin Emperors and the general Macrinus collectively represent the split personality of the original villain, Commodus. "The twins together reflect only one part of the former Emperor Commodus the other part we find in macus," he explains. This breakdown of the "Shadow archetype" into distinct, competing forces of incompetence and calculated malice adds depth to the film's political landscape.

He describes Macrinus as the embodiment of the "pathological tension" of a man trying to define himself against a father figure while simultaneously relying on that corruption. "Macrinus makes explicit this other arguably more dangerous end of The Shadow he is deliberate clever and methodical," van der Linden writes. This distinction elevates the villainy from a simple power grab to a complex ideological struggle. The film, according to this reading, is a plea to "stop blindly lashing out at the world to instead ReDiscover a true purpose." It is a sophisticated argument that the sequel is trying to be a cautionary tale about the dangers of unexamined anger in a chaotic world.

The Failure of Execution

Despite the strong theoretical framework, van der Linden pivots to the film's fatal flaw: the inability to execute this complex vision without retreading the familiar ground of the original. He argues that the movie "wants to follow in maximus's Footprints retrace the same comfortable steps and general through line of the first movie," creating a jarring friction. The setup of the protagonist, Lucius, suffers from this indecision. Unlike Maximus, whose reputation was established instantly, Lucius is introduced as a "somewhat grumpy farmer" with a vague social standing.

The author points out that the film's decision to hide Lucius's identity as a mystery for the audience was a "baffling decision" that created unnecessary distance. "It makes me wonder what the movie would have been like if we were actually on the same page as lucious right from the beginning," he muses. This critique hits hard because it identifies a missed opportunity for emotional resonance. If the audience had understood Lucius's exile and frustration earlier, his rage might have felt earned rather than performative.

The analysis of the action sequences further highlights this disconnect. While the first arena fight successfully reveals Lucius's "animal instincts" and "Primal Rage," subsequent battles fail to build on this character arc. Van der Linden notes that the rhino fight demonstrates the limitations of instinct, but the naval battle feels disconnected because the film "hadn't established that Lucius has any sea fairing skills whatsoever." The spectacle becomes a series of events rather than a narrative progression. "There's just nothing quite like it which the sequel unfortunately also makes painfully clear," he concludes regarding the lack of integration between action and character development.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's strongest argument is that Gladiator 2 is a film with the right thematic ingredients for a darker, more relevant era but is sabotaged by a lack of confidence in its own premise. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its refusal to fully embrace the deconstruction of heroism it promises, instead retreating to the safe, familiar beats of the original. Readers should watch for how future sequels attempt to balance modern cynicism with the classic structures of the hero's journey, a tension this film struggled to resolve.
