Tom van der Linden does something rare in video game criticism: he treats the medium not as a distraction, but as a philosophical laboratory capable of simulating the very human contradictions that novels can only describe. By weaving Dostoyevsky's Crime and Punishment into the fabric of a review for The Last of Us Part 2, van der Linden argues that the game's most controversial choice—forcing players to inhabit the perspective of the protagonist's murderer—is not a narrative gimmick, but a necessary evolution of the art form.

The Infection of Certainty

The review opens by anchoring the game's themes in a classic literary dream. "Towards the end of Dostoyevsky's classic novel Crime and Punishment the main character Raskolnikov remembers a dream that also perfectly captures the world of The Last of Us," van der Linden writes. He describes a world where microbes of intelligence infect humanity, making everyone "so completely in possession of the truth" that they kill each other in a "senseless spasm." This is the lens through which the author views the first game's conflict. The Fireflies believed their scientific mission justified any cost, while Joel believed his paternal love justified any crime. As van der Linden notes, "The first game introduced us to Joel... a story about rediscovering what it means to love in a hostile world and about the sacrifices we are willing to make to preserve it."

The strength of this framing is that it elevates the plot from a simple survival story to a study of competing moral absolutes. The author suggests that the infected in the game are not just monsters, but a metaphor for ideological possession. "The fungus that infects people's brains feels more analogous to this idea of how we can become so infected by our own convictions that we become enraged blind and ultimately toxic to the world around us." This is a compelling parallel, though critics might argue that equating a fungal pathogen with human ideology risks oversimplifying the biological horror into a mere allegory, potentially diluting the visceral reality of the infection.

Like the plague in Raskolnikov's dream, The Last of Us presents a world where everyone knows with certainty what it means to survive and what it takes to move forward.

The Architecture of Empathy

The core of van der Linden's argument rests on the sequel's structural gamble. The game forces the player to spend half the story as Abby, the woman who killed Joel. "Whereas the first game showed you a world of competing perspectives, The Last of Us Part 2 forces you to live it," the author observes. This is where the commentary shines, acknowledging the friction this creates. Van der Linden admits the experience is not seamless: "It was exhilarating other times it felt like a chore it was painful beautiful it was moving it was frustrating and when it was over I couldn't stop thinking about it."

The author's willingness to sit with this discomfort is what makes the review valuable. He doesn't just praise the game; he interrogates the emotional toll of its design. The narrative setup relies on the lie Joel told Ellie about the hospital. Van der Linden points out that the museum scene, often cited as a peak moment of father-daughter bonding, is actually a "stark reminder that while this may have been the story we had hoped for it is not the one we can have as it was all built on a lie." This reframing turns a beloved memory into a tragic foreshadowing, suggesting that the developers were aware we would enter the sequel with the same blind spot as the characters.

Critics might note that forcing empathy through gameplay mechanics can sometimes feel manipulative rather than organic. However, van der Linden counters this by suggesting the game's failure to be "fully successful" is part of its success as an artistic statement. "It got me thinking a lot about video games as an art form and the potential of this medium that allows the audience to actively participate instead of merely observe." The argument holds weight because it validates the player's frustration as a feature, not a bug.

The City as a Character

Shifting gears, the review applies environmental psychology to the game's level design, citing Kevin Lynch's The Image of the City. Van der Linden argues that the game's post-apocalyptic Seattle works not because it is realistic, but because it is "legible." "Lynch argues that one of the most important properties of a beautiful city is its legibility by this we mean the ease with which its parts can be recognized and can be organized into a coherent pattern." The author explains how the game uses landmarks and paths to guide the player, creating a sense of orientation even in chaos.

This analysis is particularly insightful because it connects the physical navigation of the game world to the psychological state of the characters. "Legibility is about more than enabling someone to find their way around the physical space it is also about forming a broader frame of reference about the meanings of that space." The author notes that in this world, spotting a landmark doesn't promise safety; it signals a new set of obstacles. "Every time it happens increasingly fills you with threat as you come to understand the journey to it is not going to be as straightforward as it appears."

We are not simply observers of this spectacle but are ourselves a part of it.

The author's use of Lynch's theory to explain why the game feels immersive is a standout moment of cross-disciplinary criticism. It moves beyond standard praise of graphics to explain how the game teaches the player to think like a survivor. The distinction between the "heavily urbanized center of the WLF" and the "primitive dwellings of the Seraphites" is used to show how environment shapes culture, a point that resonates with anyone interested in urban planning or sociology.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's review succeeds by refusing to separate the gameplay mechanics from the philosophical weight of the narrative. The strongest part of the argument is the assertion that the game's controversial structure is a deliberate attempt to simulate the human capacity for self-righteous violence. Its biggest vulnerability is the admission that the experience is often "frustrating" and feels like a "chore," which may alienate readers looking for a traditional critique of fun or flow. Ultimately, this piece is essential reading for anyone who wants to understand why The Last of Us Part 2 remains a polarizing masterpiece of interactive storytelling.
