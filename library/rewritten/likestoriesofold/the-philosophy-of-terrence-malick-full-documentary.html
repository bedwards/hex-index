Tom van der Linden makes a startling claim: Terrence Malick isn't just a filmmaker with a poetic style; he is a visual philosopher who successfully did what Martin Heidegger failed to do. While most critics dissect Malick's camera angles or voiceovers, van der Linden argues that the director's true genius lies in translating the abstract, "overly anemic" language of metaphysics into a cinematic experience that forces us to confront the meaning of being. This is not a standard film review; it is a philosophical excavation that suggests Malick's chaotic, script-less approach is actually a deliberate method to wake us from the "unconsciousness" of modern life.

The Hammer and the Bird

The core of van der Linden's argument rests on a specific anecdote that reveals Malick's entire worldview. He recounts how actor John C. Reilly described a massive, coordinated scene in The Thin Red Line that was abruptly halted because Malick spotted a bird. "The entire scene was put on hold to film this one creature," van der Linden notes, using this to illustrate that Malick prioritizes the spontaneous over the staged. This isn't mere distraction; it is a rejection of the "practical disposition" that Heidegger warned against. In our daily lives, we treat the world as a set of tools to be used, a mindset van der Linden calls "the great illness of who we are."

"Heidegger's ambition was to move away from philosophy as an overly anemic and intellectual endeavor... Malick realized this as well and therefore set out to explore a different kind of language."

Van der Linden effectively reframes Malick's later, seemingly disjointed films not as a loss of structure, but as an attempt to break the "technical philosophical language" that traps traditional thinkers. By abandoning scripts, Malick forces the audience to stop looking for a plot and start experiencing the "immediate experience" of existence. Critics might argue that this approach alienates viewers who crave narrative clarity, but van der Linden suggests this discomfort is the point—it is the necessary friction required to wake us up.

The Illness of Utility

The author draws a sharp line between the "world of nature" and the "world of men," specifically the world of technology. He argues that Malick's characters are often trapped in a "practical world" where they function as mere cogs, failing to question the meaning of their actions. This is vividly illustrated through Badlands, where the narrator's "careless light heartedness" about a murder spree exemplifies a life lived without engaging with mortality. Van der Linden writes, "It tells us into a kind of unconsciousness and for Heidegger as well as for Malick this seems to be the great illness of who we are."

This connection between Heidegger's concept of "Dasein" (being there) and Malick's cinema is the piece's strongest intellectual move. The author explains that our default state is not to question existence, but to use it. We only wake up when the "hammer fails." In Malick's films, this failure often comes in the form of "anxiety," a feeling that is "unbound from our world" and defined by an absence. The author posits that Malick's characters are overcome with this anxiety precisely when the noise of the world stops, creating space for a dialogue with their own mortality.

"The true destructive force is not death or temporality it is regret."

This insight reframes the tragedy in Malick's work. It is not that the characters die, but that they realize too late that they were "asleep" and had not seen "what was always right in front of us." Van der Linden suggests that the "snake" motif in these films represents the seduction into this utilitarian sleep, a temptation to live moment by moment without grandeur. While the argument is compelling, it risks painting modernity as inherently evil, overlooking the genuine progress and safety technology provides. However, the author balances this by noting that even in natural settings, the "practical approach" is ingrained in our being, not just a modern invention.

The Openness of Dasein

The commentary culminates in a discussion of freedom and responsibility. Van der Linden explains that Heidegger's "Dasein" can also be translated as "open," meaning humans possess a natural capacity to encounter the world aesthetically, not just practically. Malick's films, the author argues, foreground this freedom as a "responsibility" rather than just a choice. In A Hidden Life, the protagonist's refusal to fight for the Nazis is not a victory, but a testament to this openness in the face of an overwhelming "war machine."

"You think you can make one single man know this man's a small spark of light in an overwhelming darkness."

The author concludes that Malick's work is a call to stop treating the world as a resource and start engaging with it as a mystery. The "temporality" of all things—our homes, our wars, our lives—is not meant to induce grief, but to press questions we must answer. By operating in our default state, we "blind ourselves from the beauty from experiencing things as they are." This is a powerful, if demanding, interpretation that challenges the viewer to do more than just watch.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's analysis succeeds by treating Malick's cinema as a legitimate philosophical text, successfully bridging the gap between Heidegger's dense metaphysics and the visceral experience of film. The strongest part of the argument is the reframing of Malick's "distractions" as essential tools for breaking our utilitarian trance. Its biggest vulnerability is the assumption that all viewers share the capacity for this level of existential reflection, potentially alienating those who seek narrative comfort. Readers should watch for how Malick's upcoming work continues to challenge the boundary between the practical and the poetic.

"The true destructive force is not death or temporality it is regret."
