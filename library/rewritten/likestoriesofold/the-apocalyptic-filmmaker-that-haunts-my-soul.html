Tom van der Linden does not merely review a film; he dismantles the very framework we use to consume stories, arguing that Béla Tarr's cinema forces us to confront the uncomfortable silence left when the "God" of narrative structure dies. While most critics obsess over plot points, van der Linden suggests that the true power of Tarr's work lies in its refusal to provide the satisfaction of resolution, turning the act of watching into an existential endurance test. This is not a guide to understanding a movie, but a manual on how to stop expecting movies to make sense.

The Architecture of Decay

The author opens with a striking juxtaposition: the apocryphal story of Friedrich Nietzsche collapsing in Turin to embrace a beaten horse, contrasted against the unbroken, mechanical reality of Tarr's The Turin Horse. Van der Linden notes that while the historical truth of Nietzsche's breakdown is debatable, the film uses it to ask a different question: "what happened to the horse?" This shift in focus is the piece's first masterstroke, immediately signaling that the commentary will prioritize the victim's experience over the philosopher's legend.

He describes the film's aesthetic as "black and white photography, bleak and almost dystopian environments," where the pacing is so deliberate it feels like "the musical rendition of a factory laboring on and on." This description effectively captures the sensory experience of Tarr's work without getting bogged down in technical film theory. The author argues that these films are not just slow; they are designed to "undermine the most basic assumptions and expectations that most of us have internalized when it comes to comprehending stories."

"The question of what his films are about almost feels less relevant than the issue of why and how we pose that question in the first place."

This observation is the core of van der Linden's argument. He posits that modern film criticism has become homogenized, relying on a "collectively agreed upon framework about how stories function." By stepping outside this framework, Tarr exposes the fragility of our narrative comfort zones. Critics might argue that this approach alienates general audiences who simply want to be entertained, but van der Linden counters that this friction is the point—it forces the viewer to interrogate their own desire for meaning.

The Void of Meaning

Moving beyond style, the commentary tackles the thematic heart of Tarr's filmography: a world defined by entropy and the impossibility of escape. Van der Linden summarizes the director's vision as a depiction of "worlds in decay and about the human beings who seem immobilized within them." He illustrates this with the recurring motif in Damnation of a cable car that moves coal out of a dying village but offers no tickets for the residents to leave. The movement is cruel because it highlights the stasis of the people below.

The author connects this visual stagnation to a deeper philosophical crisis, noting that Tarr's films transcend their Hungarian political context to address "cosmic" problems. He writes, "The entire universe is entropy, the entire universe is damned." This is a bold claim, yet it is supported by the relentless, cyclical nature of the plots where characters take "one step forward, two steps back." The author suggests that Tarr's characters are not suffering from a lack of faith in God, but rather from the realization that God is history—a past tense concept that no longer offers a foundation for morality or identity.

"In Tarr's films, it appears that God is history that this Nietzsche notoriously declared that God is dead."

This interpretation reframes the "death of God" not as a theological event, but as a narrative one. Without a higher power to validate their actions, Tarr's characters are left in a "shattered illusion," vulnerable and alone. The author points out that unlike other existential filmmakers who might appeal to a higher realm, Tarr's characters "experience no apparent crises of faith" because they are already living in the aftermath. They are not searching for meaning; they are surviving its absence.

"His characters do not just live in run down villages and crumbling communities; they inhabit a shattered illusion."

The commentary effectively uses the figure of the "Rambling Man" in The Turin Horse to illustrate this ambiguity. Is he a truth-teller offering philosophical insight, or just a drunk? Van der Linden suggests that Tarr deliberately keeps this question unanswered, forcing the audience to sit with the uncertainty. This undermines the viewer's assumed validity of seeking a single "correct" interpretation, a move that is both frustrating and intellectually liberating.

The End of the Story

The piece concludes by examining how Tarr's final film, The Turin Horse, serves as the ultimate expression of this anti-creation philosophy. The narrative follows six days of a farmer and his daughter as the world slowly collapses around them—the crickets stop, the horse dies, the wind erodes the land, and finally, all light is extinguished. Van der Linden describes this not as a tragedy, but as a "simple anti-creation story" where the routine of survival becomes the only remaining ritual.

He argues that the film's power comes from its refusal to offer a redemptive arc. The characters do not overcome their fate; they simply endure it until the end. This challenges the modern obsession with "victory" in storytelling, where even a tragic ending must feel earned or meaningful. Van der Linden writes that for many, watching Tarr is like "reading Infinite Jest: a rite of passage that gives you a sense of victory in the mere accomplishment of simply having made your way through it."

"The entire universe is entropy, the entire universe is damned."

While the author's analysis is profound, one might argue that it risks romanticizing the suffering of the characters, turning their despair into an aesthetic experience for the viewer. However, van der Linden seems aware of this, emphasizing that the films are not about the "message" but about the "how" and "why" of our engagement with the story. The friction we feel is the evidence that the film is working.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's commentary succeeds by shifting the focus from what Béla Tarr's films mean to how they dismantle our expectations of meaning itself. The strongest part of the argument is the connection between the "death of God" and the collapse of narrative structure, offering a fresh lens through which to view Tarr's notoriously difficult cinema. Its biggest vulnerability is the potential for this analysis to become so abstract that it alienates readers looking for concrete takeaways, but for the patient mind, it provides a rare and necessary challenge to the way we consume stories.
