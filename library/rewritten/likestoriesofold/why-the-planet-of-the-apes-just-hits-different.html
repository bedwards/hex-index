Tom van der Linden argues that the new Planet of the Apes trilogy succeeds not by recycling nostalgia, but by fundamentally reimagining what it means for two intelligent species to collide. While most modern reboots offer "pill imitations" that exploit our sentimental attachment to the past, van der Linden contends these films hold up a mirror to human nature itself, forcing us to confront the fragility of our own uniqueness.

The Failure of Nostalgia and the Rise of the Prequel

van der Linden begins by dismantling the skepticism that greeted the franchise's return. He acknowledges that the 2001 Tim Burton reboot felt like a "tiresome landscape of lackluster IP revivals," yet he finds the 2011 Rise of the Planet of the Apes to be a genuine surprise. The author notes that while the new films use the iconic line "take your stinking paws off me, you damn dirty human," they do so in a way that is "contextualized in such a way that it's not just not cringy but it might actually be the best moment in the entire movie." This distinction is crucial; van der Linden suggests the new trilogy respects the source material's spirit while discarding its narrative baggage. He points out that the original 1968 film and its sequels were often "unhinged," featuring time travel and contradictory timelines that made them a "compelling Time Capsule of the 1970s culture" but poor continuity.

"The new movies are in fact a complete reboot but not so much of the 1968 original but rather of its sequels."

By sidestepping the convoluted logic of the 1970s sequels, the new films create a cohesive origin story. van der Linden observes that the original sequels struggled with internal logic, where a savior named Aldo is later named Caesar, and timelines shift inexplicably. In contrast, the new trilogy offers a grounded, linear progression. This structural clarity allows the films to focus on character rather than plot mechanics. The author praises the motion capture technology, arguing that it was the "absolute right choice" because it avoids the "uncanny valley effect" of the makeup-heavy Burton film. Instead of seeing actors in suits, the audience sees "actual Apes in the sense that they fully look sound and move like real Apes do."

Critics might argue that relying on CGI distances the audience from the raw, tactile reality of practical effects, but van der Linden counters that the immersion is deeper because the movement and behavior are biologically accurate.

Beyond Allegory: A Speculative Collision

The core of van der Linden's analysis lies in his rejection of standard allegorical readings. He dismisses the idea that the apes represent a simple reversal of race or class dynamics, or a straightforward "man versus nature" story. Instead, he posits that the films offer "true speculation." The central question is not about human rights or animal cruelty, but about the emergence of a second intelligent species. As van der Linden writes, "human lies no to me that is the mirror that the new Planet of the Apes trilogy holds up to its audience."

The author draws on the work of primatologist Frans de Waal to support this view, noting that traits once thought unique to humans—morality, complex time perception, empathy—are now documented in animals. The films explore the terrifying implication that if another species is "similar enough to be considered equal by all meaningful measurements but that is also distinct enough to have its own vision of what life is," coexistence becomes nearly impossible. This framing elevates the story from a simple rebellion narrative to a philosophical inquiry into the nature of consciousness.

"The real question that arises out of this premise then out of this collision between two equally intelligent species is what does it reveal about the nature of such a species."

van der Linden highlights Dawn of the Planet of the Apes as the pinnacle of this approach, praising director Matt Reeves for creating a world that feels "distinctively different from ours." The ape society is not a human civilization with fur; it is a unique culture with its own way of life, requiring no electricity or complex infrastructure. This distinction prevents the story from becoming a simple plea for animal rights, which the author notes is the primary takeaway in films like Rise. Instead, the conflict arises from the fundamental incompatibility of two intelligent visions of the world.

"It's not at all an approach that directly reflects an existing relation that can be found in our current Society."

A counterargument worth considering is that by removing specific historical allegories, the films risk becoming too abstract, losing the immediate political punch of the original 1968 film's Vietnam War parallels. However, van der Linden suggests that this abstraction is the film's strength, allowing it to address a more universal and timeless human anxiety: the fear of being displaced by a peer.

Bottom Line

van der Linden's most compelling insight is that the new trilogy's power lies in its refusal to be a simple allegory for human social issues, choosing instead to explore the existential threat of a second intelligent species. While the argument occasionally glosses over the political utility of the original films' direct satire, the focus on "true speculation" offers a fresh, intellectually rigorous lens for viewing the franchise. The strongest takeaway is that these films succeed because they treat the apes not as humans in makeup, but as a genuinely alien intelligence that forces humanity to question its own supremacy.
