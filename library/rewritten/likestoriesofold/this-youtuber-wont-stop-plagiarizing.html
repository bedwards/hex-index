Tom van der Linden does not merely accuse a fellow creator of theft; he exposes a systemic rot in the video essay community where intellectual property is treated as a communal buffet. What makes this piece startling is not the accusation itself, but the meticulous, frame-by-frame forensic audit that proves a pattern of behavior continuing even after direct confrontation. In an era of algorithmic content farming, van der Linden forces a necessary conversation about the line between inspiration and appropriation.

The Anatomy of a Copy

The narrative begins with a personal violation that quickly escalates into a broader indictment. Van der Linden recounts discovering that the channel Archer Green had lifted a specific phrasing from his own script regarding the film Oppenheimer. "These timelines continuously intercut and inform each other," van der Linden writes, noting the eerie precision of the theft. "These timelines continually intercut and inform each other until they ultimately converge in the middle which to us is the end of the movie." The author highlights the absurdity of the situation: the plagiarist even botched the original insight, shifting the perspective from the protagonist to a secondary character, proving the text was copied without comprehension.

The core of the argument rests on the initial attempt at a private resolution. Van der Linden describes reaching out to Archer Green, hoping for a simple acknowledgment. The response was a classic deflection: an apology claiming the notes were used "too heavily" during research, followed by a vague credit in the description. This is where the piece gains its teeth. Van der Linden argues that the issue was never the words themselves, but the uncredited borrowing of the underlying idea. "I'm not asking you to take down the video or anything but I do wish you could have given proper credit," he notes, emphasizing the desire for ethical standards over punitive action.

The issue was not that he didn't change the words enough but rather that he took the idea from me without clearly acknowledging that in the video.

Critics might argue that in the fast-paced world of YouTube, ideas are often in the air, and coincidental similarities happen. However, the evidence presented suggests a deliberate pattern rather than a series of accidents. The author moves from a single incident to a broader scope, revealing that fellow creator Thomas Flight had faced the exact same treatment. When Flight pointed out that Archer Green had ripped his script regarding the Coen Brothers, the response was initially amicable, with the video taken down. Yet, the behavior did not stop.

The Pattern of Recidivism

The most damning section of van der Linden's coverage is the revelation that Archer Green continued to plagiarize immediately after being called out by two prominent creators. The author details a video on Quentin Tarantino that lifted not just text, but the editing style and specific visual analysis from a channel called The Discarded Image. "By this point the Tarantino setpiece is one of his trademarks," van der Linden writes, drawing a parallel to a video by Julian from The Discarded Image. The theft extended to the very structure of the argument, emulating the "dynamic editing" and specific shot breakdowns of the original creator.

The evidence becomes overwhelming when van der Linden examines a video on Indiana Jones. He compares it to a renowned video by Every Frame a Painting and another by Cinema Sticks, finding that Archer Green had lifted entire segments. "This is a single unbroken 90-second take," the author quotes, showing how the phrasing was identical to the source material. The author points out that while borrowing stylistic elements like thumbnail formats is common and often algorithmic, crossing into copying the "entire voice and vibe" is a different matter entirely. "There is of course still a line here between just borrowing popular presentational elements to draw in an audience and fully copying someone's entire voice and Vibe," van der Linden observes.

It's just baffling really that someone after being told that they're plagiarizing would continue to do so.

The author suggests that the problem is not just a lack of knowledge about copyright, but a fundamental misunderstanding of what constitutes plagiarism. Even when Archer Green added a list of references in the description for a video on visual effects, he still lifted specific lines of reasoning and examples from a video by CG Visual Effects. "Visual effects are an illusion you're essentially trying to trick the audience into believing something is there when in reality it isn't," the text reads, a direct lift that was then followed by nearly identical examples regarding Iron Man and Transformers. This suggests that the creator is using references as a shield rather than a tool for genuine scholarship.

The Erosion of Trust

The final thrust of van der Linden's commentary is a critique of the culture that allows this to persist. He argues that the "foggy" etiquette in the YouTube community is being exploited by those who prioritize views over integrity. The author posits that if a creator is willing to steal from friends and peers after being explicitly asked to stop, the problem is not a mistake but a character flaw. "I don't want to be the judge of that," van der Linden admits regarding the extent of the theft, but the weight of the evidence leaves little room for doubt. The piece serves as a warning that the ecosystem of video essays relies on mutual respect, and when that is broken, the quality of discourse suffers for everyone.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's piece is a masterclass in evidence-based accountability, proving that plagiarism in the video essay sphere is often a calculated strategy rather than an innocent error. While the argument relies heavily on the specific actions of one channel, the implication that this behavior is enabled by a lack of clear community standards is its most potent and enduring point. The strongest takeaway is that in an age of content saturation, originality is the only currency that truly matters, and those who devalue it risk the integrity of the entire medium.
