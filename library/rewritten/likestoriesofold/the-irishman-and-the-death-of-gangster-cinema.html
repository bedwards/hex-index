Tom van der Linden makes a provocative claim that Martin Scorsese's The Irishman is not merely a gangster film, but the genre's funeral dirge—a moment where the excitement evaporates, leaving only the grim reality of what remains. While most critics focus on the film's runtime or its controversial digital de-aging, van der Linden argues the movie functions as a "post-gangster epilogue," a nostalgic reckoning by an aging crew reflecting on a life they have finally outgrown.

The Epilogue of a Genre

The core of van der Linden's argument is that The Irishman breaks the mold of the traditional rise-and-fall narrative. He suggests the film feels less like a story about the mob and more like a "solemn look back at the young life" by the filmmakers themselves. "It kind of has this prologue element to it and in some ways this feels like a post gangster epilogue," van der Linden observes. This framing is effective because it shifts the lens from the plot to the meta-narrative: the presence of Robert De Niro, Al Pacino, and Joe Pesci, all in their eighties, transforms the movie into a meditation on mortality rather than a celebration of power.

The author posits that the film acknowledges a shift in perspective that comes with age. "They now have a slightly different perspective on them or at least it sort of acknowledgment that they've grown past it or moved past it," he notes. This is a compelling read of Scorsese's intent. It explains why the film lacks the kinetic energy of Goodfellas or Casino; the characters are not living the dream, they are mourning it. Critics might note that this interpretation risks over-analyzing the director's personal feelings rather than the text itself, yet the casting choices make such a reading almost unavoidable.

"The movie itself has this kind of like long epilogue but it's it kind of itself feels like sort of an epilogue to specifically the ganks the the Scorsese gangster mob films."

The Flawed Mirror of Memory

Van der Linden does not shy away from the film's most divisive technical element: the de-aging technology. He admits that the effect is jarring, particularly in the opening scenes where the transition from old to young "breaks the immersion completely." He writes, "It just looks terrible to be honest it just takes me every time I see that that first cut from Robert De Niro's old face to his post second world war young guy face in the truck like I I it just breaks the immersion completely every time."

However, the commentary takes a nuanced turn when discussing the wider shots where De Niro's body language betrays his actual age. Van der Linden questions whether this is a technical failure or a deliberate artistic choice. "I'm almost wondering to what extent there's a deliberateness to it because as you you've talked about that scene... it's just I mean anyone on that production you know the cameraman the editors in hindsight like they must have known like this isn't this isn't selling like whatever it's supposed to be selling." He entertains the theory that the uncanny valley effect serves the theme: "maybe there was intent behind it... to sort of have the memory be construed a little bit by now having this old man sort of looking back and not being even able to fully Envision or remember his own youth anymore."

This is the piece's most insightful moment. It reframes a widely criticized flaw as a potential feature of the film's psychological realism. The technology fails to make the actors look young, but it succeeds in making them look like old men trying to remember being young. As van der Linden puts it, "it's like the whole physics day kind of yes shrivel a little bit you know the shoulders arm and the neck they get like shorter at least that's clearly what happened with the Nero."

A Legacy of Emptiness

The commentary concludes by placing The Irishman in a broader historical context, comparing it to Sergio Leone's Once Upon a Time in America. Van der Linden argues that both films share a unique trajectory where the protagonist realizes the gangster life was a "waste" only at the very end. "I think that's the kind of movie that we also see in the 2010s with the Wolf of Wall Street which I think is very much the more direct sequel or quote-unquote sequel to Goodfellas in that sense," he contrasts, noting that The Wolf of Wall Street still finds a twisted fun in the chaos, whereas The Irishman does not.

The author highlights the thematic shift from the "fun ride" of earlier gangster films to the hollow reality of the present. "There's that sense that okay maybe even if the roller coaster like crashes at the end at least it was a fun ride and maybe that yeah that leaves that space for the argument that oh maybe that's enough and I think that's the kind of movie that we also see in the 2010s." By contrasting these films, van der Linden solidifies his claim that The Irishman is the definitive end of an era. It strips away the glamour, leaving only the "reality of what exists after this life."

"All of the trappings of the genre the the music the kind of everything about it that makes it exciting and interesting to watch just Falls away and we're left grappling with the reality of what exists after this life."

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's analysis succeeds by treating The Irishman not as a failed attempt at a classic gangster movie, but as a successful deconstruction of the genre itself. His willingness to defend the film's technical flaws as potential thematic choices provides a fresh perspective on a polarizing work. The argument's only vulnerability is its heavy reliance on the assumption that the filmmakers' age and regret are the primary drivers of the film's tone, a reading that may not hold for viewers who simply see a flawed production. Nevertheless, the conclusion that the genre has reached its natural end is a powerful and necessary observation for any student of cinema.
