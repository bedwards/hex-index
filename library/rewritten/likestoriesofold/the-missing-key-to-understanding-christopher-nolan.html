Tom van der Linden makes a startling claim about Christopher Nolan's filmography: the director isn't obsessed with time or puzzles, but with the terrifying realization that we are all imprisoned by our own subjectivity. While most critics get lost in the mechanics of non-chronological storytelling, van der Linden argues that Nolan's true thesis is an existential one, rooted in the philosophy of Søren Kierkegaard. This is a vital distinction for the busy viewer who wants to understand why these blockbusters feel so emotionally heavy despite their sci-fi trappings.

The Illusion of Objective Truth

The piece begins by dismantling the common assumption that Nolan's characters are rational detectives solving logical puzzles. van der Linden writes, "Nolan's movies initially present themselves as puzzles as mysteries to be solved... but the real revelation is not the putting together of the puzzle it is about what they reveal about the characters." This reframing is crucial. It shifts the focus from "how did they do it?" to "why did they believe they could?" The author suggests that characters like Leonard in Memento or Cobb in Inception are not heroes of logic, but victims of their own desperate need for a stable reality.

The argument gains traction when van der Linden connects these cinematic struggles to epistemology rather than ontology. He notes, "The reality we experience is always the one that is constructed in our minds thereby making it inherently limited flawed and vulnerable to manipulation." This is a sophisticated take that explains why Nolan's twists often feel less like magic tricks and more like psychological breakdowns. The characters aren't being outsmarted by external forces; they are being undone by the unreliability of their own perception.

We are not just disengaged minds that think about the world from a distance we exist within it and this he believed is what makes subjectivism not just a question of knowledge but perhaps much more so a matter of identity.

Critics might note that this philosophical lens risks over-intellectualizing what are, at their core, entertainment spectacles. However, the author's evidence from the films themselves—specifically the reliance on totems and notes—supports the idea that Nolan is intentionally highlighting the fragility of human certainty.

The Leap of Faith

The core of van der Linden's analysis lies in the transition from seeking facts to embracing belief. He argues that Nolan's characters fail when they cling to "detached reason" and succeed only when they make a "leap of faith." As the author puts it, "You don't want the truth you make up your own truth." This is not a call to delusion, but an acknowledgment that our values and identities are constructed, not discovered. The most resilient parasite, as van der Linden quotes from Inception, is an idea that sticks because it has emotional significance, not because it is objectively verifiable.

The commentary effectively uses Kierkegaard to explain why the "happy ending" in Nolan's films often feels ambiguous. The victory isn't about solving the mystery; it's about accepting the uncertainty. van der Linden writes, "The victorious characters in nolan's movies demonstrate how we can replace what is objectively uncertain for that which is subjectively true." This is the piece's most compelling insight: the tragedy in Nolan's work stems from the refusal to accept that we define ourselves by our choices, not by the facts we can prove.

It's not who you are underneath it's what you do that defines you but if they come to embrace their ontological uncertainty come to accept that they are subjective beings who are defined by their beliefs instead of by facts.

A counterargument worth considering is that this interpretation might ignore the genuine scientific curiosity Nolan brings to his scripts. The films often spend significant time on the mechanics of time dilation or dream layers, which suggests a genuine interest in objective reality, not just a dismissal of it. Yet, van der Linden's point holds: even the science serves the theme of human limitation.

Bottom Line

van der Linden's strongest contribution is reframing Nolan's complexity as a moral struggle rather than a narrative one, offering a deeper reason to rewatch these films. The argument's vulnerability lies in its heavy reliance on Kierkegaardian philosophy, which may alienate readers looking for a more straightforward film critique. Ultimately, this is a must-read for anyone who wants to move beyond the plot twists and understand the human cost of the questions Nolan asks.
