Tom van der Linden exposes a quiet deception at the heart of almost every story we consume: the illusion that our lives are moving toward a meaningful destination. He argues that the "hero's journey" is not a reflection of reality, but a narrative construct built on determinism and retrospective editing that real life simply cannot support. This matters now because, in an age of curated social media feeds and self-help narratives, we are increasingly forced to view our own chaotic existence through a lens that was designed for fiction, leading to a profound sense of dissonance when the plot doesn't resolve.

The Architecture of the Illusion

van der Linden begins by dissecting the ubiquity of Joseph Campbell's "monomyth," noting that whether it is a grand space opera or a romantic comedy, the structure remains rigidly consistent. He observes that "the hero's journey articulates the transformative process or simply the adventure that a hero embarks upon which leads them outside of their own ordinary world into a new unknown one." This structure is comforting because it promises that every encounter has a purpose and every crisis leads to growth. However, van der Linden points out the fundamental flaw in applying this to our actual days: "whereas the hero's journey is clearly structured our own often feels messy and chaotic."

The author's most incisive observation is that stories are not just different from life; they are fundamentally incompatible with it because they are told backwards. He writes, "all stories are essentially told backwards by their very nature they are told already knowing where it's going to go." This means that the beginning of a story is not a random start, but a carefully curated selection of events that make sense only in light of the ending. In reality, we do not have the luxury of editing out the mundane or the awkward. van der Linden illustrates this with a sharp, almost humorous list of exclusions: "the soldier never sprains his ankle and has to sit out the big battle nor does the boxer get on set diarrhea just before the climactic fight."

We rarely see them spend a lot of time doing uninteresting ordinary of they things like eating folding laundry or filling out tax forms.

This editorial judgment is crucial. It suggests that the "meaning" we crave in our lives is often a byproduct of narrative compression, not an inherent quality of existence. By removing the friction and irrelevance of daily life, stories create a false sense of teleology—the idea that everything is moving toward a specific end. Critics might note that this view risks dismissing the genuine moments of clarity and growth people do experience, suggesting that even in chaos, humans can find agency. Yet, van der Linden's point stands: the structure of the story itself demands a clarity that life rarely offers.

The Trap of Destiny

The piece deepens its analysis by examining why heroes in stories feel "destined" to succeed or fail, a quality van der Linden identifies as the "fundamental difference between stories and reality." He argues that characters like Luke Skywalker or Harry Potter are "never truly ordinary but were already faded to become extraordinary heroes long before that first call to adventure." This determinism ensures that the narrative has a backbone; the hero's lineage or a prophecy guarantees their significance.

van der Linden extends this logic to real-life biographies, asking if we can say of historical figures that they were "destined to go on hero's journeys." He answers with a nuanced "yes," but not because of fate. Instead, he argues that "the very structure of the hero's journey and by extension the act of retelling it that grants them this purpose." When we look back at a billionaire or a revolutionary, we retroactively weave their struggles into a coherent arc, ignoring the sheer luck and randomness that likely played a larger role.

The author highlights how this narrative trap affects modern storytelling, citing films like Blade Runner 2049 and Star Wars: The Last Jedi which attempt to subvert this expectation. In these stories, characters believe they are special, only to discover "he wasn't special at all. You imagined it was you." This subversion is powerful because it forces the audience to confront the uncomfortable truth that "there is no middle ground" between a grand destiny and being an ordinary person.

It is a form of determinism that is present in most stories.

This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from the individual to the narrative form. We feel inadequate not because we lack purpose, but because we are trying to fit a linear, purposeful story onto a non-linear, purposeless reality. The tragedy of Ned Stark in Game of Thrones serves as a counter-example where the structure is inverted, yet van der Linden notes that even tragedy relies on a "fundamental flaw in their character" that drives the plot, reinforcing the idea that stories require a cause-and-effect logic that life often lacks.

The Cost of the Narrative

The commentary concludes by addressing the emotional toll of this dissonance. When we view our lives as stories, we expect a "victorious return" or a "tragic downfall," but reality often offers neither. van der Linden points to the ending of The Lord of the Rings as a rare moment of honesty, where "Frodo finds his way back to the Shire but truly returned he does not." This acknowledges that "sometimes pain is just death," a sentiment that contradicts the redemptive arc of traditional storytelling.

The author's argument is a call to recognize the fictionality of our own self-narratives. By understanding that "the end is always built into the beginning" only in fiction, we can begin to appreciate the messiness of our actual lives without the pressure to force them into a coherent plot. The implication is that finding meaning in life requires accepting the lack of a pre-written script, a difficult but necessary shift in perspective.

Bottom Line

van der Linden's strongest contribution is the identification of retrospective editing as the engine of narrative meaning, a mechanism that creates a false sense of destiny in both fiction and biography. The argument's vulnerability lies in its potential to induce nihilism; while it correctly identifies the structural differences between story and life, it risks underestimating the human capacity to create genuine, non-narrative meaning in the present moment. Readers should watch for how this insight applies to their own self-help consumption, where the pressure to "live a story" often obscures the value of simply living.
