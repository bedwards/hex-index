Tom van der Linden poses a question that cuts through the noise of self-help culture: what happens to our identity when we strip away the narrative structures we use to make sense of our lives? In a world obsessed with the "hero's journey" as a blueprint for success, van der Linden argues that this template often becomes a source of profound disillusionment rather than liberation, leaving us to wonder if we are better off without the stories that define us.

The Trap of the Heroic Template

Van der Linden begins by dismantling the modern obsession with the heroic adventure. He observes that we have twisted the original meaning of the hero's journey, turning it into a "predictive template" that forces us to compete for ever more exciting lives. This framing is crucial because it shifts the blame for life's disappointments from external circumstances to our internal expectations. "It became a pressure for people to compete with each other for ever more exciting adventures," van der Linden writes, noting how this leads to serious disillusionment for those whose lives do not match the script.

The author's analysis here is sharp, particularly in how he identifies the mechanism of failure. When reality refuses to conform to the three-act structure, we feel like failures rather than recognizing that the structure itself is flawed. He extends this critique to collective identities, noting how groups use these narratives to "forge their own cosmic destiny" while victimizing others in the process. This is a vital distinction; the hero's journey is not just a personal coping mechanism but a political tool that can justify violence and exclusion.

"We are not better off without heroic adventures without those structures that only simplify a complex reality but is that even a possibility is storytelling not the foundation of who we are?"

Critics might argue that van der Linden underestimates the psychological necessity of narrative. Humans are hardwired to seek patterns, and without some structure, life might feel truly chaotic rather than just unscripted. However, his point is not that we should stop storytelling, but that we should be wary of rigid templates that demand a specific outcome.

The Paradox of Experience and Retelling

Moving into the philosophical core of the piece, van der Linden draws on existentialists like Jean-Paul Sartre and Søren Kierkegaard to explore the gap between living and understanding. He highlights Sartre's insight from Nausea: the content of an adventure does not matter; only the form does. We turn events into adventures by experiencing them as such, transforming the familiar into the unfamiliar. Van der Linden paraphrases this beautifully, arguing that an adventure is simply the process of "familiarizing yourself with the unfamiliar," which is inherently transformative.

The author then introduces a critical tension: the difference between the raw experience of time and the structured time of a story. In daily life, time is predictable; in an adventure, it is uncertain and urgent. Yet, an experience only becomes an adventure once it is completed and recounted. "Life must be lived forwards but can only be understood backwards," van der Linden quotes Kierkegaard, emphasizing that meaning is always retrospective. This creates a paradox for the living individual: we are both the author and the character, trying to write a story while still in the middle of the plot.

"The ones admirable ambition of a partner might be recontextualized as selfish egoism or a relationship falls apart into tragedy when the initial failure might become a stepping stone to a more meaningful victory."

This observation is particularly potent for anyone navigating relationships or career changes. It suggests that our current struggles are not failures of the story, but merely unresolved chapters. The danger, as van der Linden points out, is that we often mistake the potential for adventure with the reality of a resolved narrative. We believe we are at the start of a grand adventure, only for it to fade into the mundane, or we make tiny decisions that seem insignificant until decades later.

The Illusion of the Perfect Moment

One of the most compelling sections of the commentary is van der Linden's critique of "perfect moments" in storytelling. In fiction, conflicts are resolved by singular, decisive events—a villain's defeat, a grand kiss, a plot twist that ties everything together. In reality, van der Linden argues, such moments are rare. "There is often missing from our adventures as opposed to adventures in stories is what jean-paul sartre called perfect moments moments that mark definitive resolutions in our stories." He uses the example of the current health crisis, noting that it will likely end not with a single victory but with a "slow almost inconspicuous return to a state that can be called ordinary."

This lack of closure is where the friction between reality and story is most painful. We crave the resolution that films provide, but life offers ambiguity. Van der Linden references the film Synecdoche, New York to illustrate this, quoting a character who says, "everything is more complicated than you think you only see 1/10 of what is true." The author uses this to suggest that our desire for a fully resolved story is a "temporary veil of wisdom" over a fundamentally unknowable reality.

"Are we doomed to a state of perpetual storytelling of continuous narration without closure?"

A counterargument worth considering is that the human mind is capable of finding closure even in ambiguity. We create our own "perfect moments" through ritual and memory, even if they aren't as dramatic as a movie climax. Yet, van der Linden's point stands: the expectation of a cinematic resolution is what causes the most distress when life fails to deliver it.

The Search for Cosmic Meaning

Finally, van der Linden tackles the metaphysical question: why do we tell stories? Is the hero's journey a human invention or a reflection of a cosmic order? He explores the religious impulse to believe in a "grand cosmic story" where a higher force provides intention and structure. He references Joseph Campbell's advice to "follow our bliss," suggesting that this path implies a pre-existing track laid out for us. "If you follow your bliss he wrote you put yourself on the kind of track that has been there on the Wow waiting for you."

However, van der Linden rejects this as a satisfying solution for himself. He admits that even if a cosmic plot exists, he feels like a "replicant in the original Blade Runner chasing a creator for answers I'm not going to get." The fear is that following a pre-ordained path is just an illusion of choice, leaving the individual with a life that feels like a "mere collection of moments incoherent unresolved waiting to be lost in time."

"It is therefore that jean-paul sartre proposed a sort of middle ground between the nihilism of a life without the structures of stories and the frustration of a life that's too structured."

This middle ground is the piece's most hopeful conclusion. We cannot live without stories, but we must not let them become prisons. We must accept that our lives are ongoing narratives that resist final definition, finding meaning in the act of living rather than the act of finishing.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's exploration of storytelling as identity is a necessary corrective to the self-help industry's obsession with the hero's journey. His strongest argument is that the frustration we feel with our lives often stems from forcing complex realities into rigid narrative structures. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on existential philosophy, which may feel abstract to readers seeking practical steps, but its core insight—that we must embrace the unresolved nature of life—is a powerful and enduring truth.

"Life must be lived forwards but can only be understood backwards."

Readers should watch for the next step in this conversation: how do we construct narratives that are flexible enough to accommodate the messiness of reality without collapsing into nihilism? Van der Linden hints at the answer, but the work of building that middle ground remains ours to do.
