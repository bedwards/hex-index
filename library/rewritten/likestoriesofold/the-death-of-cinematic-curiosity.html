Tom van der Linden makes a provocative claim that will sting anyone who has ever defended a blockbuster: our obsession with "relatability" isn't just a harmless preference, it is actively killing our capacity for cinematic curiosity. He argues that the film industry and its audience have entered a feedback loop where movies are engineered to be emotional comfort blankets, stripping away the friction and specificity required for genuine artistic engagement. In an era where attention is the most valuable currency, van der Linden suggests we are trading the transformative power of cinema for the safety of seeing ourselves reflected in every character, regardless of how hollow the reflection may be.

The Commodification of Connection

The core of van der Linden's argument rests on the shift from likability to relatability as the primary metric for success. He posits that this is not an organic evolution of taste but a calculated business strategy. "Relatability is the chief psychological lubricant that glides you thoughtlessly down the curated endless scroll of your feeds," he writes, citing a New York Times piece to anchor his observation. This framing is sharp; it moves the conversation away from "bad taste" and toward a systemic exploitation of human psychology. By treating relatability as a commodity, studios can guarantee attention, but at the cost of depth.

van der Linden illustrates this with the massive success of Barbie, noting how the film's cultural resonance relied on generalized themes rather than specific, challenging narratives. He observes that stories are increasingly "stripped from specificity and intimacy in favor of generalized themes and references that are designed to relate to as many people as possible." This creates a paradox where representation feels inclusive on the surface but remains superficial in execution. As he puts it, studios are "carefully calibrating the exact right balance between being specific enough to get the accolades for representation but not so much that they actually have to sacrifice marketability." This is a damning critique of modern "inclusive" storytelling, suggesting that the push for diversity has sometimes been co-opted to produce bland, market-safe products that offer the appearance of progress without the substance of risk.

Critics might argue that accessibility is a virtue, and that films serving as a "blank canvas" for audience projection allow for a broader, more democratic form of emotional connection. However, van der Linden counters that this connection is increasingly manufactured. He notes that even independent successes like Past Lives or All of Us Strangers are being criticized for relying on broad concepts of love and loss rather than deep character specificity, turning profound stories into avatars for the viewer's own feelings.

Relatability has become the natural next step from likability, transforming a positive attitude into an intimate relationship that demands nothing more than affirmation.

The "Literally Me" Trap

The commentary then pivots to the audience's role in this decline, specifically examining the "literally me" meme culture. van der Linden identifies a disturbing trend where viewers, particularly men, categorize films like Joker or Fight Club not as complex studies of alienation, but as mirrors for their own grievances. "The unifying quality here it seems is a shared frustration with and subsequent feeling of alienation from our present society," he writes. The danger, he argues, is that this categorization stops engagement dead in its tracks. Instead of examining the root causes of a character's actions, the audience uses the film as a "comfort blanket" to validate their existing worldview.

This behavior fosters a culture of "affirmative complacency," where the goal is to feel understood rather than challenged. van der Linden writes that these memes are "merely about relishing a relatable emotion at the surface... maintaining a willful ignorance towards everything else." This is a crucial distinction: it is not that the audience is incapable of deep thought, but that the current cinematic ecosystem rewards shallow identification. The result is a viewer who demands that art confirm their biases rather than expand their horizons.

The Rise of Moralistic Gatekeeping

Finally, van der Linden connects this hunger for comfort to a toxic rise in moralistic gatekeeping. When movies are judged solely on whether they align with the viewer's personal values, the potential for conflict and dialogue evaporates. He argues that we have moved from asking "Is this well-made?" to "Is this problematic?" or "Does this reflect what I believe to be good and true?" This shift treats critical discussion as an attack rather than an invitation to broaden one's perspective. He notes that the term "woke" has become a "reflexive response against any form of diversity," often used without "any actual argumentative substance."

This framing is particularly effective because it highlights how both sides of the cultural divide are trapped in the same cycle of seeking affirmation. Whether dismissing a film for being "too woke" or praising it for being "relatable," the audience is prioritizing moral alignment over artistic merit. van der Linden warns that this conditioning makes it "especially difficult" to embrace discomfort, which is essential for art to function as a catalyst for growth. He writes, "The gradual conditioning towards relatability and towards desiring affirmation also ends to our more essential values and beliefs."

We have stopped welcoming critical discussion as an invitation to broaden your horizon and now treat it as an attack that has to be deflected.

Critics might note that van der Linden's defense of "conflict" and "transgression" could inadvertently dismiss legitimate concerns about harmful stereotypes or the real-world impact of regressive media. There is a fine line between challenging an audience and alienating them with harmful tropes. However, his point remains that the refusal to engage with difficult ideas in favor of moral posturing is the greater threat to the medium.

Bottom Line

Tom van der Linden's diagnosis of the "death of cinematic curiosity" is a necessary, if uncomfortable, intervention in a culture obsessed with validation. His strongest argument lies in exposing how the business of "relatability" has turned cinema into a tool for emotional self-soothing rather than intellectual expansion. The piece's vulnerability is its potential to alienate viewers who feel that their desire for representation is being dismissed as shallow, yet the core warning holds: when we only watch movies that tell us who we already are, we cease to learn who we could become.
