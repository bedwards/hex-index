Alison Roman transforms the mundane act of feeding an infant into a profound meditation on care, patience, and the quiet rebellion of domestic life. In a world often obsessed with efficiency and optimization, Roman argues that the true value of parenting lies not in the nutritional data, but in the messy, unquantifiable moments of connection found in a bowl of purple oatmeal.

The Architecture of Care

Roman opens by dismantling the rigid expectations of modern parenting, suggesting that a child's palate is a mirror of their own. She writes, "I can't expect you to love food I myself don't love which is why I'm not surprised that you don't seem to care for avocados. You already have good taste." This reframing is immediate and effective; it shifts the burden of performance from the child to the parent, validating the natural, often frustrating, process of discovery. The author's choice to detail the constipation caused by sweet potatoes or the refusal of beets serves not as a complaint, but as evidence of a relationship built on honest feedback rather than forced compliance.

The narrative moves through a series of culinary experiments that double as lessons in love. Roman describes the physical act of feeding with a tenderness that feels almost radical in its specificity. "Now, in an act of love and service, I bite each individual berry open for you, peeling the skin away (choking hazard?) so you can then (very forcefully) nibble on the 'whole' (skinless, slightly crushed) berry with your gums, it's quite cute." Here, the commentary highlights the author's willingness to embrace risk and imperfection. By manually peeling berries, Roman rejects the sterile safety of pre-processed baby food in favor of a tactile, albeit messy, intimacy. This is not just about nutrition; it is about the physical labor of affection.

"I've now opened a small, bespoke restaurant just for you where the food is slightly under-seasoned and extremely soft. This restaurant is open two to three times a day for five to twenty minutes at a time."

This metaphor of the "bespoke restaurant" is the piece's most striking image. It elevates the repetitive, exhausting task of feeding a baby into a high-end, exclusive service. Roman acknowledges the absurdity of the situation—"I would probably not eat at this restaurant on account of all the purees and under-salting"—yet she leans into the role with a sense of duty and pride. Critics might argue that this romanticization of the labor-intensive nature of infant care ignores the reality of parental burnout, but Roman's tone remains grounded in the immediate joy of the moment rather than the long-term fatigue.

The Blending of Generations

As the piece progresses, Roman shifts from the isolation of the baby's diet to the shared experience of the family table. The creation of the spiced zucchini soup marks a turning point where the food becomes a bridge between the infant and the adults. "This is the first thing I make for you that's also for us, and that feels important." This sentence carries significant weight, signaling a move from the singular focus on the child to a reintegration of the family unit.

The recipe itself is a testament to Roman's philosophy of flexibility and intuition over rigid adherence to rules. She notes, "All to say, take what you like and leave what you don't, though I do recommend leaving the onion, zucchini, lentils (or rice) and broth. Without which you might not have any soup at all." This advice is a gentle rebuke to the algorithmic precision often demanded by modern parenting guides. Roman trusts the cook's instinct, suggesting that the essence of a meal lies in its core components, not in the exact measurements of spices.

The author's decision to avoid a blender, preferring to cook ingredients until they "nearly melt into nothing," reinforces the theme of patience. In an era of instant gratification, Roman champions the slow simmer. "I love to avoid a blender when possible, so I feel blessed." This small detail underscores the value she places on the process itself, finding joy in the transformation of raw ingredients into something nourishing through time and heat.

The Bottom Line

Roman's essay succeeds because it refuses to treat parenting as a problem to be solved, but rather as a practice to be inhabited. The strongest part of her argument is the assertion that the messiness of feeding a child—the stained clothes, the choking hazards, the under-seasoned soups—is where the real connection happens. Its vulnerability lies in its reliance on a specific kind of leisure time and resources that not all parents possess, a limitation the author hints at but does not fully address. Ultimately, the piece is a reminder that in the rush to optimize our lives, we often forget the profound beauty of simply sitting down to eat together.
