Most films about Jesus sanitize the divine, turning him into a serene, untouchable figure bathed in soft light. Brandon Grafius challenges this cinematic tradition by dissecting a new horror film that dares to ask: what if the Messiah was terrifyingly human, and perhaps, a little dangerous?

The Horror of the Human Divine

Grafius opens by noting that while Christian doctrine insists on Jesus being both fully divine and fully human, cinema almost always "place[s] a heavy thumb on the scale of divinity." He contrasts the ethereal Jesus of Cecil B. DeMille's 1927 classic with Lotfy Nathan's 2025 film, The Carpenter's Son, which filters the story through a lens of dread. The film focuses on a fifteen-year-old boy, known only as "The Boy," who is unsure of his own nature while his father, played by Nicolas Cage, fears he might be "of the angels" or a "spawn of the devil."

This framing is a deliberate departure from the sanitized versions of scripture. Grafius argues that the film attempts to fill the narrative gaps left by the canonical Gospels, which offer very little detail on Jesus' adolescence beyond a single incident at age twelve. However, he points out a critical flaw in the film's premise: it claims to be based on the Infancy Gospel of Thomas, a second-century text, yet fundamentally misunderstands its source material. In the ancient text, the young Jesus is not a confused teen being tempted by an external force; he is a "bad seed who runs around cursing people who upset him." Grafius writes, "The Infancy Gospel leaves us with the unsettling idea that Jesus might have been a divine being whose wrath could outweigh his benevolence." By removing the boy's inherent capacity for malice and replacing it with a classic Satanic tempter, the film domesticates the very horror the ancient text sought to explore.

"Through centuries of softening the edges of these stories, we've domesticated Jesus to the point where it's impossible for us to imagine him as frightening."

The Ghost in the Machine

The commentary shifts to the film's reliance on established horror tropes, drawing parallels to Martin Scorsese's The Last Temptation of Christ and Mel Gibson's The Passion of the Christ. Grafius notes that while Scorsese's film faced outrage for portraying a sexual, conflicted Jesus, its true subversion was the idea of a Messiah who might consider "throwing off the weight of his divinity." The Carpenter's Son mimics the visual language of Gibson's work, particularly the dark, blue-hued scenes in the Garden of Gethsemane where Satan tempts Jesus. Yet, Grafius observes that in Gibson's film, the temptation feels hollow because the character is too superhuman to ever truly falter.

In The Carpenter's Son, the stakes are lowered further. The boy's miracles are muted—a crushed grasshopper is resurrected, and his "deviant behavior" amounts to teenage voyeurism. Grafius suggests this is a missed opportunity. The film presents a world where the only thing to fear is the devil, not the boy himself. This stands in stark contrast to the biblical accounts where Jesus inspires genuine terror, such as when his disciples mistake him for a ghost walking on water or the women fleeing the empty tomb in Mark's original ending. As Grafius puts it, "The Gospel accounts suggest otherwise," implying that the divine presence is inherently unsettling to the human mind. Critics might note that the film's choice to make the boy relatable rather than terrifying serves a modern audience's comfort, but in doing so, it loses the theological tension that makes the source material so compelling.

A Surface-Level Exploration

Ultimately, Grafius concludes that while the film succeeds as a "late-night, unsettling mood piece," it lacks the gravity it aspires to. The performance of FKA Twigs as the mother fades into the background, serving only as a pious counterweight to the father's melodramatic doubt. The film's central question—will the boy follow his father's advice or the Stranger's path?—is resolved too neatly to provoke deep thought. Grafius writes, "It's easy to wonder if there's a deeper theological horror in this story, waiting to be uncovered." Instead, the audience is left with a story where the Boy resists temptation and is ready to enter the familiar Gospel accounts, effectively becoming a bridge to the known narrative rather than a challenge to it.

Bottom Line

Grafius delivers a sharp critique of how modern cinema sanitizes the divine, arguing that The Carpenter's Son misses its chance to explore the frightening reality of a human Jesus by retreating into standard horror tropes. The piece's greatest strength is its reminder that the original texts were willing to depict a Messiah whose power could be as terrifying as it was benevolent. The film's biggest vulnerability is its refusal to let the audience sit with that discomfort, choosing instead to reassure them that the only monster in the room is the devil.

"Maybe we're supposed to be afraid of Jesus — and maybe the unknown author of the Infancy Gospel was simply tugging on some loose threads already present in the Gospel accounts.""
