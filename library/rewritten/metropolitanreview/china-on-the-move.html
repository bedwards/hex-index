Most coverage of China today is trapped in a reactive loop, viewing every policy shift through the lens of American political drama. Kiren Gopal breaks this cycle by turning away from the noise of the White House and toward the quiet, relentless machinery of Chinese statecraft, using a new film and a new book to reveal a nation that is building its future even as it grapples with its past.

Beyond the Vortex

Gopal argues that contemporary discourse remains "trapped in what might be called an all-consuming Trump vortex," a framing that forces us to see global shifts only as reactions to American leadership. Instead, he directs our attention to two works that offer a wider lens: Jia Zhangke's film Caught by the Tides and Dan Wang's Breakneck: China's Quest to Engineer the Future. These works, he suggests, capture a country that has undergone a "rapid shift over the course of the past several decades," independent of US political cycles. This is a vital correction for a reader trying to understand the actual trajectory of the world's second-largest economy.

The author contrasts the American experience of "material decay" with China's "relentless focus on the 'real' economy." While the US has become a "lawyerly society" obsessed with procedure, Wang's account depicts Beijing's Politburo as viewing "production was a noble deed to advance communism, while consumption was a despicable act of capitalism." This dichotomy is striking, though it risks oversimplifying the complex consumer culture that now thrives in Shanghai and Shenzhen. Yet, the core observation holds: the Chinese state remains obsessed with the tangible act of building.

"The greatest trick that the Communist Party ever pulled off is masquerading as leftist."

Gopal uses Wang's insight to highlight the ideological flexibility of the Chinese government. The state can pivot from strict Marxist-Leninist production goals to embracing "Sinofuturism" and high-tech spectacle, such as the recent drone show welcoming Steph Curry. This adaptability allows the regime to maintain momentum even as the nature of its economy evolves. However, this flexibility comes at a steep human cost that Gopal does not shy away from.

The Cost of Speed

The narrative of "breakneck" growth is not without its fissures. Gopal points to the "imploded real estate bubble" and the legacy of "tofu houses"—a grim shorthand for the collapsed schools in Sichuan following the 2008 earthquake. These structures, built with corner-cutting efficiency, serve as a stark reminder that the drive for speed often sacrifices safety. The reference to the 2008 Sichuan earthquake is not merely historical context; it is a warning that the "breakneck growth encouraged" a culture where human life was secondary to the metric of completion.

Wang's book details the "harsh systems of social control" that underpin this engineering state, from the forced sterilizations of the One Child Policy to the rigid lockdowns of the zero-COVID era. Gopal recounts a particularly harrowing story of a pregnant woman denied hospital access due to a missing PCR test, who subsequently miscarried outside the facility. "The story went viral until censors deleted it," Gopal notes, illustrating the state's capacity to erase its own failures. This is where the "engineering of the soul," a phrase Wang attributes to Stalinist influence and Xi Jinping, becomes terrifyingly literal.

Critics might argue that Gopal's focus on repression overlooks the genuine improvements in living standards for hundreds of millions of Chinese citizens. Yet, the author balances this by showing that even the "most fashionable thing you can do in downtown New York these days is drink a beer on Canal street, crouched on a low plastic stool," mimicking the habits of old Chinese men. This cultural osmosis suggests that while the US may be falling behind in infrastructure, it is also adopting the aesthetic of a society that has prioritized collective order over individual liberty.

"No two peoples are more alike than Americans and Chinese... both countries are full of hustlers peddling shortcuts, especially to health and to wealth."

This observation by Wang, highlighted by Gopal, serves as a bridge between the two nations. It suggests that the "hustle" is a universal human trait, even if the mechanisms of control differ. The US, with its "lawyerly" constraints, is now attempting to revive its industrial capacity through the Abundance movement and the Biden administration's industrial policy. Yet, as Gopal notes, "the U.S. has already fallen too far behind, in spite of recent piecemeal efforts."

The Human Element

Ultimately, Gopal's commentary converges on the human cost of these grand projects. Jia Zhangke's films, which often follow characters "making similar journeys and not always finding the future they sought," provide the emotional counterweight to Wang's technocratic analysis. The director's own experience of being blacklisted for his debut film, Xiao Wu, and later navigating "censors in China," mirrors the broader tension between state ambition and individual freedom.

"As a filmmaker, the clearest issues have to do with censors in China, and how I can maintain a certain level of freedom within the limitations and restrictions imposed upon me."

This quote captures the essence of the Chinese artist's dilemma: the necessity of "coloring within the lines" to survive and create. Gopal suggests that even in a society of "worsening political repression," innovation persists in areas like solar power and electric vehicles. However, the "dislocation" felt by the characters in Caught by the Tides—wandering through a "gleaming hypermodern China" yet feeling unchanged—speaks to a deeper truth. The material progress is undeniable, but the "human connection" remains the only force that confers meaning.

Bottom Line

Gopal's piece succeeds in reframing the China conversation away from American political theater and toward the profound, often brutal, reality of state-led modernization. The strongest element is the juxtaposition of Wang's "engineering state" with Jia's humanist cinema, which together reveal the full cost of the "breakneck" rise. The argument's vulnerability lies in its potential to understate the agency of Chinese citizens who actively shape their own futures despite the constraints, but the overall verdict is clear: the world is watching a nation that builds at a speed the US can no longer match, even as it pays a heavy price for that momentum.
