Mo_Diggs delivers a startling diagnosis for why the internet, despite two decades of evolution, has failed to produce a single great narrative work. While most critics blame algorithms or smartphones, the author traces the rot back to the 1980s introduction of the remote control and the video game console, arguing that we traded the discipline of storytelling for the addiction of "flow." This is not a nostalgic rant; it is a structural critique of how our attention was engineered to fracture long before the first AI chatbot appeared.

The Dream Economy

The piece opens by contrasting the author's hopeful 2006 vision of the web as a new literary frontier with the current reality of a "post-literate society." Mo_Diggs writes, "We are about to become a post-literate society. AI is triggering delusions. What's more, people don't seem to have lives to live, let alone write about." This observation cuts through the noise of current tech debates to identify a deeper cultural hollowness. The author argues that the internet has not evolved into a medium for stories but has instead become a "dream economy" that feeds on repressed desires and a dull state of consciousness.

Drawing on Heather Parry's analysis of modern fiction, Mo_Diggs notes that online characters speak in a "detached, ironic style where you can't actually figure out what anyone feels about anything." The result is a lack of tension and propulsion; as the author puts it, "You've had lots of discussions in different bars... but no plot is to be found anywhere." This framing is effective because it shifts the blame from individual users to the medium itself. The internet does not just reflect our fragmentation; it actively prevents the sustained attention required to build a coherent plot.

Instead of a thriving life of the mind, everyone seems stuck with a life in the mind.

Critics might argue that this view romanticizes the past, ignoring that great literature has always been a minority pursuit. However, Mo_Diggs makes a compelling case that the potential for narrative was uniquely high in the early web, a potential that has since been strangled by the very infrastructure designed to keep us online.

The Roots of Collapse

The author's most distinctive move is locating the origin of "narrative collapse" not in the smartphone era, but in the 1980s with the rise of remote controls and video games. Mo_Diggs explains that while television introduced "flow," it was the ability to change channels instantly that broke the narrative spell. "Remote controls turned everyone into emperors in their own living room coliseums," the author writes, noting that if a show required patience, viewers would simply click away.

This historical pivot is crucial. The author connects this shift to the emergence of "Main Character Syndrome," arguing that video games promised an alternate life where the player, not the writer, was the hero. As Chuck Klosterman is quoted, "Every player invents the future." Mo_Diggs suggests this fundamentally altered our relationship with stories: we no longer want to witness a journey; we want to dictate it. This explains why the internet, a platform built on user agency, struggles to host passive, linear narratives.

The commentary also weaves in the concept of "stream of consciousness," referencing William James's emphasis on attention. Mo_Diggs argues that while James saw attention as the engine of a coherent self, the modern digital environment ensures that "stasis equals death" for platforms. The infrastructure prioritizes short videos and datafied responses over immersion. "Algorithmic feeds make digital switching seem like the only kind of activity," Mo_Diggs writes, creating a state of perpetual distraction that mirrors the disjointed nature of dreams.

This infrastructure often prioritizes short videos and datafied responses over 'immersion' as a way to keep users on a platform and make those users into assets.

The author draws a vivid parallel to Buster Keaton's Sherlock Jr., where the protagonist dreams he is in the movie he is projecting, shifting instantly from a street to a cliff edge. Mo_Diggs uses this to illustrate why online life feels like a dream: "In a dream, you're in a subway one minute then you turn around and you're in a schoolhouse." This analogy powerfully explains why users retain nothing; the medium is designed to prevent the deep processing required for memory and meaning.

The End of the Story

The piece concludes by examining how television, once a "cold" medium that required inwardness, evolved into a continuous stream that atrophied our capacity for complex thought. Mo_Diggs highlights the 1990s shows Beavis and Butt-Head and Space Ghost Coast to Coast as early symptoms of this collapse, where commentary overshadowed the work itself. "Deconstructed in this fashion, television loses its ability to tell stories over time," the author notes, quoting Douglas Rushkoff.

The argument suggests that the internet was born into this rubble of narrative collapse. It was never given a chance to mature into a storytelling medium because it inherited the fractured attention spans of the television age, amplified by the interactive demands of gaming. Mo_Diggs writes, "The roots of narrative collapse can be found here," referring to the moment the viewer became the player. The result is a culture where "more than eight billion main characters" are searching for a story, yet nothing ever happens.

The internet is at the center of many people's lives. It is how many communicate and how they hear news. I can't remember the last time I was informed of a death offline.

A counterargument worth considering is that new forms of narrative are emerging, just not in the traditional novelistic sense. However, Mo_Diggs's point stands that these new forms often lack the "spiritual stupor" or emotional progression that defines great art. The piece leaves the reader with a sobering question: if the medium is designed to prevent stasis, can it ever support a story?

Bottom Line

Mo_Diggs offers a profound and historically grounded explanation for the internet's narrative failure, successfully tracing the line from the 1980s remote control to today's algorithmic feeds. The argument's greatest strength is its refusal to blame users, instead indicting the structural incentives of the "dream economy." The biggest vulnerability is its somewhat pessimistic conclusion that the medium is fundamentally incapable of change, potentially overlooking the resilience of human creativity in adapting to new constraints.
