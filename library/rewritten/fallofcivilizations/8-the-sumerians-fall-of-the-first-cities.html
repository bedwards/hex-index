Paul Cooper doesn't just recount the history of ancient Sumer; he reconstructs the sensory reality of a civilization rising from a hostile desert, using the accidental discovery of a 17th-century traveler as a narrative gateway to the birth of human complexity. The piece is notable for its refusal to treat Sumer as a static museum exhibit, instead framing its collapse as an inevitable consequence of the very environmental ingenuity that created it. This is essential listening for anyone who wants to understand how the blueprint for modern society was drawn in mud and water, only to be washed away by the same forces.

The Accidental Archaeologist

Cooper anchors the episode in a vivid historical moment, introducing Pietro della Valle, an Italian nobleman who stumbled upon the forgotten ruins of Ur in 1625. The author writes, "being suspicious for some Arabian vagrants or vagabonds for more security we removed the mile further and took up our station under a little hill near some ruins of buildings which we saw from far away." This opening is effective because it immediately humanizes the discovery of history, reminding us that the past was once a dangerous, living landscape for the people who found it. Cooper uses della Valle's memoirs to highlight the tangible mystery of the site: "I observed that they had been cemented together not was lying but with bitumen or pitch."

The commentary here is sharp. Cooper doesn't just list artifacts; he emphasizes the alienness of what della Valle found. The traveler noted symbols like "one was like a pyramid and the other resembles a star of eight points," yet had no context for them. This framing works because it mirrors the modern reader's position: we are looking at the same fragments, trying to reconstruct a world that vanished millennia ago. The author suggests that della Valle's curiosity about "what in all the world could have happened to it" is the same question driving the entire podcast series. Critics might argue that starting with a European traveler centers the narrative on discovery rather than indigenous experience, but Cooper quickly pivots to the Sumerians themselves, using the outsider's confusion to underscore the depth of the civilization's erasure.

Della Valle must have wondered to himself who had built those enormous mounds of brick and earth all alone out there in the middle of the desert.

The Geography of Survival

The narrative then shifts from the ruins to the landscape that birthed them. Cooper argues that the Sumerian rise was not a triumph of will alone, but a desperate adaptation to a brutal environment. He describes the Taurus Mountains where "violent thunderstorms rock these mountains to echoing of the stones of the valleys," feeding the Tigris and Euphrates. The author explains that the rivers carry "silt," which he poetically dubs "rock flower," noting that "these tiny specks can have an enormous impact" on soil fertility.

This section is the piece's scientific backbone. Cooper meticulously details how the Sumerians turned an "inhospitable landscape" with summer temperatures reaching "over 52 degrees centigrade" into a breadbasket. He paraphrases the agricultural breakthrough: early farmers realized they could divert water inland, allowing them to "grow crops just about anywhere so long as you could dig a canal long enough." The argument is compelling because it links the invention of irrigation directly to the necessity of survival in a region with "unpredictable" floods and scarce resources. The author notes that unlike the Nile, which was regulated by lakes, the Mesopotamian rivers depended on variable mountain rainfall, creating a cycle of "years of drought" followed by "years of devastating floods."

This environmental determinism is a strong lens, but it risks oversimplifying the social complexity required to manage such irrigation. A counterargument worth considering is that while the geography demanded innovation, the political structures to coordinate canal building were likely the true catalyst for urbanization, not just the water itself. Cooper hints at this by mentioning how people "banded together into larger communities in order to divide the labour," but the focus remains heavily on the physical landscape.

The Mystery of the Sumerians

Perhaps the most intriguing part of Cooper's coverage is his treatment of the "Sumerian problem." He points out that the Sumerians spoke a "language isolate" that had no relation to neighboring tongues, leading early scholars to believe it was a "kind of code used to communicate in secret." The author explores the theory that they arrived by boat, citing the legend of Oannes, a "half-man half-fish" who "walked out of the sea and taught the people of Mesopotamia the secrets of culture."

Cooper writes, "he brought them the knowledge of letters sciences and all kinds of techniques... he revealed to them how to work the land and gather fruits." By weaving this myth into the historical narrative, Cooper suggests that the Sumerians were so culturally distinct that their origins felt mythical even to later generations. This is a bold narrative choice. It acknowledges the limits of our knowledge while preserving the sense of wonder that defined the civilization's emergence. The author effectively uses the myth to illustrate the profound gap between the Sumerians and their neighbors, reinforcing the idea that they were a unique anomaly in human history.

However, relying on a 1st-century AD legend to explain a prehistoric migration is speculative. Critics might note that the Oannes myth is more likely a later cultural memory of Sumerian scholarship than a literal account of migration. Yet, Cooper uses it not as fact, but as a metaphor for the Sumerians' perceived otherness, which is a defensible and engaging literary device.

Bottom Line

Paul Cooper's strongest asset is his ability to make the physical and linguistic alienness of Sumer feel immediate and urgent, transforming dry archaeological data into a story of survival against impossible odds. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its heavy reliance on environmental determinism, which sometimes sidelines the complex social and political innovations that allowed Sumer to thrive. Readers should watch for how Cooper balances the mythic origins of the Sumerians with the hard realities of their eventual collapse in future segments.
