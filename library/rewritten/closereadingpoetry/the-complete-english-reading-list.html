The traditional literary canon has been systematically dismantled across American universities, but one YouTube creator has dug through century-old course catalogs to recover what was lost. Close Reading Poetry spent years analyzing mid-degree requirements from Harvard, Yale, Michigan, and Columbia to reconstruct a coherent map of the English literary tradition—one organized by genre rather than chronology.

This isn't a sprint through reading lists meant to make you clever at parties. It's something far more ambitious: a vertical curriculum showing how literature's deep structure emerges from its foundations all the way to what's being written today.

The Biblical Foundations

English literature begins not with Shakespeare, but with the Hebrew Bible. Genesis traces creation, the fall, the flood, and Israel's patriarchs—establishing the symbolic grammar of sin, covenant, exile, nation, and law that would shape centuries of English poetry. Exodus follows as Israel's origin story, delivering them from Egyptian slavery through Moses.

The book of Judges offers a cyclical portrait of moral instability: there was no king in those days, and everyone did what was right in their own eyes. Job wrestles with divine silence in the face of human suffering—one of literature's most satisfying challenges. Then come 1 and 2 Samuel and Kings, containing the national epic of King David.

After these foundations, readers move into classical literature: Homer's Iliad defines heroic psychology through the Trojan War's rage and mortality, while The Odyssey tells the foundational story of homecoming, identity recovery, and longing for home—influencing everything from Kenneth Graham's Wind in the Willows to James Joyce's Ulysses. Virgil's Aeneid follows a hero from Troy to Rome's founding, representing destiny and national pride.

Ovid's Metamorphoses helps inform the transformative mythology central to storytelling itself.

The Medieval Turn

Old English gives us Beowulf—a north-Germanic heroic epic scholars still debate whether Virgil influenced. Middle English loosens the definition of epic as long-form narrative in verse: Langland's Piers Plowman is an allegorical dream vision about spiritual truth and social justice, predating the Reformation by centuries.

Sir Gawain and the Green Knight becomes one of literature's great test stories—a meditation on nightly virtue. Chaucer's Troilus and Crusade gives us tragic interiority during the Trojan War.

You're not just looking for suspense or story—you're decoding a dream of civilization. Learning how a culture dreams itself into existence.

Critics might note that organizing literature purely by genre risks oversimplifying historical influence—biblical and classical epics clearly drew upon each other, even when Hebrew poets and Greek bards didn't have access to one another's works. The author's claim that Homer didn't know the Hebrew Bible while Virgil probably did is precisely where these traditions diverge yet converge.

Bottom Line

This reading list recovers what modern English departments have largely abandoned: a cumulative, structured approach to literary tradition that trains readers to perceive patterns across centuries rather than isolated texts. The biggest strength is its scope—reconstructing comprehensive curricula from actual historical archives rather than contemporary fashion. Its vulnerability? The claim that genres don't cross-fertilize before modernism undersells how much these traditions already talked to each other. For busy readers wanting depth over breadth, this map finally offers what earlier generations took for granted.
