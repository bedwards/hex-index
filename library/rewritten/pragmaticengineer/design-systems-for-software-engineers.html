Most engineering leaders treat user interface design as a cosmetic afterthought, but Gergely Orosz argues that a cohesive design system is actually a critical business asset that determines whether software can command enterprise prices. This piece stands out because it moves beyond abstract theory to dissect the granular, often tedious reality of building a design system from the ground up, using a real-world case study from the data security firm Rubrik to prove that "premium feel" is a deliberate engineering output, not a happy accident.

The Wake-Up Call and the Cost of Chaos

Orosz opens with a stark anecdote from Rubrik, where the Chief Technology Officer dismissed the product's interface as looking "like a college project." This feedback wasn't just an insult; it was a strategic pivot point. The author notes that this observation "hit home because it was right," highlighting a common blind spot where engineers prioritize shipping features over the user experience. The narrative suggests that early-stage urgency often masks a long-term liability: a chaotic UI that fails to inspire confidence in high-stakes enterprise buyers.

The author frames the transition from a "good enough" MVP to a professional product as a necessary evolution for any company scaling past product-market fit. "Most engineers will understand the urge to build quickly and ship lots of features in order to hit seemingly-impossible deadlines," Orosz writes, acknowledging the pressure that leads to technical debt in the visual layer. However, the commentary here is crucial: this debt isn't just aesthetic; it's a barrier to revenue. A startup cannot charge premium prices if its software looks like a free template.

"If you want to charge enterprise prices, your software needs to look like an enterprise product."

This assertion is the piece's most pragmatic insight. It reframes design not as art, but as a revenue-enabling infrastructure. Critics might argue that for early-stage startups, functionality always trumps form, and that investing in a design system too early can slow down iteration. Yet, Orosz counters this by showing that the cost of retrofitting a messy interface later is far higher than the upfront investment in a system like Rubrik's "Aura Design System Library."

The Hidden Complexity of a Button

The article's most compelling section dismantles the misconception that a design system is merely a style guide. Orosz illustrates this by deconstructing the seemingly simple task of designing a button. He lists a dizzying array of decisions required for a single component: background color, text color, height, border radius, shadows, font, icon placement, animation states, accessibility for color blindness, and localization for right-to-left languages.

"Answering just these six questions can take considerable time, including multiple back-and-forth discussions," Orosz writes, emphasizing that the complexity lies in the edge cases, not the core function. The author's choice to focus on the button is effective because it forces the reader to confront the sheer volume of decisions that are usually made ad-hoc. Without a system, every engineer reinvents the wheel, leading to a fragmented user experience that confuses customers and frustrates developers.

The argument extends to the role of the Design System Engineering (DSE) team, which acts as the bridge between design intent and code reality. "The design team creates the look and feel of the software in Figma. The DSE team converts that into working React code," Orosz explains. This division of labor ensures that the visual language remains consistent across the entire product suite, turning a collection of features into a unified brand.

"A design system is important for users, too. If they become accustomed to what a button or menu looks like in one part of the software, they know what a button or menu looks like in the rest of the application."

This reduction of cognitive load is a subtle but powerful benefit. While engineers often focus on code efficiency, Orosz correctly identifies that user trust is built on familiarity. A consistent interface tells the user, "We know what we are doing," which is essential for software handling sensitive data.

The AI Era and the Limits of Automation

As the piece shifts to the role of artificial intelligence, Orosz offers a grounded perspective that avoids the hype cycle. He acknowledges that while AI can assist with many tasks, "generating a consistent design system isn't one of them." This is a vital distinction. AI might generate a button, but it cannot understand the nuanced trade-offs between brand identity, accessibility, and technical constraints without human oversight.

The author suggests that AI is better suited for supporting roles, such as using tools like Figma's Model Context Protocol (MCP) or running design system subagents, rather than replacing the strategic decision-making required for a cohesive system. "The new technology can be helpful for many tasks, but generating a consistent design system isn't one of them," Orosz writes, reinforcing the idea that human judgment remains the core of high-quality engineering.

Critics might argue that as AI models improve, they will eventually master the context required to build design systems autonomously. However, the current reality described by Orosz suggests that the value of a design system lies in the decisions made, not just the code generated. The human element of negotiating trade-offs between aesthetics and performance is something AI cannot yet replicate.

"Time is money, and a system like this makes engineers more productive because they deliver features faster, and the company can increase revenue quicker."

This economic argument ties the entire piece together. A design system is not a luxury; it is a force multiplier that allows teams to scale without collapsing under the weight of their own complexity.

Bottom Line

Gergely Orosz delivers a masterclass in reframing design systems from a "nice-to-have" aesthetic exercise to a fundamental engineering requirement for scaling businesses. The strongest part of the argument is the detailed deconstruction of a single button, which effectively exposes the hidden complexity that teams often ignore until it's too late. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that organizations have the resources and patience to invest in a system before they are forced to, a luxury many startups simply do not have. Readers should watch for how the industry adapts these principles as AI tools evolve, but the core lesson remains: consistency is the bedrock of trust, and trust is the currency of enterprise software.
