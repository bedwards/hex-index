Gergely Orosz delivers a startling verdict on the software industry: the era of manual coding is effectively over, replaced by a new paradigm where the human role shifts from writer to supervisor. This isn't a speculative look at distant future tech; Orosz argues that the tipping point arrived in late 2025, driven by specific model releases that turned coding agents into competent, production-ready partners. For busy engineers and executives, the urgency here is immediate—the tools are no longer just assistants, they are the primary workforce.

The Tipping Point Arrives

Orosz anchors his argument in a wave of personal testimonies from industry heavyweights who have suddenly found their relationship with code fundamentally altered. He highlights the shift from skepticism to adoption, noting that even prominent critics have reversed their stance. "I've never felt this much behind as a programmer," writes Andrej Karpathy, a former OpenAI co-founder, describing the profession as being "dramatically refactored." This admission from a figure known for candor carries significant weight, suggesting that the change is not merely hype but a structural shift in how work gets done.

The author details how the latest models, specifically Opus 4.5 and GPT-5.2, have crossed a threshold where the friction of manual typing outweighs the cost of supervision. Thorsten Ball, a veteran engineer, admits that after fifteen years of loving the "cadence of typing," he now finds it frustrating. "2025 was the year in which I deeply reconsidered my relationship to programming," Ball reflects. Orosz uses this emotional pivot to illustrate a broader truth: the joy of software engineering is decoupling from the act of syntax generation. The value is no longer in the keystrokes, but in the architectural vision.

"The cost of software production is trending towards zero."

This observation from Malte Ubl, CTO at Vercel, serves as the piece's most disruptive claim. If the marginal cost of writing code approaches zero, the economic models of countless startups and enterprise teams must be recalibrated. Orosz supports this by noting that Boris Cherny, the creator of the coding tool Claude Code, submitted 200 pull requests last month without ever opening a traditional integrated development environment. The evidence suggests that for greenfield projects and startups, the barrier to entry for building complex software has collapsed.

The Erosion of Traditional Expertise

The commentary takes a critical turn as Orosz examines what happens to the skills that have defined careers for decades. He argues that the value of being a "language polyglot" or a specialist in a specific stack is rapidly declining. In the past, knowing the nuances of C versus Rust or mastering the intricacies of a legacy framework was a competitive moat. Now, Orosz suggests, these distinctions are blurring as AI agents handle the syntax and standard patterns for any language.

This shift forces a re-evaluation of what constitutes "expertise." The author points out that prototyping, once a time-consuming task requiring deep technical knowledge, can now be executed by non-technical product managers. "Product managers can now generate software easier – needing fewer engineers to realize their goals," Orosz writes. This creates a paradox: while the demand for pure coding skills drops, the demand for high-level system design and product intuition skyrockets. The engineer who can only write code is at risk; the engineer who can define the problem and verify the solution is indispensable.

Critics might note that this view underestimates the complexity of maintaining massive, legacy codebases where AI agents often struggle to understand context without human intervention. While the article focuses heavily on new development, the "ugly" reality of debugging AI-generated spaghetti code in critical infrastructure remains a significant hurdle. Orosz acknowledges this, warning that "weak software engineering practices start to hurt sooner" when code is generated at scale, but the primary focus remains on the explosive potential of new creation.

The New Abstraction Layer

The most profound insight in Orosz's analysis is the emergence of a new layer of abstraction. He argues that engineers are no longer just managing code; they are managing a "programmable layer" of agents, prompts, and workflows. This requires a mental model shift comparable to the move from assembly language to high-level languages. "Clearly some powerful alien tool was handed around, except it comes with no manual," Karpathy observes, a sentiment Orosz adopts to describe the current confusion and opportunity.

The author draws a subtle parallel to historical computing shifts. Much like the transition to Lisp in the mid-20th century, which introduced powerful abstractions that changed how programmers thought about problems, the current AI revolution is forcing a rethinking of the engineering process itself. However, unlike Lisp, which required deep theoretical understanding, the new AI layer rewards speed of iteration and the ability to curate output. The "a-ha" moment for many engineers, as Orosz describes, is realizing that the bottleneck is no longer their ability to type, but their ability to imagine and verify.

Bottom Line

Orosz's argument is compelling because it relies on the lived experience of top-tier engineers rather than theoretical projections; the evidence that the tools are ready is overwhelming. The piece's greatest strength is its unflinching admission that the "coder" role is becoming obsolete, forcing a painful but necessary evolution toward product-minded engineering. The biggest vulnerability remains the assumption that AI can handle the messy, undocumented reality of long-term system maintenance, but for the future of software creation, the verdict is clear: the age of manual typing is over."
