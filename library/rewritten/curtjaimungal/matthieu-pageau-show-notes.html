Curt Jaimungal presents a rare intellectual collision where computer science, biblical exegesis, and metaphysical exile converge to redefine the nature of evil. Rather than treating Satan as a cartoonish villain, the piece argues for a functionalist view: a divine "penetration tester" whose corruption stems not from inherent malice, but from a usurpation of role. For the busy mind seeking to understand the architecture of modern spiritual crisis, this reframing offers a startlingly precise grammar for why systems fail and how they might be renewed.

The Hacker in the Garden

Jaimungal introduces Matthieu Pageau, a thinker who approaches Genesis not as ancient mythology but as a construction manual for reality. Pageau's central thesis is that the opening of the Bible establishes a symbolic grammar where "heaven is the plan" and "earth is the materials." This distinction is crucial. As Pageau explains, "Before a plan exists, materials have no purpose." The argument suggests that chaos, represented by water, is not inherently evil; it is merely unorganized matter waiting for a blueprint. When water floods, it destroys; when channeled, it renews. This duality mirrors the broader argument that function precedes morality.

The most provocative claim centers on the figure of Satan. Pageau posits that the adversary was originally created as a tester, a role analogous to a security expert hired to find flaws in a system. "God created the Satan-function to test systems for flaws, just as a company hires a hacker to probe its security," Jaimungal writes. The corruption occurs only when this tester abandons the role of service and succumbs to a "will-to-power," using discovered vulnerabilities for blackmail or dominance. This reframing is powerful because it locates evil not in a cosmic rebellion against God, but in the corruption of a necessary function. It implies that criticism itself is holy, provided it lacks the ambition to rule.

"If you have a will to power, then you're a true enemy. A fallen Satan."

Critics might note that this functionalist view risks minimizing the ontological weight of evil. If Satan is merely a corrupted employee, does the sheer magnitude of suffering in the world become a bureaucratic error rather than a profound moral rupture? Pageau anticipates this by pointing to the Book of Job, where the tester operates strictly within divine constraints, asking for permission before acting. The tragedy, he argues, is not the existence of the tester, but the moment the tester decides the rules no longer apply.

The Feminine Crown and Mathematical Limits

Pageau extends his symbolic grammar to the relationship between mathematics and the feminine principle, a connection that feels both audacious and strangely coherent. He links the biblical story of Eve to Kurt Gödel's incompleteness theorems. In this reading, mathematics represents the masculine drive for axioms and deduction, while Eve represents the "feminine" force that introduces a paradox the system cannot solve. "The incompleteness theorem is a crown to mathematics," Pageau asserts. Just as Eve brought a riddle to Adam that broke his closed loop of knowledge, Gödel proved that any sufficiently complex system contains truths it cannot prove from within.

This is not a dismissal of logic, but an elevation of it. The paradox does not destroy the system; it forces it to expand. Pageau writes, "Now I know there's some things outside my system, but I can still know things about it." This mirrors the historical tension in theology where the Book of Ruth and the story of Tamar serve as narratives of redemption through subversive action. These stories, like Gödel's theorem, show that the boundaries of a system are often where its renewal begins. The "feminine" principle, in this view, is the force that prevents a system from hardening into corruption by constantly testing its limits.

"The liar's paradox didn't break mathematics; it improved it by revealing boundaries."

A counterargument worth considering is the risk of aestheticizing rigorous mathematical results. Gödel's theorem is a precise logical proof about formal systems; mapping it onto Eve's riddle risks turning a technical breakthrough into a gendered metaphor. However, Pageau's background in computer science suggests he is not merely using math as a poetic device, but as a structural analogy for how truth operates across different domains. The argument holds weight because it treats the "riddle" not as a flaw, but as a necessary feature of any living system.

The Exile of the Internal Critic

Perhaps the most resonant section of the piece is Pageau's personal confession of spiritual homelessness. He describes himself as unable to "plant a flag" in Orthodoxy or Catholicism until certain questions are resolved. This is not a rejection of faith, but a refusal to settle for a system that cannot answer its own deepest questions. "I ate from the Tree of Knowledge of Good and Bad. I can pretend I didn't, but that's not how it works," Pageau admits. The act of knowing, once done, cannot be undone. This creates a state of exile where the critic must remain outside the walls to see the structure clearly.

Pageau distinguishes between "holy criticism," which seeks to improve the system without demanding power, and "fallen criticism," which seeks dominion. He cites the example of Miriam, who criticized Moses but was struck with plague because her critique was a bid for leadership. In contrast, the "internal critic" operates with humility, asking questions without demanding a throne. This distinction is vital for understanding the current landscape of institutional distrust. When criticism becomes a tool for personal power, it ceases to be a corrective and becomes a weapon.

"Holy criticism lacks will-to-power; fallen criticism seeks dominion; knowing the difference is the key to escaping metaphysical exile."

The piece acknowledges the danger of this position: if one cannot join the church because of unresolved questions, one remains in a state of perpetual exile. Yet, Pageau suggests this is the only honest path forward. The alternative is to pretend the questions don't exist, which is the very act of subversion the snake used in the garden. "The snake's power depends on preventing communication; keeping secrets, ensuring parties don't talk," Jaimungal notes. The antidote to this silence is the courage to ask, even when the answer is not immediately available.

Bottom Line

Jaimungal's coverage of Pageau offers a sophisticated framework for understanding evil not as an external monster, but as a corrupted function within a divine system. The strongest part of this argument is its ability to reconcile the existence of a tester with the goodness of the creator, turning the problem of evil into a problem of role integrity. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the potential to over-extend symbolic analogies, particularly when mapping complex mathematical theorems onto biblical narratives. Readers should watch for how this functionalist view of Satan might reshape their understanding of criticism and authority in their own institutions.

"You eat something, it's in you."

The ultimate takeaway is that the path to renewal requires the courage to remain in exile until the questions are answered, rather than forcing a premature return to a corrupted system. As Pageau suggests, the act of asking the right question is often the first step toward redemption.
