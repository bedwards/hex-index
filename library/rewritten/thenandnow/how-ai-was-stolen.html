Then & Now reframes the current AI frenzy not as a sudden technological breakthrough, but as the culmination of a decades-long, often deceptive history of intellectual appropriation. The author's most provocative claim is that modern generative models represent a 'heist'—a systematic colonization of human creativity by Silicon Valley giants who stripped the nuance from our collective work. This is not just a tech story; it is a philosophical investigation into whether we are witnessing the end of human agency or the beginning of a new, terrifying form of control.

The Illusion of Disembodied Intelligence

The piece opens by dismantling the popular myth that intelligence is an abstract, magical force. Then & Now writes, 'we often use these words interchangeably or at least with a lot of overlap when we drill down into what something like intelligence means we find surprisingly little agreement.' This observation is crucial because it exposes the hollowness of corporate hype. The author contrasts the CEO narrative with historical reality, noting that Sundar Pichai's claim that AI is 'more profound than... electricity or fire' sounds like marketing, yet the piece argues it might be terrifyingly accurate. The coverage effectively grounds this high-stakes prediction in the messy, empirical reality of human history rather than abstract futurism.

However, the author's focus on the 'deceptive illusions' of AI risks oversimplifying the genuine engineering marvels that occurred along the way. While the theft of data is a valid concern, dismissing the technical leap as merely 'stolen' ignores the complex architecture required to make that data useful. A counterargument worth considering is that the 'heist' narrative, while compelling, might distract from the equally important issue of how we govern the use of this stolen intelligence.

'It's a long but necessary history about the deceptive illusions of AI about big Tech Silicon Valley goliaths against ordinary Davids it's about secret treasure chests and vast libraries of stolen work.'

The Logical Trap and the Combinatorial Explosion

The commentary then dives into the historical mechanics of AI, specifically the 'symbolic approach' that dominated the field for decades. Then & Now explains that early researchers believed they could 'model symbolically by imitating or coding a digital replica of for example the human mind.' This approach relied on binary logic: if a traffic light is red, then stop. The author argues that this seemed intuitive because it aligned with the 'on or off' nature of transistors. Yet, the piece masterfully illustrates why this failed through the concept of 'combinatorial explosion.'

As Then & Now puts it, 'calculating what to do from these seemingly Simple Rules required much more knowledge and many more calculations than first assumed.' The author uses the Tower of Hanoi game to demonstrate that as variables increase, the number of possible moves becomes 'incomprehensibly vast and technologically impossible.' This is the piece's strongest technical insight: it shows that the world is too messy for simple 'if-then' rules. The real world isn't a clean game; it has graffiti, broken lights, and unpredictable children. The failure of early robots like 'Shakey' to navigate a simple room proves that logic alone cannot replicate human intuition.

Critics might argue that the symbolic approach wasn't entirely wrong, but rather limited by the computing power of the 1970s and 80s. If the author had acknowledged that modern AI uses probabilistic models rather than pure logic, the historical narrative would be more complete. Nevertheless, the explanation of why 'naive exhaustive search' failed remains a vital lesson for understanding current AI limitations.

The Knowledge Bottleneck

The narrative shifts to the 'expert systems' era, where researchers tried to manually code human knowledge into databases. Then & Now highlights the irony of this approach: 'the knowledge is currently acquired in a very painstaking way that reminds one of cottage industries.' The author describes how systems like MYCIN could diagnose blood diseases by following thousands of rules provided by doctors, yet they quickly became obsolete. The core problem was the 'knowledge acquisition bottleneck'—the inability to keep the machine's database updated with the real world's changing facts.

This section serves as a powerful metaphor for the current state of generative AI. Just as early systems struggled to ingest new information, today's models struggle with the static nature of their training data. The author suggests that the solution was never about better logic, but about finding a way to access the 'vast libraries of stolen work' that humans have already created. The piece implies that the current 'AI winter' of the 70s was merely a pause before the industry found a new, more aggressive way to harvest human knowledge.

Bottom Line

Then & Now delivers a compelling, if slightly alarmist, historical critique that successfully reframes AI not as a miracle of engineering, but as a collision of philosophy, logic, and theft. The strongest part of the argument is the detailed breakdown of why logical rules fail in the real world, providing necessary context for why modern AI behaves so unpredictably. Its biggest vulnerability is the tendency to conflate all AI development with a singular narrative of 'theft,' potentially overlooking the legitimate scientific progress made in neural networks. Readers should watch for how this historical perspective influences the upcoming debate on copyright and regulation, as the author clearly sees the current moment as the final reckoning for this 'heist.'

'The problem of knowledge acquisition is the key bottleneck problem in artificial intelligence setting the machine for... the end of humanity.'
