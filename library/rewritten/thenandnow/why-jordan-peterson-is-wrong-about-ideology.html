Then & Now delivers a devastatingly precise takedown of Jordan Peterson's most famous contradiction: the man who warns against "isms" is himself a fervent ideologue. The author doesn't just list Peterson's rules; they expose the structural hypocrisy of a thinker who condemns rigid worldviews while championing a "mythic conservatism" built on the very dogmas he claims to reject. This is essential listening for anyone tired of the false dichotomy between "ideology" and "common sense."

The Three Charges

The piece begins by dissecting Peterson's specific definition of ideology, which he frames as a dangerous simplification of reality. Then & Now writes, "an ideologue grants these small number of forces primary causal power while ignoring others of equal or greater importance." This is a sharp observation of Peterson's tendency to reduce complex social dynamics to a single variable, whether it's the patriarchy or the state. The author notes that Peterson argues this leads to a "narcissistic" belief that the world can be fixed if only one group held the controls.

The commentary effectively groups Peterson's critique into three distinct pillars: utopianism, oversimplification, and resentment. Then & Now writes, "ideology is unachievably idealistic," citing Peterson's warning against grand designs in favor of incremental peace. This framing is effective because it isolates Peterson's fear of the future from his reverence for the past. However, the author points out a glaring gap: Peterson's method of "careful particularized analysis" is rarely applied to the very systems he defends. As the author notes, it is "uncommon to see any serious social problem addressed so methodically" by Peterson himself when discussing the institutions he protects.

"Critiquing ideologues ideology while advocating for a type of conservative judeo-christian structuralism is a contradiction at best and hypocritical too."

The Definition Trap

The article then pivots to the philosophical definition of ideology, dismantling the idea that the status quo is neutral. Then & Now writes, "many often miss how the present has an ideological basis as much as marxism or feudalism." This is a crucial pivot that reframes the entire debate. The author explains that ideology is simply a "coherent and consistent set of ideas" that organizes our view of the world, whether we realize it or not. By this definition, Peterson's own worldview is undeniably ideological.

The author argues that Peterson's "mythic conservatism" fits the very criteria he attacks. Then & Now writes, "Peterson's ideology goes something like this: first arrangements must be made for our provisioning... second it must appeal to others... and four it must work today tomorrow next month next year." The author points out the irony that Peterson presents these "limited and universal rules" as the antidote to ideology, yet they function exactly as the "emotion-laden myth saturated action-related system of beliefs" that political scientists define as ideology.

Critics might note that Peterson would argue his "myths" are evolutionary truths rather than political constructs, but the author counters that the function remains the same: to provide a rigid map for behavior. The piece highlights that Peterson's reliance on the "Ten Commandments" and "hero's journey" archetypes is not a rejection of dogma, but a selection of a different, older dogma.

The Conservative Contradiction

The final section exposes the tension between Peterson's claim of "limited rules" and the reality of human history. Then & Now writes, "Peterson's limited rules seem so ambiguous so diverse and varied across cultures and history that it seems questionable to call them limited at all." The author argues that Peterson's "natural ethic" is actually a specific cultural product of Judeo-Christian tradition, not a universal biological imperative.

The commentary suggests that Peterson is essentially a Burkean traditionalist who mistrusts revolution but fully embraces a rigid worldview. Then & Now writes, "he's a type of burkian traditionalist... preferring to put its trust in experience and in the gradual improvement of tried and tested arrangements." The author finds this position intellectually inconsistent, noting that Peterson demands we accept these ancient structures as "undeniably true" while simultaneously claiming to reject the very concept of ideology.

"The myths and values of ideology are communicated through symbols in a simplified economical and efficient manner sound familiar?"

The piece concludes by pointing out that Peterson's "eternal" rules are actually subject to change, a fact he acknowledges only to dismiss it. Then & Now writes, "our knowledge of how to act in the world remains eternally incomplete partly because of our profound ignorance." This admission undermines Peterson's entire project of presenting his specific cultural myths as the final, unchangeable solution to human suffering.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's strongest move is reframing Peterson's "anti-ideology" stance as merely a preference for a specific, older ideology rather than a genuine rejection of the concept. The argument's biggest vulnerability is that it assumes Peterson's audience accepts the philosophical definition of ideology over his own rhetorical one, which may limit its persuasive power among his most devoted followers. Readers should watch for how this critique of "mythic conservatism" evolves as Peterson continues to blend evolutionary biology with religious fundamentalism.
