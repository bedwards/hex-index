Then & Now delivers a startling reframing of modern economic anxiety: the concentration of corporate power isn't just a market failure, but the quiet return of monarchical authority. By tracing a direct line from 17th-century English court battles over playing cards to today's tech giants and hotel conglomerates, the author argues that our current "oligarchy" is not an accident of capitalism, but its inevitable destination. This is essential listening for anyone who feels the weight of "quiet monopolies" but struggles to name them, offering a historical lens that transforms abstract market share numbers into a story of lost freedom.

The Quiet Architecture of Control

The piece opens with a disorienting list of everyday choices that aren't really choices at all. Then & Now writes, "you find them almost everywhere you look quite literally do you have sunglasses... peer a little closer and you'll find they're made by one company luxotica controls 80% of the market." The author's strength lies in making the invisible visible, pointing out that while we see brands like Oakley and Ray-Ban, we are actually dealing with a single entity. This observation lands with particular force because it moves beyond the usual tech-bashing to include travel, food, and media, revealing a pervasive pattern.

The argument shifts from modern examples to historical roots, suggesting that the fear of monopoly is baked into the American DNA. Then & Now notes that the origin of criticism against monopolies was "not one necessarily of corporate power per se but of royal monarchical power." This historical context is crucial; it reframes the modern struggle not as a fight against efficiency, but as a defense of the very concept of a free citizenry against a new kind of king. The author highlights how early American constitutions explicitly banned monopolies, with Maryland's 1776 Constitution declaring them "odious contrary to the spirit of a free government."

Critics might note that the author glosses over the distinction between state-granted monopolies of the past and private corporate giants of today, potentially conflating two different mechanisms of power. However, the parallel drawn regarding the effect on the individual—restraint of trade and forced dependency—remains a compelling bridge between eras.

From Invisible Hand to Visible Boardroom

The commentary takes a sharp turn to explain how we got here, moving from the theoretical to the structural. Then & Now describes the transition from Adam Smith's "invisible hand" to a system of "bureaucratic management of planning." The author argues that the market's natural balancing act was replaced by explicit decisions made in boardrooms. As Then & Now puts it, "the invisible process that was happening across the market kind of condensed and moved into boardrooms written out with pens put into spreadsheets."

This shift explains why consolidation became the dominant trend. The author points out that the central reason for this growth was simple economics: it is cheaper to produce at scale. However, the piece warns that this efficiency comes with a hidden cost. Then & Now writes, "once the competition was wiped out there was nothing to stop the raising of prices." This is the crux of the argument: the initial benefits of lower prices are a trap that eventually leads to higher costs and less innovation once the monopoly is secured.

The author draws on the muckrakers of the late 19th century to illustrate the human cost of this consolidation. Referencing Ida Tarbell's work on Standard Oil, Then & Now writes that these giants were described as "dictators and Tyran ists." The historical parallel is striking, suggesting that the "robber barons" were not just businessmen but the architects of a new, corporate autocracy. The text notes that the Economist magazine once described these tycoons as "absolute dictators neither rendering account," a phrase that resonates deeply in an era of unaccountable tech platforms.

The Totalitarian Shadow

The final section of the commentary elevates the stakes, suggesting that the trend toward bigness is not merely economic but political. Then & Now writes, "the idea of consolidation of concentration the authority to control the elephantine growth of modernity and modern institutions and modern Technologies is a story about totalitarianism too." This is the piece's most provocative claim, linking the growth of the state, the military, and big business into a single trajectory of control.

The author challenges the conventional political divide, noting that while the left fears big business and the right fears big government, both are failing to see that "big is automatically bad" is a flawed premise. Instead, the real danger lies in the concentration of authority. Then & Now argues that we must ask, "what are the benefits of getting big and what are the risks," suggesting that our current inability to answer this clearly is a sign of democratic decay. The piece concludes by warning that without understanding these conditions, we are drifting toward a future where the "restraint of trade" becomes a permanent state of affairs.

The entire period of American capitalism since the industrial revolution has been an unrelenting Trend towards consolidation.

A counterargument worth considering is whether the author underestimates the role of consumer choice and innovation in breaking up these monopolies, as seen in the tech sector's rapid churn. Yet, the historical evidence presented regarding the persistence of trusts in oil, sugar, and media suggests that market forces alone rarely reverse this trend without intervention.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's strongest contribution is the historical continuity drawn between monarchical monopolies and modern corporate giants, effectively stripping away the illusion of choice in our daily lives. The argument's vulnerability lies in its somewhat deterministic view of capitalism's trajectory, which may overlook the potential for regulatory renewal or disruptive innovation. Readers should watch for how this framing of "totalitarianism" influences future antitrust debates, as the author suggests the stakes are far higher than just price gouging.
