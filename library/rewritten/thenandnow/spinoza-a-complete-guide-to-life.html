Then & Now makes a startling claim: that the key to modern mental health isn't a new app or a trendy supplement, but a 17th-century Dutch philosopher who was excommunicated for his radical views. The piece argues that Baruch Spinoza offers a "complete scientific road map" for living, one that predates neuroscience yet aligns perfectly with our understanding of cause, effect, and the interconnectedness of all things. For the busy mind seeking clarity in a chaotic world, this is not just history; it is a functional operating system for reducing anxiety and finding freedom.

The Geometry of Existence

The author begins by dismantling our intuitive sense of separation. We feel like isolated islands, but Then & Now insists that "being is one." This is the foundational axiom of Spinoza's universe. The text explains that what we perceive as distinct objects—trees, people, stars—are merely different shapes or "modes" of a single, infinite substance. Then & Now writes, "everything us the forest animals plants stars the laws of physics and many things that are part of one thing nature." This reframing is powerful because it shifts the reader's perspective from isolation to integration. When you realize you are not separate from the ecosystem but a temporary configuration of it, the weight of individual failure often lightens.

The commentary here is particularly effective in translating dense metaphysics into plain language. Instead of getting bogged down in abstract definitions, the author uses the analogy of a leaf depending on a branch, which depends on soil and air, to prove that nothing is truly independent. Then & Now notes, "if we're defining substances strictly it turns out there's only one trees people plants animals there we're all different modes different shapes of of mata." This logical progression forces the reader to confront the illusion of autonomy. Critics might argue that this monistic view erases the unique value of individual human agency, but the author counters this by suggesting that understanding our dependence is the very first step toward true freedom.

God couldn't be outside the universe. Spinoza realized he was the ultimate laws were gravity mathematics the laws of physics of cause and effect.

The Illusion of Free Will

The piece then tackles the most uncomfortable implication of Spinoza's system: the denial of free will. Then & Now argues that we are not the authors of our actions but the observers of a causal chain we cannot see. The author quotes Spinoza directly to drive this point home: "the infant thinks that he freely wants the milk the angry child that he freely wants vengeance and the timid one that he freely wants to flee." This is a devastating critique of human ego. We believe we choose our reactions, but the author suggests we are merely "billiard balls set off by causes outside of us."

This section is the intellectual core of the article. Then & Now paraphrases the French philosopher Gilles Deleuze to describe consciousness as "a dream with one's eyes open," sitting atop a waterfall of mental events. The author's coverage is compelling because it doesn't just state the theory; it applies it to daily life. "We only think we're free because our consciousness sits between one of those causes and effect," the text explains. This realization is meant to be liberating rather than depressing. If our anger or fear is a result of external causes and not a moral failing of our character, we can study the causes rather than beating ourselves up for the effects.

However, a counterargument worth considering is that denying free will entirely could lead to a fatalistic paralysis. If we are just waves on the sea, why bother steering? Then & Now anticipates this by pivoting to the concept of "adequate ideas." The author writes, "the trick to life of course is to have adequate ideas." By understanding the causes that move us, we gain the capacity to navigate them. We move from being passive victims of circumstance to active participants who understand the mechanics of their own lives.

The Ethics of Affect

Finally, the text moves from metaphysics to ethics, asking how we should live in a world of cause and effect. Then & Now proposes a radical redefinition of "good" and "bad." These are not intrinsic properties of objects but descriptions of how things affect us. "The lion is bad for me so i run from it the berry is good for me so i'm drawn to it," the author illustrates. This relational view of morality is practical and immediate. It strips away abstract moralizing and focuses on the concrete impact of our environment on our well-being.

The author's strongest insight here is the definition of perfection. Then & Now writes, "by perfection and goodness he just means existing well." A house that stands the storm is more perfect than one that collapses; a person who understands their emotions is more perfect than one who is enslaved by them. This aligns Spinoza's philosophy with the modern pursuit of mental fitness. The text concludes that we should "surround ourselves with perfection," meaning we should curate our lives to include things that increase our capacity to act and think clearly.

Nothing is good or bad in itself but only relative to something else... these appraisals of objects ideas or people aren't actually properties that are in the thing itself but are descriptions of a relationship.

This shift in perspective is the ultimate payoff of the piece. It transforms philosophy from an academic exercise into a survival strategy. By recognizing that our judgments are relational, we can stop fighting reality and start optimizing our interactions with it. The author suggests that this is how we escape being "slaves to our emotions and other people's opinions."

Bottom Line

Then & Now successfully translates Spinoza's complex metaphysics into a usable guide for modern emotional resilience, arguing that understanding the deterministic web of nature is the only path to true freedom. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to treat Spinoza as a dusty historical figure, instead presenting him as a radical psychologist whose insights on cause and effect are more relevant than ever. The only vulnerability lies in the sheer cognitive effort required to internalize a worldview that denies free will, a hurdle the author acknowledges but does not fully smooth over for the skeptical reader.
