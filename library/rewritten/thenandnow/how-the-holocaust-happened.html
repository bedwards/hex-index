Then & Now delivers a harrowing and necessary corrective to a comforting myth: that the Holocaust was the work of a few sadistic monsters rather than a systemic failure of ordinary humanity. By grounding the narrative in the visceral reality of Reserve Police Battalion 101, the author forces a confrontation with the uncomfortable truth that the machinery of genocide relied not on psychopaths, but on working-class men who were offered a way out and chose to stay.

The Anatomy of an Ordinary Killer

The piece opens by reconstructing the desperate socio-economic landscape of 1930s Germany, where hyperinflation and national humiliation created a vacuum for authoritarianism. Then & Now writes, "Liberalism and democracy are failing... election after election failed to reach a majority and parliament that's the reichstag is unable to govern." This context is crucial; it establishes that the rise of the Nazis was not an inevitable historical force but a reaction to a collapsing social contract. The author argues that Hitler's initial appeal was economic revitalization, but the true engine of the Holocaust was the identification of an "enemy within" that connected all societal ills.

"The Holocaust was not perpetrated solely by a few sadistic psychopaths but by tens of thousands of everyday Germans, Poles, Frenchmen, Austrians, Slovaks—in fact, much of Europe took part."

This framing is the article's most powerful move. It shifts the blame from a singular evil to a collective capacity for cruelty. The author details the specific case of Major Wilhelm Trapp, a commander who wept openly before ordering his men to shoot Jewish women and children, yet offered them the chance to opt out. Then & Now notes that despite Trapp's visible distress, "statistically speaking you would become a murderer in that way." The narrative choice to place the reader in the shoes of the shooter is effective, stripping away the safety of historical distance. Critics might argue that focusing on the psychological struggle of the killers risks humanizing them too much, but the text is careful to emphasize that their hesitation was fleeting and their compliance was total.

The Mechanics of Complicity

The commentary then dissects the psychological mechanisms that allowed these men to transition from reluctant participants to efficient killers. The author highlights the concept of "compartmentalization" and the incremental nature of the atrocities. As Then & Now puts it, "The steps towards genocide were incremental so as to reduce the resistance that would have been felt if all of the steps had been carried out at once." This progression—from verbal abuse to legal exclusion, to ghettoization, to starvation, and finally to mass murder—created a slippery slope where each step seemed manageable compared to the last.

The text describes how the initial massacres were so psychologically damaging that the Nazi leadership had to innovate. "Division of labor reduced the burden as some worked on the trains, some were guards, others filled the gas," the author explains. This bureaucratic distancing is presented as a chillingly modern solution to the problem of conscience. The article cites historian Christopher Browning to support the claim that "the literature on genocide research in general supports that initial executions are usually distressing but the distress quickly subsides with each subsequent death." This observation is both terrifying and vital; it suggests that the human mind is capable of adapting to horror with alarming speed.

"Just as there is evil at the heart of every man, there is evil at the heart of even the most civilized human society."

This quote, attributed to journalist Mark Bowden, serves as the philosophical anchor of the piece. It dismantles the Enlightenment optimism that technological and educational progress inevitably leads to moral progress. The author reinforces this by noting that the 20th century was both the most advanced and the most genocidal in history. The text argues that the Holocaust "ended the optimism of what seemed like the inexorable progress of western enlightenment," a sobering conclusion that resonates deeply in an era of renewed global instability.

The Failure of the Inoculation

Finally, the piece turns to the question of prevention, asking how societies can "inoculate" themselves against such descent. The author references the UN definition of genocide to clarify the legal and moral boundaries of the crime, then returns to the psychological factors of conformity and authority. The narrative describes how group dynamics, or "groupthink," allowed individuals to shed personal responsibility. "Anonymity can result in the diminishing of personal and individual responsibility," the author writes, explaining how the uniform and the crowd protected the individual from the weight of their actions.

The account of Police Battalion 101 concludes with a grim statistic: by the end of the war, only 10 to 20 percent of the men had abstained from killing. The author notes that those who refused were often dismissed as cowards but faced no real consequences, yet the majority continued. This highlights the terrifying power of social pressure over individual morality. The text suggests that the "moral vaccine" required is an awareness of these specific psychological traps—propaganda, outgrouping, and the incremental nature of evil. While the article stops short of offering a concrete policy solution, its diagnosis is precise: we must recognize that the capacity for genocide is not a foreign virus but a latent potential within our own social structures.

"A veil of naivety was torn up with the Holocaust. Something happened that was unimaginable until then."

This final reflection, citing philosopher Jürgen Habermas, leaves the reader with a sense of profound vulnerability. The article does not offer easy answers, but it succeeds in making the unimaginable feel terrifyingly possible.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's coverage is a masterclass in using specific, human-scale stories to illuminate a global catastrophe, effectively dismantling the myth of the "evil other." Its greatest strength is the unflinching focus on the psychological adaptability of ordinary men, though it could have explored more deeply why the small minority who refused to kill were able to resist the overwhelming pressure of the group. The reader is left with a clear, unsettling verdict: the Holocaust was not an anomaly of history, but a warning of what happens when the safeguards of civilization are eroded by fear and conformity.
