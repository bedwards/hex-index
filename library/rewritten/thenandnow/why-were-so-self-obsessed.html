Then & Now delivers a startling historical diagnosis for our modern epidemic of narcissism: the very act of looking inward, once a revolutionary path to truth, has curdled into a culture of self-obsession. By tracing the genealogy of the "autobiographical self" from ancient externalism to the opium-fueled introspection of Thomas De Quincey, the author argues that our current fixation on the "inner universe" is not a natural state of being, but a specific, fragile invention of the Romantic era. This is not merely a history lesson; it is a mirror held up to the selfie generation, suggesting that the tools we use to understand ourselves were forged in a time when the "world within" was a radical, dangerous frontier.

The Shift from Gods to Ghosts

The piece begins by dismantling the assumption that self-reflection is timeless. Then & Now writes, "Before De Quincey's time... knowledge traditionally speaking was not about what's in here endogenous but what's out there exogenous." The author effectively illustrates that for the ancient Greeks and medieval Christians, emotions and actions were often attributed to external forces—gods, divine will, or social roles—rather than an internal personality. This framing is crucial because it strips away the modern arrogance of believing we are the sole authors of our own narratives. The argument lands hard when the author notes that in the Christian framework, "the self is always in reference to God... not to discover some true self within."

This historical context is vital, yet it risks oversimplifying the complexity of pre-modern interiority. Critics might note that even Augustine's Confessions, while focused on God, contained profound psychological depth that prefigured modern self-examination. However, Then & Now's point remains robust: the source of authority had shifted from the divine to the individual.

The Opium of the Inner Self

The narrative pivots to the Romantic era, where the "inner self" became a new frontier for exploration. Then & Now highlights Thomas De Quincey, whose 1821 classic Confessions of an English Opium-Eater is presented as a watershed moment. The author quotes De Quincey describing his drug-induced visions as "an apocalypse of the world within me," a phrase that captures the era's obsession with the psyche's hidden depths. Then & Now observes that De Quincey wanted to challenge the norm where people "omit the ugly parts of their character and emphasize their successes," instead choosing to expose his "moral ulcers or scars."

This focus on the "ugly parts" is the piece's most compelling insight. It suggests that modern self-disclosure, often sanitized for social media, has lost the raw, terrifying honesty of De Quincey's work. The author argues that these writers were not just navel-gazing; they were building the foundations of modern psychology. As Then & Now puts it, "The autobiographical self is the model that helps us traverse the world outside and in." This connection between literary invention and psychological reality is the essay's intellectual engine.

The age of self-obsession began when we stopped looking at the stars and started staring into the mirror, convinced the reflection held the secrets of the universe.

The Double-Edged Sword of Introspection

The commentary then addresses the unintended consequences of this shift. While Jean-Jacques Rousseau and William Wordsworth championed the idea that truth could only be found "within the self," this inward gaze eventually produced the narcissism that defines our current moment. Then & Now writes, "On the other hand, the intense focus on the eye produced the narcissism and the obsessions with the self we see everywhere today." The author contrasts the profound, universal lessons found in Wordsworth's poetry with the shallow, performative nature of modern reality TV and "tell-all interviews."

The argument here is nuanced: the same mechanism that allowed for the discovery of individual rights and democracy also paved the way for a culture where "everybody's autobiography" is a commodity. Then & Now notes that we now live in an "era of everybody's autobiography," where even politicians and celebrities sell memoirs. This is a sharp critique of how the Romantic ideal of the "unique self" has been flattened into a marketing strategy. The author suggests that while the "world within" was once a place of transcendence, it has become a trap of isolation.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's strongest achievement is linking the high-minded philosophy of the Romantics to the low-brow narcissism of the social media age, revealing a direct lineage between De Quincey's opium dreams and the modern selfie. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its tendency to romanticize the pre-modern "external" world, potentially ignoring the ways in which external forces can also suppress the self. Ultimately, the piece serves as a necessary warning: the challenge of the century is not to abandon the inner self, but to reconcile the "world within" with the "world without" before we lose our grip on reality entirely.
