Then & Now delivers a startling thesis: the viral generosity of MrBeast is not a break from American capitalism, but its most sophisticated, postmodern advertisement. While most coverage fixates on the spectacle of buried-alive stunts or the dollar amounts given away, the author argues these acts are carefully manufactured myths designed to obscure the corrosive realities of corporate power. This is essential listening for anyone who believes philanthropy and profit are mutually exclusive, as it exposes how the very machinery of wealth extraction now wears a friendly face.

The Modern Frontier Myth

The piece begins by dismantling the idea that MrBeast is a purely organic phenomenon. Then & Now writes, "what we can see through mr beast is not speculation but a perfect postmodern example of how capitalist mythology is manufactured." To prove this, the author draws a striking parallel to Davy Crockett, the 19th-century frontiersman whose legend was carefully constructed by politicians to promote a specific brand of rugged individualism. Just as Crockett was exaggerated to serve the political needs of the Jacksonian era, MrBeast is framed as the "self-made man" of the digital age, a narrative that conveniently ignores the systemic structures enabling his rise.

The author suggests that this myth serves a vital function for the culture that created it. "Crockett's meteoric rise became mythical because it seemed to come from nowhere," Then & Now notes, before revealing the historical reality: "his image was actually carefully crafted by politicians and publishers who believed his image would be useful for their calls libertarianism on the frontier." This comparison is the article's strongest intellectual move, forcing the reader to view MrBeast not as a unique anomaly, but as the latest iteration of a centuries-old strategy to sell a specific version of American identity.

Stories can be used and misused, appropriated and twisted, reimagined and re-written in calculative and disingenuous ways.

Critics might argue that equating a content creator with a 19th-century political figure stretches the analogy too far, ignoring the genuine agency of modern influencers. However, the author's point remains potent: the function of the myth matters more than the origin story. Whether in 1830 or 2024, the story of the "ordinary guy" achieving extraordinary success serves to validate the status quo.

Philanthro-Capitalism as Spectacle

Moving from history to the present, the commentary shifts to the mechanics of MrBeast's business model. The author introduces the concept of "philanthro-capitalism," a term coined by economist Matthew Bishop, to describe the merger of business efficiency with charitable giving. Then & Now explains that MrBeast's giveaways are not acts of pure altruism but are "paid for by a sponsor," turning generosity into a revenue-generating engine. "It's a kind of for-profit philanthropy," the author writes, noting that the motivation is twofold: attracting views and feeling good about helping people.

This section effectively deconstructs the emotional appeal of the videos. The author points out that while the acts are commendable, they are inextricably linked to the profit motive. "MrBeast has become something of a philanthropy capitalist himself, turning giveaways into advertising revenue, food drives into profitable entertainment, and homelessness into a kind of spectacle that pays." This framing challenges the viewer to question the purity of the intent behind the spectacle. Is the goal to solve homelessness, or to create a viral moment that sells a burger chain?

The article draws a sharp line between the surface-level generosity and the underlying corporate machinery. "At its worst, philanthro-capitalism allows sponsors, donors, and big business to whitewash, greenwash, and conceal or draw attention away from their otherwise questionable business tactics." This is a crucial distinction. The author argues that the positive PR generated by these stunts acts as a shield, allowing corporations to deflect criticism from their core operations, such as pollution or labor exploitation.

The Gilded Age Parallel

To ground this modern phenomenon in historical precedent, Then & Now pivots to the "Gilded Age" and the "robber barons" like Andrew Carnegie. The author details how these industrialists, despite their ruthless treatment of workers, became celebrated for their philanthropy. "Carnegie was the forerunner to the new capitalist philanthropist model," the text states, writing that the millionaire should be a "trustee of the poor." Yet, this benevolence was often a direct response to public backlash against brutal labor practices.

The author highlights the hypocrisy of this era with chilling specificity. While Carnegie built libraries, his workers faced "grim working conditions" and "old age at 40." The text notes that after the violent suppression of the Homestead strike, where workers were killed, Carnegie realized the power of public relations. "Carnegie knew that there was more than one way to tip the balance of public opinion," Then & Now observes, noting how he used acts of kindness to distract from the violence of his business practices.

This historical lens provides a sobering context for MrBeast's activities. The author implies that the current trend of influencer philanthropy is merely a digital evolution of the same tactic: using charity to sanitize a reputation built on exploitation. "The wealthy paternalist could dispense with their wealth in a responsible way... while discouraging the slothful, the drunken, the unworthy," the author writes, quoting Carnegie's own justification for his giving. This suggests that the narrative of the "benevolent billionaire" is a tool to maintain social order rather than a genuine solution to inequality.

At its worst, philanthro-capitalism allows sponsors, donors, and big business to whitewash, greenwash, and conceal or draw attention away from their otherwise questionable business tactics.

A counterargument worth considering is that even if the motives are mixed, the outcomes—trees planted, trash removed, money given—are undeniably positive. The author acknowledges this, stating, "at its best, philanthro-capitalism does some important work." However, the piece insists that we must look beyond the immediate good to the systemic implications. If the solution to social problems is dependent on the whims of wealthy individuals who profit from the very systems causing those problems, the long-term viability of such solutions is questionable.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's most compelling argument is that MrBeast is not an outlier but a symptom of a deeper cultural shift where capitalism has learned to wear a mask of generosity. The piece's greatest strength lies in its historical grounding, effectively using the Davy Crockett and Andrew Carnegie parallels to demystify the modern influencer economy. Its biggest vulnerability is the potential to dismiss the genuine impact of the charitable acts themselves, risking a cynical view that ignores the tangible good being done. Readers should watch for how this model of "spectacle philanthropy" evolves as corporate influence over digital media continues to tighten.
