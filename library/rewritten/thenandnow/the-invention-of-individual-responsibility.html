Then & Now delivers a jarring historical correction: the modern obsession with "100% responsibility" is not a timeless truth, but a relatively new invention used to dismantle social safety nets. By tracing the concept from medieval obligations to eugenicist pseudoscience, the piece reveals how blaming the poor for their poverty has shifted from a moral failing to a biological destiny, and finally to a cultural defect. This matters now because the rhetoric of "no excuses" is currently being weaponized to justify cutting welfare and ignoring systemic inequality.

The Shift from Obligation to Excuse

The author begins by dismantling the idea that individual responsibility has always been the dominant framework for understanding poverty. In earlier eras, the focus was on the community's duty to care, not the individual's duty to succeed. Then & Now writes, "For most of history poverty was a fact of life for almost everyone... it was often not a question of who was responsible for their own poverty but who was responsible for care." This reframing is crucial; it reminds us that the social contract once prioritized survival over self-optimization.

The text highlights how institutions like the church and feudal lords were legally and morally bound to support the vulnerable. As Then & Now puts it, "a 1536 act of parliament insisted that local authorities... extend their good and charitable arms and contributions as the poor impotent lame feeble sick and diseased people being not able to work may be provided and relieved." This historical evidence effectively counters the modern narrative that relying on others is inherently shameful. The author argues that the transition to capitalism destabilized these bonds, replacing predictable agricultural life with volatile labor markets that created a new, unpredictable class of unemployed people.

"The idea of poverty increasingly became a moral one in the 19th century being labelled a pauper someone in receipt of poor relief became a stigma a sign of moral failure a sin."

This shift from structural necessity to moral failing is the piece's central pivot. Then & Now notes that officials in Philadelphia began claiming that "the poor in consequence of vice constitute here and everywhere by far the greatest part of the poor." The commentary here is sharp: by attributing poverty to "intemperance" and "shameful indolence," society could absolve itself of the duty to provide aid. Critics might argue that personal agency always plays a role, but the author's point is that the scale of systemic causes was deliberately ignored to justify austerity.

The Biological Trap and the Culture of Poverty

As the 19th century progressed, the blame game evolved from moral judgment to biological determinism. The author details how eugenics linked heredity to destitution, creating a terrifying logic where poverty was seen as an unchangeable genetic trait. Then & Now writes, "Charles Davenport a leading U.S eugenicist wanted to purify our body politics of the feeble-minded and the criminalistic..." This section is particularly disturbing, showing how "scientific" racism and classism were used to justify sterilization laws and the exclusion of immigrants.

The piece argues that this biological framing was a dead end that ignored the reality of economic hardship. Instead, it paved the way for the mid-20th-century theory of the "culture of poverty." Then & Now explains that theorists like Edward Banfield blamed poverty on "a cultural pattern of amoral familialism" rather than economic structures. The author suggests this was a convenient way for Western academics to dismiss the structural failures of capitalism during the Cold War.

"The focus is often on a responsibility for oneself atomized isolated entrepreneurial maybe but rather than on obligations to others or duties to our communities."

This observation cuts to the heart of the modern self-help industry. The author critiques figures like Jordan Peterson and Dr. John Izzo, who promise that "taking responsibility changes everything." While these messages sound empowering, Then & Now argues they "weakened social bonds justified the dismantling of welfare programmes encourages inwardness and blame." The commentary here is vital: it exposes how the language of empowerment is often co-opted to shift the burden of systemic failure onto the individual.

A counterargument worth considering is that emphasizing personal responsibility can indeed be a powerful motivator for those trapped in cycles of poverty. However, the author's historical evidence suggests that when this concept is used to deny structural support, it becomes a tool of oppression rather than liberation.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's most compelling argument is that the "invention" of hyper-individual responsibility serves a political function: it allows society to ignore the root causes of inequality. The piece's greatest strength is its historical depth, proving that our current obsession with "100% responsibility" is a recent and fragile construct. The biggest vulnerability is that it offers no clear roadmap for reversing this cultural shift, leaving the reader with a clear diagnosis but no prescribed cure. Readers should watch for how this narrative continues to shape policy debates on welfare and social safety nets in the coming years.
