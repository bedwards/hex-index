Then & Now delivers a bracing corrective to the sanitized, self-help version of Stoicism dominating social media, arguing that the ancient philosophy is not a tool for tranquility but a philosophy of despair born from a brutal, violent world. The piece is notable for refusing to let modern readers off the hook with easy quotes, instead forcing a confrontation with the bloody reality of Roman life that made Stoicism a survival mechanism rather than a lifestyle brand.

The Illusion of Control

The author immediately dismantles the popular "dichotomy of control" championed by figures like Ryan Holiday. Then & Now writes, "The problem with this neat line between what we can and can't control is that on closer inspection it becomes a little hazy." The commentary effectively illustrates how this binary collapses in practice; we may not control the outcome of a video or a political decision, but we exert influence through effort, strategy, and engagement. By reducing the world to a rigid split between "internals" and "externals," the ancient system creates a false sense of detachment.

This argument is strengthened by the historical context provided. The author notes that Zeno of Citium, the founder of the school, had to invent the concept of "preferred indifference" to acknowledge that humans are dependent on food, shelter, and relationships. Then & Now observes, "The philosophy has no way of truly valuing some things rather than others; it cuts the world off with a stoic Guillotine." This is a striking metaphor that captures the emotional cost of the philosophy. Critics might note that modern Stoics argue this detachment is precisely the point—to prevent suffering—but the author's point stands: you cannot value something without acknowledging your connection to it.

We're not people looking out at nature but we are nature reflecting on itself. To divide the world is to alienate ourselves from it.

A Philosophy Forged in Blood

The piece shifts from abstract logic to the visceral history of the Roman Empire, providing the missing context for why Stoics were so obsessed with death and control. Then & Now details the reign of Nero, describing how the emperor "murdered two wives, one of them was pregnant," and had his own mother killed in a specially constructed boat. The author argues that the Stoic practice of premeditating misfortune was not a spiritual exercise but a psychological shield against a world where "no one could be trusted."

The historical evidence is overwhelming. The text describes Emperor Gaius laughing at the thought of cutting the throats of his dinner guests and the crucifixion of 6,000 slaves along a 120-mile stretch of road. Then & Now writes, "Stoicism is not a philosophy of life; it's a philosophy of despair." This conclusion is the article's most provocative claim. It suggests that the calmness Seneca and Marcus Aurelius sought was not a natural state of being but a desperate reaction to a reality where "hunger, pestilence, warfare and tragedy were commonplace." A counterargument worth considering is that the philosophy's endurance proves its utility beyond mere despair, offering a framework for dignity even in the face of chaos. However, the author's framing of the ancient world's brutality makes the Stoic retreat into the self feel less like wisdom and more like a necessary trauma response.

The Missing Physics

Finally, the commentary addresses a critical omission in modern Stoic marketing: the loss of the system's logical and physical foundations. The author reminds readers that for the ancients, ethics could not be separated from physics and theology. Then & Now quotes Cicero, noting that the Stoic system is "so well constructed so firmly jointed and welded into one with such close interconnection of the parts that if you alter a single letter you shake the whole structure." By stripping away the belief that the universe is ordered by a divine plan, modern interpretations leave the ethical advice floating without its anchor.

The author argues that when we remove the ancient belief that "the entire universe the physics of it was ordered exactly how God had planned it," the instruction to "live in accordance with nature" loses its meaning. Without that cosmic order, the Stoic's attempt to separate themselves from the world becomes an act of alienation rather than alignment. The piece concludes that we cannot simply adopt the coping mechanisms of the Stoics without accepting their worldview, a worldview that is fundamentally incompatible with the modern understanding of a chaotic, non-teleological universe.

Bottom Line

Then & Now succeeds in exposing the hollowness of pop-Stoicism by grounding it in the terrifying reality of its origins, proving that the ancient philosophy was a survival tactic for a violent age rather than a universal guide for modern living. Its strongest asset is the historical depth that reframes Stoic detachment as a symptom of despair, though it risks underestimating the adaptability of philosophical systems across millennia. Readers should watch for how this critique reshapes the conversation around resilience in an era of modern uncertainty.
