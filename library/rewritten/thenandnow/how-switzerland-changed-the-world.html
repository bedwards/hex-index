Then & Now makes a startling claim: that the modern Western psyche—our very sense of self, our obsession with individualism, and our democratic ideals—was forged not in the grand capitals of Paris or London, but in the rugged, fragmented valleys of Switzerland. This is not a standard travelogue; it is a forensic investigation into how a "human creation" of a nation, defying natural borders and feudal logic, exported a philosophy that reshaped the world through its most famous, contradictory son, Jean-Jacques Rousseau.

The Human-Made Nation

The piece opens by dismantling the myth that nations are inevitable geographic destinies. Then & Now writes, "unlike most countries it's not really the product of natural borders that's because it's a very human creation." This is a crucial distinction. While most European powers coalesced around rivers or mountain ranges, the Swiss Confederation was a deliberate political pact among disparate groups—peasants, herders, and guilds—who refused to be absorbed by the Holy Roman Empire. The author highlights the 1291 founding charter, where three cantons swore to accept no judge who was not "one of our own and an inhabitant of the valleys." This was a radical rejection of external authority, a "comrades of the oath" alliance that prioritized local autonomy over imperial decree.

The commentary effectively uses the "Saga of Liberation" to explain how a shared mythology can sustain a nation. Even when the reality was messier—with powerful clans often monopolizing power—the idea of freedom remained potent. Then & Now notes that "the Swiss idea of freedom was very real it would expand in something more powerful as Switzerland took shape in the form of a Modern Nation." This framing is compelling because it separates the ideal from the imperfect execution, suggesting that the power of a political concept often lies in its aspirational mythos rather than its immediate bureaucratic reality. Critics might argue that this romanticizes a system that was, for centuries, an oligarchy disguised as a democracy, but the author acknowledges this tension, noting that "a few Clans typically monopolized offices" even as the rhetoric of liberty grew.

The privilege of all male citizens of communes to participate in decision making... set the Swiss apart from aristocratic Europe.

The Crucible of Geneva

The narrative then pivots to Geneva, the city that produced Rousseau. Here, the author identifies a specific cultural friction: the clash between ancient, frugal republican values and the seductive, corrupting influence of new wealth. As the Enlightenment arrived, Geneva was a "contradictory Place full of different ideas" where the church warned that "everyday impurity worldly vanity and luxury are growing." Then & Now writes that "the importation of French fashions frenchification luxury was traditionally associated with indolence and vice." This context is vital; it explains why Rousseau, a child of this city, would become the world's fiercest critic of modern civilization.

The author argues that Rousseau's entire philosophy was a reaction to the hypocrisy of his hometown. The city preached liberty while restricting voting rights to a tiny elite, and preached virtue while worshipping wealth. Then & Now observes that "Geneva created Rousseau he created the essence of his character." This causal link is the article's strongest analytical move. It suggests that the most influential thinker of the modern age was not a product of abstract theory, but a direct response to the specific, toxic mix of oligarchy and emerging capitalism in a small Swiss city. The author paraphrases the historian Helena Rosenblatt to emphasize that Geneva was the "key to his thought," grounding high philosophy in local history.

The Critique of Politeness

Rousseau's famous 1750 essay, which argued that the progress of arts and sciences corrupts morals, is presented not as a Luddite rant, but as a psychological breakthrough. Then & Now writes, "he said that the Arts and Sciences spread garlands of flowers over the iron chains of society." This quote captures the essence of Rousseau's insight: that social politeness is often a mask for domination and insincerity. The author connects this to modern psychology, noting that "like Freud who very much admired Russo he's saying what appears on the surface is not always the truth of our actions and desires."

The piece brilliantly unpacks Rousseau's observation that "jealousy suspicion fear coldness Reserve hate and fraud like constantly concealed under that uniform and deceitful veil of politeness." This is the core of the argument: modern society forces us to wear a mask, prioritizing reputation over truth. Then & Now asks the reader to reflect: "how often do you realize that you've said something because that's what you think your boss wants to hear." This application of 18th-century philosophy to modern workplace dynamics makes the historical analysis feel immediate and urgent. The author argues that Rousseau was the first to identify the "proto-psychological" damage of social conformity, a damage that defines our current age of performative identity.

We do not ask whether a book is useful but whether it's well written rewards are lavished on wit and Ingenuity while virtue is left unhonored.

Bottom Line

Then & Now succeeds in reframing Swiss history not as a footnote to European power struggles, but as the incubator of modern consciousness. The strongest part of the argument is the direct line drawn from Geneva's specific social contradictions to Rousseau's universal critique of inequality and hypocrisy. However, the piece occasionally leans too heavily on the "myth" of Swiss liberty, glossing over how long it took for those ideals to become reality for the average citizen. The reader should watch for how this "human-made" model of democracy is tested in an era of globalized luxury and digital conformity, where the "veil of politeness" has never been more opaque.
