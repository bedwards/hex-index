Then & Now delivers a forensic takedown of Tucker Carlson's intellectual evolution, arguing that the most influential conservative on television has traded classical libertarianism for a paranoid, anti-democratic populism that relies on cherry-picked data rather than evidence. This piece is notable not just for dissecting Carlson's rhetoric, but for rigorously fact-checking the specific anecdotes he uses to justify a worldview that frames immigration as an existential threat to American society.

The Shift from Libertarian to Nationalist

The author begins by tracing Carlson's ideological drift, noting that he started as a "pro-capitalist pro-immigration classical libertarian thinker" before slowly pivoting toward a nationalist populist worldview. Then & Now writes, "Carlson embodies the Fox News formula as it's developed over its 26 years on air: that the viewer is under siege... that the elites, corrupt, lazy, greedy and incompetent are not just out to get you but that it's a concerted effort, a conspiracy, an evil plan." This framing is crucial because it explains why Carlson's arguments often feel more like a thriller novel than political analysis. The author suggests that this conspiratorial lens is not a bug in Carlson's system, but the primary feature that drives his massive ratings.

The commentary highlights how Carlson's book, Ship of Fools, diagnoses a new class system where both major parties serve the wealthy. Then & Now notes, "He writes the rich and I reside on the other side of a rope line from everyone else... Suddenly America has a new class system and he says Republicans ignore this but in a break from the past Democrats now ignore this too." This observation lands effectively because it validates a genuine economic anxiety shared by many voters, even as the author prepares to dismantle the solutions Carlson proposes. By acknowledging the validity of the inequality diagnosis, the author makes the subsequent critique of Carlson's immigration arguments more potent.

Tucker has a very specific view on the world, some of which I actually surprisingly agree with, but under the surface is a conspiratorial and paranoid worldview and a skepticism about the merits of democracy itself.

The Immigration Argument and the Failure of Evidence

The core of the piece focuses on how Carlson links mass immigration to wage suppression and cultural destabilization. Then & Now paraphrases Carlson's economic claim: "He writes for decades ever increasing immigration has been the rule in the United States... immigration for politicians and business people is a win-win... but it turns out the average person isn't for it." The author then pivots to a rigorous fact-check, exposing the thinness of Carlson's evidence base. The commentary points out that Carlson relies on a single anecdote about Storm Lake, Iowa, claiming that "the flood of cheap labor destroyed the local labor union and depressed wages." However, the author reveals that the original New York Times story Carlson cites actually attributes wage stagnation to corporate competition and efficiency gains, not immigrants, and occurred before the demographic shift Carlson describes.

The author's critique here is devastatingly specific. Then & Now writes, "I can find no studies, zero, or any evidence for the claim that immigration depressed wages in Storm Lake." This exposes a pattern of confirmation bias where Carlson selects data points that fit a pre-selected worldview while ignoring the broader academic consensus. The piece contrasts Carlson's reliance on a 1980 study of the Mariel boatlift—where he claims wages fell by 37 percent—with modern reappraisals that show a much smaller impact, or no impact at all. As Then & Now puts it, "Choosing that one study because it has the most dramatic figure is more than just selective; it's misleading." Critics might note that immigration debates are complex and that some localized wage effects are real, but the author rightly argues that Carlson's presentation lacks the nuance required for a serious policy discussion.

The Cultural Fear and the Rejection of Democracy

Beyond economics, the author examines Carlson's cultural arguments, which the commentary identifies as even more dangerous. Then & Now writes, "Immigration at this scale he says destabilizes our society... human beings aren't wired for that they can't digest change at this pace it disorients them." The author points out that this rhetoric moves beyond policy critique into a rejection of pluralism itself. The piece highlights Carlson's assertion that "countries don't hang together simply because they need a reason; what's ours now?" This question, the author argues, reveals a fundamental skepticism about whether a diverse democracy can function at all. The commentary notes that while there are honest debates to be had about immigration, Carlson's approach is defined by "very selective reasoning" and a refusal to engage with the thousands of academic studies that contradict his narrative.

The author concludes that Carlson's worldview is not just wrong on the facts, but potentially corrosive to democratic norms. Then & Now writes, "Under the surface and often bubbling above explicitly... is a conspiratorial and paranoid worldview and a skepticism about the merits of democracy itself." This is the piece's most alarming conclusion: that the most popular voice on the right is increasingly questioning the very system he operates within. The author suggests that this paranoia is a deliberate strategy to keep viewers in a state of siege, ensuring loyalty to a narrative that blames elites and outsiders for every societal ill.

Bottom Line

Then & Now's strongest move is the meticulous dissection of Carlson's data, proving that his populist appeal rests on a foundation of cherry-picked anecdotes and ignored consensus. The piece's biggest vulnerability is that it assumes a rational audience; it does not fully address why millions of viewers find emotional resonance in Carlson's paranoia even when the facts don't add up. Readers should watch for how this populist, anti-immigrant narrative continues to reshape the Republican party, regardless of its factual accuracy.
