Then & Now cuts through the noise of the 'woke' debate by asking a question most commentators avoid: what if the data we rely on to prove a culture war is actually hiding the truth? The author's most distinctive claim isn't that cancel culture doesn't exist, but that the narrative of a 'woke mind virus' collapses when you stop reading headlines and start reading the court records and termination files. For busy listeners trying to separate signal from noise, this piece offers a rare, data-driven autopsy of the very cases used to fuel the outrage.

The Definition Problem

The author begins by dismantling the vague, catch-all nature of the term 'woke,' arguing that it has become a meaningless label for anything from critical race theory to Twitter outrage. Then & Now writes, 'I've realized woke means so many different things to so many different people.' This observation is crucial because it exposes the logical flaw in treating 'wokeism' as a monolithic enemy. The author correctly identifies that critics often conflate legitimate censorship with standard social norms, noting that 'some form of censorship is the norm' in classrooms and polite company.

However, the piece makes its boldest move by challenging the idea that this current moment is historically unique. While acknowledging the 'sensorious impulse' feels new to many, the author points out that public shaming is 'perennial,' stretching from the Reformation to McCarthyism. This framing is effective because it forces the listener to consider whether the intensity of the reaction is actually different, or just the medium. Critics might note that the speed and scale of social media do create a unique pressure cooker, but Then & Now wisely refuses to let that novelty excuse a lack of historical perspective.

The woke mob represent a force that is in some sense new and in some significant ways worse than the kind of censorship or repression seen in some places in the past.

The Data Discrepancy

The core of the argument shifts to a forensic examination of the evidence provided by FIRE (the Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression) and the book The Cancelling of the American Mind. Then & Now highlights the alarming statistic that 'more professors have been terminated during the era of cancel culture than in the era of McCarthyism.' This claim, if true, would be a smoking gun for a free speech crisis. The author takes this claim seriously, noting that 'historians will be studying it in 50 to 100 years much like we study the red scare.'

But here is where the commentary turns sharp. The author doesn't just accept the numbers; they interrogate the methodology. By running the FIRE database through an AI tool and cross-referencing specific cases, the author reveals a startling gap between the public narrative and the actual reasons for termination. Then & Now observes, 'Many of them, if not most of them, aren't really about what you call woke stuff at all.' Instead of ideological purity tests, the data shows terminations for 'creating hostile learning environments,' 'past links with the Ku Klux Klan,' and 'sexual harassment.'

This is the piece's strongest contribution: it suggests that the 'cancel culture' narrative is a convenient cover for legitimate disciplinary actions. The author points out that in the case of a professor fired for using a 'phallic prop,' the real reason was sexual harassment, not the prop itself. This reframing is powerful because it restores agency to institutions, suggesting they are firing people for misconduct, not for their political views. A counterargument worth considering is that the line between 'misconduct' and 'ideological policing' is often blurred, and that universities may be using 'harassment' as a pretext to silence dissent. However, the author's insistence on looking at the full context of each case is a necessary corrective to the headline-driven outrage machine.

The Selective Narrative

The author then dissects specific high-profile cases, showing how advocates of the 'woke' narrative often omit inconvenient details. Take the case of Reverend Dr. Bernard Randall, who was dismissed after a sermon. Then & Now writes, 'Doyle leaves out a few inconvenient details,' revealing that Randall's sermon included quotes from Leviticus calling homosexuality an 'abomination' and suggesting women should stay at home. The author contrasts this with the sanitized version that paints Randall as a victim of 'woke hysteria.'

Similarly, the piece tackles the Richard Taylor case, where a professor asked if the 'positives of slavery justify the negatives.' While the initial framing suggests a brave debate on trade and biodiversity, the author uncovers that Taylor had a history of complaints and had told black students they 'wouldn't be here without slavery.' Then & Now notes that 'Luciano and Schlot don't mention... that there were multiple reports about Taylor from before the incident.' This selective storytelling is identified as a key flaw in the anti-woke argument. The author argues that 'being fired for any of these things that I've just talked about seems wrong to me,' but only after establishing that the context often justifies the firing.

The section on book banning further illustrates this point. While critics claim books are being 'burned' or 'done away with,' the author clarifies that in the Waterloo Region case, books like To Kill a Mockingbird were simply removed from the required curriculum. Then & Now writes, 'It changed the curriculum so that to kill a mocking word was no longer required reading.' This distinction between banning and curating is vital, yet often lost in the 'Orwellian' rhetoric. The author's point is clear: 'Curriculums change all the time. That's not totalitarianism.'

The truth is a little bit different. If this is the case he's talking about, then the school board didn't do away with books. Didn't rip them up. It didn't destroy them. It didn't burn them.

Bottom Line

Then & Now delivers a compelling, if uncomfortable, verdict: the 'death of the woke grift' may actually be the death of a convenient myth that allows bad actors to hide misconduct behind the banner of free speech. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to accept the 'cancel culture' narrative at face value, instead demanding a granular look at the data. Its biggest vulnerability is the risk of swinging too far the other way, potentially dismissing legitimate cases of ideological retaliation by over-correcting with examples of genuine misconduct. For the listener, the takeaway is clear: before joining the outrage mob, check the court docket. The truth is rarely as simple as the headline.
