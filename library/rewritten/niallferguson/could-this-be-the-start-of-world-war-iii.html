Niall Ferguson cuts through the alarmist noise of "World War III" headlines by asking a far more dangerous question: are we actually witnessing the slow, regional convergence that defined World War II? In a piece that treats the current conflict not as a sudden explosion but as a calculated escalation within "Cold War II," Ferguson brings a historian's cold eye to the chaos, arguing that the real risk isn't a global conflagration, but a prolonged, economically devastating regional war that could mimic the oil shocks of the 1970s.

The Historical Lens on Escalation

Ferguson immediately dismantles the simplistic comparison to 1914. He argues that unlike the rapid mobilization of empires in World War I, the current situation mirrors the fragmented nature of the 1930s. He writes, "World War II was in truth a series of regional conflicts that did not converge into a unitary global conflict until the end of 1941." This distinction is crucial; it suggests that the path to a global war is not inevitable but requires specific, preventable escalations. By framing the current U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran as a potential "staging post" rather than a final act, Ferguson forces the reader to consider the timeline of escalation rather than just the immediate violence.

The author's base case is surprisingly restrained. He posits that "the U.S.-Israeli war against the Islamic Republic of Iran cannot and will not be prolonged at the current frenetic intensity beyond a few weeks." This is a bold claim given the rhetoric from Washington and Tel Aviv, but it rests on a pragmatic assessment of logistics and political will. Ferguson suggests that while the immediate economic impact will be sharp, it will likely pale in comparison to the sustained shocks of the past. He notes that the effects on the oil market "will not be as large or as sustained as they were in Gulf War I... and will be substantially less than the oil shocks of 1973–74 and 1979."

"Blitzkrieg only works if you don't run out of Blitz."

This metaphor serves as the piece's central tension. Ferguson acknowledges the overwhelming firepower currently being unleashed—citing that the U.S. struck more than 1,700 targets in the first three days—but warns that sustainability is the true bottleneck. He points out that while missile counts are high, the "Shahed drones" remain a wild card that armchair strategists have dangerously underestimated. Critics might argue that Ferguson underestimates the resilience of the Iranian regime and the potential for asymmetric retaliation that doesn't rely on traditional stockpiles, but his focus on the exhaustion of offensive capabilities offers a necessary counter-narrative to the "infinite war" fear.

The Economic Shadow of the Strait

The most compelling part of Ferguson's analysis is his deep dive into the energy market, where he connects current events to the traumatic economic history of the 1970s. He draws a direct line from the 1973 oil embargo, triggered by U.S. support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War, to the current crisis. He writes, "If this conflict drags on, and with it the disruption of the global oil market, the worst-case scenario is that we risk reenacting the 1970s." This historical grounding is vital; it reminds readers that the geopolitical stakes are not just about borders, but about the price of gas and the stability of the global economy.

Ferguson highlights the fragility of the Strait of Hormuz, noting that "a handful of strikes on tankers in the first phase of the war sufficed to scare both shippers and insurers, so that traffic is down by 90 percent or more." The paralysis here is psychological as much as physical. He details how the closure of the strait has already forced 1.5 million barrels per day of Iraqi crude offline, a figure that could double. The author questions whether the U.S. can effectively manage this, noting skepticism about President Trump's proposal to use the Development Finance Corporation to underwrite war risk insurance. Ferguson observes that "the U.S. Navy is too busy fighting a war to provide escort services to mostly foreign shipping companies," a stark admission of the limits of American power projection.

"We are well on our way to achieving the objectives of #EpicFury... Destroy their missile launchers. Destroy their missile factories. Destroy their Navy."

Ferguson quotes Secretary of State Marco Rubio's aggressive rhetoric but immediately contextualizes it with the reality of the battlefield. He notes that while the initial strikes have degraded Iran's ability to fire missiles, the regime is not yet broken. The destruction of the Assembly of Experts' building and the targeting of hard-liners suggest a strategy of "regime alteration," yet Ferguson warns that "the regime may be in its death throes, but it is not yet dead." This nuance is often lost in the binary of "victory or defeat" that dominates political discourse.

The Unanswered Questions of a Regional War

The article concludes by listing ten critical questions that remain unanswered, ranging from the duration of the conflict to the domestic political sustainability of the war for the Republican Party. Ferguson writes, "How long can the Republican Party sustain this domestically in a midterm year?" This question shifts the focus from the battlefield to the ballot box, suggesting that domestic political fatigue could be the ultimate limiter of the war's scope. He also touches on the global spread, noting that the conflict is already affecting more than a dozen countries, from Lebanon to Cyprus to Azerbaijan.

The author's assessment of the U.S. economy is notably calm. He argues that "we shall need to see much worse news and a much bigger oil price spike for real pain to be felt on both Wall Street and Main Street." This confidence stands in contrast to the panic in the media, suggesting that the U.S. economy has enough buffer to absorb a shock that would cripple Europe or East Asia. However, a counterargument worth considering is that the current low inflation environment is fragile, and a sudden spike in energy costs could reignite inflationary pressures just as the Federal Reserve is trying to cut rates.

"The most recent U.S.-Israeli onslaught on Iran may look to future historians like a staging post to a global conflagration. But I stress 'may.'"

This final qualification encapsulates Ferguson's entire thesis: the future is not written, but the path to disaster is paved with specific, avoidable choices. He urges the reader to look beyond the immediate headlines of "World War III" and understand the mechanics of "Cold War II," where regional conflicts simmer without necessarily boiling over into a global cataclysm.

Bottom Line

Niall Ferguson's strongest contribution is his refusal to accept the "World War III" narrative as a foregone conclusion, instead grounding the crisis in the specific economic and military constraints of the 1970s and 1990s. His argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the rationality of actors who have shown a willingness to escalate beyond traditional limits, particularly regarding the Strait of Hormuz. Readers should watch the next few weeks not for the start of a world war, but for the resilience of the oil market and the political will to sustain a high-intensity campaign without a clear exit strategy.
