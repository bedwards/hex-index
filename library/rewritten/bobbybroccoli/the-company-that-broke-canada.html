BobbyBroccoli doesn't just recount the rise and fall of Nortel; he exposes how a national identity crisis, rooted in the fear of American economic dominance, accidentally engineered the very dependency it sought to escape. The piece is notable for reframing a corporate collapse not as a story of greed, but as the tragic culmination of a century of "branch plant" economics where Canada mimicked its neighbor rather than innovating. This is a history lesson that feels less like a lecture and more like an autopsy of a national dream.

The Myth of Invention and the Reality of Marketing

The narrative begins by dismantling a cherished Canadian myth: that Alexander Graham Bell invented the telephone in Canada. BobbyBroccoli writes, "The Canadian claim to the telephone is frankly very silly," noting that while Bell wrote about the mechanism in Ontario, he didn't have a working prototype until two years later in Boston. The author argues that history favors the first to market, not the first to invent, pointing out that Bell secured the patent simply because he "hired a better lawyer." This framing is effective because it immediately sets a tone of skepticism toward nationalistic narratives, preparing the reader for a story where perception often overrides reality.

"The history of industry is not about the first to invent but the first to Market."

BobbyBroccoli then pivots to the broader context of the US-Canada relationship, describing it as a lopsided dynamic where Canada is perpetually defined in opposition to its giant neighbor. He quotes former Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau: "Living next to you is in some ways like sleeping with an elephant. No matter how friendly and even tempered is the beast... one is affected by every twitch and grunt." This metaphor perfectly captures the anxiety that drove Canadian policy for over a century. The author suggests that Canada's economic struggles stem from a defensive posture against American influence, a stance that ironically made the country more reliant on American capital and technology.

Critics might note that attributing all economic missteps to anti-American sentiment oversimplifies complex global market forces, but the author's focus on the psychological underpinnings of policy offers a compelling, if partial, explanation.

The Branch Plant Economy

The core of the argument lies in the concept of the "branch plant economy." BobbyBroccoli explains how the Canadian government's "National Policy," designed to protect domestic industry with tariffs, backfired spectacularly. Instead of forcing American companies to build factories in Canada, it led to a loophole where companies like AT&T shipped parts north for minimal assembly just to bypass taxes. The author writes, "Parts would be shipped up from the US, assembled in Canada, completely negating the import tax... always the branch plant, never the brains." This is a devastating critique of protectionist policies that prioritized political posturing over genuine industrial strategy.

The narrative highlights the symbiotic yet toxic relationship between AT&T and its Canadian subsidiary, Northern Electric. BobbyBroccoli notes that Northern Electric "got access to technology that no one else did without having to do any R&D. They just took the designs that Western gave them, made their tweaks, and sold them to Canadians." This reliance on imported innovation created a fragile foundation. When AT&T was forced to divest its foreign holdings due to antitrust issues, the Canadian subsidiary was abruptly cut off from free patent licensing. The CEO of Northern Electric described the moment poignantly: "We are the child of wealthy parents... this is the boot that kicked us out the door." The author uses this quote to illustrate the sudden, jarring transition from a protected existence to a competitive reality.

"We've made a virtue of imitation."

This section lands because it connects abstract economic policy to tangible human consequences. The author argues that Canada's refusal to invest in its own research and development, preferring instead to copy American successes, left the country ill-prepared for the technological shifts of the late 20th century.

The Avro Arrow and the Cost of Capitulation

The commentary then shifts to the Avro Arrow, a stealth interceptor jet that BobbyBroccoli describes as "one of if not the best in its class in the world." The cancellation of this project by Prime Minister John Diefenbaker is presented as a pivotal moment where Canada chose American defense policy over its own industrial potential. The author writes, "The real failure here was that the government refused to support a transition for the facility it had already spent millions on... a facility that could have been converted to civilian aircraft and given Canada its own equivalent to Boeing." This argument suggests that the cancellation was not just a military decision but a strategic blunder that crippled Canada's aerospace ambitions.

BobbyBroccoli points out the irony that Diefenbaker, who campaigned on the idea that a Liberal victory would make Canada a "virtual 49th economic state," ultimately capitulated to American pressure. The author notes that by the 1960s, 58% of Canada's manufacturing was owned by foreign firms, with 80% of that being American. This data underscores the depth of the dependency the National Policy was supposed to prevent. The piece argues that the fear of becoming a "49th state" led to policies that ensured Canada remained a resource colony and a consumer market, rather than a manufacturing powerhouse.

Critics might argue that the Avro Arrow was financially unsustainable regardless of American pressure, but the author's focus on the lost potential for a domestic aerospace industry remains a powerful counter-narrative to the official historical record.

Bottom Line

BobbyBroccoli's strongest move is connecting the specific corporate failure of Nortel to the broader, century-long pattern of Canadian economic anxiety. By framing the company's collapse as the inevitable result of a "branch plant" mentality, the author provides a deeper, more structural explanation than the usual narratives of executive hubris. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its tendency to view all Canadian economic history through the lens of American dominance, potentially overlooking internal political failures and global market dynamics. However, the central thesis—that a nation that refuses to innovate for itself is doomed to fail when the safety net is removed—is a stark and necessary warning for any economy relying on imitation.
