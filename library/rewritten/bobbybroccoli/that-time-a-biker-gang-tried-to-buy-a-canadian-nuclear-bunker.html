BobbyBroccoli transforms a bizarre local rumor into a masterclass in digital detective work, proving that the truth about a nuclear bunker's near-sale to a criminal syndicate is stranger than fiction. While many might dismiss the tale of the Hell's Angels bidding on a Cold War relic as urban legend, the author uncovers a verifiable paper trail involving government panic, inflated buybacks, and a specific offer of $1.3 million that changed the fate of a Canadian facility.

The Anatomy of a Rumor

The investigation begins not with a breakthrough, but with a dead end: a vague family anecdote about a biker gang and a nuclear shelter. BobbyBroccoli writes, "my dad told me that very strange rumor and it's so absurd and specific that it begs the question where did it come from." This opening is crucial; it establishes the author's skepticism immediately, grounding the narrative in a relatable search for truth rather than a sensationalist hunt for clicks. By tracing the rumor back to a single paragraph in a 2001 Globe and Mail article, the author highlights how easily misinformation spreads. The article claimed the bunker was in Alberta and demolished for security reasons, but BobbyBroccoli notes the location error and the odd omission of the specific groups involved.

The author's methodology shines as they cross-reference this initial clue with Wikipedia and historical records. "A quick trip to wikipedia clarified a bunch of things for me but namely that this wasn't the only diefenbunker," BobbyBroccoli explains. This distinction is vital, as it separates the famous Ottawa museum from the obscure Penhold, Alberta facility that was actually the subject of the sale. The author effectively dismantles the initial confusion, showing how the "broken telephone" effect likely added the racist group and car theft ring rumors to the original biker gang story. Critics might argue that relying on Wikipedia as a primary source is risky, yet the author uses it correctly as a navigational tool to find the specific, verifiable details in the archives.

The feds had apparently sold the bunker to two red deer businessmen in 1995 for a total of four hundred and seventy two thousand dollars but by 1997 the businessmen were already looking to sell it.

The Paper Trail and the Panic

Once the location is pinned down, the narrative shifts from rumor to financial history. BobbyBroccoli uncovers a timeline where the federal government sold the bunker, watched it hit the secondary market, and then panicked when criminal organizations showed interest. The author cites a 1997 United Press International article quoting RCMP Corporal Judy Best, who expressed "serious concern with fortified properties such as the former penhold bunker being purchased by any criminal organization." This quote anchors the story in official government anxiety, moving the plot from a quirky real estate deal to a national security issue.

The financial details uncovered are staggering. The government sold the facility for roughly $472,000, only to buy it back for $1.25 million after the Hell's Angels reportedly offered $1.3 million. BobbyBroccoli writes, "prompting the feds to panic and buy back the bunker for nearly twice what it was sold for a cool 1.25 million." This reversal is the core of the argument: the government was willing to absorb a massive loss just to prevent a criminal gang from owning a nuclear bunker. The author's discovery of the demolition contract, awarded for $800,056, further cements the narrative that the state chose destruction over the risk of a sale. The evidence here is robust, relying on newspaper clippings and government records rather than hearsay.

The Human Connection

Just as the investigation seems to stall on the identity of the original private owners, BobbyBroccoli executes a brilliant pivot. While collecting footage, the author stumbled upon a YouTube comment from a user named Brock, who claimed, "my grandpa owned both bunkers." This moment of serendipity transforms the piece from a dry historical report into a personal journey. The author tracks down Brock's grandfather, Harvey Workkenton, and uncovers a final, almost cinematic detail: Lucasfilm had approached the family to use the bunker as a set for Star Wars: Episode I, but the deal fell through because the compensation was in memorabilia rather than cash.

While the Star Wars anecdote feels like a convenient Hollywood twist, BobbyBroccoli handles it with appropriate caution. "Now feel free to take this part with a grain of salt obviously it undoubtedly feels a little too good to be true," the author admits. This self-awareness strengthens the credibility of the entire piece. The final confirmation comes from a 2006 Toronto Star article where Harvey Workkenton himself confirms the Hell's Angels inquiry. BobbyBroccoli concludes, "yes the hell's angels expressed interest but that he would never actually sell it to them for obvious reasons so i don't know about you but i'm willing to take this as a win." This direct testimony from the former owner validates the entire investigation, turning a decades-old rumor into verified fact.

Bottom Line

BobbyBroccoli's piece is a triumph of modern journalism, demonstrating how digital tools and persistent curiosity can solve mysteries that traditional reporting missed. The strongest element is the seamless blend of financial records, government documents, and personal testimony to construct an irrefutable timeline. The only vulnerability lies in the reliance on a single family member's memory for the Star Wars detail, but the author wisely frames this as a colorful aside rather than a central pillar of the argument. Readers should watch for similar deep-dive investigations that use the internet not just to spread rumors, but to finally put them to rest.
