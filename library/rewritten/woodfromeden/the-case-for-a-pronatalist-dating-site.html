Tove K makes a provocative claim that cuts against the grain of modern dating discourse: the chaos of the contemporary mating market isn't a failure of technology, but a success of unchecked human selfishness that we foolishly encouraged. While most analysis focuses on algorithmic flaws or the superficiality of apps, K argues that we dismantled the very cultural guardrails that once forced compromise, leaving a vacuum where only the most predatory instincts thrive. This is a necessary, if uncomfortable, intervention for anyone wondering why the path to family formation feels increasingly impossible.

The Market's Double-Edged Sword

K begins by acknowledging the benefits of the free market before pivoting to its dangers in the realm of human connection. "The market gives us what we instantly, instinctually crave. That is both a blessing and a curse," they write, noting that while capitalism excels at satisfying immediate desires like entertainment, it fails when those desires conflict with long-term societal values. The author draws a sharp parallel between financial regulation and sexual norms, suggesting that just as we regulate greed in the economy, we once regulated mating selfishness through culture. "All major cultures have regulated the behavior of their members on the mating market," K asserts, because without such constraints, society risks devolving into a "low-intense state of everyone's war against everyone."

This framing is effective because it reframes the "dating crisis" not as a technological glitch, but as a deliberate cultural choice. K admits personal complicity in this shift, recalling a time when they viewed commitment as a trap and gender roles as oppressive. "I believed that if I played along and allowed men to hold up doors and take the bill for me, I would have to live with a man who merely wanted a woman," K confesses, explaining that the rejection of traditional courtship was a misguided attempt to force authenticity. However, this perspective overlooks the fact that for many, those "traditional norms" were not just about courtship rituals but often served to entrench power imbalances that left women economically vulnerable. Critics might note that restoring these norms could inadvertently re-impose the very constraints K claims to have escaped.

The message that everyone should always do whatever they want is just that: Lack of culture.

The Collapse of Cultural Guardrails

The core of K's argument rests on the idea that culture acts as a necessary buffer against raw human nature. They argue that the "fashionable elite" of their generation didn't just change culture; they abolished it. "We consciously shattered what culture we had in favor of rules we thought were closer to human nature," K writes, only to realize later that human nature is not inherently benign. The author points out that the modern dating market resembles the unregulated free market of the late 19th century, a period of economic chaos that eventually necessitated modern regulations like competition and environmental laws. "Something similar has happened with sexual relationships," K observes, noting that the old norms were torn down one by one until the "selfishness of the opposite sex" could be encountered in full force.

This comparison to economic history is compelling, yet it simplifies the complexity of social evolution. The article references the shift from "convenience marriage" to "love marriage" a century ago, a transition that was itself a cultural upheaval. As K notes, "Novels like Le Grand Meaulnes (1913), about two men's quest to locate the brides of their dreams, makes a naive impression today." This historical nod suggests that the current disillusionment with romantic love is cyclical, echoing the disappointments of the past when the ideal of lifelong love replaced pragmatic unions. However, the argument assumes that the "cultural fakery" of the past—where men followed norms of decency even if they felt otherwise—was a net positive, ignoring the emotional toll of living inauthentically for generations.

A Proposal for Pronatalist Platforms

In the final section, K moves from diagnosis to prescription, arguing that we cannot simply wait for culture to grow organically. "Basically you can't [create culture], because culture grows organically, from within people's minds. But you can support culture," they write. The proposed solution is the creation of non-profit, NGO-run dating sites explicitly designed for family formation, which would advocate for social norms rather than maximizing engagement or profit. K highlights the irony that in the current landscape, seeking a partner for a family is often viewed as selfish, while seeking a partner for amusement is celebrated. "The selfish option is to seek a sexual partner to amuse oneself with, the constructive option is to seek a partner to form a family with," K argues, calling for a cultural shift that values "decency higher" and "awesomeness lower."

The author draws a parallel to the concept of "marriage of convenience," suggesting that modern relationships need a similar pragmatic foundation to survive. While the idea of a "pronatalist dating site" sounds radical, K argues it is merely a return to the safe spaces of the past where norms were upheld by families and communities. "Those norms, and the norms of the meeting places and of society in general prevented most people from mating blatantly selfishly," K explains. Yet, a counterargument worth considering is whether a top-down, NGO-run platform can truly replicate the organic, community-enforced norms of the past, or if it would simply become another niche market for the already committed, leaving the broader culture unchanged.

People who can't impress a prospective partner with lively conversations and exciting leisure activities suffer an important disadvantage, also if they are loyal, good with children, good housekeepers, honest and so on.

Bottom Line

Tove K's most powerful contribution is the realization that the "freedom" of the modern dating market has come at the cost of the stability required to build families, and that culture is not a relic but a necessary infrastructure for human cooperation. However, the piece's vulnerability lies in its romanticization of past norms, which often masked deep inequalities, and its reliance on a voluntary, niche solution to a systemic cultural problem. The reader should watch for how these "pronatalist" initiatives evolve: will they become genuine community builders, or just another algorithmic filter for the elite?
