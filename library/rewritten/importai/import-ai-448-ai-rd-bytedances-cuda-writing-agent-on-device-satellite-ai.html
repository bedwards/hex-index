Jack Clark delivers a stark warning wrapped in technical detail: the pace of artificial intelligence is not just accelerating; it is actively outpacing the most sophisticated attempts to predict it. The most surprising claim isn't that AI is getting better, but that the very concept of measuring its "time horizon" for complex tasks is beginning to collapse under the weight of its own speed. For busy leaders trying to plan for the next decade, this piece argues that the future is arriving before the forecast models can even finish rendering.

The Collapse of Prediction Timelines

Clark opens with a sobering admission from Ajeya Cotra, a respected voice in AI forecasting, who realized her own predictions were already obsolete. "On Jan 14th, I made predictions about AI progress in 2026. My forecasts for software engineering capabilities already feel much too conservative," writes Jack Clark, highlighting Cotra's realization that recent data has shattered her previous models. The evidence here is compelling because it comes from an insider who has spent years calibrating these timelines, yet finds herself unable to keep up with the velocity of recent breakthroughs.

The core of the argument is that AI agents are now capable of sustained work far beyond what was thought possible just months ago. Clark notes that while Cotra predicted a 24-hour time horizon for the end of 2026, new metrics suggest agents could soon handle over 100 hours of continuous software tasks by year's end. This lands with significant force because it implies a fundamental shift in economic productivity; we are moving from tools that assist humans to systems that can execute entire weeks of work autonomously. "It's no longer very plausible that after ten whole months of additional progress at the recent blistering pace, AI agents would still struggle half the time at 24 hour tasks," Clark quotes, underscoring the speed of this transition.

Critics might argue that "time horizon" metrics are still too narrow, focusing only on coding tasks and ignoring the messy, unstructured nature of real-world business problems. However, if the bottleneck of software engineering is removed, the ripple effects on other sectors could be immediate and massive.

"Once you're talking about multiple full-time-equivalent weeks of work, I wonder if the whole concept of 'time horizon' starts to break down."

Measuring the Unmeasurable

The newsletter then pivots to a critical question: how do we govern a technology that is building itself? Clark introduces a new framework from researchers at GovAI and the University of Oxford, who propose 14 distinct metrics to track AI Research and Development Automation (AIRDA). This is the piece's most actionable section, moving from abstract fear to concrete measurement. The argument is that without specific data points, we are flying blind into a potential recursive self-improvement loop.

Clark explains that these metrics range from measuring AI performance on AI tasks to tracking how often AI subverts human goals. "AIRDA could accelerate AI progress, bringing forward AI's benefits but also hastening the arrival of destructive capabilities, including those related to weapons of mass destruction, or other forms of disruption such as unemployment," he writes, capturing the dual-use nature of the technology. The strength of this section lies in its specificity; it doesn't just say "we need to watch AI," it lists exactly what to watch, such as the ratio of compute used for safety versus capabilities research.

However, the sheer volume of these metrics raises a practical concern: who has the resources to collect and verify this data? Clark acknowledges this by suggesting that governments need confidential reporting systems and third parties need to estimate metrics using public sources. "An actor has oversight over the AI R&D process to the extent that they (1) understand the process and (2) exercise informed control over it in order to produce desired outputs," he quotes, defining the baseline for any future governance.

The Edge of Intelligence

Shifting from high-level theory to physical implementation, Clark highlights two groundbreaking projects that demonstrate AI moving from the cloud to the "edge"—the devices themselves. First, he details a project by Indian researchers who are using lightweight GPUs to create a city-wide traffic monitoring system in Bengaluru. The system processes video locally on cameras rather than sending massive data streams to a central server. "By localizing heavy video analytics at the network periphery, the system avoids centralized bandwidth bottlenecks, enabling sustainable, city-scale traffic sensing," Clark notes, illustrating how efficiency gains are enabling new scales of deployment.

This is followed by a description of "TinyIceNet," a tiny vision model designed to run on satellites to monitor sea ice thickness. The research shows that AI can now be optimized for extreme power constraints, running on FPGAs with minimal energy consumption. Clark observes that the energy efficiency of the FPGA implementation makes it "compelling for on-board satellite processing, where power availability is severely restricted."

The implication here is profound: AI is becoming ubiquitous not just in data centers, but in the physical infrastructure of our world. "In a couple of years we might expect AI agents to do this stuff themselves, procuring compute resources to let them develop and distribute small AI systems to arbitrary compute platforms for arbitrary purposes," Clark speculates. This suggests a future where the creation of specialized hardware and software is automated, leading to an exponential boom in economic activity.

A counterargument worth considering is the risk of these distributed systems becoming surveillance tools that are harder to regulate than centralized ones. As Clark points out, the balance between efficiency and authoritarianism depends entirely on the norms and laws that surround these technologies.

The Self-Driving Code Revolution

Finally, Clark turns to a development that ties all the previous points together: ByteDance's "CUDA Agent." This is an AI model fine-tuned specifically to write the low-level GPU code that trains other AI models. "The research is another sign of how people are increasingly using AI to speedup core aspects of AI development," Clark writes, noting the irony that a major Chinese lab is using US-made chips to build AI that writes code for those same chips.

This development represents a critical step toward the recursive self-improvement mentioned earlier. If AI can write the code that optimizes the hardware it runs on, the cycle of improvement accelerates. The argument is that we are approaching a point where the primary constraint on AI progress is no longer human ingenuity, but the ability of AI to optimize its own existence.

"This is one of the main ways I think we could get a sudden exponential boom in economic activity attributable to AI - AI systems will get smart enough that they can drastically improve their ability to know about and interact with the physical world through the creation of custom 'edge computing' AI systems to give them better sensory data and actuators."

Bottom Line

Clark's strongest argument is the convergence of rapid capability growth with the emergence of tools that allow AI to build itself, creating a feedback loop that traditional forecasting cannot capture. The piece's biggest vulnerability is the assumption that governance frameworks can be developed and implemented fast enough to keep pace with these technical leaps. Readers should watch for the next iteration of these 14 metrics, as they may become the standard for how governments and corporations assess AI risk in the coming year.
