Jack Clark's latest analysis cuts through the noise of incremental AI improvements to reveal a future defined by relentless, self-driving competition and automated governance. The most startling claim isn't that AI will replace human labor, but that we are entering an era where AI systems will evolve to fight each other, and eventually, police each other, rendering traditional human oversight obsolete in real-time. This is not science fiction; it is a trajectory already visible in the code of today's most advanced models.

The Digital Red Queen

Clark opens with a vivid experiment from Japanese startup Sakana, where large language models were pitted against one another in a retro programming game called Core War. The results suggest a shift from static benchmarks to a dynamic, adversarial ecosystem. "Large language models (LLMs) drive an adversarial evolutionary arms race in this domain, where programs continuously adapt to defeat a growing history of opponents rather than a static benchmark," the researchers write. Clark uses this to illustrate a broader economic reality: the future will not be a series of one-off deployments, but a continuous, high-speed struggle for dominance.

The mechanism driving this is what the authors call the "Digital Red Queen." By using quality-diversity algorithms, the system prevents the AI from getting stuck in a loop, forcing it to constantly innovate. "As DRQ is run for many rounds, warriors gradually become more generally robust, as measured by their performance against unseen human-designed warriors," Clark notes. The data is staggering: while a single attempt by an AI defeats only 1.7% of human-designed programs, an evolved set of agents can defeat or tie 96.3% of them. This isn't just a game; it's a microcosm of the cybersecurity and economic battles to come.

The world is going to look a lot like Core Wars - millions of AI agents will be competing against one another in a variety of domains, ranging from cybersecurity to economics, and will be optimizing themselves in relation to achieving certain competitive criteria.

This framing is powerful because it moves the conversation from "what can AI do?" to "what will AI do to each other?" Critics might argue that a 1980s memory game is too simplistic to model complex real-world economics, but the underlying principle of recursive self-improvement under competitive pressure is the exact dynamic that concerns safety researchers. The implication is clear: if we cannot contain this arms race in a petri dish, we certainly cannot contain it in the global market.

The Paradox of Automated Regulation

If AI systems are evolving faster than humans can track, how do we regulate them? Clark turns to a provocative argument from the Institute for Law and AI: we must let AI regulate AI. The core thesis is that as models become smarter, the cost of compliance drops, making automated enforcement not just possible, but necessary. "AI policy discourse should internalize the fact that AI progress implies reduced compliance costs, all else equal, due to automated compliance," the authors argue.

Clark highlights a specific mechanism called "automatability triggers." This is a form of "If Then Policy" where regulations only activate once a technical system exists that can enforce them cheaply and accurately. For instance, a ban on exporting powerful AI models would only take effect once an automated system can verify compliance with a false positive rate under 1%. "The requirements of this Act will only come into effect [one month] after the date when the [Secretary of Commerce], in their reasonable discretion, determines that there exists an automated system," the proposal states.

This approach acknowledges a harsh truth: human regulators cannot keep pace with the speed of AI deployment. By building compliance-automating systems, we are inadvertently building the prerequisites for automated governance. "Compliance-automating AI systems could also request guidance from regulatory AI systems, who could review and respond to the request nearly instantaneously," Clark writes. This creates a closed loop where the system polices itself.

The only way through this is a combination of creating appropriate hard laws that apply to AI and delineate what actions are unacceptable, and for everything else creating fast-acting and adaptive automated systems to regulate and police the myriad gray areas of the AI universe.

While the logic is sound, a counterargument worth considering is the risk of embedding bias into the very systems designed to enforce the law. If the automated regulator is trained on flawed data, the "fair and nondiscriminatory" terms of the trigger condition could become a veneer for systemic exclusion. The speed of enforcement is a double-edged sword; it prevents regulatory capture by slow bureaucracies but risks accelerating injustice if the guardrails are flawed.

The O-Ring Effect on Labor

Perhaps the most counterintuitive insight in the piece concerns the future of human work. Clark challenges the standard narrative of total displacement, citing research from the University of Toronto on "O-ring production." The theory posits that jobs are composed of many distinct tasks, and automating one part of a job doesn't eliminate the worker; it shifts their value to the remaining unautomated tasks.

"Automating a task not only replaces the quality of that task; it also changes the worker's time allocation and thus the quality of all remaining manual tasks," the researchers explain. Clark illustrates this with the example of bank tellers. When ATMs automated cash handling, tellers didn't vanish; they pivoted to "relationship banking" and higher-value customer interaction. Similarly, purchasing managers become "super-negotiators" as administrative drudgery is offloaded.

When only a few manual tasks remain, each manual task receives a large share of time and can be performed at high quality. This creates a rising "barrier" to automating the last tasks.

This suggests a period of "centaur-improvement," where human-AI teams outperform either humans or machines alone. The value of human labor may actually rise as the remaining tasks become more critical to the overall output. However, this optimistic view relies on the assumption that workers can successfully transition to these higher-value roles. Critics might note that this model ignores the friction of reskilling and the potential for a polarized workforce where only the most adaptable thrive, leaving others behind in the automated gaps.

The Persuasion Paradox

Finally, Clark addresses the dual-use nature of AI in the realm of truth. A new study involving GPT-4o reveals a disturbing symmetry: the model is equally effective at debunking conspiracy theories as it is at promoting them. "We find that LLMs can meaningfully increase false beliefs, and that, at least on average, this bunking effect is just as large as the debunking effect," the researchers write.

The study found that while belief in a conspiracy dropped by 12.1 points in the "debunking" condition, it rose by 13.7 points in the "bunking" condition. More alarmingly, the AI that was spreading misinformation was rated more positively and generated more trust than the one correcting it. This suggests that the very tools we hope will be our best defense against disinformation are equally potent weapons for it.

Bottom Line

Jack Clark's commentary succeeds in reframing the AI narrative from a story of human utility to one of autonomous, adversarial evolution. The strongest part of the argument is the "Digital Red Queen" concept, which forces us to confront a future where the primary actors are not humans, but competing AI agents. The biggest vulnerability remains the assumption that we can successfully deploy automated governance systems without embedding the biases and errors of their creators into the fabric of society. The reader must watch for the moment when the "automatability triggers" flip, turning the theoretical possibility of AI policing into a practical reality."
