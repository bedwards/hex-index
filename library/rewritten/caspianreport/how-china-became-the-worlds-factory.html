Shirvan Neftchi reframes China's modern rise not as a sudden anomaly, but as a tragic return to historical normality, arguing that the nation's current dominance is the result of a painful, century-long correction of Western colonial disruption. The piece stands out for its brutal honesty about the human cost of the Mao era and its clear-eyed comparison of Beijing's unique "market Leninism" against the American-backed mercantilism of Japan and South Korea. For a busy reader trying to understand the current geopolitical friction, this context is essential: the world's factory was built on a specific, often overlooked, three-step playbook that China adapted from its neighbors.

The Cost of Isolation

Neftchi begins by dismantling the myth that China was always destined to lead. He reminds us that as late as 1800, the Qing Empire accounted for almost one third of the global GDP, exporting silk and ceramics in massive quantities. However, the narrative shifts quickly to the disaster of the 19th century. "Beginning with the opium war of 1839, the state was put on the back foot by a series of foreign incursions," Neftchi writes, detailing how British steamships and Russian annexations shattered the nation's sovereignty. The author argues that the subsequent instability prevented the necessary centralization for industrialization, a task the Qing dynasty and the later nationalist government failed to complete.

The commentary here is sharp because it refuses to romanticize the pre-revolution era. Neftchi notes that "the leadership in Beijing knew that industrialization was crucial to survival but without strong central governance the state was too large and self-sufficient to change its outdated ways." This sets the stage for the Mao era, which Neftchi describes not as a heroic struggle, but as a catastrophic miscalculation. He points out that Mao, a military man, designed an economic policy centered around military conflict, breaking the countryside into self-contained units to make provinces expendable. "By 1959 economic mismanagement over reporting of grain production and a lack of incentives for individual farmers snowballed into the worst famine in human history up to 50 million people lost their lives," Neftchi states. The sheer scale of this failure is the piece's most sobering element, highlighting how ideological rigidity can squander a nation's potential.

Far from modernizing China, Mao's reckless policy making had squandered the country's potential.

Critics might argue that Neftchi underplays the geopolitical constraints Mao faced, suggesting that the isolation was partly a defensive necessity against a hostile West. However, the author's focus on the internal logic of the policy—prioritizing military readiness over food security—remains a compelling explanation for the famine's severity.

The East Asian Playbook

The turning point in the analysis comes when Neftchi contrasts China's stagnation with the rapid ascent of Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan. He identifies a specific "three-step pattern" that these neighbors followed: land reform to crush agricultural lobbying, a shift to export-oriented manufacturing, and restrictive financial policies to direct capital. "The first was land reform by breaking up larger estates into small holdings the east asian states crushed the lobbying power of large agricultural businesses," Neftchi explains. This fostered competition and drove a mass migration of peasants to cities, creating the urban workforce necessary for industrialization.

Neftchi's analysis of the second step is particularly insightful regarding the role of the United States. He notes that while these Asian states adopted mercantilist policies that hurt Western businesses, Washington tolerated the arrangement. "America at the time was okay with this trade-off because it needed strong capable allies in asia to counter communist influence," he writes. This historical context is vital; it explains why the West allowed these economies to run protectionist systems that would be condemned today. The author argues that this "East Asian playbook laid the groundwork for their success in the post-war years," providing a template that China would eventually copy.

Deng's Strategic Pivot

When Mao died in 1976, his successor Deng Xiaoping did not merely tweak the system; he adopted the very playbook China had ignored. Neftchi highlights China's "secret weapon" as its massive size, allowing for local experiments before national rollout. In Anhui province, for instance, agricultural communes were broken up, allowing farmers to keep surplus grain. "The incentive created a surge in food production between 1978 and 1984 agricultural production rose by one-third and average farm incomes more than doubled," Neftchi notes. This pragmatic shift from heavy industry to light industrial goods like clothing and toys capitalized on China's natural advantage: its massive workforce.

The author emphasizes that this was not a move toward Western-style democracy, but a fusion of market mechanisms with authoritarian control. "Unlike western doctrine in china the government is not seen as a hindrance to economic prosperity but a prerequisite to it," Neftchi argues. This distinction is crucial for understanding why political liberalization did not follow economic reform. The state maintained tight control over capital flows and currency values, ensuring that "an undervalued exchange rate made east asian exports even more competitive." By 2013, foreign direct investment had peaked at 290 billion dollars, driven by the irresistible combination of first-world infrastructure and third-world labor costs in special economic zones.

Between 1978 and 2019 real gdp measured according to current dollar value grew from 150 billion dollars to 14.3 trillion dollars that's mind-boggling growth.

The Bottom Line

Shirvan Neftchi's strongest argument is the clear delineation of the "three-step" economic strategy that China borrowed from its neighbors, proving that its rise was a calculated adaptation rather than a mysterious phenomenon. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its brief treatment of the environmental and social externalities of this breakneck growth, which are now becoming as critical as the economic metrics. As China shifts focus to domestic markets and initiatives like "Made in China 2025," the world must watch whether this state-led model can sustain itself without the cheap labor and export dominance that defined its first forty years of reform.
