Shirvan Neftchi reframes the Atlantic Ocean not merely as a body of water, but as the active architect of modern Western civilization, arguing that the "modern world was built around the Atlantic" in the same way the classical world revolved around the Mediterranean. What distinguishes this analysis is its seamless weaving of geological mechanics with geopolitical destiny, suggesting that the ocean's physical expansion and shifting currents are not just background noise, but the primary drivers of a power distribution that is now, for the first time in centuries, tilting toward the Pacific.

The Geological Engine of History

Neftchi begins by grounding the narrative in hard science, noting that the Atlantic is "in constant motion both on the surface and underneath." He details how seismic activity pushes Europe and Africa away from the Americas by centimeters annually, a slow-motion divorce that has dictated the climate and habitability of entire continents. The author highlights the Gulf Stream as a critical factor, explaining how this warm current "provides the continent with a temperate climate making Europe more habitable than other places." This geological framing is effective because it removes the mystique of European dominance, attributing it instead to a specific, fragile oceanic current that climate change now threatens to disrupt.

However, this focus on natural determinism sometimes overshadows human agency. Critics might argue that while the Gulf Stream made Europe livable, it did not dictate the specific political choices that led to colonization or the development of liberal institutions. The environment provided the stage, but the actors wrote the script.

"Just as the classical world was built around the Mediterranean the modern world was built around the Atlantic."

This assertion serves as the piece's central thesis, a bold claim that elevates geography to the status of a primary historical actor. Neftchi supports this by tracing the ocean's role as a "conduit for celebrated explorers and as a laboratory for groundbreaking infrastructure projects." The argument gains traction when he connects these physical attributes to the rise of "Atlanticism," describing it as a bond between Americans and Europeans reinforced by institutions like NATO. The coverage effectively illustrates how a natural feature evolved into a geopolitical concept, though it briefly glosses over the violent exclusion of non-Western powers that defined this "bond."

The Mechanics of Colonization and Trade

The narrative then shifts to the Age of Discovery, where Neftchi identifies the caravel, compass, and portolan charts as the "revolutionary inventions" that allowed Europeans to depart their homelands. He argues that once these tools were in hand, "previously isolated communities were drawn into interlocking political economic and Military systems." This is a crucial pivot point in the argument: the ocean ceased to be a barrier and became a bridge for a globalized system that was inherently extractive.

Neftchi does not shy away from the brutality of this expansion, noting that the driving force was an "insatiable hunger for hard income" needed to fund European standing armies. He describes how Spain and Portugal attempted to monopolize the ocean via the Treaty of Tordesillas, drawing a "massive vertical line" through the Atlantic that they believed would divide the world. The author's commentary on this treaty is particularly sharp: "the thing about lines on a map is that they are inconsequential as long as there is no real means to enforce them." This observation cuts through the diplomatic posturing of the era, revealing the raw power dynamics that actually shaped the map.

"The interaction between the new world and the old world was more than just physical it included the exchange of ideas and cultures."

While Neftchi accurately describes the "Colombian Exchange" of crops and the devastating impact of the transatlantic slave trade, the analysis could delve deeper into the long-term demographic scars left by these events. The text mentions the "slow painful demise" of native populations in North America due to a lack of centralized leadership, a nuanced point that contrasts with the rapid overthrow of the Aztec and Inca empires. This distinction is vital for understanding why the cultural and political landscapes of North and South America diverged so sharply, yet the author moves quickly past this to focus on the economic outcomes.

The Shift in Power and the Future of the Atlantic

As the timeline moves to the 19th and 20th centuries, Neftchi traces how innovations like steamboats and the airplane accelerated the "special kinship" between North America and Europe. He notes that air travel became so common that "North America and Europe developed a special kinship called atlanticism," synchronizing their economic and security ties. The author argues that this synchronization transformed Western powers into "global hegemons," a status that relied heavily on the Atlantic as a secure internal sea.

However, the piece concludes with a warning that the tide is turning. Neftchi asserts that "the Pacific is turning the tide and with that change in power distribution comes the privilege to redraw the world map as one sees fit." This is the most forward-looking and potentially contentious part of the argument. It suggests that the era of Atlantic centrality is ending, not just due to the rise of Asian economies, but because the very infrastructure of global connection—submarine cables and shipping lanes—is shifting focus.

"Underneath the oceans are submarine cables that provide seamless internet traffic across the globe."

By ending with the mention of digital infrastructure, Neftchi bridges the gap between historical geography and modern digital reality. The implication is that the physical ocean still matters, but its strategic value is now defined by the invisible networks beneath it. This is a compelling synthesis of old and new, though it leaves the reader wondering how the "Atlanticism" of the 20th century will adapt to a 21st-century reality where the Pacific is the center of gravity.

Bottom Line

Shirvan Neftchi's strongest contribution is his ability to link the geological reality of the Atlantic to the political architecture of the modern West, proving that geography is not just a backdrop but a co-author of history. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its rapid transition from the brutal realities of colonization to the abstract concept of "Atlanticism," which risks sanitizing the violence that built the very institutions Neftchi celebrates. Readers should watch for how the "redrawing of the map" in the Pacific era will challenge the specific legal and cultural frameworks that were forged in the Atlantic.
