Shirvan Neftchi drops a bombshell premise: the Cold War has effectively turned hot, not through a proxy skirmish, but via a direct, preemptive Israeli strike on Iranian soil that has shattered decades of strategic ambiguity. What sets this analysis apart is the granular dissection of the "Operation Rising Lion" campaign, moving beyond the immediate explosions to argue that the true objective is not merely nuclear delay, but a calculated provocation of internal regime collapse. For the busy observer trying to cut through the noise of 24-hour news cycles, Neftchi offers a crucial distinction between tactical military success and the chaotic, unpredictable political fallout that inevitably follows.

The Architecture of a Preemptive Strike

Neftchi paints a stark picture of the opening hours, noting that "the initial 24 hours have been nothing short of a disaster for the Iranians." He details how Israeli jets flew "almost uncontested over Iranian airspace" while Mossad operatives allegedly operated with unprecedented freedom to target nuclear and military sites. This framing is vital because it challenges the conventional wisdom that Iran's vast air defense network would have neutralized such an incursion. The author suggests that the damage, while perhaps "superficial" at the Natans nuclear site, has achieved a strategic breakthrough by degrading radar and command capabilities.

However, the analysis goes deeper than the initial blast radius. Neftchi argues that the targeting of specific groups reveals a hidden agenda. He posits that "all the Israeli attacks have so far targeted IRGC officials while avoiding the leadership of Iran's regular armed forces," suggesting a "calculated attempt at divide and rule." This is a compelling, if speculative, read of the battlefield. By focusing on the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) rather than the regular military, Israel may be trying to exploit the historical friction between these two pillars of the Iranian state.

"Regime change is less a strategy than a roll of the dice."

This observation cuts through the hype. While the military action is precise, the political gamble is chaotic. Neftchi correctly identifies that Israel's campaign name, "Operation Rising Lion," likely nods to the pre-revolution flag, signaling a desire to overturn the current government entirely. Yet, critics might note that assuming the regular Iranian military would turn against the IRGC in a power vacuum is a high-risk assumption; in authoritarian systems, external threats often unify internal factions rather than fracture them.

The Asymmetric Response and the Proxy Dilemma

The commentary shifts to Iran's predicament, where the author notes that "Hezbollah is now much weaker" and sitting on the sidelines due to infiltration and prior losses. This is a significant departure from previous conflicts where Hezbollah served as Iran's primary deterrent. Neftchi explains that Iran now faces a brutal choice: respond with weak retaliation that invites further aggression, or escalate in a way that risks drawing the United States into a direct war.

He outlines the potential for Iran to target Gulf oil infrastructure, a move that would "almost certainly cause oil prices to surge" and potentially force Gulf leaders to pressure the US to halt Israeli attacks. This is a sophisticated reading of economic leverage. Neftchi writes, "Sometimes the shortest path to victory goes through someone else's backyard," highlighting how Iran might weaponize global energy markets to achieve a political off-ramp. However, this strategy carries the risk of destroying the improving diplomatic ties Iran has recently built with Saudi Arabia and the UAE, a trade-off that could backfire diplomatically.

The most chilling possibility Neftchi raises is the nuclear option. He suggests that "if the Iranian government sees its survival at stake, the Supreme Leader may decide the cost is worth it" to conduct a nuclear test. This would signal that Iran is "no longer bluffing about its nuclear ambitions." While this would force Israel and the US to weigh the risk of a nuclear conflict, it would also trigger international outrage and deeper isolation. The author effectively argues that Iran's best path to deterrence might be a "non-kinetic response" that signals intent without immediate escalation, perhaps by leaving the Non-Proliferation Treaty.

"Precision strikes rarely end with precision outcomes."

This line serves as the thematic anchor for the entire piece. Neftchi warns that while Israel may have successfully executed the first stage of its operation, the subsequent weeks will be defined by the inability to control the narrative or the battlefield. The author notes that "drones are plentiful, but relatively easy to shoot down," while ballistic missiles are destructive but hard to replace. This creates a war of attrition where the side with the deeper stockpile and the stronger political will might prevail, regardless of tactical superiority.

Bottom Line

Shirvan Neftchi's strongest contribution is the reframing of this conflict from a simple tit-for-tat exchange of missiles into a complex game of regime survival and internal subversion. His argument that Israel is aiming for political collapse rather than just nuclear delay is provocative and well-supported by the targeting patterns he describes. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the assumption that Iran's regular military will fracture under pressure, a dynamic that is difficult to predict in real-time. Readers should watch closely for whether Iran chooses economic warfare in the Gulf or a nuclear signal, as these moves will determine if the conflict remains regional or explodes into a global crisis.
