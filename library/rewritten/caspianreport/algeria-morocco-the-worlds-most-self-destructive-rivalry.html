Shirvan Neftchi reframes the decades-long feud between Algeria and Morocco not as a simple border dispute, but as a self-destructive engine that has actively prevented two of Africa's most capable nations from becoming regional powerhouses. The piece is notable for its stark economic accounting: it argues that tens of billions spent on military hardware have yielded zero strategic advantage, leaving both countries poorer than they would have been had they simply ignored the past. For busy observers tracking North African stability, this analysis cuts through the noise of recent diplomatic ruptures to reveal a structural trap that is now tightening.

The Anatomy of a Stalemate

Neftchi begins by dismantling the assumption that these two nations are natural allies. Despite sharing "strong Arab, Berber, Muslim, and even French influences," the author argues that their post-independence paths diverged so sharply that cooperation became impossible. The core of the argument rests on the idea that historical trauma created incompatible national identities. Neftchi writes, "The origins of the Moroccan Algerian rivalry date back to the colonial era," tracing how French boundary decisions and the subsequent Sand War of 1963 instilled a deep, enduring mistrust.

This historical framing is effective because it moves beyond the immediate triggers of conflict to the root causes. Neftchi explains that while Morocco leaned toward the United States and maintained a monarchy, Algeria's revolutionary struggle against colonialism fostered a "nationalist socialist revolutionary zeal that leaned towards the Soviet Union." This ideological split during the Cold War cemented their opposition. As Neftchi puts it, "Neither side is any closer to outmatching the other just as neither is willing to let go of old habits." The author's point here is sharp: the rivalry is no longer about winning; it is about the inability to stop playing the game.

"Peace is not the absence of conflict but the ability to handle conflict by peaceful means."

Critics might note that Neftchi somewhat underplays the genuine security concerns regarding the Polisario Front and Western Sahara, treating them almost entirely as geopolitical chess pieces rather than a legitimate national liberation movement. However, the broader point about the consequences of this dispute remains robust. The author details how the 1994 border closure turned two dynamic economies into isolated silos, a decision that "cost immeasurable amounts of economic growth."

The Economics of Destruction

The most compelling section of the commentary is the financial autopsy of the arms race. Neftchi provides specific data to illustrate the absurdity of the situation: between 2005 and 2015, Morocco spent roughly $48 billion on its military, while Algeria spent $58 billion. These are not small sums; they represent resources diverted from essential infrastructure. The author argues that "every resource spent on buying weapons of destruction is money not spent on building roads, railways, schools and hospitals."

This evidence holds up well against the backdrop of current economic struggles in both nations. Neftchi points out that despite their vast natural resources, both countries rank among the world's poorest in terms of development metrics, with Algeria at 109th and Morocco at 125th. The author suggests that "economic growth and development was sacrificed to feed the self-destructive rivalry." This is a powerful indictment of the leadership in both Algiers and Rabat, who have prioritized regional supremacy over the welfare of their citizens.

As Neftchi writes, "The military hardware that Morocco and Algeria have acquired exceeds what they need to secure their respective hinterlands. The aim is solely to outmatch the other." This observation highlights the irrationality of the conflict; the hardware is not for defense, but for a symbolic contest that yields no tangible security benefits. The author notes that "the rivalry between Morocco and Algeria has gone on for so long that disagreements have become a permanent source of political propaganda."

"Both have missed opportunities to lead and prosper together."

The commentary also touches on the recent escalation involving wildfires in Algeria and accusations of Moroccan complicity with Kabylie separatists. Neftchi notes that "Algeria decided to cut diplomatic relations with Morocco over what it called hostile actions," a move that threatens to shut down airspaces and energy pipelines. While the author admits that "whether Moroccan support has gone beyond words is unclear," the political fallout is immediate and severe. A counterargument worth considering is that internal instability in Algeria, particularly the Kabylie movement and the aftermath of the civil war, is a primary driver of these tensions, rather than solely external interference. Yet, Neftchi's point stands: the rivalry provides a convenient scapegoat for domestic failures.

The Cost of Isolation

The final thrust of Neftchi's argument is that this conflict prevents North Africa from integrating into a broader African economic bloc. He observes that "nearly all the physical infrastructure in both countries is oriented towards the coast, towards exports," meaning that "local economic life is held back." By refusing to normalize relations, both nations force commercial contracts to be outsourced to distant powers like the United States, Saudi Arabia, or France.

This is a missed opportunity of historic proportions. Neftchi writes, "Algeria and Morocco have a population close to the size of Germany," yet they remain "among the world's poorest countries" due to their refusal to cooperate. The author concludes that "the list of grievances is long and it includes colonial demarcation, irredentist claims, political ideology and an arms race for regional influence." The tragedy, as Neftchi frames it, is that the solution is obvious, but the political will is absent. "Though both countries have made attempts to move away from the past," the author notes, "some old wounds never truly heal and bleed again at the slightest word."

"The rivalry between Morocco and Algeria is an old wound."

Bottom Line

Neftchi's strongest contribution is the relentless focus on the economic opportunity cost, transforming a geopolitical feud into a story of wasted potential that is easy for any reader to grasp. The argument's vulnerability lies in its somewhat deterministic view of history, which may understate the agency of current leaders who could choose to break the cycle despite the historical baggage. Readers should watch for whether the recent severing of diplomatic ties leads to actual economic decoupling or if the pragmatic need for energy and trade will eventually force a quiet, if unacknowledged, normalization.
