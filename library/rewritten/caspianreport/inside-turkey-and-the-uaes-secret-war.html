Shirvan Neftchi delivers a chilling diagnosis of Sudan's civil war: it is no longer a local struggle for power, but a lethal proxy collision between Turkey and the United Arab Emirates. The most startling claim here is not that foreign powers are involved, but that they have moved beyond covert support to direct, kinetic engagement where their own personnel are being targeted and killed. For anyone tracking the Red Sea's strategic future, this is the definitive warning that the region's stability has already fractured.

The Collapse of Neutrality

Neftchi begins by dismantling the illusion that Port Sudan was a safe haven. He describes how the city, once the last neutral zone hosting the government and foreign embassies, was struck by "thick black smoke curling into the sky" from drone attacks that no group officially claimed. "No group claimed responsibility, but evidence points to the rapid support forces, which is believed to be armed and financed by the UAE," Neftchi writes. This attribution is crucial; it shifts the narrative from a chaotic internal brawl to a calculated geopolitical maneuver. The author effectively uses satellite imagery and the specific type of weaponry—Chinese-made loitering drones—to trace the supply chain back to Abu Dhabi.

The argument gains traction when Neftchi details the retaliation. The Sudanese army, now backed by Turkish drones, struck deep into enemy territory. "This isn't just a local war anymore. It's a full-blown proxy conflict that pulls in foreign powers far and wide, most notably the UAE and Turkey," he asserts. The strength of this coverage lies in its specificity: the author doesn't just say "foreign involvement"; he names the drone models, the transit routes through Chad, and the specific financial deals. This granular detail makes the abstract concept of a proxy war feel immediate and dangerous.

Port Sudan was the last domino and it just fell.

Critics might argue that the evidence linking the UAE directly to the attacks is circumstantial, relying on flight paths and intercepted weapons rather than a smoking gun confession. However, Neftchi's point stands: in modern hybrid warfare, the pattern of logistics is often the only proof available. The denial from Emirati officials is noted, but the author rightly suggests that the "consistent flight paths and overland convoys tell a different story."

The Economics of Violence

The commentary then pivots to the economic engines driving this destruction. Neftchi paints the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) not merely as a militia, but as a "state within a state" built on smuggling and resource extraction. "The RSF has built its empire on smuggling routes, gold mines, and foreign backers," he explains. This framing is essential because it explains why the RSF can sustain a war that would otherwise collapse a conventional army. They are not fighting for ideology; they are fighting for a revenue stream that includes gold mines and rare earth minerals.

Conversely, the Sudanese Armed Forces are portrayed as the legitimate but struggling entity, reliant on Turkey to regain the initiative. Neftchi highlights a $120 million drone package from Turkey's Baykar firm, noting that "cargo logs, satellite imagery, and leaked customs data confirmed the deliveries." The author's analysis of the strategic shift is sharp: the war has moved from trench fighting to an infrastructure war where "roads, air strips, fuel depots, and drone factories" are the primary targets. This is a sophisticated observation that elevates the piece above standard conflict reporting. It suggests that the battlefield is now the entire logistics network, not just the front lines.

The Red Sea Stakes

The most compelling section of Neftchi's work is the geopolitical context. He argues that for the UAE, Sudan is a piece in a larger puzzle to dominate the Red Sea. "Port Sudan would complete the chain and turn the Red Sea into an Emirati corridor, which would then give it considerable leverage over one of the world's most strategic maritime routes," he writes. This connects the local violence to global trade security, making the conflict relevant to a much wider audience. The author effectively contrasts this with Turkey's historical ambitions, specifically the 99-year lease on the port of Suakin, which was frozen but remains a strategic goal.

The narrative takes a darker turn when Neftchi describes the direct targeting of foreign nationals. He details an attack on a cargo plane at Nyala airport that killed "four Emiratis" alongside RSF fighters. "The ambush was believed to be planned and executed by the UAE, not the leadership of the RSF," Neftchi suggests, implying that the Emirati state is now a direct combatant. This is a significant escalation. The author notes that following this, Sudan cut ties with Abu Dhabi, signaling that the diplomatic shield has been removed.

Because the more you try to control a war from afar, the closer it pulls you in.

A counterargument worth considering is whether Turkey and the UAE are truly seeking a direct collision or if they are simply miscalculating the risks of escalation. Neftchi leans heavily on the inevitability of a "collision," but regional powers often engage in brinkmanship without intending to cross the threshold into open war. However, the author's evidence of embedded military advisers and intelligence officers on both sides suggests that the line between proxy and direct engagement has already been blurred beyond repair.

Bottom Line

Shirvan Neftchi's strongest asset is his ability to connect the dots between drone wreckage, gold smuggling, and Red Sea geopolitics, proving that Sudan is the staging ground for a regional cold war. The piece's vulnerability lies in its reliance on intelligence leaks and satellite analysis, which, while compelling, can never fully replace on-the-ground verification in a denied environment. Readers should watch closely for the next phase: whether Chad and the Central African Republic will be dragged into the conflict as the fighting spills over their borders. The war in Sudan is no longer just about Sudan; it is a test of how much instability the Red Sea can absorb before the entire system collapses.
