Kamil Galeev dismantles the popular myth that Christianity conquered Rome through a singular, dramatic clash of faiths, arguing instead that its rise was the inevitable result of an elite-led spiritual vacuum filled by Eastern syncretism. He reframes the narrative from one of heroic persecution to a story of political opportunism, where emperors from Syria weaponized the new sect to consolidate power in a fracturing empire. For the busy reader seeking to understand how institutional power shifts, this piece offers a corrective to the simplistic "Good Friday vs. Pagan Rome" trope that dominates popular history.

The Elite Hypocrisy That Broke the Old Order

Galeev begins by exposing a critical flaw in the traditional narrative: the old Roman religion was already dead before it was buried. He notes that while public participation in state rituals remained compulsory, the private belief of the ruling class had evaporated. "The deconstruction and denigration of the old religion was started by the Roman elites themselves," Galeev writes, pointing out that this skepticism trickled down from the fashionable upper echelons to the masses who simply mimicked their betters. This is a compelling sociological observation; it suggests that the collapse of a state religion is often a top-down phenomenon driven by intellectual cynicism rather than grassroots rebellion. The author effectively argues that once the masses realized their masters no longer believed in Jupiter, the ritual lost its binding power.

"Popular masses have a natural instinct to mimic their social betters, and emulate their opinions. They soon started to notice that smart, sophisticated people, their masters do not believe in Jupiter anymore."

This dynamic mirrors the historical concept of religio licita, where the state attempted to manage faith as a civic duty rather than a spiritual truth. Galeev's point is that the state could not hide the elite's disbelief from the servants and slaves who knew their masters better than the masters knew themselves. The resulting "lacuna"—a void in spiritual meaning—was not filled by native Western traditions, which the Romans viewed as culturally inferior, but by aggressive Eastern imports. The author's focus on the "Hellenic-Egyptian synthesis" is particularly sharp, reminding us that the Rome of the Antonines was already a cosmopolitan hub where Serapis and Mithras were as common as the Capitoline Triad.

The Syrian Dynasty and the Politicization of Faith

The commentary then shifts to the crucial role of the Severan dynasty, specifically the Emesene family from Syria, in elevating Christianity from a fringe sect to a protected religion. Galeev highlights how Alexander Severus, a Syrian emperor, placed Christ in his home shrine alongside other deities, effectively normalizing the figure within the imperial pantheon. "It all started with Alexander Severus," Galeev writes, noting that this prince was not biologically connected to the original Severan line but was part of a priest-king dynasty from Homs that worshipped a Baal-like deity. This historical pivot is often overlooked; the rise of Christianity is frequently attributed to later figures like Constantine, yet the groundwork was laid by these Syrian rulers who saw political utility in the sect.

The argument gains further traction with the case of Philip the Arab, another Syrian emperor whom Galeev identifies as the first ruler suspected of being a secret Christian. The author suggests that the subsequent massacre of Philip's court by a usurper was later mislabeled by Christian tradition as a religious persecution, when in reality, it was a political purge of a court that simply happened to be full of Christians. "We have an interesting indirect evidence of the protection & promotion he showed to the Christian sect," Galeev notes, challenging the reader to reconsider the nature of early imperial hostility. This reframing is vital: it suggests that the "persecution" narrative is often a retrospective construction of a church that needed martyrs to build its legitimacy.

"The true, mass, and the empire wide persecution, came only at the very end of the Pagan Rome, in the age of Diocletian. By that time, the Christian Church got powerful enough, and popular enough, and influential enough to truly scare much of the existing elites."

Here, Galeev makes a provocative comparison, noting that Roman magistrates treated Christians with disdain but not the "theist zeal" that would later characterize Christian rulers. He draws a parallel to the Communist experience, observing that "Communists, when they came to power, were slaughtering each other way more diligently and zealously than they had been ever persecuted by the Tsarist authorities." This is a striking insight into the nature of ideological power: the victors are often more ruthless than the oppressors they replaced. Critics might argue that this minimizes the genuine suffering of early Christians, but Galeev's evidence regarding the inconsistency of Roman enforcement—limited to specific cities and eras rather than a systematic genocide—holds up well against the historical record.

The Civil War of Christian Protectors

The piece concludes by dismantling the myth of the Battle of the Milvian Bridge as a triumph of Christianity over Paganism. Galeev argues that by the time of the conflict, the question was not "Christian vs. Pagan" but rather "which Christian emperor would win." He points out that both Constantine and his rival Maxentius were patrons of the Christian creed, as was their eastern rival Licinius. "By the moment of the battle, both Constantine, the emperor of Gaul, and Maxentius, the emperor of Italy were patrons and protectors of the Christian creed," Galeev writes. "So what happened is that one protector of Christianity killed and defeated the other."

This analysis strips the event of its theological drama, revealing it as a standard power struggle where the religious affiliation of the contenders was secondary to their political utility. The author notes that after Diocletian's failed attempt to create a parallel Pagan church, the subsequent tetrarchy was too fractured to sustain a unified anti-Christian policy. Instead, the various claimants to the throne competed to be the "better defender" of the sect to secure its loyalty. This effectively turns the standard narrative on its head: Christianity did not conquer Rome through divine intervention or moral superiority alone; it was adopted by the state because it had become the most efficient tool for political consolidation in a crumbling empire.

"By the late stages of the civil war on the ruins of Diocletian's tetrarchy, the question was not whether a Christian prince will defeat a Pagan one, but rather which of three very and very pro-Christians princes will triumph in the end."

The author's reliance on the lack of a surviving text for Diocletian's edict serves as a reminder of how much of this history is reconstructed from vague descriptions and assumptions. Yet, the core argument remains robust: the institutional dynamics of the late empire favored a centralized, hierarchical religion, and Christianity was the only candidate capable of filling that role.

Bottom Line

Kamil Galeev's strongest contribution is his refusal to treat the rise of Christianity as a spiritual miracle, instead presenting it as a calculated political adaptation by a Syrian-led elite in a decaying empire. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its tendency to downplay the genuine theological appeal of Christianity to the lower classes, focusing almost exclusively on top-down manipulation. However, for a reader interested in the mechanics of institutional power, this piece provides a necessary, if cynical, corrective to the hagiography that usually surrounds this historical transition.
