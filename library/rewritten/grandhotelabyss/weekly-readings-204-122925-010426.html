John Pistelli makes a provocative claim that will stop a busy reader in their tracks: the literature we revere is not merely entertainment, but the surviving textual record of an actual, marginalized religion. In a cultural moment obsessed with the death of irony and the rise of algorithmic sincerity, Pistelli argues that the "religion of art" is the only viable spiritual framework left, one that quietly persists even in those who claim to be secular. This is not a plea for more reading, but a radical re-framing of why we read at all.

The Liturgy of the Imagined

Pistelli begins by addressing a common skepticism: that elevating literature to a spiritual plane is merely self-serving for writers. He dismantles this by suggesting that the canon—from Greek tragedians to modernists—preserves a "diversely populated cosmic order" that was suppressed by monotheistic orthodoxy. He writes, "a select body of imaginative literature... is the extant textual record of an actual and by no means exclusively textual religious practice and faith." This reframing is powerful because it shifts the burden of proof; the reader is not being asked to believe in a new dogma, but to recognize an ancient one that has been hiding in plain sight within the pages of Dante, Shakespeare, and Woolf.

The author draws a direct line between this literary faith and the historical mystery cults, noting that the "religion of art" is essentially a return to the "Greek mystery cults and then with various gnosticism." Just as the ancient initiates of the Eleusinian Mysteries sought a direct, transformative experience of the divine through ritual rather than rigid dogma, Pistelli suggests that the modern reader engages in a similar practice. He argues that this faith has not vanished but has "quietly crept even into the official churches by now," manifesting in the way we treat art as a site of revelation. "You place a candle on the altar every time you say, 'Who am I to judge?'" he observes, highlighting how secular language often masks religious structures.

"I am saying to read Ulysses if you want to understand consciously your once and future religion, the religion you probably practice even without knowing it."

This assertion is the piece's intellectual anchor. It suggests that the "religion of art" is not about the text itself, but about the human imagination's capacity to generate meaning in a world that often feels devoid of it. Pistelli acknowledges the risk here, citing Eric Voegelin's warning about the dangers of trying to "immanentize the eschaton"—to force the divine into the material world. He admits that this "archaic and futurist religion" has its perils, noting that "if monotheist orthodoxies won out over gnosticism and paganism initially for a reason, monotheist orthodoxies and their materialist sequelae haven't been able to sustain themselves for a reason, too." The argument is compelling precisely because it does not shy away from the potential for fanaticism, even as it champions the necessity of the imaginative leap.

The Digital Pagan and the Future of Culture

Pistelli then pivots to the modern landscape, arguing that this "religion" is no longer the exclusive domain of the literate elite. He observes that the most ardent practitioners today might be "borderline illiterates making a vision board or watching anime." This is a crucial, if uncomfortable, insight: the spiritual impulse has migrated from the library to the screen, and the content matters less than the act of devotion. "The people practicing it most ardently as we speak are... Grimes or Sigh Swoon," he writes, suggesting that the "magical" metaphysics of the Gnostics are alive in the digital subcultures of the 21st century.

He also tackles the tension between the online and offline worlds, rejecting the idea that we must choose between them. While he acknowledges that social media is in decline, he argues that the future of cultural engagement lies in using digital tools to facilitate real-world connection. "The key is to avoid getting stuck entirely inside the machine rather than using it to augment non-machinic experience," he advises. This is a pragmatic counter to the Luddite impulse that often plagues literary circles. He notes that his own book sales were boosted by the online platform, but only because it led to tangible, offline interactions. "We want online and off- to be working in concert to advance common ends," he concludes, a sentiment that feels increasingly urgent as the digital landscape fractures.

Critics might note that Pistelli's definition of "religion" is so broad it risks losing its analytical power. If everything from anime to vision boards is a form of the "religion of art," does the term still distinguish anything meaningful? Furthermore, his dismissal of the "sincere" as a threat to human consciousness could be seen as an over-correction against a culture that desperately needs more authenticity, not less. Yet, his core point remains: the human need for myth and meaning is inescapable, and literature offers a unique, time-tested vessel for it.

Bottom Line

Pistelli's most striking contribution is his ability to reframe the act of reading not as an academic exercise, but as a spiritual practice that has survived the collapse of traditional orthodoxy. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on a specific, high-culture canon to define a universal human experience, potentially alienating those who find their "mystery cults" in spaces Pistelli deems less legible. However, for the reader willing to engage with the idea that their favorite novel is a relic of an ancient faith, this piece offers a profound and necessary perspective on the enduring power of the imagination.
