John Pistelli offers a rare, unapologetic defense of the "religion of art," arguing that aesthetic experience is not a frivolous escape but a vital, ancient spiritual framework capable of outlasting both Christian conservatism and Marxist materialism. In a year defined by political turbulence and cultural fragmentation, his retrospective on 2025 suggests that the most profound resistance to chaos lies not in policy or protest, but in the deliberate, complex reordering of beauty through literature and myth.

The Return to the Short Story and the Political Subtext

Pistelli begins his year-in-review by highlighting a surprising return to the short story form, a genre he had previously declared finished. He describes the creation of his novelette, The Persephone Complex: A Letter, as a process of piecing together motifs from the ancient myth of Persephone and contemporary political anxieties. "I couldn't say no, though; I spent the last two decades having my submissions rejected, so I'm not going to turn down invitations!" he writes, framing the work as a personal victory against professional inertia. The story, published in The Metropolitan Review, features an early episode of political assassination, a detail that Pistelli notes he included before a high-profile real-world assassination attempt occurred later that summer. "Then I figured I'd been onto something," he admits, suggesting an uncanny resonance between the fiction and the unfolding reality of the era.

This blending of myth and current events is central to his argument. By invoking the Persephone myth—a story of abduction, seasonal cycles, and the underworld—he reframes modern political violence not as an anomaly, but as a recurring archetype. The inclusion of a "shell-case messaging" plot device further grounds the mythic in the tactical, creating a narrative that feels both timeless and urgently specific. Critics might note that relying on archetypal patterns can sometimes obscure the unique, systemic causes of modern political violence, but Pistelli's approach seems designed to evoke emotional truth rather than offer a policy analysis.

The New Romanticism and the Danger of Beauty

The core of Pistelli's 2025 output revolves around his advocacy for "The New Romanticism," a movement he defines by its rejection of historicist pedantry in favor of a living, dangerous engagement with beauty. He details his contribution to the journal Romanticon, where he attempted to strip the term of its academic baggage. "First, to remove discussions of New Romanticism from historicist pedantry about the old Romanticism," he explains, arguing that the movement is a tool to "advance today's culture, not to provide academic studies of yesterday's." However, this stance drew sharp criticism. He confesses that his piece was his "least liked" of the year, noting that his concession regarding the "potentially dangerous metaphysic and politic of Romanticisms old and new" was misread as an endorsement of reactionary politics.

Pistelli's defense is that the danger is inherent to the project. He suggests that the "New Romanticism" is a "reactionary-radical temperament" that refuses to sanitize the past or the present. "I mean not only the recovery of beauty, but also the redefinition of beauty to mean nothing less than an orderly recreation and therefore an augmentation of the world's intense complexity," he writes in his centenary essay on The Great Gatsby. This redefinition is crucial: beauty is not a static object of admiration but an active force that organizes and expands human understanding. The backlash he faced highlights a tension in contemporary culture: the fear that any attempt to reclaim grand narratives or spiritual aesthetics will inevitably slide into authoritarianism. Pistelli argues that this fear is a limitation, not a flaw, in the movement's potential.

The Invisible College and the Religion of Art

Perhaps the most significant development in Pistelli's year was the second season of his podcast, The Invisible College, which took listeners on a rigorous journey from ancient Greek tragedy to the modern American novel. The curriculum was vast, covering Homer, the Athenian playwrights, Shakespeare, and modern giants like Toni Morrison and Thomas Pynchon. Through this deep dive, Pistelli arrived at a startling conclusion: the critical dismissal of art as a mere pastime is a fundamental error. "The main and developing lesson of The Invisible College... is that what critics of aestheticism, whether Christian conservative or Marxist materialist, disparage as 'the religion of art' is actually a real religion," he asserts.

He posits that this "religion of art" is older than Christianity or Marxism and may be "fitter for the future than either." This claim is bold, positioning the literary imagination as the primary vessel for human meaning in a secular age. He notes a personal shift in perspective, admitting that he previously held a "prejudicial pseudo-conviction that... French literature is 'too human'" compared to the "alien intensities" of other traditions. "The French are aliens, too, I am happy to report," he concludes, signaling a move toward a more inclusive, global aesthetic. This realization underscores his broader point: the "religion of art" is not about dogma, but about the capacity of literature to reveal the "alien" or the unknown within the familiar.

For many are the pleasant forms which exist in numerous sins, and incontinencies, and disgraceful passions, and fleeting pleasures…

Pistelli's journey through the year also included a foray into cinema, where he selected Jean-Luc Godard's Je vous salue, Marie as a key text. He draws a parallel between the film's controversial reception and the broader cultural resistance to complex spiritual art. "It is a worldly man's movie, but that man is also an artist who complicates and transcends convention," he observes, defending the film against charges of wounding religious sentiment. This defense mirrors his argument for literature: that true art does not comfort the believer or the materialist, but challenges both to see the world in a new, more complex light.

Bottom Line

John Pistelli's 2025 review is a compelling, if controversial, manifesto for the power of aesthetic experience in a fractured world. His strongest argument lies in his refusal to separate beauty from danger, insisting that the "religion of art" is a necessary, ancient force for organizing human complexity. However, the piece's vulnerability is its potential to alienate readers who view any invocation of "spiritual" or "religious" frameworks in art as a step backward into dogmatism. As the cultural landscape continues to shift, the success of this "New Romanticism" will depend on whether it can prove that its vision of beauty is inclusive enough to hold the world's contradictions without collapsing into reactionary politics.
