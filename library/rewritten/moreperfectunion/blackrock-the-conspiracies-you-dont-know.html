More Perfect Union cuts through the noise of online conspiracy theories to reveal a far more disturbing reality: BlackRock's power doesn't come from a secret cabal, but from the mundane, legal mechanics of how we save for retirement. The author argues that the true threat isn't a shadowy plot, but a system where asset managers profit from market growth regardless of whether companies compete or collude, effectively turning the entire economy into a single, uncompetitive monopoly. This is essential listening for anyone who assumes their 401(k) is a passive bet on the American dream, rather than an active vote for the status quo.

The Mechanics of Universal Ownership

The piece begins by dismantling the comforting myth that BlackRock is merely a passive observer. More Perfect Union writes, "that 10.6 trillion dollar isn't really their money... it's your dad's pension fund and your insurer's massive pile of savings." This distinction is crucial because it highlights the disconnect between the source of the capital and the entity wielding the voting power. The author explains that while index funds are marketed as a low-risk way to bet on the whole market, the sheer scale of this "universal ownership" means BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street are the largest shareholders in almost every major corporation.

The argument gains traction when the author points out the concentration of this power. "5% in any individual company is actually very significant because if and when shareholders is dispersed 5% makes you in all likelihood the single largest shareholder in that company." This is a powerful observation because it reframes a small percentage as a controlling interest in a fragmented landscape. The author notes that while these firms claim to be passive, their inability to sell off massive stakes without crashing the market effectively locks them into a position of permanent influence. Critics might note that index funds are still the best option for individual investors seeking diversification, but the piece effectively argues that the collective result of this diversification is a lack of competition.

"The financial sector effectively owns itself... creating this Loop that sucks money in and never seems to spit it back out."

The Illusion of Democracy and the Reality of Stagnation

The commentary takes a sharp turn toward the human cost of this financial architecture. More Perfect Union writes, "asset managers almost always voted with what the company Executives recommended." This evidence is damning because it suggests that the theoretical check on corporate power—shareholder voting—has been neutralized. The author connects this to wage stagnation, arguing that when the same entity owns shares in competing companies like Nike and Adidas, there is no incentive for them to lower prices or raise wages to outperform each other. Instead, the "universal ownership" logic encourages all companies to maximize profits by squeezing workers, knowing that the losses from one company are offset by gains in another within the same portfolio.

The author drives this home with a stark statistic: "today the top 1% own 50% of corporate equity and mutual fund shares while the top 10% own 86%." This data point dismantles the common political argument that "what is good for shareholders is good for everyone." The piece effectively argues that this is a fallacy because the bottom 50% of Americans own virtually no shares, meaning they bear the brunt of high prices and stagnant wages without sharing in the upside. The author's framing of this as a "Neo Monopoly" where companies don't need to merge to act as one is particularly effective in explaining why competition has vanished from sectors like airlines and banking.

The Revolving Door and the Self-Audit Loophole

Perhaps the most unsettling part of the coverage is the exploration of the relationship between BlackRock and the government. More Perfect Union writes, "Black Rock doubled their political lobbying spending... and manag to dodge the oversight that other large financial institutions receive." The author details a revolving door where dozens of former regulators and central bankers find employment at BlackRock, blurring the lines between public oversight and private profit. The piece highlights the absurdity of the current regulatory framework, noting that BlackRock is allowed to "self-certify" its own passivity to avoid stricter banking regulations.

This section is compelling because it exposes a structural flaw where the entity being regulated writes the rules for its own compliance. "The part that really blows my mind is that while this one company already has their eggs in basically every basket... it goes even deeper than that. The biggest investors in BlackRock are Vanguard and State Street." This recursive ownership structure creates a closed loop that insulates the financial sector from external pressure. While the author acknowledges that the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau is working to change this, the sheer inertia of the system suggests that meaningful reform will be difficult. The argument holds up well against the counter-claim that these firms are simply efficient; efficiency here appears to be a euphemism for a lack of accountability.

Bottom Line

More Perfect Union's strongest contribution is reframing BlackRock not as a villainous conspiracy, but as the logical, legal endpoint of a financial system designed to prioritize asset growth over economic health. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that regulatory bodies will ever have the political will to break this cycle, a point the author admits is uncertain. Readers should watch for the ongoing efforts by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau to regulate asset managers like banks, as this could be the first real test of whether this "universal ownership" model can be reined in.
