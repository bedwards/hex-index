David Lat cuts through the holiday noise to deliver a legal landscape defined by a stark collision between judicial independence and federal enforcement power. While the piece offers a warm look at academic appointments, its most urgent contribution is the forensic dissection of Judge Hannah Dugan's conviction, a case that forces the legal community to confront the limits of a judge's authority when it clashes with immigration policy.

The Weight of Institutional Voice

Lat begins by shifting focus from the grim headlines of recent violence to the quiet but significant reshaping of legal academia. He highlights the selection of Cristina Rodríguez as the next dean of Yale Law School, framing her appointment as a potential turning point for intellectual diversity. Lat writes, "My hope is that Cristina Rodríguez will stand up strongly for free speech, academic freedom, and intellectual diversity at Yale Law School—even if it means occasionally taking heat from the left-leaning law students and progressive professors who predominate at YLS." This framing is crucial because it moves the conversation beyond mere pedigree—Rodríguez's résumé includes a Rhodes Scholarship and a clerkship for Justice Sandra Day O'Connor—to the political pressures she will inevitably face.

The author notes that Rodríguez's selection drew praise even from conservative voices, including Adam J. White, who commended her for being "open-minded" and "run[ning] a serious, even-handed process." Lat uses this cross-aisle support to argue that the Yale Law School community may be entering a new era of institutional stability. He points to Rodríguez's prior work on a committee that recommended university leaders "refrain from issuing statements concerning matters of public, social, or political significance, except in rare cases." While Lat supports the general principle of restraint, he expresses skepticism about the "rare cases" wiggle room, suggesting that the definition of "rare" will be the true battleground.

"My hope is that Cristina Rodríguez will stand up strongly for free speech, academic freedom, and intellectual diversity at Yale Law School—even if it means occasionally taking heat from the left-leaning law students and progressive professors who predominate at YLS."

Critics might argue that Lat underestimates the structural inertia of a deeply progressive institution, suggesting that a single dean's commitment to neutrality may not be enough to shift the campus culture. However, the fact that Rodríguez co-chaired a committee recommending such restraint indicates a genuine, pre-existing commitment to this difficult balancing act.

The Dugan Verdict and the Cost of Obstruction

The commentary pivots sharply to the conviction of Milwaukee County Circuit Court Judge Hannah Dugan, a case that Lat treats with the gravity of a constitutional crisis. The core of the issue is not just the obstruction of justice, but the specific motivation: Dugan allegedly helped an undocumented immigrant defendant evade Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agents waiting in the hallway. Lat describes the moment Dugan told a court staffer she would take "the heat" for the plan, a phrase that became the emotional anchor of the trial.

Lat writes, "In audio from her courtroom's brand-new sound system that was played to the jury, Judge Dugan was recorded telling a court staffer, who expressed concern about the plan, that she (Dugan) would take 'the heat'—i.e., accept any consequences arising out of the effort to aid Flores-Ruiz." This evidence is damning, yet Lat contextualizes it within the broader tension between the executive branch's deportation priorities and the judiciary's role. He notes that the case attracted national attention, with critics of the administration condemning it as an attempt to "intimidate judges and other state or local officials opposed to the president's deportation efforts."

The author is careful to distinguish between the legal outcome and the political narrative. Dugan was found guilty of obstruction, a felony, but acquitted of concealing a person from arrest. Lat suggests that while she faces up to five years in prison, the reality will likely be lighter, noting that the presiding judge, Lynn Adelman, is known as a "light sentencer." However, the professional consequence is absolute: "Judge Dugan will probably face at least one consequence: having been convicted of a felony, she's likely ineligible to keep her judicial office."

This case forces a difficult question: Can a judge legally obstruct federal agents to protect a defendant from deportation? Lat does not answer this definitively but implies that the "heat" Dugan promised to take may ultimately cost her the bench. The argument is effective because it refuses to paint Dugan solely as a hero or a villain, instead presenting her as a casualty of a fractured legal system where state and federal authorities are at war.

"Judge Dugan was found guilty of obstruction, a felony. She was acquitted of concealing a person from arrest, a misdemeanor."

A counterargument worth considering is that Lat's focus on the sentencing leniency of Judge Adelman might downplay the severity of the precedent set by the conviction itself. Even if Dugan avoids prison, the felony conviction sends a chilling message to any judge considering similar acts of civil disobedience against federal immigration enforcement.

The Machinery of Legal Appointments and Ethics

Beyond the high-profile drama, Lat provides a necessary digest of the legal system's moving parts, from the Senate's "en bloc" confirmation of nearly 100 nominees to the ethical quagmires facing major law firms. He notes the confirmation of 13 U.S. attorneys and new regulatory chairs, a move that signals a rapid shift in the administration's personnel strategy. Lat writes, "The group included 13 U.S. attorneys, three D.C. Superior Court judges, and new chairs for the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. and Commodity Futures Trading Corp." This procedural efficiency, he suggests, is a hallmark of the current political moment, bypassing the traditional, slower pace of judicial confirmations.

The piece also touches on the darker side of the legal profession, detailing lawsuits against firms like DLA Piper and Kasowitz LLP. Lat highlights the allegations against Kasowitz, where a former partner claims the firm was "secretly saddle[d] with huge amounts of debt to pay partner distributions" while failing to pay him millions. This section serves as a reminder that while the judiciary faces existential questions, the private bar is grappling with its own internal rot.

"The defendants deny Herschmann's allegations, calling his complaint 'a false document filled with inaccuracies and misstatements.'"

Lat's inclusion of these corporate disputes alongside the Dugan trial creates a juxtaposition that is both jarring and revealing. It suggests a legal ecosystem where high-stakes constitutional battles are happening simultaneously with low-level financial mismanagement and sexual harassment scandals.

Bottom Line

David Lat's commentary succeeds by refusing to let the holiday season obscure the hard realities of the legal landscape, particularly the dangerous friction between judicial independence and executive enforcement. The strongest part of his argument is the nuanced portrayal of Judge Dugan's conviction, which avoids political cheerleading to focus on the tragic inevitability of her professional demise. The biggest vulnerability lies in the piece's reliance on the assumption that the "heat" of political controversy will not further radicalize the judiciary, a risk that remains unquantified. Readers should watch closely to see if the Dugan appeal, potentially led by Paul Clement, sets a precedent that redefines the boundaries of judicial obstruction in the context of immigration enforcement.
