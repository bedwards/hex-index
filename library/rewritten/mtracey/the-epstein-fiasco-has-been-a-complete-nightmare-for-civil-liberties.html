Michael Tracey delivers a scathing indictment not of Jeffrey Epstein, but of the American legal system's willingness to abandon due process in the face of public outrage. While the media has been saturated with the details of the sex-trafficking scandal, Tracey argues that the real story is the "complete nightmare for civil liberties" that unfolded when the courts transformed into a theater of unverified accusations. This piece is essential listening because it exposes how the label of "victim" was weaponized to bypass the Constitution, creating a precedent where the government pays for travel to a courtroom to hear stories that are never cross-examined.

The Death of Neutrality

The core of Tracey's argument rests on the bizarre judicial maneuvering following Epstein's death in federal custody. With the defendant dead, the charges should have been dismissed immediately. Instead, Judge Richard M. Berman convened a hearing that Tracey describes as "unique, and perhaps unprecedented." The judge invited self-identified victims to speak without the defendant present to refute them. "Berman announced he was going to do something wildly aberrational," Tracey writes, noting that the hearing was technically about nolle prosequi—the legal term for dismissing charges due to the death of the accused—but served as a cover for something far more theatrical.

This framing is powerful because it highlights a specific legal mechanism, nolle prosequi, which is usually a dry procedural formality, and shows how it was twisted into a vehicle for a "judicially-authorized struggle session." Tracey draws a sharp parallel to historical political purges, suggesting the court abandoned its role as a neutral arbiter. The author notes that Judge Berman referred to these individuals as "bonafide victims" without requiring verification, effectively granting them an unchallengeable status. "Gone was any idea that assigning the label of 'victim' should be accompanied by meddlesome, outmoded qualifiers such as 'alleged victim,'" Tracey observes. This shift from "alleged" to "bonafide" without evidence is the crux of the civil liberties crisis.

Critics might argue that in cases of sexual violence, the traditional legal process often re-traumatizes victims, and that this hearing was a necessary step to give them a voice. However, Tracey counters that the lack of cross-examination and the absence of the accused turned the courtroom into a "free-for-all" where facts were secondary to emotion.

"The vindictive moralistic frenzy that attaches to this issue means that by simply calling attention to objectionable government conduct, you can expect to be instantly spun as somehow condoning the personal proclivities of Jeffrey Epstein."

The Economics of Accusation

Tracey then pivots to the financial mechanics of this spectacle, revealing that taxpayers were not just funding the legal process but actively subsidizing the accusers' travel. The author details how lawyer Bradley Edwards convinced prosecutors to pay for flights and hotels for anyone who wished to attend. "The government has never done this before," Tracey quotes prosecutor Maurene Comey as saying, yet within an hour, the agreement was made. The result was a scene where Virginia Roberts Giuffre, described by Tracey as a "proven serial fabulist," requested business class travel due to a "medical condition" that turned out to be a preference for comfort.

The author points out the absurdity of the government funding a $10,673.40 one-way ticket for an accuser to tell a story that would never be tested in court. "So not only was Judge Berman holding this elaborate, essentially extra-judicial hearing... taxpayers were also going to subsidize the brouhaha," Tracey writes. This evidence of state-subsidized storytelling is particularly damning because it suggests the government was incentivizing the creation of a narrative rather than seeking the truth. The author also highlights the case of Sarah Ransome, whose flight was paid for after she declared herself a "survivor" of an international trafficking ring, a claim that has never been substantiated in a criminal proceeding.

The Fabrication of Facts

Perhaps the most striking section of the commentary is Tracey's dissection of specific, unverified claims that were accepted by the court without scrutiny. He recounts the story of an anonymous "Jane Doe" who claimed Epstein picked her up at a Texas mall to take her to his nearby home. "Epstein is never known to have had a 'home' in Texas," Tracey notes, pointing out that his nearest property was 200 miles away in New Mexico. Despite this geographical impossibility, the story was read aloud by Gloria Allred and accepted by the judge.

Tracey emphasizes that the legal system's failure to vet these claims allowed "victimhood" to become a "metaphysical status" rather than a legal fact. "That was totally immaterial," the author writes regarding the truthfulness of the claims. The piece further details how Allred later dropped this client without explanation, yet continued to act as a conduit between federal prosecutors and new, unverified accusers. "No clients of Allred are known to have testified in the 2021 trial of Ghislaine Maxwell, or to have played any material role in that prosecution," Tracey states, undermining the narrative that these hearings were essential for justice. Instead, they appear to have been a mechanism for generating civil litigation leverage.

"Here's a neat trick for prosecutors and politicians: if you want to make the Constitution vanish, just say you're punishing 'pedos.'"

Bottom Line

Michael Tracey's most compelling contribution is his demonstration of how moral panic can be weaponized to dismantle procedural safeguards, turning a courtroom into a stage for unverified storytelling funded by the public. While the outrage over Epstein's crimes is entirely justified, the article's greatest vulnerability is its heavy reliance on the credibility of specific accusers to prove the system's failure, which risks appearing to minimize the very real harm suffered by victims. The reader should watch for how this precedent of unverified, state-funded accusations influences future high-profile cases where public anger threatens to override the rule of law.
