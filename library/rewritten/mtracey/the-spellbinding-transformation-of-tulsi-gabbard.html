Michael Tracey presents a phenomenon that defies standard political analysis: a transformation so total it transcends the usual label of hypocrisy. While critics often dismiss political flip-flops as mere opportunism, Tracey argues that Tulsi Gabbard's shift from the most vocal opponent of regime change to a key architect of the administration's aggressive foreign policy is a unique historical anomaly. This is not just a change of heart; it is a complete erasure of a political identity that was once defined by the very actions she now champions.

The Anatomy of a Metamorphosis

Tracey, who claims to have covered Gabbard more closely than any other journalist, posits that her evolution requires a new vocabulary entirely. He writes, "Her transformation is so sensational, and so without any meaningful analog in recent political history, that it uniquely cries out for intense examination, as a phenomenon that has scarcely been observed before in the natural world." This framing elevates the discussion from political gossip to a study of institutional behavior. The author suggests that while politicians routinely adapt to new data, Gabbard's case involves no new data at all.

The core of Tracey's argument rests on the timeline of Gabbard's career. He notes that her opposition to "regime change" was not a peripheral stance but the "animating theme of her 2020 presidential campaign." Tracey writes, "It was the origin story of her entire career... she served in Iraq, realized the folly of 'regime change,' and resolved to get in the political arena so she could ensure no soldier would ever again have to fight and die in vain." This context is crucial because it establishes that her previous positions were deeply personal and central to her public brand, making the reversal far more jarring than a typical policy pivot.

"She's invented a new genre of something, for which we do not even have a good name yet."

Tracey contrasts this with other political realignments, such as Ted Cruz's reconciliation with the administration. He argues that Cruz's shift was logical because the administration delivered on specific conservative priorities like judicial appointments. In Gabbard's case, however, the policy record she once condemned remained identical. Tracey points out that she had formally censured the administration for "unconstitutional and reckless actions," including the "illegally and unconstitutionally using US military forces to occupy and pillage oilfield reserves of Syria." Yet, by 2024, she was praising that same record as a model of diplomacy. Tracey writes, "There was not even an attempt to explain how, in the very recent past, she had not only denounced Trump's first-term policy record... she had cited it as an impetus to run for president, so she could defeat him for re-election herself!"

The Human Cost of Policy Reversals

The stakes of this transformation extend far beyond political maneuvering; they touch on the lives of civilians in nations targeted by US intervention. Tracey highlights Gabbard's past warnings regarding Iran, noting she had declared a "state of war" had been instigated following the assassination of General Soleimani. She had called it an "illegal and unconstitutional act of war" with "no justification whatsoever." Now, with the executive branch barreling toward renewed confrontation, those warnings are being ignored by the very person who once championed them.

Critics might note that Tracey's focus on the lack of logical explanation for Gabbard's shift overlooks the possibility that she genuinely believes the geopolitical landscape has shifted, even if the policy record hasn't. However, Tracey counters this by emphasizing the speed of the reversal, which coincided perfectly with her securing a cabinet position. He writes, "The rapid escalation of Trump's latest threats can almost make one forget that it was not even two weeks ago that he launched his spectacular excursion in Venezuela, raining down bombs, abducting its leader, and then declaring the US will now 'run' the country in perpetuity."

The article draws a grim parallel to historical crises, noting that the current administration's fixation on foreign policy mirrors the intensity of previous eras of conflict. Tracey observes, "Trump appears to be barreling toward yet another military confrontation with Iran... leaving us all to speculate what further acts of kindness and generosity might be in store." The irony is palpable: the administration now frames these interventions as "help," a euphemism that obscures the reality of bombing campaigns and regime change attempts that Gabbard once fought against with such fervor.

"All that had discernibly changed was the political circumstances, which had fortuitously presented Tulsi Gabbard with an opportunity to insinuate herself into the Trump/GOP operation, in hopes of securing some cabinet position."

The Erosion of Accountability

Tracey's most biting critique is that the political ecosystem has become so accustomed to inconsistency that Gabbard's reversal goes unchallenged. He argues that we have entered a period where "shrieking 'hypocrisy' ad nauseam will eventually have diminishing returns." The public, conditioned to expect politicians to lie or change their minds, no longer reacts with the outrage such a reversal once would have provoked. This desensitization allows the administration to pursue aggressive foreign policies without the internal dissent that Gabbard once represented.

The author suggests that this dynamic is dangerous because it removes a crucial check on executive power. When a figure who once warned that the administration was "leading us down this dangerous path towards a war in Iran" becomes a defender of that same path, the warning system fails. Tracey writes, "She could thus jettison that obscure old position and adopt a new one — even if out of sheer political expediency — without much hassle. But it's no exaggeration to say that Tulsi Gabbard's critique of US foreign policy... was absolutely central to her very essence as a public figure."

Bottom Line

Tracey's piece offers a chilling diagnosis of a political moment where principle is entirely subordinate to power, arguing that Gabbard's transformation is not a bug but a feature of the current executive branch's strategy. The strongest part of his argument is the meticulous documentation of how Gabbard's past condemnations of specific military actions directly contradict her current support, yet the analysis risks underestimating the genuine volatility of the geopolitical situation that might force even a principled opponent to reconsider. As the administration accelerates its global interventions, the silence from those who once stood against them will likely be the most telling indicator of where the real power lies.
