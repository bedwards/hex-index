Rohin Francis does something rare in science commentary: he treats a science fiction TV show not as escapism, but as a rigorous medical case study for the next frontier of human expansion. While most analyses of The Expanse focus on political allegory or propulsion physics, Francis zeroes in on the visceral, biological reality of a human body trying to survive on a planet with Earth-like gravity after a lifetime in the void. His central claim is startlingly practical: the greatest barrier to colonizing Mars or the Belt isn't the rocket, but the fact that our physiology is a legacy system designed for a specific gravitational constant that we are about to break.

The Gravity Shock

Francis anchors his argument in the character of Naomi Nagata, a "Belter" who grew up in low gravity and struggles to adapt to the 1g environment of the planet Ilus. He uses her fictional crisis to explain the very real physiological collapse that occurs when astronauts return to Earth. "A lack of gravity causes loss of bone density and strength, reduced muscle mass and function, a smaller weaker heart," Francis writes, noting that these are not abstract risks but immediate, life-threatening conditions. He argues that the body, fooled by fluid redistribution in microgravity into thinking it is overloaded, aggressively sheds fluid and blood cells, leading to a state of anemia and dehydration the moment gravity returns.

This framing is effective because it moves beyond the cliché of "muscle atrophy" to the specific, dangerous mechanics of the cardiovascular system. Francis highlights the phenomenon astronauts call "puffy face bird leg syndrome," explaining that without the muscle pumps of the legs to push blood back to the heart, blood stasis occurs. "The dangers of this were brought home very recently when the fact that astronauts have suffered from deep vein thrombosis in central large veins was published quite recently," he notes. This is a crucial update to the conversation; it's not just about feeling weak, it's about the silent, floating clots that can become fatal embolisms.

In low gravity your blood volume is reduced because blood collects in the middle of the body fooling the kidneys into thinking you're overloaded with fluid and then the kidneys pee out more fluid.

Critics might argue that Francis underestimates the potential for rapid technological countermeasures, such as artificial gravity centrifuges, which could render these biological adaptations moot before we even land. However, his insistence that "there's no shortcut" and that "exercise there's no way around it" serves as a necessary reality check against the idea that a pill or a patch will solve the problem. He correctly identifies that while drugs like bisphosphonates can protect bone, the only way to rebuild the cardiovascular system is the grueling, two-hour daily regimen currently used on the International Space Station.

The Surgical Nightmare

Moving from the internal body to the operating theater, Francis shifts his focus to the chaotic physics of surgery in space. He dismantles the Hollywood trope of clean, contained procedures by pointing out that without gravity, blood does not pool; it floats. "Arterial bleeding which this appears to be is more likely to break the surface tension and cause all kinds of mess by forming free-floating droplets that will go all over the place," he explains. This isn't just a mess; it's a contamination vector. In a sealed spacecraft, floating blood droplets could carry pathogens into sensitive equipment or be inhaled by the crew.

The author's most compelling point here is the environmental hazard of the operating room itself. On Earth, convection currents help clear airborne microbes, but in space, "hot air doesn't rise the air doesn't circulate the same way." Francis cites data showing airborne microbes on the International Space Station are recorded at ten times the level of a standard operating theater. "Because there's no convection current due to a lack of gravity... you can get colonization of bacteria," he writes. This turns the act of surgery into a high-stakes game of containment, where the primary goal isn't just stopping the bleeding, but preventing the patient's own blood from becoming a floating biohazard cloud.

Alien Biology and Convergent Evolution

The commentary takes its most speculative turn when addressing the alien pathogens on Ilus. Francis uses the concept of convergent evolution to explain why humans, despite having a completely different evolutionary history, might still be vulnerable to alien life. He notes that life on Ilus likely uses photosynthesis, and "that's where light enters the body"—the eye—making it the perfect entry point for parasites. "Humans are still warm walking reservoirs of fluid and nutrients so we might still be victim to microorganisms just looking for somewhere nice to live," he argues.

This section is where Francis's medical expertise shines, bridging the gap between sci-fi horror and real-world parasitology. He draws parallels to Earth-bound diseases like river blindness and toxoplasmosis, grounding the alien threat in biological plausibility. He also touches on the fascinating concept of "mirror life," where alien biology might use right-handed amino acids instead of the left-handed ones found on Earth. "We have no idea if that would be the case on another planet and hence the settlers on Ellis if they decided they wanted to eat a plant or an animal they might not even be able to digest it," he warns. This adds a layer of existential risk: the very food and air of a new world might be biologically inert or toxic to us.

A counterargument worth considering is that Francis may be overly pessimistic about the immune system's ability to adapt. While he notes that our immune systems might be useless against local pathogens, he also mentions research into using helminths (worms) to treat autoimmune diseases, suggesting the immune system is more plastic than we think. However, the sheer novelty of alien biochemistry likely presents a hurdle that simple adaptation cannot overcome.

Bottom Line

Rohin Francis delivers a masterclass in applying terrestrial medicine to extraterrestrial problems, proving that the most dangerous part of colonizing a new planet isn't the launch, but the landing. His strongest argument is the inescapable reality of gravity as a biological constraint, a point that remains unaddressed by most optimistic space advocacy. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the specific, somewhat grim trajectory of The Expanse, which assumes a worst-case scenario for human adaptation; however, even if the fiction is dramatized, the medical principles he outlines are undeniably sound. For anyone serious about the future of space travel, this is the essential reading on the human body's fragility in a new world.
