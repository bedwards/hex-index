Rohin Francis delivers a blistering takedown of the modern obsession with medical metrics, arguing that the very numbers we trust to identify the "best" doctors are often the most dangerous tools in our arsenal. He doesn't just critique the data; he exposes the perverse incentives that turn skilled surgeons into risk-averse gamblers who game the system to protect their reputations. In an era where patients are urged to shop for providers based on online star ratings and mortality percentages, Francis offers a crucial reality check: the surgeon with the perfect record might be the one who refuses to treat the people who need help the most.

The Illusion of the Perfect Statistic

Francis opens with a deceptively simple anecdote about two orthopedic surgeons, a married couple he dubs Mr. and Mrs. Kohley. On paper, the husband appears superior: a mortality rate of just 1% compared to his wife's 10%. The math seems to close the case instantly. Yet, Francis dismantles this binary thinking by revealing the context behind the numbers. "Mrs. Kohley works at a major trauma center in a public state hospital doing the most complex cases, major road traffic accidents, stabbing victims, or often frail elderly patients," he explains. In contrast, her husband operates in a private facility, selecting only "elective knee replacements in fit and healthy patients under the age of about 65 or 70."

This comparison is the piece's anchor. It forces the reader to confront the fact that raw mortality rates are meaningless without risk adjustment. The author's point is that a surgeon who accepts the most difficult cases will inevitably have a higher death rate, not because they are incompetent, but because the patients they treat are on the brink of death regardless of the intervention. "Why should mortality be solely attributed to the surgeon?" Francis asks, challenging the fundamental assumption of public reporting. This is a vital distinction that is often lost in media headlines, which love to rank doctors based on simplistic, unadjusted data.

"Surgeons are not being selfish when they think about their statistics... ethically and indeed medical legally allowing natural death versus doing an intervention and potentially accelerating death with an operation are very different things."

The author argues that the pressure to maintain low mortality rates creates a "commission bias," a psychological tendency to act even when inaction is the safer choice. Doctors, trained to fix problems, feel an overwhelming urge to operate. However, when the risk of harm outweighs the potential benefit, the most ethical decision is often to do nothing. Francis illustrates this with a patient who, despite a stable condition, asked why they couldn't attempt surgery for a "1% chance" of success. Francis had to explain that subjecting a patient to invasive harm without a meaningful chance of benefit is not medicine; it is assault. "The moment I prescribe a medication or I make an incision, I am doing the patient harm," he writes, a stark reminder that medical intervention is never neutral.

Critics might argue that without public transparency on outcomes, patients are left in the dark and unable to hold providers accountable. However, Francis suggests that transparency without nuance is worse than no transparency at all, as it incentivizes the wrong behaviors.

The Cobra Effect in Healthcare

Francis then pivots to the broader systemic issue, invoking Goodhart's Law: "anytime a metric i.e. a way to measure something becomes the goal, it ceases to be a good metric." He illustrates this with the famous "Cobra Effect" from British-occupied India, where a bounty on dead cobras led villagers to farm snakes, only to release them when the program ended, increasing the cobra population four-fold. He draws a direct parallel to the UK's four-hour emergency department target, where hospitals began discharging patients prematurely just to meet the clock, compromising care to satisfy a number.

This section is particularly biting because it moves beyond individual surgeons to the administrative structures that govern them. Francis notes that a hospital can achieve 100% compliance with a target while offering terrible care. He shares an anecdote about a consultant who prided himself on discharging patients quickly, generating empty beds and pleasing managers. Yet, when Francis audited his readmission rates, the data revealed that his patients returned to the hospital far more often than anyone else's. "He generated empty beds," Francis notes dryly, "but no one thought as highly of him as he did."

The implication is clear: when metrics become the goal, the actual goal—patient health—gets sacrificed. This is the "law of unintended consequences" in action. Surgeons, fearing public shaming for high mortality rates, begin to decline high-risk cases. "Surgeons became wary to take on high-risk cases," Francis observes. "They opted for simpler safer surgeries lest they be criticized for having a higher mortality rate than others." The result is a system where the most vulnerable patients are denied access to the very specialists who are skilled enough to handle their complexity.

The Myth of the "Top" Doctor

The commentary concludes by attacking the commercialization of medical reputation. Francis mocks the existence of "America's top dermatologist" or "leading cardiologist" lists found in inflight magazines and social media. He reveals the cynical truth behind these accolades: "We've all been offered I' I could pay $5,000 and I could be one of New York's top plastic surgeons." These are not objective rankings; they are paid advertisements designed to exploit patient anxiety.

He reinforces the idea that medicine is too complex for binary rankings. "A good surgeon knows how to operate. A better surgeon knows when to operate, but the best surgeons know when not to operate," he quotes an old adage. This wisdom is lost when we reduce a doctor's career to a single data point. Francis warns that within a single standard deviation of statistical noise, there is "way too much noise to conclude anything." The only valid use of these statistics, he argues, is to identify true outliers—those well outside the normal distribution—rather than to rank the middle ground.

"Nothing in medicine is binary. The outcome of resuscitation or an operation is not just complete recovery or instant death or even a return to the pre-operative status quo. But there are limitless inbetweens."

This final point reframes the entire conversation. We are not looking for doctors who guarantee a perfect outcome; we are looking for those who understand the spectrum of human suffering and can navigate the "limitless inbetweens" with honesty. The best doctor is not the one with the lowest mortality rate, but the one who understands that sometimes, the most effective treatment is to let nature take its course.

Bottom Line

Francis's argument is a masterclass in statistical literacy, exposing how the pursuit of "perfect" data can actively degrade the quality of care. While his critique of public reporting is compelling, it leaves the reader with a difficult question: how do we hold doctors accountable without creating perverse incentives? The strongest part of his case is the exposure of the "commission bias" and the danger of ranking doctors based on unadjusted mortality rates. The biggest vulnerability is the lack of a concrete alternative for patients who need to make high-stakes decisions without a clear metric to guide them. Ultimately, Francis urges us to stop looking for a "top" doctor and start looking for a doctor who knows when to say no.
