Rohin Francis tackles a pervasive modern delusion: the belief that if a health intervention sounds scientifically plausible, it must be effective. In a sprawling critique of how bad ideas masquerade as good science, he argues that we are all victims of a "mechanistic bias"—the tendency to accept a treatment because the proposed mechanism makes logical sense, even when the clinical evidence is nonexistent. This is not just a rant about quacks; it is a philosophical inquiry into why credentialed doctors and skeptical skeptics alike fall for the same trap when science gets dressed up in a lab coat.

The Demarcation Problem and the Cost of Plausibility

Francis begins by grounding his argument in the philosophy of science, specifically the "demarcation problem" of distinguishing science from non-science. He invokes Karl Popper to explain that true science requires falsifiability—the ability to be proven wrong. "Popper proposed that science isn't about what you can prove because many things can have supporting evidence without being science," Francis writes. He illustrates this with the classic swan analogy: no matter how many white swans you see, you cannot prove all swans are white, but one black swan destroys the theory.

The author's strength lies in his ability to translate high-level epistemology into the messy reality of modern health culture. He points out that pseudoscience thrives in the gray area between obvious magic and rigorous physics. "They are dressed up like science in order to borrow its respectability and cachet," he observes. This framing is crucial because it explains why intelligent people are fooled. It is not a lack of intelligence; it is a failure to recognize that a logical-sounding mechanism does not equal a working treatment. Francis notes that fields like homeopathy and chiropractic are perfect examples because they have textbooks, guidelines, and practitioners who call themselves doctors, yet they rely on mechanisms that are fundamentally flawed.

They don't ask you to believe in some Spirit or swear a Blood Oath to the blood God no they're grounded in serious logical thought and so they take in people who could clearly spot a psychic for being a fake but they are fooled by these more legitimate looking Fields.

Critics might argue that Francis sets the bar for "science" so high that it dismisses legitimate areas of study that are still evolving, such as certain aspects of nutrition or psychology. However, his distinction is not about the maturity of the field, but the presence of a falsifiable claim versus a vague, untestable one.

The Trap of Bioplausibility

The core of Francis's argument shifts to the specific cognitive trap he calls "bioplausibility." He defines this as the error of assuming that because a mechanism makes biological sense, the treatment must work. He contrasts a clear case of plausibility—using ice on a swollen joint—with the absurdity of reiki or homeopathy. "If you ingest a solution that has been diluted so many times that there is no trace of the original chemical present it is not biologically plausible that this will do anything," he states bluntly.

Francis argues that the real danger arises when people mistake the starting point of research for the finish line. He draws from his own experience as a researcher in remote ischemic preconditioning, where promising results in mice failed to translate to human clinical trials. "Time and time again people mistake the first step for the end of the journey," he writes. This is a devastating critique of how health influencers and even some academics operate. They present a plausible mechanism as proof of efficacy, bypassing the rigorous testing required to confirm it.

The author also touches on the human element, acknowledging that many of these treatments feel good because they involve human connection and care. "It's okay to say that these things have real value and feel nice without resourcing to seeing them through the lens of science," he suggests. The problem, he insists, is when we invent "quack explanations" to justify the feeling, leading people to view these interventions as superior to proven medical therapies.

Bioplausibility is important it is often the starting point for much medical research but time and time again people mistake the first step for the end of the journey.

This section is particularly potent because it addresses the emotional appeal of alternative medicine without dismissing the comfort it provides. It challenges the reader to separate the feeling of wellness from the reality of a cure.

The Universal Susceptibility

Perhaps the most striking part of Francis's commentary is his insistence that this bias is not limited to the uneducated or the gullible. He observes that the bias appears on both sides of the political and medical divide during the pandemic. "I've seen this bias demonstrated by both sides of the gaping Chasm that has opened up in society," he notes, citing both the "carnivore ice bath testosterone crew" and the "close the schools mask the babies" crowd. Both groups, he argues, make the same mental mistake of prioritizing a compelling narrative over contradictory evidence.

Francis uses his platform to highlight that even credentialed professionals are not immune. He describes a spectrum of susceptibility ranging from "med-fluencers" to "credentialed professors of medicine." The mechanism is the same: confirmation bias, novelty bias, and the seduction of a plausible story. "Everybody is affected," he writes, "from a med-fluencer fresh out of med school trying to build their Wellness brand to a supplement Hawking podcaster whose only medical training is watching a celebrity chiropractors Instagram."

I believe that shining infrared light at my scrotum increases my testosterone level and there's this very compelling study in the Journal of infrared scrotal studies demonstrating this Cutting Edge science.

While Francis's tone is often satirical, the underlying point is serious: the scientific method is fragile and requires constant vigilance against our own cognitive shortcuts. He suggests that the solution is not to abandon science, but to better understand its limitations and the specific ways our brains try to trick us.

Bottom Line

Rohin Francis delivers a masterclass in critical thinking, exposing how the seductive power of a "plausible mechanism" can derail medical progress and public health. His strongest argument is that bioplausibility is a necessary but insufficient condition for truth, a distinction that even experts frequently ignore. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its length and density, which may test the patience of readers looking for quick fixes, but the depth of the analysis is precisely what makes it necessary. The takeaway is clear: if a treatment sounds too good to be true, or too logical to fail, demand the evidence that proves it works, not just the story that explains why it should.>"
