Rohin Francis exposes a dangerous paradox in modern medicine: the very scientists most dogmatic about evidence-based practice are often the first to fall victim to the cult of personality. He argues that the medical field's obsession with "great men" has created a system where charismatic doctors can bypass scrutiny, turning desperate patients into fanatical devotees and turning scientific fraud into blockbuster headlines.

The Great Man Trap

Francis begins by dismantling the idea that celebrity doctors are a new phenomenon born of social media. He notes that "medicine has a celebrity problem that long predates social media or even television." The author suggests that while historians in other disciplines have moved away from the "great man model" of history, medicine still clings to it. This framing is crucial because it shifts the blame from individual influencers to a systemic cultural failure within the profession itself.

The core of the argument is that the medical community's reverence for authority creates a blind spot. Francis writes, "In the era of evidence-based medicine, should we still be practicing eminence-based medicine and elevating academic researchers or clinicians to rockstar status?" This question lands with force because it highlights the irony that those who claim to worship data are often the ones most easily swayed by a charismatic narrative. He observes that even discerning commentators engage in "magical thinking" when a pseudoscientific claim is "packaged in a way to appeal to their own biases, i.e. by dawning the clothes of respectable science."

Critics might note that not all celebrity doctors are frauds; some, like the late Dr. Atul Gawande, use their platform to genuinely improve public health literacy. However, Francis's point remains valid: the mechanism of fame often rewards boldness over accuracy, creating an environment where caution is mistaken for cowardice.

When we start treating scientists like stars, we end up with many of the problematic traits of fandom that we find elsewhere with the celebrity exploiting and lying to those that support them because they believe in their cause.

The Playbook of the Rockstar

Francis moves from theory to specific case studies, starting with Italian neurosurgeon Sergio Canavero, who claimed he could perform human head transplants. The author dissects the "iconoclast" trope, where doctors frame themselves as martyrs silenced by a closed-minded establishment. Francis points out that while there is historical precedent for this (such as Ignaz Semmelweis), modern fraudsters exploit it to avoid scrutiny. He notes that Canavero's claims were met with "breathless wonder" by the press, despite the scientific impossibility of the procedure.

The author's critique of the media's role is particularly sharp. Francis writes, "What actually irritates me most about this story is how journalists, especially science journalists, just reported his statements as if they were facts." He highlights the absurdity of Canavero's proposed solution for reconnecting the spinal cord: "you cut the spaetto, you apply the peg, and boom." By comparing this to a child's plan to reattach a beetle's head, Francis effectively strips away the veneer of scientific legitimacy. He argues that the failure to fact-check basic premises allowed a fantasy to be sold as a breakthrough.

The narrative then shifts to Piero Anversa, a cardiologist whose work on heart stem cells was hailed as a "blockbuster" discovery. Francis describes how Anversa's fame grew to the point where he became "too big to fail." Even as other scientists raised alarms about reproducibility, Harvard Medical School doubled down, offering him a full professorship. Francis writes, "When you build someone up as your star player, bring in the press and the big bucks publishing in the biggest journals in the world, you make them too big to fail." This section serves as a cautionary tale about how institutional prestige can become a shield for fraud.

The Human Cost of Hype

The final chapter focuses on Paolo Macchiarini, the surgeon whose trachea transplants led to his trial for aggravated assault. Francis details how Macchiarini's "genius" headlines ushered in a new dawn of hope, only to result in the deaths of his patients. The author emphasizes that the damage goes beyond financial loss; these doctors are "granted power over someone's entire life." He notes that in the pandemic, figures like Didier Raoult and Pierre Kory became "legitimate cult figures," with devotees believing in their "personal Jesus MD" rather than the data.

Francis argues that the tragedy of these cases is the waste of time and hope. He writes, "Imagine how many teams all over the world excitedly tried to do the same in vain. Just imagine how many patients got their hopes up only to have them dashed when nothing similar could be achieved." This emotional appeal underscores the real-world consequences of the "cult of personality." The author concludes that while these doctors may not have started with the intent to deceive, the system they operate in rewards the very behavior that leads to deception.

The pandemic has seen people like Didier Raoult and Pierre Kory become legitimate cult figures with devoted followings who believe with every fiber in their being that hydroxychloroquine or ivermectin respectively are wonder drugs that are being suppressed by the pharmaceutical industry.

Bottom Line

Francis's most compelling contribution is his identification of the "eminence-based medicine" trap, where the reputation of the doctor supersedes the rigor of the evidence. The argument's greatest vulnerability is that it relies heavily on high-profile failures, potentially overlooking the many respected clinicians who use their platforms responsibly. However, the piece serves as a vital reminder that in medicine, the most dangerous lie is often the one wrapped in the most convincing white coat.
