Rohin Francis dismantles the glossy marketing of celebrity-endorsed full-body scans by exposing a dangerous statistical trap: the more you scan healthy people, the more likely you are to find nothing worth finding, or worse, something that creates a lifetime of anxiety. While influencers like Kim Kardashian frame these expensive procedures as the ultimate act of self-care, Francis argues they are actually a "fishing expedition" that exploits the human fear of the unknown and the statistical inevitability of error. This is not just a critique of a specific company; it is a necessary intervention in a booming industry that sells certainty where none exists.

The Celebrity Endorsement Trap

Francis opens by highlighting the absurdity of the current landscape, where "reputable medical Authority Kim Kardashian" has become a de facto spokesperson for a private imaging company called Pruvu. He notes that while the celebrity crowd promotes these scans as life-saving, the medical community is left to "cry like Cassandra and be ignored." The author points out that the sales pitch relies on a fundamental misunderstanding of science, such as Kardashian's claim that an MRI is "like getting an MRI scan without radiation," when in fact it is an MRI scan that simply avoids ionizing radiation. This distinction matters because the real danger isn't the radiation; it's the cascade of medical interventions that follow a false alarm.

The core of Francis's argument is that these companies are selling a promise that biology cannot keep. He writes, "The promise is very easy to sell... the ability to detect cancer and diseases such as aneurysms in its earlier stages before symptoms arise." However, he immediately counters this by explaining that biology rarely deals in clear binary distinctions of "abnormal and normal." Instead, medicine is defined by managing the "huge gray area" of human physiology. By framing health as a series of detectable errors waiting to be found, these companies ignore the reality that finding a problem does not always mean fixing a disease.

Critics might argue that for the wealthy, the cost of peace of mind is worth the risk of a false alarm. Francis, however, suggests that the anxiety generated by a scan often outweighs the benefit of the information gained.

The Mathematics of False Alarms

The most compelling part of Francis's coverage is his explanation of the "base rate fallacy." He illustrates that even a highly accurate test can be useless if applied to a population where the disease is rare. "If you take the population as a whole... the rate of disease is low... this means the true positive rate is low," he explains. Consequently, in a healthy, young population, a positive result is "orders of magnitude lower" to be true than false. This statistical reality means that for most people, a positive scan is likely a lie.

Francis goes further to describe the phenomenon of "incidentalomas," which are harmless spots or lumps found on scans that have nothing to do with the patient's health. He notes that "20 to 40% of scans ordered for some clinical concern... will find an incidentaloma," yet only 1% of those turn out to be clinically significant. The result is a system where a "carefree happy go-lucky person" can be transformed into a patient overnight, trapped in a cycle of repeat scans and uncertainty. As Francis puts it, "You're in the system now," a state that can lead to "invasive and often pointless test[s]" driven by fear rather than medical necessity.

Showing perfectly healthy people every harmless imperfection in their body just to scare them into taking invasive and often pointless test is an Unholy sin.

The Illusion of Benefit

Francis addresses the emotional core of the pro-scan argument: the story of the person whose life was saved by early detection. He acknowledges that these anecdotes are powerful but argues they are often misleading examples of "lead time bias" and "overdiagnosis." He cites a popular podcaster who celebrated a young man found to have a narrowed artery, only to point out that "there are no trial data to suggest that man... needed it" or that the stent prolonged his life. This is the "illusion of benefit," where the act of finding a problem feels like a victory, even if the treatment provides no survival advantage.

The author warns that this approach disproportionately harms low-risk patients. He writes, "You're paradoxically most likely to catch Things Early that don't benefit from early intervention," citing examples like micropapillary thyroid cancer or prostate cancer, where early treatment can increase harm without improving survival. Francis suggests that the "biohackers" and "longevity Bros" are so obsessed with data that they fail to read the scientific literature showing that "catching Things Early is far more complicated than it sounds." He concludes that these predatory companies are cynically cashing in on the frustration people feel when doctors tell them they are healthy, offering a "fishing expedition" as a solution to a problem that doesn't exist.

Bottom Line

Francis's strongest asset is his ability to translate complex statistical concepts like the base rate fallacy into a clear warning about the dangers of over-testing. His argument is most vulnerable when it assumes all patients are equally rational about risk, potentially underestimating the psychological comfort some derive from the act of scanning itself. However, the verdict remains clear: in the absence of symptoms, the full-body scan is a high-cost, high-anxiety gamble that the medical evidence suggests you should not take.
