Rohin Francis dismantles the comforting myth that medical errors stem from incompetence, arguing instead that the human brain is wired to fail in predictable, dangerous ways. In a field obsessed with data, Francis reveals that the most critical diagnostic tool—the doctor's own intuition—is often the very thing leading patients astray. This isn't a takedown of the profession; it is a sobering look at why even the most well-intentioned experts fall into cognitive traps that no amount of training can fully erase.

The Illusion of Progress

Francis begins by challenging the modern obsession with "new" treatments, a bias he terms novelty bias. He observes that while technology often improves with every generation, medicine does not follow a similar trajectory. "Medicines aren't like computer components," Francis writes, "they don't keep getting better with every generation. There is no medical Moore's law." He points out how pharmaceutical companies exploit this by slightly tweaking old molecules, repackaging them as revolutionary cures while hiking the price. This is a crucial distinction for busy readers to internalize: just because a therapy is new doesn't mean it is better. In fact, history is littered with abandoned therapies that turned out to be garbage.

Critics might argue that this skepticism stifles innovation, yet Francis's point is about evidence, not stagnation. He notes that drug companies "take advantage of this" by selling the idea of a "new improved formula" when the reality is often just a marketing ploy. The danger lies in patients demanding these unproven treatments, forcing doctors into a corner where waiting for evidence is framed as negligence rather than prudence.

The Detective's Dilemma

The core of Francis's argument shifts to the diagnostic process, which he likens to a medical whodunit. He introduces a fictional patient, "Timmy," to illustrate how anchoring bias and automation bias distort clinical judgment. In the scenario, a doctor named Debbie initially rules out heart disease because Timmy is slim, only to reverse that decision seconds later because a computer readout flagged a cardiac enzyme. "In the same way that people trust their GPS sat-nav more than their own ability to read a map these days," Francis explains, "she believes the computer." This reliance on algorithms over clinical intuition is a double-edged sword; while computers are powerful, they can reinforce errors if the initial data is flawed.

Francis also highlights availability bias, where a doctor's recent experiences disproportionately influence their diagnosis. He shares a personal anecdote about diagnosing a rare condition simply because he had studied it the night before. "I became the toast of the doctors mess for a day," he admits, but immediately follows with a warning: "this isn't bad right?" The answer, of course, is that it is dangerous. When doctors over-diagnose rare conditions they just studied, they risk missing common ailments like the flu or an ingrown toenail. This leads to prevalence distortion, where the fear of missing a catastrophic illness like an aortic dissection drives doctors to order hundreds of unnecessary CT scans.

The history of medicine is littered with abandoned therapies that turned out to be garbage, yet we keep chasing the next big thing.

The Human Element and the Urge to Act

Perhaps the most poignant section of Francis's commentary addresses the psychological pressure to "do something." He describes commission bias as the urge to intervene even when inaction is the safer path. "The urge to do something or the desire to avoid any potential harm are both entirely natural and normal responses," Francis argues. He illustrates this with the example of prescribing antibiotics for a viral infection; the doctor feels proactive, the patient feels cared for, and both ignore the reality that the drug does nothing for the virus. This is a subtle but pervasive failure mode where the desire to help actually causes harm through antibiotic resistance or unnecessary side effects.

Francis also touches on framing bias, showing how the same statistic can terrify or reassure a patient depending on how it is presented. A surgeon might say there is a "90% chance of survival," while a concerned doctor mentions a "10% risk of death." "The way we describe something to a patient can affect their decision or their feelings about this," Francis notes. This is not just about communication; it is about the ethical weight of how information is delivered. Furthermore, he warns against familiarity bias, where specialists recommend treatments they know best, regardless of whether they are the optimal choice for the patient. "Look for this on the Internet," he advises, "beware the doctor who zealously endorses one small area of Medicine... because chances are they will recommend it when there are better options available."

Bottom Line

Francis's strongest contribution is his refusal to demonize doctors while simultaneously exposing their systemic blind spots; he proves that good intentions are no shield against cognitive bias. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on fictionalized scenarios, which, while effective for illustration, may oversimplify the chaotic reality of emergency medicine. However, the takeaway is undeniable: in an era of information overload, the most dangerous diagnosis is the one we are too confident to question.
