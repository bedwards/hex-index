Rohin Francis turns a standard Q&A format into a masterclass on the gap between medical reality and public perception, arguing that the most dangerous myths aren't just from quacks, but are embedded in the very fabric of modern healthcare. He dismantles the comforting illusion that a narrow artery always means a heart attack, revealing instead that the most lethal threats often hide behind mild-looking plaques. This is not just a collection of medical trivia; it is a stark reminder that the human body operates on chaos, not the clean logic of television dramas.

The Myth of the Narrow Artery

Francis immediately challenges the intuitive logic that clogged pipes lead to explosions. He explains that a ninety percent blockage that has calcified and stabilized over years is often less dangerous than a fifty percent blockage that is unstable. "There is a fundamental mismatch or there's no correlation between the amount of narrowing in a coronary artery and the risk of developing a heart attack," Francis writes. He describes how a stable, hard plaque might cause pain but won't rupture, whereas a soft, unstable one with a thin cap can trigger a catastrophic clot. This distinction is crucial because it explains why standard visual inspections often fail to predict sudden cardiac events.

The author notes that while high-tech scans can identify these dangerous features, they remain confined to research facilities rather than general practice. "We are not good just by eyeballing them," he admits, acknowledging the limitations of current clinical tools. This admission is refreshing in an industry often criticized for overconfidence. However, critics might note that relying on future technology to solve current diagnostic gaps leaves patients vulnerable in the present, especially those without access to specialized centers. Francis's honesty about this blind spot, however, strengthens his credibility as a public educator.

"The vast majority of my colleagues are just as vocal and want to help their patients just as much reject pseudoscience... but the line is not always so black and white."

The Fiction of Informed Consent

Perhaps the most provocative section of the piece is Francis's critique of the "informed consent" model. He argues that the expectation that a patient can fully grasp the nuances of complex procedures is unrealistic, especially when even specialists struggle to understand each other's sub-fields. "The concept of informed consent... is a bit of a convenient fiction that we tell ourselves," Francis states bluntly. He points out that doctors often defer to their own judgment when patients ask, "What would you do if it was you?" because true shared decision-making requires a level of understanding that rarely exists.

He illustrates this with the example of resuscitation, where patients often have a "very unrealistic view" based on movies, expecting a binary live-or-die outcome. In reality, surviving a cardiac arrest often means living with severe neurological deficits. "We are now told to discuss resuscitation with patients and their families... but it can lead to situations where the treating team will feel that resuscitation is not in the patient's best interests," he explains. This tension highlights a systemic failure: we have moved from a paternalistic "doctor knows best" model to one that burdens patients with decisions they are ill-equipped to make. The solution, Francis suggests, lies in physicians offering clear recommendations rather than abdicating responsibility.

When Medicine Goes Wrong

Francis also delves into the specific, terrifying errors unique to cardiology, moving beyond generic medical mistakes to the visceral fears of the specialist. He describes the nightmare of an air embolus, where a tiny bubble of air injected into the coronary arteries can stop blood flow entirely. "Any engineers watching or plumbers... won't need me to explain that air in a pressurized hydraulic system is a disaster," he quips, using an analogy to make the technical danger palpable. He admits that while these events are rare, they represent the "absolute nightmare" for every cardiology fellow.

This section serves as a humbling counter-narrative to the idea of medicine as an infallible science. By sharing his own fears and the potential for catastrophic human error, Francis humanizes the profession without diminishing its necessity. He also touches on the evolution of medical knowledge, noting that many "indisputable facts" taught in medical school, particularly regarding diet and nutrition, have since been proven wrong or oversimplified. "People who claim that everything's been turned on its head again are not really aware of the evidence," he argues, suggesting that while some advice has changed, the core principles of complex carbohydrates and fat management remain sound, even if the public perception has swung wildly.

Bottom Line

Francis's strongest argument is his unflinching admission that the medical system relies on "convenient fictions" like perfect informed consent, and that the line between evidence-based medicine and outdated dogma is often blurrier than the public assumes. His biggest vulnerability is the potential for his candor to erode trust in the profession among those who need reassurance more than nuance. Readers should watch for how this tension between transparency and authority plays out as medical literacy improves and patients demand more agency in their care.
