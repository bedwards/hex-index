Brian Merchant does not merely share a story; he weaponizes a piece of speculative fiction to diagnose a very real, very present rot in the American economy. While headlines obsess over the speed of artificial intelligence, Merchant pivots to the human cost of fake work, presenting a narrative where the state subsidizes millions to generate meaningless data just to keep them fed. This is not science fiction; it is a chilling extrapolation of current trends where human labor is retained not for its utility, but for its political necessity.

The Theater of Employment

Merchant frames the central conflict through the lens of the "entropy mill," a government facility where workers churn out random numbers to feed a system that could easily be automated by a simple radioactive isotope. "In reality, a gram of cesium would do a better job than these broken-down men and women ever could," Merchant writes, highlighting the grotesque inefficiency of the system. The author's choice to describe this as a "make-work project" rather than a technological necessity is a sharp critique of how the executive branch and corporate interests manage labor surplus. The story suggests that the administration prefers the appearance of employment over the efficiency of automation, even if that employment is a sham.

The narrative forces the reader to confront the physical toll of this policy. "The years gorge on them like yeast in a sugar bath," Merchant observes, describing the workers who arrive in their best Sunday suits, desperate for a day's wage. This imagery strips away the abstraction of economic statistics, replacing them with the visceral reality of aging bodies forced into performative labor. It is a stark reminder that when the economy fails to provide meaningful work, the state often steps in with a cruel pantomime of productivity.

The years gorge on them like yeast in a sugar bath.

Critics might argue that this dystopian vision ignores the potential for AI to create entirely new categories of human value, rather than just replacing old ones. However, Merchant's framing suggests that without a fundamental shift in how we value human life, the transition will be less about innovation and more about managing the destitute.

The Architecture of Despair

The setting of the entropy mill, located in a repurposed mega-mall, serves as a physical manifestation of collapsed consumerism. "Even now you can still see the phantom outline on the sides of some of the buildings, the places where the lettering was... this congealed mass of commerce the size of a small city," Merchant notes. This detail grounds the story in a specific historical trajectory, echoing the Luddite movement's fear not of technology itself, but of the social dislocation it causes. Just as the original Luddites were not simply anti-machine but anti-poverty, Merchant's characters are trapped in a system where the machine is the only thing standing between them and starvation.

The story introduces a crucial distinction between the workers and the "maintenance" staff, illustrating the class stratification inherent in this future. When the protagonist attempts to cut the line, he is met with violence, only to be saved by his access to a technical role. "You can't rid a man of the violence that lives in the chasm between the life he hoped for and the one he got," the protagonist reflects. This line cuts deep, suggesting that the social unrest we see today is not a failure of character, but a predictable reaction to a broken social contract.

The mechanism of control is subtle but absolute. Workers are monitored, blacklisted, and forced to compete for the privilege of doing nothing. "No one wants to end up on the blacklist," Merchant writes, as a worker flees from security guards who threaten to photograph him. This surveillance state is not depicted as a tool of national security, but as a method of labor discipline, ensuring that the workforce remains compliant in the face of absurdity.

The Glitch as Resistance

The climax of the piece hinges on a deliberate act of sabotage. The protagonist, whose father spent thirty-one years in the mill, decides to corrupt the system's output. "Ninety-six trillion, give or take. That's how many numbers my father fed this thing," he tells the guard, a statement that transforms a statistic into a monument of wasted life. The plan is to make the system produce only zeros, effectively halting the industries that rely on this "pure uncertainty."

This act of resistance draws a parallel to the concept of hardware random number generators, which rely on physical phenomena rather than human suffering to produce true randomness. Merchant's story implies that the current reliance on human labor for data generation is a moral failure, a "slop" that feeds the machine at the expense of human dignity. "It's important to do work of which you can be proud," the protagonist's father once said, a sentiment that the entire system is designed to crush.

The story ends not with a revolution, but with a quiet, impending silence. "I imagine the sound it'll make. A choking," Merchant writes, describing the moment the system fails. This is a powerful metaphor for the fragility of an economy built on the backs of the desperate. If the administration continues to prioritize the illusion of employment over genuine economic restructuring, the eventual collapse will be inevitable.

You can't rid a man of the violence that lives in the chasm between the life he hoped for and the one he got.

Bottom Line

Merchant's piece is a masterful use of speculative fiction to expose the hollowness of current labor policies, arguing that the state's refusal to automate away meaningless work is a form of cruelty, not compassion. The story's greatest strength lies in its refusal to offer a technological fix, instead focusing on the human cost of a system that values order over dignity. The biggest vulnerability is its bleakness; it offers no clear path forward, leaving the reader with the chilling realization that the "entropy mill" is not a distant future, but a logical extension of today's trajectory.

Bottom Line

This narrative succeeds by reframing the AI debate from one of efficiency to one of human dignity, exposing the moral bankruptcy of a system that forces millions to generate fake data just to survive. The story's most potent warning is that without a radical reimagining of work, the future will not be one of liberation, but of a quiet, suffocating stagnation where the only thing left to do is wait for the system to choke.
