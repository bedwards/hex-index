This issue of Works in Progress does something rare for a publication roundup: it treats technology not as a magic wand, but as a fragile system that requires deliberate, often counterintuitive policy to function. The lead argument—that freezing eggs in one's twenties is a rational, high-yield investment rather than a desperate gamble—shatters the pervasive myth that fertility decline is a slow slope followed by a cliff. By anchoring this personal choice in hard data, the editors reframe reproductive planning as a matter of biological logistics, not just emotional readiness.

The Biology of Timing

The piece dismantles the popular narrative found in major outlets like the New York Times, which often suggests egg freezing is a low-probability endeavor. Works in Progress reports that "Readers of the New York Times or Vox have read that egg freezing barely works, but these facts come from a study of women who froze their eggs at an average age of 38, by which point their underlying fertility had fallen, which egg freezing cannot fix." This distinction is critical. The argument hinges on the timing of the intervention, not the technology itself. When performed early, the success rates are transformative.

The editors note that "When women in their twenties freeze their eggs, their success rates later on in life are closer to 85 percent." This statistic reframes the entire conversation from one of scarcity to one of strategic foresight. Ruxandra Teslo and Luzia Bruckamp are cited as examples of women who made this choice early, arguing that "other young women should consider doing the same." The logic here is sound: biological capital, like financial capital, compounds best when invested early. Critics might note that 85 percent is still not a guarantee, and the physical or financial costs of the procedure remain significant barriers for many, but the core data point effectively neutralizes the fatalism surrounding age-related fertility decline.

Infrastructure as Policy, Not Culture

Shifting from the biological to the built environment, the issue challenges the romanticized view that Japan's superior rail system is a product of unique national character. The editors argue that "the common explanation is wrong. Japan has amazing railways because of good policy, and almost every element of the Japanese system could be reproduced elsewhere." This is a powerful corrective to the idea that certain outcomes are culturally locked in. The piece details a model of "vertically integrated regional monopolies that build the cities around their own lines, liberal zoning, private ownership, and privatized parking."

This argument gains historical weight when viewed against the backdrop of other infrastructure failures. Just as the Rail Land Development Authority in India has struggled to replicate such integration due to fragmented land rights, Japan's success proves that the technology of the train is secondary to the architecture of the system. The editors assert that "The rest of the world can have Japanese-tier railways if it chooses to." This is an optimistic, yet demanding, thesis. It suggests that the barrier to entry is political will, not technical impossibility. However, a counterargument worth considering is that Japan's density and historical development patterns may not be easily translatable to sprawling, car-dependent nations without massive, disruptive upheaval.

The Hubris of Technocracy

The issue turns a critical eye toward the British nuclear program, contrasting its 1965 dominance with its current stagnation. In 1965, Britain had built more nuclear power stations than the United States, the Soviet Union, and France combined. Today, the situation is reversed, with projects like Hinkley Point C becoming the most expensive in the world. Alex Chalmers, quoted in the piece, argues that "technocratic hubris is to blame." The narrative describes a decades-long disconnect where British technocrats "dismissed public concerns about waste and efficiency, until eventually both popular support and regulatory presumption shifted decisively against them."

This failure mirrors the broader tragedy of the commons seen in other resource management issues. Just as the centrifugal water pump allowed American farmers to empty the Ogallala Aquifer after centuries of sustainable use, the nuclear sector's failure to listen to public sentiment has led to a regulatory environment where progress is nearly impossible. The piece contends that "If Britain would like to relearn nuclear, it will have to re-earn the public's trust." This is a sobering conclusion: technology cannot be forced upon a skeptical populace, no matter how necessary it may be for climate goals. The lesson here is that trust is a prerequisite for innovation, not a byproduct of it.

The Monopoly of the Machine

In the realm of semiconductors, the editors highlight the unlikely rise of ASML, a Dutch company that now holds "the most important monopoly in the world." The piece traces its journey from a laggard in the 1980s to the sole producer of machines capable of making advanced chips. Neil Hacker explains that to survive, ASML had to take a series of unlikely decisions, including working closely with the American government and selling stakes to competitors. The piece notes that one industry figure once told its leadership that "the race has already been run," yet ASML persisted by staking billions on unproven technology.

This story serves as a reminder that monopolies are not always the result of anti-competitive behavior, but sometimes of sheer, calculated risk-taking in the face of overwhelming odds. The editors suggest that "Advanced semiconducters are the most important technology in the world," making ASML's position a matter of global strategic significance. Yet, the fragility of this monopoly is also evident; it relies on a complex, global supply chain that could be disrupted by geopolitical tensions. The piece does not shy away from the implications of such concentration, noting that the company's survival depended on decisions that would seem irrational to a standard business analyst.

The Tragedy of the Road

Finally, the issue warns of a new tragedy of the commons emerging with self-driving cars. Ben Southwood argues that "Once time spent in traffic can be used to work, to sleep, or to drink a beer with friends while your car drives itself, traffic jams will become less painful, ruining the roads for people who need to get somewhere quickly." This is a profound insight into behavioral economics: if the pain of congestion is removed, the demand for road space will explode, potentially making travel slower for everyone. The piece warns that "The window is closing fast" for pricing autonomous vehicles' road use, suggesting that political will is needed now before the technology is ubiquitous.

This argument parallels the historical lesson of the bus, which Samuel Hughes notes was "invented twice." Blaise Pascal invented the bus in 1662, but it was "promptly killed by Parisian regulation." The idea was forgotten for 150 years until a bathhouse owner in Nantes stumbled upon it again. The editors use this to illustrate that "Sometimes, the best innovations are not physical technologies, but business models." The risk with autonomous vehicles is that we might solve the physical problem of driving but fail to solve the economic problem of road usage, leading to a system that is technically advanced but functionally broken.

The rest of the world can have Japanese-tier railways if it chooses to. But for nuclear power, the challenge is not just technical; it is the re-earning of public trust after decades of technocratic hubris.

Bottom Line

This issue succeeds by stripping away the cultural mystique surrounding complex systems—whether they are biological, infrastructural, or technological—and replacing it with hard policy analysis. Its strongest argument is that success is rarely accidental; it is the result of specific, replicable choices that are often ignored in favor of convenient narratives. The biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that political will can be summoned easily to implement these solutions, a hurdle that has tripped up reformers from the British nuclear sector to the Australian border policy. Readers should watch for how these policy prescriptions play out in the next election cycles, where the gap between technical feasibility and political reality will be tested.
