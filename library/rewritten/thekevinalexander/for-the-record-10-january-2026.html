Kevin Alexander challenges a pervasive industry assumption: that an artist's value is tied to their ability to constantly monetize their past. In a cultural moment obsessed with the "legacy tour" and the relentless mining of vaults, Alexander argues that the demand for artists to perform their greatest hits forever is not a sign of fan appreciation, but a form of creative imprisonment. This piece matters because it reframes the conversation from what fans are owed to what artists owe themselves, offering a rare defense of artistic silence and evolution over commercial predictability.

The Myth of the Obligation

Alexander begins by dissecting a recent op-ed by Andy Greene in Rolling Stone, which suggests Peter Gabriel should adopt a more aggressive "legacy management" strategy similar to Bruce Springsteen. Greene argues that Gabriel has not done enough to preserve his achievements, contrasting the two artists to highlight a perceived deficit in Gabriel's career strategy. Alexander writes, "The problem is the assumption underneath the comparison: that preserving, expanding, and monetizing a legacy are universal artistic goals." This is a crucial distinction. By accepting Greene's premise, one automatically views Gabriel's selective output as a failure rather than a choice.

The author illustrates the frustration of the "play the hits" crowd through a vivid anecdote from a recent Gabriel concert in Milwaukee. Alexander describes a fan seated next to him who spent the entire show yelling for "Shock the Monkey," moving through the "5 stages of grief" as the night progressed without hearing the song. "Would he call it fantastic? My guess is that with the benefit of hindsight, he'd say yes. That night? I doubt it," Alexander notes. This observation cuts to the heart of the conflict between the artist's current vision and the audience's nostalgic expectations. The fan wanted a museum exhibit; the artist offered a living, breathing conversation about humanity's place in the universe.

If you reject the premise that legacy is a commodity to be managed, Gabriel simply looks like an artist making different choices.

Critics might argue that without the financial engine of hit songs, artists cannot sustain the resources needed to create new work. However, Alexander counters this by pointing to the long lines at the merch table and the dedicated fanbase that showed up for new material, suggesting that the market for evolution exists if the artist is willing to trust it.

The Burden of the Setlist

The commentary then shifts to the specific question of whether writing a hit song creates a permanent contract to perform it. Alexander draws a parallel to the band The Church, noting that while fans love "Under the Milky Way," it is unfair to assume the songwriter, Steve Kilbey, is "sentenced to playing 'Reptile' forever." Alexander writes, "I can't imagine that when he put pen to paper, he envisioned playing these songs 30-40 years later. That's not how art usually works." This reframing is powerful because it restores agency to the creator. It suggests that the song belongs to the moment of its creation, not to the infinite loop of a concert circuit.

Alexander contrasts this with the approach of Nada Surf, a band that has been "saddled with one-hit-wonder anchor" but chooses to acknowledge the expectation while prioritizing new material. At a recent show, when a fan yelled "Play some new shit," the band responded with gratitude and played a new track. "This show was a textbook case of an audience split between new and old," Alexander observes, noting that the merch line was "impossibly long" regardless of the setlist. This evidence suggests that the fear of alienating fans by playing new material is often exaggerated.

A Third Path: Evolution Over Repetition

Alexander proposes a middle ground that respects both the history of the artist and the need for growth, citing New Order as the ideal model. The band, which evolved from the ashes of Joy Division, still closes sets with "Love Will Tear Us Apart," a song they technically did not write but which is inseparable from their history. Yet, they do not simply replay their greatest hits album. "Even when playing older tracks, they often redo/restyle them," Alexander writes. "The opening to Subculture will sound wholly alien to you if you're not ready for it. Once those familiar opening synths kick in, though? It's rapture."

This approach mirrors the strategy of Peter Gabriel's i/o album, which was released track-by-track with each full moon. Alexander calls this "a pretty clever way to go about it," noting that it allows each song to breathe and creates a steady stream of engagement without the pressure of a traditional album cycle. It is a method that prioritizes the artistic process over the marketing machine. As Alexander puts it, "Inspiration strikes when it strikes, and to try and squish that into a fixed timeline seems like a surefire recipe for burnout, or mediocre material."

Maybe not everyone wants to spend their one wild and precious life playing the same 10-20 songs ad infinitum?

The author acknowledges that the lifespan of a fanbase is finite and that artists like Springsteen have successfully built empires on a mix of new work and deep cuts. However, Alexander argues that this is a specific strategy, not a universal mandate. "Greene goes so far as to state that Gabriel should work Genesis material into his setlists," Alexander notes, but points out that both Gabriel and the "new" Genesis are decades removed from their original partnership. To force a reunion of that nature for the sake of ticket sales is to prioritize commerce over the integrity of the current artistic project.

Bottom Line

Alexander's strongest argument lies in his refusal to accept the commodification of artistic identity as a default setting; he successfully argues that an artist's silence or slow output is not a failure of legacy management, but a valid form of expression. The piece's vulnerability is that it assumes a level of financial security that not all artists possess, potentially romanticizing the choice to ignore commercial pressures. Ultimately, the reader is left with a compelling case for the "third path": a career that honors the past without being held hostage by it, allowing the artist to remain a creator rather than becoming a curator of their own history.
