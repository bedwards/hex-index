Sam Dalrymple reframes the history of global connectivity by spotlighting a singular, surreal moment: a giraffe arriving in 15th-century China not from Africa, but as a diplomatic gift from the Sultan of Bengal. This piece does more than recount a travelogue; it uses a mythical beast to expose how deeply integrated the Bengal Delta was into the Indian Ocean world centuries before modern globalization. For a reader navigating a fragmented geopolitical landscape, this historical lens offers a startling reminder that the region now often defined by borders was once a fluid, cosmopolitan hub where the exotic was routine.

The Mythical Beast and the Globalized Delta

Dalrymple anchors the narrative in a specific, vivid event that challenges modern assumptions about the reach of pre-colonial empires. He writes, "In the year 1414, the Ming court stood stunned as a Bengali delegation presented tribute to the Yongle Emperor of China. Standing before the Yongle Emperor was a towering, spotted beast with an enormously long neck." The author highlights the court's reaction, quoting the painter Shen Du: "The ministers and the people all gathered to gaze at it, and their joy knows no end." This moment was not merely a curiosity; it was a calculated diplomatic signal. Dalrymple notes that the animal was identified as a qilin, a mythical beast whose appearance heralded a golden age, yet to the modern eye, it is clearly a giraffe.

The significance lies in the origin of the animal. As Dalrymple explains, "what is extraordinary is that the animal had not been sent from Africa, but as a diplomatic gift from the Sultan of Sonargaon, a city in the heart of the Bengal Delta." This detail underscores the extent of Bengal's trade networks. The region was so globally connected that East African fauna were accessible to the Sultan without the animal needing to traverse the entire continent under its own power. This connects seamlessly to the broader context of the era; just as the Yongle Emperor's massive treasure fleets were redefining Chinese maritime power, the Bengal Sultanate was leveraging its position as a trade nexus to project soft power. The author's choice to focus on the giraffe effectively bypasses dry political analysis to illustrate the sheer scale of these interactions.

From Golden Village to Imperial Capital

The piece then pivots to the rise of Sonargaon, tracing its evolution from a minor provincial center to a sovereign capital. Dalrymple writes, "The name Sonargaon literally means 'Golden Village,' and its pedigree is ancient." He details how the city reemerged in the 14th century as a vital "mint town" before Fakhruddin Mubarak Shah seized the moment to declare independence in 1338. The narrative describes the subsequent unification of the delta under Shamsuddin Ilyas Shah, who "chose a name for his new unified realm based on the eastern heartland he had just conquered: the Sultanate of Bangala."

This section effectively illustrates the fluidity of power in the region. Dalrymple describes Sonargaon as a "global metropolis" under the Ilyas Shahi dynasty, citing the Moroccan traveler Ibn Battuta, who found the city's goods surprisingly cheap and its connections spanning from China to the Arabian Sea. The author notes that the city became the "eastern terminus of the Grand Trunk Road," feeding goods all the way to Afghanistan. This historical depth adds weight to the claim that Sonargaon was once one of the great trading emporiums of the world, a fact that contrasts sharply with its current status as a quiet suburb of Dhaka.

Sonargaon was such a globalised region at the time, that giraffes from East Africa were available to the Sultan there.

Dalrymple's framing here is particularly strong because it humanizes the grand historical shifts through cultural exchange. He highlights the patronage of Sultan Ghiyasuddin Azam Shah, who sent an unfinished poem to the Persian poet Hafiz. Although Hafiz declined the invitation due to the dangerous journey, he completed the Sultan's poem, which remains part of the Divan-e-Hafiz today. This anecdote serves as a powerful counterpoint to the notion of cultural isolation; it shows a region where Persian literature and Bengali court culture were in active, vibrant dialogue.

The Indigenous Turn and the Birth of a New Identity

The narrative takes a critical turn when discussing the political upheaval of the early 15th century, specifically the rise of Raja Ganesh. Dalrymple argues that this period was pivotal for the region's demographic and cultural identity. He writes, "Raja Ganesh's rise would unwittingly begin Bengal's transformation into a Muslim-majority region." The author details how Ganesh, a Hindu courtier, staged a coup but was forced into a diplomatic gamble to secure legitimacy against neighboring threats. His son, Jadu, converted to Islam and ruled as Sultan Jalaluddin Muhammad Shah.

This transition is described not as a foreign imposition, but as a deliberate indigenization of power. Dalrymple notes that Jalaluddin's coins displayed a syncretic identity: "on one side his proclaims himself the 'Caliph of Allah'... On the other side is an image of a lion - the vehicle of his ancestral Goddess Devi." This visual synthesis is crucial evidence for the author's argument that the region's identity was being forged through adaptation rather than erasure. The author further explains that Jalaluddin "elevated the Bengali language," encouraging it to become the language of the mosque and making Islam feel "indigenous rather than imposed."

Critics might note that the speed and depth of this conversion are sometimes debated by historians who emphasize the role of Sufi mystics and economic incentives over political decree. However, Dalrymple's focus on the state's active role in shaping religious expression through language and architecture provides a compelling institutional perspective. The shift from Turkic to Bengali idioms in mosque architecture—sloping terracotta roofs inspired by local temples—serves as a tangible legacy of this policy.

The Fading of the Golden Age

The piece concludes by tracing the decline of Sonargaon, linking it to the rise of the Mughal Empire and the shifting geography of the delta. Dalrymple writes, "It is difficult to tell today just how central to the global economy Sonargaon once was." He attributes the city's fall to the Mughals' destruction of the anti-Mughal resistance and the silting of the port, which made it impossible for great merchant ships to dock. The capital moved to Dhaka in 1610, leaving Sonargaon to become a center of the muslin trade before its eventual abandonment during the Partition of India.

The author paints a poignant picture of the remaining structures, such as Panam Nagar, a "well-preserved stretch of approximately 52 dilapidated brick mansions." He notes that these houses were abruptly abandoned in 1947, and communal riots in 1964 pushed out the last remaining Hindu merchants. This ending serves as a somber reminder of the human cost of political fragmentation and the fragility of cosmopolitan hubs. While the article focuses on the grandeur of the past, the description of the empty mansions and the displaced communities grounds the historical narrative in the reality of loss.

Bottom Line

Dalrymple's strongest argument is the use of the giraffe as a metaphor for a forgotten era of fluid, interconnected sovereignty in the Bengal Delta, challenging the modern perception of the region as isolated or peripheral. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the romanticism of the "Golden Age," which occasionally glosses over the brutal realities of the wars and coups that underpinned these diplomatic triumphs. Readers should watch for how this model of indigenous syncretism is being re-evaluated in contemporary South Asian historiography, as it offers a nuanced alternative to narratives of pure conflict or foreign domination.
