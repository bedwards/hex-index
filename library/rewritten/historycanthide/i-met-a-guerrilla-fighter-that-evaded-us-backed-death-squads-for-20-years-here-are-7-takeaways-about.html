Kahlil Greene bypasses the sanitized headlines of foreign policy to deliver a visceral, ground-level account of how American intervention reshaped a nation. By centering the testimony of Rafael Hernandez, a former guerrilla commander turned park ranger, the piece transforms abstract geopolitical strategies into a human story of survival and memory. This is not just history; it is a necessary corrective to the current narrative that frames migration as an invasion rather than a consequence of decades of destabilization.

The Architecture of Intervention

Greene frames the conflict not as a spontaneous civil war, but as a calculated outcome of American strategic interests. He writes, "The official reason was to stop Communism. The practical effect was protecting coffee exports and the plantation owners who profited from them." This distinction is crucial; it strips away the ideological veneer to reveal the economic machinery driving the violence. The author details how Washington funneled roughly $6 billion to the Salvadoran military, a sum that sustained a regime protecting a tiny oligarchy known as "The Fourteen Families."

The piece effectively argues that the US consistently prioritized stability for capital over human rights. Greene notes, "Any dictator who slaughtered people to keep wealth flowing upward was treated as a partner in stability." This pattern, he explains, repeated globally from Guatemala to Chile, where the US supported coups against leaders who threatened to redistribute wealth. While critics might argue that Cold War containment required hard choices against Soviet influence, Greene's evidence suggests the threshold for supporting authoritarianism was remarkably low, often ignoring massacres as long as the coffee kept shipping.

The United States could fund the weapons and train the killers. What they couldn't purchase was the silence of those who survived.

The Human Cost of Training

Perhaps the most damning section of the article focuses on the School of the Americas, a US military training facility. Greene highlights that the curriculum itself was the weapon, noting, "The school's proudest Salvadoran creation, the Atlácatl Battalion, massacred over 800 villagers at El Mozote in 1981, most of them children." He points out that when the Pentagon finally released training manuals in 1996, they confirmed the curriculum "explicitly advocated extrajudicial execution, torture, and targeting civilians."

The author connects the dots between the training manuals and the specific atrocities committed by graduates, including the execution of six Jesuit priests. This is not presented as rogue behavior by local commanders but as the logical execution of doctrine taught at Fort Benning. Greene writes, "Counterinsurgency doctrine rests on a simple premise: guerrilla fighters cannot survive without civilian support. The logical conclusion is chilling and straightforward: if you cannot catch the fish, you drain the sea." This metaphor powerfully encapsulates the strategy of depopulating rural areas, which drove one million Salvadorans—20 percent of the population—from their homes.

The Limits of Power and the Persistence of Memory

Despite the overwhelming firepower of the US-backed military, Greene illustrates how guerrilla fighters adapted and survived through global solidarity and ingenuity. He describes how Rafael and his comrades adopted techniques from Vietnamese resistance fighters, such as underground stoves with buried exhaust channels, to evade aerial bombing. "Superior technology doesn't guarantee victory," Greene asserts, citing instances where lightly armed fighters knocked six aircraft out of the sky.

The article also shines a light on the domestic opposition that often goes unmentioned in official histories. Greene details how the Sanctuary movement and groups like CISPES organized an underground network to shelter refugees and protest the war. He argues that "organized citizens can jam the gears" of the military-industrial complex, forcing Congress to attach human rights conditions to aid packages even when the administration tried to bypass them.

Ultimately, the piece concludes that while the state could grant amnesty and the US could fund the military, they could not erase the truth. Greene observes that survivors kept talking, leading forensic teams to mass graves that the state had denied for a decade. "The state spent a decade denying the massacre occurred. The people who lived through it never stopped telling the truth," he writes. This emphasis on memory serves as a rebuttal to the idea that political amnesia can ever truly succeed.

Bottom Line

Greene's strongest contribution is his refusal to treat the Salvadoran civil war as a distant historical footnote, instead weaving it directly into the current crisis of migration and anti-immigrant rhetoric. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on a single narrative arc that leaves little room for the complex internal dynamics of the Salvadoran left, but its moral clarity regarding the human cost of US policy is undeniable. Readers should watch for how this historical context is used to challenge the prevailing political narrative that ignores the root causes of displacement.
