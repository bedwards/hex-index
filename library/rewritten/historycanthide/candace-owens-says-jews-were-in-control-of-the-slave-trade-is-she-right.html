In an era where historical nuance is often sacrificed for viral soundbites, Kahlil Greene offers a necessary, rigorous dismantling of a resurgent conspiracy theory. The piece confronts the dangerous assertion that Jewish people "controlled" the Atlantic slave trade, not by ignoring the complexity of history, but by leaning into the very data that debunkers often overlook. This is not a simple fact-check; it is a masterclass in how to separate regional participation from systemic dominance, revealing how a grain of truth can be ground into a lie that poisons modern racial discourse.

The Anatomy of a Lie

Greene begins by identifying the source of the modern narrative, tracing it back to a 1991 publication by the Nation of Islam rather than any new archival discovery. He writes, "The book, with its 1,275 footnotes and scholarly veneer, claims that Jews were 'key operatives' in the slave trade and played an 'inordinate' and 'disproportionate' role in the kidnapping and sale of African people." The author's analysis is sharp: the text relies on a specific rhetorical trick. "The book works through a process historians (and everyone else) would call 'cherry-picking,'" Greene notes, explaining how legitimate scholars are quoted out of context to construct a narrative they explicitly reject. This framing is crucial because it exposes the mechanism of the lie. It is not a lack of evidence that sustains the myth, but a deliberate manipulation of evidence.

The commentary effectively highlights the danger of this academic masquerade. By citing real historians while omitting their conclusions, the original authors create a "technically accurate citation" that forms a "fundamentally dishonest argument." Greene points out that this method confers a false legitimacy, noting that students often find the work "very convincing" until they hunt down the original sources and discover the cited historians argue the exact opposite.

The academic framework confers legitimacy. But when students actually hunt down the sources, they discover something striking: the very historians being cited to prove Jewish dominance of the slave trade explicitly argue the opposite.

Regional Participation vs. Global Control

Greene refuses to whitewash history. He acknowledges that Jewish merchants did participate in the trade, particularly in specific Dutch colonies. He details how, in the Dutch Caribbean, "Jews established their own semi-autonomous settlement called Jodensavanne, 'Jewish Savannah,' where they owned sugar plantations and the enslaved Africans who worked them." He cites historian Seymour Drescher to note that at certain points, Jews controlled as much as 17 percent of the Dutch Caribbean trade. This nuance is vital; it prevents the argument from collapsing into a defensive denial of history. However, Greene immediately pivots to the scale of the operation. "The Dutch trade itself represented only 5 to 7 percent of the total transatlantic slave trade," he writes. "Jewish involvement within that fraction, while morally damning for those who participated, does not remotely constitute 'control' of the broader system."

The distinction between a minority group participating in a specific sector and a minority group controlling the entire global industry is the crux of the piece. Greene marshals data to show that the Atlantic trade was a state-sponsored monopoly. "The Royal African Company, chartered by the British Crown, transported more enslaved people than any other institution in history," he reminds readers. In this context, Jewish participation was legally and politically constrained. He cites Eli Faber's research, which found that in 18th-century Britain, "Jewish merchants owned near 0% of slave ships." In the American colonies, they accounted for less than 2% of all slave arrivals. The argument holds up under scrutiny because it shifts the focus from anecdotal examples, like the merchant Aaron Lopez in Newport, to the structural reality of the trade, which was dominated by massive European corporations and monarchies.

Critics might argue that focusing on the "control" aspect lets individual Jewish slaveholders off the hook morally. Greene anticipates this, stating, "These are not trivial numbers. They represent real human suffering, and Jewish historians have documented this participation openly for decades." The point is not to absolve individuals, but to correct the historical record regarding who held the power to shape the system.

The Irony of Exoneration

Perhaps the most stinging part of Greene's commentary is the observation regarding the current political landscape. He notes the irony that the claimant, a devout Catholic, directs blame away from the institutions that were central to the trade's execution. "The Catholic Church was not a marginal player in the Atlantic slave trade. It was a central institution," Greene writes. He points to Pope Nicholas V's 1452 bull "Dum Diversas," which explicitly authorized the subjugation of non-Christians into slavery. "The Jesuits owned plantations throughout the Americas," he adds, noting that the Church baptized enslaved Africans not to free them, but to make their bondage spiritually legitimate.

This section serves as a powerful reminder that the architects of American slavery were the white Christian plantation class and European colonial powers, not a shadowy minority. Greene argues that the lie serves a strategic purpose: "For precisely such trans-ethnic, trans-racial cooperation, epitomized by the historic partnership between blacks and Jews, is what poses the greatest threat to the isolationist movement." By resurrecting this myth, the narrative seeks to break the coalition between Black and Jewish communities, redirecting legitimate frustration toward an ancient conspiracy theory.

Bottom Line

Greene's strongest contribution is his refusal to let the conversation stall on the binary of "did they or didn't they," instead forcing a necessary discussion on scale, structure, and power. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in the emotional weight of the lie; even when debunked with data, the narrative persists because it offers a simple villain for complex historical trauma. Readers should watch for how this specific historical distortion is repurposed to fracture modern political alliances, as the data proves that the system was built by the state, not a secret cabal.

The question has never been whether some Jews participated. The question is whether Jews 'controlled' the entire Atlantic slave trade, as Owens claims. And here, the distinction between regional participation and global dominance matters enormously.
