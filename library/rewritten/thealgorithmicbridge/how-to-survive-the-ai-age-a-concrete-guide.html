Alberto Romero cuts through the noise of AI panic with a startlingly simple thesis: the real danger isn't that machines will do your job, but that the job itself will vanish because it becomes unnecessary. While most experts urge you to "learn AI," Romero argues that abstract advice is useless to those drowning in uncertainty, offering instead a grounded, psychological roadmap for survival.

The Architecture of Obsolescence

Romero begins by dismantling the common fear that AI will simply automate specific tasks. He posits that true disruption happens when the task itself loses its reason for existing. "New technologies that change the world—like AI will presumably do—don't do it by automating tasks you do now, but by making them unnecessary," Romero writes. He illustrates this with the decline of branch banking, noting that "ATMs barely displaced branch bankers but iPhones and apps and digital banking killed the entire career." This distinction is crucial; it shifts the threat from a manageable skill gap to an existential paradigm shift.

This framing resonates deeply when viewed through the lens of historical economic models like the Cobweb model, where supply and demand lag behind rapid technological change, creating volatile cycles of overproduction and collapse. Romero suggests we are currently in that lag phase, where the ground beneath our careers is shifting faster than we can visualize the new terrain. "AI is that, but unlike other tech, it is also the thing that builds the next paradigm," he observes, highlighting the unique dual nature of artificial intelligence as both the destroyer of old roles and the architect of new ones.

"You don't tell someone who's drowning to 'just swim.'"

Critics might argue that Romero's focus on the "unintelligible" nature of the shift induces paralysis rather than preparation. However, his admission that "there are no gurus for the unknown" actually strengthens his credibility, moving the conversation from false promises of mastery to honest management of anxiety.

From Abstract Advice to Concrete Action

The author takes a sharp turn against the industry's standard platitudes. He dismisses phrases like "be adaptive" or "learn to learn" as "functionally empty" for those who lack the internal architecture to act on them. Romero insists that the best preparation for an unprecedented crisis is "hearing how someone survived a different one." He urges readers to "talk to someone who lived through an industry collapse," drawing parallels to the psychological stages of grief seen in the transition from handwritten books to printed ones.

This historical grounding is effective because it normalizes the current fear. Just as the Luddites feared the loom would destroy the weaving trade, Romero reminds us that "historical disruptions all have idiosyncratic and contingent and haphazard features—but the arc is constant." By connecting the current AI anxiety to the "denial phase" of the printing press era, he provides a narrative container for the chaos.

However, Romero's advice to "separate your finances and career from your identity" is perhaps the most difficult pill to swallow in a culture where professional title often equals self-worth. He argues that "if your job disappeared tomorrow, would you know who you are without it?" is a question that must be answered before the crisis hits. This separation is vital because, as he notes, "if you let these two problems stay entangled, you get this overwhelming blob of anxiety where you can't work on either one."

The Paradox of Competence

Perhaps the most counter-intuitive section of the guide is Romero's stance on skill acquisition. He warns against obsessing over "AI skills" as a generic category, comparing it to someone in 1995 trying to learn "internet skills" without a specific goal. Instead, he advocates for a targeted approach: "take the most tedious, repetitive, soul-draining task in your current job and try to get an AI tool to do it."

This pragmatic strategy is bolstered by a warning about the dangers of over-reliance. Romero cites a Wharton study on "cognitive surrender," noting that when participants had access to AI, "they followed wrong answers 80% of the time, and their confidence went up anyway!" To counter this, he proposes a radical balance: "Pick one task you do regularly and keep it AI-free." He argues that "the friction of engaging the brain in tasks is what prevents it from rotting away," urging readers to "think for the sake of thinking, despite it being less productive."

"The labor market doesn't run on truth but on what employers believe is true; act accordingly."

Romero's final point on competence versus mastery is a sobering reality check for the anxious professional. He asserts that "competence ≠ mastery" and that employers are often looking for familiarity with jargon rather than deep expertise. "Your opinion about AI is irrelevant," he writes bluntly, reminding readers that "the job market has already made its decision, and that decision is binding whether it's correct or not." This aligns with the concept of deskilling, where the barrier to entry for complex tasks lowers, but the requirement for basic fluency in the new tools becomes the new baseline for employment.

Bottom Line

Romero's guide succeeds by replacing the industry's vague, anxiety-inducing platitudes with a psychologically grounded, actionable framework that acknowledges the reality of obsolescence without succumbing to fatalism. Its greatest strength is the shift from "learning AI" to "solving specific problems," though it leaves the reader with the heavy burden of decoupling their identity from their profession—a task far harder than mastering a new software tool.
