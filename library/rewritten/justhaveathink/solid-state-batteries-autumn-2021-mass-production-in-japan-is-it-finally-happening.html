Dave Borlace cuts through a decade of hype to ask a question the industry has been dodging: is the promise of solid-state batteries finally real, or just another expensive mirage? While most analysts push mass production for electric vehicles to 2025 or beyond, Borlace highlights a startling anomaly—a major Japanese manufacturer, Murata, claiming readiness for autumn 2021 production, albeit for a much smaller, less glamorous market. This distinction is crucial; it suggests the technology is maturing in the shadows of wearables before it ever powers a car.

The History of Broken Promises

Borlace opens with a necessary dose of skepticism, noting that "there's been an awful lot of industry and media excitement around solid-state batteries for about the last decade or so." He effectively dismantles the credibility of previous announcements by citing high-profile failures. The argument gains weight when he points out that "every time a major manufacturer announces they're on the cusp of a breakthrough... their optimism turns out to have been unfounded." He details how Fisker abandoned their solid-state supercar plans entirely and how Dyson wrote off billions after acquiring the battery firm Sakti3. This historical context is vital; it frames the current Murata announcement not as a miracle, but as a test of whether this specific player has solved problems that have tripped up giants.

Critics might argue that focusing on past failures creates an undue bias against current breakthroughs, as technology often follows a non-linear path where one company finally cracks the code. However, Borlace's point stands: the barrier to entry is not just scientific but manufacturing-scale, a hurdle that has proven fatal for many.

The Physics of Safety and Density

The core of the technical argument rests on the fundamental flaw of current lithium-ion batteries: the liquid electrolyte. Borlace explains that in traditional cells, lithium ions can form "spikes that can eventually puncture the separator," leading to short circuits and "a small incendiary device going off in your trouser pocket." This vivid imagery underscores the safety risks that solid-state technology aims to eliminate. By replacing the liquid with a solid, the battery becomes "much safer" and potentially "three times" more energy-dense.

The useful reactivity of lithium also has its downside... you could find yourself with a small incendiary device going off in your trouser pocket.

Borlace correctly identifies that the benefits extend beyond safety. He notes that solid-state batteries "no longer need the cumbersome battery management systems that add weight and cost to existing lithium-ion batteries." This is a compelling economic argument that often gets lost in the technical specs. However, he also acknowledges the trade-offs, mentioning that sulfide-based materials, while conductive, can produce "very unhealthy gas when exposed to air" and struggle with moisture. This nuance prevents the piece from reading like a sales pitch and grounds the optimism in chemical reality.

The Murata Pivot and the EV Mirage

The most distinctive part of Borlace's coverage is his analysis of Murata's strategy. Rather than chasing the electric vehicle (EV) market immediately, Murata is targeting the "space somewhere between the tiny devices like pacemakers and mobile devices like smartphones." Borlace writes, "Morata's battery doesn't use sulfides in its chemistry and it's not aimed at the electric vehicle Market." Instead, they are leveraging Sony's former battery division to create a "non-combustible and highly heat resistant solid oxide ceramic electrolyte."

This strategic pivot is the piece's strongest insight. It suggests that the path to EV dominance will not be a sudden leap, but a gradual climb through smaller, less volatile markets. Borlace notes that Murata plans to start with "relatively small batches" and scale to "a hundred thousand batteries a month." This is a pragmatic approach compared to the "desperate attempt to catch up" by automotive giants like Ford and GM.

The real Holy Grail... is of course a truly affordable mass-produced solid-state battery for electric vehicles.

Borlace contrasts Murata's measured approach with the volatility of QuantumScape, a company that saw its stock soar on promises of a "300 mile battery pack" before an independent report revealed their tests were on "small prototypes not full packs." This comparison serves as a warning: the gap between a lab prototype and a mass-market product is where capital goes to die. A counterargument worth considering is that the pressure to deliver for EVs might actually accelerate innovation in ways that slow, steady progress in wearables cannot. Yet, the financial carnage of failed promises suggests caution is the only rational stance.

Bottom Line

Borlace's most valuable contribution is reframing the solid-state narrative from an imminent EV revolution to a slow, segmented rollout starting with niche electronics. His strongest argument is the historical evidence of manufacturing failures, which tempers the current hype cycle. The biggest vulnerability in the optimistic view remains the sheer difficulty of scaling solid-state chemistry without the safety nets of liquid electrolytes. Readers should watch Murata's production numbers closely; if they succeed in the wearable market, the path to the electric vehicle "Holy Grail" may finally become real, but it will likely take longer than the headlines suggest.
