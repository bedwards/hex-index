Dave Borlace doesn't just explain ocean currents; he exposes a terrifyingly slow-motion clock that humanity is actively trying to break. While most climate coverage fixates on the immediate heat of the air, Borlace shifts the lens to the deep, cold, and sluggish engine that redistributes our planet's heat and nutrients. He argues that we are staring down a "blue ocean event" in the Arctic that could stall this engine, with consequences far more complex than a simple cooling of Europe.

The Invisible Engine

Borlace begins by dismantling the common misconception that the ocean is a static body of water, noting that early oceanographers assumed the deep ocean was "perfectly static" until modern instruments proved otherwise. He introduces the concept of thermohaline circulation, a mouthful of Greek roots meaning "heat" and "salt," which he simplifies effectively as the "great ocean conveyor belt." The mechanism is elegant in its simplicity but massive in scale: warm, less dense water travels north, cools, becomes saltier as evaporation leaves the salt behind, and then sinks. Borlace writes, "The seawater that's left behind gets more salty and more dense and that makes it sink." This sinking action, he explains, is the gravitational pull that drives the entire global system, pushing cold water down to the ocean floor where it travels southward for centuries.

The author's decision to trace this journey all the way to Antarctica to explain "overturning" is a crucial narrative choice. He describes how the freezing surface water forces nutrient-rich deep water to rise, creating a "nutrient bonanza" that sustains the entire marine food chain. This is not just physics; it is biology. Borlace notes that "phytoplankton are of course the first stage of the ocean food chain an absolutely essential to all ocean life." By connecting the physics of density to the survival of whales and fish, he grounds the abstract science in tangible ecological stakes. Critics might argue that the sheer complexity of these interactions makes them difficult to model accurately, but Borlace's strength lies in his refusal to shy away from the technical terms while simultaneously translating them into plain English.

It takes about five hundred years for the conveyor belt to turn over the oceans waters and make one complete circuit around the planet.

The Gulf Stream Paradox

The commentary takes a sharper turn when Borlace distinguishes between the thermohaline circulation and the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC), the system famously depicted in the film The Day After Tomorrow. He clarifies that while often used interchangeably, the AMOC includes wind-driven surface currents, whereas thermohaline is strictly density-driven. This distinction matters because the Gulf Stream, a surface current, is currently showing signs of distress. Borlace cites evidence that the current is "at its weakest in 1,600 years," a deviation that could lead to "far more extreme winters" in Western Europe and rising sea levels on the US East Coast.

However, Borlace introduces a fascinating counter-intuitive twist: the very warming that threatens the current might temporarily mask its effects in Europe. He references Professor Ka Tung's theory that the AMOC operates on a cyclical basis, where a slowdown in the current forces more heat into the atmosphere, accelerating global warming. Borlace writes, "The slowdown in AMOC since 2004 means less heat going into the sea and more heat going into the atmosphere which is just about the worst coincidental timing we could possibly have." This is a sobering insight; it suggests that the ocean's failure to absorb heat is actually making the air hotter right now, rather than cooling the continent as one might expect from a stalled current.

A counterargument worth considering is whether this cyclical theory holds up against the overwhelming trend of anthropogenic warming. While Tung suggests the system might eventually speed up again to sequester heat, the accelerating loss of Arctic ice suggests the system may be pushed past a tipping point where it never recovers. Borlace acknowledges this uncertainty, noting that "human-caused global warming is responsible for at least a significant part of the weakening."

The Arctic Tipping Point

The piece culminates in a warning about the "blue ocean event"—a future where the Arctic is ice-free in September. Borlace connects this directly to the potential collapse of the circulation system, citing a Yale University thesis that links "Arctic sea ice decline" to a "slowdown of the Atlantic meridional overturning circulation." The logic is chilling: as ice melts, it dumps fresh water into the North Atlantic, diluting the saltiness and preventing the water from sinking. If the water doesn't sink, the conveyor belt stops.

Borlace's coverage is notable for its refusal to offer a neat resolution. Instead of a solution, he offers a warning about the interconnectedness of the system. He reminds the audience that "our health and our future is dependent on them," referring to the oceans, and that the "complicated interactions of driving forces and currents" are now being disrupted by human activity. The author's self-deprecating humor about his own "considerable ignorant and incompetent" attempt to understand the science actually serves to highlight the sheer magnitude of the challenge facing climatologists.

The warm Atlantic current linked to severe and abrupt changes in the climate in the past is now at its weakest in at least 1,600 years.

Bottom Line

Borlace's strongest contribution is his ability to humanize a five-century geological process, making the slow-motion collapse of the ocean conveyor belt feel like an immediate crisis. His biggest vulnerability lies in the uncertainty of the AMOC's cyclical nature; while he presents the "self-regulating" theory as a possibility, the accelerating pace of Arctic ice loss suggests the system may be moving toward a permanent shutdown rather than a temporary dip. Readers should watch for new data on the rate of fresh water influx in the North Atlantic, as that is the single most critical variable determining whether this conveyor belt grinds to a halt.
