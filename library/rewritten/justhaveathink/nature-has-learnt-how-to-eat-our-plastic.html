Dave Borlace reframes the plastic crisis not as an unsolvable environmental disaster, but as an evolutionary puzzle that nature has already begun to solve. The most striking claim here is that a bacterium discovered in a Japanese recycling plant didn't just survive on plastic; it evolved a specific enzyme to digest it in mere decades, a timescale that defies standard evolutionary expectations. For busy minds tracking the climate narrative, this shifts the focus from despair to a tangible, biological mechanism that could finally close the loop on our waste.

The Accidental Discovery

Borlace anchors his narrative in a moment of scientific serendipity that mirrors Alexander Fleming's discovery of penicillin. He writes, "back in 2016 scientists at a recycling plant in Kyoto in Japan discovered a mysterious sludge that seemed to be engulfing a huge pile of plastic bottles." This opening is effective because it immediately humanizes the science, moving from abstract pollution statistics to a tangible, almost cinematic moment of discovery. The author correctly identifies that the plastic in question, polyethylene terephthalate (PET), was engineered specifically to be indestructible, making the bacterium's ability to consume it a genuine anomaly.

The core of the argument rests on the idea that nature is more adaptable than our industrial designs. Borlace notes that "nature has actually evolved its own natural polyester called cutin which provides a waxy protective surface to plants." He explains that bacteria evolved to eat this natural layer, and the leap to eating synthetic PET was surprisingly small. This framing is compelling because it suggests the solution was always latent in the environment, waiting for the right conditions to emerge. However, critics might note that relying on natural evolution to solve a crisis we created in the last 70 years is a gamble; the rate of plastic production vastly outpaces natural adaptation without human intervention.

"The structure of PET effectively reduces the gap in the polymer chain to a size that's smaller than a carbon dioxide molecule."

Borlace uses this detail to illustrate why PET became a triumph of human ingenuity in 1951, doubling soda sales by keeping drinks fizzy. Yet, this same structural integrity is what makes it a 450-year nightmare in landfills. The author's transition from the success of the material to its environmental cost is seamless, highlighting the irony that the very feature that made PET a commercial success is now its greatest liability.

Engineering the Solution

The commentary shifts from passive observation to active engineering when Borlace discusses the work of Professor John McGeehan at the University of Portsmouth. The author writes, "genius is 1% inspiration and 99% perspiration and so it proved with Ideonella Sakaiensis." This quote effectively sets the stage for the grueling scientific process that followed the initial discovery. Borlace details how researchers isolated the gene responsible for the digestion enzyme and then used x-ray crystallography to map its atomic structure.

The most fascinating part of the coverage is the accidental breakthrough that followed. Borlace explains that when the team tried to reverse-engineer the enzyme back to its natural state, "by sheer chance what it actually produced was a vastly improved PETase enzyme." This is a crucial narrative beat; it suggests that the path to a solution isn't always linear or predictable. The author emphasizes that a single amino acid change widened the enzyme's "chemical jaws," allowing it to accommodate the synthetic polymer.

"The team were able to identify the subtle changes in the PETase enzyme that allowed it to break down PET."

This section is strong because it demystifies genetic engineering, presenting it as a process of tweaking existing biological tools rather than creating life from scratch. Borlace acknowledges the remaining hurdles, noting that while the enzyme is hundreds of times faster than natural degradation, it is still "far too slow to cope with the incredible rate that new plastics are being churned out." This honesty prevents the piece from sliding into techno-optimism; it admits that the science is promising but not yet a silver bullet.

The Economic Reality

Borlace concludes by grounding the scientific breakthrough in the harsh realities of capitalism and market forces. He writes, "in an ideal world this sort of research should be regarded as essential to species survival and adequately funded by every government on earth." He then immediately pivots to the real-world constraint: "it'll most likely be commercial investment that provides the key." This is a sobering but necessary addition to the narrative. The author highlights that the byproducts of this process—ethylene glycol for antifreeze and recycled materials for packaging—are marketable commodities.

"The really great thing for our dear investors is that there's very little chance they're going to run out of raw material any time soon."

This line is sharp and slightly cynical, yet it accurately reflects the motivation required to scale the technology. Borlace suggests that the sheer volume of plastic waste ensures a steady supply chain for these enzymes, turning an environmental problem into a business opportunity. A counterargument worth considering is that market-driven solutions often prioritize profitable plastics over the most harmful ones, potentially leaving a significant portion of waste unaddressed. Nevertheless, the author's point that economic incentives are the engine for scaling remains the most pragmatic path forward.

"Nature has learnt how to eat our plastic, but it needs our help to do it fast enough."

Bottom Line

Borlace's coverage succeeds by transforming a complex biochemical process into a story of evolutionary resilience and human ingenuity, avoiding both doom-mongering and naive optimism. The strongest part of the argument is the revelation that the enzyme's evolution occurred in just 50 years, proving that nature can adapt to human-made materials faster than we assumed. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that commercial viability will align perfectly with environmental necessity, a tension that remains unresolved. Readers should watch for the next phase of this research: whether the "turbocharged" enzymes can be produced at a scale that actually moves the needle on global plastic waste.
