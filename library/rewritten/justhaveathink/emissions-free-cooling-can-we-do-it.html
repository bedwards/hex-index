Dave Borlace identifies a paradox that threatens to derail global decarbonization efforts: the very technology keeping us alive in a warming world is accelerating the heat crisis. He argues that while air conditioning is a "lifesaver in many countries," it is simultaneously "one of the major causes of that warming," creating a feedback loop that demands immediate architectural and chemical innovation rather than just incremental efficiency tweaks.

The Hidden Cost of Comfort

Borlace begins by dismantling the assumption that modern air conditioning is a solved problem. He notes that the basic principle hasn't changed in over a century, yet the scale of consumption is exploding. "There are now almost 2 billion domestic AC units in use all over the world and that number is expected to Triple again to almost 6 billion by 2050," he writes. This projection is staggering, especially when paired with the fact that these systems often run on grids powered by fossil fuels. The author's framing is effective because it forces the reader to confront the sheer magnitude of the energy demand before introducing the solution.

The core of the problem, as Borlace explains, lies in the dual task of cooling and dehumidifying. Standard vapor compression systems waste massive amounts of energy forcing water vapor to change phase from gas to liquid. "According to this recent article an air conditioner operating in a tropical climate can use as much as 60 percent of its cooling power to dehumidify the air," he states. This is a critical insight; it suggests that the inefficiency isn't just in the motor or the compressor, but in the fundamental physics of how we handle moisture. Critics might note that in dry climates, this dehumidification penalty is negligible, but Borlace correctly identifies that the most vulnerable populations live in humid, tropical zones where this waste is most acute.

AC cooling systems essentially perform two tasks at the same time they cool the air and they dry the air but persuading that water vapor to change phase from a gas to a liquid and condense out as water droplets takes energy energy that could otherwise be focused on keeping the room cool.

A Return to Desiccants with a Twist

To break this cycle, Borlace pivots to an old idea revitalized by new materials: desiccants. He traces the lineage back to the Kathabar company in 1910, noting that vapor compression only achieved market dominance in the 1930s. Today, however, startups like Transcira are revisiting this approach using Metal Organic Frameworks (MOFs). These are not your standard silica gel packets. "These are sponge-like highly porous materials that have an extremely large surface area per gram," Borlace describes. The innovation here is chemical precision; because the pores are so narrow, water vapor enters but cannot turn into liquid, bypassing the energy-intensive phase change entirely.

The argument gains traction when Borlace highlights the synergy between the cooling system and the regeneration process. Instead of fighting the heat, the system uses it. "Transcira's design can utilize that heat to dry and regenerate the mof material ready to mop up more water on the next cycle," he explains. This circular logic is compelling because it turns a waste product (heat) into a necessary input. The result, he notes, is a "35 increase in overall Energy Efficiency," a figure that makes the technology commercially viable for the first time.

The Salt Solution and Indirect Cooling

While Transcira focuses on solid MOFs, Borlace introduces a second contender, Blue Frontier, which takes a liquid approach. Their system replaces silica gel with a concentrated salt solution. The engineering challenge here is the "wrinkle" of evaporative cooling: traditional methods cool air by adding moisture, which becomes unbearable in humid environments. Blue Frontier solves this with an indirect process. "The dryer then gets separated out into two new streams one stream gets sent over a thin layer of water and reabsorbs a load of moisture again through evaporation which uses up energy and lowers the temperature of the air," Borlace details. By using this cooled, humid stream to chill a separate dry air stream across a metal barrier, they achieve cooling without adding humidity to the room.

The potential impact of this separation is profound. Borlace points out that Blue Frontier claims a "60 reduction in energy use" and an "85 reduction in global warming potential or gwp" by eliminating refrigerant gases entirely. This is a dual victory for climate goals: reducing operational electricity and removing the potent greenhouse gases that leak from standard units. However, the reliance on overnight regeneration to utilize low-cost electricity suggests these systems may struggle in regions with unstable grid infrastructure or where peak demand pricing is not time-based.

It also doesn't use any of those horrible refrigerant greenhouse gases that standard AC systems use which means there's an 85 reduction in global warming potential or gwp.

The Quiet Revolution

Borlace concludes by tempering expectations with a dose of realism. These technologies are not glamorous headline grabbers; they are "quietly work[ing] away somewhere in the back of a machine." He argues that the transition away from fossil fuels will depend on these unglamorous efficiencies just as much as on solar farms or wind turbines. "Most people Now understand the magnitude of our Global decarbonization challenge not every energy saving or low carbon technology will be a glamorous headline Grabber," he writes. This is a necessary corrective to the tech-optimist narrative that assumes a single silver bullet will save us.

The strongest part of Borlace's coverage is his ability to connect the dots between historical failures, material science breakthroughs, and immediate climate urgency. He avoids the trap of promising a magic wand, instead presenting a portfolio of solutions that address the specific physics of humidity and heat. While the commercial viability of these startups remains to be seen at scale, the direction of travel is clear: the future of cooling lies in managing moisture differently, not just pushing more electricity through a compressor.

Bottom Line

Borlace makes a compelling case that the future of air conditioning depends on decoupling cooling from dehumidification, a shift that could slash energy use by up to 60 percent. The argument's greatest strength is its focus on material science and thermodynamic efficiency rather than just renewable energy integration, though the reliance on specific grid conditions for regeneration remains a potential vulnerability. Readers should watch for the commercial rollout of Transcira and Blue Frontier, as their success will determine whether this quiet revolution can scale fast enough to meet the tripling demand for cooling by 2050.
