In an era where climate skepticism often hides behind cosmic explanations, Dave Borlace cuts through the noise with a stark, data-driven reality check: the Sun is not the villain we need to blame for our warming planet. While many are tempted to retreat into the comforting narrative of natural cycles, Borlace marshals hard physics to show that even a "Grand Solar Minimum" would be a mere footnote in the story of human-induced climate change. This is essential listening for anyone tired of the debate being derailed by astronomical speculation.

The Cosmic Context

Borlace begins by grounding the reader in the sheer scale of our star, reminding us that the Sun is a "massive nuclear fusion reactor" that converts "4 million tons of matter into energy" every single second. He establishes that while the Sun is the dominant force in our solar system, its behavior is surprisingly stable over human timescales. The author notes that the Sun is "on course to stay pretty much stable for another five billion or so," yet it does undergo an 11-year cycle of activity known as the solar minimum and maximum.

The core of the argument rests on the distinction between a standard solar dip and the feared "Grand Solar Minimum." Borlace explains that while we are approaching a quiet period, the science suggests it will be "very similar to solar cycle 24," not a historic anomaly. He traces the history of solar observation from Galileo to Rudolf Wolf, noting that the "Wolfe index" has tracked these sunspots for centuries. The visual evidence is compelling; Borlace contrasts images of the Sun's surface during a maximum, which are "literally spotless" in their quietness, against the busy, magnetized chaos of a peak cycle.

"The obvious question is how big an influence these solar cycles have on the climate of our planet and what a grand solar minimum cause major cooling on the planet and bring about the onset of a mini ice age as some folks seem to be suggesting."

This framing is effective because it directly addresses the fear-mongering without dismissing the science of solar cycles. Borlace acknowledges the validity of the observation while immediately pivoting to the magnitude of the effect.

The Math of Irradiance

To dismantle the cooling hypothesis, Borlace dives into the physics of "radiant flux density," or irradiance, measured in watts per square meter. He meticulously breaks down the numbers, showing that while solar irradiance does fluctuate with sunspot activity, the variance is a tiny "one point four watts per square meter." When adjusted for the Earth's spherical shape and reflectivity (albedo), the actual radiative forcing drops to a negligible "0.25 watts per square meter."

The author then places this figure in a broader context using data from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change. He points out that carbon dioxide alone accounts for "as much as two watts per square meter of extra warming," dwarfing the solar contribution. Borlace writes, "Way down at the bottom of the chart we get two natural radiative forcing factors which is basically the Sun at an overall additional forcing over the timescale of the chart of no more than 0.1 watts per square meter. It barely registers."

Critics might argue that solar variability affects regional weather patterns more than global averages, but Borlace's focus on global radiative forcing correctly identifies the primary driver of long-term temperature trends. The data leaves little room for the Sun to be the main culprit.

The Maunder Minimum Myth

Perhaps the most persistent myth is the link between the "Maunder Minimum"—a period of low sunspot activity between 1645 and 1715—and the "Little Ice Age." Borlace systematically debunks this correlation. He notes that paleo-climate reconstructions show the Little Ice Age began "at least 50 years before the beginning of the Maunder Minimum." Furthermore, he highlights that summer temperatures during the Maunder Minimum were "very similar to summer temperatures in subsequent years."

Instead of solar inactivity, Borlace points to volcanic activity as the likely cause, citing a 2012 paper by Miller et al. He emphasizes that since 1970, "global temperatures have shot up by almost nine point seven degrees Celsius while the amount of solar energy reaching the earth has actually declined." This divergence is the smoking gun. As Borlace puts it, "The upper atmosphere is cooling while the lower atmosphere warms, a clear fingerprint of warming from greenhouse gases rather than the Sun."

"Even the grandest of grand solar minimums comes nowhere close to compensating for the warming that humans CO2 and other greenhouse gas emissions will cause."

This is the piece's most powerful takeaway. Borlace uses a chart from Filner and Rahmstorf to illustrate that even a severe solar minimum would only dim global temperatures by "no more than 0.3 degrees Celsius," a drop that would be instantly swallowed by the trajectory of human emissions.

The Human Element

Borlace concludes by addressing the psychological appeal of blaming the Sun. He suggests that attributing chaotic weather to natural cycles allows society to "delude ourselves that climate change has nothing to do with human beings." He argues that this is a "cop-out" that absolves us of responsibility. The reality, he asserts, is that extreme weather events are "entirely predictable and explainable within the context of human-induced global warming."

He connects the dots between our fossil fuel consumption, deforestation, and the unprecedented rate of warming. Borlace warns that the planet's atmosphere will continue to warm "throughout the course of this century and beyond," regardless of where we are in a solar cycle. The argument is a call to action, urging listeners to stop looking at the stars for answers and start looking at their own lifestyles.

"Blaming the Sun is a cop-out. We've caused climate change and we all need to change our lifestyles radically if we're to stand any chance at all of fixing the problem."

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage is a masterclass in using hard data to cut through climate denialism, effectively demonstrating that solar cycles are a minor player in a game dominated by greenhouse gases. While the argument relies heavily on global averages, which can sometimes obscure regional solar effects, the sheer magnitude of the radiative forcing difference makes the conclusion undeniable. The strongest takeaway is the mathematical proof that a Grand Solar Minimum would be a drop in the ocean of human-caused warming, leaving no excuse for inaction.
