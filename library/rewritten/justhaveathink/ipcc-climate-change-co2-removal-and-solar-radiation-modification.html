Dave Borlace delivers a sobering reality check: the world's most trusted climate models rely on technologies that are either unproven at scale or fraught with existential risk. While the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) presents carbon removal as a safety net, Borlace exposes the dangerous assumption that we can engineer our way out of a crisis we created by burning fossil fuels. This is not a technical manual; it is a warning that the "fix" might be more volatile than the problem itself.

The Illusion of a Safety Net

Borlace begins by dismantling the comforting narrative that reducing emissions is enough. He points out that the IPCC's own scenarios for limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius are built on a fragile foundation. "The vast majority of scenarios assessed with a 66% or better chance of limiting global warming to two degrees Celsius by 2100 include carbon dioxide removal," Borlace notes. This admission is staggering. It suggests that current global policy is betting the planet on technologies that do not yet exist at the required scale.

The author highlights a glaring omission in the official reports regarding the Arctic. While the IPCC acknowledges rapid warming, Borlace argues the report is "almost willfully negligent" for ignoring the accelerating release of methane from melting permafrost. He cites NASA's data, warning that an "influx of permafrost derived methane into the atmosphere in the mid 21st century... is not currently accounted for in climate projections." If this feedback loop triggers, the models predicting a manageable 2-degree rise could collapse entirely. Critics might argue that the IPCC must draw lines somewhere to remain actionable, but Borlace's point stands: ignoring a potential methane explosion renders the entire roadmap suspect.

"Capturing CO2 in this way is not as permanent a solution as reducing emissions from the first place."

The Land and Water Trap

When the report pivots to nature-based solutions, the scale of the challenge becomes physically apparent. Borlace details the IPCC's reliance on afforestation and reforestation, noting that we would need to reclaim an area "about two-thirds the size of Australia" to make a dent in emissions. While planting trees seems benign, Borlace warns of the risks. He describes a scenario where a "malevolent regime decides to burn them down," instantly reversing decades of carbon sequestration.

The author also touches on soil carbon sequestration, a method that locks carbon in the ground through better farming practices. This approach is promising because it is "pretty much cost effective without any climate policy." However, Borlace reminds us that soil, like forests, has a saturation point. Once the soil is full, it stops absorbing, and the timeline for this saturation is merely decades or a century, not the millennia required for geological storage. The argument here is effective because it shifts the conversation from "how many trees" to "how long do they last."

The Energy Paradox of Direct Air Capture

Perhaps the most contentious technology discussed is Direct Air Capture (DAC). The concept is seductive: suck carbon directly from the sky and store it underground. Borlace describes it as a "souped-up vacuum cleaner," but the physics are unforgiving. Because CO2 is so dilute in the air, the energy required is massive. "Research suggests that the energy consumption could be up to twelve point nine Giga joules per ton of CO2 captured," he writes.

This creates a paradox. To capture carbon, you need vast amounts of clean energy. If that energy comes from fossil fuels, the process is counterproductive. Borlace illustrates the sheer absurdity of the scale required by looking at the company Climeworks. To capture just 1% of global emissions, they would need to build "750,000 shipping containers worth of fans." He notes that this is the same number of containers that come out of Shanghai Harbor in just two weeks. The math is mind-boggling, and while the technology is feasible in theory, the logistical and energy hurdles are immense. A counterargument worth considering is that innovation could drive energy costs down, but Borlace's current analysis suggests we are decades away from viability.

Geoengineering: The Last Resort

The final section of the analysis tackles the most radical ideas: ocean fertilization and solar radiation modification (SRM). Ocean fertilization, or "iron seeding," involves dumping iron into the sea to spur algae growth. Borlace dismisses this as a "sticking-plaster than a cure," citing mixed results where algae simply got eaten or caused oxygen depletion. The international community has largely placed a moratorium on this, recognizing the ecological risks.

SRM, specifically stratospheric aerosol injection, is presented as the ultimate "lesser of two evils." Borlace explains how volcanic eruptions like Mount Pinatubo naturally cooled the planet by reflecting sunlight. The proposal is to mimic this by spraying sulfur dioxide into the atmosphere. "If you're a conspiracy theorists you might be shouting chemtrails at the screen right now," he jokes, before pivoting to the real dangers. The author warns that this could lead to "catastrophic air pollution," acid rain, and the destruction of the ozone layer.

"The scientific literature only really supports solar radiation modification as a kind of last-ditch option if we really do find ourselves totally screwed in the middle of this century."

Borlace's framing is crucial here. He does not present SRM as a solution, but as a desperate gamble. The governance of such a technology is a geopolitical nightmare, requiring a level of international cooperation that currently seems impossible. If one country deploys it to cool their region, it could alter rainfall patterns for the entire globe, potentially causing famine elsewhere.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's coverage is a vital corrective to the techno-optimism that often plagues climate discourse. His strongest argument is that the IPCC's reliance on unproven carbon removal technologies creates a dangerous moral hazard, allowing policymakers to delay immediate emission cuts. The piece's biggest vulnerability is that it offers no easy alternative; the conclusion is that we must do the hard, unglamorous work of reducing consumption and protecting existing ecosystems, rather than waiting for a technological miracle. Readers should watch for the upcoming COP24 negotiations to see if world leaders acknowledge these risks or continue to bet on the impossible.
