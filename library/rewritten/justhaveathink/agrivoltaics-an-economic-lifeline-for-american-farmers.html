Dave Borlace cuts through the usual agricultural doom-scrolling with a solution that feels almost too elegant to be true: growing food and harvesting energy on the exact same patch of dirt. While most climate discussions treat farming and renewable energy as competing land uses, Borlace presents agrivoltaics not just as a technical curiosity, but as a potential economic lifeline for a US farming sector facing an existential crisis. He brings hard data to a conversation often dominated by ideology, arguing that the sun can be harvested twice without sacrificing the harvest itself.

The Symbiotic Equation

The core of Borlace's argument rests on a biological constraint that most people overlook: plants have a limit to how much sunlight they can use. "Any extra light received beyond that point makes no difference at all to the plant's photosynthesis all it does is make the plant sweat which makes it more thirsty," Borlace writes. This observation reframes the solar panel not as a shadow-casting obstacle, but as a precision tool for water conservation. By shading crops just enough to prevent overheating while capturing the excess radiation, the system creates a microclimate that reduces water loss through evapotranspiration.

The author highlights a counterintuitive benefit that strengthens the economic case: the crops actually help the solar panels. "Ironically solar panels don't actually like getting hot it reduces their operating efficiency so anything that can draw heat off them will help to make the panels more productive," he notes. This mutual benefit is the hook that turns a theoretical concept into a viable business model. The Oregon State University study he cites suggests that this cooling effect can boost electricity generation by up to 10 percent, effectively using the same sunlight to power the farm's machinery and generate a second income stream.

"You're effectively using the sunlight twice to grow cash crops and to generate a second income stream from the solar power generation."

Critics might argue that the initial capital costs for these elevated, complex structures are prohibitive for small-scale family farms already operating on razor-thin margins. Borlace acknowledges this, noting that the levelized cost of electricity is higher than standard ground-mounted systems, but he counters that the dual revenue streams and water savings could offset the upfront investment over time.

A Global Patchwork of Adoption

Borlace's coverage effectively contrasts the slow pace of adoption in some regions with the aggressive scaling in others. He points out that while the US struggles with trade wars and commodity prices that have shuttered over 100,000 farms since 2011, other nations are moving fast. "China who as we've learned on so many occasions on so many occasions never ever do anything on a small scale installed an estimated four gigawatts of agricultural pv capacity just in the three years between 2015 and 2018," he observes. This global perspective underscores that the technology is proven; the barrier is often political will or market structure rather than engineering.

The piece shines when detailing the French viticulture experiment, where artificial intelligence adjusts panel angles to protect vines from extreme weather. "Water demand was reduced by as much as 34 percent in the pv sheltered vines thanks to that reduction in evapotranspiration," Borlace reports. This specific example moves the conversation from abstract theory to tangible, measurable results. It suggests that agrivoltaics could be particularly transformative for high-value crops like wine grapes, which are increasingly vulnerable to climate volatility.

However, the author's treatment of the Australian market reveals a significant friction point. Despite the obvious benefits, he notes that "the only obstacles to be honest a willful ignorance and an unwillingness on the part of farmers and solar developers to cooperate." This blunt assessment highlights that the technology's success depends less on the panels and more on the human element of land use negotiation. The conflict between solar developers and farmers over grid access in Australia serves as a cautionary tale for other regions where land is scarce.

The Path Forward

As the technology matures, Borlace emphasizes that innovation is rapidly addressing early limitations. He points to translucent panels that allow for finer control of light wavelengths and mobile systems that can adapt to different terrains. "The industry is still young and it's only recently started to evolve," he writes, suggesting that we are only at the beginning of a major shift in how we view agricultural land. The potential for these systems to create habitats for wildlife and improve soil health adds an ecological layer to the economic argument, making the case for agrivoltaics even stronger.

"Agrivoltaics do look very promising and the technology could play a major role in diffusing the current conflict between agricultural land owners and solar developers especially in higher populated countries where space is at a premium."

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace makes a compelling case that agrivoltaics is the most pragmatic solution to the dual crises of food security and climate change, transforming a zero-sum game of land use into a synergistic partnership. The argument's greatest strength is its focus on the biological and economic symbiosis between crops and panels, yet its biggest vulnerability lies in the high upfront costs and the cultural resistance of traditional farming communities. Readers should watch for how policy incentives evolve to bridge the gap between this promising technology and the farmers who need it most.
