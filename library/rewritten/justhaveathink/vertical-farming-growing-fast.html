Dave Borlace doesn't just ask if we can grow food in skyscrapers; he asks if we can survive the next thirty years without doing so. While many dismiss vertical farming as an expensive sci-fi fantasy, Borlace anchors his argument in the stark reality of a population that will hit 9 billion by 2050, with 70% of us living in cities. He brings fresh, hard data on energy efficiency and crop yields that challenges the assumption that traditional agriculture is the only viable path forward.

The Urban Imperative

Borlace frames the problem not as a lack of land, but as a logistical nightmare of distance and degradation. He notes that in the United States, "the average food item travels about 1500 miles before it reaches the plate." This statistic is the engine of his argument: localizing production is a carbon necessity, not just a convenience. He explains that by moving farms indoors, we eliminate the need for toxic pesticides and slash water usage by 90% through hydroponics, where roots are submerged in nutrient-rich liquids rather than soil.

The author highlights the sheer density possible in these systems. He points to a startup in San Francisco called Plenty, which "reckons to harvest the same quantity of produce from its 2 acre site as you'd get from a traditional farm taking up 720 acres of land." This comparison is staggering, yet Borlace is careful to acknowledge the elephant in the room: the energy cost. Critics might note that replacing sunlight with electricity seems like a net negative for the climate, but Borlace counters this by pointing to the plummeting costs of renewable energy and LED efficiency.

"By its very nature, the process is extremely reliable compared to traditional agriculture. There's no seasonality in the weather conditions, so you get consistent crop production all year round with vastly reduced harvesting time and no compromise in flavor or quality."

This reliability is the hidden value proposition. Traditional farming is a gamble with the weather; vertical farming is a manufacturing process. Borlace illustrates this with a facility in Copenhagen powered entirely by wind, proving that the energy burden can be decoupled from fossil fuels. However, the argument glosses over the massive capital expenditure required to build these "factories," which remains a barrier to entry for smaller operators.

Beyond the Greens

The most compelling part of Borlace's coverage is his pivot from leafy greens to the staples that actually feed the world. For years, experts agreed that cereal crops like wheat and rice were impossible to grow vertically due to their height and caloric density. Borlace challenges this dogma by citing 2020 research from the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences.

He writes, "The researchers created two growth simulation models of a 10-layer vertical farm... and in that environment, they found that the setup could yield as much as 1940 metric tons of wheat per hectare of land per year." To put that in perspective, traditional farming manages just 3.2 tons per hectare. If this simulation translates to commercial reality, the implications are existential. Borlace argues that "if that turns out to be verifiable in a commercial setting, then it really would be a game changer."

He also explores the symbiotic potential of aquaponics, where fish waste fertilizes plants, and the use of abandoned infrastructure like mine shafts to reduce heating costs. These innovations suggest a flexible, modular future for food production. Yet, the sheer scale of the wheat challenge cannot be understated; moving from a simulation to a global supply chain for a crop that provides 20% of human calories is a leap of faith that requires billions in investment.

"Our goal is to fit perishable produce into as many people's budgets as possible. Year after year, more and more fresh fruit and vegetable production will be localized. More people will be able to eat fresh fruit and vegetables than ever before. And those people will be amazed at how great those fruit and vegetables taste."

This quote, attributed to Plenty's CEO Matt Barnard, captures the optimism driving the sector. Borlace uses it to underscore that the technology is maturing from a niche experiment to a mainstream solution backed by giants like Amazon and SoftBank. The narrative shifts from "can we do it?" to "how fast can we scale it?"

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest asset is his refusal to treat vertical farming as a silver bullet while simultaneously refusing to dismiss it as a pipe dream. He effectively balances the undeniable efficiency gains in water and land use against the formidable hurdles of energy consumption and upfront capital. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in the timeline; while the technology for wheat is promising, the commercial viability for staple crops remains theoretical. Readers should watch for the next wave of pilot projects in cereal production, as that will be the true test of whether this industry can feed the world or just its salads.
