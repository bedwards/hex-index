Dave Borlace dismantles a pervasive fear-mongering narrative with a single, data-driven insight: the electric vehicle revolution isn't draining the grid; it's the grid's most promising battery. While tabloids warn of societal collapse from charging cars, Borlace leans on a forensic 2023 research paper to argue that vehicle-to-grid technology could stabilize our energy future without destroying the very batteries powering it. This is not just optimism; it's a mathematical rebuttal to the idea that your car is a liability.

The Economics of Adoption

Borlace begins by grounding the conversation in hard numbers, noting that the cost of the technology has plummeted since the early days of the Nissan Leaf. He writes, "The average cost of EV batteries in 2023 is 138 dollars per kilowatt hour," a figure that has driven prices toward parity with internal combustion engines. This economic shift is the foundation of the entire argument; without it, the grid integration discussion is moot. The author effectively highlights that we have moved past the "early adopter" phase into mass adoption, a transition predicted by visionaries over a decade ago.

"We really are now at the Tipping Point of mass adoption for this historically disruptive technology."

The author's focus on the sheer volume of underutilized assets is compelling. He points out that statistically, these powerful battery packs spend "90 percent of their existence sitting doing nothing at all." This framing transforms the EV from a mere car into a dormant energy resource. Critics might argue that relying on consumer-owned assets for grid stability shifts the burden of infrastructure costs onto individuals, but Borlace counters this by suggesting that with the right incentives, it becomes a symbiotic relationship rather than a subsidy.

The Grid as a Partner, Not a Victim

The core of Borlace's coverage addresses the "thorny question" of battery degradation. Many owners fear that sending electrons back and forth will ruin their investment. Borlace presents a study that modeled every conceivable variable, from temperature to charging speed, and found a surprisingly low cost to the hardware. He notes that "just five percent of the theoretical available battery capacity is likely to be lost as a result of battery degradation by 2050." This finding is crucial because it directly contradicts the intuition that frequent cycling equals rapid failure.

"You're actually much more likely to degrade your EV battery quickly if you constantly use modern Ultra fast charges at Motorway charging stations than you would by slowly charging overnight at home."

This distinction is the piece's most valuable insight. Borlace reframes the user experience, suggesting that the "delusional mindset" of needing instant, high-speed charging is what actually harms the battery, not the gentle, smart charging required for grid support. The argument holds up well against the backdrop of modern lithium-ion chemistry, which is far more resilient than earlier iterations. However, the piece assumes a level of regulatory sophistication—smart charge controllers and micropayments—that may not exist in all jurisdictions yet. The technology is ready; the policy framework is still catching up.

The Second Life and the Grid's Capacity

Beyond the car's primary life, Borlace explores the "end-of-life repurposing" of batteries. Even when a battery drops to 80% capacity and is no longer ideal for driving, it remains a viable tool for stationary storage. The research suggests that by 2050, the global technical capacity of these batteries could reach "somewhere between 32 and 62 terawatt hours." To put that in perspective, Borlace notes this is "almost twice the total annual electricity consumption of Denmark."

"If that fairly modest average annual capacity increase continued from today onwards then it'll only take about six and a half years for there to be enough extra capacity on the US grid to enable every single one of America's 230 million licensed drivers to switch over to an electric vehicle."

This statistic, drawn from an analysis of historical grid growth, serves as a powerful antidote to the "grid meltdown" scare stories. It suggests that the grid is more adaptable than the doomsayers claim. The author's reliance on historical data to predict future capacity is a strong rhetorical move, though it assumes that infrastructure investment will remain consistent despite political and economic volatility. The argument that we are "right in the thick of it" regarding a technological revolution comparable to the iPhone launch feels earned, given the convergence of cost, capacity, and policy.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest contribution is the empirical debunking of the battery degradation myth, proving that grid integration is a net positive for both the owner and the system. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on perfect policy implementation and widespread adoption of smart charging infrastructure, which remains a significant hurdle in many regions. Readers should watch for how governments respond to the need for these specific regulations, as that will determine whether this theoretical win becomes a practical reality.

"The technological Revolution that's about to touch all our lives will be very similar to the one we experienced shortly after Steve Jobs held up an iPhone for the very first time."
