Dave Borlace doesn't just predict an electric future; he argues we are already living through a green Industrial Revolution as disruptive as the one that birthed the modern world a century ago. While skeptics fixate on range anxiety and grid capacity, Borlace marshals a staggering array of data showing that the infrastructure, economics, and vehicle choices have already tipped in favor of electrification. This is not a distant hope but a present reality, and the speed of the transition is the story's most compelling hook.

The Economic Tipping Point

Borlace anchors his optimism in hard numbers, noting that the global number of electric vehicles grew by 55% in 2017 alone. He points to China's dominance, where sales surged 73% in a single year, driven by a leader who is "an engineer by profession" and believes "wholeheartedly in the benefits of a rapid transition to clean energy." This focus on the manufacturing and policy engine in Asia is crucial; it suggests the revolution is being built from the ground up, not just demanded by consumers.

The author's most persuasive argument concerns cost. He highlights that the biggest expense in an electric car is the battery, yet prices are plummeting. Citing Josh Brewer of Flex Gen, Borlace writes, "the price of the batteries his company sources from China and Korea have come down by 20% just in the first six months of 2018." This rapid deflation is the key to mass adoption, rendering the technology affordable without relying solely on subsidies. As Borlace puts it, "Tesla have gone one step further by announcing that they'll be building one of their Giga factories in Shanghai... to make cars and batteries but direct sale into the Chinese market." This vertical integration and localization of supply chains effectively neutralize the threat of tariffs, a point often missed in Western political debates.

Critics might argue that raw material scarcity for lithium and cobalt could eventually stall this price decline, but the sheer scale of current investment suggests the market is racing to solve these bottlenecks before they become crises.

The Landscape of Choice

Moving beyond the luxury niche, Borlace dismantles the myth that electric vehicles are only for the wealthy or the tech-obsessed. He acknowledges Elon Musk as a "genius" who owns a "place at the top end of our market genius board," but quickly pivots to the broader market. He notes that "pretty much every car manufacturer has either already got an electric model in the range or is due to release one in the next few months." From the Jaguar I-Pace to the affordable Nissan Leaf, the spectrum is widening.

The author provides a granular look at the options, contrasting the $100,000 Tesla Model S P100D with the "decent sized hatchback" Nissan Leaf, which starts at £22,000 in the UK. He emphasizes that "there are now electric vehicles in every sector of the market from the top end luxury marks right down to basic entry-level vehicles." This variety is the death knell for the argument that consumers have no choice. Borlace's coverage is effective because it moves from the abstract concept of "green energy" to the concrete reality of a buyer's brochure, listing specific ranges, horsepower, and 0-60 times that rival traditional combustion engines.

"Given that fact the more important question at least in my view is how well developed is the infrastructure to support my potential new investment."

The Infrastructure Reality Check

Perhaps the most significant contribution of Borlace's piece is his detailed rebuttal to the fear that the power grid will collapse under the weight of millions of new cars. He tackles the "scarce stories" about power outages head-on, citing the UK National Grid's estimate that even if every car were electric by mid-century, the extra demand would only require 18 gigawatts on top of a 60-gigawatt peak. "The plans for new renewable energy resources will more than accommodate this uplift in requirement," he asserts.

Borlace reframes the electric vehicle not as a drain on the system, but as a solution. He explains that "electric vehicles will actually be beneficial to the smart grids of the future" because they function as "mobile energy storage facility." By charging during off-peak hours and potentially feeding power back during spikes, these cars stabilize the grid. This is a sophisticated argument that shifts the narrative from burden to asset. He also highlights the physical rollout of chargers, noting that BP plans to install "ultra fast charging points at every one of their existing 1200 petrol stations" in the UK, delivering "a hundred miles of range in ten minutes."

A counterargument worth considering is the equity of this transition; while urban dwellers may benefit from lamp-post charging, rural residents with older housing stock may face significant hurdles in accessing this infrastructure. Borlace touches on this with the mention of induction charging, but the timeline for widespread adoption remains uncertain.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's strongest move is reframing the electric vehicle not as a niche environmental experiment, but as an inevitable economic and industrial shift driven by falling costs and expanding infrastructure. His biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that renewable energy generation will scale perfectly in lockstep with vehicle adoption, a coordination challenge that history suggests is rarely seamless. The reader should watch not just for new car models, but for the speed at which grid modernization keeps pace with the surge in demand.

"We're very close to the point where range anxiety should no longer be an issue and charging your vehicle will be as convenient if not even more so than filling up a traditional internal combustion engine car with fuel today."

The transition is no longer a question of "if," but "how fast," and the data Borlace presents suggests the answer is faster than most realize.
