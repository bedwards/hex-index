Dave Borlace doesn't just ask if hydrogen fuel cells are the future; he dismantles the very premise that they can compete with lithium-ion batteries for personal transport, using a historical analogy that is as comforting as it is damning. By comparing the hydrogen debate to the VHS versus Betamax battle of the 1980s, Borlace suggests that technical superiority is irrelevant when infrastructure and market momentum favor a less efficient competitor. This is a crucial distinction for busy decision-makers: the technology isn't failing because it doesn't work, but because the ecosystem required to support it is fundamentally broken.

The Efficiency Trap

Borlace begins by acknowledging hydrogen's theoretical appeal, noting that "on the face of it hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe" and is packed with energy. However, he quickly pivots to the harsh reality of extraction. Whether through steam reforming, which "produces a load of pollution and greenhouse gas," or electrolysis, which loses "about 30% of the energy you've put into the reaction," the process is inherently wasteful. The author's math is unforgiving: by the time hydrogen is produced, compressed, transported, and converted back into electricity in a car, the system operates at roughly 35% efficiency. In stark contrast, lithium-ion batteries achieve 75% efficiency. This gap isn't a minor inconvenience; it is a structural barrier that makes hydrogen economically unviable for mass adoption.

"By the time you get to the end user you've lost between 30 and 60 percent of the energy compared to only about 6 percent of the energy with a lithium-ion battery."

Critics might argue that this efficiency calculation assumes a static grid and ignores potential breakthroughs in renewable energy generation or storage density. Yet, Borlace's point holds weight because it focuses on the current physics of the supply chain, not hypothetical future miracles. The energy lost in compression and transport is a hard physical cost that no amount of marketing can easily erase.

The Infrastructure Moat

The argument shifts from thermodynamics to logistics, where the gap widens further. Borlace highlights that building a hydrogen network is prohibitively expensive, requiring either massive central plants with long-distance transport or inefficient small-scale production at every station. He contrasts this with the rapidly expanding battery charging network, which is "already pretty well-established and rapidly becoming almost as ubiquitous as traditional fossil fuel filling stations." The author points out that charging times are dropping, with "a hundred miles of range can already be added in only 10 minutes," effectively neutralizing the traditional speed advantage of hydrogen refueling.

The cultural factor is equally significant. Borlace notes the influence of "master marketing geniuses like Elon Musk" who have made battery vehicles desirable while publicly dismissing hydrogen as "very silly indeed." This creates a self-fulfilling prophecy where investment flows to batteries, improving them further, while hydrogen struggles to gain traction. The market isn't just choosing the better technology; it is choosing the one with the path of least resistance.

"Ultimately though for us car driving road users it'll most likely be the level of market penetration rather than any comparative technical superiority that determines the winner in this battle for consumer electric vehicle power domination."

Where Hydrogen Actually Fits

Despite the bleak outlook for personal cars, Borlace identifies a specific, high-value niche where hydrogen thrives: heavy industry and fleet operations. He argues that the technology is ideal for vehicles that "start and finish at a single Depot," such as buses, delivery lorries, and maritime ferries. In these scenarios, the infrastructure cost is contained, and the ability to refuel quickly allows for continuous operation without the downtime required for battery charging. He cites the world's first hydrogen-powered train in Germany and experimental hydrogen aircraft as proof that the technology is not dead, just misplaced for the average commuter.

The author's analysis of the Japanese market adds nuance. Despite Japan's aggressive targets and Toyota's investment in the Mirai, the vehicle remains a niche product, with the company expecting to sell primarily to fleet markets to "grow the refueling infrastructure" before prices become attainable for the average owner. This admission underscores the chicken-and-egg problem that plagues hydrogen: you need cars to justify stations, but you need stations to sell cars.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace's commentary is a masterclass in separating hype from physics, demonstrating that while hydrogen fuel cells are a viable solution for heavy transport and maritime applications, they are unlikely to dethrone lithium-ion batteries in the passenger car market. The argument's greatest strength is its reliance on hard efficiency data rather than speculative future tech, though it may slightly underestimate the speed of infrastructure development in specific regions like Japan. For the smart, busy reader, the takeaway is clear: watch hydrogen for trucks and trains, but bet on batteries for your next car.
