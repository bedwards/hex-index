Dave Borlace doesn't just explain how ground source heat pumps work; he dismantles the myth that they are a niche, prohibitively expensive luxury for the wealthy. In a landscape often dominated by solar panels and wind turbines, Borlace argues that the most reliable, high-yield renewable energy source is already beneath our feet, waiting to be tapped with a technology that has been quietly maturing for decades. The urgency here is palpable: with global heating costs soaring and carbon targets looming, the author presents a solution that offers immediate, tangible savings while tackling the elephant in the room—building emissions.

The Physics of Stability

Borlace anchors his argument in a fundamental physical truth that often escapes public discourse: the ground temperature is remarkably stable compared to the volatile air above it. "The temperature of the ground directly below us is far more stable than the temperature of the air around us which is subject to all the vagaries of the ever more extreme weather conditions," he writes. This stability is the engine of the system's efficiency. Unlike air-source heat pumps that struggle when the mercury drops, a ground source system leverages the earth's consistent warmth to heat homes in winter and its coolness to chill them in summer.

The mechanism Borlace describes is elegant in its simplicity, relying on a closed-loop cycle of fluid and phase changes. A mixture of water and antifreeze circulates through pipes buried underground, absorbing low-grade heat before transferring it to a secondary fluid with a low boiling point. This fluid vaporizes, gets compressed to raise its temperature further, and then dumps that heat into the home's heating system. "For each kilowatt of energy consumed by the system four kilowatts of heat energy are generated effectively reducing the cost per kilowatt hour by 75 percent," Borlace notes. This ratio is the killer app for the technology. Critics might note that the initial drilling and installation costs are significant, but the author counters this by highlighting the long-term operational savings that far outpace traditional fossil fuel systems.

The Global Momentum and the Grid Challenge

The narrative shifts from physics to economics as Borlace points to a rapidly accelerating global market. He cites data showing that nearly 20 million households purchased heat pumps in 2019 alone, with sales projected to exceed 68 billion dollars by 2026. This isn't just a theoretical green dream; it's a booming industry. Borlace highlights Sweden as a prime example of mass adoption, where over 300,000 domestic systems are now in use, often proving cheaper than district heating networks.

However, the author doesn't shy away from the technical hurdles that came with early adoption. In Sweden, the combination of heat pumps and backup electric boilers created massive spikes in grid demand during extreme cold snaps. Borlace explains that early systems were dimensioned to handle only about half the peak load, forcing the backup boilers to kick in and strain the national grid. "New heat pump systems are now fitted with very smart inverter technology which can handle full power heating demand," he writes. This technological evolution is crucial; it transforms the heat pump from a potential grid liability into a stable, efficient asset that reduces marginal electricity demand. The argument here is compelling because it acknowledges past failures and shows how engineering has solved them, rather than ignoring the complexity of integrating renewables into existing infrastructure.

The Policy Paralysis

Despite the clear technical and economic benefits, Borlace identifies a frustrating bottleneck: government policy. He argues that while the technology is mature, political will has lagged behind, often stifled by the influence of fossil fuel lobbies. "The power of the fossil fuel lobby is still extremely strong despite the economic kicking they've endured during the lockdown period globally that industry still receives hundreds of billions of dollars in subsidies and tax breaks," Borlace asserts. This creates an uneven playing field where renewable alternatives struggle to compete on price without significant state intervention.

He uses the United Kingdom as a case study for this policy gap. While the UK introduced the Green Homes Grant, Borlace points out its limitations: a cap of 5,000 pounds that barely scratches the surface of a 27,000-pound installation cost. He breaks down the math for a typical Victorian home, showing that while the Domestic Renewable Heat Incentive (RHI) can earn owners 4,000 pounds a year for seven years, the upfront capital requirement remains a massive barrier for most families. "The obstacle of course is that the homeowner has to pay for the system up front and claim back the incentives afterwards and not many of us have got the best part of 30 grand kicking around for a rainy day," he observes. This is the crux of the problem: the technology works, the economics work over time, but the cash flow model is broken for the average consumer.

Bottom Line

Dave Borlace makes a robust case that ground source heat pumps are the most underutilized tool in the decarbonization toolkit, offering superior efficiency and stability compared to air-based systems. His strongest evidence lies in the proven physics of the technology and the real-world success stories from Sweden, which prove that mass adoption is possible. However, the argument's biggest vulnerability is the reliance on government subsidies to bridge the upfront cost gap; without a radical shift in policy to lower entry barriers, the technology will remain out of reach for the very households that need it most. The path to net zero may well be paved with pipes buried in our backyards, but only if leaders can overcome the inertia of the status quo.
