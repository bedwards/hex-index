This "Links for April 2026" post from Scott Alexander functions less as a simple digest and more as a diagnostic tool for a world fracturing under the weight of artificial intelligence, institutional rigidity, and epistemic uncertainty. Alexander's most striking move is his refusal to treat the rise of AI as a singular technological event, instead weaving it through the fabric of labor law, mental health, and the very definition of truth. He suggests that the "technological singularity" has already arrived, not as a moment of explosion, but as a paralyzing fog where "questions about basically every medium-run policy debate collapse into arguments about the future trajectory of A.I."

The Architecture of Belief and Suffering

Alexander begins by dissecting the mechanics of how we accept reality, using Ursula K. Le Guin's The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas as a lens. He argues that the story is not a simple trolley problem but a "critique of its reader." As Alexander puts it, the narrative relies on a specific psychological pivot: "Do you believe? Do you accept the festival, the city, the joy? No? Then let me describe one more thing." He notes that this line splits the story, introducing a "ruined child, Atlasian in their suffering," which makes the utopia suddenly credible to the audience.

"It's not a literary treatment of the trolley problem; it's a critique of our inability to imagine radical goodness without tempering it with deep horrible and inhuman trade-offs."

This framing is potent because it shifts the blame from the fictional city to the reader's own ontology. Alexander suggests that walking away from Omelas is less about moral purity and more about rejecting a system where "goodness possible only if it's enabled by subterranean suffering." This connects surprisingly well to his later observations on the legal system and sports arbitration. Just as the Court of Arbitration for Sport relies on a specific, often opaque, set of rules that can be appealed to the Swiss Supreme Court, our social contracts often rely on hidden costs we agree to ignore. The historical parallel here is sharp: just as the Court of Arbitration for Sport was established in 1984 to handle international disputes, modern society is increasingly outsourcing its moral arbitration to algorithms and automated systems that lack human review.

The Institutional Squeeze

The post turns its gaze toward the machinery of governance and the labor market, where the tension between efficiency and human dignity is palpable. Alexander highlights a shift in the executive branch, noting that Jim O'Neill, described as "among the most good-parts-of-Silicon-Valley value-aligned people in the current administration," has moved from the Department of Health and Human Services to the National Science Foundation. The author admits uncertainty about whether this is a promotion or demotion but emphasizes the stakes: "he's read all of your Here's How To Fix Science Substack posts, so I hope you got them right."

This transition underscores a broader trend Alexander identifies in Nicholas Weininger's analysis of government outsourcing. The argument is stark: "for any given amount of services you want to provide, the [non-government] nonprofit is going to be way cheaper because their employees aren't subject to public-sector union contract rules." Alexander paraphrases this as a system where the state can "hire and fire them at will," creating a two-tiered labor market.

"You can pay them a lot less, and maybe even more importantly, you can hire and fire them at will."

Critics might note that this efficiency comes at the cost of institutional memory and worker stability, potentially leading to the very "insider-outsider dynamics" Alexander later identifies in European immigration policy. He cites Kelsey Piper and Alexander Kustov, who argue that rigid employment protections in Europe "create insider-outsider dynamics that hit newcomers hardest." This structural rigidity mirrors the instability of the Roman Empire, where, as Alexander notes, "low elite fertility prevented the institutionalization of hereditary monarchy," leaving a vacuum that led to chaos. The lesson from 1st-century Rome seems to echo in 2026: systems that cannot adapt their leadership or labor structures to changing demographics are prone to collapse.

The AI Paradox: Capability, Safety, and the Unknown

The most extensive thread in Alexander's curation is the paradox of artificial intelligence. On one hand, we have tools that can "build [an object] for you in Minecraft" or translate text into "medievaldonaldtrump" style with ease. On the other, the technology creates a paralysis in policy-making. Alexander writes, "Questions about basically every medium-run policy debate collapse into arguments about the future trajectory of A.I."

He explores the safety implications with a sobering eye. While some fear AI will aid bioterrorism, experiments suggest "current AI was not very good at helping a bioterrorism red team." However, the risk of automated censorship remains high. Alexander revisits a 2025 concern about the National Science Foundation potentially canceling grants based on "automated keyword search for left-wing or social-science terms." He reports anecdotes of research on "inequality," "polarization," and "diversity" being flagged without human review.

"All concepts become unmanageable when you zoom in too far and prudence tells us how far we should zoom at any given moment."

This observation, drawn from a discussion on crime statistics, serves as a meta-commentary on the AI safety debate itself. The attempt to define and regulate every potential harm with perfect precision may be as futile as trying to count every individual crime committed by a complex entity like a corporation. Alexander also touches on the geopolitical dimension, noting that China seems to be "souring on giving the rest of us cool stuff for free" regarding open-source AI, while internal power struggles favor engineers with US experience over native talent.

"The US is indeed doing well, but the rest of the world is doing even better! What's going on? It looks like the answer isn't so much that foreign economies are booming, as that a weaker dollar and US uncertainty are causing Americans to invest in foreign stocks."

This economic volatility, linked to policy chaos, creates a feedback loop where the uncertainty of the future makes present action difficult. Alexander notes that even Matt Yglesias is experiencing "writers' block" because the AI trajectory is unknowable. The "technological singularity," he reminds us, was originally defined as "a point so transformative that it's pointless to speculate about what happens afterwards."

The Human Element in a Digital Age

Amidst the macro-analysis, Alexander returns to the micro-level of human health and connection. He discusses the efficacy of Black doctors for Black patients, citing literature that shows the benefit is not necessarily due to white doctors mistreating patients, but rather that "black patients are less likely to seek help from / cooperate with white doctors." He warns that "exaggerated claims about how white doctors mistreat black patients can really do a lot of harm" by discouraging care.

Similarly, he questions the future of therapy in an age of AI. Josh Lipson's argument is that "even if it's good, it may be counterproductive" because the limited nature of human therapy is part of its benefit. "Spending one hour per week processing your life is different from having 'reassurance on demand'," Alexander writes, suggesting that constant access could "prevent patients' independent character development."

"One of the biggest political stories of the year that nobody realizes is that [Speaker] Mike Johnson has lost control of the house through discharge petitions."

This loss of control in the legislative branch parallels the loss of control in the AI sector, where the pace of change outstrips the ability of institutions to govern. Whether it is the "hut with chicken legs" of Baba Yaga derived from Sami storehouses, or the rare phenomenon of superfetation where a woman becomes pregnant while already pregnant, Alexander uses these oddities to remind us that the world is stranger and more complex than our models allow.

Bottom Line

Scott Alexander's curation succeeds by refusing to separate the technological from the human, showing how AI, policy, and ancient folklore all intersect in a single, unstable moment. The strongest part of his argument is the identification of "epistemic paralysis"—the inability to make decisions because the future is too uncertain to model. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the sheer speed at which the landscape is shifting; the anecdotes of automated grant cancellations and the collapse of open-source AI in China suggest that the "prudence" he advocates for may be impossible to maintain in a system designed for rapid, automated iteration. Readers should watch closely for how the "insider-outsider" dynamics in labor and immigration will play out as the AI revolution accelerates, potentially rendering traditional institutional safeguards obsolete.
