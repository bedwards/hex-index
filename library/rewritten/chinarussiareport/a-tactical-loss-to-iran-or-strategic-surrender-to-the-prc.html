Joseph Webster delivers a jarring reality check: the most dangerous path forward for the United States in the Middle East is not inaction, but a full-scale invasion that would inadvertently hand global hegemony to Beijing. While the political instinct is to demand victory, Webster argues that the military math of Iran makes "winning" a strategic suicide pact.

The Illusion of Tactical Victory

Webster cuts through the noise of daily skirmishes to expose a grim strategic reality. He leans heavily on the analysis of Danny Citrinowicz, former head of the Iran Division in the IDF Military Intelligence Directorate, to frame the dilemma. "The military campaign is not toppling the regime but reinforcing it," Citrinowicz argues, a point Webster uses to dismantle the idea that current skirmishes are accumulating value. The author suggests that the United States is currently scoring "one tactical victory after another, but these are not accumulating to any strategic value." This distinction is crucial for busy leaders who might mistake noise for progress.

The core of the argument rests on the geography and the nature of the threat. Webster notes that short of a massive escalation, there is no way to open the Strait of Hormuz, a chokepoint where Iranian drones and missiles already threaten unarmed merchant vessels. He points out that even a convoy system, often proposed as a middle ground, is fraught with peril. "Merchant vessels traveling in a convoy are considered legitimate military targets, complicating risk insurance," he writes, referencing the complex web of war risk insurance that would collapse under the weight of such a scenario. Furthermore, he reminds us that if Iran were to mine the strait en masse, clearing it would take months, and the only way to prevent re-mining is to control a coastline larger than Texas. This is not a police action; it is a logistical nightmare.

"Iran is a natural fort, a defender's paradise."

Webster's assessment of the terrain is particularly stark. He contrasts the flat desert of Iraq with the rugged Zagros and Alborz mountain ranges of Iran, noting that the latter is "a defender's paradise." To secure the region, he calculates that the U.S. would need "several hundred thousand troops for at least 5 years," a force size that dwarfs the peak of the Iraq War. Critics might argue that modern technology renders such massive troop deployments less necessary, but Webster counters that controlling the interior to stop drone attacks requires a physical presence that technology alone cannot provide.

The Cost of Escalation

The author does not shy away from the human and financial toll of escalation. He paints a picture of a "fractious coalition" where partners like Turkey and Pakistan would have conflicting aims, asking, "Would Turkish ground forces cooperate with Kurdish rebels on the ground in Iran?" The potential for domestic fallout is equally dire. Webster speculates on the risk of "illegals" or deep-cover assets targeting critical infrastructure in the U.S., citing the recent FBI charges against Chinese nationals at MacDill Air Force Base as a warning sign. "The gutting of the FBI... may prove disastrous," he warns, suggesting that the U.S. is ill-equipped to handle the asymmetric retaliation that would follow an invasion.

The argument here is that the U.S. is not prepared for a conflict of this duration or severity. An "improvised, snap, full-scale invasion" would be a "messy undertaking" that neither the Trump nor Netanyahu administrations are ready to manage. This is a sobering check on the hawkish impulse to "quadruple down."

The Beijing Windfall

Perhaps the most provocative part of Webster's commentary is the geopolitical payoff for China. He argues that even if Beijing does not directly fight, a protracted U.S. war in Iran would be a strategic victory for the People's Republic of China. "The PRC will reap enormous military rewards from a large-scale U.S.-led invasion of Iran," he writes, noting that the U.S. would be forced to re-orient its military away from high-end conflicts in the Indo-Pacific to counterinsurgency in the Middle East.

Webster highlights that while the Middle East conflict holds economic risks for China, its energy security and domestic production capabilities make it better positioned to weather the storm than its rivals in East Asia. "Beijing would very likely become the world's dominant power," he concludes, suggesting that a U.S. invasion would leave it "too exhausted to contest a takeover of Taiwan." This connects the dots between a regional flare-up and a global power shift, a narrative often missed in isolationist or regional-focused reporting. A counterargument worth considering is that China's economy is deeply integrated with the West and might suffer more from a global energy shock than Webster anticipates, but his point about the opportunity cost of U.S. military distraction remains potent.

"A tactical defeat in Iran will be ugly and risky, but it is far less dangerous than the alternative of strategic surrender to Beijing."

Bottom Line

Webster's strongest contribution is reframing "capitulation" not as a failure of will, but as a necessary act of strategic preservation against a greater threat. His argument is vulnerable to the political impossibility of accepting a "tactical defeat" in the current U.S. climate, yet the military and economic logic he presents is difficult to refute. Readers should watch for how the administration balances the domestic pressure to act against the sobering reality that escalation could accelerate the very outcome it seeks to prevent.
