This piece delivers a stinging diagnosis of a decade-long strategic failure: the American left didn't just fail to win; it abandoned the very class it claimed to champion. The author argues that the choice between identity and economics wasn't a false dichotomy, but a real trade-off that progressives lost, leaving them with a coalition of credentialed professionals while the working class drifted away. In an era where the top 10 percent controls nearly half of all consumer spending, this analysis forces a reckoning with why the left's current language feels so alien to the people it hopes to mobilize.

The False Promise of "Both/And"

The narrative begins in a 2016 bookstore at the University of Chicago, where Adolph Reed Jr. and Walter Benn Michaels warned that the left could not simultaneously prioritize race and class without sacrificing the latter. The author recalls the atmosphere: "a room dense with graduate students, academics, and a few journalists, the kind of crowd that fancies itself the intellectual vanguard of a revolution that never quite arrives." This setting perfectly encapsulates the disconnect the piece critiques. The core thesis, drawn from Michaels' 2006 polemic, is that emphasizing the suffering of specific groups often results in "a politics that accepts inequality as long as it is properly distributed: a rainbow-hued C-suite presiding over a Dickensian shop floor."

The author suggests this was not merely academic hair-splitting but a preview of the 2016 Democratic primary. Bernie Sanders entered the race as the closest thing to a class-first candidate since the late Jesse Jackson's Rainbow Coalition in the 1980s, arguing that wealth inequality was the root of all social ills. However, the activist class and media pushed back. When Black Lives Matter protesters confronted Sanders, demanding he speak to anti-black violence, he responded by talking about transforming the economy. The author notes that Clinton saw an opening, inverting Sanders' logic: "Income inequality, in her framing, was 'in large measure a symptom of underlying racism.'" This shift was decisive. As the author puts it, "When Sanders lost to Clinton, it wasn't merely a political defeat. The war of ideas had been won, and it foreclosed certain questions before they could be seriously asked."

Critics might argue that Sanders' failure was due to his own inability to articulate a compelling racial narrative rather than the inherent incompatibility of the two frameworks. Yet, the text suggests the institutional machinery of the party had already decided that class was a distraction.

Diversity is perfectly compatible with plutocracy.

The Professional-Managerial Class Ascendant

Following the 2016 election, the author argues that the "anti-Trump #Resistance" became an online-driven movement of credentialed professionals whose central question was not "who owns what" but "who is being harmed, erased, or insufficiently recognized." The economy that mattered most became the "attention economy." Even critiques of capitalism were reframed through the lens of corporate HR and therapeutic harm reduction. The critique shifted from the shredding of the welfare state to the fact that elites were "too white, too male, too straight, too culturally insensitive, and insufficiently 'aware of their privilege.'"

The piece illustrates this with a personal anecdote: after an interview with Walter Benn Michaels, the author faced a revolt among the Chicago Reader's staff, leading to a bogus #MeToo claim and suspension. The editor's justification—"In this political climate, we have to"—signaled that diversity was sacrosanct, even at the cost of due process. This dynamic played out nationally when Reed's insistence that the disproportionate effects of the pandemic on Black Americans were tied to poverty led to his cancellation by the New York City DSA. The author quotes Reed's blunt assessment of the backlash: "The DSA thing... that was a bunch of stupid kids."

The triumph of this worldview culminated in 2020. After the killing of George Floyd, corporate America embraced the movement not with wealth redistribution, but with DEI departments and reading lists. The author contrasts Nancy Isenberg's White Trash, a history of class stratification that was a "brief blip," with The 1619 Project, which received the institutional backing of The New York Times and a Pulitzer. The result was a reorganization of American historical understanding that centered race to the exclusion of class.

The Electoral Reckoning

The 2024 election served as the final, crushing irony. The coalition built on the premise that young, non-white voters would be a bulwark against "fascism" fractured along the diploma divide. Highly educated whites voted more Democratic than ever, while the multi-racial working class defected. The author observes that "despite all the efforts to make it go away, class reasserted itself." The left is now perceived as a vehicle for the interests of highly educated progressives who view the country as a "global service provider" while regarding the actual inhabitants with "pity and suspicion."

The outlook for organized labor, once the potential savior of this dynamic, is grim. A writer for Jacobin recently described unions as being in a "death spiral," with private-sector density down to 5.9 percent. The author notes that without radical change, the labor movement could "effectively cease to matter." The tragedy, the piece argues, is that the left "did not build the institutions it needed to matter in 2026."

There are, however, small signs of a correction. The affordability politics of New York City mayor Zohran Mamdani offer a potential path forward, focusing on rent, transit, and wages. Yet, the author points out the lingering pieties: Mamdani also established the first Mayor's Office of LGBTQIA+ Affairs and posted on Trans Day of Visibility that trans lives are "not a political issue" while making it clear they actually are. The author concludes that these are "exactly the kind of pieties that the professional activist class requires as the price of coalition membership."

The American left kept insisting that it could center both, and what it actually centered was neither.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its unflinching diagnosis of how the professional-managerial class co-opted progressive language to serve its own cultural interests while ignoring material decay. Its biggest vulnerability is the risk of dismissing the very real harms of racism as mere distractions from class, a tension the author acknowledges but ultimately resolves by siding with the materialists. The reader should watch whether the next wave of left-wing leadership can actually break the "pieties" of the activist class or if they will remain trapped in the same cycle of symbolic victory and material defeat.

My current view is fuck 'em all.
