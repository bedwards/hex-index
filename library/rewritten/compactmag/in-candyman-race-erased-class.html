This piece cuts through the noise of modern cultural debates by asking a question few dare to voice: what happens to the suffering of the white working class when it is forced into a narrative framework that only recognizes racial hierarchy? The author, writing as Various, argues that the horror in the film Candyman is not just about racial terror, but about a specific kind of abandonment that occurs when a community is severed from its history and its labor. By contrasting the American adaptation with Clive Barker's original British short story, the commentary exposes how a singular focus on race can inadvertently erase the class-based trauma of post-industrial decline.

The Horror of Abandonment

The author's central thesis rests on a striking comparison between two versions of the same monster. While the 1992 film and its 2021 remake anchor the story in the racial history of Chicago's Cabrini-Green projects, the original 1985 text by Barker places the horror in a Liverpool council estate inhabited by poor whites. Various writes, "Here the horror is not racism, but abandonment." This distinction is crucial because it shifts the blame from a specific group of oppressors to a systemic failure of the state and the economy.

The argument suggests that when the setting moves from the American South Side to the British Merseyside, the nature of the suffering changes fundamentally. In the original story, the residents are "poor white folk: single mothers, old women, pensioned men who are no longer able to defend themselves." They are not victims of racial terror, but of "class shame"—people taught to believe their destitution is a personal moral failing. This reframing is powerful because it challenges the reader to consider how different forms of marginalization are prioritized in public discourse.

"They learned to read their failure as personal and individual."

The author draws on their own experience as a sociologist in Liverpool to illustrate this point. They describe a community where "post-industrial Britain produced a population severed from work, from role, from mutual recognition." The commentary effectively uses the figure of Candyman not just as a ghost, but as a symbol of the hopelessness that takes root when a society tells its most vulnerable members that their struggles are invisible.

Critics might argue that separating class and race in this way creates a false dichotomy, ignoring how racial identity and economic status often overlap. However, the author's point is not to deny the reality of racism, but to highlight the danger of a monocausal explanation that crowds out other forms of suffering.

The Erasure of Local History

The piece takes a sharp turn into institutional critique, examining how universities and museums in Liverpool have handled the city's complex history. The author recounts a personal anecdote about a public discussion where a speaker focused exclusively on the city's links to transatlantic slavery, ignoring the lived realities of the older, white working-class attendees. Various notes, "When those who wield this story invoke 'white privilege' as a mystical essence possessed even by the city's poorest pensioners... they make it harder, not easier, to see who is actually suffering."

This section is particularly compelling because it moves from theory to the visceral experience of exclusion. The author describes the attendees as "huddled in thin anoraks, their shopping bags against their knees," feeling silenced by a narrative that recasts them as oppressors rather than victims of economic collapse. The argument is that by making race the sole "moral category," institutions risk alienating the very people they claim to represent.

The commentary also addresses the renaming of buildings, specifically the transition from "Gladstone Hall" to "Dorothy Kuya Hall." While acknowledging the historical ties of the Gladstone family to slavery, the author laments the erasure of the nuance surrounding figures like William Gladstone, who was also a champion of equality before the law for many. The text states, "Replacing the name of Gladstone with that of Dorothy Kuya... was not a cost-free choice."

"When a society comes to see itself as a source of injustice, it teaches its own dispossessed citizens that their suffering is illegitimate."

This is a bold claim, suggesting that the current approach to historical memory can be counterproductive, fostering resentment rather than understanding. The author argues that the "International Slavery Museum" and similar institutions often present a "monocausal history" that downplays Britain's role in abolishing the trade and ignores the suffering of other marginalized groups, such as the Irish famine migrants or the white poor.

A counterargument worth considering is that the focus on slavery is a necessary correction to centuries of historical silence, and that expanding the narrative too broadly might dilute the urgency of addressing racial injustice. Yet, the author's concern remains that a rigid framework can become a new form of dogma that fails to account for the full spectrum of human experience.

The Ghost in the Room

The conclusion of the piece returns to the metaphor of Candyman, suggesting that when history is simplified, the monster returns. The author warns that "Other histories—of industrial injury, unemployment, community collapse, elite betrayal, post-industrial meaninglessness—are crowded out." The fear is that by refusing to acknowledge the specific trauma of the white working class, society leaves them vulnerable to the very forces of despair that the horror genre seeks to expose.

The author's personal reflection on their own family history adds a layer of poignancy to this argument. They describe the difficulty of tracing their lineage beyond their grandmother, whose family "never owned property" and whose street was "rebuilt as part of slum clearance." This lack of recorded history is presented as a form of erasure that parallels the institutional silencing discussed earlier.

"The great mass of the poor, the marginal, the vulnerable of Liverpool disappear."

The commentary ends with a haunting image: Candyman waiting in the dark, whispering, "Don't fight it... You lose: again." This serves as a warning against the dangers of a single-story narrative. It suggests that when institutions fail to recognize the full complexity of suffering, they inadvertently empower the forces of hopelessness.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its ability to articulate a specific, often unspoken form of alienation that arises when class-based suffering is subsumed by a rigid racial framework. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the risk of appearing to minimize the ongoing realities of racial injustice in the process of defending the working class. Readers should watch for how this tension plays out in future debates over historical memory and social policy, particularly in post-industrial cities where the scars of economic decline run deep.
