This piece delivers a startling diagnosis: Europe's liberal establishment has not just conceded ground to populists, it has adopted their playbook. The author, writing under the byline "Various," argues that the old guard—leaders like Friedrich Merz, Keir Starmer, and Emmanuel Macron—have abandoned the dream of expanding liberal values to instead build a "Fortress Liberalism" designed purely for survival. This is not a story of ideological drift, but of a strategic pivot where the very institutions meant to protect openness are now the primary architects of restriction.

The Siege Mentality

The core of the argument is that European liberals now view the world through a lens of existential threat, forcing them to become the very thing they once opposed. "In Europe's capitals, partisans of the old order... have woken up to a world they did not prepare for," the author writes. This framing is powerful because it shifts the blame from a lack of vision to a sudden, overwhelming convergence of crises: Russian aggression, American unreliability, and a backlash against mass migration.

The text suggests that these leaders have concluded that "this is not a time to extend liberalism—not to the foreign adversary, not to the needy of the developing world, and not even to the large parts of their own population that vote for the populist right." This is a grim admission. It implies that the liberal project is no longer about universal application but about containment. The author notes that this has turned liberals into "immigration restrictionists" and "security hawks," justifying muscular tactics to counter what they see as anti-democratic threats. This aligns with the historical concept of "militant democracy," a term coined by Karl Loewenstein, which the piece invokes to describe the resort to "muscular tactics to counter what they regard as anti-democratic threats."

Critics might argue that this analysis overstates the agency of liberal leaders, suggesting they are reacting to forces beyond their control rather than making calculated political choices. However, the evidence provided regarding the shift in rhetoric is compelling.

Today, European liberalism is a liberalism of firewalls, militancy, restrictions, defense, and pragmatism. It is a project that can be preserved for its remaining adherents but has, at least for the time being, given up dreams of expansion.

The Migration Pivot

The article identifies immigration as the "single biggest driver of change," noting that the political landscape has shifted dramatically since the 2009 European Parliament elections, when the center-right and center-left combined held 61 percent of seats. By 2024, the forces to the right of the European People's Party had more than doubled their share, capturing 26 percent of voting seats, with polls suggesting they could soon reach one-third. This statistical backdrop explains why the "center-right and center-left have reacted by converging into an uncomfortable ruling coalition."

The author details how the rhetoric of a "people and continent under siege" has become mainstream. "Conspicuous displays of openness—Merkel embracing millions of Syrian refugees and declaring 'wir schaffen das' ('we can do this')—allowed the populist right to depict mainstream parties as more committed to a humanitarian ideology than to addressing voters' anxieties." In response, leaders like Denmark's Mette Frederiksen have embraced a "zero refugee" policy, becoming the "first conspicuous exemplar of Fortress Liberalism."

The piece highlights a crucial nuance: these policies are often sold by politicians with immigrant backgrounds to avoid accusations of racism. "Starmer's crackdown is being led by Pakistani-British Shabana Mahmood, and Frederiksen's was led by Ethiopian-Danish Matias Tesfaye." This strategy has allowed liberals to "stanched the bleeding for some liberal parties," yet it comes at the cost of the values they claim to defend. The author points out that "liberal parties have designated politicians with immigrant backgrounds as spokespeople for restrictionist policies," a move that underscores the desperation of the political moment.

The Security Imperative

Beyond borders, the article argues that the security landscape has fundamentally altered the European psyche. The author reminds us that "just over a decade ago, NATO held joint fighter jet exercises with Russia... and the European Union spent 1.3 percent of its combined GDP on defense." That era of optimism is gone, replaced by a reality where "Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022 returned war to the European continent."

The text notes that the return of Donald Trump to the White House has compounded this fear, leaving Europe "beleaguered from another direction." The author describes how the "Oval Office confrontation with Ukraine's Volodymyr Zelensky" and the subsequent "Greenland saga" brought European politicians to "new heights of shock and fear." This has led to a consensus on defense spending that was unthinkable a decade ago. "NATO Secretary-General Mark Rutte typifies the transformation," having pushed for a commitment of 5 percent of GDP on defense, far exceeding the previous 2 percent target.

The world has not yet known a liberal Europe that has no Uncle Sam to lean on.

This sentence captures the profound anxiety at the heart of the piece. The author argues that Europe is now trying to "reinvent itself" in a world where it must stand alone against Russia, compete with China, and confront a hostile United States. The result is a political environment where "attempts at persuasion have given way to forceful tactics aimed at preventing populists from taking power."

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its unflinching description of how liberal leaders have internalized the logic of their opponents to survive, effectively creating a "Fortress Liberalism" that prioritizes security and restriction over expansion and openness. Its biggest vulnerability lies in assuming this defensive posture is sustainable; history suggests that a democracy that spends its energy building walls rather than bridging divides may eventually find itself isolated from the very people it seeks to protect. The reader should watch to see if this "siege mentality" solidifies into a permanent political order or collapses under the weight of its own contradictions.
