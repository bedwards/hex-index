Sabine Hossenfelder takes a rare turn, spotlighting a Nobel laureate who believes the very foundation of modern physics is built on a logical fallacy. Gerard 't Hooft, a giant in particle physics, argues that quantum mechanics is merely a statistical approximation of a deeper, deterministic reality, a claim that has earned him near-total dismissal from his peers. This is not just academic nitpicking; 't Hooft's "superdeterminism" suggests that the universe is a giant, pre-written script, implying that the randomness we observe is an illusion born of our ignorance, not a fundamental feature of nature.

The Death of Free Will as a Physical Necessity

The core of 't Hooft's argument rests on a radical rejection of "free will" in the context of scientific experimentation. Hossenfelder explains that 't Hooft wants to improve quantum physics by "doing away with the collapse of the wave function" and restoring determinism. In this view, the universe operates like a dice roll that has already happened; we just don't know the initial conditions. "Like it or not, the experimental actions are determined by laws of physics," 't Hooft writes, noting that their decisions "logically have their roots in the distant past going back all the way to the big bang."

This is a bold move. By linking the experimenter's choice to the initial state of the universe, 't Hooft attempts to bypass Bell's theorem, the famous proof that usually rules out local, deterministic theories. Hossenfelder notes that 't Hooft argues the problem lies in the extra assumptions of Bell's theorem, specifically the idea that experimenters can freely choose what to measure. If the choice of measurement and the state of the particle are correlated from the beginning of time, the theorem's inequality is violated without requiring spooky action at a distance. Hossenfelder summarizes this counterintuitive stance: "It's not a causation. It's not that the wave function of an electron makes a particle physicist turn a knob. It's just that the laws of nature are so that the two things must always fit together."

Critics might note that this "superdeterminism" feels like a conspiracy theory of physics, rendering scientific inquiry moot if the outcome is pre-ordained to match the setup. However, Hossenfelder points out that 't Hooft is unapologetic, viewing the correlation not as a flaw but as a necessary feature of a consistent theory.

The notion of free will must be replaced with a notion that a useful model of nature should give correct predictions.

The Cellular Automaton and the Limits of Computation

To give this determinism a physical mechanism, 't Hooft proposes that the universe is fundamentally a "cellular automaton." Hossenfelder describes these as "compact or localized object[s] that proceed in discrete time steps," interacting only with their neighbors. Imagine the universe as a grid of pixels, each the size of the Planck length, evolving in discrete ticks. This framework suggests that particles are merely emergent patterns, like gears in a machine, rather than fundamental wave functions.

This has profound implications for the future of technology. If the universe is a cellular automaton, the number of possible states is finite and far smaller than standard quantum mechanics predicts. Consequently, 't Hooft predicts a hard limit on quantum computing power. Hossenfelder quotes 't Hooft's stark warning: "Factoring a number with millions of digits into its prime factors will not be possible. If engineers ever succeed in making such quantum computers, it seems to me that the cellular automaton theory is falsified." This turns the race for quantum supremacy into a direct test of 't Hooft's worldview.

However, Hossenfelder offers a sharp critique of the cellular automaton model. She argues that "anything discrete be that in space or in time will violate the symmetries of Einstein's theories," making it difficult to reconcile with the smooth, continuous nature of relativity. While 't Hooft acknowledges this, Hossenfelder believes the conflict is more serious than he admits, suggesting that other theorists like Steven Wolfram have moved to "hypergraphs" to solve this very problem.

The Ontological Gap

Despite the theoretical elegance, Hossenfelder identifies a significant practical hole in 't Hooft's proposal. He distinguishes between the wave function we use for calculations and an "ontological" wave function that represents reality. "There is, however, a different wave function which is real," 't Hooft asserts, one that always yields a 100% probability for a specific outcome. In this view, a cat is either dead or alive, never both, because the superposition is just a reflection of our lack of information.

Hossenfelder finds this distinction frustratingly vague. "I don't see the practical use because he doesn't explain what the ontological states are or how you could ever figure out what they are," she writes. Without a method to identify these underlying states, the theory remains a philosophical stance rather than a predictive tool. It is a "fresh perspective on an old problem," but one that leaves the reader asking how to test it beyond the quantum computing limit.

Bottom Line

Hossenfelder effectively frames 't Hooft's work as a courageous, if lonely, attempt to rescue physics from the weirdness of quantum indeterminacy. The strongest part of his argument is the logical consistency of superdeterminism in resolving Bell's theorem without invoking non-locality. Its biggest vulnerability, however, is the lack of a concrete mechanism for the ontological states and the severe tension with relativistic symmetries. Readers should watch for experimental results in quantum computing; if large-scale factorization succeeds, 't Hooft's deterministic universe will face its final falsification.
