Sabine Hossenfelder tackles the most disorienting question in physics with a clarity that cuts through centuries of philosophical fog: if the past is gone and the future unwritten, why does Einstein's math insist they are all equally real? She doesn't just recite relativity; she dismantles our intuitive sense of "now" to reveal a universe where time is not a flowing river, but a static landscape. This is essential listening for anyone who wants to understand why their memory of yesterday is just as physically "real" as the screen they are reading this on.

The Death of Universal Now

Hossenfelder begins by exposing the flaw in our everyday intuition. We treat time as a universal parameter, a clock ticking identically for everyone, but she points out that this view collapses under the weight of Maxwell's equations. "Herman Minkowski was the first to notice that this may not be quite right," she explains, noting that treating time as a dimension rather than a parameter allows physics to make sense of how space and time rotate into one another. This reframing is crucial because it shifts time from a background stage to an active coordinate, indistinguishable in its mathematical treatment from the three dimensions of space.

The author then introduces the concept of the "space-time diagram," a tool that visualizes how motion tilts our perspective on time. "In space we can go forward backward left right or up and down in time we can only go forward we can't make a u-turn," Hossenfelder writes. While this sounds like a comforting distinction, she quickly pivots to show that once time becomes a dimension, the labels we put on it lose their universal meaning. The core of her argument is that there is no single "now" that applies to the whole universe. "Once you have made time into a dimension the labels on it don't mean much," she asserts, forcing the reader to abandon the idea of a cosmic present.

The notion of now depends on the observer. It is observer dependent.

This leads to the famous "train" thought experiment, though Hossenfelder updates it with a friend named Alice in a spaceship. She describes how two observers moving at different velocities will disagree on whether two events happen simultaneously. "You're now is not the same as my now," she states, highlighting the relativity of simultaneity. This isn't just a theoretical quirk; it is the operational definition of reality in special relativity. If Alice and you both perform the same experiment with mirrors and light, you will conclude different moments are "now" for the same distant events. The argument holds up because it relies on the finite speed of light, a fact that has been experimentally verified countless times.

The Block Universe and the Past

The most provocative leap in Hossenfelder's commentary comes when she connects the relativity of simultaneity to the existence of the past. If your "now" includes events that are simultaneous for you, and Alice's "now" includes different events that are simultaneous for her, and both viewpoints are equally valid, then all those events must exist. "If all the events that you think happen now exist and all other observers say that the events that happen at the same time as those events exist then all events exist now," she argues. This is the "block universe" theory: a four-dimensional structure where the past, present, and future are all equally real and fixed.

She anticipates the reader's resistance to this idea, acknowledging that it feels counterintuitive. "If you find that somewhat hard to accept there is another possibility," she notes, suggesting we could define existence only as what is currently here. However, she quickly dismantles this alternative, explaining that such a definition would require a preferred frame of reference that contradicts Einstein's fourth assumption. "Unfortunately it has nothing to do with pizza if it had any observable consequences that would contradict the fourth assumption that einstein made," she quips, before returning to the serious point that any such "slicing" of time is in conflict with the theory. The logic is tight: if special relativity is true, the past cannot be "gone" in any fundamental sense.

Critics might note that quantum mechanics introduces indeterminacy, suggesting the future is not yet written. Hossenfelder addresses this head-on, arguing that even if the future is probabilistic, the past remains fixed in the block. "Even if that was the case the previous argument still applies to the past so yeah it's true for we currently know the past exists the same way as the present," she concludes. While some physicists argue that the collapse of the wave function creates a genuine "now," Hossenfelder maintains that this does not rescue the past from its eternal existence.

Bottom Line

Hossenfelder's strongest asset is her ability to translate the abstract geometry of spacetime into a direct challenge to human perception, proving that our feeling of a flowing time is an illusion created by our limited perspective. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in the tension between this deterministic block universe and the apparent randomness of quantum events, a conflict she acknowledges but does not fully resolve. Readers should watch for how future theories of quantum gravity might eventually force a revision of this static view of time.
