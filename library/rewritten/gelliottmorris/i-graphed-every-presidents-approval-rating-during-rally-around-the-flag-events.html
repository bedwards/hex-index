G. Elliott Morris dismantles a comforting political myth with cold, hard data: the idea that war automatically unites a nation behind its president is largely a fiction. While pundits rush to predict a surge in approval following military action, Morris argues that "genuine rallies are rarer and harder to detect than the conventional wisdom suggests." For busy readers navigating a polarized news cycle, this piece offers a necessary reality check, proving that history does not repeat its applause lines as often as we assume.

The Myth of the Automatic Bounce

Morris begins by confronting the immediate aftermath of the 2026 airstrikes on Iran. Despite the gravity of the conflict, the data shows a flatline. "For Trump's Iran strikes, it indeed looks like another non-rally," he writes, noting that approval ratings "didn't even twitch" in an era of deep polarization. This is a crucial distinction. Many assume that any military engagement triggers a patriotic reflex, but Morris points out that the public is far more discerning. He notes that "only three conflicts since WWII produced clear, double-digit bounces in net presidential approval: the Gulf War (+29), WWII (+19), and Iraq (+17)."

The author's methodology is rigorous, comparing every major conflict since 1941 to isolate the specific impact of war from other variables. He highlights how even "successful" operations often fail to generate lasting political capital. Consider the Gulf War, often cited as the gold standard for rally effects. Morris reminds us that while Bush Sr. saw a massive surge, "thanks to an economic recession in 1992, by Election Day, Bush was at -20 and lost to Clinton." This historical context, echoing the concept of mean reversion in finance, suggests that any political gain from war is often temporary and easily erased by domestic realities.

The conventional wisdom is that when presidents go to war, their approval goes up. But the historical record is a lot messier than that.

Critics might argue that the sample size of major conflicts is too small to draw definitive conclusions, or that the nature of modern media fragmentation makes rallies harder to measure rather than impossible to achieve. However, Morris's data on the "ambiguous" nature of conflicts like Korea and Panama strengthens his case that the effect is not a law of physics, but a fragile exception.

The Five Conditions That Must Align

Why do some presidents get a bounce while others, like Trump in this scenario, get nothing? Morris turns to political science to identify five specific conditions required for a rally, arguing that the current situation fails on nearly all of them. The first condition is a "dramatic, sudden shock." Morris explains that John Mueller's original research found rallies require events that "jolts public attention," such as Pearl Harbor or 9/11. The 2026 strikes, while significant, lacked the element of surprise or the existential threat that forces a unified national response.

The second condition is perhaps the most damning for the current administration: "bipartisan elite consensus." As Morris puts it, "rallies persist only as long as opposition elites refrain from criticizing the president." When opposition leaders immediately criticize the action, the public receives a signal that the move is partisan, not patriotic. In the case of the Iran strikes, the lack of unity among elites meant the public never received the cue to close ranks.

Morris also touches on the nuance of "noisy polling data" and high baselines. For instance, during the Vietnam War, Dwight D. Eisenhower's approval was already so high that "his upside was potentially capped." Conversely, during the Kosovo intervention, Bill Clinton's approval actually dropped. Morris notes that "the decline may have been caused by multiple factors," including a reversion to the mean after impeachment and a specific "foreign-policy drag" from the air campaign. This complexity is often lost in soundbites, but Morris's granular analysis reveals that war can be a political liability, not just an asset.

A simple explanation for Trump's non-rally is that his war is unpopular.

The author's refusal to accept the "rally" narrative without evidence is the piece's greatest strength. He dissects the Afghanistan "rally" as well, noting that it is "polluted" by the 9/11 attacks themselves. "You can't cleanly separate the effect of the war from the effect of the attack," he writes, challenging the assumption that the invasion itself drove the numbers. This level of skepticism is vital for understanding the current political landscape, where the distinction between the event and the reaction is often blurred by partisan spin.

Bottom Line

Morris delivers a compelling verdict: the "rally around the flag" effect is not a reliable political tool, but a rare convergence of shock, unity, and public sentiment that is increasingly difficult to achieve in a polarized America. His strongest argument lies in the historical data showing that even massive bounces, like the one in the Gulf War, evaporate quickly when domestic issues resurface. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that the public's reaction to the 2026 strikes will remain static, ignoring the possibility that a prolonged conflict could eventually shift the narrative. Readers should watch for how quickly the "non-rally" holds up if the war drags on, as history suggests that time is the ultimate enemy of presidential approval.
