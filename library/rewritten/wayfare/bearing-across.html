In an era where spiritual inquiry is often reduced to self-help platitudes or rigid dogma, the anonymous author of "Bearing Across" in Wayfare makes a startling claim: that the path to the divine is not through isolation, but through a rigorous, almost violent engagement with the greatest literature of human history. This is not a meditation on feeling; it is a manifesto on reading as a form of spiritual combat, arguing that to know God, one must first learn to read the minds of the dead.

The Architecture of the Soul

The piece opens with a vision that feels less like a dream and more like a summons to intellectual warfare. Various writes, "I am not ashamed, afraid, or averse to tell you what ought to be told: that I am under the direction of messengers from Heaven, daily and nightly." This sets a tone of urgent authority, immediately distinguishing the text from the passive contemplation often found in modern spirituality. The author does not ask for belief; they demand attention.

The core of the argument rests on a radical redefinition of the human soul's origin. Rather than being a created artifact, the text suggests the soul is co-eternal with the divine. As the Messenger in the poem explains, "Intelligence, unmade and increate, And independently ensphered as man, Is one of two composing all there is." This is a profound philosophical pivot. By asserting that the mind is "unmade," the author aligns with the ancient Gnostic and Neoplatonic traditions found in the companion deep dive on Temenos, where the sacred space is not built but discovered within the pre-existing structure of the self. This framing is effective because it removes the anxiety of "earning" salvation, replacing it with the burden of remembering what one already is.

"To know thy self as Other doth Is no more than all gods have done 'ere thee."

Critics might note that this view risks collapsing the distinction between the creator and the created, a theological minefield that has historically led to charges of heresy. However, the text navigates this by emphasizing that this divinity is potential, an "embryo" waiting to be recognized through the act of reading.

The Library as a Battlefield

The most distinctive move in "Bearing Across" is the transformation of the library into a holy site. The author does not merely suggest reading; they curate a specific canon as a survival kit for the soul. Various writes, "Seek ye, which is to choose, the choicest books, For learning is to choose and nothing else." This is a bold assertion that the act of selection is itself a moral and spiritual discipline.

The text then launches into a rapid-fire litany of authors, treating them not as historical figures but as living guides. "Read Plato for the youth of Christendom, And Aristotle for its Middle Age," the Messenger commands. This is not a dry syllabus; it is a spiritual lineage. The author argues that these texts are the only way to "purge thyself of fear through suffering," echoing the function of tragedy in the Seraph deep dive, where the cleansing of the spirit requires a confrontation with the tragic. The argument here is that without the "brazen lungs" of poets like Milton and Shakespeare, the modern soul is deaf to the divine.

Yet, the text acknowledges the danger of this path. The author warns against the "learned" who merely deconstruct. "The learned deconstruct; An Other's mind is thine and shall instruct," the Messenger says. This is a sharp critique of modern academic detachment. The author insists that true understanding requires a surrender of the ego, a willingness to let another's mind inhabit one's own. This is where the argument lands hardest: it suggests that reading is an act of possession, not just analysis.

The Sword and the Plow

The narrative takes a dramatic turn when the narrator encounters a "mantled poet sovereign" who draws a sword. This confrontation symbolizes the conflict between the old, violent interpretations of scripture and the new, transformative understanding. The poet asks, "What offering's that?" and the narrator replies of the old rites: "milk and honey mixed, With wine and water poured into the ground."

The Messenger intervenes, not with a weapon, but with a correction of perspective. "That land for man was made, not man for land," the poet declares, signaling a shift from a theology of sacrifice to one of stewardship. This moment resonates with the themes in the Speaking in tongues deep dive, where the true language of the spirit is not a mystical utterance but a practical, earthly clarity. The author argues that the "sword" of the intellect must be turned into a "plough" to cultivate the soil of the self.

"Language is the garment of His light, And gives to know by hand and ear and sight."

This metaphor of language as a garment is crucial. It suggests that truth is not an abstract concept but something that must be worn, lived, and felt. The author insists that we cannot command the light; we must "bend thy neck" to receive it. This humility is the counterweight to the earlier claim of the soul's divinity. We are gods in embryo, but we must still learn to walk.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of "Bearing Across" is its refusal to separate the intellectual from the spiritual, presenting the study of the classics as the only viable path to the divine. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its dense, archaic style, which may alienate readers seeking immediate spiritual comfort rather than a rigorous intellectual ascent. The reader should watch for how this argument holds up when applied to non-Western canons, as the text's focus remains firmly on the Greco-Roman and Judeo-Christian tradition. Ultimately, the piece is a challenge: to read is to be remade, and to ignore the library is to remain in the dark.
