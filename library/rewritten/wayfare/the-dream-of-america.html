In a moment when political rhetoric increasingly leans on blood-and-soil nationalism, Wayfare offers a startling theological correction: the American experiment is divinely inspired not because of who we are by birth, but because of what we promised to become. This piece refuses to let readers settle for easy patriotism or cynical dismissal, instead demanding a rigorous engagement with the paradox at the heart of the nation's founding.

The Paradox of Principles

The article begins by dissecting a recent speech where JD Vance argued that people fight and die not for ideas, but for a specific homeland rooted in generational ties. Wayfare reports, "That is our homeland. People will not fight and die for abstractions, but they will fight for their home." The piece uses this quote as a pivot point to challenge the notion that American exceptionalism relies on a mystical connection between land and lineage.

Instead, the editors argue that true reverence for the United States requires rejecting Vance's thesis in favor of an "America as an idea" framework. This is where the commentary gains its unique theological weight. The piece notes that while the Book of Mormon and Doctrine and Covenants testify to the special status of the American continent—citing specific passages like 1 Nephi 13 and Doctrine and Covenants section 101—the divine mandate was for a government established "for the rights and protection of all flesh." The argument suggests that clinging to blood-and-soil nationalism actually betrays the very scriptures often cited to support it.

Doing this means rejecting Vance's thesis, because the paradox of the American founding requires that we reject blood and soil nationalism in favor of embracing America as an idea, a set of principles that call us to return again and again to the better angels of our nature.

Critics might argue that this idealized view ignores the very real human need for cultural continuity and community identity. However, the piece counters that without the anchor of universal principle, such identity inevitably curdles into exclusionary nativism.

The Founders' Contradictions

The commentary then shifts to a stark examination of the founders themselves, refusing to sanitize history. It cites Yale Constitutional Scholar Akhil Amar, who described the U.S. founding as the "big bang" or "hinge point of history" that introduced self-governance on an unprecedented scale. Yet, Wayfare immediately juxtaposes this with the reality that many architects of liberty were also enslavers.

The piece focuses intensely on Thomas Jefferson, noting that his awareness of slavery's immorality makes his actions even more troubling rather than less. "Often, when considering the views of those from the past, we rely on the sense that the past is a 'foreign country' to persuade us that problematic views... should not trouble us," the editors write. But they insist that Jefferson's specific knowledge of the moral problem makes his retention of over 600 enslaved people impossible to excuse.

Wayfare details how Jefferson, despite working toward outlawing the importation of slaves, kept from 130 to 200 people in bondage at any given time and never granted emancipation to them. The text highlights the hypocrisy of a man who wrote that Black people were "much inferior" in reason while simultaneously fathering children with Sally Hemmings, whom he enslaved. This section is particularly effective because it does not shy away from the raw numbers or the physical brutality involved.

The piece argues that this contradiction extends beyond Jefferson to the franchise itself, which was originally limited to white male landowners, and notes that women waited until 1920 for the vote while Native peoples faced treachery. "Many aspects of the history of America feature a consistent assault on the rights of those who were powerless in favor of the desires of the powerful," the article states. This framing forces the reader to hold two truths simultaneously: the founding was a divine hinge point, and it was built on profound moral failure.

Reclaiming the Good from the Contradiction

The final section tackles the theological dilemma facing members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. If the scriptures affirm the divinity of the Constitution, how do we reconcile that with a history of systemic oppression? Wayfare suggests that the answer lies in embracing the tension rather than resolving it through denial.

The editors cite President Dallin H. Oaks, who described the principles of popular sovereignty, federalism, and personal rights as "divinely inspired." Yet, the piece insists that this inspiration does not grant immunity to the nation's sins. The argument is that the greatness of the American founding lies in its potential for self-correction—a capacity born from the very principles it enshrined.

How do we reclaim something of the Good or the Beautiful from what seems a significant degree of moral duplicity?

This question hangs over the conclusion, suggesting that the path forward is not to abandon the project but to demand more of it. The piece implies that the only way to honor the founding documents is to relentlessly apply their principles to those they originally excluded.

Bottom Line

Wayfare's strongest contribution is its refusal to let religious faith be used as a shield for nationalist exclusion, instead using scripture to demand a more honest accounting of history. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the difficulty of translating this high-minded theological paradox into concrete political action without alienating readers who seek simple narratives. The reader should watch for how other institutions grapple with this same tension between divine inspiration and human failure as the nation approaches its 250th anniversary.
