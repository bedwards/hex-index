Kristen Blair dismantles the most cherished myth of Western individualism: that true freedom requires total independence. By tracing the line from Harriet Tubman's desperate escapes to the theological concept of a sovereign God, she reveals how the "separatist self" is not a triumph of human spirit, but a fiction built on invisible labor. This is not just a spiritual critique; it is a historical indictment of a system that defines power by its ability to ignore the very dependencies that keep us alive.

The Myth of the Unencumbered Self

Blair begins not with abstract philosophy, but with the brutal reality of flight. She highlights that while Harriet Tubman is celebrated as a "Moses" leading others to freedom, the demographic reality of escape was far more gendered. "Eighty percent of these fugitives were young males in their teens and twenties who generally absconded alone," she notes, citing scholar David Blight. This statistic is crucial because it exposes the structural barrier: women were tethered by the very biology that defines the human species.

The author argues that the "myth of autonomy" ignores the biological constraints that make independence impossible for many. She points to the "obstetrical dilemma," noting that human infants are born prematurely relative to other mammals due to pelvic size, meaning they "would die without the necessary and exhausting care provided by bonds of love and obligation." This biological fact shatters the Enlightenment ideal of the unattached individual. If a human cannot survive infancy without total dependency, how can an adult claim to be sovereign?

"The sovereign being is unattached, or at least their attachments do not impede them."

Blair's framing is effective because it grounds high-concept theology in the physical vulnerability of the body. She suggests that the "sovereign" is a construct designed to exclude those who bear children or care for the vulnerable. Critics might argue that this biological determinism limits the scope of human agency, but Blair's point is precisely that the myth of agency is what causes the harm. By pretending we are unattached, we devalue the labor that actually sustains us.

The Theological Cost of Independence

The essay takes a sharp turn into theology, arguing that the concept of a "sovereign God" has been corrupted by secular political models. Blair writes, "This God endorses empire, grants power to monarchs, consolidates power among the wealthy." She contends that this theological model mirrors the "benevolent patriarchy" where men are defined as protectors and women as the protected, reinforcing a hierarchy where the "vulnerable other" is inherently less than sovereign.

This connection between political and spiritual sovereignty is the piece's most provocative move. Blair suggests that the "sovereign God" cannot be vulnerable, yet this creates a spiritual disconnect for those whose lives are defined by vulnerability. "Sovereignty, theological and secular, has thus been intertwined with the mythic ideal of autonomy, independence, and control," she asserts. The implication is that a theology of independence is actually a theology of exclusion, one that fails to account for the "messiness of questions about dependency."

"The myth of autonomy, independence, and invulnerability? It sounds amazing. Imagine being all powerful, self-sufficient, responsible to or for no one."

The author then deconstructs this fantasy by listing the hidden dependencies required to maintain it. She uses the example of Charles Darwin, whose "self-made" scientific genius was actually enabled by a wife and servants who handled his domestic life. "Behind Darwin's capacity for independence were a host of people who created the conditions for him to explore, unencumbered by demands of vulnerability," Blair explains. This historical context is vital; it shows that the "separatist self" is not a natural state but a privilege purchased by the labor of others.

Soluble and Separatist Selves

Blair introduces theologian Catherine Keller's distinction between the "separatist self" and the "soluble self." The separatist self is the mythic individual who appears independent, while the soluble self is the one who "dissolves into another, functionally enabling their myth of separation to continue." This dynamic is often gendered, with women dissolving into the lives of men to sustain the illusion of male autonomy.

The author argues that both concepts are "facades" that rely on each other. "Without solubility, separatism emerges disheveled and floundering, unable to keep up the act," she writes. This is a powerful reframing of the gender divide. It suggests that the struggle is not just between men and women, but between two interdependent modes of being that are both ultimately false. The "separatist self" is a lie, and the "soluble self" is a sacrifice that keeps the lie alive.

"We are all dependent. The web of life binds us all."

Blair's conclusion is that we must dispel the myth of independence to find a "truer model of being." She challenges the reader to accept that "to be sovereign... one cannot have significant attachments" is a false definition of power. Instead, she proposes a spirituality that embraces vulnerability. "An investigation of spiritual sovereignty for women cannot escape the messiness of questions about dependency, autonomy, and vulnerability," she insists. This is a call to redefine strength not as the absence of need, but as the courage to acknowledge it.

Critics might note that this radical interdependence could be weaponized to justify exploitation, arguing that some labor is indeed oppressive rather than liberating. Blair anticipates this by distinguishing between the myth of independence and the reality of care, but the tension between voluntary interdependence and coerced servitude remains a complex hurdle for her argument to clear.

Bottom Line

Blair's strongest contribution is her unflinching exposure of the "invisible labor" that props up the Western ideal of the autonomous individual. Her biggest vulnerability lies in the practical application: how do we build a society that honors dependency without reinforcing the patriarchal structures that currently exploit it? Readers should watch for how this framework reshapes not just theology, but our understanding of economics, politics, and the very nature of freedom.
