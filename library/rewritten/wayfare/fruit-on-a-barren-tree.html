In a religious landscape often dominated by self-help manuals and the promise that obedience guarantees reward, this piece makes a startling claim: the most spiritually vital parts of scripture are the boring lists of names we skip. The author, writing as Various for Wayfare, argues that genealogies are not dry appendices but the very mechanism that shifts our focus from human effort to divine initiative. This is a necessary corrective for anyone exhausted by the pressure to "qualify" for grace, offering a vision where God is the main character and human frailty is the setting, not the obstacle.

The Myth of Self-Improvement

The article opens by dismantling a pervasive modern assumption: that religion is a system of instructions where we trade good behavior for divine rewards. Various writes, "I think I'd win a bet that most Sunday school teachers skip the genealogies in the Bible. The passages seem to be odd footnotes or appendices with little relevance." This observation is sharp because it identifies a specific behavioral symptom of a deeper theological drift. The author suggests this drift is not accidental but cultural, tracing a line from scripture to the "relentlessly optimistic self-improvement in Benjamin Franklin's Autobiography." By connecting the Book of Mormon's "accelerated narrative of the passing of the plates" to the American genre of Horatio Alger novels, the piece argues that we have accidentally imported a secular, transactional worldview into our spiritual lives.

The core of the argument is that this "self-help" model fails because it assumes the covenant depends on our ability to be perfect. Various puts it, "Phrases like 'qualify for exaltation' or 'access the atonement' appear in Church manuals and general conference talks. These phrases bear with them the intuitive sense that religion is basically ethical, which means that it is simply a set of expectations for us to follow." This framing is effective because it names the anxiety many believers feel—the fear that one mistake voids the contract. However, critics might note that this characterization risks oversimplifying the role of agency in religious traditions, which often balance grace with genuine human responsibility. Yet, the author's pivot is powerful: scripture presents humans not as heroes to be emulated, but as flawed people whom God pursues anyway.

"The main character of the Bible is not Adam or Moses or Paul; the main character is God. The main character of the Book of Mormon is not Nephi or Alma or Mormon; it is God."

This reorientation is the piece's strongest move. It suggests that the "hero's journey" we crave is actually a distraction from the true narrative arc. As Various notes, "In scripture, religion is something that God does rather than something human beings do." This lands because it aligns with the raw honesty found in the texts themselves, where figures like Moses protest their inability and Nephi obsesses over failure. The author correctly identifies that skipping the genealogies removes the context that makes these failures bearable.

Entropy and the Barren Womb

The commentary then dives into the specific literary function of genealogies, using the transition from Genesis 11 to 12 as a case study. The author highlights how the genealogy of Shem reveals a "slow entropy," where lifespans shorten and the family line moves toward a dead end. "Sarai was barren; she had no child," the text notes, marking the climax of this decay. Various writes, "The genealogy claims that collapse is natural; that our lives, left to themselves, will eventually and ultimately simply unwind." This is a profound observation, connecting ancient text to modern "doomerism" and the feeling that institutions and bodies are inevitably failing.

The author draws a parallel between this biblical entropy and the design of modern technology, noting how products are built to fail, and how social media algorithms push negativity because it keeps us engaged. "We expect the world to fail us," the author argues. But then, the piece pivots to the theological miracle: "Barrenness is God's playground." The argument here is that God's speech in Genesis 12 echoes the creation in Genesis 1, moving into a space of emptiness to bring order. This is where the historical context of the Genealogy of Jesus becomes relevant; the entire lineage is a story of God breaking into a line that seems to have run its course, much like the barrenness of Sarah or the troubled history of the House of David.

"God lets human beings be human beings, and that His will is accomplished through or despite them but is never dependent on them."

Quoting Marilynne Robinson's interpretation of Joseph's words in Genesis 50, the author makes a crucial distinction: this is not a promise that "everything happens for a reason," but that God can use human cruelty to achieve justice. This nuance is vital. It avoids toxic positivity while maintaining hope. The author argues that the genealogies force us to zoom out from our individual failures to see a larger story of preservation. "Nothing reveals this more fully than the genealogies," Various writes, because they show that the covenant survives not because the ancestors were perfect, but because God was persistent.

The Fading Voice in Omni

The piece concludes by applying this lens to the Book of Mormon, specifically the short book of Omni. Here, the author performs a close reading that is often missed by casual readers. The narrative of the plates shifts from Nephi's spiritual purpose to a dry genealogy, and finally to a voice that seems to have given up on revelation. The author notes that Abinadom, a later keeper of the plates, claims, "I know of no revelation save that which has been written, neither prophecy; wherefore, that which is sufficient is written."

Various interprets this not as confidence, but as a tragic stasis. "Abinadom believes he has everything that he needs, knows everything that he has to know, and does not see the point in asking for more." This is a brilliant reading of the text, suggesting that the genealogy in Omni is a record of spiritual entropy, a "frozen world" where the voice of God seems to have vanished. The author points out that the language is odd—Abinadom doesn't say what is written is enough, but that that which is sufficient is written, implying a closed system. This mirrors the barrenness of Abraham's father, Terah, before God spoke.

"Suddenly Abinadom's son Amaleki bogglingly, incredibly, tells us that God one day called to Amaleki's leader, King Mosiah, and told him to go into the wilderness."

The interruption of this silence is the climax of the argument. Just as God spoke to Abraham in the face of barrenness, He speaks to Mosiah in the face of spiritual stagnation. The author suggests that the genealogy is the setup for this divine interruption. It creates the tension of decay so that the breakthrough feels like a resurrection. This reading transforms a skipped chapter into a profound meditation on hope. Critics might argue that reading such deep theological intent into a brief list of names is speculative, but the author's evidence from the shifting tone of the record holders is compelling. The argument holds that the very act of recording the names, even in a time of decline, is an act of faith that a future exists.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its reclamation of the "boring" parts of scripture as the very places where the theology of grace is most visible. By framing genealogies as a counter-narrative to American self-help, the author offers a liberating alternative to the pressure of perfection. The biggest vulnerability is the reliance on a specific literary reading of Omni that, while insightful, may not resonate with readers who view the text strictly as history rather than art. However, the piece successfully argues that whether one sees these lists as history or literature, they point to the same truth: the story is not about us, and it is not over yet.

"The main character of the Bible is not Adam or Moses or Paul; the main character is God. The main character of the Book of Mormon is not Nephi or Alma or Mormon; it is God."
