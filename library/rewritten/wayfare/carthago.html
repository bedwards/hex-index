In a literary landscape often saturated with dry theological exposition, "Carthago" by Various emerges as a startlingly intimate confession, reframing the construction of sacred space not as an architectural feat but as a spiritual siege. The author's most distinctive claim is that true liberty is found only within the confines of divine service, a paradox articulated through the voice of a prisoner addressing his jailer. This is not merely a retelling of ancient scripture; it is a visceral, first-person testimony that bridges the gap between the ancient temple rituals of the Nauvoo era and the modern seeker's desperate need for certainty.

The Architecture of the Soul

Various opens by dismantling the conventional view of confinement, asserting that the physical prison is secondary to the spiritual reality. "This prison house of mine would ill-annoy, As if a Punic enemy to stop," the author writes, immediately invoking the historical weight of Carthage to set a tone of besieged resilience. The narrative voice is not that of a distant observer but of someone who has been "seized from out the midst of family" yet claims to be "in everything made free." This inversion of power dynamics is the text's engine. The author argues that the temple described in the text is not just a building but a conduit for the divine, a place where the "veil of darkness" is parted.

The description of the temple's construction is rendered with a specificity that suggests deep historical grounding, echoing the meticulous planning seen in the construction of the Nauvoo Temple, where every architectural detail was believed to hold celestial significance. "It was the first, at last, the place we built, Wherein the way to Other's known apart," Various writes, emphasizing that the structure was a revelation rather than a design. The text details the temple's orientation and features, noting that "In facing East it looks to Other's rise," a detail that aligns with the historical practice of orienting temples toward the sunrise to symbolize resurrection. This attention to liturgical geography grounds the mystical experience in tangible reality.

"The source of Holy Writ's a constant flow And never fully poured in single draft."

The author's treatment of the temple's interior is particularly striking. They describe a pulpit "overlaid with gold" and a voice that "billowed forth... with wat'ry rush," creating a sensory experience that transcends the written word. This imagery serves to validate the physical act of worship as a necessary vessel for spiritual transmission. Critics might note that such dense, allegorical language can obscure the historical realities of the temple's construction, potentially alienating readers seeking a straightforward historical account. However, the emotional resonance of the text suggests that the author's goal is theological truth rather than architectural blueprints.

The Chain of Generations

The narrative then shifts to the arrival of messengers, framing them as the necessary links in a chain that connects the past to the present. The author introduces a "river's son" who brings tablets from on high, a clear allusion to the restorationist narrative where ancient authority is returned to the modern world. This section draws a powerful parallel to the historical context of the Temple garment, where specific clothing serves as a constant reminder of covenants made, acting as a personal "tabernacle" for the believer. "The key t'unlock the scattered's given me," Various writes, positioning the narrator as the custodian of a divine mandate to gather the dispersed.

The text emphasizes the continuity of this mission, stating that the "man unknown" was a messenger who "never raised a knife Against a son nor took a battle tithe." This pacifist stance contrasts sharply with the violent history of the "Punic enemy" mentioned earlier, suggesting a new dispensation of peace. The author argues that this mission is not about conquest but about "sealing" the generations together. "Without the chain forged in the place we built, The day when Other come's a waste and curse," the text warns, highlighting the stakes of this spiritual work. The urgency here is palpable, driven by the belief that the "day of dread and greatness" is imminent.

"O fools, who chain my faith fast as my flesh, Confine my mind and sever every tie That holdeth quick to One Besmeared Upon."

The author's critique of rigid creeds is a central theme in this section. They reject the idea that faith can be contained in a static formula, arguing instead for a "creed that never ends." This dynamic view of doctrine challenges the reader to see faith as an evolving relationship rather than a fixed set of rules. The text posits that "further light from Twofold One is known," suggesting that divine revelation is ongoing and accessible to those who seek it. This perspective resonates with the historical tension between institutional dogma and personal spiritual experience, a theme that has persisted since the early days of the movement.

The Paradox of Liberty

In the final section, the author confronts the paradox of freedom and service head-on. The text asserts that "to serve is mastery," a counter-intuitive claim that redefines the concept of liberty. The narrator, despite being in chains, claims to have found a freedom that transcends physical constraints. "Though borne in chains, in everything made free As Other taught: to serve is mastery," Various writes, encapsulating the core theological argument of the piece. This idea is reinforced by the description of the narrator's restoration, where they realize that "Myself as whelmed the most excepting none" was the necessary precursor to being lifted up.

The text concludes with a challenge to the reader to examine their own understanding of authority and freedom. The author warns against those who "stake authority Upon tradition and antiquity" or those who "Asserting independence from the trunk." Instead, the narrator claims a direct connection to the source of authority, stating, "I stand apart from these, conversing deep, With one who owns the vineyard and the tree." This direct line of communication is presented as the ultimate source of legitimacy, bypassing human intermediaries. The text ends with a rhetorical question that leaves the reader to ponder the nature of true authority: "Must not the lord's... trappings of its lord?"

"The anchor in the sea 'pon which ye sail Is as a principle that's founded on Irrevocable doctrine lest ye think That overboard's a depth that ye can plumb."

Critics might argue that this reliance on direct revelation and personal experience can lead to subjectivity and a lack of accountability. The text's dismissal of established creeds and traditions could be seen as a rejection of the collective wisdom of the faith community. However, the author's emphasis on the "Twofold One" and the balance of Justice and Mercy suggests a framework that seeks to avoid the extremes of both rigid legalism and unchecked individualism.

Bottom Line

"Carthago" succeeds as a powerful, if dense, meditation on the nature of faith, authority, and the paradox of finding freedom in service. Its greatest strength lies in its ability to weave historical allusions with personal revelation, creating a narrative that feels both ancient and urgently contemporary. The text's vulnerability is its reliance on a specific theological framework that may be opaque to those unfamiliar with the underlying traditions, potentially limiting its broader appeal. Readers who engage with this piece will find a rich, challenging exploration of what it means to be "seized" by a higher purpose and how that seizure transforms the very nature of liberty.
