Brad DeLong cuts through the fever dream of artificial intelligence to ask the one question the market is desperate to ignore: who actually pays for the trillions in data centers being built today? While the hype cycle screams "singularity," DeLong offers a sobering diagnosis of a boom driven not by proven utility, but by the terrified reflex of platform monopolies to lock out competitors. For the busy professional trying to separate signal from noise, this is the most grounded analysis of the AI infrastructure bubble currently available.

The Reality of the "Bag of Words"

DeLong anchors his skepticism in the mundane, everyday experience of using these tools, contrasting the grandiose promises of the industry with the messy reality of current outputs. He leans heavily on the experience of physicist Chad Orzel, who found that while AI can handle some tasks, it often fails spectacularly on others. DeLong notes that Orzel's initial experiments with AI for data extraction resulted in a "complete hallucination. Wrong names, wrong number of columns, made-up comments." This isn't just a minor glitch; it represents a fundamental barrier to entry for high-stakes work.

The core of DeLong's argument is that for most knowledge workers, the current technology is a "squishy lukewarm reaction" rather than a revolution. He paraphrases Orzel's realization that for infrequent but critical tasks like budgeting or staffing reviews, the time required to verify the AI's output often exceeds the time it would take to do the work manually. "Checking someone else's answers isn't all that much faster than generating the answers myself," DeLong observes, highlighting a crucial economic friction that the hype cycle glosses over.

Critics might argue that this view is too static, assuming current limitations will persist. However, DeLong's point is that until the error rate drops significantly, the "efficiency boost" remains theoretical for the vast majority of users. The technology is currently a tool for the "tech-clerisy"—those obsessed with the latest code—rather than a universal productivity engine.

"MAMLMs may be of great use, but they will not upend my workflow and daily experience, let alone that of people who are not part of the tech-clerisy."

The Platform Panic

If the utility is narrow, why the massive spending? DeLong identifies the true engine of the AI boom not as consumer demand, but as a defensive war among the "hyperscalers"—Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta. He argues that these giants are pouring capital into infrastructure not because they have a clear path to profit, but because they cannot afford to be the one left without a seat at the table. "The cloud oligopolists have now sunk hundreds of billions of dollars into AI infrastructure," DeLong writes, noting that the economics of these sunk costs force them to eliminate any independent model provider that might sit between them and the user.

This dynamic creates a self-fulfilling prophecy. The fear of being disrupted drives the very investment that creates the bubble. DeLong draws a sharp parallel to the late 90s, noting that "the history of Netscape-meets-Microsoft, rhyming, but this time with unbelievable scale datacenter investments added on." The result is a construction boom where data centers in places like Northern Virginia are consuming power on the scale of heavy industry, with AI infrastructure alone projected to exceed 4% of national electricity demand by 2024.

This is where the historical context of General-Purpose Technology (GPT) becomes vital. Unlike previous GPTs like the steam engine or electricity, which emerged organically through widespread, decentralized adoption, this AI boom is being "bootstrapped into existence by a concentrated investment wave driven by fear." The investment is front-loaded and massive, while the actual productivity gains remain "narrow and incremental."

"OpenAI as a widely loved cross-platform consumer app is not an equilibrium its nominal partners will long tolerate. They may internalize it. They may box it into the enterprise and API back-end niche. They will do their very best to starve it of distribution."

The Economics of the Bubble

DeLong warns that the narrative of "digital gold" is misleading because the "shovels and picks" are owned by the same companies building the mines. He suggests that the rush to build AI startups and consultancies is irrational given that the platform monopolies are actively working to capture all the value. The "optimistic story" that AI will add trillions to global GDP is currently just a story, unmoored from the "fully amortized datacenter carrying, depreciation, and power costs."

He contrasts the "utopian narrative" with the "cautious reading" that we are currently in a "something will turn up" mode, where the promise of future productivity is used to justify present-day excess. The "Rashomon" effect is clear: one side sees a revolution, the other sees a bubble. DeLong sides with the latter, reminding readers that "anyone offering you guarantees is selling snake oil."

He reflects on his own past misjudgment of Uber, admitting he underestimated how "patient capital" could sustain an unsustainable model for years. However, he warns against overcorrecting: "Am I at risk of learning the wrong lesson—taking one noisy data point, Uber, and universalizing it into a belief that any sufficiently well-branded and well-funded 'platform' can defy gravity long enough to create new realities?" The distinction is that Uber solved a real, immediate consumer pain point (transportation), whereas the current AI infrastructure build-out is largely solving a corporate anxiety about being left behind.

"The number of people who need to wield AI effectively for demand to skyrocket is decreasing... The economic returns from using agents aren't just impactful on the bottom line, but the top line as well... Is it any wonder that every single hyperscaler says that demand for compute exceeds supply?"

Bottom Line

DeLong's most compelling contribution is his ability to separate the genuine technological potential of AI from the financial frenzy driving its deployment. His strongest argument is that the current infrastructure boom is a defensive maneuver by platform monopolies, not a reflection of organic market demand. The biggest vulnerability in his case is the speed at which "agents" might solve the verification problem, potentially rendering the "checking is slower than doing" objection obsolete faster than anticipated. Watch for the moment when the cost of verification drops below the cost of human labor; that is the true inflection point, not the next data center announcement.
