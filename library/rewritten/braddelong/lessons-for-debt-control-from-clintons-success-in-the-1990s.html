Most economic histories treat the 1990s surplus as a political anomaly or a simple result of the tech boom, but Brad DeLong challenges the very foundation of that narrative. He argues that the era's success wasn't accidental luck, but the direct result of a deliberate, controversial fiscal strategy that defied the warnings of the very experts who later claimed it would cause a recession. For anyone trying to understand the mechanics of debt control today, this piece forces a reckoning with the idea that political theater often masquerades as economic analysis.

The Myth of Professional Consensus

DeLong's central thesis is a direct rebuttal to the popular belief that the 1993 deficit-reduction package, the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1993 (OBRA 93), was a dangerous gamble that only succeeded despite the odds. He dismantles the notion that there was a genuine, widespread fear among economists that fiscal tightening would crash the economy. Instead, he posits that the opposition was largely performative, driven by partisan loyalty rather than data. "Taking claims that Clinton's OBRA 93 would tank the economy as real fears by credible macroeconomic analysts is to mistake political bullshit for real analytical judgements and warranted fears," DeLong writes.

The author's framing is sharp: he separates the actual economic mechanics from the political noise. He points out that prominent figures like Herbert Stein and Martin Feldstein had previously supported similar deficit-reduction measures under a Republican administration, only to flip-flop when a Democrat took the helm. This suggests that the "professional concern" cited by critics was not a reflection of economic reality, but a "one-way bet" designed to protect political interests. "The polarity is reversed: that it was their opposition to OBRA 93 rather than their support of OBRA 90 that was subservience to their political masters," DeLong argues. This is a compelling, if provocative, take that forces the reader to question the objectivity of the economic establishment during that era.

Critics might note that dismissing the concerns of top-tier economists as purely political ignores the genuine uncertainty of the time; predicting the interaction between fiscal tightening and a fragile recovery was not a trivial task. However, DeLong's evidence regarding the bond market behavior supports his skepticism. He notes that the feared bond-market selloff never happened immediately after the bill's passage; instead, the market reacted later to the sheer strength of the economy and technical shifts in mortgage-backed securities.

"Macro reality beats tribal signaling."

The Real Engine of Growth

Once the political theater is stripped away, DeLong identifies the actual drivers of the 1990s boom: a specific policy mix that combined fiscal credibility with monetary support and technological advancement. He argues that the administration's approach "crowded in private investment when the central bank leans against demand shortfalls and technology makes capital cheaper." This is a crucial distinction. The success wasn't just about cutting deficits; it was about creating a stable environment where the Federal Reserve could manage interest rates without the threat of a debt crisis, while the internet boom lowered the cost of capital.

DeLong highlights that the policy was not a blunt instrument of austerity. He points to the expansion of the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) as a critical component, noting that Gene Sperling successfully secured "the EITC expansion that was then and is now the biggest pro-working poor expansion of the American social insurance system ever." This detail adds necessary depth, showing that the strategy was not merely about balancing the ledger but about restructuring incentives to boost productivity and wages. The result, he claims, was a decade where "fiscal credibility, pro-work redistribution, and a supportive Fed plus falling ICT prices crowded in investment."

The author's assertion that this specific combination of factors made America 15% richer than it would have been otherwise is a bold claim. It reframes the 1990s not as a period of natural prosperity, but as a managed economic outcome. "Labeling this 'austerity' misses how structure + demand + technology produced more capital, higher productivity, and a richer America," DeLong writes. This reframing is vital for modern readers who often conflate deficit reduction with economic stagnation. The argument suggests that the fear of "austerity gone wrong" was a misdiagnosis of a policy that actually stimulated growth by reducing risk premiums and boosting confidence.

However, the piece relies heavily on the assumption that the 1990s conditions—specifically the unique convergence of the tech boom and a supportive Fed—can be easily replicated. The argument glosses over the fact that the 1994 yield-curve shift, which DeLong attributes to growth strength, was also complicated by the endogenous duration of mortgage-backed securities, a financial instrument that has since evolved into a source of systemic risk. While the historical lesson is clear, the applicability to a modern, more complex financial system remains an open question.

The Partisan Reality

Perhaps the most stinging part of DeLong's commentary is his assessment of the political landscape. He reveals that despite the bipartisan rhetoric often associated with the era, the 1993 bill passed with zero Republican votes in both the House and the Senate. "We got zero Republican votes for OBRA 93," he states, contrasting this with the 1990 package which had significant Republican support. This stark reality undermines the idea that the policy was a "bring us together" initiative. Instead, it was a partisan victory that succeeded in spite of unified opposition from the other side.

DeLong suggests that the opposition was rooted in the identity of the president rather than the policy itself. "Had OBRA 93 been proposed under a second-term Bush 41 presidency, they would have been strong advocates as well," he writes. This insight exposes the fragility of fiscal consensus in the American political system. It implies that the "credibility" of fiscal policy is often contingent on who holds the executive branch, rather than the objective merits of the plan. The author's conclusion is that the administration's success was a triumph of "left-neoliberal" strategy that managed to override the political noise, even if the political cost was total partisan division.

"The Clinton 1990s really were a fabulous decade that delivered rising employment, low inflation, and real wage gains."

Bottom Line

Brad DeLong's analysis offers a powerful corrective to the myth that the 1990s surplus was a fluke or the result of pure luck. His strongest argument lies in exposing the performative nature of the economic opposition at the time, revealing that the "professional consensus" against the 1993 deficit-reduction package was largely a political construct. The piece's biggest vulnerability, however, is its reliance on a unique historical convergence of technology and monetary policy that may not be replicable in today's economy. For the modern reader, the takeaway is clear: fiscal credibility can indeed crowd in investment, but only when the political will exists to separate economic strategy from partisan signaling.
