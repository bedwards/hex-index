This piece delivers a scorching indictment of modern media leadership, arguing that a single executive decision to kill a story about a foreign detention facility represents a moral collapse comparable to the darkest chapters of the 20th century. Brad DeLong does not merely critique a newsroom error; he frames the event as a deliberate act of censorship that aligns American journalism with the propaganda machinery of authoritarian regimes. The argument is startling not for its anger, but for its specific historical anchoring, connecting a 2025 editorial decision to the 1938 Anschluss and the staged deceptions of Theresienstadt.

The Erosion of Institutional Trust

DeLong opens by contrasting the enduring legacy of Edward R. Murrow with the perceived fragility of contemporary media values. He writes, "Murrow was a truth-teller and a serious man in a world gone mad and consumed by lies. His was a voice that the American people could trust in a moment of enormous consequence — just like they can trust the voice of Sharyn Alfonsi today." This comparison serves as the emotional core of the piece, elevating the stakes from a routine editorial dispute to a battle for the soul of public truth. The author suggests that the trust built over decades by Murrow's generation was not lost to market forces alone, but actively dismantled by corporate leadership that prioritized political alignment over journalistic integrity.

The commentary draws a sharp distinction between historical authoritarianism and current political movements. DeLong argues, "A key difference between the Nazis and MAGA is that, while both practiced cruelty, MAGA glorifies it. The Nazis downplayed their viciousness. They hid it from the world, themselves and their own citizens to the maximum degree possible." This observation reframes the current political climate not just as a deviation from norms, but as an inversion of the tactics used by previous totalitarian regimes. While the Nazis utilized camps like Theresienstadt to create a "show camp" illusion of humane treatment for the Red Cross, the author contends that modern political actors are openly marketing their cruelty as a virtue.

Trust builds by drops, but is lost in buckets — a fact about which the majority of America's media mandarins seem oblivious or wholly indifferent.

Critics might note that equating current political rhetoric with Nazi propaganda risks diluting the historical specificity of the Holocaust. However, DeLong's focus is not on the scale of the atrocities but on the mechanism of deception and the complicity of media figures in enabling state-sponsored abuse. The argument gains weight when applied to the specific incident involving the CECOT prison in El Salvador, where the author claims the administration's silence was a deliberate signal to the newsroom.

The Mechanics of Censorship

The piece centers on the decision to suppress a "60 Minutes" report regarding the treatment of deportees in a Salvadoran supermax prison. DeLong describes the interaction between the newsroom and the executive branch as a direct confrontation with authoritarian power. He writes, "Bari Weiss reached for Stephen Miller's number like he was her preferred cat sitter, and demanded that his voice be included in a story for which he had already made the decision to include his voice by refusing to share it." This vivid metaphor underscores the author's view that the suppression was not a neutral editorial choice but an active collaboration with the administration's desire to hide the reality of the detention center.

The author posits that the administration's refusal to comment was itself a form of communication, one that the media executive failed to interpret correctly. "The silence was the point, and the point was very simple. What Bari Weiss lacks is the common sense to interpret the message, which was perfectly clear. It was aimed at Sharyn Alfonsi, '60 Minutes,' CBS News and the American people. The message was, 'F*#k you.'" Here, DeLong shifts from historical analogy to a direct accusation of incompetence and moral failure, suggesting that the executive understood the threat but chose to comply rather than resist.

The narrative connects this specific incident to a broader pattern of behavior, noting that the prison has become a pilgrimage site for politicians seeking to signal toughness. "The El Salvador supermax prison is becoming the new Ohio Diner. It's the new Iowa State Fair. It's the new Jeffrey Epstein jet: It's where every political leader needs to visit, the place to see and be seen if you're ambitious and in politics today." This satirical observation highlights the performative nature of modern political cruelty, where the suffering of detainees is treated as a backdrop for photo opportunities rather than a humanitarian crisis.

Bari Weiss didn't just kill the story for Trump. Bari Weiss laughed at the story with Trump and Stephen Miller. She's in on it with them.

The Human Cost of Indifference

DeLong grounds his argument in the human reality of the detention center, refusing to let the political maneuvering obscure the suffering of the individuals involved. He references the specific case of a mistakenly deported Maryland man who was denied release despite diplomatic efforts, noting that the leaders of two nations simply "cannot find that one random wrongly deported man, and everyone should move along." The author implies that the man's fate is sealed, and the media's silence ensures that his disappearance goes unchallenged.

The piece also touches on the broader implications for democratic institutions. "Democracy is what keeps you safe and protects your liberty. This does not." By contrasting the ideals of freedom with the reality of the prison system, DeLong argues that the current administration is actively undermining the very foundations of American liberty. The author suggests that the media's failure to report on these abuses is not just a professional failure but a betrayal of the public trust.

Critics might argue that the author's tone is overly polemical, potentially alienating readers who are looking for a more balanced analysis of the situation. Yet, the piece's power lies in its unapologetic stance against what it perceives as a fundamental moral failing. The argument is that in the face of such clear evidence of abuse, neutrality is complicity.

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this commentary is its ability to weave a specific, contemporary news event into a broader historical narrative about the fragility of truth and the dangers of media complicity in authoritarianism. By invoking the legacy of Murrow and the specific deceptions of the Nazi era, DeLong forces the reader to confront the gravity of the situation without resorting to hyperbole. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on a singular, highly charged interpretation of the executive's motives, which may be difficult to verify independently. However, the piece succeeds in its primary goal: to make the reader question the integrity of the institutions that are supposed to hold power to account.
