Brad DeLong offers a chilling reframing of Bari Weiss's recent media maneuvers, suggesting her apparent incompetence is actually a calculated performance of submission to power. Rather than dismissing her actions as mere amateurism, he argues they serve a specific function in an authoritarian media landscape: demonstrating that even critics will bow when the stakes are high enough. This perspective forces readers to look past the surface-level news cycle and examine the deeper mechanics of how truth is suppressed not by force, but by the voluntary surrender of those who claim to defend it.

The Performance of Submission

DeLong begins by dismantling the defense that Weiss is simply unskilled. He points to the controversy surrounding a 60 Minutes segment on the CECOT prison in El Salvador, which was shelved despite having damning video evidence of prisoner abuse. While critics like Dan Drezner argue Weiss failed to understand the difference between reading about abuse and seeing it, DeLong suggests this misses the point entirely. He writes, "Stop asking whether Weiss is good at journalism. She is good at the job she's actually doing: performing submission to power and recruiting others to the ritual."

This argument gains traction when examining the timing. The segment was pulled just as Paramount Global, which owns CBS, was engaged in a hostile bid to acquire Warner Bros. Discovery, a deal requiring regulatory approval from the administration. DeLong notes that shelving a critical story "in the middle of a battle for the president's approval of a merger, is not routine." The implication is that the silence was a signal sent to the executive branch, a gesture of good faith from a media empire seeking favor.

In today's media oligopoly, that job pays.

The core of DeLong's analysis is that Weiss's behavior follows the maxim "the purpose of a system is what it does." If the system rewards those who align with power, then those who succeed are doing exactly what the system demands. He contrasts this with the traditional role of a journalist, which is to advance public reason. Instead, Weiss appears to be engaging in a ritualistic act of compliance.

The Orwellian Ritual

To explain the psychology behind this compliance, DeLong turns to George Orwell's Nineteen Eighty-Four, specifically the scene where the character O'Brien forces Winston Smith to admit he sees five fingers when only four are held up. DeLong distinguishes between two types of authoritarianism: the totalitarian regime that wants you to genuinely believe the lie, and the mere authoritarian regime that wants you to know the truth but dare not speak it. He argues, "The Trumpists... are perfectly happy if you know damned well that there are four fingers, but dare not say anything other than that there are five."

This distinction is crucial. It suggests that the goal is not to change reality, but to enforce a public performance of humiliation. The more obvious the lie, the more powerful the display of submission. DeLong writes, "The more plain it is to outsiders that you know there are four fingers, the more convincing is their power and your humiliation when you say that there are five."

Critics might argue that this interpretation is overly cynical, attributing strategic intent to what could be genuine confusion or incompetence. However, DeLong counters this by pointing to Weiss's specific actions following the shelving of the story. She publicly offered to help the 60 Minutes team by tracking down cell numbers for key officials, an offer that DeLong finds absurd given the network's resources. He writes, "There is no way that Bari Weiss does not know that '60 Minutes' has a bigger contacts rolodex database than she does."

The Signal of Complicity

DeLong interprets Weiss's offer of help not as assistance, but as a clear signal of alignment. By stepping in where a professional news team does not need help, she is effectively saying, "I am in the tank. Get in here with me!" This behavior, he argues, is the "icing on the cake" of her performance. It reinforces the narrative that the media is no longer an independent check on power, but a participant in the administration's agenda.

He draws a parallel to the Free Press's reaction to the Department of Justice's handling of the Epstein files, where the outlet sent a reporter to push a narrative that aligned with the administration's interests. DeLong notes, "Every time something from the Free Press brushes past my awareness, it almost invariably turns out to be deeply, stupidly, and even ignorantly wrong." Yet, despite these errors, Weiss succeeds. She has found a paying audience and parlayed that into a television operation.

She is succeeding at a job, even if it is not the one of writer advancing public reason or editor herding a posse of intellectual cats doing same.

The success of this strategy highlights a disturbing reality: in a polarized environment, the ability to signal loyalty to power is more valuable than the ability to report the truth. The audience for this performance is not interested in public reason; they are interested in the spectacle of submission.

Bottom Line

DeLong's most compelling contribution is the shift from judging Weiss by journalistic standards to analyzing her as a political actor performing a specific function. The strongest part of his argument is the use of the "five fingers" analogy to explain why the absurdity of the performance is actually its strength. The biggest vulnerability is the difficulty of proving intent; while the timing is suspicious, the line between incompetence and calculation is often blurred. Readers should watch to see if other media figures begin to adopt similar strategies of performative submission as the administration tightens its grip on regulatory approvals.
