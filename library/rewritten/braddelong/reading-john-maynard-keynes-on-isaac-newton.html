Brad DeLong does something rare: he dismantles the sanitized, marble-bust version of Isaac Newton to reveal a man vibrating with occult intensity and profound isolation. In this commentary on John Maynard Keynes's 1942 essay, DeLong argues that we have been wrong for two centuries about who Newton was, suggesting the great physicist was not the first rationalist of the Enlightenment, but the last of the ancient magicians. This reframing matters because it challenges our modern assumption that scientific progress is a clean march away from mysticism, revealing instead how deeply intuition and esoteric belief fueled the greatest discoveries in human history.

The Last of the Magicians

DeLong leans heavily on Keynes's startling thesis: Newton was not a man of cold, untinctured reason, but a seeker of divine cryptograms. "Newton was not the first of the age of reason," DeLong writes, channeling Keynes. "He was the last of the magicians, the last of the Babylonians and Sumerians, the last great mind which looked out on the visible and intellectual world with the same eyes as those who began to build our intellectual inheritance rather less than 10,000 years ago." This is a powerful corrective to the standard narrative. It forces the listener to reconcile the man who defined gravity with the man who spent over a million words trying to decode the timeline of the Apocalypse.

The argument gains texture when DeLong highlights Newton's psychological profile. He was not a cheerful genius but a "profoundly neurotic" recluse with a "paralyzing fear of exposing his thoughts." DeLong notes that Newton's greatest gift was not his experimental technique, but his ability to hold a problem in his mind for days or weeks until it surrendered. "His peculiar gift was the power of holding continuously in his mind a purely mental problem until he had seen straight through it," DeLong observes. This insight explains why the Principia, our gold standard of geometric rigor, was merely a "dressed-up" version of insights Newton had already intuited years prior. The formal proofs were a shield, not the engine of discovery.

Newton was the last wonderchild to whom the Magi could do sincere and appropriate homage.

Critics might argue that emphasizing Newton's alchemy and theology risks diminishing his scientific legacy, suggesting a false equivalence between his physics and his mysticism. However, DeLong counters this by showing that Newton's "madness" had "extreme method." His theological writings were just as rigorous as his physics; the difference lay only in the premises, not the intellectual discipline. This distinction is crucial—it suggests that the scientific method didn't replace magic; it evolved from the same intense, solitary concentration.

Voyaging Through Strange Seas

The piece becomes most evocative when describing Newton's retreat from the world. DeLong paints a picture of a man who needed total isolation to function, fearing "discordant breakings in or criticism" more than failure. "He believed that by the same powers of his introspective imagination he would read the riddle of the Godhead... if only he could persevere to the end, uninterrupted, by himself," DeLong writes. This context sheds new light on Newton's later move to London in 1696. It wasn't just a career promotion; it was a rescue mission. His friends, recognizing he was teetering on the edge of madness, pulled him back into human society.

This biographical detail connects to the broader historical context of the Royal Mint. When Newton took the post as Master of the Mint, he wasn't just trading quills for coins; he was trading the "strange seas of thought" for the concrete, social reality of catching counterfeiters. The transition marked the end of his "magician" phase and the beginning of his public life as an Enlightenment grandee. DeLong suggests that without this intervention, the world might have lost the Principia entirely, or at least seen it buried under an avalanche of unpublished theological ramblings.

The author also touches on the friction Newton caused with his peers, noting his "ignoble quarrels" with figures like Robert Hooke and Gottfried Leibniz. These conflicts were not petty squabbles but symptoms of a man who viewed his discoveries as sacred, half-forbidden secrets. "Like all his type he was wholly aloof from women," DeLong notes, citing the historical record of Newton's bachelorhood and his intense, singular focus. This isolation was the price of his genius, a trade-off that allowed him to see what others could not.

The Intuition of the Invisible

DeLong's analysis ultimately rests on the nature of discovery itself. He challenges the idea that science is purely deductive or experimental. Instead, he presents Newton as a man who knew the answer before he had the proof. The famous story of Newton telling Edmond Halley, "I've known it for years... If you'll give me a few days, I'll certainly find you a proof," illustrates this perfectly. The geometry was the packaging; the intuition was the product.

This perspective reframes the entire history of science. It suggests that the "rationalist" label we slap on the Enlightenment era is a retrospective simplification. The real engine of progress was often a kind of disciplined madness, a willingness to stare into the void until it blinked. "He was, in his rôle as physicist, not one of those who started with rock-solid premises and sought to derive from them by logical deduction," DeLong argues. He was a man who trusted his "muscles of intuition" above all else.

The proofs, for what they are worth, were dressed up afterwards - they were not the instrument of discovery.

Bottom Line

DeLong's commentary succeeds in humanizing a figure often treated as a statue, revealing the messy, fearful, and mystical man behind the laws of motion. The strongest part of the argument is the demonstration that Newton's scientific rigor and his occult obsessions were not separate tracks, but the same intense cognitive process applied to different riddles. The vulnerability lies in the risk of romanticizing isolation, but DeLong wisely frames it as a dangerous necessity rather than a virtue to be emulated. For the busy listener, this piece offers a profound reminder: the greatest leaps in human understanding often come not from clear, cold logic, but from the willingness to voyage alone through strange seas of thought.
