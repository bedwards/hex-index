Brad DeLong dissects a startling cultural moment where the glorification of ancient conquest is weaponized as a critique of modern historical scholarship. The piece is not merely about Alexander the Great; it is a forensic examination of how the language of "Western civilization" is being deployed to shield violent imperialists from moral scrutiny. DeLong argues that the real story here is not a loss of tradition, but a refusal to imagine the victims of empire as human beings.

The Battle Over Memory

The catalyst for DeLong's analysis is a volatile exchange involving James W. Hankins, a Harvard professor, and historian Bret Devereaux. Hankins recently lamented that Western history is "phased out or allowed to die on the vine" at elite institutions, claiming his colleagues view the canon as "maximally 'white supremacist'." DeLong notes the intellectual whiplash in Hankins's subsequent attempts to walk back these claims, first accusing colleagues of "obeisance to global history" and then suddenly praising them as "truly outstanding learned scholars."

DeLong finds this inconsistency revealing. He suggests the real friction isn't about curriculum breadth, but about the moral lens through which history is viewed. Hankins defends Alexander by arguing that modern critics are "too petty" and "stunted in our outlook" to appreciate greatness. He quotes the ancient historian Arrian to suggest that anyone who reproaches Alexander should reflect on their own "lesser" status. DeLong, however, sees this as a dangerous rhetorical trap. He points out that Hankins ignores the evolution of scholarship that has moved beyond ancient biographers who were often courtiers themselves.

"Once you imagine Alexander's victims as people against whom Alexander is launching an unprovoked invasion, it becomes really hard to like Alexander, because as a society we no longer measure greatness by who is the best at killing."

This framing is the piece's most potent contribution. DeLong argues that the ancient sources Hankins relies on, such as Arrian, were written by men deeply embedded in imperial power structures. Arrian, for instance, served in the court of Emperor Hadrian; writing a scathing critique of Alexander could have been read as a critique of the emperor himself. DeLong notes that this context is "basic enough to be acknowledged as a matter of course" in modern historiography, yet Hankins treats the ancient texts as if they were written "beyond fear or favor."

The Human Cost of "Greatness"

DeLong's commentary sharpens when he addresses the specific atrocities Hankins glosses over. Hankins admits to the "annihilation of Thebes" and the "slaughter and enslavement of enemies," but dismisses them as "normal practices in ancient warfare." He suggests that modern shock at these events is a form of "moral myopia," comparing it to the acceptance of firebombing in World War II. DeLong rejects this moral equivalency. He argues that recognizing the horror of these acts is not a failure of imagination, but a triumph of moral progress.

The piece highlights how Hankins attempts to elevate Alexander's character by focusing on his "remorse" and his love of Homer. Hankins writes, "Alexander, in other words, according to ancient ideas had the perfect formation to be a philosopher-king." DeLong counters that this ignores the core theme of later ancient accounts, including Arrian's own, which suggest that "imperial power corrupted and degraded his once admirable character." The shift in scholarship, championed by historians like Ernst Badian, is not about being "anti-Western," but about taking the suffering of the conquered seriously.

"That isn't 'standing in a well, unable to see the sky is wide' but rather, standing at the foot of a giant pile of corpses and, at long last, refusing to declare it a great work of art."

DeLong points out the irony that Hankins, while decrying the loss of "Western civilization," is actually promoting a very specific, narrow version of it—one that equates moral worth with military success. He notes that even the ancient poet Pindar, whom Hankins cites to praise the "divine element" in Alexander, wrote of human life as a "dream of a shadow." DeLong suggests that Hankins misses the point: the ancients knew their heroes were flawed, but modern scholars have the luxury of seeing the full human cost of their victories.

Critics might argue that DeLong is imposing modern sensibilities on an ancient context where war was indeed the primary metric of success. However, DeLong's point is not to judge the past by today's standards, but to refuse the modern glorification of that past. The argument holds that we can study Alexander's military genius without endorsing his atrocities or the ideology that calls them "greatness."

The Politics of the Canon

The commentary also touches on the broader political stakes. DeLong mentions Adrian Vermeule, a legal scholar who endorsed Hankins's view, yet simultaneously dismissed the language of "Western civilization" as a "destructive rhetorical flame thrower." DeLong finds this contradiction fascinating, noting that Vermeule prefers the term "Dover Circle" to describe the cultural sphere. This suggests that the debate is not about history itself, but about who gets to define the narrative of the West.

DeLong writes, "The idea that Arrian's history in particular was influenced by his context isn't particularly new." He emphasizes that Hankins's failure to engage with decades of scholarship on Alexander's victims—specifically the work of Badian and Bosworth—is a deliberate choice. It is a choice to maintain a myth of the "philosopher-king" rather than confront the reality of the "murderous psychopath" who launched unprovoked wars for glory.

"We are shocked by these things, and by the failure of the ancients to condemn them, forgetting that our grandfathers saw no crime in the fire-bombing of German cities from the air or the nuclear incineration of two Japanese cities filled with innocent civilians."

DeLong uses this comparison not to excuse ancient violence, but to highlight the danger of normalizing it. If we accept that "greatness" is defined by the scale of one's conquests, we risk repeating the same moral failures in the present. The piece serves as a warning against the seduction of power, urging readers to look at the "pile of corpses" rather than the statue on top.

Bottom Line

Brad DeLong's commentary succeeds in reframing a debate about curriculum into a profound moral inquiry about how we value human life. The strongest part of the argument is its insistence that historical empathy for victims is not a modern bias, but a necessary corrective to ancient propaganda. The biggest vulnerability lies in the potential for this moral clarity to be dismissed as presentism by those who insist on judging history solely by the standards of the time. However, DeLong's verdict is clear: we must stop measuring greatness by the efficiency of killing, and start measuring it by the capacity for justice.
