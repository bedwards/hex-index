Brad DeLong tackles the most enduring myth in political economy: the idea that history moves in a straight line toward a utopian revolution. In a piece that feels less like a history lesson and more like a forensic audit of 19th-century theory, he dissects Karl Marx's 1859 Preface to separate the useful sociology from the "millenarian theological fantasy." For anyone trying to understand why the world is changing so fast right now, yet feels so stubbornly stuck in old political patterns, DeLong offers a crucial distinction: technology drives the economy, but it does not guarantee a revolution.

The Six Threads of a Broken Promise

DeLong begins by acknowledging that the 1859 Preface is often treated as a sacred text, yet he argues it contains six distinct claims that must be evaluated separately. He notes that while the text promises an imminent end to exploitation, the evidence since 1870 points to something far messier. "The evidence since 1870 points to rotating, sectoral upheavals—not synchronized social revolutions: sectoral churn and institutional lag," DeLong writes. This reframing is vital because it moves the discussion away from the binary of "capitalism will collapse tomorrow" to the more realistic "capitalism is constantly being reshaped by new tech, often with a lag." He suggests that the Marxist apparatus, stripped of its utopian theology, still explains a great deal about how economies function, even if it fails to predict the timing of political change.

"Historical materialism, soft-true; stage theory and millenarianism, not so much."

DeLong's analysis of the "Stage Theory of History" is particularly sharp. He challenges the rigid sequence of tribal, ancient, feudal, and bourgeois modes of production, arguing that these categories are too static to capture the speed of modern change. Instead, he proposes a new metric based on a "human technological capability index," suggesting that the shift from the steampower era to the mass-production era was more profound than the transition from feudalism to capitalism. "My rough guesstimations as to how different the techno-economic underpinnings are at a global scale gets us something more like: Attention info-bio tech: 2040... Mass-production: 1960," he posits. This quantitative approach forces the reader to confront the sheer velocity of the current AI and bio-tech revolutions, which may be rendering our traditional historical categories obsolete.

Critics might argue that reducing history to a "technological capability index" ignores the role of culture, ideology, and human agency in shaping societal transitions. After all, the Industrial Revolution was not just about steam engines; it was about the social upheaval that followed. However, DeLong's point is that the material conditions set the boundaries of what is possible, even if they don't dictate the exact outcome.

The Superstructure and the Reality of Conflict

The core of DeLong's argument rests on the relationship between the economic base and the legal or political superstructure. He quotes Marx directly to highlight the original claim: "It is not the consciousness of men that determines their existence, but their social existence that determines their consciousness." DeLong accepts this as "soft-true," arguing that human relations of production must be fitted to the technology of society to remain stable. However, he rejects the idea that this fit always leads to a clean, revolutionary break. "The reworking does not have to be driven by anything one would call 'social revolution'," he asserts, noting that institutional change often happens through slow, grinding adaptation rather than sudden upheaval.

"To pick up one (or more) of these six claims and then to try to develop it and demonstrate its truth... is what it is to be a 'Marxist' in any sense meaningful."

Here, DeLong makes a bold claim about intellectual integrity. He suggests that anyone who identifies as a Marxist without engaging with these specific analytical threads is merely engaging in tribal signaling. This is a provocative stance, especially in an era where political labels are often worn as fashion statements rather than analytical tools. He further argues that the "millenarian" aspect of Marx's work—the belief that history has an arrow pointing toward justice—is simply "nonsense on stilts." The arc of history, he contends, does not bend toward justice; it bends toward whatever technology allows, for better or worse.

This perspective resonates with the concept of "creative destruction" discussed in DeLong's companion deep dives, where he notes that technological progress often destroys old industries and social structures without necessarily creating a more equitable society. The 1859 Preface promised that the contradictions of capitalism would lead to a solution, but DeLong points out that history shows the contradictions often just shift shape. "History and its conflicts are sometimes not about forces and relations of production at all, but are really about other things," he writes, citing religious wars as examples where the material base was not the primary driver of conflict.

"The arc of history does not bend toward justice, or even prosperity."

This is a sobering reminder for those looking for a guaranteed path to a better future. DeLong's analysis suggests that the "social revolution" Marx predicted is not an inevitable law of physics, but a contingent event that requires specific material conditions and human agency to occur. Without those conditions, the "superstructure" may remain rigid, even as the economic base shifts beneath it.

Bottom Line

Brad DeLong's dissection of the 1859 Preface is a masterclass in separating useful analytical tools from ideological baggage. His strongest move is reframing historical materialism as a "soft-true" constraint rather than a deterministic law, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of why modern societies struggle to adapt to rapid technological change. The argument's biggest vulnerability is its potential to downplay the role of human agency and political will in driving change, but it remains a necessary corrective to the fatalism that often plagues discussions of economic history. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: technology drives the economy, but it does not write the script for our future.
