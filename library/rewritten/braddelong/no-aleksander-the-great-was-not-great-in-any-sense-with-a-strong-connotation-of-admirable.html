This piece is a masterclass in dismantling a dangerous historical revisionism that seeks to excuse mass violence by attributing it to divine favor. Brad DeLong does not merely critique a biography; he exposes how a specific intellectual framework can be weaponized to rehabilitate the image of a conqueror whose legacy is built on the slaughter of civilians. The argument is urgent because it reveals how easily the language of "greatness" can be detached from human suffering when the observer refuses to see the victims as people.

The Theological Shield

The core of the controversy lies in James Hankins's claim that Alexander's extraordinary success was not a result of strategy or luck, but of direct divine intervention. Brad DeLong highlights the absurdity of this position, noting that Hankins believes "the ancients saw [divine favor] but... we fail to see, or... prefer not to see." DeLong's reaction is visceral: he suggests the author should "put down the keyboard, back up away from the internet, and not return." This is not hyperbole; it is a necessary rejection of a worldview that sanctifies conquest.

The danger here is that by framing Alexander's atrocities as the result of a "gleam of splendour given of heaven," the author removes moral agency from the historical actor. As DeLong points out, this allows Hankins to dismiss the annihilation of Thebes and the enslavement of surrendered enemies as merely "normal practices in ancient warfare." This framing is deeply problematic because it treats the destruction of cities and the death of thousands as acceptable collateral damage in the pursuit of a "great soul." It is a classic example of how elite discourse can sanitize violence by elevating the perpetrator to a mythic status.

Once you imagine Alexander’s victims as people against whom Alexander is launching an unprovoked invasion, it becomes really hard to like Alexander, because as a society we no longer measure greatness by who is the best at killing.

DeLong argues that the modern refusal to admire Alexander is not a failure of vision, but a moral evolution. The shift in scholarship, led by historians like Ernst Badian, forces us to confront the reality that Alexander launched "unprovoked wars, for what seems to be the glory and fun of it." This is a crucial distinction that Hankins ignores. The ancient sources, such as Arrian, were written in a specific political context where criticizing Alexander could be seen as a critique of the current emperor, Hadrian. DeLong notes that Arrian's work, far from being a neutral account, contains a "fairly clear theme" that "imperial power corrupted and degraded his once admirable character." By ignoring this context, Hankins presents a distorted view of history that serves a modern ideological agenda.

The Cost of "Greatness"

The article's most powerful moment comes when DeLong contrasts the ancient admiration for Alexander with the modern understanding of the human cost. Hankins quotes the Chinese poet Han Yu, suggesting that modern critics are like "a man living at the bottom of a well" who thinks the sky is small. DeLong turns this metaphor on its head. He argues that the real myopia lies in failing to see the "giant pile of corpses" at the foot of Alexander's achievements.

The argument that Alexander's "obsession with his own image" was justified because the gods rewarded him with success is a dangerous precedent. It implies that success is the ultimate metric of virtue, regardless of the methods used to achieve it. DeLong writes, "The Greeks and Romans found that admirable, but we don't need to." This is a rejection of the idea that history is a linear progression toward greater virtue. Instead, it suggests that we have the capacity to learn from the past and refuse to glorify those who caused immense suffering.

Critics might argue that applying modern moral standards to ancient figures is anachronistic and unhelpful. However, DeLong counters that this is not about judging the past by today's laws, but about refusing to let the past dictate our current values. The comparison to Adolf Hitler and Genghis Khan, which Hankins dismisses as a modern bias, is actually a necessary tool for understanding the scale of Alexander's violence. As DeLong notes, the "atrocities for which he was responsible" include the "slaughter and enslavement of enemies who had surrendered to him," actions that are abhorrent to any moral framework that values human life.

We are shocked by these things, and by the failure of the ancients to condemn them, forgetting that our grandfathers saw no crime in the fire-bombing of German cities from the air or the nuclear incineration of two Japanese cities filled with innocent civilians.

This comparison is not meant to equate the moral failings of different eras, but to highlight how easily societies can normalize extreme violence. The "moral myopias of our own time" are real, but they are not the same as the "moral blindness of the ancients." The difference is that we have the tools to see the victims clearly, and we have a choice about whether to glorify their killers.

Bottom Line

Brad DeLong's commentary is a vital correction to a trend that seeks to rehabilitate the image of historical tyrants by attributing their violence to divine will. The strongest part of the argument is its insistence on centering the victims of Alexander's campaigns, reminding us that "greatness" measured in conquest is often just a euphemism for mass murder. The biggest vulnerability in the opposing view is its reliance on a theological framework that excuses atrocities as the price of greatness. As we navigate a world where the language of power is often used to justify violence, this piece serves as a necessary reminder that true greatness cannot be built on a foundation of corpses.
