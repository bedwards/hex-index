Brad DeLong dismantles the comforting myth of a continuous "Western Civilization" with a historian's precision and a provocateur's flair, arguing that what we call the "West" is actually a recent, geographically specific accident of history centered on a 300-mile radius around Dover, England. For busy readers navigating a fractured global order, this isn't just semantic nitpicking; it is a necessary correction to the ideological baggage that has long justified imperial overreach and blinded us to the true sources of modern prosperity. DeLong suggests that clinging to the idea of a torch passed from Gilgamesh to Silicon Valley is not just historically inaccurate, but politically dangerous.

The Myth of the Unbroken Torch

The core of DeLong's argument is a rejection of the "Olympic-torch relay race" narrative that dominates popular history. He writes, "There was no single torch. There were no hand-offs." Instead of a smooth lineage from ancient Mesopotamia to modern democracy, DeLong points to a chaotic, discontinuous history where the "Western core" has moved like a bubble, popping and reforming across continents. He leans on the work of Stanford historian Ian Morris to illustrate this instability, noting that for nearly all of agricultural history, the true "Eastern core" was the Yellow and Yangtze River valleys, while the "Western core" was a shifting mess of locations from Basra to Thebes, then to Italy, and finally to the American Northeast.

This framing is powerful because it strips away the moral superiority often attached to the term "West." DeLong argues that the continuity we imagine is an illusion: "What has the civilization of Uruk in -3000 have to do with the civilization of Silicon Valley today?" The only link, he suggests, is that modern academics read the Epic of Gilgamesh, a text that the ancient people of the Thames Valley would have found alien. This is a crucial intervention for a world where policy is often justified by appeals to a shared, ancient heritage that never actually existed.

Critics might argue that discarding the "Western" label throws out valuable cultural cohesion, but DeLong counters that the label itself was a construct of the early 20th century, designed to immunize society against fascism. He quotes his former teacher, Judith Shklar, who observed that the post-WWII push for "Western Civilization" was an ideological project aimed at "constructing a fake but usable past for a post-World War II New Deal Order." The goal was to present fascism as an aberration rather than a potential outcome of the very systems being celebrated.

"They wanted a different past, a 'good' West, a 'real' West, not the actual one that had marched into the First World War and onward."

The Dover Circle: Geography Over Destiny

If the "West" is a myth, what is the reality? DeLong proposes a new, more precise term: the "Dover Circle-Plus." He defines this as "those economies and societies that are, that have received very large settler inflows from, or that have strained every nerve to emulate the particular economic structures and patterns and practices that developed in the years after 1500 in a 300-mile radius circle centered on the port of Dover."

This shift from cultural identity to institutional geography is the piece's most significant contribution. DeLong argues that the explosion of wealth and power was not the result of some inherent "Western" superiority, but rather a specific set of institutional developments that occurred in a small pocket of Europe. He highlights two key factors: the Medieval Church's war on cousin marriage, which created "diffuse sociability" and allowed strangers to trust one another, and the unique legal traditions described by Harold J. Berman. These were not universal values; they were local accidents that happened to scale.

The argument effectively debunks the idea that these societies were always destined to rule. DeLong notes that as late as 1500, the Dover Circle was "no longer a backwater" but held "no place special in world civilization." It had a narrow edge in ships and gunpowder, perhaps a "1.1 over the other high civilizations of Eurasia," but nothing that guaranteed dominance. The Ottoman Empire was still besieging Vienna in 1688, and the Omani from Muscat ruled the Indian Ocean coasts. The rise of the Dover Circle was not inevitable; it was a specific historical convergence.

"In the -50s, Roman Senator and Proconsul Marcus Tullius Cicero snidely snarked argued that the Britons had no silver and were too stupid and uneducated to make good slaves—hence they were not worth imperializing."

The Danger of the "Good" Past

DeLong is particularly sharp when addressing why we cling to the "West" label despite the evidence. He suggests it is a form of collective self-deception born from the trauma of the World Wars. The "Western Civilization" curriculum at Harvard in the 1950s, he argues, was a reaction to the horror of the 1940s, a way to sanitize history and create a narrative where democracy and capitalism were the natural, inevitable endpoints of human progress.

He contrasts this with the reality of the "Eastern Core," which maintained a continuity of political and genetic descent that the "Western Core" never achieved. "If you want continuity starting at the Western edge of Eurasia of the same kind as you see in the 'Eastern Core'—political, cultural, linked chains of influence, some continuity of genetic descent—you certainly cannot start before 800, and almost surely not before 1500." This distinction forces readers to confront the fact that the modern global order is built on a very recent, very specific set of institutions, not an ancient, unbroken tradition.

The piece also pushes back against the idea that the "West" is a monolith. DeLong notes that even within the Dover Circle, there were deep divisions. Athens had little tolerance for Jerusalem, and Jerusalem had even less for Athens. The idea of a unified "Western" identity is a modern invention, one that obscures the violent and fragmented reality of European history.

Bottom Line

DeLong's "Dover Circle" framework is a necessary corrective to the lazy essentialism of "Western Civilization," offering a more accurate, institutional explanation for modern prosperity. However, the argument risks underestimating the political utility of the "West" label as a unifying force in an increasingly fragmented world; while historically imprecise, the myth of continuity may still serve a functional purpose in maintaining alliances. Readers should watch for how this institutional lens is applied to current global challenges, particularly as the "Dover Circle-Plus" faces competition from societies that are rapidly emulating its economic structures without adopting its cultural myths.

"There was no single torch. There were no hand-offs. And if there had been a single torch, it would come with all kinds of things that we do not like at all.""
