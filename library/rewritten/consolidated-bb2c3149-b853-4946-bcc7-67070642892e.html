Two recent lectures, one from Andrew Henry of Religion For Breakfast and another from a Yale Courses session on the Gospel of Thomas, converge on a single uncomfortable fact: the Christianity that survived into the canon was only one option among many. Read together, they form a kind of stereo recording of early Christian strangeness — Henry zooming in on the ritual machinery of the Gospel of Philip, the Yale lecturer pulling back to show how texts like Thomas reveal that there was never a single Christianity to begin with, only competing christianities jostling for survival.

The Missing Word Is Not the Point

Henry opens with the line everyone knows and almost no one understands. "The companion of the Savior is Mary Magdalene," he reads, "He loved her more than all the disciples, and he used to kiss her often on her," — and then the manuscript breaks. "Did it say mouth, face, forehead, feet? We don't know," he admits, before brushing the question aside as a distraction. The Gospel of Philip, he insists, "is far less interested in romance than in rituals and less interested in who kissed whom and more about how a person becomes a Christ."

That pivot is the entire argument. Philip is not a love story with a hole punched in it; it is a technical manual for deification, a "hodgepodge of material strung together with no obvious system" that Henry suspects was assembled as a working notebook of ritual ideas. The damaged page is a Rorschach test for modern readers; the actual text is a blueprint for becoming divine.

Two Bodies, Two Births, Two Fathers

The strangeness compounds when Philip turns to Jesus himself. Henry quotes the author's blunt rejection of the virgin birth: "Some say that Mary conceived by the Holy Spirit. They are wrong. They do not know what they are saying. When did a female ever conceive by a female?" In the Semitic linguistic world the text inhabits, the Holy Spirit is grammatically feminine, and Philip refuses to let theology paper over grammar. Jesus, in this telling, has a biological father in Joseph and a spiritual father in God — and crucially, two bodies.

The second body arrives at the baptism. "He who was begotten before all things was begotten again," Henry reads. "On that day he came out from the bridal chamber like the one who came into being from the bridegroom and the bride." Henry's gloss is precise: "According to the birth metaphor, the union between Jesus and the Holy Spirit issues in a new body for the human Jesus. He must therefore possess two bodies." This is not adoptionism, where a human is promoted to divinity. It is something stranger — an ontological doubling, a second self generated through ritual union.

The world came into being from a transgression. For the one who made it wanted to make himself imperishable and immortal. He perished and he did not obtain what he hoped for.

The Yale Frame: There Was Never One Christianity

The Yale lecturer, working through the Gospel of Thomas, supplies the wider angle Henry's close reading needs. "One of the themes of the course, maybe the main theme of the course, is the diversities of early Christianity," the lecturer says, before correcting the phrase mid-sentence: "a lot of scholars like to talk about not Christianity in the first 100 years but christianities." The plural is the entire point. Thomas, like Philip, was edited out of the canon, and its rediscovery in the twentieth century "created something of a sensation" precisely because it did not match the Jesus most modern readers had been handed.

Thomas offers its own startling claim: that Jesus had a twin brother named Didymus Judas Thomas. The lecturer pauses to note the linguistic joke buried in the name — "Didymus is simply the Greek word for twin," and also, with a dry aside, "the Greek word for testicles, for obvious reasons." The detail is throwaway, but it points at something Henry's analysis of Philip makes explicit: these texts are obsessed with doubling. A twin brother for Jesus in Thomas. A second body for Jesus in Philip. A believer who is "no longer a Christian but a Christ." The pattern is not coincidence; it is the shared grammar of a Christianity that believed identity was permeable and transferable.

Becoming the Christ

Where the Yale lecture maps the territory, Henry's reading of Philip names the destination. "A person is no longer a Christian but a Christ," he quotes — and this, he argues, is the engine of the entire text. The bridal chamber rituals are not metaphors for a distant heaven but instructions for replicating, in the body of the initiate, the same transformation Jesus underwent at his baptism. "What happens to him is exactly what's supposed to happen to every single Christian."

The Gospel of Philip is far less interested in romance than in rituals and less interested in who kissed whom and more about how a person becomes a Christ.

Set against the Yale lecturer's insistence on plural christianities, this democratization of divinity stops looking like a fringe heresy and starts looking like one perfectly coherent answer to a question the early movement was openly debating: what, exactly, is salvation for? The canonical gospels answer with forgiveness and resurrection. Philip answers with metamorphosis. Thomas answers with secret knowledge and twinship. None of these answers were obviously wrong in the second century; they were simply the ones that lost.

The Counterpoint Neither Source Raises

Both lectures are generous to their texts, and neither pauses to ask the harder question: if Philip and Thomas are so theologically rich, why did they lose? The honest answer is probably institutional rather than spiritual. A theology in which every believer becomes a Christ is impossible to govern. A bishop cannot mediate a sacrament whose entire purpose is to dissolve the distinction between mediator and initiate. The canon that survived was the canon that could be administered — gospels with clear narratives, clear authorities, and a clear hierarchy between the divine son and his merely human followers. Philip's permeable boundary between human and Christ was not just strange; it was ungovernable. That, more than any doctrinal dispute, may explain why the manuscript ended up in a jar in the Egyptian desert instead of on a lectern in Rome.

It is also worth noting what the Yale lecturer only gestures at: the rediscovery of these texts in the twentieth century did not just expand the academic record. It rewired how educated readers imagine the first Christian centuries. The Da Vinci Code phenomenon Henry rolls his eyes at is downstream of a real scholarly revolution — the recognition that the winners wrote the canon, and that the losers, when their words survive, often turn out to have been asking more interesting questions.

Bottom Line

Henry's close reading of Philip and the Yale lecture on Thomas are doing the same work from opposite ends of the telescope. One shows the ritual machinery of a single radical text; the other shows the wider field of competing christianities in which such a text felt normal. Together they argue, more persuasively than either could alone, that the Christianity most modern readers inherit is not the original article but a survivor — chosen, edited, and institutionalized out of a much weirder and more democratic set of possibilities. The missing word in Philip will probably never be recovered. The missing christianities, thanks to scholars like Henry and the Yale lecturer, are slowly being read back into the record.
