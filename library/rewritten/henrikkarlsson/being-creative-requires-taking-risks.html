In an era where artificial intelligence and human expertise alike risk converging into a single, predictable voice, Henrik Karlsson offers a startling diagnosis: we are all suffering from "mode collapse." This isn't just a technical glitch in generative algorithms; it is a cultural and psychological stagnation where the pursuit of "good enough" kills the very novelty that defines a creative life. Karlsson argues that the path forward requires not just more data, but a deliberate, often painful willingness to risk reputation and income to stay interesting.

The Trap of the Algorithmic Self

Karlsson begins by drawing a sharp parallel between childhood development and machine learning. He notes that children naturally produce wild, diverse imagery until about age five, when they learn to replicate established symbols like stick figures and triangular roofs. "The wild diversity is gone," he writes, describing how this shift toward "crude, habitual symbolism" mirrors what happens in machine learning when a model stops exploring and starts optimizing for a narrow set of rewards.

The author extends this analogy to the current state of language models, observing that while early versions were "deranged but could write spectacularly surprising prose," today's systems are smarter yet stylistically uniform. "And honestly? That isn't just sad—it's stylistic trauma," Karlsson asserts. He suggests that just as a model learns to avoid tokens that might lower its reward score, humans learn to avoid ideas that might disrupt their career stability or social standing. Once we learn that grass is "supposed" to be green, we lose the ability to see the blue or red hues that reality actually presents.

This framing is potent because it reframes creative stagnation not as a lack of talent, but as a rational response to a reward system that punishes deviation. However, critics might argue that this comparison oversimplifies the human capacity for self-correction; unlike an algorithm, humans can theoretically choose to ignore their training data without a technical reset. Yet, Karlsson counters this by pointing to the psychological weight of reputation, noting that "the opportunity cost of remaining uncollapsed increases" as one becomes more skilled.

Once we've learned that grass "is" green, it becomes almost embarrassing to make it blue, even though real grass often is blue, as good painters learn when they start to pay closer attention to reality.

The High Cost of Staying Interesting

The core of Karlsson's argument shifts from diagnosis to prescription, examining how individuals can actively prevent this collapse. He cites Nabeel Qureshi's observation that interesting people "design their lives to avoid [mode collapse] happening to them" by constantly exposing themselves to contradictory information and novel experiences. Karlsson agrees, but he highlights the brutal reality: this gets harder the older you get.

He illustrates this with the career of Brian Eno, a musician who has remained vital for fifty years by repeatedly abandoning his successes. Karlsson details how Eno, after finding fame with Roxy Music, quit the band to play classical music with "an orchestra consisting of only bad musicians," a move that exasperated his manager. Later, after inventing ambient music, he turned down lucrative offers to work on light art in Thailand. "Eno is able to remain interesting because he's willing to risk everything he's achieved again and again," Karlsson writes.

This section serves as a powerful counter-narrative to the modern obsession with scaling and optimization. While the business world often rewards doubling down on what works, Karlsson suggests that true creativity requires a "high-risk strategy" that looks like self-sabotage to the uninitiated. He acknowledges the survivorship bias here—most people who blow up their careers don't get a second act—but argues that for those wired for deep creative work, the alternative is a slow slide into irrelevance.

Critics might note that Eno's ability to take such risks is a privilege not available to everyone; his early successes provided a financial safety net that allows him to experiment without the threat of starvation. Karlsson concedes this, admitting that "it is very, very important that some people optimize for money," but insists that for the specific goal of avoiding creative collapse, one must be willing to sacrifice other things.

If you have a hit and can build up some savings, that is meant to fund bigger risks going forward, not keeping up with the Joneses.

The Fear of the Unknown

Karlsson turns the lens inward, applying his own theory to his career choices in 2024. He lists several potential projects, from writing a book to compiling an essay collection, but identifies a specific fear around doing reportage and interviews in the style of Werner Herzog. He admits that this path scares him because it is a "different process than the one I'm used to" and risks eroding the steady stream of essays that pays the bills.

He describes the internal conflict of the "mode collapse" fear: "I might end up plowing months into it, and come out on the other end with little to show for it." Yet, he concludes that this fear is actually a good sign, indicating he is pushing against the boundaries of his comfort zone. The author resolves to try things in "small, low-cost ways," carving out time for "illegible projects" alongside the work that keeps the food on the table.

This personal reflection grounds the abstract theory in tangible reality. It moves the discussion from the theoretical danger of AI homogenization to the immediate, personal risk of a writer choosing to explore the unknown. The reference to the No Wave movement of late 1970s New York, which Eno helped midwife, serves as a historical anchor here—a reminder that the most influential art often emerges from a deliberate rejection of the established "mode."

The playful wildness that made them successful gets replaced by increasingly hollow repetition as they struggle to maintain the career they worked so hard to achieve.

Bottom Line

Karlsson's most compelling contribution is his redefinition of success not as stability, but as a continuous, risky engagement with the unknown. While the argument relies heavily on the exceptional case of Brian Eno, the underlying principle—that safety is the enemy of novelty—resonates deeply in an age of algorithmic optimization. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its assumption that readers have the agency to reject lucrative, safe paths, a luxury not universally available. Nevertheless, the call to intentionally court discomfort and risk failure remains a vital antidote to the creeping stagnation of the modern creative landscape.
