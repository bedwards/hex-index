Matt Stoller delivers a startling diagnosis for the modern American economy: the very concept of a "fair price" has been legally dismantled, leaving consumers navigating a labyrinth of hidden fees and discriminatory rates. This isn't just about healthcare costs; it is a structural collapse of the market transparency that has underpinned American commerce for a century. Stoller argues that we have moved from a society where a price tag was a public promise to one where price is a secret weapon used to consolidate power and extract wealth from the vulnerable.

The Death of the Price Tag

Stoller opens with a deceptively simple cultural touchstone: the 1956 game show The Price Is Right. He uses this to highlight a fundamental shift in our economic reality. "There's an important political assumption behind that question, which is that every retail item has one single price," Stoller writes. "And any buyer anywhere in America can pay that price to acquire it." This assumption, he argues, was never natural; it was a legal construct designed to ensure fairness. By invoking the game show, Stoller effectively illustrates how alien the idea of a single, public price has become to the average person today.

The author traces the origins of this transparency to John Wanamaker, the 19th-century retail magnate who invented the price tag. Before Wanamaker, pricing was a haggling game based on power and relationships. Stoller notes that Wanamaker's innovation was revolutionary because it democratized information. "Before Wanamaker, shoppers haggled with merchants, and paid based on relationships and power," Stoller explains. "It was a more localized economy, so fairness was inherently embedded in closer relationships, but pricing for giant systems... were highly contested and political." This historical context is crucial; it reminds us that the current opacity is not an inevitable result of market forces, but a regression to a pre-modern, predatory system.

We no longer live in such a society, because the legal framework behind a single public price for an item has fallen apart.

The Pharmacy Benefit Manager Shell Game

The piece pivots sharply to the most egregious example of this breakdown: the pharmacy benefit manager (PBM) industry. Stoller dissects how three massive entities—CVS, UnitedHealth Group, and Cigna—have turned drug pricing into a multi-billion dollar opacity scheme. He points to a recent investigative report by Hunterbrook, which alleges these companies collude with drugmakers to inflate prices rather than negotiate them down. "There are no real prices in most health care markets," Stoller asserts. "You couldn't play The Price Is Right for medicine, because there is no actual one price for anything."

To demonstrate the absurdity of the current system, Stoller cites the case of Gleevec, a cancer drug. Depending on the pharmacy or delivery method, the same drug could cost $97, $9,000, or nearly $20,000. "We've created plan designs to aggressively steer customers to home delivery where the drug cost is ~200 times higher," Stoller quotes an executive admitting to the strategy. This is not a market inefficiency; it is a deliberate design to maximize revenue through confusion. The mechanism relies on rebates, where PBMs take a cut of the inflated list price, creating a perverse incentive to keep prices high.

Critics might argue that complex rebate structures allow for lower net prices for large insurers, theoretically benefiting the system as a whole. However, Stoller dismantles this by showing that the savings rarely reach the patient, who often pays copays based on the inflated list price. The system has evolved into a shell game. "They just renamed those rebates 'fees' and paid those 'fees' to different subsidiaries with different names than the one negotiating drug prices," Stoller writes, noting that these subsidiaries are often located in tax havens like Switzerland. The sheer complexity is the point. "It's all very complex, intentionally, and it's designed to confuse people into not understanding what looks like a shell game."

The Legal Erosion of Fairness

Stoller then takes a deep dive into the legal history that allowed this to happen, contrasting the populist movements of the late 19th century with the neoliberal turn of the 1980s. He reminds readers that the fight against price discrimination was once a bipartisan consensus. The Grangers and populists fought railroad rebates, and even the conservative Heritage Foundation in 1980 argued against "selective price gouging." "The think tank attacked airline regulators for allowing a discount to a large buyer as 'contrary to basic American precepts of justice,'" Stoller notes. This historical irony is potent: the very principles of fair pricing once championed by the right have been eroded by the same ideological shift that championed deregulation.

The turning point, Stoller identifies, was the rise of the Chicago School of economics and its influence on antitrust law. Scholars like Robert Bork argued that price discrimination was efficient and even progressive. This intellectual shift led to concrete policy changes. In 1987, Congress created a "safe harbor" for group buying entities to accept rebates, effectively legalizing the conflict of interest where the buyer's agent is paid by the seller. "Enabling kickbacks created a clear conflict of interest, since a PBM is supposed to be negotiating for the buyer, but could now be paid by the seller," Stoller writes. He compares this to a lawyer taking money from the opposing party—a clear ethical breach that was codified into law.

To draw an analogy, imagine if a lawyer you hired to represent you in a lawsuit could also take money from the person you were suing. We all know that's unethical and would lead to your lawyer undermining your interests.

The consequences of this legal shift are staggering. Stoller points out that today, a single PBM can maintain over ten thousand different price lists for the same drug, updated daily. This means the price of a medication depends not on its value or cost to produce, but on the specific contractual web surrounding the buyer. "The price of a particular drug depends on who you are, when you bought it, other drugs you might have purchased, or what the PBM executive had for lunch," Stoller quips, using hyperbole to underscore the randomness and unfairness of the system.

The Broader Economic Implications

The commentary concludes by widening the lens beyond healthcare. Stoller argues that the disappearance of posted prices is a systemic issue affecting the entire economy, from soft drinks to retail. He cites a recent FTC complaint against Pepsi, alleging the company used secret rebates to collude with Walmart to inflate prices and block competitors. "The goal for Walmart was to keep a 'price gap' of Pepsi products between itself and rival stores who might want to discount to attract customers," Stoller explains. This creates a market where power, not efficiency, dictates price.

Without public prices, the incentive structure shifts entirely toward consolidation. Small players cannot compete with the opaque, negotiated rates of massive conglomerates. Stoller warns that this dynamic is eroding the foundation of competitive markets. "Without real public prices, having power in negotiations becomes a lot more important. And that's an incentive to consolidate," he writes. The result is an economy where the rules of the game are hidden from the players, and the house always wins.

Bottom Line

Matt Stoller's argument is a powerful reminder that market transparency is not a given; it is a fragile legal achievement that has been systematically dismantled. The piece's greatest strength lies in its historical grounding, showing that the current chaos is the result of specific policy choices, not inevitable market evolution. However, the path to reversing this trend is daunting, as the entrenched interests benefiting from opacity are vast and politically connected. The most critical takeaway for the reader is that the next battle for economic fairness will not be about setting prices, but about forcing the disclosure of them.

The legal framework around pricing in America is fairness. Federal law bars 'unfair and deceptive practices,' as well as 'unfair methods of competition,' but even back to the earliest of colonial times, there was legal pricing standardization around milling.
