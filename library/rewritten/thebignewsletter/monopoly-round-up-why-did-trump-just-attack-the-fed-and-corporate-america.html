Matt Stoller delivers a jarring diagnosis of the current political economy: the administration's sudden populist fury against corporate greed is a desperate political maneuver that collapses under the weight of its own contradictory actions. While the White House publicly champions affordability, Stoller reveals a parallel reality where the Justice Department quietly greenlights massive monopolies, suggesting that the era of "Make America Wealthy Again" has simply been rebranded, not replaced.

The Pivot from Wealth to Affordability

Stoller begins by dismantling the narrative that the administration has genuinely shifted its economic philosophy. He notes that the initial domestic agenda was explicitly about "Make America Wealthy Again," a strategy relying on tax cuts and deregulation to spur corporate investment. However, the political fallout from the 2025 elections, including a Democratic surge and Zohran Mamdani's upset victory in New York City, forced a recalibration. The public's anger over high prices has become the administration's primary vulnerability.

"The President announced attempts to crack down on credit card companies, big landlords, and defense contractors, and his administration is now pursuing a high-profile criminal investigation of the Chair of the Federal Reserve, Jay Powell," Stoller writes, highlighting the aggressive new rhetoric. He argues this is not a change of heart but a survival tactic. The administration is attempting to pivot to an "affordability" theme to salvage its standing before the midterms, yet Stoller questions the sincerity of this shift. "The moves are all happening, more or less, because of an increasing panic in the White House over high prices, as the President sees that the public is unhappy with his performance."

This framing is effective because it strips away the performative nature of the announcements. Stoller suggests that without a structural change in how the government views corporate power, these attacks are merely theater. A counterargument might be that political pressure can sometimes force genuine policy shifts, even if the initial motivation is cynical. However, Stoller's evidence of simultaneous deregulation suggests the theater is the only reality.

The Defense Industrial Complex and Shareholder Primacy

The commentary shines when dissecting the administration's attack on defense contractors. Stoller points out that the executive order banning stock buybacks and dividends for these firms addresses a very real problem: the financialization of the military-industrial base. He draws on his previous reporting to illustrate how companies like TransDigm Group have exploited their monopoly power. "I traced the issue in 2023 with a piece titled 'The Military-Industrial Stock Buyback Complex,'" Stoller notes, reminding readers that this isn't a new phenomenon. He cites the specific example of TransDigm acquiring Stellant Systems, a sole-source contractor, and subsequently hiking component prices by thousands of percent.

"Many large contractors, while underperforming on existing contracts — pursue newer, more lucrative contracts, stock buy-backs, and excessive dividends to shareholders at the cost of production capacity, innovation, and on-time delivery," the administration's order states, a sentiment Stoller validates as a necessary critique of the current system. The involvement of Deputy Secretary Steve Feinberg, a private equity figure, adds a layer of irony to the crackdown. Stoller argues that while the rhetoric is populist, the administration's broader goal of keeping the stock market high creates a fundamental conflict. "Trump is certainly conflicted in his goals, he wants the stock market to go up, but he also needs costs, and thus the profits holding up equity values, to decline."

The Shadow Agenda: Monopoly in Real Estate

The most damning section of Stoller's piece focuses on the Justice Department's silent approval of the $1.6 billion merger between Compass and Anywhere Real Estate. While the administration screams about housing affordability, it is allowing the creation of a massive home-buying monopoly. Stoller details how Compass bypassed a rigorous antitrust review by appealing directly to Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche, bypassing the Antitrust Division head, Gail Slater.

"The deal cleared much earlier than the companies themselves expected it to. Why? Corruption," Stoller asserts, attributing the outcome to the influence of Trump-aligned lawyer Mike Davis. This merger threatens to give Compass exclusive power to list properties nationwide, inevitably driving up costs for consumers. Stoller contrasts this with the administration's public pledge to ban institutional investors from buying single-family homes. "Trump called for a ban on institutional investors buying single-family homes... he's also encouraging Fannie and Freddie to buy mortgage backed securities to lower mortgage rates." Yet, the reality of the Compass deal undermines these promises.

This contradiction highlights a critical failure in the administration's staffing. Stoller argues that the team in place knows how to deregulate and coerce, but lacks the expertise or will to actually reduce consumer costs. "His administration knows how to deport people, they get how to coerce small countries, and they can work with big business to deregulate. But they by and large simply do not believe that you can reduce costs to consumers by taming corporate power."

The Federal Reserve and the Credibility Trap

Finally, Stoller addresses the administration's aggressive move against the Federal Reserve, specifically the investigation into Chair Jay Powell for alleged perjury. Stoller views this as a pretextual power grab designed to force interest rate cuts to aid homebuyers. "The Trump Justice Department is investigating Fed Chair Jay Powell for perjury over his testimony about building construction. It's entirely pretextual, the point is for the administration to pressure the Fed to lower interest rates."

However, Stoller warns that this clumsy attack could backfire, alienating key Republican allies like Senator Thom Tillis, who has threatened to block future Fed nominees until the investigation is dropped. Furthermore, the administration's broader regulatory stance on financial markets remains hostile to consumers. Stoller notes that while the Biden administration had begun to challenge the power of Visa and Mastercard, the current administration has reversed course, with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau withdrawing rules on late fees and dismantling the agency's capacity.

Critics might argue that the administration's focus on the Fed is a legitimate attempt to address the root cause of high housing costs. But Stoller's analysis suggests that without addressing the underlying monopoly power in the financial sector, rate cuts alone will not solve the affordability crisis. The administration's actions are a "near-billion dollar payday for Dimon, and much higher bonuses on Wall Street," while the public faces rising costs.

Bottom Line

Matt Stoller's argument is strongest in its exposure of the deep fissure between the administration's populist rhetoric and its pro-monopoly actions. The piece effectively demonstrates that the "affordability" pivot is a political survival strategy rather than a genuine policy shift. The biggest vulnerability in the administration's approach is its lack of a coherent strategy to actually lower costs, leaving it trapped between the demands of the stock market and the needs of the public. Readers should watch for whether the administration can sustain this contradictory balancing act or if the tension between its announced agenda and its real agenda will eventually cause the entire framework to collapse.
