Kings and Generals reframes the famous death of Pompey not as a triumphant conclusion to a civil war, but as a catastrophic political blunder that nearly cost Julius Caesar everything. By focusing on the chaotic aftermath in Alexandria rather than the battlefield victory at Pharsalus, the author reveals how a single assassination robbed Caesar of his preferred narrative and forced him into a desperate siege against a vastly superior force. This shift in perspective transforms a familiar historical footnote into a tense study of crisis management, proving that winning the war was only half the battle.

The Cost of a Martyr

The piece opens by dismantling the assumption that Pompey's flight to Egypt signaled the end of the conflict. Kings and Generals writes, "the winner was decided during the last battle [Pharsalus] but that wasn't the end of the war as Caesar had to contend with the remainder of the optimate party and external enemies." This distinction is crucial; it reminds the audience that military victory does not automatically translate to political stability. The author notes that while Caesar secured a numerical advantage by integrating surrendered troops, the real threat remained the influential politicians who still held sway.

The narrative takes a sharp turn when Caesar arrives in Alexandria to find Pompey already dead. Kings and Generals writes, "when Caesar was first presented with Pompey's head he turned away disgusted he was next presented the seal ring of Pompey at which point Caesar broke down in tears." The commentary here is particularly astute, weighing the conflicting historical interpretations of this reaction. While Plutarch suggests genuine grief over a former ally, Dio argues it was a performance. The author leans into the pragmatic reality: "Caesar had a genius for public relations capturing Pompey forcing him to renounce the Senate and then pardoning him as was Caesar's habit would have been the ultimate c[lever] move." Instead, the assassination turned Pompey into a martyr, a narrative outcome Caesar desperately tried to avoid. Critics might note that the author's reliance on ancient sources like Plutarch and Dio leaves the true nature of Caesar's emotions open to debate, but the strategic analysis of why the death was a disaster remains sound.

Caesar's genius for public relations would have been to capture Pompey, force him to renounce the Senate, and pardon him; instead, Pompey's assassination made him a martyr for the optimate cause.

The Alexandria Trap

Once the focus shifts to Egypt, the author details how a diplomatic mission spiraled into a military siege. The text explains that Caesar arrived to arbitrate a dispute between Ptolemy XIII and Cleopatra, only to find the local regent, Pothinus, actively hostile. Kings and Generals writes, "Pothinus... saw Caesar's arrival as a threat to Royal Authority and initially refused to pay the debt he even went so far as to bring Ptolemy's Royal Army into Alexandria." This defiance forced Caesar's hand, turning a negotiation into a standoff. The author highlights the absurdity of the situation: a Roman dictator with only 4,000 men attempting to control a city of 20,000 veterans and an angry mob.

The description of the ensuing naval battle is where the author's storytelling shines. Kings and Generals writes, "Caesar quickly realized he did not have enough men to win this fight and defend the harbor instead Caesar effectively fought a delaying action holding off the Egyptian Force long enough for his men to set fire to the Egyptian Fleet." The decision to burn the fleet, which accidentally damaged the Great Library, is presented not as a reckless error but as a necessary tactical sacrifice to prevent total encirclement. The author notes that despite the fire, "the damage was relatively minor fortunately and the library would continue for number of centuries afterwards," a nuance often lost in popular retellings. This section effectively illustrates the high stakes of the siege, where a single miscalculation could have ended Caesar's career.

The Siege of Thirst

As the stalemate deepens, the author introduces the clever counter-tactics of the Egyptian commander, Ganymedes. The narrative describes how Ganymedes blocked the city's water supply and polluted the remaining cisterns with seawater. Kings and Generals writes, "Ganymedes therefore ordered that those sections that flowed into the parts of the city controlled by Caesar be blocked and then had these sections polluted with seawater rendering them undrinkable." This detail underscores the desperation of Caesar's position; he was not just fighting an army, but the environment itself. The author praises Caesar's leadership in this moment, noting that he ordered wells to be dug day and night, turning a potential disaster into a manageable crisis. However, the author also points out the fragility of the situation, as some of Caesar's men began to panic, believing retreat was their only option.

The arrival of reinforcements from Asia Minor provides a turning point, but not without further peril. Kings and Generals writes, "Caesar was not eager for battle he needed all his ships for the [reinforcements] and with no infantry or Marines on his ships the Egyptians would have the advantage in boarding." The subsequent naval engagement, where Caesar's fleet relied on ramming rather than boarding to defeat a larger enemy force, is described as a triumph of seamanship over brute numbers. The author emphasizes that this victory was only possible because Caesar's crews fought with "remarkable seamanship," buying time for the main fleet to close in. A counterargument worth considering is that the author glosses over the logistical nightmare of sustaining such a small force for so long, focusing more on the dramatic battles than the supply lines that kept them alive.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals delivers a compelling narrative that shifts the focus from Caesar's strategic genius to his reactive survival instincts during the Egyptian crisis. The piece's strongest asset is its detailed breakdown of how a diplomatic failure escalated into a life-or-death siege, using direct historical accounts to humanize the conflict. Its biggest vulnerability is a slight tendency to dramatize the naval battles at the expense of the broader political context of the Egyptian civil war, but for a busy reader seeking a gripping account of the war's second phase, this coverage is both illuminating and essential.
