Kings and Generals dismantles the popular myth that Rome was solely an infantry empire, revealing a military machine that aggressively evolved its cavalry from a support role into a decisive, heavy-hitting force. The author's most striking claim is not just that Roman horsemen existed, but that the Empire systematically adopted the very enemy tactics that once nearly destroyed it, transforming defeated foes into the elite shock troops of the late Republic and Imperial eras. This is a crucial correction for anyone who visualizes Roman warfare as a static line of shields; the reality was a dynamic, adaptive engine of conquest.

The Evolution of the Auxiliary

The narrative begins by acknowledging Rome's historical vulnerability to cavalry. Kings and Generals notes that "some of the worst setbacks Rome's legionary infantry faced... were inflicted by armies skilled in the traditions of cavalry warfare such as the light Numidian riders of Hannibal and the horse archers of the Parthian Empire." This framing is essential because it establishes the motivation for change: survival. The author argues that rather than stubbornly clinging to their infantry roots, the Romans absorbed these lessons, eventually creating "one of the deadliest most elite strike forces that the ancient world had to offer."

The coverage then dives into the organizational nuance of the early Imperial period, distinguishing between the elite alae (wings) and the mixed cohortes equitatae. Kings and Generals writes, "the various alae were intended as front-line battle ready crack cavalry units... in contrast their comrades in the inferior cohortes equitatae functioned partly as military units as partially in a support role." This distinction is vital; it shows that Rome did not have a monolithic cavalry force but a tiered system where quality dictated function. While the elite units were the "Empire's highly mobile strike force," the mixed cohorts handled the gritty work of "patrolling to ensure all was in order policing the provinces and performing escort duties." This hierarchy reflects a pragmatic approach to resource allocation, reserving the best horses and training for the front lines while using cheaper, mixed units for garrison duty.

"Despite being formally considered supplemental to the legions mounted units were in fact becoming ever more important as the military's role changed from expansion to the defense of the frontiers."

The author supports this shift with compelling statistics, noting that while legionary infantry strength grew by only 28% in the first century, "cavalry forces more than doubled from 30,000 to 65,000." This data point effectively counters the notion that the legions remained the undisputed kings of the battlefield throughout the Empire's history. Critics might argue that these numbers still represent a minority of the total force, but the strategic impact of doubling a specialized arm cannot be overstated.

The Human Element and Equipment

One of the most fascinating aspects of the text is its focus on the origins of the cavalrymen. Kings and Generals points out that "Horsemen of the auxilia were instead drawn from former enemies who had been nurtured in the saddle such as Germans, Scythians, Thracians and especially Gauls." This highlights a Roman paradox: their greatest cavalrymen were often the very people they had conquered. The author illustrates this with the unit Ala Gallorum et Thracum Classiana, a name that tells a story of Gallic origins, Thracian reinforcements, and imperial honors. The text suggests that ambitious men sought these posts for "prestige honor and material benefits," turning military service into a ladder for social mobility.

The discussion on equipment reveals how Rome standardized gear based on ethnic utility rather than tradition. Kings and Generals explains that "Celtic helmets... were created especially to deal with the problems that mounted troops had to face in battle possessing a large nape guard to deflect blows aimed at the neck." This practical adaptation shows a military culture willing to borrow from the enemy to solve specific tactical problems. The description of the "four horned saddle" is particularly vivid; it allowed riders to "fight in melee with shield and spear or to throw missiles effectively whilst controlling the horse with the legs," a technological leap that separated Roman cavalry from their Germanic contemporaries who lacked such stability.

"Analysis of nine horse remains found at the Newstead archaeological site in southern Scotland showed that roman horses were similar in their build to be relatively small modern Welsh mountain pony."

This detail is a powerful image-shifter. It forces the reader to abandon the mental image of the massive medieval destrier. These were not warhorses built for trampling; they were compact, sturdy mounts designed for endurance and load-bearing. The author's insistence on this point grounds the entire discussion in archaeological reality rather than romanticized fiction.

The Rise of the Cataphract

As the narrative moves into the second century, the focus shifts to the adoption of the cataphract—the heavily armored shock cavalry. Kings and Generals traces this evolution to the pressure from the East, stating that "continuing pressure from the formerly nomadic Parthian empire in the east and the Sarmatian threat beyond the Danube... made the acquisition of Roman units of the same kind increasingly appealing." The author argues that Rome did not just copy the enemy; they integrated them, noting that the first definitive proof of Roman cataphracts appears under Emperor Hadrian.

The text tackles the confusion between cataphracts and clibanarii (often translated as "metallic furnace"), offering several scholarly theories. Kings and Generals suggests that the distinction might have been tactical rather than purely equipment-based, citing scholar Marius Mielczarek who argued that "cataphracts were heavy mounted spearmen fighting in extremely close ranks... while the clibanarii were employed in shock based wedge formations." This nuance adds depth to the argument, suggesting that the Roman military was sophisticated enough to field different types of heavy cavalry for different battlefield roles.

"Their distinctive protection and weaponry gave Roman cataphracts a reputation of almost invincibility which comes down to us in the sources even today."

The author describes the weaponry of these units with precision, highlighting the contus, a 13 to 15-foot lance that required two hands to wield. This weapon forced a change in tactics, as the rider could no longer hold a shield, relying instead on the sheer momentum of the charge and the protection of their armor. The inclusion of exotic weapons, such as a Chinese sword found in a Romano-Thracian burial, serves as a reminder of the vast reach of the Empire's military supply chains.

Critics might note that the sources for the late Roman cavalry are sometimes ambiguous, and the distinction between unit types like equites sagittarii clibanarii (heavy horse archers) and standard cataphracts remains a subject of debate among historians. However, Kings and Generals handles this uncertainty well by presenting the competing theories rather than forcing a definitive conclusion where evidence is thin.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals delivers a compelling case that the Roman cavalry was not a static afterthought but a dynamic, evolving force that mirrored the Empire's own adaptability. The strongest part of the argument is the use of archaeological evidence to debunk myths about horse size and the clear distinction between the roles of auxiliary wings and mixed cohorts. The biggest vulnerability lies in the scarcity of direct primary sources for the specific tactical distinctions between late Roman heavy cavalry types, though the author navigates this by presenting multiple scholarly interpretations. For the busy reader, this piece offers a vital correction to the "infantry-only" view of Rome, proving that the Empire's longevity depended as much on its horsemen as its legions.
