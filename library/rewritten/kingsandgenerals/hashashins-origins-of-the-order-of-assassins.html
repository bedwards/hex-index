Kings and Generals tackles a historical narrative that popular culture has thoroughly distorted, peeling back the layers of the Assassin's Creed myth to reveal a far more complex political reality. The piece is notable not for rediscovering the Hashashins, but for rigorously separating the sectarian schism of the Ismailis from the later, sensationalized reputation of the order. In an era where video games define history for millions, this distinction is urgent: understanding the Nizari Ismaili state requires looking past the "renegade antihero" trope to see a desperate, sophisticated survival strategy against a collapsing empire.

The Schism That Built a State

The author correctly identifies that the story begins long before the first dagger was drawn, rooting the conflict in the theological fractures of early Islam. Kings and Generals writes, "many men and women of violence have been romanticized by people of later eras driven by pieces of popular culture such as the Assassin's Creed games." This opening is a necessary corrective, immediately grounding the reader in the gap between fiction and the "ruthless murderer" reality of the medieval world. The commentary effectively traces the lineage from the Sunni-Shia split to the specific Ismaili breakaway, noting how the death of Imam Ismail in 762 created a doctrinal crisis that would echo for centuries.

The narrative arc moves swiftly through the rise of the Fatimid Caliphate, describing a period where "the Fatimids entered what historian Farid Daftary dubs the Ismaeli century." This is a crucial pivot point; the author uses this historical high-water mark to set the stage for the inevitable decline. The coverage is strongest when it details the 1094 succession crisis, where the vizier Al-Afdal Shahanshah bypassed the rightful heir, Nizar, to install a puppet ruler. Kings and Generals notes that "Nizar wasn't going to take it," leading to a revolt that ultimately failed but birthed the independent Nizari state. This framing is effective because it shifts the motivation from religious fanaticism to a concrete political struggle for legitimacy. Critics might argue that the text glosses over the theological nuances of the Nass (divine designation) doctrine, which was the true spiritual engine of the movement, but for a general overview, the political focus holds up.

"The crowning achievement of this sectarian revolution was the establishment of the Fatimid Caliphate in North Africa, although the Fatimids only occupied a relatively small land peripheral area at first."

The Mastermind of Alamut

The profile of Hasan-i Sabbah is the centerpiece of the piece, transforming him from a cartoonish villain into a strategic genius. The author details his journey from a student in Rey to a missionary in Egypt, and finally to the architect of the mountain fortress network. Kings and Generals writes, "Hasan traveled their empire as a missionary for nine years gauging their military strength and formulating a strategy of resistance." This is a vital insight: the assassins were not born from blind rage but from a calculated assessment of asymmetrical warfare. The text describes his infiltration of Alamut in 1090, where he "slipped into Alamut unnoticed" and slowly turned the garrison against the governor.

The commentary on the resulting state is particularly sharp. It describes the Nizari realm not as a contiguous kingdom but as a "mosaic of squabbling thieves ruled by religious and military leaders" surrounded by hostile territory. Kings and Generals argues that "the various Citadel's of Hassan's fortress Network were almost always surrounded by potentially hostile territory therefore the disunited Ismaili Mountain cities acted both as an impenetrable defensive Bastion and as a base of operations." This explanation of their geography is essential; it explains why they could not fight conventional wars and why assassination became their primary tool. The author notes that "the Seljuk Empire's weakness removed the immediate threat... the decentralization also made his ultimate goal the total overthrow of the Seljuqs almost impossible." This is the core of the argument: assassination was a rational response to a fragmented enemy, not a ritualistic madness.

The Myth of the Hashashin

The piece wisely addresses the etymology of the term "Hashashin," acknowledging the rumors of cannabis use while pivoting to the more likely political reality. Kings and Generals writes, "this part of Nizari history has been distorted by groups who were hostile to or unfamiliar with them and their practices." The text correctly identifies that the term likely originated from Latin sources and later European travelers, rather than being a self-designation. The author highlights the term fida'i, meaning "those willing to sacrifice," to reframe the assassins as soldiers of faith rather than drug-fueled killers.

The coverage of their tactics is chillingly pragmatic. The text recounts how Hassan-i Sabbah used the threat of assassination as a weapon of diplomacy, citing the story of Sultan Ahmad Sanjar waking up to find a dagger in his bed. Kings and Generals writes, "the threat of assassination clearly worked as well as the deed itself." This is a sophisticated observation on psychological warfare. The author notes that Sanjar, "slightly terrified," gave the Nizaris a pension and stopped his campaigns. This anecdote serves as powerful evidence that the order's reputation was a strategic asset, perhaps more valuable than their actual kill count. A counterargument worth considering is that the text relies heavily on the accounts of their enemies (like the Seljuqs and Crusaders) for some of these stories, which may have exaggerated the frequency of successful missions to justify the fear they inspired.

"Knowing how effective it could be, this part of Nizari history has been distorted by groups who were hostile to or unfamiliar with them and their practices."

The Legacy of the Old Man of the Mountain

The final section traces the order's expansion into Syria under Rashid al-Din Sinan, the legendary "Old Man of the Mountain." The author connects the Persian and Syrian branches, noting that despite being "far removed from one another territorially, the various Nizari enclaves all took their orders from the central leadership in Alamut." This centralized command structure is a key detail that contradicts the idea of a loose band of mercenaries. The text details Sinan's failed attempts to assassinate Saladin, illustrating that the order was not invincible and that their political influence had limits against a unifying force like Saladin.

The narrative concludes by emphasizing the resilience of the state, which survived for nearly two centuries despite being surrounded by empires. Kings and Generals writes, "the transformation of the Nizari state into a permanent one... Hassan's attention turned to scholarly pursuits and peaceful relations though the latter was often achieved by illicit means." This duality—the scholar and the killer—is the enduring legacy of the order. The piece ends without a neat resolution, acknowledging the eventual fall of the Mongols, but the focus remains on the order's ability to punch far above its weight class through intelligence and terror.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals delivers a compelling, historically grounded rebuttal to the pop-culture myth of the Assassins, successfully arguing that their violence was a calculated political strategy born of desperation rather than religious insanity. The strongest part of the argument is the detailed breakdown of how geography and political fragmentation forced the Nizaris to adopt asymmetrical warfare, turning the "Hashashin" into a psychological weapon. The biggest vulnerability is a slight reliance on the sensationalized accounts of their enemies to illustrate their methods, but the overall verdict is clear: the real history of the Order of Assassins is far more strategic and human than the games suggest.
