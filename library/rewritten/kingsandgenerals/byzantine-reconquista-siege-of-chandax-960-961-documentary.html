Kings and Generals reframes the Byzantine Empire not as a decaying relic, but as a ruthless machine of resurgence that nearly reversed centuries of Islamic expansion. The author's most striking claim is that the recovery of Crete in 961 was not a lucky break, but the culmination of a deliberate, multi-generational strategy that exploited the fracturing of the Abbasid Caliphate. This narrative matters because it challenges the modern tendency to view medieval history as a static era of decline, revealing instead a dynamic period of aggressive, calculated warfare that reshaped the Mediterranean.

The Shadow of Decline

The piece opens by dismantling the popular image of the Eastern Roman Empire as the "sick man of the middle ages." Kings and Generals writes, "amidst the constant territorial retreat there were periods when they pushed back against the invading tide." This sets the stage for a narrative of resilience rather than inevitable collapse. The author effectively contrasts the grand ambitions of Emperor Justinian with the grim reality of the 9th century, where the empire had lost Egypt, Syria, and North Africa to the "meteoric rise of a nato islamic faith." The description of the Muslim Arabs transforming from desert dwellers into the "most deadly sailors of the mediterranean coast" provides crucial context for why the loss of Crete was so catastrophic. It wasn't just a territorial loss; it was the establishment of a "perfect base from which arab pirates launched constant and devastating raids into the coastal towns of the aegean sea."

This framing is effective because it establishes high stakes immediately. The author notes that by the early 9th century, Byzantium looked like "a polity in terminal decline." Yet, the text pivots quickly to the rise of Basil I, a stable boy who became emperor. The author highlights his unlikely ascent, noting he "attracted the attention of important courtiers by his tall stature enormous strength and ability to break in the wildest horses." This anecdote serves to humanize the political machinations, showing how personal charisma and military prowess could override rigid class structures. Basil's reign is described as the "most successful that eastern rome had seen in centuries," laying the groundwork for a "renaissance of byzantine power in the italian peninsula."

The Fragility of Victory

However, the author is careful not to paint a picture of uninterrupted success. The commentary on the period following Basil's death is particularly sharp, detailing how the empire faltered under the pressure of Bulgarian expansion and renewed Arab naval power. The text notes that "the games basil the first had made to ensure the security of byzantine borders had begun to falter." This admission of vulnerability adds credibility to the narrative. The author describes the terrifying raid on Thessaloniki in 904, where the city was "put to the torch," and the subsequent failed expedition to retake Crete by Admiral Himerios. The failure is attributed to a combination of imperial illness and a devastating ambush, where "emerious fleet was utterly destroyed and all the gains he had made were reversed in the space of a year."

Critics might note that the text glosses over the internal political intrigues that often plagued Byzantine military campaigns, focusing heavily on the external threats. Yet, the author does acknowledge the court's role in the downfall of the brilliant general John Kourkouas, who was "punished for his rising popularity by the scheming imperial court." This detail serves as a reminder that the empire's resurgence was often hamstrung by its own internal politics, even as it achieved stunning victories like the capture of Melitene.

The Pale Death of the Saracens

The narrative then shifts to its central figure: Nikephoros Phokas, the "pale death of the saracens." Kings and Generals writes, "nikiforos sought to continue this family legacy," establishing a sense of dynastic destiny that drives the campaign. The author paints a vivid picture of the sheer scale of the 960 invasion force, describing it as a "truly gigantic mass of soldiery" numbering between 30,000 and 50,000 men. This is contrasted with the armies of Western European kings, who "could barely muster armies more than a few thousand apiece." The inclusion of Norse axe-wielders and the massive fleet of 100 dromons and 200 transport ships underscores the logistical sophistication of the Byzantine state.

The siege of Chandax is described with granular detail, from the "tall battlements of earth and goat and pig hair mixed together" to the "two outspread and thoroughly impassable moats." The author uses the chronicler Leo the Deacon to bring the fortifications to life, emphasizing the difficulty of the task ahead. The initial landing is depicted as a clash of wills, where Nikephoros, "undaunted," ordered his army to form up and charge, leading to a rout of the defending forces. However, the author quickly pivots to the complexities of the siege, noting that the Byzantines were soon fighting a "two-front battle" against the garrison and the rural guerrillas.

The text highlights the brutality of the counter-insurgency, describing how Nikephoros unleashed his "most battle-hardened warriors" to strike back, turning the countryside into a zone of total war. The author notes that "roman death squads put local c" (the text cuts off here, but the implication is clear: the Romans were ruthless in their suppression of resistance). This shift from a conventional siege to a brutal counter-insurgency adds a layer of realism often missing from romanticized accounts of medieval warfare.

Bottom Line

Kings and Generals delivers a compelling argument that the Byzantine resurgence was a product of strategic opportunism and overwhelming military force, rather than mere luck. The strongest part of this coverage is its detailed reconstruction of the logistical and tactical challenges of the Siege of Chandax, which brings the abstract concept of "resurgence" down to the muddy reality of siege warfare. The biggest vulnerability is the abrupt ending of the source text, which leaves the final outcome of the siege unaddressed, though the historical record confirms the eventual Byzantine victory. Readers should watch for how this narrative of aggressive expansion continues to shape the empire's identity in the following decades.
