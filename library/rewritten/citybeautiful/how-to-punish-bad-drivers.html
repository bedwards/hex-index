Dave Amos delivers a jarring reality check: the legal system treats killing a pedestrian with a car as a manageable accident rather than a criminal act, while simultaneously weaponizing license suspensions against the poor for unpaid fines. This is not just a story about traffic; it is an indictment of a justice system that protects drivers from accountability while punishing vulnerable residents for the crime of needing to get to work. In a nation where over 42,000 people died in crashes in 2022, Amos exposes why the public's rage on social media rarely translates to actual consequences on the road.

The Myth of the "Accident"

Amos begins by dismantling the language we use to describe traffic violence. He argues that our collective refusal to assign blame is baked into our vocabulary. "There's an old dark joke in transportation planning that says that if you want to get away with murder you should hit someone with your car," Amos writes. This observation lands with a thud because it highlights a cultural blind spot: we view crashes as acts of God rather than the result of human choices or flawed infrastructure. The media reinforces this by using passive phrasing like "a car hit a pedestrian" instead of "a driver killed a pedestrian," effectively absolving the human operator of responsibility.

This linguistic sleight of hand has real-world consequences. When prosecutors face a jury of fellow drivers, they know those jurors are only one bad decision away from the defendant's seat. Consequently, prosecutors often opt for plea deals that result in probation or community service, even in cases involving death and flight. Amos illustrates this with a harrowing New York case where a driver who killed someone, fled the scene, and attempted to hide the damage received a plea deal resulting in probation. It was only after being arrested for a separate sex abuse charge that he served 90 days in jail. The system is designed to be lenient because, as Amos notes, "our laws are lenient because we believe drivers deserve leniency."

Critics might argue that strict liability laws could lead to overcriminalization of minor errors, but the data suggests the current system is far too forgiving of gross negligence. The burden of proof for murder is so high that it effectively immunizes most drivers from serious time behind bars.

The Punishment That Punishes Everyone Except the Driver

The article takes a sharp turn when Amos examines license suspensions. The intuitive solution to bad driving—taking away the keys—fails spectacularly in a car-dependent society. Amos contrasts the experience of a driver in Manhattan, who can easily rely on subways and buses, with a rural Wisconsin dairy farmer for whom a suspended license means total isolation and job loss. "One punishment a license suspension has now turned into a real life ruiner," he observes. This is where criminal justice collides with city planning; we have built a country where driving is a prerequisite for survival, making the loss of a license a disproportionately harsh sentence.

The statistics Amos uncovers are staggering. Estimates suggest that between 5% and 17% of all licensed drivers in the United States have a suspended license at any given time. More shockingly, "up to 91% of the people who lose their licenses do so for non-driving related offenses." In California, a list of offenses that trigger suspension includes failure to pay child support, underage drinking (even without a DUI), and failure to appear in court for a traffic ticket. This creates a vicious cycle where the poor cannot pay fines, lose their licenses, cannot get to work to pay the fines, and end up driving illegally just to survive.

"We have built a country so entirely relying on cars that removing access to the roadway is seen as a harsh sentence with far-ranging impacts on someone's life."

Amos points out that this system is not only ineffective but counterproductive. A study found that people driving with suspended licenses actually drive more safely than others, likely because they are terrified of getting caught. Yet, the threat of suspension remains a blunt instrument that fails to target the most dangerous drivers while crushing low-income families. The solution, as seen in a 2021 California appeals court ruling, requires decoupling financial penalties from driving privileges, a move that reinstated licenses for 400,000 people.

The Drunk Driving Loophole

Finally, Amos turns his attention to the one area where we should have tough laws: drunk driving. He uses his home state of Wisconsin as a case study for legislative failure. Despite high rates of alcohol-related crashes, the state legislature, influenced by the Tavern League lobbying organization, has kept penalties remarkably light. A first-time DUI is not even a criminal offense in Wisconsin; it is treated like a speeding ticket with a $300 fine. "Wisconsin's DUI arrest rate is 43.37 per 100,000 people," Amos notes, contrasting it with the national average. The state requires a driver to commit a felony-level offense multiple times before facing significant jail time, a ratcheting system that Amos implies is far too slow to protect the public.

This leniency stands in stark contrast to the harsh, often unjust penalties for non-driving offenses. The disparity suggests a system that prioritizes the comfort of the drinking class over the safety of pedestrians and cyclists. While some might argue that harsh penalties for first-time offenders could clog the courts, the human cost of the current approach is measured in thousands of preventable deaths annually.

Bottom Line

Dave Amos's strongest argument is his exposure of the disconnect between our moral outrage over bad driving and the legal reality that protects drivers while punishing the poor. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on state-by-state data, which can be fragmented, but the trend is undeniable: our justice system is broken. The reader must watch for the growing movement to treat traffic violence as a public health crisis rather than a series of isolated accidents, as the current path leads only to more deaths and more injustice.
