Dave Amos doesn't just trace the history of American housing; he dismantles the comforting myth that the current crisis is an inevitable market force, revealing it instead as a deliberate architectural failure built over a century. While most analyses fixate on recent interest rate hikes or the 2008 crash, Amos takes a hard, historical look at how the very tools we use to manage cities—zoning, financing, and public policy—were weaponized to restrict supply and prioritize investment over shelter.

The Illusion of Abundance

Amos begins his journey in 1925, a year that feels like a different planet compared to today's real estate landscape. He notes that prior to this era, housing booms were regional, but the mass adoption of the automobile changed everything. "Over half of US households had access to the car by this point," Amos writes, "and this opened up vast amounts of real estate around cities for new housing construction." This shift allowed developers to bypass the constraints of streetcar lines, building homes wherever a road could reach. The result was a period of unprecedented abundance where land was plentiful and regulations were virtually non-existent.

However, this era of freedom was short-lived. Amos points out that the housing loans of the 1920s were precarious, often requiring a "50% down payment" with a large balloon payment due in just five years. When the stock market crashed, these fragile loans collapsed, contributing to the Great Depression. The core of Amos's argument here is that the market was capable of building cheap, abundant housing, but the financial infrastructure was too brittle to sustain it. This sets the stage for a pivotal moment in 1926, when the Supreme Court ruled in Village of Euclid v. Ambler Realty Company that cities could zone land as they wished. Amos explains that while zoning was initially seen as a way to separate incompatible uses, it quickly evolved into a tool to dictate lot sizes and building heights, effectively limiting what could be built. Critics might argue that zoning was originally intended to protect public health and safety, but Amos's historical arc suggests these protections were quickly co-opted to restrict density.

"Zoning is about more than just separating incompatible uses. It includes development standards that dictate lot size, building heights, lot coverage, and that sort of thing."

The Post-War Boom and the Policy Trap

The narrative shifts dramatically after World War II, a period Amos describes as a "night and day" transformation in construction capacity. With veterans returning to a housing shortage, builders like William Levitt revolutionized the industry by treating home construction like an assembly line. Amos highlights that a new house cost "just over $100,000 in today's dollars," making ownership accessible to the masses. This boom was fueled by the Federal Housing Administration, which began guaranteeing loans and extending terms from five years to thirty, drastically lowering monthly payments. Consequently, home ownership rates "skyrocketed from about 43% of households owning a home to 65% by the 1960s."

Yet, this success story has a dark underbelly that Amos exposes with surgical precision. While private developers built suburbs, public housing policy was steered away from increasing the overall supply. Amos details how the Housing Act of 1937, championed by advocate Katherine Bower, was amended to require that "one dilapidated slum home for every new public housing home built." This policy ensured that public housing did not add to the total stock of homes but merely replaced existing ones, often concentrating poverty in specific areas. As Amos puts it, the bill was "handicapped by amendments that she personally disapproved of," turning a potential social safety net into a tool for slum clearance. This is a crucial distinction: the government didn't just fail to build enough; it actively structured policy to prevent the creation of a robust, mixed-income social housing program.

The Rise of NIMBYism and Financialization

The true inflection point for the modern crisis, according to Amos, arrived in the 1970s. Before this decade, housing prices generally tracked construction costs. Afterward, prices began to decouple, rising faster than the cost to build. Amos attributes this to the rise of "NIMBYism" (Not In My Backyard), where communities used zoning to exclude lower-income residents and maintain property values. "Nimies at the time figured out that they could oppose multifamily housing and enact zoning regulations that made it impossible to build anything but upper income homes," he writes. This was a subtle but powerful shift from explicit racial discrimination to exclusionary zoning, effectively preserving the "American Dream" for a select few while pricing out everyone else.

Simultaneously, the cultural perception of the home changed. Amos argues that housing was no longer just a place to live but a financial asset. "When you start paying attention to property values, you start wanting to ensure that your property values continue to go up," he observes. This mindset fueled the financialization of housing, where investors and Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) began treating homes as commodities to be traded rather than shelter to be inhabited. The result is a market where the primary goal is asset appreciation, not affordability. A counterargument worth considering is that homeowners naturally want their largest asset to grow in value, and Amos doesn't fully address how to align individual financial incentives with collective housing needs. Nevertheless, his point stands: the system is now rigged to prioritize the investor over the resident.

Bottom Line

Dave Amos's most compelling contribution is his demonstration that the housing crisis is not a natural disaster but a series of policy choices made over the last century, from the Supreme Court's zoning rulings to the deliberate stifling of public housing. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on historical causality, which can sometimes oversimplify the complex interplay of global economic forces and local politics. However, the piece succeeds in forcing a necessary reckoning: if we built abundance once, we can build it again, provided we are willing to dismantle the regulatory and financial barriers we erected to protect our own backyards.
