Dave Amos challenges a pervasive suburban myth: that adding a walking path is merely a cosmetic amenity for the fitness-minded, rather than a critical piece of infrastructure for daily life. While many planners dismiss suburban trails as dead ends, Amos brings a unique, data-driven perspective from his own dissertation research, revealing that the problem isn't the paths themselves, but how they are (or aren't) connected to the wider world. For busy professionals navigating car-dependent sprawl, this distinction is the difference between a pleasant weekend stroll and a viable alternative to the morning commute.

The Disappointing Data and the Hidden Potential

Amos begins with a candid admission that might surprise those expecting a cheerleader for suburban trails. He surveyed residents in three distinct Sacramento-area neighborhoods, ranging from a 1950s development to a modern build, and found that "the results honestly were a little bit disappointing to me." The data showed that most users were exercising, not commuting. "I was hoping to see that people would you know walk or bike to a variety of destinations including work school shopping and more," Amos notes, acknowledging that while walking is the top adult exercise, it doesn't automatically replace the car.

This finding is crucial because it prevents the common error of blaming the infrastructure for a lack of usage without examining the context. Amos argues that isolated paths are indeed insufficient. "My results suggest that the paths themselves are not enough for people to ditch their cars in the suburbs," he writes. However, he quickly pivots from this disappointment to a more nuanced solution, arguing that dismissing these paths entirely would be "a little bit shortsighted." The issue isn't the existence of the trail, but the absence of a network that connects it to destinations.

"The paths themselves are not enough for people to ditch their cars in the suburbs, but that's still a little bit shortsighted."

Critics might argue that if people aren't using paths for commuting, the investment is a waste of public funds. Yet Amos counters this by highlighting the long-term planning horizon. He points to the Southport plan, where a regional path system is designed to stitch together neighborhoods, schools, and commercial centers, suggesting that the utility of these paths is often a function of time and connectivity rather than immediate adoption.

From Fitness Loops to Destination Connectors

To solve the connectivity puzzle, Amos proposes a taxonomy of path types, moving beyond the generic idea of a "trail." He identifies "fitness or recreation loops" as valuable for public health, noting that "walking is the number one physical activity done by adults in the US." While these serve a vital role in combating the obesity epidemic, Amos insists they are only one tool in the toolbox.

He then introduces the concept of "stitch or zipper paths," which preserve the quiet, cul-de-sac nature of suburban streets while preventing them from becoming dead ends for pedestrians. "They also often lead to a neighborhood destination like a park or school," he explains, creating a grid-like circulation for walkers that feels seamless to the car-bound observer. This is followed by "connector paths," which are short, utilitarian links to nearby commercial areas. "They aren't fancy but they get the job done and can help people get out of their neighborhoods safely," Amos writes.

The most compelling part of his argument is the distinction between internal circulation and external connection. He uses the example of San Louis Ranch, a new development where paths are not just loops but arteries. "Residents of San Louis Ranch are encouraged to use these paths to get out of the neighborhood not just circulate within it," he observes. Here, the infrastructure includes protected intersections and direct links to major retailers like Target and Costco, proving that modern suburban design can support active transportation for errands, not just exercise.

"When you have them all working together you have the basis for a pretty great active transportation system in the suburbs that's no small thing."

Amos contrasts these modern examples with historical precedents like Radburn, New Jersey, and Village Homes in Davis, California, where paths were integrated into the very fabric of the neighborhood design. He notes that while these older examples are impressive, the sheer density of destinations in modern projects like San Louis Ranch offers a more robust case for replacing car trips. "The paths connect to schools and a nearby commercial area," he says of the Radburn model, but adds that the modern iteration feels "something more modern and different" because of the volume of accessible destinations.

The Systemic Requirement

The core of Amos's argument is that isolated infrastructure fails, but a system succeeds. He emphasizes that "each of these types of path networks serve different functions but when you have them all working together you have the basis for a pretty great active transportation system." He warns that having only a fitness loop or a stitch path without the destination connectors creates a fragmented experience that cannot compete with the convenience of a car.

He envisions a future where regional systems, often repurposed from rail lines or power corridors, link these neighborhood networks into a city-wide web. "Suburban arterial roads are often so wide that a path could be inserted into the street right of way with minimal hassle," he suggests, offering a practical, low-cost method for expanding the network. This systemic view is what separates his analysis from standard urbanist platitudes; he isn't just asking for more paths, he is asking for a specific type of path in the right configuration.

Critics might note that retrofitting existing suburbs with this level of connectivity is politically and financially daunting, often facing NIMBY (Not In My Backyard) opposition. Amos acknowledges the difficulty but points to the success of newer developments and the potential of "rails to trails" projects as proof of concept. He argues that the lack of usage in older neighborhoods is not a failure of the path, but a failure of the network design.

Bottom Line

Dave Amos's most powerful contribution is his refusal to accept the binary choice between "paths for exercise" and "paths for transit," instead arguing that the former can evolve into the latter through strategic connectivity. His biggest vulnerability lies in the assumption that new, well-connected developments will be the primary driver of change, potentially underestimating the political friction of retrofitting established car-centric suburbs. Readers should watch for how these "stitch" and "connector" paths perform as these new networks mature over the next decade, as that will determine if the suburban path can truly save the American suburb.
