Dave Amos delivers a startling revelation: the mundane grid of American suburbs isn't an accident of urban planning, but the direct result of a 1700s land grab designed to sell dirt to farmers. While many know 8 Mile Road as the cultural dividing line in Detroit or the backdrop for an Eminem film, Amos argues it is actually a federal survey baseline, a rigid line drawn across the continent that literally mapped 70% of the United States. This is not just a history lesson; it is an explanation for why American cities feel so disjointed, why we drive so much, and why our streets ignore the natural landscape entirely.

The Jeffersonian Grid

Amos traces the origin of this mile-spaced network to the desperation of the Articles of Confederation era. With a weak central government unable to tax, the Founding Fathers needed a revenue stream, and land was the only asset they had. "Thomas Jefferson believe that if every household owned a small farm it would be virtuous and Democratic," Amos writes, framing the survey system as the physical manifestation of the American Dream. The problem was that the old British system of "meets and bounds"—relying on landmarks like "a large oak tree" or "a stake in the ground"—was too chaotic for a continent-sized project.

The solution was the Rectangular Public Land Survey System, a grid of townships six miles on a side, subdivided into one-square-mile sections. Amos notes that "farmers were expected to donate some of their land back to public roads," which is why these roads sit precisely at one-mile intervals. This logic is compelling because it reveals that our arterial roads were never designed for cars or pedestrians; they were merely the byproduct of selling 160-acre farm plots. The system was so pervasive that it even dictated the location of schools, with section 16 of every township dedicated to public education.

The rectangular Survey System is the simplest and most beautiful that could be devised, and like a well-laid out City on a grand scale.

This quote, drawn from an 1882 surveying guide, highlights the hubris of the era. Amos uses it effectively to show how surveyors viewed their work as a civilizing force, yet the execution was grueling. Surveyors had to stake out corners regardless of terrain, often digging pits or building mounds to mark lines that cut straight through swamps, hills, and forests. The result was a grid that respected no natural features, creating a landscape where roads must cross streams multiple times or cut through impossible topography.

Critics might note that Amos slightly romanticizes the "virtuous" intent of Jefferson, overlooking how this system also facilitated the displacement of Indigenous peoples and the rapid commodification of nature. However, his focus on the physical legacy of the survey remains the most potent part of the argument.

The Cost of Straight Lines

The consequences of this rigid geometry are still visible today, particularly in the over-construction of infrastructure and the fragmentation of communities. Amos points out that under a flexible system, a road might follow a riverbank, but the mile grid forces a road to slice across it, necessitating expensive bridges. "A line surveyed right here will have to cross the stream several times if a road is located there," he explains, illustrating the inefficiency baked into the system.

Furthermore, the mile grid created a disconnect between urban cores and their suburbs. While downtown blocks are often 200 to 400 feet, the suburban grid is based on the massive 5,280-foot sections. This forces developers to create neighborhoods that fail to connect to one another, funneling all traffic onto the mile roads. Amos contrasts this with cities like Fresno, which built arterial streets at half-mile intervals. "The arterial spacing is perfect for mass transit," he argues, noting that a half-mile grid would keep everyone within a quarter-mile of a bus stop. This comparison is a powerful indictment of the mile grid's incompatibility with modern transit needs.

The clash of grids is also evident in older cities like Green Bay, Wisconsin, where a French fur-trader grid perpendicular to the river collided with the federal mile grid in 1831. "This map from 1837 shows the mismatch of the grid systems," Amos says, highlighting how these arbitrary lines create confusing and dangerous intersections. The legacy is a patchwork of urban fabrics that struggle to integrate, forcing drivers onto wide, dangerous arterials designed for rural travel.

It would really be interesting if all cities had cut roads at half mile intervals; would all cities have better Transit accessibility as a result?

This question serves as the piece's most provocative thought experiment. Amos admits that Thomas Jefferson never envisioned a world of mass transit, but the implication is clear: the survey system locked the U.S. into a car-dependent future. The argument lands because it connects a dry historical policy to the daily frustration of commuting in a sprawling suburb.

Bottom Line

Dave Amos successfully reframes the American landscape as a product of a specific, rigid historical policy rather than organic growth. His strongest point is the revelation that our most important roads are merely the boundaries of 160-acre farm plots, a fact that explains the disconnect between our urban and suburban environments. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its brief dismissal of the human cost of this surveying project, which prioritized geometric perfection over ecological and social reality. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: the next time you hit a mile-long stretch of road with no cross-streets, you are driving on a 250-year-old land sale receipt.
