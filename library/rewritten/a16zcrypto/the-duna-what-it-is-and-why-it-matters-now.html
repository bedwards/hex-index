Miles Jennings and Aiden Slavin, writing from a16z crypto, have produced a careful and somewhat self-interested case for a legal structure called the DUNA—the Decentralized Unincorporated Nonprofit Association. Their argument is that decentralized organizations have been operating in a legal no-man's-land for a decade, and that the foundations most DAOs have used as a workaround are fundamentally mismatched to the entities they are supposed to house. The DUNA, they claim, is the first structure actually designed for how decentralized networks work.

The Foundation Problem

The authors don't mince words about existing arrangements.

"Many foundations became convoluted workarounds to appear decentralized—something we've also called decentralization theater."

The critique lands because it is coming from inside the ecosystem that built those foundations. Jennings and Slavin are essentially admitting that a decade of DAO governance has involved structures that looked decentralized in marketing materials and behaved centrally in practice. Their framing is generous: foundations were well-intentioned responses to regulatory pressure, not deliberate theater. But the effect was the same, and the authors are ready to move on.

What a DUNA does differently, they argue, is provide genuine legal personhood without requiring centralized management. "The DUNA," they write, "provides legal status for decentralized organizations and limited liability protections for their members and administrators." A DUNA can contract, appear in court, own intellectual property, pay taxes, and comply with regulations—all the practical capacities an organization needs—while remaining legally separate from the blockchain network it governs. That separation is doing a lot of work in the argument. "A DUNA is not a legal representation of the blockchain network to which it may relate," they write, "but over which it has no control."

The Timing Pitch

The essay's strategic frame is urgency. Jennings and Slavin argue that the CLARITY Act and broader federal market-structure legislation will eventually impose requirements on decentralized organizations, and that early DUNA adopters will be well-positioned while late movers scramble to retrofit. "Markets don't reward ideas," they write; "they reward structures that can hold weight." It is the kind of line that functions as both analysis and sales pitch, which is fair enough given the authors' institutional perch.

They also make an interesting rhetorical move on nonprofit status. "Being a nonprofit doesn't constrain your business," they write. "It unleashes it." The claim is that the DUNA's nonprofit framing removes regulatory friction without requiring the organization to sacrifice commercial activity. This is technically accurate and strategically significant, but it glosses over the way the word "nonprofit" functions in public discourse, where it still carries connotations the authors are quietly trying to shed.

The Honest Admission

To the authors' credit, they are explicit about the gravitational pull of centralization.

"Decentralized organizations haven't had a legal structure that natively fits how they (vs. centralized companies) operate."

The diagnosis is correct, and the DUNA is a plausible response to it. The question is whether a legal wrapper can compensate for the deeper coordination problems that make decentralization difficult in practice. Jennings and Slavin treat the legal gap as the primary bottleneck. It may not be.

What The Piece Avoids

Several significant counterpoints go unaddressed. The first is governance quality. Research on actual DAO voting patterns consistently finds low participation rates, whale concentration, and vulnerability to governance attacks. The DUNA clarifies who can be sued and who pays taxes, but it doesn't fix any of that. Legal structure and governance quality are different problems, and conflating them flatters the proposal.

Founder control dynamics are the second gap. The authors criticize foundations for enabling founder exit without accountability, but DUNA adoption doesn't automatically eliminate founder influence—it just changes where that influence lives. A founder who designs the initial token distribution retains enormous leverage over governance outcomes regardless of whether the wrapper is a foundation or a DUNA. The legal form doesn't touch the underlying power dynamic.

Adoption barriers are the third. Three states have enacted DUNA statutes, which the authors cite as momentum. Forty-seven states have not, and the essay doesn't explore whether the holdouts reflect genuine policy concerns, lobbying from traditional entity interests, or simply slow legislative calendars. Without broad adoption, a Wyoming DUNA governing a network with users in fifty jurisdictions provides limited practical protection.

Tax treatment is the fourth and arguably most important. The essay celebrates the DUNA's capacity to pay taxes but doesn't address the unresolved question of how individual members are taxed. Pass-through partnership treatment, nonprofit member treatment, or something else entirely—the IRS hasn't said, and the DUNA statute doesn't answer. Adopters are buying clarity at the organizational level and uncertainty at the individual level.

Finally, the international dimension is almost entirely absent. Many prominent DAOs operate across jurisdictions, and a U.S. legal structure doesn't solve problems in Singapore, Switzerland, or the Cayman Islands. The authors focus on domestic legitimization as if that were the whole terrain.

Bottom Line

Jennings and Slavin have made a coherent case for a legal structure that addresses a real problem, and readers involved in DAO governance should take the proposal seriously as a practical tool. The DUNA is almost certainly better than the foundation workarounds it replaces, and the timing argument about federal legislation is plausible even if it depends on predictions about future policy. What the essay does not do is what its title implies: explain why it matters now beyond the authors' commercial interest in accelerating adoption. The deeper problems of decentralized governance—whale concentration, low participation, founder leverage—are untouched by any legal wrapper, and readers should approach the DUNA as a useful clarification of liability and tax posture rather than as a solution to the coordination challenges that actually determine whether decentralized organizations work.
