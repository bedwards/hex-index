Noah Smith identifies a paradox that defies conventional economic wisdom: the world's most intense cultural obsession with a nation is happening while that nation remains largely unaware of its own global appeal. This isn't just a travel trend; it is a potential economic lifeline for an island nation struggling with stagnation, driven by a bottom-up cultural wave that official policy failed to engineer.

The Blind Spot in Tokyo

Smith's central thesis rests on a startling disconnect between perception and reality. He recounts a pivotal moment from 2015 when a manager at the publishing giant Kodansha told him, "Americans don't want to see Asian faces." Smith notes his own shock, observing that this executive's worldview was completely detached from the exploding popularity of manga, K-pop, and anime in the United States. The author argues that this blindness isn't isolated to one publisher but represents a broader institutional failure to recognize a seismic shift in global taste.

The evidence Smith marshals is overwhelming and spans decades of cultural integration. He points out that "Asian faces"—or more accurately, the products of Japanese and Korean imaginations—are now the dominant force in global entertainment, citing the success of shows like Shogun and films like Parasite. But the argument goes deeper than box office numbers. Smith illustrates how Japanese culture has become a "memetic shorthand" for younger generations, influencing everything from startup office decor to the way children draw in their notebooks. He writes, "Anime tropes like 'Notice me, senpai!' have crept into the American lexicon," proving that the influence is linguistic and structural, not just superficial.

"In the 19th and 20th centuries, high class in America was defined as 'anything French', while in the 21st century it's defined as 'anything Japanese'."

This reframing of cultural capital is the piece's most compelling insight. Smith suggests that the premium placed on Japanese aesthetics—from the obsession with Wagyu beef to the ubiquity of Muji and Uniqlo—has fundamentally altered the global hierarchy of taste. However, critics might note that this "cool factor" often relies on a curated, sanitized version of Japan that ignores the country's rigid social structures and labor challenges. The global love affair is with the idea of Japan, which may not always translate to a willingness to navigate its complex realities.

From Tourism to Talent

The commentary shifts from cultural consumption to economic opportunity, arguing that the tourism boom is the catalyst for a necessary transformation in foreign direct investment. Smith highlights that while the Japanese government has long pursued "Cool Japan" strategies, the current surge is organic. "Earth became a weeb planet in spite of the failure of the Japanese government's attempts to promote Japanese culture overseas," he writes. This bottom-up phenomenon has familiarized the world with the country, dispelling the old stereotype of Japan as a closed-off, xenophobic fortress.

The data supports a dramatic shift in visitor numbers, with tourism rebounding sharply to exceed 2019 records. But Smith's real interest lies in what happens after the vacation ends. He notes that the tourism boom is forcing Japanese cities to become more user-friendly, with English signage and foreign-friendly services becoming standard. This infrastructure shift is crucial because it lowers the barrier for the next step: migration. Smith points to a 2020 survey where Japan topped the list of countries Americans and Canadians would like to move to, a stark contrast to the historical dominance of poorer Asian nations in Japan's immigration statistics.

The author uses the example of Sakana AI, a cutting-edge artificial intelligence startup founded by Westerners who moved to Tokyo, to illustrate this new trend. He writes that founder Llion Jones "fell in love with Japan after spending a holiday there and moved to Tokyo in 2020." This anecdote serves as a microcosm for a larger potential shift: the conversion of cultural admiration into high-value human capital. Smith argues that this "greenfield platform investment"—where foreign entities build new operations from the ground up—is the missing piece in Japan's economic puzzle.

"The world loves everything Japanese, but Japan doesn't realize it."

While the potential is clear, a counterargument worth considering is whether Japan's rigid corporate culture and lifetime employment systems can truly accommodate the agile, risk-taking mindset of these new tech entrepreneurs. The "weeb" enthusiasm of the consumer market does not automatically guarantee a welcoming environment for foreign workers in the boardroom.

Bottom Line

Smith's argument is strongest when he connects the intangible metric of global affection to the tangible need for economic revitalization, suggesting that Japan's cultural cachet is its most underutilized asset. The piece's vulnerability lies in assuming that the soft power of anime and cuisine can easily overcome the hard barriers of Japan's labor laws and corporate hierarchy. The reader should watch whether the "weeb" phenomenon evolves into a genuine workforce migration or remains a lucrative tourist attraction that fades as the novelty wears off.
