Noah Smith delivers a stark warning that cuts against the grain of conventional free-trade wisdom: Europe's embrace of cheap Chinese imports isn't a bargain, but a strategic trap that could leave the continent defenseless and economically hollowed out. While many analysts focus on the immediate consumer savings, Smith argues that the "Second China Shock" represents a fundamental shift in global power dynamics that demands a painful, protective response. This is not a debate about tariffs for tariffs' sake, but a survival calculation for a continent facing a resurgent Russia and an unreliable ally.

The Anatomy of the Shock

Smith identifies the root cause not as simple market efficiency, but as a deliberate state strategy born of domestic distress. He writes, "China's economy is still suffering from the prolonged effects of the real estate bust that began in late 2021. In response, Xi Jinping's government has unleashed the most expensive and wide-ranging industrial policy the world has ever seen." Because domestic Chinese consumers cannot absorb this massive output of high-tech goods, the surplus is dumped overseas at cut-rate prices. The result is a flood of electric vehicles, ships, and machinery that European manufacturers simply cannot match on price.

The author highlights how currency manipulation exacerbates this imbalance. Citing Shanghai Macro Strategist, Smith notes, "The current level of the yuan is simply too cheap to support a sustainable rebalancing of global trade." This devaluation, combined with falling relative prices, makes Chinese goods "extraordinarily cheap in global terms." The disparity is so extreme that Smith points out a night at a luxury hotel in Beijing costs roughly $250 compared to over $1,160 in New York, a gap that reflects currency policy rather than productivity.

Critics might argue that this focus on currency undervaluation ignores the genuine efficiency gains China has achieved through decades of infrastructure investment. However, Smith's data on the divergence between domestic consumption and export volume suggests the current pricing is indeed artificial and unsustainable for competitors.

The Illusion of a Gift Horse

A common counter-argument, which Smith addresses directly, is that Europe should simply enjoy the cheap goods and shift its economy toward services. He references The Economist, which suggested that "de-industrialisation, in other words, need not be synonymous with decay." The logic follows that since manufacturing accounts for only 16% of the EU's GDP, letting it wither in favor of a service-based economy is a rational adaptation.

Smith dismantles this view by introducing the military dimension, a factor often ignored in economic calculus. "Health care and education and entertainment are nice things to have, but to fight a modern war you need drones, missiles, satellites, ships, planes, and vehicles of all kinds," he writes. With Russia's economy now heavily oriented toward military manufacturing and supported by Chinese supply chains, Europe faces a grim reality: if it deindustrializes, it loses the capacity to repurpose civilian factories for wartime production.

This argument gains historical weight when viewed through the lens of the "War economy" deep dive referenced in the piece's background. Just as the 1930s demonstrated that peacetime economic structures cannot be instantly converted for total war, Smith warns that allowing civilian manufacturing to collapse leaves Europe with "military capabilities... limited to whatever the government can purchase in peacetime." The human cost of this strategic failure would be catastrophic in the event of a broader conflict.

The Trap of Unbalanced Trade

Beyond the military risk, Smith argues that the trade relationship itself is structurally broken. He explains that Europe is not trading goods for goods, but is instead "writing IOUs." He quotes Robin Harding of the Financial Times, who warns, "If China does not want to buy anything from us in trade, then how can we trade with China? ... without exports, we will eventually run out of ways to pay China for our imports." A trade deficit, in this context, is not a gift but a loan that must eventually be repaid, effectively borrowing against the prosperity of future generations.

The author leans on a recent Goldman Sachs report to suggest that this dynamic might actually make Europe poorer in the long run. As Greg Ip summarizes the findings, "Chinese displacement of domestic manufacturing swamps any positives from cheaper goods." Smith interprets this as a "beggar thy neighbor" policy where China's "dual circulation" strategy is designed to make the world dependent on Chinese supply chains while China becomes self-sufficient.

A trade deficit is not a gift; it is a loan. The Europeans who take out the loan may be insufficiently long-term in their thinking, effectively borrowing against their own futures to consume in the present.

Smith also introduces the concept of "pecuniary externality," suggesting that by buying cheap Chinese goods, Europe is inadvertently transferring economic rents and innovative capacity to China. He cites the work of economists David Autor and Gordon Hanson, who note that owning high-tech sectors yields "geopolitical heft from shaping the technological frontier." If Europe stops manufacturing electric vehicles, the engineers, the "learning by doing," and the subsequent innovations will all happen in China, leaving Europe with a diminished capacity to innovate.

The Path Forward: Protectionism as Necessity

Given these stakes, Smith concludes that the "good options are gone, and the bad are all that is left." He argues that Europe must embrace protectionism, not as an ideological choice, but as a survival mechanism. "Protectionism, be it with tariffs or non-tariff barriers, is essential in order to de-risk the reindustrialization of Europe," he asserts. Without a "sheltering wall," European companies will be too afraid to invest, knowing that China's massive industrial policy could swoop in and destroy them at any moment.

However, Smith is careful to distinguish between protectionism against China and openness to allies. He advocates for "allied scale," where the EU lowers barriers for friendly nations to help them achieve the economies of scale necessary to compete. He writes, "EU tariffs and other trade restrictions should only be on China — not on any other country (unless it's doing large-scale transshipment of Chinese-made goods)." This nuanced approach attempts to rebuild a manufacturing base without isolating Europe from its partners.

A counterargument worth considering is whether protectionism could trigger a global trade war that hurts European consumers even more in the short term. While Smith acknowledges the damage such a path would cause, he frames it as a necessary evil to prevent the total collapse of European industrial capacity.

Bottom Line

Smith's most compelling contribution is reframing the "Second China Shock" not as an economic opportunity for consumers, but as an existential threat to European sovereignty and security. His argument is strongest when connecting the loss of manufacturing capacity to the inability to wage modern war, a link that free-trade orthodoxy often obscures. The piece's vulnerability lies in the political feasibility of the proposed solution; convincing a continent accustomed to cheap goods to accept higher prices and protectionist barriers is a monumental task that may prove impossible to execute before the damage is done.
