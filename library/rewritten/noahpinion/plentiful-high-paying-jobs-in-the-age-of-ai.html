Noah Smith challenges the prevailing doom-and-gloom narrative about artificial intelligence by arguing that human labor will remain plentiful and well-paid, not because we are better than machines, but because machines face physical limits we do not. In a field dominated by technologists who view automation as an inevitable job-killer, Smith offers a counterintuitive economic framework that suggests our future depends less on human superiority and more on the scarcity of computing power.

The Misunderstood Threat

Smith begins by addressing the anxiety permeating the tech industry, noting that many engineers view their work as a zero-sum game against human employment. "The economic danger of AI isn't really that it'll take all our jobs; the danger is that it'll gobble up all the land and energy, leaving too little for human use," he writes. This reframing is crucial; it shifts the conversation from a philosophical debate about human obsolescence to a concrete discussion about resource allocation. The author suggests that the real bottleneck isn't human creativity, but the finite nature of the infrastructure required to run AI.

This perspective gains historical weight when considering the constraints of industrial expansion. Just as the Great Famine of 1876–1878 was exacerbated by the inability to move grain quickly enough due to infrastructure limits, Smith implies that future AI growth could be throttled by the sheer physical impossibility of powering data centers everywhere. The argument is compelling because it grounds a futuristic fear in the mundane reality of electricity bills and real estate.

The Power of Comparative Advantage

The core of Smith's argument rests on a fundamental economic principle that is often misunderstood: comparative advantage. He dismantles the common fear that AI will render humans obsolete by explaining that "everyone — every single person, every single AI, everyone — always has a comparative advantage at something!" This is not a claim that humans are superior at specific tasks, but rather that the opportunity cost of using AI for every single task is too high.

Smith illustrates this with a vivid example of a venture capitalist who is faster at typing than his secretary yet still hires the secretary. "He ends up paying someone else to do something that he's actually better at," Smith notes, highlighting that the executive's time is better spent on high-value deal-making. This logic applies even if the AI is better at everything. "The AI has a competitive advantage over humans in both electrical engineering and doctoring. But it only has a comparative advantage in electrical engineering, while the human has a comparative advantage in doctoring," he explains.

The concept of comparative advantage is really just the same as the concept of opportunity cost.

Critics might note that this model assumes a frictionless market where resources are perfectly allocated, ignoring the reality that displaced workers cannot instantly pivot to new comparative advantages. However, Smith's point remains robust: as long as there is a finite supply of compute, there will be tasks where using a human is the most economically efficient choice, regardless of the AI's raw capability.

The Compute Constraint

Smith identifies the ultimate limit on AI dominance not as intelligence, but as physics. "AI requires some amount of compute each time you use it... there will always be a limited amount of it in the world," he argues. This constraint acts as a producer-specific limit, similar to the time limit a human faces. As AI becomes more capable, the cost of using it for low-value tasks rises because that compute could be deployed for high-value problems elsewhere.

This argument is strengthened by looking at "Moore's second law," which observes that the cost of computing power has historically risen as demand outstrips supply, rather than falling indefinitely. If the cost of compute remains high due to physical constraints, the economic incentive to automate every human task evaporates. Smith concludes that "it's possible that many of the jobs that humans do today will continue to be done by humans indefinitely, no matter how much better AIs are at those jobs."

Bottom Line

Smith's strongest move is decoupling job security from human superiority, replacing it with a logic of scarcity that is far more defensible. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its assumption that compute constraints will remain binding; if energy breakthroughs or new architectures shatter these limits, the economic calculus could shift rapidly. Readers should watch how the industry navigates the tension between infinite software ambition and finite physical resources.
