Noah Smith cuts through the official narrative to ask a question that has been deliberately avoided in mainstream discourse: why are federal agents killing Americans with such apparent impunity? By dissecting video evidence of the killing of Renee Good, Smith exposes a stark disconnect between the White House's claims of "domestic terrorism" and the physical reality of a woman driving away from officers. This is not just a story about one shooting; it is a forensic look at how an agency's culture of aggression is being weaponized against the very citizens it is sworn to protect.

The Gap Between Video and Rhetoric

Smith anchors his argument in a meticulous frame-by-frame analysis of the incident in Minneapolis, contrasting the visual record with the administration's immediate spin. He writes, "A frame-by-frame analysis of video footage, however, raises questions about those accounts." The footage shows the agent moving out of the way before firing, and crucially, the subsequent shots coming from the side of the vehicle as it moved in the correct direction of traffic. Smith notes that "Cars cannot drive sideways," a simple geometric fact that dismantles the claim that the agent was under imminent threat during those final shots.

The administration's response was swift and unequivocal. Homeland Security Secretary Kristi L. Noem labeled Good a "terrorist," while the President claimed she "violently, willfully and viciously ran over the ICE officer." Smith dismantles this narrative by pointing out the timeline: the agent was standing behind the SUV, then moved to the side, and fired while the vehicle was already veering away. He argues that if the threat had passed, the use of lethal force was not self-defense but retaliation. "It's possible Ross shot Good on a pretext of self defense, because he was simply angry at her for refusing his demands to open the car door," Smith suggests, highlighting the volatility of the encounter.

"If someone threatens you and then runs away, you're not allowed to shoot them in the back as they run. That's not self defense."

This distinction is vital. The legal standard for self-defense requires an immediate, unavoidable threat. Smith's analysis suggests the agent, Jonathan Ross, may have crossed that line the moment the car began to move away. Critics might argue that split-second decisions in high-stress encounters are difficult to judge in hindsight, yet the video evidence Smith presents shows a clear window where the agent had time to reposition and fire from a safe angle, undermining the necessity of the second and third shots.

A Pattern of Escalation and Impunity

Smith does not treat this as an isolated incident. He weaves in a disturbing catalog of recent events to show a systemic shift in how federal agents are operating. He lists a series of videos and reports: agents beating unarmed citizens, arresting people in retail stores, and even arresting a person whose car they hit in a traffic accident. "The Minneapolis shooting shares characteristics with others the Journal reviewed: Agents box in a vehicle, try to remove an individual, block attempts to flee, then fire," he writes, citing a Wall Street Journal review that found at least eight people shot in similar vehicle incidents since July.

The article connects this aggression to a broader political strategy. Smith points out that the administration has leaped to the defense of the agent, with Vice President JD Vance making false claims about the shooting angle and ignoring the Fourth Amendment's prohibition on unreasonable searches and seizures. This is not just a policy disagreement; it is a fundamental clash with constitutional norms. Smith notes that the administration's rhetoric has shifted from standard law enforcement to an "invasion" narrative, with DHS recruiting agents with slogans like "Defend your culture!"

The human cost of this escalation is becoming impossible to ignore. Public opinion is shifting rapidly. Smith cites a YouGov poll showing that disapproval of ICE tactics has surged, with a majority of Americans now viewing the agency's methods as "too forceful." The killing of Good appears to have accelerated this trend, turning a niche policy debate into a mainstream crisis of legitimacy. "Two days is probably far too early for the killing of Good to have shifted national opinion radically," Smith concedes, but he argues the underlying drift is due to a "consistent record of brutality, aggression, dubious legality, and unprofessionalism."

The Ideology Behind the Badge

Perhaps the most chilling part of Smith's analysis is his exploration of the ideological underpinnings driving these actions. He argues that the "Great Replacement Theory" is not fringe anymore; it is the operating system for a significant portion of the immigration enforcement apparatus. "Many of the ICE agents now going around kicking in doors, beating up and threatening protesters, arresting citizens on pure suspicion, and occasionally shooting people believe that they are engaged in a race war," Smith writes.

He points to DHS recruitment materials that reference "100 million deportations," a number that far exceeds the total number of immigrants in the country and aligns closely with the non-white population. This suggests a mission that goes beyond enforcing immigration law and into the realm of demographic engineering. Smith connects this to the hiring practices of the agency, noting reports of rushed recruitment drives that have lowered standards, bringing in agents who are "barely able to read or write" and have failed background checks. Yet, he also notes that the agent in the Good shooting was well-trained, suggesting the problem is not just incompetence but a deliberate culture of aggression fueled by extremist ideology.

The political rhetoric surrounding these events has become increasingly authoritarian. Smith quotes Texas Representative Wesley Hunt: "The bottom line is this: when a federal officer gives you instructions, you abide by them and then you get to keep your life." This statement, Smith argues, is a "perfect description of an authoritarian police state" that is antithetical to American values. The administration's response to the killing has not been to pause and investigate, but to double down, with agents telling protesters they have "not learned from the past couple of days."

Bottom Line

Noah Smith's most compelling contribution is his refusal to accept the administration's framing of the Renee Good killing as a justified act of self-defense against a terrorist. By grounding his argument in video evidence and connecting it to a broader pattern of violence and ideological radicalization, he forces readers to confront the reality of an agency operating with a mandate for impunity. The piece's greatest strength is its synthesis of forensic detail with political analysis, revealing how a specific tragedy is symptomatic of a deeper, more dangerous shift in American governance. The biggest vulnerability lies in the difficulty of changing the trajectory of a movement driven by deep-seated fear and hate, but Smith makes a convincing case that the cost of inaction will be paid in blood.

"Is this America now? A country where unaccountable and poorly trained government agents go door to door, arresting and beating people on pure suspicion, and shooting people who don't obey their every order or who try to get away?"

The article serves as a stark warning: when the state's monopoly on violence is wielded by agents who view their fellow citizens as an invading force, the result is not order, but chaos. The public's growing disapproval suggests a breaking point is near, but whether that leads to accountability or further escalation remains the critical question for the weeks ahead.
