Noah Smith cuts through the noise of 2025's economic chatter with a counterintuitive thesis: the path to revitalizing small towns and protecting local businesses lies not in protectionism, but in radical abundance. While the political establishment fixates on tariffs and trade wars, Smith argues that the real enemy of the mom-and-pop shop is regulatory gridlock, and the real solution is building more of everything—housing, infrastructure, and technology.

The Abundance Advantage for Small Business

Smith dismantles the perceived conflict between antitrust advocates and the "Abundance" movement, pointing out a fundamental economic truth that often gets lost in rhetoric. "Market power decreases abundance (since there's a deadweight loss from monopoly)," he writes, noting that these two groups should theoretically be allies. He argues that the primary barrier for small businesses isn't competition from giants like CVS or Walgreens, but the crushing weight of red tape.

The author explains that regulatory costs function as a "thumb on the scale" for big chains, which have the capital to absorb expensive permitting processes and labor regulations. "Big chain pharmacies like CVS and Walgreens can easily pay those costs... But for small independent pharmacies, these regulatory costs and delays are a huge burden," Smith observes. By contrast, an abundance agenda focused on deregulation and infrastructure investment would lower entry barriers, allowing local entrepreneurs to compete on merit rather than bureaucratic endurance.

This framing is particularly sharp because it shifts the blame from market forces to policy failures. It suggests that the decline of small towns isn't inevitable but is the result of a system that makes it too hard to build new things. Smith notes that "building infrastructure — roads, sewers, and electrical grids — will help people move to the declining town and revitalize it," creating the very customer base local businesses need.

"Abundance isn't purely a deregulatory project — there's a lot of other stuff involved — but it definitely wants to reduce these sorts of regulatory overhead costs."

Critics might argue that deregulation alone cannot solve the deep-seated demographic shifts affecting rural America, but Smith's point stands: you cannot fix a broken foundation by building higher walls.

The Housing Paradox: Luxury Builds Affordability

Turning to the housing crisis, Smith tackles one of the most persistent myths in urban policy: that building high-end housing drives up rents for everyone else. Citing data from Austin, Raleigh, Phoenix, and Denver, he highlights a clear correlation between construction booms and falling rents. "If rents were falling because demand for housing in these cities were falling, we would probably not see housing booms there," he reasons, dismissing the idea that these cities are simply losing population.

The article explains the mechanics of this phenomenon with clarity. When high-income earners are provided with new, market-rate options, they stop bidding on older, existing stock. Smith describes this as a "yuppie fishtank" that prevents an influx of wealthy newcomers from sparking a price war that displaces working-class residents. "You can call this a 'trickle-down' effect all you want, but a nasty-sounding label doesn't change the basic economics of the situation," he asserts.

This argument gains weight when viewed through the lens of deadweight loss. Just as monopoly power creates inefficiencies in the goods market, artificial scarcity in housing creates massive economic drag. By refusing to build, cities are effectively taxing their own residents. Smith's reliance on "Econ 101" theory here is a deliberate choice to cut through the emotional noise of housing activism with cold, hard supply-and-demand logic.

The Tariff Illusion and Economic Resilience

Perhaps the most timely section addresses the surprising resilience of the U.S. economy in the face of aggressive tariff policies. While headlines scream about trade wars, Smith points out that the actual economic impact has been muted by a complex web of exemptions. "The statutory tariff rate... was 27.5% in September, but the actual rate U.S. importers were paying was only 14%," he notes, citing data from Gopinath and Neiman.

The author attributes this gap to the administration's use of carve-outs for specific companies and strict adherence to the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA). This trade pact, which replaced NAFTA in 2020, allows goods with sufficient regional value to avoid tariffs entirely. Smith explains that because imports make up only about 14% of the U.S. economy, a 14% tax on that slice results in a total economic drag of only about 2%. "Raising taxes on the U.S. economy by 2 percentage points... is unlikely to crush the economy," he concludes.

However, Smith offers a crucial caveat: the pain may be delayed. Referencing a paper by Besten and Känzig, he notes that historical data suggests the full macroeconomic impact of tariffs often takes one to two years to materialize. "It has only been eight months since 'Liberation day', so we will probably be revisiting this topic a year from now," he warns. This nuance is vital; it prevents the reader from assuming the current stability is permanent, while acknowledging that the immediate catastrophe predicted by some has not yet arrived.

The Fraud Paradox: Trust vs. Capacity

Smith pivots to a provocative discussion on government fraud, challenging the narrative that political ideology alone can solve institutional rot. He acknowledges the scale of fraud, from the Minnesota Medicaid scandal to California's fake college applications, but argues that the solution isn't simply to elect a different party. "Those who believe that the key is to simply elect Republicans will be sorely disappointed," he writes, pointing out that outsourcing government services often increases vulnerability to fraud.

The author makes a striking observation about the role of civil service. "Lots of this fraud could have been caught by having the government hire a lot more auditors, but the GOP tends to slash civil service positions and decrease state capacity," Smith argues. He highlights the irony that it was the Biden administration that caught the Somali fraudsters in Minnesota, while the current executive branch has pardoned notorious fraudsters like Philip Esformes.

This section serves as a defense of institutional competence over political posturing. Smith suggests that the real "trust offensive" requires hiring more auditors and tightening oversight, regardless of which party holds the gavel. He notes that even right-wing initiatives like DOGE have failed to find significant waste, whereas professional investigators have uncovered billions in fraud.

"Tighter auditing of state spending is just one example of how America needs a 'trust offensive' — a comprehensive program to increase trust in U.S. institutions."

The Bottom Line

Smith's strongest contribution here is his refusal to accept the status quo of economic anxiety, whether it stems from housing shortages, trade wars, or fears of small business extinction. His argument that "the best way to make kids less anxious about math was simply to teach them math" serves as a metaphor for his entire piece: the solution to complex economic problems is often just more of the thing we are afraid to build.

The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its optimism about the speed of political change; dismantling decades of regulatory inertia is far harder than the author's analysis suggests. Yet, the core insight remains undeniable: abundance, not scarcity, is the only path to a robust, inclusive economy. As Smith puts it, "Progressives have consistently focused on dumbing down math education in order to produce more 'equitable' outcomes, resulting in worse education quality across the board." The same logic applies to policy: trying to protect the few by restricting the many only ensures that everyone ends up poorer. The reader should watch for the delayed impact of tariffs in the coming year, but the immediate lesson is clear—build more, regulate less, and trust the data.
