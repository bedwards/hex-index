Noah Smith delivers a blistering indictment of European policy inertia, arguing that an ideological aversion to air conditioning is actively killing citizens as climate change intensifies. While the continent grapples with record-breaking temperatures and a surge in heat-related mortality, Smith exposes a stark disconnect between elite environmental dogma and the desperate reality on the ground.

The Human Cost of Ideology

Smith opens by dismantling the geographic excuse often used to justify Europe's lack of cooling infrastructure. He notes that "Europe is at a very high latitude, and people think of it as a cold place that doesn't need AC," but quickly pivots to the lethal reality: "climate change is increasing the frequency of punishing, brutal heat waves all across Europe, and the region's lack of AC is causing huge numbers of deaths." The evidence he marshals is grim; citing World Health Organization estimates, he points out that "More than 200,000 people have died because of heat in Europe in just the past four years."

The argument gains urgency when Smith highlights the inadequacy of current infrastructure, noting that "Even many hospitals lack AC, leaving patients lying in pools of their own sweat (and dying in droves)." This is not merely a comfort issue; it is a public health catastrophe. The scale of preventable death is staggering. Smith draws a chilling comparison to illustrate the severity: "So the death rate from heat in Europe is almost twice the death rate from guns in America." If the United States treats firearm violence as an emergency, he argues, the European heat crisis demands even greater immediate action.

The technology that prevents it is air conditioning. Wherever AC gets rolled out, heat-related death plunges.

Smith supports this with historical data, referencing Barreca et al. (2016) to show that in the U.S., "the diffusion of residential air conditioning explains essentially the entire decline in hot day–related fatalities." He estimates that adopting similar technology could save as many as 100,000 European lives annually. This framing is effective because it moves the debate from abstract carbon footprints to concrete human survival.

The Elite Disconnect

The most searing part of Smith's commentary targets the reaction of European leadership. He describes a scenario where "European elites have been trying to keep their people from getting the relief they need," often spreading what he calls "blatant disinformation." Examples include German officials claiming portable units don't work and British experts suggesting citizens apply yogurt to windows. Smith characterizes this as a moral failure, stating that these figures are "demanding that their citizens die for a minuscule climate benefit."

He illustrates the class nature of this disconnect with a damning anecdote about the European Commission headquarters. While lower-level staff were cut off from cooling during a heatwave, leadership retained theirs. Smith quotes an anonymous official who described the situation as "feudalism," noting that "It's like feudalism... referring to the fact that upper floors housing commissioners got to keep their AC on." This incident underscores his broader point about cognitive dissonance: elites are unwilling to admit they were wrong, even when the cost is measured in lives.

Critics might argue that Smith underestimates the complexity of retrofitting Europe's historic building stock or the genuine concern over energy grid stability during peak demand. However, he counters this by pointing to Japan as a counter-example. Japan successfully adopted "wakon yosai" (Japanese soul, Western technology), absorbing foreign innovations without losing cultural identity. Smith argues that unlike Japan, which embraced mini-split units universally, Europe remains trapped in "cultural conservatism," viewing AC as an unacceptable American import.

These people regard these decisions not just as their personal lifestyle choices, but rather than a model for all of society.

Smith attributes this resistance to the "poisonous ideology of degrowth" and a desire to avoid losing face before the United States. He suggests that the urban haute bourgeoisie views AC as a symbol of wasteful American excess, insisting on "cooperating with nature" even when that cooperation leads to death. This cultural analysis adds depth beyond simple policy critique, suggesting that the barrier is psychological and sociological rather than purely technical.

Bottom Line

Smith's strongest asset is his ability to quantify the human cost of abstract environmental debates, transforming a policy disagreement into a moral emergency. His argument is most vulnerable where it relies heavily on cultural generalizations about European elites, potentially oversimplifying legitimate grid constraints. Readers should watch for whether this pressure forces a rapid policy shift or if the continent continues to prioritize ideological purity over public safety.
