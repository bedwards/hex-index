Noah Smith turns a debate about pizza cutters into a warning shot for American progressivism: rejecting useful technology because of ideology is not just stubbornness—it's strategic self-sabotage. While most analyses of artificial intelligence focus on economics or ethics, this piece argues that the real danger lies in the cultural refusal to adopt tools that could reshape power dynamics, using the absurdly specific analogy of a rolling pizza wheel versus Japanese washlets to expose a dangerous blind spot.

The Physics of Bad Tools

Smith opens with a provocative claim: "A pizza wheel — also known as a rolling pizza cutter or just a 'pizza cutter' — is not a great tool for cutting pizza." He doesn't just state an opinion; he dissects the mechanics. As Smith explains, "when you roll a cutting wheel forward, your hand isn't pushing straight down — it's pushing forward and down at the same time, meaning that only some of the force from your cut is being delivered to the pizza itself." This physical limitation forces users into inefficient workarounds, whether it's re-cutting slices and dragging cheese or blunting the blade on a cutting board.

The argument works because it grounds abstract resistance in tangible friction. Smith notes that despite the existence of superior alternatives like scissors or rocking cutters—tools he calls "very versatile" and "easy to clean"—Americans cling to the inferior wheel. He writes, "I couldn't find reliable survey data, but browsing on Amazon, talking to people, and consulting AI all suggest that pizza wheels are very common in American households." This persistence despite better options sets the stage for his larger point: habit and cultural inertia often trump utility.

The Washlet Paradox

Smith pivots to a more luxurious example of technological rejection: the Japanese washlet. He describes a device with "a heated seat," "a jet of water that washes your butt," and "a warm air jet that dries your butt." Despite near-universal adoption in Japan since 1980, Smith observes, "they're an incredibly rare sight in America — in general, only rich people own them." The irony is stark: Americans correctly identify the most useful technology in other domains but fail to adopt it here.

The core of Smith's argument is that resistance isn't always about cost or availability; sometimes it's about a cultural refusal to change. He notes that once you use a washlet, "it's very hard to go back to a basic toilet," yet the technology remains niche in the U.S. This parallels his concern about AI: just as people reject better tools for cutting pizza or cleaning themselves due to habit, they are rejecting transformative technologies based on ideological preconceptions.

There is no law of the Universe that useful technologies are adopted quickly by everyone who could make use of them.

The Progressive Blind Spot

The essay's true target emerges when Smith recounts a party in Washington D.C. where educated professionals expressed deep skepticism about artificial intelligence. He writes, "Not one person that I talked to was positive about the technology." One lawyer dismissed AI as being "about as good as a 2nd-year associate," while an art historian argued it couldn't produce real art because it lacked human input. Smith challenges this, noting that similar objections were raised against photography and film before they became respected forms.

Smith points out a contradiction in public sentiment: while polls show Americans fear AI taking jobs, "they're using AI more and more, both at work and for personal reasons." Yet, he argues, "Democrats consistently poll more negatively than Republicans, both on AI in general, and in terms of data center construction." On progressive forums, he notes, "anti-AI animus is near-universal," creating a culture where admitting to using the technology risks social ostracization.

He cites Dan Kagan-Kans, who argues that "the left has not been willing to engage seriously with AI — despite its potential to affect the lives and livelihoods of billions." Smith echoes this, warning that dismissing AI as merely "fancy autocomplete" or a bubble prevents serious engagement with actual risks. He writes, "by dismissing AI's potential... they make it harder to think about the actual serious risks AI might pose."

Critics might argue that Smith underestimates the valid concerns regarding algorithmic bias, labor displacement, and environmental costs of data centers. However, his point isn't that AI is perfect, but that wholesale dismissal prevents the nuanced policy work needed to manage those very real threats.

The Cost of Dismissal

Smith's final warning is historical: nations that reject innovation pay a price. He notes that "historically, countries that adopted gunpowder, industrial technology, computers, and other cutting-edge innovations had an edge over those that turned up their noses at them." Sometimes the consequence was economic; sometimes it was conquest.

He applies this to the current moment, quoting Brad DeLong on how educated professionals have enjoyed a long period of security where innovation disrupted blue-collar work but spared white-collar roles. "That's probably over now," Smith writes. He warns that if progressive elites refuse to adapt, "they could find themselves left behind by the tide of history — and the consequences will be worse than dirty butts and poorly cut pizza."

The argument is compelling because it frames technology adoption not as a consumer choice but as a geopolitical and cultural imperative. Smith suggests that the refusal to engage with AI is a form of "unilateral disarmament" in a future where these tools will define economic and social power.

Bottom Line

Smith's strongest move is reframing the AI debate from one of ethics to one of utility, using the visceral examples of pizza cutters and washlets to illustrate how cultural inertia can blind even intelligent people to better tools. The argument's vulnerability lies in its assumption that adoption equals acceptance; simply using a tool doesn't guarantee it will be governed wisely. Yet, his warning remains urgent: rejecting a transformative technology because it feels uncomfortable or ideologically suspect risks leaving an entire political movement—and the country—stranded in the past.
