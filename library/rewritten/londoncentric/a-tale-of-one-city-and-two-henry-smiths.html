This piece does something rare in modern journalism: it uses a 400-year-old coincidence to expose the unchanging mechanics of London's property market. Michael Macleod doesn't just tell two stories; he constructs a mirror where the 17th-century landlord Henry Smith reflects the 21st-century one, revealing that the city's greatest fortunes are built on the same bedrock of ruthless speculation and performative philanthropy. For the busy listener, this isn't just history; it's a forensic audit of how wealth is extracted from the vulnerable and laundered through charity, a cycle that has repeated itself since the Elizabethan era.

The Mirror of Two Smiths

Macleod opens by establishing the eerie parallel between the two men, stripping away the veneer of modernity to show the identical business models. "Henry Smith is a London landlord and sometime money lender. Through a series of canny land deals he has built a great fortune, surfing a wave of change in the capital to his personal benefit," the author writes, describing the modern figure before pivoting to his ancestor with nearly identical phrasing. This structural choice is brilliant; it forces the reader to confront the idea that the "new" London is merely a renovation of the old, with the same actors playing the same roles. The argument lands because it refuses to treat the modern landlord as an anomaly. Instead, Macleod frames him as the latest iteration of a centuries-old archetype.

The author details how the historic Smith, described as a "shrewd and often ruthless businessman, a moneylender, and a property speculator," built his empire by buying foreclosed mortgages from profligate aristocrats. This historical context is crucial. It dismantles the myth of the benevolent founder. Macleod notes that Smith was a "prickly and controlling type" who used his wealth to cement a legacy that would outlive him, ensuring his debts were paid even after his death. The modern Smith follows this playbook precisely, using his wealth to fund a charity called The Wickers after his payday loan business, CFO Lending, collapsed under the weight of "serious failings" that caused "detriment to many customers." The parallel suggests that philanthropy is often the final step in a cycle of extraction, not a separate moral endeavor.

The reality, that Smith was a moneylender who wanted to exert precise control over his legacy and ensure those who owed him money did not escape repayment, was a less attractive tale to the Victorian population of London.

The Human Cost of Speculation

Where the piece truly shines is in its refusal to let the historical abstraction overshadow the modern human cost. Macleod zooms in on Deptford, where the modern Smith's company issued eviction notices to 150 residents during Christmas. The author captures the cruelty of the moment: "The email informing them they were to be made homeless was sent from a company account that signed off with the email signature 'Merry Christmas'." This detail is devastating. It highlights the disconnect between the landlord's public persona and the private reality of his tenants.

The narrative then shifts to the tenants' resistance, a necessary counterpoint to the landlord's power. Maryam, a resident, tells Macleod, "All of us, we were working professionals, we're paying our rent and paying our taxes. We were not criminals. We were just people, families, kids." This quote is the emotional anchor of the piece. It challenges the dehumanizing language often used in housing disputes. The author's reporting shows that these tenants, far from being passive victims, organized with local MPs and rights groups to force substantial payouts from Smith's company. This victory is significant, but Macleod is careful not to frame it as a total triumph; it is a costly, reactive measure against a system designed to extract value.

Critics might note that focusing on individual evictions risks missing the broader structural forces at play, such as the lack of rent control or the scarcity of social housing. However, Macleod's approach is effective because it grounds the abstract policy failures in the specific, visceral experience of being kicked out of your home during the holidays. The evidence holds up because it relies on direct testimony and documented legal victories, making the systemic issue undeniable.

The Legacy of Control

The piece concludes by tracing the long tail of the historic Smith's will, which purchased land in Brompton (now South Kensington) to generate income for his descendants and the poor. "Among the beneficiaries are thousands of his descendants, traced down through modern ancestry software, who through a happy accident of birth are still able to apply for a portion of his spoils in times of financial need," Macleod writes. This is a striking revelation: the modern Henry Smith's charity, which claims to help the poor, operates on a model that has enriched a specific lineage for centuries. The author implies that the modern Smith's attempt to replicate this legacy is less about altruism and more about securing a similar dynastic advantage.

The author notes that the historic Smith's will included conditions for the poor, excluding "heavy drinkers, 'whoremongers, common swearers, pilferers, or otherwise notoriously scandalous'." This moral gatekeeping is mirrored in the modern Smith's charity, which aims to show young people "there's another way, the proper way to earn money." The parallel suggests that the definition of "deserving" poor has remained remarkably consistent, often serving to police the behavior of the vulnerable rather than address the root causes of their poverty. The author's framing here is sharp: the charity is a tool for social control, not just relief.

The more we dug, the more we were intrigued by an Elizabethan businessman whose actions shaped, and continue to shape, modern London.

Bottom Line

Macleod's strongest argument is that the modern London property crisis is not a new phenomenon but a continuation of a 450-year-old cycle of ruthless speculation masked by philanthropy. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the coincidence of the shared name, which, while effective for narrative, risks oversimplifying the complex economic forces that drive housing inequality today. Readers should watch for how the modern Smith's charity evolves, particularly whether it continues to prioritize the "deserving" poor or expands its scope to address the systemic issues it helped create.
