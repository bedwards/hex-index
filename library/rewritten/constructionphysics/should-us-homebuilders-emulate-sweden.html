In a housing crisis defined by soaring costs and stagnant supply, the instinctive answer from many policymakers is to look abroad—specifically to Sweden's factory-built homes. But Brian Potter, writing for Construction Physics, dismantles the popular assumption that Sweden's high rate of prefabrication has solved the productivity puzzle. Instead of a blueprint for efficiency, Potter presents a sobering data set suggesting that the Swedish model may be more expensive and less productive than its American counterpart, challenging the very foundation of the "modular revolution" currently being championed in Washington and state capitals.

The Myth of the Factory Fix

Potter begins by addressing the seductive narrative that has captured the imagination of leaders like California Governor Gavin Newsom. The argument is simple: if we move construction from chaotic job sites to controlled factories, we replicate the efficiency of the auto industry. As Newsom noted on the Ezra Klein show, "Guys show up with hammers. Same way they have been since the beginning of time. This is why productivity is down." Potter acknowledges the appeal but immediately pivots to the hard numbers, noting that "for single family home construction, I don't see much evidence that prefab has been particularly helpful in improving construction productivity, or driving down building costs."

The author's use of Swedish government statistics is the piece's analytical anchor. By comparing the construction cost index against the building price index, Potter reveals a troubling trend: costs and prices in Sweden have risen faster than overall inflation, mirroring the US trajectory. "Since 1995 Swedish construction costs have risen at about the same rate as the price of US single family homes, but prices have risen much faster," Potter writes. This data directly contradicts the expectation that factory methods should decouple costs from inflation. Furthermore, productivity growth in the Swedish construction sector has been stagnant since the mid-1990s, precisely the era when prefab adoption surged. "This is not what I'd expect to see if prefabrication were driving substantial productivity improvements," Potter observes, a line that serves as a quiet but devastating rebuttal to the industry's hype.

"The promise of factory-built construction is that it should achieve better quality AND greater productivity/lower costs, not trading off one against the other."

The Cost of Quality?

When the cost disparity is pointed out, the defense often shifts to quality. Critics of Potter's analysis might argue that comparing a $166 per square foot US home to a $286 per square foot Swedish home is unfair because the Swedish product is vastly superior in energy efficiency. Potter engages with this head-on, acknowledging that Swedish homes are indeed more efficient. However, he dismantles the idea that this efficiency comes at a massive premium that justifies the price gap. He points to the Passive House standard, which demands extreme insulation and airtightness, noting that "most estimates I've seen place the Passive House premium at around 5-20% over conventional US construction."

To illustrate the gap, Potter cites specific examples of Passive House builds in Rhode Island and Pennsylvania that cost roughly $163 to $165 per square foot in 2018. In contrast, the average Swedish price that same year was $265 per square foot. "Higher energy efficiency of Swedish homes can partly explain this, but the idea that Swedish prefabrication is more efficient/cheaper once you take this into account seems hard to defend," he concludes. The argument gains further weight when Potter references the climate context; he notes that Minneapolis shares a similar climate to Stockholm yet maintains lower construction costs, suggesting that geography and weather are not the primary drivers of the Swedish price premium.

The Multifamily Exception and the Reality of Trade-offs

The article does offer a nuanced concession regarding multifamily housing. Potter notes that Swedish multifamily prices per unit area remained "roughly flat between 2017 and 2023, even as costs were rising." He cautions that this cannot be entirely attributed to prefab, as many projects still involve significant on-site work, but he admits the trend is "telling." This distinction is crucial for policymakers focusing on density rather than single-family sprawl. However, even here, the evidence remains "highly uncertain," and Potter warns against over-interpreting a single data point without controlling for project complexity or location.

The piece also highlights the trade-offs inherent in the Swedish model. A Lindbäcks executive, a major Swedish prefab firm, admitted to the New York Times that "Building quality homes, whether on-site or off-site, will never be cheap." The value proposition, Potter explains, is not lower upfront cost but predictability and speed. "Real profit, long-term profit, comes from streamlining the building system for predictable outcomes and fast delivery." This reframing is vital: the Swedish model isn't a cost-saving miracle; it's a risk-mitigation strategy. For a busy reader, the takeaway is that adopting this model requires a shift in priorities from "cheapest house" to "most predictable house," a distinction the administration and local governments often blur when promising affordable housing solutions.

Bottom Line

Brian Potter's analysis is a necessary corrective to the romanticized view of European construction, proving that high prefab adoption does not automatically equal low costs or high productivity. While the Swedish model offers genuine benefits in quality control and schedule certainty, it fails to deliver the cost reductions that US housing advocates desperately need. The biggest vulnerability in the pro-prefab argument is the assumption that factory methods are a silver bullet; Potter's data suggests that without addressing the underlying regulatory and labor constraints, moving the factory to the site won't fix the math. Policymakers should watch for the multifamily sector specifically, where the evidence for efficiency is slightly stronger, but remain skeptical of any single-family housing boom built on the Swedish precedent.
