Devin Stone transforms a childhood cartoon into a high-stakes legal thriller, arguing that the most dangerous villain in Disney's The Little Mermaid isn't a sea witch, but a poorly drafted contract. While most audiences focus on the romance or the music, Stone brings a litigator's eye to the screen, asserting that Ariel's agreement with Ursula is riddled with fatal legal defects that would crumble in any real courtroom. This isn't just a fun re-watch; it's a masterclass in contract law disguised as pop culture commentary, proving that even magic cannot override the rule of law.

The Illusion of a Valid Deal

Stone begins by dismantling the premise that a magical pact can be binding. He notes that while the eels, Flotsam and Jetsam, act as legitimate agents for Ursula, the core agreement fails on the basics of clarity. "The term kiss of true love is probably an amorphous ill-defined term uh Concept in the law," Stone writes, highlighting the impossibility of enforcing a condition based on subjective emotion rather than objective action. This framing is brilliant because it forces the reader to confront the absurdity of the plot: a life-altering contract hinges on a feeling that no judge could ever quantify.

He further argues that the contract suffers from a lack of mutual understanding. Stone points out the glaring issue of Ariel's inability to communicate after signing: "later on in the movie why didn't she just take out a piece of paper and write dear Eric I'm the mermaid who saved you... that would have saved a whole bunch of time." By paraphrasing the narrative gap, Stone exposes the narrative convenience that overrides legal logic. Critics might note that in a fantasy setting, magic often supersedes written communication, but Stone's point stands: a contract requires a "meeting of the minds," which is impossible if one party is literally silenced.

"Lawyers are more powerful than a magic sea witch."

The Minor's Right to Walk Away

The most compelling part of Stone's analysis centers on Ariel's age. He shifts the focus from the magic of the transformation to the legal status of the signer. "In most jurisdictions contracts signed by minors are voidable meaning that the miner can either honor the deal or choose to disaffirm the contract until they turn the age of 18 years old," Stone explains. This is the piece's strongest legal hook. It reframes Ariel not as a victim of a witch, but as a minor with the statutory right to cancel a bad deal.

Stone then addresses King Triton's intervention, labeling it a failed "novation." He argues that substituting a new party requires the consent of all original parties, which Ariel clearly did not give. "Here Ariel is very clearly not approving of the substitution which makes this Novation totally invalid," he states. This distinction is crucial; it shows that even a father's love cannot legally override a contract's specific formation rules without the minor's agreement. The argument holds up well, emphasizing that family dynamics do not erase statutory protections for minors.

Unconscionability and the Price of a Soul

Finally, Stone tackles the sheer brutality of the penalty clause. He argues that the contract is "unconscionable," a legal doctrine reserved for terms that are so one-sided they shock the conscience. "Courts will enforce a contract that's just bad for someone and not substitute in their own judgment for the Contracting party but they will not enforce contracts that are overly harsh unduly oppressive or so one-sided as to shock the conscience," Stone writes. He effectively uses this doctrine to argue that turning a teenager into a polyp for eternity is not just evil, but legally unenforceable.

He also points out the procedural unfairness: Ursula rushed a vulnerable, grieving child into signing without allowing time for counsel. "Ursula doesn't give Ariel much time to think about her options and in one four-minute song Ursula successfully pressures a 16 year old girl," Stone observes. This highlights the power imbalance that defines unconscionability. Furthermore, Stone reminds us that the contract attempts to sell something that cannot be owned: "it's illegal to own another person... involuntary servitude was not legal in the 1830s in Denmark and it's not legal in the United States today." This historical and legal grounding elevates the commentary from a simple movie review to a serious discussion on human rights.

Critics might argue that applying modern secular law to a magical, fictional world is a category error, but Stone's strength lies in using the real-world legal framework to expose the narrative's internal contradictions. By treating the magic as a contract, he reveals the story's inherent injustice.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's analysis succeeds because it treats the fantastical with the seriousness of a real legal brief, revealing that Ariel's salvation lies not in true love, but in the voidability of a minor's contract. The argument's greatest strength is its reliance on the doctrine of unconscionability to dismantle the witch's power, though it occasionally glosses over the fact that Disney's magic operates outside human jurisprudence. For any busy reader, this is a reminder that the most powerful tool against a bad deal isn't a trident, but a good lawyer.
