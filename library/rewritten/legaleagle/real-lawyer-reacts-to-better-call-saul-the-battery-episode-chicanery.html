Devin Stone doesn't just watch "Better Call Saul"; he dissects it with the precision of a prosecutor, revealing that the show's most dramatic courtroom moments are often the most legally accurate. While most viewers see a brotherly feud, Stone identifies a masterclass in bar disciplinary procedure, arguing that the episode "Chicanery" gets the rules of evidence and professional conduct right in ways Hollywood rarely dares to try.

The Reality of the Disciplinary Hearing

Stone immediately dismantles the common misconception that witnesses simply appear in court to tell their truth. "i love my brother but ted kaczynski's brother loved him too oh what you thought that witnesses just showed up at trial having never practiced their testimony before oh you sweet sweet summer child," he writes, setting a tone that is both humorous and rigorously professional. He explains that the episode correctly portrays a bar hearing as a distinct legal creature—less formal than a criminal trial but carrying the same weight of law. The show's decision to turn off the lights for Chuck's alleged electromagnetic sensitivity is not just a plot device; Stone notes that while he can't verify the specific protocols of the New Mexico State Bar, the depiction of an arbitration-style proceeding is plausible and legally grounded.

The author highlights how the show accurately cites the specific rules of professional conduct. Stone points out that the charges against Jimmy McGill—engaging in criminal conduct and acts reflecting on honesty—align perfectly with the American Bar Association's model codes. "any reasonable person would agree that assaulting another lawyer in his own home reflects badly on mr mcgill's fitness as an attorney," Stone observes, confirming that the writers did their homework on the ethical violations. This attention to detail elevates the drama from fiction to a genuine case study in legal ethics.

"you can often guild the lily in trial... if you already have a rock solid case and you have a witness who doesn't play well on the stand... then it's best not to put that witness forward."

The Strategy of Testimony and Objection

Stone's commentary shines when he breaks down the tactical errors made by the characters. He praises the show for depicting the strategic dilemma of whether to call a difficult witness like Chuck, noting that real lawyers often avoid putting weak witnesses on the stand to prevent them from "tearing the case to shreds." However, he also critiques the show's portrayal of objections. In real life, Stone argues, critical evidentiary battles are fought in pre-trial motions, not during the trial itself. "almost always on tv there's this back and forth between these objections... in reality in real life it's done in a very dry way," he writes, explaining that the dramatic objections seen in the episode would likely have been resolved in a motion to suppress long before the hearing began.

He also points out a significant ethical breach that the show hints at but doesn't fully explore: Kim Wexler's role as both counsel and a material witness. Stone suggests that representing Jimmy while being a central figure in the events is "pretty improper," a nuance that adds a layer of professional tragedy to Kim's character arc. The author notes that while the show captures the emotional weight of the moment, the legal reality is that Kim's dual role compromises her ability to effectively defend her client.

Critics might note that Stone's focus on procedural accuracy sometimes overlooks the narrative necessity of the show's dramatization; without the live objections and the emotional testimony, the episode would lack its visceral impact. Yet, Stone's analysis remains compelling because it validates the show's intelligence rather than nitpicking its fiction.

The Verdict on Legal Accuracy

Ultimately, Stone concludes that "Chicanery" is a triumph of legal storytelling because it respects the audience's intelligence. He praises the writers for understanding that "the probate value of playing this exhibit is outweighed by how prejudicial it is," a real legal standard that the characters debate with authenticity. The episode succeeds because it treats the law not as a backdrop, but as the central character driving the conflict. Stone's breakdown confirms that the show's depiction of a lawyer's downfall is not just a story about a bad brother, but a precise illustration of how the legal system actually works.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's analysis proves that "Better Call Saul" is the most legally accurate show on television by demonstrating how the episode's dramatic beats align with real-world rules of evidence and professional conduct. While the show takes liberties with the timing of objections for dramatic effect, Stone's verdict is clear: the core legal mechanics are sound, making the episode a rare instance where entertainment and education collide perfectly. Readers should watch for how this accuracy deepens the tragedy of Jimmy's fall, as the law he tries to manipulate ultimately becomes the instrument of his undoing.
