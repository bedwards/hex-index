Devin Stone doesn't just dissect Jurassic Park; he prosecutes it, transforming a beloved sci-fi thriller into a masterclass in civil and criminal liability. While most viewers see a cautionary tale about genetic hubris, Stone reveals a legal nightmare where every safety protocol is a violation and every character is a potential defendant. For the busy professional who wants to understand how negligence, conspiracy, and the felony murder rule collide in a single narrative, this breakdown offers a startlingly clear lens on real-world law applied to fiction.

The Safety Paradox

Stone begins by dismantling the illusion of safety within the park's perimeter. He points out the absurdity of the security measures, noting that "OSHA regulations do not permit you to have a loaded shotgun as well as dozens of men surrounded with cattle prods." This observation is sharp because it highlights a fundamental failure in risk management: the environment is so inherently dangerous that standard safety gear like hard hats become a joke rather than protection. Stone argues that the reliance on armed guards rather than engineering controls creates a "terribly designed system" that invites disaster.

The commentary on the raptor enclosure is particularly damning. Stone writes, "This thing easily could have been designed in such a way to prevent the cage from moving back away from the wall and keeping all of these individuals safe." He correctly identifies that the failure wasn't just bad luck; it was a foreseeable design flaw. By requiring human intervention to open and close a cage containing apex predators, the park operators created a scenario where injury was not just possible, but probable. This is negligence in its purest form: failing to implement a simple, automated solution to a known danger.

The bottom line is that expenses were spared, and in the legal world, that is often the difference between a successful business and a lawsuit.

Critics might argue that Stone applies modern safety standards to a fictional, cutting-edge technology that didn't exist, but the legal principle remains constant: if a risk is foreseeable, the duty to mitigate it is absolute. The park's failure to do so is a textbook example of liability.

Conspiracy and the Felony Murder Rule

The legal stakes skyrocket when Stone shifts focus to the corporate espionage plot. He details the agreement between Dennis Nedry and Dodgson, labeling it a "civil and criminal conspiracy" to steal trade secrets. Stone explains that because Nedry was paid millions to steal embryos, the theft escalates to "grand larceny," carrying a potential sentence of five to twenty years. This is a crucial distinction that elevates the crime from a simple breach of contract to a felony with severe criminal penalties.

However, Stone's most chilling analysis involves the "felony murder rule." He explains that if a death occurs during the commission of an inherently dangerous felony, the perpetrator can be charged with first-degree murder, regardless of intent to kill. "If I was a prosecutor... I would argue that theft in this particular case... are one of the things that qualify as being inherently dangerous," Stone writes. This means Nedry could be on the hook for the deaths of the tourists and his colleagues, not because he killed them directly, but because his theft set the chain of events in motion.

This application of the felony murder rule is a powerful narrative device. It underscores the idea that in law, the consequences of an action often extend far beyond the actor's immediate intent. Stone effectively uses this to show how a single act of greed can ripple out to destroy lives and careers.

The Duty of Care

Returning to the park's owner, John Hammond, Stone tackles the core of tort law: negligence. He breaks down the four elements required to prove a case—duty, breach, causation, and damages. Stone argues that as the proprietor of a theme park, Hammond owed a "duty" to his guests to keep them safe. When the electrified fence failed due to a lack of backup systems, Hammond breached that duty.

Stone suggests that a reasonable proprietor would have implemented redundancies. "Maybe you have a backup generator that goes into effect immediately when the first line of electricity goes down," he posits. Or perhaps the design should have relied on natural barriers, like elevation changes, rather than a single point of failure. The fact that the T-Rex escaped because the power went down is not an "act of God" or force majeure; it is a foreseeable risk that was ignored.

The dinosaurs in this park are not naturally occurring animals... these are essentially products that they created, and when you put a product into the stream of Commerce you are often responsible for the damages that occur.

Stone also addresses the defense of force majeure, suggesting that while a hurricane is an act of God, the failure of the park's infrastructure to withstand it is not. This distinction is vital. It shifts the blame from nature to the park's inadequate preparation. The argument is compelling because it strips away the excuse of bad luck and replaces it with the reality of poor planning.

The Fate of the Bad Actor

Finally, Stone considers the irony of Dennis Nedry's death. While Nedry was a criminal, Stone notes that under the doctrine of "comparative fault," he might theoretically recover damages for his injuries if he hadn't been a participant in the crime. However, Stone quickly pivots to the doctrine of "unclean hands," which prevents a wrongdoer from profiting from their own bad acts. "Nedry's acts are so bad in this case," Stone concludes, "I think he would probably be precluded from recovering anything."

This section serves as a moral and legal capstone. It reinforces the idea that the law does not protect those who actively undermine the system they are trying to exploit. Even in death, Nedry's conspiracy leaves him with no legal recourse, a fitting end for a character who thought he could outsmart the law.

Bottom Line

Stone's analysis is strongest when he connects the dots between specific design flaws and broad legal principles, turning a movie plot into a rigorous case study in liability. His biggest vulnerability is perhaps the sheer volume of laws broken, which risks overwhelming the narrative with technicalities, but his clear, accessible explanations keep the reader engaged. The takeaway is undeniable: in the eyes of the law, Jurassic Park wasn't a miracle of science; it was a ticking time bomb of negligence waiting to explode.
