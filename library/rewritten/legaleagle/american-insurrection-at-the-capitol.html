Devin Stone doesn't just recount the chaos at the U.S. Capitol; he frames the event as a direct, personal failure of leadership that transcends typical political gridlock. While many commentators focus on the mob's actions, Stone zeroes in on the specific, calculated inaction of the president, arguing that the breach was not an accident but a consequence of a leader who "basks in this mayhem." For a listener short on time, this distinction is critical: it shifts the narrative from a security failure to an act of political treason.

The Architecture of Betrayal

Stone opens by contrasting the historical weight of the Capitol with the absurdity of the day's events. He notes that while Confederates during the Civil War only reached Fort Sumter, today a Confederate flag flew inside the U.S. Capitol itself. This historical parallel is striking because it elevates the incident from a riot to an insurrection with deep, dark roots in American history. Stone writes, "the Capitol is a building that oozes history and unfortunately we are witnessing a history of a different kind." This observation lands with force because it forces the listener to confront the physical reality of the attack on the nation's symbols.

The core of Stone's argument rests on the president's dual failure: inciting the crowd and then refusing to stop them. He points out that Trump told his supporters, "we're going to walk down to the Capitol and we're going to cheer on our brave senators," while simultaneously failing to deploy the National Guard. Stone argues that this wasn't mere incompetence, but a choice. "The president turned a blind eye... because they were on his team," he asserts. This framing is powerful because it rejects the defense of "he didn't know better" in favor of "he didn't care."

The president failed to deploy the National Guard or properly defend the capital both before the mob arrived and long after it started to attack the capital.

Critics might argue that the logistical challenges of deploying the National Guard in the District of Columbia are complex and involve multiple layers of approval, suggesting the delay was bureaucratic rather than malicious. However, Stone's evidence of the president praising the rioters while they attacked suggests a motivation that overrides bureaucratic hurdles.

The Complicity of the Enablers

Stone expands his critique beyond the president to the legislators who fueled the conspiracy theories. He lists a string of senators, including Josh Hawley and Ted Cruz, who he claims "promoted this fantasy" for political expediency. Stone highlights the irony of Cruz arguing that ignoring the claims of fraud would alienate half the country, despite the fact that figures like Cruz helped create that belief. He writes, "Senator Cruz cited statistics about a large number of Americans who believed in this ridiculous conspiracy theory... which is ironic because a large number of people believe it because people like him promoted the ridiculous conspiracy theory."

This section of the commentary is particularly effective because it connects the abstract political maneuvering to the physical violence outside. Stone notes that while the mob rushed the chambers, some of these same senators were fundraising. The visual of politicians profiting from the chaos they helped ignite is a damning indictment. Stone's conclusion here is sharp: "Trump loaded the gun and Congress pulled the trigger." This metaphor simplifies a complex web of responsibility into a clear cause-and-effect relationship that is easy for a listener to grasp immediately.

The Verdict on Leadership

Stone concludes by addressing the immediate political fallout, specifically the call for impeachment and the limitations of the 25th Amendment. He dismisses the idea that the cabinet would act, stating, "that's too good for him." Instead, he argues for a permanent removal from office to prevent future pardons for traitors. Stone writes, "let this be a lesson being a patriot doesn't mean wearing an American flag it means standing up for our institutions and fighting conspiracy theories." This redefinition of patriotism serves as a strong closing argument, reframing the rioters' display of flags as a betrayal of the very concept they claimed to defend.

While Stone's tone is unequivocal, some might argue that focusing solely on the president and his allies ignores the broader systemic issues within law enforcement and intelligence that allowed the breach to happen. Yet, Stone's point remains that the political leadership's failure was the primary catalyst.

We are stronger than this small man. We are stronger than this pathetic mob.

Bottom Line

Stone's most compelling contribution is his refusal to treat the Capitol breach as a mere security lapse, instead presenting it as the inevitable result of a leader who weaponized his own supporters against the government. The argument's greatest strength is its clarity in assigning blame, though it risks oversimplifying the complex chain of command failures that allowed the rioters to succeed. Listeners should watch for how the legal arguments regarding incitement and treason evolve, as Stone hints these are just the beginning of the reckoning.
