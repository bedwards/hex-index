Devin Stone doesn't just review a movie; he dismantles the military justice system's portrayal in cinema by bringing a Judge Advocate General (JAG) and artillery officer to the witness stand. While most legal analyses focus on courtroom procedure, Stone and his guest, Spencer, expose the absurdity of military culture, jurisdiction, and the very definition of a "Code Red." This is not a simple film critique; it is a masterclass in distinguishing Hollywood drama from the rigid, often bizarre reality of the Uniform Code of Military Justice.

The Hierarchy of Honor and the "Soldier" Sin

Stone opens by highlighting a detail that would make any service member cringe: the misuse of rank and branch terminology. "In my experience on the internet it seems like everyone has an expertise in military law and military tactics," Stone notes, setting the stage for a guest who actually served. The core of the early argument rests on the distinction between calling a Marine a "soldier," a mistake Stone's guest Spencer corrects with near-religious fervor. "You do not call marine soldiers you just don't do all right uh how should i have referred to them just refer to them as marines," Spencer insists, joking that the error is "worse than murder worse than espionage."

Stone uses this moment to illustrate how A Few Good Men gets the culture right even when the plot is fiction. The film's tension relies on a strict chain of command that the movie treats with reverence, yet the legal reality is far more nuanced. The authors point out that the "silent drill platoon" performing in front of JAG headquarters is a real, historical phenomenon, creating a "synergistic relationship" between the lawyers and the drill team that is hard to describe but deeply ingrained in military tradition. This attention to cultural texture gives Stone's legal analysis a weight that generic reviews lack.

The Myth of Internal Affairs and the Code Red

The commentary shifts to the legal mechanics of the film, specifically the fictional "Internal Affairs" department and the infamous "Code Red." Stone argues that the movie's portrayal of military oversight is fundamentally flawed. "To the extent that there is something called internal affairs in this movie uh i've never heard of it before... the reality of the situation is the way that the military works everyone is internal affairs all the time," Stone explains. This is a crucial distinction: in the real military, every officer is responsible for the conduct of their subordinates, rendering a specialized "Internal Affairs" unit for routine hazing unnecessary and unrealistic.

Stone then tackles the central legal question: the defense of "following orders." The script suggests that a "Code Red" was a common, accepted disciplinary engagement. Stone dismantles this by explaining the legal standard for unlawful orders. "It's not a defense if you either know that the order is unlawful or a person of ordinary sense and understanding would have known the orders to be unlawful," Stone clarifies. He emphasizes that the "ordinary person" in this context is a trained Marine, not a civilian, meaning the bar for knowing an order is illegal is significantly higher. This reframes the movie's climax not as a battle of wits, but as a failure of the defendants to recognize the illegality of a hazing ritual.

"Any enlisted marine is going to refer to an officer as sir or ma'am period... this perfectly encapsulates lieutenant caffey's character he has no concept of wearing the uniform he has no concept of what it means to be an officer."

Critics might note that the film takes artistic license to create a dramatic courtroom showdown, and Stone acknowledges that the "Code Red" term may have been invented for the movie, with modern forces simply using the term "hazing." However, the legal principle remains sound: the defense of superior orders is not a get-out-of-jail-free card for reckless conduct that leads to death.

The Theater of the Courtroom

Stone concludes by analyzing the courtroom tactics, noting where the film succeeds in portraying legal strategy and where it fails. He praises the defense team's preparation, specifically their "red team" approach to cross-examining the medical examiner. "This is not bad cross-examination prep and it's something that a lot of lawyers forego," Stone observes. The defense's strategy of creating a narrative that the victim's death was an accident caused by lactic acidosis, rather than a murder, is a legitimate legal maneuver.

However, Stone points out the theatrical excesses that betray the reality of a military tribunal. The opening statement, where the defense attorney runs into the jury box and argues facts that are undisputed, is a violation of courtroom decorum. "He is talking about the things that he is going to prove uh in the course of a trial... but then he's in the well of the court without permission," Stone notes. This highlights the film's primary goal: entertainment over accuracy. The drama requires a chaotic, emotional performance, while real military justice demands rigid adherence to procedure.

Bottom Line

Stone's commentary succeeds because it treats the film as a cultural artifact rather than a legal textbook, using real-world military experience to expose the gaps between Hollywood fantasy and the Uniform Code of Military Justice. The strongest part of the argument is the distinction between the "ordinary sense" of a civilian versus a trained Marine when evaluating unlawful orders, a nuance often lost in pop culture. The biggest vulnerability is the film's reliance on the "Code Red" as a plausible military practice, which Stone rightly identifies as likely fictional, yet the legal lesson on the limits of the "following orders" defense remains powerfully relevant.
