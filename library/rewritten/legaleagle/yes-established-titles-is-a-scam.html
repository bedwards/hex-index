Devin Stone doesn't just ask if a viral marketing campaign is a scam; he dissects the legal architecture of a business model that has convinced millions they are buying nobility. While other commentators rely on skepticism, Stone brings the weight of contract law and Scottish heraldry to prove that the "Lordship" you bought is legally worthless. This is essential listening for anyone who has ever clicked on a sponsored video promising a title, a deed, or a piece of history, because the reality is far more mundane—and far more profitable for the seller—than the fantasy.

The Illusion of Nobility

Stone begins by dismantling the core promise: that buying a one-foot square of dirt makes you a Lord. He notes that the company's marketing is a masterclass in ambiguity, stating, "they don't explicitly state that you become officially recognized as a Scottish Noble... and in many ways they explicitly walk that back." This distinction is crucial. The company hides behind fine print that admits the purchase is merely a "personal dedication for a souvenir plot," while the headline screams "Become a Lord Today." Stone argues that this creates a deceptive impression without technically lying, a tactic that thrives in the gray areas of consumer protection law.

To settle the debate, Stone turns to the ultimate authority: the Court of the Lord Lyon, the body responsible for Scottish heraldry. He cites their definitive ruling: "Ownership of a souvenir plot of land does not bring with it the right to any description such as Laird, Lord or Lady." Stone emphasizes that "Laird is not a title but a description applied by those living on and around the estate," and that the term cannot properly describe someone who owns a tiny fraction of a larger property. This evidence is devastating because it comes from the very institution the company claims to honor. The implication is clear: the title is a costume, not a legal status.

The words Lord and Lady apply to those on whom peerage has been confirmed and do not relate to the ownership of land.

The Myth of Land Ownership

The second pillar of Stone's argument addresses the physical reality of the purchase. He points out that the land in question is too small to be registered in Scotland, meaning the buyer receives no deed and no "real right of ownership." Instead, they get a contract. Stone offers a brilliant analogy to explain this legal nuance: "It's like buying an MP3 from Apple Music. You have a contract, they will provide a certain thing to you, a song. Do you own it? No, no you don't, not in any real sense." He concludes that the company is essentially "making land NFTs," selling a digital record of a contract rather than actual real estate.

Critics might argue that the company is transparent enough in its terms of service, but Stone counters that the sheer volume of misleading marketing overshadows the fine print. The Land Registration (Scotland) Act of 1979 defines a "souvenir plot" as land of "inconsiderable size or no practical utility," and the Keeper of the Registers is required to reject such applications. Stone notes that this law doesn't validate the sale; it explicitly categorizes the land as something the registry will not recognize. The buyer is left with a piece of paper that has no standing against third parties.

The Economics of Deception

Stone shifts gears to the financial engine behind the scam, revealing that the company is backed by a Hong Kong venture capital firm and has spent millions on YouTube sponsorships. He reveals the scale of the operation, noting that while he was offered $7,000 for a video, other creators were paid "five and six figures" to promote the product. This massive ad spend is funded by the sheer volume of sales, which Stone calculates by looking at the tree-planting claims. The company claims to have planted over 2 million trees, and since the cost is at most one dollar per tree, Stone deduces that the revenue must be astronomical to sustain such a loss-leader strategy.

He also highlights the predatory nature of the checkout process, where customers are automatically signed up for recurring charges for tree planting under the guise of an included benefit. Stone observes that the company's CEO admitted the language was misleading, especially when amplified by YouTubers, yet the business model continues to thrive. "They probably realize that 2 million trees sounds way more impressive than 42,000 pounds," Stone suggests, pointing to a deliberate shift in marketing language to maximize perceived value over factual accuracy.

They're basically making land NFTs, selling a contractual dedication that means nothing in the eyes of the law.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's most powerful contribution is his ability to translate complex Scottish property law into a clear narrative of consumer fraud, proving that the "Lordship" is a legal fiction sold as a fact. While the company technically avoids explicit lies by hiding in fine print, the aggregate effect of their marketing is undeniably deceptive. The biggest vulnerability in their defense is the sheer scale of the disconnect between the fantasy sold and the contractual reality delivered, leaving buyers with nothing but a framed certificate and a false title.
