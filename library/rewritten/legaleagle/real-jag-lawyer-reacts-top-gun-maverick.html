Devin Stone doesn't just review Top Gun: Maverick; he puts the film on trial, leveraging his unique dual background as a former Judge Advocate General (JAG) lawyer and a trained Joint Terminal Attack Controller (JTAC) to dissect the movie's legal and tactical realism. While most critics focus on the spectacle, Stone identifies a critical tension: the film is a masterclass in storytelling that deliberately flouts military law at every turn. He argues that the movie's greatest strength is its willingness to let the protagonist break the rules, even when the legal consequences should be catastrophic.

The Helmet Paradox and the Chain of Command

Stone immediately zeroes in on the mundane details that often get overlooked in blockbuster analysis, starting with Tom Cruise's motorcycle ride. He notes that while the original film predated California's helmet law, the sequel is set in the present day, making the violation a clear misdemeanor. "It's still Top Gun it's the sequel and it's in present day and he's not wearing a helmet might be Petty but it's a $250 fine uh and or a year of probation," Stone writes, highlighting the absurdity of a high-ranking officer ignoring basic safety protocols. This observation sets the stage for his broader argument: Maverick operates in a zone where personal instinct consistently overrides established regulation.

The commentary deepens when Stone addresses Maverick's refusal to obey a direct order from Admiral Kazansky to stand down the experimental aircraft program. Stone explains that under the Uniform Code of Military Justice, specifically Article 92, disobeying a superior commissioned officer is a serious offense. "Article 92 the uniform code of military Justice you have to obey orders by Superior commissioned officers and he just got one and he disobeyed it," he states, noting that Maverick's actions would theoretically jeopardize not just his own career but the careers of his subordinates who followed his lead. The legal reality is stark, yet Stone acknowledges that the film uses this disobedience as a narrative device to showcase Maverick's relentless drive. "It's just like it's such great storytelling of having him just push a little bit too far... he's got to go he always has to go to the N degree," Stone observes. Critics might argue that this romanticizes insubordination, but Stone suggests the film is honest about the cost: Maverick is a Captain who should have retired or been promoted years ago, a status maintained only because he refuses to die.

The Hard Deck and the Danger Zone

The analysis shifts to the training sequences, where Stone clarifies the concept of the "hard deck." He explains that this is not merely a suggestion but a notional ground line; crossing it means the pilot is "dead" in the simulation. When Maverick lowers the hard deck for the final mission, Stone points out the logical inconsistency. "The hard deck is the notional ground right they have to treat the hard deck as if it is the ground... if they're flying at 5200 feet at in actual reality that is notionally 200 ft above the hard deck," he paraphrases, emphasizing that the rules are designed to prevent catastrophic error. The violation of these rules in the training mission is a major infraction, yet the film treats it as a necessary evil to prove the mission's viability.

Stone also tackles the geopolitical backdrop, noting the film's deliberate vagueness. "Once again they're being completely generic about all of the the bad guys... there's no reason to vilify whether it's Russia or Iran or North Korea they're just the bad guys," he writes. This generic approach allows the story to focus on the mechanics of the mission rather than complex international relations, a choice Stone appreciates for its narrative efficiency. He compares the final mission to a "Star Trek trench run with better graphics," acknowledging that while the tactics are Hollywood-fied, the emotional core remains grounded in the reality of the characters.

The Legal Verdict on Maverick's Actions

Perhaps the most striking part of Stone's commentary is his assessment of the film's climax, where Maverick steals a multi-million dollar aircraft to prove the mission can be flown. Stone lays out the legal devastation awaiting the protagonist. "I have everything I need to have you court marshaled and dishonorably discharged," he quotes the Admiral, underscoring that Maverick's success does not absolve him of his crimes. The film skirts the edge of a court-martial, relying on the fact that the mission was successful to save Maverick from the consequences he legally deserves. Stone finds this tension compelling, noting that the movie is essentially a story about a man who breaks the law to save the system that created him.

He also touches on the personal history between Maverick and Rooster, explaining that while a father commanding a son would be a conflict of interest in the civilian world, the military has specific protocols for this. "It's really your obligation and duty to inform your Superior officer and maybe get reassigned... a father should not be commanding his son or daughter," Stone explains, suggesting that the film simplifies these ethical hurdles for the sake of the drama. The emotional payoff of the film, particularly the inclusion of Val Kilmer's character with throat cancer, is praised by Stone as a brilliant piece of screenwriting that transcends the legal and tactical nitpicking. "I love this so much... not only did they roll with it but they worked with Val Kilmer to make it work," he says, highlighting how the film balances technical accuracy with human emotion.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's commentary succeeds by treating Top Gun: Maverick as a legal case study rather than just an action movie, revealing how the film's most thrilling moments are built on a foundation of illegal acts. His strongest argument is that the movie's power lies in its refusal to let Maverick face the full weight of his insubordination, creating a fantasy where genius overrides the Uniform Code of Military Justice. The biggest vulnerability in this narrative, as Stone notes, is the romanticization of a pilot who should have been grounded decades ago, yet the film's emotional resonance makes the legal impossibility feel strangely inevitable.
