Devin Stone doesn't just dissect a viral Reddit post; he exposes the dangerous gap between internet bravado and actual contract law, proving that a "tip" is legally distinct from a "bargain." While the internet screams that a man who bought a drink deserves half a lottery win, Stone methodically dismantles this entitlement by tracing the lineage of gift promises versus enforceable agreements. This isn't just a funny story about a creepy patron; it is a masterclass in why "he gave me the ticket" does not equal "we have a deal."

The Anatomy of a Creepy Tip

Stone begins by contextualizing the absurdity of the situation, noting that "free legal advice on the internet is shall we say bad and unfortunately some of the worst takes come from one of my favorite places on the internet reddit's r slash legal advice." He immediately identifies the core conflict: a regular customer who "tipped exclusively in scratch tickets" and "specifically called to ask for my schedule" is now demanding a 50% cut of a significant win. The author's tone here is sharp, cutting through the legal noise to address the human behavior first. He advises the bartender, "please don't ask out your bartenders who are just working," and warns that "persistence in the face of no isn't romantic in this day and age it's creepy." This framing is crucial because it sets the emotional stage before the legal analysis begins, reminding the reader that the patron's behavior was already a red flag before the money was even won.

"Persistence in the face of no isn't romantic in this day and age it's creepy so come on let's just not do that."

The commentary then shifts to the employer's role, which Stone describes as a "total move" when the manager handed the patron the bartender's email address. Stone clarifies that while this feels like a violation, in Florida, "the disclosure of most employee personal information to third parties is largely unregulated" unless it meets a high bar of offensiveness. Critics might note that this leaves employees vulnerable in a way that feels unjust, yet Stone accurately reflects the current statutory reality in Florida, where privacy laws are surprisingly permissive regarding employer disclosures.

The Illusion of a Contract

The heart of Stone's argument lies in the distinction between a gift and a contract. He explains that "most people assume that a contract has to be written but that's actually not the case," yet he quickly pivots to why an oral agreement still fails here. The core of his reasoning is that "all enforceable contracts must have an offer an acceptance of that offer and consideration." Stone breaks down the legal definition of "consideration" as a negotiated exchange of benefit or detriment, which is entirely missing in a tip. "A gift promise is not an invitation to bargain because the promissor is offering something without demanding anything in return." This is the piece's strongest legal insight: the patron gave the ticket freely, without a prior discussion of splitting winnings, meaning there was no "bargain" to enforce.

To bolster this, Stone draws a powerful parallel to a real-life precedent involving Tonda Dickerson, a Waffle House waitress who won $10 million on a tipped ticket. Stone notes that the patron, Edward Seward, sued for the entire prize, claiming fraudulent misrepresentation because she didn't buy him a truck as he allegedly implied. Stone writes, "the trial court ruled against seward holding that the tipped ticket was an unconditional gift and found no evidence of fraud." The Alabama Supreme Court upheld this, reinforcing that a tip is a gift unless specific conditions were attached before the handover. Stone concludes, "based on what we know i'm sorry to the bar patron but you are not entitled to anything from the lottery winnings." This application of case law transforms a hypothetical Reddit drama into a concrete legal lesson.

"A gift promise is not an invitation to bargain because the promissor is offering something without demanding anything in return."

The Cost of Litigation

Stone also addresses the procedural nightmare of being sued, even when the suit is meritless. He points out that "in america of course you can sue anyone at any time over just about anything and even if a lawsuit is completely meritless you still have to defend the case." He highlights the danger of default judgments, where a defendant loses simply for failing to respond. "If a defendant fails to file any response or otherwise abandons the case the plaintiff can request the court enter what's called the default judgment against the absence he defendant." This section serves as a vital warning: the threat of a lawsuit is often a weapon used to intimidate, regardless of the legal merit. Stone suggests that while the patron can sue, the bartender "technically correct the best kind of correct" has no obligation to reply to pre-litigation demands, though she must respond if formally served.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone's analysis is a definitive rebuttal to the "I bought the ticket, I own the win" mentality, grounding the argument in the strict requirements of contract law rather than internet sentiment. His strongest move is the comparison to the Dickerson case, which proves that courts consistently treat unconditional tips as gifts, regardless of the winner's windfall. The argument's only vulnerability is the emotional toll on the defendant; while Stone is legally correct that the bartender owes nothing, the reality of defending against a frivolous lawsuit remains a costly burden that the law does not fully protect against.
