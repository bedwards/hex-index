Devin Stone doesn't just review memes; he dissects the bizarre, unglamorous reality of the legal profession during a global crisis. While the internet treats the pandemic as a punchline, Stone uses humor to expose the genuine friction between courtroom tradition and the absurdity of virtual proceedings. He argues that the shift to remote work has stripped away the performative aspects of law, leaving only the raw, often ridiculous mechanics of justice trying to function in a living room.

The Theater of Virtual Justice

Stone opens by highlighting the jarring disconnect between the gravity of legal proceedings and the casual reality of home offices. He notes that "since everyone loves lawyers and coronavirus, might as well combine everyone's two favorite things together," but quickly pivots to the serious implications of this new normal. The most striking evidence he presents isn't a legal theory, but a visual one: the image of Florida attorneys appearing in court without shirts. Stone writes, "if you're in Florida this is what you look like because you're not wearing a shirt because apparently they're not wearing shirts in Florida." This observation serves as a perfect metaphor for the entire era: the formal structures of society are collapsing under the weight of logistical necessity.

He argues that the legal profession's high costs were often driven by the inefficiencies of physical presence, such as "going into court and waiting there for hours just to have a minor hearing." Stone suggests that video conferencing has proven these inefficiencies unnecessary, yet the transition has been messy. The argument lands because it humanizes a profession often viewed as aloof. By showing lawyers struggling with basic technology or appearing in their underwear, Stone dismantles the aura of invincibility surrounding the legal system. Critics might note that this casualness could undermine public trust in the judiciary, but Stone's point is that the alternative—total gridlock—is far worse.

"What it's like doing a virtual depo right now... you're having a video conference and the witness is still under oath."

The Human Cost of Billable Hours

Beyond the procedural quirks, Stone delves into the psychological toll on attorneys. He paraphrases a common sentiment among lawyers, noting that symptoms like "headache, body aches, shortness of breath, fever... this is not the corona virus these are the effects of being an attorney." This hyperbole effectively communicates the chronic stress inherent in the job, which has only been exacerbated by isolation. Stone explains that the profession often confuses being a jerk with having a "disposition," suggesting that the adversarial nature of the work fundamentally alters one's personality.

The commentary shifts to the grueling nature of "billable hours," where lawyers must track their time down to the minute. Stone illustrates the absurdity of this system, asking, "do I stop my time as if I go to the toilet?" This detail highlights the dehumanizing aspect of corporate law, where even biological needs are measured against profit margins. The argument is compelling because it reveals a systemic flaw that exists regardless of the pandemic, yet the lockdown has made it impossible to ignore. Stone's inclusion of international perspectives, such as a UK lawyer's experience with toilet paper shortages and the pressure to maintain a professional appearance on camera, broadens the scope beyond American idiosyncrasies.

The Absurdity of the New Normal

Stone weaves in pop culture references, from Friends to Tiger King, to ground the legal discussion in shared cultural moments. He observes that "people are kind of like Carol is so crazy... but he literally keeps 200 tigers in his like land estate in the middle of America." This comparison underscores how the pandemic has blurred the lines between reality and absurdity, making even the most outlandish scenarios feel plausible. Stone's point is that the legal system is trying to operate in a world where the rules of reality have shifted.

He also touches on the social isolation of the profession, noting that many lawyers are "so stir crazy because I don't have someone to just like break out and talk to." This vulnerability is rare in legal discourse. Stone's coverage is effective because it balances the technical aspects of law with the emotional reality of the people practicing it. He doesn't shy away from the fact that "the wheels of the judicial system have to go on," even when the operators are exhausted and confused.

"I feel like we're all at home doing nothing but sitting around and drinking Mr. Pibb and Red Vines and talking about Friends."

Bottom Line

Stone's strongest move is using memes not just for laughs, but as a lens to examine the fragility of institutional norms. His biggest vulnerability is the risk of trivializing the genuine suffering of those in the legal system, particularly regarding prison infection rates, which he mentions only briefly. However, the piece succeeds in making the invisible labor of lawyers visible and relatable. Readers should watch for how these temporary adaptations to virtual justice might become permanent fixtures, reshaping the profession long after the lockdown ends.
