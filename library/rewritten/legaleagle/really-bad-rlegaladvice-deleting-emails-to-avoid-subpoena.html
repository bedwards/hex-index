Devin Stone doesn't just debunk bad legal advice; he exposes the terrifying gap between internet bravado and the brutal reality of federal discovery rules. In a landscape where Reddit users casually threaten to "destroy evidence" to spite a boss, Stone leverages his decade of experience at top-tier firms to explain why such a move isn't a power play—it's a career-ending legal catastrophe. This isn't a theoretical lecture; it is a forensic breakdown of how a single IT professional's panic could trigger sanctions that destroy a company's entire defense.

The High Cost of Spoliation

Stone begins by identifying the core danger: the boss's order to delete metadata is a direct path to "spoliation of evidence." He writes, "if this person goes through and destroys that evidence that's called spoliation of evidence ie the destruction of evidence and as you can guess that is in fact illegal." The distinction here is vital. Stone clarifies that while deleting files might seem like a simple IT task, in a legal context, it is an active attempt to obstruct justice. He notes that the boss is likely trying to either make document review impossible or hide evidence of wrongdoing, but the method chosen is legally fatal.

The commentary effectively demystifies the technical jargon surrounding electronically stored information. Stone explains that metadata is simply "the data about data," which includes timestamps and sender information that turn a static document into a searchable, sortable asset. He argues that courts are no longer fooled by the tactic of burying opponents in paper; they expect digital efficiency. As Stone puts it, "if you try and pull that crap you just go into court and say look it's ridiculous that they're trying to give us paper documents when it would've been easier for them to turn over just a hard drive full of information." This framing is powerful because it shifts the reader's perspective from the IT worker's technical struggle to the litigator's strategic nightmare.

Destroying the metadata could easily lead to sanctions by the trial court based on the assumption that the employer had something to hide.

Stone details the three tiers of punishment for this behavior: inference sanctions, monetary sanctions, and litigation-ending sanctions. He describes the inference sanction as a "court required admission" where the jury is told to assume the destroyed evidence was damaging to the company. Critics might note that Stone simplifies the threshold for these sanctions, which often require proof of "willful" or "grossly negligent" destruction rather than simple obedience to a boss. However, his point stands: the risk of a judge assuming the worst is a gamble no company should take.

The IT Professional's Dilemma

The piece shines when it pivots from legal theory to practical survival for the employee. Stone addresses the Reddit user's threat to "walk out the door right now and never coming back" or perform a "full win 10 reset." He warns that while the user's anger is justified, their proposed retaliation is a trap. Stone writes, "the correct method of dealing with that is not to delete the documents and the information the correct way of dealing with that is called a motion to quash." This is the critical pivot: the law provides a formal mechanism to challenge unreasonable requests, but it does not provide a license to destroy them.

Stone draws on a high-profile example to illustrate the stakes, referencing the Waymo versus Uber litigation where the destruction of text messages led to a negative inference that effectively decided the case. He emphasizes that the only way to protect oneself is through documentation. He suggests the IT professional should "get this in writing" from the boss, forcing the employer to explicitly order the illegal act. Stone argues, "most states have laws that protect employees who refuse to do illegal things and then are fired by their employer and the only thing better than doing the right thing is having a paper trail of doing the right thing." This advice transforms the employee from a potential accomplice into a whistleblower with a shield.

As Stone puts it, "if you have been instructed to permanently delete the metadata off of your computer or the company's computer such that it can never be recovered that's definitely spoliation of evidence." He contrasts this with the nuanced scenario where metadata is stripped only for production, a common and legal practice if agreed upon. The distinction is subtle but life-altering. Stone's analysis holds up because it refuses to treat the Reddit post as a simple morality tale; instead, it treats it as a complex procedural error waiting to happen.

Bottom Line

Stone's strongest asset is his ability to translate complex federal rules of civil procedure into immediate, actionable warnings for a non-lawyer audience. His argument is most vulnerable only if the reader ignores the nuance between "stripping metadata for production" and "permanently deleting it from the server," a distinction he makes but which is easily missed in a panic. The ultimate takeaway is clear: when a boss asks you to break the law, the only winning move is to document the request and refuse, not to become the architect of your own legal destruction.
