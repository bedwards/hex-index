Devin Stone reframes the infamous Leopold and Loeb case not as a triumph of criminal genius, but as a catastrophic failure of arrogance. While history often fixates on the "perfect crime" narrative, Stone argues that the killers' downfall was baked into their very philosophy, proving that intellectual superiority is no shield against human error.

The Nietzschean Delusion

Stone opens by dismantling the myth of the infallible prodigy, painting a picture of two young men whose intellects were matched only by their hubris. He notes that Nathan Leopold, at nineteen, spoke fifteen languages and held a Phi Beta Kappa honor, while Richard Loeb, at eighteen, was the youngest graduate of the University of Michigan. Yet, Stone points out a crucial disconnect: "Loeb is not excited by intellectual pursuits he'd rather be playing tennis or reading detective stories." This observation is vital; it suggests their criminality was born less from deep philosophical conviction and more from a teenage desire to solve a puzzle.

The author traces their descent into madness to a specific misreading of Friedrich Nietzsche. Stone writes, "Leopold and Loeb had separately been lauded as prodigies since they were toddlers together they come to believe that they are truly superior to nearly everyone else in fact 99% of society." This belief system led them to adopt the concept of the "Superman," a figure Stone describes as "a transcendent individual who possesses extraordinary and unusual capabilities which makes them above the laws that bind normal society."

"Riding a Superman is on account of certain superior qualities inherent in him exempted from the ordinary laws which govern men he's not liable for anything he may do."

Stone's coverage effectively highlights the tragic irony here: the killers believed their intellect made them immune to consequence, yet their arrogance blinded them to the mundane details that would eventually catch them. Critics might argue that Stone oversimplifies the psychological complexity of their psychosis, but the narrative power lies in showing how a grand theory collapsed under the weight of a dropped pair of glasses.

The Anatomy of a Botched Plan

The middle section of the piece details the gruesome execution of the murder of fourteen-year-old Bobby Franks, contrasting the killers' cold planning with their sloppy execution. Stone describes how the duo "resolved to aim higher not arson or battery no this time they plan a murder one so cold and callous it will become one of the most read and studied legal cases of all time." They spent seven months preparing, yet their plan was riddled with holes from the start.

Stone emphasizes their lack of foresight regarding the physical evidence they left behind. "The two demented prodigies spend 7 months making their murderous plans at last on May 21st 1924 on a warm and cloudless spring day Leopold and Loeb put their long plotted gruesome plan into action." Despite their meticulous planning, they failed to account for the simplest realities of human interaction and physics. Stone notes that "unbeknownst to either of the killers Leopold's glasses are left at the dumping site their twisted sense of superiority prevents them from ever considering the possibility of human error."

The author's most chilling observation comes when describing the killers' reaction to the crime itself. "The brutal murder has no effect on these two monsters in fact they grab a bite to eat on their way to dispose of the poor boys remains shockingly they even eat in the car with the corpse as they drive along." This detail serves as a stark reminder that their "genius" did not extend to emotional detachment; rather, it was a performative arrogance that crumbled under pressure.

"The two were acting in a blind panic now they're smug intelligence proving the crumbling facade as the net closes in."

Stone's narrative arc here is compelling because it shifts from the killers' perceived invincibility to their frantic, irrational attempts to cover their tracks. They tried to frame an innocent ornithology student, George Louis, a move Stone mocks as "birdbrain" logic. "Leopold's genius ideas to frame one of his bird enthusiast students because some of them frequent the part of Indiana where Bobby's remains were found Leopold doesn't even consider that Luis may have a rock-solid alibi." This section underscores the author's central thesis: their intelligence was a facade that could not withstand the scrutiny of reality.

The Collapse of the Perfect Crime

The final act of Stone's commentary focuses on the rapid unraveling of the case, driven by the very evidence the killers ignored. The discovery of the chisel, the body, and most importantly, the glasses, turned the tide against them. Stone writes, "The most damning evidence Leopold's forgotten glasses the police follow one clue after another for six days identifying the glasses owner was simple."

The author details how the owner of an eyewear shop recognized the rare design, noting that "only three customers on that set and one of them is Leopold." This moment of clarity dismantled the entire "perfect crime" narrative. Stone concludes that their downfall was inevitable because "their twisted sense of superiority prevents them from ever considering the possibility of human error."

Critics might suggest that the police work was more robust than Stone implies, but the author's focus remains on the psychological failure of the perpetrators. The narrative effectively shows that the "perfect crime" was a myth constructed by the killers' own egos.

Bottom Line

Devin Stone delivers a compelling critique of the Leopold and Loeb legend, stripping away the glamour of "genius" to reveal a story of profound incompetence and hubris. The strongest part of the argument is the connection between their Nietzschean philosophy and their fatal inability to anticipate human error. The piece's biggest vulnerability is a slight tendency to treat the killers as caricatures of evil rather than complex psychological cases, but this serves the narrative's purpose well. Readers should watch for how this case continues to influence modern legal discussions on the death penalty and the insanity defense.
