Richard Coffin delivers a crucial reality check on the euphoria surrounding the latest US-China trade truce, dismantling the narrative that a "total reset" has occurred. While Wall Street celebrates a 90-day tariff pause, Coffin argues that the deal is less a breakthrough and more a strategic retreat to a dangerous baseline that still threatens global supply chains and inflation. For busy investors and policymakers, the takeaway is stark: the market's relief may be premature, and the underlying friction remains dangerously unresolved.

The Illusion of a Reset

Coffin immediately challenges the triumphant tone from Washington. He writes, "As much as I hate being a party pooper, not really," regarding the idea that this agreement marks the end of the trade war. His analysis suggests that the "deal" is merely a walking back of the most extreme escalation, returning tariffs to pre-crisis levels plus a 10% surcharge. This framing is vital because it shifts the reader's focus from the headline number—a reduction from triple-digit tariffs—to the reality that the baseline remains historically high. The author notes that the US is effectively rescinding its "liberation day" escalation, but the core issue of the massive trade gap remains unaddressed.

"While markets rising to be roughly flat year to date probably gives the impression that the worst is behind us, the two countries are still very much in a tricky trade situation."

This observation cuts through the noise of stock market volatility. Coffin points out that while the advertised rates are now 30% for Chinese goods and 10% for US goods, the real effective tariff rates are significantly higher due to pre-existing levies. He highlights that certain key industries still face tariffs far exceeding the advertised baseline, a nuance often lost in the initial news cycle. Critics might argue that any reduction from the brink of a total trade freeze is a victory, but Coffin's data on the cumulative effect of these taxes suggests the economic drag is far from over.

The Hidden Costs and Legacy Tariffs

The commentary deepens as Coffin dissects the specific layers of taxation that remain in place, revealing a complex web of costs that the "deal" does not touch. He explains that the US is maintaining Section 232 tariffs on steel and aluminum, as well as Section 301 tariffs on specific Chinese goods like solar cells, which face an additional 50% levy. "In that case we still have a very high tariff rate of 80%," he notes, illustrating how the headline 30% figure is misleading for many sectors. This evidence is compelling because it exposes the gap between political rhetoric and the actual cost of doing business.

Furthermore, Coffin draws attention to a policy change that has "flown under the radar": the closure of the de minimis exemption for cheap imports. He writes, "The country will still be maintaining its cheap imports tax whereby it applies either a 54% tariff or $100 per parcel for packages that are valued below $800." This is a critical point for anyone tracking consumer inflation or the business models of companies like Shein and Temu. By removing the duty-free status for small parcels, the US is effectively taxing a tenth of its total imports from China, a move that could reshape retail dynamics far more than the headline tariff rates suggest.

"Even lower double-digit tariffs in certain industries can still be enough to ultimately inhibit trade."

The author's analysis of the energy sector reinforces this point. He notes that despite the diplomatic thaw, US exports of liquefied natural gas to China have "already ground to a halt," and crude oil exports are effectively paused. This demonstrates that even the "reduced" tariffs are economically prohibitive for certain industries, rendering the diplomatic success largely theoretical in practice. A counterargument worth considering is that this pause allows supply chains to stabilize, but Coffin rightly points out that without a signed, concrete agreement, companies lack the incentive to invest in onshoring or long-term planning.

The Volatility Trap

Perhaps the most sobering part of Coffin's coverage is his assessment of the long-term economic impact. He argues that the volatility of US trade policy is a greater deterrent to investment than the tariffs themselves. "Until the situation stabilizes it's unlikely that we'll see that companies don't face much incentive to invest in the United States when the trade policy has proven so volatile," he observes. This highlights a fundamental flaw in the current approach: the constant threat of escalation creates an environment where businesses prefer to wait rather than build.

Coffin also revisits the history of the 2018 trade war, noting that the previous agreement, which promised a $200 billion increase in Chinese purchases, largely failed to materialize. He writes, "The trade gap between the two countries remains quite large and the country fell well short of that target with the agreement largely being viewed today as a failure." This historical context serves as a warning against optimism regarding the new promises made in Geneva. While Trump has stated that China has agreed to "open itself up to American business," Coffin suggests that without enforceable mechanisms, these promises may be as hollow as the last.

"To see a fundamental improvement here, we still need to see more explicit trade deal agreements signed by countries and a demonstration from both the US and China that they will stay true to their words."

This final point underscores the fragility of the current situation. The 90-day pause offers a window for inventory building, but it does not solve the structural issues driving the trade war. The market's reaction, driven by the hope that Trump might "180 on this hardline approach," may be betting on a political shift that Coffin views as unlikely to deliver substantive results without a signed treaty.

Bottom Line

Richard Coffin's analysis is a necessary corrective to the market's celebratory mood, effectively arguing that the "deal" is a tactical pause rather than a strategic victory. The piece's greatest strength lies in its granular breakdown of how legacy tariffs and the closure of the de minimis exemption keep effective trade barriers dangerously high. Its biggest vulnerability is the inherent uncertainty of the next 90 days; if the talks fail, the market's relief could turn into a sharp correction, but if they succeed, the structural issues Coffin identifies will still need to be addressed to prevent future escalation.
