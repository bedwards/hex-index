Richard Coffin doesn't just explain the bond market; he dismantles the panic selling it, arguing that while the risks are real, the Twitter-fueled narrative of imminent US collapse is a dangerous distortion of a complex reality. In a financial landscape where fear travels faster than facts, his ability to separate the signal of rising yields from the noise of doomsday headlines offers a rare moment of clarity for investors navigating a volatile 2025.

The Anatomy of the Selloff

Coffin begins by acknowledging the sheer volume of alarmist rhetoric surrounding recent market movements. He notes that "many people have really run with these headlines and perhaps gone with some over-the-top posts over the impending collapse of the United States." This framing is crucial because it validates the reader's anxiety before immediately pivoting to the data. The author correctly identifies that the core issue isn't a sudden failure of the US government, but a shift in the cost of borrowing. He explains that "the 20-year and the 30-year US Treasury bond yield... both reach above 5%," a level that signals the government is paying significantly more to attract investors.

The author's breakdown of why this matters is particularly effective for a non-specialist audience. He simplifies the inverse relationship between price and yield, noting that "as the market price decreases indicative again of lower demand the yield and as a result the interest rate on new government issues increases." This is the mechanical heart of the crisis: lower demand forces higher rates. Coffin then contextualizes the timing, pointing out that this spike coincides with a massive refinancing need. "In 2025... the government actually needs to refinance a whopping $9.2 trillion," he writes, highlighting that the US is trying to roll over a third of its total debt right as the price of that debt has skyrocketed.

The higher refinancing costs for US government debt are actually a key contributor here, with interest expense alone expected to rise from 18% of revenue in 2024 to 30% by 2035.

This projection from Moody's, which Coffin cites, is the piece's most chilling statistic. It suggests a future where nearly a third of all tax revenue goes merely to service existing debt, leaving little for defense, infrastructure, or social programs. Critics might argue that economic growth could outpace these interest costs, rendering the percentage less alarming, but the trajectory Coffin outlines—where interest expenses top defense and Medicaid—is a structural shift that cannot be ignored.

The Three Pillars of Pressure

Coffin structures his analysis around three specific catalysts for the recent yield spike, moving beyond vague fears to concrete events. First, he addresses the credit rating downgrade. "On May 16th, we saw Moody's decrease America's Treasury bond rating from AAA... to AA1," he reports. This is not a minor adjustment; it is the last of the three major agencies to strip the US of its top-tier status. The implication is that the market now perceives US debt as riskier, demanding a higher return for holding it.

The second factor is legislative. Coffin points to the "one big beautiful bill act," a piece of legislation that, despite its name, is "far from fiscally conservative." He details how this bill is estimated to add "$4 trillion to the US debt load over the next decade" while simultaneously cutting benefits for lower-income programs. The author's tone here is measured but firm, noting that "these spending cuts... are not expected to offset the other costs of the bill and tax reductions." This creates a feedback loop: more debt issuance meets a market that is already wary of the US fiscal trajectory.

The third and perhaps most immediate trigger was a "lackluster 20-year Treasury bond auction." Coffin describes how the government had to offer yields as high as 5.046% to sell the bonds, a sharp increase from the previous month. This event demonstrated a tangible lack of appetite from institutional investors. "The auction not only directly increasing the yield... but also further deteriorating the sentiment around US Treasury bonds," he observes. This is a critical distinction; it's not just theory, but a real-time market rejection of US debt at previous price points.

Treasury yields are sort of gravity on the market. The higher they rise, the more pressure there is on economic activity.

Coffin uses this metaphor to explain the downstream effects. When yields rise, they act as a benchmark that pulls up mortgage rates, corporate borrowing costs, and the cost of living. He notes that mortgage rates have already climbed to "nearly 7%," making homeownership increasingly difficult and raising the risk of defaults. Furthermore, he highlights the risk to financial institutions, drawing a parallel to the Silicon Valley Bank collapse. Banks holding long-term bonds bought at low rates are now sitting on assets that have lost value, creating a potential liquidity crisis.

The Japan Myth and the Bottom Line

Amidst the speculation, Coffin takes a moment to debunk a popular conspiracy theory: that Japan is actively dumping US debt. He writes, "So far though, we haven't had any official figures to back up these claims. In fact, some experts have cast doubt on the idea that Japan has meaningfully reduced its treasury holdings." This is a vital correction. In an era of misinformation, the author's insistence on data over narrative prevents the reader from chasing ghosts. The real issue is domestic demand and fiscal policy, not foreign retaliation.

The author's conclusion is a call for perspective rather than panic. He argues that while the situation warrants monitoring, "it perhaps doesn't warrant the alarmism we've seen online." This balanced approach is the piece's greatest strength. It acknowledges the severity of the debt trajectory without succumbing to the apocalyptic framing that dominates social media. By grounding the discussion in the mechanics of auctions, credit ratings, and legislative deficits, Coffin provides a roadmap for understanding the market rather than just reacting to it.

The government's going to be paying a lot more in interest on even just its outstanding debt, and that comes with a number of implications.

Bottom Line

Richard Coffin's strongest contribution is his ability to translate the abstract mechanics of bond auctions and credit ratings into a tangible narrative about the future cost of American governance. His biggest vulnerability lies in the political uncertainty; while he notes the Senate may revise the deficit-increasing bill, the ultimate fiscal path remains a moving target. Readers should watch the 2035 interest-to-revenue ratio closely, as that metric will determine whether the US faces a manageable adjustment or a genuine fiscal crisis.
