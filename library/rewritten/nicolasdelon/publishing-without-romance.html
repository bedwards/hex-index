Nicolas Delon delivers a jarring but necessary provocation: the academic world's sudden war on artificial intelligence tools may have less to do with preserving truth and more to do with protecting the economic rents of a privileged guild. In a landscape where journals scramble to ban AI authorship, Delon applies the cold, hard lens of public choice theory to ask a question no one else is asking: who actually benefits when we make research harder, slower, and more expensive? This is not a Luddite rant; it is a structural critique suggesting that the very institutions tasked with advancing knowledge are acting as rational actors protecting their own scarcity.

The Incentive Structure of Publishing

Delon begins by grounding his argument in the work of economists like James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock, reminding us that "incentives matter" even in the high-minded realm of philosophy. He writes, "When considering any regulation or policy, we can ask, whose interests does it serve?" This simple query dismantles the assumption that academic policies are always designed for the public good. Instead, he posits that policymakers, including journal editors, are "rational (if partially ignorant) actors pursuing their own interests within political institutions."

The author draws a sharp distinction between knowledge as a public good and journal prestige as a "positional good." While the ideas themselves should be free and non-rivalrous, the stamp of approval from a top-tier journal like Ethics is strictly excludable and rivalrous. Delon argues that "scholars have concentrated interests in delegitimizing AI assistance to preserve their funding stream—from grants to salaries—while taxpayers and students bear the dispersed costs of supporting potentially less efficient research." This reframing is powerful because it exposes the hidden friction between the stated mission of academia and the economic reality of its gatekeepers.

"The cynical (or public choice) interpretation is that policies can result from rent-seeking activity (protecting the status and scarcity of human-only labor)."

Critics might argue that this view is overly reductive, ignoring the genuine ethical risks of AI hallucinations or the erosion of critical thinking skills. However, Delon's point is not that AI is perfect, but that the response to it is shaped by self-interest. He notes that in the history of regulatory capture, "small, organized interest groups can successfully lobby for 'wrong-headed' rules that benefit them at the expense of a large, unorganized group." The academic elite fits this description perfectly.

The Burden of Disclosure

The commentary then turns to the specific mechanics of the new policy at the journal Ethics. Delon acknowledges that the editors are tackling a real problem, noting that "AI-restrictive policies seem reasonable in a rapidly evolving context" given the flood of low-quality submissions. Yet, he quickly pivots to the practical absurdity of the proposed solution. The policy demands that authors "track down the original source of an example or objection generated by an AI tool and cite it."

Delon dismantles this requirement with surgical precision. He writes, "Demands that authors track down sources that may not exist, or that would require examining the AI's entire training corpus, makes substantial AI use effectively impossible." This creates a massive transaction cost that negates the efficiency gains of the technology. The policy, he argues, "regulates production methods, not content," effectively punishing researchers for using tools that could democratize high-level analysis.

The author highlights the hypocrisy of this stance by comparing it to how we treat other research aids. "Philosophers typically don't disclose their use of reference management software, university library databases, Google Scholar, PhilPapers, and many casual conversations with colleagues," he observes. "These tools are unmarked and presumed to be legitimate." By singling out AI for special scrutiny, the policy creates an asymmetry that serves to protect the status quo rather than ensure integrity.

"Even if your AI-assisted work is excellent—even if it advances understanding more than purely human work—it arrives marked as suspect, 'less desirable,' by virtue of its provenance."

This section is the most damning because it reveals how the policy functions as a barrier to entry. Delon points out that "content that does not originate with the author may be deemed less desirable and publishable." This effectively tells the academic community that the process of human labor is valued more than the output of truth, a stance that aligns perfectly with rent-seeking behavior where the goal is to limit supply to maintain value.

Bottom Line

Delon's application of public choice theory to academic publishing is a masterclass in looking behind the curtain of noble intentions. His strongest argument is the identification of journal prestige as a positional good that editors are incentivized to protect through restrictive regulations. However, the piece's vulnerability lies in its potential to understate the unique risks of AI, such as the subtle erosion of authorial voice or the propagation of bias at scale, which might justify some level of restriction beyond mere rent-seeking. As the debate moves forward, readers should watch whether these policies evolve to focus on the quality of the final argument or remain fixated on policing the tools of production.
