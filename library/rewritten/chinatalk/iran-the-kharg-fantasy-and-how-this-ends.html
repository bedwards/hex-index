Jordan Schneider and his co-panelists dismantle the prevailing optimism surrounding a potential US invasion of Kharg Island, revealing a strategic vacuum where military capability meets a lack of political vision. This is not a standard war analysis; it is a forensic dissection of how "lethality maximization" without a theory of victory leads to strategic paralysis. For busy leaders tracking global energy security, the piece offers a chilling reality check: the US Navy may possess superior firepower, but it currently lacks the ships, the logistics, and the exit strategy to hold the Persian Gulf.

The Kharg Island Delusion

The discussion opens by exposing the absurdity of treating a complex amphibious assault like a video game scenario. Schneider notes that while the administration teases seizing the island to compel Iranian capitulation, the physical reality is far grimmer. "How are you going to take Kharg Island? You have no ships in the Persian Gulf," Schneider quotes the prevailing military assessment, highlighting a critical gap between political rhetoric and naval deployment. The argument is effective because it strips away the abstraction of "American power" to reveal a specific, immediate deficit: the US has only one ship trapped in the region, unable to project force without becoming a sitting duck for drone swarms.

The panelists draw a sharp contrast between the administration's hope for a quick coup de main and the logistical nightmare of moving massive assets into a contested zone. Schneider paraphrases the military reality, noting that signaling a buildup now merely creates targets for Iranian missiles. This lands hard because it connects directly to the lessons of the 2026 Strait of Hormuz campaign deep dive, where the lack of pre-positioned assets proved fatal. The authors argue that trying to move heavy equipment like C-5 transport planes into the theater after conflict has started is a recipe for disaster. "Trying to get it in once the conflict has started puts you so far behind," Schneider observes, emphasizing that every asset arriving late is a potential casualty.

Critics might argue that the US could rely on overwhelming air power alone to neutralize Iranian defenses before an amphibious landing. However, the panel dismantles this by pointing out the extensive cave systems and "hidey holes" along the Iranian coastline that make air strikes insufficient for securing ground control.

A focus on the gunfight is why we're in this strategic mess to begin with. There's no amount of successful engagements that will become strategically meaningful if you don't have a vision of victory.

The Illusion of Lethality

The commentary shifts to a broader critique of US strategy, which the authors term "lethalitymaxxing." Schneider argues that the current approach assumes that a high density of strikes will eventually break the Iranian will to resist. "They think you can effectively capitulate a will to resist by conducting a sufficient density of strikes," Schneider writes, describing a flawed gambit that ignores the nature of the Iranian regime. This framing is crucial because it challenges the conventional wisdom that superior firepower guarantees strategic success. The authors suggest that the US is fighting a war of attrition without the industrial or political capacity to win it.

The panel draws on historical context, specifically referencing the Silesian Wars, to illustrate how seizing territory without a clear endgame can backfire. Just as Frederick the Great's seizure of Silesia led to a prolonged conflict rather than a quick resolution, Schneider warns that taking Kharg Island could turn the US into a hostage of its own operation. "We're going to take Kharg Island hostage. Wait a minute, now we're the hostages," Schneider paraphrases the grim realization that the US might find itself unable to withdraw without catastrophic loss.

This argument is strengthened by the observation that Iran has already demonstrated a sophisticated irregular warfare capability. Even if the US were to decapitate the leadership, the vast Iranian plateau allows for lone operators to continue disrupting trade. "The idea that they did not already have some form of shadow government in place and ready to continue carrying out orders is asinine," Schneider asserts, pointing out that the US is fighting a decentralized enemy that does not rely on a single command structure.

The Global Economic Suicide Pact

The final section of the commentary addresses the broader geopolitical implications, framing the conflict as an "economic suicide pact" that validates Chinese strategic theories. Schneider argues that the US inability to keep the Strait of Hormuz open confirms Beijing's belief that global supply chains are fragile and that the US cannot sustain prolonged pressure. "Nowhere in Beijing are they like, 'Man, all of our theories are invalidated,'" Schneider writes, noting that China has already stockpiled oil and built pipelines to insulate itself from such shocks.

The authors highlight the irony that Iran's leverage is actually increasing as the conflict drags on. Because the US cannot guarantee the safety of shipping lanes, other nations may seek side agreements with Tehran to secure their own energy needs. "If you're Iran, why give anyone a side agreement?" Schneider asks, turning the logic of coercion on its head. The panel suggests that the US is trapped in a game theory scenario where any deal made is unlikely to be upheld, making negotiation futile for Iran.

If you do this escort operation, it's going to take every available destroyer on the East Coast and in Europe for the duration.

This stark assessment of naval requirements underscores the strategic overreach. The authors warn that dedicating the entire Atlantic and European destroyer fleet to escort duties would gut US deterrence in the Pacific, creating a dangerous power vacuum. Critics might note that the US could rely on allies to share the burden, but the panel counters that the sheer scale of the threat—requiring constant whack-a-mole operations against mines and drones—makes this a uniquely American liability.

Bottom Line

Schneider's most compelling contribution is the exposure of the disconnect between tactical lethality and strategic victory, proving that bombing campaigns cannot force open a strait controlled by a resilient, irregular enemy. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its assumption that the US political will to escalate further is nonexistent, potentially underestimating the administration's willingness to accept high costs for a symbolic victory. Readers should watch for whether the US attempts to force the issue with a full-scale amphibious assault, a move that would likely confirm the panel's direst predictions.
