Jordan Schneider exposes a hidden economy where AI safety in China is not a philosophical debate but a mandatory, monetized service industry. While Western discourse often frames safety as a brake on innovation, Schneider reveals a market where compliance is the engine, creating a unique ecosystem of vendors selling "ideological alignment" alongside technical robustness. This is essential listening for anyone trying to understand how China's regulatory state actually functions on the ground, far beyond the headlines of censorship.

The Compliance Marketplace

Schneider begins by dismantling the assumption that the Chinese government's safety list is merely a bureaucratic formality. He notes that while over 6,000 companies are approved, the path to that list is opaque. "The CAC doesn't clearly explain is how to actually get on that list," Schneider writes, highlighting a gap between regulation and execution that has spawned a cottage industry. This observation is crucial because it shifts the narrative from state control to market adaptation; the state sets the rules, but private vendors are the ones selling the keys.

The author's investigation into Taobao, China's massive e-commerce platform, reveals the sheer pragmatism of this system. "Search 'AI evaluation testing' on Taobao, China's largest e-commerce website, and you'll find a cottage industry of vendors advertising services that map onto CAC requirements," he observes. The prices are shockingly low on the surface, yet the real cost is hidden in the complexity of the service. Schneider's undercover work as a fake student startup demonstrates that these vendors are not just selling forms; they are selling access to specialized knowledge of the review process. "We are responsible for writing the materials for the large-scale model registration, while you are responsible for optimizing the model," one vendor told him. This division of labor suggests that for many startups, navigating the Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC) is a technical hurdle as significant as building the model itself.

In AI terms, that kind of delay can feel like an eternity, with your hot product today facing the risk of becoming obsolete.

Schneider rightly points out the asymmetry in this system. A five-person startup faces the same regulatory maze as Alibaba, yet lacks the resources to navigate it. "The CAC doesn't distinguish between billion-dollar incumbents and five-person founding teams; all these different players have to navigate the same compliance maze," he argues. This creates a market where safety services become a survival mechanism for smaller players. However, critics might note that this "cottage industry" could also be a vector for corruption or inconsistent enforcement, as the quality of these third-party validators is not standardized.

Safety as a Business Model

Moving beyond the informal vendors, Schneider explores the rise of formal third-party safety firms like RealAI and BotSmart. These companies are not merely compliance shops; they are positioning safety as a full-fledged business model. "Firms like RealAI aren't just trying to sell you on passing the CAC requirements, though they'll do that for you if you ask," Schneider writes. They offer end-to-end infrastructure, including adversarial testing and content filtering.

This section draws a sharp contrast with the West. In the United States, safety efforts are often philanthropically funded or driven by voluntary risk management. "When the state doesn't set the terms, the market does, and markets have little patience for those asking them to slow down," Schneider explains. In China, the state sets the terms, which paradoxically creates a more robust commercial market for safety tools. The author notes that "safety becomes not just best practice, but a prerequisite for launch," forcing companies to invest in these services to exist.

This dynamic mirrors the evolution of China's "social credit system," where compliance mechanisms were once theoretical but are now deeply embedded in commercial and social life. Just as that system created a market for credit scoring and monitoring, the AI safety regime has birthed a market for ideological verification. Schneider highlights that these firms are "more 'safety-pilled' than simple compliance shops," engaging in genuine research on interpretability and loss-of-control scenarios, even if their primary revenue driver is regulatory approval.

In the West, governance vendors help enterprises document risk and prepare for frameworks like the EU AI Act. But these businesses are largely capitalizing on voluntary (or at least not mandatory) demand.

The author's comparison is illuminating. While Western companies might view safety as a reputational shield, Chinese companies view it as a license to operate. This removes the friction between "safety" and "progress" that plagues Western discourse. "In China, that dichotomy feels less pronounced, where both AI safety and market direction are assumed to be the state's responsibility," Schneider concludes. This is a powerful insight: by making safety a state mandate, the ideological battle over whether to prioritize safety is effectively removed from the corporate boardroom.

The Limits of Policy-Driven Growth

Despite the booming market, Schneider remains skeptical of the most optimistic projections. BotSmart, a key player, predicts the market will reach 242 billion yuan by 2028. "I'm skeptical of this prediction, not just because of its inflated numbers, but also its assumption of an inevitable increase in safety regulation," Schneider counters. He points out that China's open-source culture allows companies to build on models that have already cleared review, potentially reducing the need for new filings.

Furthermore, the scope of "safety" in China remains narrowly defined. "Chinese regulators have so far focused narrowly on political and social content control," he writes, noting that frontier risks like chemical or biological misuse (CBRN) are largely ignored. This suggests the market might hit a ceiling if regulators do not expand their definition of safety beyond ideological compliance. The rise of "agentic AI," however, could change the calculus. "Agents lack a dedicated national regulatory regime and are generally subject only to provincial-level review," Schneider notes, leaving a gap that could either stall growth or trigger a new wave of regulation.

This growth is largely policy-driven. The Interim Measures for the Management of Generative Artificial Intelligence Services establish a principle of "mandatory review before launch," turning AI security into a rigid, unavoidable requirement for companies.

The author's skepticism is well-founded. While the immediate market is driven by the need to pass the CAC's "safety list," the long-term viability of these firms depends on whether the state demands deeper, more technical safety measures. If the focus remains solely on political keywords, the market may become a race to the bottom on price rather than a driver of genuine technological safety.

Bottom Line

Schneider's most compelling argument is that China has successfully industrialized AI safety, turning a regulatory burden into a thriving commercial sector. The piece's greatest strength is its on-the-ground reporting, which reveals the human and financial cost of compliance for startups. However, the argument's vulnerability lies in its assumption that this model will scale indefinitely; if the definition of "safety" remains limited to political compliance, the market may struggle to justify the massive growth projections cited by industry players. Readers should watch how the regulatory framework adapts to agentic AI, as that shift could either validate the current safety industry or render its current focus obsolete.
