Jordan Schneider doesn't just interview Jen Pahlka; he arms her with a specific, terrifying metric that reframes the entire debate on government efficiency: 7,119 pages. In this ChinaTalk episode, the host and the former White House CTO move beyond the usual platitudes about "modernization" to confront the sheer, unadministrable weight of American bureaucracy. The piece is notable because it stops treating AI as a magic wand for automation and starts treating it as the only tool capable of mapping the labyrinth of regulatory cruft that has accumulated since the Eisenhower administration. For the busy listener, this is the crucial pivot: we aren't waiting for a political savior; we are waiting for the technical ability to even articulate what a functioning government looks like.

The Architecture of Cruft

Schneider and Pahlka immediately dismantle the nostalgia for a "golden age" of the administrative state. Pahlka argues that the mid-20th-century government was effective not because it was perfect, but because it had a built-in mechanism for self-renewal. She points to a specific historical practice under the Eisenhower administration known as "work simplification," where agencies constantly streamlined their own processes. "What we lost was that notion of constantly re-examining things," Pahlka explains. "We got lazy and let policy and process accumulate like layers of cruft — archaeological layers you can dig back through."

This historical framing is vital. It suggests that the current dysfunction isn't a result of malice or a deliberate dismantling of state capacity, but rather a failure of maintenance. The argument holds up well against the counter-narrative that the bureaucracy was intentionally hollowed out; instead, it was allowed to calcify. As Pahlka notes, "Our legislators and policymakers came to believe that success means adding rules, mandates, and constraints, instead of constantly asking — what should this process look like? What do we need to remove to make it effective?"

We have undone state capacity by doing too much in a certain way. It's primarily the laziness of not cleaning up our messes rather than the intentional undoing of anything.

Critics might argue that this "laziness" ignores the political incentives that reward complexity. In the Progressive Era, for instance, new regulations were often added to solve specific, visible crises without a mechanism to remove them later. Pahlka's focus on the lack of a "cleaning" culture is persuasive, but it assumes a political will to delete that rarely exists when every rule has a constituency that benefits from it.

The AI Illusion and the Political Reality

The conversation takes a sharp turn when addressing the role of Artificial Intelligence. There is a temptation to view AI as the solution that will simply rewrite the 7,000 pages of New Jersey unemployment insurance regulations into something manageable. Pahlka is brutally honest about the limits of this technology. "The AI cannot do anything about the political will required to unwind the memos, guidance, policy, regulations, and statutes that need to be unwound," she states. "Humans have to do that."

This is the piece's most sophisticated contribution. It separates the technical feasibility of rewriting a law from the political reality of passing it. AI can generate the text, but it cannot build the coalition to enact it. Pahlka warns that we risk creating a new set of barriers under the guise of safety. "While people can create guardrails that sound perfectly reasonable on paper, in a risk-averse, overburdened bureaucracy, those guardrails don't function as guardrails. They function as barriers you simply cannot overcome."

The danger here is that we might regulate AI into irrelevance before we even understand its potential to simplify the underlying code of government. As Schneider and Pahlka discuss, the "binding constraint isn't the AI. It's our political system." This reframes the entire debate from "how do we stop AI?" to "how do we use AI to make the political case for simplicity?"

The Recoding America Fund and the Operating Model

Pahlka introduces the Recoding America Fund not as a policy think tank, but as a field catalyst designed to bridge the gap between technical possibility and political action. The fund's mission is to build a cross-ideological coalition around the idea that the government's operating model is broken. "Whether your issue is education, housing, transportation, or criminal justice, what you realize is that you can bring in better policy and still not get the intended impact," Pahlka argues. "That's because... you can't iterate meaningfully on policy when the basics aren't covered."

She uses a powerful analogy: just as Maslow's hierarchy dictates that you cannot achieve self-actualization without food and shelter, you cannot have effective policy without a functioning operating model. The current model is described as "industrial-era," a system that was excellent for its time but is now incompatible with the speed of the AI era. "We slapped websites on the front end of it when the internet arrived without fundamentally adapting it, and now we're entering the AI era needing to leapfrog it entirely."

The strategy relies on state-level experimentation. "States are valuable because you have more opportunities to find where the energy is, prove it works, and let other states and cities adopt it," Pahlka says. This approach mirrors the "laboratories of democracy" concept from the early 20th century but applies it to digital governance. The hope is that a successful pilot in one state can create a proof-of-concept that the federal government can then adopt, bypassing the paralysis of Washington.

The basics are the operating model of government — and ours is an industrial-era model that was excellent for its time.

A counterargument worth considering is the difficulty of scaling state-level successes to the federal level. The federal government has unique constraints, particularly in national defense and social security, that state governments do not face. While Pahlka argues that the operating model is largely independent of the policy area, the political dynamics of federal reform are significantly more complex than state-level experimentation.

Bottom Line

Jordan Schneider and Jen Pahlka deliver a rare, clear-eyed assessment of government reform that refuses to hide behind technocratic jargon or political platitudes. The strongest part of their argument is the realization that the barrier to progress is not a lack of ideas, but a lack of a clear target; we cannot fix what we cannot even describe. The biggest vulnerability remains the political economy of bureaucracy: even if AI can map the 7,119 pages of regulations, the constituencies that benefit from that complexity will fight tooth and nail to keep them. Watch for the Recoding America Fund to test whether a cross-ideological coalition can actually form around the unglamorous work of deleting rules.
