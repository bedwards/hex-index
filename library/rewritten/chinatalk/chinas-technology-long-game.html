Jordan Schneider strips away the myth of a sudden, Xi-era pivot to reveal a chillingly consistent forty-year strategy: China's technology drive is not a reaction to the West, but a relentless, generational pursuit of self-reliance. By dissecting decades of Five-Year Plans, Schneider proves that today's obsession with "chokepoints" and AI is merely the latest chapter in a story that began with Deng Xiaoping's 1978 mandate that modernization is impossible without science.

The Long Game of Persistence

Schneider's most compelling evidence lies in the sheer stubbornness of China's targets. He writes, "China has been relentlessly persistent at tackling the same core technologies over decades." This observation reframes the narrative from one of opportunistic copying to one of strategic endurance. The author notes that sectors like automotive, energy, and semiconductors appear in plan after plan, not because China is failing, but because these are high-stakes industries where the global frontier keeps moving.

This persistence is not accidental; it is institutionalized. Schneider points out that the 1978 National Science & Technology Development Plan targeted semiconductors and biotech, goals that remain central today. The continuity is so strong that it mirrors the long-term capital allocation seen in the 863 Program, which launched in 1986 to specifically fund high-tech research in areas like robotics and aerospace. As Schneider puts it, "Many of the target technologies identified by the 1978 plan have remained central to China's tech-industrial policy ever since." This suggests that while Western policy often shifts with every election cycle, Beijing's industrial policy operates on a timeline that spans generations, allowing them to outlast short-term market fluctuations.

Critics might argue that this persistence also signals inefficiency, pointing to decades of wasted capital in sectors where China still lags behind global leaders. However, Schneider's data suggests a different reality: the repetition is a feature, not a bug, designed to wear down the "latecomer advantage" of the West through sheer volume of effort.

"The key to the Four Modernizations is the modernization of science and technology. Without modern science and technology, it is impossible to build modern agriculture, modern industry, and modern national defense."

From Catch-Up to the Chokepoint

The article's most critical insight is the evolution of China's mindset, shifting from the optimism of "leapfrog development" to the defensive urgency of securing "key core technologies." Schneider explains that in the 1990s and 2000s, China viewed technology as an opportunity to skip stages of development. But the tone changed drastically after the US targeted Huawei and ZTE. "China sees itself as painfully vulnerable to technological 'chokepoints' and races to develop 'key core technologies'... to make China more resilient to external threats," he writes.

This shift is not merely rhetorical; it is a fundamental restructuring of the economy. Schneider notes that the 2006 launch of the Medium-and Long-Term Plan marked a turn toward "indigenous innovation," a move started under Hu Jintao long before Xi Jinping took power. This historical context is vital, as it debunks the idea that this is a personality cult project. The drive for self-reliance is a systemic imperative. The author highlights that the 14th Five-Year Plan now treats AI and embodied AI not just as products, but as cross-cutting technologies that will turbocharge every other sector, similar to how the internet transformed the economy in the West.

A counterargument worth considering is whether this "chokepoint" mentality actually accelerates innovation or merely creates a fortress of inferior technology. While Schneider emphasizes the strategic necessity, he acknowledges the difficulty of catching up in fields like advanced semiconductors where the global frontier is a "rapidly moving target."

"Rather than merely import foreign technology, Chinese leaders believed that China must be able to able to truly create and own the technology itself through innovation."

The Energy Security Imperative

One of Schneider's strongest analytical threads is the link between energy anxiety and technological dominance. He argues that China's massive push into solar, wind, and batteries is not just about climate change, but a decades-long response to energy insecurity. "The seeds for China's clean tech boom were already planted as far back as the 6th Five-Year Plan (1981-1985)," he notes.

This historical depth adds weight to current headlines about China's dominance in green tech. Schneider writes that earlier plans focused on energy-saving machinery, but over time, the focus shifted to a "massive push in clean technology." This evolution mirrors the trajectory of the National Fund for Technology Transfer and Commercialisation, which was designed to bridge the gap between lab research and industrial application, ensuring that these energy technologies actually reached the market. The author's point is clear: China's green revolution is the result of forty years of planning, not a sudden pivot to ESG goals.

"China has been heavily focused on energy-saving technologies and alternative energy sources for decades, driven by long-standing anxieties over energy security."

Bottom Line

Schneider's most powerful contribution is dismantling the illusion that China's tech rise is a sudden, reactive phenomenon; it is a calculated, multi-generational strategy that has survived leadership changes and geopolitical shifts. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in assuming that state-directed persistence can overcome the fundamental inefficiencies of central planning, particularly in the rapid, chaotic field of artificial intelligence. The reader should watch not just for what China invents, but for how quickly its "indigenous innovation" can scale without the global collaboration it once relied upon.
