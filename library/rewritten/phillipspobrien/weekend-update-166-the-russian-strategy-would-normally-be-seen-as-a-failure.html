O'Brien cuts through the noise of 2026 with a jarring, data-driven reality check: the Russian war machine is not inexorably rolling toward victory, but is instead trapped in a self-destructive cycle of attrition that the West is failing to recognize. While Washington narratives suggest Ukraine is withering, O'Brien marshals casualty figures and territorial maps to argue that Russia is achieving less than 1 percent of its objectives at a cost that should, by all historical standards, constitute a strategic collapse. This is not just a battlefield update; it is a challenge to the very framework through which we are viewing the endgame of the conflict.

The Math of Attrition

The core of O'Brien's argument rests on a stark discrepancy between perception and the brutal arithmetic of the front lines. He points to Institute for the Study of War data showing that throughout 2025, Russian forces seized merely 0.8 percent of Ukrainian territory. "During all of 2025, and at vast cost, the Russians seized less than 1 percent of Ukrainian territory," O'Brien writes, noting that these gains were often just empty fields taken by troops running to the next tree line only to be killed or wounded. The author emphasizes that these micro-advances cannot be exploited because there are no logistical build-ups behind them to support a breakthrough. "There will not be any in the foreseeable future," he asserts, describing a widening "kill zone" that forces soldiers to struggle longer just to reach the front.

The human cost of this stagnation is staggering. O'Brien cites BBC reports and leaked Russian data indicating total casualties for 2025 likely reached 400,000, with recruitment flatlining at the same number. "Gaining .8 of 1 percent of Ukraine for 400,000 casualties could turn into gaining .5 of 1 percent of Ukraine for 500,000 casualties—and that would represent a death spiral of the Russian army," he warns. This framing is powerful because it strips away the mystique of Russian momentum, replacing it with a simple economic equation of resources versus returns that favors the defender. Critics might argue that O'Brien underestimates the psychological impact of territorial loss on Ukrainian morale, regardless of the strategic value of the land. However, the sheer scale of the losses suggests that the Russian military's capacity to sustain this pace is the more immediate existential threat.

In almost all historical cases, this would be considered a Russian strategy of failure.

A Shift in Kyiv's Command

Amidst the static front lines, O'Brien highlights a significant political maneuver in Kyiv that signals a shift in Ukraine's long-term posture. President Zelensky's appointment of Kyrylo Budanov, the head of Military Intelligence, as Chief of Staff replaces the outgoing Andrii Yermak. O'Brien notes that Budanov is not a traditional insider and has historically faced tension with the President, yet his selection suggests a pivot toward a prolonged conflict. "We need to prepare for a long, exhausting struggle. And this is exactly the approach that Budanov represents," O'Brien paraphrases a lawmaker from Zelensky's party, linking the appointment to a rejection of immediate peace deals. The author observes that Budanov's intelligence service has been responsible for some of the war's most effective operations, including targeted strikes on Russian officers, making him a figure who is "hardly identified with the peace party."

This move is framed not just as a personnel change, but as a strategic signal that Ukraine is preparing for a war of endurance rather than a quick resolution. O'Brien suggests that while some speculate this is a political play to counter General Zaluzhny's popularity, the appointment of a hardline operator like Budanov fundamentally alters the government's capacity to negotiate. "Zelensky has not opted for an insider patsy," O'Brien writes, arguing that Budanov's prominence will help the government deal with outside powers. This analysis holds weight given the recent corruption scandal that forced Yermak out; replacing a political operator with a military intelligence chief suggests a prioritization of operational security and warfighting capability over domestic political maneuvering.

The Narrative Trap

Perhaps the most biting section of the commentary addresses the disconnect between the reality on the ground and the narrative emanating from Washington. O'Brien argues that the administration's refusal to fully support Ukraine has inadvertently handed a "lifeline" to Moscow, allowing a failing strategy to persist. "If the USA and Europe together had jumped in to help Ukraine last year, the trajectories would have gotten worse for Russia—casualties would be higher, territory gained smaller, and almost certainly Ukrainian casualties would be lower," he contends. The author suggests that the current narrative of Ukrainian collapse is a self-fulfilling prophecy fueled by a lack of political will in the West, rather than battlefield reality.

This section draws a sharp parallel to the concept of a "Pyrrhic victory," where a victory is so costly that it is tantamount to defeat. Just as the ancient King Pyrrhus lost so many men defeating the Romans that he could not continue the war, O'Brien implies that Russia is winning battles only to lose the war of attrition. The danger, he argues, is that the West is misreading this exhaustion as strength. "It just proves that states do not always (or even often) do what is in their interest," O'Brien writes, lamenting that the opportunity to exploit Russian vulnerabilities is being squandered by fear and political inertia. The argument is compelling because it shifts the blame from the battlefield to the diplomatic table, suggesting that the war's outcome is being decided by the hesitation of allies rather than the actions of the belligerents.

The Bottom Line

Phillips P. O'Brien delivers a necessary corrective to the prevailing optimism about Russian momentum, grounding his analysis in casualty data that reveals a Russian strategy of self-destruction. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to accept the narrative of inevitable Russian victory, instead highlighting the unsustainable cost of minimal gains. However, its biggest vulnerability lies in assuming that Western allies will suddenly recognize this opportunity and act, ignoring the deep political fractures that currently prevent a unified response. The reader should watch for whether Ukraine's new leadership can leverage this stalemate to secure the resources needed to turn Russia's attrition into a decisive collapse, or if the window for intervention is closing.

Russia's strategy of failure has been handed a lifeline by Washington, but it could still result in overall failure if Ukraine's friends step up.
