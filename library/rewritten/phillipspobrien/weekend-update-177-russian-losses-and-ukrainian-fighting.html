O'Brien delivers a jarring pivot from the prevailing Western narrative, arguing that the war in Ukraine has fundamentally shifted not through manpower, but through a technological revolution that is inflicting record-breaking casualties on Russia. While the media cycle obsesses over "manpower collapse," O'Brien presents evidence that Ukrainian drone units are rendering traditional infantry assaults obsolete, even as political winds in Washington shift decisively against Kyiv.

The Death of the Infantry Assault

The core of O'Brien's argument is that the Ukrainian military has successfully reimagined its doctrine to leverage machines over men. He writes, "This is not... the result of Ukrainians adding drones to existing units, it is because they have re-imagined their military organization and created entire drone-based units." This distinction is critical; it suggests a structural evolution rather than a tactical patch. The author supports this with staggering data from mid-March, noting that Russian losses spiked to nearly 10,000 casualties in a single week, with two record-breaking days exceeding 1,700 losses each.

"The bet on invisibility under old military canons was supposed to work... over a hundred enemy bodies were put face down into the ground by Unmanned Systems Forces fighters even before midnight."

O'Brien cites Ukrainian commander Robert Brovdi, known as "Madyar," to illustrate how Russian attempts to use fog and weather for cover were utterly neutralized by drone swarms. The commentary here is compelling because it moves beyond abstract statistics to the visceral reality of the battlefield: a "worm group" of infiltrators is destroyed before they can even engage. This aligns with the strategic philosophy of the non-profit Come Back Alive, which O'Brien highlights for its work on robotic land vehicles designed to save human lives by handling logistics and evacuation in the kill zone.

Critics might argue that relying heavily on electronic-dependent technology creates a fragile vulnerability if Russian electronic warfare capabilities improve, but O'Brien counters this by highlighting the rise of fiber-optic drones. These devices, connected by thin cables to their pilots, cannot be jammed. He notes a recent strike launched "to a depth of 50 kilometres — into Russia itself," a feat that underscores the terrifying reach of this new warfare.

The Political Pivot

While the battlefield narrative is one of adaptation, the political narrative O'Brien presents is one of abandonment. He argues that the United States, under a Trump administration, has moved from performative support to active facilitation of the Russian war effort. "Ever since the last time Trump tried to pretend he might come down hard on Putin... he has given up the pretense that he would ever do anything to hurt the Russian dictator," O'Brien asserts.

The author details a series of policy shifts that he claims are designed to bolster the Russian economy under the guise of unrelated geopolitical maneuvers. He points to the relaxation of oil sanctions, which he estimates could generate an additional $5 billion for Russia in March alone. Furthermore, he highlights the delisting of Belarusian potash producers and financial institutions, describing the move as "blasting open an economic corridor for Putin."

"Using the pretext of his decision to attack Iran, Trump pushed a series of moves designed to help Russia, most of which had absolutely nothing to do with what is happening in Iran."

This section of the piece is aggressive and specific, naming individuals like Boris Gennadiyevich Vorontsov and institutions like Belinvestbank to illustrate the breadth of the sanctions relief. The argument draws a sharp parallel to historical failures of deterrence; much like the diplomatic miscalculations preceding the Battle of the Somme, where attrition was accepted as the only path forward, O'Brien suggests the West is now accepting a path of economic capitulation to Russia while Ukraine fights on. The author notes that Defense Secretary Hegseth has begun blaming aid to Ukraine for depleting US stocks, signaling a future where support is not just reduced but actively curtailed.

The Manpower Myth

Perhaps the most significant contribution of this update is the dismantling of the "manpower collapse" narrative that has dominated Western analysis for months. O'Brien references a new strategic paper by Andriy Zagorodnyuk, a respected Ukrainian thinker, to reframe the issue. "How forces fight matters more than how many soldiers they field," the author paraphrases from the new doctrine.

"Units that adapt doctrine to new environments—integrating dispersion, concealment, reconnaissance, and unmanned systems—generate greater operational effect per soldier while reducing attrition."

This perspective suggests that the Western media's obsession with troop numbers is a failure of imagination, ignoring the reality that modern technology acts as a force multiplier. The success of Ukrainian operations in late 2025 around Kupyansk is cited as proof that they can halt and reverse Russian offensives with minimal physical presence. While some analysts might worry that this high-tech approach is unsustainable without Western industrial backing, O'Brien's evidence suggests that Ukraine is currently out-innovating Russia's mass mobilization.

"The technological character of the battlefield has changed profoundly... Ukraine's drone-enabled units provide the clearest illustration."

Bottom Line

Phillips P. O'Brien's strongest argument lies in his synthesis of battlefield data and strategic doctrine, proving that Ukraine's drone-centric model is inflicting unsustainable costs on Russia without the manpower crisis Western pundits predicted. However, the piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on a specific, volatile political interpretation of US policy that could shift rapidly, potentially undermining the very economic pressure Ukraine needs to sustain its technological edge. Readers should watch whether the "fiber-optic revolution" can scale against Russian countermeasures before the political will to support it evaporates.
