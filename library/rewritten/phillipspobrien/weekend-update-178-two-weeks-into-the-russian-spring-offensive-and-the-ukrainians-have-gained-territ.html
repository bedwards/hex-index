O'Brien delivers a jarring counter-narrative to the prevailing gloom: two weeks into a massive Russian spring offensive, the attackers have gained almost nothing while the defenders have quietly taken ground. The piece stands out not for speculation, but for its granular, data-driven dismantling of the idea that Russia's current momentum is sustainable, backed by casualty figures and specific drone strike logs that suggest a fundamental shift in the war's trajectory.

The Collapse of the Russian Assault

O'Brien's central thesis rests on a stark contradiction: Russia is launching its most intense ground campaign of the war, yet achieving zero strategic movement. He writes, "Starting around March 17, there was a massive spike in Russian ground attacks in Ukraine... the Russians launched more than 600 different assaults across the front." Yet, despite the sheer volume of violence, the outcome is a stalemate that favors the defender. The author notes that "Russian gains have been in practical terms non existent," a claim supported by the fact that Ukrainian forces actually regained four square miles during the offensive's first week.

This analysis is bolstered by a grim assessment of the human cost required for such futile advances. O'Brien points out that "the enormous losses that the Russians have suffered, and their insatiable need to send more and more forces into the attack, seems to be reaching a point of diminishing returns." The data is brutal: daily casualties averaging over 1,000, with some days exceeding 1,300. This creates a vicious cycle where the only way to maintain the offensive is to lower training standards, which in turn increases losses. As O'Brien observes, "the amount of training that Russian soldiers are getting before being sent to the front is down to one week."

"The tactics are resulting in such high losses and low morale that the extremely nationalistic Russian milblogger community is now regularly bemoaning the present state of the Russian army."

The argument here is compelling because it moves beyond simple casualty counts to explain the mechanism of failure. By highlighting that soldiers are being sent forward "barely trained" and "understand that they are signing up for close to certain death," O'Brien illustrates a systemic rot that cannot be fixed by sheer numbers. Critics might argue that attrition favors the side with deeper manpower reserves, but the author effectively counters this by showing that the quality of those reserves has degraded to the point of uselessness.

The Asymmetric Ranged War

While the ground war has stalled, the author shifts focus to a more dynamic front: the ranged war. O'Brien contrasts Russia's massive, indiscriminate bombardments with Ukraine's surgical strikes on economic infrastructure. He details how Russia launched "the largest ranged bombardment of the war" with 982 drones and missiles in a single 24-hour period, aiming to break civilian will and drain air defense stocks.

In response, Ukraine has executed a sophisticated campaign against Russian oil terminals on the Baltic, specifically targeting Primorsk and Ust-Luga. O'Brien writes, "Ukraine is now going after the whole Russian production/exporting system and that Ukraine now understands that it takes repeated attacks on the same facilities to put them out of action." This is a critical evolution in strategy. The author notes that these strikes have forced Russian exporters to the brink of declaring "Force Majeure," a legal declaration that contracts cannot be fulfilled, and have already caused a temporary halt in refined fuel exports.

"Ukraine is transforming in front of our eyes into a world leader in military tech. It is the best example of the point I tried to make a few weeks ago, that Europe needs Ukraine as much as Ukraine needs Europe."

The comparison between the two sides' ranged strategies is effective. Russia is trying to win by destruction, while Ukraine is winning by disruption. The author also weaves in the context of unmanned systems, referencing a recent discussion with Bohdan Zhelobchuk of Come Back Alive regarding Unmanned Ground Vehicles (UGVs). Just as UGVs are becoming "increasingly ubiquitous" for saving lives on the front lines, the success of the Baltic strikes demonstrates that Ukraine's mastery of drone warfare extends far beyond the immediate battlefield. This mirrors the historical lesson from the Battle of Bakhmut, where the sheer density of artillery and drones made traditional human advances nearly impossible, a reality that now seems to be locking Russia into a defensive posture despite their offensive labels.

Ukraine as a Global Defense Partner

Perhaps the most surprising pivot in the piece is the discussion of Ukraine's new role as a defense exporter. O'Brien highlights defense agreements signed with Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and the UAE, noting that these nations have concluded that "the USA is not the best partner for this vital work." Instead, they are turning to Ukraine for its expertise in counter-drone measures and AI.

This section reframes the entire narrative of the war. O'Brien argues, "People need to stop looking at Ukraine simply as a state that needs help and support." The evidence lies in the 10-year defense pacts that include joint production factories and technology transfer. This is not just about selling weapons; it is about exporting a doctrine of survival. The author suggests that while the US and European defense industries are "stuck in 2021," Ukraine is already operating in the future of warfare.

"When it comes to one of the most pressing military threats facing states these days, the USA is not the preferred partner of choice, nor are any of the traditional European defense firms—it is the Ukrainians."

A counterargument worth considering is whether these Gulf nations are seeking Ukrainian tech or simply hedging their bets against US political volatility. However, O'Brien's point stands that the perception of Ukraine as a leader is now a geopolitical reality. The speed of this transition—from aid recipient to strategic partner—is the most significant long-term takeaway of the article.

Bottom Line

O'Brien's strongest argument is the data-driven demonstration that Russia's "Spring Offensive" is a hollow victory that is actively degrading its own military capacity through attrition and poor training. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on Ukrainian casualty reports and forward-looking economic projections that could be disrupted by external factors like oil price spikes. Readers should watch for whether the Gulf states' defense deals translate into tangible hardware for Ukraine, as that could be the key to sustaining this technological edge.
