O'Brien makes a provocative claim that cuts against the grain of current geopolitical anxiety: the administration's escalating military failures in Iran may be the very catalyst needed to force a long-overdue American withdrawal from the Middle East. While headlines focus on the immediate violence, O'Brien argues that the exposure of US strategic weakness is not a disaster, but a necessary painful lesson that could end decades of costly interventionism.

The Illusion of Leverage

O'Brien begins by dismantling the narrative of American strength, observing that despite aggressive rhetoric and renewed bombing campaigns, the executive branch has achieved no substantive concessions from Tehran. He writes, "There has never been an actual basis for ending this war, as Iran has agreed to not a single one of Trump's demands." This stark assessment reframes the recent escalation not as a show of force, but as a desperate attempt by the White House to find a "fig leaf" to cover a strategic defeat. The author notes that the Memorandum of Understanding was always a vague document where Iran made no real concessions, yet O'Brien suggests this failure is revealing a deeper truth about American power.

The core of his argument rests on the idea that the US has exhausted its ability to dictate outcomes in the region. "He has no real leverage over Iran (less by by day), but feels he cannot walk away without a fig leaf to hide his defeat," O'Brien observes. This dynamic mirrors historical precedents where great powers, realizing their tools are blunt, double down on futile actions rather than admit strategic obsolescence. Critics might note that assuming the US can simply "walk away" ignores the complex web of alliances and security guarantees already in place; a sudden vacuum could destabilize the region further before any new equilibrium is found.

The US has so humiliated itself, while exposing its weakness and the pointlessness of its military posture, that, perhaps, the country can do what it should have done years/decades ago and wind down its military presence in the Middle East.

The Cost of Sunk Costs

Moving beyond the immediate conflict, O'Brien connects the current crisis to a thirty-five-year pattern of "sunk-cost fallacy" driving US policy since the end of the Cold War. He argues that the First Gulf War offered a golden opportunity to shift from an interventionist posture to a supportive role for regional partners, but that window was missed. Now, he contends, the administration's actions are merely reinforcing the futility of direct military engagement. "The USA is much weaker militarily than it seemed even a few years ago," O'Brien writes, pointing to high loss rates of advanced weaponry like Reaper drones and the depletion of stockpiles as evidence that the defense industrial base can no longer sustain global hegemony.

This analysis is particularly sharp when applied to the Strait of Hormuz. Just as historical operations in the region highlighted the difficulty of controlling maritime chokepoints without local cooperation, O'Brien notes that Iran is now actively enforcing tolls and controlling shipping lanes, effectively ignoring US demands. "You know what Iran and Israel share? They are both showing how weak the USA is by playing with it," he states. The administration's rage at these developments only underscores a reality: regional actors no longer fear American retaliation enough to alter their behavior.

A Strategic Retreat or a Vacuum?

Perhaps the most controversial aspect of O'Brien's commentary is his suggestion that the US should actively encourage its allies to take over their own security, even if it means abandoning them in the short term. He draws a parallel to the Chinese approach, noting that Beijing has benefited from avoiding direct military entanglement while securing reconstruction contracts and strategic influence. "The Chinese have shown the real advantage of working through allies and partners while not being militarily involved itself," O'Brien argues. This perspective challenges the traditional view that US presence is the only barrier against regional chaos.

However, this argument requires a sober acknowledgment of the human cost that accompanies such a withdrawal. While O'Brien focuses on strategic efficiency and financial waste, the immediate consequence of reducing the US footprint could be increased suffering for civilians caught in crossfire or economic collapse. The "good" emerging from "bad" scenarios is not guaranteed; it depends entirely on whether regional powers can negotiate stability without great power intervention. As O'Brien puts it, "At some point the US needs to understand that it does not make things better to use military force, and the only way to make sure this stops in the future is not to have military force in the region ready to go."

It has entrenched a more radical version of the Iranian government in power, emboldened it, and will lead to more suffering from those in Iran who want a free country.

Bottom Line

O'Brien's strongest contribution is his unflinching diagnosis that the administration's military posture is not just failing tactically, but is fundamentally unsustainable strategically, offering a rare argument for disengagement based on weakness rather than moral imperative. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its optimism that a US withdrawal would lead to regional self-determination rather than immediate chaos, an assumption that glosses over the potential for power vacuums to be filled by more aggressive actors. Readers should watch whether the administration can find a diplomatic off-ramp or if the cycle of "pointless bombing" continues until the political will to withdraw finally breaks through.
