O'Brien cuts through the noise of military statistics to reveal a brutal geopolitical truth: Donald Trump's desire for a quick exit from the Iran conflict is being held hostage by the very economy he claims to protect. While the administration touts thousands of destroyed targets, O'Brien argues that a single Iranian drone strike on a supertanker carries more strategic weight than the entire US air campaign, creating a "strategic prison" from which the President cannot easily escape.

The Illusion of Metrics

O'Brien dismantles the administration's reliance on raw numbers to declare victory. He points out that while Secretary of Defense Hegseth and General Dan Caine boast about striking 11,000 targets, these metrics fail to capture the actual strategic reality. "The metrics do not actually tell you what Hegseth and Caine want them to," O'Brien writes, noting that Iran does not need to match US firepower to win; it only needs to convince the world's insurance markets that shipping is too dangerous. This is a sharp, necessary correction to the "body count" mentality that often plagues war reporting. The author effectively uses the recent attack on the Al Salmi, a tanker carrying 2 million barrels of fuel, to illustrate that the value of a single successful Iranian strike—estimated at $350 million in cargo and ship value—outweighs the cost of the US response in terms of global economic stability.

"In war, the enemy gets a vote."

Critics might argue that O'Brien underestimates the long-term degradation of Iran's military capacity, suggesting that sustained bombing will eventually force compliance. However, the author's focus on the immediate economic stranglehold of the Strait of Hormuz offers a more urgent and tangible measure of failure than abstract military attrition. The comparison to the strategic implications of Goodhart's law is apt here: once a metric (target count) becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure of success.

The Shifting Goalposts of Victory

The piece excels in exposing the cognitive dissonance within the Trump administration regarding what constitutes a win. O'Brien highlights the chaotic evolution of victory conditions, noting that while the State Department lists "minimalist" achievements like the destruction of missile factories, Trump himself has demanded regime change and the total obliteration of the nuclear program. "If he cannot have his victory, there is no point to all of this," O'Brien observes, capturing the psychological trap the President has built for himself. The author's analysis of Trump's contradictory statements—claiming regime change has already happened while simultaneously admitting the nuclear program is deeply buried—is particularly damning. It suggests a leader desperate to declare a win that the facts on the ground do not support.

"He would love to be able to declare victory over Iran, end the campaign, and return to daydreaming about 30 foot tall gold statues of himself."

The author draws a parallel to the concept of "mowing the grass," a term often used in Israeli military strategy to describe temporary, non-decisive campaigns. O'Brien argues that if the US settles for destroying factories that can be quickly rebuilt, it is merely engaging in a costly, endless cycle that benefits no one but the Iranian leadership's narrative of resistance. This framing strips away the political theater to reveal a potentially endless quagmire.

The Gulf States' Dilemma

Perhaps the most chilling section of the commentary is the assessment of the Gulf States' position. O'Brien notes that while Saudi Arabia and other neighbors may have wanted maximum damage inflicted on Iran, they are now left vulnerable if the US withdraws. The author highlights Trump's transactional nature, quoting his insult to Saudi leader Mohammed bin Salman: "He thought I would be just another American president of a country going downhill... But now he has to be nice to me." This interaction underscores the fragility of alliances when they are based solely on personal loyalty rather than strategic necessity.

"If he walks away from the Strait, and Iran still attacks shipping, that is a colossal strategic failure no matter what he says, and the US consumer will have the bills to prove it."

O'Brien correctly identifies that the Gulf states lack the capability to secure the Strait of Hormuz themselves. If the US leaves, they are forced to negotiate with the very regime they sought to weaken, effectively handing Iran control over global energy flows. This section serves as a stark reminder that in geopolitics, the vacuum left by a withdrawing superpower is rarely empty; it is quickly filled by the adversary.

Bottom Line

O'Brien's strongest argument lies in his ability to reframe the conflict from a military contest of attrition to an economic siege where the US is losing despite its air superiority. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that Trump's personal vanity will prevent him from accepting a messy compromise, a variable that is inherently unpredictable. Readers should watch closely for tonight's address to see if the administration attempts to redefine "victory" to include a closed Strait of Hormuz, a move that would signal a profound strategic retreat.
