Ted Gioia argues that the most potent resistance to our current technological overreach isn't a new policy or a political candidate, but a cultural return to the human spirit. He posits that we are witnessing the birth of a "New Romanticism," a movement that mirrors the 19th-century backlash against the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that the very metrics we use to measure progress are now the source of our stagnation. This is not merely a literary observation; it is a structural diagnosis of why the modern world feels increasingly claustrophobic despite its digital abundance.

The Trap of Pure Rationalism

Gioia begins by dismantling the assumption that technological optimization equals human flourishing. He draws a sharp parallel between the current algorithmic dominance and the "dark Satanic mills" of the early 1800s, noting that just as the original Romanticists sought to protect human dignity from industrial exploitation, we now face a system that reduces people to data points. "The most important things in human life can't be reduced to software code or numbers on a spreadsheet," Gioia writes, listing love, trust, and creativity as the casualties of a purely rationalist worldview. This framing is powerful because it reframes the current sense of malaise not as a failure of technology, but as a failure of application—a system that has outgrown its moral compass.

The author suggests that the current era of "New Rationalism" is defined by a specific character type: the technocrat who views human values as variables in a maximization problem. He points to figures like Sam Bankman-Fried, who "mocked Shakespeare" and treated human interaction as a math test, as the archetype of this mindset. Gioia argues that this mindset inevitably leads to a "zero degree of personality," where leaders are trusted with spreadsheets but not with the care of a child. This is a provocative claim, but it resonates with the growing public fatigue toward leaders who prioritize efficiency over empathy.

"The system aims to expand its control no matter the consequences for people inside the system. It starts to feel intrusive and oppressive."

Critics might argue that this dichotomy between "rationalism" and "romanticism" is too binary, ignoring the ways in which data-driven approaches have solved genuine human problems, from disease eradication to supply chain logistics. However, Gioia's point is not to reject science, but to highlight the danger when it becomes an end in itself. He notes that "rationalism has created tremendous benefits for society, but in its final stages it becomes self-serving," a distinction that adds necessary nuance to the debate.

The Hunger for Enchantment

As the commentary shifts to the psychological impact of this shift, Gioia identifies a profound loss of "enchantment" in the modern world. He draws on the historical context of the Luddites, noting that their destruction of machinery was a desperate, albeit futile, attempt to reclaim agency in a world that had become too mechanical. Today, the resistance is less violent but equally urgent. "People now feel the horror and claustrophobia of this imposed disenchantment. This creates a hunger for magic that the system cannot provide," he observes.

This section of the argument is bolstered by the rise of "fantasy" and "romantasy" genres in literature and television, which Gioia sees as evidence of a public seeking escape from "hard-headed realism." The parallel here to the original Romantic movement is striking; just as the 19th century saw a surge in Gothic novels and emotional poetry as a reaction to the cold logic of the Enlightenment, we are seeing a similar cultural pivot. Gioia writes, "Rationalism tries to compensate for its lack of a soul by imitating a religion or cult. It establishes its own dogmas and rituals with the fervor of true believers."

The author suggests that the technocracy's obsession with Artificial Intelligence is symptomatic of this void. He argues that AI is the "god created in its own image" by a rationalist culture that has forgotten how to feel. "No matter how smart AI gets, it will never create an app that can forgive. Or fall in love. Or feel the pangs of parenthood," Gioia asserts. This is a strong emotional hook, but it also invites a counterpoint: is the fear of AI a genuine existential threat, or a projection of our own inability to connect in a digital age? Gioia leans toward the latter, suggesting that the "built-in dishonesty" of the system is the real problem, not the technology itself.

"The word progress no longer refers to actual progress, merely an expansion of technological control—so every software upgrade feels like a downgrade."

The Path Forward: Human-Centric Constraints

In the final propositions, Gioia moves from diagnosis to prescription, arguing that the solution lies in re-establishing constraints on the system. He references the historical success of the original Romanticists, who "demanded protections for workers, laws against child labor, an end to slavery," and notes that these constraints actually accelerated economic growth rather than stifling it. "Protecting humans actually added to prosperity," he writes, challenging the dominant narrative that regulation is the enemy of innovation.

The core of his argument is that the backlash is already underway, driven by a "counterculture" that resists the overreach of the dominant system. He warns that a society that silences this counterculture "has destroyed its most valuable feedback loop." This is a crucial insight for policymakers and business leaders: the demand for a more human-oriented society is not a fringe movement, but a mainstream shift that will define the coming decade.

"Healing begins with each individual. This is still possible, even in repressive situations. Rebellion emerges first in the inner life—which the Rationalists can't control."

Gioia acknowledges that Romanticism itself can be dangerous if it swings too far toward nationalism or irrationality, citing the 50-year period after 1800 as a cautionary tale. However, he maintains that in the current context, it serves as a "healthy corrective." The movement is not about rejecting technology, but about ensuring that "the system is forced to serve the people, instead of the people serving the system."

Bottom Line

Gioia's strongest contribution is his historical framing, which reveals that the current crisis of technology is not unprecedented but a recurring pattern in human history. His argument that "protecting humans actually added to prosperity" offers a compelling counter-narrative to the efficiency-at-all-costs mindset. The biggest vulnerability in his piece is the potential for the "New Romanticism" to be co-opted by the very forces it seeks to oppose, turning a genuine cultural shift into a marketing trend. Readers should watch for how this movement translates from cultural sentiment into concrete policy and institutional reform.
