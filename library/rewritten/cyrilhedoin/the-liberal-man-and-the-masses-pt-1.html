Most political analysis treats the rise of populism as a failure of communication or a temporary emotional flare-up. Cyril Hédoin argues something far more structural and unsettling: that the very engine of modern prosperity—the division of labor—has created a society where the liberal individual is systematically disempowered, turning the 'liberal man' into the 'mass man' by design. This is not a story about bad leadership or a specific election cycle; it is a diagnosis of why the promise of self-governance feels increasingly hollow in complex economies.

The Foundation of Liberalism

Hédoin begins by dismantling the usual suspects for what makes liberalism work. We often point to tolerance, pluralism, or public reason as the bedrock of free societies. But Hédoin contends these are insufficient. "Pluralism and public reason are plausibly compatible with non-liberal principles and institutions," he writes. He notes that one can believe in a clash of values without believing in individual liberty, or demand public justification while still enforcing illiberal social norms.

The author pushes past the idea that mere tolerance is enough. Citing Karl Popper, he warns that a society that tolerates the intolerant is doomed to instability. Furthermore, he argues that "there is something potentially illiberal in mere tolerance because it can readily go alongside treating others with contempt and disrespect." This is a sharp, necessary correction to the idea that simply 'getting along' is a moral victory. True liberalism requires a deeper respect that acknowledges the agency of the other.

So, what is the true core? Hédoin identifies it as normative individualism. This is the idea that individuals, in their capacity to form intentions and interact with the world, are the ultimate source of value and justification. "Normative individualism rejects social and political moralities that make the tribe, the nation, religion, or any other non-individual entity the source of justification for constraints imposed on individuals," he explains. This framing is powerful because it shifts the debate from 'what rules we follow' to 'who gets to decide the rules.'

The Hayekian Turn

The essay's most compelling move is connecting this philosophical ideal to the material reality of modern economics. Hédoin leans heavily on Friedrich Hayek to explain why individualism isn't just a moral preference but a practical necessity. He argues that modern prosperity relies on the "division of labor and the dispersion of knowledge." No single mind can hold all the information required to run a complex society.

"From the awareness of the limitations of individual knowledge and from the fact that no person or small group of persons can know all that is known to somebody, individualism also derives its main practical conclusion: its demand for a strict limitation of all coercive and exclusive power."

This is the crux of the argument: central planning fails not because of malice, but because of epistemic limits. The more complex society becomes, the more we must rely on the dispersed knowledge of individuals. However, Hédoin points out a tragic irony here. While this complexity requires individualism to function, it simultaneously erodes the individual's sense of agency. We are dependent on a web of causal relations we cannot see or control.

Critics might note that Hédoin's reliance on Hayek risks romanticizing the market's ability to self-correct, potentially downplaying how corporate concentration can distort the very 'dispersed knowledge' Hayek champions. If a few giants control the flow of information, the 'individual' is no longer the locus of knowledge, but a data point in a monopoly's algorithm.

The Predicament of the Mass Man

This leads to the essay's most provocative claim: the tension between the ideal of the self-governing individual and the reality of the mass society. Hédoin references Alexis de Tocqueville, who famously warned that democratic equality could lead citizens to "cut himself off from his fellows and to withdraw into the circle of his family and friends." But Hédoin suggests the problem is worse than isolation; it is disempowerment.

In a mass society, the individual's values and agency "barely count as anything in the complex web of causal relations." This creates a dangerous dissonance. People are told they are the masters of their fate, yet they feel powerless in the economy and politics. "This creates the risk of a dissonance between individuals' publicly stated and self-conceived normative significance and the observed and felt disempowerment in multiple domains of social life," Hédoin writes.

This is where the argument connects to the current political moment without naming names. The inability of institutions to resolve this feeling of powerlessness creates fertile ground for populism. When the liberal man realizes his self-governance is an illusion, he is ripe to become the mass man—someone who surrenders agency to a strongman or a mob to regain a sense of control.

"The inability of contemporary liberal democracies to respond to this predicament may account for the rise of populist politics."

This is a sobering assessment. It suggests that the backlash against the status quo isn't just about policy disagreements, but a fundamental crisis of identity. The system that liberated us has also made us feel small.

Bottom Line

Cyril Hédoin's essay succeeds by reframing the populist surge not as a failure of character, but as a structural failure of liberal theory to address the psychological toll of mass society. The strongest part of the argument is the link between the dispersion of knowledge and the erosion of individual agency. However, the piece leaves the reader with a difficult question: if the complexity of the modern world necessitates this disempowerment, is there any way to restore the 'liberal man' without dismantling the prosperity that depends on the division of labor? That is the puzzle the next two parts of this essay series must solve.
