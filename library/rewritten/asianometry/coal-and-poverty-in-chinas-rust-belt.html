Asianometry exposes a brutal paradox at the heart of China's modernization: the very regions that built the nation's industrial foundation are now its most dangerous political liabilities. While global narratives focus on China's soaring skyscrapers and tech giants, this piece forces a reckoning with the "eldest sons" of the revolution—Liaoning, Jilin, and Heilongjiang—who are now left behind, impoverished, and simmering with resentment. This is not just an economic story; it is a warning about how the failure to transition from heavy industry to a modern economy threatens the stability of the Chinese Communist Party itself.

The Betrayal of the Eldest Son

The author frames the Northeast not merely as an economic laggard, but as a region that was sacrificed for the greater good of the nation's coastal boom. Asianometry writes, "Chairman Mao called them the country's eldest son an acknowledgment that they were amongst the first to join the Communist Party and to industrialize under communism." This historical weight is crucial; it explains why the decline feels like a personal betrayal to the locals. The region was rich in coal, iron, and oil, and the state-owned enterprises there provided lifetime employment, housing, and healthcare. But as the economy opened up, these guaranteed perks became unsustainable burdens.

The core of the argument is that the transition from a planned economy to a market one was too abrupt for these cities to survive. Asianometry notes, "Total reformation means having to fire people and take away their benefits but having too many unemployed people laying around the city would lead to social unrest something the Chinese Communist Party does not tolerate." This creates a classic "rock and a hard place" dilemma for local officials. They are trapped between the need to cut costs to survive and the political mandate to maintain social stability. The result was a slow-motion collapse where cities like Fushun doubled down on exhausted coal reserves until the resources literally ran out.

"Nature finally failed Fushun in the early 1990s when the coal reserves gave out and then in 1998 a reckoning the state-owned enterprise shut down many of their mines and laid off 200,000 people."

This evidence is stark and effectively illustrates the human cost of macroeconomic policy. The author describes how the local government, desperate for revenue, turned to selling rural land to developers, creating ghost cities of unsold apartments while shanty towns sprang up nearby. Critics might argue that the author underplays the global context of the 1990s, where many industrial regions worldwide faced similar deindustrialization, not just China. However, the unique factor here is the political imperative: in a democracy, voters might punish a party for this; in China, it risks a revolution.

The Zombie Economy and Failed Revitalization

The piece then shifts to the central government's clumsy attempts to fix the problem, revealing a pattern of top-down mandates that ignore local realities. Since 2001, Beijing has tried to "make the Northeast great again" through massive infrastructure spending and forcing state-owned enterprises to invest in new sectors. Asianometry writes, "The companies being controlled by the government had no choice but to comply and so open offices and tried to make the best of it but the inferior economics associated with this push meant that its benefits could not last long."

The author details how the government tried to pivot Fushun from coal to food processing, and later to wind power. In the wind power example, the government directed central enterprises to build massive wind farms. While this looked good on paper and provided "photo ops," the economics didn't work. Asianometry puts it bluntly: "They were not able to do it more efficiently than the market... In other words they become zombie companies." These are firms that survive only on subsidies, unable to generate profit, hire new workers, or pay back the government's investment.

This analysis cuts deep into the inefficiency of China's state-capitalist model. The author argues that these policies are driven by political necessity rather than economic viability. "The local government left at the opportunity offering the central enterprises near unlimited public resources to make it happen," Asianometry writes, highlighting the disconnect between central planning and market reality. A counterargument worth considering is that these "zombie" investments might be a necessary evil to prevent immediate social collapse, acting as a form of disguised welfare. Yet, the author rightly points out that this is a temporary bandage on a gaping wound.

The Political Time Bomb

The most compelling part of the commentary is the link between economic despair and political survival. The author suggests that the Northeast is a ticking time bomb because the population feels abandoned by the very party they helped build. Asianometry writes, "Left behind cities like Fushun incubate political threats to the Communist Party's role without meaningful well-paying work people get frustrated at their government they radicalize looking for a way to break the wheel."

The argument is that the "eldest son" label is now a source of grievance rather than pride. The population is aging, the young and educated have fled to Shanghai and Beijing, and those left behind are "uneducated" and "middle-aged." The author notes, "Nobody calls for revolution unless they're unhappy about their life circumstances." This framing elevates the story from a regional economic report to a geopolitical risk assessment. The government is so incentivized to solve this that they are "willing to break Communist Party taboos and accept that their policies over the past 10 to 20 years have been a failure."

However, the path forward is unclear. Some experts suggest pivoting to light industry like textiles or electronics, but Asianometry dismantles this idea: "Chinese labor is already expensive and much of the labor left that machine isn't that great for intricate electronics picking work." The infrastructure is wrong, the workforce is the wrong age, and the location is too far from global shipping lanes. The only remaining solution, the author suggests, is a fundamental administrative overhaul that local governments are unlikely to execute on their own.

"Ever wonder why China's poor Northeast's are called the eldest son of the Republic it is in part because there are amongst the first to join the Communist revolution and nobody calls for revolution unless they're unhappy about their life circumstances."

Bottom Line

Asianometry delivers a sobering verdict: China's Rust Belt is not just an economic problem but a direct threat to the regime's legitimacy, and the current "zombie" solutions are failing to address the root causes. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to treat the Northeast as a footnote, instead positioning it as the canary in the coal mine for China's entire development model. The biggest vulnerability in the argument is the assumption that the central government has no better tools than the ones it has already tried; it leaves the reader wondering if a political shock is the only remaining catalyst for change.
