Asianometry delivers a sobering autopsy of India's semiconductor ambitions, revealing that the country's current manufacturing void isn't a lack of talent, but a catastrophic failure of execution in the late 1980s. The piece is notable for its granular focus on the Semiconductor Complex Limited (SCL), a state-owned enterprise that nearly succeeded before a single fire derailed the nation's industrial trajectory for decades. In an era where global supply chains are under scrutiny, understanding why India remains 100% dependent on imports despite having world-class design engineers is not just historical trivia; it is a blueprint of what happens when infrastructure and capital cannot keep pace with ambition.

The Promise of the National Champion

The author begins by dismantling the common misconception that India lacks semiconductor capability. "India is a semiconductor designed Powerhouse nearly every major Semiconductor Company has a presence in India designing some of the most advanced chips in the world," Asianometry writes, immediately establishing the paradox at the heart of the story. The narrative then pivots to the 1980s, when the Indian government, inspired by the Asian tigers, attempted to build a "national champion" to manufacture chips domestically. The vision was grand: a state-owned enterprise, SCL, headquartered in Mohali, staffed by the brightest minds from the Indian Institutes of Technology, and backed by significant government capital.

The author meticulously outlines the four pillars required for a successful fab: financial capital, human capital, government infrastructure, and manufacturing technology. While India excelled in human capital, the other pillars were fragile. "You need substantial near 100 government buy-in as well as stable Power and Water Supply," the text notes, highlighting a critical vulnerability. The argument here is compelling because it isolates the specific point of failure: it wasn't a lack of will or intelligence, but the inability to maintain the delicate environmental controls required for chipmaking. A single power outage or water shortage could ruin months of work, a reality that Western investors like Fairchild had already recognized decades earlier when they chose Malaysia over India.

"The company would be headquartered in the planned city of Mohali in the state of Punjab at the time the Punjab economy was mostly agricultural based but the area was up and coming."

This focus on the agricultural context of the chosen location is a subtle but devastating detail. It underscores the sheer difficulty of transplanting high-tech manufacturing into an environment not yet ready to support it. Critics might note that the author places too much blame on infrastructure without fully exploring the geopolitical isolation of India at the time, which made importing equipment and technology even more difficult. However, the evidence of SCL's rapid initial progress suggests that the potential was real, making the subsequent collapse all the more tragic.

The Fire That Changed Everything

The narrative takes a dramatic turn with the 1989 fire that destroyed SCL's production capabilities. Asianometry treats this not as a mere accident, but as the defining moment that separated India from the semiconductor revolution. "Those hopes ended in 1989 when a devastating fire broke out at scl the cause of the fire remains unknown," the author states, before explaining the cascading damage caused by toxic chemicals and water sprinklers. The eight-year gap between the fire and the restart of production allowed competitors like TSMC and Samsung to surge ahead, capturing the scale and market share that SCL could never regain.

The author's analysis of the aftermath is particularly sharp. Instead of a swift recovery, the government "lurched through failed proposal after failed proposal," attempting to sell the fab or retool it for smart cards, neither of which worked. The text highlights the financial hemorrhage: "In 2005 to 2006 its final year as a company SEO made 3.5 million in revenue and turned a loss of 2 million USD." This data point is crucial; it demonstrates that even with a captive government customer, the entity could not achieve economies of scale. The restructuring of SCL into a research and development center in 2005 was effectively a surrender of commercial ambitions.

"It took SEL nearly a decade to reach that 180 nanometer node they have been working on it since at least 2011."

This sentence encapsulates the sluggishness that has plagued India's efforts. While the rest of the world raced toward single-digit nanometers, India was still struggling to master a node that was already considered mature. The author effectively uses this timeline to show that the gap wasn't just about money; it was about the loss of institutional knowledge and the inability to iterate quickly. The comparison to Skywater Technologies, which runs a 130 nanometer node commercially, further emphasizes how far behind India has fallen, even in the realm of older, less advanced chips.

The Capital Chasm and Modern Attempts

The commentary then shifts to the modern era, where the barrier to entry has become exponentially higher. "Today's Leading Edge chip factories regularly run up into the tens of billions of dollars," Asianometry writes, illustrating why even massive Indian conglomerates like Reliance struggle to enter the space. The author contrasts the 15 billion dollars Reliance spent on its LTE network with the 20 billion dollars TSMC spends on a single gigafab, driving home the sheer scale of capital required. This section is vital for understanding why recent government initiatives have failed to attract serious private investment.

The text details several failed attempts to revive the industry, including a 2014 proposal involving JP Associates and Tower Jazz that collapsed due to debt and commercial viability concerns. "The Firm had a lot of debt and said that a semiconductor plant was not commercially viable at the moment," the author notes, quoting the consortium's withdrawal. This highlights a recurring theme: the Indian market alone is not enough to justify the investment, and global partners are wary of the execution risks. The cancellation of the HSMC project in 2019, followed by a similarly named Chinese company's failure, serves as a grim reminder of the global volatility in this sector.

"India lacks substantial semiconductor fabrication capacity in fact there is hardly any semiconductor fabrication capacity at all as of this writing 100 of India's chips logic memory all of it have to be imported from abroad."

This stark admission is the piece's most powerful conclusion. It connects the historical failure of SCL to the current geopolitical reality, where 37% of India's chip imports come from China. The author argues that this dependency has critical implications for national security, especially given the tense relations between the two nations. The argument is strengthened by the mention of the 2020 semiconductor shortage, which paralyzed India's smartphone industry, proving that design prowess is useless without a reliable manufacturing base.

Critics might argue that the author is too pessimistic about India's future, ignoring the recent policy shifts and the global push for supply chain diversification that could finally bring fabs to India. However, the historical evidence presented suggests that without solving the infrastructure and execution issues that doomed SCL, new policies may face the same fate. The distinction between design and manufacturing is clear, and the author is right to emphasize that India has mastered the former but remains a novice in the latter.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's strongest asset is its ability to trace a single point of failure—a fire in 1989—to a decades-long industrial stagnation, proving that timing and execution are as critical as capital. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its focus on the past, which may understate the potential impact of India's current aggressive policy reforms and the global desire to reduce reliance on East Asia. Readers should watch for whether the next generation of Indian fabs can finally overcome the infrastructure deficits that have held the country back for forty years.
