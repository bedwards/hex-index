Asianometry delivers a masterclass in industrial history by reframing SMIC not merely as a Chinese tech firm, but as a geopolitical chess piece that survived through regulatory arbitrage and aggressive talent acquisition. The piece's most striking revelation is how the company's early growth was fueled less by organic innovation and more by a unique business model where local governments built the factories while SMIC simply operated them. This is essential listening for anyone trying to understand why China's semiconductor ambitions have been so volatile, yet so persistent.

The Founder and the Foundry

The narrative begins with Richard Chang, a figure whose background perfectly encapsulates the complex diaspora dynamics of the tech industry. Asianometry writes, "Chang is an interesting fellow born in Nanjing, his family fled to Taiwan along with a million other ethnic Chinese migrants... grew up in Taiwan but held deep roots to the Chinese mainland." This biographical detail is crucial; it explains the dual loyalty that drove Chang to leave a comfortable retirement at Texas Instruments to build a rival in mainland China. The author effectively contrasts Chang's operational experience with his competitor, Grace Semiconductor, noting that "being a rich guy's son does not help much in competitively running a semiconductor foundry."

The commentary highlights a critical distinction in the early days: while Grace relied on political connections, SMIC relied on execution. The author points out that "Grace could not keep up... SMIC had two [fabs] in Shanghai, a bunch more on the hopper, and was already prepping for their New York Stock Exchange IPO." This rapid scaling was not accidental. It was a calculated move to secure market dominance before the government could shift its support elsewhere. Critics might argue that the author downplays the role of state subsidies in Grace's initial survival, but the evidence of SMIC's superior speed in reaching mass production holds up.

The Business Model: Regulatory Arbitrage

Perhaps the most insightful section of the piece is the breakdown of SMIC's capital strategy. The author likens the foundry model to professional sports, stating, "A foundry needs an expensive multi-billion dollar fab to do their work... neither their organizations can raise or risk the billions of dollars to self-finance one so get someone else to do it." This analogy clarifies why SMIC turned to Chinese municipalities for funding. The company engaged in a form of regulatory arbitrage, "shopping their services to cities around the nation to find the best deal."

This approach allowed SMIC to expand aggressively without depleting its own balance sheet. Asianometry notes, "It allowed SMIC to grow extremely fast while keeping their actual invested capital low." However, this strategy carried inherent risks. As the author observes, "As SMIC ascended up the chain and the leading edge process got more and more expensive, these capital expenditures became onerous even for the richest local governments." The eventual friction between SMIC and its partners in Wuhan and Chengdu, who terminated partnerships due to "seemingly unending operating losses," proves that this model was a double-edged sword.

Foundry work is in many aspects an art that cannot be easily boiled down into steps; not everything is or can be written down, so it's critical to have with you the best chefs.

The Talent War and Intellectual Property

The article pivots to the human element, arguing that hardware is useless without the engineers to run it. The author details how SMIC lured talent from competitors like TSMC with massive stock option packages, offering "80,000 shares... [which] at the IPO price would be worth 1.4 million dollars." Beyond money, the draw was ideological and professional. The author writes, "Many of the engineers were ethnic Han Chinese... and wanted to give back to their country... [and] many of them felt that they had hit the glass ceiling in an American or Taiwanese company."

This talent influx, however, triggered a massive legal backlash. The piece details TSMC's lawsuit, which alleged that SMIC's rapid progress was impossible without stolen trade secrets. "TSMC launched a pioneering intellectual property theft lawsuit... the jury found that SMIC had indeed infringed on TSMC intellectual property." The settlement, which forced SMIC to give TSMC a 10% stake, was a pivotal moment. It underscores a harsh reality: in the semiconductor industry, speed without a foundation of proprietary knowledge invites legal and reputational catastrophe. The author effectively uses this legal saga to illustrate the high stakes of the technology race.

The Path to Profitability and Independence

The narrative concludes by tracing SMIC's long, painful journey toward financial sustainability. After years of losses and leadership changes, the company finally stabilized under new management that prioritized R&D over rapid expansion. The author notes that the new leadership realized "how important it is to research, create, and thus own the process technology itself, not to take shortcuts." This shift is evidenced by recent reports that SMIC is nearing high-volume production of 7-nanometer chips, a feat that would have been impossible under the old growth-at-all-costs model.

The piece ends on a note of national significance, observing that SMIC's recent dual listings in Hong Kong and Shanghai signal that "the Chinese national government is starting to invest real financial resources in actually developing an indigenous semiconductor space from the ground up." While the author is optimistic about this return to form, a counterargument worth considering is whether SMIC can truly compete with TSMC and Samsung given the ongoing US export controls that limit access to the most advanced manufacturing equipment. The path to independence remains fraught with external hurdles.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's coverage succeeds by peeling back the layers of SMIC's success to reveal a company built on a fragile foundation of local government debt and stolen IP, which only recently began to stand on its own technological merits. The strongest part of the argument is the clear delineation between SMIC's early growth strategy and its current necessity for genuine innovation. The biggest vulnerability remains the geopolitical reality that China's access to the global supply chain is increasingly restricted, a factor that no amount of domestic investment can easily overcome. Readers should watch closely to see if SMIC's 7-nanometer breakthrough can be sustained without the advanced tools it once relied upon.
