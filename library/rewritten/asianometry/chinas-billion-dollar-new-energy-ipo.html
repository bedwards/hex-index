In a market often obsessed with the next semiconductor breakthrough or mobile app unicorn, Asianometry directs attention to a far more grounded, yet equally transformative force: China's renewable energy infrastructure. The author's most striking claim is that the country's biggest IPO of 2021 belongs not to a tech giant, but to Three Gorges New Energy, a subsidiary of the state-owned corporation behind the world's largest dam. This piece matters now because it decodes the specific mechanics of how Beijing is engineering its green transition, revealing a corporate structure designed to separate stable cash flows from high-growth ambitions.

The Strategic Split

Asianometry begins by clarifying a common misconception about the company's name and operations. "The business unit does not actually own or operate the Three Gorges dam; more like branding," the author notes, explaining that the dam's operations belong to a sibling company, China Yangtze Power. This distinction is crucial for understanding the parent company's strategy. Three Gorges Corporation, the parent entity, created a separate vehicle specifically for wind and solar projects to align with government policy classifications.

The author argues that this separation is not arbitrary but a calculated response to regulatory frameworks. "The Chinese government is pushing the development of quote-unquote new energy sources... hydropower is not included and so does not receive the same treatment and feeding tariffs," Asianometry writes. By isolating the renewable assets, the parent company can present a pure-play growth story to investors, distinct from the mature, dividend-yielding hydro business. This structural nuance explains why the new entity trades at a price-to-earnings ratio of 38.8, compared to just 16 for the hydro-focused sibling. The author effectively uses this financial disparity to illustrate how policy drives valuation in China's state-led economy.

"The two companies' P/E ratios reflect this: Three Gorges New Energy has a 38.8 ratio, while Yangtze has 16."

Critics might note that relying on state classification for valuation premiums carries inherent policy risk; if the government redefines what constitutes "new energy," the valuation gap could collapse. However, the author's analysis of the parent company's intent remains robust: they are segmenting their portfolio to maximize capital efficiency across different growth stages.

Scaling the Green Transition

The commentary then shifts to the sheer velocity of the company's expansion, framing it as a microcosm of China's broader industrial policy. Asianometry highlights that installed capacity grew twelve-fold from 2008 to 2020, driven by the state's 14th Five-Year Plan. "The party has set a path for China to be a leader in offshore wind energy, thus this is a special focus for the company," the author observes. This focus is not merely aspirational; it involves mastering complex engineering challenges like deep-water installation and indigenous submarine cables.

The author makes a compelling point about the company's strategic pivot away from manufacturing. "Three Gorges New Energy used to manufacture and sell their own wind turbines but in 2017 they withdrew from this business and fully committed to developing power generation plants," Asianometry writes. This decision to exit the low-margin manufacturing sector and focus on asset ownership allowed the company to improve its gross margins to nearly 60 percent. The logic is sound: in a value chain where midstream manufacturers often get squeezed by price wars, owning the end-user infrastructure provides a more stable profit center. The author rightly identifies this as a "great decision" that insulated the company from the volatility of component pricing.

Navigating Market Realities

Despite the growth narrative, Asianometry does not shy away from the structural headwinds facing the sector. The author points out that electricity prices are often regulated, creating a complex patchwork of profitability across different provinces. "It appears I guess Yunnan is like the Texas of China," the author quips regarding the market-based pricing in that region, contrasting it with government-fixed rates elsewhere. This regulatory uncertainty forces the company to diversify geographically to maintain margins.

Furthermore, the author warns that the era of easy growth may be hitting a ceiling. "The number of areas with ideal solar and wind properties is limited... the marginal benefit of the next site declines," Asianometry explains. As the best sites are developed, profitability naturally erodes. Additionally, the government is beginning to reduce feed-in tariffs, which "shrinks the whole pie for everyone in the industry." This is a critical counterpoint to the bullish growth narrative: the state is simultaneously driving expansion and tightening the financial leash. The author acknowledges that while Three Gorges New Energy is well-positioned as a state-owned enterprise to secure the best sites, being state-backed is "no golden ticket" in a competitive market.

"Fundamentally, Three Gorges New Energy is a utility company operating in a favorable macro space... the parent company now has two publicly traded subsidiaries with strong value for their investor audiences: Yangtze for those wanting income and now New Energy for those looking for growth."

Bottom Line

Asianometry's strongest contribution is the clear dissection of how Chinese state capital is being deployed to create a dual-engine growth strategy, separating mature hydro assets from high-growth renewables to satisfy different investor appetites. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on continued government support; if policy shifts or subsidy cuts accelerate faster than the company can diversify, the high valuation multiples may prove unsustainable. Readers should watch closely how the company manages the transition from subsidy-dependent growth to market-driven profitability in its offshore wind projects.
