Asianometry delivers a sobering economic autopsy: Hong Kong didn't just lose its electronics industry; it actively chose financialization over industrial survival, a decision that cost the city its future in semiconductors. While other Asian Tigers ascended the value chain, Hong Kong's leadership adhered to a rigid laissez-faire ideology that proved fatal when the global tech landscape shifted. This is not merely a history lesson; it is a warning about the hidden costs of "not picking winners" in an era of state-driven industrial competition.

The Great Divergence

The author begins by establishing the unique starting position of the region. "Singapore, South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong: together these four the four tigers led the way in making prosperity for its residents," Asianometry writes. The narrative quickly pivots to the critical divergence point. As the 1970s arrived, low-cost assembly became unsustainable. "South Korea, Taiwan and Singapore managed to upscale their efforts and ascend the value chain but Hong Kong managed neither and they got left behind," the author notes. This framing is crucial because it rejects the idea that Hong Kong's decline was inevitable; it was a choice. The author argues that to survive, economies must either build global consumer brands like Samsung or master complex manufacturing like the semiconductor foundry model adopted by Taiwan. Hong Kong did neither.

"For companies and economies who initially compete on the idea that they are 'cheaper' there is always another cheaper labor supply somewhere else."

This observation cuts to the heart of the economic trap Hong Kong fell into. The author suggests that without a strategic pivot, the "workshop for the world" model is a dead end. Critics might note that the pressure to move was immense, and the capital requirements for upgrading were staggering, but the piece insists that the lack of government will was the primary failure. The argument holds up well against the historical record of the other Tigers, who all utilized state resources to force their industries upward.

The Policy Vacuum

The commentary then dissects the specific policy failures of the colonial and early post-colonial governments. Asianometry identifies a deep structural issue: "The Hong Kong government adopted a fiscally conservative industrial policy that emphasized the general macro economy." The administration refused to intervene, believing the market should dictate outcomes. "They took the stance that their administration should not pick winners or losers," the author explains. This philosophy, while successful in building a robust financial hub, left the manufacturing sector exposed. The government focused on infrastructure like the MTR and public housing but ignored the specific needs of high-tech R&D.

The financial incentives were misaligned. The author points out that "the Hong Kong government as a whole pushed land prices higher and higher because that was how they made money." With revenue heavily dependent on land sales, the state effectively priced its own manufacturers out of existence. "Manufacturers already struggling to compete against foreign competition suffered not only from a rising cost of labor but also surging land rents," Asianometry writes. This creates a damning picture of a government prioritizing short-term fiscal health over long-term industrial capacity. The result was a mass exodus of factories to mainland China, leaving behind only sales and marketing roles.

The Missed Semiconductor Opportunity

Perhaps the most tragic element of the story is the near-miss in semiconductor design. The author highlights that Hong Kong once held a lead in chip design, home to companies like Valence Semiconductor and a strong Motorola division. "Motorola's Hong Kong division in the early 1990s was very strong in microprocessor design," the text states. There was a genuine potential for Hong Kong to become a hub for fabless chip designers like Qualcomm or Nvidia. However, the ecosystem lacked the necessary support to scale. "Lacking sufficient know-how, investment capital and government support, the industry weathered away," Asianometry concludes.

"If every other kid at your kid's school are getting private lessons from their parents after class then you are going to need to do the same with your kids if you want them to stay ahead of the curve."

This analogy perfectly captures the futility of Hong Kong's passive approach in a hyper-competitive global market. When competitors like Singapore and China began offering aggressive incentives to retain talent and attract firms, Hong Kong's refusal to play the game sealed its fate. The author notes that when Motorola management asked for state support in 2002 to stay, they were "turned down." The decision was final, and the talent drain was immediate. "Talented young engineers would end up going abroad to find well-paying work," leaving the local industry hollowed out.

The Human Cost

The piece concludes by addressing the social fallout of this economic shift. The transition from a manufacturing economy to a finance-dominated one was not seamless for the average worker. "Hong Kongers have felt the consequences of this blue collar collapse ever since," the author writes. The assumption that a vibrant service sector would naturally replace the lost industrial base proved false for many. "They became losers who stayed lost over time," Asianometry observes, highlighting the permanent scar left on the workforce. The loss of a tax base and the erosion of middle-class manufacturing jobs created a structural inequality that persists today.

Critics might argue that the shift to finance brought immense wealth to the city as a whole, but the author's focus on the distributional consequences provides a necessary counter-narrative to the standard "success story" of Hong Kong's economy. The piece suggests that the "vibrant finance and services industry" rose at the direct expense of the industrial workforce, creating a polarized society.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's strongest argument is the rejection of economic determinism; Hong Kong's failure was a policy choice, not an accident of history. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its somewhat binary view of industrial policy, potentially underestimating the sheer difficulty of pivoting a mature, service-oriented economy back toward heavy industry. However, the core lesson remains vital: in an age of state-capitalist competition, a hands-off approach can be a losing strategy.
