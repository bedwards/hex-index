In an era where national security and economic dominance hinge on silicon, Asianometry exposes a staggering $20 billion deception that nearly derailed China's semiconductor ambitions. The author doesn't just recount a corporate failure; they dissect a sophisticated grift where local bureaucrats, desperate for a "TSMC in their own city," were bamboozled by a company that never intended to build a chip factory. This is not merely a story of incompetence, but a case study in how the pressure to achieve impossible technological leaps can birth a culture of fraud.

The Architecture of a Grift

Asianometry begins by establishing the high stakes: China's reliance on imports is a critical weakness, prompting a massive state fund to foster domestic production. This desperation, the author argues, creates a perfect environment for charlatans. The subject, Wuhan Hongxin Semiconductor Manufacturing (HSMC), promised the moon: spinning up a facility capable of 30,000 wafers a month within two years, matching the output of Taiwan's industry giant. As Asianometry notes, "their demands will always give rise to charlatans." The speed of this promise was the first red flag; while TSMC achieved similar milestones, it required immense, proven expertise, not just a press release.

To manufacture credibility, HSMC executed two high-profile stunts. First, they hired a 75-year-old former TSMC executive, Chang Shan Yi, to serve as CEO. Second, they purchased a state-of-the-art lithography scanner from ASML, a machine worth nearly $90 million. Asianometry highlights the absurdity of their marketing: "they would claim that they had the only device in china capable of creating seven nanometer node generation chips." This claim was demonstrably false, yet it served its purpose in generating hype.

"The bank statement says the device is new and was never used. This is not a good sign. First you know there's the lying and second they immediately turn around and re-mortgage it for cash."

This moment reveals the core of the fraud. The company didn't buy the machine to make chips; they bought it to use as collateral for a loan, effectively spending nothing while appearing to have made a massive capital investment. Critics might note that in the high-stakes world of semiconductor financing, using assets as leverage is common practice, but the immediate re-mortgaging of a brand-new, critical piece of equipment suggests a liquidity crisis from day one rather than a strategic financial maneuver.

The Flow of Capital and the Silence of Results

The funding for HSMC came largely from the Wuhan city government, which invested billions in hopes of securing high-value jobs and technological prestige. Asianometry points out the disconnect between the inflow of cash and the lack of tangible output. "The public relations article announcing this investment said that hong king had received the most money... they had received 636 acres of land... the total plan cost of the project will be 128 billion rmb." Yet, by mid-2020, the company was being sued by construction subcontractors for unpaid bills totaling $5 million.

The author digs into the ownership structure, revealing that the public entity was 90% owned by a shell company run by individuals with no semiconductor background—one owned a liquor retailer and catering businesses. "The shell company did not invest any money into hong king," Asianometry writes, noting that the majority of funds came from the government or loans from the very contractors they later failed to pay. The result was a collapse of wages, frozen bank accounts, and the government seizing back 300 acres of land.

"If the company really received those billions of dollars from the wuhan government as claimed in that press release where did it go? Why can't it pay a measly 5 million to its construction subcontractor?"

This question cuts to the heart of the mystery. The author suggests a "Silicon Valley bootstrap vibe" where the founders used other people's money to sustain a hype cycle, betting they could string along local governments until they secured central funding. The strategy relied on the fact that local officials were eager to believe in a local TSMC, even when the financial reality didn't add up.

The Pattern of Fast Failure

HSMC was not an isolated incident. Asianometry contextualizes this failure within a broader "fever" in China's tech sector, citing similar collapses like Huai Semiconductor and Dakema, where billions were raised but no chips were ever produced. The author draws a sharp contrast between the speed required for internet startups and the patience needed for foundries. "Semiconductor manufacturing is way different from an internet startup... you don't start a foundry without access to serious national level amount of resources." The drive for rapid results, often seen in China's "get in and out fast" business culture, is identified as a fatal flaw in this specific industry.

"Doing so is a formula for fast failure and you don't even need to be a grifter to kind of create failure out of out of that situation."

The author observes that even the founders of HSMC have already moved on, with one co-founder launching a new semiconductor venture in a different city, promising the same impossible results to a new set of local officials. This cyclical nature of the fraud suggests that as long as the political incentive for rapid industrialization remains high, the grift will continue.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's most compelling contribution is the distinction between a legitimate failure of ambition and a calculated fraud; while the line is blurry, the evidence of asset stripping and shell-company ownership points strongly toward the latter. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on public records and court filings, which may not reveal the full extent of the internal decision-making or the potential involvement of higher-level officials who may have turned a blind eye. Ultimately, the story serves as a stark warning: in the race for technological supremacy, the pressure to move fast can blind even the most sophisticated systems to the cost of the grift.

"You don't start a foundry without access to serious national level amount of resources not just a mount kilimanjaro of cash but also a base of expertise infinite patience and so much talent." "
