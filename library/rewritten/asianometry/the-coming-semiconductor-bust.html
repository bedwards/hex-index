While the world panics over chip shortages, Asianometry cuts through the noise with a chilling prediction: the semiconductor industry is not just volatile, but structurally engineered to crash. The author argues that the current frenzy to build factories is not a solution to scarcity, but the very mechanism that will trigger a devastating glut. For busy investors and industry watchers, this is not a story about supply chains; it is a warning about the inevitable physics of overcapacity.

The Mechanics of Overproduction

Asianometry begins by dismantling the idea that the current shortage is a permanent state. The industry is defined by a cycle where "semiconductor shipments can soar seventy percent one year and crash forty percent the next." This volatility is not an anomaly; it is the baseline. The author points out that the current shortage is exacerbated by "double booking," where vendors, terrified of running out, place redundant orders with manufacturers like TSMC. As Asianometry notes, "TSMC chairman Mark Leo cites double booking as one of the major reasons why the shortage is not as bad as it looks."

This behavior creates a false signal of demand. When companies order twice what they need to ensure survival, manufacturers ramp up production to meet this phantom demand. The author suggests that while macroeconomic factors like oil shocks or trade tensions play a role, the real trigger for downturns is internal. A 2005 study cited in the piece found that "what is more significant in the triggering of semiconductor down cycles is not a decrease in demand rather it has more to do with surges in semiconductor inventory levels and fab capacity." The industry is trapped in a cycle where the fear of shortage drives the very oversupply that causes the bust.

The nature and economics of the industry forced them to make like rabbits until the whole thing collapses.

The Economics of the Fab

The core of Asianometry's argument rests on the staggering cost of building a semiconductor factory, or "fab." The author explains that while Moore's Law predicts doubling transistor counts, "Moore's second law also known as Rock's law... states that the cost of semiconductor tools doubles every four years." The financial barrier to entry has become astronomical, with a leading-edge fab now costing nearly $20 billion. Because these costs are fixed, once a factory is built, the marginal cost of producing another chip is "basically nil." This creates a perverse incentive: "you want to make and sell as many chips as possible to amortize out those fixed costs."

This economic reality forces giants like Samsung and TSMC to prioritize market share over profitability. Asianometry writes, "The industries titans Samsung and TSMC value market share over profitability; they refuse to mothball capacity less that business go to any potential competitors." Even when prices collapse, these companies keep producing because they can survive at a "survival price which is far lower than everyone else's in the industry." This strategy burns out smaller competitors, leading to bankruptcies and consolidation, before the cycle resets. Critics might note that this model assumes rational actors who will always prioritize long-term dominance over short-term cash flow, but history suggests the fear of losing ground is a more powerful motivator than profit margins.

The Subsidy Trap

The piece then turns to the massive government interventions currently reshaping the landscape, from the U.S. CHIPS Act to China's $20 billion plan. Asianometry expresses deep skepticism about the long-term viability of these efforts. While subsidies can successfully bring manufacturing to a new location, the author warns that "unless the subsidies go on forever... the subsidized fab should have to stand up on their own two feet afterwards." The concern is that these political incentives will accelerate the build-out of capacity without a corresponding increase in genuine consumer demand.

The author highlights the sheer scale of the impending supply wave, noting that a single new TSMC facility in Arizona could produce "3.5 billion dies a month"—a volume that dwarfs the annual purchases of the entire U.S. Department of Defense. Asianometry questions the logic of this expansion: "Did we really create tens of millions of chips worth of demand over the past year?" The implication is that governments are pouring money into a sector that is about to flood, potentially creating a bubble that will burst with even more force than previous cycles.

Bottom Line

Asianometry's most compelling insight is that the semiconductor industry's greatest strength—its relentless drive to expand capacity—is also its fatal flaw. The argument holds up well against the current hysteria, grounding the analysis in the hard economics of fixed costs and market share wars. However, the piece underestimates the potential for demand shocks from emerging technologies like artificial intelligence, which could absorb some of this excess capacity. The reader should watch not just for the glut, but for whether the next wave of innovation arrives fast enough to prevent a crash.

It would be irrational to say that that won't have any effect on the market once that capacity is all built.
