Matt Christman delivers a startling thesis: the entire history of modern socialism, from the Bolshevik Revolution to the labor movements of the 20th century, is not a secular departure from religion, but rather Christianity's final, materialist incarnation. He argues that the working class, stripped of its ability to imagine a supernatural heaven, simply relocated the divine promise to Earth, turning Marxism into a "holy book" for a new era of apocalyptic struggle. For busy readers trying to make sense of today's polarized, seemingly irreconcilable world, this framework offers a terrifyingly coherent explanation for why political conflicts feel so much like religious wars.

The Death of God and the Birth of Materialism

Christman begins by tracing the trajectory of Christian millennialism, noting how early movements like the Anabaptists and Hussites were driven by a "swelling yearning to align our understanding... with the world that we find ourselves living in." He posits that these movements failed because they lacked a working class capable of seizing the means of production, leading to their brutal suppression and the eventual institutional capture of faith by the owning class. As Christman puts it, "God dies and it's replaced by this Collective materialism."

This is a provocative reframing of the Enlightenment. Christman suggests that the secularization of society didn't kill the religious impulse; it merely displaced it. The "Enlightenment conception" that now suffuses our politics is, in his view, the new vessel for the same desperate hope for salvation. He argues that reactionaries today are essentially people who want to "die in battle" in a righteous conflict, while the progressive impulse is to "die in their bed" surrounded by loved ones, having built a just society. This distinction between a death of annihilation and a death of peace becomes the central axis of his historical analysis.

Critics might note that equating the complex socio-economic theories of Marx with the theological fervor of the Anabaptists risks oversimplifying the intellectual rigor of the former. However, Christman's point is not about theological accuracy but about the psychological function these systems serve for the masses.

"The Socialist movement broadly stated with Marxism as the approximation of a holy book at its heart because you need a book it becomes the next phase of a materialized Christianity that has given up on the hope of going to heaven."

Armageddon as Class Warfare

The core of Christman's argument lies in his interpretation of the period from 1789 to 1945. He does not view these centuries as a series of political revolutions or economic shifts, but as a literal "Armageddon." He writes, "This is a life and death blood fight this is a literal holy war and it's fought everywhere." In this view, the World Wars were not merely conflicts between nations, but the expression of a sublimated class war where the working class, unable to seize power at home, exported their violence outward through colonial expansion and global conflict.

He offers a specific, and somewhat controversial, reading of the Bolshevik Revolution. He suggests the Bolsheviks were not cold-blooded strategists but "religious believers" who acted with the decisiveness of martyrs because they believed they were triggering a world revolution. "The Bolsheviks were religious believers they believed in this extension of the cosmic conflict between good and evil that is going to be resolved at Armageddon," Christman asserts. This explains their willingness to die for the cause; they were acting out a script written by centuries of Christian eschatology, now rewritten in the language of history and materialism.

The narrative suggests that the working class was ultimately defeated in 1945, leaving the world in a state where "Satan won the battle." Yet, Christman finds a strange comfort in this defeat. He argues that the loss of the supernatural promise doesn't mean damnation, but rather a reconciliation with the reality of the world. "It just means that the World As We Know It will never be reconciled to us but we will all as people be reconciled," he writes. This is a radical acceptance of a godless universe where the only heaven left to build is the one we create for each other.

"God didn't go away and it's just not God isn't where the language of God is that's what I mean."

The Limits of the Analogy

While the parallel between religious eschatology and political revolution is compelling, there are moments where the analogy stretches thin. Christman claims that the 20th century was a single, continuous holy war, but he glosses over the significant ideological fractures between different socialist movements that had little in common with Christian theology. A counterargument worth considering is that reducing all revolutionary fervor to a single religious impulse ignores the specific, secular grievances of the working class regarding wages, hours, and safety.

Furthermore, his assertion that the working class was "defeated" in 1945 overlooks the massive social gains made in the West during the post-war boom, which were achieved through the very political mechanisms he describes. The "peaceful death" he envisions was, for many, a reality for several decades, not just a distant dream. Nevertheless, his diagnosis of the current moment—where the institutions that once articulated a social vision of apocalypse have collapsed—feels urgently relevant.

Bottom Line

Matt Christman's most powerful contribution is his insistence that the spiritual hunger driving modern politics cannot be satisfied by secular rationality alone; it requires a new kind of faith, one that accepts mortality while fighting for a better world. The argument's greatest vulnerability is its tendency to flatten distinct historical movements into a single theological narrative, but its strength lies in explaining the emotional intensity of our current political divides. Readers should watch for how this "materialized Christianity" evolves in an era where the working class no longer holds the leverage it once did.
