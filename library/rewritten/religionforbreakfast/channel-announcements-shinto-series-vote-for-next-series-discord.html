Andrew Henry does something rare in the crowded landscape of religious education: he treats his audience not as passive consumers, but as co-curators of a global curriculum. In a live stream that feels less like a broadcast and more like a town hall, Henry announces a pivot away from his traditional focus on ancient Christianity to tackle the complexities of Shinto, while simultaneously handing the keys to his future content over to his supporters. This isn't just a schedule update; it is a deliberate attempt to correct a long-standing imbalance in how Western audiences encounter Asian spiritual traditions.

The Shinto Series and the Question of Globalization

Henry kicks off the stream by unveiling a five-episode "Intro to Shintoism" series, structured to move from basic literacy to complex cultural analysis. He outlines a clear trajectory: starting with the fundamentals of kami veneration, moving through the historical entanglement with Buddhism, and culminating in a modern examination of Shinto's identity. "Episode 4 is on this concept of world religion: can we call Shinto a world religion or is it an indigenous religion just to Japan?" he asks, setting the stage for a nuanced discussion on how a faith travels. This framing is crucial because it avoids the trap of treating Shinto as a static, museum-piece relic of Japan, instead presenting it as a living, evolving system that is currently expanding its footprint.

The series promises to explore the tension between national identity and global spread, a theme Henry notes is particularly relevant given the rise of overseas shrines. "We're kind of playing with this category of is it a Japanese indigenous religion or is it a world religion? Is it both?" he explains. By dedicating an entire episode to this classification, Henry signals that he understands the academic friction involved in defining non-Western faiths. He even plans to close the season with a look at pop culture, specifically the influence of Shinto in Studio Ghibli films, acknowledging that for many modern viewers, this is their primary point of contact with the tradition.

"The reason why I'm focusing on so many Asian religions here is because Religion For Breakfast has for way too long focused so much on Christianity."

This admission is the emotional core of the announcement. Henry acknowledges that his own research background in ancient Christianity made it "very easy" to produce content on the New Testament or Constantine, but he recognizes that this ease created a blind spot. "When I look at like the back catalog of Religion For Breakfast videos it's like, you know, so much ancient and some people are like, hey, where's the Buddhism? Where's the Hinduism? Where's the Shinto?" he admits. This self-reflection is refreshing; it transforms the channel from a one-man lecture hall into a responsive platform that is actively trying to broaden its scope based on audience demand.

Democratizing the Syllabus

Perhaps the most distinctive move Henry makes is the decision to let his patrons vote on the next major series. He is not guessing what the audience wants; he is asking them directly. "I want to challenge the whole audience to do is to vote on the next series... either Confucianism or Buddhism," he states, opening a poll for supporters at the two-dollar tier. This approach effectively crowdsources the curriculum, ensuring that the next deep dive will address a genuine gap in the current offering rather than the creator's personal comfort zone.

Critics might argue that allowing a vote could lead to the most popular rather than the most academically rigorous topic being selected, potentially prioritizing trendiness over depth. However, Henry's track record suggests he will maintain high standards regardless of the winner. He frames the choice between Confucianism and Buddhism not as a competition, but as a necessary expansion of the channel's Asian religious coverage. "I'd be very curious to see what's the... what does the audience want to see most," he says, emphasizing his desire to align his output with viewer curiosity.

The Academic Reality Behind the Screen

Beyond the content announcements, the stream offers a rare glimpse into the precarious reality of modern religious studies scholarship. Henry reveals that he has just finished his PhD after eight years, a timeline he notes is "average" for the humanities but far from ideal. "It took me two years to finish coursework, two years to do my exams, and then I spent four years writing the dissertation," he recounts, candidly admitting that "two of those was just major procrastination that was running a YouTube channel instead of writing."

This honesty about the slow, often frustrating pace of academic progress adds a layer of authenticity to his work. He is not a detached professor speaking from an ivory tower; he is someone who has navigated the "terrible" academic job market and is now embarking on a postdoctoral fellowship in Cairo to study Coptic Christianity. "I'll be moving to Egypt whenever this pandemic ends... and I'll be focusing on late antiquity Coptic Christianity," he shares. This personal context helps explain why his content often bridges the gap between rigorous scholarship and accessible storytelling; he is living the tension between the two worlds.

"I started Religion For Breakfast in my third year of my PhD program... yeah, so it's been five years since starting this channel."

The stream also touches on the future of the channel's scope, with Henry confirming that he has footage and scripts ready for videos on the Druze and Mandaean religions. He explains that the Mandaean video is prioritized because a research institute provided him with "gigabytes and gigabytes of footage of Mandaean rituals," allowing him to "just plug and play" into the production. This logistical detail highlights the unique advantage of his platform: the ability to utilize primary source materials that might otherwise remain inaccessible to the general public.

Bottom Line

Andrew Henry's announcement is a masterclass in community engagement, successfully reframing his channel as a collaborative effort to diversify religious literacy. The strongest part of his argument is the honest admission that his previous focus on Christianity was a result of personal ease rather than a comprehensive strategy, and his commitment to correcting this through a structured Shinto series and a patron-led vote. The biggest vulnerability remains the sheer volume of content required to do justice to these complex traditions, but his transparent planning and academic rigor suggest he is up for the challenge. Readers should watch closely to see how the vote between Confucianism and Buddhism plays out, as it will signal the next major direction in the global study of religion on his platform.
