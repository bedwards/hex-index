Andrew Henry transforms a mundane quarantine livestream into a profound meditation on how religion survives when the physical gathering—the very heart of faith—is stripped away. While most pandemic commentary focuses on logistics or politics, Henry leverages his unique position as a religious studies scholar to dissect the potential loss of "collective effervescence," the emotional energy generated only when people worship in the same room. This is not just a Q&A session; it is a real-time case study in whether the digital age can truly replicate the visceral power of shared ritual.

The Social Architecture of Faith

Henry's central thesis rests on the work of Émile Durkheim, a foundational sociologist who argued that religion is fundamentally a social phenomenon. Henry writes, "I can't think of religion as anything but social, the collective experience of people in the same room together chanting or singing together, dancing together." He suggests that the shift to Zoom services and live-streamed prayers might capture the content of the ritual but fails to capture the cognitive and physiological experience of being physically present with others. This distinction is crucial; it moves the conversation beyond "can we do church online?" to "what are we losing when we do?"

The author's skepticism is grounded in the idea that religion relies on a feedback loop of human energy. He notes, "There is a cognitive physiological aspect to religion, chanting a prayer next to a person, banging a drum next to a person, and if you are lacking that physiological aspect of religion, not being in the same space, I'm just curious to see what religion only happens online would look like." This framing is effective because it acknowledges the adaptability of faith while warning against the illusion that technology can fully substitute for human proximity. Critics might note that Henry underestimates the historical flexibility of religious communities, which have often thrived in isolation or through written texts, but his point about the loss of the collective high remains a compelling, if unsettling, possibility.

"Part of me thinks that you can't do religion solely online... it misses a huge aspect of the collective, what I would think of as the social psychological experience of religion where people are kind of working off of each other."

The Scholar in the Wild

What makes Henry's commentary particularly valuable is his refusal to treat his academic credentials as a barrier to casual conversation. He seamlessly weaves his doctoral research into the chat, explaining the difference between theology and religious studies with clarity. "Religious studies is studying the human aspect of religion, the stories, the rituals, the history, why do humans do this, why do humans believe that, while theology is more of exploring the questions that certain religions raise," he explains. By demystifying the field, he invites the audience to see religion not as a set of abstract truths, but as a human behavior that can be observed and analyzed.

His approach to the pandemic itself is equally nuanced. He resists the urge to capitalize on the crisis for content, stating, "I don't want to be a profiteer during a crisis. I want to make sure it's actually educating and adding value to what's a very anxious time for global society." This ethical stance adds weight to his observations; he is not an outsider looking in, but a participant trying to make sense of the moment without exploiting it. He even draws a parallel between the current lockdown and ancient monasticism, noting that "all of us living in our house, not socializing, I think we've kind of taken on a monastic life." This observation reframes the isolation of quarantine not as a punishment, but as a historical recurrence of a spiritual discipline.

The Digital Frontier

Despite his skepticism, Henry acknowledges the incredible adaptability of religious institutions. He points out that religions have always embraced new technologies, from the codex to the printing press to the iPad. "Religions are ridiculously flexible when it comes to technological advance," he admits, citing the shift to virtual services in mosques and churches. However, he maintains that flexibility does not guarantee equivalence. The question remains whether the "collective effervescence" can be digitized or if it is strictly bound to physical co-presence.

He also touches on the fascinating intersection of pop culture and theology, noting that while video games like World of Warcraft borrow heavily from religious concepts like "mana," they often lack the deep historical roots of real-world faiths. Yet, he finds value in these fictional worlds as a way to explore religious structures. "The history of religions in that world is sophisticated," he says of Game of Thrones, highlighting how even fantasy narratives rely on the same polytheistic and monotheistic tensions found in human history. This willingness to engage with pop culture as a legitimate site of religious inquiry broadens the scope of his analysis, making it accessible to a generation that may find traditional texts less relatable.

Bottom Line

Andrew Henry's commentary succeeds because it treats the pandemic not just as a logistical challenge, but as a theological crisis that forces a re-evaluation of what makes religion work. His strongest argument is the distinction between the content of worship and the experience of worship, a nuance often lost in the rush to digitize faith. The biggest vulnerability in his position is the assumption that the "collective effervescence" is entirely dependent on physical proximity, ignoring the ways digital communities might generate their own unique forms of social energy. As religious institutions navigate this brave new world, Henry's warning to watch what is lost—and what might be gained—remains essential listening.
