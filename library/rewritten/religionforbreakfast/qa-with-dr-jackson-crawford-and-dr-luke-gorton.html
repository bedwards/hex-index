Andrew Henry captures a rare, unscripted moment where three leading scholars of ancient languages and religions collide live on camera, transforming a technical glitch into a genuine exploration of how sacred language shapes belief. Rather than a polished lecture, the piece offers a candid look at how experts navigate the gap between academic precision and public expectation, revealing why archaic phrasing often feels more "holy" than accurate translation.

The Sacred Dialect of English

Henry sets the stage at the American Academy of Religion annual meeting, bringing together Dr. Jackson Crawford, a former academic turned full-time YouTuber specializing in Norse myth, and Dr. Luke Gorton, whose work bridges ancient linguistics and religion. The conversation quickly pivots from conference logistics to a profound observation about the King James Version (KJV) of the Bible. As Andrew Henry writes, "It it shapes it kind of provides a a a dialect that that many people feel is like a a sacred dialect of English." This insight is crucial: the KJV's impact isn't just about specific phrases, but about creating a psychological distance that signals the divine.

Dr. Gorton expands on this, noting that the KJV has a "deeper level of impact" similar to Shakespeare, providing a linguistic register that feels set apart from everyday speech. The argument holds significant weight because it explains the persistence of "KJV onlyism" among certain Protestant groups; it is less about textual superiority and more about the emotional resonance of a language that feels ancient and authoritative. Critics might note that this reverence for archaic English can obscure the actual meaning of texts, turning translation into a performance of piety rather than a pursuit of clarity. Yet, as Dr. Crawford points out, this expectation bleeds into other fields, noting that modern public domain translations of the Norse Poetic Edda often adopt a "King Jamesy" style because "people want it needs to sound like that to be ancient and holy."

Cross-Cultural Encounters and Mystical Realities

The discussion then broadens to the intersections of ancient polytheism and emerging monotheisms, as well as the geographical reach of Norse culture. Henry guides the guests through questions about the linguistic friction when Norse gods met Christian concepts. Dr. Gorton suggests that early Christian literature often reframed Norse deities not as non-existent, but as "demonic beings that you might really encounter In the Flesh," citing King Olaf Tryggvason's legendary encounters with Thor. This reframing is a fascinating example of how religious dominance operates linguistically, absorbing and redefining the vocabulary of the conquered.

The conversation also touches on the surprising connections between Northern Europe and the Islamic world. Henry highlights the account of Ahmad ibn Fadlan, an Arab traveler who documented a Viking funeral in the 10th century. The guests note that while the Norse didn't write much about the Arab world, the material culture tells a different story: "the best bling is to be found in Baghdad Constantinople and a lot of that stuff gets buried with high status people." This detail underscores the global trade networks that predate the popular Viking stereotype of isolated raiders.

Finally, the group tackles the definition of mysticism in the context of Jesus and the Gospels. When asked if Jesus was a Kabbalist, Dr. Crawford clarifies that Kabbalah is a medieval development, but the roots of mystical experience are older. He distinguishes between apocalypticism and mysticism, suggesting that while Jesus may not have engaged in the later traditions of Kabbalah, events like the Transfiguration fit the definition of a mystical experience where "famous Heroes of the faith appearing next to your your guy Jesus." This distinction is vital for understanding the evolution of religious thought, separating the historical Jesus from later mystical traditions that claimed his legacy.

Bottom Line

Henry's coverage succeeds because it prioritizes the messy, conversational reality of scholarship over a polished script, allowing the experts to correct each other and refine their definitions in real time. The strongest part of the piece is its revelation that the "holiness" of religious language is often a constructed aesthetic rather than an inherent quality of the text. The biggest vulnerability is the lack of deep dive into the specific linguistic mechanisms of how these translations were made, leaving some of the technical claims slightly surface-level. Readers should watch for how these scholars continue to challenge the public's romanticized view of ancient texts in future collaborations.
