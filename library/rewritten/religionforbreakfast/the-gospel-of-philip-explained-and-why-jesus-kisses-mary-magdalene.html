Andrew Henry does not merely recount the famous missing word in the Gospel of Philip; he dismantles the obsession with it to reveal a far stranger, more radical theology about how humans become divine. While pop culture fixates on whether Jesus kissed Mary Magdalene on the mouth or the forehead, Henry argues that the text is actually a manual for spiritual transformation where the believer doesn't just follow Christ, but becomes one. This is a crucial pivot for any reader tired of sensationalist conspiracy theories, offering instead a rigorous look at how early Christians imagined the mechanics of salvation.

The Missing Word and the Real Mystery

Henry begins by addressing the elephant in the room: the damaged manuscript that has fueled centuries of speculation. "The companion of the Savior is Mary Magdalene. He loved her more than all the disciples, and he used to kiss her often on her, and then the manuscript simply breaks off." He notes the frustration of the gap, asking, "Did it say mouth, face, forehead, feet? We don't know." Yet, he immediately pivots, asserting that the missing word is a distraction. "The Gospel of Philip is far less interested in romance than in rituals and less interested in who kissed whom and more about how a person becomes a Christ." This reframing is the piece's strongest move. It forces the reader to stop looking for a hidden love story and start looking for a hidden spiritual technology.

Critics might argue that the romantic speculation is culturally significant regardless of the author's intent, but Henry's focus on the text's actual theological machinery offers a more substantive historical inquiry. The text is not a biography; it is a "hodgepodge of material strung together with no obvious system," which Henry suggests might be a deliberate "notebook or scrapbook of ideas." This lack of narrative structure, far from being a flaw, serves the text's purpose of presenting fragmented, paradoxical truths.

A Cosmology of Two Bodies

The commentary then dives into the text's most startling theological claim: the rejection of the virgin birth in favor of a dual-birth theory for Jesus. Henry explains that the author mocks the traditional narrative, stating, "Some say that Mary conceived by the Holy Spirit. They are wrong. They do not know what they are saying. When did a female ever conceive by a female?" This linguistic argument, rooted in the feminine grammar of the Holy Spirit in Semitic languages, is a bold move that overturns centuries of dogma. Henry clarifies that for Philip, Jesus had a biological father, Joseph, and a spiritual father, God.

But the real transformation happens later. "He who was begotten before all things was begotten again... On that day he came out from the bridal chamber like the one who came into being from the bridegroom and the bride." Henry breaks this down to show that Jesus's baptism was not just a ritual but a second birth where he received a new, spiritual body. "According to the birth metaphor, the union between Jesus and the Holy Spirit issues in a new body for the human Jesus. He must therefore possess two bodies." This is a profound shift from adoptionism to a complete ontological change. The argument holds up well against the backdrop of Valentinian cosmology, where the physical world is a flawed creation by a lesser deity, the demiurge.

The world came into being from a transgression. For the one who made it wanted to make himself imperishable and immortal. He perished and he did not obtain what he hoped for.

Becoming the Christ

The ultimate goal of this complex cosmology is not just to explain Jesus, but to explain the reader. Henry highlights the text's central thesis: "A person is no longer a Christian but a Christ." This identity fusion is the engine of the entire text. The rituals described, particularly the "bridal chamber," are not symbolic metaphors for a distant heaven but practical instructions for the initiated to replicate Jesus's transformation. "What happens to him is exactly what's supposed to happen to every single Christian." This democratization of divinity is both the most exciting and the most challenging aspect of the Gospel of Philip. It suggests that the barrier between the human and the divine is not fixed, but permeable through specific spiritual practices.

The text's reliance on a pre-existing myth about the demiurge and the fall of Sophia allows it to skip the heavy lifting of explaining why the world is broken, focusing instead on how to fix it. Henry notes that the world is a "botched project" where the creator failed to achieve immortality, leaving humanity in a state of fracture. The solution is a return to wholeness through the same process Jesus underwent.

Bottom Line

Andrew Henry's coverage succeeds by stripping away the sensationalist veneer of the Gospel of Philip to reveal a sophisticated, albeit bizarre, soteriology where the believer is the protagonist of their own deification. The argument's greatest strength is its refusal to let the "missing word" overshadow the text's radical claim that humanity can achieve the same spiritual rebirth as Jesus. However, the piece assumes a level of familiarity with Valentinian cosmology that might leave some readers struggling to grasp the full weight of the "demiurge" and "eons" without more context. For the busy reader, this is a vital reminder that early Christianity was far more diverse and mystical than the canonical gospels suggest.
