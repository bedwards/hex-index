Heather Cox Richardson delivers a searing indictment of the administration's latest attempt to weaponize video evidence, only to have that footage expose the very brutality it was meant to justify. In a piece that reads like a historical autopsy of authoritarian overreach, Richardson argues that the release of the shooting of Renee Good was not a strategic blunder, but a symptom of a leadership completely isolated from the reality of the American public. This is not just about a single incident in Minneapolis; it is a case study in how the executive branch's narrative machinery is breaking down under the weight of its own contradictions.

The Video That Backfired

Richardson opens by dissecting the administration's disastrous decision to release a cell phone video captured by the shooter, Jonathan Ross, in an effort to prove he was acting in self-defense against a "domestic terrorist." The footage, however, tells a story of calm civility met with lethal aggression. Richardson notes that as Ross approached the vehicle, Good smiled and said, "That's fine, dude. I'm not mad at you," repeating the sentiment as the officer walked alongside her. The contrast between her de-escalation and the officer's escalating hostility is stark.

The administration, led by Vice President J.D. Vance, believed this visual evidence would vindicate their narrative. Instead, it provided irrefutable proof of the opposite. Richardson writes, "The release of this damning video as an attempted exoneration reminds me overwhelmingly of the release of the video of the murder of Black jogger Ahmaud Arbery in February 2021." She draws a direct line between the two events, noting that in both cases, the perpetrators and their protectors were so insulated in their own bubbles that they genuinely believed the public would agree with their version of reality. In the Arbery case, the video led to convictions; here, Richardson suggests it has only deepened the public's revulsion.

The thread that runs through both is the assumption that an American exercising their constitutional rights must submit, without question, to a white man holding a gun.

This comparison is powerful because it moves the discussion beyond the specifics of one shooting to a broader pattern of impunity. Richardson argues that the administration's isolation is not just political but psychological, preventing them from seeing how their actions appear to ordinary citizens. While critics might argue that the administration is simply reacting to a chaotic situation, Richardson's framing suggests a deliberate, ideological blindness that prioritizes control over truth.

The Escalation of Federal Force

The commentary then shifts to the broader deployment of federal resources in response to the incident. Richardson details how the administration pulled 200 agents from a crackdown in Louisiana to reinforce a force in Minnesota that already numbers over 2,000—more than three times the local police force. This massive show of force is accompanied by a recruitment push that Richardson describes as targeting "gun rights supporters and military enthusiasts" with ads placed near UFC fights and gun shows.

She points out the irony of this strategy: the administration is recruiting from the very demographics that often claim to value law and order, yet the agency's actions are increasingly viewed as lawless. Richardson cites the hiring of Greg Bovino, who led the Border Patrol during the family separation policy, to head the agency now responsible for these operations. "Border Patrol... remains at the center of complaints of cruelty, racism, and violation of civil rights," she writes, linking current events to the traumatic policies of the past. The administration's attempt to rebrand ICE as a heroic force is undermined by its own history and the visible brutality of its agents.

The administration's response to dissent has also hardened. Richardson notes that Secretary of Homeland Security Kristi Noem restricted congressional oversight of ICE facilities, blocking members of Congress from entering detention centers. This move, she argues, is part of a larger pattern of silencing opposition and controlling the narrative. The administration's claim that they are protecting the public is contradicted by their actions: detaining Indigenous leaders, freezing funding for child care in Minnesota, and attacking local officials.

This is the larger meaning of federal agents from Immigrations and Customs Enforcement and Customs and Border Patrol in U.S. cities. While they are attacking primarily people of color, the message they carry is directed at all Americans: you must do what the Trump administration and its loyalists demand.

Richardson's analysis here is particularly sharp. She identifies the underlying message of these operations: a demand for absolute submission. The targeting of Minnesota, with its large Somali-American population, is not coincidental but a calculated move to punish a community the administration has long disparaged. The administration's rhetoric about "corrupt" states and "stolen" votes, which Richardson quotes directly from the former president's remarks, reveals a worldview where political opponents are enemies to be dismantled rather than citizens to be served.

The Collapse of Public Trust

The final section of Richardson's piece focuses on the tangible consequences of these policies: a plummeting approval rating for ICE and a growing wave of protests. She cites data showing that ICE's approval rating has swung from positive to deeply negative, with 52% of Americans now disapproving of the agency. Richardson writes, "There is... a growing and intense, angry opposition to [ICE] across America."

This shift in public opinion is not merely a political setback; it is a fundamental erosion of the social contract. Richardson argues that the administration's reliance on force and fear is backfiring, creating a backlash that threatens to destabilize the very order they claim to protect. The administration's attempt to frame the shooting of Good as a victory for law enforcement has instead become a rallying cry for those demanding accountability.

Critics might argue that the administration is simply facing a difficult political moment that will pass, but Richardson's evidence suggests a deeper, structural crisis. The administration's refusal to acknowledge the reality of its actions, combined with its aggressive tactics, is driving a wedge between the government and the governed. As she notes, the administration's isolation is not just a failure of communication; it is a failure of governance.

Bottom Line

Richardson's strongest argument lies in her ability to connect the specific tragedy of Renee Good's death to a broader, systemic failure of the executive branch to understand the American public. Her use of historical parallels, particularly the Ahmaud Arbery case, effectively highlights the recurring pattern of authoritarian overreach and the inevitable public backlash. The piece's biggest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that the administration will continue to make such glaring errors, but the evidence of their escalating isolation suggests this is a reasonable projection. Readers should watch for how the administration responds to the growing protests and whether they will attempt to double down on their current strategy or seek a path toward de-escalation. The stakes are high, and the cost of inaction is measured in lives and the very fabric of American democracy.
