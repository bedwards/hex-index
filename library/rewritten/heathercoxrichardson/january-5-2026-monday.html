Heather Cox Richardson delivers a chilling historical autopsy, arguing that the events of January 6, 2021, were not an isolated riot but the culmination of a decades-long strategy to dismantle the federal government and enforce minority rule. By framing the recent past through the lens of the 1974 Impoundment Control Act and the 1861 secession crisis, she forces a re-evaluation of current institutional collapses not as anomalies, but as the intended destination of a specific political project. This is essential listening for anyone trying to understand why the machinery of American democracy is currently seizing up.

The Architecture of Insurrection

Richardson begins by dismantling the romanticized narrative of the January 6 rioters, who viewed themselves as modern Founders. She writes, "In fact, it was not 1776 but 1861, the year insurrectionists who had tried to overthrow the government in order to establish minority rule tried to break the U.S." This distinction is critical; it shifts the reader's perspective from a misunderstanding of democracy to a deliberate attempt to replicate the Confederacy's failure. The author meticulously traces how the Republican Party, since the 1980s, has cultivated a narrative that the federal government is an enemy of individual liberty, a rhetoric that eventually justified armed resistance against federal officials.

This framing connects the dots between the Brooks Brothers riot and the armed standoff at the Cliven Bundy ranch in 2014. Richardson notes that by 2014, Senator Dean Heller had labeled Bundy's armed supporters "patriots" when they met Bureau of Land Management officials to prevent the impoundment of his cattle. This historical thread reveals that the idea of "reclaiming" the country from the federal government was not a sudden outburst in 2020, but a cultivated ideology that normalized the use of force to settle political disputes. The author argues that this mindset created a fertile ground where the idea that "individuals and paramilitary groups must 'reclaim' America" became embedded in the party's DNA.

The rioters wanted to take away the right at the center of American democracy—our right to determine our own destiny—in order to keep [the former president] in the White House, making sure the power of elite white men could not be challenged.

The Legal Precedent for Autocracy

The piece pivots sharply to the legal mechanisms that enabled this shift, specifically focusing on the violation of the Congressional Budget and Impoundment Control Act of 1974. Richardson highlights the 2019 Ukraine scandal as the moment the administration openly flouted this law, withholding congressionally appropriated funds to coerce a foreign leader. She writes, "The attempt to withhold congressionally appropriated funds in order to tilt an election was a glaring violation of the 1974 Impoundment Control Act codifying the executive branch's duty to execute the laws Congress passed."

This section is particularly potent because it moves beyond personality clashes to institutional decay. The author points out that while Republicans in 1974 turned against President Richard Nixon for far less, the party stood behind the current administration's actions. Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell's justification—that the issue was "about flipping the Senate" rather than the accused conduct—exposes a prioritization of power over principle. Richardson argues that once the administration was acquitted, the concept of checks and balances collapsed entirely. The former president declared, "When somebody is President of the United States, the authority is total," a sentiment that Richardson identifies as the death knell of the rule of law.

Critics might argue that the administration's actions were a necessary evolution of executive power to bypass a gridlocked Congress, but the evidence presented suggests a deliberate strategy to bypass the legislature entirely rather than negotiate with it.

The Strategy of Chaos

Richardson then dissects the tactical planning behind the 2020 election denial, revealing a pre-meditated strategy to exploit the "red mirage" of early election returns. She quotes Steve Bannon, who told a private audience, "Trump's going to take advantage of it... He's gonna declare himself a winner. After then, Trump never has to go to a voter again."

This quote is the smoking gun of the article, illustrating that the chaos of January 6 was not a spontaneous reaction to a lost election, but a calculated move to prevent the certification of results. The author details how the administration planned to submit false slates of electors in seven battleground states, a scheme designed to send the election to the House of Representatives where Republican delegations could install the former president despite his loss. Richardson writes, "Two slates would enable Vice President Mike Pence to refuse to count the electors from the now-contested states, so that either Trump would be elected outright, or Pence could say there was no clear winner and send the election to the House of Representatives."

The human cost of this strategy is palpable in Richardson's description of lawmakers hiding in their offices while the Capitol was breached. She notes that the former president did nothing for more than three hours while his supporters attacked, only issuing a statement to send them home once it was clear the National Guard would overpower them. The author emphasizes the sheer audacity of the moment: "Remember this day forever!" he tweeted, even as the government he led was being dismantled by his own supporters.

The New Normal

The commentary concludes by projecting these historical patterns into the present, describing a scenario where the administration, having secured a second term, openly flouts the Constitution. Richardson describes a White House that ignores the 1974 Impoundment Control Act again, seizes people off the streets, and ignores court orders to release the Epstein files. She quotes White House deputy chief of staff Stephen Miller, who told CNN, "We live in a world, in the real world, Jake, that is governed by strength, that is governed by force, that is governed by power."

This quote encapsulates the article's central thesis: the rejection of the rules-based international order and the American system of checks and balances in favor of raw power. Richardson contrasts this with the vision of Abraham Lincoln in 1863, who called for a "new birth of freedom" and a government "of the people, by the people, for the people." The author argues that we are now witnessing the unraveling of that system, replaced by a doctrine where the executive branch is immune to accountability.

Today, we are learning what it means to have a president who has demonstrated his determination to overthrow our democracy and who does not have to answer to the law.

Bottom Line

Richardson's strongest argument lies in her ability to connect the 1974 Impoundment Control Act to the current crisis, proving that the erosion of democratic norms is a legal and structural failure, not just a political one. Her biggest vulnerability is the speculative nature of the 2025-2026 timeline, which relies on the assumption that the Supreme Court's immunity ruling will be fully exploited without pushback, a scenario that remains contingent on future judicial and legislative resistance. Readers should watch closely for how the administration attempts to operationalize the "iron laws of power" Miller described, as this will define the next chapter of American governance.
