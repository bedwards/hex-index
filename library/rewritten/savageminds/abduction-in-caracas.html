This piece delivers a jarring, high-stakes narrative that reframes a hypothetical military abduction not as a triumph of law enforcement, but as a catastrophic collapse of democratic norms and a return to imperial overreach. Tariq Ali does not merely report on the kidnapping of a head of state; he dissects the legal farce surrounding the charges and the historical amnesia that allows such operations to proceed. For a reader seeking to understand the mechanics of modern interventionism, Ali's argument that this is a deliberate "Noriega formula" applied to a sovereign nation offers a chilling lens through which to view current geopolitical tensions.

The Noriega Script Reimagined

Ali anchors his analysis in a specific, terrifying prediction made years ago by Hugo Chávez regarding the tactics the United States would eventually deploy. He writes, "Years ago, someone told me: 'They're going to end up accusing you of being a drug trafficker—you personally... They're going to try to apply the Noriega formula to you.'" This framing is crucial because it shifts the narrative from a spontaneous reaction to a premeditated strategy of delegitimization. The author argues that by labeling the Venezuelan leader a "drug trafficker," the executive branch creates a legal pretext where "anything goes against a 'drug trafficker president'."

The commentary suggests that the administration's reliance on this narrative is a cynical play to bypass the complexities of international law. Ali notes that the details of the operation were immediately confused, with the White House claiming a "large scale strike" had occurred while sources indicated secret negotiations had been ongoing. This dissonance highlights a government that is more interested in theatrical displays of power than coherent policy. The author points out that while the administration offered Maduro a "well-cushioned retirement in Turkey," he refused, choosing instead to rally his people against the military build-up.

"The Trump administration seems incapable of grasping that—whatever people think of Maduro—very few Venezuelans welcome an invasion of their country by the United States."

This observation lands with particular weight because it ignores the internal dynamics of Venezuelan politics to focus on the universal human cost of foreign occupation. Ali draws a direct line to the 1989 invasion of Panama, noting that Manuel Noriega had effectively worked for the CIA for decades before falling out of favor. He argues that the current operation mirrors that history, where a leader is trained by the US, utilized for intelligence, and then discarded when they assert national sovereignty. Critics might argue that equating Maduro with Noriega ignores the significant differences in their respective relationships with Washington, but Ali's point remains that the method of removal—the "Noriega formula"—is the consistent variable.

The Institutional Decay of American Democracy

The piece takes a sharp turn to critique the domestic implications of this foreign adventure, suggesting that the abduction reveals a deeper rot within the United States itself. Ali writes, "The absence of any serious opposition in the country capable of insisting on the authority of Congress suggests a process of decay within the institutions of American bourgeois democracy itself." He characterizes the legal proceedings as a "farce," specifically pointing to the indictment of a sitting president for "conspiracy to possess" automatic weapons while that same president was being held in custody by US forces.

The author argues that the appointments to the second cabinet were made on "loyalty rather than competence," selecting officials who would not question the President's "crazy ideas." This is a bold claim that challenges the traditional view of the executive branch as a check on itself. Ali further complicates the narrative by introducing the work of investigative journalist Seth Harp, whose book The Fort Bragg Cartel allegedly documents drug trafficking within the special forces unit responsible for the abduction. He states, "So this criminal US operation was carried out by its own drug cartel."

"To accuse a sitting president, whom you've just kidnapped while bombing his capital, of 'conspiracy to possess' automatic weapons is grotesque."

This section is the most provocative of the article, as it suggests that the very agencies tasked with upholding the law are operating outside of it. While the claim about Delta Force's involvement in narcotics is presented as established fact by the author, relying on a single investigative book as the primary evidence for such a grave accusation is a vulnerability. However, the rhetorical power of the argument lies in its inversion of the "narco-terrorist" label, suggesting that the accusers are the true criminals.

The Human Cost of Imperial Ambition

Ali refuses to let the geopolitical chess game obscure the human reality on the ground. He emphasizes that the "dismay goes well beyond the support base of the regime," citing a leading anti-Maduro Catholic leader who told the BBC, "No, we are not happy. We don't like our country being occupied." This quote serves as a powerful counter-narrative to the administration's claim of liberating the Venezuelan people. The author connects this sentiment to the legacy of Simón Bolívar, who warned that Latin America must "beware the new empire to the north and resist exchanging Spanish for American domination."

The piece also touches on the tragic precedent of Jean-Bertrand Aristide in Haiti, whose removal Ali describes as a "Franco-American operation" that silenced a leader who dared to demand reparations for slavery. He recalls a conversation with Chávez, who expressed regret for not offering Aristide asylum in time. This historical context adds a layer of tragedy to the current events, suggesting a recurring cycle of intervention that punishes leaders who challenge US hegemony.

"Maduro is the latest in a long line. The attacks against him recall the assaults on Chávez, continually accused by the Western media of being a dictator. Why? Because he wore a uniform."

Ali's argument here is that the label of "dictator" is often a convenient shorthand used to justify intervention against leaders who are actually popular, as evidenced by Jimmy Carter's observation of fair elections in Venezuela. He acknowledges the economic failures of the Bolivarian regime, noting that "the real economic deterioration was a result of the US siege" and sanctions that led to millions of refugees. By admitting these internal weaknesses, Ali strengthens his critique of the external aggression, arguing that the US siege was a deliberate strategy to destabilize the country.

Bottom Line

Tariq Ali's commentary is a masterclass in connecting historical precedent to current events, framing the abduction not as an isolated incident but as the culmination of decades of imperial strategy. The strongest part of the argument is its unflinching exposure of the legal and moral contradictions inherent in the administration's actions, particularly the irony of charging a kidnapped leader with crimes while the kidnappers operate with impunity. Its biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on controversial claims about US special forces that, while compelling, may be difficult for a general audience to verify without further corroboration. Readers should watch for how the international community responds to this precedent, as the normalization of kidnapping heads of state could fundamentally alter the rules of global engagement.
