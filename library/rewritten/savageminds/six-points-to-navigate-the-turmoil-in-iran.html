Vijay Prashad delivers a jarring counter-narrative to the dominant Western media coverage of unrest in Iran, arguing that the current turmoil is less a spontaneous uprising for democracy and more a destabilization campaign orchestrated by foreign powers to install a monarchy. While mainstream reports focus on the grievances of protesters, Prashad insists the violence has been hijacked by "influence operations aiming to install Reza Pahlavi as Shah of Iran," transforming legitimate labor struggles into a potential regime change operation. For the busy reader seeking clarity beyond the headlines, this piece demands attention because it reframes the conflict from a simple "people vs. government" dynamic to a complex geopolitical chessboard where the lives of ordinary Iranians are collateral damage.

The Roots of Discontent and Foreign Interference

Prashad begins by acknowledging the very real economic pain driving the unrest, noting that "what began as work stoppages and inflation protests drew together a range of discontent, with women and young people frustrated with a system unable to secure their livelihood." He is careful to distinguish between the internal causes of the anger and the external forces that have amplified them. The author argues that while the United States has waged an "economic war" against Iran, the resulting turmoil is not directed at Washington but at the government in Tehran. However, Prashad contends that foreign intelligence services have seized upon these sincere protests to manipulate the outcome.

The piece offers a stark assessment of the opposition's leadership, stating that "the United States has 'chosen' the Shah's son, who lives in Los Angeles, as the figure of this uprising." This framing suggests that the movement lacks organic political depth and is instead being steered by a figurehead with little connection to the current generation's struggles. Prashad writes, "It is the United States and Israel that have been systematically trying to erode Iran's power in the region," citing a long history of intervention from the 1953 coup to recent assassinations and sabotage operations. This historical lens is crucial; it reminds the reader that the current instability did not emerge in a vacuum but is the latest chapter in a century of foreign interference.

Critics might argue that attributing the protests entirely to foreign orchestration risks dismissing the genuine agency and suffering of Iranian citizens who are demanding real change. Yet, Prashad's evidence regarding the sudden shift from peaceful assemblies to "high-intensity urban sabotage" suggests a coordinated effort that goes beyond organic dissent.

The Mechanics of Destabilization

The most contentious part of Prashad's analysis focuses on the nature of the violence itself. He describes a rapid escalation where "the 'protests' shifted overnight from peaceful assemblies to high-intensity urban sabotage resulting in the deaths of roughly 100 law enforcement officers." The author points to specific, brutal incidents—claims that officers were burned alive, a security member was beheaded, and a medical clinic was torched—as evidence of a strategy designed to maximize domestic tension. Prashad asserts that "the use of close-range small arms fire against civilians further suggests an attempt to maximize domestic tension and provide a pretext for foreign intervention."

The geopolitical orchestration behind the chaos became undeniable as the US State Department and Mossad openly cheered the violence in real-time.

Prashad argues that the economic collapse, marked by a "30-40% currency drop," bears the hallmarks of external financial manipulation rather than purely domestic policy failure. He notes that when authorities disabled Internet access, the protests significantly lost strength, which he interprets as proof that the movement relied on external coordination rather than spontaneous mass mobilization. This argument challenges the reader to question the narrative of a purely grassroots revolution. The author suggests that the administration has favored certain import-export sectors, creating a fragile economic environment that was easily exploited by external actors to trigger a currency crash.

The Human Cost and the Path Forward

Ultimately, Prashad's commentary centers on the human cost of this geopolitical maneuvering. He warns that as the mobilizations lose steam, the United States and Israel might "strike Tehran and other cities with more force than it did in June 2025." This potential for aerial bombardment is framed not as a liberation but as a catastrophe for the Global South. Prashad writes, "Real problems bedevil the population, but these problems are not going to be solved through a hyper-imperialist aerial bombardment by the United States and Israel."

The author concludes with a plea for a different kind of solidarity. He observes that while it is easy for Western observers to express sympathy for protesters, it is much harder to demand the end of the sanctions that are strangling the Iranian economy. "It is easy to say 'solidarity to the Iranians' in the West... And somehow, it seems to be much harder to say 'end the sanctions,' and therefore allow the Iranian people to breathe into their own future." This final point forces a moral reckoning: true support for the Iranian people requires opposing the very policies that are exacerbating their suffering.

The Iranians will need to sort out their own problems. The sanctions regime and the threats of violence do nothing to allow that to happen.

Bottom Line

Prashad's strongest contribution is his refusal to separate the economic grievances of the Iranian people from the geopolitical machinations of the United States and Israel, offering a necessary corrective to the simplistic "democracy vs. tyranny" narrative. However, the argument's vulnerability lies in its heavy emphasis on foreign orchestration, which, while supported by historical precedent, risks underestimating the depth of domestic frustration with the Islamic Republic's own failures. Readers should watch for whether the violence continues to escalate into a full-scale foreign intervention or if the movement reverts to a purely internal political struggle.
