Boaventura de Sousa Santos delivers a jarring diagnosis of current global affairs, arguing that what looks like a sudden geopolitical shock is actually the return of a centuries-old logic: colonialism. He challenges the comfortable assumption that we live in a post-colonial world, asserting instead that the treatment of certain populations as "subhuman" remains the engine of modern capitalism. This piece is notable not just for its historical sweep, but for its refusal to treat recent interventions as anomalies, framing them instead as the predictable output of a system that has always relied on the "abysmal line" separating the powerful from the expendable.

The Architecture of Surprise

Santos begins by dismantling the notion that the recent capture of a political leader in Caracas was a surprise. He argues that the world's shock is a form of collective denial, a refusal to see the patterns laid out in the National Security Strategy of November 2025. "The less informed would say since Donald Trump came to power. But it is mainly since the publication of the National Security Strategy in November 2025, which states that the US reserves the right to intervene in any country whenever its interests are at stake," he writes. By anchoring the event in policy rather than personality, the author shifts the focus from a specific administration to a structural shift in how the executive branch views its global prerogatives.

He draws a stark parallel to September 1939, noting that the world was similarly stunned by the invasion of Poland, despite clear warning signs. "The winner is never asked if what he said was the truth or a lie. As far as starting and fighting a war is concerned, there is no law—victory is the decisive factor," he quotes from the Nazi justification, highlighting how historical precedents of manufactured aggression are being recycled. This comparison is effective because it forces the reader to confront the continuity of imperial tactics, suggesting that the "surprise" is merely a failure of political imagination among the elite.

Colonialism is the treatment of a people or social group considered subhuman and, as such, unworthy of being defended by international or national law.

The Trophy of Empire

The commentary then pivots to the physical act of capturing and displaying leaders, a theme that resonates with the historical precedent of the United States invasion of Panama. Santos recalls the 1990 capture of Manuel Noriega but pushes the timeline back to the era of historical colonialism to show the depth of the tradition. He details the fate of King Ngungunyane, the "Lion of Gaza," who was defeated in 1895 and paraded through Lisbon as a "trophy of war." Santos notes, "Displaying symbols of resistance (sometimes the leaders themselves, their skulls, or their art objects) as trophies in the metropolis is a consistent practice of colonial rule."

This historical weaving is powerful. By referencing the 1897 decapitation of King Toera in Madagascar, whose head was kept in a Paris museum until recently, the author illustrates that the dehumanization of leaders is not a modern invention but a persistent feature of imperial dominance. The argument suggests that the location of the "deposit"—whether an island, a museum, or a center in New York—is merely a matter of convenience for the victor. Critics might argue that equating modern geopolitical maneuvering with 19th-century colonial trophy-taking oversimplifies the complexities of contemporary international law, yet the author's insistence on the "subhuman" status of the target makes the comparison difficult to dismiss.

Recolonization and the Abysmal Line

Santos challenges the idea that colonialism ended with independence movements. "Nothing could be further from the truth," he asserts, defining colonialism as a permanent component of capitalism rather than a historical phase. He critiques Karl Marx for viewing colonial violence as merely an initial phase of "primitive accumulation," arguing instead that "Colonial violence is permanent, and without it, capitalism would not exist." This reframing is the intellectual core of the piece, suggesting that the current global order relies on the same extraction and dispossession that defined the age of sail.

He applies this lens to current conflicts, contrasting the treatment of leaders in Ukraine and Venezuela. "Vlodymyr Zelensky’s legitimacy is as great or as small as that of Nicolás Maduro, but while the former is welcomed as a hero, the latter is captured and treated as a criminal," he observes. The author argues that the double standard is driven by strategic interests: one leader serves to annoy a rival power, while the other controls resources that the West seeks to monopolize. "Maduro’s crime was not handing over the oil until now," he writes, stripping away the moral rhetoric to reveal the resource calculus.

If there is anything new in the world, it is not techno-feudalism, it is techno-colonialism.

The Strategy of Vassalage

The piece concludes with a grim assessment of the National Security Strategy's implications for Europe and Latin America. Santos describes a plan to transform the US sphere of influence into a "fortress defended by loyal vassals," specifically targeting Central, Eastern, and Southern Europe. He points to a specific policy formulation: "Building up healthy nations in Central, Eastern, and Southern Europe through commercial ties, weapons sales, political collaboration, and cultural and educational exchanges." He interprets this as a deliberate exclusion of major EU powers like France and Germany, aiming to reduce the European Union to irrelevance.

In Latin America, the strategy is portrayed as even more aggressive, with the author warning of "massive intervention by BigTech" and "selective digital blackouts" to immobilize resistance. He notes the strategic error of Brazil blocking Venezuela's entry into BRICS, which contributed to its isolation. The human cost is implicit but heavy; the author mentions the indigenous peoples of Venezuela, whose ancestral territories are exploited for mining, noting that "the Venezuelan people knew nothing and were taken by surprise." This highlights the disconnect between high-level geopolitical games and the lived reality of those on the ground.

Critics might suggest that the author's view of Europe as a collection of "self-mutilated" vassals ignores the agency of European leaders and the genuine security concerns driving their alignment with the US. However, the piece's strength lies in its unflinching look at how economic dependency is weaponized to ensure political loyalty.

Bottom Line

Santos's most compelling argument is that the "return" of colonialism is a misnomer; it is the reassertion of a system that never truly left, now accelerated by digital tools and a revised security doctrine. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its tendency to view all geopolitical actors through a singular lens of imperial logic, potentially underestimating the internal fractures within the West and the agency of the Global South. Readers should watch for how the "abysmal line" between "us" and "them" is drawn in the coming months, as it will determine who is protected by international law and who is treated as a resource to be extracted.
