Michael Albert confronts a disturbing trend within the very movements Chomsky spent a lifetime nurturing: the rush to cancel a legend based on circumstantial associations rather than substantive evidence. This piece is notable not for defending Chomsky's perfection, but for exposing the intellectual laziness and self-defeating dynamics of a left that prioritizes moral purity over strategic unity. In an era where nuance is often the first casualty of outrage, Albert asks a question that demands a sober answer: are we destroying our most effective critics because they refused to stop engaging with the very monsters they fight?

The Weight of a Lifetime

Albert begins by anchoring Chomsky's legacy in his final, collaborative act: co-signing the "Twenty Theses for Liberation." He notes that while Chomsky did not agree with every word, the shared aim was to "Propel an ongoing conversation to continually update the broadly shared vision and strategy." This context is crucial. It frames Chomsky not as a distant icon, but as a working organizer committed to "mutual aid, collective support, listening, empathy, patient collective self-correction, and outreach." Albert argues that dismissing Chomsky now ignores decades of this specific, grounded work.

The author's central thesis is that the current backlash relies on a fundamental misunderstanding of Chomsky's methodology. "Did Noam lie when he signed that? And in his lifetime of literally countless words and deeds, did he lie tens of thousands of times?" Albert writes, challenging the notion that a single association invalidates a century of dissent. He suggests that the impulse to revile Chomsky stems from a desire to find a "fellow traveller" in the system, a narrative that fits a convenient villain but ignores the complexity of a man who "routinely related to all sorts of stuff and people that most of us would never go near."

"If we are to counter the lies and violence, and especially if we are to do better, such reporting is needed. The task needs doing, and it turns out that I am able to do it. So I do."

This explanation—that Chomsky engaged with figures like Stephen Bannon or Jeffrey Epstein not out of admiration, but out of a relentless curiosity to dismantle the lies they propagated—is the piece's strongest analytical move. It reframes the controversial photos and letters not as evidence of complicity, but as the byproduct of a rigorous, almost obsessive commitment to understanding the machinery of power. Albert posits that Chomsky's willingness to "burrow into piles and piles of reportage of violent and disgusting evil" was a strategic necessity, not a moral failing.

The Trap of Guilt by Association

Albert does not shy away from the gravity of the crimes in question. He is unequivocal that Epstein was a "sex trafficker, a woman defiler, and a rapist thug," and that institutions like JP Morgan Chase and Deutsche Bank deserve "critical attention" for their role in sustaining such networks. However, he sharply critiques the speed with which some progressives have pivoted from analyzing these systems to attacking Chomsky personally. "Why do some of us so often quickly assume the worst interpretation of others based on meager information and even against massive evidence that supports the opposite interpretation?" he asks.

The author draws a parallel to the Faurisson affair, a historical flashpoint where Chomsky's defense of free speech for a Holocaust denier was misinterpreted as support for denialism. Just as that controversy was often weaponized to dismiss Chomsky's broader critique of state power, the current Epstein allegations are being used to erase his contributions. Albert warns that this is a "self-defeating circular firing squad dynamic," where the left's demand for absolute moral clarity leads to the exclusion of its most formidable intellectual weapons.

Critics might argue that Albert underestimates the symbolic damage of associating with such figures, regardless of intent. In a movement built on accountability, the optics of sharing a jet with a predator can be as damaging as the act itself, potentially alienating the very victims Chomsky claims to support. Yet, Albert counters that this reaction often ignores the distinction between the perpetrator and the system: "Noam has always hated injustice, hypocrisy, and lying. But he hasn't very often drifted into hating the perpetrators. Just the system."

"Is this just the left's self-defeating circular firing squad dynamic at work? Is it cancel culture on steroids? You did or said something different from what I think I would have done or said. You are therefore guilty."

Albert's critique of this "guilty until proven innocent" mentality is particularly biting. He notes that the mainstream media, long an enemy of Chomsky, is now "joyously rush[ing] toward the disparaging explanation," while some on the left are eager to join in. The author suggests that this rush to judgment is not only factually weak but strategically disastrous. If we cannot distinguish between an interviewer who engages a thug to expose them and a thug who is being exposed, we lose the ability to conduct effective political analysis.

The Path Forward

The article concludes with a call to action that transcends the defense of one man. Albert urges readers to look past the "side two" of the scale—the few pictures and letters—and weigh them against the "side one" of a life dedicated to addressing injustice. He challenges the movement to "change Noam's epitaph... to 'He Helped'" by following his example of organizing rather than mourning. The ultimate goal, he argues, is to "win a new world that each helps all to win," a task that requires unity, not fragmentation.

The piece serves as a reminder that the fight against systems of oppression requires engaging with the messy, uncomfortable realities of power, even when it means sitting across the table from the devil. As Albert puts it, "If we don't take that particular document's implicit path, I am confident that Noam would suggest that we should take some other shared path to reach the same revolutionary end."

"Perpetrators of vile misogyny deserve abhorrence. More to the point, however, the institutions that produce, sustain, and indeed reward vile misogyny deserve replacement."

Bottom Line

Michael Albert's defense of Chomsky is a compelling, necessary intervention that challenges the left to resist the seductive simplicity of cancel culture in favor of the difficult work of strategic engagement. While the argument risks minimizing the visceral harm of Chomsky's associations, its core insight—that we must distinguish between the system and the individuals who inhabit it to effectively dismantle both—is vital. The strongest part of this piece is its refusal to let the narrative be hijacked by those who would rather destroy a critic than understand their method; its biggest vulnerability lies in the difficulty of convincing a movement in pain that engagement with monsters is not the same as endorsing them.
