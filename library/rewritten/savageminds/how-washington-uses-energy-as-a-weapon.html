Michael Hudson delivers a chilling diagnosis of modern geopolitics: the United States no longer leads by offering prosperity, but by threatening to cut off the energy that powers the world. In a piece that reads less like standard analysis and more like an autopsy of a dying order, Hudson argues that Washington has transformed oil from a commodity into a weapon of mass coercion, targeting nations that dare to trade outside the dollar's orbit. For busy leaders tracking the next decade's flashpoints, this framing is essential because it explains why economic sanctions are escalating into direct military threats, not just as policy errors, but as calculated necessities for a superpower with no other tools left.

The Weaponization of Energy

Hudson's central thesis is that the US has lost its ability to attract allies through economic growth, forcing it to rely on "destructive leverage." He writes, "The overriding aim of today's US diplomacy... is to block the withdrawal of countries from the US-controlled world economy and frustrate the emergence of a Eurasian-centered economic grouping." This is a stark departure from the post-WWII era when American power was backed by industrial might and the promise of reconstruction. Today, as Hudson notes, the administration's strategy is defined by what it can take away rather than what it can give.

The author points to the deliberate strangling of European industry as a case study in this new ruthlessness. He observes that "Germany's deindustrialization and loss of competitiveness since its oil and gas trade with Russia was blocked was the sacrifice demanded of it... by the United States." This argument reframes the energy crisis not as a tragic byproduct of war, but as a strategic asset for Washington. Critics might argue that this view oversimplifies the complex security concerns driving European allies, yet the economic data on European manufacturing decline supports Hudson's grim assessment of the costs incurred to maintain US hegemony.

"The US foreign policy of creating choke points to keep other countries dependent on oil under US control... is one of America's key means of making other countries insecure."

The Erosion of International Law

The piece takes a darker turn when Hudson examines how these economic choke points have evolved into direct military aggression. He argues that the "unwritten US rules-based order" has effectively replaced the UN Charter, prioritizing American security needs over the sovereignty of other nations. Hudson writes, "America's military attack on Venezuela and abduction of President Maduro is the most recent example of this." While the article presents these actions as established facts, it is worth noting that the specific details of a 2026 abduction remain speculative in the current timeline, serving here as a narrative device to illustrate the author's fear of unchecked executive power.

Hudson highlights the administration's justification for such actions, quoting advisor Stephen Miller: "We live in a world, in the real world... that is governed by strength, that is governed by force, that is governed by power." This admission, according to Hudson, strips away the diplomatic veneer of international law. The author suggests that the US now operates on a principle of "Might Makes Right," where the threat of force is the primary currency of diplomacy. This framing forces the reader to confront the uncomfortable reality that the institutions designed to prevent war are being systematically dismantled by the very power that helped build them.

The Petrodollar's Last Stand

At the heart of Hudson's analysis is the fragility of the petrodollar system. He explains that since 1974, the US has demanded that oil-exporting nations recycle their earnings into US Treasury securities, a deal he describes as being enforced by the threat of war. "The result was creation of the petrodollar market that became a pillar of the US balance of payments and hence of the dollar's strength," Hudson writes. Now, as nations like Venezuela and China explore pricing oil in non-dollar currencies, the administration views this not as a market evolution, but as an existential threat to the American financial system.

The author details how the administration is responding to this challenge by demanding "priority privileges for itself in world trade in vital raw materials." He cites a recent Energy Department announcement where proceeds from Venezuelan oil sales must "settle in US controlled accounts... at the discretion of the Trump administration." This level of direct control over foreign sovereign assets marks a significant escalation. Hudson argues that this is the logical conclusion of a system where the US can no longer compete on merit and must instead rely on coercion to maintain its financial dominance.

"It is the US rules that serve as the operative law to which other countries are subject, at least those in the US trade, financial and military orbit."

Bottom Line

Michael Hudson's most compelling contribution is his unflinching assertion that the US has shifted from a provider of global public goods to a predator of the global economy. The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its deterministic view of US decline, potentially underestimating the resilience of the dollar or the willingness of allies to absorb short-term pain for long-term security. However, the piece succeeds in forcing a critical question: if the US must now threaten its friends and enemies alike to sustain its financial system, how much longer can that system survive? The reader must watch for whether the administration's aggressive posture will ultimately fracture the very alliances it claims to protect.
