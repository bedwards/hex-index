Dan Snow's conversation with historian Dr. Eleanor Janega dismantles the modern, Hollywood-style notion of the ghost as a tragic victim seeking vengeance. Instead, they reveal a medieval afterlife where the dead are not passive spirits but active, demanding creditors of the soul, returning to fix specific moral and financial debts. This distinction offers a startling window into a society where the boundary between the living and the dead was porous, and where the afterlife was less a destination and more a bureaucratic waiting room for purification.

The Demanding Dead

The core of Janega's argument rests on a fundamental shift in how ghosts functioned socially. In our modern imagination, a ghost is usually a victim of violence who needs their story told. Janega flips this script entirely. "The way that we tend to think about ghosts now is that they're kind of like tragic spirits that are floating around usually because something's gone really wrong," she explains, contrasting this with the medieval view where a ghost's presence signals a "break in the order of how things are meant to be." This framing is crucial because it transforms the ghost from a horror trope into a moral agent. The dead do not haunt because they are sad; they haunt because they are stuck.

This dynamic turns every encounter into a transaction. Janega notes that for medieval people, if a spirit appeared, it was "usually in hell and they need your help." The ghost's goal is rarely to scare the living for sport, but to secure a release from purgatory or to correct a specific injustice. "Medieval ghosts have done something wrong and they want something from you," Janega states, a sharp departure from the modern trope where the ghost simply wants acknowledgment. This reframing suggests that medieval anxiety was not about the fear of death itself, but the fear of unresolved social and spiritual obligations.

"If you've shown up, then there's been some kind of a break in the order of how things are meant to be."

The evidence Janega brings to light is particularly striking when she discusses the specific demands of these spirits. They are not asking for revenge; they are asking for restitution. In one case, a ghost in a "Harley Quinn haunt" (a parade of the dead) demands that his family return a mill they had unfairly profited from. "Please, please, can you go tell my wife and children to go return that mill?" the ghost pleads. This detail is vital because it reveals that medieval morality extended into the economic sphere. Unfair profit was not just a sin; it was a spiritual hazard that could trap a soul in hell. Critics might argue that focusing on these moral tales ignores the chaotic, non-religious folklore of the peasantry, but Janega counters that the surviving records are inherently filtered through the church, which naturally prioritized these moral lessons.

Shapes of Fear and Gendered Warnings

The visual language of these ghosts also defies modern expectations. Janega highlights the story of "Snowball the Taylor," a ghost who shifts forms from a raven to a dog, and mentions even stranger apparitions like "haunted haystacks" or wheels. "I like how many times the ghost changes shape," Janega admits, noting that the fluidity of these forms reflects a world where the supernatural was deeply integrated into the mundane landscape. The horror was not in the uncanny valley of a human-like specter, but in the violation of the natural order itself.

Perhaps the most potent example of these stories serving as social control comes from the legend of Guinevere's mother. In this Arthurian tale, a grotesque figure appears with "frogs that are suckling on her breasts," revealing herself as a woman punished for vanity and infidelity. Janega points out that this story serves a specific function: "It's kind of helping to underline specific gender norms." The ghost is not just a warning about sin in general, but a targeted admonition against women who engaged in courtly love or prioritized appearance over piety. "Courtly love is all well and good to write about and sing about and think about, but don't you dare do it, otherwise this is what happens," she summarizes. This suggests that ghost stories were a primary vehicle for enforcing social boundaries, particularly regarding female behavior, in a way that sermons alone could not achieve.

"If you take advantage of those [financial transactions], then that's something that can land you in hell as well."

Janega also addresses the nature of the sources themselves, acknowledging that most stories we have were written by monks who had a vested interest in moralizing. She cites the autobiography of Holy Roman Emperor Charles IV, who casually mentions a ghost smashing a cup before returning to a discussion of battles. "There's no moral there at all," Janega observes, noting that such anecdotes are rare because "monks are going to like religious it up just the stitch." This admission adds a layer of nuance to the argument: while the surviving texts are morality tales, the underlying belief in the supernatural was likely more chaotic and less didactic in the daily lives of the illiterate majority.

The Propaganda of Decay

The conversation concludes with a look at "transit tombs," monuments that depicted the deceased in two states: alive in their finery and dead as a rotting skeleton. Janega interprets these not as humble reflections on mortality, but as "a form of propaganda." The wealthy and powerful used these tombs to project an image of piety while simultaneously celebrating their status. "You don't make a big grand tomb in a cathedral for no reason," she argues. The juxtaposition of the rotting corpse with the rich effigy was a calculated move to suggest that despite their wealth, they understood the ultimate equality of death, all while ensuring their name and image were immortalized in stone. This duality reveals a society deeply concerned with how they would be remembered, using the imagery of decay to sanitize their accumulation of power.

Bottom Line

Dan Snow and Dr. Eleanor Janega successfully argue that medieval ghosts were not merely spooky figures but essential tools for maintaining social, economic, and gender norms. The strongest part of their coverage is the revelation that these spirits were active agents demanding restitution, turning the afterlife into a realm of bureaucratic accountability. However, the argument relies heavily on texts produced by the church, which may have sanitized the more chaotic, non-moralistic folklore of the common people. Readers should watch for how these moral frameworks evolved as the church's grip on narrative power began to loosen in the later Middle Ages.
