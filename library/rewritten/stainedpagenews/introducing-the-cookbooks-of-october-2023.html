Paula Forbes doesn't just list cookbooks; she curates a cultural shift, arguing that October 2023 marks a departure from generic lists toward a "much more heavily-curated" selection of substantial, encyclopedic works. She challenges the busy reader to make room on their shelves for books that demand attention, framing this month's releases not as mere recipe collections but as definitive treatises on specific cuisines and techniques. This is not a casual glance at the bestsellers; it is a call to invest in the deep, often overlooked corners of global food history.

The Era of the "Large Book"

Forbes identifies a striking trend: the return of the massive, comprehensive volume. She notes that the month is defined by "a lot of LARGE BOOKS," ranging from Sohla El-Waylly's beginner guide to encyclopedic tomes on Latin American and Korean cooking. This framing suggests a market correction where readers are seeking depth over the bite-sized content that dominated recent years. The sheer physical weight of these books serves as a metaphor for the seriousness of the culinary exploration they offer.

She highlights specific examples to prove her point, pointing to Sandra A. Gutierrez's Latinísimo, which packs "five hundred and ninety two pages packed with recipes from every single Latin American country." Forbes argues that organizing by ingredient rather than nation allows readers to see "how beans are treated across the continent, or corn, or quinoa." This structural choice is significant; it moves beyond a tourist's view of a region to a granular understanding of its agricultural and culinary DNA. It forces the cook to engage with the common threads that bind diverse cultures together.

"No catch-all listicle here, these are books I 100% think you should get excited about."

The emphasis on volume is not just about page count but about authority. Forbes points to the release of The Korean Cookbook by Junghyun "JP" Park and Jungyoon Choi, which they are calling "the first comprehensive book on Korean home cooking." While Forbes playfully notes that this claim might invite a response from established figures like Maangchi, she maintains that the ambition of the project is what matters. The argument here is that the field is maturing, moving from introductory guides to definitive, scholarly works that respect the complexity of the cuisine.

Critics might note that the sheer size of these books can be intimidating for the home cook, potentially creating a barrier to entry for those looking for quick weeknight solutions. However, Forbes counters this by suggesting that the investment is in the long game of culinary education, where the book becomes a reference library rather than a one-time read.

Beyond the Plate: Heritage and Identity

Forbes goes beyond the ingredients to explore the human stories behind the recipes, emphasizing how these books serve as vessels for cultural preservation and personal narrative. She highlights Chef Loretta Barrett Oden's Corn Dance, which draws on her childhood in Oklahoma and her journey as a chef to tell the story of Indigenous cuisine. This is not just a cookbook; it is a historical record of a people and their relationship with the land.

She also points to the personal nature of these releases, noting that Monica Lee's Sohn-mat is a "master class in making the iconic Korean stew, soondubu jjigae," born from "decades and the trial and error that went into creating the version of this dish that patrons of Beverly Soon Tofu loved so much." Forbes argues that this depth of experience is what elevates these books above standard recipe collections. They are the result of a lifetime of practice and a deep connection to community.

"The book is a master class in making the iconic Korean stew, soondubu jjigae, with recipes for everything to serve alongside it."

The coverage also touches on the evolution of specific culinary traditions, such as Michał Korkosz's Polish'd, which explores "modern vegetarian Polish food." Forbes contrasts this with the stereotype that "Eastern European food often gets pegged as being very heavy," showing how the new generation of chefs is reimagining their heritage. This reframing is crucial; it challenges the reader's preconceived notions and invites them to see these cuisines through a contemporary, sustainable lens.

The Technical Renaissance

A significant portion of Forbes' commentary is dedicated to the technical mastery being showcased in these new releases. She celebrates the return of the "fish book" with Josh Niland's Fish Butchery, noting that "everyone loves the fish books" because they offer a level of expertise rarely found in home cooking guides. The inclusion of "fish bits on a cover" is presented not as a gimmick, but as a badge of honor for those willing to engage with the whole animal.

Similarly, she highlights Danny Childs' Slow Drinks for its focus on "seasonal approaches to foraging, fermenting, and infusing your way into truly unique concoctions." Forbes argues that these books are pushing the boundaries of what a home cook can achieve, moving beyond simple mixing to a deeper understanding of flavor chemistry and ingredient sourcing. The inclusion of books like The Encyclopedia of Cocktails by Robert Simonson, described as a "witty and opinionated presentation of the bar world," further underscores this trend toward specialized, high-level knowledge.

"I am very, very particular about my chocolate chip cookies, and I keep thinking that at some point I will perfect my version."

Even in the realm of comfort food, Forbes finds a narrative of perfectionism and dedication. She discusses Katie Jacobs' The Chocolate Chip Cookie Book, which offers over 100 variations, framing it as a sign that "this winter is my winter" for those seeking to master the art of the cookie. This personal connection to the food is a recurring theme; the books are not just instructions, but invitations to a lifelong pursuit of culinary excellence.

Bottom Line

Paula Forbes successfully reframes the October cookbook landscape as a moment of serious, encyclopedic ambition, moving away from fleeting trends to enduring resources. Her strongest argument lies in her ability to connect the physical heft of these books to the depth of cultural and technical knowledge they contain. The only vulnerability in her curation is the potential exclusivity of these massive volumes, which may feel daunting to the casual cook, but her enthusiasm for the depth of content suggests that the reward is well worth the investment.
