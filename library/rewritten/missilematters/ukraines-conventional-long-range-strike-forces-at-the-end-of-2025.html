Fabian Hoffmann delivers a sobering inventory of a war that has become a laboratory for autonomous destruction, arguing that by the end of 2025, Ukraine's ability to strike deep is no longer just about Western aid, but a precarious race to industrialize its own heavy weaponry. The piece stands out not for predicting victory, but for mapping the grim arithmetic of attrition: a shift from imported heavy missiles to a bloated fleet of light, one-way drones that can reach deep but struggle to destroy hardened targets. This is a critical read for understanding how modern conflicts are evolving from high-tech precision to mass-produced, low-cost saturation.

The Weight of the Payload

Hoffmann's central thesis rests on a stark categorization of Ukraine's arsenal into heavy, medium, and light effectors, a framework that immediately reveals the strategic gap Ukraine faces. He writes, "The vast majority of Ukraine's conventional long-range strike weapons will fall in the light category," noting that while manufacturers have diversified, they "still struggle to integrate payloads of 100 kilograms or more." This distinction is vital because it dictates the difference between a nuisance strike and a strategic blow. Light effectors, carrying under 100 kilograms, are cheap and easy to produce, but Hoffmann points out their fatal flaw: "The small lethal radius often makes it difficult to compensate for terminal inaccuracies."

This analysis forces a confrontation with the reality of "precision" in a war of attrition. When a drone with a 50-kilogram warhead misses a hardened bunker by even a few meters, the result is often a crater in a civilian neighborhood rather than a disabled command center. Hoffmann's data suggests that Ukraine is flooding the zone with light drones not because they are the ideal weapon, but because they are the only ones they can make in volume. Critics might note that this focus on payload weight underestimates the psychological and logistical toll of constant, low-level strikes, which can paralyze an economy just as effectively as a single massive blow.

"Given potentially growing pressure from the United States to accept an unfavorable negotiated settlement, these missile systems can provide a major source of independent strategic leverage."

This sentence captures the high-stakes geopolitical subtext of the technical review. Hoffmann argues that indigenous heavy missiles are not just military tools but diplomatic shields, allowing Kyiv to negotiate from a position of strength rather than desperation. However, the evidence he presents suggests this leverage is currently theoretical. The heavy category relies heavily on Western stockpiles that are dwindling, while domestic heavy programs face "uncertainties" and production bottlenecks.

The Heavy Hitters and the Long Shadow

When discussing the heavy missiles, Hoffmann provides a granular look at the systems that actually threaten strategic infrastructure. He details the ATACMS, noting that "Ukraine is unlikely to retain a substantial reserve stockpile" by the end of 2025, as most rounds have been expended. This creates a dangerous dependency. The article highlights the FP-5 "Flamingo," a domestic land-attack cruise missile with a massive 1,150-kilogram warhead and a range of 3,000 kilometers. Yet, Hoffmann tempers the optimism, observing that despite ambitious claims, "use of the missile has so far not been widely reported."

The reliance on imported systems like the Storm Shadow and SCALP-EG is equally precarious. Hoffmann notes that these missiles, manufactured by MBDA, feature a robust guidance suite combining inertial navigation, satellite guidance, and terrain-contour matching (TERCOM) to navigate without GPS. This technology, which dates back to the Cold War era of Tomahawk development, remains the gold standard for hitting hardened targets. However, he warns that "European partner stocks are largely exhausted." The human cost of this depletion is implicit but heavy: as the heavy missiles run out, the burden shifts to the lighter, less accurate drones, increasing the risk of collateral damage against the very cities they aim to defend.

"Ukrainian manufacturers have produced a diverse range of systems capable of striking deep, but they still struggle to integrate payloads of 100 kilograms or more."

This limitation defines the current trajectory of the conflict. The "Bulged" Neptune and the Sapsan ballistic missile represent attempts to bridge this gap, but Hoffmann describes their status as "highly uncertain." The Sapsan, for instance, has moved to serial production on paper, but the reality of scaling a complex ballistic missile program in a war zone is fraught with delays.

The Drone Deluge

The most striking section of Hoffmann's analysis is the catalog of light effectors and long-range drones. He lists a dizzying array of systems: the AQ-400 Scythe, the AN-196 Liutyi, the Banshee, the Bars, and the modified two-seater aircraft. These systems represent a shift in warfare where the unit cost is low, but the volume is high. Hoffmann describes the Banshee as a "weaponized version of target drones," and the Dart 250 as a system that can locate targets even in jammed environments.

The sheer variety of these systems—from the British-made Banshee to the Ukrainian Morok, developed by volunteer engineers and charity fundraising—illustrates a decentralized innovation ecosystem. However, Hoffmann remains skeptical about their strategic impact against hardened targets. He notes that for many of these, "integration of a terminal guidance seeker has not been reported." Without a seeker to correct the final approach, these weapons rely on pre-programmed coordinates, making them vulnerable to GPS jamming and prone to missing their mark.

"These systems are relatively easy to produce and affordable."

This affordability comes at a price. The proliferation of these light drones suggests a future where the skies are choked with low-flying threats, forcing defenders to expend expensive interceptors on cheap targets. It also raises the specter of indiscriminate strikes. When a drone with a 25-kilogram payload misses a military depot, it often hits a school or a hospital. Hoffmann's technical focus on range and payload inadvertently highlights the humanitarian tragedy: the more these systems are used, the more likely the war is to bleed into civilian spaces.

A counterargument worth considering is that Hoffmann's focus on "hardened targets" may overlook the strategic value of these light drones in disrupting logistics and morale. Even if they cannot destroy a bunker, a swarm of them can force an enemy to halt movements, repair infrastructure constantly, and divert air defense resources. Yet, this tactical utility does not negate the strategic reality that Ukraine cannot yet match the industrial output of its adversary in the heavy category.

Bottom Line

Fabian Hoffmann's analysis is a masterclass in separating hype from reality, revealing that Ukraine's long-range strike capability is a patchwork of dwindling Western stockpiles and ambitious but unproven domestic projects. The strongest part of his argument is the clear link between payload capacity and strategic leverage: without heavy missiles, Ukraine's ability to shape the war's outcome remains constrained. The biggest vulnerability in this trajectory is the timeline; the "uncertainties" surrounding the Sapsan and Flamingo could leave a dangerous gap in 2026 just as diplomatic pressure mounts. Readers should watch not just for new missile launches, but for the industrial capacity to actually produce them in the numbers required to matter.
