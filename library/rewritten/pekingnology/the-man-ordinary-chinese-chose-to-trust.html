Zichen Wang delivers a piercing cultural autopsy, arguing that the massive mourning for education influencer Zhang Xuefeng was not merely grief, but a collective verdict by ordinary Chinese citizens against a system of elite hypocrisy. The piece's power lies in its refusal to treat Zhang as a mere internet personality, instead framing his blunt, often vulgar advice as the only honest currency left for families navigating a rigged educational landscape.

The Verdict of the Ordinary

Wang opens with a striking observation about the funeral in Suzhou, where thousands lined up to bid farewell to a man who died suddenly from cardiac arrest. "As the Chinese saying goes, the people see with clear eyes," Wang writes, noting that in an era where the powerful expose themselves as "self-serving mediocrities," the public has not lost its judgment. The author suggests that the outpouring of grief was a direct rejection of the sanctimonious elites who speak of virtue while protecting privilege. This framing is compelling because it shifts the narrative from a simple celebrity death to a sociological event; the mourning was a "verdict delivered by countless ordinary Chinese on a man they felt had spoken to them, stood with them, and never pretended to be above them."

The argument gains depth when Wang contextualizes Zhang's rise against the brutal realities of the Gaokao (National Higher Education Entrance Examination). The author points out that while China is a unitary state, its university admissions are deeply fractured by geography. Wang cites a stark statistic: in 2022, Tsinghua and Peking University admitted 550 students from Beijing's 54,000 candidates, but only 424 from Henan, a province with 1.2 million candidates. "Numbers do not lie: if you grow up in the wrong province, your chance of getting into China's indisputably top two universities is at least 25 times more difficult," Wang asserts. This data is crucial; it explains why Zhang's pragmatic, almost cynical advice resonates so deeply. He isn't offering inspiration; he is offering survival tactics in a system where the odds are mathematically stacked against the non-elite.

"In an era when the rich and the powerful, in China and beyond, keep shattering expectations and exposing themselves as little more than self-serving mediocrities, ordinary people have not lost their judgment."

The Pragmatism of Survival

Wang highlights Zhang's controversial stance on career choices, specifically his declaration that he would "knock him out" if a child insisted on majoring in journalism. This wasn't just a soundbite; it was a reflection of the "anxious middle class caught in the middle of the rat race." Wang paraphrases Zhang's rebuttal to a university professor who defended the versatility of journalism: "If you come from a well-off background, you have more choices... But for most families, conditions are not that favorable, so you should choose a major that suits you and can secure your future livelihood." The author effectively argues that Zhang's vulgarity was a feature, not a bug; it stripped away the polite fictions that the elite use to maintain their status.

The commentary then dives into a transcript of Zhang's pep talk, where he contrasts the difficulty of studying with the horrors of the workplace. Zhang argues that "studying is one of the simplest things you will encounter in your entire life," because the workforce demands you "tolerate an irritating colleague" or "dine with nauseous middle-aged men" to secure a deal. Wang uses this to illustrate the specific pressure facing non-elite undergraduates, for whom a degree from a prestigious university is the "best - if not the last - hope to climb the ladder." This section is particularly effective because it validates the reader's exhaustion; it confirms that the struggle isn't just about grades, but about navigating a social hierarchy that demands constant, humiliating compromise.

Critics might note that Zhang's advice is deeply cynical, potentially discouraging intellectual curiosity in favor of pure transactional survival. However, Wang suggests this cynicism is a rational response to a slowing economy where "the best Chinese companies commonly exclude those outside prestigious universities." The author's coverage implies that in such a climate, idealism is a luxury the poor cannot afford.

The Illusion of Home

The piece takes a poignant turn as Wang recounts Zhang's personal story of moving to Beijing. Despite owning an apartment near tech giants like Baidu and Tencent, Zhang confessed, "I have never felt like Beijing is my home." Wang details the logistical nightmare of living on the outskirts, where a taxi to the airport costs a staggering 450 yuan, forcing Zhang to spend thousands monthly on ride-hailing services. "Every time I return to Beijing, my thought isn't, 'I'm coming home.' Instead, it's, 'How am I going to get home?'" Wang writes. This anecdote humanizes the abstract statistics of urban inequality, showing that even a successful influencer with seven million followers feels the alienation of the city's economic machinery.

Wang connects this to Zhang's motivation: a phone call from a girlfriend's father who dismissed him as a "poor guy from Northeast China" and vowed to "tear you two apart." This personal slight fueled Zhang's drive, leading him to declare, "Damn it, I'm going to venture into Beijing." The author uses this to explain the raw, emotional core of Zhang's appeal. He wasn't selling a dream; he was selling the grit required to survive the rejection of the elite. Wang notes that Zhang defined "financial independence" not as having money to do whatever one wants, but as having "so much money that you can choose not to do things you don't want to do." This redefinition strikes a chord because it acknowledges that for most, freedom is the ability to say "no" to the indignities of the workplace.

"Studying is much simpler... You have no choice but to drink even though you cannot drink anymore. Which one do you think is more challenging?"

Bottom Line

Wang's strongest asset is the ability to link Zhang's blunt, often offensive rhetoric to a systemic failure in Chinese education and social mobility, proving that the public's embrace of Zhang was a rejection of elite hypocrisy. The piece's vulnerability lies in its reliance on Zhang's personal narrative as the primary lens for a complex national issue, potentially oversimplifying the diverse struggles of the Chinese working class. Readers should watch for how this "verdict" translates into policy or social change, or if it remains a fleeting moment of catharsis in an unyielding system.
