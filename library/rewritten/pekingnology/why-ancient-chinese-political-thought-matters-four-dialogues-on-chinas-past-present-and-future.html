Zichen Wang introduces a provocative premise: the future of Chinese foreign policy and domestic governance may hinge not on modern ideology, but on debates held over two millennia ago. By framing Daniel A. Bell's new book as a bridge between ancient philosophy and contemporary crises, Wang suggests that understanding the "Warring States" period is no longer just an academic exercise, but a strategic necessity for anyone trying to decode Beijing's current moves.

The Method of Serious Play

Wang highlights Bell's unique structural choice: placing the descendants of ancient philosophers in modern settings to debate current issues. This is not a dry history lesson; it is a creative reconstruction. "Bell makes China's ancient debates feel alive, witty, and politically urgent," Wang notes, quoting Alexandre Lefebvre. The author argues that this approach is essential because the original texts often lack the narrative structure Western readers expect. Instead, Bell organizes these ideas into "lively dialogue," providing the "lucidity and coherence" that the raw classics sometimes lack.

This framing is effective because it bypasses the intimidation factor of classical Chinese texts. By using fictitious descendants, Bell allows the ancient arguments to breathe in a modern context without claiming to be a literal translation. As Roger T. Ames observes, Bell furnishes these early thinkers with a "stage on which to debate both their own theories and the contemporary issues that he would have them answer." The result is what Anna Sun calls "serio ludere," or serious play, where drinks are poured and jokes are exchanged even as the fate of nations is discussed.

Ancient political thought matters because it grapples with questions about human society—loyalty, power, culture, and justice—that remain urgent for rethinking the political today.

The Four Enduring Debates

The commentary details how Bell structures the book around four specific conflicts that define Chinese political thought. First is the tension between harmony and freedom, illustrated by a dialogue between a descendant of Confucius and a Daoist in a Hong Kong bar. They argue over whether family obligations should be legally enforced. Wang explains that while the Confucian argues for the law's role in promoting filial piety, the Daoist remains skeptical of state interference in personal life. This sets up a fundamental question: can a society be both harmonious and free?

Second, the text tackles corruption through the lens of Legalism versus Confucian moralism. A descendant of Han Feizi argues that only "harsh legal punishments" can curb corruption, while a follower of Xunzi insists that social rituals and community belonging are the only sustainable solutions. Wang points out the emotional intensity of this exchange, noting that the dialogue is "heated and emotional," even imagining the student calling for the teacher's elimination before they share a banquet. This dramatization underscores the deep, unresolved friction in Chinese governance between rule by law and rule by virtue.

The third debate concerns the allocation of resources: material welfare versus cultural flourishing. In a rural setting, a Mohist descendant argues for prioritizing the basic needs of the poor, while a music scholar defends state funding for the arts as essential for human flourishing. Wang writes that these debates are "set in different parts of China in the near future," making the ancient arguments feel like immediate policy proposals. Critics might note that reducing complex philosophical traditions to binary debates risks oversimplifying the nuances of each school, but Bell's goal is to highlight the tension rather than resolve it.

Why This Matters Now

Wang emphasizes that these are not merely historical curiosities. The author writes, "China's political thinking is rooted in its past," and argues that these ancient frameworks serve as "background assumptions and ideals" for modern officials. The book suggests that to understand whether China will use military force on Taiwan or how it will handle internal dissent, one must understand the ancient debates on realism versus idealism in international relations.

The argument holds weight because it challenges the Western tendency to view Chinese politics solely through the lens of communism or modern authoritarianism. As Baogang He puts it, Bell is "transcending the boundaries of Western theoretical traditions and bridging cultural and intellectual traditions." By showing that these debates "recur in different forms over the next two millennia," Wang implies that the future of China's political trajectory is already written in the dust of the Warring States period.

Bottom Line

Wang effectively positions Bell's work as an essential key to unlocking the logic of modern China, arguing that the country's leaders are still wrestling with questions posed by Confucius and Han Feizi. The strongest element is the demonstration that ancient philosophy is not dead history but a living, breathing framework for current policy. However, the approach relies heavily on the reader accepting the premise that modern Chinese officials consciously reference these specific ancient texts, a link that, while plausible, remains difficult to empirically verify in every instance.
