Zichen Wang delivers a counterintuitive diagnosis of China's social policy: the failure to deliver a major pension hike for farmers in 2026 is not a defeat, but the clearest sign yet that the political ground has shifted beneath the state's feet. While the government's 20-yuan increase looks like a meager concession, Wang argues it masks a seismic change in public discourse where the moral and economic case for rural justice has finally moved from the fringe to the center of elite debate.

The Politics of the Meager Increase

Wang begins by dismantling the assumption that policy stagnation equals public apathy. He notes that despite intense pressure during the Two Sessions, the 15th Five-Year Plan and the Government Work Report stopped short of a substantial increase. "That is striking because calls to raise pensions for urban and rural residents became arguably the biggest social-policy story of this year's Two Sessions," Wang writes. The core of his argument is that the dual-track pension system has created a moral debt that can no longer be ignored. He highlights the stark disparity: in 2024, the average rural pension was just $35, while private sector retirees received $548. This gap is not merely economic; it is historical. Wang reminds us that today's rural elderly are the generation that built the infrastructure for China's modernization, yet they remain at the bottom of the hierarchy.

This framing is powerful because it reframes the issue from a request for charity to a demand for "historical justice." However, a counterargument worth considering is whether the state's reluctance stems from fiscal constraints rather than a lack of political will, especially given the broader economic slowdown. Wang acknowledges the lack of immediate results but insists the campaign has entered a new stage where social awareness is outpacing political action.

"The issue was everywhere across the internet. More importantly, the terms of the debate have changed. A 20-yuan increase is no longer an amount that can plausibly be defended."

The Consensus Among Elites

The most compelling evidence Wang offers is the sudden alignment of China's most powerful economists and former regulators. He details how figures like Liu Shijin, a former Deputy Director of the Development Research Centre of the State Council, and Guo Shuqing, a former chairman of the China Securities Regulatory Commission, have publicly advocated for specific targets, such as raising pensions to 620 yuan within three years. Wang observes that "economists were, in fact, the single most important group pushing for higher farmers' pensions in 2025." Their argument is pragmatic: boosting rural pensions is the most effective way to unlock domestic consumption, a critical need for the Chinese economy.

This shift from moral pleading to economic necessity is a brilliant strategic pivot. By linking rural welfare to the broader goal of stimulating demand, these experts have made the issue harder for the state to dismiss as mere sentimentality. Wang notes that even media outlets like Caixin and Beijing News have moved from accepting small increases to demanding substantial ones, signaling that the "small but quick steps" narrative is dead.

"In his view, the single most important reform for the next five years is to raise pension income for urban and rural residents."

The Snowball Effect of Public Opinion

Wang credits ordinary citizens for turning this into a defining issue, citing the viral backlash against social security expert Zheng Gongcheng's comment that pensions should help the poor, not the secure. This remark, which initially seemed to defend the status quo, instead triggered a massive wave of discussion that forced Zheng to reverse his stance and advocate for substantial raises. Wang describes this as a "virtuous cycle" where public amplification compels influential figures to speak out.

He draws a parallel to the historical weight of the rural contribution, implicitly echoing the legacy of the People's Commune era, where rural labor was the engine of early industrialization but was systematically under-compensated through the Hukou system. Wang argues that the current public sentiment is finally correcting this decades-long imbalance. The sheer volume of online engagement suggests that the state can no longer treat this as a marginal issue without risking significant social friction.

"The issue gathers momentum like a snowball rolling downhill. That is how the question of farmers' pensions came to look like the single defining issue of this year's Two Sessions."

Bottom Line

Wang's most striking insight is that the absence of a policy breakthrough is actually proof of the movement's success; the debate has matured from asking "if" to arguing "how much." The argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its optimism regarding the state's willingness to act on this consensus, given the tight fiscal realities of the Chinese economy. Readers should watch whether the economic arguments for consumption will finally outweigh the political costs of redistribution in the coming Five-Year Plan.

"The seeds have already been planted. They have already begun to take root and sprout. Looking across the fields now, they are already a sea of vibrant green."

Bottom Line

Wang's core thesis is that the political window has opened, even if the door hasn't fully swung open yet. His strongest evidence is the unprecedented alignment of top economists and regulators, who have successfully reframed rural pensions as an economic imperative rather than a charitable burden. The piece's biggest risk is assuming that public pressure alone will force the state's hand in a system where fiscal caution often trumps social demands. The next critical test will be the specific funding mechanisms proposed in the 15th Five-Year Plan.
