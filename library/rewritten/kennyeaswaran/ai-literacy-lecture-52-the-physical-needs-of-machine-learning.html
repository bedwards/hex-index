Kenny Easwaran reframes the AI boom not as a software miracle, but as an industrial energy crisis with a global supply chain bottleneck. While most commentators obsess over model weights and algorithmic breakthroughs, Easwaran argues that the true constraint on artificial intelligence is physical: the sheer volume of electricity required to generate heat, the scarcity of clean power to cool it, and the geopolitical fragility of the chip supply chain. This is a vital correction to the narrative, forcing us to see data centers not as abstract clouds, but as massive, heat-generating factories competing with electric cars for the grid.

The Hidden Energy Tax

Easwaran begins by dismantling the assumption that digital services are cost-free. He notes that the business model has shifted from selling ads to selling user data, but the physical cost is even higher. "In this lecture I'm mainly going to be talking about the physical needs associated with machine learning," he states, grounding the discussion in reality rather than hype. He draws a parallel to the 1880s electricity wars, suggesting we are on the cusp of a similar inflection point where demand will outstrip supply. The author points out that while US electricity generation has leveled off since 2000, the rise of AI and electrification will force a dramatic reversal.

The argument gains traction when Easwaran highlights the inefficiency of cooling. He explains that for every watt used to compute, a significant fraction is wasted on heat management. "Back in 2007 it was nearly two and a half times as much electricity needed to run a data center as the amount that the computers themselves were using," he writes, noting that while efficiency has improved, the ratio remains a massive burden. This evidence is compelling because it quantifies the invisible cost of our digital convenience. The location of data centers, he argues, is no longer about proximity to talent but about proximity to cheap, clean power. He points to Iceland and Portland as prime examples where geothermal and hydroelectric power attract these massive clusters.

Critics might note that Easwaran's focus on current efficiency ratios could understate the rapid pace of hardware optimization, which might decouple compute growth from energy growth faster than anticipated. However, the sheer scale of the exponential demand he outlines makes this a risky bet.

The biggest distinctive new demand for neural net-based AI is computational power, and it is growing at a rate that defies historical trends.

The Exponential Gap

The core of Easwaran's analysis lies in the disconnect between hardware improvement and AI demand. He illustrates this with a stark logarithmic comparison: "GPT 4 released just over a year ago took about 100 times as much computation to train as gpt3 did trained in 2020 and gpt3 itself took nearly a million times as much computation as alexnet way back in 2012." This is the piece's most arresting statistic. It reveals that Moore's Law—the steady doubling of transistors—is being utterly overwhelmed by the exploding appetite of neural networks.

Easwaran traces the history of computing hardware to show how we arrived here. He reminds us that in 1950, computers used vacuum tubes and mercury acoustic memory, and by 1980, they had shifted to magnetic discs and silicon transistors. The pivotal shift, he argues, came with the Graphics Processing Unit (GPU). Originally designed for video games to handle parallel matrix multiplication for pixels, these chips found a new life in AI. "It turns out that the computations needed to train neural Nets are this same sort of thing," Easwaran explains, highlighting how a tool built for entertainment became the engine of the intelligence revolution.

This reframing of the GPU's origin story is effective because it demystifies the technology. It shows that AI's current dominance is partly a lucky accident of hardware specialization. However, the author also warns that this specialization creates a new dependency. The reliance on parallel processing means that general-purpose chips are no longer enough, creating a bottleneck that only specific architectures can solve.

The Geopolitical Supply Chain

The final and perhaps most critical section of Easwaran's commentary exposes the fragility of the AI supply chain. He details how a handful of companies control the entire ecosystem. "At present in 2024 these gpus are suddenly extremely essential to current generations of neural Nets and neural Nets are Central to AI but these gpus are mostly made by a single company NVIDIA," he writes. The market dominance is staggering, with NVIDIA holding 80% of the GPU market and recently surpassing major oil and tech giants in valuation.

But the bottleneck extends even further down the chain. Easwaran notes that NVIDIA and AMD do not manufacture their own chips; they rely on foundries like TSMC in Taiwan. The situation becomes even more concentrated at the equipment level. "Nearly 80% of the world's supply of this lithography equipment comes from a single Corporation asml based in the Netherlands," he observes. This creates a geopolitical choke point where the US, South Korea, Taiwan, Japan, and the Netherlands hold all the cards, while China is largely excluded.

A counterargument worth considering is that this concentration might spur innovation in alternative computing architectures, such as quantum computing or neuromorphic chips, which could bypass the need for traditional silicon lithography. Yet, Easwaran's point stands: for the foreseeable future, the future of AI is held hostage by a few factories in the Pacific Rim and a single Dutch machine shop. He concludes by questioning whether export controls will cause China to fall behind in AI development similar to the 1973 oil embargo, or if they will force a rapid, independent industrial buildup.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's strongest contribution is his ability to map the abstract world of algorithms onto the concrete realities of energy grids and semiconductor factories, proving that AI is as much a physical industry as it is a digital one. The argument's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on current hardware trajectories, which could be upended by unforeseen breakthroughs in chip architecture or energy efficiency. Readers should watch the next 12 months closely, as the tension between exploding AI demand and finite physical resources will likely define the next decade of technological progress.

The future of artificial intelligence is not just about better code; it is about who controls the electricity, the water, and the machines that make the chips.
