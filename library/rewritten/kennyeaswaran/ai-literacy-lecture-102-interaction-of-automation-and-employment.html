Kenny Easwaran challenges the dominant narrative that artificial intelligence will inevitably strip humanity of its economic purpose by reframing our very definition of work. Rather than offering a speculative forecast, he anchors the debate in historical paradoxes, arguing that efficiency often expands demand rather than extinguishing it. This is a crucial pivot for busy readers who assume the AI revolution will look like the extinction of the horse-drawn carriage, ignoring the complex ways technology has historically created new categories of labor.

The Industrial Paradox of Efficiency

Easwaran begins by dismantling the assumption that unemployment is a natural state of human existence. He writes, "the state of not being employed for wages outside the home is not by itself inherently problematic for the majority of human history." This is a vital reminder that our current anxiety stems from the specific conditions of the Industrial Revolution, where work became the sole source of both income and identity. By isolating this historical contingency, Easwaran prepares the reader to question whether AI will destroy work or simply force a redefinition of it.

The core of his argument rests on a counterintuitive economic principle known as the Jevons Paradox. He explains that when technology makes a resource cheaper to use, we don't necessarily use less of it; we often use more. As Easwaran puts it, "when consumption becomes more efficient often people start consuming more and then often the underlying good whose consumption became more efficient can sometimes go up." He illustrates this with the history of coal and steel: as coal became more efficient at producing steel, steel didn't disappear; it became cheap enough to use for spoons and forks, not just bridges. This historical evidence holds up well, suggesting that the fear of AI making labor "obsolete" might be a failure to imagine how cheap labor could unlock entirely new markets.

Efficiency did not mean that consumption went down but actually consumption went up.

Critics might note that this logic assumes infinite human desire, which may not hold if AI creates a ceiling on what goods are desirable. However, Easwaran's point is that we rarely predict these new desires until they are already here.

From Cotton Mills to Elevator Music

To test this theory, Easwaran moves from the textile industry to the music business, a sector often cited as a victim of automation. He details how the Industrial Revolution in textiles led to a paradoxical boom: "by the middle of the century there were over a million workers working in textile mills in Britain... when just as much work can be done with less coal we talk about it as efficiency while when just as much work can be done with less labor we usually talk about it as productivity." The result was not mass unemployment, but a society where everyone owned multiple suits of clothes and changed them daily.

He then applies this to the 20th-century musician. One might assume that recording technology, which allows a single performance to reach millions, would decimate the need for live bands. Yet, Easwaran notes, "the US Census was tracking the number of people employed as musicians... and we can see there was a general rough increasing Trend." The technology didn't kill the job; it created new niches. As he observes, "you would never have had a live band in the waiting room of a doctor's office if you had to hire all the musicians to play there but if you can just buy a recording you can do that." The demand for music exploded into advertising, background ambience, and video game soundtracks, absorbing the labor force even as individual productivity skyrocketed.

This is the most compelling part of the lecture: the idea that productivity gains are "basically always benefits to the consumer who can either consume more of the goods that are being produced or they can save their spending power for other goods and services." While the worker might face short-term disruption, the long-term trajectory often involves a shift in the type of work rather than a net loss of work.

The Baumol Effect and the Limits of Automation

However, Easwaran introduces a necessary caveat with the concept of Baumol's cost disease. He points out that not all sectors experience productivity gains. He writes, "the number of audience members that could fit into a concert hall to hear a live performance of a Beethoven string quartet remained the same... so there was Zero productivity increase for string quartets." While a factory can make a million shirts with fewer workers, a string quartet still requires four people to play for an hour. This creates a tension where high-productivity sectors subsidize low-productivity ones, potentially driving up the cost of human-centric services.

This distinction is critical for the AI debate. If AI automates high-productivity tasks (like coding or data analysis) but leaves low-productivity, human-centric tasks (like caregiving or live performance) untouched, we may see a divergence in wages and employment stability that the textile or music examples don't fully capture. A counterargument worth considering is that AI might eventually crack the code on "low productivity" tasks, perhaps by generating hyper-personalized content that feels human, thereby eroding the unique value of live performance.

The state of not having work has become conceptualized as a problem... both to the material quality of life and to their emotional or spiritual quality of life.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's strongest contribution is his insistence that the future of work is not a linear path to obsolescence but a complex negotiation between efficiency and new demand. His biggest vulnerability is the assumption that new industries will always emerge fast enough to absorb displaced workers, a transition that has historically been painful and unequal. Readers should watch for which sectors AI treats like the textile industry (expanding demand) versus which it treats like the string quartet (preserving human limits).
