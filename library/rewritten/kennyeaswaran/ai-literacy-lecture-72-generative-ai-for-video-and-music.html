Kenny Easwaran cuts through the hype of generative artificial intelligence by arguing that the real revolution isn't in creating a single prompt that conjures a masterpiece, but in the messy, multi-step orchestration of different AI models to mimic human creativity. While the public fixates on the promise of instant video generation, Easwaran offers a sobering reality check: the complexity of time and physics makes video and music fundamentally harder for machines to master than text or static images. This distinction is crucial for anyone trying to understand where the technology actually stands in August 2024 versus where the marketing suggests it is.

The Hierarchy of Artistic Complexity

Easwaran begins by mapping the landscape of the arts, noting that while large language models and diffusion models have already made inroads into literature and visual arts, they mostly serve as tools for prototyping rather than replacing human masters. He observes that "large language models and these diffusion models are producing things that might fit into these art forms at well above the level of the average novice human," yet they fall short of museum-quality work. This is a vital nuance; the technology is a powerful assistant, not a replacement. The author correctly identifies that physical arts like sculpture or tapestry remain largely untouched by generative AI, not because it's impossible, but because the physical act of creation is still a bottleneck.

However, Easwaran makes a provocative prediction about the future of consumption. He suggests that as digital art floods the market, the value of the physical object will surge. "I predict that one unexpected consequence of the rise of computer generated art will be a countervailing rise in the number of people who actually go to physical museums to look at physical art," he writes. This insight reframes the threat of AI from a destroyer of art to a potential catalyst for a renewed appreciation of the tangible. Critics might argue that this is wishful thinking, given that most people already consume art primarily through screens, but the psychological desire for authenticity in an age of infinite digital copies is a strong counter-force.

The Data and Dimensionality Gap

The core of Easwaran's argument rests on why video and music have lagged behind text and images. He identifies two massive hurdles: the sheer volume of training data and the inherent complexity of the medium. "Text and still images just are much simpler inherently than video and music that they are one or two dimensional structures as opposed to these three or four dimensional structures," he explains. The author illustrates that while a still image only needs pixels to align horizontally and vertically, video requires every pixel to make sense not just in space, but across time.

This temporal dimension creates a unique challenge. Easwaran notes that "making a sequence of them in a row that are all individually plausible two-dimensional representations of a real three-dimensional object and on top of it that is moving in a realistic way in a three-dimensional space that just introduces a whole extra layer of complexity." This is the crux of the difficulty. A single frame can be beautiful but physically impossible; a moving sequence must be beautiful, physically possible, and consistent with the laws of physics frame-by-frame. The scarcity of high-quality, labeled data for video and music compared to the endless stream of social media text and photos further exacerbates this gap.

Making a sequence of images that are individually plausible and move realistically in 3D space introduces a whole extra layer of complexity.

The Structured Approach vs. The Magic Prompt

Perhaps the most practical takeaway from Easwaran's lecture is his rejection of the "magic prompt" in favor of a structured, pipeline-based approach. He argues that the most effective current methods do not try to generate a finished movie from a single text prompt. Instead, they break the task down: "we could use one generative AI to generate a script... then use another sort of generative AI to simulate actors... then use a third AI to take that three-dimensional structure... and convert it into film." This modular strategy acknowledges that different aspects of creativity require different computational models.

He points out that while companies like OpenAI are teasing direct text-to-video tools like Sora, "as far as I know as of August 2024 there isn't really a significantly effective version of either of these things that's available for the public to use." The real work, he insists, is happening in the middle steps of the pipeline, such as text-to-speech synthesis and digital performance capture. This is where the technology is actually being used by professionals, not just in academic demos.

The Ethics of Likeness and the Hollywood Strikes

The discussion inevitably turns to the ethical implications of this technology, specifically the rise of "deep fakes." Easwaran defines these as videos that use deep neural networks to create "visually plausible fake video," often swapping the likeness of one person onto another's performance. He connects this directly to the 2023 Hollywood strikes, where actors fought for protections against their digital replicas being used without consent or compensation. "The Screen Actors Guild one of the things they specifically requested and were able to win in their strike was a guarantee that actors would be paid not just for the days that they are actually on set filming but also for the days that they would have been on set filming if their likeness is used by the studio to generate new scenes in the future," he writes.

This legal victory highlights a critical shift in the industry: the recognition that a human performance is a distinct asset that can be extracted and reused. Easwaran's coverage here is particularly timely, as it moves beyond the fear of AI replacing actors to the specific economic mechanics of how AI is being integrated into production. While the music industry faces similar challenges with voice cloning, the legal frameworks are still catching up, leaving a gray area where artists' voices can be mimicked without clear recourse.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's strongest contribution is his insistence on structural realism over magical thinking; he convincingly argues that the future of generative video and music lies in complex, multi-stage pipelines rather than single prompts. His biggest vulnerability is the rapid pace of change, as the landscape he describes in August 2024 could shift dramatically within months, potentially validating the "magic prompt" approach he currently dismisses. Readers should watch for how the legal battles over digital likeness play out, as these will define the economic viability of the very pipelines Easwaran describes.
