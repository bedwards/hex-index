Kenny Easwaran tackles a problem that often paralyzes scientists and philosophers alike: how do we quantify the moment evidence actually changes our minds? In this second installment of his analysis on Bayesianism, Easwaran moves beyond the abstract justification of probability to confront the messy reality of scientific inference, exposing a critical ambiguity in how we define "confirmation" that has confused thinkers for decades.

The Trap of Absolute Certainty

Easwaran begins by dismantling a common intuition about scientific support. He argues that we often confuse the result of an experiment with the value of the experiment. "One of the important early uses of probability theory in philosophy was in Rudolf Carnap's book The Logical Foundations of Probability," Easwaran writes, noting that Carnap sought a quantitative theory of how evidence supports a hypothesis. However, Easwaran points out that Carnap's framework suffered from a fatal flaw: it failed to distinguish between how confident we are in a belief and how much that belief has changed.

To illustrate this, Easwaran offers a strikingly modern example involving pregnancy tests. He describes a scenario where a test returns negative, leading to extreme confidence that one is not pregnant. "However, it seems that there was very little increase in firmness," he notes, because the person was already highly confident before the test. This distinction is crucial. If we only measure the final state of belief (firmness), a test that confirms what we already know looks just as powerful as a test that overturns a deep-seated assumption. Easwaran clarifies that true scientific confirmation must be measured by the incremental shift, not the static destination.

Critics might argue that in high-stakes fields like medicine, the absolute level of confidence (firmness) matters more than the incremental change. A doctor needs to know you are safe, not just how much safer you are than before. Yet, Easwaran's point holds for the philosophy of science: a theory that never updates is not a scientific theory.

"Are you paying attention to how much difference a piece of evidence makes, or are you paying attention to how confident you are after receiving the evidence?"

The Mechanics of Belief Update

Once the distinction between firmness and increase is established, Easwaran dives into the mathematical machinery that drives the update. He introduces the standard notation used throughout the field: $H$ for hypothesis and $E$ for evidence. The core mechanism is Bayes' Theorem, which dictates that a hypothesis is confirmed if and only if the probability of the evidence given the hypothesis is higher than the prior probability of the evidence itself. "The posterior is greater than the prior if and only if the likelihood is greater than the prior of the evidence," Easwaran explains.

This leads to a counterintuitive definition of a technical term that trips up even seasoned researchers. In everyday language, "likelihood" refers to how probable a hypothesis is. In Bayesian statistics, however, "likelihood" refers to how probable the evidence is given the hypothesis. "The word likelihood is used for probability of E given H," Easwaran states, warning that this is "confusing the ordinary word likelihood is used for how probable the thing in question is but in this sort of context likelihood always is about how probable the hypothesis makes the evidence not the hypothesis itself." This precise terminology is the bedrock of objective scientific agreement; scientists may disagree on their starting beliefs (priors), but they can often agree on the likelihoods—how well a specific hypothesis predicts a specific outcome.

The Crisis of Measurement

The most contentious part of Easwaran's analysis arises when he asks: how do we measure the amount of confirmation? The most obvious metric—the simple difference between the posterior and the prior—fails spectacularly in real-world scenarios. Easwaran uses his own experience with rapid COVID tests to demonstrate this failure. "I was already pretty confident that I wasn't sick on any of those days but I want to say that these tests gave me some significant evidence," he recounts. Yet, mathematically, if you start at 99% confidence, you can only move to 99.9%. The difference is negligible, yet the evidence felt substantial.

Because the "difference" measure makes it impossible to strongly confirm theories that are already well-supported, Easwaran highlights alternative metrics, such as the ratio of likelihoods or the difference in confidence between the hypothesis and its negation. "Many other measures other than this difference have been proposed disagreeing about the relative strengths of different confirmations," he observes. This reveals a deep fracture in the field: while everyone agrees on when confirmation happens, they cannot agree on how much it happened.

The Raven Paradox and the Limits of Logic

Easwaran concludes by applying these refined tools to one of philosophy's most famous puzzles: the paradox of the ravens. Classical logic suggests that observing a green apple (a non-black non-raven) confirms the hypothesis "all ravens are black." This feels absurd to human intuition. Easwaran notes that "a number of bayesians eventually showed that on their account neither of these confirmation relations necessarily holds," suggesting that the Bayesian framework can resolve the paradox by showing that the evidence provided by a green apple is so infinitesimally small that it effectively amounts to zero confirmation.

This resolution is elegant, but it relies heavily on the specific measure of confirmation chosen. If one uses a different metric, the paradox might resurface. As Easwaran hints, the choice of mathematical tool determines the philosophical outcome.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's strongest contribution here is the rigorous separation of "firmness" from "increase," a distinction that saves Bayesianism from the accusation that it merely rationalizes pre-existing biases. However, the piece leaves the reader with a lingering vulnerability: the lack of consensus on how to quantify that increase means that "confirmation" remains a fluid concept rather than a fixed standard. For the busy professional, the takeaway is clear: never trust a scientific claim based solely on the final confidence level; demand to see the delta, the actual shift the evidence forced upon the theory.
