This piece from Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol does something rare in modern political commentary: it refuses to treat the current administration's actions as mere policy disputes, framing them instead as a fundamental existential threat to the American experiment itself. By anchoring their argument in Thomas Paine's 1776 Common Sense, the authors force a stark choice between the rule of law and the rule of a single man, a framing that feels particularly urgent as the Supreme Court prepares to rule on the legality of massive global tariffs. For a listener navigating a fog of daily headlines, this is the clarity they need: the question is no longer about economic numbers, but whether the law is still the king of America.

The Paine Parallel

The authors open by invoking a historical moment that defined the very concept of American liberty. "Two-hundred fifty years ago tomorrow, on January 10, 1776, in Philadelphia, Thomas Paine published his pamphlet Common Sense," they write, noting that in proportion to the population, it may have had the largest circulation of any book in American history. This historical grounding is not merely decorative; it establishes the stakes. The authors remind us that Paine's central thesis was that "in free countries the law ought to be king; and there ought to be no other." By contrasting this ideal with the current reality, the commentary suggests that the administration is actively dismantling the very foundation of the republic.

The argument gains traction when the authors connect this historical ideal to specific, recent events that undermine judicial and investigative independence. They point to the exclusion of Minnesota's Bureau of Criminal Apprehension from the investigation into the killing of Renee Good, noting that the FBI blocked access to "case materials, scene evidence or investigative interviews necessary to complete a thorough and independent investigation." This is a chilling detail. The authors argue that this is not an isolated incident but part of a "sustained and conscious effort to undermine the rule of law," describing a pattern where federal agents are shielded from accountability rather than held to it.

"From Minneapolis to Caracas, from the White House to the Department of Justice to the Department of Homeland Security, the Trump administration has engaged in what the Declaration called 'a long train of abuses'—the object of eviscerating the rule of law and reducing us to mere subjects rather than self-governing citizens."

The authors' choice to link domestic investigations with international military actions creates a cohesive narrative of authoritarian overreach. They argue that the administration's behavior aligns with the definition of authoritarianism provided by political scientist Jeffrey Isaac: "contempt for the very idea of law" and an "embrace of the power politics of domination and conquest." This reframing is powerful because it moves the conversation away from partisan policy wins and losses to the structural integrity of the government itself. However, critics might argue that this binary framing—law versus tyranny—leaves little room for the complex, often messy reality of executive power in a modern superpower, where national security decisions often operate in legal gray zones.

The Economic and Legal Stakes

The commentary pivots to the immediate economic news, using it as a lens to examine the scope of executive authority. The authors highlight the Supreme Court's impending decision on the "Liberation Day" tariffs, which were implemented under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act. This specific legal vehicle is crucial context; the Act, passed in 1977, was designed to give the president broad powers during declared national emergencies, but its use for permanent trade policy is a contentious expansion of that authority. The authors note that if the Court rules the administration exceeded its authority, it could result in a refund of nearly $150 billion in collected duties, a move the president has described as "literally LIFE OR DEATH for our country."

This hyperbolic language from the executive branch is used by the authors to illustrate the danger of unchecked power. They quote the president's admission in a New York Times interview that he respects no legal limits on his power in international matters, stating, "My own morality. My own mind. It's the only thing that can stop me." The authors seize on this as a "living illustration" of the absolute monarchy that Paine ridiculed. The argument here is that when the head of the executive branch explicitly rejects legal constraints in favor of personal whim, the system of checks and balances is effectively broken.

The piece also touches on the Department of Justice's failure to release the Epstein files by the statutory deadline of December 19, 2025. Citing a letter from Representatives Ro Khanna and Thomas Massie, the authors highlight the conclusion that "the DOJ cannot be trusted with making mandatory disclosures under the Act." This failure to comply with a clear congressional mandate reinforces the authors' central thesis: the administration is not just bending the rules, but ignoring them entirely when it suits their interests. The juxtaposition of the tariff ruling and the Epstein files creates a picture of an executive branch that is simultaneously aggressive in asserting power and evasive when held accountable.

The Human Cost and Political Backlash

Beyond the legal and economic arguments, the commentary does not shy away from the human consequences of the administration's foreign policy. The authors discuss the raid in Venezuela, where a foreign leader was forcibly extracted from a compound, resulting in the deaths of dozens of people. They note that while the administration claims this was a necessary operation, the reality of "forcibly extracting a foreign leader" and killing civilians cannot be dismissed as a "semantic difference." This section of the commentary is vital because it centers the human cost of these power plays, refusing to let the narrative be dominated solely by geopolitical strategy.

The authors then pivot to the domestic political fallout, specifically the erosion of support among young men, a demographic that was crucial to the administration's 2024 victory. Citing polling from the Speaking with American Men (SAM) project, they report that the president's favorability among young men has dropped from 56 percent to 46 percent. The data suggests that this group, which hoped for economic relief, feels let down by a focus on foreign conflicts. "Only 27 percent of young men agreed that Trump is 'delivering for people like you,'" the authors write, while nearly half believe he "creates chaos and makes things worse."

This shift in sentiment is presented as a direct consequence of the administration's prioritization of "power politics of domination" over domestic stability. The authors argue that the administration's actions, from the Venezuela raid to the sidelining of intelligence professionals like Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard, demonstrate a governance style based on whim rather than good information. "Trump's policies aren't based on good information. They are based on his whim," they assert, pointing out that Gabbard was excluded from key decisions while she posted photos of herself on the beach. This detail serves as a metaphor for the disconnect between the administration's self-image and its actual performance.

"The rule of law or the rule of Trump?"

The authors conclude by returning to the Paine framework, asking whether the nation will stand for liberty or acquiesce to a "mad King George." The argument is that the current moment is a defining test of the American experiment. The administration's actions, from the tariffs to the Venezuela raid, are not just policy choices but symptoms of a deeper rejection of the rule of law. The authors suggest that the administration's reliance on personal authority over legal constraints is unsustainable and dangerous.

Critics might note that the authors' focus on the administration's legal overreach sometimes overshadows the genuine policy disagreements that exist on the merits of the tariffs or the necessity of the Venezuela operation. By framing every action as an attack on the rule of law, the piece risks alienating readers who might agree with the policy but disagree with the authoritarian characterization. However, the authors' strength lies in their ability to connect disparate events—economic data, court rulings, and military raids—into a single, coherent narrative about the health of the republic.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its ability to synthesize legal, economic, and military developments into a unified critique of executive overreach, using the historical weight of Thomas Paine to elevate the stakes beyond the daily news cycle. Its biggest vulnerability is the potential for its stark binary framing to be dismissed as partisan hyperbole by those who view the administration's actions as legitimate exercises of executive power. The reader should watch for the Supreme Court's ruling on the tariffs, as a decision striking down the administration's use of the International Emergency Economic Powers Act could serve as a critical test of whether the law remains king or yields to the whim of the executive.
