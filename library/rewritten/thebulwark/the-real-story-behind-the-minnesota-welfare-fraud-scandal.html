Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol deliver a rare, sobering look at a political scandal that refuses to be dismissed as mere partisan noise. While the internet is awash in conspiracy theories about immigrant fraud, these authors argue that the real story is a catastrophic failure of American public management—a failure that threatens to destroy the very safety net programs vulnerable families rely on. This is not a story about one politician's ego; it is a case study in how outsourcing essential services without adequate oversight creates a vacuum for industrial-scale theft.

The Scale of the Grift

The authors immediately strip away the political theater to reveal the staggering financial reality. They note that while local scandals have existed for a decade, the scope has exploded recently. "Last month, the assistant U.S. attorney for Minnesota announced the Justice Department had evidence suggesting that the total amount of fraud uncovered may reach well into the billions," they report. This is not the sporadic overbilling of a few bad apples; it is a systemic collapse.

The piece highlights how the pandemic created the perfect storm for this corruption. With the urgent need to distribute aid quickly, standard verification safeguards were stripped away. The authors explain that the thinking was to catch the plunderers later, but the result was a decade of unchecked looting. As they put it, "It's tough to run public programs effectively—and to keep up public support—when grifters have an easy time stealing the funds." This observation is crucial: it shifts the blame from the recipients of aid to the architects of the distribution system.

"What we see in Minnesota is not a handful of bad actors committing crimes. It's staggering, industrial-scale fraud."

The authors are careful to distinguish between the criminal acts and the political weaponization of those acts. They acknowledge that while the fraud is real, the narrative being pushed by the executive branch and online influencers is a distortion. They point out that the administration's rhetoric often blames entire communities for the actions of a few, ignoring the fact that 96 percent of federal childcare spending is valid. This framing is effective because it refuses to let the scandal be used as a cudgel against the concept of social support itself.

The Outsourcing Trap

A central thesis of the commentary is that the American model of welfare is uniquely prone to this kind of corruption. Longwell, Miller, and Kristol contrast the U.S. approach with peer nations in Western and Northern Europe. In Europe, the welfare state is often universal, and services like housing or childcare are provided directly by the government. In the United States, the government pays private entities to deliver these services.

This structural difference creates a massive incentive for fraud. As the authors note, "The United States, by contrast, outsources more of that work to the private sector, which means there are more opportunities for organizations and businesses to raid the public treasury." They cite Don Moynihan, a public policy expert, who argues that this system only works if there is aggressive auditing. "As long as you don't have government directly provide those services, then you either have to invest more in auditing and monitoring these service provisions—or you have to be willing to deal with some failures and some scandals that come up every now and again," Moynihan is quoted as saying.

Critics might argue that privatization is necessary for efficiency and innovation, a point the authors acknowledge as "intellectually defensible." However, they rightly point out that the current political climate is doing the opposite of what is required: cutting the very oversight mechanisms needed to make privatization work. The article notes that the executive branch has been firing inspectors general and dismantling agencies like the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, which were designed to catch exactly this kind of malfeasance.

The Political Fallout

The commentary does not shy away from the political consequences. The authors describe how the scandal has become a rallying cry for the opposition, who have latched onto the Somali-American community's involvement to stoke racial animus. They quote the former president's inflammatory rhetoric, where he claimed that up to 90% of the fraud was caused by illegal immigrants from Somalia, despite a lack of evidence. The authors call this out as "dishonesty, hypocrisy, and immigrant-bashing."

Yet, they warn that dismissing the entire saga as "MAGA hysteria" is a mistake. The underlying failure of governance is real, and it is being exploited. The authors write, "The Trump people are going to take this thing and they're going to run with it so aggressively, it becomes a little bit easier for us to overlook the important substance here." This is a vital warning for Democrats and policy advocates: if they do not address the management failures, they will lose the public's trust in the safety net entirely.

The piece also touches on the irony of the administration's response. While they claim to be fighting fraud, their actions—such as freezing childcare funding in states where fraud is alleged without providing evidence—threaten to punish the very people the programs are meant to help. The authors quote Harold Pollack, a social sciences professor, who warns, "If someone takes from this controversy that we have to stop offering services, you're just compounding the harm that the initial people who committed the fraud already perpetrated against the community."

"A really bad thing happened, and it demonstrated a real failure of public management that has to be addressed. And the scale of it was just incredible."

The authors also highlight the role of the media ecosystem in amplifying the scandal. They describe how a YouTube video by an "independent journalist" with questionable methods was treated as gospel by high-profile figures, despite state inspectors having recently verified the presence of children in the facilities. This illustrates how the modern information environment rewards sensationalism over substance, making it harder to have a rational debate about policy solutions.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its refusal to let the political noise obscure the structural rot in the American welfare system. By focusing on the mechanics of outsourcing and the erosion of oversight, Longwell, Miller, and Kristol provide a roadmap for understanding how such massive fraud could occur. The biggest vulnerability, however, is the political reality: even if the diagnosis is correct, the political will to reinvest in robust oversight may be impossible to generate in a polarized environment. The reader must watch whether the administration's proposed cuts will solve the problem or simply accelerate the collapse of public trust in social safety nets.
