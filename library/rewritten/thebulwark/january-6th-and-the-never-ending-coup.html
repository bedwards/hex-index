This piece from Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol delivers a chilling diagnosis: the January 6 insurrection was not a historical anomaly, but a successful stress test that liberated a political movement from the constraints of democratic norms. While the public fixates on the release of thousands of documents related to Jeffrey Epstein, the authors argue that the far more dangerous story is the administration's current willingness to treat international treaties as optional and domestic power as permanent. They contend that we are not in a post-January 6 era, but rather in the middle of a prolonged crisis where the rules of the game are being rewritten by force.

The Never-Ending Coup

The authors reject the comforting narrative that the Capitol attack was a final, dying spasm of a specific political moment. Instead, they frame it as a dawn of "total liberation" for the movement. "Last week, an excellent New York Times editorial described the insurrection of January 6th as a riot that never ended—a turning point, but not the one it first seemed to be," Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol write. They argue that the event proved a terrifying theory: that a base would commit felonies and accept absurd lies if told to do so by a leader, provided that leader promised a future reward.

The commentary suggests that the administration has internalized this lesson, operating on the belief that no act of selfishness is too grotesque to survive if the base is kept sufficiently agitated. "There was no act of selfishness or vindictiveness too grotesque for him to survive, provided he kept his people adequately juiced in the belief that their enemies were worse—and provided he could claw his way back to actual, hard power," the authors observe. This framing is effective because it shifts the focus from the personality of the leader to the structural vulnerability of the base and the institutions they are testing.

The most unsettling projection in the piece is the scenario for 2028. The authors posit that if a Democrat wins the next election, the stakes for the outgoing administration will be existential due to pending legal consequences, making a refusal to leave office far more likely than in 2020. They point to current actions in Venezuela as a blueprint for this potential future. "Trump has described Nicolás Maduro's 2024 re-election as fraudulent—correctly, for a change—and explicitly compared it to his own defeat in 2020," they note. However, rather than installing the rightful winner, the administration has struck a deal with a preferred proxy, delaying elections under the guise of "fixing the country first."

"We have to fix the country first," he said. "You can't have an election . . . We have to nurse the country back to health."

The authors warn that this logic could easily be transplanted to Washington: "Trump himself would just have to stay on—just until they can get to the bottom of the election fraud that obviously took place." Critics might argue that the institutional guardrails in the United States are stronger than in Venezuela, making such a scenario implausible. However, the piece counters this by noting that the only things standing between us and that reality are the "integrity of the men and women who make up the structures that restrain the will of a runaway president." They remind us that these structures do not support themselves; they require active defense.

The Rejection of the Rule of Law

The second half of the commentary, led by Bill Kristol, turns its gaze to the administration's disdain for international norms, specifically regarding the potential seizure of Greenland. The authors highlight comments by Stephen Miller, a top aide, who suggested that the United States has the right to take foreign territory by force. Miller dismissed treaties as mere "international niceties," arguing that the world is governed by "strength, force, and power."

Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol contrast this worldview with the foundational intent of the American government. They invoke the Jay Treaty of 1794, signed by George Washington, to illustrate that the Founders viewed treaties as binding legal obligations, not optional suggestions. "One of the notable moments in George Washington's presidency was the Treaty of Amity, Commerce, and Navigation... signed by President Washington on November 19, 1794," they recount, noting its ratification by the Senate. This historical reference adds necessary depth, showing that the rejection of treaty obligations is not just a modern policy shift but a fundamental departure from the Constitution's design.

The authors argue that the Constitution explicitly elevates treaties to the "supreme Law of the Land," a status Miller's rhetoric ignores. "The point of the United States, one could almost say, was to move as much as possible beyond the world Stephen Miller relishes, a world simply 'governed by force, governed by power,'" they write. The piece notes that European allies, including Denmark, have rightly recognized the gravity of this threat, with Danish Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen warning that an attack on a NATO ally would cause the alliance to collapse. The authors deliver a stinging verdict: "It is a sad fact—but a fact nonetheless—that one can have more confidence in the government of Denmark to respect election results and the peaceful transfer of power than in the Trump administration in the White House."

The Erosion of Public Trust

Beyond the high-stakes constitutional questions, the authors briefly touch on the administration's actions that erode public trust in science and the military. They criticize the reduction of recommended childhood vaccines by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, a move they attribute to the "funhouse-mirror logic" of Health and Human Services Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. The authors point out the irony that the "loss of trust" cited as the reason for cutting vaccines is largely a result of the very disinformation campaigns Kennedy has led for decades. "What contributed more to 'loss of trust' in vaccine... the fact that scientists had developed safe, effective shots... or the fact that a group of malcontents went around scaring parents for years with lies?" they ask.

Similarly, the piece addresses the administration's reaction to Senator Mark Kelly, a veteran, for reminding troops of their duty to refuse unlawful orders. The authors describe the administration's response—labeling Kelly a "seditious" threat and threatening his pension—as an attempt to chill legitimate public speech. "Your pattern of conduct demonstrates specific intent to counsel servicemembers to refuse lawful orders," Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth wrote in a censure letter, a claim the authors dismiss as a "hilariously anticlimactic punishment" that reveals the administration's desperation to control the narrative.

It is a sad fact—but a fact nonetheless—that we have in the White House a fundamentally anti-American administration.

Bottom Line

The strongest element of this commentary is its refusal to treat January 6 as a closed chapter, instead presenting it as an ongoing strategic victory for the administration that has emboldened them to test the limits of power in Venezuela and domestically. The argument is vulnerable only if one assumes that the institutions described as "worn down" will suddenly snap back to strength without a crisis; the authors wisely argue that this resilience is not automatic. The reader must watch whether the "men and women who make up the structures" can hold the line when the next test arrives, likely in the form of a contested 2028 election or a direct challenge to international alliances.
