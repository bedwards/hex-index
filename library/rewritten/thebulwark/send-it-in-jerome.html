This week's coverage from Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol delivers a startling pivot: the moment the Federal Reserve Chair stopped absorbing abuse and fought back, the entire political machinery of the executive branch suddenly stalled. The piece is notable not for recounting the insults hurled at Jerome Powell, but for documenting the precise second the administration's strategy of intimidation collided with a coalition of institutional defenders. It forces a re-evaluation of whether the executive branch's momentum is actually unstoppable or merely unopposed until now.

The Only Language They Understand

The authors frame Jerome Powell's recent shift from silence to confrontation as a watershed moment for American governance. For months, the Federal Reserve Chair had endured a barrage of personal attacks and threats of removal from the White House, attempting a "grizzly-survival approach" by ignoring the noise. But as Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, Bill Kristol write, "Powell had tried the conciliatory approach... But this mostly just gave Trump the opportunity to do his standard anti-Powell shtick for the cameras while standing right next to the guy and wearing a hard hat." The authors argue that this passive strategy failed because it allowed the executive branch to operate without consequence.

The turning point came when Powell publicly accused the Justice Department of launching a pretextual criminal investigation to coerce his interest rate decisions. This move triggered an unprecedented reaction. The authors highlight a statement from a bipartisan coalition of former officials who condemned the move as "mafioso tactics" that belong in "emerging markets with weak institutions." This framing is powerful because it strips the administration of its nationalist rhetoric, recasting their actions as a betrayal of the very rule of law they claim to uphold. The authors note that the backlash was immediate and severe, with Senator Thom Tillis issuing an ultimatum that could block future appointments to the Federal Reserve board.

The reported criminal inquiry into Federal Reserve Chair Jay Powell is an unprecedented attempt to use prosecutorial attacks to undermine that independence.

The commentary suggests that the administration's sudden silence from the President and the defensive posturing from U.S. Attorney Jeanine Pirro reveal a critical vulnerability. When the executive branch faces organized resistance, it often retreats. The authors observe that "Trump, who never misses an opportunity to deride Powell, suddenly clammed up," and Pirro's office scrambled to claim they were merely responding to ignored outreach. This suggests the administration's power is often performative, relying on the assumption that no one will push back hard enough to expose the fragility of their legal overreach.

Critics might argue that the investigation into the Fed building's cost overruns is a legitimate matter of fiscal oversight rather than political coercion. However, the timing and the specific targeting of the Chair's testimony strongly suggest the authors' interpretation holds water: the investigation is a tool of leverage, not justice.

The Cost of Silence and the Rise of Resistance

Beyond the Federal Reserve, the piece weaves in a darker thread regarding the human cost of the administration's enforcement policies. The authors do not shy away from the violence that precipitated a broader corporate backlash. They note that the federal government may have blocked local investigations into the killing of Renee Good by Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agents, yet the feds are now investigating Good's "possible connections to activist groups." As Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, Bill Kristol put it, "They wouldn't want to miss any further opportunities to smear her name. Happy Tuesday." This biting sarcasm underscores the moral bankruptcy of an administration that investigates victims while shielding perpetrators.

This context is vital because it explains the surge in private-sector resistance. The authors report that over 150 senior executives from major technology firms are circulating a letter to break ties with the administration's immigration enforcement. This is not just political posturing; it is a direct response to the administration's lawlessness. The piece argues that the administration "frequently seeking the path of least resistance" is a fatal miscalculation when the public and private sectors finally align against them.

He and his political operation style themselves an unstoppable juggernaut, but in reality they're frequently seeking the path of least resistance.

The authors also touch on the internal fractures within the Republican party, noting that Senate Republicans are beginning to vote against military overreach in Venezuela and Greenland. This signals a potential thaw in the era of "permanent Republican quiescence." However, the piece also offers a sobering counterpoint regarding the Democratic party's own challenges. In New York, the authors critique Mayor Zohran Mamdani's appointment of housing activist Cea Weaver, whose past rhetoric about seizing private property and "white supremacy" may alienate the moderate voters necessary for long-term success. The authors warn that "elevating Mamdani help with that task? That depends on his policies. To the extent that he brings people like Weaver along with it, it seems doubtful."

This dual focus—on the administration's overreach and the opposition's internal contradictions—creates a nuanced picture. The authors are not simply cheering for resistance; they are analyzing the conditions under which it succeeds or fails. The argument is that resistance works, but only if it is broad-based and strategically sound.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its demonstration that the executive branch's aggression is not a monolith of power but a series of calculated risks that crumble when met with unified opposition. The piece's biggest vulnerability lies in its optimism about the durability of this resistance, given the administration's history of absorbing political blows. Readers should watch closely to see if the bipartisan coalition defending the Federal Reserve can translate its rhetorical victory into lasting policy constraints, and whether the private sector's threat to sever ties with immigration enforcement leads to tangible changes on the ground.
