Sarah Longwell, Tim Miller, and Bill Kristol make a startling pivot in their latest analysis: they argue that the political calculation to delay impeachment until after the next election is no longer viable because the administration's actions have already crossed into active, daily emergencies. This is not a theoretical debate about constitutional thresholds; it is a urgent call to document a series of unfolding crises—from public health collapses to extrajudicial military killings—as they happen, rather than waiting for a future political victory to address them.

The Shift from Futile to Immediate

The authors, who have long been skeptical of premature impeachment efforts, admit a dramatic change in their own stance. Longwell, Miller, and Kristol write, "I realized with a jolt that later is already here." This admission anchors their entire argument: the window for political maneuvering has closed because the administration's conduct has accelerated beyond the point where patience is a virtue. They point to specific, recent escalations, including the use of military force to oust a foreign leader and the killing of a U.S. citizen by federal agents, as the catalysts that transformed impeachment from a political liability into a necessary documentation of fact.

The piece reframes impeachment not as a tool for partisan vengeance, but as a mechanism to expose a "crime spree" that is actively harming citizens. They cite Democratic strategist Simon Rosenberg, who noted, "It's not a presidency, it's a crime spree." The authors argue that this framing is essential because the evidence is no longer abstract; it is visceral. They highlight the death of Renee Nicole Good, a U.S. citizen killed by an ICE agent, to illustrate the human cost of the administration's policies. This focus on specific, tragic events grounds the legal argument in human reality, making the case that waiting for 2027 would be a dereliction of duty.

"The proceedings could also underscore that in order to stop Trump from naming new Supreme Court justices, Democrats need a Senate majority."

However, the authors acknowledge the political risks. They note that some critics, like Matt Bennett of Third Way, argue that focusing on impeachment distracts from the more immediate concern of affordability. Bennett suggests, "if it's not about winning this year, don't do it." Longwell, Miller, and Kristol counter this by arguing that the administration's actions are directly driving up costs and undermining public safety, thereby linking the two issues. They write, "Trump is preoccupied with his lethal pursuit of power, money and resources, not the wellbeing of voters." This connection attempts to bridge the gap between legal accountability and economic anxiety, though it remains a difficult sell to voters who prioritize immediate financial relief over constitutional crises.

Targeting the Cabinet and the Executive

The commentary moves beyond the president to target specific cabinet members, arguing that the "wrecking-crew leadership" deserves equal scrutiny. The authors detail impeachment articles filed against Health and Human Services Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., citing his role in undermining public health. They reference Rep. Haley Stevens' article, which accuses Kennedy of "abuse of authority and undermining of the public health" through actions that have led to surges in measles and flu. This is a significant departure from traditional impeachment targets, focusing on policy failures that have direct, lethal consequences.

Similarly, the piece addresses Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, highlighting charges of "murder and conspiracy to murder" related to U.S. attacks on Venezuelan fishing boats. The authors quote Rep. Shri Thanedar, who stated, "Pete Hegseth has been using the United States military to extrajudicially assassinate people without evidence of any crime." This charge is particularly heavy, invoking the historical context of regime change operations. The authors note that this aligns with historical precedents where executive overreach in foreign policy has led to significant civilian casualties, drawing a parallel to past debates on the legality of such interventions.

The authors also discuss Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem, citing Rep. Robin Kelly's articles against her for obstruction of Congress and violation of public trust. Kelly's statement, "Secretary Noem is violating the Constitution while ruining—and ending—lives," underscores the gravity of the charges. The piece suggests that these actions are not just political missteps but fundamental breaches of the rule of law. This focus on cabinet-level accountability is a strategic move to broaden the scope of impeachment beyond the president, potentially creating a coalition of lawmakers from both parties who are concerned about specific abuses.

Critics might note that targeting multiple cabinet members could dilute the focus on the president and fragment the political momentum needed for a successful impeachment. Additionally, the reliance on House forums rather than formal impeachment proceedings in the short term may be seen as a compromise that lacks the teeth of actual removal. However, the authors argue that these forums serve a dual purpose: publicizing atrocities and laying the groundwork for future action.

The Human Cost of Policy

The most compelling aspect of the piece is its refusal to treat these events as abstract political maneuvers. The authors emphasize the human cost, describing the "blood-covered airbag and stuffed animals spilling out of the glove compartment" of the car where Renee Nicole Good was killed. This imagery is designed to evoke empathy and outrage, forcing readers to confront the reality of the administration's actions. They argue that "tens of millions of Americans care about Trump's offenses against the law, the Constitution, and democracy," and that ignoring these concerns is a mistake.

The authors also touch on the broader implications for democracy, noting that the administration's actions threaten the separation of powers and the independence of the judiciary. They cite Rep. Al Green's articles against the president for intimidating federal judges, which the House voted to table. Despite this setback, the authors argue that the diversity of support for impeachment, including ranking members of committees, indicates that the president is in trouble. This suggests that the political landscape is shifting, even if the immediate outcome is uncertain.

"All you have to do is look at the photo of Renee Nicole Good's front seat, with its blood-covered airbag and stuffed animals spilling out of the glove compartment, to grasp not just the enormity of what Trump is inflicting on America but also the relatability of the tragedies playing out in families and neighborhoods everywhere."

Bottom Line

Longwell, Miller, and Kristol's strongest argument is their urgent reframing of impeachment as a necessary response to immediate, life-threatening abuses rather than a distant political strategy. Their willingness to pivot from skepticism to advocacy, driven by specific, tragic events, lends the piece a compelling moral weight. However, the biggest vulnerability remains the political feasibility of their proposal; linking impeachment to economic concerns is a high-wire act that may not resonate with a public weary of political drama. The reader should watch for how Democrats navigate the tension between holding hearings and mounting a formal impeachment effort in the coming months, as the success of this strategy will depend on their ability to maintain public attention on the human cost of these policies.
