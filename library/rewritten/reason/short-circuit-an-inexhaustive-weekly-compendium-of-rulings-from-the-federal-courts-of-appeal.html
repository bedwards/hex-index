The federal appellate courts grind out thousands of decisions every week, most of them invisible to anyone not directly affected. Reason's Short Circuit column, produced by writers at the Institute for Justice, exists to make that invisibility slightly less total — and this week's edition is a reminder of how much law gets made in the spaces most people never look.

Twenty-Two Years to a Verdict

The longest-running story in this week's roundup deserves its own moment of quiet astonishment. In 2004, eleven American families sued the Palestine Liberation Organization and the Palestinian Authority over Second Intifada terror attacks in Israel. A jury awarded them $655 million in 2015. Then came eleven more years of jurisdictional combat: Congress passed laws to establish jurisdiction, the Second Circuit ruled those laws insufficient, Congress clarified, the Second Circuit ruled that clarification unconstitutional, and the Supreme Court reversed that ruling in 2025. The piece summarizes the denouement with the brevity the absurdity deserves: "Second Circuit (2026): Okay fine, we recall our mandate from our first go at the case and affirm the judgment and jury award."

Twenty-two years. The families who filed that lawsuit in 2004 were, in many cases, the parents and spouses and children of people killed in the early 2000s. Whatever justice means in such cases, it does not arrive quickly. The roundup does not editorialize on this point — it does not need to. The timeline is its own argument.

Prison Medicine and the Limits of Judicial Reach

Two prison cases in this week's column cut in opposite directions, and the tension between them illuminates one of the central disputes in federal constitutional law: how much power federal courts actually have to fix broken institutions.

Angola, Louisiana's notorious prison — "once dubbed the worst in America," as the piece notes — was the subject of a lawsuit filed in 2015. Inmates died from preventable medical failures. The case went to trial in 2018. A liability finding came in 2021. A remedial order came in 2023, complete with special masters to oversee court-mandated improvements. Then the Fifth Circuit, sitting en banc, largely unwound it. The court's rationale: Congress enacted the Prison Litigation Reform Act to rein in what it called "judicial adventurism," and federal courts must "maintain a delicate balance among the prerogatives of public institutions, the demands of federalism, and the judiciary's limited remedial role."

The phrase "judicial adventurism" is doing considerable work in that sentence. It is the legislature's characterization, adopted by the court, of what happens when judges order prisons to actually fix things. Critics of the Prison Litigation Reform Act have long argued that the law was designed less to rein in genuine judicial excess than to make it structurally harder for incarcerated people to obtain relief for constitutional violations. The Fifth Circuit's en banc ruling does not resolve that dispute — it largely sidesteps it in favor of procedural deference.

Meanwhile, a Texas pretrial detainee gave birth alone in her cell, two weeks before her due date. The baby did not survive. The jail's medical director had not read an email indicating she had refused breakfast and was experiencing abdominal cramps that morning. The Fifth Circuit's ruling: qualified immunity. He had not seen the email. The piece reproduces the court's logic without comment, which is itself a form of comment.

"The jail's medical director didn't read an email that indicated the detainee had refused breakfast and was experiencing abdominal cramps that morning. Fifth Circuit: Qualified immunity. He didn't see the email, how was he to know?"

Qualified immunity — the doctrine shielding government officials from civil rights liability unless their conduct violated "clearly established" law — appears repeatedly in this week's column, sometimes defeated, sometimes not. The doctrine has attracted critics across the ideological spectrum, who argue that it has drifted far from its original purpose of protecting officials making good-faith judgment calls. The Angola case and the Texas detainee case, read together, suggest a federal judicial system in genuine tension with itself about the scope of constitutional protection inside institutional walls.

The AI Problem in Federal Courts

The column touches twice on artificial intelligence and legal practice, and neither instance is flattering to the attorneys involved. The Third Circuit admonished and sanctioned a lawyer in a DEA case whose brief contained AI-generated hallucinations — invented citations, nonexistent cases. The piece notes with dry precision that "the hallucinations at issue are not the DEA's standard fare (whether of drugs themselves or of federal drug policy) but of the AI-induced variety. Things might have gone better had the attorney not doubled down."

The Seventh Circuit case is slightly more complicated. An attorney included two nonexistent sources in a brief but claimed she had not used artificial intelligence herself — she had copied language from another brief, apparently without verifying the citations. The court admonished but did not sanction her, noting her profuse apology. It also, in a moment of institutional self-awareness, "cast shade on opposing counsel for not catching the errors themselves."

This is a genuinely new category of legal malpractice problem, and the appellate courts are still working out how to respond. Sanctions are one tool. Admonishments are another. The variation in outcomes between the Third and Seventh Circuit cases — similar conduct, different consequences — reflects an emerging but unsettled area of professional responsibility. Critics might note that focusing on attorney sanctions does not address the upstream question of why lawyers feel pressure to produce more citations, more quickly, with fewer resources for verification. But the courts can only address what comes before them.

Warrants, Wants, and the Fourth Amendment

One of the cleaner constitutional arguments in this week's column involves a Wisconsin man arrested based on a felony "want" — a law enforcement alert indicating an officer believes there is enough evidence for a warrant, but without any judicial review. The Seventh Circuit's ruling is unambiguous: "No matter how much you may want it otherwise, the Fourth Amendment says warrants — and a warrant requires sign-off from a 'neutral and detached magistrate.'"

The piece includes a rare editorial observation in brackets: "Federal agencies famously active in the Seventh Circuit's largest city may care to take note that warrants must come from a judge." Chicago is the Seventh Circuit's largest city. The comment is oblique enough to be deniable and clear enough to land. The underlying principle — that the warrant requirement exists precisely to interpose judicial review between law enforcement belief and government action — has renewed relevance in an era of expanded interior enforcement operations.

Whistleblowers and the Price of Integrity

An Atlanta police officer's story, as the piece tells it, is a case study in institutional retaliation. He had previously accused his superiors of racial discrimination. He then reported them for downgrading traffic tickets issued to a former mayor's grandson. One week after that report, his flexible shift schedule — something he had relied on for years — was abruptly made fixed. He sued for First Amendment retaliation. The Eleventh Circuit mostly sided with the city, but found that a jury could reasonably conclude his superiors had a retaliatory motive for pulling his schedule. The qualified immunity defense fails on that claim. A jury will decide.

The First Amendment retaliation doctrine is one of the more complicated areas of public employee constitutional law. It protects speech on matters of public concern, but courts have historically given employers considerable latitude to argue that adverse employment actions had legitimate justifications. The Eleventh Circuit's partial reversal here suggests the facts were close enough — the timing, the pattern, the prior discrimination complaint — to survive summary judgment.

A Food Truck Victory

The column ends on a win. The piece describes what it calls a "victory" in Parksley, Virginia, where a federal court ruled that a town and a city councilman violated the Fourth Amendment when the councilman cut the water pipe running to the Eben-Ezer Food Truck, destroying more than a thousand dollars in food. The food truck's owners, Theslet Benoir and Clemene Bastien, filed suit in January 2024 after the councilman, according to the piece, "repeatedly harassed them, cut the pipe, and, according to Theslet and Clemene, told them to 'go back to your own country.'"

The court found the constitutional violation so egregious that it denied qualified immunity — a notable outcome in a doctrine that frequently protects government officials even in cases of obvious misconduct. The Institute for Justice represented the food truck owners, which explains the column's uncharacteristic warmth in describing the result. The victory is real. It is also, measured against the scale of the week's other rulings — the woman who gave birth alone in her cell, the families who waited twenty-two years, the Angola inmates who saw a decade of litigation partially unwound — a reminder of the distance between individual legal victories and structural change.

Critics might fairly note that a column curated by an advocacy organization will naturally emphasize cases that fit its priorities — property rights, qualified immunity reform, civil liberties. The Angola remedial order rollback, for instance, receives factual coverage but no sustained critique of the underlying PLRA framework. That is not a failing unique to this column; it reflects the limits of any weekly digest trying to cover a week's worth of federal appellate output in readable form.

Bottom Line

Short Circuit does something genuinely useful: it makes the federal appellate courts legible to people who do not have law degrees and do not have time to read slip opinions. This week's edition is particularly rich, tracing threads from AI malpractice to prison medicine to warrant requirements to a two-decade Palestinian Authority litigation — each story a small window into how law actually operates, slowly, unevenly, and with consequences that arrive long after anyone thought to pay attention. The gap between constitutional promise and institutional reality is the column's real subject, week after week.
