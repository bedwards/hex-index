"The American economy has outpaced Europe for decades, creating a wealth gap so wide that an average worker could retire in October and still out-earn a French colleague working year-round. Yet, as this piece from Reason observes, there is one glaring exception to this success story: the inability of Americans to build enough homes to house themselves."

The Housing Paradox

The editors at Reason open with a striking comparison between American prosperity and European stagnation, using air conditioning as a metaphor for divergent economic trajectories. They note that while central air remains a luxury in much of Europe, it is standard in the U.S., a difference driven by cumulative growth. "An extra annual percentage point of GDP growth has added up to the point where your typical American could stop working in October and still make more money than the average Frenchman working until the end of the year," Reason reports. This macroeconomic success, however, is undermined by a micro-level failure in land use.

The piece argues that restrictive zoning laws are not just a problem for superstar cities like New York but are stifling construction everywhere, even in low-cost regions like Buffalo. Citing housing writer Kevin Erdmann, the article posits that "broad upzoning, or lending reform... applied to all of Buffalo, would increase construction and lower rents," challenging the assumption that only high-demand areas suffer from supply constraints. This is a crucial insight: regulatory friction creates a nationwide drag on production, compounding over time into a crisis.

Housing is a micro exception to America's macro success story. Our land use regulations cause a little less housing to be built in almost every city every year.

Critics might argue that local control over zoning is essential for preserving community character and preventing overcrowding, suggesting that rapid upzoning could degrade neighborhood quality of life. However, the piece counters this by pointing out that without broad reform, even the most permissive regimes fail to unlock the latent capacity for growth in places like Buffalo or New York.

The Bureaucracy of Disaster Recovery

The commentary shifts sharply from policy theory to a grim reality in Los Angeles, where six men face felony charges for attempting to build accessory dwelling units (ADUs) on fire-ravaged properties without state licenses. In the wake of the Palisades Fire, which destroyed thousands of homes and killed 12 people, the rebuilding process has been agonizingly slow: "Some 18 months later, only 28 homes have been completely rebuilt and issued with certificates of occupancy."

Reason highlights the contradiction in the administration's approach. While Governor Gavin Newsom and Mayor Karen Bass issued executive orders to expedite permits, they simultaneously suspended laws allowing duplexes on single-family lots and cracked down on unlicensed builders. The piece notes that prosecutors justified the felony charges as consumer protection, yet "the defendants in this case are not alleged to have harmed anyone. They merely offered their services to willing buyers."

This enforcement stance seems particularly harsh given that illegal ADU construction is rampant; a 2024 study found that most ADUs in San Jose were built without permits. The article suggests that instead of loosening licensing requirements to accelerate recovery, officials are "vigorously enforcing licensing requirements to ensure nothing gets built without the proper paperwork." This creates a perverse incentive where the fear of criminal prosecution may slow down the very reconstruction needed for displaced families.

Streamlining Evictions and Blocking Development

In South Carolina, the narrative turns to property rights as the state enacts an expedited process for removing squatters. The new law allows courts to issue orders within 24 hours based on a property owner's assertion that an occupant was never invited. "We wanted to make sure that it didn't become a real problem here," state Rep. Weston Newton told reporters, framing the legislation as prophylactic rather than reactive.

While the piece acknowledges that slow judicial processes have allowed scammers to exploit tenants in places like California, it raises concerns about the new law's safeguards. The statute caps damages for wrongly removed occupants at just $1,000, a figure Reason contrasts with Florida's reforms which allow claims for triple the fair market rent. "A process that's too deferential to owners could risk the contractual rights of tenants or other lawful occupants," the editors warn, suggesting that speed should not come at the cost of due process.

The final segment examines a bizarre reversal in Palm Bay, Florida, where city officials are using eminent domain to buy a shuttered golf course specifically to prevent it from being developed under the state's "Live Local Act." This law was designed to bypass local opposition by granting administrative approval for projects that include affordable units. The piece notes that because the law is so effective at overriding local objections, "the City of Palm Bay is rushing to buy a 135-acre... property" to stop a 703-unit residential project.

Clearly, the upzoning it requires is not enough to entice builders to proceed with what should be an attractive project.

This move highlights a deep institutional resistance to density; when state policy successfully empowers developers, local governments are resorting to purchasing land merely to maintain the status quo. It mirrors similar conflicts in Massachusetts, where towns have gone to extreme lengths to technically comply with housing mandates while effectively neutering their impact.

Bottom Line

Reason's strongest argument lies in its reframing of zoning not as a local nuisance but as a systemic brake on American economic potential, comparable to how regulatory friction affects other sectors. The piece's vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that deregulation alone will solve supply constraints without addressing construction costs or labor shortages. Ultimately, the coverage reveals a nation where policy innovation at the state level is being actively sabotaged by local obstruction and bureaucratic rigidity.
