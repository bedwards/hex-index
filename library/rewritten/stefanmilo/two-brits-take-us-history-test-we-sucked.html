Opening Pitch:

What happens when two British guys who've lived in America for a decade decide to take a US history test? Embarrassment, confusion, and surprisingly honest admissions of ignorance. Stefan Milo and Pete Kelly — both Englishmen who call the US home — venture into American colonial history with zero confidence and maximum humor. Their answers range from confidently wrong to hilariously uncertain. This isn't just a quiz; it's a portrait of two foreigners trying to navigate the most embarrassing gaps in their knowledge.

The Setup

Stefan Milo and Pete Kelly live in America. They've spent years there. Yet when it comes to US history, they're essentially blank slates. The premise itself is comedic: two British content creators deciding to take an American history quiz, knowing full well they'll probably fail spectacularly.

"I think this is US as a nation state history," Milo said before the quiz began. "And it's kind of funny because America is such like um a huge cultural imprint around the world. I do feel like I know considering we didn't have any education in US history."

They admit they've done nothing but answer one question to test if the quiz worked. Pete, who studied the Great Depression and FDR's New Deal at A-level, felt slightly optimistic about his chances.

The Quiz Begins

The first question: "Who was the first European to explore the Great Lakes and St. Lawrence River?" The options included Jacqu Cartier, Francis Drake, Vasco de Gama, and John Cabot.

Pete picked Jacqu Cartier — which turned out to be correct. For the second question about Walter Rally's settlement attempt, they guessed Jamestown but got it wrong; the answer was Rowan Oak. When asked where Virginia's capital moved after Jamestown, they debated between Richmond and Williamsburg, ultimately guessing Richmond.

The Appalachian Mountains came up in a question about the 1763 proclamation limiting colonial settlement — both guessed correctly that it referred to the Appalachians. Then came the Stamp Act question: which group organized against it?

"I think Sons of Liberty," Pete said. "They were the guys with the Boston Tea Party, I think."

That was right too.

The Humor of Ignorance

What makes this test so entertaining isn't just wrong answers — it's the commentary between questions. When asked about Benedict Arnold offering something to the British, they deduced it couldn't be James Town (no longer capital by that point) and Washington DC "didn't exist" during the Revolutionary War. Their process of elimination led them to New York.

The quiz moves chronologically through American history: colonial settlement, the French and Indian War, the Stamp Act, the Revolutionary period with Benedict Arnold. Each question finds them either embarrassingly wrong or surprisingly correct — often by accident rather than knowledge.

They joke about Americans being friendly, their own experience in rural Ohio where locals assumed he was a medieval traveler, and how his "Queen's English" got him a door man job at a bank. The bit about Stonehenge being rebuilt in Washington — "Stonehenge. I didn't know that" — adds to the absurdity.

Counterpoints

Critics might argue these two Brits are simply playing up their ignorance for content. But even if performative, there's something refreshing about publicly admitting what they don't know. A quiz on Hungarian history would leave them equally stumped. The humor works because they're not pretending to expertise they don't have — they're just two guys having fun with history.

Pull Quote

"I feel like I know more than average about US history than like I don't know the history of Hungary for example."

This single sentence captures their charm: comparing themselves to what they actually know less about, rather than claiming any real expertise.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this piece is its willingness to be funny through honesty. No pretense, no performance of knowledge — just two Brits admitting they don't know anything and making jokes about it anyway. The vulnerability is the comedy. What they're willing to say about Americans ("I think they're a friendly bunch") adds warmth without ever being mean-spirited. If you want to hear people fail cheerfully at American history while laughing at themselves, these two deliver.
