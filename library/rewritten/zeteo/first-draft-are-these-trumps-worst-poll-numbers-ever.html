Mehdi Hasan opens with a chilling juxtaposition: the 1981 assassination attempt on Ronald Reagan, which led to a historic insanity defense, against the current political reality where Donald Trump's approval ratings have plummeted to what may be the lowest of his career. The piece's distinctiveness lies not just in the polling data, but in its exclusive revelation that even the President's most loyal base is fracturing over a potential ground war in Iran, a conflict the administration is actively preparing for despite overwhelming public opposition.

The Illusion of Support

Hasan immediately dismantles the narrative of a unified MAGA movement by presenting a UMass Amherst poll that finds only 33% of Americans approve of Trump's job performance, with 62% disapproving. He writes, "Only 33% of Americans approve of the job he is doing, and 62% disapprove, according to the UMass Amherst national poll." This figure is presented not merely as a number, but as a potential career nadir for the President. The author's framing is effective because it contrasts the President's delusional self-assessment with hard data; while Trump claims his polls are better than ever, the evidence suggests a nation turning against him.

The coverage then pivots to the specific issue of military escalation, where the data becomes even more damning for the administration. Hasan notes that while the war has some support among the base, the appetite for direct American involvement is non-existent. "Only 8% of Americans believe the US should send ground troops, and 67% disagree with the idea, per the UMass poll." This statistic is crucial because it highlights a disconnect between the President's rhetoric and the public's reality. The author points out that even among "very much" MAGA identifiers, the split is nearly even, with 36% disagreeing with sending troops. This suggests that the "Mad King" narrative is not just a rhetorical flourish but a reflection of a base that is increasingly wary of the President's foreign policy ambitions.

The American public does not seem to be buying what President Donald Trump is selling when it comes to the war with Iran.

Hasan connects this public skepticism to the logistical nightmares facing the administration, specifically the plan to seize Kharg Island. He references reports that senior officials have been briefed that seizing the island, a decades-long goal for Trump, would result in "considerable" American casualties. This historical context is vital; just as the Helms–Burton Act of 1996 sought to tighten sanctions on Cuba by penalizing foreign investment, Trump's current strategy seeks to seize Iranian oil infrastructure, yet the public is far less willing to pay the human price. The author argues that the President's desire to "take the oil in Iran" is being met with a wall of public resistance, noting that 79% of Americans believe the invasion will spike oil prices.

The Distraction of Epstein and Immigration

Beyond foreign policy, Hasan argues that the administration is using the war as a smokescreen for domestic scandals, particularly the release of information regarding Jeffrey Epstein. The polling data here is stark: 59% of Americans believe the administration is hiding information, and 50% believe the attacks on Iran are a distraction. Hasan writes, "Those figures mirror the findings of a recent Data for Progress poll conducted on behalf of Drop Site News and Zeteo." This convergence of polling sources strengthens the argument that the "distraction" theory is not a partisan talking point but a widespread public perception.

The author also highlights the administration's failure on immigration, a signature issue for Trump. "Immigration – Trump's signature issue – has 'quickly become one of the president's most pressing vulnerabilities,'" Hasan quotes from the poll director. With only 35% approval on immigration, the President's signature issue has become a liability. The piece details the human cost of this failure, noting that over 11,000 US citizen children have had their parents detained by ICE, with nearly 60% resulting in deportations. This section is particularly powerful because it moves beyond abstract polling numbers to the tangible trauma of family separation, a direct parallel to the Insanity Defense Reform Act of 1984, which was passed after the Hinckley verdict to tighten the standards for insanity defenses, reflecting a public demand for accountability that the current administration seems to be ignoring.

Critics might note that the focus on polling numbers could overlook the President's ability to mobilize his base through media ecosystems that do not rely on traditional approval metrics. However, the sheer breadth of the disapproval across all demographics, including the base's hesitation on war, suggests a deeper structural issue than mere media bias.

The Political Fallout

The commentary concludes by examining the political maneuvering within the Democratic party and the broader landscape. Hasan details how Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez is pushing back against critics on the left regarding her votes on Israel aid, asserting, "I never have [voted to approve weapons sales or major military aid packages to Israel]." This defense is framed as a necessary correction to a narrative that has been weaponized against her. The piece also touches on the chaos within the Department of Homeland Security, where Republicans have failed to control the funding battle despite holding all branches of government.

The author's tone is one of urgent warning, suggesting that the President's "delusional" belief in his own popularity is a dangerous precursor to further escalation. "Right now my polls are better than they've ever been before," Hasan quotes Trump, labeling it a lie. This final contrast between the President's words and the nation's reality serves as the piece's emotional anchor.

Bottom Line

Hasan's strongest argument is the irrefutable data showing that the President's base is not as monolithic as he claims, particularly regarding the prospect of a ground war in Iran. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on the assumption that public opinion will directly halt executive action, a historical precedent that often proves false in times of war. Readers should watch for whether the administration proceeds with the Kharg Island operation despite the overwhelming public opposition, as this will test the limits of Trump's political capital.
