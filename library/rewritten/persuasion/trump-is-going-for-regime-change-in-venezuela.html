This piece presents a startling premise: that the United States has already successfully executed a regime change operation in Venezuela, capturing the nation's leader and forcing an immediate pivot to nation-building. Yascha Mounk, writing for Persuasion, argues that the debate over the legality of this intervention is now a distraction from the far more difficult task of managing the violent aftermath and the complex reality of restoring democracy. The article forces readers to confront a grim irony: an administration that campaigned against "forever wars" is now embroiled in one of the most delicate state-building exercises in history, with the fate of millions of refugees hanging in the balance.

The Irony of Intervention

Mounk opens by dismantling the idea that the United States can simply remove a dictator and walk away. "The Trump administration is now engaged in bringing about regime change in Venezuela, and will be embroiled in a nation-building exercise there for the foreseeable future," he writes. This framing is crucial because it shifts the conversation from the moral justification of the strike to the practical nightmare of what comes next. The author highlights the contradiction in an administration that once mocked endless conflict now facing a scenario where "nation-building is really, really hard, and the United States doesn't have a good track record in this regard."

The argument gains depth by contrasting Venezuela with previous American failures. Mounk notes that Afghanistan and Iraq were fundamentally different contexts, lacking the democratic traditions that Venezuela possesses. "The problem in Afghanistan began with the fact that it never possessed a real state, much less a modern one," he observes, pointing to the country's tribal fragmentation and lack of centralized authority. In contrast, Venezuela has a history of democratic governance dating back to the 1958 Pacto de Punto Fijo, which ended a dictatorship and established a system where "regular elections, a growing middle class, and citizens who enjoyed a high degree of individual freedom" were the norm for decades.

The problem in Afghanistan began with the fact that it never possessed a real state, much less a modern one.

Critics might note that Mounk's optimism about Venezuela's democratic resilience overlooks the deep institutional rot that occurred under the Chávez and Maduro administrations, where the rule of law was systematically dismantled over two decades. While the democratic tradition exists, the capacity of civil society to function without state support is severely degraded.

The Trap of the Security Apparatus

The most chilling section of the analysis focuses on the specific nature of the threat remaining in Venezuela. Mounk argues that the real danger lies not in a power vacuum, but in a desperate, armed resistance from the regime's enablers. "The Maduro regime's power was based on the military, and a network of supporters hailing from poor neighborhoods around the country that it organized into militias," he explains. These are not merely political opponents; they are a criminalized security apparatus with a direct financial stake in the regime's survival.

The author draws a sobering parallel to Cuba, where the security apparatus evolved into a self-perpetuating machine of criminal activity. "The Castro regime was originally driven by ideology, but after the passing of Fidel Castro and his brother Raúl, it has fallen into the hands of a powerful security apparatus that profits directly from criminal activity," Mounk writes. This comparison is vital for understanding why a simple change of leadership could trigger a civil war. The generals and admirals in Venezuela, many of whom run drug-smuggling and sanctions-busting operations, have "no future in a democratic Venezuela" and will likely fight to the death to avoid prosecution.

The piece also touches on the historical context of these militias, noting how they mirror the dynamics seen in the rise of the Colectivos—paramilitary groups that have long served as the regime's street-level enforcers. Mounk warns that the administration seems unaware of this complexity. "Trump in his initial news conference after the intervention has said that the United States will run Venezuela directly for the time being. He doesn't have the faintest idea what he is getting into," he asserts. This lack of strategic foresight is presented as the greatest risk to the mission.

Motives, Legality, and the Future

Mounk then tackles the elephant in the room: the stated motives of the intervention. He suggests that the administration's focus on resources rather than democracy could derail the entire effort. "At his news conference on Saturday, he made it clear that his major objective was securing Venezuela's oil resources, which he claims the United States 'owns,'" the author notes. This framing raises the specter of a new strongman being installed to secure assets, rather than a genuine restoration of liberty. "If this is the real motive going forward, then Trump will be strongly tempted to make a deal with some strongman leader, including one coming out of the Maduro regime, who promises to stabilize the country through any means necessary," Mounk argues.

On the legal front, the author takes a pragmatic, if cynical, view. He dismisses the debate over international law as largely academic, stating, "The sad fact is that there is no such thing as legally binding international law." Instead, he argues that history will judge the intervention based on its outcomes, not its legal technicalities. "If the United States succeeds in restoring to power a democratically elected regime that is stable and able to welcome back the millions of refugees currently in exile, then people are not going to worry about the means by which this was accomplished," he writes. However, he warns that failure would be catastrophic: "if the United States puts a new dictator in place and grabs its oil assets, then history will judge this as an unprecedented act of piracy that will set a horrible example for world order."

The author acknowledges the human cost that hangs over every decision. He reminds readers that the intervention is happening in a country where "eight of the country's 28 million inhabitants" have already fled, creating a humanitarian crisis that demands a solution far more complex than a military strike. The reference to the De-Ba'athification process in Iraq serves as a warning here; just as that policy dismantled the Iraqi state and fueled insurgency, a hasty dismantling of Venezuela's security structures without a clear plan for reintegration could lead to similar chaos.

Bottom Line

Mounk's strongest contribution is his refusal to let the narrative stop at the "success" of the capture, forcing a confrontation with the brutal reality of the security apparatus that remains. His argument is most vulnerable, however, in its assumption that the democratic opposition is ready to govern a country that has been stripped of its institutions for a generation. The piece serves as a stark warning: the hardest part of the war begins only after the dictator is gone, and the United States has a poor history of winning that second battle.
