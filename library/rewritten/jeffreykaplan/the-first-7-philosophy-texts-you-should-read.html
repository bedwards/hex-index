Jeffrey Kaplan makes a counterintuitive claim that would stop any busy reader in their tracks: the best way to start philosophy is not by reading the most famous texts, but by skipping the ones that are too difficult or too obscure. He argues that the goal of reading philosophy is not to feel sophisticated, but to actually understand the activity of thinking, a distinction that immediately separates this list from standard academic syllabi.

The Art of the Accessible Start

Kaplan begins by dismantling the traditional hierarchy of ancient philosophy. He suggests starting with Plato rather than Aristotle, not because Plato is superior, but because Aristotle's writing style is a barrier to entry. "Basically everything Aristotle wrote goes like this: let us consider question X. I shall begin by spending 20 pages reviewing the implausible and incomprehensible theories of X put forward by no one you've ever heard of," Kaplan writes. This is a sharp, necessary critique of how philosophy is often taught. By contrast, he champions Plato's Euthyphro for its conversational format, which allows readers to witness the Socratic method in action rather than just reading a dry thesis.

The piece then jumps two millennia to René Descartes, but Kaplan is careful to manage expectations. He acknowledges the gap in time but insists on the necessity of Meditations on First Philosophy to understand the famous "I think, therefore I am" phrase, even noting that the Latin phrase itself doesn't appear in the text. He guides the reader through the specific meditations, advising them to skip the confusing fourth one and focus on the foundational arguments for God's existence and the reality of the external world. This editorial curation is vital; it transforms a daunting 700-year-old text into a manageable roadmap.

The point of reading a philosophical work on topic X is to understand topic X to come to some insights about it. The point is not to feel like a smarty pants or to convince other people or to convince yourself that you're sophisticated or cool.

The Power of Precision and Dialogue

One of the most striking inclusions is a letter from Princess Elizabeth of Bohemia to Descartes. Kaplan highlights how a few sentences from a 17th-century princess introduced the central problem of the philosophy of mind: how an immaterial soul can move a physical body. He describes her inquiry as "a staggeringly good example of a clear precise question that cuts to the heart of a philosophical theory and sort of blows the whole thing up in one move." This choice underscores the author's commitment to clarity over canon; he values the question that breaks a system more than the system itself.

Moving to the Scottish Enlightenment, Kaplan explicitly rejects David Hume's masterpiece, A Treatise of Human Nature, in favor of Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion. He argues that the former is too long and was unread when published, while the latter is "totally readable" and offers a "clear but subtle and utterly riveting" discussion of the problem of evil. This pragmatic approach to reading lists is refreshing. It prioritizes the reader's engagement over the prestige of the text.

The commentary then shifts to language with H.P. Grice's "Logic and Conversation." Kaplan uses the example of a professor recommending a student by only mentioning their excellent handwriting to illustrate how context changes meaning. He explains that language is a "cooperative enterprise," and we understand the hidden meaning because we assume the speaker is cooperating. This section demonstrates how philosophy applies to everyday communication, making the abstract concrete for the listener.

Critics might note that skipping foundational texts like Kant's Critique of Pure Reason entirely risks leaving new readers without the necessary historical context for modern philosophy. However, Kaplan's stance is that starting with incomprehensible texts leads to annoyance rather than learning, a trade-off that seems reasonable for a beginner's guide.

Law, Ethics, and the Uncomfortable Truth

The final selections move into law and applied ethics. Kaplan recommends specific chapters from H.L.A. Hart's The Concept of Law, praising his ability to present an opponent's theory fairly before dismantling it with counterexamples. He calls this "a great clear example of what a counter example to a theory looks like," emphasizing the mechanical skill of philosophical argumentation.

The list concludes with Peter Singer's "Famine, Affluence, and Morality," a paper Kaplan admits might keep readers awake at night. He notes that Singer argues the moral reaction of affluent people to global starvation "cannot be justified" and that our "moral conceptual scheme needs to be altered." This is the piece's emotional climax. Kaplan warns that this is not just a model of clear writing but a text that challenges the reader's entire way of life.

This is the kind of philosophical paper that not only is a model for how clear philosophy should be done but is also the kind of paper that like will make it hard for you to sleep for several days after reading it.

Bottom Line

Kaplan's strongest asset is his ruthless editing of the philosophical canon, prioritizing clarity and engagement over historical completeness. His biggest vulnerability is the potential alienation of readers seeking a rigorous, comprehensive academic foundation, but for a busy audience, his focus on the "activity of doing philosophy" rather than memorizing history is a compelling and necessary approach.
