Jeffrey Kaplan, a philosophy professor at the University of North Carolina at Greensboro, flips the script on academic struggle by framing the most effective study habits not as rigorous discipline, but as a form of "cheating" that is impossible to get caught. His provocative premise—that the difference between a difficult semester and an easy one lies entirely in the student's strategic approach rather than the course's inherent difficulty—offers a refreshing, if slightly cynical, roadmap for the busy learner.

The Art of Strategic "Cheating"

Kaplan begins with a hook that sounds like a warning but functions as a strategy guide. He proposes a method where students categorize course material into "easy stuff" and "hard stuff," then systematically eliminate the hard stuff by leveraging the professor's expertise. "The first step in this method is to divide the course topics into the easy stuff and the hard stuff," Kaplan writes. He argues that while students must still attend lectures and take notes to identify these categories, the real work happens when they translate their gaps in knowledge into specific questions for office hours.

This approach reframes the traditional student-professor dynamic. Instead of viewing office hours as a last resort for confused students, Kaplan suggests using them as a primary study tool to extract answers before the exam. "You take your list of questions and you go to the professor's office hours and you ask them all the questions on the list," he explains. The logic is sound: if a professor explains a concept to you, they have effectively done the heavy lifting of teaching it. Kaplan admits the irony, noting, "Wait that's not cheating that's just taking the course and going to office hours... no it is cheating because it's just like you know finding a student who's already taken the course and getting the answers from them except for you're not getting the answers from another student you're getting the answers from a professor."

Critics might argue that this strategy relies on professors having the time and willingness to engage deeply with every student's pre-packaged list of questions, which may not be feasible in large lecture halls. However, the underlying principle—that proactive engagement is superior to passive cramming—remains robust.

The Calculator Analogy and the Cost of Bad Habits

To illustrate how self-inflicted obstacles make education harder, Kaplan shares a personal anecdote about a statistics exam where calculators were permitted. He describes a student who failed because they forgot their calculator, noting, "The exam was built and designed to be taken with a calculator and so if you try to take that same exam without a calculator then you've just made it way harder impossibly hard in fact." Kaplan uses this to argue that many students voluntarily choose the "hard way" by neglecting basic preparation.

He applies this logic to open-note and open-book exams. The "easy way" involves taking notes throughout the semester so that during the exam, a student can quickly locate information. "If you looked over them before the exam it'll remind you where everything is right," Kaplan says. Conversely, the "hard way" is skipping note-taking or borrowing a friend's notes, which fails to trigger the memory associations needed to find answers quickly. "Reading the notes because someone else wrote them doesn't trigger in your memory all of the the extra details," he observes. This distinction highlights that the difficulty of a course is often a function of the tools the student brings to the table, not the complexity of the material itself.

The Gift of Question Banks and the Danger of Ignoring Instructions

Kaplan extends his argument to courses that provide exam question banks or explicit study guides. He recounts a colleague whose students sat silently during review sessions because they didn't ask the questions that were literally handed to them. "If they had just asked me the questions from the question bank i would have just told them the answers during the review session," Kaplan paraphrases his colleague. He emphasizes that students often ignore these gifts, effectively choosing to struggle.

He shares a recent experience where he emailed his own students a clear list of exam topics and even recorded a video for those who preferred audio. Despite this, a student emailed two days before the exam asking for the same information. "If you don't know what topics are going to be on the exam you have to study all the topics so you just made this exam way harder than it needs to be," Kaplan concludes. The lesson here is that efficiency is a choice. Students who ignore available resources force themselves to study everything, turning a manageable task into a monumental one.

Redefining Self-Care as Academic Discipline

The piece culminates in a sharp critique of the modern concept of "self-care." Kaplan challenges the notion that skipping a reading assignment to scroll on the internet is an act of self-preservation. "One natural thought is that if you're exhausted... and you decide to practice some self-care and so you decide to scroll on the internet or watch a television show... no that's not self-care that's self-sabotage," he asserts.

Instead, Kaplan argues that true self-care is the discipline of doing the work early to reduce future stress. "Actual self-care would be forcing to do the reading the night before class and then forcing yourself to go to class and take notes and then forcing yourself to review those notes after class," he writes. This redefinition is the essay's most powerful move, shifting the focus from immediate gratification to long-term well-being. By doing the hard work upfront, students make their coursework easier, which is the ultimate form of caring for oneself. He even concedes a small point to the indulgent side of self-care: "What about getting guacamole on your burrito... yeah that's awesome that's fine," but maintains that it doesn't replace the structural work of studying.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's argument is a masterclass in reframing academic anxiety as a solvable logistical problem. His strongest point is the radical redefinition of self-care as the proactive management of one's learning process rather than the avoidance of work. The only vulnerability in his thesis is the assumption that all students have the executive function or institutional access to execute these strategies perfectly, but for the motivated reader, the takeaway is clear: the easiest way to pass a course is to stop making it hard for yourself.
