Jeffrey Kaplan delivers a rare clarity on one of philosophy's most impenetrable thinkers, arguing that Immanuel Kant's moral theory is not a rigid set of rules, but a consent-based framework rooted in human dignity. While many introductions drown readers in German terminology, Kaplan cuts through the noise to reveal a startlingly practical test: could the person you are interacting with rationally agree to your plan? This is not just academic history; it is a direct challenge to the utilitarian calculus that dominates modern decision-making.

The Architecture of Duty

Kaplan begins by dismantling the intimidating label of "deontology," noting that the word simply means "relating to duty." He wisely acknowledges the difficulty of engaging with Kant's original texts, opting instead to channel the insights of Onora O'Neal, whose work provides a clearer path to Kant's central principle. "Kant's writing is very difficult uh even after it's been translated from the original German," Kaplan admits, setting the stage for a more accessible interpretation. This pragmatic choice to rely on a secondary summary rather than the primary source is a bold move for a philosophy overview, but it pays off by keeping the focus on the logic of the argument rather than the language of the text.

The core of the piece rests on the "categorical imperative," which Kaplan breaks down into its linguistic components: "Categorical just means unconditional... imperative just means a command or something that you must do." He then narrows this vast concept to a single, powerful formulation: "Never use a person as a mere means." This reduction is the article's strongest asset. It transforms an abstract metaphysical concept into a concrete interpersonal guideline that any busy professional can apply immediately.

Never use a person as a mere means.

The Distinction Between Means and Mere Means

Where the commentary truly shines is in its dissection of the word "mere." Kaplan explains that using someone as a means is often necessary and acceptable, such as interacting with a bank teller to withdraw money. "You're using them to get something else... This is a perfectly fine interaction," he writes. The moral violation only occurs when that interaction excludes the other person's ability to consent to the underlying plan. He defines this violation with precision: "It is to use someone as a mere means is to involve them in a scheme of action to which they could not in principle consent."

This distinction is crucial. It allows for the complexity of modern commerce and social interaction without sacrificing ethical rigor. Kaplan illustrates this with the example of an insincere promise. If you promise to attend a ballet recital with no intention of showing up, you are manipulating the other person's trust. "If the promise is accepted, then the person to whom it was given must be ignorant of what the promiser's intention maxim really is," Kaplan notes. The brilliance of this argument lies in its reliance on the hypothetical consent of the victim. If the victim knew the truth, they would never agree to the scheme. Therefore, the action is morally prohibited, regardless of the outcome.

Critics might argue that this framework is too rigid, failing to account for "white lies" told to spare feelings or protect someone from trauma. However, Kaplan's focus on the maxim—the general rule behind the action—suggests that if a lie cannot be universalized without destroying the concept of trust itself, it remains impermissible under this theory.

The Sheriff Dilemma: Deontology vs. Utilitarianism

The piece reaches its climax by contrasting Kant's theory with utilitarianism, specifically in the famous "sheriff example." In this scenario, a sheriff must decide whether to frame an innocent person to stop a violent riot that would kill many. Utilitarianism, Kaplan reminds us, would likely demand the framing of the innocent person to minimize total suffering. Kant's theory, however, delivers a starkly different verdict. "The person who you would frame could never agree, could never consent to the form of action of framing someone," Kaplan argues. "And therefore, deontology... gets the result that's opposite to the one given by utilitarianism."

This is the piece's most provocative claim: that morality sometimes requires us to do nothing, even when doing nothing leads to greater suffering. "According to this principle, then that behavior is prohibited," Kaplan states, emphasizing that the prohibition holds even in the face of a mob. The logic is that justice cannot be bought with a lie, because the lie destroys the very possibility of consent.

It's not that everyone has to consent to everything. If that was the case, then this moral theory would collapse.

Kaplan is careful to clarify that this is a theory of potential consent, not actual agreement. The test is whether the action could be rationally accepted by all parties if they knew the full truth. This nuance prevents the theory from devolving into a simple popularity contest. It forces the moral agent to look inward at their own intentions and ask if their plan could survive the light of day.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's commentary succeeds by stripping away the academic jargon to reveal Kant's moral theory as a rigorous test of respect for human agency. The strongest part of the argument is its clear distinction between using people as tools and using them as "mere means," a nuance that saves the theory from being impractical in daily life. Its biggest vulnerability, however, is the potential for moral paralysis in extreme emergencies, where the refusal to violate a principle could lead to catastrophic loss of life. For the busy reader, this piece offers a vital counterweight to outcome-based thinking, reminding us that the integrity of our intentions matters just as much as the consequences of our actions.
