Jeffrey Kaplan attempts the impossible: compressing a semester of philosophy into twenty-two minutes without losing the intellectual stakes. His most startling move isn't the speed, but the specific moral terror he uncovers in Peter Singer's work—the claim that buying a coffee is not just a luxury, but a moral failing. For the busy professional trying to navigate a world of competing ethical demands, Kaplan's rapid-fire synthesis offers a rare clarity on why our moral intuitions often clash with our actions.

The Arithmetic of Pleasure

Kaplan begins by dismantling the complexity of Jeremy Bentham's utilitarianism, stripping it down to its rawest form. He explains that Bentham viewed morality as a simple calculation: "we are morally required to do whatever produces the greatest total of pleasure minus pain." This framing is effective because it immediately exposes the theory's cold logic. Kaplan notes that for Bentham, "the game of pushpin is of equal value with the arts of music and poetry if the game of pushpin furnish more pleasure." The point is radical in its simplicity: the quality of the experience doesn't matter, only the quantity. Kaplan argues that this "arithmetic theory of morality" treats everyone's pleasure as equal, creating a system where the only metric that counts is the net sum of happiness.

However, Kaplan quickly pivots to the theory's fragility. He introduces Robert Nozick's "experience machine" thought experiment to show that humans crave reality, not just sensation. Kaplan writes, "there must be something that matters to us, something that's good for a human being, other than pleasure." If we would refuse to plug into a machine that guarantees a life of perfect, pre-programmed joy, then pleasure cannot be the sole measure of a good life. This is a crucial distinction for the modern reader, who often conflates satisfaction with happiness. Kaplan suggests that we value "controlling your life," "actual friendship," and "actually doing things" over the mere illusion of these experiences.

"If it is rational to not opt to be plugged into the experience machine, then it must be that there's a whole bunch of things that are good for people that matter other than just pleasure."

The Radical Demands of Altruism

The commentary shifts from abstract theory to the brutal practicality of Peter Singer's 1972 paper, "Famine, Affluence, and Morality." Kaplan highlights Singer's shocking conclusion: "everyone that you've ever met is a morally bad person." This isn't hyperbole; it is the logical endpoint of applying utilitarian principles to global poverty. Kaplan paraphrases Singer's argument that we are obligated to give away our luxuries—like new clothes or restaurant meals—to organizations like Oxfam or UNICEF because that money could literally save lives from famine. Kaplan emphasizes the severity of this claim: "he thinks that it's not okay to buy coffee at a coffee shop... if you don't give to famine relief you're doing something evil every day."

To ground this abstract duty, Kaplan deploys Singer's famous "shallow pond" analogy. He explains that if you saw a child drowning in a pond, you would wade in and save them, even if it meant ruining your clothes. Kaplan notes that Singer argues the distance to a starving child in another country is not a morally relevant difference. "Because of the existence of these relief agencies you can help them," Kaplan writes, dismissing proximity as an excuse. He further dismantles the idea that "other people" could help, arguing that the presence of bystanders does not absolve the individual of responsibility. "If someone showed up at your house and said I passed by this child... there were all these other people they didn't help either so I'm good right? No you're not good you're a monster," Kaplan paraphrases, capturing the visceral weight of Singer's argument.

Critics might note that this level of demand is psychologically unsustainable for most people, potentially leading to moral burnout rather than increased charity. Yet, Kaplan presents the argument with such force that the discomfort feels intentional, forcing the listener to confront their own complicity.

The Shift to Duty

As the video accelerates toward Immanuel Kant, Kaplan signals a fundamental shift from consequences to intentions. He introduces the concept of a "maxim," defining it as an intention stripped of specific details. "When determining whether an action is right or wrong you figure out what the maxim is that that action is based on," Kaplan explains. This sets the stage for deontology, a theory where the morality of an action is judged by the rule behind it, not the outcome. Kaplan's transition is swift, moving from the "arithmetic" of Bentham to the rigid rules of Kant, hinting that the consequences-based approach has a fatal flaw.

That flaw is illustrated through the "sheriff" counter-example, where utilitarianism seems to demand the framing of an innocent person to prevent a riot. Kaplan describes the scenario: the sheriff knows an innocent person is not the criminal, but if they don't frame them, a mob will kill hundreds. "According to utilitarianism what is the sheriff supposed to do? It's pretty simple... frame the innocent person in order to avoid the riot," Kaplan states. He then delivers the knockout blow to the theory's intuitive appeal: "Mccloskey thinks that this isn't right like it's just obvious that it's wrong for the sheriff to frame an innocent person." This counter-example suggests that some moral rules—like "do not kill the innocent"—might be absolute, regardless of the mathematical calculation of pain and pleasure.

"It just doesn't make sense... to hang people that do this thing that doesn't cause pain."

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's condensed course succeeds not by simplifying philosophy, but by exposing the high stakes of ethical reasoning. His strongest move is linking Bentham's abstract calculus to Singer's radical demand for charity, forcing the listener to realize that their daily choices have moral weight they often ignore. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its brevity; the transition from utilitarianism to Kant feels rushed, leaving the listener with the tension of conflicting moral frameworks rather than a resolved answer. For the busy reader, the takeaway is clear: the question of what we owe each other is not settled, and the math of morality is far more dangerous than it appears.
