Jeffrey Kaplan attempts the impossible: compressing a semester's worth of ethical philosophy into a single video lecture, and in doing so, exposes the fragile foundations of the moral code we take for granted. His most provocative claim is that our modern insistence on humility and fairness isn't a discovery of universal truth, but the result of a historical "slave revolt" by the powerless against the strong. For the busy professional navigating a complex world, this isn't just academic history; it's a challenge to the very software running your decision-making processes.

The Genealogy of Morals

Kaplan begins by dismantling the Aristotelian view that morality leads to happiness, pivoting sharply to Friedrich Nietzsche's radical assertion that "don't be moral because morality is for losers." This is a jarring opening, but Kaplan quickly clarifies that Nietzsche isn't advocating for chaos; he is dissecting the origins of "conventional morality," which Kaplan defines as "the accepted moral code of a society" rather than any objective truth. The author argues that Nietzsche's project is a "genealogy" designed to show us that our current values—kindness, meekness, fairness—are not inherent goods but the result of a specific historical power shift.

Kaplan explains that in ancient Rome, "good" simply meant "aristocratic" or "powerful," while "bad" meant "common" or "plain." He writes, "the word good originally just meant aristocratic or rich or powerful and the word bad had no inculpatory implication." This etymological shift is the crux of Nietzsche's argument: the "slave revolt" was not a physical war, but a moral one where the powerless redefined their weakness as virtue and the strength of the nobility as evil. Kaplan notes that Nietzsche believes once we understand this origin story, we might realize that "those are sort of silly" and feel free to abandon the moral code handed down by society.

Nietzsche provocatively labels this genealogy the slave revolt, where the powerless convinced everyone that being aggressive or dominating is actually evil.

This framing is effective because it strips morality of its divine or natural aura, presenting it instead as a human construct born of resentment. Critics might note that Nietzsche's historical account of the "slave revolt" is often viewed by modern historians as an oversimplification of complex social dynamics, yet the psychological insight remains potent: our values are often inherited, not chosen.

The Search for Objective Truth

Having destabilized conventional morality, Kaplan moves to the question of whether any objective moral facts exist at all. He introduces David Hume, the Scottish Enlightenment philosopher, who applies a rigorous empiricist principle: "only believe in the existence of stuff that you or someone else trustworthy have experienced." Kaplan paraphrases Hume's reasoning, noting that we believe in trees or electrons because they explain our experiences, but we cannot find "moral badness" in the world itself.

Kaplan quotes Hume directly to illustrate this gap: "do you experience the wrongness... the badness is the vice entirely escapes you as long as you consider the object." The argument is that when we witness a murder, we see the knife and the blood, but the "vice" is not an object we can touch; it is a "sentiment of disapprobation" that arises within us. Kaplan concludes that if morality is just a feeling "it lies in yourself not in the object," then believing in objective moral facts is irrational.

This is a formidable argument for the skeptic, but Kaplan immediately pivots to the theological counter-argument often used to save objective morality. He outlines John Locke's position that moral facts stem from God, arguing that because God created humans, humans are God's property, and therefore "we must not harm someone else's property." Kaplan acknowledges that this argument might be logically valid, but he identifies a fatal flaw: it assumes the very thing it tries to prove.

You can't use an argument of this style in order to show where all objective moral facts come from in the first place.

Kaplan points out that Locke's argument relies on the prior moral fact that "we must not harm someone else's property." If you need to assume a moral rule to prove the existence of moral rules, you haven't explained their origin. This leads Kaplan to the ancient dilemma posed by Plato in the Euthyphro dialogue, asking whether things are good because God loves them, or if God loves them because they are good. Kaplan summarizes the dilemma sharply: if God loves them because they are good, then goodness exists independently of God; if they are good only because God loves them, then morality is arbitrary.

The Unresolved Tension

Kaplan's coverage leaves the audience in a state of productive uncertainty. He has successfully shown that conventional morality is a historical construct (Nietzsche), that objective moral facts are not empirically verifiable (Hume), and that divine command theory cannot explain the origin of those facts without circular reasoning (Locke/Plato). The author admits that he must "go back to David Hume" to address the problems with his own argument, acknowledging that the debate is far from settled.

The strength of Kaplan's approach is his refusal to offer a tidy conclusion. Instead, he forces the reader to confront the possibility that "there are no objective moral facts" while simultaneously showing why that conclusion is difficult to accept. He writes, "if there were objective moral facts where would they come from," a question that remains the central anxiety of modern ethics.

If the reason that the gods love them is because they're already virtuous, then what made them virtuous in the first place?

Critics might argue that Kaplan's rapid-fire delivery risks oversimplifying the nuanced defenses of moral realism that have emerged in contemporary philosophy, such as the idea that moral facts are like mathematical truths—abstract but real. However, for a condensed overview, his ability to connect ancient etymology with Enlightenment empiricism and theological logic is impressive.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's commentary succeeds not by providing answers, but by rigorously dismantling the easy ones. His strongest move is exposing the circular logic in theological arguments for morality, leaving the reader with the unsettling realization that our moral compass may be entirely self-made. The biggest vulnerability is the lack of a constructive alternative; if Nietzsche is right and Hume is right, the reader is left with a void that the video promises to fill in a future installment, but leaves them hanging for now.
