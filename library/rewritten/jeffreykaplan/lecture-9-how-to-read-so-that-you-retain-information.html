Jeffrey Kaplan, a philosophy professor at the University of North Carolina at Greensboro, makes a counterintuitive claim that challenges the very nature of how we consume text: true reading speed is not about moving your eyes faster, but about slowing your mind down to a crawl. He argues that the most efficient way to retain information is not to skim, but to engage in a rigorous, time-consuming process of marginalia that forces the brain to synthesize meaning paragraph by paragraph. For busy professionals who feel they are drowning in information but starving for understanding, Kaplan's method offers a surprising solution: the only way to save time is to spend more of it thinking.

The Muscle Memory Trap

Kaplan begins by dismantling the common assumption that reading is a passive activity. He compares the experience of reading without focus to the phenomenon of "highway hypnosis," where a driver arrives at a destination with no memory of the journey. "If you drive a lot then it all sort of becomes muscle memory and you don't have to do it in like a in like an engaged focused way," Kaplan observes. He posits that this autopilot mode is the enemy of retention. When we simply "pass our eyes over the words," we are not learning; we are merely scanning.

The author's core argument is that retention requires a forced interaction with the "semantic content" of a text. He defines this simply as the meaning behind the words. To achieve this, he rejects the vague advice of "try harder" in favor of a specific, mechanical procedure. This procedural approach is the piece's most distinctive feature. It transforms reading from a consumption activity into a production activity. "There is no way to summarize a text like this without thinking through the material and understanding it and retaining it," Kaplan asserts. This is a powerful reframing: the act of writing the summary is not a byproduct of reading; it is the mechanism of reading itself.

"If you want to use your time efficiently so that you have time to go sailing or play dodgeball or whatever... then you got to do this."

The Cumulative Summary Procedure

Kaplan outlines a specific algorithm for marginalia that escalates in difficulty as the text progresses. The method starts simply: read the first paragraph and write a one-sentence summary in the margin. The constraint is key; one cannot summarize six sentences into six sentences without thinking. The compression forces the reader to identify the central core idea.

However, the method evolves. By the third paragraph, the reader must write two sentences: one summarizing the new paragraph, and a second one that synthesizes the first two paragraphs into a single sentence. "What that forces you to do is it forces you to connect these ideas," Kaplan explains. As the reader moves to the fourth paragraph, the first margin note must condense the ideas of paragraphs one through three, while the second note handles the fourth paragraph alone. This cumulative burden ensures that the reader is constantly reviewing and integrating new information with what came before, preventing the isolation of ideas.

Critics might note that this method is incredibly labor-intensive and may not be scalable for every type of text, such as a novel or a light news article where deep synthesis is unnecessary. Kaplan anticipates this objection, arguing that the time invested upfront eliminates the need for massive cramming sessions later. He presents a hypothetical calculation: spending three hours per reading to achieve an "A minus" understanding versus spending two hours per reading to achieve a "D plus," followed by twenty-five hours of cramming to reach a "C plus." The math, he insists, favors the slower, deeper method.

"This method took you 65 hours over the course of the semester and it generated a c plus this method took 60 hours and it generated an a which is more efficient."

The Long-Term Payoff

The strength of Kaplan's argument lies in his personal testimony as evidence. He recounts his own academic journey, from a high school student in 2002 to a professor who graduated in the top two percent of his class at Williams College and earned a PhD from Berkeley. He attributes his success not to innate genius but to this specific habit. He shares a vivid anecdote from a 2006 economics course where he was the only student able to define economics on the first day because he had actively processed the definition in the margin of his textbook.

He describes the mental work he did at that moment: "I rephrased it in my own words and then I just sort of stopped reading and thought about it for a minute." He connected the abstract concept of "scarce resources" to tangible examples like money and sand, creating a mental hook that allowed him to recall the definition fifteen years later. "I remember the definition of economics from that course 15 years later," he states with confidence. This long-term retention is the ultimate proof of concept for his method. While other students "read the chapter" and forgot it, Kaplan's active engagement created a durable memory.

"I didn't plan to tell you this story during this lecture video... I decided to tell you this story about my college economics course in 2006... I remember the definition."

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's argument is compelling because it reframes efficiency not as speed, but as the depth of processing. The strongest part of his coverage is the specific, escalating algorithm of marginalia, which provides a concrete tool rather than abstract advice. However, the method's biggest vulnerability is the high barrier to entry; it requires a level of discipline and time commitment that may feel prohibitive to the average reader. For those willing to invest the initial effort, the payoff is a transformative shift from passive consumption to active mastery.
