Jeffrey Kaplan tackles one of the most persistent myths in modern philosophy: the idea that a computer running the right code is literally conscious. His most distinctive move is not just explaining the mechanics of the argument, but exposing the sheer absurdity of the "strong AI" position by tracing it back to its functionalist roots, arguing that if you believe software equals mind, you must accept that a machine made of beer cans and windmills could feel pain.

The Target: Software as Soul

Kaplan begins by dismantling the target before the thought experiment even starts. He identifies "strong AI" not as a vague hope for the future, but as a specific philosophical claim that the human mind is merely a program running on the hardware of the brain. He writes, "This view that is strong AI has the consequence that there is nothing essentially biological about the human mind." This framing is crucial because it shifts the debate from engineering to ontology. Kaplan argues that under this theory, the physical substrate doesn't matter; only the logical structure does.

He illustrates the extremity of this claim with a vivid hypothetical: "If you made a computer out of old beer cans powered by windmills, if it had the right program, it would have to have a mind." This is a powerful rhetorical device. It forces the reader to confront the implication that consciousness is entirely substrate-independent. The argument lands hard because it strips away the mystique of the brain, reducing it to a mere container for code. However, critics might note that strong AI proponents often argue for biological plausibility rather than literal beer-can consciousness, suggesting Kaplan may be attacking a slightly starker version of the theory than what is held by most contemporary researchers.

"The point is not that for all we know, it might have thoughts and feelings, but rather that it must have thoughts and feelings because that is all there is to having thoughts and feelings."

The Distinction: Syntax vs. Semantics

To build his case, Kaplan meticulously defines the terms of engagement, specifically the difference between syntax (form) and semantics (meaning). He uses a Hebrew example to show that one can manipulate symbols perfectly without understanding what they represent. "You can know all sorts of things about these symbols... like this symbol right here is taller than the other symbols... You can know all sorts of stuff about these symbols without knowing their meaning," he explains. This distinction is the bedrock of the entire argument. Kaplan effectively argues that computers are, by definition, syntax manipulators. They process the shape and order of symbols, not their content.

He reinforces this by invoking Alan Turing, the father of computer science, to describe the theoretical machine that underpins all digital computing. Kaplan notes that Turing's machine "doesn't know that these ones and zeros represent numbers... It doesn't know anything about numbers. All it knows is that when it sees a one, it ignores the one and moves on to the next cell." This is a masterful simplification of complex computer science for a general audience. By grounding the argument in the actual mechanics of how a Turing machine operates, Kaplan makes it impossible to claim that the machine "understands" in the human sense. The machine produces the correct output, but the process is devoid of intent or comprehension.

The Chinese Room: The Final Blow

With the definitions set, Kaplan pivots to the core of John Searle's thought experiment. The setup is simple: a person who speaks no Chinese is locked in a room with a rulebook. When Chinese characters are slipped under the door, the person follows the rulebook to manipulate the symbols and slip the correct Chinese characters back out. To an outside observer, the room appears to understand Chinese. But the person inside knows nothing of the language.

Kaplan writes, "Even though this damn machine didn't understand anything about addition, didn't know anything about numbers, didn't have any meaning or semantics at all... it successfully added three and two by just following some basic instructions." He extends this logic to the Chinese room: the system produces the right output, but there is no understanding inside. The argument is that strong AI confuses the simulation of understanding with the reality of understanding. Kaplan's commentary here is sharp; he highlights that the "mind" in the strong AI view is just a set of instructions being followed, which, as the Chinese room shows, is insufficient for genuine thought.

"It's just him saying that there really are scholars, theorists, researchers, whatever, who actually believe in strong AI... Herbert Simon of Carnegie Melon University says that we already have machines that can literally think."

Kaplan uses Searle's own snark to underscore the disconnect between the theory and reality. He points out that if functionalism were true, then the distinction between a human mind and a sophisticated calculator would vanish. The argument holds up well against the specific definition of strong AI Kaplan provides, though it leaves open the question of whether "understanding" is an emergent property of complex systems that the Chinese room scenario fails to capture.

Bottom Line

Kaplan's commentary is a masterclass in making dense philosophical concepts accessible without losing their rigor. His strongest asset is the clear, step-by-step deconstruction of functionalism, showing how the "beer can" logic leads to absurd conclusions. The argument's biggest vulnerability lies in its reliance on a specific definition of "understanding" that some cognitive scientists might argue is too narrow, ignoring the possibility that meaning could emerge from the complexity of the system as a whole. Readers should watch for how this debate evolves as AI models become increasingly indistinguishable from human conversation, testing the limits of the Chinese room's relevance.
