Jeffrey Kaplan takes a mundane question—what is a jet ski good for?—and uses it to dismantle the complex architecture of utilitarianism, revealing a moral framework that reduces the universe's worth to a simple arithmetic of pleasure and pain. This is not a dry recitation of 19th-century philosophy; it is a practical guide to understanding why we might be morally required to do the "right" thing even when it feels wrong, all by tracing a chain of means and ends back to a single, intrinsic good.

The Jet Ski Test and the End Goal

Kaplan begins by stripping away the layers of instrumental value that clutter our daily decision-making. He walks the reader through a familiar chain: money buys a jet ski, the jet ski provides a ride, and the ride provides pleasure. But he stops there, insisting that the chain cannot go on forever. "Pleasure is just good," Kaplan writes, "it's good for you to feel pleasure it doesn't get you something else." This distinction between something being good as a means versus good as an end is the cornerstone of his argument. By isolating pleasure as the only intrinsic good, Kaplan sets the stage for a radical simplification of ethics.

This approach is effective because it forces the reader to confront the ultimate "why" behind their actions. If money, law degrees, and jet skis are merely tools, what is the tool for? Kaplan's answer is stark: "The hedonist is someone who thinks that the one and only one thing that's good for a person as an end is pleasure and the one and only one thing that's bad for a person as an end is pain." Critics might argue that this ignores other intrinsic goods like virtue, knowledge, or beauty, but Kaplan's clarity here is undeniable. He isn't just defining a preference; he is defining the very currency of moral value.

"Hedonism is not competing with utilitarianism these are two different moral theories they're not even answering the same question."

The Arithmetic of Morality

Once the value of pleasure is established, Kaplan introduces the mechanism for weighing it against the pain of others. This is where the theory shifts from individual psychology to collective ethics through the concept of aggregation. Kaplan explains that to judge a situation involving multiple people, we must simply sum up the individual scores. "Aggregation is one answer and it says you add addition that's how you figure out how good or bad a whole outcome is." He illustrates this with a hypothetical scenario where one person's massive pain is outweighed by the aggregate pleasure of several others, resulting in a net positive score.

This mathematical framing is the theory's greatest strength and its most controversial feature. It treats human experience as quantifiable data points. "If you can quantify goodness right if you can quantify pleasure or pain... this scenario according to aggregation is 11 points good." The logic is seductive in its simplicity: if the total sum of happiness increases, the action is morally right. However, this raises immediate red flags for those concerned with minority rights. If the math works out, does the suffering of the one truly not matter? Kaplan acknowledges the existence of alternative aggregation methods, such as prioritizing the worst-off person, but he firmly anchors utilitarianism in the additive model.

The Consequentialist Mandate

The final piece of the puzzle is consequentialism, the bridge between what is good and what we must do. Kaplan clarifies that utilitarianism is a specific flavor of this broader theory. "Consequentialism just like utilitarianism is a moral theory it's just that consequentialism is a rather more vague moral theory and utilitarianism is a more specific version of consequentialism." He uses a provocative example of breaking a promise to take drugs to illustrate the point: if the outcome of breaking the promise yields a better total score of pleasure over pain, and no one is the wiser, then consequentialism dictates that breaking the promise is the moral choice.

This is where the rubber meets the road for the busy reader. Kaplan's argument suggests that our moral obligations are not based on rules, promises, or duties, but strictly on the results. "According to consequentialism it's the outcome that matters." This creates a high-stakes environment for decision-making where the intent is irrelevant. A counterargument worth considering is that this view could justify horrific acts if the numbers align, a critique Kaplan hints at but does not fully resolve in this introductory overview. He leaves the reader with the realization that utilitarianism is a machine: input the pleasure and pain of everyone involved, and the output is the only moral action available.

"Utilitarianism is the theory that we are morally required to do whatever produces the greatest total of pleasure - pain and we're talking about the greatest total for everyone in the universe."

Bottom Line

Kaplan's commentary succeeds by demystifying utilitarianism, breaking it down into three digestible components: hedonism, aggregation, and consequentialism. The strongest part of his argument is the clear distinction between what is good for an individual and what is the right thing to do for the group. The biggest vulnerability remains the theory's reliance on quantifying human experience and its potential to override individual rights for the sake of the greater sum. For the time-pressed reader, this is the essential framework to understand why modern policy often prioritizes the "greater good" over individual sentiment.
