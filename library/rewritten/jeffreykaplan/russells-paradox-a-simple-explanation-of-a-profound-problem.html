Jeffrey Kaplan doesn't just explain a mathematical puzzle; he dismantles the very foundation of how we understand reality. In a brisk eight-minute journey through the history of logic, Kaplan argues that the solution to Russell's Paradox isn't a clever fix, but a fundamental failure of the logicist project itself. For busy minds trying to grasp why the rules of mathematics might be broken, this is the rare piece that treats the paradox not as a curiosity, but as a crisis that remains unresolved.

The Architecture of Numbers

Kaplan begins by stripping away the familiar to reveal the abstract nature of mathematics. He distinguishes sharply between the physical symbol and the concept it represents, noting, "the number itself is something that no one has ever seen or touched but we seem to know things about." This distinction is crucial because it sets the stage for the logical revolution that followed. He traces the shift from Immanuel Kant's view of math as a human construction to the German and English philosophers who insisted on objectivity.

As Jeffrey Kaplan puts it, "according to logicism mathematics is a branch of logic and the most basic type of math which is arithmetic could be reduced to just first order basic logic and set theory." This reductionist dream was the engine of early 20th-century mathematics. The argument is compelling because it promises a unified theory of truth, where numbers are merely collections of objects. Kaplan explains that Georg Cantor invented set theory to prove that "some infinities were larger than other infinities," a concept that sounds wild but is mathematically rigorous. The author's ability to simplify this into the idea that "a set is a collection of objects" makes the subsequent collapse of the system all the more dramatic.

Critics might note that by focusing so heavily on "naive set theory," Kaplan glosses over the sophisticated axiomatic systems developed later to patch these holes. However, his point is that the intuitive logic we use every day is what leads to the contradiction, not just the formal math.

"A set is a many that allows itself to be thought of as a one."

The Rules of the Game

To understand the explosion, Kaplan carefully lays out the rules of the game before pulling the trigger. He lists ten rules of naive set theory, from unrestricted composition to the ability of sets to contain other sets. He uses vivid, modern examples to ground the abstract, suggesting that a set could contain "LeBron James the four-time NBA champion and the top half of the Eiffel tower." This juxtaposition highlights the absurdity of the rules: anything imaginable can be a member.

The tension builds as Kaplan introduces the eleventh rule: "sets can contain themselves." He illustrates this with the set of all sets, which must contain itself because it is a set. But then he pivots to the specific condition that breaks the system. He asks the reader to consider "all the sets that do not contain themselves." This is the pivot point where the logic turns inward and consumes itself. The author's pacing here is masterful; he leads the reader through the mundane rules only to reveal that one of them generates a "terrible problem."

As Jeffrey Kaplan writes, "the logical problem that Russell discovers in 1901 concerns one or more of the rules of set theory." He argues that if you create a set of all sets that do not contain themselves, you hit a wall: if the set contains itself, it shouldn't; if it doesn't, it must. The argument lands because it shows that the very definition of a set is self-contradictory under these rules. This is not a calculation error; it is a structural flaw in the foundation of logic.

The Unresolved Crisis

Kaplan's most distinctive claim is that the historical solutions to this paradox are inadequate. While mathematicians like Russell and Whitehead developed complex type theories to avoid the contradiction, Kaplan contends that they merely sidestepped the issue rather than solving it. He asserts, "Russell himself and many other mathematicians thought that they could solve this paradox but I argue that they can't and they don't." This is a bold stance that challenges the standard narrative of mathematical progress.

He explains that the logicists thought numbers were simply sets: "the number one just is the set of all singleton sets." But with the paradox, this definition collapses. The author suggests that the paradox reveals a deep incompatibility between our language and the structure of reality. The argument is effective because it refuses to offer a comforting resolution. Instead, it leaves the reader with the unsettling feeling that the bedrock of science is cracked.

A counterargument worth considering is that modern axiomatic set theory (like ZFC) successfully avoids this paradox by restricting how sets can be formed, effectively making Kaplan's "naive" rules obsolete rather than proving them unsolvable. Yet, Kaplan's point remains that the intuitive, natural-language version of set theory—the one we actually use to think—is fundamentally broken.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's commentary succeeds by refusing to treat Russell's Paradox as a solved historical footnote, instead presenting it as an active crisis in the philosophy of mathematics. The strongest part of the argument is his insistence that the paradox exposes a flaw in the very concept of "set" as we intuitively understand it. Its biggest vulnerability is the dismissal of formal axiomatic solutions as mere workarounds, which may oversimplify the success of modern logic. Readers should watch for how this paradox continues to influence debates in computer science and artificial intelligence, where self-referential loops remain a critical challenge.
