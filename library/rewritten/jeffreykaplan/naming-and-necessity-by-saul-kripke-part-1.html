In a field often dominated by dense abstraction, Jeffrey Kaplan delivers a rare moment of clarity, reframing a 1970 philosophical revolution not as a dry academic dispute, but as a fundamental correction to how we understand language itself. The piece's most striking claim is that the very way we think about names—treating them as shorthand for descriptions—is not just slightly off, but fundamentally broken, a mistake that has persisted for decades in the minds of even the most brilliant thinkers.

The Collapse of the Cluster

Kaplan sets the stage by transporting the reader back to a cold January in New Jersey, where a thirty-year-old Saul Kripke stood before the Princeton philosophy department to dismantle the prevailing orthodoxy. The dominant theory at the time, championed by John Searle, was descriptivism. Kaplan explains that this theory posited that a proper name like "Abraham Lincoln" is merely synonymous with a cluster of descriptions: the 16th president, the issuer of the Emancipation Proclamation, the man who freed the slaves. As Kaplan puts it, "descriptivism is the idea that proper names like Abraham Lincoln are really just synonymous they really have the same meaning as descriptions like the 16th president of the United States." This framing is effective because it immediately grounds a high-level metaphysical debate in a concrete, relatable example.

However, Kaplan highlights the fragility of this view. If the name is just a bundle of descriptions, then if Lincoln had lost the 1860 election, he wouldn't be Lincoln. Kaplan notes the absurdity of this implication: "Abraham Lincoln could have actually lost the election of 1860 he wouldn't be the 16th president of the United States but he would still be Abraham Lincoln." This is the crux of the attack. The theory fails because it conflates what we know about a person with who that person is. Kaplan writes, "the main thing to understand for now is this Abraham Lincoln is a guy and we could describe him... but this doesn't mean the same thing as this." The editorial strength here lies in how Kaplan strips away the jargon to reveal the logical inconsistency: a name must stick to the person, not the properties they happen to hold.

If the name Abraham Lincoln just means by definition the person who was the 16th president of the United States and who issued the Emancipation Proclamation... then you should be able to know that Abraham Lincoln did these things a priori.

The Rigidity of Reference

Kaplan then dissects Searle's six theses, methodically showing how each collapses under the weight of reality. The most damaging blow comes from the concept of necessity. Kaplan explains that under descriptivism, statements about a person's achievements would have to be necessarily true—true in every possible world. Kaplan argues this is false: "it's not necessarily true that Abraham Lincoln was the 16th president of the United States... maybe he just stayed in Kentucky where he was born... and never became a politician or anything like that." This is a powerful rhetorical move. By asking the reader to imagine a world where Lincoln remains a humble rail-splitter, Kaplan makes the abstract concept of "possible worlds" instantly graspable.

The author introduces the concept of the "rigid designator" to solve this puzzle. A name, Kaplan explains, points to the same individual regardless of what they do or what properties they possess. "Kripke's point is just that associated with the name Aristotle there's a whole cluster of descriptions but it's not necessary that Aristotle did any of that stuff it could have been that Aristotle never showed up to Plato's Academy." This distinction is vital. It shifts the focus from the content of our knowledge to the mechanism of our reference. Critics might note that this relies heavily on the reader accepting the philosophical framework of "possible worlds," which can feel counterintuitive to those who prefer strict empiricism. Yet, Kaplan's use of the Aristotle example—imagining him as a ditch digger—successfully bridges that gap.

The Failure of Uniqueness

The commentary then moves to the second thesis: that the cluster of descriptions must uniquely identify the individual. Kaplan dismantles this with a dose of humor and realism. He points out that most people cannot uniquely identify historical figures based on a cluster of descriptions. "For example Cicero was the man who denounced Cataline the average person according to this... is saying something like the man who denounced Cataline and thus has picked out a certain man uniquely." Kaplan observes that in reality, people often have vague associations. "In fact most people when they think of Cicero just think of a famous Roman orator without any pretension to think either that there is only one famous Roman orator or that one must know something else about Cicero to have a reference for the name."

Kaplan extends this to modern figures, using Richard Feynman as a case study. He mocks the idea that the average person can differentiate Feynman from other physicists through a precise cluster of descriptions. "Everyone here I'm sure can State the contents of one of fineman's theories so as to differentiate him from Gil man... however the man in the street not possessing these abilities may still use the name finan when asked he will say well he's a physicist or something." This is a brilliant critique of the theory's elitism. It suggests that descriptivism only works for experts who have done the homework, not for the actual users of language. The argument holds up because it exposes a disconnect between philosophical theory and linguistic practice.

The Gödel Thought Experiment

Finally, Kaplan introduces the most famous counter-example: the Gödel-Schmidt case. This fictional scenario imagines that the famous mathematician Kurt Gödel did not actually prove the incompleteness of arithmetic, but rather stole the work from a man named Schmidt. If this were true, would we still refer to Gödel as the prover? Kaplan writes, "I hope that Professor girdle is not present okay that sounds like a joke but I don't think it's a joke right so this super famous mathematician kurt girdle kpy is giving this lecture in 1970 in Princeton and he's saying like I'm going to make up this story about girdle." This anecdote serves as the knockout punch. Even if the cluster of descriptions ("the man who proved incompleteness") fits Schmidt, the name "Gödel" still refers to Gödel. The name is not tied to the description; it is tied to the person.

Associated with the name Aristotle there's a whole cluster of descriptions but it's not necessary that Aristotle did any of that stuff it could have been that Aristotle never showed up to Plato's Academy he never learned from Plato he never did any of the stuff he never wrote any of the books he could have just been like a ditch digger that's possible.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's commentary succeeds by transforming a dense philosophical lecture into a compelling narrative about the nature of identity and language. The strongest part of the argument is the relentless dismantling of the idea that names are merely shorthand for descriptions, using vivid, accessible examples like Lincoln and Feynman to prove that reference is rigid, not descriptive. The biggest vulnerability lies in the reliance on the "possible worlds" framework, which, while explained well, remains a metaphysical leap for some readers. However, the piece's ultimate verdict is clear: names do not describe; they designate, and understanding this distinction is crucial for any serious engagement with language and logic.
