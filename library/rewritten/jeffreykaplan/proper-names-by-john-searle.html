Jeffrey Kaplan takes a familiar philosophical puzzle—what makes a name a name?—and injects it with a surprising twist: the very act of teaching a name might require us to abandon the theory that names have no meaning beyond the object they point to. By dissecting John Searle's 1958 paper, Kaplan reveals a thinker caught in a fascinating tug-of-war between two giants of logic, ultimately suggesting that even the simplest names carry a hidden weight of description.

The Mill-Frege Tug-of-War

Kaplan begins by setting the stage with a personal anecdote, noting that while Searle was "an old man" on his dissertation committee, his 1958 paper proves he "used to be pretty good at this philosophy thing." This framing is effective; it humanizes the abstract debate while signaling that the text ahead is a masterclass in precision. The core conflict Kaplan outlines is between John Stuart Mill, who believed a name is just a label for an object, and Gottlob Frege, who argued names have a "sense"—a layer of meaning like a description—that sits between the word and the thing.

Kaplan explains that Searle initially seems to lean toward Frege. He argues that we learn names by identifying objects, either by pointing (ostension) or by describing features. "In both cases we identify the object in virtue of certain of its characteristics," Kaplan writes, noting that even pointing is just a way of picking out specific features. This is a crucial insight: the method of learning a name seems to bind the name to a description. If this were the whole story, Mill's theory would fail, and Frege would win.

"It's not obvious that whatever is needed to teach or learn a name in the first place needs to stay attached to that name throughout the life of the name."

Here, Kaplan highlights Searle's pivot. Searle argues that the description used to teach a name isn't part of the name's permanent meaning. He uses the example of Aristotle: if we teach the name by saying he was born in Stagira, but later discover he was born in Thebes, we don't say the name "Aristotle" now refers to someone else. We just say our description was wrong. Kaplan summarizes this well, noting that "once you've learned the meaning of the name Mill's Theory ends up being right." This is a strong move by Searle, as it protects the stability of names against factual errors. Critics might note, however, that this assumes a clear line between "teaching" and "meaning" that isn't always so distinct in how we actually use language in the wild.

The Problem of Non-Existence

The argument takes a sharper turn when Kaplan introduces the problem of names that refer to nothing, like "Zeus." If Mill is right and a name's meaning is just its object, then "Zeus" should be meaningless because Zeus doesn't exist. Yet, the sentence "Zeus did not exist" is clearly meaningful and true. Kaplan explains how Mill tries to save his theory by claiming such sentences are actually about the name itself, not the object. But Kaplan shows how Searle dismantles this.

Searle points out that if someone says "Aristotle did not exist," they aren't merely saying the name "Aristotle" has no reference. They are talking about the specific historical figure. Kaplan writes, "if you were to find some guy in Hoboken New Jersey in 1903 who happened to have the name Aristotle you wouldn't thereby disprove someone who had said Aristotle did not exist." This distinction is vital. It proves that the meaning of the name isn't just the object, but a cluster of descriptions that define which object we are talking about.

"We say of Cerberus and Zeus that neither of them existed without meaning that no object ever bore these names but only that certain kinds descriptions of objects never existed and bore these names."

This is the paper's turning point. Kaplan argues that Searle forces us back to a Fregean view: names must have a "sense" (a description) to make sense of non-existence claims. The name "Zeus" works because we have a description of the king of gods, even if no real person fits that description. The logic is tight, but it leaves the reader wondering: if names are just shorthand for descriptions, how do we handle the fact that descriptions can be vague or contradictory?

The Role of Presupposition

To resolve the tension between having a "sense" and still referring directly to an object, Kaplan introduces Searle's concept of "presupposition." This is where the commentary shines, translating dense linguistic theory into plain English. Kaplan uses the example of Beyonce: "Beyonce left Destiny's Child" presupposes she was a member, but doesn't assert it as the main point of the sentence.

Kaplan explains that Searle uses this to argue that proper names refer without needing to assert their descriptive content every time. "Unlike demonstratives a proper name refers without presupposing any stage setting or any special contextual conditions surrounding the utterance of the expression," Kaplan writes. This is a sophisticated move. It suggests that while names have a sense (a cluster of descriptions), they function differently than descriptions do. They carry their meaning in the background, allowing us to talk about the object directly.

"All of this points back towards Frege's view something like the view that a name always has a sense some kind of description or group of descriptions associated with it and then sometimes hopefully most of the time the name also has a reference."

Kaplan's analysis here is compelling because it doesn't just pick a side; it synthesizes the two. He shows that Searle isn't rejecting Mill entirely but refining the idea of how names work. The name has a sense that anchors it, but the reference is the real workhorse. A counterargument worth considering is whether this "cluster of descriptions" is stable enough to handle cases where our understanding of a figure changes radically over centuries, potentially breaking the link between the name and its original sense.

Bottom Line

Kaplan's commentary on Searle's "Proper Names" is a masterful guide through a labyrinth of logic, successfully showing how a 1958 paper still holds the key to modern debates about language. The strongest part of the argument is the demonstration that names cannot be mere labels; they must carry descriptive weight to explain how we talk about things that don't exist. Its biggest vulnerability lies in the practical application: if names are just clusters of descriptions, how do we resolve conflicts when those clusters change or contradict? Readers should watch for how this theory applies to modern contexts, like AI or digital identities, where the "object" of a name might be entirely constructed.

"We say of Cerberus and Zeus that neither of them existed without meaning that no object ever bore these names but only that certain kinds descriptions of objects never existed and bore these names."

The takeaway is clear: names are not just tags; they are complex tools that balance description and reference, a balance Searle spent a lifetime refining and Kaplan has now made accessible.
