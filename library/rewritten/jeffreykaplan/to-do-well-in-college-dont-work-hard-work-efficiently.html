Most advice for college success screams 'grind harder,' but Jeffrey Kaplan flips the script with a counterintuitive thesis: the path to an A isn't about more hours, but about strategic compression. In a landscape saturated with generic productivity tips, Kaplan offers a granular, mathematical breakdown of why 'working hard' often leads to failure, while a specific, disciplined workflow yields top grades in less time.

The Math of Efficiency

Kaplan begins by dismantling the cultural myth of the overworked student. "In order to get good grades, great grades in your college courses, you don't need to work hard. What you need to do is work efficiently," he asserts. This distinction is the backbone of his entire argument. He posits that the average student wastes the most valuable asset they have: their presence in the classroom.

He illustrates this with a hypothetical calculation that feels almost clinical in its precision. Kaplan argues that reading before class, even with imperfect understanding, provides a crucial scaffold. "Even if you didn't understand all the reading, you'll have that background information in your head so that you can actually absorb and understand what's being said in class." He claims this simple step boosts comprehension from a baseline of 50% to 95% when combined with lecture attendance. The logic is sound: you are already paying for the time in class; the variable is how much of that time you convert into retained knowledge.

Notes are a time-saving tool if you're willing to take them.

The author's most aggressive claim is that the act of note-taking is not a passive recording exercise but an active cognitive filter. "What taking notes does is it first of all, it forces you to pay attention during class," Kaplan writes. He suggests that without this friction, students drift into a passive state where they hear words but retain nothing. By forcing the brain to transcribe, the student anchors the information. Critics might note that this assumes a level of executive function and note-taking skill that many freshmen simply haven't developed yet, potentially overwhelming those who struggle with processing speed. However, Kaplan's point stands: the absence of notes guarantees a lower ceiling on retention.

The Cost of Cramming

Kaplan then contrasts this efficient model with the "ineefficient method" used by the majority of students. He describes a scenario where a student skims material, skips note-taking, and relies on last-minute cramming. The numbers he constructs are stark. While the efficient student spends roughly 51 hours to secure an A, the inefficient student spends 60 hours and ends up with a failing grade or a low D.

He breaks down the inefficiency of cramming: "You really get way less out of that hour of lecture if you haven't focused and taken notes and done the reading properly before class." The result is a compounding deficit. Without the initial 50% comprehension from pre-reading, the lecture only bumps understanding to 25%. When exam time arrives, the student has no notes to review and must relearn the entire course from scratch. "That's 5%... In most courses, 55% that's an F. That's a failing grade," Kaplan calculates, showing how the "shortcut" of skipping prep actually costs more time and yields worse results.

The argument here is compelling because it addresses the emotional reality of the student experience: the panic of the final week. Kaplan notes that students who cram "freak out," realizing too late that they cannot leverage the lectures that have already passed. He frames the semester not as a series of isolated tasks, but as a cumulative investment where early deposits (notes, pre-reading) yield interest (retention) later.

The Bodybuilder Analogy

To drive the point home, Kaplan introduces a vivid analogy involving a conversation between two bodybuilders. He recounts a story where a muscular man explains his success not to the amount of time spent in the gym, but to the intensity of the work during that time. "I see you in here every day or every other day. You're in here just as much as I am. But unlike you, when I'm in this gym, I'm not looking at myself in the mirror... I lift weights the whole time," the strong man says.

Kaplan applies this directly to the academic setting. "When you go to college, you should think of your time there like the bodybuilder." The lesson is about eliminating the "juice bar" moments—the distractions, the phone checking, the passive staring at the text. "Read with focus. Just like that weightlifter lifts with focus the whole time that they're in the gym. That's the secret to getting gains." This analogy is effective because it reframes studying from a chore of endurance to a discipline of intensity. It shifts the metric of success from 'hours logged' to 'gains made.'

The difference between you and me is that we both spend the same amount of time in the weight room, but I spend that time lifting weights.

While the bodybuilder story is memorable, it simplifies the cognitive load of learning compared to physical exertion. Learning requires rest and consolidation, which the analogy might inadvertently downplay. Yet, the core message remains: presence without engagement is a waste of resources.

Bottom Line

Jeffrey Kaplan's piece succeeds by replacing vague platitudes with a rigid, almost algorithmic approach to learning. The strongest element is his demonstration that 'hard work' without a system is often just performative busyness that leads to failure. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on idealized numbers; not every student can achieve 99% comprehension through note-taking alone, and the cognitive load of constant note revision can be exhausting. However, the fundamental insight—that distributed, focused effort beats sporadic, panicked cramming—is a vital lesson for any student looking to maximize their return on investment in higher education.
