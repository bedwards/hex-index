Jason Slaughter delivers a devastating reality check on the global obsession with building cities from scratch, arguing that the failure of these projects isn't a technological glitch but a political and financial design flaw. While urban planners often dream of greenfield utopias, Slaughter's on-the-ground reporting in South Korea reveals that "the problems of urban planning aren't technological, architectural, or logistical. They're political." This distinction is crucial for any busy leader trying to understand why modern infrastructure so often fails its residents.

The Illusion of the Smart City

Slaughter begins his investigation in Songdo, a project pitched as a "new modern green, lowcarbon smart city built on reclaimed land." The marketing materials promised a pedestrian paradise with "40% of the space in Songo dedicated to parks and green space." However, the author's physical experience shattered this digital rendering. He describes arriving at a metro station only to face a "narrow sidewalk next to this wide road" where cars were "driving really fast here and it was not comfortable at all." The disconnect between the brochure and the pavement is stark.

The core of Slaughter's argument here is that these projects are often designed for investors, not inhabitants. He notes that the financing model relies on public-private partnerships where "the ultimate goal is to make a profit." This financial imperative dictates the design. As Slaughter observes, "private companies are generally not willing to take on the risk of building something totally new, which usually means sticking to the car ccentric status quo." The result is a city that looks futuristic from a drone but feels hostile to a human on foot. He captures this absurdity perfectly: "What's the point in having an ultraodderern green smart echo city if every time you walk somewhere you have to spend several minutes waiting to cross a six-lane road while staring at a Volvo dealership?"

In short, they're unlikely to greenlight any project that isn't accessible by Lamborghini.

Critics might argue that Songdo was never intended to be a fully self-contained utopia but rather a business district designed to attract foreign capital, which it successfully did. However, Slaughter's point stands that a city cannot claim to be "green" or "smart" if its fundamental layout prioritizes vehicle throughput over human safety and connectivity.

The Power of Incremental Design

Leaving the sterile, car-dominated streets of Songdo, Slaughter travels to Sandbon, a town built in the 1990s to address a housing shortage. The contrast is immediate and visceral. Upon arrival, he notes, "it was immediately obvious that this was a place where people come first, not cars." The streets are narrow, speeds are low, and traffic lights are unnecessary because "drivers have to wait for the people walking and not the other way around."

This section highlights the author's most compelling insight: successful urbanism often comes from constraints, not unlimited resources. Sandbon wasn't built on a blank slate with a blank check; it was built to solve a specific problem with a focus on density and walkability. Slaughter praises the "raised crossings with this ramp, similar to the continuous sidewalks in the Netherlands," noting that "when it comes to slowing down drivers, physics always works better than signs." The presence of a vibrant, car-free market path lined with small tents demonstrates how the right design fosters community. He writes, "This really shows how the right kind of development allows for so much more human interaction and community building that would not be possible in a place full of wide roads and cars."

The argument here is that "people-first" design doesn't require a new technology; it requires a shift in priority. While Sandbon still has surface parking and wide arterial roads on its periphery, its core functions as a living neighborhood rather than a corporate showcase. Slaughter admits, "I much prefer the mid-rise and row home neighborhoods in the Netherlands," but concedes that after the "sterile car-infested roads of Songdo, this place was like paradise."

The High-Speed Trap

The final stop is Pangyo, a high-tech hub connected by the new GTX high-speed commuter train. Slaughter describes the journey as "weird to be on a train going 180 km hour without being able to see anything outside for the entire ride." Yet, the station exit offers a glimpse of what a new town could be. He is encouraged to see "kids cycling through a park" immediately upon exiting, noting that "exits from any train or metro station should be in a pedestrianized area whenever possible."

This observation reinforces the piece's central thesis: the integration of transit and pedestrian space is the single most important factor in creating livable new towns. The park design in Pangyo is "well integrated into the surrounding residential buildings," allowing residents to move without interacting with cars. However, Slaughter hints that the story is not yet finished, as the town is still evolving. The success of Pangyo's station area suggests that when the political will aligns with transit-oriented development, the results can be transformative, even in a new city context.

Bottom Line

Jason Slaughter's reporting proves that the failure of new cities is rarely about a lack of technology or vision, but rather a failure of political will to prioritize people over profit. The strongest part of his argument is the visceral contrast between the car-centric sterility of Songdo and the human-scale vibrancy of Sandbon, which serves as a powerful rebuttal to the idea that we need to start from scratch to fix our urban problems. The biggest vulnerability in the new city model remains the reliance on private capital, which inherently favors the status quo of car dependency; until financing models change, the "smart city" will likely remain a marketing slogan rather than a lived reality.
