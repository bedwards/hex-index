Jason Slaughter doesn't just review trains; he dismantles the assumption that high-speed rail is merely a utility for getting from point A to point B. While most travel writing fixates on the Shinkansen's speed, Slaughter argues that Japan's true transportation genius lies in its ability to turn a commute into a destination, whether that destination is a Hello Kitty fantasy, a corporate boardroom, or a stargazing observatory.

The Engineering of Perfection

Slaughter begins by establishing the baseline: the Shinkansen is a marvel of reliability that makes driving or flying obsolete for intercity travel. He writes, "even with all those trains running on the network their average annual delay in including those caused by earthquakes and typhoons is less than 1 minute." This statistic is staggering, but the author pushes further to highlight a safety record that defies logic. "There has never been a shin conen crash ever since they started in 1964," Slaughter notes, forcing the reader to confront the sheer scale of this achievement compared to cars or planes.

The core of his argument here is that this perfection isn't accidental; it is the result of a dedicated, segregated network. He explains that these trains "have no crossings" and are "governed by a coordinated signaling system that keeps trains clear of one another." This engineering rigor creates a user experience where speed is almost imperceptible. Slaughter illustrates this by attempting to balance a bottle of water upside down at 320 km/h, noting, "you would never know this train is riding so fast when you're on it."

"Japan really can be the most backwards futuristic country sometimes."

Critics might argue that focusing on the "crazy" novelty of these trains distracts from the mundane reality that most Japanese citizens simply use them for work. However, Slaughter's point is precisely that the system is robust enough to support both the mundane and the extraordinary simultaneously.

Themed Travel as a Service

The commentary shifts as Slaughter explores how Japanese rail companies differentiate themselves through extreme theming. He describes the Hello Kitty Shinkansen not as a gimmick, but as a fully realized ecosystem where "the farther you get from the shop in car 1 the less Hello Kitty they become." This observation is sharp; it highlights the commercial logic behind the design, where the most intense experience is monetized near the gift shop.

Slaughter writes, "Book car two if you want to experience Peak Hello Kitty," offering a practical tip that underscores the train's dual nature as both a family attraction and a business tool. He notes that despite the "pink monstrosity full of children," there is no extra cost, and it runs on the same schedule as any other train. This accessibility is key: the novelty doesn't come at the expense of efficiency.

Moving to the luxury end of the spectrum, Slaughter examines the Sapphire Odoriko. Here, the train is not just transport but a venue. He describes the private compartments as being "set up with a table with chairs around it like a conference room," suggesting a shift in how business travel is conducted. The author points out the friction in booking these, noting he "ended up taking over 20 minutes while the woman in the ticket office consulted a paper manual." This detail serves as a counterpoint to the high-tech image of Japan; the system is so specialized that it sometimes relies on analog processes.

Niche Use Cases and Recreational Rail

Perhaps the most compelling part of Slaughter's coverage is his focus on trains designed for specific hobbies rather than general transit. He details the BB Bas, a weekend train for cyclists, noting it allows riders to "bring their bicycles right to the platform without any stairs." This stands in stark contrast to the strict bagging requirements on most other lines. Slaughter argues that "fundamentally trains should be available for all kinds of trips not just commuting," a principle that is often lost in Western transit planning.

He also explores the Kot Train Gaku, which features carriages decorated for each season, including Zen gardens. Slaughter observes that while the Shinkansen is faster, "this train is much more interesting plus it brings you right into the main train station rather than Shin Osaka." This highlights a strategic advantage of slower, thematic trains: they connect travelers to city centers, not just peripheral hubs.

The Stargazing Misunderstanding

The narrative takes a humorous turn with the High Rail 1375, a train Slaughter admits he booked without research. He expected a "stargazing train" where he could look out the window at night, but instead, the experience involved a stop at a station with bright lights and a planetarium presentation. "This was truly bizarre because we were literally in the parking lot of the station and they didn't turn off the lights or anything," he writes.

This section serves as a meta-commentary on the difficulty of navigating such a specialized system as a foreigner. Slaughter admits, "I probably should have done a little bit more research," but ultimately frames the experience as a testament to the country's commitment to unique experiences, even if they are executed with a quirky, bureaucratic logic. He concludes that the train is "really interesting" but recommends taking it during the day to see the views, turning a potential failure into a useful travel tip.

"Japan has created some crazy trains that just don't exist anywhere else."

Bottom Line

Slaughter's coverage succeeds because it moves beyond the standard "trains are fast" narrative to reveal a transportation culture that treats the journey as an event in itself. His strongest evidence is the sheer variety of specialized trains, from bike carriers to luxury pods, which proves that rail can be both highly efficient and deeply human-centric. The piece's only vulnerability is the occasional reliance on the author's own confusion to drive the story, but this ultimately reinforces the complexity and richness of the Japanese rail network.
