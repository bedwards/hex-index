Jason Slaughter doesn't just ride a train; he exposes the structural rot beneath the tracks of North American passenger rail. While many commuters accept delays as an inevitable nuisance, Slaughter argues that the slowness of the Amtrak Empire Builder isn't bad luck—it's a deliberate consequence of a system where freight companies own the infrastructure and passenger trains are merely tolerated guests. His journey from New York to Toronto serves as a damning case study in how the richest nations on earth fail to provide basic mobility, contrasting a 12-hour slog with the efficiency of European and Japanese networks.

The Illusion of Choice

Slaughter begins by dismantling the myth that flying is the only viable option for intercity travel, only to reveal that the train alternative is compromised by design. He notes that while the trip was scheduled for 12.5 hours, the reality was a "best case scenario" that still dragged on for nearly 13. "The real reason to take this train is the views," he admits, acknowledging that the journey's value lies in scenery rather than speed. This is a concession that stings: a mode of transport that should be defined by efficiency is reduced to a sightseeing tour because the infrastructure cannot support faster movement.

The author highlights a critical friction point that most travelers overlook: the legal and physical priority given to freight. "Amtrak recommends using their app to track the progress of the train," Slaughter writes, "but the law says they're supposed to give priority to passenger trains so whenever a segment of track is needed by two trains the freight train usually wins out over the passenger train." This observation cuts to the heart of the problem. It isn't that the trains are too slow; it's that the tracks are owned by private corporations with no incentive to let passenger trains pass. Critics might argue that building new dedicated lines is prohibitively expensive, but Slaughter's comparison to other nations suggests that the cost is a political choice, not an economic inevitability.

"It's just so frustrating that the richest country in the world can't run a train better than this between the most populous city in the US and the most populous city in Canada."

The Infrastructure Gap

The commentary shifts from the American experience to the Canadian side of the border, where the situation deteriorates further. Slaughter describes the transition from US tracks to those owned by Canadian National (CN) as a step down in quality. "The quality of the track was noticeably worse on the Canadian side," he observes, noting that the train rocked violently even at reduced speeds. This isn't merely a complaint about comfort; it is evidence of a systemic neglect of public infrastructure. The author points out that once the train finally reached government-owned tracks, the speed increased, proving that the bottleneck is ownership, not geography.

He draws a sharp, data-driven comparison to illustrate the absurdity of the situation. "New York to Toronto is similar to the distance between Amsterdam and Berlin, a trip I just took a few weeks ago and which took just over 6 hours," Slaughter writes. He further notes that the distance is nearly identical to the Tokyo to Hiroshima route, which takes less than four hours on a Shinkansen. "If Amtrak were as fast as a Japanese Shinkansen my trip would have taken a third as long." These comparisons are devastating because they remove the excuse of "distance" or "terrain." The difference is purely policy and investment. A counterargument often raised is that North American geography is simply too vast for high-speed rail, but Slaughter's evidence of the 800-kilometer sweet spot for electric trains directly refutes this for the Northeast Corridor.

The Canadian Rant: A New Low

The piece takes a turn from structural critique to a specific grievance against VIA Rail, Canada's passenger service. Slaughter details a new baggage policy that mimics airline restrictions, a move he finds baffling for a ground-based mode of transport. "VIA Rail has now implemented a new luggage policy and it's basically the same as an airline," he states, questioning the logic of weighing bags on a train. He explains that VIA Rail cites labor laws preventing employees from lifting over 50 pounds, but Slaughter finds this justification weak given the difficulty of boarding the trains in the first place.

The financial disparity he uncovers is equally stark. He compares the cost of a family trip to London, Ontario, with a similar journey in the Netherlands. "VIA Rail wanted over €330," he notes, while a comparable trip in the Netherlands for a family of four would cost a fraction of that with group discounts. "This trip would be about €180 here in the Netherlands... meanwhile VIA Rail wanted over €330." This pricing structure, combined with the limited frequency of service—six trains a day versus fifty-five in the Netherlands—paints a picture of a service that is actively hostile to its customers. The author's frustration is palpable as he describes being stopped for a lunch bag that pushed his luggage over the arbitrary weight limit.

"If you think Amtrak is bad, VIA Rail has a new baggage policy and oh boy do I need to rant about it."

Bottom Line

Jason Slaughter's account is a powerful indictment of a transportation system that prioritizes freight profits over public mobility, using the New York-to-Toronto corridor as a microcosm of a continental failure. The strongest part of his argument is the relentless comparison to international standards, which strips away the excuse of "American exceptionalism" to reveal a simple lack of will. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability is its focus on the passenger experience without offering a concrete political roadmap for how to break the freight monopoly on tracks. Readers should watch for the growing momentum behind high-speed rail advocacy, as Slaughter's frustration suggests the status quo is becoming increasingly untenable for the modern traveler.
