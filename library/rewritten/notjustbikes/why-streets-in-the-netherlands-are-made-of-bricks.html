Jason Slaughter doesn't just explain why Dutch streets are made of bricks; he reveals how a simple material choice exposes a fundamental philosophical divide between car-centric sprawl and human-scaled design. While most observers see aesthetics, Slaughter argues that these "clinkers" are actually a sophisticated engineering solution that prioritizes longevity, repairability, and the exclusion of high-speed traffic. This is not a travelogue about charming cobblestones; it is a technical indictment of the disposable nature of asphalt infrastructure.

The Philosophy of the Brick

Slaughter begins by dismantling the assumption that asphalt is the default "correct" choice for roads. He acknowledges its utility for highways, noting that "asphalt is a great material for roads it provides a smooth even surface that makes a road safe and comfortable for high-speed traffic." However, he quickly pivots to the Dutch reality where "the streets are different most Dutch streets are built out of preconstructed brick pavers called clinkers." This distinction is crucial. The author suggests that the material itself dictates the speed and behavior of the user. When a surface is rough and noisy, drivers instinctively slow down. Slaughter writes, "when parking spots are on the road if there aren't any park cars then the street becomes bigger which we know encourages people to speed." By moving parking to the sidewalk level or using bricks that discourage high speeds, the physical environment enforces safety without heavy-handed policing.

The durability of these bricks is not accidental. Slaughter explains that the clay bricks are "baked at a high temperature so that they are much more durable than the bricks used in buildings." This industrial process creates a surface that can withstand decades of use, contrasting sharply with the temporary nature of asphalt. He points out that while asphalt roads typically need resurfacing every 10 to 15 years, "a street made of clinkers can last 30 years or more." This longevity is not just about convenience; it is a financial argument. Slaughter asserts that "clinkers are more expensive than asphalt to install initially but maintenance is relatively inexpensive and the street lasts a long time so overall streets and sidewalks made with clinkers are actually less expensive than those made with asphalt and concrete." This reframes the Dutch approach not as a luxury, but as a fiscally responsible long-term investment.

The gaps in the clinkers allow for expansion so these streets deal much better with changes in temperature.

Critics might note that the initial cost barrier and the potential for uneven surfaces if not perfectly maintained could make this model difficult to replicate in cities with different budget cycles or climate challenges. However, Slaughter's evidence regarding the lifecycle cost suggests that the "cheap" asphalt option is often a false economy.

The Engineering of Repair

Perhaps the most compelling part of Slaughter's coverage is the discussion on maintenance and the "asphalt scars" that plague North American cities. He contrasts the Dutch ability to dig up a street, fix the pipes, and restore the surface perfectly against the North American tendency to patch holes with "blobs of asphalt." He writes, "when work has been done on the street the surface quality is almost always better after the construction." This is a profound observation about infrastructure quality. The modular nature of bricks means they can be removed and reinstalled without heavy machinery, whereas asphalt requires massive trucks and creates a rough, permanent patch.

Slaughter highlights the sustainability of this approach, noting that "any clinkers that are too worn down aren't thrown away in fact they're actually in very high demand." He describes how old, weathered bricks are harvested from one part of the city and used to restore historic areas, creating a circular economy of materials. "This is the historic Square in Harlem and you can see that there are these nice old and weathered clinkers... these are the bricks that were worn and weathered and they were saved so that they could be used somewhere like this which is an absolutely brilliant idea for reusing old material." This stands in stark contrast to the linear consumption model of asphalt, which requires imported petroleum and energy-intensive recycling processes.

Furthermore, the environmental impact extends to heat. Slaughter notes that "asphalt is also a significant contributor to the urban heat island effect that can make cities even hotter in hot weather which doesn't really happen with clinkers because they absorb much less heat than asphalt does." The material choice directly impacts the livability of the city, reducing the need for cooling and improving comfort for pedestrians and cyclists.

The Limits of the Brick

Despite the overwhelming praise for clinkers, Slaughter is careful not to present them as a panacea for all road types. He admits that "ultimately a street like this is not going to hold up well to a large amount of motor vehicle traffic the clinkers will quickly deteriorate and develop ruts and grooves." This limitation is not a failure of the material, but a feature of Dutch traffic philosophy. The author explains that the Dutch use asphalt for high-volume distributor roads and reserve clinkers for neighborhood access streets. This separation is part of the "sustainable safety" program created in the 1990s, which explicitly distinguishes between roads designed for moving cars quickly and streets designed for people to live on.

Slaughter writes, "this brings up an extremely important aspect of Dutch Street design the very clear distinction between distributor roads and neighborhood access streets." By acknowledging that bricks cannot handle heavy truck traffic, he reinforces the argument that the Dutch system is not about banning cars, but about segregating traffic functions to ensure safety and durability. A counterargument worth considering is whether this level of segregation is politically feasible in cities with entrenched car culture and mixed-use zoning that relies on through-traffic for local commerce. Slaughter implies that the Dutch model requires a holistic redesign of the network, not just a material swap.

Bottom Line

Jason Slaughter's coverage succeeds by moving beyond the superficial charm of brick streets to reveal a rigorous, cost-effective, and sustainable engineering philosophy. The strongest part of his argument is the demonstration that the "expensive" Dutch street is actually cheaper over its lifecycle, while the "cheap" asphalt street is a recurring financial and environmental liability. The biggest vulnerability for other cities is not the material itself, but the political will to redesign street networks to prioritize local access over high-speed throughput. Readers should watch for how this philosophy of "sustainable safety" is applied to other infrastructure challenges beyond paving.
