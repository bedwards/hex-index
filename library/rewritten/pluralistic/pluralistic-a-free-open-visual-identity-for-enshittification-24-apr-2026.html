Cory Doctorow has done something rare in the tech policy world: he turned a vulgar metaphor for platform decay into a shared, open-source visual language. While most activists struggle to make abstract concepts like "enshittification" stick, Doctorow weaponized a modified poop emoji to create a movement that transcends the screen and enters the physical world.

The Visual Strategy

Doctorow's core insight is that people ignore distant, abstract tech problems until they are framed in concrete, immediate terms. "For nearly 25 years, I've worked with my comrades at the Electronic Frontier Foundation to raise the salience of these abstract, technical ideas," he writes. "I've come up with metaphors, parables, framing devices, narratives, and then... a dirty little word: enshittification." This linguistic shift worked, but the visual component proved even more potent. When the US edition of his book featured a cover designed by Devin Washburn—depicting an angry poop emoji with a grawlix (a string of typographic symbols like @#$%!) over its mouth—the reaction was instant.

The grawlix is a fitting choice here; it evokes the censorship and frustration inherent in platform decay, a concept deeply rooted in the history of comic book language that Doctorow has long championed. The image became a "go-to visual shorthand," and Doctorow notes, "I remixed it over and over." The demand for this imagery was so high that he initially distributed it as free swag, but the public's desire to own and use the symbol forced a strategic pivot. "But my pins and stickers weren't merch (stuff you could buy) — they were swag (stuff I gave away)," he explains. "I had no interest in getting into the merch business! But you folks kept asking."

"My goal here is to create a free, open, remixable visual language for talking about platform decay, not owned by me or anyone, a part of the commons."

This decision to release the design under a Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 license is the article's most significant move. By handing the rights to the public, Doctorow ensures the symbol cannot be co-opted or diluted by corporate interests. He explicitly invites semantic drift, encouraging users to apply the image to politics and non-digital phenomena alike. "Semantic drift is good, actually," he argues, recognizing that a symbol's power grows when it escapes its creator's control. Critics might argue that releasing such a potent brand into the wild risks dilution or misuse, but Doctorow's history with open licensing suggests he views this as a feature, not a bug.

The Mechanics of the Commons

The piece serves as both a manifesto and a logistical guide, pointing readers to repositories like Wikimedia Commons and the Internet Archive where high-resolution files and editable PSDs are available. This aligns with Doctorow's lifelong commitment to the Creative Commons movement, which he helped launch in 2002. He reflects on the organization's impact, calling it "one of the most amazing feats of stunt-lawyering ever attempted," noting that it now covers tens of billions of works, including all of Wikipedia.

To support the organization's 25th-anniversary fundraiser, Doctorow is offering a limited edition of his collage book, Canny Valley, with proceeds going to the cause. The book itself is a testament to the physical permanence of art in a digital age, printed on heavy paper with a binding designed to last generations. This stands in stark contrast to the ephemeral nature of the digital platforms he critiques. He writes, "I've supported Creative Commons literally since the very beginning," and his actions here reinforce that long-term commitment. The EFF has already begun producing their own merchandise, ensuring that the "disenshittifying force" has a tangible fundraising tool.

The Broader Context

The article also touches on the urgent need for this visual language through a series of rapid-fire links and historical reflections. In a section titled "Object permanence," Doctorow revisits a 2006 court case where the RIAA attempted to sue a young girl, a moment that highlighted the absurdity of corporate overreach. He also notes the 2016 incident where Atari attempted to claim exclusive rights to make "haunted house games," a clear example of the copyright maximalism that fuels platform decay. These historical anchors remind the reader that the fight against enclosure is not new, but the tools to fight it must evolve.

"With all proceeds going to the Electronic Frontier Foundation, the most profound and powerful disenshittifying force on the planet Earth!"

The commentary extends to current events, linking to discussions on California's privacy laws and the FCC's regulatory challenges, suggesting that the "enshittification" framework applies to government policy as much as corporate behavior. The administration's approach to tech regulation, often reactive and fragmented, stands in contrast to the proactive, community-driven approach Doctorow is modeling. By making the visual identity of this movement open, he effectively bypasses the gatekeepers who usually control the narrative.

Bottom Line

Doctorow's argument is strongest in its refusal to treat intellectual property as a barrier to activism; by open-sourcing the symbol of platform decay, he has created a resilient tool for public discourse that cannot be easily silenced. The strategy's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on voluntary adoption and the potential for the symbol to be co-opted by bad-faith actors, yet the open license ensures that the community can remix and correct the narrative as needed. For busy observers of the digital landscape, this is a rare example of a tech policy concept successfully breaking out of the echo chamber and into the physical world.
