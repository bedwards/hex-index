Matthew Yglesias makes a counterintuitive claim that defies the usual political script: the most promising path for the American left isn't a retreat to the center, but a progressive who becomes ruthlessly pragmatic to survive. In a landscape often defined by ideological purity tests, Yglesias argues that Zohran Mamdani's early tenure offers a rare case study in how a self-identified socialist can govern effectively by prioritizing results over rhetoric. This is not just a profile of a new mayor; it is an analysis of a potential new political operating system where the incentives of executive office force ideological evolution.

The Pragmatic Pivot

Yglesias begins by acknowledging his own initial skepticism, noting that before the primary, he viewed Mamdani as a "potentially catastrophic" choice compared to other options. Yet, the author observes a distinct shift in Mamdani's behavior once victory was within grasp. "My preferred path forward for American politics would be for politicians who I like to take over the Democratic Party on an explicit program of moderation," Yglesias writes, "but a plausible second-best scenario is one in which self-identified progressive factionalists take over and then become ruthlessly pragmatic because they want power."

This framing is compelling because it moves beyond the binary of "sellout vs. ideologue." Instead, Yglesias suggests that the desire to hold power can be a more reliable engine for moderation than a genuine change of heart. He points to Mamdani's reversal on police funding as the clearest evidence. While the candidate previously labeled the police department a "rogue agency," he later apologized and moved to retain the police commissioner, Jessica Tisch. Yglesias is candid about the motivation: "Do I think he is now tough on crime as a matter of profound conviction? Not really. Do I think he is now seriously worried that a rise in crime would derail his political career and therefore he needs to work extremely hard to avoid one? Yes. And that's probably good enough."

This pragmatic calculation is rooted in a specific historical tradition. Yglesias notes that Mamdani is familiar with the "sewer socialist" tradition of Milwaukee, where the goal was to be a good mayor who delivers good city services rather than engaging in abstract ideological battles. This historical anchor strengthens the argument that Mamdani isn't abandoning his principles, but rather applying a different, more effective methodology to achieve them. Critics might argue that this pragmatism is merely a temporary performance that will vanish once the political pressure eases, but the structural incentives of running a major city suggest otherwise.

The worst of all worlds is a continuation of the establishment trajectory where moderate figures are so terrified of losing power to the left that they abandon the substance of moderate politics.

The Abundance Agenda

The commentary then shifts to policy specifics, where Yglesias identifies a surprising alignment between progressive values and pro-growth economics. He highlights Mamdani's embrace of the "YIMBY" (Yes In My Backyard) movement, which advocates for increased housing density. Yglesias observes that while traditional leftists often view deregulation as a right-wing tool, Mamdani has successfully framed housing abundance as a progressive necessity. "Mamdani's base not only lives in high-density neighborhoods, but is disproportionately composed of people who deliberately moved to New York City from elsewhere," Yglesias notes, explaining that this demographic is more open to the aesthetic and symbolic appeal of density.

This section of the piece is particularly insightful because it decouples the concept of deregulation from its usual ideological baggage. Yglesias points out that Mamdani has promised to "free small-business owners from the shackles of bloated bureaucracy," a move that aligns with the outgoing administration's goals but contrasts sharply with the more restrictive policies of the previous governor, Andrew Cuomo. The author argues that this creates a unique political opportunity: "If you let profit-seeking developers build market-rate housing... you will get a revenue windfall. That windfall will make it easier to invest in transit, mental health, child care, or whichever public service you like."

However, Yglesias does not shy away from the internal tensions within this approach. He acknowledges that appointing figures from the leftward end of the previous administration creates a clash of worldviews. "You fundamentally can't have housing abundance without embracing the legitimacy of the profit motive — which is not how these regulatory appointees think," he writes. This admission adds necessary nuance, suggesting that while the strategy is sound, the execution may be fraught with ideological friction.

The Art of Distraction

Perhaps the most distinctive observation Yglesias makes is about Mamdani's communication style. He notes that the mayor possesses a "Trump-like quality of keeping both friends and foes focused on what he's saying rather than what he's doing." The author cites the inaugural address, where Mamdani spoke of replacing "the frigidity of rugged individualism with the warmth of collectivism," a phrase that immediately drew conservative fire. Yglesias argues this was a strategic masterstroke: "Progressives, in turn, spent days defending their man and poking fun at the Zohran Derangement Syndrome on the right rather than infighting over his moves to the center."

This analysis suggests that Mamdani understands the media ecosystem better than many of his peers. By providing a provocative soundbite, he controls the news cycle, allowing his substantive policy shifts—on education, policing, and housing—to proceed with less scrutiny. He has managed to "narrow his focus," dropping the promise to end mayoral control of schools, a longtime goal of teachers' unions, in favor of maintaining a status quo that ensures accountability.

Being the kind of progressive who champions good progressive ideas while dropping unsound ones is smart.

Bottom Line

Matthew Yglesias presents a sophisticated argument that the most viable future for the American left may lie in a form of "left-abundance" where progressive goals are achieved through market-friendly mechanisms and pragmatic governance. The piece's greatest strength is its refusal to romanticize ideology, instead focusing on the hard incentives of executive power that force moderation. The biggest vulnerability remains whether this pragmatic pivot can be sustained long-term or if it is merely a survival tactic for the first term. Readers should watch to see if Mamdani can translate his early political capital into lasting structural changes in housing and public safety without alienating his core base.
