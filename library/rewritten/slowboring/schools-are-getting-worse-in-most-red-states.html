Matthew Yglesias delivers a jarring reality check to the prevailing narrative that conservative governance is the secret sauce for American education. While much of the political discourse fixates on a narrow "Southern surge," Yglesias argues that this success is a statistical mirage created by the fact that schools in most other states are actively getting worse. For busy leaders trying to understand the true state of the nation's classrooms, this piece cuts through the partisan noise to reveal a national crisis that transcends red and blue lines.

The Myth of the Southern Surge

Yglesias begins by acknowledging the genuine achievements in states like Mississippi, Alabama, Louisiana, and Tennessee, where test scores have risen through rigorous curriculum implementation. However, he immediately pivots to challenge the broader political conclusions drawn from these outliers. "It's not a significant rise in test scores in states such as Mississippi, Alabama, Louisiana, and Tennessee. It's just those four states," he writes. This distinction is crucial because it prevents the misattribution of success to a specific political ideology when the reality is far more localized.

The author points out that Louisiana's turnaround occurred under a Democratic governor, further complicating the partisan story. More importantly, he highlights the statistical anomaly at play: "One reason that Mississippi has shot up the rankings is that not only have its scores been going up, but scores in most places are getting worse." This framing forces the reader to reconsider what "success" actually looks like in a declining national environment. A state can be a top performer today with results that would have been considered average a decade ago. Yglesias argues that while Mississippi is doing excellent work, the national background has deteriorated so significantly that the "surge" is relative, not absolute.

Critics might argue that even relative success in a failing system is worth emulating, but Yglesias suggests that focusing on the four success stories ignores the larger tragedy unfolding elsewhere. He notes that if conservative governance were truly the answer, we would see broad-based improvement, not a handful of bright spots surrounded by a sea of decline. The core of his argument is that we are witnessing a "Southern sag" in states like Texas and Florida, where scores are dropping despite their reputation as conservative education powerhouses.

The achievements in MS/AL/TN/LA are genuinely worth celebrating and paying attention to, but the fact that Texas and Florida and all kinds of other places are moving backward also underscores that you can't just blame Democrats or teachers unions or believe that Republicans have this figured out.

The Failure of Privatization and the Loss of Accountability

The commentary then shifts to the policy mechanisms driving these divergent outcomes. Yglesias observes that the current conservative approach often prioritizes dismantling teacher unions over improving instructional quality. He writes, "The trend on the right has been to behave as if the goal of education policy is to dismantle teacher unions." This focus has led to a heavy reliance on vouchers and education savings accounts, which Yglesias argues have generally negative effects on student achievement.

He draws a sharp parallel to higher education to illustrate why market-based solutions fail in K-12: "Schools don't really compete on the basis of being highly effective at teaching students. The 'best' colleges in America are the ones that attract the strongest applicants." This insight is powerful because it exposes the flaw in assuming that parental choice will naturally drive up quality. Instead, it often just allows families to sort themselves by socioeconomic status without improving the underlying educational product.

Yglesias suggests that the real solution lies in re-embracing the accountability measures of the No Child Left Behind era, despite the bipartisan backlash that led to its dismantling. "The simple step of measuring results and imposing (generally very mild) consequences for bad performance was good," he argues. This is a provocative stance, as it requires progressives to accept that unions opposing accountability can lead to worse outcomes for students. He posits that "you can't actually improve at something unless you're willing to get some negative feedback," a principle that applies to school systems just as it does to any other institution.

The Political Equilibrium and the Path Forward

The piece concludes by examining the political incentives that keep these bad policies in place. Yglesias notes a strange equilibrium where conservatives are happy to run cheap, underperforming school systems, while Democrats often prioritize spending over results. "If nothing else, they generally want to spend a lot of money on public schools — in which case they really ought to care a lot about whether they're doing a good job," he writes. This is a stinging critique of the progressive tendency to equate funding with success without demanding accountability for the outcomes.

He looks to Virginia as a potential test case for a different approach, where a new Democratic administration could adopt accountability recommendations that were previously developed by the outgoing Republican administration. Yglesias argues that "there's no real reason that 'schools should do a good job of teaching' should be a right-coded idea." This call to action is directed at Democrats to reclaim the mantle of educational quality, moving beyond the defense of unions to the defense of student learning.

We can't have public money without public accountability. But then you have to have accountability in your public system!

Bottom Line

Yglesias's strongest contribution is his refusal to let the "Southern surge" narrative obscure the broader national collapse in education, forcing a re-evaluation of both conservative privatization and progressive spending habits. The argument's vulnerability lies in its reliance on standardized test scores as the primary metric of success, which can sometimes miss the holistic development of students, though the data trends are undeniably alarming. The most critical takeaway is that regardless of political affiliation, the path to recovery requires a return to rigorous accountability and a focus on instructional quality rather than ideological battles over funding or union power.
