Adam Tooze cuts through the noise of geopolitical speculation to propose a startling thesis: the United States has effectively constructed a modern oil empire, not through conquest, but through the sheer aggregation of Western Hemisphere production. This is not a story about a single leader's whims, but a structural shift in global energy power that renders traditional constraints on American foreign policy obsolete. The piece forces a re-evaluation of how the executive branch leverages resource dominance, suggesting that the era where war was checked by the fear of spiking oil prices may have finally ended.

The Architecture of the "Donroe Doctrine"

Tooze begins by engaging with Javier Blas's provocative framing of a new "Donroe Doctrine," a belligerent reimagining of the Monroe Doctrine centered on energy control. He acknowledges the rationality behind the logic while pushing the analysis further into the realm of raw geopolitical leverage. "Start with the oil production of the US and add Canada. Then include Venezuela and the rest of Latin America... Together they account for nearly 40% of the world's oil output," Tooze writes. This aggregation is the crux of the argument: the United States no longer needs to negotiate with external suppliers because it can command the resources of its entire hemisphere.

The commentary suggests this shift is profound. By controlling the flow of barrels from Texas to the Orinoco Belt, the administration possesses a tool that no president since Franklin D. Roosevelt has held. Tooze notes, "Whatever the word, President Donald Trump now has his very own oil empire. And I'm talking about actual barrels already flowing into the market, not underground reserves that would take time and money to be developed." This distinction between potential reserves and active production is vital; it implies immediate, rather than theoretical, power.

Having de facto control of the Western Hemisphere's petroleum wealth is a geopolitical game changer.

The implications of this control are stark. Tooze argues that for decades, military adventurism was constrained by the economic fallout of disrupting energy markets. That constraint is now gone. "The past 18 months have already shown what these new hydrocarbon riches mean for US foreign policy," he observes, citing the willingness to target Iranian nuclear facilities and Russian refineries. The logic follows that if the US can dictate supply from its own backyard, it can afford to be aggressive elsewhere without fear of self-inflicted economic wounds.

The Reality Check: Viscosity and Economics

However, Tooze is careful not to conflate political will with economic reality. He pivots to the specific challenges of the Venezuelan asset, particularly the Orinoco Belt. While the region holds the world's largest hydrocarbon accumulation, the oil is "extra-heavy" and possesses a viscosity so high it is "syrup-like" compared to standard crude. This physical reality creates a massive barrier to entry. "Mr Trump seems to think those firms would rush to sign big cheques," Tooze notes, but the financial and technical hurdles are staggering. Rystad Energy estimates a need for $110 billion in capital expenditure just to restore output to levels from 15 years ago.

The human cost of this potential extraction is also a factor. The industry has suffered a "huge brain drain," with tens of thousands of skilled engineers and geologists having fled the country. The state oil company, PDVSA, is now largely run by the armed forces, raising questions about its viability as a partner for Western firms. "To form viable joint ventures with Western firms, the 70,000-strong company would be run by the armed forces... It may not be able to serve as a viable partner for many years," Tooze paraphrases the grim outlook from industry analysts.

Critics might note that Tooze's reliance on the "Donroe Doctrine" narrative risks oversimplifying the complex web of sanctions, legal liabilities, and market dynamics that actually govern oil flows. The idea that the US can simply "decline offers" from Russia because it has enough domestic supply ignores the global nature of oil markets, where price is set by marginal barrels regardless of origin. Furthermore, the assumption that American refiners will eagerly absorb Venezuelan crude overlooks the fact that the global market is already saturated. The International Energy Agency expects supply to outstrip demand through the end of the decade, potentially driving prices below the breakeven point for Venezuelan fields.

The Human and Strategic Cost

The commentary also touches on the geopolitical ripple effects, particularly for neighbors like Cuba and China. With shipments to Cuba falling by three-quarters in recent years, the humanitarian impact of these shifts is already being felt. "Cuba, which has long bought from Venezuela on favourable terms, will turn to Mexico and Russia for help," Tooze writes, highlighting how energy politics directly dictate the survival of vulnerable populations. The removal of a hostile regime in Caracas might stabilize the region, but it also disrupts established trade flows that have sustained economies for decades.

The piece concludes with a nuanced view of the administration's strategy. While the political narrative is one of dominance, the economic reality is one of marginal gains. "All this could benefit America commercially and geopolitically, but only at the margin," Tooze argues. The real prize may not be the difficult-to-extract Venezuelan oil, but the security umbrella extended over new discoveries in Guyana. By neutralizing the threat posed by the Maduro regime, the US secures the future of the Guyanese basin, a far more accessible and profitable asset.

The issue with Venezuela oil is its viscosity. The difference that viscosity makes to the economics of oil is huge.

Bottom Line

Tooze's most compelling contribution is the reframing of US energy policy from a defensive necessity to an offensive geopolitical lever, effectively ending the era where oil prices constrained military action. However, the argument's greatest vulnerability lies in its optimistic assessment of how quickly the Venezuelan oil industry can be revived, ignoring the decades of decay, brain drain, and technical complexity that stand in the way of a rapid production surge.
