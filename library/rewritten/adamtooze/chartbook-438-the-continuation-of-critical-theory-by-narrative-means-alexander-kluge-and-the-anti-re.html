Adam Tooze delivers a startling eulogy for a living giant, arguing that Alexander Kluge's death in March 2026 forces us to finally grasp a theory of time that refuses to be linear. Tooze does not merely summarize a career; he reveals that Kluge's entire body of work was a "continuation of critical theory by narrative means," challenging the very rigidity of reality we accept as fact. For the busy mind seeking to understand how history, emotion, and politics collide, this piece offers a rare key: the idea that our internal feelings are not soft distractions, but the hard, material engine of history itself.

The Architecture of Multiple Realities

Tooze begins by dismantling the conventional clock. He posits that for Kluge, time is not a river flowing in one direction, but a crowded room where the child, the adult, and the ancestor all speak at once. "For Alexander Kluge... it was always clear that time is multiple, overlapping, broken," Tooze writes. This is not poetic abstraction; it is a structural claim about how human beings process the world. The author suggests that our "three grammatical times" are present in every single moment, creating a "vessel of time between birth and death" where three generations engage in a constant act of storytelling.

This framing is particularly potent when viewed against the backdrop of the Frankfurt School's intellectual lineage. Tooze notes that while Jürgen Habermas became the "official academic wing" of the school, Kluge, alongside Oskar Negt, served as the "left-wing cultural counterpart." This distinction matters because it highlights a shift from pure philosophical critique to a "materialism with the feelings put back in." Tooze argues that Kluge's genius lay in recognizing that "reality is a crude invention, a cocoon we fashion around ourselves so as to be able to withstand the real world."

"I believe the sharpest ideology that exists is the one that considers reality to be real."

This quote serves as the piece's intellectual pivot. Tooze uses it to explain that what we call "reality" is often just a brittle shell we build to ignore the chaos beneath. He draws a sharp line between the "objective" wall an inmate hits and the "subjective" reality of the feelings that persist despite it. Critics might argue that this elevation of subjective feeling risks slipping into solipsism, where personal emotion overrides material conditions. However, Tooze counters this by insisting that for Kluge, feelings are "more obstinate and more like concrete than anything else I can think of, because they do not fundamentally change over the course of 2,000 years."

The Engine of History and the Ghost of War

The commentary deepens as Tooze connects Kluge's theory of feelings to the brutal mechanics of industrial warfare. He does not shy away from the grim specifics of the First World War to illustrate his point. Tooze describes the "horrible clash of stockpiled munitions" at Verdun, where miners from opposing sides dug tunnels that would eventually detonate, turning "highly specialized labor power" into "dead labor."

Here, Tooze invokes the Marxist concept of the Gesamtarbeiter (collective worker) to show a tragic divergence: while capitalism coordinates effortlessly through commodity exchange, the workers themselves are "disconnected, objective, and subjective realities" that fail to unite. "The collective worker is tragic because he could develop a self-consciousness about his labor power, but instead he works against his own interests," Tooze explains. This is where Kluge's "anti-realism of feelings" becomes a historical tool. It explains why the "real historical impact" of war is not just the destruction of bodies, but the shattering of the emotional bonds that could have prevented it.

The author weaves in a haunting personal anecdote about Kluge's father, a doctor in East Germany, who operated in "four realities simultaneously" on a single May Day. While the National People's Army marched outside, the father was mentally revisiting the Battle of the Marne and physically attending to a woman in labor. "You are perhaps starting to see how disparate this reality is," Tooze writes, noting that the father's internal landscape was as real as the marching soldiers. This example effectively bridges the gap between the personal and the political, showing how history is lived in the "catacombs, wells, and abysses" beneath the surface of events.

"The things that move humans from within are indeed significantly stronger and more powerful than anything that happens on the outside."

Tooze uses this assertion to challenge the standard historical narrative. He suggests that institutions only become solid when "filled with feelings," much like the "Celts" who lived invisibly within the Roman Empire. This echoes the spirit of Montaigne, another of Kluge's heroes, who sought to understand what "abolishes religious wars" by testing every sensation from "lies to the fabrication of illusions." Tooze implies that without this emotional inventory, our understanding of history remains flat and incomplete.

A Legacy of Polyphony

In the final analysis, Tooze presents Kluge not as a distant theorist, but as a companion whose death in 2026 leaves a specific void. The author reflects on his own transition from a young intellectual to a peer, noting that "Kluge is a comfort, but also a mystery." The piece concludes with a call to recognize the "multidimensionality" of human experience, where "narrative is polyphony."

Tooze's coverage is effective because it refuses to simplify Kluge's complex, fragmented style into a neat thesis. Instead, he mirrors Kluge's own method, assembling a "chronicle of feelings" that feels more "materialist" than a standard chronicle of events. The argument holds up because it treats emotion not as a weakness, but as the "engine" of human agency. A counterargument worth considering is whether this focus on the internal life of the individual can ever truly scale to solve the systemic, "nonhuman relations" of global capitalism that Kluge also critiqued. Yet, Tooze suggests that the very act of recognizing these feelings is the first step toward a different kind of reality.

"The basic story is that Europe exited the early modern period the wrong way."

Bottom Line

Adam Tooze's most compelling contribution here is his reframing of "feelings" as the most durable material force in human history, a concept that transforms how we read both Kluge and the past. While the argument risks over-privileging the subjective, its strength lies in exposing the "brittle" nature of the objective reality we take for granted. Readers should watch for how this "anti-realism of feelings" might reshape future critiques of political institutions, which Tooze argues are merely "feelings converted into space."
