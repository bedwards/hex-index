Adam Tooze dismantles a trendy geopolitical binary by revealing that the distinction between "electrostates" and "petrostates" is often a political distraction rather than an economic reality. His most provocative claim is that the United States is not inherently a fossil-fuel dependent nation, but rather a sophisticated economy capable of freezing itself in a 20th-century model purely by political choice. In an era where energy narratives are increasingly weaponized, Tooze offers a necessary corrective: the path to electrification is a matter of rationality and grid capacity, not just natural resource endowments.

The Definition Trap

Tooze begins by exposing the looseness of the term "electrostate." He defines it simply: "We call a country an electrostate, if it draws a large and increasing share of its total final energy consumption in the form of electricity." This is contrasted with societies that burn gas or oil directly in cars, ships, and factories. The author's point is that electrification is the universal key to the clean energy transition, yet the label has been hijacked to create a false dichotomy between China and the West.

He argues that China fits the "electrostate" mold not just because of state control, but because of sheer scale and speed. "China has been singled out as the archetypal electrostate," Tooze notes, pointing to the rapid expansion of renewables and the dominance of Chinese manufacturers in the electric vehicle sector. This is a compelling observation, especially when considering the infrastructure required to support such a shift. For context, the sheer ambition of China's grid is evident in its deployment of ultra-high-voltage transmission lines, which allow electricity generated in remote western provinces to power coastal cities thousands of miles away—a feat of engineering that redefines what an "electrostate" can achieve.

"The degree to which you are an electrostate is an expression not of underlying factor endowments, or of economic structure, but of economic and governmental rationality."

This reframing is the article's intellectual anchor. Tooze suggests that being an electrostate is a choice of modernization, not a destiny of geography. He illustrates this by noting that the Soviet Union was once an "electrical engineering powerhouse," and that even the Gulf states are now eager importers of Chinese solar panels. The argument holds water because it strips away the romanticism of "green" versus "dirty" and replaces it with a cold calculus of efficiency and cost.

The American Paradox

The piece takes a sharp turn when addressing the United States. Tooze challenges the growing habit of labeling America a "petrostate." He argues that the term was historically reserved for economies dependent on oil rents for survival, like Angola or Russia. "To be designated a petrostate, in other words, means that you were at a relatively low level of economic development, or a low-level of state capacity," he writes. Applying this to the U.S., where oil and gas account for only 7-8 percent of GDP, is, in his view, a "discursive construction" rather than an economic fact.

However, the author admits the U.S. has a unique problem: its massive domestic demand. "The US economy is not just the leading oil producer in the world... it is also the number one oil consumer," Tooze observes. This creates a dangerous feedback loop where the country produces over 13 million barrels a day but consumes nearly 20 million. Critics might note that ignoring the sheer volume of fossil fuel infrastructure embedded in the American economy risks underestimating the political inertia required to dismantle it. Yet, Tooze's point remains that the U.S. has the capacity to pivot, unlike true petrostates that lack the capital or technology to diversify.

"The US is not not an electrostate. It is an electrostate, just one that is slouching rather than racing towards the new era of ultra-low cost electric power."

This distinction is vital. The U.S. isn't lacking in renewable potential; Texas, for instance, is already the leading generator of utility-scale renewable energy in the nation. The bottleneck is not a lack of sun or wind, but a broken grid. Tooze highlights that hyperscaling AI companies are desperate to build out an American electrostate but are stalled by regulatory gridlock. He writes, "regardless of who sits in the White House, the process of grid expansion, connection and interconnection is so broken, that even if generating capacity becomes available, it cannot be hooked up efficiently."

The Choice of a Closed Petrostate

The most chilling part of Tooze's analysis is his prediction of a "closed petrostate" scenario. He argues that the U.S. is large and rich enough to ignore global decarbonization trends entirely. "If the United States chooses to use its endowment of fossil fuels and the size of its economy to freeze in place the early 20th-century status quo... that will not be a natural fact, but a matter of political choice," he asserts.

This is a departure from the standard narrative that climate change will force a transition. Instead, Tooze suggests that ideological opposition to a mixed economy could lead the U.S. to deliberately abandon the "all of the above" model favored by previous administrations. "The push to electrify mobility would be aborted," he warns, envisioning a future where the U.S. relies on domestic oil and gas turbines, excluding renewables by regulatory decree.

"In an inversion of familiar logic, the US is large enough, rich enough and sophisticated enough to simply freeze in place the late 20th century model for the foreseeable future."

This possibility scrambles the usual models of causation. Normally, economic necessity drives change. Here, political will could drive stagnation. While the economic logic of electrification remains superior, Tooze points out that the main obstacle is not cost, but the inability to manufacture enough gas turbines or connect new capacity to the grid. The danger is that the U.S. could become a pioneer of a new, isolated fossil-fuel model, decoupling itself from the global shift toward clean power.

"The US would be the pioneer of a novel form of 'closed petrostate'."

Bottom Line

Tooze's strongest contribution is reframing the "petrostate" label as a political choice rather than an economic inevitability, exposing the U.S. risk of self-imposed obsolescence. The argument's vulnerability lies in its assumption that economic rationality will eventually override ideological gridlock, a premise that recent political history suggests may be overly optimistic. Readers should watch for how the U.S. grid expansion debate evolves, as this will be the true test of whether America remains a "slouching" electrostate or becomes a "closed" fossil-fuel fortress.
