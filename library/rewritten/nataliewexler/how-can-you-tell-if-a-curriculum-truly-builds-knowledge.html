Natalie Wexler cuts through the marketing noise of the education industry with a sharp, necessary diagnosis: a curriculum can be labeled "knowledge-building" while failing to actually build knowledge. She doesn't just theorize; she dissects the specific mechanics of a widely used program, Benchmark Advance, revealing how "faux cohesion" and vague themes masquerade as deep learning. For busy leaders and educators, this is the difference between buying a tool that works and buying a box of parts that looks like a tool.

The Illusion of Cohesion

Wexler anchors her argument in the work of Olivia Mullins, a science teacher with a PhD in neuroscience, who scrutinized the "Observing the Sky" unit for first graders. Wexler writes, "The brief articles all relate to the sky in some way, but they don't refer to each other or build on one another." This observation strikes at the heart of the cognitive science behind learning. Without repetition and connection, new information fails to stick.

She explains that true knowledge accumulation requires texts to reinforce key vocabulary and concepts across multiple readings, a principle that aligns with the "Matthew effect," where those who start with a knowledge advantage pull further ahead because they can connect new information to what they already know. In contrast, Wexler notes that in this curriculum, "Some texts have too little content, while others introduce too many concepts without adequate explanation." The result is a fragmented learning experience that leaves students with isolated facts rather than a structured mental framework.

"Kids who get a true knowledge-building curriculum are far more likely to acquire the knowledge that will equip them for later academic success and for assuming their responsibilities as citizens of a democratic society."

Wexler's critique extends to the second-grade unit, "Investigating the Past," which she describes as a haphazard collection of topics ranging from Alexander Graham Bell to a fictional diary entry on the Oregon Trail. She points out a critical flaw in the design: "None of these articles supply any explanatory context." This lack of context forces students to rely on their existing background knowledge to make sense of the text, effectively penalizing those who lack that prior knowledge.

The Decoding Trap

A particularly insightful point Wexler makes concerns the obsession with "decodable" texts. She argues that while it is important for children to practice decoding, restricting them to simple texts during the early years limits their ability to learn complex concepts. Wexler writes, "When children are still learning to decode... it's far more efficient to build knowledge of a new topic by having teachers read aloud from texts that are more complex than those students can read on their own."

This distinction is vital. It challenges the prevailing trend of forcing young readers to struggle through simplified texts that lack substantive content. By prioritizing the mechanics of reading over the richness of the material, these curricula inadvertently stunt the very knowledge growth they claim to foster. Critics might argue that decodable texts are necessary for building fluency, but Wexler's point is that fluency and knowledge acquisition are not mutually exclusive; they can be addressed simultaneously through read-alouds of complex texts while students practice decoding on simpler ones.

The Marketing Mirage

Why do so many districts adopt these flawed curricula? Wexler suggests the answer lies in the gap between marketing and reality. She notes that Benchmark Advance's promotional materials are "practically every other word 'knowledge'." Yet, the substance doesn't match the slogan. Wexler observes that the curriculum's "essential questions" remain vague, such as "Why is the past important?", which fails to guide students toward specific, cumulative learning.

She highlights a telling anecdote from a district administrator who found that even after updating to the 2023 version, the curriculum was still "very thematic. It didn't go into any depth to build knowledge." This persistence of the problem despite updates suggests a fundamental design flaw rather than a simple oversight. The reliance on broad themes rather than specific topics prevents the deep, sequential knowledge building that research supports.

"If kids are learning about a topic, that's 'knowledge-building' to some people."

Wexler also addresses the role of third-party ratings, noting that influential organizations like EdReports have given Benchmark Advance top ratings. This creates a dangerous feedback loop where districts trust the ratings over their own observations. She contrasts this with the Knowledge Matters Campaign, which does not list Benchmark Advance as effective, suggesting that the criteria for "knowledge-building" vary wildly depending on who is doing the evaluating.

The Cost of Convenience

The article concludes by acknowledging a pragmatic reality: Benchmark Advance is "relatively easy for teachers to use." Wexler writes, "A curriculum consultant I spoke with told me that one attractive feature of Benchmark Advance is that it's relatively easy for teachers to use, especially as compared to most knowledge-building curricula." This ease of use is a powerful draw for overworked educators, even if the pedagogical payoff is lower.

However, she warns that this convenience comes at a cost. While some districts report rising test scores, Wexler cautions that these gains may be due to other factors, such as the use of a different curriculum in earlier grades or supplemental programs. She notes that in one district, teachers use CKLA for K-2 and Benchmark for 3-5, making it impossible to isolate the impact of the latter. The temptation to choose the "easy" option over the "effective" one remains a significant barrier to educational equity.

"The distinction has huge consequences for educators and students—and ultimately, for all of us."

Bottom Line

Wexler's analysis is a masterclass in separating educational buzzwords from pedagogical reality, exposing how "faux cohesion" can masquerade as rigorous instruction. Her strongest argument lies in the concrete examples of disjointed texts that fail to build the cumulative knowledge required for long-term success. The piece's vulnerability is its reliance on a single case study, but the underlying principles of cognitive load and the Matthew effect provide a robust theoretical foundation that extends far beyond one curriculum. Readers should watch for how districts respond to this critique, particularly whether they will prioritize ease of implementation over the proven benefits of deep, sequential knowledge building.
