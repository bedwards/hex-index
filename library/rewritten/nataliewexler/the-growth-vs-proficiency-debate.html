Natalie Wexler cuts through the celebratory noise surrounding Washington, D.C.'s education system to expose a dangerous disconnect between statistical growth and actual student readiness. While headlines trumpet the capital as the nation's fastest-improving urban district, she argues that this metric masks a grim reality where the vast majority of students remain functionally unprepared for college or career. This is not merely a debate over data points; it is an urgent critique of how policymakers use "growth" to manufacture success while allowing deep-seated inequities to fester.

The Illusion of Progress

The core of Wexler's argument rests on the distinction between two competing metrics: growth, which measures improvement over time, and proficiency, which measures absolute mastery of grade-level standards. She notes that while a prestigious analysis ranks D.C. first in growth, the raw numbers tell a different story. "A report by the D.C. Policy Center found that in 2025, only 26 percent of students scored proficient in math and only 38 percent in reading," Wexler writes. This stark contrast suggests that while schools are moving faster, they are still starting from such a low baseline that the destination remains out of reach for most.

Wexler is particularly critical of how officials leverage growth data to deflect from these deficiencies. She points out that Deputy Mayor Paul Kihn has crowed about D.C. being "the fastest-improving urban school system in the country," a claim Wexler suggests is designed to elicit a standing ovation rather than address systemic failure. This framing is effective because it highlights how administrative narratives can diverge from student reality. However, one must consider that without growth metrics, schools serving high-poverty populations might be unfairly penalized for factors entirely outside their control, such as housing instability or lack of early childhood resources.

"The rising tide may have lifted all boats, but it hasn't brought them any closer. In fact, they're slightly wider apart."

This observation is the piece's most damning insight. Wexler demonstrates that while test scores for almost all subgroups have risen, the gap between affluent white students and their Black or economically disadvantaged peers has not narrowed; in reading proficiency, it has actually widened by a few points over the last decade. This connects to the historical "Matthew effect" often discussed in education reform—the phenomenon where those who start ahead pull further ahead because they possess the foundational knowledge to absorb new concepts more quickly. By focusing solely on aggregate growth, the administration risks celebrating a rising tide that leaves the most vulnerable students stranded.

The Demographic Shift and the Curriculum Gap

Wexler challenges the narrative that D.C.'s success is purely the result of pedagogical brilliance in the classroom. She argues that much of the "improvement" is actually attributable to basic operational fixes—like heating buildings and delivering textbooks—which have made the public system more attractive to affluent families who previously fled to suburbs or private schools. Consequently, the demographic makeup has shifted: "The share of white students in the city's public schools has grown from 4.5 percent in 2000 to about 14 percent today."

This demographic shift complicates the data. Wexler notes that Mike Petrilli of the Fordham Institute tries to contextualize low college-readiness rates by comparing them to national averages for poor students, but she dismantles this defense by pointing out that the few D.C. students who are ready are overwhelmingly white. "In 2025, only 4 percent of economically disadvantaged D.C. students met the college-and-career bar, while 70 percent of white students did," she writes. This disparity suggests that the system is not failing to lift all boats; it is actively failing its poorest constituents despite overall score increases.

The author then pivots to a structural critique: the curriculum itself. She suspects that an inadequate, "homegrown" elementary literacy curriculum is a primary driver of these persistent gaps. Unlike the era of No Child Left Behind, which often prioritized test prep over content, Wexler argues that D.C. officials failed to adopt commercially available, knowledge-rich curricula when they had the chance. Instead, they relied on teachers with no training in curriculum design to create a system from scratch. "Maybe that was an understandable choice at the time," she concedes, "but homegrown curricula are rarely effective."

This argument is strengthened by her critique of EdReports, the influential organization that rates educational materials. Wexler highlights a troubling conflict of interest: the new leader of EdReports is Dr. Lewis D. Ferebee, the former DCPS chancellor who oversaw the retention of this unproven homegrown curriculum. "During his seven-year tenure as DCPS chancellor, he didn't adopt an elementary literacy curriculum that had been reviewed by EdReports," Wexler writes. This creates a circular logic where the gatekeeper of quality has a vested interest in defending the status quo of the district they once led.

"If you don't start building kids' academic knowledge early, the gaps between high- and low-performing students increase every year and become almost impossible to narrow."

Critics might argue that curriculum adoption is only one piece of a complex puzzle involving teacher training and community engagement. Wexler acknowledges this, noting that even a perfect curriculum can fail without proper implementation. Yet, her point stands: you cannot implement your way out of a lack of content-rich material. The reliance on standardized tests as the primary measure of success remains a fundamental flaw in this entire ecosystem.

Bottom Line

Wexler's most compelling contribution is her refusal to accept "growth" as a sufficient proxy for educational equity, forcing readers to confront the reality that D.C.'s celebrated progress has not translated into readiness for its most vulnerable students. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its heavy reliance on standardized test scores as the ultimate arbiter of success; while she calls these "blunt instruments," her entire argument is built upon their data points. Ultimately, the reader must watch whether the new leadership at EdReports can break free from the very system it was tasked to evaluate, or if the cycle of mediocrity will continue under a new banner of reform.
