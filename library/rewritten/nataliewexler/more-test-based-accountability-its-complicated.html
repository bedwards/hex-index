Natalie Wexler dismantles a convenient but dangerous narrative: that simply returning to strict test-based accountability will fix declining reading scores. She argues that the current obsession with measuring abstract comprehension skills has actually warped instruction, forcing schools to waste time on strategies that fail students who lack background knowledge.

The Illusion of Accountability

Wexler begins by challenging the assumption that the decline in test scores is a recent phenomenon caused solely by the pandemic. She points out that data shows a downward trend starting well before 2020, specifically around 2015 when the federal No Child Left Behind Act (NCLB) was replaced by the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA). While some politicians argue this policy shift caused the drop, Wexler notes that progress had already stalled years prior. "Progress basically stalled after 2008, the year of the Great Recession—and seven years before NCLB was replaced by ESSA," she writes. This timing suggests that socioeconomic factors played a larger role than the specific federal mandate.

The core of her argument rests on a critical distinction between math and reading. Math tests generally measure concepts taught in school, whereas standardized reading tests purport to measure abstract skills independent of content. Wexler cites cognitive psychologist Daniel Willingham to drive this home: "Standardized reading tests... are really 'knowledge tests in disguise.'" This is a pivotal insight for busy leaders trying to understand why test prep isn't working; if the test measures knowledge, but schools are teaching only generic strategies, they are setting students up for failure.

Standardized reading tests have led to "mindless test-prep and instruction in 'reading strategies'"—strategies that don't work in the absence of relevant knowledge.

Critics might argue that removing accountability entirely allows low-performing schools to hide their failures, a point Wexler concedes. She acknowledges that without standardized tests, inequities based on race and income could remain invisible, masking the reality that "an A grade at one school doesn't necessarily mean the same as an A at another." However, she insists that the current method of accountability is counterproductive because it pressures educators to focus on the wrong things.

The Knowledge Gap and the Texas Experiment

Wexler explains why reading scores are harder to raise than math scores. At lower grade levels, decoding words matters most, but as students advance, tests assume a "minimum threshold" of background knowledge that many lack due to their elementary school experiences. This connects directly to the Matthew Effect in education, where early gaps in vocabulary and knowledge compound over time, leaving older students unable to demonstrate comprehension regardless of their actual reading ability. She notes that by high school, "it may be too late to make up for all they've missed during an elementary school experience that was largely devoid of content."

To solve this, Wexler suggests we need accountability tied to curriculum content rather than abstract skills. She highlights a controversial move in Texas, where officials are creating a mandatory reading list aligned with state standards. While the media has focused on the inclusion of Bible passages and cultural diversity debates, Wexler sees a structural opportunity: "Texas is doing what I and other advocates for knowledge-building have urged: specifying content to be covered and attaching accountability to teaching that content."

However, she remains deeply skeptical about how this will play out politically. The specific choices made by Texas officials—such as including Margaret Thatcher's eulogy for Ronald Reagan alongside Shakespeare while omitting context on McCarthyism in relation to The Crucible—risk turning a pedagogical shift into a culture war. Wexler warns that "those kinds of choices... could well lead to pushback from school districts, especially those that would prefer not to get embroiled in controversies." If knowledge-building is perceived as inherently conservative, the necessary reforms may be rejected by districts across the political spectrum.

The idea that comprehension should be taught as just a set of abstract skills and strategies is now so deeply ingrained that simply easing up on testing requirements isn't enough to change that approach.

Bottom Line

Wexler's strongest contribution is exposing how current accountability measures actively prevent schools from teaching the knowledge students need to read complex texts. Her biggest vulnerability lies in the political feasibility of her solution; tying tests to a specific curriculum invites intense ideological battles that could derail progress before it begins. The path forward requires not just better tests, but a consensus on content that transcends partisan divides.
