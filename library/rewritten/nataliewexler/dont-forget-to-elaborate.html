Natalie Wexler identifies a dangerous blind spot in the current education reform movement: the obsession with memorization is crowding out the very thinking skills schools claim to prioritize. While the science of learning has finally validated the need for explicit instruction, Wexler argues that stopping at retrieval practice is a strategic error that leaves students unable to apply what they know. This is not just a pedagogical tweak; it is a fundamental correction to ensure that 'evidence-informed' teaching doesn't devolve into rote drills that fail the most vulnerable learners.

The Retrieval Trap

Wexler acknowledges the massive shift occurring globally, noting that "a top-down and bottom-up movement in England and parts of Australia is infusing scientifically grounded principles into classroom teaching." She rightly celebrates the rejection of the old myth that factual knowledge is harmful, observing that "scientific studies, in contrast, have yielded abundant evidence that these teaching strategies are hugely beneficial, especially for the most vulnerable students." The energy in these new classrooms is undeniable, with teachers maintaining a "brisk pace" that prevents attention from wandering.

However, Wexler spots a critical gap. She writes, "What I didn't see, for the most part, were questions that required students to make new connections or add their own ideas." The danger, she argues, is that good ideas are being taken to extremes. As education researcher Carl Hendrick has argued, retrieval practice has become "the darling of the new evidence-informed movement, the miracle treatment that promises deeper learning simply by asking students to bring information to mind." This framing is effective because it exposes a new dogma replacing an old one. Critics might note that for students with severe learning gaps, the cognitive load of elaboration can be paralyzing, but Wexler anticipates this by suggesting scaffolding rather than abandonment of the technique.

Laying the groundwork without building on it doesn't work either.

The Power of Elaboration

The core of Wexler's argument rests on the concept of "elaboration," which she defines as "adding something to information you've absorbed." This goes beyond simple recall. She cites cognitive scientist Jeffrey Karpicke, who states, "We want students to engage in deep, meaningful, thoughtful processing. And that is what we really mean when we're talking about elaboration." Wexler presents compelling evidence from a 2024 study showing that students who generated their own examples achieved deeper comprehension, regardless of whether they were forced to retrieve the info from memory first or could consult the text.

This finding is significant because it challenges the rigid hierarchy often imposed on learning strategies. Wexler paraphrases the study's implication: when you are elaborating, you are simultaneously retrieving information. The argument lands because it offers a practical solution to the "retrieval trap"—teachers can use elaboration to reinforce memory without the high cognitive cost of pure recall tasks for complex topics. It shifts the focus from "did they remember?" to "can they use it?"

Balancing Cognitive Load

A significant portion of the piece addresses the fear that students lack the background knowledge to elaborate. Wexler engages directly with John Sweller, the father of cognitive load theory, who suggests that elaboration is only for those who know a topic "really, really well." Sweller argues that for novices, it is more efficient to study "worked examples" produced by experts rather than attempting to generate their own connections.

Wexler respectfully disagrees, arguing that "you don't need to wait until students are experts to make elaborative tasks worthwhile." She proposes a middle ground: modulating cognitive load through structured writing. Instead of demanding a full essay, teachers can provide sentence stems like, "Lincoln is considered a great president, but ____________." This approach allows students to engage in high-order thinking without being overwhelmed. The strength here is the actionable specificity; she moves from abstract theory to a concrete classroom tool that bridges the gap between novice and expert thinking.

Writing is an inherently elaborative activity, even at the sentence level.

Democratizing Deep Thinking

The final thrust of Wexler's commentary is a call for equity. She warns that classrooms focused solely on repetition "play into the hands of those who reject science-informed instruction as nothing more than 'rote memorization.'" To protect the credibility of the cognitive science movement, schools must demonstrate that it involves more than just drills. She highlights that elaboration is not just for the gifted; in a second-grade class she visited, students successfully compared Ancient Greek civilization to others, proving that "elaboration" is accessible to young learners when properly scaffolded.

Wexler concludes that enabling all students to elaborate is essential for them to "realize their full academic and cognitive potential." She points to the Writing Revolution method, where embedding writing instruction across subjects enhanced not just writing skills but "learning ability, reading comprehension—and thinking." This is the piece's most powerful insight: the mechanism for deep learning is not a separate subject, but a way of interacting with content that must be taught explicitly.

Bottom Line

Natalie Wexler's argument is a necessary corrective to the current fixation on retrieval practice, successfully proving that memorization without synthesis is a dead end. Her strongest contribution is the practical framework for modulating cognitive load, making deep thinking accessible to novices rather than reserving it for experts. The biggest vulnerability lies in the implementation burden; without robust teacher training and curriculum support, the shift to elaboration could easily collapse into confusion, but the roadmap she provides is the most coherent solution currently available.

To ensure that the green shoots of the science of learning movement continue to grow, schools in the vanguard need to demonstrate that following cognitive science involves more than that.
