In a landscape where education debates often devolve into culture war shouting matches, Natalie Wexler offers a rare, data-driven intervention: the new Texas social studies standards are flawed, but their greatest sin is not bias—it's that they are finally specific. While critics scream about patriotism and content density, Wexler argues that the real crisis in American education is a decades-long vacuum of factual knowledge caused by vague, "useless" guidelines that leave students ill-equipped to understand democracy itself.

The Specificity Paradox

Wexler immediately dismantles the assumption that detailed standards are inherently dangerous. She notes that arriving in the wake of federal efforts to promote patriotic education, the Texas State Board of Education's new rules have been attacked as pro-Christian and anti-Muslim. Yet, she points out a glaring contradiction in the opposition: "It might surprise lay people to learn that this untested approach is to present historical events in chronological order." The American Historical Association (AHA) has labeled this logical sequencing as "experimental and untested," a claim Wexler finds baffling given that the alternative—spiraling through topics without coherence—is what has failed for generations.

The core of her argument rests on the dismal state of current student knowledge. She cites national assessments showing only 13 percent of eighth graders scoring proficient in U.S. history and 22 percent in civics. "Vague social studies standards aren't the only reason for this dismal situation, but they surely haven't helped," she writes. The problem isn't that schools are teaching too much; it's that they are teaching nothing concrete.

Wexler contrasts the new Texas rules with the "inadequate" standards found in 20 states, including Illinois, where a history strand covering all grade levels was deemed "so general as to be useless." She highlights how previous Texas standards reduced the entire 20th century to a "single twenty-eight-word expectation," mentioning industrialization and world wars without context. By demanding content specificity, the new standards force teachers to engage with actual history rather than abstract concepts like "Change, Continuity and Context."

The Chronological Shift and Historical Gaps

The piece takes a sharp turn into pedagogical theory when discussing the shift from spiraling curricula to chronological progression. Wexler explains that while critics fear this approach ignores marginalized groups, the new standards actually include more non-Western content than acknowledged. Fourth-graders now study Mansa Musa and the Malian Empire alongside European history. However, she acknowledges a valid tension in how local priorities distort global context.

She illustrates this with the inclusion of the Clovis civilization, which flourished in North America 13,000 years ago. While historically significant to Texas, Wexler questions whether it should displace the Fertile Crescent, where humans first transitioned from hunter-gatherers to farmers, inventing writing and the wheel. "That might be an interesting topic, but history standards focus on particular parts of the world in certain eras because that's where influential things were happening," she argues.

Critics might note that prioritizing the Fertile Crescent over indigenous North American history risks reinforcing a Eurocentric worldview, even if the intent is to teach "influential" innovations. Wexler balances this by noting the standards do discuss slavery as a "horrendous institution" and include various Indian tribes frequently. The danger, she suggests, lies not in the content itself but in the sheer volume: the standards are so dense that teachers may struggle to cover them meaningfully.

But generally, state history standards take a 'spiraling' approach, reintroducing similar content at higher grade levels in greater depth... Perhaps they mean it's untested at the K-12 level. But you could argue that the standard spiraling approach, while 'tested,' hasn't been working very well.

Wexler also critiques the forced connections between world events and Texas history. While linking Rome to American government makes sense, she finds it a stretch to expect third graders to understand how Sam Houston compared himself to Gaius Marius or how the printing press influenced the American Revolution 300 years later. These forced linkages create a "whirlwind tour" that risks becoming superficial.

The Implementation Gap and Political Polarization

The most sobering section of Wexler's commentary addresses the reality of implementation. Even the best standards are useless if they sit on a shelf. She points out that few states test social studies, meaning teachers often skip the subject until high school. Texas is an exception, but testing only occurs in eighth grade, creating a "disaster" scenario where students face a capstone course without foundational knowledge.

She contrasts the polarized environment in Texas with Virginia's recent success. There, a board evenly split between Republicans and Democrats adopted standards that were specific yet balanced, noting that "America is both exceptional and imperfect." In Texas, votes on the standards ran along party lines, fueled by rhetoric about the "intrinsic superiority" of the state.

Wexler concludes that the partisan firestorm distracts from the real issue: students are not learning history at all. She cites James Traub's observation that the problem isn't biased ideology but a lack of content entirely. The push for specific standards is often mischaracterized as conservative, yet it is essential for any functional democracy.

It's unfortunate that some of those specifics have, in both instances, sparked partisan controversy that could result in distracting pushback from districts, schools and parents.

The author urges other states to look beyond Texas's divisiveness and focus on the necessity of content-rich curricula backed by testing. Without this foundation, students will lack the background knowledge to understand complex civic issues, regardless of their political leanings.

Bottom Line

Wexler's strongest move is reframing the debate from "what we teach" to "whether we teach anything at all," using hard data on student failure to justify rigorous standards. However, her argument stumbles slightly in assuming that chronological density automatically leads to better understanding without addressing how teachers will manage such a heavy workload. The verdict: specific content is non-negotiable for civic literacy, but without early testing and teacher support, even the best standards risk becoming another shelf-sitter.
