Benn Jordan turns a mundane inventory task into a brutal, self-deprecating audit of his own creative life, arguing that 90 to 95% of his daily work is "fueled by Spite." Rather than a standard gear review, he uses a tier-list format to expose the gap between expensive equipment and actual utility, proving that the most valuable tool in a studio is often the one that forces you to work harder, not easier.

The Architecture of Spite

Jordan frames this massive undertaking not as a service to fans, but as a necessary act of insurance documentation and personal catharsis. He admits that the video's existence is a direct response to the internet's tendency to police creator behavior. "I would guesstimate that maybe between 90 and 95% of my daily activities are fueled by Spite," he writes, setting a tone that is equal parts humorous and brutally honest. This framing is effective because it disarms the viewer; he isn't selling you a dream, he is showing you the messy reality of a working artist.

The author establishes a rigorous, almost absurdly specific ranking system to categorize his collection. He defines the bottom of the barrel with surgical precision: "F tier is dog [__] there's nothing redeemable about it at all other than human civilization gaining the knowledge of where Rock Bottom is." Conversely, he reserves the highest praise for items that fundamentally alter artistic output, noting that an "S tier product comes but once a decade in an entire category of products." This distinction is crucial. It shifts the conversation from technical specifications to emotional impact, a metric that is far more difficult to quantify but infinitely more valuable for a creator.

Critics might argue that a tier list is too reductive for complex musical instruments, but Jordan anticipates this by acknowledging his own biases. He admits that some items are rated down simply because they don't fit his specific workflow, not because they are objectively bad. This transparency strengthens his credibility; he is not a detached reviewer, but a user with a specific, flawed relationship with his tools.

The Paradox of Utility

The most compelling section of Jordan's commentary is his treatment of gear that is technically impressive but practically useless. He highlights the Akai APC 64, a device he claims "transformed not only the way that I think about music but the way that I think about creativity," only to reveal he "literally never even turn this thing on." The reason? It only works with specific software he doesn't use. This is a stark reminder that the most hyped tools often fail in the real world because of ecosystem lock-in rather than poor design.

He contrasts this with the Roland TR-8, an older emulation that he prefers over newer, more feature-rich successors. "I like this thing way better it's way simpler it's super easy to program a beat," he argues, giving it a B tier despite its age. The logic here is sound: complexity often breeds friction, and friction kills creativity. Jordan's willingness to rate a "lesser" machine higher than a "superior" one challenges the industry's obsession with feature creep.

However, not every piece of gear survives his scrutiny. He delivers a scathing verdict on the Yamaha QY10, calling it "for people who hate themselves" and labeling it the "worst experience" he's ever had programming music. He also dismisses the Antelope Audio Zeno interface, praising its clocking but trashing its user interface and its "DLC" model for effects. "I hate the fact that you can buy effects for it that there's a little store where you get DLC for your audio interface," he writes. This critique of the "software-as-a-service" model bleeding into hardware is a vital counterpoint to the industry's current trajectory.

The Survivor's Guilt of Gear

Jordan's assessment of the Behringer BCF 2000 offers a fascinating look at durability and history. He describes the unit as a UFC fighter who "absorbed insane amounts of punishment and then would come out winning on top," noting that he has toured the world with it while it literally fell apart. "I'm going to give it an A," he concludes, despite the physical decay. This suggests that for a working musician, reliability and history often outweigh pristine condition.

Yet, he is equally quick to discard gear that feels like a gimmick. The Elisis Air Effects unit, once a performative staple, is now rated D tier because the effects are "deplorable by today's standards." He admits he looked foolish using it on stage, a rare moment of vulnerability for a professional. "I probably looked like an [__]," he confesses. This self-awareness is the video's secret weapon; by admitting his past mistakes, he validates the viewer's own regrets about gear purchases.

I literally never even turn this thing on because I didn't realize that it only works with Ableton.

The author's final verdict on the Cyclone Analogic Bass Line is a testament to the power of specialization. He calls it the "best acid machine I've ever used in my life," an S-tier tool that led him to sell his legendary TB-303. "If you care about acid music and I care about acid music a whole lot it is an S tier synthesizer," he states. This reinforces his central thesis: a tool is only as good as its fit for the artist's specific needs.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's tier list succeeds because it prioritizes the human element over the technical spec sheet, proving that the best gear is the one that disappears into the work. While the format is playful, the underlying argument is serious: in an era of endless features and digital lock-in, simplicity and reliability are the new luxury. Readers should watch for how this philosophy applies to their own creative bottlenecks, rather than just looking for a shopping list.
