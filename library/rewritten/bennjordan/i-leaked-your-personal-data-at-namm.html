Benn Jordan doesn't just survey musicians; he weaponizes statistics to expose the absurdity of the industry's culture and the financial opacity of its governing body. By cross-referencing gear preferences with political leanings, sexual orientation, and even bowel habits, he constructs a satirical mirror that reflects a community more obsessed with identity than income. This is not a standard industry report; it is a forensic audit of the music world's soul, wrapped in the guise of a data joke.

The Data as Satire

Jordan begins by dismantling the romantic notion of the struggling artist with cold, hard numbers. He notes that "80% of musicians spend more money on making and Publishing music than they make back from it." This statistic sets a grim stage, but he quickly pivots to the bizarre correlations that define the modern musician's psyche. He writes, "People who use Apple headphones are 25% more likely to lose money from their music people who use burer Dynamic headphones are 29% more likely to make money from their music." The humor here is sharp, yet it underscores a genuine divide between perceived prestige and actual financial survival.

The survey's methodology is intentionally ridiculous, blending serious inquiries with the absurd to highlight how little we actually know about the people behind the gear. Jordan points out that "using FL Studio actually reduces diarrhea by 50% whereas using Ableton increases diarrhea by 64%." He immediately undercuts this with a crucial disclaimer: "we probably don't need to remind you of this but correlation is not causation." This self-awareness is vital; it prevents the piece from becoming mere misinformation and instead frames it as a critique of how easily data can be twisted to fit a narrative.

"If you change your music software to treat your irritable bowel syndrome that would be really F stupid."

The commentary on political and social divides is where the piece gains its sharpest edge. Jordan observes that "Trump voters are 42% more likely to favor Distortion as their favorite effect" and that "people who own Pioneer Pro Audio products are 63% more likely to believe that Trump won the 2020 election." While these correlations are presented as data points, they serve as a mirror to the industry's deepening polarization. Critics might argue that reducing complex political views to gear preferences is reductive, but Jordan's intent is clearly to show how tribal these preferences have become. He even touches on the most sensitive demographics, noting that "bisexual people like Dolby Atmos about 30% less than gay lesbian and pansexual people," weaving identity politics into the technical discussion of audio formats.

The Nonprofit Paradox

The tone shifts dramatically when Jordan turns his attention to the National Association of Music Merchants (NAMM). He strips away the industry's glossy veneer to reveal a financial structure that looks suspiciously like a private piggy bank. He writes, "one thing Nam succeeds at is their excellent CEO pay packages in 2022 n had two different CEOs simultaneously earning a total of over $1 million salary." This stands in stark contrast to other major charities, as he notes, "N routinely pays their CEO more than the CEOs of the Red Cross Planned Parenthood ACLU."

The argument here is not just about executive compensation; it is about the mission drift of a supposed educational charity. Jordan highlights that "in 2021 n lost 13.7 million in publicly traded stocks in a year when the S&P 500 gained a impressive 20 8%." He questions the transparency of these losses, stating, "I'm really curious on what stocks these were that lost so much that year but they forgot to disclose what they were in their 990 IRS forms." This lack of disclosure is a critical vulnerability in the organization's claim to be a public trust.

"A Good Charity gives away most or all of the money that it makes."

Jordan further exposes the ambiguity of NAMM's spending, noting that "one of nam's biggest expenses is other and what that means or where that goes is only known by God." This phrase cuts through the bureaucratic jargon to suggest a level of opacity that would be unacceptable in any other sector. He acknowledges the organization's historical contributions, mentioning that "since 1994 Nam has raised and contributed hundreds of millions of dollars towards music education," but he insists that the current financial management is unsustainable. The juxtaposition of the organization's educational mandate with its "decade of pillaging through an endowment" creates a powerful indictment of its leadership.

Raw Demographics and Industry Reality

In the final section, Jordan abandons the correlations to present the raw data, which is often just as revealing as the jokes. He reveals that "most people's favorite song has a medium Tempo but Peppe is also popular and chill is also popular," a mundane detail that grounds the survey in reality. However, the political data remains striking: "42.7% said Kamala 5% said third party only eight of the 548 respondents voted for Trump." This skew suggests that the survey sample is heavily biased toward a specific demographic, a limitation Jordan implicitly acknowledges by noting that "about half of people that filled out are not American."

The survey also touches on the friction between creators and platforms. Jordan writes, "I have made two times more off of shorting Spotify than I've made from my music on Spotify with 1.7 million listeners." This is a damning admission of the broken economic model for artists. He advises, "if you're not get the middleman out and the entire pie grows and Everybody Eats," a call to action that resonates with the financial frustrations expressed earlier in the piece. The data also shows a deep distrust of subscription models, with "85.5% of respondents do not like subscription software."

"I'm taking it extraordinarily easy on them and not mentioning anything other than what has been publicly filed."

Despite the biting satire and financial criticism, Jordan concludes with a nuanced view of NAMM's legacy. He admits that "despite what appears to me as a decade of pillaging through an endowment at the expense of donors I do think that the organization has been an overall net positive." This balance prevents the piece from being a simple takedown, acknowledging the complex role the organization plays in the ecosystem. He even notes a potential shift, seeing documents that suggest "the new CEO got a pay cut in 2024 is now only making $460,000 a year," leaving the door open for reform.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's piece is a masterclass in using data to tell a story that is both hilarious and deeply concerning. The strongest part of the argument is the juxtaposition of absurd correlations with serious financial malfeasance, forcing the reader to question the integrity of the institutions they trust. Its biggest vulnerability is the inherent bias of the survey sample, which may not represent the global music community, but the financial data regarding NAMM stands on its own. Readers should watch for how the industry responds to these revelations, particularly regarding executive compensation and the transparency of nonprofit finances.
