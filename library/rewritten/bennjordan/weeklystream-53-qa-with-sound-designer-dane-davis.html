Benn Jordan's interview with legendary sound designer Dane Davis does more than recount the making of The Matrix; it exposes the terrifying fragility of digital art history. While most retrospectives focus on visual spectacle, Jordan highlights a startling truth: the very sounds that defined a generation of cinema were built on software that no longer exists, on hardware that has rotted away, and on a workflow that was essentially a gamble against obsolescence. This is not just a story about creativity; it is a cautionary tale about how we preserve the cultural artifacts of the digital age.

The Aesthetic of the Virtual

Jordan frames the conversation around a specific, almost paradoxical challenge Davis faced in 1997: how to make the "virtual world" of The Matrix sound beautiful, rather than the typical "crappy" digital noise of the era. As Benn Jordan notes, "digital sound meant crappy sound... the grainy quantized quality of sort of low res quality of the simulation." Davis needed a texture that felt simulated but not ugly. The solution came from an unlikely source: a piece of software called "Medicine," which allowed for graphical audio manipulation. Jordan writes, "It was an epiphany because it output... a very beautiful very natural organic feel to it even though I was deliberately making things that weren't real." This approach was revolutionary because it treated sound design as a performance rather than a linear editing task. Davis could move sliders in real-time, capturing the "inertia" and "grain" of the software to create the unique sonic signature of the digital realm.

"I could feel it was like I was looking at a code screen right and I could see the matrix movie through that sound through the pro tools window."

The interview reveals that the iconic "code" sound wasn't synthesized from scratch but was a result of aggressive, iterative processing. Davis took a recording of raindrops in a barrel and subjected it to a brutal cycle of stretching, reversing, and noise reduction. Jordan explains that Davis would use digital noise reduction "in reverse" to strip away consistent frequencies, then re-import the result into Medicine to stretch it again. After "five or six or seven generations," the mundane sound of rain became the liquid, electric code of the Wachowskis' vision. This process underscores a vital point often missed in film analysis: the most futuristic sounds are often just mundane reality pushed through a distorted lens until it breaks.

The Green Screen Paradox

One of the most compelling sections of the interview, as curated by Jordan, is the comparison between the sound designer's experience and the film's narrative. Davis describes working on green screens, imagining the "sentinels" and the code that didn't exist yet. Jordan observes, "My creative world is a lot like a cipher... I'm looking at these green screens all day long imagining what a sentinel is going to look like and what it's going to sound like." This creates a poetic irony where the sound designer, like the characters in the film, is projecting reality onto a void. Critics might note that this romanticizes the often-frustrating lack of pre-visualization tools in the late 90s, but the comparison holds up: both the characters and the crew were navigating a fabricated reality with only fragments to guide them.

The discussion also touches on the "digital temporary rate," a cynical observation Davis makes about the lifespan of digital media. "If it is digital it is temporary," Davis asserts. This is not hyperbole; it is a logistical nightmare. Jordan details how the original backups for The Matrix were stored on DDS tapes, then migrated through AIT, VXA, and LTO formats. The problem isn't just the physical degradation of the tape, but the disappearance of the entire ecosystem required to read it. "The software is gone and the operating system that the software runs on is gone and the hardware that the operating system runs on is gone," Jordan writes. This creates a situation where even a simple screenshot for a documentary becomes a Herculean task, requiring the hunting down of ancient Macs that can run 1998-era operating systems.

"If it is digital it is temporary... most of my stuff I learned very quickly to just have parity drives like RAID parity with everything and that's it."

The Fragility of Memory

The interview concludes with a sobering reflection on the loss of the original "Medicine" software and the sounds created with it. While the resurrected version of the software helped with The Matrix Resurrections, the original assets remain perilously close to being lost forever. Jordan points out that the team had to migrate everything in 2003, but the sheer number of format shifts over two decades means that much of the original creative process is irretrievable. The fact that a documentary about sound design, Making Waves, struggled to find still images of the tools used to create The Matrix is a damning indictment of how little we document our digital workflows. As Jordan puts it, "We didn't think back then as we do now... we don't really adequately document our work."

This section serves as a stark reminder that our cultural history is not stored in stone, but on spinning platters and magnetic tape that are actively decaying. The effort to preserve these sounds is not just archival; it is an act of rescue. The interview suggests that without a concerted effort to emulate old hardware and software, we risk losing the "texture" of entire eras of cinema.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's curation of this interview succeeds by shifting the focus from the glamour of the final film to the precarious engineering behind it. The strongest argument here is that digital art is inherently ephemeral, and our current methods of preservation are insufficient to stop the erosion of our cultural memory. The biggest vulnerability in the narrative is the reliance on individual heroics—like Davis keeping ancient Macs—to save history, rather than systemic solutions for digital archiving. Readers should watch for how the industry evolves its preservation standards, because if The Matrix sounds are this hard to save, what happens to the next generation of digital cinema?
