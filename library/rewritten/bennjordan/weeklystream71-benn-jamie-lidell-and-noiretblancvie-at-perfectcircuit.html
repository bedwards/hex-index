In a stream that reads less like a technical review and more like a philosophical manifesto on the nature of creativity, Benn Jordan dismantles the modern obsession with software efficiency to argue for the irreplaceable value of tactile hardware. While the conversation begins with the whimsical promise of "little shot glasses" for a live stream at Perfect Circuit, it quickly pivots to a rigorous defense of why musicians should continue to buy expensive, fragile gear in an era where a laptop can emulate it all. Jordan's central claim is that the friction of physical interaction—the smell of tape, the resistance of a knob, the risk of blowing a circuit—is not a bug in the system, but the very feature that makes art human.

The Case for Tactile Friction

Jordan does not shy away from the absurdity of the hobbyist mindset, admitting that he has "spent ridiculous amounts of money on things that have actually" questionable utility. Yet, he frames this expenditure as a necessary investment in the creative process itself. He draws a sharp distinction between the utility of a tool and the experience of using it, asking the audience to consider the validity of oil painting when an iPad can perfectly mimic the result. "Why do an oil painting when on an ipad you can make a drawing that looks like an oil painting?" he asks, immediately following up with the devastating retort: "that's true... and what a ridiculous thing that actually is to say."

This argument lands because it reframes the debate from sonic fidelity to sensory engagement. Jordan suggests that the mouse, the primary interface for modern digital production, is an "industrial office" tool that disconnects the artist from their work. He notes that while software offers perfect recall and polyphony, it lacks the "instant touch" that saves a producer thousands of micro-decisions. "You had just having the instant touch rather than having to move a mouse across the screen huge," he observes, highlighting how the physical act of turning a knob changes the mental state of the creator. The evidence here is anecdotal but deeply resonant for anyone who has ever felt the numbness of a mouse-based workflow.

Critics might note that this romanticization of hardware ignores the accessibility and reproducibility that software provides, allowing artists without thousands of dollars in gear to create world-class work. However, Jordan acknowledges this trade-off, suggesting that the "fetishization" of equipment is a valid artistic choice, not a financial necessity.

The Danger of Closed Systems

Beyond the tactile argument, Jordan launches a scathing critique of the industry's shift toward closed, proprietary standards, using the Swedish design firm Teenage Engineering as a case study. He recounts his decision to step away from their modular system specifically because it lacked "reverse circuit protection." In a world where a single mistake can fry thousands of dollars of equipment, Jordan argues that omitting a diode costing "about point seven cents" is a catastrophic failure of responsibility. "Why would you do that like why you know that was just kind of an oversight that," he questions, framing the omission not as a design choice but as a symptom of a company too focused on aesthetics and "lambo life" to care about the user's safety.

He extends this critique to the broader industry, contrasting the open standard of MIDI with the closed protocols of modern audio networking like Dante. "Dave Smith doesn't get money every time you that's right buying a buy it's like that," Jordan explains, lamenting that the industry is moving away from open collaboration toward monetized silos. This is a crucial point that often gets lost in gear reviews: the long-term health of a creative ecosystem depends on open standards that allow for interoperability and repair.

The argument is bolstered by his personal experience with the Teenage Engineering PC tower case, which he describes as "loaded with problems" due to a lack of basic engineering considerations like fan placement. "It's a little more complicated than that there's like there's a lot of things to consider," he asserts, challenging the notion that a company can simply "put some metal together" and sell a premium product without addressing fundamental functionality.

The Human Element in a Digital Age

Ultimately, Jordan's commentary circles back to the human body and the sensory experience of making music. He cites fellow artist Marta Salami, who uses only tape machines because she needs to "use my body to use my hands to use my smell." Jordan agrees, noting that the mechanical nature of these devices provides a connection that digital interfaces cannot replicate. "I'm a light you know you're a human you know," he says, emphasizing that the artist is not just a controller of sound but a physical participant in its creation.

He acknowledges the irony that despite decades of technological advancement, the primary interface for computer interaction remains the mouse, a device he describes as "strangely industrial." While he admits that trackballs and touchpads offer some relief, he maintains that the mouse is a poor substitute for the direct manipulation of physical controls. "We've come all this way and we use a mouse yeah like that's how we interact because that's all we've got," he reflects, suggesting that the industry has stagnated in its approach to human-computer interaction.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's most compelling argument is that the value of hardware lies not in its sonic superiority, but in its ability to re-engage the human body with the creative process, turning production from a clinical task into a tactile experience. While his defense of expensive, fragile gear may seem elitist to some, his critique of closed standards and the dehumanizing nature of the mouse interface offers a necessary counter-narrative to the industry's relentless push toward software efficiency. The strongest part of his case is the realization that "if i enjoy it then there must be some value to it," a simple truth that challenges the utilitarian metrics often used to judge artistic tools.
