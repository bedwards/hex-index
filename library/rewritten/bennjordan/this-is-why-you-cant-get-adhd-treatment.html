Benn Jordan transforms a personal health crisis into a forensic investigation of American pharmaceutical failure, arguing that the ADHD medication shortage is not a supply chain accident but a calculated business strategy. While most coverage focuses on the DEA's quotas, Jordan brings a rare, on-the-ground perspective: he actually bought stock in the company profiting from the shortage to hear the CEO's unvarnished logic. This is not just a story about missing pills; it is a damning indictment of a system where shareholder value is prioritized over the 19% reduction in mortality that treatment provides.

The Hidden Cost of Untreated ADHD

Jordan begins by dismantling the stereotype of ADHD, noting that for high-functioning adults, the disorder often manifests as an inability to improvise rather than an inability to focus. He writes, "I expected Adderall to make me feel cracked out but it made me realize that I feel cracked out by default when I'm not taking it." This personal revelation grounds the subsequent data, which is far more alarming than the anecdote. The author presents a stark reality: untreated ADHD is a life-threatening condition, not a mere inconvenience.

He cites a study showing that medicated patients face a "19% lower risk of death" than those untreated, a statistic that frames the shortage as a public health emergency. Jordan paraphrases the broader consequences, noting that untreated adolescents are twice as likely to abuse substances and young drivers are three times more likely to crash. The argument here is compelling because it shifts the narrative from "drug abuse" to "preventable mortality." Critics might argue that correlation does not equal causation in some of the risk factors, but the sheer volume of data regarding suicide, accidents, and comorbidities creates a weight that is hard to dismiss.

"Having untreated ADHD is incredibly dangerous, and that's on top of studies showing that untreated ADHD results in poor job performance, poor school performance, higher divorce rates, and so on."

The DEA's Blunder and the Corporate Opportunity

The piece pivots to the mechanics of the shortage, identifying the Drug Enforcement Administration's 2022 decision to cap amphetamine production as the catalyst. Jordan points out the absurdity of this move, stating, "there's actually no evidence to support this like at all" regarding the claim that telehealth prescriptions caused a massive spike in abuse. He argues that the DEA, described as "chronically allergic to facts," punished legitimate patients for the actions of a tiny minority.

However, Jordan's most incisive critique targets the pharmaceutical industry's response. He notes that manufacturers are not required to explain shortages, yet the data shows they are producing far less than the DEA allows. "Our data showed that in 2022 manufacturers did not produce the full amount that these limits permitted them to make," he quotes the DEA administrator, highlighting a gap between regulatory permission and corporate action. The author suggests that companies are intentionally withholding supply to drive up prices for their proprietary, non-generic alternatives.

This section is where the argument gains its sharpest edge. Jordan describes his own ordeal trying to fill a prescription, a labyrinth of insurance denials and pharmacy shortages that forces patients to pay exorbitant prices. He details how one manufacturer, UCB BioPharma, actively markets its expensive alternative, Vyvanse, with coupons that work everywhere except where the patient actually needs them. "It is more expensive but at you the manufacturer has this coupon that works with everyone literally everyone," Jordan notes, exposing the cynical marketing machine that thrives on the chaos.

The Investor's Perspective

The climax of Jordan's investigation is his decision to buy equity in the manufacturer to attend an investor-only livestream. There, he hears a CEO admit that the company made a strategic choice to "wind down any operation that we don't believe can drive near-term shareholder value." Jordan writes, "ultimately what that has netted us is an opportunity to become exquisitely focused... specifically looking at the prescription business most notably attention deficit hyperactivity disorder ADHD." The CEO then casually admits, "we're taking advantage still to this day of the fact that Adderall... have been intermittently in and out of supply."

This is the smoking gun. Jordan does not need to accuse the company of corruption; the CEO has confessed to prioritizing profit over patient access. The author's observation that "it's not like they're somehow connected to a generic Adderall manufacturer other than the private settlement agreement" suggests a coordinated effort to eliminate competition. The argument is that the shortage is a feature, not a bug, of the current pharmaceutical business model.

"Just because patients are 19% more likely to die as a result of our little drug shortage doesn't mean that y'all have the right to take it out on us shareholders."

Critics might note that supply chain disruptions are complex and that blaming a single company ignores global manufacturing constraints. However, Jordan's evidence that companies are producing below their legal quotas while simultaneously marketing expensive alternatives undermines the "supply chain" defense. The existence of services like "Needle" and "Ino Medfinder," which charge patients to make phone calls to find pills, further illustrates the market failure.

Bottom Line

Benn Jordan's coverage is a masterclass in investigative storytelling, using a personal journey to expose a systemic rot where human life is secondary to quarterly earnings. The strongest part of the argument is the direct evidence of corporate intent, captured in the CEO's own words during an investor call. The biggest vulnerability is the lack of a clear policy solution beyond exposing the problem, leaving the reader with the grim realization that the market has no incentive to fix itself. Until regulators force transparency on production quotas and break the link between shortage and profit, patients will remain trapped in this dystopian loop.
