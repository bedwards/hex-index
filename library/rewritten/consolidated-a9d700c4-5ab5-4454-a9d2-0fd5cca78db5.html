Across North American suburbs, a particular architectural form has come to define the landscape: the windowless, single-story, 50,000-square-foot retail shed set in a sea of asphalt. Two urbanist commentators — Dave Amos of City Beautiful and Jason Slaughter of Not Just Bikes — have recently trained their cameras on these structures, and while they arrive at the subject from different angles, together they sketch a fuller portrait of both the problem and the path out of it.

Slaughter's critique is the more scorching of the two. He describes big box stores as "single-story, windowless, boring, ugly ass buildings made from the cheapest possible materials," and argues that their damage radiates far beyond their own footprint. "When a big box store gobbles up a bunch of land," he observes, "it also ruins the land around it, encouraging sprawl, stroads, and car-centric infrastructure that makes it dangerous or impossible to walk anywhere." For Slaughter, the cheap prices advertised on the marquee conceal a hidden municipal subsidy: the stores, he contends, achieve their everyday low prices "by literally bankrupting cities" that must maintain the vast infrastructure these low-density developments demand.

"The way they achieve their everyday low prices is by literally bankrupting cities." — Jason Slaughter

Amos, by contrast, is less interested in prosecuting the big box than in asking what happens when one dies. And they do die, in remarkable numbers. He notes that Bed Bath & Beyond, which peaked at over 1,000 locations in 2009, is now entirely gone; that Party City closed 800 stores last year; that Kohl's is shuttering locations to cut costs. Meanwhile, Walmart still operates more than 4,600 U.S. stores and Target nearly 2,000 — a vast inventory of buildings that will, eventually, outlive their original tenants.

Amos's through-line is adaptive reuse. He opens with a vacant Lowe's in Aliso Viejo, California, which has been subdivided into smaller retailers including Daiso, had part of its parking lot carved away, and will soon host 343 apartments — 10 percent of them affordable to low-income households. "Big box stores have been adaptively reused into libraries, schools, offices, healthcare facilities, smaller shops, and more," he notes. The windowless box, it turns out, is surprisingly flexible once a city is willing to imagine it as something other than a store.

Two Diagnoses, One Disease

The two commentators are in fact describing the same underlying pathology from opposite ends. Slaughter is explaining why big boxes should never have been built the way they were; Amos is explaining what to do now that they exist and are failing. Read together, they suggest that the adaptive-reuse projects Amos celebrates are not merely clever design exercises but an overdue correction to the fiscal and spatial distortions Slaughter describes.

"Big box stores have been adaptively reused into libraries, schools, offices, healthcare facilities, smaller shops, and more." — Dave Amos

There is a useful tension between them as well. Slaughter's framing implies that the buildings themselves are the problem — ugly, anti-urban, fundamentally incompatible with a walkable city. Amos's Aliso Viejo example suggests the opposite: that the shell is salvageable, and that what needs to change is the land around it. Both can be true. The most successful reuse projects, the ones that include housing and reclaim parking lots, are precisely the ones that dismantle the sprawl geometry Slaughter indicts while preserving the structural bones Amos sees value in.

What the Sources Don't Address

Neither commentator spends much time on the political economy of reuse. Converting a dead Lowe's into apartments and small retail is a zoning fight as much as an architectural one; most suburban municipalities still have single-use commercial codes that would make the Aliso Viejo project illegal by right. Nor do they dwell on the question of who owns these buildings. Many big boxes sit on long-term ground leases or are held by real estate investment trusts whose incentives run counter to adaptive reuse — an empty building carried on the books at a favorable valuation can be worth more to a landlord than a renovated one generating modest rent.

There is also a labor dimension neither source raises. The small businesses and restaurants that move into subdivided big boxes are rarely unionized and often pay less than the anchor tenants they replace. A library or school is an unambiguous civic upgrade; a food hall of precarious franchises is a more complicated trade.

Bottom Line

Slaughter is right that the big box store, as a building type and a land-use pattern, has been a net drain on the cities that hosted it. Amos is right that the buildings themselves need not be demolished to undo that damage — that a windowless shed with a parking moat can become housing, civic space, and a genuine neighborhood if a city has the will and the zoning to allow it. The coming decade will produce a great many more empty boxes. The question is whether municipalities treat them as blight to be bulldozed, assets to be flipped, or raw material for the walkable places Slaughter wants and Amos is already documenting.
