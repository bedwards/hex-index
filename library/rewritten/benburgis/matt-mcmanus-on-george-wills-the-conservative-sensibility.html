Ben Burgis dissects George Will's attempt to salvage American conservatism by stripping it of its populist excesses, only to reveal a foundation built on a romanticized history that ignores the very people it claims to protect. The piece is notable not for its defense of right-wing liberalism, but for its surgical exposure of how that defense relies on a selective memory of the Founders that conveniently omits the institution of slavery. In an era where political identity is increasingly fractured, this analysis forces a confrontation with the uncomfortable reality that the "conservative sensibility" may be less about preserving a coherent tradition and more about curating a flattering myth.

The Architecture of a Selective Tradition

Burgis opens by acknowledging Will's intellectual heft, noting that The Conservative Sensibility is "easily the best defence of right-wing American liberalism in recent memory: erudite, analytically deep, powerfully literary." Yet, this praise is immediately undercut by the observation that the book's clarity is also its fatal flaw. Will attempts to seal the cracks in the conservative coalition by defining the movement strictly as the custodian of classical liberalism, a tradition rooted in John Locke and James Madison. Burgis argues that this definition is a "strength of the book, but it is also a weakness" because it requires the author to ignore the "competing strands" that actually define modern conservatism.

The author points out that Will's narrative conveniently sidesteps the "uglier manifestations" of the movement, including "Southern conservatism, segregationist populism (in the style of George Wallace), postmodern Trumpist conservatism or McCarthyite anti-communism." This is a crucial intervention. By treating these movements as mere anomalies rather than organic outgrowths of the broader conservative ecosystem, Will fails to address why his preferred brand of "genteel" conservatism has struggled to hold power. Burgis notes that "fusionism and neoconservatism make only cameo appearances," despite being "crucial ingredients in the stew of American conservatism." This omission is not just a historical gap; it suggests that Will's project is more about wishful thinking than political reality. A counterargument worth considering is that Will is intentionally purging these elements to save the movement, but Burgis effectively counters that you cannot fix a house by pretending the rotting beams never existed.

Will's book would have benefited from spending more time discussing them—and exploring the possibility that the classical liberal project he cherishes might, ironically, have helped empower them.

The Myth of the Flawless Founders

The commentary shifts to a more damning critique of Will's treatment of the American Founders. Will posits that the Founders were the "most admirable men in history," a claim Burgis dismantles by highlighting the stark contradiction of men who declared liberty a natural right while owning human beings. Burgis writes, "He dismisses contemporary historians' focus on their moral failings, rather than acknowledging that this focus gives us a more realistic portrait of the world." This refusal to engage with the full scope of history is presented as a philosophical weakness, not just a moral one. If the foundation of the republic is built on a lie, the structure cannot stand.

Burgis invokes Frederick Douglass to illustrate the hollowness of Will's idealization. He quotes Douglass's 1852 address: "What, to the American slave, is your 4th of July? I answer: a day that reveals to him, more than all other days of the year, the gross injustice and cruelty to which he is the constant victim." The commentary uses this to argue that Will's "sentimentalizes his heroes" approach is a form of intellectual dishonesty. The author suggests that Will's refusal to acknowledge that "thousands of people fought for the principles that the Founders articulated"—and many died doing so, unlike the Founders themselves—devalues the actual history of the struggle for liberty. This framing is powerful because it shifts the focus from the elite architects of the nation to the masses who actually sustained its ideals.

Critics might argue that focusing on the Founders' moral failings distracts from their political genius, but Burgis insists that the hypocrisy is central to understanding the limits of their vision. "For Will to persuade readers otherwise, he would need to marshal hard evidence, rather than simply complaining that focusing on the bigger picture leads us to devalue his heroes," Burgis asserts. This is a sharp rebuke of the hagiographic style that often plagues political biography.

The Logical Trap of Natural Rights

The final major thrust of the commentary targets the philosophical underpinnings of Will's argument: the doctrine of natural rights. Burgis argues that Will's defense of this doctrine is "neither describes Locke's account of human nature in sufficient depth, nor addresses the many criticisms of it that have been raised over the centuries." The author identifies a fundamental logical error in Will's reasoning: the "naturalistic fallacy," where one attempts to derive what ought to be from what is. Burgis writes, "one cannot simply infer the existence of human rights from the facts of human nature—to do so would be to rely on the naturalistic fallacy."

Furthermore, the commentary brings in the critique of Immanuel Kant regarding property rights. Burgis explains that, for Kant, property is not a pre-political natural right but a "project of statist artifice" that requires a community consensus to exist. "What Locke calls a natural right to property, flowing from human nature, is instead very much a project of statist artifice," Burgis notes. This undermines Will's entire argument for a limited government that merely secures pre-existing rights. If rights are created by the state, then the state has a legitimate role in shaping them, which contradicts the conservative desire for a minimal government.

The author also highlights the dangerous implications of Will's acceptance of Locke's premise: "If this premise is true, then unequal outcomes are not unjust... and those who are poor have no one to blame but themselves." This logic leads to a worldview that admires the wealthy and despises the poor, a conclusion that Burgis finds both "ironic" and deeply problematic. The commentary suggests that this philosophical rigidity prevents Will from offering a coherent response to the systemic inequalities that plague modern society.

Housing policy was built on racist foundations, and we never tore them up. We just stopped talking about it.

Bottom Line

Burgis delivers a devastating critique of Will's attempt to redefine conservatism, exposing the intellectual contortions required to ignore the movement's populist and racist history. The strongest part of the argument is the demonstration that Will's "classical liberal" ideal is incompatible with the actual history of the American right, particularly regarding slavery and the role of the state. The biggest vulnerability in Will's position is its reliance on a flawed philosophical foundation that cannot account for the reality of human nature or the social construction of rights. Readers should watch for how this tension between idealized theory and messy reality continues to fracture the conservative coalition as the administration grapples with the limits of limited government.
