Samuel Arbesman delivers a startling diagnosis for the stagnation of scientific innovation: the very people trying to build the future are unconsciously rebuilding the past. By applying the biological concept of canalization to the sociology of research institutions, he argues that a high-dimensional space of possible organizational forms is being funneled into a narrow, predictable set of structures. This is not merely an observation of similarity; it is a critique of how risk aversion, capital constraints, and legal codes actively suppress the "weird" experiments necessary for breakthrough science.

The Biological Mirror

Arbesman, a lapsed biologist turned metascience commentator, begins by borrowing a term from genetics to explain why new research labs look so much like old universities. He writes, "In biology, genetic variation often results in things that look more or less the same... This is known as canalization, the idea that, despite genetic variation, environmental forces, and randomness, lots of genotypes yield the same phenotype." He applies this to the "high-dimensional space of possible institutional forms," noting that while we might expect a proliferation of unique models, we instead see a "canalization of organizational forms."

The author's framing is particularly sharp because it moves beyond blaming individual leaders or bad ideas. Instead, he identifies a systemic pressure where "many of these organizations are funneled by their own staff, not necessarily even consciously, into similar kinds of forms." This is a crucial distinction. It suggests that the problem isn't a lack of creativity, but a structural trap where the incentives for the individuals inside the system force the system itself to regress to the mean. A counterargument worth considering is that this convergence might be a sign of maturity rather than failure; perhaps the "university" model is simply the most efficient way to organize complex knowledge, and the search for alternatives is a distraction. However, Arbesman's evidence suggests that this efficiency comes at the cost of exploring truly novel frontiers.

"The perennial question within such organizations is: if I hew to local aspirations, will that damage my chances of getting a job anywhere else?"

The Shadow of the Future

The most compelling part of Arbesman's analysis is his explanation of why researchers self-censor their institutional ambitions. He points to what metascience commentators Michael Nielsen and Kanjun Qiu call the "shadow of the future." As Arbesman puts it, "Scientists considering working for Jazzy Not-for-Profit (or For-Profit) Startup Institute must ponder: do they really want to give up publication in high impact journals? Or to work on risky projects that may not pan out?"

This creates a paradox where the talent required to build something new is the same talent that is most afraid to leave the safety of the old system. The author notes that because "other potential employers haven't changed their standards," employees are driven to perform "academic-like activities" even in non-academic settings. This dynamic effectively neutralizes the potential for structural innovation. The pressure is so intense that it mirrors the concept of canalization in genetics, where despite the potential for diverse outcomes, the developmental path is constrained to a few robust phenotypes. In the context of research, this means we see a "regression to the institutional mean" rather than the explosion of diversity we need.

The Capital and Legal Constraints

When the article shifts to venture-backed research, Arbesman identifies a second canalizing force: the imperative of the market. He cites Ben Reinhardt, who argues that "there is a tradeoff between an organizational culture that is good at addressing market uncertainty and one that is good at addressing technological uncertainty." This tension often forces a "dramatic gear shift" that kills the research potential before it can mature.

Arbesman illustrates this with the history of Interval Research, founded by Paul Allen in the 1990s. Despite starting with a "Let a thousand flowers bloom" approach, the organization eventually pivoted to commercialization and shuttered by 2000. The author uses this to show that even with deep pockets and mission-aligned founders, the gravitational pull of the startup model is overwhelming. Furthermore, he points out a more subtle constraint: the tax code. He asks, "Is it not odd that our tax codes have reduced the complexity in how researchers think about science?" By forcing a binary choice between non-profit and for-profit, the legal system collapses a vast landscape of possibilities into just two boxes.

Critics might argue that the venture capital model is simply doing its job—allocating capital to scalable returns—and that asking investors to ignore market signals is unrealistic. Yet, Arbesman's point is that this realism is exactly what prevents the emergence of new, hybrid forms of science that don't fit the current mold.

"Trying to bend this kind of structure and make it weird and capacious for research can yield valuable science, but it is unlikely to yield a fundamentally different and novel kind of institutional form."

Breaking the Mold

Despite the heavy weight of these constraints, Arbesman refuses to accept canalization as destiny. He highlights counterexamples like Ink & Switch, which operates on a "Hollywood movie model" where teams assemble for specific projects, and Fast Forward Labs, which was bootstrapped and funded by selling reports rather than seeking venture capital. These examples prove that the "high-dimensional space" is not empty; it is just hard to access.

To break the cycle, the author suggests radical shifts in hiring and legal structures. He proposes that organizations might need to "hire more weirdos or misfits" who are not concerned with the "shadow of the future" of academic tenure. He even speculates on the need for a new legal designation, perhaps an "R corp," to accommodate research that doesn't fit the traditional non-profit or for-profit molds. He also calls for a different kind of philanthropy, urging wealthy donors to "give money to interesting teams and then walk away," minimizing oversight to allow experiments to play out without premature pressure to scale or commercialize.

The National Science Foundation's recent Tech Labs initiative is cited as a promising, albeit early, attempt to fund teams for long stretches outside the traditional academic setting. Arbesman acknowledges that even if an organization succumbs to canalization, it is not a total failure, citing OpenAI's outsized impact despite its evolution into a traditional startup. However, the core challenge remains: if we want to explore the full spectrum of scientific potential, we must actively fight the forces that make everything look the same.

Bottom Line

Arbesman's strongest contribution is reframing institutional stagnation not as a lack of ideas, but as a biological-like constraint driven by human risk aversion and rigid economic incentives. The argument's vulnerability lies in its optimism about the ability of "weirdos" and new legal structures to overcome decades of entrenched academic and market norms. The reader should watch for whether the proposed "R corp" or similar legal innovations gain traction, as this will be the true test of whether science can escape its own developmental canalization.
