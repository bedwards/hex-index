Sam Jennings doesn't just list the hits of the 1990s; he argues that the decade's music was haunted by a collective sense of finality, a "ghostly" awareness that the millennium was ending before it even began. While most retrospectives focus on the explosion of grunge or the rise of hip-hop as cultural dominance, Jennings offers a far more intimate thesis: the decade was defined by a tension between the "interior" world of headphones and drugs, and the "environmental" pressure of mass media, all underpinned by a strange, pre-emptive nostalgia. This is not a history of chart-toppers, but an excavation of the "metaphysics" of sound, asking what it felt like to live in a time that felt like an ending.

The Metaphysics of an Ending

Jennings frames the 1990s not as a time of new beginnings, but as a period where "the central theme... is that it is an ending." He suggests that this awareness of closure created a unique cultural paradox: "Perversely, the sense of an ending is also the only thing capable of sustaining the feeling of infinity." This is a compelling, if melancholic, lens through which to view the era's output. The author argues that the music of the time was already looking backward, even as it was being made. "It's hard to escape the feeling, listening to certain tracks... that the music was already nostalgic for its own time, in a way distinct from any other decade." This observation reframes the decade's "golden" moments not as celebrations of the present, but as elegies for a future that hadn't arrived yet.

The piece posits that this era was the last time subcultures could genuinely influence the mainstream before the internet fragmented everything. Jennings writes, "In the Nineties, the barrier between the two became briefly slimmer, and many artists crossed it very quickly, in ways that have never really been possible since." He identifies this as the "high era of musical taxonomy," a time when physical media and curated lists held real power. Critics might argue that this romanticizes a period of intense commercialization and that the "slim barrier" was often a corporate co-opting of underground trends rather than a genuine cultural merger. However, Jennings' focus on the "last great romance of physical objects" captures a specific texture of the era that digital streaming has irrevocably altered.

The Nineties was the last time cool really mattered in culture—the last time subcultures had functional gatekeepers and whole industries stood ready to raid their closets for the next hip trend.

Interiority and the Sonic Cocoon

Jennings divides the decade's musical output into two competing forces: the "interior," driven by personal listening habits and drug culture, and the "environmental," designed for public spaces like clubs and MTV. He notes that popular music in the Nineties "most often pulls in two directions: interior, reflected by various production trends, the increasing use of headphones, and the return of a popular drug culture... and environmental, perfectly engineered for specific spaces." This duality explains why the decade produced such a wide array of sounds, from the hazy, introspective trip-hop of Sneaker Pimps to the anthemic, stadium-ready rock of the era.

When discussing the genre of trip-hop, Jennings describes it as "junkie, shadowy music, with almost no light in it," yet paradoxically "the most-Nineties genre of all." He connects this sound to a specific mood of the late millennium: "It was music for a sleepwalking world... trying desperately to mute that ever-growing Y2K anxiety." The author suggests that the "leisurely, unhurried present" of this music is what modern listeners, particularly younger generations, actually yearn for when they express nostalgia for the decade. "When Zoomers who never lived through it get all nostalgic for the Nineties, I suspect it's just this sense of infinite, unrushed ease that they long for most of all." This is a sharp insight into the psychology of retro-nostalgia, suggesting that the appeal lies in the perceived slowness of a pre-algorithmic time.

The Anti-Music Video and the End of Glamour

Perhaps the most striking part of Jennings' analysis is his treatment of Cat Power's "Cross Bones Style," which he describes as "as much as anti-music video as a real video, as much non-song as song." He contrasts this stark, repetitive, and hallucinatory work with the glamour of the 1980s, noting that "The fun and the glamour are gone; the humor isn't." Jennings argues that this track represents a shift away from the "sectarian zeal or punk fervor of the Eighties underground" toward something more absorbed in its own "creative impulse." The author sees this as a rejection of the "broader world of pop or fashion," a move toward a music that was "unbothered by its own subterranean milieu."

By focusing on these specific, often overlooked tracks—from Lush's "Sweetness and Light" to The Pharcyde's "Passin' Me By"—Jennings constructs a narrative where the 1990s was a decade of transition, where the boundaries between the underground and the mainstream, the public and the private, were fluid and fragile. He writes, "This is a chase—and it's never easy. But it's always, always fun," capturing the spirit of the decade's musical exploration. The argument holds weight because it moves beyond the usual suspects of Nirvana and TLC to find the decade's soul in the "tiniest, vague filigrees of sound and tradition and technology."

Bottom Line

Jennings' piece succeeds by treating the 1990s not as a historical artifact but as a living, breathing emotional state defined by the anxiety of an ending. The strongest part of the argument is its identification of "interiority" as the decade's defining musical characteristic, a shift that anticipated the solitary listening habits of the digital age. Its biggest vulnerability is a potential over-romanticization of the era's "cool," which may have overlooked the commercial machinery driving those trends. Readers should watch for how this framework of "the end" applies to our current cultural moment, as we navigate our own sense of historical closure.

The central theme of the 1990s is that it is an ending. At the end, all things are tried, all combinations are considered, and all new technology rushes outwards past the ability of anyone to foresee where it might go.
