Justin E. H. Smith does not merely translate the Voynich Manuscript; he resurrects a forgotten civilization trapped beneath a glass dome, turning a centuries-old cryptographic puzzle into a chilling narrative of totalitarian control. While previous scholars have chased linguistic ciphers, Smith argues that the manuscript is actually a first-person confession from a botanist living under the rule of the "Glaziers," a ruling class that has physically and philosophically sealed humanity away from the natural world. This is not a dry academic exercise but a bold reimagining that forces us to ask if the manuscript's "nonsense" is actually a deliberate code for a society that has lost its history.

The Architecture of Ignorance

Smith's central claim is that the manuscript describes a city where the inhabitants have been gaslit into believing their artificial dome is the natural sky. He writes, "The children of the City —can we even call them that? anyhow the Citizens generated after the construction of the dome— do not know that it was constructed at all." This framing is brilliant because it recontextualizes the manuscript's strange botanical illustrations not as magical plants, but as the desperate, suppressed knowledge of a botanist who remembers a world outside the dome. Smith suggests the Glaziers have replaced the ancient cosmological model of Eudoxus with their own manufactured reality, insisting that "there are twenty-seven celestial spheres in all, the closest of them being the one they themselves built."

The author's decision to anchor this fiction in the specific historical debate over the number of celestial spheres adds a layer of intellectual weight that is rare in speculative fiction. By linking the Glaziers' doctrine to the lost work of Eudoxus, Smith implies that the City's rulers are not just tyrants but pedants who have weaponized ancient philosophy to maintain control. As Smith puts it, the Glaziers insist the dome "shares in the same nature as the most distant and empyrean of them all," a lie designed to make escape seem cosmologically impossible.

"You can kill us, which seems to have been one of the strategies Cocalus considered some years back, though in the end he understood he needed us for our expertise in sundry fields, such as mine, in botany."

This quote highlights the pragmatic cruelty of the regime. The ruler, Cocalus, spares the elders not out of mercy, but because they possess the technical knowledge required to maintain the dome. Smith's narrative voice here is weary and cynical, perfectly capturing the tone of a scholar who knows too much to be safe. Critics might argue that this anthropomorphizes the manuscript too heavily, risking the loss of the text's original ambiguity, but the emotional resonance of the narrator's fear is undeniable.

The Geography of Escape

Smith moves beyond the philosophical to the physical, detailing two specific routes out of the City that have been forgotten by the "hatchlings." He writes, "The first is obvious, though the hatchlings would never think to consider it: the dome penetrates only six ells into the ground at its deepest." The second route involves the thermal baths, which the author describes as sites of "rites of generation and hatching" that hide a network of caverns leading to the surface. This section is particularly compelling because it grounds the high-concept sci-fi elements in the gritty reality of medieval geography.

The narrator's reference to the "Antipodes" serves as a crucial historical anchor. Smith notes that "we do not know the shape of the zodiac on the other side of the world (which in the old geography is nothing other than the Antipodes)," a nod to the ancient fear that the southern hemisphere was uninhabitable or that people there would fall off the earth. By invoking this specific historical anxiety, Smith connects the City's isolation to a much broader human history of fearing the unknown. The narrator's memory of his friend Cacus, named after the mythological cow-thief, further deepens the tragedy, as Cocalus used the name to justify the friend's execution: "Cocalus having decided on the name of the mythological cow-thief for my friend, he subsequently decided that this name he himself made up must be reflective of a bad character."

This use of mythology is not just decorative; it reveals the psychological manipulation of the regime. The ruler creates a narrative where names dictate fate, a tactic that feels eerily modern. Smith writes, "I know the way well, and someday I will find the courage to take it," a line that transforms the narrator from a passive observer into an active agent of rebellion. The tension between the narrator's knowledge and his fear is palpable, making the manuscript feel less like a puzzle and more like a countdown.

The Intimacy of Memory

In a surprising pivot, Smith shifts the tone from political thriller to intimate memoir, recalling a visit to the baths at Montecatini with a lost love. He writes, "Let us reminisce instead, for that is far more pleasing than to learn the layout of our City." This section serves as a counterweight to the oppressive atmosphere of the City, grounding the narrator's humanity in a specific, sensory experience. The reference to the "little flies" and the "Tuscan marshes" evokes a real historical setting, likely the early fifteenth century, where malaria was a constant threat.

Smith's description of the narrator singing "like a troubadour in Occitan" adds a layer of cultural depth, suggesting that the narrator's identity is rooted in a pre-dome world of art and poetry. He writes, "I sang to you like a troubadour in Occitan — words of love as if I were joking. But if I joked, it is only because I was a coward, and could only speak truth by pretending I was insincere." This admission of cowardice is a powerful moment of vulnerability that humanizes the narrator, making his eventual decision to escape feel more earned. The juxtaposition of the plague victims drinking "the earth's hot mineral broth" with the narrator's moment of love creates a stark contrast between the desperation of the outside world and the sanctuary of memory.

"I assumed the little flies and the bogs and the night themselves could not fail to see the truth of my Love for you."

This line encapsulates the narrator's belief that nature itself bears witness to human truth, a belief that stands in direct opposition to the Glaziers' artificial reality. Smith's commentary here suggests that the manuscript is not just a record of a dystopia, but a testament to the enduring power of human connection in the face of erasure. The reference to the "boot" shape of Italy, which Smith notes was not a common metaphor until the sixteenth century, adds a layer of anachronistic tension that hints at the manuscript's complex origins.

Bottom Line

Justin E. H. Smith's translation is a masterclass in blending historical scholarship with speculative fiction, turning the Voynich Manuscript into a narrative about the fragility of truth and the resilience of memory. The strongest part of his argument is the way he uses specific historical references, from Eudoxus to the baths of Montecatini, to ground the fantastical elements in a tangible reality. However, the biggest vulnerability is the risk that readers may dismiss the piece as pure fiction, missing the deeper commentary on how language and history can be weaponized to control populations. As Smith continues to peel back the layers of the manuscript, the question remains: is he decoding a lost language, or writing a new one?
