Mary Cadwalladr delivers a scathing, musicological manifesto that rejects the frantic churn of 2025's cultural output in favor of a deep, almost obsessive excavation of the 1960s. While most critics scramble to validate the present, Cadwalladr argues that true artistic genius is often invisible until decades have passed, using the raw energy of Texas blues and the orchestral soul of the late 60s to expose the mediocrity of the current era.

The Curatorial Lens on the Past

Cadwalladr anchors her argument in a specific, overlooked artifact: a 1966 episode of the Dallas TV show The !!! Beat. She uses this historical window not just for nostalgia, but to illustrate a complex cultural moment where the psychedelic explosion met the desegregation-era South. She writes, "Hoss himself comes across as a real institution, a symbol of the complexity of cultural identity in the desegregation-era South." By focusing on the dynamic between white host Bill "Hoss" Allen and Black musician Clarence "Gatemouth" Brown, Cadwalladr reframes the era not as a simple narrative of conflict, but as a "loving and curatorial approach" to shared musical roots.

The author's choice to highlight Louis Jordan, a veteran performer in 1966, serves to dismantle the myth that innovation belongs solely to the youth. Jordan, she notes, was "ready to cede his place to the youth, but as long as his humble services are still in demand he will be happy to sing 'Caldonia' for you in 1966 with just as much good humor as he had done in 1945." This observation challenges the modern obsession with novelty, suggesting that mastery is a form of continuity rather than disruption. Cadwalladr argues that Jordan's "novelty" music, often dismissed as comic, actually contained "encrypted potentials for violence and its subdued explosiveness." This is a bold claim: that humor was a mechanism to "launder the dangerous imagination," allowing subversive ideas to slip past the censors of the time.

Critics might argue that finding deep political subtext in "novelty" songs risks over-intellectualizing entertainment that was meant to be light. However, Cadwalladr's evidence of the era's tension—the backdrop of Civil Rights and the "psychedelic explosion"—supports her reading that even the "blandest humor" carried weight.

"Some people are funny mostly because humor is an effective way to launder the dangerous imagination, with the result, I suspect, that even the blandest humor, even the corniest novelty song, must be attended with an ear to its encrypted potentials for violence."

The Architecture of Soul and the Critique of Modernity

Moving from blues to soul, Cadwalladr identifies The Dells' 1968 rendition of "Stay in My Corner" as a forgotten masterpiece that rivals the psychedelic rock of the same period. She describes the track as "plainly post-Sgt. Pepper's, yet its dilations and its crests also seem to have been learned in church rather than by any drug-induced shortcut." This distinction is crucial to her argument: she posits that the expansion of musical form in the 60s was driven by spiritual and communal traditions just as much as by the counterculture's drug use.

She coins the term "hot-oil" soul to describe this specific, velvety quality that peaked with Isaac Hayes before devolving into what she calls "meandering" and "invertebrate" later generations. The critique is sharp and unapologetic, targeting figures like R. Kelly as a symptom of a decline in artistic discipline. Cadwalladr writes, "When I hear the note that the group's lead baritone Marvin Junior holds for around 17 seconds on the first syllable of the word 'baby'... this sounds to me something like an echo of the large-scale orchestral texture that the Beatles hoped to adapt from Luciano Berio." By linking soul directly to avant-garde classical composition, she elevates the genre's technical sophistication.

The author then pivots to a broader critique of music criticism itself, accusing peers of prioritizing lyrical content over musical composition. "My fellow critics have often made the mistake of looking for artistic inspiration primarily in lyrical content," she asserts, noting that this bias leads them to overrate lyricists like Bob Dylan while missing the "deep—if, for some, undignified—truth" that musical turns are often delivered through simple words like "baby." This is a provocative stance that demands the reader listen differently, to the sound rather than the story.

The Case for Joanna Newsom and the Failure of the Present

The essay culminates in a defense of Joanna Newsom, whom Cadwalladr elevates to the status of Whitman or Gershwin. She argues that Newsom is "grossly underappreciated by the critics," who focus on her voice or themes of womanhood rather than her status as a "universal poet." Cadwalladr insists that Newsom's lyrics, particularly on the 2006 album Ys, "can be meaningfully read as text-only literary creations." This is a rare claim in an era where lyrics are rarely treated as standalone poetry.

She contextualizes Newsom not as a hipster folk revivalist, but as an artist rooted in the avant-garde, noting her time at Mills College and the influence of Pauline Oliveros. "In the modal drift and the cosmic textures of her harpistry one hears more Alice Coltrane than, say, Jean Ritchie," she writes. This reframing strips away the "hipster" label and places Newsom in a lineage of serious American experimentation.

The piece then turns its full force against the present day. Cadwalladr is brutally honest about the lack of quality in 2025's music, stating, "Did any good music come out in 2025? I don't know, maybe. Who cares." She argues that the pressure to cover current events is a symptom of economic desperation in the media, not a reflection of cultural value. "There are only two reasons people preoccupy themselves with the present as if it mattered more than the past," she writes, "because they are vapid and don't know any better, or because their vapid and ignorant readership expects it of them and their prosperity therefore depends on their willingness to do so."

This is the most contentious part of the argument. While it resonates with the fatigue many feel regarding the endless churn of new content, it risks dismissing the genuine cultural shifts and innovations that do occur in real-time. A counterargument worth considering is that ignoring the present entirely prevents critics from understanding how current struggles shape future art. Yet, Cadwalladr's conclusion is a defiant rejection of the status quo, ending with a line from Newsom that perfectly encapsulates her sentiment: "Cold, cold cupboard, Lord / Nothing to chew on!"

"Here at The Hinternet, unlike, say, the New Yorker or the New York Times, there simply is no economic imperative to pretend that we are not living in an era of decline and mediocrity, or to make as if some recent culture-industrial production is worthy of our current attention, when in fact it is not."

Bottom Line

Cadwalladr's piece is a masterclass in using historical depth to critique contemporary superficiality, offering a refreshing antidote to the relentless demand for newness. Its greatest strength is the rigorous musical analysis that elevates soul and folk to the level of high art, but its vulnerability lies in its absolute dismissal of the present, which may alienate readers looking for a bridge between the past and the future. The reader should watch for how this "retro-optimism" influences the next generation of critics who may finally stop chasing the news cycle to find the actual music.
