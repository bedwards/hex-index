Mick Ryan delivers a sobering reality check for 2026, arguing that while the world's eyes are fixed on the potential for American ground troops in Iran, the actual war that matters most is unfolding in the muddy fields of Ukraine. He posits that the most significant military development of the week wasn't a new Russian offensive, but rather a Ukrainian disruption so effective it forced Moscow to burn through its own spring reserves. This is not just a battle report; it is a strategic warning that the West is dangerously distracted while a critical front line holds the line against a collapsing Russian will.

The Southern Disruption

Ryan cuts through the noise of daily casualty figures to identify a profound operational shift: Ukraine is no longer just reacting; it is dictating the tempo. He writes, "The most consequential military development of the past few weeks is not what Russia has done. It is what Ukraine has prevented Russia from doing." This framing is vital because it reframes the war from a story of Russian aggression to one of Ukrainian agency. By launching counterattacks in the south, Kyiv has liberated over 400 square kilometers and, more importantly, shattered Moscow's timeline for a decisive spring-summer campaign.

The author details how Ukrainian forces have created a "cascading effect" across the frontline, threatening the rear of Russian armies and forcing them to shift from offense to defense. Ryan notes that "Ukrainian advances in the Oleksandrivka direction threaten the rear of Russian 5th Combined Arms Army elements... potentially compelling them to shift from offensive to defensive posture." This is a masterclass in operational art, yet it comes with a brutal cost. The author acknowledges the inherent tension: "Ukraine's ground force resources are not infinite." By strengthening the south, Kyiv has inadvertently weakened the northern outskirts of Pokrovsk, allowing Russia to launch new pushes there.

Critics might argue that Ryan underestimates the long-term sustainability of this "resource-constrained" strategy, but his data suggests the trade-off is currently paying dividends. The Russian military is burning through reserves earmarked for their main effort, a sign that their operational depth is thinner than advertised. As Ryan puts it, "This is a good indicator that Russian forces are burning through reserves earmarked for spring offensive operations."

The price of this Ukrainian operational success, however, is being paid elsewhere. You cannot be strong everywhere.

The Drone Revolution and the Fog of War

Perhaps the most striking evidence Ryan presents is the failure of Russia's "fog of war" tactic. Expecting weather to ground Ukrainian drones, Russian assault groups launched into the mist on March 17, only to be decimated by ground-based drone operators who proved capable even in low-visibility conditions. Ryan highlights that "Ukrainian Unmanned Systems Forces drone units had reportedly killed 292 Russian soldiers and wounded 221 others" in a single night, with 900 casualties over 36 hours without a single Russian breakthrough.

This success is not accidental; it is the result of a systematic campaign to hunt Russian air defenses. Ryan explains that Ukrainian units have been "systematically hunting Russian air defence systems, particularly Pantsir, at a pace that Russia's military-industrial complex cannot match." The strategic consequence is a shift in the cost asymmetry of the war. Ukraine is now intercepting expensive Russian missiles with cheap drones, a dynamic Ryan describes as "a developing strategic advantage for Ukraine, and an important component of any cost imposition strategy against Russia."

However, the author is careful not to paint a picture of total dominance. Russian pressure remains relentless, with nightly strikes involving hundreds of drones and missiles. Ryan notes that "Each night in the past week the Russians have fired between 100 and 200 drones and missiles at Ukraine," causing significant civilian casualties. The war of attrition is far from over, and the margin for error for Ukraine is narrowing.

The Diplomatic Dead End

While the battlefield sees fluidity, the diplomatic front remains a stone wall. Ryan captures the essence of Moscow's intransigence with brutal clarity: "Moscow continues to condition any ceasefire on concessions that amount to Ukrainian capitulation." He points to Russian Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov's dismissal of European peace efforts and the refusal to engage in any settlement that doesn't concede all Russian territorial demands.

The author suggests that Russia's strategy is to isolate Ukraine from its European allies and deal directly with a U.S. administration that may prioritize "resolution over quality." Ryan writes, "The exclusion of Europe from the trilateral US-Ukraine-Russia framework remains a core Russian negotiating objective." This is a dangerous game, as it attempts to fracture the Western alliance. Yet, as long as Putin believes his forces can still achieve his objectives on the ground, there is no incentive to negotiate.

Putin will only change his stubborn negotiating position when he accepts that his forces are stuck.

The Iran Distraction

The piece pivots sharply to the Middle East, where the question of "boots on the ground" in Iran looms large. Ryan warns that the U.S. and Israel have achieved tactical success in the air and at sea, but "military success in the air and at sea does not automatically translate into political outcomes on the ground." He cites retired Lieutenant General James Dubik, who asks the uncomfortable question: "Our military won every tactical engagement in Vietnam, Afghanistan, and Iraq. American citizens have every reason to ask, therefore, if we fight so successfully, why did we lose?"

Ryan's commentary here is a necessary brake on escalation. He argues that sending ground troops into Iran would likely lead to "a larger and more difficult American military commitment to the Middle East with uncertain results." The author suggests that the U.S. is currently "stuck in another Middle East war and absorbed in a campaign of abuse against allies," which leaves the Pacific security guarantor vulnerable to China's coercion.

Critics might argue that without ground pressure, Iran's nuclear program and regional proxies will eventually overwhelm U.S. air power. Ryan acknowledges this risk but emphasizes the historical lesson that tactical victories do not equal strategic wins. The core of his argument is that the U.S. must not repeat the mistakes of the past by confusing destruction with victory.

Bottom Line

Ryan's strongest move is reframing the Ukraine war not as a stalemate, but as a dynamic contest where Ukrainian agency is actively degrading Russian operational capacity. His biggest vulnerability is the uncertainty of Western political will; he admits that the fundamental question for 2026 is whether partners will sustain the material and political support needed to hold the line. The reader must watch closely to see if the disruption in the south can be sustained without collapsing the defense in the east.
