Mick Ryan delivers a jarring diagnosis of the current Middle East conflict: what looks like a single war is actually two entirely different games being played on the same board. While Washington fights a conventional military campaign to destroy infrastructure, Tehran is waging a global economic war designed to break the American political will. This distinction is not just semantic; it explains why the U.S. can dominate the skies while losing the strategic initiative, a reality that has immediate, terrifying implications for the Pacific theater.

The Two-War Paradox

Ryan cuts through the noise of daily headlines to argue that "there are two wars occurring concurrently." He posits that the United States and Iran have entered a conflict defined by fundamentally different theories of what victory means, different methods for pursuing it, and different audiences they are performing for. This framing is essential because it explains the apparent disconnect between American military success and strategic stagnation. The U.S. is winning the battles but may be losing the war because the two sides are not playing by the same rulebook.

The American approach, Ryan notes, relies on "the precise application of intelligence and western weaponry" to decapitate leadership and destroy nuclear capabilities. It is a narrow, high-tech campaign executed with "remarkable precision," with over 7,000 targets struck in the first three weeks alone. Yet, Ryan warns that "the ends remain opaque." The administration has oscillated between preventing nuclear weapons, demanding unconditional surrender, and hoping for a popular uprising.

"A theory of victory must embrace objectives beyond military activity... notions of victory must include economic, diplomatic, and societal long-term needs as well as short- and medium-term military outcomes."

This observation lands hard because it exposes the "foggy ends" of the U.S. strategy. While the military means are clear, the political destination is not. Ryan points out that "winning the war" is one thing, but "winning the peace" requires a plan for governance and reconstruction that simply does not exist. Critics might argue that regime change is too ambitious a goal for a limited strike campaign, but Ryan's point is that without a clear end-state, even a tactical victory becomes a strategic trap.

The Economic Front

While the U.S. focuses on air superiority, Iran has pivoted to a strategy of endurance and economic strangulation. Ryan describes Tehran's war as "a regional and global economic war with an internal societal dimension." Iran's core instruments are not its depleted air force, but the Strait of Hormuz, drone swarms, and the price of oil. The goal is not to defeat the U.S. military in a traditional sense, but to answer a single question: "who can take the pain the longest?"

The economic leverage is real and dangerous. With oil prices spiking and food supplies threatened, Iran is betting that the cost of the war will fracture the American political coalition. Ryan highlights a particularly chilling development: "The condition, if formalised, would represent the most significant challenge to the petrodollar system in its fifty-two-year history." By demanding oil payments in Chinese yuan, Iran is attacking the financial architecture of American power rather than its military assets.

"Tehran does not need to win – at least not in a way that western military organisations define 'victory'. It just needs to cause sufficient economic disruption to force a U.S. backdown so the regime can present it domestically as a victory."

This is the crux of Iran's theory of victory: survival and the appearance of strength. The war serves as a consolidating event for the regime, turning external aggression into internal legitimacy. Ryan notes that "external aggression by the Great Satan is always good for clerical legitimacy in Iran." This dynamic is reinforced by the regime's ability to use cheap drones to saturate defenses, creating an unsustainable cost exchange ratio for the U.S.

"Cheap Shahed drones – costing a few thousand dollars each – are being countered by interceptors costing millions. Secretary of State Rubio acknowledged publicly that this arithmetic is unsustainable for the United States."

However, this strategy is not without its own fatal flaws. Ryan draws on analysis from RAND's Raphael Cohen to argue that Iran is making a "fundamental strategic miscalculation" by alienating its neighbors. By striking Arab states, Tehran has damaged the regional relationships that formed 60 percent of its trade base.

"Even a surviving Iran that declares victory will have triggered its own long-term strategic decline; their strategic gambling over the past few decades has finally cost more than the winnings."

This is a sobering counterpoint to the idea of Iranian resilience. While the regime may survive the immediate bombardment, it risks emerging poorer, weaker, and more isolated than at any point since 1979. The proxy network that once served as a deterrent shield has been dismantled, and the nuclear program has been struck twice. The short-term victory of regime survival may come at the cost of long-term state viability.

The Pacific Lesson

The most critical insight in Ryan's piece is what this conflict teaches America's allies in the Pacific. China and Russia are watching "from a careful distance," learning from both the American military campaign and the Iranian economic strategy. They are observing how a superpower can be bogged down by a smaller, asymmetric adversary.

"So are America's Pacific allies, though what they are learning may not be particularly reassuring for them."

The resource strain is already visible. Ryan points out that "about 40 percent of U.S. Navy ships ready for operations are in the Middle East," forcing the removal of THAAD components from the Korean Peninsula and delaying missile deliveries to Japan. This "Mowing the Grass" strategy demands resources that are already stretched thin across Ukraine and the Pacific.

"Winning in Iran while maintaining deterrence in Europe and Asia requires a level of resource coverage and concurrent activity that the United States does not currently possess."

This connects directly to the broader strategic context. Just as the U.S. struggled to balance its commitments in the Middle East, the lessons from Operation Praying Mantis show how quickly naval dominance can be challenged by asymmetric tactics. The Iranian use of drone swarms and the threat to the Strait of Hormuz mirrors the potential challenges in the South China Sea, where China could similarly exploit economic chokepoints and cost-imposition strategies.

"A narrow theory of victory – degrade and deter, establish a new baseline, then enforce it – is viable. Regime change as a theory of victory requires a plan for the day after."

The danger for the Pacific is that the U.S. may find itself unable to sustain the pressure required to deter China while simultaneously managing the fallout in the Middle East. The "foggy ends" of the Iran campaign could lead to a premature withdrawal or a failure to enforce a new baseline, leaving a power vacuum that adversaries are eager to fill.

Bottom Line

Ryan's strongest argument is the stark contrast between the U.S.'s clear military means and its confused political ends, a disconnect that allows Iran to win by simply outlasting American patience. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its assumption that the U.S. political system will inevitably fracture under economic pressure, a variable that has proven unpredictable in the past. Readers should watch closely to see if the U.S. can pivot from a narrow military campaign to a sustainable long-term strategy before the resource drain in the Middle East compromises deterrence in the Pacific.
