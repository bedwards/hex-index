Mick Ryan delivers a sobering assessment of a war that has fundamentally rewritten the rules of modern conflict: the era of the "safe rear" is over. While much of the global conversation fixates on diplomatic posturing or the shifting attention of the American executive branch, Ryan zeroes in on a stark battlefield reality where 1,700-kilometer drone strikes are now dismantling the very aircraft Russia once considered invulnerable. This is not just a tactical update; it is a strategic shockwave that suggests the war's momentum is shifting not through grand offensives, but through the relentless erosion of an enemy's ability to protect its own assets.

The End of Strategic Depth

Ryan anchors his argument in a specific, devastating event: the strike on the Shagol airfield in the Chelyabinsk region. He notes that this operation, carried out by Ukraine's Unmanned Systems Forces, hit four combat aircraft, including two fifth-generation Su-57 fighters, at a distance that defies traditional logistics. "The most significant deep strike of the past week was Ukraine's long-range drone strike on the Shagol airfield... at a distance of approximately 1,700 kilometres from the Ukrainian border," Ryan writes. The significance here is not merely the destruction of expensive hardware—each Su-57 carries a price tag between $100 million and $120 million—but the psychological and operational collapse of Russian defense planning.

Ryan argues that Moscow's strategy relied on the assumption that moving assets deep into the Urals would shield them. That assumption has been shattered. "Russia has redeployed much of its aviation fleet to bases deep inside Russian territory on the assumption that these assets were beyond Ukrainian reach. That assumption no longer holds," he observes. This is a critical pivot point. By forcing Russia to scatter its air defense resources to protect cities like Chelyabinsk, Ukraine is creating vulnerabilities closer to the front lines. As Ryan points out, this dynamic mirrors the strategic challenges seen in the Pacific, where the "First Island Chain" is no longer a secure buffer but a contested zone of deep engagement.

"Ukrainian drones are now capable of strikes exceeding 2,000 kilometres, meaning there is no safe haven for Russian aircraft within the country's European landmass."

Critics might argue that destroying a few aircraft does not equate to winning a war of attrition, especially when Russia still possesses a massive industrial base. However, Ryan's framing suggests that the rate of loss for high-value, hard-to-replace assets like the Su-57 is the true metric of failure. The production capacity is already strained, as evidenced by a recent fire at the Komsomolsk-on-Amur Aircraft Plant, raising questions about whether Russia can even replace what it loses.

The Diplomatic Theater and the Human Cost

The commentary shifts to the diplomatic arena, where Ryan exposes the hollowness of recent overtures from the Kremlin. As the executive branch in Washington grapples with conflicts in the Middle East, diverting attention from Eastern Europe, Moscow has attempted to leverage this distraction. Ryan details a proposal from President Putin for a temporary truce during the upcoming Victory Day parade on May 9. "Putin floated the idea of a temporary truce for the Victory Day celebrations... Trump expressed support, describing it as a small but welcome step," Ryan notes, highlighting the disconnect between the Russian offer and the reality on the ground.

The offer was not a gesture of peace but a tactical maneuver to protect a political spectacle. Ryan writes, "The Kremlin cited operational security concerns... analysts believe the possibility of Ukrainian drone strikes on Moscow's staging areas made a full display of armour and missiles an unacceptable risk." This admission is damning. It reveals a regime so fearful of its own vulnerability that it must scale back its most important national holiday, canceling the display of military vehicles for the first time in decades.

Yet, amidst these high-level maneuvers, the human cost remains the central, unglamorous truth of the conflict. Ryan does not shy away from the brutality of the ongoing drone campaigns. "A Russian drone struck a civilian bus in Kherson on the night of 2 May, killing at least two people and injuring seven," he reports. This is not a footnote; it is the daily reality that persists regardless of diplomatic truces or parade cancellations. While the administration in Washington debates troop drawdowns and the Kremlin plans a scaled-back parade, civilians continue to bear the brunt of a war that shows no signs of abating.

Reforming the Army for a Long War

Perhaps the most pragmatic section of Ryan's analysis addresses Ukraine's internal struggle: manpower. With the war dragging into 2026, the exhaustion of the Ukrainian population is palpable. Ryan highlights President Zelensky's announcement of a major military reform designed to address "manpower shortages, inadequate soldier compensation, and the absence of any clear rotation or discharge mechanism." The financial incentives are substantial, with frontline infantry contracts offering up to $9,000 per month, but the structural changes are even more critical.

Ryan emphasizes the psychological toll of indefinite service. "With the systemic lack of soldiers come greater tensions... that could define the war of 2026 for Ukraine," he writes, quoting a previous observation on the strain between exhausted soldiers and their commanders. The new reform attempts to break this cycle by introducing fixed terms of service and clear pathways for demobilization. "The most politically sensitive element is the planned phased demobilisation of long-serving troops," Ryan explains, noting that this is essential for maintaining public confidence and military morale.

However, a counterargument worth considering is the sustainability of these financial commitments. Ukraine remains heavily dependent on foreign budget support, and as Ryan acknowledges, "Whether the financial commitments can be sustained... remains an open question." If the economic foundation crumbles, the structural reforms may falter, leaving the army once again stretched to its breaking point.

Bottom Line

Mick Ryan's analysis is a masterclass in connecting tactical innovations to strategic shifts, stripping away the noise of diplomatic theater to reveal the hard mechanics of a war that has entered a new, more dangerous phase. The strongest part of his argument is the demonstration that Russia's "strategic depth" has evaporated, forcing a fundamental rethinking of how the war is fought and defended. The biggest vulnerability in the current trajectory, however, lies in the fragility of Ukraine's own human and economic resources; without sustained Western support and successful internal reform, the very momentum Ryan identifies could stall. The reader must watch not just for the next deep strike, but for the stability of the Ukrainian state itself as it attempts to fight a long war with a mobilized, yet exhausted, population.
