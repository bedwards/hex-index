Mick Ryan cuts through the noise of global geopolitical theater to deliver a chilling diagnosis: Russia's latest ballistic missile strike on Lviv is not a sign of strength, but a desperate cry from a leader who knows the war is slipping away. In a landscape often dominated by speculative headlines about regime change or sudden peace deals, Ryan offers a grounded, evidence-based assessment of the battlefield reality and the psychological state of the Kremlin. This is essential listening for anyone trying to distinguish between the performative aggression of a declining power and the actual trajectory of the conflict in 2026.

The Psychology of the Strike

Ryan immediately reframes the narrative surrounding the Oreshnik intermediate-range ballistic missile (IRBM) strike on Lviv. While the weapon's technical capabilities are terrifying—traveling at speeds over 12,000 kilometers per hour with a range of 5,500 kilometers—Ryan argues that its deployment is primarily a psychological tool rather than a strategic military necessity. He writes, "The employment of an Oreshnik missile overnight against a target near Lviv makes enormous strategic sense to Putin. Their use is the sign of a fearful, worried leader and not one that is confident and anticipating victory." This is a crucial distinction. It suggests that the escalation is a reaction to perceived weakness, driven by recent setbacks including the failure to support allies in Venezuela and the momentum behind a peace deal unfavorable to Moscow.

The author connects this military action to a broader "cognitive war" aimed at intimidating the West. Ryan notes that the strike was timed to coincide with a surge in global instability, including civil strife in Iran and diplomatic tensions involving Greenland and Mexico. He argues that the missile is an instrument of fear, designed to project power where Russia's conventional forces are faltering. "It is not the first time he has done so as part of Russia's ongoing cognitive war against democracies. It is unlikely to be the last," Ryan asserts. This framing effectively strips the attack of any aura of invincibility, revealing it instead as a symptom of a leader cornered by his own failures.

However, one must consider the human cost that often gets lost in this geopolitical analysis. While Ryan correctly identifies the strategic desperation behind the strike, the reality on the ground in Lviv involves the destruction of civilian infrastructure and the loss of life. The psychological impact on the population is a weapon in itself, and the analysis of the missile's range and speed should not overshadow the immediate suffering of those targeted. Critics might argue that focusing too heavily on the leader's psychology risks minimizing the tangible horror of the attack on a city.

The Diplomatic Landscape and the Paris Declaration

Shifting from the battlefield to the negotiating table, Ryan details the intense diplomatic activity in Paris, where a "Coalition of the Willing" gathered to discuss post-ceasefire security guarantees. The outcome was the Paris Declaration, a document outlining a framework for long-term support. Ryan highlights the commitment of European leaders to establish a "multinational force for Ukraine" to assist in rebuilding military capacity and providing deterrence after a ceasefire. He quotes the British Prime Minister, who stated, "Alongside President Zelenskyy and President Macron, we went even further today. We signed a Declaration of Intent on the deployment of forces to Ukraine in the event of a peace deal."

This development marks a significant shift from ad-hoc aid to a structured, legally binding security architecture. Ryan emphasizes that the coalition agreed to a monitoring and verification framework, as well as a body to review ceasefire violations. The inclusion of the United States in this coordination cell, despite the administration's often erratic messaging, suggests a pragmatic alignment of interests. Ryan writes, "The Coalition of the Willing members expressed their readiness to enact a range political and legal commitments once a ceasefire is achieved." This is a robust attempt to create a safety net that prevents Russia from simply restarting the war after a pause.

Yet, the effectiveness of these guarantees hinges on the political will to enforce them. The article notes that the administration's rhetoric often contradicts these diplomatic efforts. Ryan points out that while the administration's National Security Strategy aligns with European defense, the public messaging has been inconsistent. "This follows the clear messaging of the recent U.S. National Security Strategy on European defence and security and aligns with the Trump administrations' growing library of anti-European narratives," he observes. This tension between diplomatic commitment and political posturing creates a fragile environment for peace.

The Ground War and Russian Desperation

Amidst the diplomatic maneuvering, Ryan brings the reader back to the grim reality of the trenches. He describes the Russian offensive operations as a desperate attempt to seize as much ground as possible before a potential peace deal is forced upon them. "Clearly the weather has an impact, but with Russia now seeking to advance concurrently in the south, east and north, might their aim to gain as much ground as possible before Putin is forced into a peace deal be bigger than their resources allow," he asks. The data supports this: Russian territorial gains have been at their lowest in a month, and the army is resorting to unsustainable tactics, including the use of horse-mounted infantry in the 21st century.

Ryan's assessment of the Russian military's effectiveness is stark. He argues that the inability to sustain high casualty rates while fighting on multiple fronts indicates a system nearing its breaking point. "An army forced to employ horse-mounted infantry in the 21st century is not one that is demonstrating high levels of military effectiveness or innovation. They are showing a high level of desperation though," he writes. This observation serves as a powerful counter-narrative to the Kremlin's propaganda of inevitable victory. It suggests that the war is not a stalemate of equals, but a grinding attrition that Russia is ill-equipped to win in the long term.

The Pacific Theater and Hypocrisy

Finally, Ryan turns his attention to the Pacific, where China is attempting to position itself as a stabilizing force following the U.S. raid on Venezuela. The Chinese Foreign Ministry issued statements condemning the use of military force, with a spokesperson noting, "Military means are not the solution to problems, and the indiscriminate use of force will only lead to greater crises." Ryan, however, exposes the hollowness of this rhetoric by pointing to China's own military expansion and aggressive posturing around Taiwan. He writes, "We would all love to think that China will practice what it preaches with regards to its statement that 'military means are not the solution to problems'. But the massive growth in its military... demonstrate just how hollow and hypocritical the Xi regime is."

The analysis of the PLA's "Justice Mission" exercises further underscores this point. Ryan cites K. Tristian Tang's report, which details how the exercises advanced invasion-related rehearsals, including scenarios for seizing the Penghu Islands. This suggests that China is not merely posturing but actively preparing for conflict. The juxtaposition of China's diplomatic statements with its military actions reveals a strategic duality that threatens regional stability. Ryan's commentary here is vital for understanding the global implications of the conflict in Ukraine, as it highlights how other powers are watching and preparing for their own contingencies.

Bottom Line

Mick Ryan's analysis is a masterclass in separating signal from noise, offering a clear-eyed view of a leader driven by fear rather than confidence. The strongest part of his argument is the psychological reframing of the Oreshnik strike as a symptom of Russian weakness, a perspective that cuts through the usual bluster of nuclear threats. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability lies in the uncertainty of the diplomatic front; the Paris Declaration is promising, but its success depends on a political will that remains volatile. Readers should watch closely to see if the coalition's commitments hold when the pressure mounts, and whether the administration's rhetoric aligns with its actions in the coming weeks.
