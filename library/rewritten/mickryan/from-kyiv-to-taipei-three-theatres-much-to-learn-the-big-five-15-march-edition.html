Mick Ryan delivers a stark correction to the AI-generated narratives of certainty, arguing that the war in Ukraine has just pivoted on its axis while a new, chaotic conflict in the Middle East threatens to derail global diplomacy. He presents a rare convergence of tactical brilliance on the Ukrainian front and strategic blundering in Washington, suggesting that Ukraine may have seized the initiative just as the U.S. administration loses its grip on the peace process. For the busy strategist, this is not just a weekly update; it is a warning that the battlefield is dictating terms that the diplomats are no longer equipped to manage.

The Ground War Shifts

Ryan's most striking claim is that Ukraine has moved from a reactive defense to a deliberate, pre-emptive offense that has successfully disrupted Russia's entire spring offensive calendar. He writes, "Ukraine's near-complete liberation of occupied territory in Dnipropetrovsk Oblast... was a deliberate, planned series of attacks." This is not the chaotic scrambling of a desperate army, but the calculated maneuvering of a force that understands operational art. The author details how Ukrainian forces advanced 10 to 12 kilometers on multiple axes, exploiting winter weather that suppressed Russian drone operations and communications. This evidence holds up because it is grounded in specific geography and unit movements rather than vague assertions of "progress."

The strategic impact of this maneuver is profound. Ryan notes that the operation was "designed to pre-empt Russia's anticipated spring offensive," forcing Moscow to redeploy troops from other sectors and effectively canceling their planned attacks before they began. This is a critical distinction: Ukraine is not just holding ground; they are breaking the enemy's timeline. As Ryan puts it, "If Russia's spring-summer offensive planning has indeed been disrupted before it commenced is a significant achievement." This framing is effective because it shifts the metric of success from territory gained to enemy plans thwarted, a subtle but vital shift in how we evaluate modern warfare.

Critics might argue that focusing on a secondary axis in the south allows Russia to concentrate its main effort on the fortress cities in the east, potentially creating a dangerous imbalance. However, Ryan counters this by highlighting the cumulative effect of these strikes on Russian logistics and morale, suggesting that the disruption of the enemy's central plan is worth the risk.

The decision to conduct a pre-emptive offensive to disrupt Russia's spring plans... reflects a military organization that is growing in its tactical, operational and strategic effectiveness.

Striking the Brain of the War Machine

Beyond the trenches, Ryan identifies a qualitative leap in Ukraine's deep strike capabilities, specifically targeting the microelectronics sector that serves as the "brains and nervous system" of Russian weaponry. He writes, "Seven Storm Shadow cruise missiles struck the Kremniy El microelectronics plant in Bryansk, Russia... The extent of the damage indicates production at the plant has been completely halted." This is not a nuisance raid; it is a surgical strike on the industrial backbone of the Russian war effort. The author's analysis of the Kremniy El facility's output—producing components for everything from Iskander missiles to S-400 air defense systems—provides the necessary context to understand why this single strike matters more than a dozen drone attacks on oil refineries.

Ryan argues that this campaign has three mutually supporting aims: reducing pressure on the front, boosting Ukrainian morale, and signaling to the U.S. administration that a Russian victory is impossible. He writes, "Demonstrate to the Trump administration that Russian victory now is impossible and hope that Trump will begin to put more pressure on Putin, and less on Ukraine, in peace talks." This is a bold assertion of how military success can be leveraged for diplomatic gain. The evidence of the strike's precision and the destruction of the central building suggests Ukraine is finally capable of degrading Russia's high-tech capabilities, not just its mass-produced hardware.

A counterargument worth considering is that Russia has long relied on smuggling and third-party intermediaries for microchips, so a single factory strike may not be as crippling as claimed. Yet, Ryan's point stands that Kremniy El was the "backstop of that domestic capability," and its degradation forces Russia to rely even more heavily on fragile, illicit supply chains that are vulnerable to further interdiction.

The Diplomatic Maze and the Iran Distraction

The piece takes a darker turn as Ryan examines the diplomatic landscape, where the chaos of a new conflict in Iran threatens to overshadow the war in Ukraine. He describes the diplomatic track as "more an achievement in choreography than a genuine step forward towards peace." Ryan highlights the absurdity of the current situation, where President Trump pleads for international assistance in the Middle East while simultaneously telling President Zelenskyy to "make a deal" with Putin. He writes, "The Iran War appears to have given Putin a temporary reprieve from American diplomatic pressure. It is using that reprieve... to re-posture itself in negotiations."

This is the piece's most uncomfortable truth: the U.S. administration's distraction with Iran is actively aiding Russia's strategy. Ryan notes that Kremlin officials are using U.S. military operations against Iran to "discredit the legitimacy of U.S.-led mediation in the Ukraine peace talks." The author's analysis of the Trump-Putin phone call and the subsequent meetings in Florida reveals a diplomatic process that is not moving toward peace, but rather toward a settlement that favors Russian maximalist goals. The rescheduling of trilateral talks and the vague readouts from Washington suggest a lack of coherent strategy.

Critics might suggest that the U.S. is simply managing two crises simultaneously and that the distraction is a necessary evil to prevent a wider regional war. However, Ryan's argument that this distraction is being weaponized by Putin to weaken Western support for Ukraine is a compelling and dangerous dynamic that cannot be ignored.

Bottom Line

Ryan's strongest argument is that Ukraine has successfully seized the strategic initiative on the battlefield, forcing Russia onto the defensive just as the U.S. diplomatic effort begins to fracture under the weight of a new Middle Eastern conflict. The piece's greatest vulnerability lies in its reliance on the hope that military success will eventually force a change in the Trump administration's stance, a gamble that ignores the deep ideological currents driving U.S. foreign policy. Readers must watch whether Ukraine can consolidate its gains in Dnipropetrovsk before Russia can reconstitute its forces, and whether the distraction in Iran will prove fatal to the peace process.
