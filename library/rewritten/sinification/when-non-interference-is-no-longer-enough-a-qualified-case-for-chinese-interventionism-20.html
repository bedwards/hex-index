Zheng Yongnian, one of China's most prominent public intellectuals on international affairs, has published something unusual: a direct argument that the non-interference doctrine Beijing has treated as sacred for decades needs to be quietly retired. Writing from the School of Public Policy at the Chinese University of Hong Kong, Shenzhen, Zheng frames his case as adjustment rather than rupture, but the implications are larger than his careful prose admits.

A Hobbesian World

Zheng's diagnosis is stark. "The world today," he writes, "resembles what the British philosopher Thomas Hobbes described as the state of nature—a lawless condition of anarchy marked by a ruthless, predatory logic." The invocation of Hobbes is deliberate and slightly shocking in the context of Chinese foreign-policy writing, which usually prefers the vocabulary of harmony and multipolarity. Zheng is signaling that the familiar slogans have stopped describing the terrain.

From that premise he builds his central proposal.

"I believe the traditional doctrine of non-interference urgently needs to be reexamined and adjusted."

What Zheng calls "interventionism 2.0" rests on three triggers. China would intervene, he argues, when its overseas interests are violated by host countries, when third parties threaten those interests, or when overseas factors significantly affect domestic stability. He is careful to wall this off from what he views as American excess: "China should protect our legitimate rights and interests through proactive means" without mimicking "American hegemonic practice." The distinction carries most of the argument's moral weight, and it is also where the argument is weakest.

The Ladder Metaphor

Zheng's most evocative passage pivots to development. "Having achieved development itself," he writes, "China seeks to extend the ladder outward, allowing other countries to climb as well." The contrast with what he sees as Western ladder-pulling is explicit. "When all countries are able to develop," he continues, "the international community will naturally become more harmonious." This is the essay's most ideologically confident moment, and it is where the non-interference tradition and the new interventionism are supposed to reconcile. Development as a shared project justifies the protective interventions, because the interventions are framed as preserving a common modernization pipeline rather than imposing a single model.

Zheng also makes a pointed critique of Western inconsistency, highlighting how supply-chain weaponization and semiconductor controls get described as legitimate policy while Iran's Strait closure gets described as coercion. The double standard is real, and Zheng is right to name it. Whether naming it justifies a new interventionist framework is a different question.

The Telling Example

One of the briefing's most revealing moments is Zheng's invocation of overseas telecom fraud as a case where Chinese intervention would be justified. He treats this as a clean example of legitimate interest protection, but it is also the example that most clearly illustrates how elastic the new framework could become. Telecom fraud operations shade quickly into law-enforcement overreach in third countries, and from there into surveillance infrastructure and political pressure. The same logic that protects Chinese citizens from scams can protect the Chinese state from critics. Zheng doesn't engage this slippage.

"We must not fall easily into the logic of war, while maintaining strategic resolve and the responsibilities of a major power."

This is the essay's most honest sentence. Zheng knows that the framework he is proposing creates exactly the pressure he is warning against. His answer is essentially discipline—Chinese policymakers, unlike their American counterparts, will know when to stop. History does not offer much evidence that any great power has ever successfully implemented that kind of self-restraint.

Counterpoints Zheng Skips

The essay has several structural gaps. The most important is definitional. Zheng never specifies how China would distinguish legitimate interest protection from hegemonic intervention in operational terms. The American interventions he criticizes were, at the time, also described by their authors as defense of nationals and strategic interests. Without a mechanism that constrains the definition, "interventionism 2.0" is just interventionism with a Chinese accent.

Zheng also underestimates the reciprocal cost. Expanding overseas intervention exposes China to counter-intervention and, more importantly, erodes the Global South coalition that has been central to Chinese strategic positioning. Many of those countries joined the Belt and Road precisely because China promised not to do what Zheng is now proposing. The framework risks fracturing the constituency it depends on.

Finally, the essay lacks institutional specificity. Zheng calls for engagement with multilateral institutions but doesn't explain how existing structures—the Security Council, regional organizations—would accommodate this framework, or whether new institutions would need to emerge. Without that architecture, "interventionism 2.0" is a posture rather than a policy.

Bottom Line

Zheng Yongnian has written something consequential even where he is wrong. The essay is evidence that Chinese strategic thinking is actively processing the tension between its global economic footprint and its traditional non-interference posture, and that the older doctrine is losing internal credibility. Zheng's Hobbesian framing and his double-standard critique of the West are both sharper than the usual Beijing-adjacent commentary. But the framework he proposes lacks the constraints that would prevent it from collapsing into exactly the hegemonic practice he denounces. Readers should treat this piece as a window into where Chinese foreign-policy discourse is heading, and as a warning that the destination is less novel than its author believes.
