In the final weeks of November 2025, the war in Ukraine quietly migrated into a domain where Russia had long assumed it held the advantage: the sea. Two complementary developments — Ukraine's drone strikes on shadow-fleet tankers in Turkish waters and the apparent destruction of a manned Russian submarine inside the fortified harbor of Novorossiysk by an unmanned underwater vehicle — together mark the most significant naval escalation of the conflict. Taken in isolation, each is striking. Taken together, they reveal a coherent strategy: if Ukraine cannot match Russia soldier for soldier on land, it will bleed the Kremlin's war economy and sink its fleet from beneath.

Two Fronts, One Logic

Good Times Bad Times frames the surface campaign in stark economic terms. Ukraine's Sea Baby drones struck the tankers Kyros and Virat, vessels nominally flagged to Gambia but functionally part of the shadow fleet that moves Russian crude past Western sanctions. The attacks threaten as much as 40 to 45 percent of Russia's seaborne oil exports, and they pair with a relentless aerial campaign that has knocked more than 30 percent of Russian refining capacity offline. As the analysis puts it, Kyiv is now waging economic warfare on two fronts simultaneously, “squeezing supply through refinery strikes and suppressing demand by making it dangerous and expensive to ship Russian crude.”

Perun, writing from a more tactical vantage, complements this with a forensic look at what happened beneath the water at Novorossiysk. Ukraine's previous successes against Russian submarines had been opportunistic — the Rostov-on-Don was caught on the surface, in dry dock. Once the Black Sea Fleet retreated to Novorossiysk, a port Perun calls “a fortress inside a fortress,” that approach was no longer viable. Ukrainian asymmetric weapons, Perun observes, “don't work particularly well if the target is submerged.” The answer was to build something that could.

“Navies around the world might be taking from the fact that once again another naval warfare first has been carried out by a country without much of a navy.” — Perun

The Fortress Breached

Perun's reconstruction of the Novorossiysk strike is the most detailed open-source account yet assembled. Walking through satellite imagery and released Ukrainian footage, he identifies a “pretty significant detonation that does appear to begin below the surface of the water,” and tentatively names the victim as the B-271 Kolpino, identified by paint markings unique to that boat. The implication is sweeping: a country without a navy has just demonstrated a capability — striking a manned submarine inside a defended harbor with an unmanned underwater vehicle — that the world's established naval powers are still struggling to field.

The strategic stakes are real. Russia's improved Kilo-class diesel-electrics carry land-attack cruise missiles and can lay mines capable of strangling Ukrainian merchant traffic. As Perun notes, “that missile attack might come with limited warning and from an unexpected angle.” Removing even one of these boats has operational value. Demonstrating that none of them are safe in port has strategic value an order of magnitude greater.

The Shadow Fleet at Sea

The surface campaign is, if anything, more politically fraught. The tankers struck off Turkey were not in Russian waters, and Turkish President Erdogan condemned the attacks as unacceptable. A third vessel, the MT Mersin, may have been hit off the coast of Senegal. These are nominally civilian ships sailing under third-country flags, and Ukraine's legal justification — a July 2023 warning from its Ministry of Defense that vessels bound for Russian Black Sea ports could be treated as carriers of military cargo — has never been tested in any international court.

The counterargument, advanced compellingly in the Good Times Bad Times analysis, is that the shadow fleet exists precisely to evade the rules-based system whose protection it now invokes. These ships operate outside standard insurance regimes, mask their ownership through flag-of-convenience arrangements, and serve as the circulatory system of Russia's war economy. If sanctions are to mean anything, the infrastructure built to circumvent them cannot be sacrosanct.

“Make money, not war” is becoming the unofficial motto of parts of the Washington elite attempting to build Europe's security architecture on a foundation of new business ties with the Russian Federation.

Washington's Counter-Current

Even as Ukraine escalates at sea, the political winds in Washington blow the other way. Good Times Bad Times documents a quiet effort by figures connected to the executive branch and to American business — reportedly including senior advisors and envoys — to “bypass the traditional US national security apparatus and convince the administration to view Russia not as a military threat but as a land of bountiful opportunity.” The pitch is that commercial entanglement with Moscow will produce a more durable peace than sanctions ever could.

This is a recycled thesis. It is precisely the logic of Germany's Wandel durch Handel — change through trade — which the 2022 invasion demolished. European energy dependence did not moderate the Kremlin; it financed the army now grinding through Ukrainian cities. That American deal-makers are reviving the argument while sanctioned oligarchs reportedly send representatives to quiet meetings with US firms suggests the White House's peace initiative may be less about ending a war than about opening a market.

Europe's Frozen Lever

Meanwhile, the single most consequential economic weapon available to Ukraine's allies sits inert at Euroclear in Belgium: roughly 185 billion euros in frozen Russian sovereign assets. Belgian Prime Minister Bart De Wever has signaled openness to converting this capital into a 140-billion-euro reparations loan for Ukraine, only to be undercut days later by Foreign Minister Maxime Prevot, who warned the move could complicate peace talks — a position conspicuously aligned with Washington's preferences. The cruel irony, as Good Times Bad Times observes, is that the country which does not hold the assets wants to dictate their use, while the bloc that does hold them cannot summon the collective will to act.

Counterpoints the Sources Underplay

Neither analysis dwells on what could go wrong for Ukraine. Striking civilian-flagged tankers in the territorial waters of neutral states risks alienating exactly the middle powers — Turkey, India, Gulf states — whose tacit cooperation Kyiv needs. Insurance markets could respond by raising premiums on all Black Sea traffic, hurting Ukrainian grain exports as much as Russian oil. And the underwater-drone genie, once out of the bottle, will not return; the same technology that sank a Russian submarine in Novorossiysk could one day be used against a NATO vessel in a future conflict by an actor with very different politics. Perun acknowledges in passing that this capability is “unlikely to get put back in the bottle anytime soon.” That cuts both ways.

Bottom Line

Ukraine's maritime campaign is a calculated wager that economic and tactical pressure at sea can offset manpower shortages on land. The evidence so far is encouraging: Russian refining capacity is degraded, Rosneft's profits have reportedly collapsed to roughly a tenth of last year's, and the Black Sea Fleet has just learned that even its fortified anchorages are not safe. But the campaign is hostage to forces beyond Kyiv's control — the political appetite in Washington for a transactional deal with Moscow, the paralysis in Brussels over the frozen assets, and the diplomatic patience of countries whose flagged ships now sit in Ukraine's crosshairs. Both belligerents are approaching exhaustion. Exhaustion does not guarantee peace; it guarantees only that whichever side endures longer on diminishing resources will shape what comes next.
