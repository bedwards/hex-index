In a twist that feels less like a policy report and more like a corporate plot twist, the author argues that Xcel Energy's current reliability crisis is not a victim of bad luck, but the direct result of its own aggressive green lobbying. The piece is notable for its willingness to strip away the usual "unforeseen challenges" narrative, instead presenting a timeline where the utility company explicitly championed the very coal plant closures that have now left it scrambling for power.

The Self-Inflicted Wound

The author, writing under the collective byline of Various, opens with a biting assessment of Xcel's public relations strategy. "One almost feels bad for the predicament the company finds itself in," the text admits, before immediately pivoting to the core accusation: "Xcel is simply reaping the entirely foreseeable consequences of its own self-serving actions." This framing is effective because it refuses to let the utility off the hook with the standard excuse of regulatory unpredictability. Instead, it points to a decade of deliberate strategy.

The argument hinges on a specific historical pivot point. Various notes that in 2018, Xcel became the first investor-owned utility to declare a self-imposed goal of making its system carbon-free by 2050. This was not a mandate forced upon them; it was a choice. "As the economics of clean energy have improved, it became clearer that we could meet more aggressive goals without sacrificing affordability or reliability for our customers," the utility's own manager, Nick Martin, claimed at the time. The author dismantles this confidence by citing the recent "Comanche Report," which admits the company faces a "capacity deficit" through at least 2028.

The Comanche Report has determined that this was incorrect.

This stark contradiction is the piece's strongest evidence. The author highlights that Xcel didn't just passively follow state laws; they actively lobbied for them. The text details how Xcel supported House Bill 19-1261 and Senate Bill 19-236 in Colorado, which set aggressive emissions targets. By accelerating the retirement of Comanche Unit 3 from 2040 to 2030, the company created a massive gap in baseload power. The author points out a crucial financial nuance here: the accelerated retirement increased the cost of the state's energy plan by $8 billion alone, yet Xcel continues to project massive profits from the resulting rate base expansion.

Critics might argue that the utility is simply being pragmatic now, acknowledging that the transition to renewables is harder than anticipated. However, the author counters this by noting that Xcel's own "Steel for Fuel" initiative was marketed as a win-win, a strategy where "everybody benefits." The reality, as the author sees it, is that the benefits were one-sided, with consumers stuck with high costs and a fragile grid.

The Illusion of Safety

The commentary then shifts to the technical reality of the grid, specifically the concept of "resource adequacy." The author explains that Xcel's analysis of various scenarios reveals a grim truth: "Under all analyzed scenarios, the Company's loss of load probability exceeds the planning standard of less than 0.1." To put this in perspective, the standard allows for a loss of power one day in ten years; Xcel's data suggests the risk is now over ten times higher than that benchmark.

This section effectively uses the utility's own data against its public messaging. While Xcel's investor presentations paint a picture of a giddy future filled with wind, solar, and batteries, the internal reports tell a story of desperation. "Difficult decisions about extending baseload units may be needed," the Comanche Report states, a polite way of admitting that the company needs to keep its coal plants running to avoid blackouts.

The author draws a parallel to the broader energy debate, noting that Xcel's strategy of blocking natural gas plants—while simultaneously retiring coal—has left them with no reliable backup. "The new gas plants were blocked by the very interest groups Xcel helped cultivate," the text observes. This creates a scenario where the utility is trapped by its own alliances. The author suggests that Xcel hoped to "feed the crocodiles" by funding green advocacy groups, only to find themselves as the next meal when the grid began to falter.

Democracy is the theory that the common people know what they want, and deserve to get it good and hard.

Quoting H.L. Mencken, the author frames the situation as a political tragedy where voters supported policies that have now backfired. The financial implications are severe, with residential electricity prices potentially skyrocketing by 55 percent by 2029 if load growth doesn't meet forecasts. The author argues that this is not a failure of the market, but a failure of a specific, self-serving corporate strategy that prioritized rate-base growth over grid stability.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its forensic tracing of Xcel's own lobbying efforts to the current reliability crisis, effectively dismantling the narrative of the utility as a victim of circumstance. Its biggest vulnerability, however, is the assumption that the utility could have easily pivoted to natural gas without political backlash, ignoring the genuine difficulty of building any new fossil-fuel infrastructure in the current regulatory environment. Readers should watch to see if the Colorado Public Utilities Commission forces Xcel to keep the coal plants online, or if the company doubles down on expensive battery storage to solve a problem it helped create.
