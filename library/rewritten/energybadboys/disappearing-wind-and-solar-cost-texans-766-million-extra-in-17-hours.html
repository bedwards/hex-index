In a landscape often dominated by ideological shouting, this piece from Energy Bad Boys cuts through the noise with a stark, data-driven claim: the very resources championed for lowering costs actually triggered a $766 million price spike when Texas needed them most. The author, Various, does not merely recount the failure of wind and solar during Winter Storm Fern; they dissect the mechanics of the collapse, challenging the narrative that intermittent renewables saved consumers money. This is not a story about weather, but about the structural fragility of a grid that has bet its reliability on resources that can vanish in an instant.

The Math Behind the Meltdown

The article's core strength lies in its refusal to accept industry talking points at face value. When the American Clean Power Association (ACPA) claimed renewables saved Texans $200 million, the author didn't just nod along; they dug into the raw numbers. Various writes, "ACPA doesn't disclose how it calculated those figures, and it didn't respond to our emails requesting more information, so we did a little analysis of our own." This skepticism is the engine of the piece. By reconstructing the cost baseline, the author reveals a grim reality: "Our analysis found that it is exceedingly unlikely that wind and solar saved Texas any money. In fact, our analysis found that wind and solar disappeared just when they were needed most, costing Texans an additional $766 million in wholesale power costs in just 17 hours."

The argument gains traction by contrasting the theoretical capacity of the grid with its actual performance under stress. The author points out that while wind and solar made up a significant chunk of installed capacity, they failed to deliver during the peak of the storm. "This is despite the fact that U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) data show wind and solar constituted roughly 23 of the total installed capacity on the grid in January, meaning they punched below their weight when the grid was stressed." The distinction between having a generator on paper and having it running when the temperature plummets is the crux of the reliability crisis. Critics might note that thermal plants also faced outages, but the author's data suggests the thermal fleet's availability remained significantly higher, hovering between 88 and 90 percent, while renewable output collapsed.

The Scarcity Mechanism

The piece moves beyond simple blame to explain the economic engine driving the price spikes. In an energy-only market like the Electric Reliability Council of Texas (ERCOT), prices are the primary signal for scarcity. The author explains that "high prices are the primary mechanism for keeping reliable plants online, as they capture huge amounts of revenue during these 'scarcity events.'" This is a crucial insight for readers trying to understand why prices didn't just rise, but skyrocketed. The correlation is undeniable: as the reserve margin—the buffer between supply and demand—dipped, prices exploded.

The author quantifies this relationship with striking precision. "The wholesale power price skyrocketed when reserve margins dipped below approximately 26 percent. For the statisticians in the audience, this model has an R² of 0.91, meaning about 91 percent of the variation in ERCOT wholesale prices in this dataset is explained by changes in available reserve margin." This statistical weight lends the argument a level of authority that goes beyond anecdotal evidence. It suggests that the problem isn't a lack of total energy, but a lack of dispatchable energy that can be turned on when the wind stops blowing. As the author notes, "The solution is to add more dispatchable capacity that can boost the reserve margin, rather than rely on generation resources that could fall off the grid at any moment."

The market structure in Texas specifically incentivizes reliable generators to exit the market. This isn't a bug of the ERCOT market; it is a feature.

The Cost of Inaction

Perhaps the most provocative section of the commentary is the counterfactual analysis. The author asks a question that policymakers often avoid: what if Texas had invested in natural gas instead of more wind and solar? The answer is staggering. "This analysis found that the new gas capacity could have saved Texas families and businesses $1.27 billion in power costs." This figure dwarfs the $766 million in extra costs incurred, suggesting that the current trajectory is not just unreliable, but economically inefficient.

The author traces this failure back to a systemic issue: the energy-only market structure fails to value reliability. "ERCOT hasn't added any net dispatchable capacity since 2003," Various writes, noting that the market design "dissuades new thermal generators from entering the market at a time when power demand is soaring." The piece highlights a quote from a 2018 report that the author describes as a "parasitic effect," where renewables drive down the revenues of reliable plants, leading to their retirement or preventing new ones from being built. "Texas continues to add more wind every year... This will ultimately mean more hours where coal and gas plants are not operating, and more retirements of conventional generation." While the author is critical of the industry's rhetoric, the underlying economic logic—that you cannot build a stable grid on resources that are not always available—is hard to refute.

Critics might argue that the cost of new gas infrastructure is high and that battery storage could eventually solve the intermittency issue. However, the author's point remains that the current mix is failing to meet demand during extreme events, and the market signals are not encouraging the necessary investment in reliability. The piece suggests that the pursuit of renewable targets has come at the expense of grid resilience, a trade-off that Texas consumers are now paying for in the billions.

Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its rigorous rejection of industry spin in favor of hard, wholesale market data, proving that the timing of renewable failures directly correlates with catastrophic price spikes. Its biggest vulnerability is the assumption that natural gas is the only viable alternative, potentially overlooking the long-term potential of storage or demand response technologies. Readers should watch for how the Electric Reliability Council of Texas responds to these findings, as the pressure to reform the market structure and value reliability will only intensify as demand continues to grow.
