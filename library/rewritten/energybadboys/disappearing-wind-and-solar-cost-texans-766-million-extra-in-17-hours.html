While industry lobbyists claim wind and solar saved Texas billions during a recent winter storm, a sharp new analysis from Energy Bad Boys flips the script entirely. The authors argue that these intermittent sources didn't just underperform; they vanished at the critical moment, driving wholesale prices up by $766 million in less than a day. For busy leaders watching energy markets, this is not just a weather report—it's a stark warning about the hidden costs of grid dependency.

The Disappearing Act

The piece opens by dismantling the narrative pushed by the American Clean Power Association (ACPA), which claimed renewable energy saved consumers $200 million in Texas during Winter Storm Fern. The authors reject this outright, noting the lack of transparency in the industry's math. "ACPA doesn't disclose how it calculated those figures, and it didn't respond to our emails requesting more information, so we did a little analysis of our own," they write. This refusal to engage with independent scrutiny is a telling detail; it suggests the industry is more interested in spin than in the hard data that actually matters to ratepayers.

The core of the argument rests on a specific timeline: the moment the wind stopped blowing and the sun went dark, the grid's reliability evaporated. The authors point out that while wind and solar made up roughly 23% of installed capacity, they delivered a mere 8% and 2% respectively when the storm hit its peak. "Wind and solar disappeared just when they were needed most, costing Texans an additional $766 million in wholesale power costs in just 17 hours," they assert. This framing is effective because it moves the debate from abstract capacity numbers to concrete financial pain. It forces the reader to confront the difference between having a generator on paper and having power on the wire.

Critics might note that the analysis relies heavily on a specific 17-hour window and assumes a baseline price that could be debated, but the correlation between the drop in renewable output and the price spike is visually and statistically compelling in the data presented.

The Anatomy of a Price Spike

The commentary then digs into the mechanics of the ERCOT (Electric Reliability Council of Texas) market, explaining why prices didn't just rise—they skyrocketed. The authors identify a critical threshold: when the reserve margin (the buffer of available power) dips below 26%, prices go vertical. "In an energy-only market like ERCOT... high prices are the primary mechanism for keeping reliable plants online, as they capture huge amounts of revenue during these 'scarcity events,'" they explain. This is a crucial insight for anyone trying to understand energy economics; the market isn't broken, it's working exactly as designed to punish scarcity, but the design fails when the "scarcity" is caused by the very resources meant to solve it.

The authors highlight that thermal plants (coal, gas, nuclear) remained remarkably reliable, with availability staying between 88% and 90% during the crisis. In contrast, the intermittent sources simply weren't there. "The falling reserve margin is highly correlated with rising electricity prices," they note, backing this up with a statistical model showing an R-squared of 0.91. This means 91% of the price variation can be explained solely by the lack of available backup power. The argument lands hard because it strips away the ideological fog and presents a simple, mathematical reality: when you lose your backup, you pay the premium.

"This isn't a bug of the ERCOT market; it is a feature."

The Cost of Inaction

Perhaps the most provocative part of the piece is the counterfactual analysis: what if Texas had built natural gas plants instead of more wind and solar? The authors estimate that adding 10,000 MW of new gas capacity would have cost $25 billion upfront but saved the state $1.27 billion in avoided scarcity prices during this single event. "The solution is to add more dispatchable capacity that can boost the reserve margin, rather than rely on generation resources that could fall off the grid at any moment," they conclude. This reframes the entire energy transition debate from a moral imperative to a risk management calculation.

The authors are particularly scathing about the market structure that discourages building reliable plants. They quote a 2018 report that bragged about the "parasitic effect" wind and solar have on the revenues of reliable generators. "Texas continues to add more wind every year... This will ultimately mean more hours where coal and gas plants are not operating, and more retirements of conventional generation," they warn. This is a bold claim that challenges the prevailing orthodoxy in energy policy. It suggests that the current path isn't just inefficient; it's actively dismantling the safety net required for future storms.

Bottom Line

Energy Bad Boys delivers a devastating critique of the renewable-heavy grid model, using hard data to show that reliability has a price tag that intermittent sources cannot cover. The strongest part of the argument is the direct link between the drop in wind/solar output and the massive price spike, which makes the abstract concept of "grid reliability" feel immediate and expensive. However, the piece's biggest vulnerability is its narrow focus on a single storm event, which may not account for the long-term benefits of renewables in non-storm conditions or the full lifecycle costs of gas infrastructure. Readers should watch for how ERCOT responds to these findings, as the market structure that incentivizes this volatility remains the central hurdle to any solution.
