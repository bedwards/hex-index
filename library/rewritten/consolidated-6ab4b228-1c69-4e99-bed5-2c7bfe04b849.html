Two recent explainers — Kenny Easwaran's lecture on the history of artificial intelligence and Crash Course's inaugural Futures of AI episode narrated by Kushian Avdar — set out to answer the same question: how did we get here? Their answers diverge in instructive ways. Easwaran, a philosopher, walks backward from ChatGPT through Turing, Leibniz, and a ninth-century Persian mathematician named al-Khwarizmi. Crash Course starts in the 1960s with Gordon Moore and races forward toward scaling laws and superintelligence. Read together, they form a stereoscopic view of a field whose present moment neither can fully explain on its own.

Two Clocks, Two Stories

Easwaran's clock runs in centuries. His lecture treats AI as the latest chapter in a very old human project: systematizing thought. The word algorithm is itself a Latinization of al-Khwarizmi's name, and Leibniz dreamed of a universal calculus that could settle philosophical disputes by computation. Crash Course's clock runs in decades. Avdar opens with an image of refrigerator-sized 1960s machines and the smartphones that replaced them, framing the story as one of raw exponential acceleration.

The difference is not merely stylistic. Easwaran uses the long view to make the current moment feel humbling; Crash Course uses the short view to make it feel vertiginous. "The change was unbelievably fast," Avdar writes. "It was like nothing history had ever seen. And yet, it's possible that it won't even hold a candle to what's happening now." Easwaran would likely nod at the second sentence while gently contesting the first — people in 1956, or 1997, or 2012 all felt they were living through unprecedented change, too.

Turing's Shadow

Where the two accounts genuinely converge is on Alan Turing, though they emphasize different halves of his legacy. Easwaran devotes his strongest passages to Turing's 1950 paper and its behavioral definition of intelligence:

If you can have that sort of extended conversation with the machine, that's all that matters, regardless of whether the machine is running on punch cards and vacuum tubes or brain cells and blood.

But Easwaran also pulls out a quieter line from the same paper — Turing's suggestion that rather than program an adult mind, researchers should "program the computer to learn and then raise the computer like a child." That offhand proposal, Easwaran argues, turned out to be the blueprint for everything that followed. Crash Course tells the same story from the other end. Avdar contrasts IBM's Deep Blue, which beat Garry Kasparov in 1997, with its modern successors: "Deep Blue, like all those other early chess bots, was still an example of symbolic AI. It's a kind of AI that relies on the logic coded directly into its hardware and makes moves by hard-coded search and evaluation." The pivot from hand-coded rules to systems that learn from data is, in Avdar's telling, the single most important shift in the field's history — and it is precisely the shift Turing had gestured toward seventy years earlier.

Winters and Scaling Laws

The accounts part ways again on the question of what happens next. Easwaran's history is structured around AI winters — the funding collapses of the early 1970s and late 1980s that followed waves of overpromising. He lingers on Marvin Minsky's infamous 1966 decision to assign computer vision as a summer project, and on the machine-translation debacle in which "the spirit is willing but the flesh is weak" came back as "the vodka is good but the meat is rotten." The pattern, in Easwaran's telling, is metronomic: progress, extrapolation, disappointment, retreat.

Crash Course barely mentions the winters. Avdar's frame is scaling laws — the empirical observation that model performance improves predictably with more data, more compute, and better algorithms. "In general, AI neural networks that are fed more data perform better," Avdar writes. "The more books a large language model reads, the better it'll be at writing its own." The conclusion is optimistic but hedged: "Scaling laws see a big future for AI. As algorithms improve and data and compute keep increasing, AI could deep learn its way to some pretty advanced stuff like finding new treatments for cancer or achieving super intelligence."

The ideas were sound. The silicon was not ready.

These two frames — winters and scaling laws — are not quite compatible. If Easwaran is right that the field's history is a cycle of hype and correction, then scaling laws are simply the current hype, and a correction is overdue. If Crash Course is right that scaling laws describe a genuine mathematical regularity, then past winters were artifacts of insufficient compute rather than genuine conceptual dead ends, and the current moment is categorically different. Both accounts could turn out to be partially correct. Neural networks were invented in 1957 and backpropagation in the 1980s; the ideas waited decades for hardware. A future historian may look back at the 2020s and see either a third winter or the moment when scaling stopped mattering because something better replaced it.

What Both Accounts Miss

Neither lecture engages seriously with the political economy of the current boom. Easwaran nods at it but does not develop the point; Crash Course mentions that Microsoft and Google dominate because they have the resources to feed enormous models, but treats this as a neutral fact rather than a structural problem. Yet the concentration of frontier AI capability in a handful of firms — and the entanglement of those firms with national security agencies and executive-branch priorities — is arguably the most consequential fact about AI in 2026. The Pentagon's interest in autonomous systems, the State Department's export controls on advanced chips, and the White House's shifting posture toward compute governance will shape the field at least as much as any architectural breakthrough.

Nor does either account reckon with the labor that makes modern systems possible: the annotators in Kenya and the Philippines who label training data, the moderators who scrub toxic outputs, the writers and artists whose work was scraped without consent. A history of AI that begins with al-Khwarizmi and ends with GPT-5 can make the whole enterprise look like a pure contest of ideas, when in fact it has always been a contest of ideas plus capital plus cheap human attention.

A sharper counterpoint both accounts sidestep: the behavioral definition of intelligence that Turing proposed, and that Easwaran treats as the conceptual foundation of the field, was never philosophically settled. John Searle's Chinese Room argument — that symbol manipulation without understanding is not thought — has grown more urgent, not less, as large language models produce fluent prose without any apparent model of truth. Crash Course's scaling-law optimism and Easwaran's winter-inflected caution both assume that "more capable" and "more intelligent" point in the same direction. They may not.

Bottom Line

Easwaran offers the long view: AI is an old project, its history is cyclical, and confident predictions usually look foolish in retrospect. Crash Course offers the short view: something genuinely new is happening, the math says it will keep happening, and the only real question is whether the curve bends before or after it matters. Neither view is wrong, and neither is sufficient. The most honest reading is that the current moment is simultaneously a continuation of a seventy-year pattern and a departure from it — that scaling laws are real until they aren't, that winters are possible but not guaranteed, and that the infrastructure being built around AI right now will persist regardless of whether the technology itself delivers on its promises. Readers looking for certainty in either direction should be suspicious of anyone selling it.
