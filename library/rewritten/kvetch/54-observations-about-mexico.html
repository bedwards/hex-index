Oz delivers a startlingly unvarnished field report from Mexico, bypassing the usual travelogue tropes to expose a society where the state has largely abdicated its most basic duties. The piece's most provocative claim is not about crime or culture, but about the sheer impossibility of building a functional middle class in a system where "equity is ~the only path to wealth." For a reader trying to understand the mechanics of emerging markets or the fragility of public trust, this is essential listening.

The Architecture of Inequality

Oz frames Mexico not as a chaotic developing nation, but as a rigidly stratified society where social mobility is "super sticky" due to deep-rooted class and ethnic divisions. The author argues that the system rewards "tight in-group capital" over abstract merit, a dynamic that echoes the rigid caste structures of the colonial era, much like the Casta system that defined social hierarchy in New Spain for centuries. Oz writes, "Diaspora ethnic groups succeeded because they arrived with merchant traditions, internal credit, intra-marriage, and distance from indigenous caste logic." This observation is sharp; it suggests that success in Mexico often requires an outsider status that allows one to bypass the entrenched local hierarchies that trap the native-born.

The commentary on social stratification extends to the very language of service. Oz notes that the common term for a waiter, joven (young man), carries a condescension that feels "between garçon and boy, as once used in South Africa." This linguistic parallel is not merely an observation of etiquette; it is a window into the "ethno-classist" nature of the society. The author suggests that the Mexican elite, while avoiding the legal apartheid of South Africa, have cultivated a similar environment where the rich live in "endless fortifications" and the poor are relegated to the margins.

"Once a society normalises private provision of order, it's incredibly hard to claw back. You need a broadly vested elite, upfront. Which is extremely difficult (impossible?) in an ethno-classist state like Mexico."

Critics might argue that this view overlooks the vibrant civil society and the genuine efforts of the Mexican middle class to carve out spaces of dignity. However, Oz's point about the "path dependence" of broken incentives holds weight: when the state fails to provide basic security, the wealthy retreat, and the social contract fractures. The author's comparison to South Africa is stark, noting that while Mexico avoided legal segregation, the practical outcome of "more crime, more dysfunction" creates a similar reality for the average citizen.

The Failure of Public Goods

The piece pivots to a scathing critique of state capacity, using the simple, daily act of drinking water as the ultimate metric. Oz argues that "the single greatest test for state capacity is drinking water on tap," a requirement for "boring competence and coordination across decades." In Mexico, this competence is absent, leading to an enormous bottled water industry that serves as a "paean to state capacity" in reverse—a symbol of dysfunction rather than order.

This failure extends to infrastructure and urban planning. The author describes the "cargo cult modernity" of recycling bins in homes where no collection service exists, and the "jagged concrete jungle" of cities like Queretaro, which lack any genuine urban design. Oz writes, "There is no such thing as urban design in Mexican cities, beyond the old planned Spanish city centres." This is a damning indictment of modern governance, suggesting that without a strong central authority, development becomes a chaotic sprawl of "Soviet khrushchyovkas" and unregulated growth.

The author also touches on the pervasive corruption that underpins the economy, describing an industry of factureras where fake invoices are sold to launder money and avoid taxes. "ChatGPT tells me this accounts for 1 – 2% of Mexican GDP," Oz notes, highlighting how the tax system itself incentivizes criminal behavior. This is not just a bureaucratic glitch; it is a structural feature of a state that cannot enforce its own rules.

"Abundant potable water is a paean to state capacity, a symbol of functionality and order — like houses of worship."

A counterargument worth considering is that the private sector's ability to fill these gaps—providing security, water, and healthcare—demonstrates a form of resilience. Yet, Oz counters this by pointing out that this "private provision of order" creates a two-tiered society where the rich live in a First World bubble while the rest struggle with a "dog's breakfast public health service." The author's observation that "Australia tends to have a far narrower band of 'fine to good' outcomes" serves as a sobering contrast to Mexico's extreme variability.

Cultural Grandeur and Global Convergence

Despite the grim assessment of governance, Oz finds genuine beauty in Mexico's cultural vibrancy. The author is struck by the "operatic culture" where everything is "grandiose, a telenovela writ large," from the music of megastars like Luis Miguel to the "bizarro theme park" of Kidzania. The piece notes that unlike Australia, where pop music is often treated with irony, Mexico has a "cross-class and unironic appreciation" for its own cultural icons.

The author also reflects on the global economic shifts, noting the "meteoric rise of the Chinese auto industry titan" and the potential threat to Mexico's manufacturing sector. "Mexico feels at its feet," Oz writes, contrasting this with Australia's position "on the fringes of empire." This geopolitical observation adds a layer of urgency to the piece, suggesting that Mexico's internal fractures are being tested by external forces.

The commentary concludes with a personal reflection on the "high chaos / high agency" mix that once appealed to the author but now feels like "just sucks." This shift from "third world chic" to a recognition of "general dysfunction" mirrors the author's own life arc from a "young single man" to a "risk averse" father.

"The ability of a state to maintain public goods and social order is surprisingly hard and probably path dependent on a bunch of things."

Bottom Line

Oz's piece is a powerful, if pessimistic, diagnosis of a nation where the state has failed to provide the basic foundations of a modern society. The strongest part of the argument is the linkage between the lack of public goods—like tap water and security—and the entrenchment of a rigid, ethno-class hierarchy. The biggest vulnerability is the tendency to view these issues through a lens of cultural determinism, potentially underestimating the capacity for institutional reform. However, the warning that "once a society normalises private provision of order, it's incredibly hard to claw back" is a crucial takeaway for anyone watching the trajectory of emerging economies.
