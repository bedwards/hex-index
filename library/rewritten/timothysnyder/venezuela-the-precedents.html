Timothy Snyder does not merely analyze a foreign policy shift; he exposes a dangerous pattern of American behavior that treats international law as optional and human lives as collateral. In a piece that refuses to accept the administration's official narrative, Snyder argues that the recent extraction of Nicolás Maduro is not a triumph of democracy, but a calculated act of imperial overreach that mirrors the worst moments of the 20th century. This is essential listening for anyone trying to understand why the United States is rapidly losing its moral authority while simultaneously destabilizing its own political future.

The Illusion of a Clean Break

Snyder opens by dismantling the idea that this intervention is a unique or necessary response to a specific crisis. He frames the event not as a rescue mission, but as the latest chapter in a long history of the United States asserting an implicit right to choose leaders in Latin America. "Throughout the cold war, and indeed long before, the United States has intervened in Central and South America, asserting an implicit right to choose leaders," Snyder writes. He points out that while past interventions were cloaked in anti-communist rhetoric, the current operation lacks even that pretense, relying instead on the "essentially fictional concept of 'narco-terrorism'" to justify the removal of a sitting president.

The author's critique of the administration's economic logic is particularly sharp. He notes that while the White House claims to be opening Venezuela's oil to American companies, the reality is that the country requires massive investment and stability to produce profit—conditions that are impossible to achieve through military extraction. "One of the powerful arguments in its favor is continuity: that it offers a chance to move on from a calamity," Snyder argues, suggesting that the obvious path forward—recognizing the winner of the 2024 election, Edmundo González—has been ignored in favor of a chaotic power grab. Critics might note that the administration could argue that the current regime is too corrupt to allow any economic recovery, but Snyder's point stands: military force cannot manufacture the political stability required for investment.

The obvious thing to do now in Venezuela would be to hold elections, yet the administration prefers the essentially fictional concept of 'narco-terrorism' to justify its actions.

Echoes of Iraq and the Failure of Regime Change

The commentary deepens as Snyder draws a direct line between the current operation and the 2003 invasion of Iraq. He reminds readers that the Iraq War was predicated on the belief that removing a dictator would automatically create the conditions for democracy, a notion that proved catastrophically wrong. "The United States had made only very limited plans for the political future of the country, the Bush administration imagining that the defeat of an army, the removal of a dictator, and a ban on a political party would themselves suffice to create the conditions for democracy," Snyder writes. He observes that the current strategy in Venezuela suffers from the same fatal flaw: the belief that removing Maduro will predictably result in a Venezuela that America "runs."

Snyder highlights the absurdity of the administration's current position, where they are effectively backing the very people they claim to oppose. He notes that Delcy Rodríguez, a figure installed through a stolen election, is now being offered the backing of American violence. "In Iraq, although it was embarrassing to say so, the American occupiers were reduced to cooperating with the people they said that they had overthrown," he notes, adding that in Venezuela, this evolution took mere hours rather than years. This comparison is effective because it strips away the rhetoric of liberation to reveal the mechanics of occupation: the US is now propping up a regime it previously condemned, relying on the same secret services and civilian gangs that were once the target of sanctions.

The Global Cost of Ignoring International Law

Perhaps the most alarming section of the piece is Snyder's analysis of how this intervention aligns the United States with authoritarian powers like Russia and China. He points out the linguistic parallel between the administration's description of the operation as an "extraordinary military operation" and the language used by Vladimir Putin to justify the invasion of Ukraine. "The American government made no effort to justify its extraction of Maduro in terms of international law, which is an obvious Russian intellectual victory," Snyder writes. By bypassing legal frameworks, the US is inadvertently validating the very worldview that China and Russia are trying to impose: a world where might makes right and international law is a joke.

The author argues that this is not just a diplomatic error, but a strategic blunder that aids China's long-term goal of remaking the international order. "Until now, the Russians who have been doing the dirty work in China's effort to remake the international order as simply a matter of power politics carried out by dictators in the service of personal priorities. Now the Americans are also helping to bring about a Chinese world order," he asserts. This framing forces the reader to confront the irony that an American military action is accelerating the decline of the liberal international order it was designed to protect.

The Domestic Agenda Behind Foreign Wars

Snyder concludes by turning the lens inward, suggesting that the true purpose of this intervention is domestic political consolidation. He draws a chilling parallel to fascist regimes of the 1930s, which used foreign wars to legitimize the oppression of internal enemies. "Fascist regimes were defeated in 1945, but they were, while they lasted, legitimated by war," Snyder writes. He argues that the administration is using the "war on drugs" narrative to unite external and internal enemies, creating a pretext for a larger paramilitary force and a crackdown on political opponents at home.

The author warns that this strategy relies on the assumption that the public will accept a quick victory without questioning the long-term consequences. "Trump and his advisors seem to want the political gains of fighting a war without actually having to fight one," he observes. However, Snyder cautions that this shortcut to fascism is fragile. "It only succeeds as fascism if Americans allow it to do so," he writes, urging journalists and judges to recognize the connection between foreign adventures and domestic authoritarianism. The piece ends on a note of cautious hope: if the public understands the mechanics of this manipulation, the cycle can be broken before it leads to full-scale conflict.

This act of war is more about regime change in the United States than it was about anything in Venezuela. It only succeeds as fascism if Americans allow it to do so.

Bottom Line

Snyder's strongest argument is his ability to connect the dots between the administration's foreign policy actions and the erosion of democratic norms at home, framing the Venezuela intervention as a domestic political tool rather than a genuine security measure. His biggest vulnerability is the assumption that the public and institutions will recognize and resist this manipulation, a hope that may be optimistic given the current political climate. Readers should watch closely to see if the administration escalates its rhetoric to justify further military involvement, which would validate Snyder's warning about the slide toward fascism.
