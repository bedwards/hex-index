Michael Ruhlman transforms a humble deli staple into a lens for examining culinary discipline, the erosion of public discourse, and the quiet heroism of private conservation. In a newsletter format often dominated by fleeting trends, Ruhlman anchors his argument in the tactile reality of food preparation, suggesting that the act of making a proper chicken salad is a radical assertion of care in an increasingly chaotic world.

The Architecture of Taste

Ruhlman does not merely offer a recipe; he constructs a philosophy of texture and ingredient integrity. He argues that the common reliance on convenience foods often leads to a fundamental failure in the final dish. "Don't use grocery-store, rotisserie chicken (unless you need the convenience, in which case it's fine)," he writes, immediately distinguishing between necessity and choice. He posits that the grocery-store option results in meat that is "mealy," a texture that undermines the entire composition. Instead, he advocates for a methodical approach: buying bone-in breasts, seasoning them simply, and baking them until "just cooked through." This insistence on control over the cooking process is the first pillar of his argument.

The author's voice is particularly distinctive when he challenges conventional wisdom regarding flavor pairings. While many recipes rely on the sweetness of fruit to balance the richness of the dressing, Ruhlman takes a firm stand against it. "Grapes on the other hand? No, thanks," he declares, citing a chef friend who "is not big on grapes in food." This rejection of the popular "chicken salad with grapes" trope is not just a matter of personal preference; it is a critique of the homogenization of taste. He argues that a great chicken salad requires a specific interplay of textures: the creaminess of the mayo, the acid of the lemon, and the "crunch" of celery or cashews. He notes that "two additional textures are best," a principle that elevates the dish from a simple mixture to a complex culinary experience.

"The way to begin a great chicken salad is as follows. If this is all you do, you will have an excellent chicken salad on your hands."

Ruhlman's framing of the "base" recipe—shallot, lemon, mayo, salt, pepper—is a masterclass in minimalism. He suggests that once this foundation is mastered, the cook can "go to town" with variations like jerk paste, tarragon, or curry. This approach mirrors the historical evolution of the dish itself. He briefly references the coronation of Queen Elizabeth II in 1953, noting that the original recipe included apricots, while modern iterations often feature Major Grey's chutney. By weaving in this historical context, Ruhlman reminds the reader that chicken salad is a living tradition, one that adapts to cultural moments without losing its essential character. The British practice of serving it in a split-open baked potato, which he admits "sounds weird," is cited as a testament to the dish's versatility.

The Erosion of Discourse

The commentary takes a sharp turn from the kitchen to the cultural landscape, where Ruhlman identifies a troubling shift in how food and ideas are discussed. He contrasts the "aughts and early teens," a time when "actual discussion and debate about food issues was not only possible, it was embraced," with the current climate of caution. He observes that today, "you have to be very careful about what you say for fear of offending any number of people." This is not a nostalgic lament but a critical observation of how the digital age has sanitized public conversation.

He reflects on a panel discussion from 2010 where he was "rather vocal" on the issue of being "too busy to cook," a time when "dissent and opinionated writing flourished." The contrast he draws is stark: the past allowed for "ways to answer back," whereas the present demands a performative neutrality that stifles genuine engagement. "Remembering those long ago days," he writes, he recalls the vibrant exchange of ideas that has since been replaced by a fear of backlash. This section of the piece serves as a broader commentary on the state of free speech, suggesting that the loss of robust debate in food writing is a microcosm of a larger societal retreat from conflict.

Critics might note that Ruhlman's nostalgia for the pre-TikTok era risks romanticizing a time when online discourse was also rife with toxicity and misinformation. However, his point is not that the past was perfect, but that the mechanism of disagreement has changed. The ability to be "vocal" and have one's voice met with a counter-argument, rather than silence or cancellation, is what he values. He frames this as a loss of intellectual freedom, where the "freedom of speech issues rumble below almost every hot topic today."

Beyond the Plate: Conservation and Culture

Ruhlman's scope widens further as he connects the personal act of cooking to global themes of conservation and cultural preservation. He highlights the story of Cecilia Durán Gafo, a 72-year-old landowner in Chile who decided to protect King Penguins on her farm. Her story is presented as part of a "global trend" where "15,000 private protected areas now help to conserve underrepresented biomes and highly threatened regions that government action alone cannot." This anecdote serves as a powerful counterpoint to the idea that individual action is insignificant. Just as a cook can transform simple ingredients into something extraordinary, a single landowner can protect a fragile ecosystem.

He also touches on the cultural landscape, reviewing recent books and shows with a discerning eye. He praises Liz Strout's novel for its focus on "human carelessness" and "adultery," finding delight in the messy reality of human relationships. He discusses the "trad wife phenomenon" and the book Yesteryear, which explores the complexities of women who "pretend to embrace" a traditional lifestyle while actually living it. This analysis of gender politics and social media performance adds depth to his culinary commentary, suggesting that the choices we make about food are inextricably linked to the choices we make about identity and community.

"I've been doing a lot of looking back at our national food discourse from 20 years ago to now, and it has reminded me of the days when you could say what you wanted and people had ways to answer back."

The piece concludes with a celebration of the simple pleasures: a mezcal rickey, a chicken salad on a brioche bun, and a flinty chablis. These recommendations are not just about consumption; they are about the ritual of gathering and the joy of sharing a meal. Ruhlman's final thought is a reminder that in a world of uncertainty, the act of preparing and sharing food remains a constant source of connection and meaning.

Bottom Line

Ruhlman's strongest argument lies in his ability to elevate the mundane into the meaningful, using the chicken salad as a metaphor for the care and intention required in all aspects of life. His critique of the modern decline in public discourse is a necessary reminder of the value of honest, unfiltered conversation. The piece's greatest vulnerability is its reliance on a somewhat idealized view of the past, but this does not diminish the urgency of its message. For the busy reader, this commentary offers not just a recipe, but a philosophy: that in a chaotic world, the deliberate act of creation—whether in the kitchen or in public life—is the most powerful form of resistance.
