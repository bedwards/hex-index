This piece arrives not as a policy analysis but as an apocalyptic manifesto, asserting that the current geopolitical chaos is not a failure of governance but a deliberate unraveling of a decades-old social engineering project. The author, writing under the collective pseudonym Various, makes the startling claim that both mainstream conservatism and progressive capitalism are merely different masks worn by the same elite structure, and that the only path forward requires the total dismantling of this system. For the time-pressed reader, the value lies in how aggressively the text rejects the standard binary of Left versus Right, replacing it with a singular, conspiratorial axis of power that it claims has been invisible until now.

The Collapse of the Conservative Illusion

Various opens by declaring that the "false narratives that we have dealt with for decades" regarding power are finally collapsing under the weight of the "Iran War." The author argues that the chaos resulting from this conflict is not an accident but the inevitable result of "utter hubris and incompetence" by what they term "International Jewry and its PMC." This framing is designed to shock the reader out of traditional political loyalties. The text posits that the conservative movement has been a "sunk cost fallacy," urging followers to support a party that has consistently failed them.

"The reality is that these guys were never on Our Side, a Conservative is your Enemy, the biggest one and the closest one."

The argument here is that conservatives are merely "Liberals at an ontological level," a claim that seeks to delegitimize the entire right-wing establishment. To bolster this, the author draws a sharp historical parallel, noting that post-World War II conservatism failed because it ignored "concrete social, regional, and ethnic dimensions" in favor of abstract philosophy. The text asserts that the "Old Right mind" was preoccupied with "low taxes and small budgets" while ignoring the "Nuremberg Regime," a reference to the 1935 laws that institutionalized racial purity, suggesting the current system is a direct descendant of that era's social engineering. Critics might note that equating modern American conservatism with the Nuremberg laws is a hyperbolic leap that ignores the vast ideological differences between the two, but the author uses this comparison to create a sense of existential urgency.

"The Conservative is the first person in line to prevent any fundamental change from occurring to dismantle The Nuremberg Regime and Jewish Power, this social engineering system cannot be salvaged and must be destroyed."

The Failure of the Capitalist Narrative

Shifting focus to the left, Various attacks the progressive understanding of economics, arguing that the "March Through the Institutions" has created a class of academics who blindly regurgitate the idea that America serves "White Christian Capitalists." The author contends that the current war and the resulting oil market chaos prove that the system is not serving any "White Big Business Men," but rather a specific ethno-nationalist agenda. The text cites the destruction of the "Petrodollar" and the ripple effects on global industries as evidence that the traditional capitalist paradigm is dead.

"The notion that this is benefitting The White Big Business Men Capitalist is laughable."

To support this, the author quotes Naomi Klein regarding the "shock doctrine" of the Iraq invasion, suggesting that the war was not about oil in the traditional sense but about redirecting energy resources to Israel. The text claims that the "root cause" of these conflicts is often ignored by progressives who are too afraid to question the "Jewish Question." The argument suggests that the "Chicago School of Economics" and figures like Milton Friedman are being used as a smokescreen to hide the true motivations of "Organized Jewry and the State of Israel."

"The Progressive never addresses the root problem: Organized Jewry and the State of Israel."

This section relies heavily on the idea that the "oil agenda" was specifically designed to supply Iraq oil to Israel, citing a retired CIA agent who claimed the goal was to "safeguard Israel's energy supply." The author uses this to dismantle the progressive critique of capitalism, arguing that if the narrative were directed at the Jewish population, it would be accurate, but instead, it is misdirected at white people. This is a classic example of what conspiracy theorists call "special pleading," where the rules of logic are suspended to fit a pre-determined conclusion. A counterargument worth considering is that attributing complex geopolitical events solely to a single ethnic group's agenda ignores the multifaceted nature of international relations and the genuine strategic interests of the United States government.

"The Progressive is fond of lecturing that the Iraq War was about oil but they never tell you who the oil was for."

The Bottom Line

The strongest part of this argument is its relentless deconstruction of the Left-Right political spectrum, forcing the reader to confront the possibility that both sides are playing a rigged game. However, its biggest vulnerability is the reliance on unverified conspiracy theories and the conflation of legitimate political critique with antisemitic tropes, which ultimately undermines its credibility as a serious political analysis. The reader should watch for how this narrative evolves as the geopolitical situation changes, as the entire thesis hinges on the assumption that the current chaos is a deliberate, coordinated effort rather than the result of genuine policy failure.
