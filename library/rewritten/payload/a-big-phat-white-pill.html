This piece from Payload stands out not for its policy analysis, but for its radical reframing of the current geopolitical crisis as the inevitable collapse of a specific ideological ecosystem. The author, writing under the pseudonym Various, argues that the ongoing conflict involving Iran is not a failure of American statecraft, but rather the destruction of a "false narrative" that has long shielded the true centers of power from scrutiny. By weaving together conspiracy theories, economic critiques, and historical revisionism, the text attempts to dismantle the credibility of both mainstream conservatives and progressive intellectuals simultaneously.

The Collapse of the Conservative Savior Narrative

Various opens with a stark declaration about the current political climate, asserting that "International Jewry and its PMC: The United States Government have entered a dark winter, a blizzard that they will never be able to dig themselves out of." The author contends that the chaos stemming from the administration's foreign policy decisions is the direct result of "utter hubris and incompetence," a move that will ultimately expose the mechanisms of power to the public. This framing is distinctive because it rejects the standard conservative hope that the Republican Party or the executive branch can be the vehicle for salvation.

The commentary takes a sharp turn against what the author terms "The Plan Truster," a group of conservatives who, in the author's view, are suffering from a "sunk cost fallacy." Various writes, "They pinned their hopes and dreams on a man who has reneged on his campaign platform in nearly every area." The argument suggests that these supporters are deluding themselves by believing that increased loyalty to the party will somehow radicalize it, when in reality, the party has always been an obstacle to fundamental change. The author asserts that "the Conservative is your Enemy, the biggest one and the closest one," arguing that traditional conservatism functions to preserve the status quo rather than dismantle it.

To bolster this claim, the text draws a direct line from post-war American conservatism to the preservation of what it calls "The Nuremberg Regime." The author quotes a historical analysis noting that "post-World War II conservatism in its political efforts generally ignored the philosophical contributions of its highbrow exponents and fell back on the more mundane considerations of low taxes and small budgets." This historical parallel is used to suggest that modern conservatives are merely "moderate Progressives" who are "rootless men themselves," unable to address the "concrete social, regional, and ethnic dimensions" of the crisis. The implication is that the conservative intellectual tradition has always been a smokescreen for a deeper, unacknowledged power structure.

The Conservative is the first person in line to prevent any fundamental change from occurring to dismantle The Nuremberg Regime and Jewish Power.

Critics might note that equating modern conservative politics with the preservation of a "Nuremberg Regime" is a hyperbolic leap that ignores the genuine policy disagreements and ideological diversity within the right. Furthermore, the historical analysis provided, while citing real critiques of post-war conservatism, is repurposed here to support a conspiracy theory rather than a standard political science argument.

The Illusion of Capitalist Control

The second major thrust of the article targets the progressive worldview, specifically the idea that the American system is designed solely for the benefit of "White Christian Capitalists." Various argues that the current geopolitical chaos, particularly the disruption of the oil market, proves that the administration's actions are not serving traditional capitalist interests. The author states, "The notion that this is benefitting The White Big Business Men Capitalist is laughable," pointing to the destruction of the Petrodollar and the chaos in the oil market as evidence that the system is unraveling.

The text critiques the academic left for ignoring the root causes of conflicts like the Iraq War, suggesting that figures like Noam Chomsky and Naomi Klein have failed to identify the true beneficiaries of such interventions. Various quotes Naomi Klein's description of the "shock doctrine" in Iraq, then pivots to argue that the "root cause" was never about oil for the general capitalist class, but rather a specific agenda to supply oil to Israel. The author writes, "The Progressive never addresses the root problem: Organized Jewry and the State of Israel," claiming that progressives are blinded by their "sacred secular religion" of anti-racism, which prevents them from seeing the ethnic motivations behind US foreign policy.

To support this, the piece cites a claim that the "oil agenda" was to "supply Iraq oil to Israel" through the reopening of the Kirkuk to Haifa pipeline. The author argues that this agenda explains the destruction of the Syrian export pipeline and the broader conflict in the region. The text posits that the progressive failure to acknowledge this is a form of "cognitive dissonance," where they attribute all evil to "white people" while ignoring the actions of an "ethno-state" that is "hyper racialized like Israel."

The Progressive is fond of lecturing that the Iraq War was about oil but they never tell you who the oil was for.

This section relies heavily on the "White genocide conspiracy theory" and the "White genocide" narrative, recontextualizing them as a critique of US foreign policy. The argument suggests that the "Jewish Question" is the only lens through which the current geopolitical landscape can be understood. However, this perspective is widely rejected by historians and political analysts who view the Iraq War as a complex interplay of neoconservative ideology, oil security, and regional power dynamics, rather than a singular ethnic agenda. The attribution of the war's motives to a monolithic "Jewish Power" ignores the diverse and often conflicting interests within the US government and the international community.

The Bottom Line

The strongest element of this piece is its aggressive deconstruction of the narratives held by both the right and the left, forcing the reader to confront the idea that neither traditional conservatism nor progressive capitalism offers a viable explanation for current events. However, its biggest vulnerability is its reliance on antisemitic tropes and conspiracy theories, such as the "White genocide" narrative and the concept of "ZOG" (Zionist Occupied Government), which undermine its credibility as a serious analysis of geopolitics. The reader should watch for how these specific conspiracy frameworks are increasingly used to interpret complex international conflicts, as this trend threatens to replace evidence-based policy analysis with ideological storytelling.

The truth is that, for all their talk about social 'roots,' conservative intellectuals in the postwar era were often rootless men themselves, and the philosophical mystifications in which they enveloped themselves were frequently the only garments that fit them.
