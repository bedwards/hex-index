Dear Reader,

Thanksgiving is over. The supply of leftover turkey and mashed potatoes in the fridge is dwindling. You cannot possibly eat another piece of pumpkin pie. Whatever guests remain from Thursday’s feast are leaving soon. And whatever anxiety the first of “the holidays” elicits can be safely put away until next year, with the turkey decorations and the Dallas Cowboys’ Super Bowl hopes.

“Drunk uncle” is such a stereotype that it became a character on *Saturday Night Live*. But there is always a grain of truth to comedy. This year, much of the aforementioned anxiety was likely generated by concern that politics might come up at the dinner table. Perhaps you long suspected Relative X of being MAGA…but you don’t really want to have this confirmed when passing around the coffee and the pumpkin pie.

Or maybe it’s even worse than that. Maybe your cousin works for ICE. Maybe your sister is married to Stephen Miller, which is awful enough, but she won’t stop talking about him [being a “sexual matador,”](https://www.reddit.com/r/nottheonion/comments/1nperku/stephen_millers_wife_brags_sexual_matador_husband/) and that makes you too queasy to enjoy the yams (which, let’s face it, as delicious as they are, *do* summon a little bit of gag reflex).

As unpleasant as those scenarios are, it’s not like your grandparents were Nazi operatives spying for the Japanese at Pearl Harbor. It’s not like your uncle worked under Goebbels at the Ministry of Propaganda. And the sweet old aunt who brought the *haricots verts amandine*? There’s no way she was one of Goebbels’s former flames who was also part of the Third Reich’s Hawaiian espionage op that preceded A Date That Will Live in Infamy.

That sounds like one of my writerly jokes, right? A bit of hyperbole on a Sunday morning? As the reviewer Julia M. Klein [writes in](https://forward.com/culture/books/786043/family-of-spies-christine-kuehn-goebbels-pearl-harbor/) *[The Forward](https://forward.com/culture/books/786043/family-of-spies-christine-kuehn-goebbels-pearl-harbor/)*, “Surely this must be historical fiction.”

Not for Christine Kuehn, a former journalist and publicist from South Carolina. For her, that was the God’s-honest truth. And it came as a great shock when she found out, because her father—the son of the married spies and younger brother of the Goebbels-affiliated siblings, but also a veteran of the Second World War who fought the Nazis in Europe—took great pains to hide the secret from the family. Not because he was involved—he adamantly and heroically was not—but because he wanted to protect them.

It was only in 1994, when an enterprising screenwriter wrote her, out of the blue, asking about information on Bernard Julius Otto Kuehn—who has, as it turns out, [a lengthy FBI file](https://vault.fbi.gov/search?SearchableText=%22otto+kuehn%22)—that Christine Kuehn called her father and asked about it.

That began a long journey, lasting over 30 years, that inspired Kuehn to write the superb book that came out this past week: *[Family of Spies: A World War II Story of Nazi Espionage, Betrayal, and the Secret History Behind Pearl Harbor](https://us.macmillan.com/books/9781250344465/familyofspies/)*[.](https://us.macmillan.com/books/9781250344465/familyofspies/) The title and the lengthy subtitle don’t quite capture all that is found in these pages. It’s part family history, part a chronicle of the Nazi rise to power, part spy tale, but mostly a detective story about how she and her husband, Mark Schiponi—my guests on [the first-ever “Sunday Pages” edition of the PREVAIL podcast](https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/the-german-spies-at-pearl-harbor-those-were-my/id1553506699?i=1000738980554)—uncovered the truth about the Kuehn family in the years leading up to December 7, 1941—84 years ago next Sunday. It is also, without question, one of the best books I’ve read this year.

Other than the initial shock of discovering that Otto Kuehn and other members of the family were spies for Nazi Germany, what, I asked her, was the most surprising thing she learned over the course of her research?

“Well, I’ll be honest, everything surprised me,” Kuehn told me. “And every time I learned something, it was one more tragedy after the next. But I mean—I think Mark would say this too—when I saw \[my uncle\] Leopold in his Nazi uniform…you know, everything else was just words on paper, all the research we did. But when you saw the pictures, it just came to life.”

The letter from Hollywood in 1994 jump-started the project, but there was an earlier moment when Christine and Mark had some suspicions. He recalls:

> The mystery really started back in 1987. We were both in Columbia, South Carolina, and her dad called Chrissy and said, ‘Hey, do want to meet your Aunt Ruth?’ And her dad had always been very secretive about that side of the family. So she was thrilled. She’s like, ‘Yeah, well, let’s go.’
> 
> So we hopped in the car, drove two hours to Charleston, where Ruth lived with her husband in a retirement community. We get to the door, and it’s a very nice one-bedroom apartment. We walked in and met Ruth and she was…probably more frail and probably older looking than we had expected, but very sweet. You know, just kind of like your sweet little grandmother.
> 
> So we sit down and have a very nice discussion for half an hour, an hour or so. And most of the questions are, Ruth wanted to find out about Chrissy and her dad, who was her baby brother.
> 
> And toward the end, Chrissy sees a picture on the table next to Ruth, and it’s this old wedding photo of this good-looking couple. And she says, “Are those my grandparents?” And Ruth just nodded, just nodded yes. So Chrissy said, “Well, can you tell me something about them? My dad says nothing about your side of the family. I’d really like to know more.”
> 
> Ruth just shook her head no, added nothing to it. So Chrissy pushed her and was like, “Please, tell me something, because I’d really love to know more about the German side. It’s really interesting.”
> 
> Ruth cut her off finally and kind of stiffened and pointed her finger at her and said, “Don’t ask any more questions. You have a good life. You don’t want to ruin it with the past. Don’t ask me any more about the family, your grandparents, or Pearl Harbor.”
> 
> Pearl Harbor? What? So that started the whole mystery.

Later, they learned that her sweet old Aunt Ruth had her very own entry—highly sensationalized and not quite true—in a book about The Greatest Woman Spies of All Time.

In lesser hands, *Family of Spies* would have turned out differently. Although her father, Eberhard, was objectively a good person and true hero—so much so that it makes me tear up thinking about it; I absolutely love the guy—Christine resists the urge to lionize him. Nor does she present the material like it’s a series of cover stories for the *New York Post*; there’s nothing sensationalized here. There is just enough history woven into the narrative to understand the context—and even though I’ve done a ton of reading over the last year about the rise of the Nazis, I still learned a lot. We also get snippets about the Serbian spy Ian Fleming handled who was the inspiration for James Bond; a job interview to be head of the Gestapo; Shirley Temple on the beach; and a dutiful FBI agent in hot pursuit of the enemy spies (in exactly the way Kash Patel would not be).

But what really makes the book sing is the emotional aspect. What do you *do* with the knowledge that your grandparents were not just working for the bad guys, but were complicit, albeit in a small way, in the deadliest foreign attack on U.S. soil until 9/11? *Family of Spies*, as Schiponi succinctly puts it, is “the story about the only person ever tried and convicted for the bombing of Pearl Harbor.” How do you process that person being your grandfather? Does it change how you feel about your father? Does it change how you feel about yourself? And, more urgently: will it change how other people feel about *you*?

Another way the book could have gone sideways is if she’d made it all about *her*. Kuehn doesn’t fall into that trap. Both in her writing and in conversation, her empathy shines through. She applies that empathy to all the characters in the book, but doesn’t insert herself into the narrative when it doesn’t belong. On the contrary, Schiponi said that she had to be coaxed by her editor to put more of her insights into the book.

“I feel like it’s been a journey of personal reckoning, and coming to terms with your family’s past,” Kuehn tells me. “Because I look at it and I feel like it was a secret, but this is a story that belongs to history, right? It’s not just my story. And I felt like it deserved to be part of the bigger picture.”

We are all, ultimately, our own people, individual and distinct, free to make our own choices and form our own opinions. But our parents inform who we are, for better or worse—for better, in Kuehn’s case.

“I am definitely a result of my dad,” she says. “I mean, I think about the fact that he walked away from his family—I’m telling a little bit here; spoiler alert—and he never saw them again. But because of what he did, he created a life for me that I wouldn’t have had.”

How much, I wonder, does our familial inheritance inform who we are?

Christine Kuehn’s paternal grandparents were Nazi spies; her grandmother and uncle, especially, were true believers in Hitler’s movement. She turned out to be a lovely, caring, empathic human being, like her father.

Jared Kushner’s grandfather [was a Holocaust survivor; his grandmother was a hero of the Jewish resistance](https://gregolear.substack.com/p/shame-cometh-the-jared-kushner-story). He turned out to be a staunch supporter of and counselor to the evil, Hitlerian wannabe strongman who is now our president—a relationship he’s profited upon shamefully, while turning a blind eye to all the woe his father-in-law has wrought, in the country and around the world.

Stephen Miller’s ancestors emigrated to the United States from the Pale of Settlement to escape the violent anti-Jewish pogroms there. His great-great-grandfather Wolf-Leib “came to the U.S. just a few years before the fear and prejudice of the ‘America first’ nativists of the day closed U.S. borders to Jewish refugees,” [his uncle explained back in 2018](https://www.politico.com/magazine/story/2018/08/13/stephen-miller-is-an-immigration-hypocrite-i-know-because-im-his-uncle-219351/). “Had Wolf-Leib waited, his family likely would have been murdered by the Nazis along with all but seven of the 2,000 Jews who remained in Antopol.” Miller turned out to be the author of Trump’s racist anti-immigration policies, and the spiritual father of the ICE Gestapo. An American Nazi, in other words.

How does that happen? What makes some of us turn out like Christine Kuehn, and others like Jared Kushner or Stephen Miller? Why do some people go Nazi, and others do not? Is it [generational trauma](https://www.apa.org/monitor/2019/02/legacy-trauma) manifesting itself in radically different ways? Is it some dark ratio of nature to nurture? Too much Red Dye No. 3?

“Secrets eat love like acid,” Kuehn writes. “But love regenerates.”

Let’s hope, for the sake of humanity, that the same is not true of hate.

**The German Spies at Pearl Harbor? Those Were My Grandparents (with Chris­tine Kuehn & Mark Schiponi)**

What happens when you discover that the reason your father never speaks about his parents or his childhood growing up in Hawaii is because his mother, father, and older sister were Nazi spies? And not just any Nazi spies—the spies sent by Berlin to help the Japanese surveil Pearl Harbor.

Chris­tine Kuehn was living a qui­et sub­ur­ban life when a mys­te­ri­ous let­ter in 1994 pierced that bub­ble, send­ing her on a thir­ty-year quest to dis­cov­er the truth behind a hor­ren­dous fam­i­ly secret kept hid­den for half a cen­tu­ry. The result is the new book, FAMILY OF SPIES. Fol­low­ing a career in jour­nal­ism, pub­lic rela­tions, and non­prof­its, Chris­tine now lives in Mary­land with her hus­band, Mark Schiponi, who helped her research the book for 30 years.

In this conversation, Greg Olear talks to the couple about how the book came to be, the long and unusual research process, and the emotional impact of learning the truth.
