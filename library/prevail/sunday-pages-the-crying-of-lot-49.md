Dear Reader,

When I was in my early twenties, a novelist on the make, there were three writers who, for me, comprised the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost of literary fiction—not so much for their output, but for their technical brilliance. Writing is about writing, after all, and no one else *wrote* better, on a granular level, than these three.

This is all subjective, I realize, and of course there are plethoras of talented writers unknown to me 30 years ago, or not yet operational, who are just as good. But as I saw it back in the 90s, no one constructed sentences of greater beauty than the Holy Trinity of Vladimir Nabokov, J.D. Salinger, and Thomas Pynchon.

Together, they cover three generations: respectively, the Lost (b. 1899), Greatest (b. New Year’s Day 1919), and Silent (b. 1937). And all three are loosely related. Pynchon took Nabokov’s writing class at Cornell and, owing to his reclusiveness—which is not reclusiveness as much as a reluctance to have his photo taken—was thought, by at least one conspiracy theorist of Alex Jonesian imagination, to *be* Salinger.

None of these connections amounted to much. Nabokov gave Salinger a great review but did not remember Pynchon from his lectures; Pynchon was not, *is* not, Salinger.

In the early 90s, when the collective personality of “Generation X” was still being formed, what we most wanted was to write a novel that some esteemed Boomer book critic would call “the *Catcher in the Rye* for this generation.” There were a few such Gen X *Catchers in the Rye* already in circulation by then: *Less Than Zero; Bright Lights, Big City; Slaves of New York; Generation X* itself. When I was young and stupid—as opposed to middle-aged and slightly less stupid—*that* was the literary moon I was shooting for.

My first (and blessedly unpublished) novel was called *My Brain Is Full*; the title is derived from a *Far Side* comic strip. I wrote it my senior year of college. There was a lot of senior-year-of-college stuff going on, but the plot, such as it was, involved an aspiring young novelist-in-training making a pilgrimage to Cornish, New Hampshire, to the home of the (genuinely) reclusive Salinger. Much like an Athenian of old approaching the Oracle at Delphi, our youthful first-person narrator hoped to find wisdom at the great writer’s remote, rustic farmhouse. What he found instead was an old man, half deaf, chattering dully with an old woman, more interested in watching “Wheel of Fortune” than explicating the Meaning of Life. The theme: no one knows everything; we have to acquire wisdom on our own. (Or, if you like: it isn’t seeing J.D. Salinger that matters; it’s all the friends we meet along the way!)

I have been writing these “Sunday Pages” for almost six years, and, until today, have not written about any of the Big Three. Part of that is literary intimidation, for sure. I have nothing to fear from Nabokov, whose pale fire extinguished in 1977, or Salinger, whose perfect day for bananafish came on January 27, 2010. But by the grace of Thalia, Melpomene, and Calliope, Pynchon is still with us. Which means he might read this! (That does happen every once in a while, most recently when William Gibson retweeted [my piece about](https://gregolear.substack.com/p/sunday-pages-neuromancer?utm_source=publication-search) *[Neuromancer](https://gregolear.substack.com/p/sunday-pages-neuromancer?utm_source=publication-search)* and I had a minor heart attack.)

Time was, we all *wanted* Pynchon to read what we wrote. He might blurb it—like when he wrote the liner notes for that Lotion album. Plus, he was for a long time married to the powerhouse literary agent Melanie Jackson. He might blurb one’s manuscript *and* pass it along to his wife, who could secure a fat book deal! We should be so lucky! These days, I am perfectly content with Thomas Pynchon having no idea who I am, just as I would have no idea who he was, were he to plop down in the next booth at the diner.

The other reason I have eschewed the Holy Trinity is because two of them are…*problematic*. Salinger’s obvious obsession with teenage girls, in print and in person, is hard to reconcile. Put it this way: if he were born in 1949 instead of 1919, he may well have been among the disgraced men photographed in the company of Jeffrey Epstein—although of course we wouldn’t recognize Salinger, because: recluse. As for Nabokov, he created, in Humbert Humbert, the exact sort of sick perv who used to hang out at Zorro Ranch and Little St. James. *Lolita* is a masterful novel, but I don’t have the energy right now to explain why—not with the extreme care such an effort would require, in the Year of Our Lord 2025.

And Pynchon? Until this weekend, I had no “take.” A lot of his stuff, frankly, is over my head. I’ve read his rollicking 1963 debut novel, *V.*, twice, from cover to cover. I’m still not entirely sure what’s going on. (My joke used to be that the eponymous “V.” the characters are half-assedly searching for is actually a plot.) *Gravity’s Rainbow,* published ten full years later, is his signature work—a dense, brilliant, funny, profound, and super weird novel set during the Second World War, and involving too many sillily-named characters to keep straight. Twice I have tried to read it, and twice I have failed. One day, when my brain is not full of this Trump stuff, I will finish the damned thing and fully appreciate its genius. (Pynchon is, I should add, beyond brilliant.)

\---

In between those two behemoths, Pynchon cranked out a short novel, almost a novella, called *The Crying of Lot 49*. I love it. Other than *The Great Gatsby* and *The Sun Also Rises*, this is the book I have read the most times. I still don’t fully understand what’s going on—but then, neither does the protagonist, one Oedipa Maas, a California housewife, registered Republican, psychotherapy patient, and something of a lush.

*Lot 49* is a paranoid adventure, a sort of Dark Night of the Soul that goes on for weeks and weeks. It involves a dead multi-millionaire, a conspiracy involving rival postal services, a nerdish counterculture, a child actor all grown up, a thought experiment debunking the second law of thermodynamics, rare stamps trollishly defaced, swastika armband manufacturers, Jacobean revenge plays, obscure used books, Nazi psychotherapists, disc jockeys and knockoff Beatles bands with a yen for “beautiful women…many of them on the younger side,” gay bars, a symbol of a muted postal horn, physics, engineering—and those are just the highlights.

The whole vibe of *Lot 49* is contained in its opening sentence:

> One summer afternoon Mrs Oedipa Maas came home from a Tupperware party whose hostess had put perhaps too much kirsch in the fondue to find that she, Oedipa, had been named executor, or she supposed executrix, of the estate of one Pierce Inverarity, a California real estate mogul who had once lost two million dollars in his spare time, but still had assets numerous and tangled enough to make the job of sorting it all out more than honorary.

From this we get a taste of the era (Tupperware parties, fondue, and kirsch being vestiges of the middle 60s), the setting (California, the summer), the sobering jolt of unexpected news (she is loopy on kirsch, after all—a clear liqueur made from sour cherries), her marital status (Mrs), a little gender confusion (executor/executrix), the personality of the dead guy (extremely wealthy, reckless, unmarried, and careless enough to designate another man’s wife to sort out his estate), and a hint at the plot (sorting out said estate).

There are also—and this, I think, is important, when you’re writing a novel, and therefore asking a person you don’t know to spend a few hours in your literary company—appeals to the reader’s curiosity: questions we want answered. What is the relationship between the housewife and the dead millionaire? What will she, Oedipa, get *herself* from the estate? How did Pierce Inverarity die? And: who the fuck is named Oedipa Maas[1](#footnote-1) or Pierce Inverarity?[2](#footnote-2) Even for California in 1964, those are far-out names.

And as we make our way through the book, we encounter other names as oddball as the characters: Genghis Cohen, Mike Fallopian, Emory Bortz, Randolph Driblette, Dr. Hilarius; a law firm called Warpe, Wistfull, Kubitschek and McMingus; a town called St. Narcisso; a gay bar in San Francisco called The Greek Way. (“Thurn and Taxis” is also an oddball name—but that one is real, as we will discuss later.) These are absurd, not names that anybody in real life would have.

But then we look at real life in America, eleven months into the Trump Redux. How many names in the MAGA-verse would feel right at home in the universe of *Lot 49*? Elon Musk, Rex Tillerson, Steve Mnuchin, Lee Zeldin, Peter Thiel, Laura Loomer, even good guy Reality Winner. And none of Pynchon’s nine novels contain a single moniker more Pynchonesque than Reince Priebus.

Out-there, too, is the plot. Oedipa sets about fulfilling her duties as executrix. Inverarity, who was once her lover, was a practical joker, and very wealthy, and plugged in in ways she doesn’t understand. He was also a philatelist, with an extensive stamp collection,

> his substitute often for her—thousands of little colored windows into deep vistas of space and thyme: savannahs teaming with elands and gazelles, galleons sailing west into the void, Hitler heads, sunsets, cedars of Lebanon, allegorical faces that never were, he could spend hours peering into each one, ignoring her. She had never seen the fascination. The thought that now it would all have to be inventoried and appraised was only another headache. No suspicion at all that it might have something to tell her. Yet, if she hadn’t been set up or sensitized, first by her peculiar seduction, then by the other, almost offhand things, what after all could the mute stamps have told her, remaining then as they would’ve only ex-rivals, cheated as she by death, about to be broken up into lots, on route to any number of new masters?

Making her rounds, Oed notices a symbol—a muted postal horn—that she then starts seeing all over the place. She encounters a stamp, a blue 4¢ Abe Lincoln issue, but the word “POSTAGE” is rendered “POTSAGE”—a sly weed joke. A troll. A proto-meme, even. This leads her down a labyrinth of weirdness involving the official postal service—the one run by the Thurn and Taxis family for centuries—and the underground one, W.A.S.T.E., run by a person or people called Tristero. At first it’s curious. Then it’s weird. Then it’s sinister and a bit dangerous—or so she comes to believe.

Is Oed imaging the whole thing? Is she going mad? Or, as Pynchon puts it, in the final chapter,

> Either Oedipa in the orbiting ecstasy of a true paranoia, or a real Tristero. For there either was some Tristero beyond the appearance of the legacy America, or there was just America and if there was just America then it seemed the only way she could continue, and managed to be at all relevant to it, was as an alien, unframed, assumed full circle into some paranoia.

If I tried to describe the full plot, in all of its kooky details, you would say, “That makes no sense! Why would anyone write a book about that?” But then we recall another plot, this one of a terrible show now in its ninth season: a New York real estate mogul, a creature of organized crime, after running afoul of the Russians, is co-opted by the Kremlin; sold to the American people as a successful businessman because of a reality show that portrays him as such; is installed in the White House by dint of an insidious social media campaign that demonizes his opponent; meets his Russian overlord in Helsinki, where he cowers before him despite being much taller; denies being a puppet; maintains the support of the American people because of “fake news;” botches the response to the first *bona fide* plague we’re had in 100 years; is voted out; leads an attack on the Capitol to remain in place, fails, and reluctantly leaves; is indicted four times in four jurisdictions only to have three of the cases get tossed, because the state prosecutor hired her sidepiece, and the special counsel brought the cases in Florida, where they are kiboshed by a corrupt judge no one bothers to investigate; is found guilty in the fourth, but serves no prison time; loses several cases in civil court so owes hundreds of millions of dollars, doesn’t pay; pretends to be injured in an assassination attempt; sells merch based on the images from the assassination attempt; wins election soon after, because his original opponent had a senior moment on national TV and America is racist and sexist; immediately forms a secret state police to deport residents and eventually dissidents (we all assume); sabotages our relationship with our allies and resets our foreign policy to align with Moscow’s; plunders from the U.S. taxpayer, issues fake money, accepts lavish gifts from foreign entities including a jumbo jet; invites conspiracy theorists to advise him and maybe even perform fellatio upon him; gins up a fight with Venezuela for some reason; says bat-shit stuff on TV on the regular; shits his pants; uses the full power of the presidency and the DOJ and the FBI to conceal his intimacy with one of the two most notorious child sex traffickers of all time, wishes the other one well and contemplates pardoning her; pardons drug dealers and swindlers and thugs who give him money; fails to properly flip a coin at a football game—and that’s just off the top of my head. Worst of all, *we don’t know how this ends*. No outcome is off the table. Like, *none*. Consider: a leaked sex trafficker email suggested that one president had sucked off another—and we didn’t immediately dismiss that as crazy.

*Lot 49* is a children’s book by comparison!

\---

There are many theories on what *Lot 49* is “about.” I’ve heard that the alternative “W.A.S.T.E.” postal system—established a few centuries hence by the mysterious Tristero organization as a rival to the (real-life) mail-delivery monopoly enjoyed by the Thurn and Taxis family—is a vision of what would become the Internet. I’ve heard it’s about a lonely woman having a nervous breakdown. I’ve heard it’s a bad acid trip. I’ve heard it’s a literary take on the vain attempt to reverse the entropy of the universe—a topic of much fascination to Pynchon, who before conquering the field of literature studied physics and engineering at Cornell.

Maybe it’s because I’ve been down the Jeffrey Epstein rabbit-hole for too long, but for me, the weirdo conspiracy at the heart of *Lot 49* presages the Epstein situation: a conspiracy that keeps getting bigger and bigger, and more and more perplexing, the more I dig into it. *Lot 49* may well describe the murky confusion of the offshore finance system (“assets numerous and tangled”). Or the Epstein sex trafficking network itself—there are, in the book, a few references to pedophilia, and many parallels to the here and now: Nazis, perverts, secret societies, mad real estate developers, science, tech, big money, obscure law firms, conspiracy—even proto-memes, as discussed, in the form of the defaced stamps.

At one point Oed, worried about her physical health, goes to a doctor; lies that she thinks she’s pregnant, so she will get seen. Nothing resolves. As Pynchon writes, in one of those beautiful and poignant sentences, “Your gynecologist has no test for what she was pregnant with.”

Same, same. I am Oedipa, my brain is full, I doubt my own sanity at times, I have a cocktail or two on Friday nights to calm my jangled nerves, I crank out piece after piece after piece about the Epstein madness, about the evil men at the heart of our darkness, and for all my relentless detective work, I am no closer to the truth.

The emails, the photographs, the survivor accounts, the civil suits, the foreign intelligence services, the shell companies, the banks, the lawyers, the models, the scientists, the trafficked girls, the massage tables, the creepy masks on the wall, the bad artwork, the money, the power, the influence: the enduring mystery. Names we know, IRL characters we’ve met—we look at them differently once they know that we know. But *do* we know, really? *What* do we know? What is there *to* know? And: do we even *want* to know? After all, the whole point of *Oedipus Rex*, to which titular king the name of *Lot 49*’s protagonist clearly alludes, is that there are some secrets we’re better off not discovering.

One of the many things I most dig about *Lot 49* is that the title is realized at the very end of the book—**which I am about to reveal, so, you know, SPOILER ALERT**.

Finally there is a way to find the answer to Oedipa’s Sphinxian riddle! The solution will be found in the stamps. Inverarity’s stamps. As we are told in the early chapter previously quoted, the stamp collection will be inventoried, appraised, and sold off in lots. (The same thing happens to coin collections, I happen to know.)

The final scene of the book is the build-up to that auction. There is a mystery bidder. No one, even in the philately community, has any idea who the mystery bidder is. Is it a representative of Tristero, sent to buy up the stamps that are the only hard evidence of the organization’s existence? Or is it only a coincidence? Oedipa will finally, once and for all, find what she’s looking for.

First, we are told what the second word in the title means, by which we can infer the rest:

> “We’re in luck. Loren Passerine, the finest auctioneer in the West, will be crying today.”
> 
> “Will be what?”
> 
> “We say an auctioneer ‘cries’ a sale,” Cohen said.

And then, the chilling last paragraph:

> The men inside the auction room wore black mohair and had pale, cruel faces. They watched her come in, trying each to conceal his thoughts. Loren Passerine, on his podium, hovered like a puppet-master, his eyes bright, his smile practiced and relentless. He stared at her, smiling, as if saying, I’m surprised you actually came. Oedipa sat alone, toward the back of the room, looking at the napes of necks, trying to guess which one was her target, her enemy, perhaps her proof. An assistant closed the heavy door on the lobby windows and the sun. She heard a lock snap shut; the sound echoed a moment. Passerine spread his arms in a gesture that seemed to belong to the priesthood of some remote culture; perhaps to a descending angel. The auctioneer cleared his throat. Oedipa settled back, to await the crying of lot 49.

Will the release of the Epstein files teach us anything? Will we ever find out why Donald Trump is so dead-set against the disclosure of the documents? We don’t know what happens after the crying of lot 49, after all—we’re left hanging. Which nape, on which neck, will belong to the *real* villain of the Trump/Epstein story?

\---

The first time I read *Lot 49*, I assumed all of the kooky historical stuff Pynchon was alluding to was made up, a product of his hyperactive imagination. I remember happening to glance at a TV, many years later, and the anchor was talking about a Prince of Thurn and Taxis—an eight-year-old who’d just come into the largest possible inheritance. The boy was, they said, the youngest living billionaire. That’s when I realized that this was an *actual* House, that Thurn and Taxis really *did* enjoy a monopoly on the Holy Roman Imperial post—and, therefore, a lot of the other stuff Pynchon mentions, which I’d discounted as fiction, is also real. The stamps he describes, for example, can all be easily found in the Scott catalogue (which he also mentions).

The name came up again recently, in my research into Leonard Leo. Dowager Princess Gloria von Thurn und Taxis, in her Stuttgart youth a punk chick known in the tabloids as “Princess TNT,” married into the family; later in life she became a staunchly conservative Catholic, got in deep with the Vatican, [hung out with Sam Alito](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/10/22/us/politics/samuel-alito-princess-gloria.html), canoodled with Leo (the dark money guy, not the Pope), and is associated with Steve Bannon. (She’s also, somehow, close friends with Hillary Clinton, because federal law clearly states that every conspiracy has to involve the Clintons.) Remember when Bannon wanted to set up a “gladiator school” in Rome? He was reportedly trying to hit up the Dowager Princess for money to pay for it.

But that’s not all.

Princess Gloria von Thurn und Taxis *is alluded to in the Epstein emails!* Bannon and Epstein are mocking her appearance. One of them openly wonders if she looks like Trump when she wakes up in the morning. So there is an actual, IRL overlap between a royal family in *Lot 49* and a royal family in Epstein World! A tenuous overlap, yes—but an overlap just the same. Maybe this *is* a simulation?

And of course, now that I go back to the Epstein email database and try to find the Thurn and Taxis reference, it seems to have vanished. Poof. But that, too, is of a piece with Pynchon, and Oedipa Maas, and my own take on what W.A.S.T.E. stands for, in the wake of all the Epstein stuff:

*We Await Sick Trump’s Exposure.*
