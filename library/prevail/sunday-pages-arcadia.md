Dear Reader,

In “Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead,” the insanely brilliant 1966 play that put Tom Stoppard on the literary map, the head player of an acting troupe the two titular characters meet on their way to see Prince Hamlet explains that his company is from “more of the blood, love, and rhetoric school.”

When Guildenstern asks them to choose one of the three for the performance, the player says that they are “hardly divisible…I can do you blood and love without the rhetoric, and I can do you blood and rhetoric without the love, and I can do you all three concurrent or consecutive, but I can’t do you love and rhetoric without the blood. Blood is compulsory—they’re all blood, you see.”

Thus does Stoppard broad-brush the entire catalogue of William Shakespeare.

In 1995, I had just graduated from college, found a job in the video library of an advertising agency, and was living for a few months in the Jersey suburbs with my parents. *Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead*, the film version of the play, starring Tim Roth and Gary Oldman and directed by Stoppard, had come out a few years prior, putting the playwright on our radar—“our” being me and my friend Chris, who was also living in my parents’ house while we waited to move to an apartment in Hoboken.

That was the year, and the season, that Stoppard’s new play, “Arcadia,” opened at Lincoln Center. And we must have hung around the city after work one summer day and taken in the show during the week, because [according to](https://playbill.com/production/arcadia-vivian-beaumont-theatre-vault-0000011241#carousel-cell180664) *[Playbill](https://playbill.com/production/arcadia-vivian-beaumont-theatre-vault-0000011241#carousel-cell180664)*[,](https://playbill.com/production/arcadia-vivian-beaumont-theatre-vault-0000011241#carousel-cell180664) the play only ran from March through the end of August 1995—and I definitely saw it.

But then, my memory is faulty. I also recall that the actor playing Septimus Hodge was a dashing young Rufus Sewell. But no less an authority than the World Wide Web has it that, while Sewell originated the role in the London production, he didn’t come to New York; the Broadway production starred an also-dashing Billy Crudup. But why, if I had not seen Rufus Sewell in “Arcadia,” would I remember the name Rufus Sewell, long before Rufus Sewell came back into my consciousness playing the American Nazi in *The Man in the High Castle*? Was there another, subsequent Broadway production of “Arcadia” that Google has memory-holed? But no—I distinctly remember us being interested in seeing Robert Sean Leonard, fresh off his star turn in *Dead Poets Society*, in a stage play. And even though Paul Giamatti had never been in a Broadway play before, let alone in *Sideways* or *Billion*s, I am quite sure that it was him we saw as the cuckolded Mr. Chater. So: Rufus or Crudup, Billy or Sewell? It is a mystery worthy of the play itself, which is a sort of hybrid period piece and low-stakes detective story, concerning, among many other things, who knew what when.

\---

Shakespeare might be all blood, love, and rhetoric, but not Tom Stoppard. There is no blood in “Arcadia,” although there is an unsuccessful duel and death by fire; and the love, such as it is, is *for* rhetoric—and, also, for Lord Byron, Lady Caroline Lamb, Fermat, idyllic rather than Gothic styles of British gardens, chaos theory, the Second Law of Thermodynamics, physics, mathematics, entropy, determinism, and whether a nineteenth-century hermitage did or did not contain a real-life hermit.

Above all, “Arcadia” is about the pursuit of knowledge. Each of the characters seeks to *know more*. The precocious Thomasina—and by extension her brilliant tutor Septimus—wants to know more about why heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold but never the other way around. Hannah, a bestselling author of a book on Lamb, wants to know more about the gardens, and specifically its hermitage, at the lovely Derbyshire country house, in one room of which the entire play takes place. Valentine, a scientist, wants to know more about the patterns of grouse that he is studying, using data in the game books at his family estate. And Bernard, a vain and arrogant academic, wants to know more about a singular event in the short life of Lord Byron: did he or did he not kill Mr. Chater—that is, Paul Giamatti—in a duel?

Writing it all out like that, “Arcadia” seems impossible to pull off. How could all of those disparate ingredients be boiled down into a single novel, let alone a single play, let alone a single play set in the same room that time-jumps between 1809 and now? The simple answer is that Tom Stoppard wrote it, and there’s no literary feat, however Herculean, that man could not perform.

Stoppard died a little over a week ago, on the penultimate day of November. To most people, I suspect, he is best known for writing the Oscar-winning screenplay for *Shakespeare in Love*. In that film, his Stoppardian powers are on full display. There is his enviable facility with turn-of-the-seventeenth-century English, his love of wordplay, his buoyant wit, his fascination with behind-the-scenes stories and of historical mysteries, and, God bless him, his staunch refusal to dumb anything down—even for an American audience.

Only when he died did I realize I knew almost nothing about him. I think I just sort of assumed he was the product of the best possible British education and left it alone. I was surprised to discover that he was born Tomáš Sträussler in Zlín, a small Czech city on the Dřevnice River near the Slovakian border, known for being the headquarters of the Bata shoe company. He was from a small family of non-practicing Jews—which, because he was born in 1937, soon became perilous. Fortunately, his father’s employer, a member of the Bata family who owned the shoe factory, was anti-Nazi, and, reading the German writing on the wall, transferred the company’s Jewish employees to Bata’s factories in other countries, for their safety.

In May of 1939, a few months before his second birthday, Tomáš left with his family for Singapore, which was then still under British control. In 1941, after the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor—so, 84 years ago to the day, give or take—he was evacuated again, this time to Darjeeling, deep enough inside India to keep him safe for the rest of the war. His father, a doctor, volunteered to stay behind in Singapore, to serve in the military medical unit; he died in 1942, under fog-of-war circumstances the family could only guess at for decades.

Tomáš spoke English from a very young age, but like a number of notable masters of the language—Conrad, Nabokov, and Aleksandar Hemon, to name but three—he was not a native speaker. And it was an American pre-school, not a British one, that first educated Tomáš Sträussler, who became—after his mother’s marriage to the British army major Kenneth Stoppard, and after the Americanization of his Czech first name, and after the family’s post-war return to England—Tom Stoppard.

“I fairly often find I’m with people who forget I don’t quite belong in the world we’re in,” he once recalled. “I find I put a foot wrong—it could be pronunciation, an arcane bit of English history—and suddenly I’m there naked, as someone with a pass, a press ticket.” That feeling of not quite belonging, of imposter syndrome, of having a dual persona, informs a lot of his work.

Nor was Stoppard, who seemed so professorial, an academic of any kind. On the contrary, he left school at the age of 17, to go into journalism.

None of this was what I assumed.

\---

“With the single exception of Homer,” the playwright George Bernard Shaw once said, “there is no eminent writer, not even Sir Walter Scott, whom I can despise so entirely as I despise Shakespeare when I measure my mind against his.” Tom Stoppard is one of the writers I feel that way about—although, not being as competitive as Shaw, I feel not despisal but true awe. I find it immensely comforting to know that there are people way smarter than I could ever be.

Stoppard is Shakespearean in that he combines the high and the low to great effect. In “Arcadia,” there are men and women of genius contemplating complex scientific and literary thoughts, and doing so in both the elegant English of George III and the academic patois of the here and now. But there is also plenty of ribaldry.

At rise, 22-year-old Septimus—either the dashing young Rufus Sewell or the dashing young Billy Crudup; choose your tutor—is sitting in the room with his pupil, Thomasina, 13. Both of them are reading; him a long poem, her a mathematics textbook involving Fermat’s last theorem.

This continues for a bit, until she breaks the silence: “Septimus,” she asks, “what is carnal embrace?”

“Carnal embrace,” he shoots back, immediately and authoritatively, “is the practice of throwing one’s arms around a side of beef.”

“Is that all?”

“No… A shoulder of mutton, a hatch of venison well hugged, an embrace of grouse…” Then he grouses, “I thought you were finding a proof for Fermat’s last theorem.”

“It is very difficult, Septimus. You will have to show me how.”

“If I knew how, there would be no need to ask *you*,” he says, not incorrectly. “Fermat’s last theorem has kept people busy for 150 years, and I hoped it would keep you busy long enough for me to read Mr. Chater’s poem in praise of love with only the distraction of its own absurdities.”

Eventually he relents and supplies an honest answer. “Carnal embrace,” he tells her, “is sexual congress, which is the insertion of the male genital organ into the female genital organ for purposes of procreation and pleasure. Fermat’s last theorem, by contrast”—and here, the audience burst into laughter; Stoppard and only Stoppard could write a good joke about Fermat’s last theorem— “asserts that when *x, y* and *z* are whole numbers each raised to the power of *n*, the sum of the first two can never equal the third when *n* is greater than 2.”

The comedy is enhanced because we don’t expect a play set in 1809 to begin this way. But the dialogue also advances the plot. As it turns out, Thomasina has a good reason to ask this question—she overheard one of the servants exclaiming that a Mrs. Chater, the wife of a minor poet (Paul Giamatti!) who is a guest at the estate, and whose terrible book Septimus is now reading, was found “in carnal embrace” in the gazebo. Septimus, meanwhile, is trying very subtly to suss out what exactly she heard, because the man found in carnal embrace with Mrs. Chater is, as you might have surmised, Septimus Hodge.

We also get a feel for the relationship between Thomasina and Septimus; her radiant precocity; his estimation of her considerable genius; a sneaky mention of grouse (which bird comes up later); and, in Fermat’s last theorem, a historical mystery many eminent minds have invested considerable energy trying to solve—a trivial proof, in the grand scheme of things, but so what. All of this conveys the larger themes of “Arcadia,” and we are too busy being amused to even realize it.

Mr. Chater bursts into the room, accusing Septimus of “insulting” his wife in the gazebo. He corrects him: “You are mistaken. I made love to your wife in the gazebo.”

This prompts Chater to challenge him to pistols at dawn. “I demand satisfaction!” he cries. To which Septimus, unperturbed, replies, in true Stoppardian fashion, “Mrs. Chater demanded satisfaction, and now you are demanding satisfaction. I cannot spend my time day and night satisfying the demands of the Chater family.”

More important than the looming duel is Thomasina’s intellectual curiosity. Early in the play, she posits, “If you could stop every atom in its position and direction, and if your mind could comprehend all the actions thus suspended, then if you were really, *really* good at algebra you could write the formula for all the future; and although nobody could be so clever as to do it, the formula must exist just as if one could.”

The idea of a precocious woman in the early 1800s conceiving of original thoughts on physics and the nature of the universe is itself derived from history. Ada Lovelace, Lord Byron’s daughter (the poet had nothing to do with her upbringing), was a brilliant mathematician, so far ahead of her time she is widely regarded as the first computer programmer—even though computers, as we know them, would not be invented for another century and a half.

Lord Byron, too, is central to the play, although he never appears. He is friends with Septimus, we find out. And, in the action set in the present day, he is the obsession of Bernard Nightingale, the annoying academic, who theorizes that Byron killed Mr. Chater in a duel at that very country house—a duel we have already seen hinted at—and *that* is why he fled to the Continent so abruptly in 1809.

In articulating his theory, Bernard comes into conflict with Hannah, who—correctly, as we also find out—thinks Bernard’s theory is bullshit; and with Valentine, the grouse-studying scientist. I still remember, thirty years later, laughing at this bit of dialogue between Bernard, who is propositioning her, and Hannah, who would sooner set herself on fire than hook up with him:

> HANNAH  
> Sex and literature. Literature and sex. Your conversation, left to itself, doesn’t have many places to go. Like two marbles rolling around a pudding basin. One of them is always sex.
> 
> BERNARD  
> Oh well, yes. Men all over.
> 
> HANNAH  
> No doubt. Einstein—relativity and sex. Chippendale—sex and furniture. Galileo— “Did the Earth move?” What the hell is it with you people?

The play’s intellectual concerns are laid out in four short monologues. First, we have Septimus replying to Thomasina’s lament about the collected art and knowledge lost when the library of Alexandria was burned by the Romans. “How can we sleep for grief?” she wants to know.

He tells her:

> By counting our stock. Seven players from Aeschylus, seven from Sophocles, *nineteen* from Euripides, my lady! You should know more grieve for the rest than for a buckle lost from your first shoe, or for your lesson book which will be lost when you were old. We shed as we pick up, like travelers who must carry everything in their arms, and what we let fall will be picked up by those behind. The procession is very long and life is very short. We die on the march. But there is nothing outside the march so nothing could be lost to it. The missing plays of Sophocles will turn up piece by piece, or be written again in another language. Ancient cures for diseases will reveal themselves once more. Mathematical discoveries glimpsed and lost to view will have their time again. You do not suppose, my lady, that if all of Archimedes have been hiding in the great library of Alexandria, we would be at a loss for a corkscrew?

Into that monologue, Stoppard sneaks in the reference to her lesson book—which, as it turns out, will very much *not* be lost.

Then we have Valentine, exclaiming on the insufficiency of both the Newtonian laws of physics and Einstein’s theory of relativity to explain the universe—and why that is so exciting for a scientist:

> The unpredictable and the predetermined unfold together to make everything the way it is. It’s how nature creates itself, on every scale, the snowflake and the snowstorm. It makes me so happy. To be at the beginning again, knowing almost nothing. People were talking about the end of physics. Relativity and quantum looked as if they were going to clean out the whole problem between them. A theory of everything. But they only explained the very big and the very small. The universe, the elementary particles. The ordinary-sized stuff, which is our lives, the things people write poetry about—clouds— daffodils—waterfalls—and what happens in a cup of coffee when the cream goes in—these things are full of mystery, as mysterious to us as the heavens were to the Greeks. We are better at predicting events at the edge of the galaxy or inside the nucleus of an atom than whether it’ll rain on auntie’s garden party three Sundays from now. Because the problem turns out to be different. We can’t even predict the next drip from a dripping tap when it gets irregular. Each drip sets up the conditions for the next, the smallest variation blows prediction apart, and the weather is unpredictable the same way, will always be unpredictable. When you push the numbers through the computer, you can see it on the screen. The future is disorder. A door like this has cracked open five or six times since we got up on our hind legs. It’s the best possible time to be alive, when almost everything you thought you knew is wrong.

Bernard takes umbrage at Valentine’s not-intended-to-be-judgmental suggestion that Byron is “trivial,” and that scientific progress is more important than what Valentine dismisses as “personalities.” It doesn’t matter if Newton or Leibnitz discovered calculus, he asserts, only that calculus was discovered. To which the Byron scholar retorts:

> Oh, you’re going to zap me with penicillin and pesticides. Spare me that and I’ll spare you the bomb and aerosols. But don’t confuse progress with perfectibility. A great poet is always timely. A great philosopher is an urgent need. There’s no rush for Isaac Newton. We were quite happy with Aristotle‘s cosmos. Personally I preferred it. Fifty-five crystal spheres geared to God‘s crankshaft is my idea of a satisfying universe. I can’t think of anything more trivial than the speed of light. Quarks, quazars—big bangs, black holes—who gives a shit?

And, a few lines later:

> If knowledge isn’t self-knowledge, it isn’t doing much, mate. Is the universe expanding? Is it contracting? Is it standing on one leg and singing ‘When Father Painted the Parlor’? Leave me out. I can expand my universe without you. ‘She walks in beauty, like the night of clouds, climes and starry skies, and all that’s best of dark and bright meet in her aspect and her eyes.’ There you are, he wrote it after coming home from a party.

Finally, Hannah’s aghast response to Valentine’s suggestion that, upon death, Bernard and Lord Byron would confab in the afterlife and settle their differences:

> It’s *all* trivial—your grouse, my hermit, Bernard’s Byron. Comparing what we’re looking for misses the point. It’s wanting to know that makes us matter. Otherwise, we’re going out the way we came in. That’s why you can’t believe in the afterlife, Valentine. Believe in the after, by all means, but not the life. Believe in God, the soul, the spirit, the infinite, believe in angels if you like, but not in the great celestial get-together for an exchange of views. If the answers are in the back of the book I can wait, but what a drag. Better to struggle on knowing that failure is final.

\---

Speaking of the finality of failure, I’m quite sure that, despite my liberal insertion of Stoppard quotes, I have failed to adequately convey the genius of “Arcadia.” It’s a difficult play to describe. And while it is generally held in high regard, not everyone feels as strongly about it as I do.

Lloyd Rose, the theater critic for the *Washington Post*, [was left cold by the play](https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-srv/local/longterm/theater/reviews/arcadia.htm), which he saw in 1996 in DC. “Stoppard’s wit and erudition are as impressive as ever. He links chaos theory with sexual desire. He uses the two time frames to make ironic jokes about historical interpretation. And his optimism is invigorating: He seems to regard curiosity as one of the virtues. But his facility undermines him here. He does too much too well, and the result is that he does nothing wonderfully,” Rose writes. “For all its surface brilliance, ‘Arcadia’ lacks passion and urgency…. \[T\]here are scenes in ‘Arcadia’ that you could get just as much out of from reading as from seeing acted, no matter how well.”

I disagree. There’s something emotionally magical about learning something new—about having one’s intellectual curiosity pay off. For me, at least, it warms my heart. I can *feel* it, in my very bones. Bernard is a pompous asshole, but when he quotes the Byron poem and explains how those lines were dashed off after a party, all of his pretensions fall away, and we glimpse, however fleetingly, the awe he feels when engaging with poetical genius.

There is great emotional power in discovery. I am just as moved at Thomasina stumbling upon, like Leif Erickson *vis à vis* Columbus, the Second Law of Thermodynamics decades before anyone else published it, as I am by the love between her and Septimus (which becomes romantic later, after she comes of age).

What I didn’t know in 1995, because the World Wide Web was still called the World Wide Web, with three capital W’s, and was still in the larval stages, is what the title of the play refers to. The full Latin, which Septimus, Thomasina, and Thomasina’s mother all take turns translating during the play, is “*Et in Arcadio Ego,*” or, “Even in Arcadia”—that is, the perfect idyll—“there I am,” with the “I” implicitly referencing the Grim Reaper. It is a *memento mori*, a reminder that even in the loveliest of clouds, climes, and starry skies, Death is never far away.

Thomasina dies in a fire on the eve of her seventeenth birthday—and we don’t see her die, but we put together how it happens. Almost two centuries later, the maths in her lesson book are plugged into the computer by Valentine, who is blown away by what he sees—and her work helps him in his study of the grouse. The real-life hermit in the garden turns out to be Septimus Hodge. Lord Byron didn’t kill Mr. Chater in a duel. And what’s delightful is that none of the characters know *all* of this—but *we* do.

The British mathematician Andrew Wiles solved Fermat’s last theorem in 1995, publishing his proof at the same time “Arcadia” opened in London.

Rosencrantz and Guildenstern are dead, and so are Lord Byron and Tom Stoppard, but all of them will live forever. Or, [as Shakespeare put it](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poems/45087/sonnet-18-shall-i-compare-thee-to-a-summers-day)—better than I ever could, as is his wont—“So long as men can breathe or eyes can see / So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.”

Everything is trivial—but nothing is.

Curiosity *is* one of the virtues.

And *wanting to know* really does make us matter.

At the end of the day, as at the end of the play, it’s all we have.
