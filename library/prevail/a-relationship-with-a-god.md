**“What a Friend we have in Jesus”**

In the year 1855, in a town in Ireland, lay a woman terribly sick. She had a son living across the globe in Canada, his name was Joseph Scriven. Joseph was a writer and conveyed his feelings in the art of poetry. Thus, in the form of communication that he knew best, he wrote his mother a poem meant to comfort in her dire circumstance. This story may not sound familiar but the text of this poem will be recognized by millions of Christians around the world. Joseph wrote:

> What a Friend we have in Jesus, All our sins and griefs to bear! What a privilege to carry everything to God in prayer!

The poem became the lyrics to the hymn, “What a friend we have in Jesus”. This hymn has become one of the most loved of all time and has been sung millions of times in countless congregations. Versions of the song are sung by Alan Jackson, Aretha Franklin, Al Green, just to name a few. This brings a question to my mind: why do we love this little poem? why is it that we are drawn so heavily to the notion of Christ as a friend?

While serving as a missionary in west Texas and east New Mexico I had the great opportunity to meet members of various different religions. I would often hear — in one form or another — the testimony of faithful Christians, “Jesus is my best friend”. This concept was not foreign to me, but it invariably caused me to ponder on its meaning. Did I feel that way? Are they saying that because it sounds ‘nice’? In answering these questions for myself I felt that I was missing out on a further intimate relationship with the Savior. This led me to reflect on the topic and to wrestle with the how of a relationship with God. I craved that the words that Joseph Scriven wrote many years ago would become a part of my soul, I desired to blazon with boldness my relationship with the Savior to anyone that questions pronouncing, “Oh, what a friend \[I\] have in Jesus”.

### **The Creation & Our Relationship to God**

In pursuing my study on relationship development with Diety I decided that the beginning was a fitting place to commence. Where better to learn of our relationship with God than the creation of the world? Michael A. Goodman too had this idea when writing the article entitled “The Creation: An Introduction to Our Relationship to God”. Goodman does a splendid job of illustrating the conversations between Moses, Enoch, Abraham, and Jehovah in a way that demonstrates how Jesus Christ is associated with us. Goodman demonstrates the manner whereby God introduces (1) us to Him and (2) us to ourselves. Goodman cites Alma 18:32 which states, “\[God\] looketh down upon all the children of men; and he knows all the thoughts and intents of the heart; for by his hand were they all created from the beginning.” This verse allowed my mind to extend its understanding.

I produce art: I love to design, draw, build, and more. When I create something with my hands and mind: I form a connection or a relationship to the art. It is a beautiful exprience that produces a bond. I would be convinced in saying that the things we create will be significant to us. The added time and effort we put into our creation the stronger our relationship will be with the created. A further example of this concept is in the formulation of the child within the mother. The mother develops a powerful bond with the baby. Her body is tasked to create in months of development and growth. In the first moments of human contact between mother and child, it is clear that a solid, lasting relationship has been instituted.

In this sense, we may see deeper into our relationship with the Savior. Think of the time that he spent creating us and nurturing us. Think of the time he spent suffering with us. That amount of effort and care has produced within Him a deep relationship founded on creation. He is a part of our history whether we recognize it or not.

Christ compares Himself to us in many styles of relationship: a mother (the hen and her chicks), a father (our spiritual father), a literal spiritual brother, and a husband/wife (the bridegroom). In the story of creation, we not only observe Christ as a father and mother, but He introduces Himself as a parent (Moses 1:4). Personally, this may be a roadblock in imagining Christ as a ‘friend’. Do the questions present themselves: is Christ to be my parent or my peer? Am I thinking of the word friend inaccurately? Or perhaps we can have more than one relationship with the being known as Jesus Christ? Let’s keep investigating.

### **Historical Examples of Personal Relationships**

The scriptures provide many role models and testimonies of men and women who professed to have personal relationships with the Savior. I would like to touch on but three of these examples: the Brother of Jared, Moroni, and Elder Bruce R. McConkie. These men are from three separate dispensations and three completely different situations, yet they have a major element in common — a strong testimony of the personability and charisma of our Lord and Savior.

***Brother of Jared (Ether 1–3)***

In the time of the Tower of Babel lived a man named Jared and his brother, the Brother of Jared. Jared was a man who was “highly favored of the Lord” (1:34) and his family depended on him to communicate with God. Their family needed the Lord’s help because the people due to wicked people and had no map. Long story short, the Brother of Jared and his family are told to flee from their homes in order to find a “choice” land by the hand of the Lord (1:42).

The Brother of Jared is tasked with developing the mode of transportation. He inquires of the Lord and has an experience that is one of a miraculous recount. In conversing with the Lord the Brother of Jared has a powerful conversation with the Savior and ends up seeing His figure. This causes fear and amazement on the part of the Brother of Jared. The Lord speaks to him as one man speaks to another. This is where I want to focus, I was surprised in examining how the Lord conversed with the Brother of Jared. Christ is all-powerful and all-knowing. Yet, He took the time to have a two-way conversation with a mortal. He asks the Brother of Jared what he thinks would be a good solution to the problems that he finds himself in. He responds well and offers some insight and advice from His point of view, but ultimately it is the Brother of Jared who devises a plan that solves his problems, and the Lord steps in to help just as any good friend would. This revealed to me that our relationship with God is not a one-way street. It is a conversation, and we have just as much to input into what is said. This is evident in prayer, it is always the mortal who initiates the conversation with the deity. How amazing that the Creator of the Cosmos is listening to what it is we have to say and hears our ideas and solutions with an open mind and warm response.

***Moroni (Ether 12)***

Next, we push forward a thousand years or so to Ancient America to observe the conversation between the Lord and a prophet named Moroni. In the book of Ether chapter 12, Moroni finds himself with problems, however, these problems are less physical/analytical (as in the Brother of Jared’s case) and more emotional. Moroni is struggling with his self-worth and perceived inadequacy. Moroni is a great man and goes to the Lord for guidance on what to do. the Lord interacts with Moroni not in a ‘holier than though’ manner, but rather with charity and encouragement. Christ never interrupts Moroni in expressing his concerns but allows him to fully speak his mind. The Lord provides both comfort and counsel to Moroni in learning about human weakness and instructs him on how to view it. This is a fantastic set of highly personal scriptures.

This expands further on the relationship of (wo)man and God. Christ will listen to, and be there for our needs. He will not turn away, nor leave us ‘high and dry’. He will figuratively take a knee to look us in our eyes, analyze our circumstances, and strengthen our weary souls.

*Bruce R. McConkie (Bruce R. McConkie’s Final Testimony)*

Now we study a latter-day Apostle of the Lord. Bruce R. McConkie was a scholar and a seasoned disciple of our Lord. In his final years, Elder McConkie wrote a Hymn that has become a favorite of many, titled “I Believe in Christ”. The final two verses I particularly enjoy. They demonstrate the level of relationship that this Apostle developed with the Lord.

> I believe in Christ; he stands supreme! From him I’ll gain my fondest dream; And while I strive through grief and pain, His voice is heard: “Ye shall obtain.” I believe in Christ, so come what may, With him I’ll stand in that great day When on this earth he comes again To rule among the sons of men.

In these words, you feel the depth. You can see how the Lord was beside him. You feel the yearning to be close to the Lord as though with a close relative unseen in a long while. Concluding, you see clearly Bro. McConkie standing next to the Savior as a follower and friend.

Elder McConkie’s son, Joseph Fielding McConkie (a scholar in his own right) wrote a piece on this “final testimony” of Elder McConkie entitled, “Bruce R. McConkie’s Final Testimony”. It further illustrates the relationship that Elder McConkie had with the Lord. Joseph McConkie explained that his father was seriously ill prior to an opportunity to speak at a General Conference session for the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints. Elder McConkie had prepared a powerful address and wanted to bear a special witness of Christ. His family prayed and fasted for health enough to give this talk, and with their faith he was able to. Listen to these words spoken at that very conference,

> And now, as pertaining to this perfect atonement, wrought by the shedding of the blood of God — I testify that it took place in Gethsemane and at Golgotha, and as pertaining to Jesus Christ, I testify that he is the Son of the Living God and was crucified for the sins of the world. He is our Lord, our God, and our King. This I know of myself independent of any other person. I am one of his witnesses, and in a coming day I shall feel the nail marks in his hands and in his feet and shall wet his feet with my tears. But I shall not know any better then than I know now that he is God’s Almighty Son, that he is our Savior and Redeemer, and that salvation comes in and through his atoning blood and in no other way.

A beautiful conclusion to a lifelong relationship with the Savior. This testimony was born out of experimentation on the principles and doctrines found in the Holy Scriptures. We too have these resources and can learn for ourselves who the Savior is, and how he relates to us.

**Guidance from Church Leaders on the Subject**

Our relationship with God has been discussed by many prophets and apostles in the past 200 years. I will highlight quotes from three Latter-Day Saints (Elder Jeffery R. Holland, Elder James E. Faust, and President John Taylor) and discuss what I can learn from each.

I will begin with James E. Faust. In 1976 he gave a discourse entitled “A Personal Relationship with the Savior”. The quote I pulled from this reading states this, “We may not feel a closeness with Him because we think of Him as being far away, or our relationship may not be sanctifying because we do not think of Him as a real person… As we pray, we should think of Him as being close by, full of knowledge, understanding, love, and compassion, the essence of power, and as having great expectations of each of us”. This lesson from Elder Faust taught me to focus on perspective. We would benefit from taking a few moments to ponder on how we perceive Christ in our lives. Do we see Him close enough in relation to us that there is a possibility of an intimate relationship? Asking questions like this can open our minds to stumbling blocks we place in our path to seeing the Savior as He truly is.

Secondly, Elder Jeffery R. Holland gave a devotional address to a group of BYU students in 2000 titled, “How Do I Love Thee”. This talk is specifically about the relationship between a husband and wife, however, it can be applied clearly to our ‘spouse-like’ relationship with the Savior. Here is a paragraph from his remarks:

> The prophets tell us that true love “beareth all things, believeth all things, hopeth all things, endureth all things” (1 Corinthians 13:7). Once again that is ultimately a description of Christ’s love — He is the great example of one who bore and believed and hoped and endured. We are invited to do the same in our courtship and in our marriage to the best of our ability. Bear up and be strong. Be hopeful and believing. Some things in life we have little or no control over. These have to be endured. Some disappointments have to be lived with in love and in marriage. These are not things anyone wants in life, but sometimes they come. And when they come, we have to bear them; we have to believe; we have to hope for an end to such sorrows and difficulty; we have to endure until things come right in the end … One of the great purposes of true love is to help each other in these times. No one ought to have to face such trials alone. We can endure almost anything if we have someone at our side who truly loves us, who is easing the burden and lightening the load.

Christ is the perfect example to us of love within a relationship. The word that stands out to me after reading this text from Elder Holland is patience. In building our relationship with Christ we should remember this principle. Christ will have perfect patience with us and we should try our best to have patience with ourselves and with Him in deepening our relationship.

Finally, I searched the early days of the Church and found a principle taught by President John Taylor on the subject of relationships with God. In the book, The Teachings of Presidents of the Church: John Taylor we read this beautiful conclusion,

> There is not a man upon the earth that has put his trust in God, I do not care what part of the world he has been in, but what can say that he delivered him. I know that has been the case with me, emphatically so. I have been satisfied, when in foreign lands and in strange countries, where I had no access but to the Almighty, that he was on my side, and I know that he has answered my prayers.

How beautiful that we can trust in our friend, the Savior, to always be there for us. Not only that, but He will deliver us from the difficulties of life. A friend like this seems unbeknown to the world, yet we have access to Him at all times. The principle that comes to mind here is faith, without faith in our Friend we cannot fully feel the association we have with him. May we develop faith in order to develop a firm foundational relationship with the Savior.

**The Character of Christ**

How can we come to know the personality of the Savior without learning of who He is? In this spirit, I concluded my research with a study of the article “Jesus Christ: The Savior Who Knows” and the powerful, biblical chapter of John 17.

The article mentioned prior was written by Frank F. Judd Jr., a scholar of Jesus Christ. Judd guides us in comprehending a bit further how it is that the Lord knows us. By suffering for us and literally taking upon Him our burdens Christ has come to know us, in all we are. He is with us in all things, perfectly. Judd poses the question, “Once we understand that our Savior has a perfect knowledge of us, what should we do about it?” This is a question to reflect on personally in each one of us. For me, I feel a new sense of connection with the Lord. I see further into the incomprehensible value of my involvement with Him. I feel unspeakable gratitude for the effort that He has placed in creating a connection with each and every one of us.

Lastly, John chapter 17 lays upon our lap some of the most powerful words ever recorded. It is the transcript of a prayer that our Savior offers on behalf of all of us. This to me shows first, where His mind and heart were — on us. His purpose was in His relationship with us. He desires with His whole soul to be close to us and to lift us up. Jesus, Himself speaks directly of us in saying,

> Neither pray I for these alone, but for them also which shall believe on me through their word; That they all may be one; as thou, Father, art in me, and I in thee, that they also may be one in us: that the world may believe that thou hast sent me. And the glory which thou gavest me I have given them; that they may be one, even as we are one: I in them, and thou in me, that they may be made perfect in one; and that the world may know that thou hast sent me, and hast loved them, as thou hast loved me.

Can you see His fiery desire to be one with us? Can you feel the strain in His spirt as he asks the Father to have us join Him in the most precious relationship that we can have? This is what Christ desires for you and me. Hear Him and be one with Him!

### **Conclusion**

To conclude a compelling study on our connection to Jesus Christ I would like to highlight what it is that I learned about building a relationship with the Savior and Redeemer Himself:

-   From the Creation: Christ has assisted in creating for us a mortal exprience and in so doing produced a strong bond to each one of us. Our relationship with Him was established from the foundation of time
    
-   From the Brother of Jared: Our relationship with Christ is not meant to belittle us. Christ is willing to listen and even harken to our desires and ambitions.
    
-   From Moroni: Christ is a perfect listener and is always willing to console us in our afflictions.
    
-   From Bruce R. & Joseph F. McConkie: We can all obtain a passionate testimony of Jesus Christ and look forward to the moment we see Him again, as our dearest friend.
    
-   From Jeffery R. Holland: Love and Patience will produce in us the makings of a forever relationship. Christ had perfect love and patience for us and we should work towards the same with Him.
    
-   From James E. Faust: We need to examine our perspective of the Savior and eliminate any preconceived notions of the Savior and our relationship to Him — Christ wants to be our greatest friend.
    
-   From John Taylor: Trust in God will result in deliverance. Faith will be the foundational building block of our bond with Christ.
    
-   From Frank F. Judd Jr.: Christ knows us perfectly, when we come to understand this the view of our relationship with Him can change our life.
    
-   From John Chapter 17: Christ’s greatest desire is to be One with us. He wants nothing more than to be close to us.
    

This study and reflection have given my relationship with the Lord new life. I have learned more about my Savior and how to move nearer to Him. I know that God loves you, I know that your Father in Heaven and your brother Jesus Christ desire you to be with them for eternity. They know you individually. An intimate, personal relationship with them is within reach for all of us. May we reach out to our God and in turn get closer to Him, and closer together.
