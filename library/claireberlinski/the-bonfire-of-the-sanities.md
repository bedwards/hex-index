# **THE BONFIRE OF THE SANITIES**

#### **WHAT’S GONE WRONG WITH AMERICA?**

***By Tom Millard***

#### 2016-2026: THE AGE OF TRUMP AND PUTIN

**“We will not be terrorized. We will not be divided. And we will not allow fear to change who we are.”**

— President Obama, December 5, 2015

**“Donald J. Trump is calling for a total and complete shutdown of Muslims entering the United States until our country’s representatives can figure out what the hell is going on.”**

—Donald Trump, December 7, 2015

It’s been a long decade. The two reactions above to the San Bernardino terrorist attack, from the sitting president and from the man who would replace him in January, 2017, set the tone for the complete bifurcation of America. The majority of the country initially rejected Trump’s projection of hate. But a third of the Republican primary electorate would vote for him on Super Tuesday, four months later, cementing his front-runner status in the battle for his party’s nomination. And a big enough proportion of the larger electorate was so repulsed by the prospect of a Hillary Clinton presidency that it handed him power in a head-to-head that November. The people were asked to pick a side, and they soon self-sorted into two roughly equal halves.

The very people who warned that Trump was anywhere between a fool and a danger to the Republic now collaborate with the zeal of converts. In 2016, Trump’s vice-president, JD Vance, wrote an article for *The Atlantic* titled **[Opioid of the Masses](https://www.theatlantic.com/politics/archive/2016/07/opioid-of-the-masses/489911/),** describing Trump as “cultural heroin” for the working class::

> What Trump offers is an easy escape from the pain. To every complex problem, he promises a simple solution. He can bring jobs back simply by punishing offshoring companies into submission. As he told a New Hampshire crowd—folks all too familiar with the opioid scourge—he can cure the addiction epidemic by building a Mexican wall and keeping the cartels out. He will spare the United States from humiliation and military defeat with indiscriminate bombing. It doesn’t matter that no credible military leader has endorsed his plan. He never offers details for how these plans will work, because he can’t. Trump’s promises are the needle in America’s collective vein.
> 
> … He makes some feel better for a bit. But he cannot fix what ails them, and one day they’ll realize it.

He told a former roommate that **[he was torn between](https://www.washingtonpost.com/elections/2024/07/15/jd-vance-trump-criticism/)** thinking Trump was “a cynical asshole” or “America’s Hitler.” In Tweets he has since deleted, he offered views such as these: “Fellow Christians, everyone is watching us when we apologize for this man. Lord help us.” “Trump makes people I care about afraid. Immigrants, Muslims, etc. Because of this I find him reprehensible. God wants better of us.” He might, he added, “have to hold my nose and vote for Hillary Clinton.”

Trump insulted broadcaster Megyn Kelly in grotesque misogynistic terms in 2015. She later accused him of trying to cultivate her favor by sending her unsolicited gifts. Nevertheless, she eventually **[succumbed to his irresistible charm](https://nypost.com/2025/04/24/media/megyn-kelly-reveals-why-she-regained-love-for-trump-despite-strained-past-that-included-prez-bashing-ex-fox-news-anchor/)**s, claiming she “fell in love with Trump, the president,” even **[rebranding him](https://abcnews.go.com/Politics/megyn-kelly-endorses-trump-calling-protector-women/story?id=115508795),** improbably, as a “protector of women” in 2024.

One only imagines how the Vance of 2016 would react upon being told he would one day conjure up tales of migrants eating family pets in deference to this man. Or how the Kelly of the previous decade would view the Kelly who now twists herself into pretzels to **[minimize the crimes](https://www.npr.org/2025/11/18/nx-s1-5612509/what-does-15-look-like-teens-and-former-child-actors-weigh-in-online-after-megyn-kelly-comments)** of Trump’s fellow sexual predator, Jeffrey Epstein.

In reality, they haven’t been won around by Trump’s intellectual framework or his irresistible powers of persuasion. Vance and Kelly are cynical, amoral opportunists who have discarded their principles in exchange for proximity to power. Trump loves this about them: They’ve debased themselves so completely in his service that they’re now broken people. They’re infinitely pliable, and deeply compromised by their implication in his sordid project to desecrate the country. Trump had them sussed from the beginning. He knew they were up for sale. He knew the entire Republican party was up for sale—and that potentially half of America would be too.

He wasn’t the only one.

#### PUTIN’S WAGER

On the other side of the world, in snowy Moscow, Russian president Vladimir Putin was rolling the dice. Putin’s first bet, in 2013, involved pressuring his ally, then-Ukrainian president Viktor Yanukovych, to reject the nation’s association agreement with the EU in favor of closer ties with Moscow. This sparked public protests in Kyiv’s Maidan Square, dispersed with brutal force by Yanukovych’s riot police. The violence continued, peaking in February, 2014, when government forces fired on protesters, killing dozens. Yanukovych was removed from office and fled to Moscow. A Europe-oriented government took power.

Regarding the revolution as a Western-backed coup, Putin ordered his special forces and unmarked troops to invade Crimea. Meanwhile, backed by Russia, separatists in eastern Ukraine rebelled against the new government, occupying parts of the Donetsk and Luhansk oblasts. After holding phony referenda in these territories, Putin illegally annexed Crimea and announced the creation of new proxy states in Donetsk and Luhansk. Eight years later, Putin launched a full-scale invasion of Ukraine, aiming to decapitate the government, install a regime loyal to Moscow, and subjugate the entire country in permanence.

At least until November 2024, Putin’s bet appeared to have failed miserably: Three years after the beginning of the “special military operation” that was supposed to be over in three days, Russia occupied only about a fifth of Ukraine’s landmass. This had come at an unimaginable cost: over a million troops wounded or killed; Russia reduced to pariah status with its former primary trading partners; its military and its inflation-wracked war economy now reliant on China, Iran, and North Korea. Putin revealed himself to be a terrible strategist, immersed in his own warped historical narratives, oblivious to the nature of Ukrainian nationhood, and the recipient of flawed information from his policy delegates.

But by the time of Trump’s re-election, Putin was reaping the rewards from his second bet, made more than a decade before, when he detected something in ordinary Americans that their own leaders hadn’t. Putin perceived that Americans were weak, complacent, and decadent enough that with a little help from his agents—and for a small fraction of the budget of his intelligence services—its citizens could be encouraged to turn against each other.

Russian state actors used provocateurs and troll farms to sow discord on social media platforms, amplifying divisions on topics such as immigration, race, vaccine efficacy, and politics. State-run media arms such as RT and Sputnik reinforced this effort. Putin funded extremist parties in Europe, too, all with the aim of weakening trust in democratic institutions and undermining the cohesion of NATO and the EU. Aligning with the winning sides in both the UK’s Brexit referendum in June 2016 and the US presidential election that November, Russia’s famed foreign influence operations succeeded in driving wedges both within and among Western democracies.

For a few years between 2020 and 2023, it appeared that Putin’s bet would fall just short. Trump failed to win a second term, then withdrew from the political scene in disgrace after obstructing the peaceful transfer of power to his successor. It seemed that after a wake-up call, the American people had pulled themselves back from the brink.

But Trump came back from the dead and won the presidency once again. Putin’s long-shot gamble paid off spectacularly. A critical mass of the population succumbed to Putin’s targeted efforts—leveraged into a perfect storm by the candidate himself—with the rest too distracted, confused, or complacent vigorously to oppose it.

The American crisis is a direct result of this unimaginably successful disinformation campaign. It has, in one decade, turned Russia’s primary geopolitical enemy into a country that works against its own interests and for Moscow’s. So spectacularly did America turn against itself that it twice elected a president committed to relinquishing the US’s position as the cornerstone of global stability, free trade, and Western prosperity.

Donald Trump was last seen defending Steve Witkoff, his golf buddy and Russia envoy, after a leaked recording revealed that Witkoff had personally coached Kremlin figures, explaining to them how best to manipulate the American president—all while protecting his epic conflicts of interest in the business world. For any previous administration, this would have been a crippling scandal. For this one, it was just another day.

#### AMERICAN AMNESIA

It’s to America’s eternal shame that figures as damaged as Putin and Trump were able to read the national psyche so clearly. Why weren’t its citizens able to resist the seductions of an obvious conman like Trump? The two 2024 presidential candidates stood side-by-side in Philadelphia two months before the election: One of them ranted about migrants eating pets, while the other patiently explained the dangers of the Project 2025 governing blueprint and laid out the national security implications of her opponent once again folding to Putin. No one could have watched that debate in good faith and concluded that Trump should be anywhere near elected office.

Voters already knew this from personal experience. They had lived through Trump’s response to a once-in-a-lifetime global pandemic, which cost the lives of more than a million Americans—a response characterized by his pre-Covid dissolution of the Directorate for Global Health Security and Biodefense; his downplaying of the virus in public even as he privately acknowledged its lethality; his lauding of Xi Jinping for controlling its spread; his insistence that slowing testing would cause cases to fall; his suggestion that the virus might yield to “injecting disinfectant;” his transformation of masks into a culture war prop; and his own epic carelessness in contracting the virus himself.

They had already seen Trump’s attempt to overturn an election he lost by persuading officials to “find” non-existent votes in Georgia and submit fake slates of electors in seven swing states. They’d witnessed the culmination of this operation in a violent mob that stormed the Capitol on January 6; they’d seen him accuse his vice-president of cowardice even as the mob bayed for Pence to be lynched; and they’d watched him refuse to call in the National Guard in a historic dereliction of duty. They’d seen him charged with stealing thousands of sensitive government files and leaving them scattered around his pretend palace in Florida, a honeypot for any foreign agent worth their salt. They were aware of dozens of credible allegations of sexual assault. They knew all of this.

Yet not only did Trump earn a shade under half of the votes cast, 69.3 percent of the total electorate failed to vote against him. Either through opposition or apathy, seven in 10 Americans neglected to cast a vote for Kamala Harris—an imperfect bulwark against authoritarianism, yet by comparison an unequivocal paragon of personal integrity, and certainly someone committed to the continuation of liberal democracy. That statistic suggests that the emergence of such a Caesar figure was, at some point, inevitable. Trump, perhaps, was just a convenient vehicle for a deeply entrenched worldview.

What makes this worldview even more distinct is how completely it collapses once it leaves American shores. Trump’s mystique is a uniquely domestic phenomenon, unintelligible beyond the country’s borders. The rest of the world sees him plainly: as a naked emperor and a fool.

#### THE FIFTY-YEAR ILLUSION?

What if Trump’s popularity is a reckoning with divisions that have been embedded since America’s founding? Most in positions of authority took as given that America’s postwar status as an inclusive, pluralist democracy was settled and accepted on a bipartisan basis. But what if the half-century between the granting of practical universal suffrage and the emergence of Trump was an aberration?

Postwar liberal elites believed that the pluralist America that emerged in the wake of the civil rights movement was the logical fulfillment of the Founders’ vision. But what if this was just a story they told the country and themselves? What if this interpretation of the Founders’ vision never had broad support in the first place?

The architects of the early republic never explicitly envisioned pluralism as an endpoint, and neither did vast tracts of the country. In the US, unlike, say, in Britain or France, which boast a more cohesive civic model, one of the two major political parties has always been rooted in a tradition that rejects the legitimacy of multiracial democracy. **[As Thomas Zimmer argues](https://thomaszimmer.substack.com/p/what-is-america-and-for-whom)**, the post-1965 pluralist consensus rested upon institutions, norms, and elite commitments far more than it did on mass conversion. Underneath it, a powerful counter-tradition opposed to an inclusive democratic order never went away.

From this perspective, the brief period of egalitarian democracy was the exception to the American tradition, not its fulfillment. In this reading, what appeared to be a broad consensus in favor of the New Deal, a creedal form of nationalism, civil rights, environmental protection, and enlightenment values was the aberration—and perhaps even a function of American preoccupation with external affairs, such as the entanglement in Vietnam, the fight against communism, and the War on Terror. The 2008 financial crash and the Obama presidency then marked the end of this brief consensus—one that nearly came three decades earlier, in the form of Pat Buchanan’s challenge to the Republican Party establishment.

But if indeed the end of this holiday from history was inevitable, why did it not occur until 2016?

#### **THE ATTENTION ECONOMY**

America had demagogues and conspiracy theorists in the 1990s, and it certainly had polarization. What it didn’t have was an information environment calibrated to reward the angriest people in the country for shouting the loudest. The biggest change since the age of Buchanan v. Bush has been the arrival of a media system designed to capture people’s attention without keeping them anchored to a shared reality.

Social media dates from the start of the internet era, but the introduction of the iPhone, in 2007, ushering in the smartphone age, ensured its ubiquity. Facebook soon afterward changed the algorithm that determined the order in which items were presented to viewers. So did Twitter. They no longer offered users a chronological stream of content from friends or celebrities; instead, an opaque algorithm injected into their feeds the items that seemed to maximize their engagement and keep their eyeballs attached to the screen. As it turned out, nothing keeps people engaged so effectively as fear and rage, so from then on, every glance the phone brought users fresh terrors and outrages. Meanwhile, the introduction of the “like” button, which served up little dopamine hits in a random-reinforcement pattern, ensured that these platforms became irresistibly addictive.

YouTube might have been the most pernicious example of the phenomenon. Whistleblower Guillaume Chaslot, testing the algorithm, discovered that it gradually steered users toward radical and extreme content, even when they began with benign, non-political videos.

The result: The big platforms are no longer “social” media. Their primary purpose is no longer to connect us with friends; instead, they have become vehicles for consuming posts that an algorithm has decided will have the maximum possible impact on the amount of time you spend on a platform—a means to convert attention into impressions for advertisers. The algorithms push what they predict will make you react rather than what they predict you will like. The inevitable result is further polarization, and a loss of empathy for those whose views differ. The platforms magnify differences, even when more connects users than separates them.

In his 2021 book, **[Breaking the Social Media Prism](https://www.amazon.fr/Breaking-Social-Media-Prism-Polarizing-ebook/dp/B0BW5R5FMN/ref=sr_1_2?__mk_fr_FR=ÅMÅŽÕÑ&crid=3OHYEVJPCP9D1&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.1jOqb6knY89u0LJ8oiIDxx7oiDI7b2NHAv6C6ev51O9795-KrA0cGYOd8ipVXxwfo5W-JorKJGqYTGqUrAQX7HUZAYZ6U8zeUJwhhNLnEx8fGGofKTPMonJqCX2xUlwHL3mGHMeqe7N8esyO4O3Kz00xOlzluNhMEfPxwoKdx3TPN81tk-gyDYghQWlrvWnHPp6IamI8l_FI3ZHvLL-RJIC09SrU1gQKZ1o96LX8UmnOqZwi6SPx-TSNe3PXrtb0FDqC8VVdpSijKDAQSIx4zxQuOzfCR-DHxVeq5x2t2hQ.ZYeEE9TDV12xZyw_vas6Bgbw1f5HuokxAbpwHttgi0s&dib_tag=se&keywords=Chris+Bail&qid=1765450211&sprefix=chris+bail%2Caps%2C63&sr=8-2)**, Chris Bail characterizes this form of social media as a prism, because it ingests a vast, messy cloud of human expression and rather than reflecting it back to users like a mirror, refracts it as a kaleidoscope of angry, negative shards. Users react more intensely to content from the ideological pole they dislike. Algorithms treat that feedback as a positive signal, so a center-left user ends up drowning in far-right content, and vice versa. The extremes thus dominate perception, even if they remain a minority of citizens. What’s more, the algorithms dramatically amplify the views of a viral minority of extreme users, warping the public’s perceptions of what others believe and radicalizing them against their perceived political opposites. The longer people stay inside the prism, the more plausible it seems to them that the other side is irrational, dangerous, and unhinged—because such voices are the ones that rise to the top.

#### MEDIA ECHO SYSTEM

By late 2016, social media was unequivocally dominating and distorting Americans’ sense of reality. In the final three months of the election, the 20 top-performing fake election stories on Facebook **[generated more engagement](https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/craigsilverman/viral-fake-election-news-outperformed-real-news-on-facebook)** than the 20 top-performing stories from major outlets such as *The New York Times*, *The Washington Post,* and NBC News.

Simultaneously, trust in the institutions that once mediated reality eroded sharply. Traditional media no longer commanded shared audiences. In the UK, as broadcaster **[Mark Urban notes](https://markurban.substack.com/p/can-the-bbc-survive),** algorithmic platforms have pulled viewers away from broadcasters like the BBC. The BBC’s main evening news show once attracted 10 million viewers nightly; now, it is watched by less than 3 million. Trust in the corporation fell from 75 percent, in 2018, to the mid-50s in 2023. The Beeb’s channels still command bigger audiences and higher trust than their independent rivals, with almost half the population watching BBC News on TV or radio at least once a week, but the old relationship between audience and authority has frayed.

News consumers in the UK are relatively lucky; they are protected from the firehose of disinformation by the BBC’s Royal Charter and the OFCOM broadcast regulator. First Amendment protections in the US leave news consumers far more vulnerable to bad faith actors. But there are also signs that the public at large isn’t interested in being protected. Telling people unwelcome truths used to be the foundation of public-interest journalism; it now seems a reliable path to losing them as people wallow in their self-selected echo chambers, content to be told exactly what they want to hear, dozens of times a day.

Partisan media consumption patterns now diverge alarmingly. **[An NBC poll in April 2024](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/amp/rcna149497)** had President Biden taking 70 percent of the vote among readers of newspapers. Trump led, taking 55 percent of the vote to Biden’s 39 percent, among voters who primarily got their news from YouTube and Google. Those who didn’t follow political news at all broke 53-27 for Trump. Is it a stretch to say that in any other era, Trump would have long been in jail or hounded out of public life?

The news environment thus became poisoned. Once a society where curious citizens sought out information, America is now a place where information seeks out citizens, sorting them into adversarial poles by means of opaque tools designed to trigger primal emotions. It’s no surprise to find that this shift has radicalized users. Algorithms reward content that provokes engagement or outrage, crowding out complexity and nuance, giving users permission to indulge their worst instincts and prioritize the misfortune of enemies over any constructive goal. Whole careers now thrive on trading rage for money.

Political scientist Francis Fukuyama, initially a sceptic of the social-media-as-protagonist theory, now **[concedes that technology and the internet](https://www.persuasion.community/p/its-the-internet-stupid)** “stand out as the most salient explanations for why global populism has arisen in this particular historical period,” arguing that social media platforms have shattered the shared information environment required by a liberal society:

> While previously “truth” was imperfectly certified by institutions like scientific journals, traditional media with standards of journalist accountability, courts and legal discovery, educational institutions and research organizations, the standard for truth began to gravitate instead to the number of likes and shares a particular post got. The large tech platforms pursuing their own commercial self-interest created an ecosystem that rewarded sensationalism and disruptive content, and their recommendation algorithms, again acting in the interest of profit-maximization, guided people to sources that never would have been taken seriously in earlier times.

Historian John Ganz, on **[a podcast with Max Read,](https://substack.com/@johnganz/p-173216887)** [](https://substack.com/@johnganz/p-173216887)likens the way we’ve built the internet to constructing a new city without sewage, zoning, or waste management. Users have had to improvise their own sanitation systems, which means the community quickly poisons itself; the city feels real on its own terms, even as it drifts inexorably away from reality.

#### **THE AUTOPHAGIC STATE**

Ambient distrust has now migrated into government itself. The Trump administration has made a virtue of disparaging expertise and treating professional knowledge as a partisan signal. In an article about changes at the Heritage Foundation published in the wake of the Tucker Carlson–Nick Fuentes farrago, **[The Wall Street Journal reporte](https://www.wsj.com/politics/policy/the-crack-up-at-the-heritage-foundation-is-a-warning-sign-for-maga-world-747a2c69?st=yXv6FA&reflink=desktopwebshare_permalink)**d that Heritage employees working on Ukraine policy were instructed to watch Carlson’s monologues:

> Heritage, which had previously backed support for Ukraine, initially flew a Ukrainian flag outside its building. But within days, the flag was taken down and, over time, internal communications reflected a policy reversal on Ukraine, according to people familiar with the matter.
> 
> Employees working on Ukraine policy were asked to watch Carlson’s monologues, which were rife with conspiracy theories about the war, to delete past tweets in support of Ukraine aid and to write papers reflecting the new, more isolationist policy that Roberts had embraced, according to Luke Coffey, the former director of Heritage’s foreign policy center.

John Ganz aptly notes that **[this is remarkable](https://www.unpopularfront.news/p/operation-drumbeat)**: Whatever one thinks of the change in policy, the Heritage Foundation—once a prestigious think tank—is now asking its subject-matter experts to cast aside serious study and watch propaganda instead. Ganz concludes that the Republican Party “and the right-wing apparatus in general have become totally dominated by propaganda and propagandists.” Professionals have been replaced by podcasters.

For more evidence, consider Robert F. Kennedy Jr., perched atop the Health and Human Services Department, who is sidelining experts with generations of experience across medicine and science and replacing them with discredited cranks. A children’s author and a conspiracy-addled podcast host lead the FBI. Tulsi Gabbard heads up intelligence community. The revolutionaries are in charge, and they’re eagerly breathing the fumes of their own hallucinations.

#### REFLEX REPLACES REFLECTION

Such a deterioration in the information environment will inevitably have profound effects on public life, particularly in political systems that demand engagement and vigilance. Across disciplines, a similar picture emerges: a society drifting away from the slow, effortful reasoning that healthy civic participation relies on in favor of immediate gratification. Cultural, psychological, and historical analyses all converge on this theme.

*New York* magazine recently published a cover story titled **[A Theory of Dumb](https://removepaywalls.com/https://nymag.com/intelligencer/article/american-adult-lower-iq-scores-cognitive-decline-technology-flynn-effect.html)**, pointing out what until now has been unsayable in the mainstream press. It isn’t your imagination: American IQs have been sharply declining, as Elizabeth Dworak of Northwestern University’s medical school discovered when she analyzed the results of nearly 400,000 IQ tests taken in the US between 2006 and 2018. She had expected to see rising IQ—the so-called Flynn effect, observable throughout the 20th century. Then she ran the numbers.

> “I felt like I was in *Don’t Look Up,*” the movie in which an astronomy grad student played by Jennifer Lawrence discovers a comet speeding toward Earth. “I spent weeks going back through all the code. I thought I’d messed something up and would have to delay submitting. But then I showed my adviser, and he said, ‘Nope, your math is right.’”
> 
> The math showed declines in three important testing categories, including matrix reasoning (abstract visual puzzles), letter and number series (pattern recognition), and verbal reasoning (language-based problem-solving). The first two, in which losses were deepest, measure what psychologists call *fluid intelligence,* or the power to adapt to new situations and think on the fly. The drops showed up across age, gender, and education level but were most dramatic among 18-to-22-year-olds and those with the least amount of schooling.

In the view of *New York*’s reporter, Lane Brown, we’re increasingly drawn towards “routines, entertainment, and entire belief systems that ask less and less of our brains,” because the media now shrinks the world instead of enlarging it. It places each of us at the center of a small private universe, “surrounded by voices that insist everything is simpler than it really is.” In this environment, loud, low-effort certainty travels further than careful thought.

The *Times’* writer James Marriott explores the cost of this transformation in a post on Substack titled, “**[The dawn of the post-literate society—and the end of civilization](https://jmarriott.substack.com/p/the-dawn-of-the-post-literate-society-aa1)**.” We are slipping into a “post-literate” era, he argues, in which deep reading and sustained attention, essential for navigating the complexity of modern life, have collapsed under the pressure of smartphones and short-form media.[1](#footnote-1) He warns that the habits of mind needed for serious thought and democratic citizenship are eroding.

Philosophy professor Joseph Heath discusses the psychological dimension of this phenomenon in a fascinating Substack piece titled, **[Populism, fast and slow](https://josephheath.substack.com/p/populism-fast-and-slow)**.[2](#footnote-2) Populism is a natural fit for our modern technological environment, he reasons, because it relies on the style of cognition the psychologist Daniel Kahneman has called “fast”—intuitive, emotional—and sidesteps the “slow” style—analytical, more effortful—that expert-guided policy requires. Populism, in his view, is less an ideology than a strategy, a political approach optimized for the psychology of modern publics operating under conditions of uncertainty, complexity, and media overload.

But in his essay, **[Weimar: ‘Close And Familiar,](https://roddreher.substack.com/p/weimar-close-and-familiar?utm_source=publication-search)**’ conservative essayist Rod Dreher remarks that this impatience with thought is not new. He cites the revolutionary socialist Ernst Toller, who wrote, in 1933: “The people are tired of reason, tired of thought and reflection. They ask, what has reason done in the last few years, what good have insights and knowledge done us.”

The implication is that people are prone to become weary of cognition itself: After enough shocks and disappointments, argument feels like an extra burden rather than a way out. When politics is experienced mainly as noise and failure, a promise to replace thought with certainty is tempting. It’s hardly a stretch to draw parallels with the attempts of this US administration to flatten complexity, to present a good-versus-evil version of reality to its supporters.

#### DELUSION PROFUSION

Erich Fromm spent much of the mid-20th century trying to answer a question that still feels uncomfortably current: Why do perfectly ordinary people end up believing obviously untrue things in perfect synchrony? A psychoanalyst formed by the wreckage of Weimar Germany, Fromm watched millions slip into what he called regression: a retreat from maturity, autonomy, and reality into the warm bath of collective fantasy. To capture that phenomenon, he extended the concept of a *folie à deux—*a delusion shared by two people—to the scale of nations. *Folie à millions* was his term for the phenomenon; it describes an entire society embracing a fantasy, repeating it to one another, and treating it as normal simply because everyone else seems to believe it, too.

Fromm stressed that mass delusion represents an emotional shortcut. When people feel powerless, frightened, or humiliated, they look for ways to lighten the psychological load; sometimes numbing themselves with consumption, sometimes fusing their identity to a movement or leader who promises certainty and belonging. Either way, the price is contact with reality. As he put it in **[The Sane Society](https://books.google.co.uk/books/about/The_Sane_Society.html?  id=CLvZAAAAIAAJ&source=kp_book_description&redir_esc=y)**, a “consensual validation” needs have no bearing on truth or sanity. A society can be deeply unwell while insisting, loudly and confidently, that everything makes perfect sense.

He had historical examples in mind. In **[Escape from Freedom](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/25491.Escape_from_Freedom)**, Fromm describes Weimar Germans sliding into a regressive dreamworld, where Hitler embodied their wounded pride and Jews became an all-purpose vessel for fear and resentment. In **[The Anatomy of Human Destructiveness](https://www.amazon.co.uk/Anatomy-Human-  Destructiveness-Erich-Fromm/dp/0712674896)**, he extends the analysis to other cultic politics: movements that reorganize reality around a leader’s mythology, punish doubt as treason, and reward fantasies that make people feel safe or important. Mass movements of people who prefer a tidy fiction over the untidy world in front of them represent archetypal cases of *folie à millions.*

**[As the Cosmopolitan Globalist has previously noted](https://claireberlinski.substack.com/p/impeach-him)**[,](https://claireberlinski.substack.com/p/impeach-him) the cult of Trump maps uncannily onto Fromm’s framework. The MAGA phenomenon would be instantly recognizable to him. A large bloc of Americans has come to inhabit a shared narrative in which their leader is uniquely honest, uniquely persecuted, and uniquely entitled to power. Elections he wins are legitimate by definition; elections he loses are stolen by definition. Any institution that contradicts this becomes part of the plot. But whereas Fromm’s 20th-century environment required rallies, radio, and newspapers, today’s version runs on social media feeds engineered to deliver grievance and fear on an industrial scale. Some form of *folie à millions* might well have emerged without the digital ecosystem, but it wouldn’t have happened so quickly, nor would it have been so immersive and self-reinforcing.

Fromm is bleak, though not hopeless. He argues that mass delusion usually ends only when the fantasy can no longer keep delivering emotional relief and people are forced back into contact with the world as it is. That can come through economic failure, institutional collapse, or simply the exhaustion of the myth. His hope, thin though it is, lies in people recovering what he called “positive freedom”—the capacity to act with honesty, responsibility and autonomy rather than seeking refuge in a leader who thinks for them.

But such positive freedom flourishes only when people feel secure, connected, and informed enough to think for themselves rather than outsourcing their judgment. That in turn requires an information environment that rewards truth and clarity rather than provocation. The information environment in today’s America is instead built to inflame, distract and divide, conditions that compound mass delusion. It’s hard to imagine the psychological independence Fromm hoped for taking root in a system built to keep everyone perpetually outraged.

So this is what went wrong: Putin saw, more than a decade ago, that Americans were more easily manipulated than they or their leaders realized. He recognized that investing in a few well-placed provocations would be enough to heighten the country’s internal contradictions and sunder what remained of its civic harmony. Trump, with a little help from his friends in the Kremlin, then proved the bet more successful than anything the provocateurs could have imagined, converting a modest foreign influence operation into a country producing its own misinformation at industrial scale, guided by algorithms that privilege anger and fear over reflection and contemplation. What began as an opportunistic experiment in destabilization morphed into a homegrown *folie à millions*, with citizens encouraged to disbelieve their doctors, their scientists and eventually their own lying eyes.

The brief postwar illusion of pluralist stability depended on norms, good-faith disagreement, and a shared concept of reality. All three proved ephemeral when a sufficiently skilled showman exploited the attention economy to set the information environment ablaze. The consequence is a spectacular self-immolation no adversary could have engineered alone, and one the country so far shows little capacity to arrest.

What follows now is in the hands of the same public that allowed its civic fabric to catch fire, one that must now decide whether the last decade was an aberration or the template for the ones to come. Less than a quarter of the way through Trump’s second term, all signs suggest that **[things will get worse](https://seearoundcorners.substack.com/p/scenario-analysis-the-fate-of-the)** before they get better.
