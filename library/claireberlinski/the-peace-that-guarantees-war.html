There are moments when a nation must decide whether it wishes to see or to will its own blindness. This is one of them. The 28-point “peace plan” for Ukraine—leaked in outline, circulating Washington now with a surreal air of normalcy—would be the most consequential document produced by an American politician in decades, except that it was clearly written by a Russian. It is not a peace plan. It is not even an armistice plan. It is a Russian rearmament plan, and its adoption would mark the most craven betrayal of an allied nation since the 1930s.

To anyone with even a cursory knowledge of Russian strategic culture, twentieth-century European history, or the mechanics of deterrence, it’s obvious that this instrument contains the seeds of a much greater conflagration—one that will not spare the United States. Yet there’s been almost no public outcry in America, further evidence that we have lost the capacity to grasp the scale of the danger gathering on the horizon.

Let me attempt to restore that sense of scale. The essential features of the plan are now clear. Russia keeps Crimea, with its water, land, and rail logistics secured. Ukraine cedes all of Donetsk and Luhansk—with their industry, manpower, and mining—and gives Moscow a fortified land bridge to Crimea. The remaining front lines would be frozen in place. Kyiv would hand over a vast swathe of territory it now controls, territory that Russia has been unable to conquer despite four years of war, four years in which wave after wave of Russian men have been mown down, shredded up, and left to rot in no-man’s-land. It puts immediate demobilization pressure on Ukraine. Above all, Russia gets the single most important resource it now lacks: time.

This plan is exactly what Russia was hoping for in 2022, when its offensive stalled. Ukraine will be permanently barred from joining NATO; NATO will be barred from further expansion. Ukraine will be required to gut its armed forces. It will not be allowed to possess weapons that could reach Russia. Russia will not be required to shed any of its allies nor reduce any aspect of its military force. No Western troops or long-term deployments will be allowed—ever—on Ukrainian soil, even though only last month, encouraged by Trump, Europeans agreed that in the event of a truce, they would send troops to secure it, and Trump grandly offered to provide air defenses and intelligence. He was lying. It was all a con, as Phillips O’Brien writes, and a very deliberate one. Trump never had any intention of doing this. He has been determined to betray Ukraine since his first presidential campaign. Ukraine will be required to hold elections within 100 days. Russia will not be required to hold elections, ever.

“It is expected,” the draft says, “that Russia will not invade neighboring countries.” Who expects this? It doesn’t say; if it’s Steve Witkoff, he’s surely the only one. If, contrary to Witkoff’s expectations, Russia invades Ukraine, “in addition to a decisive coordinated military response, all global sanctions will be reinstated.” It doesn’t commit anyone in particular to providing this decisive response, but it does specify that “the US will receive compensation for the guarantee.”

Who will ensure compliance with the agreement? “A joint American-Russian working group.” Ukraine is not invited. (Graciously, Russia “will not prevent Ukraine from using the Dnieper River for commercial activities,” which is mighty white of them, seeing as the river is in the middle of Ukraine.)

Ukraine will be required to give the Russian Orthodox Church—an arm of Russian intelligence—protected status. Both parties will outlaw “Nazi ideology;”this term is undefined, but Putin believes that Zelensky and his entire government are Nazis. Russian war criminals will receive unconditional amnesty. The most depraved war crimes Europe has seen since Hitler? Forgive and forget.

Russia will be given sanctions relief, “reintegration into the global economy,” and readmission to global institutions like the G8. Russia’s frozen assets, worth about US$300 billion, will be unfrozen; a third will be invested in a fund for Ukraine’s reconstruction, to be managed by the US. The EU will contribute another US$100 billion to this fund—meaning that Europe will pay more in reparations for Russia’s invasion of Ukraine than Russia—and the United States will receive 50 percent of the profit from these activities.

The rest of the money will be put in a joint American-Russian investment fund to strengthen our “mutual economic interests.” ﻿The United States will

conclude a long-term economic cooperation agreement with Russia aimed at mutual development in energy, natural resources, infrastructure, artificial intelligence, data centers, Arctic rare-earth mining projects, and other areas of reciprocal corporate opportunity.

Artificial intelligence? Do we plan to give them the high-end microchips to go with it? (Will we even have any left after giving them to the UAE?)

The administration consulted neither Ukraine nor our NATO allies. Nor did it consult Congress. From the sound of it, they’re very proud of themselves:

Almost as an aside, the plan notes that “European fighter jets will be stationed in Poland.” What does that mean? It sounds anodyne at first blush, but as Stuart Dowell, the author of Poland Watch, points out, it makes sense if you assume that the word “only” is missing, as in “only European fighter jets … ”Have Washington and Moscow have agreed—without consulting Poland—that US fighters will not be allowed to operate in Poland? Have we offered Russia a veto over our security posture and that of our allies?

Ukraine has been given until Thanksgiving to accept the terms. Four more days. If they decline, the US will stop brokering deals for arms transfers to Ukraine (we’ve already stopped funding them) and withdraw its support from the war effort, meaning we’d cut off the satellite and electronic intelligence that’s been crucial to Ukraine’s survival on the battlefield. Trump would almost certainly proceed, unilaterally, to lift sanctions on Russia, whether or not Congress agrees. Zelensky will “have to like it,” Trump said. “You remember, right in the Oval Office, not so long ago, I said, ‘You don’t have the cards.’”1

The investigative journalist Christo Grozev (best known for his work with Bellingcat) claims that he saw an early version of this plan some six months ago:

It was a purely Russian concept and was nearly identical to what we see today (certainly the 50/50 Russian assets + EU investment in Ukraine reconstruction was there). However, two items are glaringly missing. First, a proposal that US (investors) bail post-war Russia out of a pending cash- and investment-strapped recession, “like in the 90s.” This was meant as an inducement for Trump (whether as a legitimate US sweetener or as one for friends and family, that wasn’t explicit). The second “offering” was a new Russia-US alliance against China. A lot of “Christian alliance” demagoguery was included under that item.

I am absolutely certain that this is a Russian, not a jointly-developed, proposal. I also cannot imagine the Russians didn’t include the first of the two extra items. I can see however how that wouldn’t be part of the publicly leaked plan. On item 2, can’t be sure if Russia dared to include the anti-China offer. Again, if it did, it wouldn’t be public.

How could such a plan even exist? We can see that the leaked document was translated from Russian into English by a native Russian speaker. It’s filled with mistakes that only a Russian would make, like spelling Kherson ﻿“Kherzon.” (“Kherzon” is a transliteration of the Russian spelling, Херсон.)2 When I first saw it, I thought it plausible that the whole thing was a Kremlin ruse meant to demoralize Ukraine, sow confusion, and breed distrust between the US and its allies. But no: This is Trump’s plan. He’s giving it his full-throated backing. The whole administration is now saying, proudly, “Yep. This really is our proposal.

It is obscene. It is a surrender instrument that punishes the victim of savage aggression and rewards the aggressor. It demands that Ukraine disarm itself at the moment when its enemy is wounded but not defeated. It asks a country that has buried tens of thousands of its citizens to accept, without resistance, its own mutilation, the abnegation of its security, and the certainty—yes, certainty—of renewed Russian attack once Moscow has rebuilt its forces. If a hostile power wished to design the optimal conditions for a second, more decisive invasion of Ukraine in several years’ time, it would draft this plan. If you’re seeking a path to global war—in the nuclear age—look no further.

Treaties and guarantees are not imbued with some metaphysical force. They function only insofar as the signatories believe that breaking them carries a cost greater than the benefit of the violation. Russia does not believe this, and why would it? Since 1994, Russia has violated every agreement involving Ukraine it has signed. It violated the Budapest Memorandum, which guaranteed Ukraine’s borders in exchange for Ukraine’s surrender of its nuclear weapons. It violated the 1997 Treaty of Friendship, which recognized Ukraine’s territorial integrity—including Crimea. It violated Minsk I and II, using them to buy time, rearm separatist forces, and prepare for the next round of war. It violated the cease-fire arrangements in Chechnya, Georgia, and Syria, exploiting them tactically, discarding them when convenient. It violated the INF Treaty years before formally withdrawing from it. Russian strategists do not treat agreements as sacred. They treat them as tools to manage the tempo of a conflict—phases in the campaign, opportunities to regain momentum, windows for regrouping and rearming.

Nothing in this plan imposes costs on Moscow for breaking its word—for invading its peaceful neighbor; slaughtering its civilians; raping not just Ukrainian women but Ukrainian toddlers; stealing everything in sight, even Ukrainians’ toilet seats; murdering a whole generation of young men; terrorizing Ukrainians nightly; kidnapping their children; devastating Ukraine’s environment, economy, and agriculture; inflicting trauma that will endure generations. Crime after crime after crime. There is no savagery Russia has not permitted itself—and this is just Ukraine, never mind countries throughout Africa, Chechnya, Syria, Georgia, and every other country that has caught Russia’s acquisitive eye. This plan is a reward for that behavior. As anyone who has put a rat in a Skinner box knows: What is rewarded is repeated.

The most dangerous component is the forced demilitarization of Ukraine—a cap of 600,000 troops, no long-range strike systems, no NATO membership, no allied basing. This is an outrageous demand. Ukraine is a sovereign nation. It was Russia that invaded Ukraine, not vice-versa, whatever Donald Trump might believe. Not only would this deprive Ukraine of the ability to defend itself, it would deprive Europe of that ability: Ukraine is Europe’s bulwark against Russia. This is not neutrality but a diktat to our allies: roll over and bare your bellies before that monstrous ogre.

Neutrality only works when the threatening power accepts it. Russia does not. Putin has repeatedly declared, in public and private, that Ukrainian statehood is a historical error, a Western fabrication, a fiction that must be corrected. Under these conditions, neutrality is a Maginot Line made of tissue paper. It means nothing.

If Ukraine withdraws from Donetsk Oblast, the borders of its rump state will be impossible to defend. It will lose its strategic depth and cede to Russia all of the fortifications it’s built behind the 800-mile front. Russia will reinvade Ukraine with a rested and reconstituted military. At a time of his choosing, Putin will march into the southern Kharkiv and eastern Zaporizhia and Dnipropetrovsk oblasts directly from the supposedly “frozen” frontline. And who will stop them? Would that be the same people who are stopping them now?

The plan transforms Ukraine into the dream-object of Russian military doctrine: a buffer state incapable of resisting renewed invasion—another Belarus. “Ukraine agrees not to join NATO,” and “Ukraine agrees to place itself in the exact position Russia exploited in 2014 and again in 2022”—are, strategically speaking, identical propositions. No serious strategist would ever accept this.

And they won’t stop there. How much more clear about this can they be? They make no secret of it: They say it over and over. Vladimir Putin is waging a war of conquest to reestablish the Russian empire and overturn the post–Cold War international order. Russia’s war economy is on overdrive, amassing imported North Korean shells by the shipload; there are Iranian drone factories on Russian soil, reopened tank plants operating on triple shifts, a military budget now in excess of 7 percent of GDP. Give Russia three to five years of this and unfettered access to the global economy, and it will be capable of fielding a larger, better-equipped force than it had in 2022. Give Ukraine those same three to five years of forced demilitarization and it will be unable to resist the next blow.

This is a time bomb set to detonate in Europe—but not just in Europe. If a nuclear-armed aggressor can invade neighbor after neighbor, slaughter civilians, annex territory, and be rewarded by recognition of its conquests, relief from sanctions, membership of the G8, and a guarantee that its victim won’t rearm, the international system has declared: “Aggression works. Borders can be changed by force.” There is no limit to this principle. This is not just about Ukraine: This is about Taiwan, the South China Sea, the Baltics, Moldova, Armenia, the Balkans—it is about the entire world. This plan hollows out the entire post-1945 state system.

What happens then? Here is the most likely trajectory. Not “one possible trajectory,” but, now, the most likely one:

  • 2025–27: Russia uses the cease-fire to rebuild its military, import North Korean munitions, and expand its capacity to mass-produce drones.

  • 2027-28: Russia conducts political subversion in Ukraine to destabilize a government weakened by the “shame treaty.”

  • 2028: Russia launches a new, bigger invasion, massing 500,000–700,000 troops for a decisive offensive, with greatly improved military-industrial support. Ukraine, its military limited by treaty, is steamrolled. Europe, divided and demoralized—with France, Germany, the UK, or all three under the control of one of Putin’s proxy parties—is unable to mount a coordinated intervention. Russia destroys what remains of Ukrainian sovereignty. If Trump is still alive, he’ll say, “They violated the deal, not my fault.”

  • 2030: Russia invades Estonia, Lithuania, or Latvia. NATO collapses.

  • Soon thereafter, or simultaneously: China invades Taiwan. Iran detonates a nuclear weapon. North Korea invades South Korea.

  • 2032: Americans begin to sense that something’s gone wrong overseas.

  • 2033: We are alone in a world that is comprehensively hostile to us. China, Russia, Iran, and North Korea are determined to ensure we never rise again.

This isn’t alarmism. Russian planners themselves openly describe this, in their journals, as a “phased approach to strategic depth restoration.” The terminus of this approach has been lovingly described by Mikhail Yuriev, the author of the Kremlin’s favorite book, The Third Empire: Russia as It Ought to Be:

There is no mystery about what awaits Ukraine—and Europe—under the Russian boot. Ukraine knows it well. After losing a war with Russia in 1919, Ukraine was absorbed into the Soviet Union. Millions of Ukrainians starved to death in the Holodomor. The Ukrainian language was banned. Ukraine’s scientists, writers, artists, and poets were systematically executed. In Bucha, Russian forces again sought out teachers of Ukrainian history and literature and executed them. This is why Ukrainians won’t stop fighting.

Even were we to disavow this insane plan today, its publication—never mind its implementation—has done irreparable damage. It’s not just that it has “exacerbated tensions” with our European allies, as some news reports are delicately phrasing it. The world is nauseated by us.

Contempt for the United States is now global, felt most intensely by those who once most admired us.

Nations, as Lord Palmerston remarked, have no permanent allies. But we can no longer be trusted to act in accordance with our interests, either. Putin leads Trump by the nose, and Americans applaud. Who can repose faith in a superpower determined to commit seppuku and to take its friends with it?

Ukraine cannot accept this deal, and won’t. This plan is functionally a Russian war plan, one that gives Russia territory, time, breathing space, sanctions relief, international legitimacy, and the dismantling of Ukraine’s military. It spells the end of NATO. In strategic terms, this is not even subtle: It is exactly the settlement Russia sought after 2014 and again in February 2022: a suzerain-vassal arrangement, dressed up as neutrality, imposed by an external power.

Nor can Europe accept it. European nations understand all too well that this agreement means one thing only: they’re next. If Europe and Ukraine are forced to choose between this plan and losing the United States, they will choose losing the United States.

I have no idea who this writer on Twitter is, but he’s absolutely right:

Why a break with Ukraine would be the biggest defeat in US history

If Ukraine walks away, the United States loses more than a partner. It loses the foundation of its power. American strength has never been based on weapons alone. What made the US a superpower was that other nations chose to align with it. Influence came from being the centre of a network of democracies that trusted Washington more than any rival.

A rupture with Ukraine would expose how fragile that network has become. Europe has already begun shifting its long term defense spending away from the US. Losing Ukraine would confirm that the United States is no longer the natural leader of the Western alliance. When partners stop depending on Washington, American power stops scaling. Its military remains large, but its reach shrinks.

The economic consequences follow immediately. Once Europe reorganizes its defense market around its own industry, American suppliers lose access to the richest democratic market in the world. Those contracts do not return. Neither does the leverage that comes from being indispensable.

The strategic cost is even clearer. Ukraine has been the most visible test of American commitment since nineteen forty five. If the US cannot hold that line, allies will conclude that Washington’s guarantees are conditional and unpredictable. They will design their own security strategies without the US at the center. That is how superpowers lose status: partners stop believing, and the world recalculates.

Ukraine will continue fighting for survival. The United States will be left fighting for relevance. And losing that fight is far more damaging than losing any battlefield.

There seems to be some stirring from the GOP. Trump’s power is ebbing. Congress surprised itself by defying Trump over the Epstein files; some seem to have noticed that contrary to received wisdom, they did not immediately die. The GOP is restive: Marjorie Taylor Greene has flounced off; the other rats might be tempted to swim away from the ship:

Perhaps they will decide, at long last, to stand up to him. I can only beg them: Swim! Swim!

But I’ve been hoping for this too long, in vain, to think it likely.

Too many Americans fail to grasp that NATO is not a charity project. It’s a self-defense mechanism designed to prevent the United States from being drawn into another world war. If Ukraine falls, the alliance’s front line becomes Poland, Romania, the Baltics, and Finland. The risk of an Article 5 crisis would rise from hypothetical to probable.

And then what? When Russia—armed, emboldened, victorious, vicious—tests the limits of NATO’s resolve, we will face a choice: respond militarily, or accept the collapse of the alliance that has prevented great-power war for 80 years.

Peace will not be restored by sacrificing Ukraine. Nor will it be restored by sacrificing Europe. A world in which borders may be redrawn by force is a world in which, eventually, the United States will fight. Then there are only two possibilities: unimaginable bloodletting in a conventional conflict, or nuclear war.

The strangest part of this story is the American public’s indifference. This is not the leading story in any major American news organ, even though American children will die in the war this imbecilic plan ensures. This plan is categorically, diametrically opposed to American interests. It is in Russia’s interests; and because it dethrones us from our superpower perch, it is in China’s interest. No American, except those in Trump’s circle who will profit from the corruption, will benefit from it in any way.

The silence of the American public is the dog that doesn’t bark. I have trouble imagining another country where this could happen: Who else would tolerate the sight of their leader proudly, visibly betraying them like this? It is not normal.

It is not good enough to say, “Americans don’t care about foreign policy.” This is not an arcane doctoral thesis about water rights in the Nile River Delta. This, for Americans, is a life-or-death matter. Earlier generations of Americans would have understood this perfectly.

A decade of Russian disinformation has worked. Large segments of the electorate believe that Ukraine is corrupt, unimportant, or “not our problem.” They do not understand NATO as homeland defense; they imagine the alliance as an act of generosity, not an act of self-preservation. The US is exhausted and inward-facing. Our political dysfunction has sapped our national appetite for strategic thought. Trump’s dominance of the Republican Party has created an internal veto. Many politicians know the truth; they are simply too afraid to say it. The silence is dangerous. Strategic disasters in democracies rarely come from lack of intelligence; they come from lack of attention.

For the United States to impose this plan on Ukraine would be a betrayal of a nation we pledged to defend in 1994, a partner that has fought more bravely than anyone could have imagined, a postwar order built on the premise that borders are not changed by force, and the idea that freedom is not negotiable. It would also be a betrayal of ourselves: When a great power rewards aggression it is not purchasing peace—just a few more years of quiet before the storm breaks, one that will cost more blood, treasure, and lives than Americans can presently imagine.

The tragedy is that a just peace is possible—one that secures Ukraine, contains Russia, safeguards NATO, and preserves the international order. But such a peace requires clarity, unity, strategic patience, and the understanding that deterrence is cheaper than war.

The real question isn’t whether this plan is bad. It’s whether the US still possesses the capacity to recognize danger before it’s too late. Ukraine sees the danger. Europe sees the danger. Russia sees the opportunity. It’s only America that can’t see the war barreling toward it.

Let me put it as plainly as possible. If this plan is implemented, the next war in Europe will not be limited to Ukrainians. It will involve Americans.

And it may very well be a nuclear conflict.
