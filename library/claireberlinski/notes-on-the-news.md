Having read **[my account of the mood in France](https://claireberlinski.substack.com/p/the-view-from-france)**, a reader sent me an email with two questions. First, he asked, why are the French panicking *now*?

After all, last February, Pete Hegseth and JD Vance flew to Europe, accompanied by Jack Posobiec of Pizzagate fame. Hegseth told our allies that as far as the he was concerned, they were effete pansies, and if they sent their silly little euroweenie troops to Ukraine, they’d be on their own. After eviscerating NATO’s deterrence, he told them to pony up anyway: The vig was now five percent.

Vance then lectured a gathering of European security analysts about a collection of outrages he’d read about on Gateway Pundit. He chastised them for failing to smooth the far-right’s path to power. (**[The speech was not well-received.](https://www.lemonde.fr/en/opinion/article/2025/02/21/behind-the-words-of-jd-vance-s-historic-munich-speech_6738424_23.html))** NATO has been pining for the fjords ever since, he wrote, so how come the French only just noticed?

  
His second question concerned European economic diplomacy. Europe, he thought, could “end this war on reasonably favorable terms if they could only concert their economic diplomacy by way of secondary sanctions to stop countries like India and China from buying Russian huge amounts of Russian fossil fuels.” This could be done much faster than rebuilding the French army. What was stopping them from doing so?

> It’s just hard to believe that European diplomats are so dumb that they would prefer to spend the whole wad on a kinetic solution to the problem than make a few trade balance sacrifices to get a non-kinetic one. It suggests something of a moral delusion on their part.

He thought I should publish the email I sent him in response. I wasn’t sure—I wrote it in haste—but he said so three times, and he’s a good judge of these things, so I’ve published it below, at his urging. (I’ve edited it a bit and added a few points that I didn’t think of when I first sent it.)

My response:

American commentary, I notice, is shot through with many misunderstandings of Europe, but one error in particular seems to be leading to all kinds of analytic mishaps. Americans seem to think of the EU as a unitary state, one that’s capable of pursuing a state-like foreign policy. It simply isn’t. It’s an empty empire, with no center capable of imposing its will on the periphery. Even the EU’s closest ancestor, the Austro-Hungarian empire, had that—the Hapsburgs could still send troops to Prague.

The EU is a customs union, not a state. It can’t do much of anything without the unanimous agreement of all of its members. It rarely has it. It certainly doesn’t have it with respect to China, not least because Hungary is now an eastern Chinese province. Hungary’s alignment with China isn’t just rhetorical—it’s infrastructural: telecoms, battery supply chains, universities, rail corridors, police tech, something far closer to complete regulatory capture than diplomatic friendship. Von der Leyen only managed to sign the MERCOSUR treaty because France was having such a lavish political seizure that it had no government with which to object; otherwise, the whole trade agreement would have been scuttled by a claque of furious French farmers.

Perhaps because our own federal reflex leads us to misread things, many Americans don’t seem to grasp that the EU has a legal personality without sovereign coercive power. Foreign policy and sanctions require unanimity, which gives every Hungary a nuclear veto. Von der Leyen can issue thunderous declarations, but she can’t compel a recalcitrant capital to absorb real economic pain for a war its voters still experience as abstract. Treating “Europe” as if it had a unitary executive, treasury, army, and strategic culture is a category error; it has none of the four in any binding sense. (Some Americans may misunderstand this because their far-right interlocutors in Europe tell them that the EU is a superstate that deprives them of their sovereignty. This is nonsense. Would that it were true.)

Another crucial point: Secondary sanctions on China and India aren’t just “economic pressure.” They’re systemic financial warfare against the non-Western trading bloc—and the EU is hardly in a position to launch a war like that while the US is waging economic war (and political war, and no one can even firmly rule the possibility of military war) on Europe. We’re not talking about leaning on Turkey or the UAE, after all. We’re talking about China, the world’s manufacturing core and the EU’s largest trading partner in goods; and India, the demographic engine of future consumption and a key swing state in the emerging multipolar order. To sanction their purchase of Russian energy is to say, ““Your energy policy requires Atlantic permission.” That’s 1947 logic in a 2025 distribution of power.

It would automatically trigger three structural consequences: Accelerated RMB/rupee trade routing outside dollar–euro rails; financial infrastructure bifurcation (CIPS, SPFS analogues); and the retaliatory industrial murder of the German, French, and Italian export sectors in Asia. The EU can’t absorb that shock. The US might barely survive attempting it. Europe? It just can’t. Especially not with Putin’s highly-developed fifth column in Europe sitting in the wings and salivating at the thought. Secondary sanctions only work when you control the reserve currency, the maritime insurance system, reinsurance, and settlement. Europe controls none of those independently.

Here’s the arithmetic: Europe has already absorbed an energy price shock (only part of which was owed to its own stupid and self-imposed policies), deindustrialization at the margins, defense recapitalization, our trade war, and refugee absorption. Meanwhile, Russia re-routed hydrocarbons east, China re-exported dual-use goods through third states, and India arbitraged refined fuel back into Western markets. For Europe to impose secondary sanctions now would mean cutting *itself* off from Chinese supply chains, Indian refining, and Gulf transshipment, all while lacking strategic energy autonomy (France apart), full military deterrence, and a unified fiscal backstop. This isn’t blandly “non-kinetic”—it’s economic self-evisceration with no guarantee of coercive success.

It’s far from clear, in any case, that economic coercion is a morally cleaner substitute for military force. Probably it is, but large-scale sanctions kill quietly, regress public health, destroy middle classes, and radicalize politics. Europe’s politics are already on the knife’s edge. That’s what’s so clever about Russia’s strategy: Anything Europe does to repel Russia militarily or economically risks handing them Europe electorally.

Also, the kind of economic policy you’re suggesting would require total narrative discipline at home, and Europe doesn’t have that (to say the least). Not in France, not in Italy, not in Germany, not in Slovakia, not anywhere. It’s far easier for European elites to spend on armaments (diffuse, slow, supply-chain-buffered) than to impose immediate factory closures, Asian export collapse, pension fund drawdowns, and car-industry annihilation. So European behavior only looks irrational if you unconsciously use American state capacity as your baseline of feasibility.

One more point: Even if Europe were a unitary state, secondary sanctions on China and India wouldn’t be sanctions in the classical sense. That would be the opening move of financial world war between trading blocs, with no neutral ground left.

So the question isn’t, “Why won’t Europe be rational?” It’s, “Why does Europe find it hard to obtain unanimous consent from all of its member states—which are to varying degrees subverted by Russia and dependent on China, and which do *not* share the same historical memories of Russian imperialism—for a plan to light the fuse on planetary economic war?” If you ask that question, the answer is pretty obvious.

Economic warfare isn’t a painless technocratic instrument. It’s a form of mass coercion with blowback embedded in its DNA. Europeans, rationally, suspect they can’t fight that war and win. They may be wrong to think this—I don’t know—but they’re certainly not nuts to think it. SoEurope’s “moral delusion” is, in fact, something a lot colder and more concrete: strategic paralysis under conditions of declining supremacy, with a US that’s switched sides.

None of this would be true if the US and its allies were still acting in concert. Together, that coalition was formidable enough to sustain these mechanisms for decades. (In fact, the system was so strong that most of the world built its calculations around their permanence.) But instead of sustaining that order, we dismantled it in a fit of pique, having convinced ourselves that we were the victim, not the primary beneficiary, of the very structures from which we derived our power.

There’s a symmetrical point that Americans haven’t yet absorbed. Across the political spectrum, we’re speaking as if we still control the world’s finance, force, shipping, insurance, aid, and technology standards, just as we did at mid-century. In that world, secondary sanctions are just another tool, like the Marines or the IMF. But by the beginning of the 21st century, we no longer controlled those things exclusively. We controlled them as the leader of a powerful coalition. And we’ve just blown that coalition to smithereens.

Europe can’t use the instruments Washington prescribes because those instruments belonged to an order—Atlantic hegemony, dollar primacy, allied consent—that no longer exists. They worked because the West acted as a bloc. Take the bloc away and the tools lose their purchase. Europe now finds itself trapped inside a degrading system. It isn’t refusing to pull a lever that could end the war; it’s discovering, in real time, that the lever no longer connects to anything.

As for “why now,” as opposed to last February, or better still, a decade ago, when Trump won the GOP nomination—God, I wish I knew. But **t**his kind of [](https://claireberlinski.substack.com/p/profiles-in-cowardice-1ea?utm_source=publication-search)**[willful blindness](https://claireberlinski.substack.com/p/profiles-in-cowardice-1ea?utm_source=publication-search)** has a precedent. I used to think people capable of learning from history. I’ve realized now that yes, they are, but only for two generations. When the generation with a direct memory of that history dies, it’s back to the *tabula rasa*.

Why this week, rather than last week? It was the combination of that insane “peace plan,” the sight of those imbeciles in Moscow with Putin leering at them like Jeffrey Epstein welcoming a troop of Girl Scouts, and the new threats to nuke France, in particular: France isn’t as used to that as we are. It’s very obvious, as the panel said, that this is a strategy aimed directly at European publics. When you add to that the NSS, any remaining denial or hope of course correction becomes impossible to sustain.

Also, it’s *not* just now: As the guests on the show pointed out repeatedly, the military’s been warning of this for twenty years. But no one listened.
