The observations Josh makes in this article are rarely made, both because they’re disturbing and because they open the author to charges of elitism, which most writers find unpleasant. This is why it’s all the more important to ask if he might be correct.

Longtime readers of the Cosmopolitan Globalist will hear in his argument a familiar echo. It’s that of the (unrelated) political scientist Shawn Rosenberg, whose work I wrote about in 2019:

Rosenberg argued in a now-famous article titled, “**[Democracy Devouring Itself: The Rise of the Incompetent Citizen and the Appeal of Populism,”](https://www.academia.edu/38564962/Democracy_Devouring_Itself_The_Rise_of_the_Incompetent_Citizen_and_the_Appeal_of_Populism_docx)** [](https://www.academia.edu/38564962/Democracy_Devouring_Itself_The_Rise_of_the_Incompetent_Citizen_and_the_Appeal_of_Populism_docx)that citizens, generally, lack the cognitive skills for democratic governance:

> Drawing on a wide range of research in political science and psychology, I argue that citizens typically do not have the cognitive or emotional capacities required \[for democratic governance\]. Thus they are typically left to navigate in political reality that is ill-understood and frightening. Populism offers an alternative view of politics and society which is more readily understood and more emotionally satisfying. In this context, I suggest that as practices in countries such as the United States become increasingly democratic, this structural weakness is more clearly exposed and consequential, and the vulnerability of democratic governance to populism becomes greater. The conclusion is that democracy is likely to devour itself.

Much like Rosenberg, Josh sees the cognitive limitations of the citizenry at the heart of our dysfunction. Institutions that were once insulated from the whims of stupid citizens, he argues, must now cater to their demands. Many have heralded these transformations as a salutary democratization of public life. But paradoxically, he argues, the Reign of the Idiots is destroying democracy itself.

#### **EXPERTS AND ARSONISTS**

The ongoing right-wing populist challenge to scientific knowledge and objective truth has deep antecedents in our history. Exactly 100 years ago, the Scopes monkey trial captivated the nation, elevating a debate about how the state should define scientific truth—in accordance with expert consensus, or by majority opinion?—to national prominence.

But the breadth of the populist far-right’s current assault on institutional knowledge is unprecedented. In the second Trump administration, anti-vax charlatans have been elevated to the top of our public health bureaucracy. Climate change has been rejected. Politically inconvenient data at the Bureau of Labor Statistics has been suppressed. Medical research and the National Science Foundation have been defunded. Conspiracy theorists, podcasters, and boozy Fox-news hosts have been assigned to top Cabinet positions. Never before in American history has a political movement so sweepingly, recklessly, and completely reoriented public policy away from established knowledge and toward conspiracy theories, medieval superstitions, and partisan fantasies.

The most distinctive feature of contemporary far-right populism is its primary villain, which is neither big business nor hereditary aristocracy. The object of its hostility is an expert class composed of academics, researchers, scientists, journalists, and government bureaucrats. A major reason for this—which functions as both cause and effect—is educational polarization: The diploma divide has replaced wealth and race as the primary fault line in American politics.

The educated class is not blameless. Elite institutions have moved leftward politically, and at times, analysis and decision-making in these institutions have been politicized in ways that have undermined their mission and corroded public trust. At the height of the pandemic, for example, the scientific and medical establishment became notorious for conflicting messaging, incomplete risk-assessment, and lack of transparency, particularly regarding the virus’s origins.

These failures emerged in the context of a larger trend in which prestigious scientific and medical institutions, such as *Scientific American* and the American Academy of Pediatrics, embraced politicized junk science so better to placate increasingly illiberal and illogical progressives who, for example, insisted that sex was a spiritual and not a biological concept. In conjunction with the similarly illiberal and unpopular efforts to socially engineer a new American elite that “looked like America,” such self-indulgent left-wing fads in science and medicine surely contributed to populist animosity towards elite technocrats.

But a persistent pattern of elite failure and ideological capture does not, by itself, explain the way expertise has taken the place of wealth and class as the chief object of populist rage. For example, an especially striking feature of this new flavor of populism is the growing prominence in recent years (and especially since Elon Musk’s Twitter takeover) of a collection of online grifters, quacks, frauds, and trolls who have amassed massive audiences by acting as merchants of doubt. These figures do not confine their attacks to corrupt, out-of-touch technocrats: Many are firmly opposed to the West’s greatest scientific achievements, particularly in immunology and public health.

This phenomenon requires an explanation. Why does it suddenly pay so well to be an arsonist? Why is there such a voracious demand for personalities who deploy their story-telling abilities and reputations to obliterate public confidence in the technologies that make modern life possible? And why have some of the richest people in the world rushed to jump on this bandwagon?

#### **PARADIGM SHIFT**

The different strands and manifestations of far-right populism—the obsession with the “Deep State,” vague conspiratorial language, attacks on expertise and credentialism, the deification of a blustering ignoramus—are united by a central goal: the subversion of modern epistemic hierarchies. We are not witnessing merely a transfer of power from one set of experts and elite gatekeepers to another. This is a far more significant paradigm shift whereby public policy, on issues ranging from public health and trade to monetary policy and the management of our alliances, is informed not by careful study, expert consensus, or a corpus of scientific and technical knowledge amassed over generations, but by popular passions and the collective *Id*, embodied in a charismatic leader.

The ramifications of this transition extend beyond public policy. In media and journalism, too, the maxim, “Respect the audience”—meaning, “Do not tell the audience anything it doesn’t want to hear”—has escaped Fox News to become the dominant ethos in mainstream publications and newsrooms. See, for example, CBS News’s anchor, Tony Dokoupil, explaining the network’s new “audience-centered” journalistic approach:

The term “audience capture” describes the process by which journalists, influencers, and other online content creators gradually adapt and alter their commentary and personae in response to their audience’s demands. In theory, this sounds benign: Isn’t this simply the market at work? But in practice, it transforms previously sober-minded people into deranged, conspiracy-addled cranks.

Fundamentally, audience capture constitutes a form of overfitting. The combined forces of the ruthlessly competitive attention economy, online information silos, and high-resolution audience feedback—in the form of likes, shares, comments, subscriptions, and detailed data about how many readers have read an article or downloaded a podcast—act together to ensure that writers and broadcasters who defy or challenge their audiences do so at the expense of their bank accounts. Those who succeed are, necessarily, those who are most willing to be untethered from fixed principles and beliefs. The result is a race to the bottom in which those who best confirm the biases of their increasingly niche audiences extract the most lavish rewards.

#### **ELITISM IS GOOD**

Audience-capture syndrome underscores the critical importance of institutional distance. The institutions in question have traditionally been aloof and elite for a reason. As we’re seeing, when they lack insulation from the vicissitudes of public opinion, they cease performing their intended roles as guardians of truth and knowledge and instead pander to popular sentiment.

The paradox of far-right populism is that this highly democratic approach to politics and civic engagement is a mortal threat to liberal democracy. This may at first seem puzzling, but ultimately, it reflects the fundamental tension between **[democracy and liberalism](https://substack.com/home/post/p-76988903?source=queue).**

Tension, however, does not imply incompatibility. In fact, the way populist experiments in countries such as Hungary, Turkey, Venezuela, and the Philippines have collapsed into something approximating dictatorship suggests that the plebiscite model—true democracy, unmediated by strong and independent institutions—may be incompatible with human nature at scale, much like other failed political ideologies.

#### **THE REVOLT OF THE COGNITIVELY DISENFRANCHISED**

This bottom-up epistemology has jumped from the online fever swamps to legacy institutions. But why has the ongoing defenestration of institutional gatekeepers spawned this *particular* worldview? Why the lies? Why the conspiracy theories? Why the incessant and incoherent usage of the pronoun *they* to vaguely indicate some unspecified group of elite oppressors? (“*They* won’t tell you this.” “*They* want you to think.” “*They* killed Charlie.”) Most of all, why the idolatrous worship of a functionally illiterate and clearly demented sociopath?

The answer is this. The most profound appeal of far-right populism is that it offers its adherents *legibility*—the illusion of an intelligible world. In this illusory world, people of average intellectual ability can “do their own research” to uncover conspiracies and distinguish truth from lies.

The majority of Americans are unable to read beyond a sixth-grade level. They have been forced to navigate an increasingly complex world in an atomized media environment. This has meant that to the average voter, politics and government have seemed growingly remote and incomprehensible. Until Trump rose to power, this cognitive disenfranchisement left his base sensing, uneasily, that they had been locked out of the dominant culture. They had spent decades listening to politicians and other elites who spoke to them in a language they found inaccessible and stilted. Then Trump invited them in. He spoke, at an elementary level, in simple terms about simple ideas that required no intellectual sophistication to understand. This allowed many Americans to feel, for the first time, like a part of this country and its politics.

These empowered audiences are now reshaping our government and other public-facing institutions. As a consequence, these institutions have progressively ceased to operate in accordance with rigorous intellectual principles. They are instead orienting themselves toward meeting the short-term psychological demands of a scared, confused public.

In its essence, the Trump phenomenon and its culture of lies, conspiracy theories, audience capture, and muddled language is a crowd-sourced, imaginary reality engineered to make the cognitively ill-equipped feel less inadequate about their own confusion and alienation. Trump encourages his base to believe that it experiences these negative sentiments because shadowy forces are conspiring against them, not because they’re intellectually limited and poorly educated.

This is a culture that has evolved to allow people who are overwhelmed by the modern world to feel like sleuths, uncovering dark hidden truths, not hopelessly confused bystanders.

#### **ARTIFICIAL STUPIDITY**

Although far-right populism is animated by hostility toward institutional elites, it could not succeed to the extent that it has without elite acquiescence. Why do MAGA’s more sophisticated adherents go along with it?

The protagonist of Kurt Vonnegut’s 1961 novel, *Mother Night,* is Howard W. Campbell, an American spy posing as a Tucker-Carlsonish shock jock who broadcasts Nazi propaganda to the English-speaking world from Berlin. Campbell likens the totalitarian mind to a cuckoo clock in Hell:

> …a mind which might be linked unto a system of gears where teeth had been filed off at random. Such a snaggle-toothed thought machine, driven by a standard or even by a substandard libido, whirls with the jerky, noisy, gaudy pointlessness of a cuckoo clock in Hell … The dismaying thing about the classic totalitarian mind is that any given gear, though mutilated, will have at its circumference unbroken sequences of teeth that are immaculately maintained …Hence the cuckoo clock in Hell—keeping perfect time for eight minutes and twenty-three seconds, jumping ahead fourteen minutes, keeping perfect time for six seconds, jumping ahead two seconds, keeping perfect time for two hours and one second, then jumping ahead a year. The missing teeth, of course, are simple, obvious truths, truths available and comprehensible even to ten-year-olds in most cases. The willful filling off of a gear of teeth, the willful doing without certain obvious pieces of information … That was how Nazi Germany could sense no important differences between civilization and hydrophobia. That is the closest I can come to explaining the legions, the nations of lunatics I’ve seen in my time.

The willful decision to discard reason selectively, which I’ll call “artificial stupidity,” should by now be familiar. We’ve frequently seen it in powerful people—people who should know better—as they accommodate, excuse, embrace, or ignore America’s rapid transformation from liberal democracy and backbone of the Western alliance to patrimonial police state, friendlier to the world’s most repressive regimes than its erstwhile European allies.

What unites Vonnegut’s prototypical totalitarian and authoritarian apologists like Lee and CAP is the ability to accommodate insanity on a societal scale through the psychological trick of treating craziness as noise and the rest as signal. An attempted coup, the reorientation of American foreign policy away from Europe and toward fascist Russia, a White House that accepts billions in bribes and openly sells presidential pardons, the population of the Cabinet with deranged conspiracy theorists, the use of the FCC to extort favorable news coverage, threats to invade NATO allies—they make their peace with all of it by abstracting it away and placing it in a mental box with a derisive label: “mean tweets,” “toxic externalities,” “Trump being Trump.” A story of autocratic consolidation and the descent of a superpower into corruption, lawlessness, and insanity is thereby laundered so that the willfully deluded can sell it to themselves as a severe but necessary application of the omelet-egg principle.

In the hyper-networked 21st century, citizens are not left to perform this feat of psychological jiujitsu on themselves without help. It’s far easier to willfully file off one’s teeth, as Vonnegut would put it, with a little help from a large group of like-minded friends. Humans are instinctively tribal. While few want to accept that they largely lack minds of their own, history provides enough examples of healthy societies descending into collective madness that this conclusion is hard to escape: There are few limits to the barbarity and insanity most people will embrace if others around them are doing the same.

Social media is an especially potent generator of artificial stupidity—so much so that it’s spawned a “*they*”-heavy, degenerate dialect of English optimized to allow otherwise intelligent people selectively to adopt the mental habits of imbeciles so better to win a game of intellectual hide-and-seek with themselves. The objective of the game: to somehow *not* see the enthusiastic self-immolation of a superpower.

#### **THE AUTHORITARIAN TWO-STEP**

Consider this interview in *The New York Times*, by Andrew Ross Sorkin, of the frenetic Palantir CEO Alex Karp:

For the first fifteen minutes, the two men discuss, *inter alia,* Karp’s decision to support Trump in 2024 even though he had previously condemned him—unambiguously, colorfully, and repeatedly, especially after January 6.

At 04:15, Karp, a self-described lifelong progressive Democrat, excoriates progressive academia for attacking “Western values” such as meritocracy and the rule of law. At 07:30, he accuses Democrats of cowardice for failing to confront the party’s far-left flank, particularly on immigration. Reasonable enough, in isolation.

Things go off the rails, however, when Sorkin asks Karp to reconcile these comments with his (and Palantir’s) support for the lawless and repressive conduct of the Trump administration. At 09:15, Karp avows that the only issues he cares about are immigration and reestablishing deterrence. On both, he argues, Trump has delivered. At 11:45, Sorkin asks him about Palantir’s role in helping the administration to carry out extra-judicial executions in the Caribbean and dispatch masked ICE agents, in unmarked vans, to grab people from the street and spirit them off to foreign gulags. Karp replies with a lecture about the media’s lack of empathy for the working class. If people from fancy schools were regularly dying of fentanyl overdoses, he says, nobody would worry about the constitutionality of blowing up drug boats laden with fentanyl. (None of those boats, as far as we know, were transporting fentanyl, nor is Venezuela a major source of fentanyl.)

On the one hand, it’s not so extraordinary to see the CEO of a company that depends on federal contracts cozying up to the occupant of the White House. But on the other, Karp’s evasiveness represents precisely the kind of elite cowardice he deplores, not to mention a betrayal of the “Western values” he claims to revere.

His condemnation of the academic Left and the Democratic Party may be condign,[1](#footnote-1) but his determination to see no evil in the Trump administration’s unprecedented corruption, criminality, and authoritarianism exposes him as little more than a craven opportunist—one who pontificates about meritocracy and the rule of law, but jettisons both the moment his own financial interests are involved.

Out of expedience, Karp has adjusted his political priorities to align, precisely, with those of a vindictive autocrat. This autocrat, notoriously, delights in using the machinery of the state to punish media elites and business leaders who refuse to toe the line. When challenged, Karp invokes “the working class” like a talisman that absolves him of the responsibility to defend the values he claims to espouse—values now under relentless assault by a man who has power over *him*. In reprimanding the Democratic Party, he is a transparent phony.

This species of willful misdirection and evasive bluster, which I call the *authoritarian two-step*, is now routine. A large collection of media moguls and business leaders, flinching in the face of autocratic pressure, have likewise recast their own pusillanimity in these self-flattering terms: They are merely making a good-faith effort to correct past mistakes and restore trust with the “forgotten man.”

Here’s oligarch Jeff Bezos’s version of the two-step. He explains his **[eleventh-hour intervention](https://claireberlinski.substack.com/p/farewell-washington-post?utm_source=publication-search)** to kill the *Washington Post*’s endorsement of Kamala Harris as follows:

> Most people believe the media is biased … Presidential endorsements do nothing to tip the scales of an election. What presidential endorsements actually do is create a perception of bias. A perception of non-independence. Ending them is a principled decision, and it’s the right one.

(In related news, the heroic Mr. Bezos has responded to the Trump regime’s recent unprecedented FBI raid of a *Washington Post* reporter’s apartment by hiding under his desk and saying absolutely nothing. Also a principled decision, and the right one, I’m sure.)

Here’s recently-anointed CBS News editor-in-chief Bari Weiss doing her best rendition after pulling a *60 Minutes* story about the Trump administration’s El Salvador gulag, sparking a media firestorm:

> Right now, the majority of Americans say they do not trust the press. It isn’t because they’re crazy. To win back their trust, we have to work hard. Sometimes that means doing more legwork. Sometimes it means telling unexpected stories. Sometimes it means training our attention on topics that have been overlooked or misconstrued. And sometimes it means holding a piece about an important subject to make sure it is comprehensive and fair.

The authoritarian two-step isn’t an argument, exactly. It’s a form of sophistry, a rhetorical tactic deployed by bigwigs who lack the courage of their convictions and hope to escape the public’s contempt for their compromises with state repression. While some of its practitioners may not be beyond redemption, all of them have shown that they’re willing to betray freedom—and us—for the right price.

It takes a lot for a once great nation to destroy itself. One corrupt leader can’t do it alone. But we now face a perfect storm: mass ignorance; revolutionary technological change; feckless politicians who are unwilling to enforce the law; craven elites who are prepared to make their peace with a tyrant; and foreign subversion, welcomed by the lawless politicians who benefit from it. Together, and without a shot fired, this storm has brought the world’s greatest empire to its knees. What’s more, in a hyper-networked world, the damage that ensues when a global hegemon collapses this way will not be localized.

The United States will probably remain rich and powerful for many more years. But the accelerating chaos of the past year leaves little doubt that the Pax Americana is no more. Our artificially stupid elites may be able to persuade themselves, for a time, that in acquiescing to madness, they’ve merely followed the dictates of their conscience. No doubt yes-men and their carefully cultivated followers on social media will reinforce them in this belief—for now. But rest assured that those yes-men and social media fans will abandon them when the bill for the madness comes due. Which it will.

It always does.
