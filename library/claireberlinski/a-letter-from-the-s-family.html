They’re still in Islamabad, and they’re still waiting for the word from the Canadian government that their visas have been approved and their travel has been booked. They’ve been through every stage of this process, except for the last: the final approval and issuance of visas.

JIAS—their sponsorship agreement holder— warned us that this wait is often very long and very taxing, emotionally. They weren’t wrong. The most difficult thing is that we just don’t know what’s happening or how much longer it will take. They could be notified tomorrow that they’re flying to Canada in a week’s time, or it could take many more months.

According to the Canadian government’s website, processing times depend on:

  • the number of applications to be reviewed (we don’t know, but we’re sure it’s a lot)

  • the number of refugees we can welcome in a given year (see the Immigration Levels Plan) (the link takes us to a page that’s out of date)

  • the security situation in the area where the refugee is living prior to resettlement (not great)

  • our ability to communicate with the refugee (excellent)

  • how long it takes to complete the medical exam and security screening (done)

  • the refugee’s family profile (family size and age of family members may impact processing time of security screening and medical exams) (it’s a big family)

  • whether dependents are added to the application during processing (no)

  • whether the refugee needs an exit permit to leave the country they are in (no)

  • other barriers that cannot be controlled or anticipated (?)

In other words: We don’t know.

Our uncertainty deepened when we heard the appalling news that an Afghan national had murdered two National Guards in Washington. M— , the son, sent me this message after the attack.1 (I’ve published this before, but you may have missed it.)

This terrible news shocked me and made me very sad, it shocked the whole world, the Afghans here in Pakistan were also really sad. This ugly and inhuman act has also made the lives of millions of Afghans hopeless and confused. This is truly shameful for all Afghans, although crime is a personal act but it is truly regrettable that an Afghan committed this ugly and inhuman act.

Unfortunately, this attack has truly changed the lives of many. Two soldiers who were working hard to keep people safe lost their lives. The families of these two soldiers are also grieving for their loved ones, and the attacker himself destroyed his life and that of his family with this inhuman act. Unfortunately, thousands of immigration applications pending here in Pakistan have also been suspended and these people have also been affected.

In Afghan society, not all Afghans are the same. The Pashtuns of Afghanistan are relatively religious and this person was also a Pashtun. You may know that over 95% of the Taliban are also Pashtuns and the motive for this attack may have been extremist, who knows?

As a Tajik of Afghanistan, I am very afraid of the Pashtuns because they are really dangerous and very religious people. A normal person would never do such a stupid thing. Imagine going from the worst country in the world (Afghanistan) to one of the best countries in the world that is full of opportunities for a better life and instead of building your life and making the future of your children and your wife better, you go and do an inhuman thing. This is really a disgusting thing and a normal person would never do such a thing.

The US government should make it illegal to carry guns because it is extremely dangerous. Because an abnormal person who has access to a gun can harm himself and others, and this is one example of it.

I wish eternal peace to these two American soldiers.

The Canadian government has given no indication that they’re rethinking their asylum policies because of this, or revisiting their vetting process. But it’s hard to imagine that it will have no effect. I’m sure the Canadian bureaucracy will be asking itself—rightly—whether there are lacunae in its vetting processes. Every immigration officer must dread the thought of admitting a refugee who turns out to be a murderer. The professional price for any mistake is surely very steep. Refugees waiting for asylum don’t vote, so there’s no downside to delaying their admission while reviews are conducted, procedures debated, dossiers reviewed twice. It’s easiest, when faced with a decision that might be disastrous, to make no decision at all. So we fear the waiting time has grown longer.

Almost all of the money you’ve sent for this family is waiting for them in a trust fund in Canada. We reserved from funds to cover their expenses for a year in Islamabad, but I sent the last of that to them last month. (I’ve covered their expenses for this month: a big thank you to the new subscribers who made it possible for me to do that). I wasn’t sure that it made sense to do another round of fundraising when they might get word any day that they’ve received their final approval. But I figure nothing is going to happen between now and the New Year, so at the very least, they’ll need money for their rent and expenses in January.

I’m now thinking that “it could happen any day, so we probably don’t need more money” isn’t a rational plan—and it certainly isn’t one that reduces their stress. I think, therefore, that I should ask you for enough money that they won’t have to worry about running out for several more months, at least. But because I simply don’t know how much longer they’ll be waiting, I can’t say, specifically, how much more we need.

So what I suggest is this: Right now, any amount you can give would help. If it turns out that they get good news tomorrow, or in early January, we’ll return any money they don’t need. They have a trust fund waiting for them in Canada, so all they need is enough to cover their expenses for the rest of this waiting period, however long it is.

If this drags on for another year, I’ll have to ask again. But M— is hopeful that this won’t be the case. He says that a few days ago, the Canadian Deputy Minister of Immigration visited Pakistan, and the IRCC announced a staff increase in Islamabad. He takes both as signs that cases will now be processed faster.

Your generosity has thus far been a force of nature. I’m hoping very much that this is the last time we need to ask. And again, even a small contribution is a huge help. Those really add up.

The S— family asked me to share the message below with you.

Dear Friends,

We hope this message finds you in good health and peace. We respectfully request your humanitarian support during this exceptionally challenging time.

We are currently awaiting the processing of our resettlement application to Canada. While we hope to rebuild our lives in safety and dignity, the waiting period has placed an immense strain on our family, both financially and emotionally. As Afghan refugees in Pakistan, we face numerous hardships, including restricted access to many human needs. Additionally, the constant threat of deportation and lack of long-term security makes very difficult situations.

The situation for Afghan refugees here is extremely difficult. Many families live in precarious conditions with limited resources, relying on temporary work or support from charities. Our family is no exception, and without assistance, meeting even basic needs such as food and shelter has become an urgent challenge.

We humbly seek your support to help us navigate this period of uncertainty. Your contribution will directly provide food, daily living supplies, housing, and basic utilities. Your generosity has made a tangible difference, offering relief, dignity, and hope to a family struggling under circumstances beyond their control. Any support you are able to provide, no matter the size, will have a meaningful impact on our well-being during this critical time.

While we are waiting here in Pakistan, we are studying online. T— is studying Law and Political Science at the American University of Afghanistan based in Doha, Qatar. She successfully got a scholarship from American University last year. M— and A— are also studying English-language courses online and they study really hard. When we arrive in Canada it will help them to resettle in the Canadian community easily. Unfortunately, because of the current situation in Pakistan, we as Afghan citizens can not go to the park or outside of the house because of the risk of detention and deportation. This makes us very stressed. Not just our family but most Afghan refugees here in Pakistan are facing a lot of problems because the Pakistani government does not extend visas for us and puts pressure on us.

We are hoping, after these difficult times, to finally arrive in Canada as soon as possible. We hope it will happen soon, so we can start a new life without fear, we will have the right to go to school and university, and we can build a new life for ourselves.

In the current situation, the only hobby for the children of the family is painting: B— and M— (the two youngest children of the family) spend their time painting and studying English, although they should also have the right to go to the park for playing in an open area, and at this age, they should go to school, but unfortunately they don’t have access. Here we will share their painting with you.

Sometimes, for hobbies, our family watches movies. Yes, we have access to fast internet! We usually speak about our new life in Canada when we sit together at dinner. B— always speaks about school, parks, and playing areas, and she is so excited to have a better life in Canada, like all of us.

M— and his father usually go for shopping and to bring groceries home. Because of the risk of deportation, they go for shopping at night. During the day, Pakistani police are searching Afghan citizens to arrest them and deport them back to Afghanistan. There are a lot of Afghan refugees here in Pakistan with same situation like us. When we sent email to IRCC-Islamabad they answer to the email that they are aware of situation of Pakistan and they are working with all capacity to process the cases faster. The good news is the High Commission of Canada accelerated the process of applications, and we hope cases process faster.

We deeply appreciate your compassion and consideration and hope for your support in helping us maintain stability until our resettlement is completed.

With sincere gratitude and respect,

The S— family.
