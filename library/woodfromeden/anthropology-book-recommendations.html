Now to the post on anthropology books:

Anthropology is nothing less than raw data about the limits of human existence. Social studies, economics, engineering and history all study the opportunities of human existence. They collect evidence of what humans are capable of doing under favorable circumstances. Anthropology studies the baseline: What humans can become, under all known circumstances.

Anthropology provides the constraints that prevent evolutionary psychology from becoming pure speculation. The human mind can make up a multitude of perfectly logically possible theories about the past and future of the human race. Anthropology helps sorting the few plausible theories from the many thinkable theories.

Or at least, it should do so. Studying anthropology is difficult. In theory, it should be very simple: Just find the anthropological literature and read it. But finding that literature is really hard. It is hidden in plain sight: Available, but unknown.

The most efficient method I have found is to read a book by an anthropologist that refers to many different books and to meticulously go through the references and make a list of titles to search for. I did this with a book called Sick Societies by Robert Edgerton. Not because I specifically wanted to learn about maladapted cultures, but because I figured out that such a book must contain references to books about cultures in general.

I often think: There must be a better way of doing this. Isn't there some kind of club where people interested in anthropology can share information? I have looked and found nothing in operation. But I can do one thing to reach out to other people who are interested in anthropology: Publish a list about the anthropology books I know and ask everyone who sees it to publish their own.

When choosing anthropology books, I apply one basic rule: I want to be as close as possible to the source. Anthropologists are mere humans, and the rest of us depend on observations filtered through their minds. But the fewer minds through which the information is filtered, the better. For that reason I thoroughly avoid books by non-fieldworkers who challenge the findings of fieldworkers. Those who were there didn't understand everything and didn't always get things right. But those who weren't there know nothing at all.

In the absence of a more organized way to share knowledge of anthropology books, I urge everyone who is interested in anthropology to share their knowledge of books worth reading (or not worth reading) in the comments field. Here is my list:

Anthropology books Tove K has read, partially or entirely, listed by continent:

(Warning! It is a long list. Only for people who are interested in anthropology.)

Australia

Tiwi Wives by Jane Goodale, 1953

About a group of Aborigines of Northern Australia

I have a general problem when reading anthropology books: They make me fall asleep. I don't consider myself sleep deprived. And still, some books make me nod off time after time, making reading almost impossible. As if my subconscious is protecting me from irrelevant information.

On a principal level, the kinship systems of societies around the world are very interesting. The function of those systems is to regulate who is allowed to marry whom and who will provide for whom. In sparsely populated societies, females tend to be seen as major assets. So all in all, the kinship systems of primitive societies are systems for allocating resources. That is, much like the tax code of modern societies.

The fact that humans can create and enforce something like tax codes is plain fascinating. The tax code in itself…not so much. I really want to know the rough outlines of the tax code of all countries of the world. I really don't want to read the tax codes of all countries in the world.

One problem with anthropology is that many, or most, anthropologists really did write down the equivalence of the tax code, word for word, in the societies they studied. As if an alien came to a modern Western country, was intrigued by the redistribution mechanism and sought out the geekiest tax lawyers as their favorite informers. That way they would be very well-informed about one aspect of Western society. But only one aspect. The rest would have been up to the reader to guess from the very detailed reports on the tax law.

This is how many, if not most anthropology books I come across appear. When they don't make detailed reports on primitive tax code, they write detailed reports on ceremonies. As everybody knows, one important point of ceremonies is to distinguish the in-group from the out-group. The course of a ceremony can never be fully guessed by logical thinking. Instead, it must be learned, and that is the point of it: To be so arbitrary that only people who have learned it from other groups members can possibly know it. This phenomenon is in itself extremely interesting. The exact rules of all ceremonies all over the world are not. They are, to a certain extent, merely arbitrary.

Tiwi Wives is the worst kinship and ceremony fixated book I have read (or half-read, since I fell asleep too often and couldn't finish it). It is at the same time sleep-inducing and titillating. The book mentions the intriguing fact that men were obliged to give unlimited amounts of resources to their mothers in law. A mother in law was appointed even before she had a daughter. That way, the son-in-law was normally older than her. She became a mother-in-law just because she was expected to start having children soon. When she then, hopefully, actually had a daughter, the son-in-law adopted that daughter when she was about ten years old.

The idea is intriguing. In effect, men did not only provide for their own children. They financed the production of their wives through supporting their future mother. The costly upbringing of girls was financed by the men who wanted a girl to marry. Or was it? The only thing Jane Goodale writes is that a son-in-law legally had unlimited obligations towards his mother-in-law. She doesn't write what that formal rule actually meant in practical life. Her book contains such teasers slipped in between page after page of sleep-inducing accounts of the exact course of some ceremony or every detail of laws regulating who has the right to marry whom.

Among Cannibals by Carl Lumholz, 1888

About Aborigines of Northern Australia

Carl Lumholz lived between 1851 and 1922. He wrote entirely readably. He visited the dangerous, non-civilized Australian Aborigines of his time. In other words, his book is a first-hand account of a pre-civilizational stone age society. Can anthropology get any better than this?

The Life and Adventures of William Buckley by John Morgan, first published in 1852.

About Aborigines of south-eastern Australia

William Buckley was a British man born in 1776. At the age of 23 he was sentenced to deportation to Australia for theft. After a short time in a prison camp he decided to escape together with two other inmates, trying the reach Sydney. After some time the men took different paths and Buckley was found by Aborigines who believed that white people were spirits of dead family members. Buckley thereby came to be treated as a family member. He lived with the Aborigines for 32 years before he managed to reconnect with white society. During that time he became a very highly appreciated member of his aboriginal community and was given several wives.

Buckley's inside account of uncontacted Aboriginal society has been discredited because Buckley was a convict and because both he and John Morgan, who wrote the book, were poor and could use the money from it. I find that criticism very strange. A man who lived with Aborigines for most of his life must be lying because he was poor, but academics who desperately claw for tenure should be assumed to be truthful? A rare eyewitness account that deserves to be read.

Castaway: The extraordinary survival story of Narcisse Pelletier, a young French cabin boy shipwrecked on Cape York in 1858, by Robert Macklin, 2019

About Aborigines of northern Australia

At the age of 14, Narcisse Pelletier, a boy from Marseille, France, was shipwrecked and adopted by a group of Aborigines. He lived with them for 17 years. He was initiated into their ceremonial life the same way as native teenage boys. His “rescue" wasn't entirely voluntary from his side and he never established well in white society again. His story was recorded a few years after he returned to France. Although he chose to remain vague on some subjects he told his interviewer about others.

Islanders and Aborigines at Cape York: An ethnographic reconstruction based on the 1848-1850 "Rattlesnake" journals of O.W. Brierly and information he obtained from Barbara Thompson by David R. Moore

About Torres Strait Islanders

At age 16, Barbara Thompson, a girl from modest circumstances in New South Wales, suffered a shipwreck together with her husband William Thompson. She was the only survivor of the shipwreck and was adopted by the Kaurareg people, who, like in the case of William Buckley, believed white people were spirits of their own dead. Barbara replaced a recently deceased teenage daughter of the chief of her group. When rescued she chose freely to return to white civilization. The book is detailed and contains valuable information on the lives of women.

Sketch of a Residence Among the Aboriginals of Northern Queensland for Seventeen Years by James Morrill, 1863

About Aborigines of northern Australia

James Morrill was an English sailor who suffered shipwreck in 1846, when he was 22 years old. Like Narcisse Pelletier, he was saved by Aborigines and spent 17 years with them. When Queensland was increasingly colonized he managed to contact the colonizers and return to white society. He wrote a shorter text about things he knew about the people he lived with for such a long time.

The aborigines of Victoria: with notes relating to the habits of the natives of other parts of Australia and Tasmania by Robert Brough Smyth, 1878

Very long and detailed. The parts on marriage and relationships between the sexes are particularly informative.

The aboriginal Australians: The first pioneers by Ronald and Catherine Berndt, 1983

Summary volume about Australian Aborigines

I have found it extremely difficult to find a readable and comprehensive volume about Australian Aborigines. This one proved to be the exception.

The Australian Aborigines : a portrait of their society, by Kenneth Maddock, 1972

Summary volume about Australian Aborigines

I read this early on and it gave me a sense of hopelessness over anthropology. It is filled with theoretical bullshit of its time and its author tries to fit in his information about Aboriginal Australia in that bullshit. Contains little useful information about native Australians.

Growing up Aboriginal in Australia, by Anita Heiss, 2018

About contemporary Australian Aborigines

I didn't know that parodies of Woke and Woke itself were so similar. This book contains close to zero information, except that people with some Native Australian blood can have a lot of feelings, in particular if someone asks them how many percent aboriginal they really are.

Africa

The Hadza: Hunter-Gatherers of Tanzania by Frank Marlowe, 2010

An ordinary ethnography of the Hadza. Since those people have been pacified and contacted since more than a century ago, they hardly represent the past. Nonetheless, they have an interesting lifestyle.

Nisa: The Life and Words of a !Kung Woman by Marjorie Shostak, 1981

When studying the Bushmen of Kalahari, anthropologist Marjorie Shostack chose the best female storyteller she could find among them and wrote her life story. Nisa, then in her fifties, was probably not the most representative Bushwoman. But I think that is the point of anthropology: It is about the possibilities of human existence. Just as anthropology is not about describing the average human society, it doesn't need to describe average people of the societies it studies either.

The Nuer: A Description of the Modes of Livelihood and Political Institutions of a Nilotic People by Edward Evan Evans-Pritchard

A very ordinary ethnography.

The Forest People by Colin M. Turnbull, 1961

Sympathetic description of Mbuti Pygmies in central Africa. Entirely readable.

The Mountain People by Colin M. Turnbull, 1972

The worst-written anthropology book I have ever opened. It is a mystery how the same man could write both a readable book on the Pygmies and an entirely unreadable book on the Ik of Tanzania. The concept is really interesting: A society falling apart into appalling selfishness. Unfortunately it is so badly written that I couldn't read through it. Colin Turnbull consistently writes about the wrong things, like his car trips between different Ik settlements, instead of actually writing about the Ik.

A Year in Azimba and Chipitaland: The Customs and Superstitions of the People by H. Crawford Angus, 1898

A shorter text by the leader of an expedition sent out to scout for labor in central Africa.

America

Books about the Yanomamö (the biggest recently contacted people of the Amazon rainforest)

Yanomamö - The Fierce People by Napoleon Chagnon, first published in 1968

Some of his colleagues accused Napoleon Chagnon of excessive self-promotion. I have no idea about that, but I know one thing: He most probably wouldn't need to self-promote much to reach his level of fame, because he had an ability that most anthropologists lack: He could write. Most anthropology books are badly written in one way or another. Napoleon Chagnon’s books are not. So why wouldn't people want to read them?

Noble Savages: My Life Among Two Dangerous Tribes - the Yanomamo and the Anthropologists by Napoleon Chagnon, 2013

Napoleon Chagnon wrote one book early in life and one book late in life, with a kind of continuum of different alerted editions in between. The latest book includes an account of the terrible smear campaign he had to endure. But mostly it summarizes the research of his life and provides more details about the Yanomamö.

Into The Heart: One Man's Pursuit of Love and Knowledge Among the Yanomami, 1991

Kenneth Good is most of all known as the forty-something anthropologist who married a Yanomamö teenage girl and took her to the United States, where she bore him three children. Into the Heart is his autobiographical account of this story.

As an anthropology book it is not great. Kenneth Good worked with weighing hunted animals for Napoleon Chagnon. The book says very little about how much the animals actually weighed. It only alludes very vaguely that it was too little to support Napoleon Chagnon's hypothesis that the Yanomamö ate adequate amounts of protein. There is too much focus on Kenneth's point of view. For example, he writes about an incident where a woman had previously been abducted and her former husband came to take her back. The former husband and the abductor pulled the woman in one arm each for half an hour or so, with the woman screaming that any of them could take her, she didn't care, they just had to stop pulling her in two directions. An old man got tired of the disorder, took up his arrows and said he would stab the woman to make it stop. Then Kenneth yelled: “Don't do that!”. The man then turned back and did not stab the woman. That's the end of the story. We don't get any more information. We don't get to know how the conflict developed after Kenneth intervened. (Page 71 of print version)

The main merit of the book is to confirm Napoleon Chagnon's assertions about the Yanomamö. Kenneth Good describes his conflicts with and dislike of Napoleon Chagnon in detail. So if he reports about the same phenomena as Napoleon Chagnon, that can't be because he liked him, but presumably because life among the Yanomamö really was that way.

The Way Around: Finding My Mother and Myself Among the Yanomami, by David Good, 2016

David Good is the eldest of the three children of Kenneth Good and his Yanomamö wife. He was five years old when his mother remained in the rainforest while Kenneth took their children back to the United States.

David tells about the difficulties of being abandoned by his mother (who, he means, didn't leave her children entirely voluntarily) and his efforts to find his mother in the rainforest. The Yanomamö who David met were much more influenced by white society than those his father met, but his accounts of Yanomamö society in the early 2000s is exciting in its own right. (David Good is not officially an anthropologist, but a biologist).

Yanoama: The Story of Helena Valero, a Girl Kidnapped by Amazonian Indians by Ettore Biocca, 1966

As the title indicates. I summarized it in a post called The Survival Skills of Helena Valero. One of the most valuable anthropology books of all times.

Spirit of the Rainforest - The Story of a Yanomamo Shaman by Mark Andrew Ritchie, 1996

Novel-style account of the memories and inner world of Jungleman, a Yanomamö shaman who lived through a time of great change. The psychological depth of this book makes it absolutely unique among anthropology books.

Growing Up Yanomamo: Missionary Adventures in the Amazon Rainforest by Mike Dawson, 2006

Mike Dawson is the son of missionary Joe Dawson, who is mentioned frequently by Jungleman in Spirit of the Rainforest. Mike comes across as a sympathetic but rather jockish person. His writing style and (I suppose) personality reminds me of physicist Richard Feynman in his memoir You Surely Must be Joking Mr Feynman. No deep intellectual insights or intrusive observations to be found in this book. But a few interesting anecdotes.

Tales of the Yanomami - Daily Life in the Venezuelan Forest by Jacques Lizot, 1985

Jacques Lizot, a linguist, spent 20 years among the Yanomamö. His book is journalistic rather than analytic. It closely describes some details of everyday life, like children's play and love affairs.

Another book, Spirit of the Rainforest, describes in detail how Lizot (who is not being named in the book) bought sex from young boys for trade goods, how he poisoned food that he knew Yanomamö people would steal and how he stole the head of a dead young man because someone had promised to pay a significant sum for such a head. If that is true, Jacques Lizot himself was even more amoral than the main informer he chose for Tales of the Yanomami.

According to Wikipedia, Lizot confirmed having intimate relationships with Yanomamö males, but called the boys “consenting adults". That gives an explanation to how Lizot could know more than other anthropologists about the sexual exploration of Yanomamö boys: He took part in it himself, or hoped that he was doing so.

A book worth reading, but I skipped the long parts on mythology.

Books about other South American peoples:

Ache Life History - The Ecology and Demography of a Foraging People by Kim Hill and Magdalena Hurtado, 1996

One of the greatest challenges of anthropologists in the late 20th century was that almost everyone who was willing to talk to an anthropologist had adopted a peaceful and sedentary lifestyle. Kim Hill and Magdalena Hurtado ingeniously solved this through asking old people who have lived much of their lives in their forest about their past. They achieved an impressive demography, unique in its kind I think.

Hans Staden's True History: An Account of Cannibal Captivity in Brazil by Hans Staden, 1557

One of the oldest books in my collection. Hans Staden was captured by cannibals in Brazil and scheduled to be eaten at their next feast. He only escaped through manipulating his way out, helped by his Christian faith that also started to impress his captors. His truthfulness has been questioned, but again: Why would a person who lived with savages be less truthful than an academic in need of tenure?

The Narrative of Cabeza de Vaca by Alvar Nuñez Cabeza de Vaca, 1542

The story about the only four survivors of a disastrous expedition to North America in 1527. The four managed to get the status as prophets or even gods among the natives, who were extremely impressed by their Christian religion. For the rest of his life, Cabeza the Vaca strongly recommended soft methods of subjugating native peoples, erroneously believing that all natives were as easily impressed as those he had encountered. That way, Cabeza de Vaca can probably be counted as the world's first Woke person.

Reading about the phenomenon of 16th century Woke was interesting, but I had hoped for more information about the native Americans. Little is told about their societies.

Don't Sleep, There Are Snakes: Life and Language in the Amazonian Jungle by Daniel Everett, 2008

Missionary Daniel Everett visits a small group of Native Americans who don't believe in supernatural things much at all. They also refuse to believe that one plus one equals two. One of many stories about how semi-isolated groups of humans can organize themselves.

Uttermost part of the earth: Indians of Tierra del Fuego by Lucas Bridges, 1948

Lucas Bridges was the son of missionary Thomas Bridges. He got to know the native Americans of Tierra del Fuego intimately before they all died (except some half-bloods) in epidemics in the 1930s. Through writing his memoirs before he died, Lucas Bridges saved knowledge that would otherwise be lost forever.

Never in Anger: Portrait of an Eskimo Family by Jean Briggs, 1976

Jean Briggs, then about 35 years old, lived with an Inuit family in northern Canada for one and a half years. The Inuits in question were still nomads who lived from hunting and fishing, but they lived peacefully, were devout Christians and sent their children to boarding school. The book focuses excessively on childrearing, as if child rearing methods were thought to be extremely important by that time.

The Hutterites in North America by Rod Janzen, 2010

Self-explaining. Who doesn't want to know about the Hutterites?

Cut Me Loose: Sin and Salvation After My Ultra-Orthodox Girlhood by Leah Vincent, 2014

Leah Vincent tells the story of her upbringing and what made her leave Ultra-Orthodox Judaism. Through her writing she comes across as a both honest and sane person, which can't be said about all autobiographies by people who have left the Ultra-Orthodox community: Unorthodox by Deborah Feldman contains an almost certainly false anecdote about a man murdering his 13 year old son after amputating his penis with a jigsaw (I don't want to think too much about it, but is that even physically possible?). The real story behind the rumor was a spectacular suicide where a 20 year old man managed to behead himself with a circular saw.

And another leaver autobiography, Brazen, is written by a woman who brags extensively over lying about her age. Being proud of lying is not a good property of an ethnographer. In contrast to this colleague, Leah Vincent comes across as an honest and critically thinking person. It could be that Leah is mentally ill and attention-seeking as her father suggests, but that is really not the impression she makes in writing. Her book is nuanced and not always overly flattering to its author.

Leah is not representative of her former community because she left and most young people stay. The problem is that people who are loyal to the community hardly ever write naked autobiographies about its inner workings. I think that in the absence of an inside perspective, Leah Vincent's book is a good substitute, because she makes it very clear that it was small details that made her leave. For example, when she told her mother that she wanted to go to university, her mother bluntly threatened to send her to a mental hospital and thereby shatter her marriage prospects if she persisted. Leah states that if her mother had acted a bit more patiently to that expression of rebellion, she would probably have stayed in the Ultra-Orthodox community. All in all, her parents appear to have employed a low-investment child rearing strategy. It is easy to imagine how the system where Leah failed to fit in can work better with slightly warmer people making a bit more forgiving everyday decisions.

Strictly Observant: Amish and Ultra-Orthodox Jewish Women Negotiating Media by Rivka Neriya-Ben Shahar, 2024

Rivka Neriya-Ben Shahar follows an Israeli Ultra-Orthodox woman and an American Amish woman for a short time. Since her subjects are self-selected for being friendly and willing to show off their everyday life, her account provides a good contrast to leaver autobiographies like the one of Leah Vincent.

The Longest Mile: A vivid chronicle of life in an Appalachian hollow by Rena Gazaway, 1969

A bestseller in its time, it probably said more then compared to now. My main impression of the hillbillies was that they seldom washed. For a non-native English speaker like me the phonetic dialogue is plain incomprehensible.

Jungle People: A Kaingang Tribe of the Highlands of Brazil by Jules Henry, 1941

A fairly readable account of a people who, according to themselves, had been agriculturalists in the 18th century but were now hunter-gatherers. According to Jules Henry, they were on the road to extinguishing themselves through revenge spirals.

Mehinaku: The Drama of Daily Life in a Brazilian Indian Village by Thomas Gregor, 1977

Every time needs its bullshit. And theorizing about daily life as theater seems to have been bullshit in vogue in the 1970s. Otherwise interesting to the degree that isolated and pacified Amazonian tribes of a few hundred people are interesting.

Origin: A Genetic History of the Americas by Jennifer Raff, 2022

Specked with Woke sermons and too vivid made-up anecdotes, as if written by someone who entertains students for a living. But on the whole on the readable side. And I didn't find any other book about the genetics of Native Americans.

Europe

Bury Me Standing: The Gypsies and Their Journey by Isabel Fonseca, 1995

About Gypsies in the Balkans in the early 1990s. Sometimes the author herself reveals strange opinions, like when she both writes that her study subjects steal clothes from clotheslines in their neighborhoods and that the hostility of their neighbors must be based on prejudice (clothes were not exactly growing on trees in Eastern Europe in the 1990s). But the book seems both ambitious, truthful and honest.

The Moral Basis of a Backward Society by Edward C. Banfield, 1958

Describes southern Italy in the 1950s as lacking in civic spirit. But not hyper deep really.

Germania by Tacitus, circa 98 AD

One of the earliest works of anthropology in world history and that also features the probable ancestors of Northern Europeans, that is, both myself and many of the people reading this. To my knowledge, it is the best source those of us who are of Northern Europeans blood have for the lives of our ancestors.

Asia

The Dugum Dani: A Papuan Culture in the Highlands of West New Guinea by Karl G. Heider, 1970

Gardens of War: Life and Death in the New Guinea Stone Age by Robert Gardner and Karl G. Heider, 1974

Not the most readable books. But they provide very interesting content about densely populated and still very primitive cultures.

Guests of the Sheik: An Ethnography of an Iraqi Village by Elizabeth Warnock Fernea, 1965

Elizabeth Warnock married anthropologist Robert Fernea in 1956 and followed him to an Iraqi village where women were highly secluded. As a woman she could get access to their world.

Bartered Brides: Politics, Gender and Marriage in an Afghan Tribal Society by Nancy Tapper, 1991

Slightly badly written book on a very interesting subject. There is juicy information in here, but hidden among boring stuff.

Excess and Restraint: Social Control among a New Guinea Mountain People by Ronald M. Berndt, 1962

I found this book through a reference to its grisliest story about a woman accidentally cutting off a man's penis when he was doing necrophilia while she thought it was time for cannibalism. This book describes a human society that did something very unusual: They ate people unceremoniously, just for the meat. I wouldn't say that it must necessarily be false. There are so many groups of humans, so why wouldn't one small group develop in a weird direction? But it is also true that people all over the world like to trick anthropologists with weird stories. So I think that the weirder a story, the higher the probability that it is made-up. This book lacks reports about how the anthropologists made their fact-checking. For example, the story about the accidentally amputated penis is told like any anecdote. We don't get to know for how long after the man survived and we don't get to know whether the anthropologists ever saw a man without a penis themselves. Information that would be very useful to have, given that penis amputations are very hazardous operations under primitive conditions. Also, penis amputations seem to happen more often in people's imagination than in reality: See the review of Cut me Loose.

And did Ronald and Catherine ever witness the casual eating of a human, or were they just told that people did so? That is also not entirely obvious from the book.

General

The Evolution of War - A Study of Its Role in Early Societies by Maurice R Davie, 1929

One of the first anthropology books I read, more than a decade ago. By then I hadn't formed the habit of always checking the publication date before I read a book. So I was a bit confused over the frequent use of the word “negro". Otherwise I didn't notice that it was an old book; good anthropology books age well. And this is a good introduction to patterns of warfare among primitive peoples a hundred years ago, at a time when there were many more not-yet-pacified small-scale societies.

The book is an excellent introduction to anthropology. Its author, Maurice Rea Davie, 1893-1964, seems to be more or less forgotten.

After opening the book again, I see that it could serve as a collection of older anthropology sources. Contrary to in most sciences, in anthropology age is a big advantage, since the longer back we get, the more diverse the human race becomes.
