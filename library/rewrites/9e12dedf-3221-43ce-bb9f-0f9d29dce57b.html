The Billion-Dollar Promise of Seeing Inside Yourself

Kim Kardashian posted about her full-body MRI scan to 363 million Instagram followers. Paris Hilton endorsed the same company. Cindy Crawford and Miranda Kerr followed suit. Cardiologist Rohin Francis, who goes by Med Life Crisis on YouTube, watched this parade of celebrity endorsements and decided to explain why the medical profession keeps telling people not to do what the world's most famous influencers are telling them to do.

The video is a careful dismantling of the consumer full-body scan industry, delivered by someone with genuine credentials in the field. Francis is a practicing cardiologist who is also accredited in cardiac CT, ultrasound, and MRI, and who completed three years of doctoral research in MRI. He is not a generalist speculating about imaging. He is someone who orders these scans daily and lives with their consequences.

The False Positive Trap

Francis opens with what he considers the fundamental problem: false positives. The promise of full-body scanning rests on the intuition that more information is always better, that looking inside a healthy body can only help. But Francis argues this intuition collides with basic statistics.

In a healthy population, the rate of serious disease is low. When the pre-test probability of disease is low, even a highly accurate test will produce far more false positives than true positives. Francis calls this the base rate fallacy:

If you take the population as a whole, thankfully the rate of disease is low. This means the true positive rate is low. Therefore, even with a positive test result, there's a high likelihood that that is a false positive.

The math is unforgiving. A 99 percent accurate test applied to a thousand unselected people looking for a disease with one percent prevalence will flag roughly ten false positives for every true positive. The younger and healthier the person being scanned, the worse the ratio becomes.

Then there are incidentalomas, the medical term for blobs, spots, and lumps found on scans that have nothing to do with why the scan was ordered. Francis notes that 20 to 40 percent of clinically indicated scans, tests ordered because a doctor suspects a specific problem, find incidental abnormalities. Of those, only about one percent turn out to be clinically significant. On an asymptomatic screening scan, everything is an incidental finding, because there was no presenting complaint in the first place.

The Cascade Nobody Warns You About

What happens after a scan finds something ambiguous is where Francis's argument gains its sharpest edge. A five-millimeter spot on a lung could be a harmless nodule or early cancer. Nobody knows. The radiologist recommends a follow-up scan in six months. The patient, who walked in feeling fine, is now in the system.

A healthy person can go from being a carefree, happy-go-lucky person to a patient in the blink of an eye. You're in the system now. Pulmonary nodule, unclear significance, repeat in six months. Imagine the anxiety this could cause some people.

Francis calls this snowballing effect a "cascade of care." One test leads to another, which may lead to a biopsy, which may lead to surgery. The expenditure is not merely financial but emotional and temporal. And for those in insurance-based healthcare systems, a finding of uncertain significance can create problems obtaining coverage.

When "Catching It Early" Is an Illusion

The most sophisticated section of Francis's argument tackles the cluster of statistical biases that make early detection stories so misleading. He identifies three in particular.

Lead time bias occurs when a screening scan detects a cancer before it causes symptoms, making it appear that the patient survived longer after diagnosis when in reality the date of death has not changed at all. If a cancer arises in 2020 but would not cause symptoms until 2022 and kills by 2025, diagnosing it in 2020 creates the appearance of five years of survival instead of three, without any therapy having changed the outcome.

Length bias means that slow-growing, indolent cancers are far more likely to be caught by periodic screening than fast-growing aggressive ones, precisely because they sit around for years doing little harm. The dangerous cancers that would actually benefit from early detection are, paradoxically, the ones least likely to be found this way.

Overdiagnosis is where abnormalities that would never have caused problems are treated anyway. Francis cites the well-known medical aphorism about prostate cancer:

Men tend to die with prostate cancer, not from prostate cancer.

He brings devastating real-world evidence to support this concern. Between 1979 and 2009, rates of thyroid cancer diagnosis in the United States increased 300 percent as radiologists diligently reported every thyroid nodule they found. Thousands of people underwent surgical thyroid removal and lifelong hormone replacement therapy. Yet deaths from thyroid cancer remained unchanged. South Korea experienced the same phenomenon, with a 15-fold increase in thyroid cancer rates. In 2014, the Korean government ran a public information campaign to dissuade people from getting thyroid screening scans. Cancer rates dropped. Thyroidectomies plummeted. Deaths from thyroid cancer did not increase.

The Counterpoint: Personal Agency and Edge Cases

Francis is careful not to present himself as an absolutist. He acknowledges that some individuals have been helped by these scans and that he does not advocate banning the companies that provide them. He quotes radiologist and author Saurabh Jha, who captured the tension neatly in an exchange with Mark Cuban on Twitter:

If I put my physician cap on, then I realize that this is all just humbug bordering on quackery. But then I have another cap, which is a sort of libertarian cap, which is that people can do whatever the hell they want with their organs and their money.

There is also the legitimate argument that medicine has historically been paternalistic, that gatekeeping information from patients can itself cause harm, and that some people have genuine risk factors that their primary care doctors have missed. Francis concedes that doctors sometimes fail to listen and that bad experiences with the medical profession are real. His objection is not to patient agency but to companies that exploit dissatisfaction with mainstream medicine while glossing over the statistical realities of unselected screening.

It is also worth noting that Francis's analysis leans heavily on population-level statistics. For any individual who does turn out to have an early-stage cancer caught by a screening scan, the population-level data is cold comfort. The challenge is that neither the patient nor the scan company knows in advance which side of the statistics any given person will land on.

The System-Level Costs

Francis raises a point that rarely surfaces in the celebrity-driven conversation about full-body scans: the downstream burden on public health systems. Even if only one percent of screening results require additional testing, the absolute numbers are enormous if millions of healthy people start getting scanned. In the United Kingdom, Francis reports already receiving referrals from general practitioners whose patients obtained private scans that found incidental results. A vascular surgery colleague describes clinics overwhelmed by patients who had carotid artery scans in supermarket parking lots.

This is not a uniquely British problem. Francis points out that the United States spends more of its GDP on healthcare than any other country, with worse outcomes. Any increase in unnecessary testing raises costs for all taxpayers, regardless of whether the system is publicly or privately funded.

Bottom Line

Francis delivers a thorough, evidence-based critique of the consumer full-body scan industry. His strongest contributions are the explanations of lead time bias, length bias, and overdiagnosis, concepts that are genuinely counterintuitive and that the celebrity endorsement ecosystem never addresses. The thyroid cancer data from the United States and South Korea is particularly compelling, offering a natural experiment in what happens when a population embraces and then retreats from aggressive screening.

The video's weakness is structural rather than substantive. It originated as a YouTube video, and the transcript retains the conversational digressions, sponsor reads, and self-deprecating asides of that format. The core argument, which could be stated in a tight 1,500 words, is buried in roughly five times that length. Francis also does not engage deeply with the strongest version of the pro-screening argument: that imaging technology is improving rapidly, that artificial intelligence may soon reduce false positive rates dramatically, and that the statistical calculus could shift as resolution and specificity improve. His critique is correct for today's technology, but the industry's bet is on tomorrow's.

What Francis gets most right is the emotional architecture of the sales pitch. The scan companies sell the illusion of control to people who are anxious about their health. The celebrities who promote them are, by Francis's charitable reading, genuinely trying to help. But the statistical reality remains that for a healthy person without symptoms or risk factors, the most effective health interventions are the ones everyone already knows: diet, exercise, sleep, and not smoking. No scan changes that prescription, and one might even undermine it. As Francis half-jokes, a clean scan result might be the worst thing for his diet, giving him permission to eat fast food every night with a clear conscience.
