When a Murder Becomes a Mirror

Rohin Francis, the British cardiologist behind the Med Life Crisis YouTube channel, recorded this off-the-cuff video from a hospital car park, just days after the murder of UnitedHealthcare CEO Brian Thompson and the subsequent arrest of suspect Luigi Mangione. The result is less a polished analysis than a real-time reckoning with why so many Americans greeted a killing with laughter instead of grief. Francis brings the perspective of a National Health Service physician watching from across the Atlantic -- close enough to understand the system, distant enough to see what those inside it sometimes cannot.

The video's central observation is blunt: the public response to Thompson's murder was not sympathy but something closer to catharsis. Francis describes a "meme pandemic" that united left and right, a wave of black humor from medical professionals and ordinary citizens alike.

It was a murder. It was a murder of a middle-aged guy, a father, and it provoked, let's be honest, kind of sheer glee.

He is careful to state that killing is wrong, that Thompson did not personally engineer the American healthcare system, and that no individual deserves to be executed on a sidewalk. But he argues the reaction is intelligible -- even predictable -- given the scale of institutional cruelty that health insurance companies have normalized.

UnitedHealthcare as a Case Study in Corporate Indifference

Francis lays out the specifics. UnitedHealthcare, at the time of Thompson's death, was one of the largest publicly traded companies in America, pulling in over 370 billion dollars in revenue. Its claims rejection rate was reportedly double the industry average. The company had been investigated for using artificial intelligence systems known to produce higher denial rates -- a detail Francis frames as emblematic of the "faceless, emotionless, cold interaction" that defines the patient experience for millions of Americans.

Thompson himself had faced scrutiny over insider trading allegations. None of this, Francis emphasizes, justifies murder. But it explains why the usual script -- public mourning, solemn condolences, calls for unity -- failed to materialize. The man killed was the face of a company that many Americans experienced as an active obstacle to staying alive.

These are not things that people do through fault of their own. Nobody should be blamed for their health, particularly if you think of examples like somebody having a cardiac arrest and then being taken in an ambulance to a hospital that's out of network. How could they have changed that? They're unconscious.

Violence by Spreadsheet

The most philosophically ambitious stretch of the video concerns the definition of violence itself. Francis raises a question that had been circulating widely in the aftermath: is violence only something committed with a gun or fists, or can the systematic denial of life-saving treatment constitute its own form of lethal force?

He draws a parallel to the 2008 financial crisis, where the abstract destruction of livelihoods through banking malpractice prompted a similar cultural moment -- the rise of "late-stage capitalism" memes, the sense that the powerful had escaped consequences while ordinary people suffered. The pandemic, Francis notes, accelerated this dynamic, producing an enormous upward transfer of wealth while the majority struggled.

The comparison between stealing a wallet and stealing a livelihood is not new, but Francis extends it: when the corporation doing the stealing is in the business of health, the stakes are not merely financial but existential. A denied claim is not just a billing dispute. It can be a death sentence.

There is a counterpoint worth considering here. Insurance companies operate within a regulatory framework that permits -- and in some cases incentivizes -- aggressive claims management. Individual CEOs inherit these structures; they did not create them from scratch. The systemic nature of the problem means that removing one executive changes nothing about the incentive architecture. If anything, the focus on Thompson as a symbolic target risks obscuring the policy failures that enable the behavior in the first place. Legislative reform, not retributive violence, is the mechanism by which insurance practices actually change.

The View from Outside

Francis's most useful contribution may be his outsider's vantage point. He acknowledges that the British National Health Service is not functioning well -- "criminally underfunded" for roughly fifteen years, he says -- but pushes back against the tendency of both Americans and Britons to treat the United States and the United Kingdom as the only two models that exist.

Australia, New Zealand, South Korea, Japan, Germany, France, Sweden, and Singapore all maintain healthcare systems that outperform both the American and British models on key metrics. The American insistence on unrestricted market capitalism in healthcare, Francis argues, produces outcomes that would be unthinkable in peer nations. A system designed for selling iPhones does not translate to a system for keeping people alive.

This is a fair point, but it also simplifies the challenge. The United States is not merely choosing the wrong policy from a menu of proven alternatives. Its healthcare system is entangled with employer-based insurance, pharmaceutical lobbying, federalism, and a political culture that treats any government intervention as ideologically suspect. The countries Francis cites did not simply decide to have better healthcare; they built those systems over decades under different political conditions. The prescription is correct -- look at what works elsewhere -- but the path from here to there is considerably harder than the comparison implies.

Humor as the Last Resort of the Powerless

Francis spends time defending the dark humor that erupted after Thompson's death, particularly among healthcare workers. Doctors and nurses, he notes, have always used gallows humor as a coping mechanism. But the memes that followed this killing were not confined to medical professionals. They spread across the entire population, crossing political lines in a way the recent election had not.

When you leave people feeling powerless, what do they have left except their anger and laughter as a coping mechanism?

He closes by quoting Jia Tolentino's article in The New Yorker, which noted that healthcare workers absorb 73 percent of all non-fatal workplace violence -- the rage and panic of a system that generates billions in profit precisely because it denies care to desperate people. Tolentino's question, which Francis adopts as his own, is pointed: who will drop their indifference first? It will not be the patients, who have a lifetime of evidence that the system does not care about them. Can the executive class afford to care while still delivering record quarterly profits?

Francis also invokes the famous line attributed to Clarence Darrow -- "I've never celebrated a man's death, but there are some obituaries that I've read with great pleasure" -- and a quote from Robert F. Kennedy: "Those who make peaceful revolution impossible make violent revolution inevitable." The pairing captures the video's uneasy conclusion. The murder was wrong. The response was understandable. And the conditions that produced both remain entirely unchanged.

Bottom Line

Rohin Francis delivers an honest, if rambling, assessment of why the murder of a health insurance CEO was met with mockery rather than mourning. The video's strength lies in its willingness to hold two truths simultaneously: that killing a person is indefensible, and that the American healthcare system inflicts its own form of violence on millions of people every year. The weakness is the one Francis himself acknowledges -- this is off-the-cuff commentary, not a structured argument. He digresses, repeats himself, and leaves several threads dangling. But the core question he raises -- whether systematic denial of care constitutes violence, and what happens when people conclude that it does -- remains one of the most important questions in American public life. The fact that the conversation has not meaningfully advanced since Thompson's death suggests that the conditions for the next such crisis remain firmly in place.
