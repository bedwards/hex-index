A Viral Screenshot and the Death of Reading Comprehension

Financial social media has a recurring disease: someone posts a decontextualized screenshot, thousands of people share it without checking the source, and a wave of apocalyptic commentary follows. Richard Coffin of The Plain Bagel diagnoses the latest outbreak, tracing how a single Bloomberg bullet point about the Federal Reserve's Jackson Hole speech mutated into widespread claims that Jerome Powell had abandoned the 2% inflation target and was steering the United States toward hyperinflation.

The actual story, as Coffin discovered by doing what he drily calls "the unthinkable" — reading the 20-minute speech transcript — is considerably more mundane. The Fed announced it would drop its post-2020 average inflation targeting framework and revert to its pre-pandemic approach of simply targeting 2% inflation year by year. That is not an abandonment of the inflation target. It is, if anything, a reaffirmation of it.

The Word That Changed Everything

The misinformation hinged on a single word: "average." The Bloomberg bullet point noted that the Fed was moving away from its average inflation target and placing greater emphasis on employment. Technically accurate, but catastrophically misleading when stripped of context.

If you look ever so closely, you'll see the word average in there, which is playing a monumental role in changing the meaning of this bullet point.

The average inflation targeting framework, adopted in 2020, was itself a response to the post-2008 environment of persistently low rates and below-target inflation. The idea was that if inflation ran below 2% for several years, the Fed would tolerate it running above 2% for a compensating period. It was a makeup strategy — letting the price level catch up to where it would have been under consistent 2% inflation.

The pandemic rendered that framework obsolete in the worst possible way. Inflation did not need to be coaxed upward; it surged past the target and proved stubbornly resistant to decline. The lesson, as Coffin frames it, was stark: allowing inflation to creep above target, even under supposedly transitory conditions, risks losing control entirely. Reverting to simple year-over-year targeting was the pragmatic response.

What Powell Actually Signaled

The speech was not without substance. Coffin acknowledges that Powell's remarks did represent a meaningful shift in tone. The Fed chair highlighted weakening employment data, downward GDP growth revisions, and the complicating effects of tariffs, reduced immigration, and fiscal policy changes. The key line from the speech was direct:

The baseline outlook and the shifting balance of risks may warrant adjusting our policy stance.

That language opened the door — slightly — to a possible rate cut. After months of FOMC members signaling they preferred to hold rates steady, and Powell himself stating that tariff uncertainty precluded action, this was a notable rhetorical pivot. Markets and crypto both jumped on Friday in response, and they were not wrong to do so. The shift was real; it simply was not the shift that social media claimed.

Coffin is careful to draw the distinction between a door being opened and a commitment being made. The Fed did not promise a September rate cut. Even if one materialized, rates could remain above the neutral level, meaning monetary policy would still be restrictive. The speech amounted to the Fed saying it would watch incoming data and remain flexible — hardly the reckless money-printing spree that viral posts described.

The Broader Liquidity Picture

There is a reasonable case for concern about financial conditions, and Coffin touches on it briefly. The reverse repo facility had been drawn down significantly, a sign that financial institutions were becoming less willing to park excess cash with the Fed. Quantitative tightening — the process of letting the Fed's bond holdings mature without reinvestment — was pulling additional liquidity from the system.

These are legitimate pressures. But they are pressures that the Fed was already monitoring, and they represent a planned normalization of the extraordinary monetary expansion that followed the pandemic. Conflating deliberate tightening with an imminent crisis requires ignoring the Fed's own stated intentions and the tools it retains to intervene if conditions deteriorate.

A Pattern, Not an Anomaly

What makes Coffin's analysis valuable is not just the debunking itself but his insistence on placing it within a recurring pattern. Financial social media regularly produces these panic cycles. He draws a parallel to the "petrodollar agreement" scare, in which a supposedly expiring document was said to threaten the dominance of the US dollar. That, too, collapsed under the weight of even cursory research into the actual source material.

It's frustrating because all this could be avoided if people just took the 10 minutes to read the source material, the primary document that they're spewing all these opinions on.

The frustration is well-placed, but the diagnosis may be incomplete. The problem is not simply that people fail to read primary sources. It is that social media incentive structures actively reward speed over accuracy. The accounts that posted the misleading interpretation first gained the most engagement. By the time corrections circulated, the misinformation had already shaped investment decisions and public perception. The asymmetry between the speed of a viral claim and the speed of its correction is not a bug in social media; it is the core mechanic.

The Counterpoint Worth Considering

Not all criticism of the Fed's stance was misinformation, and Coffin is honest about that. Some commentators argued, with legitimate reasoning, that the rhetorical shift toward potential rate cuts was premature given persistent inflation risks and tariff-driven price pressures. If inflation proves stickier than the Fed expects, easing too early could produce exactly the stagflationary outcome that the alarmists feared — just through a different mechanism than they described.

There is also a structural critique worth noting. The Fed's dual mandate — price stability and maximum employment — creates inherent tension when both objectives are under threat simultaneously. Powell's speech acknowledged this tension without resolving it, which is arguably the most honest thing a central banker can do. But honesty about uncertainty does not eliminate the risk that the eventual policy choice will be wrong.

Coffin rightly notes that rate cuts do not inherently produce higher market returns, since they typically respond to deteriorating economic conditions. The market's Friday rally may have been celebrating the possibility of cheaper money without fully pricing in the economic weakness that would prompt it. That tension — between the relief of easier policy and the alarm of the conditions requiring it — is the real story that the misinformation cycle buried.

Bottom Line

The Federal Reserve did not abandon its 2% inflation target. It abandoned a specific averaging mechanism adopted in 2020 that the pandemic had already rendered counterproductive, reverting to straightforward annual targeting. The speech did signal increased flexibility on rate policy, but committed to nothing. Coffin's core message — that substantial claims require substantial evidence, not "some obscure screenshot of someone else's summary of what allegedly happened" — is sound advice for navigating financial media. The harder question, which no amount of individual media literacy can fully answer, is what happens when the platforms themselves are optimized for exactly the kind of rapid, decontextualized sharing that produced this episode in the first place.
