The Long, Humbling Road to Artificial Intelligence

Kenny Easwaran's lecture on the history of artificial intelligence traces a lineage that most casual observers of the current AI moment would find surprising. The story does not begin with ChatGPT, or even with the 1956 Dartmouth conference that gave the field its name. It begins in ninth-century Baghdad, with a Persian mathematician whose work was so foundational that his Latinized name became a word everyone now uses without thinking: algorithm.

This framing matters. In an era when AI discourse tends toward breathless futurism or existential dread, Easwaran grounds the conversation in something older and more durable: the human impulse to systematize problem-solving. Muhammad al-Khwarizmi compiled Greek, Egyptian, and Babylonian mathematical procedures and streamlined them using the Indian numeral system. Centuries later, Leibniz dreamed of universal algorithms that could settle not just mathematical disputes but philosophical ones. The ambition was magnificent. The results, as Easwaran methodically documents, were repeatedly humbling.

The Turing Pivot

The lecture's strongest section concerns Alan Turing, and Easwaran handles it with admirable clarity. Turing's 1936 insight -- that while no single algorithm can solve every problem, a universal algorithm can implement any algorithm that does solve a given problem -- is presented not as an abstract mathematical curiosity but as the conceptual blueprint for every computer that has existed since. The theoretical became devastatingly practical during World War Two, when Turing's codebreaking machines at Bletchley Park contributed to an earlier Allied victory.

But it is Turing's 1950 paper, "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," that Easwaran identifies as the true turning point for AI as a discipline. Turing's proposed test for machine intelligence was radical in its simplicity:

If you can have that sort of extended conversation with the machine, that's all that matters, regardless of whether the machine is running on punch cards and vacuum tubes or brain cells and blood. As long as it's doing things that we can't predict in a way that keeps us coming back, that's the real test of intelligence.

This behavioral definition of intelligence -- judge the output, not the substrate -- remains contested seventy-six years later. Philosophers like John Searle have argued, through thought experiments like the Chinese Room, that conversational fluency does not imply understanding. Today's large language models have made this debate more urgent rather than settling it. A system can produce remarkably coherent prose while possessing no internal model of truth, no goals, and no experience. Whether that constitutes "thinking" in any meaningful sense is a question Turing's test was never designed to answer.

The Pattern of Boom and Bust

The most instructive thread running through Easwaran's lecture is the recurring cycle of hype and disappointment. The pattern is almost metronomic. Researchers make genuine progress. Funders and the public extrapolate wildly. Reality reasserts itself. Funding evaporates.

The examples are pointed. In 1964, Joseph Weizenbaum's ELIZA -- a program that did nothing more than reformat user statements as questions -- fooled people into believing they were conversing with a real therapist. The U.S. military poured money into machine translation, only to discover that word-by-word substitution produced nonsense. As Easwaran recounts the classic illustration:

The sentence "the spirit is willing but the flesh is weak" turned into "the vodka is good but the meat is rotten."

Perhaps most telling is Marvin Minsky's 1966 decision to assign computer vision as a summer project for his students. Nearly sixty years later, computer vision remains an active research frontier. Minsky then predicted human-level intelligence was just a few years away. When it did not materialize, the first AI winter arrived in the early 1970s, and the British government slashed funding while researchers fled to other subfields.

The second AI winter, beginning in 1987, followed the same script. Symbolic reasoning systems hit their limits, requiring too much hand-coded knowledge to scale. And the emergence of the World Wide Web drew computer scientists toward the internet, leaving AI research as something of a backwater through the 1990s.

What Turing Got Right

Easwaran's lecture builds to a conclusion that deserves more attention than it typically receives in popular AI narratives. Turing did not just propose a test for intelligence. He also proposed a method for achieving it:

Rather than trying to program a computer to have conversations like a human adult, it might be easier to program the computer to learn and then raise the computer like a child and let this learning result in meaningful conversations.

This is, in essence, the approach that has driven the current AI revolution. Modern neural networks are not programmed with explicit rules about language or vision. They are trained on vast datasets and learn statistical patterns. The conceptual architecture -- artificial neurons whose outputs are determined by their inputs, first built into working systems by Frank Rosenblatt in 1957 -- waited decades for hardware powerful enough to make it practical. When GPU computing made large-scale neural networks feasible around 2010, progress accelerated dramatically.

The irony Easwaran highlights is significant: much of the conceptual work was done long before the machines existed to run it. Neural networks were invented in the 1950s. Backpropagation training methods emerged in the 1980s. The ideas were sound. The silicon was not ready.

What the Lecture Leaves Out

For all its strengths as a historical survey, the lecture does not grapple with several questions that any honest assessment of AI history must confront. There is no discussion of the social costs of AI development -- the labor of data annotators, the environmental impact of training runs, or the concentration of AI capability in a handful of well-funded institutions. The narrative of steady progress toward intelligence, punctuated by winters, can obscure the fact that each wave of AI development has produced real harms alongside its benefits: algorithmic bias in criminal justice, surveillance infrastructure, labor displacement.

Easwaran also passes quickly over the distinction between narrow AI -- systems that excel at specific tasks -- and artificial general intelligence, which remains theoretical. Deep Blue's 1997 chess victory and AlexNet's 2012 image classification breakthrough are presented as waypoints on a single road, but there is a credible argument that they represent fundamentally different kinds of achievement. A chess engine that searches millions of positions per second has almost nothing in common, architecturally or conceptually, with a neural network that learns to classify images from examples.

The lecture's closing note -- that another AI winter is possible, or that AI may have permanently crossed a threshold -- is appropriately agnostic. But it understates the degree to which the current moment differs from previous booms. The integration of AI into commercial products used by billions of people, the scale of capital investment, and the geopolitical competition around AI development all suggest that even if the technology disappoints, the infrastructure built around it will persist.

Bottom Line

Easwaran's lecture is a valuable corrective to the presentism that dominates AI discourse. The history of artificial intelligence is not a story of sudden breakthroughs but of ideas that waited decades or centuries for the right conditions. The recurring pattern of overconfidence followed by disappointment should give pause to anyone making confident predictions about what AI will or will not achieve. Turing's core insight -- that learning machines would outperform programmed ones -- proved correct, but it took sixty years and exponential growth in computing power to vindicate him. The lesson is not that progress is inevitable, but that the gap between a good idea and a working implementation can be far wider than anyone expects.
