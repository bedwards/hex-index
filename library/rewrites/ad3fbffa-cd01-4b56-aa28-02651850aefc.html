The Hidden Cost of Good Advice

Richard Fain, the portfolio manager behind The Plain Bagel, used his third annual birthday livestream to field audience questions on investing, career advice, and market conditions. What emerged across the unscripted, freewheeling session were several threads that cut to the heart of personal finance tensions most YouTube channels gloss over: the real price of financial advice, the limits of index fund orthodoxy, and why bonds finally deserve a second look.

The S&P 500 Concentration Problem

When asked whether dollar-cost averaging into the S&P 500 could eventually make those 500 companies "wildly overvalued," Fain offered a measured but revealing answer. He acknowledged the index's long track record while flagging something most passive investing evangelists prefer to downplay:

I'm always cautious to put too much Blind Faith into it... what's the stat that like 10 stocks make up the vast majority of the performance for the S&P 500 so I think there are risks with relying too heavily on indices like the S&P 500.

This is a thread worth pulling. As of the stream, the top ten holdings in the S&P 500 represented roughly a third of the index's total market capitalization. That level of concentration means investors who believe they are diversified across 500 companies are, in practice, making an outsized bet on a handful of mega-cap technology firms. Fain stopped short of a definitive verdict, but his instinct to question indexing dogma is well-placed. The mechanical nature of market-cap weighting means the most expensive stocks automatically receive the most capital, a feedback loop that critics like Michael Burry have compared to the structural distortions of CDOs before 2008.

Financial Advisors and the Emotional Tax

The question of whether an "average wage slave" should hire a financial advisor drew Fain's most nuanced commentary of the session. He could have taken the easy populist route and declared advisors unnecessary. Instead, he acknowledged the fee problem while defending something less tangible:

A big part of investing is sticking to it, the behavioral side of things... I think you would benefit from an adviser at any size... that emotional piece of investing is really important and if you struggle with that then I think you would benefit from an adviser.

The behavioral finance literature overwhelmingly supports this position. Vanguard's own research on "Advisor Alpha" estimates that behavioral coaching alone adds roughly 1.5 percentage points of annual return by preventing clients from panic-selling during downturns. The counterpoint, however, is that a 2% annual management fee can easily exceed that behavioral benefit, particularly in a mutual fund wrapper where the advisor's compensation is baked into the expense ratio. Fain acknowledged this tension honestly, noting his own bias as a portfolio manager, but the uncomfortable truth remains: the people who most need behavioral guardrails are often those least able to afford the fees that come with them.

The Performance Fee Fantasy

A viewer asked about investment advisors who charge only success fees. Fain referenced Guy Spier's "The Education of a Value Investor" and the appeal of performance-only compensation before explaining why it rarely works in practice:

It would be hard to run a company with that sort of variable fee if you have a large enough team... there are some years where regardless of how good you are at investing you probably will be down so it's hard to run a company if you have a whole year where you don't make any money.

This is an underappreciated point. The hedge fund model of "2 and 20" (2% management fee plus 20% of profits) exists precisely because the fixed component keeps the lights on during drawdown years. Strip away the management fee entirely and only the most capitalized or solo practitioners can survive a bear market. It explains why performance-only fee structures remain vanishingly rare despite their obvious appeal to clients. The incentive alignment is theoretically perfect but operationally fragile.

Bonds Are Back, With Caveats

When asked whether treasuries deserve a larger place in portfolios, Fain delivered what may have been his sharpest macro observation of the session. He contrasted the current rate environment with the near-zero rates of recent years:

Five years ago it just really didn't make sense because not only were you getting a small yield you had that significant risk of interest rates Rising because with interest rates at zero they can't go down a whole lot further... so not only are you accepting a long-term yield for a low rate you also have that price depreciation risk.

The logic is clean and correct. When the 10-year yield sat below 1%, bond investors faced asymmetric downside: minimal income with substantial duration risk if rates normalized. With yields now materially higher, bonds once again serve their traditional portfolio function of providing real income and acting as a partial hedge against equity drawdowns. Fain noted that many investors continued buying bonds on autopilot during the zero-rate era, a quiet indictment of the "set it and forget it" mentality that pervades target-date funds and robo-advisors.

The Warren Buffett Question

Asked about allegations of front-running connected to Warren Buffett, Fain refused to either defend or condemn. His response revealed a healthy skepticism toward financial hero worship:

I'd be careful getting too attached to idols like that... some people worship Warren Buffett and all this stuff and obviously my go-to is... I'd rather just wait to see how the cards play out.

The finance industry has a long history of pedestals that eventually crack. Bill Gross, once the "Bond King," departed PIMCO amid allegations of erratic behavior. Neil Woodford, the UK's most celebrated fund manager, saw his empire collapse in a liquidity crisis. Fain's counsel to learn from figures like Buffett without emotionally investing in their infallibility is the kind of advice that sounds obvious but proves remarkably difficult to follow when billions of dollars of brand loyalty are at stake.

Bottom Line

Across a casual livestream full of birthday wishes and bagel jokes, Fain consistently returned to the same underlying message: financial markets reward patience and skepticism in roughly equal measure. The most valuable threads in this Q&A were not the specific recommendations but the recurring admission that simple-sounding questions (Should you index? Should you hire an advisor? Should you buy bonds?) have answers that depend heavily on individual circumstances, market conditions, and temperament. That willingness to say "it depends" without retreating into vagueness is what separates useful financial commentary from the confident certainty that saturates the rest of finance YouTube.
