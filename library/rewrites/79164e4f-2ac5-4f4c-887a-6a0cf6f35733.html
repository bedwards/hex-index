A Cardiologist's Case for Assisted Dying, Built on Personal Loss

Dr. Rohan, a UK cardiologist who runs the popular YouTube channel Medlife Crisis, steps outside his usual format of medical deep dives to make an intensely personal argument for legalizing assisted dying. His mother died of progressive bulbar palsy, a form of motor neuron disease, in 2018. She spent her final year consumed by anxiety about the suffering to come, and she did not have the option of a medically assisted death. The video is part medical ethics primer, part family memoir, and part advocacy piece. It is also, by the creator's own admission, unlike anything he has previously published on the channel.

The personal disclosure serves a rhetorical function that a purely analytical treatment could not. By describing his mother's nine months of misdiagnosis, her deteriorating ability to speak, eat, and eventually breathe, and her dark jokes about her cardiologist son acquiring lethal medication from the hospital, he grounds the policy argument in the specific texture of terminal illness. The details are unflinching:

Your breathing muscles become affected. Breathlessness relentlessly increases until you can't even catch your breath sitting still. You can't complete a sentence. And then finally you are suffocated to death, or you die of a chest infection because you can't ventilate your lungs.

This is not abstraction. It is a son watching his mother die by degrees, knowing the endpoint, and knowing he could not legally help her avoid the worst of it.

Defining the Terms

Before making his case, he draws careful distinctions between forms of medical intervention at the end of life. Assisted dying, as he defines it, applies only to terminally ill, mentally competent patients who choose to control the manner of their death. He separates this from euthanasia, which does not require a limited life expectancy, and from involuntary euthanasia, which is legal nowhere. He also distinguishes it from allowing a natural death, such as withdrawing ventilation from a patient with irreversible brain damage.

The taxonomy matters because opponents, he argues, routinely conflate these categories. Calling assisted dying "assisted suicide" reframes a terminally ill person's desire for control as a psychiatric crisis. Conflating it with euthanasia imports fears about state-sanctioned killing that have nothing to do with the narrow proposals actually under consideration in the United Kingdom.

Someone with a terminal illness who wants to control their own death is not suicidal.

This is a strong framing, though it does elide the genuine difficulty of determining when a patient's desire for death reflects autonomous choice and when it reflects undertreated depression, inadequate palliative care, or social pressure. He addresses some of these concerns later, but the definitional section is doing argumentative work that deserves scrutiny.

The International Evidence

The strongest portion of the argument is empirical. He surveys jurisdictions where assisted dying is legal, with particular attention to Oregon, whose Death with Dignity Act has operated for roughly thirty years. In that time, he notes, there have been no documented abuses of the law, no widening of eligibility criteria, and the system remains strictly limited to mentally competent, terminally ill patients. California and Washington modeled their own laws on Oregon's track record.

He also cites evidence presented to the UK Parliament's assisted dying inquiry. Professor James Downar, head of palliative care at the University of Ottawa, testified that Canada has seen its strongest growth in palliative care since introducing assisted dying legislation in 2016. Professors from Belgium and the Netherlands reported that legalization in those countries has been intrinsically linked with improvements in palliative care, not a replacement for it.

Against this, he examines the Dignitas model in Switzerland, where terminally ill patients from other countries travel to die. The cost is approximately fifteen thousand pounds. His mother considered it but became too frail to travel. This creates a perverse incentive: patients who want the option must go earlier in their disease course, sacrificing quality time with family, or be wealthy enough to manage the logistics while critically ill. The access inequality is, in his view, an argument for domestic legislation rather than continued reliance on medical tourism.

Steelmanning the Opposition

He attempts to present the strongest versions of the counterarguments, with uneven results. On the slippery slope, he is persuasive. The claim that legalizing assisted dying for the terminally ill will inevitably lead to euthanasia of disabled or cognitively impaired people is not supported by evidence from any jurisdiction where it has been tried. He draws a pointed analogy to same-sex marriage, where opponents predicted societal collapse that never materialized. He also notes, with personal authority, that his own brother has profound intellectual disability, making him particularly attuned to any genuine risk of expanding eligibility.

On the palliative care objection, the argument that better end-of-life care eliminates the need for assisted dying, he is both respectful and firm. Palliative care is invaluable but cannot eliminate all suffering. His mother's case is the evidence. Even with palliative support, progressive bulbar palsy imposes a death by suffocation that no amount of symptom management can fully ameliorate. More importantly, palliative care cannot address the psychological burden of losing autonomy:

Why do we only hope to give someone control over their symptoms but not over something as fundamental as their own life?

Where the steelmanning falters is on the question of religious opposition. He argues that much opposition to assisted dying is religiously motivated but disguised behind secular language about the sanctity of life. This is probably true in many cases, but dismissing an opponent's stated reasoning by attributing hidden motives is a rhetorical shortcut that undermines the intellectual generosity he promises elsewhere. Some secular philosophers genuinely hold that the medical profession should not be in the business of deliberately ending life, and this position deserves engagement on its own terms rather than being reduced to covert theology.

The Harder Question: Mental Illness

He ventures briefly into the more contested territory of assisted dying for people with severe mental illness, as practiced in the Netherlands, Belgium, and Canada. Here his confidence visibly diminishes, and usefully so. He acknowledges that unlike motor neuron disease, depression is not categorically irreversible. He worries that in countries where mental health care is difficult to access, assisted dying could become a substitute for adequate treatment. He does not claim certainty.

A Canadian man with a disability, identified pseudonymously as Steven, emailed concerns that assisted dying had been suggested to people simply for being disabled. The creator takes this seriously, and quotes Steven's formulation of the core tension:

It has to be as easy to choose to live as it is to choose to die. And this conundrum exists far less with terminally ill patients because they're not choosing to die. That choice has been made for them by their body. They're just choosing how.

This is the most philosophically precise statement in the entire piece, and it comes not from the doctor but from a member of the public. It captures why the terminal illness case is relatively straightforward while the mental illness case remains genuinely difficult.

What the Video Does Not Address

Several significant objections receive little attention. The institutional pressure on under-resourced healthcare systems, where assisted dying could become a cost-saving measure rather than a patient-centered one, is not examined. The experience of countries like Canada, where eligibility has expanded beyond terminal illness, receives only surface treatment. The complexities of determining mental competence in patients with advanced disease are mentioned but not explored. And the question of what "terminally ill" means in practice, given that prognosis is an imprecise science, is left unasked.

These gaps are understandable in a video that prioritizes personal narrative over comprehensive policy analysis, but they are worth noting because they represent the terrain where this debate will actually be fought in legislatures.

Bottom Line

The video succeeds most powerfully as testimony. A doctor who talks to patients about death daily, and who watched his own mother die of a disease that stripped her of speech, food, and breath, makes a case that the current legal framework in the United Kingdom forces needless suffering on the terminally ill. The empirical evidence from Oregon and other jurisdictions supports his core claim that assisted dying can be legalized with adequate safeguards and without the slippery slope critics predict.

The piece is weakest where it conflates the strength of the terminal illness case with the much murkier questions around mental illness and disability, and where it dismisses religious opposition rather than engaging with the strongest secular versions of similar arguments. The creator is transparent about his bias, which is to his credit, but transparency about motivation is not the same as rigor in argument. Still, for anyone unfamiliar with the assisted dying debate, this is an accessible and emotionally honest entry point, anchored by a personal story that makes the stakes impossible to ignore.
