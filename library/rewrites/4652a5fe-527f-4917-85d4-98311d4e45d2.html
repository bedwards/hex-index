A Short King's Case Against Breaking Your Femurs

Dr. Rohin Francis, the cardiologist and creator of the Medlife Crisis YouTube channel, takes on cosmetic leg lengthening surgery -- a procedure in which surgeons deliberately fracture the femur and sometimes the tibia and fibula, then slowly separate the bone fragments over months using an internal or external fixation device. The bone regenerates to fill the gap, and the patient ends up a few inches taller. The recovery is brutal: six months or more of daily physiotherapy, relearning how to walk, and enough pain to make a root canal seem like a spa treatment.

What makes Francis's take worth watching is that he is squarely in the target demographic. Standing at 169.5 centimeters (about five feet seven inches), he is a self-described "short King" who has never once considered the procedure. That personal distance from the insecurity that drives men to the operating table gives him an unusual vantage point -- sympathetic but genuinely puzzled.

The Article That Started It All

The video was prompted by a longform article profiling a man named John who underwent leg lengthening to reach six feet. Francis expected John to be young, single, deeply insecure. Instead, John was around forty, married with children, and by all accounts a normal, likable person. He started at five feet eight -- taller than Francis himself.

He was around 40 years old. He was married with kids. And the journalist said he was a normal likable guy. Our lives actually sounded pretty similar.

That detail reframes the entire conversation. This is not exclusively a procedure sought by teenagers spiraling on Reddit. Grown men with established lives, careers, and families are paying north of $100,000 and enduring what amounts to a controlled catastrophic injury because heightism has burrowed so deeply into their self-image that decades of functional adult life could not dislodge it.

Heightism Hiding in Plain Sight

Francis spends a portion of the video surveying the landscape of height discrimination, and the evidence he presents is hard to dismiss. Dating app profiles routinely state minimum height requirements in language that would be considered unacceptable if applied to race or weight. The UK television show Love Island featured contestants openly mocking a five-foot-seven male participant. Corporate data consistently shows taller men earning more and advancing faster, with height functioning as a proxy for perceived leadership capability.

We've been able to have conversations in society about immutable characteristics like race and sex being discriminated against in the workplace. But we don't talk about attractiveness and height anywhere near as much.

He references the so-called "666 rule" circulating on dating subreddits -- the expectation that men be six feet tall, earn six figures, and possess six inches or more below the belt. Even allowing for internet exaggeration, the underlying sentiment reflects a genuine frustration among men who feel that their physical stature disqualifies them before they have spoken a word.

A counterpoint is worth raising here. While heightism is real and measurable, the online discourse around it can veer into self-reinforcing despair. The Reddit communities Francis references are the same ones that sometimes slide from legitimate grievance into bitter resignation, where the proposed solution is not self-acceptance or social change but expensive surgery or withdrawal from dating entirely. The existence of discrimination does not automatically validate every response to it.

The Commodification of Major Surgery

The most incisive section of the video is not about height at all. It is about the transformation of serious surgical procedures into consumer products. Francis draws a direct line from Instagram cosmetic surgery accounts offering Black Friday deals and two-for-one fillers to the leg lengthening industry's aggressive online marketing, particularly targeting medical tourists in India and Turkey where prices can drop to a quarter of Western rates.

They talk about major operations like Black Friday deals. Two for one on fillers. Refer a friend for a nose job and get 20% off. 30% off a Brazilian butt lift if you also buy a boob job. And most exploitative of all, a discount if you agree to have your operation and your before and after pictures used for the plastic surgeon's social media account.

The comparison lands because it highlights a structural problem that transcends any single procedure. When qualified medical professionals employ the same marketing tactics as fast fashion brands, the patient-doctor relationship is fundamentally altered. The surgeon becomes a vendor. The patient becomes a customer. And the safeguards that traditionally existed to protect people from making irreversible decisions under emotional duress -- informed consent processes, psychological screening, cooling-off periods -- get treated as friction in the sales funnel.

Francis notes that some British surgeons offering leg lengthening do require psychological assessment first, a practice he endorses as a minimum standard. The fact that this is noteworthy rather than universal says something uncomfortable about the state of cosmetic medicine.

Gender, Dysphoria, and Who Gets to Decide

Francis raises a provocative question about the taxonomy of surgical modification. If breast augmentation for a flat-chested cisgender woman is cosmetic vanity, but the same procedure for a transgender woman is gender-affirming care, what exactly is the meaningful distinction? He notes that some clinics offering leg lengthening also offer leg shortening for trans women seeking to feminize their stature. Short stature dysphoria and gender dysphoria share structural similarities -- deep dissatisfaction with an immutable physical characteristic that causes genuine psychological distress.

If we classify some operations as gender affirming and hence life-saving but others as simply for vanity reasons, is that actually an artificial demarcation?

He is careful not to equate the two, and the question is stronger for that restraint. The point is not that heightism is equivalent to transphobia in severity or kind. The point is that society's framework for deciding which body modifications are legitimate and which are frivolous is less coherent than it pretends to be. A woman who says she never felt like a woman until she had breast implants is expressing something real. A man who says he has been passed over professionally and romantically his entire life because of his height is also expressing something real. The medical profession has not developed a consistent framework for adjudicating between these claims.

The Complication Problem

For all his sympathy, Francis does not soft-pedal the medical reality. The procedure carries serious risks: infection, nerve damage, uneven lengthening, chronic pain, and in the worst cases reported from less regulated markets, permanent disability. China banned cosmetic leg lengthening in 2006 after a wave of severe complications. The orthopedic surgeons Francis consulted for the piece were largely skeptical, with some accusing colleagues who offer the procedure of chasing cosmetic surgery profits rather than practicing responsible medicine.

If one of my patients suffers a complication, it was at least in pursuit of life-saving surgery.

The risk-benefit calculation for cosmetic leg lengthening is genuinely difficult to defend on purely medical grounds. A healthy person is choosing to sustain an injury comparable to being hit by a bus, then spending a year recovering, in exchange for two to three inches of height. The article that inspired the video captured the absurdity and the wonder of it simultaneously: it is a medical miracle deployed in service of being slightly more attractive on a dating app.

Bottom Line

Francis lands in a place that is more emotionally honest than most commentary on this topic. He started with bemusement, moved through sympathy, and arrived at unease. He understands why men do it. He does not think they are foolish. He simply wishes the world did not make them feel they needed to.

The video works because Francis refuses to occupy the easy positions. He does not mock the men who choose the surgery. He does not celebrate the procedure as empowerment. He does not condemn cosmetic medicine wholesale. Instead, he sits with the discomfort of a world where a married father of two will pay $100,000 and endure months of agony to gain three inches, and where that decision is, by the logic of how society actually treats short men, not entirely irrational. The most damning thing about cosmetic leg lengthening is not that people choose it. It is that the choice makes a terrible kind of sense.
