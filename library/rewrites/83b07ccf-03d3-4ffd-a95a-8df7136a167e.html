The Class Politics of Moral Panic

Richard Hanania opens with a broadside against libertarian podcaster Dave Smith, whose recent clip declared that a fifty-five-year-old man having sex with an eighteen-year-old constitutes pedophilia worthy of lethal violence. From there, Hanania builds an ambitious, deliberately provocative argument: that obsession with pedophilia as a political cause is fundamentally a marker of low social status, and that elite culture is right to treat it as a minor concern rather than a civilizational crisis.

The thesis is blunt. Hanania contends that people with less education and lower social standing fixate on pedophilia because it provides a rare opportunity to claim moral superiority over those above them. As he puts it:

"So they tell themselves 'at least I'm not attracted to children.' Talk about having low standards."

He extends this into a theory of conspiratorial thinking, arguing that the leap from disgust to grand narrative is short and psychologically predictable:

"Pedophiles are running society! They're much higher status than I am, but I'm still better than them because they're evil, and I get my news from Alex Jones and Dave Smith instead of mainstream newspapers, which are either run by pedophiles or cover for them."

Defining Deviance Down -- or Up

A central move in the essay is Hanania's insistence that the definition of pedophilia has been stretched beyond recognition. He points to the Epstein case as an example where the language of child abuse gets applied to post-pubescent teenagers, and then further expanded to cover legal adults in age-gap relationships. The definitional creep, he argues, is the tell that something other than child protection is at work.

"They've decided to get mad at adults having sex with children first, and then will keep playing with definitions until they get the thing that they are desperate to be mad at."

There is something to this observation. The word "pedophilia" does have a clinical meaning -- attraction to prepubescent children -- that differs substantially from its popular usage. And moral panics do tend to expand their definitions over time. But Hanania's framing elides a genuine and difficult question: where should societies draw lines around sexual exploitation, and how much definitional precision can we reasonably expect from public discourse? The clinical distinction between pedophilia and ephebophilia may matter to psychiatrists, but it is not obvious that it should govern public moral intuitions about, say, a forty-year-old pursuing a sixteen-year-old.

The Class Dimension

Hanania draws on J.D. Vance's memoir to illustrate his point about lower-class anxiety around child molestation. He quotes the scene where Vance's grandparents, panicking when the young Vance goes missing at a funeral home, arm themselves with handguns and begin searching cars. Hanania's commentary is characteristically dismissive:

"Ok, I guess."

The contempt is quiet but unmistakable. And it points to a tension in the essay. Hanania wants to argue that pedophilia panic is irrational, but the Vance anecdote describes people in communities where rates of abuse genuinely are higher than in affluent neighborhoods. Child sexual abuse correlates with poverty, substance abuse, and household instability -- all features of the world Vance describes. The grandparents' reaction may have been extreme, but it was not baseless. Hanania's framing treats their fear as evidence of low human capital rather than as a rational, if disproportionate, response to a real environment.

He is more persuasive when he turns the lens on how this anxiety gets weaponized politically. QAnon is the obvious case:

"Before the Epstein craze, you had QAnon, which understands the entirety of the Trump phenomenon as a cosmic struggle against Satanic pedophiles. Note this started completely organically and unlike most political movements was not responding to signals coming from elites."

The observation that QAnon emerged bottom-up rather than top-down is genuinely interesting. Most political movements in American history have been shaped or at least catalyzed by elites. QAnon was different, and Hanania's explanation -- that it reflects the particular psychological needs of low-status populations -- is at least plausible, even if reductive.

Left and Right Variants

One of the essay's stronger analytical moves is distinguishing between right-wing and left-wing versions of the same phenomenon. The right builds conspiracy theories about elite cabals. The left frames the issue through power differentials and systemic critique. Hanania sees both as downstream of the same impulse but filtered through different ideological lenses:

"Left-wing pedo hysteria makes sense in the context of a larger political project involving using government and social coercion to infringe on individual liberty, while the right-wing version is part of the larger story of conservatism's human capital decline."

This is a tidy framework, though it may be too tidy. The left-wing critique of age-gap relationships and power dynamics did not emerge from low-status resentment but from academic feminism -- precisely the elite institutions Hanania elsewhere holds up as more rational. And the right-wing version has been amplified by figures like Steve Bannon who are anything but low human capital. The class story is real but incomplete.

Extended Childhood and the Stakes of Language

Hanania's argument about language and infantilization is among his strongest points. He notes that calling sixteen- and seventeen-year-olds "children" is something people only do when discussing sex:

"Imagine you say to someone that you went to a restaurant and your waitress was a child. Or that you accidentally hit a child with your car. Nobody would think you were talking about a 17-year-old in either case."

The linguistic observation is sharp. There is a genuine inconsistency in treating older teenagers as competent enough to drive, work, and in some states marry, but too fragile to make decisions about sexual relationships. Whether this inconsistency should be resolved by granting them more autonomy or less is a separate question Hanania does not fully engage with.

He ties this to broader concerns about extended adolescence and therapy culture, arguing that treating teenage sexual experiences as inherently and permanently traumatic does more harm than good. This is a defensible position in some contexts, but stated as broadly as Hanania states it, risks minimizing cases where the trauma is real and the power imbalance genuinely exploitative.

Bottom Line

Hanania has written a deliberately incendiary essay that contains real insights buried under layers of class contempt. He is correct that the definition of pedophilia has expanded well beyond its clinical meaning, that moral panics around sex tend to metastasize, and that conspiratorial thinking about elite pedophile rings serves psychological needs unrelated to child protection. He is less convincing when he dismisses the fears of lower-income communities as pure status anxiety, ignores the actual prevalence of abuse in those communities, and treats elite indifference as the rational baseline. The essay's most useful contribution is its taxonomy of how different political factions deploy the same moral charge for different purposes. Its least useful feature is the assumption that intelligence and social status reliably track moral seriousness.
