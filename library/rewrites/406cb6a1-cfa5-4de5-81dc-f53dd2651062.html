The Shadow Fleet Gambit: Ukraine Takes the War to Sea

Ukraine's decision to strike Russia's shadow fleet tankers in late November 2025 represents one of the conflict's most audacious escalations, extending the battlefield far beyond Ukrainian or even Russian territory into Turkish waters and possibly as far as the coast of Senegal. The attacks on the tankers Kyros and Virat, carried out by unmanned maritime drones known as Sea Baby, signal a fundamental shift in Kyiv's strategy: if you cannot defeat Russia on the battlefield alone, bleed its war chest dry by attacking the oil logistics that fund the Kremlin's military machine.

The strategic logic is straightforward. Russia's seaborne oil exports flow through a narrow set of chokepoints, with the Baltic terminals of Primorsk and Ust-Luga and the Black Sea hub of Novorossiysk handling the bulk of maritime shipments. Disrupting this corridor threatens up to 40 to 45 percent of Russia's total maritime oil exports. Combined with an intensifying campaign against Russian refineries on land, which according to tracking data has taken more than 30 percent of Russian refining capacity offline, Ukraine is waging economic warfare on two fronts simultaneously: squeezing supply through refinery strikes and suppressing demand by making it dangerous and expensive to ship Russian crude.

Make Money, Not War

The maritime campaign unfolds against a backdrop of extraordinary cynicism in Washington. The Wall Street Journal's reporting on backroom dealings between Trump-connected business figures and sanctioned Russian oligarchs paints a picture that should alarm anyone invested in the rules-based international order. According to the article's account, figures like Jared Kushner and Steve Witkoff have been working to:

bypass the traditional US national security apparatus and convince the administration to view Russia not as a military threat but as a land of bountiful opportunity.

The aim, reportedly, is to position American companies to enter Russia's commodities market ahead of European competitors. The logic being peddled to the Trump administration is that business ties will guarantee the most durable form of peace. This is not a new argument. It was precisely the theory behind decades of European energy dependence on Russia, a theory that the invasion of Ukraine demolished entirely. Germany's Wandel durch Handel, change through trade, did not moderate the Kremlin. It enriched it and funded the very military now grinding through Ukrainian cities.

That American deal-makers are recycling this discredited thesis, while sanctioned oligarchs like Gennady Timchenko and the Rotenberg brothers send representatives to secret meetings with US companies, suggests that the Trump administration's peace initiative may be less about ending a war than about opening a market.

Europe's Frozen Asset Dilemma

Europe finds itself in a characteristically awkward position. With roughly 185 billion euros in frozen Russian assets held at Euroclear in Belgium, European governments possess one of the most significant economic levers in the conflict. Yet deploying it has proven agonizingly difficult. Belgium fears bearing sole legal responsibility for a move of questionable standing under international law, and the conditions it has set for releasing the funds, including binding EU-wide guarantees and shared legal risk across all member states, remain unmet.

The reported willingness of Belgian Prime Minister Bart De Wever to accept a conversion of the frozen capital into a 140-billion-euro loan for Ukraine represented a potential breakthrough. But just two days later, Belgian Foreign Minister Maxime Prevot undercut the initiative, suggesting the reparations loan could complicate peace talks, a position suspiciously aligned with Washington's preferences. The question of whether Prevot received a nudge from the US administration hangs in the air.

This dynamic illustrates a cruel irony: the United States, which does not hold the frozen assets, wants to control how they are used, while Europe, which does hold them, cannot muster the collective will to act independently.

The Kremlin's Real Fear

One of the analysis's most penetrating observations concerns Russia's underlying motivation for the war. The conventional framing of NATO expansion and security concerns, while not irrelevant, obscures a deeper anxiety. The argument that Russia launched what amounts to:

a defensive war waged in defense of the post-Soviet social order, a vertically structured system of power

is both historically grounded and strategically clarifying. If Ukraine were to succeed on a Western path, developing strong institutions, a market economy, and the rule of law, it would pose an existential question to the Russian system. A Slavic, culturally proximate nation thriving under democratic governance would expose the inefficiency and brutality of Moscow's autocratic model in ways that no Western lecture ever could.

This framing helps explain why Secretary of State Marco Rubio's stated goals for Ukraine, however well-intentioned, are fundamentally incompatible with any deal Moscow would accept. Rubio articulated a vision in which Ukraine would:

emerge from this war, not just to rebuild their country, but to build it back in a way that will be stronger and more prosperous than it's ever been.

A prosperous, secure, Western-aligned Ukraine is precisely the outcome Moscow went to war to prevent. No peace deal can reconcile these positions. Former Commander-in-Chief Valerii Zaluzhnyi, now Ukraine's ambassador to London, made the same point more bluntly, noting that Moscow's ultimate interest remains the abolition of Ukraine as an independent state.

The Risks of Maritime Escalation

Ukraine's shadow fleet strikes are not without significant risk. The tankers Kyros and Virat sailed under a Gambian flag and were hit in Turkish territorial waters. Turkey's President Erdogan condemned the attacks as unacceptable. The tanker MT Mersin, possibly also targeted by Ukraine, was struck off the coast of Senegal. These are not Russian waters, and the vessels, however complicit in sanctions evasion, are nominally civilian ships.

Critics rightly point to the potential for backlash from middle-power states like Turkey and India, as well as from pro-Ukrainian countries that could face rising shipping and insurance costs as collateral damage. Ukraine's legal justification, that its Ministry of Defense warned in July 2023 that vessels heading to Russian Black Sea ports could be considered carriers of military cargo, is creative but has not been tested in any international court.

The counterpoint, however, is equally compelling. The shadow fleet operates outside standard insurance systems precisely because it exists to evade sanctions. These vessels are the circulatory system of Russia's war economy. If international sanctions are to mean anything, the infrastructure that circumvents them cannot be treated as sacrosanct. Ukraine has concluded, perhaps correctly, that the war has reached a stage where the risk of inaction outweighs the risk of escalation.

Zelensky's Eroding Mandate

Domestically, the political ground beneath President Zelensky continues to shift. His approval, which surged to 90 percent in the early days of the invasion, reportedly collapsed by 40 percentage points following the NABU scandal and the departure of Andriy Yermak, his long-serving chief of staff. Polling data from Polyarina, though unofficial, puts his support at roughly 20 percent, the same level it sat at on the eve of the war.

Zaluzhnyi's essay in the Telegraph, ostensibly a strategic analysis of the war's trajectory, reads as a political manifesto. His pointed references to the 2021 military budget cuts under Zelensky's watch and his allusions to corruption in the president's inner circle carry an unmistakable subtext. As the analysis notes, the unspoken message is simple: he wants to return and become president of Ukraine.

Whether Zaluzhnyi would make a better wartime or postwar leader is an open question. But his emergence as a political alternative underscores the fragility of Ukrainian domestic politics at a moment when unity is most needed.

Bottom Line

Ukraine's campaign against Russia's shadow fleet represents a calculated bet that economic warfare at sea can compensate for manpower shortages on land. With more than 30 percent of Russian refining capacity offline and Rosneft's profits reportedly a tenth of what they were a year ago, the pressure is real. But so are the risks: diplomatic fallout with Turkey, potential precedent-setting attacks on civilian shipping, and the ever-present danger that Washington's appetite for deal-making with Moscow will leave Ukraine isolated. The frozen assets in Brussels remain the single most consequential economic weapon available to Ukraine's allies, and the inability to deploy them speaks to a broader failure of collective Western resolve. Both sides are approaching exhaustion, but exhaustion does not guarantee peace. It guarantees only that the next phase of the conflict, whether at the negotiating table or on the battlefield, will be shaped by whichever side can endure longer on diminishing resources.
