Venezuela burst onto global front pages on September the 2nd when President Donald Trump released a video showing a Venezuelan vessel being destroyed by the US Navy. According to Trump, the boat was being used by members of the Trendaranga gang to smuggle narcotics. Since then, similar strikes have multiplied and Washington has begun amassing increasingly substantial military assets across the Caribbean. The Trump administration, Vice President J.

D. Vance and Secretary of State Marco Rubio included, has forcefully defended these actions, branding them a war on quote narco terrorists. The choice of the word terrorist is no accident. It is central to a broader strategy the administration has adapted toward Venezuela.

But to understand that strategy, we need to go back several months. In February 2025, the US government designated several Latin American gangs as foreign terrorist organizations. Among them, the now infamous Trenaranga. The move was unprecedented.

Historically, the FDO list was reserved for ideologically or religiously motivated groups like ISIS or al-Qaeda, not criminal syndicates. But the consequences of the designation are enormous. Travel bans, asset freezes, and most crucially, it empowers the president to use military force against such organizations without congressional authorization. The decision to designate Trenaranga as terrorist organization allowed the Trump administration to rewrite the rules of engagement in the Caribbean.

Instead of intercepting drag boats, US forces began destroying them outright from the air. But that was only the beginning. The administration quickly turned the FTO designation into a legal foundation for something far more ambitious, a potential strike on Venezuela itself. How the White House has begun claiming that the trenda gang is directly linked to Venezuelan President Nicolas Maduro.

In support of this narrative, the cartel de los soles cartel of the sons is set to be added to the list of foreign terrorist organizations on November 24th. According to the Americans, this cartel is personally led by the Venezuelan president. The case is shaky at best. Even leaked US intelligence assessments cast doubt on any operational links between Maduro and Trenarangua.

The same is true for the cartel de los soles. Many experts question whether it even exists in the centralized cartel form suggested by Washington, describing it instead as a loose web of corrupt officials and opportunistic smugglers rather than a coherent criminal enterprise. The inconsistencies grow even starker when we look at the trafficking data itself. Trump argues that the destroyed vessels were laden with fentinel and cocaine, but according to the US Drug Enforcement Administration, the fentinel flooding America's streets is produced almost entirely in Mexico.

Cocaine does transit through Venezuela, but the country is not a major producer. Colombia remains the dominant source and Venezuela is not even the main corridor for shipments heading to the United States. According to DEA figures, roughly 74% of cocaine reaching the US travels along the Pacific route from Colombia and Ecuador. The Trump administration is aware of these nuances.

It simply doesn't consider them relevant. The objective is not to wage a drug war. It is to remove Maduro. The current escalation should not come as a surprise.

It springs from a deeply ingrained tradition in the US foreign policy. The belief that Washington enjoys special prerogatives in the Western Hemisphere. The foundation of that mindset is the Monroe Doctrine of 1823. Originally aimed at preventing European colonial powers from meddling in Latin America.

Over time, it evolved into a justification for US regional hegemony, most famously expressed through President Theodore Roosevelt's big stick diplomacy, which sanctioned the use of force to protect American interests and turned the Caribbean into what many saw as an quote American inner lake. Trump's second term has been defined by a revival and a hardening of this worldview which analysts in Washington have dubbed the quote don doctrine. A fusion of Donald and Monroe. The doctrine blends traditional hemispheric hegemony with a sharply ideological antisocialist agenda aimed at the so-called troa of tyranny.

Venezuela, Cuba, and Nicaragua. Layered onto that is a new great power imperative pushing China, Russia, and Iran out of what the administration describes as quote America's own backyard. In this updated version, the Monroe Donro doctrine is no longer merely about deterring outside powers. It frames Latin America as an extension of the American homeland, a space where Washington can act unilaterally to eliminate actors it deems hostile.

It is also important to remember that for Trump, Venezuela is more than just a doctrinal issue. It is personal. He already tried to remove Maduro once back in 2019. At the time, Washington recognized opposition leader Juan Guaido as Venezuela's legitimate interim president and imposed sweeping sanctions aimed at severing Maduro from the country's oil revenues.

The goal was to spark either a popular uprising or a military coup that would topple the regime. But the plan failed. Venezuela's military elite remained largely loyal to Maduro and the entire operation ended in diplomatic embarrassment for Washington. Returning to the White House for a second term, Trump appears determined to finish what he started with far more radical tools.

For months, the United States has been quietly massing military power on Venezuela's doorstep. The scale is unmatched in decades. Roughly 10,000 troops, eight warships, F35 fighter squadrons, MQ9 Reaper drones, and B-52 and B1 strategic bombers. The arrival of the USS Gerald R.

Ford carrier strike group completed the picture. At the same time, the administration has launched a psychological campaign built on deliberate ambiguity. Trump oscillates wildly. One day, he threatens strikes on land and declares that Maduro's days are numbered.

The next day, he publicly denies that he's considering an invasion. The aim of this strategy appears to be twofold. to sow paranoia within Maduro's inner circle, convincing him that the confrontation is inevitable and to signal to the quote dissolution elements of the regime that the time to defect has come. It is in this light that Trump's public announcement that he had authorized the CIA to conduct covert operations in Venezuela should be understood.

Presidents almost never acknowledged such directives openly. This time, Trump wanted the enemy to hear it. The goal is to ignite panic inside the Miraaf Flores palace and trigger an internal witch hunt that could immobilize Madura's regime from within. This sharp escalation was not the only option on the table.

That it became policy is the result of an internal power struggle inside the Trump administration, one in which the Florida hawks ultimately prevailed. The chief architect of the current strategy is Marco Rubio. Holding both the post of Secretary of State and National Security Adviser, Rubio has amassed near total control over US foreign policy. As the son of Cuban immigrants, he has long viewed the downfall of leftist regimes in Havana, Karakas, and Managua as a personal mission.

He's backed by other Florida Republicans, a fact that is no coincidence. Florida is home to large and politically active exile communities from across Latin America, especially Cuba and Venezuela. This influential block arbor's deep anti-communist and anti- authoritarian sentiments and anti-maduro attitudes among floritar republicans are particularly intense. This dynamic sidelined an alternative diplomatic track led by Richard Grenell, a proponent of transactional dealmaking.

Grenell has been negotiating with Maduro on a potential arrangement. In exchange for avoiding a US intervention, the Venezuelan leader would grant American companies preferential access to the country's oil reserves and commit to reducing the presence of China, Russia, and Iran. Trump publicly boasted about the offer, saying, quote, he Maduro offered everything. You know why?

Because he doesn't want to mess with the United States. Yet in early October 2025, Trump ordered Grenell to halt all negotiations with Karakas. Rubio and the Florida Hawks had prevailed. The exact reasons, as is often the case with Trump, remain opaque.

Perhaps the president decided that televised footage of blown up drug boats and tough talk aimed at the socialist dictator in Karakas would boost his domestic standing, after which he could always return to the negotiating table. But it is equally possible that Trump hardened his position because he genuinely intends to use military force and remove Maduro once and for all. For now, it is impossible to say which path Trump will ultimately choose. What is becoming increasingly clear, however, is that the drive toward confrontation with Venezuela is generating growing unease within America's own military machinery.

In mid-occtober, Admiral Alvin Holsey, the commander of US Southern Command, responsible for all military operations in Latin America, abruptly announced his retirement after less than a year in the post. US media reports indicate that Hoy had raised concerns about the legality and risks of the new mission. He was also reportedly frustrated at being cut out of the decision-making coup regarding strikes on narotraicante vessels responsibility for which had been handed over to US special operations command. Maduro is preparing for the worst.

The armed forces have been placed on a maximum alert. The Bolivarian militia has been mobilized and Karakas has reached out to China, Russia, and Thran for military assistance. While Venezuela's military could not stop a full-scale American invasion, it still possesses enough strike capabilities to post real dangers to US forces in the opening stages of any conflict. For most among those are Venezuela's fleet of 21 SU30 fighter jets capable of carrying Russian HA 31 supersonic anti-hship missiles.

The US Navy must also contend with Iran supplied CM90 anti-ship missiles with a range of roughly 90 km. It is worth noting that despite mounting US pressure, Maduro's regime shows no signs of fracturing. As political scientist Javier Corales argues, the durability of the system has little to do with public legitimacy and everything to do with what he calls quote a fusion of functions. Venezuela's elite, senior military officers, judges, politicians, has been granted access to lucrative economic ventures, both legal, such as state contracts and illicit.

They have become in effect shareholders in the regime, profiting from drug trafficking, illegal gold mining, and fuel smuggling. For them, a transition to a pro-American opposition government would not merely threaten their wealth. It would almost certainly mean extradition and prosecution in the United States, where many of them, Maduro included, already face federal indictments. And so as the USS Gerald R.

Ford takes a position at the gates of America's backyard, analysts in Washington are asking the same question. How does this end? The hawks in Trump's administration are clearly hoping for a replay of Operation Just Cause, the lightning fast 1989 invasion of Panama that toppled Manuel Noriega, another dictator accused of drug trafficking. But that analogy is both false and dangerous.

Venezuela is not Panama. It is 12 times larger with 10 times the population and critically without US military bases on its soil. Even the 10,000 American troops currently deployed in the Caribbean are far too few to contemplate a just cause style operation. Analysts at CSIS estimate that such an invasion would require anywhere from 50,000 to as many as 200,000 US troops.

So, what are the plausible endgame scenarios? What can President Trump actually do? The first option, and the one Trump appears to want most, is a psychological victory. Faced with overwhelming US military pressure, Maduro's inner circle cracks or a coup replaces him with a government more prepared to deal with Washington.

As noted earlier, this outcome is quite unlikely. There is simply no evidence of fractures within Maduro's power structure. The second option is a set of limited strikes on landbased targets. The Pentagon already has target lists prepared.

The risk, however, is obvious. Rather than break the regime, such strikes could rallied, allowing Maduro to consolidate power under the banner of defending national sovereignty. The third scenario and the riskiest is a special operations raid. Analysts have speculated about a Delta Force or Navy Seal mission to capture or kill Maduro.

Such an operation would carry extreme danger. A failure, American commandos captured and paraded on Venezuelan television, would be a political catastrophe for Trump and humiliation on the scale of the Bay of Pigs in 1961. Yet, the greatest risk of this highstakes gamble may not be failure at all, but paradoxically, success. Sources in Washington acknowledged that the administration has spent far more time planning potential strikes than envisioning the quote day after Maduro.

Some experts warned that if Maduro is removed by force and the state apparatus collapses, Venezuela could descend into Libya style chaos. Local warlords, criminal syndicates, and Colombian paramilitary groups already operating inside the country could fill the vacuum. At this point, the most likely path appears to be the second scenario. Limited US strikes on military targets inside Venezuela.

But given Trump's personal vendetta against Maduro, magnified by the president's own temperament, events could escalate quickly and every option remains on the table. Predicting Trump's next move is especially difficult because even he may not know it. His Venezuela policy is driven by contradiction. In July 2025, at the very moment the Pentagon was finalizing its Caribbean deployments, the administration quietly renewed Chevron's license, allowing the US oil giant not only to continue, but to expand its operations in Venezuela.

So on one hand, the president threatens to bomb the country. On the other, he authorizes America's largest energy corporation to invest billions there, putting its assets and personnel at a direct risk. It is a vivid illustration of the internal incoherence of White House policy. Trump is pursuing two mutually exclusive strategies at once.

The first of Trump's two competing policies is an ideological crusade for regime change driven by the Florida faction under Marco Rubio, an effort to forcibly uproot left-wing autocracies from the Western Hemisphere in the worst tradition of the Monroe Doctrine. The second is a blunt transactional realism focused on securing natural resources and blocking China's expansion. Beijing would gladly snap up Chevron's contracts if the company withdrew. For Trump, Chevron is both a hostage in his confrontation with Maduro and an insurance policy for the future.

It is like a foot wedged in the door if the regime collapses and a safeguard if Maduro survives, allowing Trump to abandon the crusade at any moment and to return to the negotiations he himself previously torpedoed. Still, we should acknowledge that oil may be mostly a red herring. Something Luke Skkea has argued in his ex post. Yes, Venezuela has the world's biggest oil resources, but 80 to 90% of them are ultraheavy crude from the Orinoco belt, thick, difficult to extract, and dependent on imported diluent and a stable power grid, something the shattered country lacks.

That is why production collapsed from 400 million barrels per day in the 1970s to under 400,000 in 2020, recovering only thanks to Iranian condensate and Chevrons return. And although US GF coast refineries value heavy grades, the global shortage of such oil does not change the essential fact. A rapid rebound in Venezuelan output is unrealistic. According to the Baker Institute, a return to three million barrels per day would require a decade and billions of dollars in annual investment.

If oil matters in this story, it is only to prevent China from claiming it by default and not because Washington expects a quick bonanza from Venezuelan fields. Is it the prelude to an invasion, a tool to trigger a coup, or merely the most expensive bluff in the history of American gunboat diplomacy?
