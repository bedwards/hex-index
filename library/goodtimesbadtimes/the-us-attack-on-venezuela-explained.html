Trump presents Operation Absolute Resolve as the first concrete implementation of the new national security strategy published by his administration in early December last year.

The new strategy unveiled by Trump draws heavily on the Monroe Doctrine of 1823.

Originally, the doctrine was meant to prevent European colonial powers from interfering in Latin America. Over time, however, it evolved into a justification for American hegemony in the region. Trump is now proposing a revival and a radicalization of this approach, one that analysts in Washington have dubbed the Danro doctrine. One issue that has set commentators ablaze and one for which President Trump has offered few concrete answers is Venezuela's oil. Venezuela does indeed hold the world's largest proven oil reserves, but between 80 and 90% of them consist of ultraheavy crude from the Orinoco belt, thick, difficult to extract and dependent on imported light dilluent as well as a stable power grid.

something the country in its current state simply does not have. As a result, Venezuela's oil sector is only a shadow of its former self. Production has collapsed from a historic peak of 3 million barrels per day to roughly 900,000 barrels today. Trump has declared that he wants to rebuild Venezuela's oil industry and that this reconstruction should involve American companies investing billions of dollars in the country.

Meanwhile, analysts at Rristat Energy estimate that restoring production to 3 million barrels per day would require roughly $110 billion in investment. And that figure covers upstream exploration and production alone. It is a staggering sum, roughly equivalent to what all US oil majors invested globally in 2024 combined. The challenge is compounded by the fact that Venezuelan output cannot be ramped up quickly.

Even under optimistic assumptions, reaching 3 million barrels per day would [music] take close to 9 years. As a result, American companies are unlikely to rush into rebuilding Venezuela's oil sector, especially since giants like Exxon Mobile and Konoko Phillips have already been burned once before. Their assets in Venezuela were nationalized under the government of Hugo Chavez. The best positioned company today is in fact Chevron, which already operates in Venezuela under a special license and accounts for roughly 20% of the country's current production.

Chevron has the necessary infrastructure and could scale up its investments, leading to a modest increase in output, though certainly not the kind of boom President Trump appears to be hoping for. Even if Venezuela's oil production cannot be significantly expanded, those reserves still carry major geopolitical weight. US control over Venezuelan oil would block the expansion of Chinese companies in the region. and perhaps more importantly, increased pressure on Cuba, which imports around 30,000 barrels of Venezuelan oil per day, covering roughly 25% of its energy needs.
