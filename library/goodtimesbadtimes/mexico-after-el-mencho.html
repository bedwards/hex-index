The new generation Halisco cartel began as a splinter faction from networks previously tied to the Sinaloa cartel. Over time, it absorbed smaller criminal groups across western Mexico along with paramilitary units originally formed to fight a gang known as the Knight Templar. In practice, this amounted to far more than a reshuffleling of the criminal landscape. These spirit factions joined forces and in just a few years transformed from a second tier local outfit into one of the largest criminal empires on the planet.

Today, the New Generation Haliscoco Cartel operates across most of Mexico, but its reach extends well beyond the country's borders. The organization maintains operational networks in the United States, Latin America, Europe, Asia, and even Africa. According to US country narcotics assessments, the cartel now has a presence in over 40 countries. Its operating model is a hybrid, part corporation, part paramilitary force.

Much like the Japanese industrial conglomerate known as Ketsu, CJNG runs a strikingly diversified portfolio. Drug trafficking, fentanyl, above all, illegal mining of industrial and precious metals, extortion, cyber crime, fuel smuggling, human trafficking, control of agricultural production, and money laundering. The key to their success, however, has been a strategy of expansion through absorption. swallowing smaller groups whole.

The cartel commands serious firepower. Assault rifles, RPG launchers, landmines, armed drones, anti-aircraft guns, and improvised explosive devices. This is not theoretical. In May 2015, CJNG fighters used a Russian RPG27 launcher to shoot down a Cougar military helicopter, killing nine Mexican special forces soldiers.

in the hands of assassins trained by military deserters and foreign mercenaries, some with experience from Colombia or Ukraine. These weapons have been turned on Mexican armed forces, police, rivals, and even civilians. Halisco cartel is responsible for spectacular acts of broad daylight violence and high-profile public executions. In June 2020, the cartel attempted to assassinate Mexico City's security chief, Omar Garcia Haruj.

The ambush killed two of his bodyguards and a bystander. The organization also became notorious for executions filmed and posted to social media with victims bodies left hanging from bridges tagged with the letters CJNG. Then in October 2025, three CJNG drones detonated explosive charges outside the state prosecutor's office in Tijuana, just steps from the California border. The devices were packed with nails and metal sharpel.

Though the cartel operates as a formidable money-making machine, it functions more like an armed organization than a traditional mafia. Its methods, territorial control enforced through violence, intimidation of civilians, direct attacks on the state are closer to FARC, Hezbollah or the Vagner group than to the Italian Kosan Nostra, Chinese triads or the Japanese Yakuza. This unchecked expansion would not have been possible without tacit consent from those in power. Organized crime on this scale always needs friends at the top, and this case was no exception.

A recent example comes from the heart of Mexico's Tequila country. In February 2026, just 3 weeks before Elmeno's death, Mexican authorities arrested the mayor of the city of Tequila, Diego Rivera Navara, along with three officials. And this is hardly an isolated case. Corruption in other words reached not just into municipal government but into the federal center of power itself. The future of the new generation Halisco cartel is now deeply uncertain.

Organized crime is an environment where betrayal and internal power plays are the norm. El Mancho's death has left a clear power vacuum at the top of the organization. Drawing on historical precedent from Mexico and elsewhere, four scenarios are plausible. A smooth uncontested succession by a designated error.

Second, a violent seizure of power by a rival. Third, a bloody war of succession that splinters the cartel into waring factions. And finally, a gradual implosion from within. For now, it remains unclear which of these is most likely to unfold.

Elmento's natural successor, his son Reuben Osquera Gonzalez, known as Elmanito, has been serving a life sentence in the United States for several years, which further complicates the question of succession. Some analysts therefore point to a more probable outcome, a takeover by regional cartel commanders or members of the inner circle of the Osguera family. One thing, however, is certain. Cutting the head off a major criminal organization does not mean the Hydra has ceased to exist.

Removing a CEO doesn't destroy the company he run. In the short term, the decapitation strategy works. It destabilizes networks and sends a signal that certain lines cannot be crossed. But the aftermath of the Sinaloa cartel's fragmentation and El Chapo's arrest tells a cautionary tale.

The problem didn't disappear. It produced CJNG. Eliminating leaders alone rarely solves a systemic problem. Meanwhile, as reported by the Center for Studies on Impunity and Justice at Univers Deas Americas, up to 99% of crimes in Mexico result in no conviction.

Without reform of the justice system, every tactical victory is temporary. That is why the Mexican state should seize this pivotal moment to strike at the cartel's hidden financial infrastructure, tighten customs controls, and dismantle the entire support network. The people and institutions that enabled the cartel for years. Over the longer term, such efforts would weaken and perhaps even collapse this particular organization.

But security specialists know well that the total elimination of organized crime is unlikely. Why does this ecosystem keep regenerating? The reasons are structural. First, money.

The fentinel market alone generates billions of dollars a year for CJNG and demand north of the border shows no sign of failing. Second, geography. Mexico is a vast mountainous country riddled with thousands of remote valleys and coastlines where the state has never exercised full control. Third, third is culture, specifically narco culture.

But history shows that in fighting threats like these, governments often have to settle for the lesser evil. That is why for the Mexican government, the elimination of Elmeno is an enormous political victory. In a sense, it also marks a symbolic break with the previous government strategy known by the slogan hacks not bullets promoted by former President Andres Manuel Lopez Orador. that policy sought to limit direct confrontation with cartels, focusing instead on social programs and addressing the root causes of crime.

The symbolic collapse of that strategy came in October 2019 when Mexican forces arrested Ovidio Guzman, the son of El Chapo.
