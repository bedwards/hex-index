The origins of US interest in the island date back as far as 1867 when Secretary of State William Seward, the same man who negotiated the purchase of Alaska from Russia, proposed expanding America's borders by buying Greenland and Iceland from the Kingdom of Denmark. Although the plan ultimately failed, Sewardart's proposal left a lasting imprint on American strategic thinking. From that point on, Greenland began to be seen as a natural extension of the North American continent and a key piece in completing US dominance over the Western Hemisphere.

The United States came the closest to acquiring Greenland during the presidency of Harry Truman. During World War II, the island was under American military control. And as the confrontation with the Soviet Union loomed, Washington was determined to keep it that way permanently. In 1946, the Truman administration formally offered Denmark $100 million in gold for the island.

Notably, the US proposal also included an alternative option, an exchange of strategically important areas of Greenland for oilrich territories near Point Barrow in Alaska, home today to the largest oil field in North America. Despite severe postwar economic hardship, Denmark ultimately rejected Truman's offer. Instead, both sides settled on a compromise that served their respective interests. In 1951, the United States and Denmark signed the Greenland defense agreement, which effectively enshrined Washington's role as the island's primary security guarantor, while reducing Denmark's role largely to that of a civilian administrator.

The agreement granted the United States extensive rights, including the authority to establish so-called defense areas on Greenland, zones that were in practice exempt from Danish jurisdiction. Under this framework, the United States built more than a dozen military bases on the island. At the height of the Cold War, roughly 10,000 American troops were stationed there. At that point, US servicemen accounted for nearly 30% of the entire population of the Green Island.

With the collapse of the Soviet Union and the end of the Cold War, the United States gradually scaled back its presence in Greenland, shutting down successive bases and withdrawing troops. This strategic pause in the Arctic, however, proved short-lived. Since around 2010, we have witnessed not only a steady expansion of Russia's presence in the region, but also the arrival of a new player, the People's Republic of China. Combined with climate change and the melting of Arctic ice, competition for the region has turned into a ruthless race for strategic resources and new trade routes.

Greenland, meanwhile, has found itself at the very center of this struggle. It is difficult to overstate Greenland's strategic importance for Washington and for US national security planning. Let's begin with the military dimension. Pitafik space Base known until 2023 as Tulle air base is the northernmost military installation operated by the United States.

It serves as the eyes and ears of America's defensive architecture. Pafix's significance stems from its unique geographic location which allows for radar coverage of areas inaccessible to other groundbased systems. At the heart of the base lies the ballistic missile early warning system, BMEWS, a radar network capable of detecting and tracking intercontinental ballistic missiles launched by Russia or China toward North America over the Arctic and the North Pole. Without data from Pitafik, Washington's response time to a nuclear attack would be dramatically reduced, undermining the credibility of US nuclear deterrence.

In recent years, the base has also grown in importance in the space domain. Now operated by the US Space Force, Pafik plays a crucial role in monitoring satellites and objects in polar orbit. Yet Greenland's strategic value extends far beyond missile defense alone. The island also forms the western pillar of the so-called GIUK gap.

Greenland, Iceland, and the United Kingdom. This maritime choke point separates the Norwegian Sea from the open Atlantic Ocean. Control of the GIUK gap is strategically vital not only for the security of the United States but for the entire North Atlantic alliance. Coming back to China, the scale of China's Arctic ambitions is best illustrated by the fact that in 2018, Beijing declared itself a quote near Arctic state.

China's policy in the region follows a dual track approach. On the one hand, Beijing seeks to develop what it calls the polar silk road. On the other, it aims to secure permanent footholds in the Arctic by taking control of strategic infrastructure and gaining access to local deposits of rare earth elements. In 2018, the Chinese state-owned China's communication construction company came close to securing a contract worth approximately $550 million to modernize Greenland's airports in Nook, Ilisad, and Quacorto.

Under pressure from the United States, however, the Danish government intervened and blocked the Chinese bid. A similar failure followed China's attempt to acquire the Groadal naval base in southern Greenland. Chinese efforts to gain control over Greenland's rare earth deposits have likewise fallen short. Although China already controls around 60% of global rare earth mining and roughly 90% of their processing, gaining access to Greenland's resources would still be of enormous value to Beijing as it would further strengthen China's monopoly.

Beneath Greenland's ice sheet lies one of the world's largest untapped reserves of strategic raw materials. According to reports by the US Geological Survey and Denmark's Geological Services, the island contains deposits of 25 out of 34 raw materials classified as critical by the European Union and 43 out of 50 minerals deemed vital to US national security. On the contrary, as climate change accelerates and the Arctic ice cap continues to melt, opening up new shipping routes, the contest for the Arctic is only beginning to intensify.

At the same time, Moscow is investing heavily in the infrastructure on the northern sea route, which runs along Russia's northern coastline and forms part of the northeast passage, linking the Atlantic and the Pacific. And yet, these arguments are largely flawed and disconnected from hard data. As we have already noted, every Chinese attempt to gain a foothold in Greenland has ended in failure. Each time Washington signaled its opposition to a Chinese project to Denmark and Greenland, American objections were taken into account and China one way or another was removed from the equation. As for Greenland's natural wealth, the island does indeed hold vast deposits of rare earth elements as well as oil and natural gas.

However, the lack of infrastructure and the harsh climate make extraction extremely costly, though this may change in the coming decades. The core issue, however, lies with Greenland's local population. Under Greenland's self-government act of 2009, the island's inhabitants hold exclusive rights to manage and benefit from their natural resources. In recent years, Greenland's authorities have adopted a series of decisions that sharply restrict resource exploitation.

In 2021, the government introduced a ban on uranium mining as well as on projects in which uranium would appear as a byproduct, effectively killing the field project. In the same year, Greenland's government also decided to stop issuing new licenses for oil and gas exploration. Despite these constraints, American investors have little reason to complain. The US-l project focused on extracting clean rare earth elements without uranium continues to expand.

Greenland's government places considerable hope in this project, viewing the resulting revenues as a potential path toward reducing dependence on Danish subsidies. For Greenland's 57,000 inhabitants, most of them Inuit, a sudden surge of global interest in their island is a double-edged sword.

The desire for full sovereignity from Denmark runs deep. Polls conducted in 2025 show that as many as 84% of Greenlanders support independence. Yet replacing dependence on Denmark with a new dependence on the United States enjoys little public backing. When asked which country they would prefer to align with politically, 85% of respondents said they favored a loose association with Copenhagen over incorporation by Washington.

Greenland's prime minister Yens Frederick Nielson is therefore walking a tight rope. He firmly rejects the idea of selling the island while stressing that Greenland is quote open for business and eager to cooperate with the United States on a partnership basis. Seeing this as an opportunity for development rather than subordination, the central obstacle on the road to independence is economic. Greenland remains dependent on an annual Danish subsidy, which in 2025 amounted to roughly $600 million.

That transfer accounts for more than 50% of Greenland's public revenues and around 20% of the island's GDP. Without it, maintaining current levels of public services, education, or healthcare would be impossible. At the same time, surveys consistently show that Greenlanders support independence only if it does not lead to a decline in their standard of living. Washington has hinted it could assume the financial burden of sustaining the island.

Investments in mining and tourism, the argument goes, could replace the Danish subsidy. Experts, however, warned that even a rapid expansion of mining would be unlikely to fill the gap left by Danish transfers in the near future.
