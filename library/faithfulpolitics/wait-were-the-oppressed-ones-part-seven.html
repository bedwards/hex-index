It will surprise absolutely no one that discrimination exists in America. The real debate is over who’s being discriminated against. According to a Pew Research Center survey from April 2025, most Americans believe there’s “a lot” or “some” discrimination against immigrants in the U.S. illegally (82%) and against transgender people (77%). But when you focus on who Republicans feel are being discriminated against a different picture emerges.

In the same Pew survey, just over half of Republicans (55%) said white people face “a lot or some” discrimination, while 63% said the same of religious people, and 57% specifically cited evangelical Christians. Those numbers are higher or on par with many racial or ethnic minority groups, reflecting how perceptions of discrimination diverge sharply along partisan, religious, and even cultural lines.

This understanding of Republican’s beliefs about Christian persecution is important context if you want to better understand the contents of the Initial Report by the Task Force to Eradicate Anti-Christian Bias released June 2025. The first of three reports required by the Executive Order establishing the task force, alleged that the Biden administration “engaged in an egregious pattern of targeting peaceful Christians while ignoring violent, anti-Christian offenses.”

The Initial Report

If you already believe Christians are being unfairly targeted, this report will likely confirm that belief. And if you think claims of anti-Christian bias are exaggerated or politically driven, you’ll probably find reasons to believe that too. Despite its sweeping title, the report isn’t a rigorous investigation into government misconduct—it’s a collection of testimonies, case studies, and agency summaries—some credible, others questionable—woven together to tell a story of discrimination.

The best way to read it isn’t to accept or reject it outright, but to pay attention to the stories and the lived experiences behind them. Many of those who provided testimony for the report genuinely believe their faith was under attack, and that conviction deserves to be understood, even if some claims don’t hold up under scrutiny. As a Christian, I wanted to see how this broader narrative of persecution stacks up. So I looked more closely at several examples from the report to see which, if any, withstand a deeper look. In this piece, I focus on one case in particular—inviting you to decide whether it truly reflects anti-Christian bias, or something more complicated.

The Case of Paul Vaughn

One of the first stories the Task Force highlights is Paul Vaughn’s. He’s a father of eleven and a longtime pro-life activist who became something of a cause célèbre in conservative and Christian media. Vaughn was arrested by armed federal agents at his home after what he and his lawyer described as a “peaceful prayer protest” outside a Tennessee abortion clinic. He and others sang hymns and prayed, believing they were engaging in civil disobedience to “rescue” women seeking abortions.

When photos of Vaughn being handcuffed in his driveway hit social media, with his kids watching from the porch, it lit a fire. To many Christians, it looked like the government had criminalized faith itself, or at least that’s how it was portrayed.

There’s no doubt Vaughn is sincere. He’s the president of Personhood Tennessee and a man of conviction. Additionally, in interviews on multiple media outlets, he deeply believes his arrest was tied to his faith, I’m not questioning that. But the facts of his case are far more complex. When you compare what’s in the court record to how the Anti-Christian Bias Task Force portrays his story, the two don’t entirely line up.

Here’s how the anti-Christian bias report presents Vaughn’s case:

These first-hand accounts began with a video highlighting the story of Mr. Paul Vaughn, who was arrested at gunpoint at his family home and in front of his wife and children for praying and singing outside of an abortion facility. Mr. Vaughn was tried and convicted by the BidenGarland [sic] DOJ under the Freedom of Access to Clinic Entrances Act, on an unprecedented and untested theory. President Trump later pardoned Mr. Vaughn along with nearly two dozen other victims of the Biden-Garland DOJ’s weaponization of the FACE Act against peaceful, pro-life demonstrators.

It’s true, Vaughn was charged under the Freedom of Access to Clinic Entrances Act, better known as the FACE Act, which has been on the books since 1994. It doesn’t punish prayer or protest. It punishes physical obstruction. Judge Aleta Trauger, who presided over the case, made that crystal clear. “The FACE Act,” she wrote, “criminalizes neither protesting nor any other activity protected by the First Amendment.” The problem wasn’t the praying.

Vaughn’s lawyers argued that he was selectively prosecuted, that the DOJ targeted pro-life Christians while ignoring vandalism at pregnancy centers, which is worth looking at and something we’ll get to later. But the claim that DOJ targets pro-life Christians didn’t hold up. The evidence was mostly anecdotal and drawn from ideological media sources, not data. As the judge pointed out, organized daytime blockades and late-night property damage aren’t the same kind of crime.

When Vaughn appealed, his case went before three Republican-appointed judges: Alan Norris (Reagan), Raymond Kethledge (Bush), and Joan Larsen (Trump). The last two identify as Christians, couldn’t find much for Norris. All three denied his request to delay sentencing and agreed there was no evidence of selective prosecution. In a time of increasing polarization, conservative judicial consensus like this makes it difficult to argue that the Biden administration was motivated by anti-Christian bias.

About FACE: Understanding the Law at the Center of the Debate

To really understand some of the political dynamics around Vaughn’s case, you have to understand what the FACE Act is, which is a mid-1990s bill aimed at stopping violence against medical clinics and religious institutions.

In the early 90s, violence against abortion providers was spiraling. Clinics were firebombed, doctors were threatened, and in 1993 an abortion provider, Dr. David Gunn, was murdered. Groups like Operation Rescue organized mass “rescues,” physically blocking clinic doors to stop abortions. So Congress stepped in.

The FACE Act was introduced by Senator Ted Kennedy and Representative Chuck Schumer, and it even had support from moderate Republicans like Arlen Specter and Nancy Kassebaum, who saw it as a law-and-order issue, not a moral one. It passed with bipartisan support, 69 to 30 in the Senate and 241 to 174 in the House, and was signed into law by President Bill Clinton in 1994.

The law made it a federal crime to use force, threats, or physical obstruction to keep someone from accessing or providing reproductive health services. On paper, it also provides similar protections for places of religious worship. In practice, though, most prosecutions involved abortion clinic blockades because that’s where the physical confrontations happened.

From the start, some pro-life activists felt the law was unfairly applied and efforts are currently underway to repeal it despite its success in reducing attacks on medical clinics. The effort to dismantle a law deemed unfair only when it shields those they dislike is a paradox the task force itself seems unwilling to solve. Nevertheless, what was meant to be a measure against violence quickly came to be seen as proof that the government was disproportionately targeting Christians.

Much of the perception of selective prosecution stems from the fact that the Freedom of Access to Clinic Entrances (FACE) Act protects two distinct categories of facilities: those that provide reproductive health services and places of religious worship.

That perception has hardened over time and when the Dobbs opinion was leaked on May 2, 2022, the increased attacks on many Christian affiliated organizations made that perception even more real.

After Dobbs: When Perception Meets Reality

After the Dobbs decision on June 24, 2022 overturned Roe v. Wade, there was a spike in attacks on pregnancy centers and churches, including vandalism, graffiti, and arson. As a result, Republicans in Congress later passed a resolution calling:

Upon the Biden Administration to use all appropriate law enforcement authorities to uphold public safety and to protect the rights of pro-life facilities, groups, and churches.

It passed the House of Representatives January 11, 2023.

But to say that the Biden administration was not tuned in to the increased violence against churches would ignore the fact that they launched the Protecting Places of Worship Interagency Policy Committee, a partnership between the Department of Justice, the Department of Homeland Security, and others, to improve threat sharing and security planning for faith-based organizations. The DOJ also expanded its Place to Worship Initiative, which educates officials and religious leaders about land-use protections under the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act. And through its Community Relations Service, the DOJ has hosted Protecting Places of Worship forums across the country, connecting clergy, law enforcement, and security experts to talk about real-world safety issues. I was unable to find any reports that show the efficacy of these programs.

Critics of the Biden administration often point to what they see as a double standard: a lack of attention to attacks on pro-life facilities, churches, and advocacy groups, paired with continued enforcement of FACE Act cases that mostly involve abortion clinics. And to be fair, the numbers support at least part of that perception—nearly all FACE Act prosecutions since the law was enacted in 1994 have involved facilities that provide abortions.

But that’s only part of the story. Many attacks on churches or other religious institutions are prosecuted under hate crime statutes, not the FACE Act, which helps explain the difference in enforcement. The federal government already has multiple tools and programs dedicated to protecting faith communities.

One of those laws, 18 U.S.C. § 247, known as the Church Arson Prevention Act, makes it a federal crime to damage, destroy, or threaten religious property—or to use force or threats to obstruct anyone’s free exercise of religion—because of that property’s religious character.

Another, 18 U.S.C. § 249, commonly referred to as the Hate Crimes Prevention Act, applies to offenses motivated by a person’s actual or perceived race, color, religion, or national origin. It carries severe penalties: up to ten years in prison for bodily injury or attempted bodily injury, and up to life in prison if the offense results in death or involves kidnapping, aggravated sexual abuse, or an attempt to kill.

While the DOJ doesn’t maintain a public running tally, several church arson cases during the Biden administration—including prosecutions in Ohio and Arizona—show that such crimes continue to be aggressively pursued under existing hate crime laws.

But not all prosecutions lead to arrests, as some of the most serious incidents, like the fire bombings at CompassCare in New York and Life Choices in Colorado, remain unsolved despite FBI investigations and reward offers. Others, such as vandalism and threats in Florida and Texas, have led to arrests and prosecutions.

What It All Means

It’s true that FACE enforcement hasn’t been consistent. But that’s not the same as saying the Biden administration “engaged in an egregious pattern of targeting peaceful Christians” as the Anti-Christian Bias Taskforce alleges. Or as White House spokeswoman Taylor Rogers said on Fox News, “Joe Biden weaponized the full weight of the federal government against Christians and trampled on their fundamental First Amendment rights.” That’s a bit of a stretch when looking at just the Paul Vaughn case.

In Paul Vaughn’s case, seeing armed agents at his home and hearing that his kids were briefly detained feels wrong. Excessive, even, and I think there’s a strong argument that bipartisan agreement could be found in investigating judicial overreach and law enforcement reform. But court filings and appellate rulings make it clear that Vaughn wasn’t prosecuted for being a Christian. He was prosecuted for breaking the law. His defense team argued that since Roe v. Wade was overturned, the FACE Act no longer applied. Three Republican judges disagreed.

Seeing the Tree for the Forest

The Eradicating Anti-Christian Bias report tells us less about the government than it does about ourselves. It shows how easily perception becomes conviction and how politics can turn faith into a weapon. For those convinced Christians are under siege, the report feels like proof. For others, it confirms that religion has become another partisan talking point. Both sides see what they want to see.

Unsurprisingly, the truth is more complicated. Real bias against people of faith exists and should be taken seriously. But much of what is labeled “anti-Christian” looks more like the friction of misunderstanding and nuance, taking the time to research each claim is a lot of work and most Americans are generally okay with face value propositions by our elected leaders. .

Paul Vaughn’s case captures that tension. His faith is genuine, but his prosecution was not about belief. It was about blocking a clinic entrance under a law that has existed for decades. Calling that persecution flattens the story and misses the nuance that truth requires.

The same is true for the report’s other examples, from denied vaccine exemptions in the military to fines against Christian colleges. Each deserves scrutiny, but the evidence rarely matches the conclusion. The report insists on bias without proving intent.

That is where the Task Force stumbles. In trying to defend religious freedom, it ends up making the same mistake it condemns by narrowing a complex reality into a single story. It calls out bias while practicing its own.
