Cities are better when there are more involved citizens, and they're at their best when the involved citizens mirror the views and demographics of the city as a whole. I say this because it wasn't always the case. Here in the United States, we have a history of not listening to low-income residents and people of color when making important decisions. One example of this is the phase in our history where a policy called Urban Renewal wre havoc on our central cities.

Urban renewal is officially called the Housing Act of 1949. And the purpose of the bill was to eliminate slums and replace it with higher quality housing. Unfortunately, there's little to no outreach done with the people who lived in those neighborhoods about whether or not they wanted to be evicted. Whole neighborhoods disappeared and new public housing went up in its place.

Later, the bill was modified so it became easier to evict residents and transfer that land to private developers for the construction of things like urban shopping malls. By the 1960s, public opinion soured on urban renewal, and it became clear among reformers that slum Claris was unjust and unwarranted. At the same time, the United States was going through all sorts of social upheaval as a result of the Vietnam War and the civil rights movement. Professional planning began to understand that they needed to value all voices in the process.

And by the 1970s, urban renewal was dead and replaced with policies that required community input first. I breezed through a lot of history on a very important topic there.
