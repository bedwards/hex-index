In April 2017, during the first Trump administration, thousands of scientists and science advocates gathered in Washington D.C. and cities across the world for the [March For Science](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/04/22/science/march-for-science.html) to protest policies that proposed, among other things, dramatic reductions in funding for scientific research.

Nearly eight years later and faced with even more urgent threats to scientific research, scientists again organized a local movement that quickly became global. On March 7, 2025, scientists and their supporters went out into the streets for the Stand Up For Science 2025 rally to protest Trump administration policies aimed at defunding and disintegrating scientific research in the United States. Stand Up For Science had capacity to support rallies at 32 sites across the U.S., but “it ended up being a lot more than 32,” said [JP Flores](https://jpflores.rbind.io/), our most [recent guest on Big Biology](https://www.bigbiology.org/episodes/2025/7/24/ep-137-student-spotlight-finding-passion-in-science-communication-and-advocacy-with-jp-flores). “It ended up going global. People in France were also in on it.”

*PhD student JP Flores*

Flores is a Ph.D candidate in bioinformatics and computational biology at the University of North Carolina and was one of the organizers of the Stand Up For Science rally. His involvement was a response to the feeling that something had to be done to combat government attacks on science. “I just felt hopeless for the first time in my life. And as I was looking for answers…I thought about the March For Science. And I don't know why this idea got planted in my head, but I was like, why don't we just do that again?”

The rally on March 7 has become a launching point for a larger movement that is taking shape and that aims to highlight the importance of science to the local communities it seeks to serve. “It's not, oh, we're in the ivory tower, and we need to tell everyone to support us. I don't think that's how this should work,” Flores says. “It is—How do you get scientists into communities and have them talk to everyone such that the community takes them in and actually cares and supports them? That's what we're trying to do.”

Below is a description of the various science advocacy groups and ways you can get involved, regardless of your experience with science or advocacy.

**Science For Good**

[Science For Good](https://sciforgood.org/) is a non-profit organization co-founded by Flores and other SUFS team members that is a partnership-focused initiative bringing scientists out of academic settings and into communities to share the meaning behind their research. “The idea with \[Science for Good\] was, we were thinking about how to rebuild science once we weather this storm,” Flores explains. “How do we think about science communication and how do we embed that into the fabric of science? How do we get science policy and advocacy in the minds of scientists, and how can we help develop this workforce so that they are thinking about these things?”

One of their current initiatives is called Brewing Scientists, where “we basically pair research labs with breweries in their local town, and put their research facts on beer labels,” Flores describes. “So we're trying to figure out how, if we can't bring people to us, how do we just get science into communities? This is one way we thought would be really innovative and cool.”

Another Science For Good initiative called “What’s the big idea?” hosts science open mic nights at sites across the U.S “where scientists are just going up to a microphone and talking about how the funding cuts affect them and how it affects the public,” Flores shares.

Ultimately, Science For Good’s focus is engaging local communities with local scientists, and vice-versa. “Our whole premise with Science for Good is we are nothing without communities, the only people, the only things that we should be thinking about right now are communities and how we could best have our impact there.”

*How to get involved:* Scientists at any career stage can sign up for email updates through the [Science for Good website](https://sciforgood.org/get-involved/) to receive calls to action; information about events and organizing opportunities; and toolkits, templates, and community success stories. Additionally, anyone with an idea for an event or initiative that they’d like to start or collaborate on can email the Science For Good team ([info@sciforgood.org](mailto:info@sciforgood.org)) to receive guidance and materials and to be connected with others doing similar work.

**Scientist Network for Advancing Policy**

The [Scientist Network for Advancing Policy](https://snapcoalition.org/) (SNAP) is “an international group of early-career scientists dedicated to mobilizing for large-scale initiatives and building capacity to bridge gaps between science and society” (from the SNAP website). This grassroots organization led primarily by graduate students seeks to “build up a workforce of scientists that will go to the representatives and talk about science,” Flores explains.

*How to get involved:* Scientists and science advocates with any level of experience are welcome to fill out the [SNAP interest form](https://docs.google.com/forms/d/e/1FAIpQLScBfsc62aqefilfjhyTRLAwjU4jPsyk434IagRqKWIkcwH-PA/viewform) to learn more and join the network. This month (August 2025), SNAP is organizing [congressional district visits](https://snapcoalition.org/initiatives/district-visits/) to members of Congress while they are away from Washington D.C. Anyone interested in participating can reach out to Alex Rich ([a.rich@yale.edu](mailto:a.rich@yale.edu)) or SNAP ([SNAPscipolorg@gmail.org](mailto:SNAPscipolorg@gmail.org)) with questions. SNAP also hopes to host [science policy courses](https://snapcoalition.org/initiatives/) in the future, so follow them on social media, fill out the interest form, and/or check their website to stay up-to-date!

**McClintock Letters & Science Homecoming**

The [McClintock Letters](https://blogs.cornell.edu/asap/events-initiatives/the-mcclintock-letters/) and [Science Homecoming](https://sciencehomecoming.com/) initiatives are efforts to have scientists publish op-eds in their local newspapers covering how their research impacts the local community and how the local community is important for their research. Publishing in local newspapers will reach an audience that might not otherwise hear from scientists and help demonstrate to local community members the significance of their support for science.

*How to get involved:* Anyone interested in writing an op-ed/letter to the editor in their local newspaper is encouraged to visit the website of their local newspaper to determine the requirements for publication (if you are unsure of the newspaper for which you should write, see the map on the [Science Homecoming website](https://sciencehomecoming.com/)). Science Homecoming and McClintock Letters both offer [tips for writing](https://sciencehomecoming.com/) and [examples that have been published](https://blogs.cornell.edu/asap/events-initiatives/the-mcclintock-letters/published-mcclintock-letters/). Once you have your piece drafted, you can send it to [editor@sciencehomecoming.com](mailto:editor@sciencehomecoming.com) for a free editing service. Finally, if you publish an article, make sure to send it to either the Science Homecoming or McClintock Letters team so that they can add it to their count!
