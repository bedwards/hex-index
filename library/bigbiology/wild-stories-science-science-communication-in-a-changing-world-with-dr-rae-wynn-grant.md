Most people are probably familiar with Hollywood’s biggest awards like the Oscars, the Emmys, and the Grammys. An award people may not be so familiar with are the Gracies, which are sponsored by the [Alliance for Women in Media](https://allwomeninmedia.org/gracies/) and “recognize exemplary programming created by women, for women and about women in all facets of media and entertainment.”

[Dr. Rae Wynn-Grant](https://www.raewynngrant.com/), last week’s featured guest on [Big Biology](https://www.bigbiology.org/episodes/2025/7/3/ep-136-the-call-of-the-wild-with-rae-wynn-grant), is the recipient of this year’s Gracie Award for Best Television Host for her work as co-host of Mutual of Omaha’s reboot of [Wild Kingdom: Protecting The Wild](https://www.mutualofomaha.com/wild-kingdom/protecting-the-wild). The show, which airs Saturday mornings on NBC and is available for streaming on Peacock, highlights successful conservation projects across the world that are aiding the recovery of endangered species.

Co-hosting the show has been a dream-come-true for Wynn-Grant, who recognized from an early age that she wanted to be the host of a nature show. “I grew up in inner city areas and had a wonderful upbringing…but my family didn’t do hiking and camping or skiing or hunting,” she explains. “It was great…I had this amazing childhood, but my exposure to nature and the outdoors was on TV. And so I would watch nature shows, and I didn’t just enjoy them…I very much was like, ‘When I’m a grown-up, I want to do this job. I want to spend 100% of my professional time doing what I’m seeing these guys \[nature show hosts\] do.’”

**A long, winding road**

The path to her role as a TV host was anything but straight, especially as an Black kid growing up in the city. “It was complicated, mostly because there’s a lack of representation. You know, I was a seven year old Black girl from an inner city upbringing and watching British and Australian middle-aged white men who just seemed to inherently know everything about nature, right?”

But she never let this lack of representation crush her dream. Upon arriving at college, Wynn-Grant asked her advisor what she should study to become a nature show host. The first recommendation was theater. When it quickly became clear that theater wasn’t the route for her, the next recommendation was journalism. When journalism didn’t feel like the right fit, either, a chance encounter with someone from the environmental science department changed everything. “They said, ‘Oh, wild animals and their conservation. Girl, did you know that there's actually a whole field of studies surrounding this? It's called science…join us.’ And so that was a hit,” Wynn-Grant recalls.

She quickly joined the environmental science major and spent a semester abroad in East Africa, “and that's where I saw my first wild animal, that's where I got involved in my very first research project. And that's where it solidified for me that—I'm going to be an ecologist, and I’m going to do science.” She hasn’t looked back. Wynn-Grant spent the rest of her time as an undergraduate and then Master’s student studying lions in Tanzania before shifting to study how [human activity influences black bears](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S235198941730224X) for her [Ph.D](https://www.proquest.com/docview/1748049507?pq-origsite=gscholar&fromopenview=true&sourcetype=Dissertations%20&%20Theses).

All the while, she held tight to her dream of hosting a nature show “ultimately…to get more inner city Black kids interested in ecology.” She hired an agent and began pitching herself to different networks, but “I got a lot of rejection,” Wynn-Grant says. One particular network executive was impressed with what Wynn-Grant had to offer but told her “You don’t have the look of a wildlife nature show host. Look at all the nature show hosts out there. They're not urban, millennial, Black women. Sorry, that's just not going to work.”

Eventually, Wynn-Grant decided to leave her nature show host dream behind. “I stopped. I really stopped. I thought to myself, You know what? I am satisfied. I travel around the world. I work to save endangered species, I mentor graduate students. I really believe that the work that I do makes a difference, and that's the main point, right?” Few would disagree. It was only after letting go of that dream that the opportunity found her. “I'm telling you I did not audition. I didn't apply. I had no idea that Wild Kingdom was returning to the air. It was a huge surprise when they came knocking asking me to consider joining them.” Clearly, Wynn-Grant has proven to be a natural: as the first Black woman to host a nature show, she is also the [first scientist to win a Gracie](https://www.instagram.com/p/DKAHy_wpY2Q/?utm_source=ig_web_copy_link&igsh=MTZycXgzM3gxeDA2MQ%3D%3D&img_index=6) in the 50 year-history of the award.

**Advice for aspiring science communicators**

As the co-host of a nature show, the host of the award-winning podcast [Going Wild with Dr. Rae Wynn-Grant](https://www.pbs.org/wnet/nature/podcast/), and author of the memoir [Wild Life: Finding my Purpose in an Untamed World](https://www.raewynngrant.com/wild-life-a-memoir), Wynn-Grant has plenty of advice for aspiring science communicators.

She recommends that “a good way to start is to find your small pond and be a big fish in it. So whether that’s your university or your neighborhood or your hometown or your state. I really do think that any student that I have could call up some newspaper, local channel, or radio show and be offered a few minutes to talk about their work and why it's important. And that really adds up.” It’s not all about having the biggest platform, Wynn-Grant explains. What matters more is making an impact wherever you are and however you can.

Wynn-Grant is quick to admit that her first foray into science communication wasn’t anything flashy. During a class for her Master’s degree at Yale, she learned that trees sequester carbon dioxide and thought to herself, “I would bet you that no one I know in my community knows anything about carbon sequestration.” Wynn-Grant wanted to change that, so she made a PowerPoint presentation and went to the New Haven Public Library. “I reserved a room, I printed out flyers, and I had it the next day.”

The turnout? “I think three people came, and two of them were my roommates,” Wynn-Grant laughs. “And I gave a presentation, and it obviously made zero impact on anybody, right? No one was even there. But for me, it felt like a really important service to my community and it was something I could do on my own. I didn’t need a network, I didn’t need funding. I was like, I can do science communication to my community about stuff that’s impacting my community. It kind of lit this fire in me to just do what I can instead of looking for the biggest platform ever.”

Though she acknowledges that climate change and the other environmental crises facing our world are increasingly urgent, Wynn-Grant leaves room for hope. “One of the things that keeps me hopeful in times like this is that we have the answers, particularly to the climate crisis,” she says. “Scientists have been working really hard for many, many years to give us the solutions that we need. So I feel a little bit of peace knowing that we know what to do, and it’s just the fight that we’re fighting is getting those policies and plans implemented.” The task is monumental, and everyone will need to contribute. But inspiring that action starts with those who can translate science into stories that move people. In a world facing serious challenges, Wynn-Grant reminds us that the path forward depends on helping people see themselves not just as bystanders, but as vital agents of change.

“I do believe that scientists and science communicators have a more important role than ever in reaching all kinds of people in an accessible way, in an entertaining way, in an inspiring way,” Wynn-Grant reflects. “We all have a lot of work to do to help people realize that they, too, can do this and can be leaders. So that’s what I’m definitely trying to pivot towards as well, to help folks feel that no matter what stage of life they’re in, they can have some power in shaping the future.”
