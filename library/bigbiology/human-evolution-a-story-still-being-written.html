This week’s episode of Big Biology features a re-release of our most popular episode: The Origin of Us: Human Evolution with Kate Wong. Wong is a Senior Editor at Scientific American where she has covered the field of biological anthropology for more than two decades. In the episode, Wong, Art, and Marty discuss the article she wrote to commemorate Scientific American’s 175th anniversary (which occurred back in 2020) entitled “How Scientists Discovered the Staggering Complexity of Human Evolution.” This week’s blog will provide a deeper dive into the various hypotheses of human evolution that they mention in the episode, as well as a look at the present-day relevance of human evolution research.

The Hypotheses

It wasn’t long after Darwin published his seminal book, On the Origin of Species , that biologists started debating the nature of human evolution. In her article, Wong mentions that Thomas Henry Huxley and Charles Lyell published the first books about human evolution in the 1860s, shortly before Darwin published Descent of Man in 1871. A lack of fossil evidence, however, and a general resistance to the idea that humans first evolved in Africa meant that early theories of human evolution were often influenced by racial biases, which slowed progress in understanding our species’ origins until new fossil discoveries in the 20th century began to clarify our evolutionary history.

In the mid-20th century, the Multiregional Evolution Hypothesis proposed that “Homo sapiens emerged from archaic populations that are found around throughout the Old World,” Wong explains. According to this view, gene flow between these populations prevented human ancestral species from diverging while allowing the different groups to maintain regional variation. The Multiregional model explained regional variation as the result of long-term local evolution, but it struggled to account for the growing fossil record. By the late 20th century, evidence (especially genetic data) was mounting that modern humans arose more recently in Africa, leading many scientists to favor a new hypothesis: Out of Africa.

In the 1980s, new fossil and genetic evidence started to shift the scientific consensus to the Out of Africa Hypothesis, which suggested that “modern Homo sapiens evolved in Africa, spread out across the world, and displaced all other hominids that were there.” The Out of Africa Hypothesis holds that modern Homo sapiens evolved in Africa between 200,000 and 300,000 years ago and migrated outward, replacing other hominin groups such as Neandertals and Denisovans with little to no interbreeding. Studies of mitochondrial DNA tracing all living humans back to a common maternal ancestor in Africa—the proverbial “mitochondrial Eve”—helped solidify the Out of Africa Hypothesis as one of the leading hypotheses explaining human evolution at the time, often pitted directly against the Multiregional Evolution Hypothesis.

The real story appears rather more complicated.

Our current state of knowledge dismisses the purest forms of either of these popular hypotheses. “Pure Multiregional Evolution and the strictest Out of Africa scenario…are really not supported at this point,” Wong says in the episode. Instead, she explains that a hybrid of both hypotheses is gaining traction. “Multiregional evolution within Africa is a newer idea that’s being embraced,” she elaborates, which is the idea that there were “multiple populations of Homo sapiens that split apart, evolved along their own trajectories behaviorally and anatomically, and then came back together.” It is from this pattern of human populations isolating, evolving, gathering and interbreeding that “who we are today sort of coalesces, from all of that diversity,” Wong writes.

These alternating periods of isolation and interbreeding were often mediated by changing climate conditions. Wong explains in the episode that “when there's a drying of the climate, deserts expand, and these little populations are pushed away from each other. And then when it gets wetter and the climate is more favorable, they are more easily able to connect and interbreed and exchange culture and technology.” While Wong affirms that the evidence supporting the notion that Homo sapiens first emerged from Africa is indisputable, she makes it clear that our ancestors did not evolve in isolation from all other hominin species. Rather, Homo sapiens are “the sole surviving twig on what was once a luxuriant evolutionary bush,” she writes. Why our species is the only one left remains an open question.

Why does it all matter?

Learning more about our species’ evolutionary history might sound like an Indiana Jones-style adventure, but the relevance of our deepening understanding of human evolution to our current context should not be overlooked.

In her article, Wong describes how early scientists attempted to manipulate Darwin’s theory “to support notions of racial superiority. Social Darwinism, for one, misapplied Darwin's ideas about the struggle for existence in natural selection to human society, providing a pseudoscientific rationalization for social injustice and oppression.” Advances in DNA technology have dramatically improved our ability to study human evolution and have helped dispel the harmful and oft-cited misconception that “races are biologically discrete groups with separate origins,” writes Wong. She continues, “Modern human variation is continuous, and most variation exists within populations rather than between them—the product of our demographic history as a species that originated in Africa with populations that mixed continuously as they migrated around the world.”

Advances in DNA technology have also helped us realize that Homo sapiens DNA is riddled with genes from other hominin species, particularly Neandertals and Denisovans, some of which has health consequences for humans living today. For example, a 2022 study by Kerner and Quintana-Murci found that certain variants on chromosome 12 inherited from Neandertal ancestors reduced the risk of hospitalization due to COVID-19 by 22 percent, but other variants on chromosome 3 inherited from Neandertal ancestors increased the risk of hospitalization by 60 percent.

Additionally, there is much more we are still learning about human evolution. A new study published in Nature last month by Villmoare et al. uncovered fossil evidence for an entirely new species in the Australopithecus genus, found in the Ledi-Geraru Research Project area of Ethiopia. These new fossils, which are teeth between 2.6 million and 2.8 million years old, add a fourth distinct hominin lineage—this new Australopithecus species in addition to early Homo, Paranthropus, and Australopithecus garhi, lineages —living in eastern Africa between 3 million and 2.5 million years ago. In a recent article she wrote about the study, Wong summarizes that “the new find underscores the complexity of human origins. Although Homo sapiens is the only hominin species on Earth today, for the vast majority of humanity’s existence, multiple hominin species shared the planet.” Human evolution research continues to unearth new details about our species’ history, the full significance of which we have yet to understand.

Taken together, these insights show us that human evolution is much more than a story about the distant past. Understanding our evolutionary history not only explains the origins of our species but can also guide how we confront challenges related to global health and social justice. As the only extant species in our genus, it is easy to forget that our ancestors once lived among a “luxuriant evolutionary bush” of other hominin species. Recognizing this diversity reminds us that what makes us human is the product of a long, complex, and shared evolutionary journey—one that continues to shape who we are and who we may become.
