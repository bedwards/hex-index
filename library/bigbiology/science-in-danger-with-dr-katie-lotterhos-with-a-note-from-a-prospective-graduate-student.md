Science in the U.S. is in grave danger. Funding cuts, censorship of scientific terms, travel restrictions, and the accompanying uncertainty that comes with all of these problems are bringing much of the scientific machinery in the United States to a halt. Not only are these actions having devastating effects on major scientific research projects, the rate at which such actions are being taken are making it difficult to track their full impact. [Dr. Katie Lotterhos](https://cos.northeastern.edu/people/katie-lotterhos/), an associate professor at Northeastern University and [our most recent guest on Big Biology](https://www.bigbiology.org/episodes/2025/10/30/ep-141-vulnerability-in-science-and-in-genomes-with-katie-letterhos), is aiming to change that.

*Dr. Katie Lotterhos, associate professor at Northeastern*

As the secretary for the [American Society of Naturalists](https://www.amnat.org/home.html), Lotterhos helped coordinate and implement a [survey of scientists](https://sites.google.com/view/scientiststoriesfederalimpacts/impacts/2025-july-survey) from all sectors—academia, government agencies, non-profit organizations, and industry—to understand the impacts of the Trump administration’s policies on ecology, evolution, marine science and environmental science. “I see \[the survey\] as a snapshot in time that in 5 years or 10 years, we can look back to and say we have this evidence that things changed dramatically at this time point,” Lotterhos explains in the episode. “We can… use \[the survey\] to justify that…in the future, we’re going to need more resources to get back to where we were before.”

While the data in the survey will certainly be an important historical reference, for Lotterhos, “the goal of the survey was more to give people a platform to share their stories,” she says. “I wanted to provide them with a voice when they didn’t feel comfortable speaking out themselves.”

Nearly 1400 scientists completed the survey. The responses paint a sobering picture of the damage inflicted on science since January. Lotterhos and her team received accounts describing projects stalled, partnerships severed, and language constrained. One federal scientist reported receiving a “banned word list” that prohibited the use of words such as, “climate change, climate science, clean energy, solar power, affordable home, low income housing, runoff, microplastics, water pollution, and safe drinking water” among a much longer list that included a total of 48 terms that are routinely used in scientific grants and papers.\*

Others described how funding freezes and administrative restrictions halted urgent public health and environmental projects. “My team was awarded funding to respond to a disease that threatens to devastate the pork industry,” one wildlife researcher wrote. “Despite having the expertise, infrastructure, and urgency to act, we were stopped in our tracks. The funding freeze and administrative restrictions made it impossible to move forward. In many ways, this harm is irreparable.”

Another respondent studying salt marshes noted that their NOAA grants had been cut, making it impossible to even mention “global change” in proposals. Others lamented the disappearance of senior federal partners whose decades of experience in biodiversity monitoring and coastal protection had no replacement. As one participant put it, “These partnerships are highly collaborative and nonpartisan... their effectiveness derives from political neutrality... abrupt loss of access to federal personnel means projects are now being advised with markedly less experience.”

And it’s not just the direct funding cuts that are having an impact. The [report](https://sites.google.com/view/scientiststoriesfederalimpacts/impacts/2025-july-survey) notes: “Direct cuts or uncertainty caused by the threat of cuts caused disruption to summer data collection projects and long-term monitoring programs.” Direct cuts and uncertainty also “caused a severe narrowing of the job market, cancellations of undergraduate training programs, cuts to graduate admission programs by universities, rescinded offers, and cuts to merit-based fellowship programs.” One professor shared: “My entire department hired just one graduate student this year because although our funding has not yet been cut, the threat that it might be cut means we cannot commit to paying graduate students in the future.”

This latter point strikes a personal chord with me as a current prospective graduate student. Attempting to navigate an already-stressful application process has become even more difficult due to current funding cuts and uncertainty about future funding, which have together made the number of advisors able and willing to accept new graduate students disconcertingly small. In addition to the usual application emotions of anxiety and excitement, I now feel a certain sense of desperation as deadlines begin to approach. Is this my only shot at making it to graduate school? If I don’t get in this year, will I have to wait until a more science-friendly administration is in office for another chance? Should I look abroad? Or will I decide to simply forgo graduate school altogether?

Of course, it is far too early to answer any of these questions, and much could change in the coming weeks, months, and years. However, the approach of the Trump administration towards science makes me fearful for my own future as a scientist and for the future generations of scientists who will undoubtedly be affected by actions taken by the federal government in the past year.

All in all, the current state of affairs for science and scientists is quite discouraging. But people like Lotterhos remind us that how we respond to such discouragement is still within our control. We can choose to wallow and allow our inaction to breed hopelessness. Or, we can take action in whatever form is available to us, like coordinating a national survey or pushing forward with graduate school applications. And many scientists *are* opting to take action. Working to [rescue invaluable data](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-00374-y) from [government databases](https://www.datarescueproject.org/about-data-rescue-project/), [organizing grassroots advocacy efforts](https://bigbiology.substack.com/p/science-communication-policy-and) to defend evidence-based policy, and writing graduate school recommendations while being a furloughed government scientist are only a few of the many ways in which the science community is stepping up in the face of adversity and providing us with seeds of hope.

The challenge before us is only growing, however, and we need all the help we can get. To get started, Lotterhos recommends that “the best thing that listeners \[and readers\] can do with this survey is to share the [link](https://sites.google.com/view/scientiststoriesfederalimpacts/impacts/2025-july-survey) with people they know and share it with their representatives and governments at the state level and at the federal level. Share it with the staff of those representatives. Get to know who those staff are. Talk to them about their concerns. Because honestly, I think a whisper campaign could be more powerful now than a big press hoopla.”

The stories collected through this survey offer both a warning and a call to action—a warning of how fragile the scientific enterprise can be under political pressure, and a call to protect the openness and freedom that make discovery possible.

Indeed, reflecting on the survey as a whole, one of Lotterhos’ biggest takeaways was the affirmation that “we really need freedom for science to work.” She explains: “Having the freedom to exercise values like objectivity and rigor, the freedom to describe natural phenomena with the correct terminology, the freedom to question and debate what we believe to be true. These are freedoms that we need for science to operate and to advance knowledge. And they’re not freedoms for scientists alone, either, they’re fundamental requirements for a society that values truth and the common good.”
