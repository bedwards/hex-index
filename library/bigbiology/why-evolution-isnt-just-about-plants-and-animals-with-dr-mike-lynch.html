Darwin’s finches. Orchids and hawk moths. Peacocks. Apes and humans. Thinking about evolution often evokes images of charismatic and iconic plants and animals that we learn about in high school biology or through popular media. Dr. Mike Lynch, our most recent guest on Big Biology and a professor at Arizona State University, has a different perspective. “Animals and vascular plants are the oddballs of evolutionary biology,” writes Lynch in his most recent book Evolutionary Cell Biology: The Origins of Cellular Architecture.

“Oddballs” might sound like an unusual way to describe plants and animals. And yet, in terms of the number of individual organisms on the planet, “land plants and animals would be out in the range of 0.001% of all individuals,” Lynch explains in the episode. “I mean, there’s something like 1031 viruses on the planet, and the number of just bacteria and archaea is about 1031 so that’s far, far beyond the numbers of most multi-cellular things.”

Lynch is quick to affirm that plants and animals have a significant ecological impact on the planet. “Not to say we shouldn’t study animals and land plants,” he adds. “But it’s kind of an interesting commentary on where we are in biology. We’re studying the things that aren’t most of biology.”

These numbers comparing total plants, animals, and microbes in the world aren’t just fun facts—they have an immense impact on how evolution works. This impact comes primarily from vastly different effective population sizes among plants, animals, and unicellular organisms. Effective population size is a “pretty complicated technical topic,” Lynch admits, but it is fundamental to understanding how the forces of selection and drift interact to drive evolution.

Effective population size is “sort of a composite way of putting everything together that influences the noise in the evolutionary process,” Lynch explains. “The way to think about it is, if you have some number you call the effective population size, the inverse of that is related to the noise in the evolutionary process.” In other words, having a high effective population size translates to less noise in a population’s evolution. Having a high effective population size makes natural selection a major force driving changes in a population’s allele frequencies. In contrast, having a low effective population size turns up the noise, meaning that genetic drift tends to dominate a population’s evolutionary trajectory. So, “the fundamental question is,” Lynch says, “is the level of noise greater than the strength of selection operating on a nucleotide?”

He offers an analogy to illustrate an example when noise is greater than selection. “It’s like driving down the highway in a fog,” he begins. “You’ve got the pedal to the metal”—that is, natural selection is pushing an allele forward—“but you end up in New Mexico instead of Utah or something”—that is, genetic drift (the fog) obscured the signal of selection, sending the population off course.

The differences in effective population size between unicellular and multicellular organisms means that evolution plays by slightly different rules across the tree of life. Because microbe populations tend to be enormous, the relative influence of genetic drift is reduced, allowing natural selection to operate more efficiently. Importantly, however, even these extremely large effective population sizes don’t grant natural selection omnipotence. “It turns out, there are many things in cell biology that I think are quite compatible with neutral forms of evolution,” Lynch says. “There’s a lot of cellular complexity that seems to evolve for reasons that can’t easily be explained by selection.”

This contrast becomes even more pronounced in plants and animals, which typically have much smaller effective population sizes. In these organisms, genetic drift exerts a stronger influence relative to selection, turning up the noise in the evolutionary process. Natural selection is still very much at work in plants and animals, but it operates against a less predictable backdrop. This matters because the smaller a population’s effective size, the harder it is for natural selection to consistently shape traits. In these cases, random genetic drift can overpower selection, leading to evolutionary changes that aren’t necessarily adaptive. Lynch calls this the “drift-barrier,” a limit beyond which selection loses its grip and neutral processes take over.

What does all of this mean? For Lynch, it means turning to the cellular level—and especially to single-celled organisms—to explore evolutionary mechanisms. In these systems, where effective population sizes are often huge, the signal of selection is clearer, and the interplay between adaptive and neutral forces can be studied with greater resolution.

Studying evolution at the cellular level opens up new possibilities for understanding how biological complexity arises—not just through adaptation, but through the accumulation of neutral changes. Lynch points to phenomena like the rewiring of molecular networks and the structure of the eukaryotic ribosome as examples of cellular features that likely evolved without direct selective pressure. These insights challenge the assumption that complexity must always be adaptive, and instead suggest that the architecture of life may be shaped by chance and constraint more than previously thought.

Lynch’s single-celled organism of choice to study these questions? “We’ve gravitated toward ciliates,” Lynch shares. “Ciliates are pretty interesting.”

Plants and animals tend to be the main characters in evolutionary research, and understandably so. Their complexity, visibility, and impact on the environment make them important and attractive subjects for ecological and evolutionary investigation. However, as Mike Lynch makes apparent in this episode of Big Biology, plants and animals are far from the full story. Indeed, the next big evolutionary insights may not come from plants or animals at all, but instead from the tiny, abundant, and endlessly inventive micro-organisms that make up so much of life on Earth.
