#### **Unsung heroes: Marine invertebrate superpowers in a changing ocean**

By Clayton Glasgow

I’m a high school biology teacher, and I recently asked my students the age-old question, “If you could have any superpower, which would you choose?” Many answered with the classics you might expect: flight, invisibility, teleportation, telekinesis, x-ray vision, or super speed. One creative student said she wanted the power to make anyone she didn’t want to hear talk be silent (she promised it wasn’t directed at me). All of my students scoffed at my desire to be fluent in all forms of communication, although I still think it would be an amazing (albeit, slightly nerdy) superpower.

[Dr. Drew Harvell](https://drewharvell.com/), a recent featured guest on Big Biology, is an expert on superpowers, but not on the superpowers of comics, movies or students’ imaginations. She studies the real-life superpowers of some of the ocean’s most important and fantastic creatures. Her new book, [The Ocean’s Menagerie: How Earth's Strangest Creatures Reshape the Rules of Life](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/723673/the-oceans-menagerie-by-drew-harvell/), is an exploration and celebration of some of the ocean’s unsung heroes—invertebrates—and how marine invertebrate superpowers might inspire new breakthroughs in medicine and beyond.

**Sponges: Simply Sophisticated**

Sea sponges are probably not the first ocean creature that comes to mind when you think about superpowers, given the primitive nature of these marine invertebrates. “The biology of sponges is basically a collection of cells without organs, or in most cases, real tissue,” explains Harvell, “And yet you have this elegantly designed, functioning, very sophisticated biology embedded in that very simple looking organism.”

So, what makes these seemingly simple sponges so sophisticated and special? Harvell identifies three major traits as sponge superpowers: their movement of water, production of secondary compounds, and cellular plasticity.

“Sponges are creatures of current,” says Harvell. Tiny collar cells create micro-currents, drawing water through a network of precisely designed canals. The water enters through tiny pores, delivers food and oxygen, and exits cleaner than when it entered, making sponges some of the best natural filtration systems on the planet.

Sponges and their bacterial symbionts are also experts in producing defensive secondary compounds. “That was really what I called the superpower in the sponge chapter,” Harvell explains. “The phenomenal sophistication and complexity of the compounds that were being found, many of which had real biological potency in the sense of disrupting cell division or or reducing or controlling inflammation.”

Many of these compounds are not only useful for the sponges, but have major [biomedical applications](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5666418/). “Tropical sponges had the highest hit rate on the planet for the discovery of new anti-cancer chemicals,” says Harvell. This point connects to one of Harvell’s themes throughout the interview and her book: protecting the ocean is not just about the ocean’s health—it is inseparable from human health.

Perhaps the most captivating of the sponges’ superpowers are its cells’ totipotency. Totipotency means that specialized cells—cells with a specific function—can “de-differentiate” or become unspecialized so that they can take on a new function that the sponge needs. In sponges, these cells are called archaeocytes or amebocytes. They play a central role in digestion and waste disposal, but they also maintain the ability to specialize into any other cell type within the sponge, including gametes. Harvell compares this sponge superpower to Marvel’s Deadpool, “which is the superhero that can de-differentiate into different functions. Sponges are like that.”

**Corals: Calcifying Castles**

Unlike sponges, corals are commonly placed among the ocean’s superheroes, and for good reason. Coral reefs sustain an estimated 25 percent of all marine biodiversity, and they also help sustain many fisheries across the globe. The key to corals’ success is one of the ocean’s most remarkable superpowers: symbiosis.

Hard corals owe their reef-building capabilities to a partnership with microscopic algae called zooxanthellae. These algae live inside the coral’s tissue and, through photosynthesis, provide the coral polyp with the energy and raw materials it needs to build its calcium carbonate skeleton. But the superpower goes beyond just the algae providing the coral with the energy it needs.

“Having a symbiosis with algae is a double win, because not only is the algal photosynthesizing, but it's also changing the pH inside the cells of the coral and optimizing the calcification process,” Harvell explains. And the benefits go even further. She describes the building of the reef as “the building of castles” and the calcium carbonate skeleton as “a hall of mirrors.” It was [recently discovered](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/marine-science/articles/10.3389/fmars.2019.00472/full?utm_source=chatgpt.com) that the shape, planes, and architecture of the calcified reef skeleton “reflect light in a way that also contributes to the speed and strength of the photosynthesis,” Harvell describes. “So there's just layers upon layers of adaptations associated with this particular superpower.”

**Sea Stars: Sneaky & Sticky**

Of all the superpowers Harvell describes, those of sea stars might be the most sinister: mutable collagenous tissue. In other words: sticky skin. Sea stars’ skin is under neural control, meaning the skin can stiffen or slacken on command. This ability allows sea stars to cling tenaciously to surfaces, resist predation, or—remarkably—pry open the shells of bivalves like clams.

Here’s how it works: a sea star uses its tube feet, which have hundreds of tiny suction cups on their undersides, to latch onto a clam. Once attached, the sea star stiffens its skin. Its stiffened skin allows it to maintain pressure for hours, slowly prying the clam open just a millimeter. The moment that tiny gap appears, the sea star everts its stomach, extending it into the clam’s shell to digest its prey from the inside out. “Obviously, that’s a dangerous maneuver,” Harvell says, “so the sea star's got to be really sure it can hold that clam open when it's sticking its stomach inside.”

But this “smart skin” does more than enable a unique feeding technique. Sea stars can also autotomize—that is, intentionally drop an arm when threatened—and then regrow it later. Certain species, like the giant sunflower star, can even sprint across a tank to snag falling prey, acting almost like underwater dogs that recognize and “beg” for food.

**Every superhero has a kryptonite**

These three groups of marine invertebrates—sponges, corals, and sea stars—undoubtedly possess superpowers and are some of our ocean’s most important superheroes. Unfortunately, however, they each have a kryptonite that human-induced climate change is exacerbating.

The symbiotic sponge-bacteria and coral-algae relationships are sensitive to temperature. When temperatures are too high, these relationships break down, leaving [sponges without their ammonia-detoxifying microbes](https://www.unsw.edu.au/newsroom/news/2023/06/A-marine-mystery-finding-the-link-between-climate-change-and-sea-sponge-loss) and corals without their photosynthetic algae. If the rupture of these symbiotic relationships lasts too long, the effects can be fatal for sponges and corals alike. Sea star wasting disease has affected over 20 species of sea star, and research done by Harvell and colleagues has shown that [the disease’s impacts are stronger at higher temperatures](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.aau7042). Consequently, as temperatures continue to rise and marine heatwaves become [more frequent](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-03732-9), the superpowers of marine invertebrates will become increasingly vulnerable.

Despite these imminent threats, Harvell leaves room for hope. “I haven't thought of any new superpowers that have emerged in the last 200 million years,” she says, “but putting these animals in the ocean under enormous environmental stress, we could expect some pretty crazy stuff to pop up. So I’m going to be watching for that.”

In the end, Harvell says we need to be protecting our oceans and its creatures because all of us, no matter where we are, depend on and are connected to these vast, diverse marine ecosystems. “We should realize that the most valuable resource on our planet is not oil or metal, it's our living resources and the web of life that links them together.”
