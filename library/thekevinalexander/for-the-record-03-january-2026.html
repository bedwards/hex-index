The first time I transferred to Madison was in July of ’99. As the secretary gave me the dime tour, I met a bunch of guys I’d never see again, whose pending retirements made this move possible in the first place. As I extended a hand to one guy, he warned me not to get to know him too much. He was a nice guy, he said, but he wouldn’t be there much longer. He had retirement in sight and even kept a handwritten countdown taped inside his locker.

That countdown became a running inside joke between us, with me betting him a gift card that I’d retire first. Ten years later, he and his wife enjoyed a trip to Texas Roadhouse on my dime. Life’s funny that way.

I’m now in the spot he was in (sorta). If all goes to plan, I have a little over two years left—689 days as of this writing. That’s aspirational more than anything, and just as hard to fathom as the idea that that July day was almost 27 years ago. This date also lines up with a few other life events: a milestone service anniversary (God, I’m old) and my youngest graduating (how did we get here?!). His countdown is 523 days, or roughly a year and a half. It might as well be 2057, as abstract as it seems. It all feels right around the corner and a lifetime away. Both are true.

My calendar tells me that a year and a half ago, I was lucky enough to finally see The Church and Afghan Whigs play. My mind tells me it’s been a decade. Around that same time, that same kid was playing in one of his first rugby tournaments.

The strangest thing about being middle-aged is how squishy time has become. I used to roll my eyes when people said it only gets faster. Turns out they were right. Why that guy told me he’d be gone soon makes sense now, too. Eighteen months? Twenty-four? Those will be here next weekend—or feel like it anyway. Eighteen months ago, on the other hand, might as well have been 1992 in my mind’s eye.

My point here is this: 2025 felt like it would take forever, and as hard as it fought to prove us right, it was gone before we knew it.

I have no idea what will happen globally in 2026, but I do know what I plan to make happen here.

Continued community focus:

Terry once called this the nicest place on the internet. In 2026, I plan to keep that going—not as a house of toxic positivity, but as an oasis of fun in a digital landscape that often isn’t. I’m mindful that “community” is in very real danger of becoming as overused as “gratitude” once was, but it truly is this project’s superpower. I’m pretty good at shouting into the void, but it’s much more fun to have conversations with people. I love all the twists and turns the comment sections & replies take. Or put another way: “Good conversations have lots of doorknobs.”

And it’s not just me that’s been thinking about it. Substack head Hamish Mackenzie also recently touched on this, noting:

The traditional media system once thrived on direct relationships, with big brands building trust with audiences over decades. That all fell apart in the social media era, when tech companies learned how to monopolize attention—and ad dollars. Those companies aren’t giving that up anytime soon, which means people who care about trustworthy media will have to rebuild direct relationships with audiences all over again, this time without relying on programmatic advertising or letting platforms dictate what they can and can’t do. In 2025, it’s become clear that platforms like Substack allow publishers to benefit from network effects without sacrificing control of audience relationships.

Sure, Hamish is talking his own book here, but yes to all of that.

More writing:

Talking about building meaningful connections is one thing. Putting it into practice is another. That’s where “more writing” comes in. One place that will show up is a small series tentatively titled Records I Left on the Table1. Albums I meant to write about and didn’t-blind spots, ones I ran out of time for, and records that deserve a second look now that some time has passed. I left a lot of reviews unwritten last year, and I’d like to fix that. Many of these will be short, web-first entries, similar to how the Best Record of ’89 posts were handled. Wasn’t that just last weekend, too? Others will go out as posts.

Speaking of past blind spots, 2001 is a year I’ve barely scratched the surface of. The next bracket challenge will focus on it. If that was your time to shine and you’re itching to yap about a record from that year, let’s talk. If someone you know has a strong point of view on an ‘01 release, please send ‘em my way.

Of course, we’ll also be covering new releases and new-to-me records.

None of that will mean flooding your inboxes. No one wants that. What people do want, though, is an antidote to the cocaine-like pull of the attention economy.

From Cal Newport:

As the platforms of the digital attention economy transition from social network models to providing maximally distracting short-form videos, more of the content available online is devolving toward that paragon of low-quality forgettability, commonly referred to as slop.

Nobody wants that, either. There will be some short videos here and there, but no one’s “pivoting to video.” You’ll never think you accidentally landed on TikTok.

More Artistic Tithing

I didn't make that phrase up, but I sure wish I did. In short, one of the things we do here is regularly reinvest some of the project’s revenue into the wider artist/musician community. Last year, that took the form of things as varied as filling a touring musician’s gas tank while they were out on the road, to being part of a crowdfunding campaign for a record (we did two of those, actually. One is out. The other should be on its way later this year?). To date, these have been ad hoc; we kicked in as people found projects that aligned with the community. I’d like these to be more regular events, ideally quarterly. The suggestion box is always open! I should note that these don't have to be music-specific. The only real barometer is that it helps make the world a little brighter.

Collaboration

If one goal for 2026 is worthwhile/engaging content, the other is to make sure it’s created with and alongside interesting people. That means more collaborations…As always, one of my goals for the coming year is to increase the frequency of collaborations. My track record’s been…a mixed bag so far. A few are already on the drawing board, with more to come. I’m happy to share that my monthly series with will be coming back (2026 goal: come up with a better title for that too). If you—or someone you know—has something you think would be a good fit, please reach out. I genuinely want to hear from you. Reply to this email, and we’ll get the ball rolling.

Conversely, it seems like a good time to note that I’m always open to writing guest posts, appearing on your podcast, and any other sorts of collabs (or whatever the kids are calling these things now) you may have in mind.

There will also be (more) regular giveaways, exclusive offers for supporters, artist interviews, and other surprises (Merch? Discord? Radio Show?) that I hope people will enjoy. The elevator pitch is simple: I want this to be one of your favorite places online. Writing a bog-standard music letter is easy enough to do—trust me; I see a ton of paint-by-numbers stuff while doing freelance editing work/ghostwriting. They read like cardboard. Hard pass.

Speaking of milestones, the current iteration of this project turns five later this year. It’s a dynamic page and will continue to evolve, but at its heart it remains a community, with music discovery and sharing as its north star.

Who indeed? I will. And I hope you will too.

Longer Read:

They treated it like excess inventory, never once considering what happened inside that studio. A studio that housed hundreds of thousands of records, CDs, and cassettes—one of the largest physical music archives in the region; now sealed away in silence. Decades of donations and rarities carefully cataloged by generations of programmers. It was built for the insomniac searching for something real at 2:00 am, for local weirdos and comedians using the airwaves like a pressure valve no commercial station would ever allow, and most importantly, for students, it was a petri dish. A place where someone who had never felt they belonged suddenly discovered they had a voice, and they can fail over and over again with very low stakes until they figured out how to be on air. It cannot be replaced by an internship or corporate programming. College radio DJs gave us R.E.M., the Replacements, and Sleater-Kinney; corporate programming gave us Kenny G.
