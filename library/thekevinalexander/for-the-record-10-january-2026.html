In 2023, I had the good fortune of seeing Peter Gabriel perform. He was touring in support of i/o, a record he promoted by releasing a track with each full moon. Honestly, a pretty clever way to go about it! It gives each song a chance to breathe, gets a steady stream of hype, etc. And for anyone who finds terms like “promotion” and “marketing” oily, this is a good (and original!) workaround. There is no “Games Without Frontiers” or “Big Time” on this record.

It was a fantastic show...For most of us… The guy seated next to me spent the entire time yelling for Gabriel to play “Shock the Monkey.” You could watch him move through the 5 stages of grief as the show progressed. Would he call it fantastic? My guess is that with the benefit of hindsight, he’d say yes. That night? I doubt it.

Gabriel isn’t exactly a prolific artist, with just a few albums of original material in the last (almost) 4 decades. Bob Pollard he ain’t.

And now there is another release. Once the lunar promo cycle is complete, will there be another tour? Time’ll tell.

As news of this latest release has rolled out, I’ve been thinking about that guy--and what he represents-- a lot lately. The “play the hits” crowd is large. He’s not alone, at any rate. I recently read an op-ed in Rolling Stone where Andy Greene made the case that Gabriel should, in essence, “play the hits.” He starts by noting that i/o didn’t really make a lot of waves outside of Gabriel’s established fan base. Anecdotally, that’s fair- we saw him in Milwaukee, and the upper tiers of Fiserv Forum were blocked off. But the lines at the merch table were also impossibly long, so…

Greene’s deeper critique is that Gabriel hasn’t promoted his work aggressively enough or taken sufficient steps to ensure his achievements are preserved (“managing his legacy”). He contrasts Gabriel with Bruce Springsteen, framing Springsteen as an exemplary model of legacy management: frequent releases, deluxe reissues, documentaries, memoirs, vault projects, and a biopic—all arriving with considerable fanfare.

There’s nothing wrong with that latter model. It clearly works for Springsteen. The problem is the assumption underneath the comparison: that preserving, expanding, and monetizing a legacy are universal artistic goals. If you accept that premise, Gabriel looks like he’s falling short. If you reject it, he simply looks like an artist making different choices.

Which brings me to two questions:

Setting aside for a second the idea that Gabriel really doesn’t have to do anything if he doesn’t want to. Does an artist have an obligation to fans to produce? Or are we on their schedule (for lack of a better term)? Wouldn’t that pressure to maintain a set schedule mean less focus on the muse and more on the machination?

I don’t think it’s fair to demand this of any artist, be it a musician, writer, whatever. Inspiration strikes when it strikes, and to try and squish that into a fixed timeline seems like a surefire recipe for burnout, or mediocre material—we’ve all seen that, too. So maybe that’s the easier of the two points here.

The stickier one is this:

Does an artist band have an obligation to play the hits, as it were? It’s fair to say that a lot of people buy tickets hoping to hear their fave song, and the odds are good that it is/was a popular one. But does writing a song that lands with fans mean you’re now obligated to play it forever? For context, I’m thinking about artists that are –at least nominally- still producing new material, and not the nostalgia acts plying the high seas on cruise ships or the county fair circuit.

When I saw The Church, the biggest reactions were for songs like “Under the Milky Way” and “Metropolis.” And selfishly, it made for a more enjoyable experience for me. But does that mean Steve Kilbey is sentenced to playing “Reptile” forever? I can’t imagine that when he put pen to paper, he envisioned playing these songs 30-40 years later. That’s not how art usually works.

Greene goes so far as to state that Gabriel should work Genesis material into his setlists, which, I mean…ok. Both Gabriel and the “new” Genesis are a long way from where they were when they first went their separate ways. And I understand that this sort of thing would do numbers at the ticket office—prog fans aren’t shy about showing up cash in hand, but that’s not exactly an expanding fan base, either. Sure, a one-off might be cool (and hey, you could probably wring a release out of it), but night after night? I don’t think so.

Nada Surf has been saddled with one-hit-wonder anchor. To their credit, they ignored it and just kept on making incredible records. There aren’t many hits to play, but they make sure to note that, yes, they will get to “Popular” before the night is out. At the show I was at, a fan yelled, “Play some new shit.” Moon Mirror has just been released, to which Matthew Caws responded with gratitude. They were delighted people still wanted to hear their latest material, he said, before busting into one of the new songs. This show was a textbook case of an audience split between new and old. Worth noting the merch line at that show was impossibly long as well.

Back to Greene and The Boss for a second:

“Look at Bruce Springsteen, Gabriel’s old Amnesty International touring buddy. The New Jersey icon continues to record new albums and tour behind nearly all of them. At the same time, however, Springsteen always keeps an eye on the past. He’s written a memoir, personally overseen deluxe re-releases of his albums, paired many of them with new documentaries, filmed special shows where he plays them straight through, released shelved albums that sat in his vault for decades, and even cooperated on a major Hollywood biopic about his life. And when these projects are released, they arrive with a lot of fanfare.”

Again, that’s all well and good, except that these are two very different artists operating in very different universes. I have to believe Greene is looking at this through the lens of a well-intentioned fan; he even notes that The Boss’s schedule “ensures [his] achievements aren’t forgotten and are discovered again by new generations.”

And maybe there’s a point to be made from that angle. The lifespan of a fanbase is finite. Someday we’ll live forever, but we’re not there yet, and the reality is that the clock is running for everyone, Gabriel and Springsteen included. BUT—and this is a big but— maybe not everyone wants to spend their one wild and precious life playing the same 10-20 songs ad infinitum? Ultimately, I think a part of the reason Greene’s op-ed bothered me was that it asks for opposing goals.

Here’s the other note from the i/o tour that Greene might not have caught. Gabriel used the new tracks to weave together a larger story about humanity’s timeline and our place in the universe. I don’t know that ripping into “And Through the Wire” or similar would’ve fit here. As it was, they did well to weave in the classics they did.

And Gabriel has done the play the hits thing, too. Been there, done that. On a surface level, this was a great show (albeit hotter than the surface of the sun). From an artistic perspective, I have to believe the i/o show was much more fulfilling for him and the band.

So now what?

There’s another way here that splits the difference:

New Order offers a useful example. They still end almost every set with Joy Division’s “Love Will Tear Us Apart.” A song that (technically) they didn’t even make, but one inseparable from their history–and one fans dearly love.1 That said, they also don’t just show up and play Substance (a de facto greatest hits). It’s true that their later records aren’t regarded as canon, the same as those that, say, Power Corruption and Lies might be, but it’s still new work, and they still incorporate those tracks into the sets.

Further, even when playing older tracks, they often redo/restyle them. The opening to Subculture will sound wholly alien to you if you’re not ready for it. Once those familiar opening synths kick in, though? It’s rapture. Same story with “Waiting for the Siren’s Call.” In fact, I’d argue that the live/evolved version is preferable to the original. So you’ve got a mix of new material and old standards, some of which are newer, reworked versions, which keep both fans and band members interested.

This third path may be the way. I wish that guy from the Gabriel show were around to ask. I’d love to hear what you think.

Longer Read:

KYMC 89.7: In those same 1980s midwestern suburbs, there was a tiny little radio station broadcasting from a tiny little shack via a tiny little tower. It was way at the left end of the radio dial, and played what we weren’t yet calling “alternative music,” defined then as “whatever the big radio stations at the other end of the dial were playing.” R.E.M. for sure, and every song on Tim, but also Marshall Crenshaw and Squeeze and Figures on a Beach. It would come in clearly within about a five-mile radius, from Ballwin almost all the way to Town & Country. In the summer of 1987, I rode my bike there and filled out an application and lightly lied about my age and got a weekly Saturday 4-7 pm shift. That same summer, The Cure released Kiss Me Kiss Me Kiss Me— an instant KYMC Classic — and by winter, all seven listeners were calling in to request “Just Like Heaven.” It’s a perfect example of what The Cure can do; a song jam-packed with joy and lust and longing and excitement— your basic Summer Emotions— that somehow only sounds exactly right on a very cold and dark winter night. We’re entering prime “Just Like Heaven” season, try it for yourself.

A classic from 2023. A bunch of the cvoolest writers you know each shared their top 5 songs by the Cure. This one’ll take you a while to get through. You’ll enjoy every second!
