One of my favorite events in December is Giving Tuesday. On Tuesday, December 2nd, not-for-profit organizations are highlighted to provide an opportunity for us to become aware of and help our favorite charities. What are your favorites? Here are my favs:

  • Community Unity Network is a local organization my daughter started to help struggling single mothers and families with necessities. It has grown to become a hub that helps several local charities. The mission of the Community Unity Network is to unite churches, ministries, and servant-hearted people to meet the needs of the community whether it be groceries, clothing, school supplies, furniture, cleaning up neighborhoods, revitalizing parks, or connecting them to local churches and resources that can help them. Proceeds from Who Told You That are donated to Community Unity Network.

  • First Light is a Child Advocacy Center that offers services to child victims and non-offending family members at no cost. They serve victims of physical abuse, sexual assault, witness to a violent crime, placed in a drug endangered environment, and/or are recovered from a kidnapping. The victims are interviewed by forensically-trained professionals in a relaxed manner. I support this organization because I know first-hand how much their services are needed. As a young teen who reported my stepfather for abuse, I was interviewed repeatedly by police officers, detention center workers, and social service agents. I was even hooked up to a lie-detector machine. First Light records the victims so they do not have to be subjected to such trauma! Proceeds from Break The Cycle are donated to First Light.

  • Isaiah 117 House is a charity organization that provides emergency shelter and supplies for children awaiting foster care placement so they do not have to stay in a social worker’s office for long hours when they are removed from their home. Proceeds from Lighthouse 117 are donated to Isaiah 117 House.

Author Feature: Dr. Mike Nelson (Michael Deeze)

Dr. Michael “Mike” Nelson is a 74-year-old retired chiropractor who started writing when he was 67. He has written books under his name and under the pen name, Michael Deeze.

Mike has lived or worked on six of the seven continents and all fifty states. He now lives in a small Southwestern Wisconsin town, that he says is called the driftless area. “It is a town that time forgot and we don’t have to lock our doors when we go out somewhere.”

When Mike’s not writing, he works in his wood shop where he makes various things to sell at craft fairs, flower shops, and general stores. He enjoys going on destination road trips with his dear friend, Kate. They recently returned from Rhode Island after visiting her daughter.

Read on to learn more about Mike and his writing journey:

How many books have you written?

I have written six novels to date. I also have a few short stories that were published, two of which received national awards.

What is the most recent book you have published?

I recently published The Deathbed Confessions, a psychological crime thriller. It’s the story of a young FBI agent who is assigned to sit with a lifelong criminal and is dying. The hope is that the subject will repent and confess to other crimes that are still on the books. The dying man is still very clever and the young agent is in over his head, until he realizes a connection between himself and the subject. It is a fast-paced study in strong characters and a race against time and a challenge for both of them to stay alive. Reviews have been good.

What do you hope readers will take away from your book?

In all of my books, but especially this most recent one, I try to show how there is no black and white interpretation of good and bad, moral or immoral. The world mostly runs on the grey areas somewhere in the middle. The take-away, I hope, is that although the rules often get bent to fit the situation, it’s important to maintain one’s own values or you might become part of the problem that you originally came to fix.

What was the most challenging aspect of getting your book published, and how did you overcome it?

It is difficult to not be caught up in all the hype. I enjoyed the writing, but once you publish the book your focus shifts to hoping that others might read and enjoy it. I found that it was easy to get caught up in expecting validation from sales and reviews. It can suck you in and leave you disappointed. It took me a little while to get past that and recognize that I had done my best and the books were good on their own without someone else telling me they are.

How did you come up with the idea for your book?

In August of last year a group of authors/publishers announced that they had created a competition. It was to be 5 authors pitted against each other to test their writing skills. I was asked to be one of the competing writers. Each week the competitors were given a task to perform in a set amount of time. One of the tasks was to create a story line and a first paragraph of a new completely unrehearsed idea in fifteen minutes. I only had a few minutes to think about it and I came up with the opening lines of this most recent book. I finished in a tie for first place and promptly forgot all about my idea. Then, during the winter months I picked it up again and the story sort of wrote itself after that.

What is your favorite genre?

Two of my books embrace fantasy and legend. They were the most fun to write. It’s easier if you’re trying to get a message across you can use a variety of characters to bring the message, especially the hard messages, while at the same time keeping it entertaining.

What is your writing routine, and any specific habits or rituals that have helped you stay productive?

When I am writing I devote a few hours in the morning to it. When the time is up I make myself stop and find something else to do. Also, when I’m writing I must have music. I enjoy having my headphones on with the volume turned up. It helps my ADD to keep from being distracted so easily.

How do you handle writer’s block or moments when you feel creatively stuck?

I am a casual writer, if I get stuck in a cul-de-sac or are just plain blocked, I put it aside for a while. I can go out to the shop and work on a wood project and let my mind gain some perspective from a distance. Sometimes it only lasts a day or two and sometimes it can be a month or two. During those periods, I think about what I want to write, but I maintain my involvement with the world around me. It also helps that I’m usually writing two different books at the same time so if I get stuck, I can just hop over to the other one and work on it for a while.

What are you working on next?

My fantasy series was meant to be a trilogy. I’m working on the third installment, working title Revenge of the Colors, right now and hope to have it finished and ready for editing by April or May next spring.

If you could do a book signing anywhere in the world, where would it be?

Dublin, Ireland if I had a choice, otherwise Universal Studios once they make the books into a movie.

Where is the most exciting or unusual place you have promoted your book?

Las Vegas, Nevada. I had never been there before and it is a crazy place to be sure. It was unusual because of that and exciting because there were a lot of other authors there. I was humbled by their presence and nervous to speak in front of them.

What advice do you have for aspiring authors?

Start small, don’t begin with the idea of a 500-page New York Times best seller. Take the time to build your story completely so that it has depth and substance.

What do you love about being an author?

I would have to say that it’s the opportunity to immerse yourself in different worlds, to create the world completely so that the reader can see the scenery, and feel the created environment. I truly enjoy putting one in the scene, so they can participate in the story and witness its outcome.

What do you dislike about being an author?

The marketing. There is so much to learn about marketing and algorithms and how to manipulate them to your advantage. Honestly it takes a lot of time and effort and it can get into your head and preoccupy your consciousness.

What has helped you sell the most books?

When I learned the ins and outs of the Amazon algorithms, it was an eye opener. Once I figured out how to use ‘key words’ I had a big spike in sales.

What is the most valuable lesson you have learned on your journey as an author that you wish you had known before you started?

Writing a book turns out to be the easy part. What comes after, the editing, cover design, fonts, page size, platforms and marketing take up the lion’s share of the effort. I was not prepared for it with my first book.

What is your favorite quote?

“There are many hallways in memory. Each lined with filing cabinets of our past. Events both real and imagined. Some are dusty from neglect and forgetfulness, and some are visited regularly. When the heart is restless, it is the dusty cabinets it seeks, and often the contents prove difficult to read.” (Mine)

Where can we find you?

My website is: authormikenelson.com, and I’m on most of the social media platforms, Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, etc.

Is there anything else you want to share?

I try always to emphasize quality over quantity. I see a lot of books on the market these days that had no business getting through the editing phase. Some are just terrible, yet they are out there right along with the good books. There are an awful lot of books on the market these days but not very many good books. Everyone thinks that they’ve written the perfect book, and to some extent so do I, but I recognize my flaws and work very hard to not create too many of them. As such, it takes me a lot longer to write a book, but it has to pass the smell test with me before I ask other people to judge it. I never thought I could be a writer, had never written anything of substance before. I started trying to write short stories to entertain my kids with stories of my past life before children. One chapter became two and so on until it turned out to be three full length novels, from then on I couldn’t get enough and now writing is a big part of my life.

New Book Releases

Fellow Fireflies: An Inspirational Children’s Picture Book About Kindness, Friendship, and Shining Your Light by Terra Chism

Fellow Fireflies is the first children’s book by my daughter, Terra Chism. I edited and formatted this book in two days! She wanted to have it available to purchase and arrive before Christmas, so we went with Amazon KDP.

I learned that hardcover books have to be a minimum of 76 pages to publish on KDP. I also learned that soft cover books can be 24 pages IF you choose premium color. If you choose standard color it also has to be 76 pages.

Fellow Fireflies was illustrated by talented local artist, Tashema Davis. The picture children’s book helps young readers discover that everyone has a special light inside, and when we share that light with others the world becomes a brighter, kinder place. It’s the perfect book for bedtime reading, classrooms, and gift-giving.

The Babylon Blueprint - Timeless Strategies for Wealth, Wisdom, and Well-Being by David Hancock (Morgan-James Publishing Founder)

Scribe Bytes

My daughter asked me to insert a single photo into a family photo. I tried ChatGPT and Nano Banana (Google Gemini). Both AI programs botched the photo which gave us a good laugh. I ended up using Canva Pro to remove the background and insert the missing family member in the photo. Web 2.0 for the win. AI isn’t always faster or better!

3rd Annual Words in Motion Poetry Contest

Gut Punch Literary Poetry Submission Deadline: December 13, 2025

John Hinton, Gut Punch Literary founder, is working with Stage 1 Dance Academy for their 3rd Annual Words in Motion project that fuses poetry and dance. The theme for this year is “Family.” They are looking for positive, encouraging poems on this topic. The dancers then select the poems they like best and choreograph dances to interpret those poems through movement. The poets whose poems are selected will be given the opportunity to read their poem at the performance. Therefore, this opportunity is for Indiana poets only. Submissions are open until December 13, 2025. One (1) poem per poet. Please email your submissions to gutpunchliterary@gmail.com. Two guidelines: 12-point font, Times New Roman or similar, and no form/shape poetry. Performance of selected poems will be on April 12th, 2026, with the location to yet be decided. If you have any questions, please email gutpunchliterary@gmail.com
