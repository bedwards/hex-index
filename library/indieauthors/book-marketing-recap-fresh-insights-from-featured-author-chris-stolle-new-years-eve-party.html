Book Marketing Recap: What helps you sell the most books?

One of the most popular questions asked by indie authors!

  • “Speaking, building relationships, and sharing my message with people face-to-face has helped me sell the most books. Connection beats advertising every time.” ~ Randy Dupre

  • “When I learned the ins and outs of the Amazon algorithms, it was an eye opener. Once I figured out how to use keywords I had a big spike in sales.” ~ Michael Deeze

  • “Book signings and selling direct via my website. ALSO better Meta Data and Book Descriptions. Get the software ‘Publisher Rocket‘ to research your books and your competition. Have confidence in your work! Selling when you have to look in a reader/customer’s eyes can work wonders. Ask yourself, ‘Why should someone buy this book?’” ~ Tom McAuliffe

  • “Making one-on-one connections and developing new and old relationships. I’ve been overwhelmed at the support and enthusiasm people have shown when they learned I wrote a book. I’ve had people reach out who I haven’t talked to in more than a decade to let me know they read my book. And I’ve been able to form new connections in my community through doing interviews and local book signing events. I’m so grateful to every one of my readers and supporters.” ~ Marissa Malson

  • “Time and persistence. I spent a few years practicing in public, mostly through the The Shepard & Kelly Mystery Series, and that really helped me get a sense of what worked and what didn’t. In that, I built a reader base and a great community of other writers who like to support one another. That’s made all the difference in the world.” ~ Benjamin Bradley

  • “Selling online on Amazon and selling in my hometown.” ~ Kathy Marcuson

  • “Press releases have been one of the best ways for me to reach potential readers, so I continue to use them to communicate with my audiences. I’ve built a solid email list, but I’m not sure it has been as effective as it is for many authors. Facebook advertising has also been great for building my readers.” ~ J.T. Kelly

  • “In the past, I sold the most by speaking with groups—sometimes in businesses, sometimes community get-togethers, sometimes places of worship. I would also speak at bookstores.” ~ Jill Ebstein

Featured Author: Chris Stolle

Christopher “Chris” Stolle lives in Richmond, Indiana. He’s lived in other places such as Texas, Germany, and Florida, but always comes back to Richmond where his family is. Chris loves writing, reading, listening to music, playing fantasy sports, and cooking.

Chris has written two books: 101 Leadership Lessons From Baseball’s Greatest Managers (Coaches Choice, 2013) and 101 Leadership Lessons From Basketball’s Greatest Coaches (Coaches Choice, 2015) Chris also plans to publish a chapbook of short stories in 2026.

Read on to learn more about Chris Stolle:

What do you hope readers will take away from your books?

Although the books are geared toward managers and coaches, there are many lessons and takeaways for people in other roles and jobs. Many of the lessons can be applied in myriad ways for myriad people. My partner is a teacher and she’s used the book. I have a friend who’s a nurse who said she bought copies for other nurses. I believe there’s something for everyone in this book. My mom isn’t a baseball fan and she read the book in a day.

What was the most challenging aspect of getting your book published, and how did you overcome it?

I knew the research would be the hardest part—and it was. For the baseball book, I thought I had several months to complete the manuscript. But I was asked to write it in four weeks. So I spent an intense two weeks doing all the research I could—and then I spent the third week writing the book and the fourth week having friends read a chapter each for their thoughts. Because each manager has a featured quote, it was sometimes quite challenging to find a good one for some managers, especially those who managed in the early 20th century. It wasn’t from a lack of available texts. It was because of my limited ability to get access to those texts. So in some cases, I had slim pickings of quote material. But for those men, I focused more on what leadership lessons I could gain from them rather than trying to find a lot of quoted material. In this way, some profiles have a slightly different perspective, which I don’t think is a bad thing.

How did you come up with the idea for your book?

I was working for Coaches Choice as a copy editor. I was editing 101 Leadership Lessons From Football’s Greatest Coaches and I felt like I could write the baseball version of the book. I asked my supervisor there whether I could do this. She said I needed to put a proposal together. I hired one of my editing friends to help and I was excited when they agreed to my proposal. I don’t know that I would have come up with this idea on my own, but I had a variety of life and work experiences I thought would fit well into this book.

What is your favorite genre?

I’m drawn to historical fiction because I love thinking about specific historical figures from different perspectives or totally re-imagined ones. I love writing historical fiction too. In fact, all the stories in my upcoming chapbook are historical fiction.

What is your writing routine, and any specific habits or rituals that have helped you stay productive?

I’m not generally a creature of habit when it comes to writing. In other areas of my life, yes. But with writing, I write when I feel like writing. I think that allows me to make sure I have fun with writing. If it’s not fun, it’s not worth doing. I’m not being paid to write, so I want to write when the time feels right. And I think forcing yourself to write every day from 9 a.m. to noon can lead to more bad writing than you can handle, especially if your mind becomes drawn to other things at that time of the day. I have done NaNoWriMo many times, but I didn’t always write at the same time each day. I still “won” NaNo several times. I had days where I wrote 2,000 words and days when I could barely manage 500. But it all worked out in the end. I think that kind of “competition” showed me that routine wasn’t going to work for me.

How do you handle writer’s block or moments when you feel creatively stuck?

I feel like writer’s block is an excuse created by a bad writer. No one has writer’s block. I should say that no one needs to have writer’s block. There are endless resources and endless prompts to help you write something. Maybe what you write isn’t related to your work in progress, but at least you’re able to write something when you’re feeling good and itching to write. So if I feel like I want to write but don’t have an immediate idea, I’ll find a prompt or I’ll research the etymology of a word and then write about that. Something is better than nothing. But moments of writer’s block (whether it’s real or not) are great times to work on rewriting something. In the act of revision, there’s always the opportunity to create.

What are you working on next?

Right now, I’m working on putting together three distinct poetry collections. I’ve been writing and publishing poetry for almost 40 years, so it’s well past time to be doing these. I was diagnosed with Parkinson’s disease in early 2024 and I’ve been feeling the urgency more and more in case my symptoms really worsen and my body and brain won’t allow me to do such things as this.

If you could do a book signing anywhere in the world, where would it be?

I’d like to have a book signing wherever in the afterlife my dad is. While he wasn’t necessarily interested in reading my poems and stories, I got into writing because he was a sportswriter (and my mom wrote features). I got to be a sportswriter for a while and we were able to work together for a couple years. That’s one of the highlights of my writing career.

What advice do you have for aspiring authors?

Read. This helps you learn what you like and what you don’t like. You have to always be thinking about the process of writing, not the end result. You’ll seldom get the end result you hope for. So let that be a surprise while you focus on learning how to write, how to rewrite, and how to rewrite again. Writing shouldn’t be hard, but it’s not necessarily easy either. Reading and writing as often as you can—no matter how bad the book or how bad the writing seems—will give you continued experience in trying to discover how to become a better writer. People can try to teach you that, but it’s a personal journey. Writing is akin to taking apart a clock, remembering how the pieces fit together, and then putting the pieces back in their proper place. This is just what we do with words.

What do you love about being an author?

I love the reactions and questions I get from people. I also love sharing what I write with others, especially poetry, because I want someone who has a limited voice to be able to find a poem or story that resonates with them and gives them words they’ve been struggling to find. I can’t say I write as much of that kind of poetry as I once did, but you never know what someone will find in your writing that will help them answer a question, discover something about themselves, or encourage them to try writing.

What do you dislike about being an author?

There’s nothing to dislike about being an author. Every writer has different goals. I find it curious that there are successful writers who are recluses or never do interviews or don’t want fans. What did you think would happen if you became a successful writer? Why did you pursue that knowing all that might be required of you? Perhaps I don’t understand that perspective because I enjoy being an author.

What has helped you sell the most books?

Promoting my books on Facebook is what led to almost all the sales of my books. If my publisher has an offer of a discount for a few days, I let my Facebook friends and followers know about that.

What is the most valuable lesson you have learned on your journey as an author that you wish you had known before you started?

Writing used to be something that was done by very few people. At least in terms of what was published. These days, with all the technology available, anyone can become a published author with the click of a button. It’s not for me to say that’s unfair or it’s cutting corners. I’ve worked in book publishing as an editor. I know how hard it can be for people to get published. I do think everyone has a story worth telling. I wish I knew how to cut out a lot of the noise. I wish I had known that it was going to require a lot more effort than I was putting in to get published. Not that I wanted it to be easy. I just thought it would be something I could do to supplement my regular income. But it requires more time, patience, and effort than I felt able to commit. I wish I didn’t have that kind of attitude toward writing, but if I committed more, I’d have to choose who or what would lose more of my time.

What is your favorite quote?

“Outside of a dog, a book is a man’s best friend. Inside of a dog, it’s too dark to read.”

This has always been attributed to Groucho Marx, although there is no source that can verify that. I still think it’s a fantastic quote about books and about life.
