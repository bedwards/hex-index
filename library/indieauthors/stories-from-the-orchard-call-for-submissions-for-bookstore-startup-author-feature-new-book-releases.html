Author Feature: Tom McAuliffe

Tom McAuliffe is an award-winning author and seasoned storyteller based on Florida’s Emerald Coast, where he lives with his wife and their ever-watchful cat. A former Navy photojournalist with the elite Combat Camera Group, Tom is a graduate of the Department of Defense’s Mass Communications program at Syracuse University.

With over 25 years of bylines to his name, Tom has served as both a magazine editor and writer, bringing depth, grit, and journalistic precision to every story he tells. His work reflects a lifetime of experience behind the lens and at the keyboard—blending real-world insight with compelling fiction.

Explore more at authortommcauliffe.com.

Welcome, Tom! How do you come up with ideas for your stories?

I love real life stories, 100%. There are so many stories that need to be told and documented. I am exploring a fairly new genre of what I call pseudo fiction or creative fiction. We take a real world event, and then extrapolate from our research about what might’ve occurred. For example, I recently published a book, called Dark, Open Sky – The Disappearance of MH 370. While we know that the plane has disappeared,we don’t exactly know what occurred in the cabin. I try to fill in those gaps. It makes for some compelling semi fictional story telling.

What do you hope readers will take away from them?

I was paid a great compliment recently, when a reader wrote to me and said that they had written to tell me they had read three of my novels, and then all three were impressed at the fact that I included strong women protagonists. I try to show men and women overcoming the obstacles and challenges of life and well, not overly religious. I often include the fact that God helps us. I want my readers to be entertained and also informed. I hope they walk away after reading one of my books and say hey, I’m still gonna read that I learned something I didn’t know.

What was the most challenging aspect of getting your books published?

At first, it was learning the Amazon/KDP rules and procedures… Learning how to format, etc. but most of all the challenges happen after publication with marketing, getting reviews, advertising. I think it’s burn authors. Need to understand that in today’s market place, marketing and promotion can be even more important than producing a great book. I often say that new authors should plan on spending at least as much on marketing and advertising as they did on production. This aspect of the business is the Achilles’ heel of most authors. I know dozens of great storytellers, but they will never sell a lot of books because they don’t understand the Amazon algorithm and promotion. Moreover, many people are just establishing their own websites and selling directly, which I think it’s a good thing.

What do you do when you get writer’s block?

I usually pour a good stiff drink or roll a doobie! Seriously though I find that using dictation I rarely get writer’s block anymore. And it’s all about just getting it down on the page, editing later, getting the story out and on the page and then you’ll have something to work with. I love the story of Michelangelo, after he had created a wonderful statue people came by and marveled at how beautiful it was and they had. I told him I said wow you have really created something wonderful and beautiful… And he said to them I created nothing. The image of the statue was already there in the rock, I just chipped away the excess. Great stories are out there. It’s up to us to go and find them and share them. And remember, if it was easy… Everybody would do it.

If you could do a book signing anywhere in the world, where would it be?

The Library at Trinity College in Dublin Ireland

What advice do you have for aspiring authors?

WRITE! It’s really that simple… And there’s really no excuse. You need to write, and you need to write every single day. Don’t worry about the words, don’t worry about the story just sit down and write… Or dictate… Consider keeping a journal. Give yourself writing assignments like “Describe the wind,” “Describe the color Blue” or “Write a 500 word story about a couple in a car breaking up”, etc. Keep a tab/log on how many words you write every day.

What has helped you sell books?

Book signings and selling direct via my web site. ALSO better Meta Data and Book Descriptions. Get the software ‘Publisher Rocket‘ to research your books and your competition. Have confidence in your work! Selling when you have to look in a reader/customer’s eyes can work wonders. Ask yourself, “Why should someone buy this book?”

What is the most valuable lesson you’ve learned as an indie author?

That every shithead and his brother thinks that they can do what we do. An AI is not making it any easier, but I think the most valuable lesson is that you can’t keep a good story down. If the underlying story is captivating and inspiring… It will rise to the top. It may take a while, but that’s the other thing, have patience. Rome was not built in a day, and it takes time to learn this business to learn your craft, and to learn the art and science of producing the best seller. I’m not the sharpest tool in the box, so it took me five years.

What are your favorite quotes?

“Writing is like sex. First you do it for love, then you do it for your friends, and then you do it for money.” ~Virginia Woolf

“Writing is its own reward.” ~ Henry Miller

Where can we find you?

Either my website or YouTube, I hang out in both places.

Anything else you would like to share?

Yes. Two things: most folks die with the stories left inside them… Don’t let that be you. And number two...Don’t be afraid to put yourself and your story out there gonna try something new.

Remember… If it was easy, everybody would do it.

Is it a good idea to crowdfund a bookstore startup?

A group of local authors, including me, are in the planning stages of starting an indie author focused bookstore! The synergy of working with other creative artists is exciting. I wondered if we could crowdfund the startup costs rather than get a loan or dip into our personal funds. ChatGPT searched the web and returned a response that crowdfunding a bookstore would mostly appeal to people in the area. But, a book would appeal to book lovers around the world. That was a lightbulb moment for me as someone who has created many anthologies for good causes. Why not create one for us all to pursue our dreams? From there Stories From The Orchard anthology begins…

Stories From The Orchard — Call for Submissions

Do you write poems, stories, or essays? Are you a blogger or book-lover with a way with words? Add your voice to Stories From The Orchard, an anthology celebrating nature, creativity, and community—and help bring the Bookworm Orchard independent bookstore to life.

Stories From The Orchard is a curated collection of short stories, essays, and poems that honor orchards and seasons (metaphorically or literally), creative courage, connections, community, curiosity… and the magic of everyday wonder.

What you can submit

  • Up to three pieces total (any mix of poems / stories / essays)

  • Previously published work is welcome if you own the copyright

  • All voices, styles, and experience levels encouraged

What selected contributors receive

  • A full-page feature in the book and the Indie Author Insights Newsletter

  • Lifetime membership to the Indie Authors Association

  • 10% bookstore discount once Bookworm Orchard opens

Note: There’s no monetary payment; all proceeds support the bookstore’s startup fund.

How to submit

Email your piece(s) to kat@bookwormorchard.org with:

  • Your bio (photo optional)

  • Website/social links

  • Your submission as a .docx, PDF, or Google Doc share link
