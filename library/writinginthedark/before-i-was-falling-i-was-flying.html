Dear writers,

Guess what? I am on my way home as of this morning, heading out as soon as I hit publish on this post! Going to arrive to more than a foot of fresh snow, with forecasts of 70 degree weather by Saturday. We’re living in an age of extremes.

Meanwhile, I am so excited about today’s post, and so very very grateful to my past self who not only did the extraordinary amount of work involved in building the curriculum for this intensive in 2023, but also the more recent past self who recognized it was time to offer it again. I know that some of you who are working through this intensive are doing it for the second time, and discovering riches that were out of reach to you three years ago. Brava! This is how writing works. Each time we approach the page, we are a new version of ourselves, and, therefore, a new writer.

During the first iteration of the Essay in 12 Steps intensive, and I rolled out a new feature: Voice Memos. This new feature was/is an informal one wherein I, along with sometimes Billie (and other writers, too!) discuss close reads of literary essays, specific elements of writing craft, and peculiar challenges of the writing life. We do Voice Memos occasionally, on no particular schedule, along with Video Memos, Live Salons, Write-Ins, etc., all features that are normally paywalled.

Meanwhile, both this post itself and the Voice Memo focus on a gorgeous essay on the search for home and what travels between us and those we love, by Iselin Gambert. And in preparation for our close read discussion of Gambert’s essay in the Voice Memo, Billie and I first read the whole essay loud to each other.

How did that go?

Well, Gambert’s essay made me sob. And when I became thus overwhelmed by tears, Billie would take over … until they could no longer go on, at which point I would try again, and so on. To note, Billie and I were a bit fragile when we first recorded the Voice Memo shared with today’s post, as I wrote about on back then in a Lit Salon post. But fragility or not, the essay is devastatingly beautiful.

Please don’t let my tears scare you away from this gorgeous essay (or the Voice Memo; Billie and I did all our crying entirely off mic, and we made sure we were sufficiently recovered before we began recording, though maybe we sound just a little stuffy from the aftermath of tears). Bottom line? “The Great Chimera” is a beautiful, beautiful essay and I look forward to discussing it with all of you in the comments this week.

Meanwhile, on to further thoughts about devastation and the purpose of art (remember how Franz Kafka said books were to be the axe to break open the frozen sea inside us? Yes, that).

When I was three years old, I tripped while running on the sidewalk. Before I was falling, I was flying, flying in my beloved brown leather saddle shoes with their slick leather soles and loose laces, my white acrylic knee-highs slouched around my ankles. Sloping above me, the ancient glacial rock of Lake Superior’s North Shore, dandelion and lupine sprouting from its crevices. Below me, another heaving, cracked-up sidewalk like all the others then and now in the West End of Duluth, Minnesota. When I ran, I flew. When I crashed, I crashed.

There was gravel on the cement, and when I crashed, a round pebble lodged itself under the skin of my split knee. No one washed the cut. When the cut scabbed over and the split skin fused itself back together, the pebble remained, a pea-sized bump. For many years, I could wiggle the hidden pebble from side to side with my finger. The sensation of rock on bone was always pleasantly painful, a sharp surprise.

One day the pebble dissolved. I don’t know exactly when, because at first it left behind a hard lump of scar tissue which itself only softened very slowly, over time, so that that its gone-ness was unknowable.

Acclaimed fiction writer Joy Williams has this to say about good writing:

[It] startles the reader back into Life. The work—this Other, this other thing—this false life that is even less than the seeming of this lived life, is more than the lived life, too. It is so unreal, so precise, so unsurprising, so alarming, really. Good writing never soothes or comforts. It is no prescription, either is it diversionary, although it can and should enchant while it explodes in the reader’s face.” Good writing, Williams seems to be saying, is writing that makes us feel.

I want to understand the mechanics of creating such writing, the principles of craft that consistently apply to it. I also want to understand this: what does it mean, exactly, to feel? My graduate school advisor says that what he seeks from a piece of writing is to be “devastated.” What does it mean for a piece of writing to devastate? Or to startle us back into Life—with a capital L?

I want a handful of the magic hay from which such writing is spun. No, I want the spinning wheel and I want each and every part that makes it go. I want the orifice and the bobbin, the flyer and the maidens and the treadle and the legs, I want the footman and the drive wheel, and, finally—how glorious is this— I want the “mother-of-all.”

I want rock on bone, the pebble that never dissolves.

Disclaimer: You do not have to be writing sad things in order for your work to be devastating. We can be swallowed whole by joy, as happens for me each time I read Brian Doyle’s “Joyas Valadoras.” So, if you do not wish to write about suffering, you can always write about joy or hilarity or even mundanity. Even then, you can still devastate us—that just means you steal our breath away in the most arresting manner. For me, that generally means writing with clarion precision and unflinching truth. It can be funny, it can be ecstatic, it can be strange, it can be sad, it can be so happy it hurts. I love when writers give me these intense experiences, and I am eternally interested in how they do it.

With regard to “The Great Chimera,” Billie and I try, in our Voice Memo, to identify the prominent elements of craft (beyond the careful, precise language and imagery and melodic sentences) that make Gambert’s essay do what it does emotionally, and we came up with the following incomplete list:

  • Restraint

  • Rich, complex braiding comprising several subjective topics/threads as well as multiple objective topics/threads

  • A central metaphor of the chimera and microchimeric cells

  • A central question around the meaning of home, which, though unspoken, connects directly to the narrator’s grieving of her mother (with our mothers being, as Michele Filgate says in her own beautiful lyric essay, “What My Mother and I Don’t Talk About,” our first homes)

  • Repetition

There is more, of course, at work in Gambert’s essay. Let’s consider her intricate, powerful opening:

You will always exist in the universe in one form or another — Suzuki Roshi

While it’s been known for over a century that a mother’s cells can travel through her placenta into her unborn child, it wasn’t until 1979 that scientists discovered that the reverse is also true, finding Y-chromosome cells in a pregnant woman’s blood. In 1996 a geneticist found male fetal cells in a woman’s blood 27 years after she gave birth. These “microchimeric cells”—cells of one person that have embedded themselves into the bodies of another—are named after the monstrous fire-breathing she-creature Chimera from Greek mythology, whose sighting was an omen of disaster. The infamous 15th-century anti-witchcraft treatise Malleus Maleficarum cast women as the embodiment of Chimera, describing her as a “monster […] of three forms; its face was that of a radiant and noble lion, it had the filthy belly of a goat, and it was armed with the virulent tail of a viper.” In other words, the treatise explained, those who embody the Chimera are “beautiful to look upon, contaminating to the touch, and deadly to keep.”

Cell-sharing between mothers and children isn’t limited to blood: a 2012 study found that most of us have our mother’s cells embedded in our brains, and vice versa. That means that our mother’s brain is likely home not only to some of our cells but also to some of her mother’s cells: three generations of cells inside a single brain, living and functioning alongside each other for years, ultimately accompanying each other into the grave.

I came across this study a few months after my own mother died, following a harrowing five-year ordeal with breast cancer that spread to her brain and robbed her of her ability to walk. Her name was Gry. She was 59 years old. While the prospect of her death had loomed large for me for half a decade by then, nothing could have prepared me for the unmooring I experienced in its wake. From the moment she died, I felt the whole world shift somehow: colors appeared muter, sounds less vibrant. I felt a gnawing feeling deep within me that part of me had died, too.

Of course, many parts—or versions—of me died when my mother did. The Iselin who called her mom each evening after work as she unlocked her bicycle for the ride home: she died. The Iselin who relied on her mom to help pick out glasses frames that had just the right combination of quirky and elegant: she died. The Iselin who would sit curled up on the couch for hours with her mom gossiping about politics, literature, religion, celebrities, and the unbearable sweetness of my brother’s children: she died.

And on and on, so many versions of me died that day. Every one had up until that moment been bound up with the physical manifestation of my mother and every future version of me that could now never manifest. The Iselin who could tell her mom that she would one day work as an academic in Australia and Sweden: she died. The Iselin who could boast to her mom that the Law Review article she spent a year writing was accepted for publication: she died. The Iselin that may still one day have a child and who would have had her mom with her in the delivery room, who would have brimmed with joy at the sight of her mom holding her child for the very first time; she died. She died, she died, she died.

It wasn’t until I stumbled onto the study about microchimeric cells of unborn children being found, years later, embedded inside their mothers’ brains that I realized it was actually true. And not only in some metaphorical, existential sense. I knew it, I thought. Part of me really did die that day.
