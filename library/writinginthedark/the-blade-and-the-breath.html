And Now: The Blade and the Breath: What “A Little Tooth” Teaches Us About Micro Flash

Micro flash—stories or essays under about 300 words, sometimes under 100, though definitions across flash vary—is an act of distillation. It is writing that works like a blade: sharp, efficient, gleaming. But it’s also breath: a single exhalation that holds an entire life inside it. The best micro flash doesn’t summarize experience—it embodies it, compressed to the point of transformation, like coal under pressure becoming diamond.

Thomas Lux’s poem “Little Tooth,” though written as verse, functions with the precision and compression of the best micro flash. In just sixty-six words, Lux delivers the entire span of a life—from infancy to dusk—without summary, sentiment, or waste. This is the essence of micro flash: not smallness but density. Each word is weighted, each image inevitable.

This is what we will do this week: we will cover, in a single, linear breath, an arc of time as vast as a human life, compressed in a container under 100 words, and we will do it step by step, taking Lux’s poem apart like a clock and reassembling it with our own images and imaginings.

The Context: A Poet of the Everyday Sublime

Thomas Lux (1946–2017) was a poet who fused working-class vernacular with philosophical weight. His work often balanced absurdity and ache, humor and heartbreak. That balance makes “Little Tooth” such a potent bridge between poetry and micro flash: it moves like a prose piece boiled down to its essence, yet it breathes like a lyric. In Lux’s world, a baby’s tooth becomes a cosmology; a domestic milestone becomes a revelation about time and tenderness.
