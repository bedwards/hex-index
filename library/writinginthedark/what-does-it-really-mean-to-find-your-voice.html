The Voices I Have Lived In

A craft essay

The writing life does not proceed linearly—and this is both its great frustration and its great mercy. It accumulates gradually—long dry stretches, sudden storms, seasons when the light is strange and everything you write seems to shimmer, seasons when the words land lifeless and you bury them in drawers. The voice we are searching for on the page is not simply “our voice,” as though such a thing exists whole and waiting, like a room with the lights already on. The voice is something we construct from wreckage and from wonder, from the places we have written and the places we have been too afraid to write and from the generosity those who, at the just right moment, hand us what might eventually become a well-lit word in a dark corridor.

My friend Norah offered me that generosity. Or rather: she and her rooftop did. Norah’s rooftop is a summer I return to often in my memory, my twelfth summer, which exists in the amber of my childhood when everything felt provisional and possible and terrible in equal measure. Norah’s rooftop was flat, or at least the portion we had access to was flat—the widow’s walk. A widow’s walk is a railed rooftop platform, common on 19th-century New England coastal homes and used—so goes the folklore—by wives watching for husbands returning from sea. Norah and I climbed onto the widow’s walk by way of a spiral staircase from her third floor, and from that rooftop platform we could see all the way to the edges of downtown Casper dwarfed against the backdrop of Casper mountain. The sky above had, as western skies do, a depth you could fall into as Norah and I talked about school and books and boys and the prospect of junior high, which, though coming for us the next school year, still felt foreign and far away. And we wrote poemish things up there, things that were mostly playful and silly and clumsily rhymed, but that sometimes reached toward things we couldn’t yet name. The rooftop was a kind of permission: that language could be important, that our voices counted, that we could sit in the air and make something out of the nothing, and this could be the whole of what was required in a given moment. All that mattered were the words we were making.

Norah and I didn’t exactly “find our voices” on the rooftop. First of all, we were eleven and twelve. But, also, voice is not a single thing. A writer does not have a voice the way a person has a fingerprint, fixed and unchanging. A writer has voices—a chorus of them, gathered over decades, from the basements and rooftops and classrooms and kitchen counters of a life. The skill—and it is a skill, hard-won and never fully possessed—is learning to recognize and refine the sound of our own complicated vocal blend and discern which voice belongs to which story.

Sometimes this happens when we finally access a part of our voice that’s been stifled. For years I tried to write about my childhood in the voice of a woman in her thirties, then her forties—a voice that had perspective, that had processed, that had done the therapeutic work and the literary work and arrived somewhere thoughtful and reflective and composed. The voice was, I think, at least some of the time, good writing. But it was not the right voice for my childhood stories. It was too knowing. It had already survived what the story was trying to survive. It had gotten ahead of the material.

I have written before about the moment my child narrator arrived, so I will say it only briefly here: she arrived somewhat accidentally, as the truest things in writing often do. I was drafting a piece about childhood, about Wyoming, about the cold light of certain places where you first understood that the world can be strange and dangerous—and suddenly there she was, this child narrator, speaking in a voice I hadn’t planned for, a voice with gaps in it, deliberate unknowings, a voice that could see clearly what the adult writer had spent years trying to see, and could report it in the plain, strange, defamiliarizing way of a child for whom the world is still a buffet of discovery.

The child narrator doesn’t explain. She doesn’t analyze. She doesn’t have the vocabulary to translate what she sees. Instead, she stands in front of the terrible thing the way a child stands in front of the ocean—without the defenses of interpretation, without the mediation of retrospect—and reports it. A child narrator can say, I don’t believe in jackalopes. But people have to make up their own minds about these things. And that child’s voice holds disbelief and belief simultaneously without resolving the tension, something no adult narrator in my possession could have done. That single sentence introduced the whole paradox of childhood: that you can live in a world that is both magical and brutal, and simply report both at once, without insisting that one cancels out the other.

The first real thing I wrote in my own true voice happened in fifth-grade social studies class, in a spiral Mead notebook, while my teacher talked about something I’ve now forgotten. This is the first law of the writing life: the thing that matters happens right alongside the thing that is supposed to matter. The poem was about winter. Or something like winter. There was a field and sky. There were trees with branches like black lace. And there was a person alone in a clearing in the center of all that. That’s what I remember of the subject, but I’ll never forget that sensation of a key turning, of a mechanism I hadn’t known I possessed suddenly engaging. Something slid into alignment. The room kept going around me—the chalkboard, the laminated maps with their color-coded territories, the smell of elementary school with its asphalt and milk and the specific loneliness of childhood—and there I was inside the language, inside the making, which meant I was, briefly, inside something that felt like home.

I was ten years old. I didn’t know yet what I had discovered. But I knew it was mine.

After Norah’s rooftop, we moved back to Minnesota and I was sent to live in my dad’s basement. To write about the basement is to enter a country I don’t visit lightly. I lived down there during my junior high years, that bleak and paralytic time when childhood was becoming something else and the something else was frightening in ways I didn’t have words for. My father’s basement. The buzz of the overhead fluorescent lights. The feeling of being underground by every definition—underground from the family, underground from light, underground from the version of myself I had glimpsed in the fifth-grade classroom with the Mead notebook and the turning key.

In the basement, my voice grew small. But I still wrote in secret notebooks. This is important: the secrecy was not incidental. The secrecy was the whole condition of the writing. Because to write in secret is to write in a space that belongs entirely to you, and when you are twelve or thirteen and the world is rearranging itself into shapes that feel threatening and wrong, the secret notebook is where the self goes to survive. I wrote whatever I needed to write. Not good writing—I know that now, though I didn’t know it then, at least not fully. But I know now in the way you come know something about a place you’ve left: you see it clearly from a distance. What lived in those notebooks was not craft. It was need. It was the mind’s way of staying alive when staying alive required somewhere protected to go.

I have come to believe that this is one of writing’s oldest, deepest functions: to make a room inside language where the self can breathe. This is where our voice first emerges. Craft comes eventually. But survival comes first.

High school knocked the wind out of me and my voice went with it.

Or rather—I let it go, I set it down, the way you set down something heavy when you’ve been carrying it too long and you need your hands free. High school was about trying to become acceptable, which is the oldest and saddest project of adolescence. I was writing, sometimes, but not in the way I mean when I say writing. Not in the way that is the key turning. I was filling pages with words that didn’t cost me anything, which meant they were worth approximately what they cost.

I think about students who tell me they used to write and then stopped, the way they say it with a kind of mourning, as though something was taken from them. And what I tell them is this: it will come back to you if you let it, if you bring yourself back to it. That’s another paradox of writing: that sometimes what takes us our voice away—this necessity of fitting ourselves into a world that did not originally make room for us—is the very thing that eventually drive us back to the page to reclaim the voice we lost. Exile from ourselves can be its own kind of education.

The poetry class I took in college was the kind of class you remember the way you remember certain dreams: not all of it, but the texture of it, the shape and movement of light, the sense upon waking that something inside you has changed.

And what changed inside me—I credit Sharon Olds for this—was a new understanding that poetry could hold the most difficult material—grief, strangeness, desire, unspeakable things lodged in the body—without explaining. That a poem could be both beautiful and honest, that those two qualities weren’t in conflict. That sometimes, beauty is what allows honesty onto the page. I had been separating these things in my mind, pretty words on one side and truth on the other. Poetry class collapsed that false division. I wrote that semester with a ferocity that surprised me. My poems weren’t quite honest, but they weren’t dishonest, either. The key was turning again, and this time I had some idea what I was doing when I turned it.

And this is what craft can give us: the knowledge of what we are doing, even in the midst of doing it. The fifth-grader with the Mead notebook had only instinct and need. The junior high student sought survival. The high-schooler grasped for safety at all cost, even silence. But by college, I was beginning to have something else, something that could work alongside instinct and need: attention, precision, the deliberate cultivation of the right word in the right place. And these are all elements of voice. Indeed, our voice strengthens as we discover that form is not the enemy of feeling but instead its rigorous ally.

Then came the curiously long, wet days of early motherhood, that time of expansion and drowning. The universe was suddenly enormous and terrifyingly close and I was writing all the while, though the writing happened in the margins. Naptime essays. Post-midnight poems. The notebook on the kitchen counter, open to a half-finished sentence that might wait days before its second half arrived, if it ever did.

What I wrote in those years, I’d call essays. And the essay was teaching me what an essay was through the writing itself. The essay, I discovered, could hold a grief that didn’t yet have a name, could circle something dark and necessary without forcing it into the shape of an argument or a conclusion. But the essays I wrote in those years were still too pretty. I was reaching for beauty the way you reach for a railing on a dark staircase—not because the railing is the destination, but because you need it to keep moving. The prettiness was real. But it was also, I see now, a kind of permission slip I was writing to myself: you are allowed to be here, on this page, with this subject. Sorrow was present in all of it, flowing like a dark current beneath the surface of the language, present in what I was not yet saying even as I circled the not-saying with great care.

As a teacher, I now tell writers that we sometimes write around the edges of what we cannot yet write directly, and that this circling is not failure but preparation. The pretty essays of my early motherhood were my years of circling. I was getting closer. I didn’t know it yet, but my voice was growing into itself.

I have been thinking about what it means to find your voice on the page. Not in the self-help sense, which tends to mean permission to be yourself, though permission is not nothing and should not be dismissed. But I mean in the craft sense. I mean: what does it actually take?

I know it takes time, which is the most obvious answer and the most resisted one, because we live in a culture that is deeply impatient with the slow accumulation of capacity. But the fifth-grade poem and the basement notebooks and the rooftop and the poetry class and the naptime essays and the child narrator who finally cracked it open—these were not detours. They were the work. They were the decades of circling the material, gathering the tools, failing in instructive ways, discovering in the failures what the work actually needed.

It takes the willingness to write badly—genuinely, sometimes heroically badly—on the way to writing well. The secret notebooks in the basement were not good writing. They were the precondition for good writing. The pretty essays of early motherhood, with their beauty-as-railing, were not yet what they were reaching for. They were practice in the deepest sense: not rehearsal for the real thing, but the real thing being prepared.

It takes, perhaps most of all, courage, the kind of courage that is not the opposite of fear but its companion. To write into the material that most needs writing—the basement, the bleak years, the things the adult self has learned to manage and explain—is to do so without the protection of distance. The child narrator works, when she works, because she is inside the experience, not above it. That proximity is both the terror and the power.

That child narrator, when I discovered her, unlocked more than a technique. That narrator—her voice—was an architecture, a way of assembling all the other voices I had lived in. Because once you have found the right voice for the central material, the other voices begin to arrange themselves around it. The girl on Norah’s rooftop, writing poems into the sky. The girl in the basement. The college student discovering that form and feeling could share a room. The young mother reaching for beauty as a railing. These were not separate writers. They were the same writer, moving through time, accumulating the capacities she would eventually need.

This is what I mean when I say that voice is architecture: it’s more than the sound you make on the page, it’s the structure that can hold the weight of what you need to say. The child narrator was the load-bearing wall I hadn’t known the building needed. Once she was in place, everything else could lean against her.

My granddaughter tells me, as I have always told her, that she came from the stars. She says it with certainty, the way children say true things—not because they have reasoned toward the truth but because they haven’t yet learned to reason away from it. She came from the stars, and the stars are still in her, she insists, and when she says it I think: yes, and so did I, and the writing life has been, in some sense, the long work of finding my way back there—back to the plain and startling language of the unprotected self, back to the girl with the Mead notebook in the social studies class who felt the key turn and didn’t yet know what it unlocked, only that it unlocked something, and that the unlocking was enough.

The child narrator taught me this: that the self who can most clearly see the story is not always the self who has survived it longest. Sometimes it is the self who was inside it, before the long survival began. Sometimes the most truthful voice is the most vulnerable one. Sometimes the way to show what happened is not to understand it, but to stand very close to it, in the plain and astonished language of someone for whom the world is still capable of surprise.

And the voices, all of them—the fifth-grader, the rooftop poet, the girl in the basement, the young mother reaching, the woman who found the child and let the child speak—are not separate selves. They are the same self, and she has always been writing, and the writing has always been the same thing: a room inside language where the self can breathe, where the key turns, where the part that burns also, finally, glows.

Love,

Jeannine
