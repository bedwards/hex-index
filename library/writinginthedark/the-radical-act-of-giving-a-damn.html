I’ve been trying so hard my whole life.

Openly striving. Nakedly reaching after various hopes and dreams—whether to get through high school, get a job, make a family, grow a garden. Most recently, it’s been trying in all kinds of different ways: to make a Substack, create a giant camp for writers in the middle of Wisconsin, get an agent, write a book. And why not try to make that book one that will sell, maybe even be a bestseller? Why not try to make a book that makes a difference?

Obviously, that’s a long shot. But why not? Why not at least try?

Well, lots of reasons, if you listen to contemporary culture.

In his essay, “Full House,” in Dress Your Family in Corduroy and Denim, David Sedaris describes a moment where he is stripped down, literally and figuratively, in the middle of a childhood sleepover, playing strip poker with boys he is terrified to be attracted to—and he names, in more or less the words I’m about to paraphrase, one of the most primal fears of adolescent life: there is nothing worse than for other people to see that you actually care.

I’ve taught middle school. I know the truth of that sentence like I know my own handwriting. I watched eleven- and twelve-year-olds perform elaborate choreographies of indifference day after day—too cool for the assignment, too cool for the field trip, too cool for everything, including, often, their own best friends. The goal was total invulnerability. The goal was to need nothing and reveal nothing and want nothing that anyone else could see. Caring was the thing most likely to get you marked, socially speaking.

What we don’t talk about enough is how little this changes, even after puberty. At least in contemporary American culture, the need to not be observed caring doesn’t dissipate, it just gets subtler, which is worse.

A twelve-year-old’s cool is obvious—it has the blunt force of a hockey puck. But adult cynicism slides in quietly. Even the non-cynical among us tend to have a kind of protective layer against “trying too hard,” a layer that’s disguised as sophistication or pragmatism or even modesty or humbleness. We carry a kind of been-there wisdom that tells us to hedge our bets before we’ve even placed them, to want things privately or not at all, and to to guard ourselves from the specific humiliation of visible longing. Oscar Wilde, in Lady Windermere’s Fan, defined a cynic as someone who knows the price of everything and the value of nothing. But the cruelest part of adult cynicism is less about how it affects our assessment of others (though that’s problematic, to be sure) and more about what it does to our relationship with our own effort. It teaches us to be embarrassed by trying.

The advertising executive and creative director John Hegarty put it plainly: “Cynicism is the death of creativity.” He’s right, and the reason for this is structural. To make anything—a painting, a novel, a business, a relationship, a life you recognize as your own—you have to be willing to be seen wanting it to work. You have to be willing to care out loud. Brené Brown, who researches vulnerability and shame, has spent years documenting what happens when we don’t, writes that we armor up at work “using things like cynicism, perfectionism—needing to be the knower and be right versus the learner and get it right.” The armor feels like protection. But it functions as amputation.

It’s no wonder that Brown’s research resonates with me and always has—because I can’t seem to wear that so-called armor even if I try. I am constitutionally incapable of hidden effort. I seem to be, as a person, somewhere between a golden retriever and a very earnest public radio pledge drive. Whatever I care about, I care about visibly. I love things hard and pursue things hard and when I fail, I fail where everyone can see me. This is not a practice I cultivated or a virtue I take credit for. It’s more like a factory defect, a dial that got stuck on sincere somewhere around the time everyone else’s turned toward cool. A former publisher even said to me once that I was, as a writer, simply too sincere. So it’s no wonder I tried to fight this trait in the way that everyone does starting by the age of twelve or thirteen. I was no different. Of course I wanted to be cool. It’s just that those efforts didn’t take. The armor never fit. No matter how many times I tried it on, it kept sliding off.

So at some point—and fairly early on, certainly by the time I became pregnant with my first child at age twenty-one, which unleashed in me a torrent of raw caring—I stopped fighting my temperament. Once I let myself care openly about the baby I’d soon give birth to, it became harder to obscure how much I cared about everything else. So I gave in to caring in plain sight.

For those of us who make art from words, caring can take myriad forms. We can care about whether the words sing and sear and seethe and serenade in ways that feel urgent, necessary, and true. We can care about whether we finish things. We can care about whether anyone reads the words we write—and whether they like those words. We can care about agents and editors deeming our words worthy of publication. If we’re lucky enough to jump that hurdle, we can care about how far those words reach, whether through clicks and shares or book sales. We can care if critics praise our words or judges honor them with prizes. When we are making art through the heart, there is always something to care about because there is always something at stake, and that something is a part of ourselves.

What happens, I wonder, if we tell ourselves, over and over and over again, in all the ways we can, that what we are doing, this thing we call writing, this thing we claim as our art, doesn’t matter that much, isn’t that important, probably won’t amount to anything, won’t likely mean anything to anyone, was never intended to be a big deal, etc. etc. etc. What do those messages do to us over time? How do they gradually whittle away at that “part of ourselves” that we need to make art, that “part of ourselves” that in fact, becomes the art itself?

That thing you are working on or want to be working on? It’s not “no big deal.” It’s not “just whatever.” It’s not “probably nothing anyway, but.” That thing you’re working on is important. That doesn’t mean you can’t hold it lightly and lower the stakes and take some risks and allow it to become absolute rubbish if that’s what it wants, in the end, to be. Because holding the work lightly enough for it to breathe is a prerequisite for bringing it fully alive. Brilliance can rarely emerge from a work suffocated by relentless premature demands of greatness. But nor does brilliance surface in a swamp of cynical self-protection. No, the trick is that we can and must—in addition to holding the work lightly—allow ourselves the exquisite pain of caring deeply about it.

I’m not saying we all need to want our writing to be the most important thing of all, or that we need to want to win awards or write bestsellers. But I am saying that if we’re bothering to try to make art at all, if we’re giving ourselves over to what Mary Oliver described as, “the call to creative work, [our own] creative power restive and uprising,” then it matters. It matters enough to try our hardest. It matters enough to be seen trying our hardest. It matters enough to care, right in front of everyone.

You know I’ve been on a Georgia O'Keeffe kick lately (I wrote about her in depth last week in The Shape of a Life). And O’Keeffe was absolutely an artist who understood her work as an inseparable expression of her own identity and internal vision. Never did she reduce her art to a mere reproduction of some aspect of reality. Indeed, she famously insisted that her creativity extended far beyond her canvasses. "Your life is your art,” she said, “as well as the thing you call your art.”

Franz Kafka also described creative work in a way that has always felt, to me, as if it points to something larger: “Don’t bend; don’t water it down; don’t try to make it logical; don’t edit your own soul according to the fashion. Rather, follow your most intense obsessions mercilessly.” To follow Kafka’s advice means you’re going to be visible. It means you’re going to be the person in the room who is obviously, undeniably trying—and in a culture that has confused guardedness with depth and irony with intelligence, to be the person openly trying is to accept what can be an uncomfortable level of exposure. And being openly exposed means failure costs more. To openly try means you might openly fail.

But consider the cost of the alternative. Brown describes the people who never enter the arena—those who fill “a million cheap seats,” she writes, “who will never be brave with their lives but who will spend every ounce of energy they have hurling advice and judgment at those who dare greatly. Their only contributions are criticism, cynicism, and fearmongering.” There is no art in the cheap seats. There is no love there, no discovery, no surprise. There is only the cold comfort of having never been wrong, because you never tried anything. The philosopher in the cheap seat knows, as Wilde would say, the price of everything. The person in the arena is the only one learning what anything is worth.

Martha Graham understood this. She wrote that creative expression has “a vitality, a life force, an energy, a quickening that is translated through you into action, and because there is only one of you in all time, this expression is unique. And if you block it, it will never exist through any other medium and will be lost.” What blocks it is almost never lack of talent. What blocks it is the decision, made quietly and usually in adolescence and then renewed every decade or so, that it is safer not to try too hard in the light of day, where anyone can see you.

I have made the opposite decision. If I had a headstone—I won’t, because I want to be cremated, but hypothetically—I think it would say: She tried so hard.

I’m okay with that. Because the thing that Sedaris named as the worst thing for his pubescent self—being seen to care—turns out, on the other side of adolescence, to be the only way through. Something Sedaris knows and lives. For example, not only does he revise his own essays dozens of times, but he also reads his work in progress during his national tours, making little hatch marks in the margins of his manuscript to indicate where the audience laughs or not—all so he can revise again, later. The pubescent David Sedaris was, like almost all of us at that age, afraid to be seen trying, but the adult Sedaris operates on an entirely opposite principle. He cares whether we like his work, and he toils openly toward that aim.

In truth, cynicism doesn’t protect us against loss at all. It’s just its own distinct version of loss, a slower and quieter version than the big and small public losses we all fear (I think of myself looking over my shoulder, indignantly uncool, when I trip on the sidewalk). But cynicism is the kind of loss where you don’t notice what’s gone until you look around one day and the arena is empty and you’re the only one left in the cheap seats, safe, unwounded, and completely alone with the price of everything you never reached for.

Love,

Jeannine

Writers are told constantly that editors, agents, and readers look for prose with a powerful voice — a distinctive voice, a voice that pulls us into the story and keeps us there. A voice we will “follow.” But few workshops unpack what voice actually is, how it works, or how to dynamize voice deliberately.

This workshop changes that.

In three lively, interactive hours, we’ll pull voice apart at the seams — we’ll examine its actual components, explore why it sometimes falls flat, and learn how to unlock the deeper, more distinctive version of your own voice. We’ll look at the work of writers whose voices are unforgettable — writers like James Baldwin, Joan Didion, Toni Morrison, George Saunders, Maggie Nelson, Ocean Vuong, Nora Ephron, Roxane Gay, Annie Dillard, and more — and we’ll use their work not just for inspiration, but as a laboratory.

We’ll also explore a distinction that many craft books overlook—the profound difference between your writer’s voice (the deep and recognizable sensibility that runs through everything you write), the voice of a specific project (the tonal identity of a specific book, essay, or collection), and distinct character voices (the various, separate interiorities you must inhabit in fiction and memoir). Understanding these three levels is the key to voice singularity, voice range, and the strange alchemy of making a reader feel like they know you and want to listen to you forever.

You’ll leave with a clear framework for analyzing and strengthening voice in any draft you’re working on—plus two writing exercises designed to crack open parts of your voice you may not have even known were there.
