Meanwhile, once again, congratulations, Nicole, on the publication of your essay, as well as the courage to make it in the first place, and then take the risk of submitting it in effort to do something bravest of all, which is to let it live its own life in the world.

Nicole’s email inspired me to finish up something I’ve been putting together on the topic of publication—a topic I think about a lot, because, for me, publication is not just about the thrill and the honor of having our work selected by editors or judges. It’s about something far deeper and more profound, which is our own development and transformation as writers and humans, and the inexplicable, alchemical exchange that occurs when our work lives its own life in the wild.

At a certain point in our lives as writers, most of us come to know the exquisite joy and pain of the moment our work leaves our hands. Before the manuscript goes to the agent, before the essay is submitted to the journal, before the poem is sent to a journal in hopes that strangers might read it. Just before that precarious moment—while we are still the sole custodians of what we have made—a tremor of fear might move through us, and the question we have been deferring for months or even years suddenly presents itself with full force: What if I let them see this?

The fear is real. But fear can’t always be taken as a sign that we’re not ready, or that the work is not ready. Georgia O’Keeffe (read my recent essay on O’Keeffe, “The Shape of a Life”)—who painted the desert and the sky and the wide bones of the world with an audacity that changed what painting could be—understood this and offered that understanding to us most generously when she said, “I’ve been absolutely terrified every moment of my life and I’ve never let it keep me from doing a single thing that I wanted to do.” She confesses a life lived in full and constant knowledge of fear as companion, as context, as the medium in which all the real work gets done. How marvelous to realize that fear was never the enemy. Only the stopping would have been.

For writers, the fear of releasing our work is specific and intimate in a way that other fears are not. To be afraid of heights or open water is quite distinct from being afraid of being genuinely seen, genuinely known. After all, the work we make is drawn from the deep well of the interior, built from memory and longing and private grief and the surprising texture of the world as we alone have experienced it. Handing that interior life to strangers is to say, Here is what I noticed. Here is what I could not stop turning over in my mind until it became sentences. Here, then, is a snapshot of what defines me. This is why publication is an act of extraordinary vulnerability. But then again, so is not publishing. Maya Angelou, who knew something about the cost of keeping things hidden, once named the other side of the risk this way: “There is no greater agony than bearing an untold story inside you.” The fear of being seen and the agony of remaining unseen are both real, and every writer lives between them.

But what happens when we choose the agony of invisibility over the terror of exposure? C.S. Lewis, writing about love in The Four Loves, described the logic of this choice—at least, relative to the risk of love, which, I would say, is not so far afield from the risk of self-exposure:

To love at all is to be vulnerable. Love anything and your heart will be wrung and possibly broken. If you want to make sure of keeping it intact you must give it to no one, not even an animal. Wrap it carefully round with hobbies and little luxuries; avoid all entanglements. Lock it up safe in the casket or coffin of your selfishness. But in that casket — safe, dark, motionless, airless — it will change. It will not be broken; it will become unbreakable, impenetrable, irredeemable.

He was, of course, writing about love between people, but, as I said, the principle holds for the risk of exposing our love of making, creating, imagining, and synthesizing with words what it means to be a human being—what it means to be this human being, the one who we are right now, in this body, in this lifetime, with this mind and heart, these hands, and this many words to capture it all. The writer who keeps the pages in a drawer, who endlessly revises without ever releasing, who waits for some future moment of perfect readiness that never arrives, is not necessarily protecting anything. Even Emily Dickinson who wrote some 1800 poems while only publishing ten in her lifetime, seemed to understand that for art to be art, there must be some human exchange. While she was alive, Dickinson’s poetry was read by a small, private circle of family members, notably her sister-in-law Susan Huntington Gilbert Dickinson, a few close friends, and her mentor/correspondent Thomas Wentworth Higginson. In other words, it did not sit in some version of Lewis’s airless casket and coffin.

Louise Erdrich, in The Painted Drum, gave us a dictum for living that applies equally well to writing:

“Life will break you. Nobody can protect you from that, and living alone won’t either, for solitude will also break you with its yearning. You have to love. You have to feel. It is the reason you are here on earth. You are here to risk your heart. You are here to be swallowed up.”

You are here to be swallowed up.

Imagine that. So, we’re not meant to avoid the possibility of being misunderstood or dismissed or quietly ignored, but to step straight into the mouth of the world, knowing it has teeth. Edna St. Vincent Millay—Pulitzer Prize winner, poet of radical aliveness—wrote to her mother in 1927 that “a person who publishes a book willfully appears before the populace with his pants down. If it is a good book, nothing can hurt him. If it is a bad book, nothing can help him.” The courage in that framing goes beyond bravado: it means that once the work is genuinely good, exposure cannot harm what matters. The vulnerability, however mortifying it may feel in the moment of release, is ultimately beside the point. What endures is the work itself and what it does in the world.

And what the work does in the world, well—I love thinking about the singular beauty of it—the way publication sets in motion a process we simply can’t control or predict. Once released, the work is no longer entirely ours. It never was, really, but now that fact becomes visible and undeniable. A reader picks up your novel on a Tuesday in November, in a city you have never visited, and something in your third chapter reaches into a grief she has been carrying for years and gives it a name and a shape. Maybe she will write to you. More likely, she won’t, and you will never know it happened. But it did happen—although what she experienced was not quite the thing you wrote. It can’t ever be. It can only ever be a wholly new thing, made in the collision between your words and her life, her accumulated losses and unique way of moving through the world. Your sentences and her history produced something neither of you could have made alone. This is the profound, irreversible strangeness of publication, the alchemical magic of it: the work becomes, in some essential sense, as much the reader’s as your own. It’s not taken from you—you still have it—but you’ve also given it away, and in the giving, the work replicates itself into as many versions as there are people who encounter it.

When Robert Frost famously said, “No tears in the writer, no tears in the reader. No surprise in the writer, no surprise in the reader,” he meant that genuine feeling in the making of the work required for genuine feeling in its reception. But the feeling in the reader is their own feeling, shaped by their own life, and cannot and will not be identical to whatever we felt in the making. The poem you wrote about your father will be read by someone who has no father, who will experience it as being about absence itself. The story you set in the landscape of your childhood will be read by someone who has never seen that landscape, who will receive it as being about longing in general. Your meaning will be received, transformed, and sent back into the world changed, and in this way, we have literature doing precisely what literature is for.

Toni Morrison, speaking to the graduating class at Barnard College in 1979, said, “The function of freedom is to free someone else.” She was speaking about human freedom in the largest sense, but the principle applies with elegance, on a smaller and albeit less urgent scale, to the freedom of the writer who gathers the courage to publish honestly and in so doing make space for others to do the same. Such courage entails first making the real thing itself, not the safe version, not the acceptable version, but the real work that requires something of us, and then releasing it to the world—and courage like that does not diminish in the spending. It propagates. Readers who encounter writing that is truly brave are shown that it can be done, that the interior life can be rendered and given away and survived, and they carry that knowledge forward. In that sense, they are more free.

But we should also acknowledge what the process of making the work ready does to the maker—which may be the most underestimated gift of the writing life, and the most transformative. The first draft is written in the heat of having something to say, before you know exactly what that is. Then comes the long, patient, sometimes painful work of revision: reading back what you have written and discovering how far short it falls of your intention; showing it to a trusted reader and hearing, in the places where they went quiet or confused, where you lost the thread; submitting it to a teacher or editor and learning, with some necessary humiliation, that the sentence you were most proud of is precisely the one that doesn’t do the real work. The one that should, to serve the art, be cut. None of this is merely craft development—it’s self-discovery and transformation.

To care about a piece of work enough to revise it—to sit with its failures without abandoning it, to subordinate our attachment to the original version in service of what the work is actually trying to become—is to practice a form of love that changes us, not just as writers but as humans. The discipline of attention that serious revision requires, which amounts to the ability to see clearly, to hold the ideal and the actual simultaneously, to be neither dishonest about the gap between them nor defeated by it, is the discipline of wisdom, learned nowhere more directly than at the desk where the work refuses to be finished. As novelist Michael Crichton observed: “Books aren’t written — they’re rewritten. Including your own. It is one of the hardest things to accept, especially after the seventh rewrite hasn’t quite done it.” He’s describing a reckoning, and writers who genuinely submit to it emerge from it changed—more honest, more supple in the mind, better acquainted with both what they can control and what they cannot.

Acclaimed poet Theodore Roethke wrote, in one of his most celebrated lines, “I learn by going where I have to go.” This is the writing life at its most true. We don’t write in order to release what we already know; we write in order to discover something true—then we revise in order to understand what we have discovered, and publish in order to send that new true thing into the world, where it might find someone who needs it. The process is not linear. Nor is it entirely safe, and to claim that it is would be a disservice. But the process is whole, and each stage—the drafting, the revising, the releasing—is crucial to the transformation of both the work and the writer.

Rilke counseled his young poet to live the questions rather than seek the answers, to trust that he would one day “live into the answers. Something similar applies to any writer who wonders whether the work is good enough, brave enough, finished enough to share. The sharing is part of the process. The release is part of the making. You will not know what the work truly is until it has been read, any more than you will know who you truly are until you have acted in the world and borne the consequences of those actions, for better or worse.

So I offer this as an imperfect clarion call for those at the edge of the decision—who have the manuscript in hand and the address of the agent, or the essay finished and Submittable open, the poem polished and the publish button blaring before them like a threshold they’ve been standing on forever: the fear is real, and the fear is right. It knows something true about what’s at stake: that you are about to be seen, that the interior life you have spent months or years tending and shaping and trying to render faithfully is about to meet the world on the world’s terms, which will not always be yours, which will sometimes be careless or indifferent, but which will also—sometimes, in the way that matters most—be so attuned to what you made that you will feel the rarest thing a writer ever feels, which is the sense of having been genuinely received, of having cast the thing out into the dark and then hearing, from somewhere across the water, an answering voice.

In this light, the nights spent reworking the chapter, the workshop where the story was taken apart, the editorial letter that asked you to go deeper into the very place you most wanted to skim past—these are not obstacles. They are the work itself, and they are, more than we usually realize at the time, no matter how many times we go through this, the making and remaking of ourselves.

Erdrich is right: we are here to risk our hearts. We are here to be swallowed up. And the writer who releases the work and survives the vulnerability of that release —who tastes as many of the apples as she can, who steps into the mouth of the world and discovers she is still breathing—that writer grows in ways the writer hoarding pages in a drawer cannot.

Toni Morrison said that the function of freedom is to free someone else, and this is in a sense what we do when we send our work into the world—because every act of true artistic courage makes the next one slightly less impossible. Each book launched, each essay published, each poem handed to a stranger are all part of a life lived in full ownership of terror, and lived fully nonetheless.

Georgia O’Keeffe spent a lifetime painting her unknown known—the shapes and colors of the world as they appeared specifically and only to her. And she wisely observed that “making your unknown known is the important thing—and keeping the unknown always beyond you.” There is always another threshold, another moment of gathering ourselves before the leap. Fear does not diminish with experience so much as it becomes familiar, and familiarity with fear is its own kind of courage.

So, go ahead. Let the work go.

Love,

Jeannine
