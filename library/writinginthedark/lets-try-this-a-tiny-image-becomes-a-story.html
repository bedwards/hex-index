Meanwhile, for our fifth of six installments of the Infinite Small intensive on micro prose, I want to talk about imagery as the heart of this form.

The writer Sean Glatch in his craft essay1 actually identifies four specific elements needed to make flash work:

  1. Concise Word Choice

  2. Imagery

  3. Theme

  4. Experimentation and Hybridity

What good flash is not driven by is explanation.

And, to my mind, image must provide the foundation (usually) needed for two of the other three components Glatch names—theme and experimentation/hybridity—to work their hardest. As for concise word choice, that’s inherent in this form! Doesn’t mean it’s easy or that it is just a matter of adhering to a lower word count, though. Concise word choice means weighing and measuring and considering each and every word and being willing and able to cut it or swap it as necessary to amplify the power of the micro prose.

All this said, I can readily acknowledge that for me, the best flash lives and breathes through image. In such a small space, the writer leans on sensory detail the way a body leans toward warmth: instinctively, urgently. An image can hold what there isn’t room to say outright—memory, metaphor, ache, astonishment.

And imagery isn’t only what we see. It includes the salt sting of language on the tongue, the shift of gravity when a feeling moves through the chest, the ghost of a smell that calls back a whole life. Motion counts. Temperature counts. The pulse beneath the surface counts.

The best flash leaves something lingering—an aftertaste, a shiver, a flicker in the mind’s dark. Long after the last sentence, the reader isn’t just remembering the piece. They’re carrying it.

So, the piece we’ll look at today, a wonderful, strange micro by Andrew Bertina,2 runs with the idea of image, from a meta perspective—the whole writing about writing within a piece of writing—which is fun in flash, too, because it hits the experimentation/hybridity mark.

I’m excited to take a closer look at this interesting and emotive piece—which is new to me. I love getting right up to the words, getting all the way inside of the choices the writer made on the page while building a piece of writing, so that I can better understand the tools available to me to create similar effects.

Which, by the way, is in part what I did yesterday in my short essay, “I don’t usually sob when I hear ‘Old Man’ by Neil Young, but” which starts with song as an image and spins out from there. I wrote it after studying a flash piece that took a somewhat similar approach.

It’s good for us to remember, as writers, that what Natalie Goldberg said in Writing Down the Bones is true: we learn to write by looking closely—very closely!—at the way other writers build their work, the words they use, the order they arrange them in, the images they create, the rhythms and rhymes of the piece, its order and structure, etc. None of these things belong to anyone. They’re all elements of writing that can be recognized, understood, and applied in our own work to help us create the effects we’re after and provide readers the experience we wish for them to have. Which, by and large, for me, is an emotional experience in which they recognize some resonant truth of being alive, of living in a human body, of wandering the world in the context of a finite life span in which we suffer, thrive, grow, despair, celebrate, overcome, fight, reunite, and hopefully, somewhere in all of that, discover something urgent about ourselves, others, and the world.

Bertina opens the piece with a professor sharing with his writing class an image of cranes—but as Sean Glatch says about the piece:

You can tell this piece was written by a poet, and not just because it references the poetic craft in the first line. Each image offered in this gorgeous prose cuts closer and closer to the professor’s emotional core, painting a kind of portrait-by-proxy, painted through periphery.

There’s an idea in the craft of poetry called the initiating and generated subject. Essentially, the idea that gets you into the poem is not where the poem ends, nor is it what the poem is really “about.” Successful poetry discovers something and takes a leap.

I feel this is happening here. The professor telling the class about cranes and imagery is just a doorway into what this piece discovers about childhood, nostalgia, memory, change.

And he does all that in a mere 318 words!

So, with that said, let’s take a closer look at this piece by Andrew Bertina and see what we can learn from it—then try to apply it ourselves, in our own work, in this week’s writing exercise!
