Happy third birthday to *Writing in the Dark*!

What a strange and lovely thing it is to grow older: to keep waking up inside a life we’re still making, word by word, breath by breath, sometimes bewildered, sometimes brave. And the things we make grow older, too—like *Writing in the Dark*, which turned three yesterday.

I would have posted about it yesterday, too, but I was at the hospital with Jon for 15 hours for his sinus surgery, which was rough but went well. We are tentatively hopeful for better breathing (and overall health!) ahead. Thank you for your many well wishes. I haven’t had time to respond individually, but your words mean the world to me.

In fact, I’m thinking, as *Writing in the Dark* turns three, about how this community has become one of the truest gifts in my life—thousands of creative hearts gathered around the same strange flame, keeping each other warm.

To celebrate another turn around the sun for this community, I’m offering a [birthday sale of 15% off new annual subscriptions](https://writinginthedark.substack.com/05641095). A small invitation, a quiet nudge, a lit match: if you’ve been hovering at the edge of this circle, maybe this is your moment to step closer. I would love to write into the year ahead with you. Especially since we’re kicking off January with a reprise of the incredible [Essay in 12 Steps](https://writinginthedark.substack.com/t/essay-in-12-steps) intensive. I can’t wait.

Meanwhile, back to Jon’s surgery. As I said, it went overall well, though we will not know for several months if addressing the chronic sinusitis will in turn clear up the adult onset asthma. The surgeon, when he consulted with me afterward, before Jon was fully out of anesthesia, said he is cautiously optimistic but also wants to manage our expectations.

The human body is such a precarious marvel. One minute it’s carrying us up the stairs or across the kitchen floor, all Steady Eddy like; the next it reminds us without permission how breakable we are. A vertebra slips, a heart stutters, a tendon forgets its job, a lung refuses its only task. We live inside this delicate machinery, aware on some subterranean level that it can falter at any moment. And yet, there’s a sweetness in that frailty too: the way someone cups a hand beneath your elbow when you stand, or how we notice the breath of a person we love just to be sure it’s still there. Our vulnerability may scare us, but it also softens us. It invites tenderness into the room.

And isn’t this exactly what art asks of us? To take the uncertain, sometimes bruised materials of our lives and make something luminous from them? I use that word a lot—luminous—because there is no better word to express what I am trying to say. There is light in this work. This work is lit from within. It has to be, because there is no other way to shape language—or paint, or clay—into a beauty that is often born of pain, though not defined by it.

We begin this work with blankness, yes: an empty page, an untouched canvas, a lump of earth. But life is never just one beginning. Life is a series of beginnings as we start over again and again and again. We write over our own drafts. We paint over old colors and shapes, even ones we once loved. We smash the clay and fold it back into itself, or we press more clay into the cracks to strengthen whatever form is emerging or threatening to come apart. Slowly, we sculpt our days into a kind of topographical map of our own unfolding. Valleys and ridges, fault lines and unexpected clearings. Fragile, yes. Imperfect, always. But also stubbornly beautiful.

Sitting in the hospital all day while Jon underwent his surgery—waiting in that family lounge with the other anxious, hopeful people whose loved ones were also somewhere behind closed surgical doors—I found myself thinking of a much smaller version of me. I was either four or six years old; I had two early childhood surgeries—a tonsillectomy and an exploratory lymph node surgery—and I no longer know which one this was, only that I stayed overnight alone.

What I remember is climbing out of the tall hospital bed, slipping past the humming machines, and wandering the hallway in search of a popsicle, tiny and determined and unsupervised. A nurse found me and walked me back to my room. I expected to get in trouble, but I didn’t. In fact, I think I got my longed-for popsicle, but I can’t be sure anymore. That might be simply a story I’ve told myself. Either way, the memory made me suddenly, unexpectedly sad—the loneliness of that child in the hospital on her own. But, it also filled me with a startled joy, a kind of astonished pride in that brave little girl who is still, in some essential way, me.

And I thought of our grandson Z, who, at five, is even braver than I was as a child—in fact, seemingly fearless. And I thought of how he is now, at last, softening enough to occasionally whisper to me that something scared him—falling backward off a kitchen stool, or slipping on a stair—and how he lets me hold him afterward. I love this boy’s bravery; it’s stitched into him. But I also want him to know he doesn’t have to carry that bravery every minute of every day. That he can feel whatever he feels, even if that’s fear. And that he can falter, fall, and even fail. I won’t say, “and rise again,” because not everything has to be a redemption story. So I’ll say instead that he can fail and survive failure. He can try again, or try something else. Well, maybe that is, actually, all it really means to rise again. I’m not sure I know. But I *do* know that all of this is Z’s human birthright. And mine. And yours.

And we exercise that birthright every single time we make art.

That’s why I’m here. I hope it’s why you’re here, too. Thank you for exercising your birthright with me, and allowing me to exercise mine with you.
