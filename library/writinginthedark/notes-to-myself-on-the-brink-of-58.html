Notes to Myself on the Brink of 58

1. Writing saved your life, so you teach it like it might save others. You’ve said this so many times it’s become true the way a prayer becomes true: by repetition and faith and by the fact that you keep testing it and it keeps holding.

2. The book that took your whole life to write came out at the height of the pandemic—Joyce Carol Oates called it simply beautiful, Dorothy Allison said she loved it and was grateful it was in the world, you spent a million hours on Zoom talking about it, and, in a weird way, the that lonely pandemic launch was a perfect antidote to the loneliness you wrote in those pages, because it was the first time you’ve ever felt so loved in isolation.

3. You’ve been afraid a lot in the last year. You have also made a book and launched a new writing community and hosted a giant camp and been invited to teach in France, twice, and attended your son’s wedding, and become a grandmother again. Fear and joy do not take turns. They sit down together and eat everything on the table.

4. You finally signed with a literary agent this year and sold a book proposal to Random House. Even under the heavy weight of the work itself now, the book is still standing. So are you. Neither of you is taking this for granted.

5. You lost your ability to read—or, at least, finish books. Since the second Trump onslaught and all it unleashed, including job loss and insecurity combined with Jon’s health problems and the ICE attack on Minneapolis, just for starters, anxiety became a signal-blocking static. You knew this was happening and you couldn’t stop it. You were too tired to try. Then your friend Tyler told you something simple and powerful: get a Kindle. The larger print, the light weight in your tired hands, the way you didn’t have to hold the spine open at 11 p.m. while Jon slept fitfully beside you struggling to breathe easier after his third procedure in as many months. Now, you finish books again and cry about this privately.

5. The creative life is often what you notice while waiting to make something. You sat in three emergency rooms and two surgical waiting rooms this year. You were afraid and exhausted and you wanted, in the most primal and specific way, to sleep. To see the person you love not be in pain and not be a mystery to the doctors trying to help him. But you also noticed, in those ERs and waiting rooms, so many people, so many snippets of conversation and so many tears, you noticed anger and fear and love and hunger, you noticed all those earnest caregivers working together and apart, you noticed so many stories unfolding in the harsh fluorescent light of hurting and healing, and you are grateful for the gift of attention, even when it’s painful.

7. In all your fifty-seven years, you had never stayed in a friend’s casita, but you did it twice this year, and are now wondering if maybe you can be a casita person. The delight of being someone’s guest—the towels they chose for you, the coffee on the counter, the fact that someone made a space and said this is for you—is a form of love you had not let yourself receive. You are learning, on the brink of 58, to receive.

8. You stood in the vortexes of Sedona, covered in red dust, and felt it, whatever it is. You keep looking at the quartz dish you bought there, and the Joshua Tree pottery pieces you brought home. Even Billie said, about the large, cylindrical clay vase, “This is really pretty, Mom.” And the Leslie Dill piece above the fireplace, from Valentine’s Day last year, which you don’t really celebrate, except last year, when Jon did. You are building the interior life of your home the way you build the interior life of a sentence: one precisely chosen object at a time.

9. You highlighted your hair one too many times. You knew this as you were doing it and did it anyway, because sometimes being creative means being stubborn, and sometimes being stubborn means lots of hair masks.

10. After decades of being a body in the world—a body that’s been looked at and misread and celebrated and diminished and survived and celebrated again—you found jeans that fit halfway decently. The body wants to be comfortable in its container. You are its container.

11. You built, with Gina Frangello and Emily Rapp Black, a creative writing community called CRAFT SCHOOL. It started in 2021 when you reached out to Gina, a stranger, because you loved her book. You said so out loud, and now here you are, building something together: three women in their fifties who write and teach and believe in the transformative power of language. The creative life requires this, the willingness to say, I love your work, let’s make something. The willingness to believe that the right people will say yes.

12. You are in awe of Writing in the Dark, your home of the heart, where we gather around language the way we used to gather around fires: for warmth and for story and for sitting shoulder to shoulder in the night as the sparks fly, knowing that we are the sparks.

13. You brought forty writers to Camp Wandawega, a historic camp in the middle of the country, to write together in person. One of them flew in from England. One was folding the experience into a cross-country RV journey. They mostly already knew each other, at least a little, from Writing in the Dark, from the exercises and from the comments section of your posts. They arrived as strangers who were not strangers. This is what community looks like when it’s real.

14. Your son got married. You stood at his wedding and watched a child you made become someone’s partner, someone’s person. The body that made him is the same body that writes. Now, he and Kaela are expecting their own baby. The making begins again.

15. Billie is in grad school for speech pathology, its own kind of language work, and you’ve been there for them in your imperfect best attempts. The imperfect best attempt is underrated as a form of love. It means you showed up. It means you tried. It means you love someone more than you fear not being enough.

16. You welcomed two new grandchildren this year and are now a grandmother of seven with another coming and a manuscript at Random House and plenty of Retin-A and, finally, jeans that fit. Go figure.

17. You learned to make veggie curry from a turkey curry recipe, which required imagination and substitution and the willingness to both follow instructions and ignore them—which is, it turns out, also how you write.

18. You can eat authentic sourdough now, as long as the bread is genuinely fermented, and you have learned the difference. The creative life, too, is about knowing the difference: between what nourishes and what only looks like nourishment. Between the real thing and the simulation of the real thing. The creative life is about recognizing the slow-rise of naturally leavened truth.

19. You’ve watched more good television this year than ever, despite having less time than ever, because when you are the kind of tired that needs a story delivered horizontally, in a dark room, by actors who do the work so you can just receive. The creative life includes receiving, being a listener, being a viewer, being an audience. It includes the radical act of watching something beautiful and not producing anything in return.

20. You are late on your own doctor’s appointments. This is the sentence in the list that nobody wants to include but that has to be here, because you teach that the true thing, the hard thing, transforms the work. But remember, your body is not a tool for making things. It is the thing itself. Make the appointments.

21. You have stood in the red vortexes of Sedona and the alien stillness of Joshua Tree and the honest wildness of the Boundary Waters, and soon you will stand somewhere in France and you will teach writing to people who have come from wherever they came from to be in a room with you, which means they have come to be changed, which means they believe that change is possible through art, which means everyone in that room—you included—still believes that the world can be made new by the careful work of attention and language.

22. Toni Morrison said: we do language. That may be the measure of our lives. You have built an entire philosophy, an entire community, an entire creative life, around this. You found her words and made them your tagline—for people who do language—because the best ideas belong to everyone who needs them. You needed them. Now eighteen thousand people need them. The creative life multiplies.

23. The E.L. Doctorow quote you love best is, Writing is like driving at night in the fog. You can only see as far as your headlights, but you can make the whole trip that way. You have been making the whole trip this way. You have been driving in the dark for fifty-seven years with your headlights on, toward a destination you can’t fully see, writing everything down.

24. In two days you will be fifty-eight. The wrinkles, in the right light— especially with your husband behind the camera—sometimes appear to sparkle. This is what he sees, so this is what the camera finds. Life works that way.

25. The creative life is not its own special room—it's the way you move through every room: the emergency waiting room and the surgical hallway and the Sedona mesa and the casita and the waterpark with the grandchildren and the French classroom and the Wisconsin camp at midnight when the writers are still awake, still making things, still sitting in the circle under the stars, feeling things they haven't felt before and craving more. This is what you’ve been making. This is what’s been making you.

Keep writing in the dark. We’ll see you there.

Love,

Jeannine
