The literary world of Minneapolis-St. Paul is large enough to sustain a genuine community of writers, editors, publishers, and readers, and small enough that nearly everyone is connected to nearly everyone else by one or two degrees of separation.

Laurie Hertzel, longtime books editor at the Minneapolis Star Tribune, occupies a kind of central node in that web. She is the person who, back in 2021 (thanks to my friend and wise advisor, ), assigned the review of my memoir The Part That Burns to Marion Winik, who compared it to Lidia Yuknavitch’s The Chronology of Water, thereby earning herself a permanent place in my heart. I have never met Laurie in person, and yet I certainly feel connected to her—as a writer, as someone who has spent her career championing books and the people who make them, and as a fellow Duluth girl.

That last connection matters more than it might seem. Duluth is not a place that loosens its grip on you. The cold, the Lake, the hills, the harshness of those winters—these things get into your body and stay there. My own memoir is not set primarily in Duluth, but the city shaped the narrator who lived through the events I wrote about, and the geography of the North Shore runs beneath my book like bedrock. When I read Laurie’s new memoir, Ghosts of Fourth Street, set in Duluth where she lived with her parents and nine siblings, I recognized that same bedrock—the way those hills, that neighborhood creek and ravine (hers was on the East Side, mine in the West End), can become the whole world to a child, and go on being the whole world in memory long after you’ve left.

Ghosts of Fourth Street centers on a family tragedy: the drowning of Laurie’s brother Bobby when Laurie was nine years old, and the long silence her family drew around his death like a curtain. But the book is not simply an excavation of grief. It is about, as Laurie says, “stories, legend, family, books, a certain time and place.” It is also a meditation on the way stories are kept and lost within families, on what it means to remember, and on what a writer owes to the living and the dead when she decides to put those memories on the page.

These are questions every memoirist eventually has to answer for herself, and the answers are rarely simple. Place shapes us. Structure shapes meaning — where we begin a story, where we end it, and how much space we give to an event that haunts everything else: all of these decisions carry weight.

And then there is truth—perhaps the most contested word in the entire conversation around memoir—and how it turns out to mean different things to different writers. Some, like Laurie, trained as journalists, hold hard to the factual record. Others work from what Pam Houston has called emotional truth, allowing themselves more latitude with detail and dialogue in service of what they understand to be the deeper shape of what happened. Neither approach is without integrity, but what every approach requires, in my view, is transparency. Readers deserve to know the code of ethics by which the writer has navigated her material—whether that comes in an author’s note, a preface, or woven into the narrative itself. I combined characters in my own memoir and said so plainly. Laurie changed the names of living siblings and said so plainly. The specific choices we make are our own. The obligation to be honest with readers about those choices is not.

In the conversation that follows, Laurie and I talk about all of this—the hold that Duluth has on both of us, the structural decisions that shaped her book, the family opposition she faced, what finally moved her past it, and the question of truth in memoir that writers in this form never quite stop wrestling with. I hope it opens up some of those same questions for you, wherever you are in your own work.

Your story is set in Duluth, Minnesota where you grew up. I also grew up in Duluth and although The Part That Burns is not set there, the geography of Duluth and the North Shore do play a significant role in my memoir in terms of how this geography shaped the narrator. How do you feel the Duluth setting played into the shaping of your story, and the shaping of the lives inside your story, including your own?

My story takes place mostly in the years 1965-1966. I was eight, then nine, years old. The neighborhood where we lived—the East End of Duluth—was my whole world. My family never traveled; there were too many of us (ten children). We never went out to dinner, we never went anywhere. But to me, that neighborhood was enough. It was roughly bordered by Holy Rosary Cathedral, Old Main, and Endion Elementary, and I knew every inch of it, crossed neighbors’ backyards without a thought, stole apples, climbed down hills into ravines and hopped from rock to rock along creeks.

There are scenes in the book where my father walked most of this route (except for the rock-hopping) and I secretly followed him; later in the book, I walk it myself. We had to spend at least an hour a day outside, even in winter, and so in the book and in my bones runs the chill of those intensely cold winter days, the deep snow, the quiet, sleeping, glittering world. I have visited Duluth many times since moving away, but in my mind it is always that triangle of neighborhood, and I am always wandering along a creek.

Ghosts of Fourth Street explores your family’s tragic experience of your brother Bobby’s drowning—an experience they met with silence and avoidance that continued far into the future. How has your family responded to the publication of your memoir?

I have a big family—though my parents and two siblings have died, I still have seven other siblings, and they have not all responded in the same way. Some of my siblings made it very clear before the book was published that they disapproved; other than one or two nasty emails they have mostly remained silent and I don’t know if they read the book. (Although they reviewed it on Amazon. One star.) Other siblings like it but find that my memories and theirs do not always mesh.

The next generation—my nieces and nephews—have responded quite positively; I think they didn’t know a lot of these stories and one of them wrote and told me that he had never been clear on the circumstances of my brother’s death and he appreciated knowing.

You’ve said in other interviews that years ago, you published a story about your brother in a small literary journal, thinking no one would see it (as we often think!), but, instead, someone actually shared a copy with your mother, who never spoke to you again, and disinherited you from her will. That’s such an extreme reaction. And yet, this story kept gnawing at you to write it, and I read that you even had a manuscript stuck in the proverbial drawer for years. How did you finally, after all of the pressure to stay silent, allow yourself to write it?

The writing of it happened over the course of many years, and for a big part of that time I wasn’t even aware that I was writing a book; I was just writing memories. It started coming together as a book when I was working on my MFA at Queens University in Charlotte, N.C., about 15 or more years ago. But yes, I then put it in a (proverbial) drawer (actually a folder on my laptop) and stopped thinking about it. I knew that the University of Minnesota Press, which ultimately published the book, was interested in it, but I didn’t want to hurt my mother by having her read about her oldest son’s death.

Even after she was shown my essay (which deals with the death) I didn’t think about publishing the book. There was so much opposition that I felt that publishing it wasn’t an option. But as time went on and my mother continued to refuse to speak to me, and some of my siblings did the same, I started thinking, What am I waiting for? Why am I protecting the feelings of these people who are so unconcerned about what I’m doing but only want to condemn me?

So in a way, their strong disapproval led to my publishing the book after all. I approached the U of M Press and asked if they were still interested; they were. This was a month or two before my mother died, but because I had such restricted access to my mother I had no idea she was near death. She died before I signed the contract, so she never knew that it was going to be a published book.

Did you end up reshaping and revising the manuscript as it was, or did you significantly change your approach?

I am a constant editor /revisor, so yes, I did a lot of that. Even when I was reading page proofs, I’d realize oh, no, that is the wrong word! It must be changed! And it always felt like once I made that one change of that one word the book would be so much better!

I did not reshape or revise the book to appease my family and my mother, though I did, in the end, make a number of compromises. The biggest compromise was I changed the names of all living siblings. Not all of them asked for that, but some did, and I figured if I was changing some it would be weird not to change them all, so I did. I also didn’t use photographs in the book, because I knew it would upset some of my siblings, even though the photos were, for the most part, more than 50 years old. And it’s true that when I gave the U of Mn publicist a group photo my family taken in 1959 to use in promotions, one sibling immediately wrote and said he wanted me to take the picture off the web. So I did.

Those compromises were not hard to make; I wanted to respect the feelings of my siblings as much as I could without harming the book.

In Ghosts of Fourth Street, you tell us in the first line of the first chapter you do announce Bobby’s death, saying, “We never called our brother by his real name until after he was dead.” From there, you follow a mostly chronological structure from the narrator’s earliest years through Bobby’s accident, which takes place in chapter 12, around page 130 of 150. Can you say more about this structural choice? In some ways it seems like the book centers itself on Bobby’s death, but, on the other hand, relatively little space is allotted to it.

The book was never intended to be a mystery, or his death a surprise; I wanted the narrative to lead in that direction but to be about other things: stories, legend, family, books, a certain time and place. I wanted the reader to have that echo of his impending death in their head as they read, so that the events that came before were a little more resonant. The death of my mother’s brother in the war, for instance. Or the litany of all the ways members of my father’s family had died—“everybody dies, but Hertzels die especially well.” Or my father’s harsh actions toward Bobby—I wanted the reader to think, oh, no, Guv, you’re going to regret this. Which he did.

You come from a journalism background, where facts are paramount, and you’ve said before that when it comes to memoir, you don’t make things up. You told Publishers Weekly that there are very few quotes in Ghosts of Fourth Street, “because it took place 60 years ago. There are a few things that were said that I do remember verbatim, and they’re in there, but I don’t make up dialogue.” You warned, too, that when we start making things up in memoir, it can easily slip into fiction, because it’s no longer true, so you committed to “change no detail, no action, no conversation.” But some memoirists define truth differently, focusing on the larger shapes of the story and the central elements of its emotional core. For example, when I teach, I tell my students who worry about not being able to remember enough to bring their memoirs to life that if a certain detail does not change the central truths of the story—say, the color of a sweater of what food was served in a particular meal—I have no problem with making that up as long as the embellishment doesn’t hurt anyone. And Pam Houston, on the far end of the continuum, addresses truth vs. fact in memoir in her essay “Corn Maze,” examining what “truth” really means in memoir, embracing a much blurrier line than you, or even I, ascribe to. Since Writing in the Dark is a community of writers, many of whom wrestle with their own challenges around the ethics of memoir, I’d love to hear what you tell your own students about this issue. Do you advise them to draw a hard line, or to determine their own transparent code of ethics when it comes to writing about real events?

I do advise the hard line because I am a journalist, even as I am aware that not everyone adheres to it (including at least one of my professors at Queens). You’ll notice that there are few descriptions of clothing (other than my Mary Jane shoes and my coat with the fuzzy white collar, which I remember so well) or specific actions (nothing like, “Guv sighed and rolled his eyes”) because I cannot remember those tiny details in the precise moment. I think that one can write a powerful memoir without resorting to fictionalizing. That said, I have read memoir where the writer makes it clear they don’t remember every detail but are surmising, and I think as long as the writer is transparent that is fine. (“I might have been wearing my coat with the collar, because I wore it all the time…”) That approach would not have worked in this book because I was trying to keep to the nine-year-old child’s point of view, not the adult narrator looking back over 50 years.

I don’t ascribe to Pam Houston’s philosophy; her books are popular and sell well, but that’s not how I judge things. When I was in grad school there was a lot of debate about truth vs. emotional truth—I said then, and I still say, that memoir cannot be fact-checked the way autobiography can, but that the facts still matter. It comes down to intent: if I got something wrong in the book (and I did, a few things) it was because my memory failed me and I was writing what I believed to be true or what I had been told was true. Deliberately making something up remains, in my judgment, wrong. It turns nonfiction into fiction.

In addition to your father being an accomplished writer and English professor, reading and writing seemed to permeate the rest of your large family, as well—and you included a line of Bobby’s poetry in the book:

it’s dark in here but it’s darker outside. and I listen for the raining night.

It’s quite beautiful. Do you have more of your brother’s poetry or other writing in your possession?

Bobby’s poetry was one reason I wanted the book to be published; I wanted his poems to be remembered. In my original drafts, I did not just quote his poems, I ran them in full. But I ran into a copyright problem—I don’t have the rights to these poems, because my mother disinherited me. (The rights went to her heirs.) And I knew that some of my siblings would forbid me from using them.

But yes, I have a copy of his book, ‘Nineteen Poems and a Fable,’ which my father had published after Bobby’s death.

After wanting to write this story for so long, how does it feel to finally have it in the world?

It feels a little scary, because it is so personal. My fear was that people would think my father was a horrible person, but he was not—he was cruel to Bobby, but I hoped I made him a nuanced enough character that people could see the good sides to him as well. I have been surprised and really pleased that so many people like the book and find their own childhoods in it even if they didn’t grow up in Duluth, or in a big family, or in a family that experienced tragedy. That is the miracle of books! That connection. And it absolutely amazes and thrills me that my book connects for some.

What’s next for you?

Both of my memoirs—Ghosts as well as my earlier book, News to Me—sprang from stories I told on a blog that I started as a way to make myself write every day. I ended the blog a long, long time ago but after Ghosts was published it occurred to me that I need to write every day again, and so I have started a Substack. It’s mainly (so far) about my difficult and deeply loved dog, Angus, who I used to write about in a column for the Star Tribune. So at the moment, I am thinking that Angus might become my next book. But who knows? I never planned News to Me or Ghosts. They just happened accidentally. So maybe something else will emerge.

Several people have told me that after finishing Ghosts they wanted to know more about my family and asked if there would be a sequel. There will not be. That story is complete.
