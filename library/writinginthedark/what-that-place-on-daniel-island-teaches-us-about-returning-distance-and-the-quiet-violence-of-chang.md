What makes *That Place on Daniel Island* astonishing is the way the story trusts restraint.

And by the way, in workshop last night, a writer asked for the distinction between constraint and restraint, and I know that whenever one person wonders something, it means that many others do, as well, so let me pause here and define the two terms—define them in the context of their use in literary writing, that is. Their meanings beyond the confines of the literary are more elastic.

But in literary terms …

**Constraint** is a broad concept that refers to writing techniques that involve rules and limitations. These rules and limitations might relate to form (we do a lot of that here at Writing in the Dark in our exercises!), and the limitations can range in strictness level. Some would say that any kind of prompt or exercise is inherently a constraint, but more limiting constraints can, for example, limit you to the use of 5-letter words, or forbid the use of a specific letter, or require you to write in second person, or suggest that you include a subtle rhyme pattern, etc. The reason we use constraints in creative writing (and all other art forms) is because constraints boost our creativity. There’s a ton of hard science on that. If you want a deep dive on how and why I became a constraint devotee, my essay, “**[The One (and Only) Technique that Finally Helped Me Write About Trauma](https://writinginthedark.substack.com/p/the-one-and-only-technique-that-finally)**” tells the whole story, and unpacks a good bit of the science.

**Restraint,** on the other hand, is, as defined by Google, “… the deliberate choice to use fewer, more precise words and controlled expression to create deeper impact, allowing readers to infer meaning rather than being explicitly told, often through subtle actions, sparse detail, or charged silence, embodying the “less is more” principle to build tension and reader engagement. It’s about withholding excessive description or emotional exposition, letting subtext and imagery carry the weight, and trusting the reader to fill in the gaps for a more resonant experience.” In other words, when you write with restraint, you rely much more heavily on showing over telling, and you rely on your skill with concrete specific details, subtext, and implication to carry the weight of meaning.

Ultimately, then, a literary constraint is something that the writer responds to, whereas literary restraint is something the writer exercises on the page.

Back to “That Place on Daniel Island,” and the use of restraint. In just one thousand words, F. R. Martinez gives us a reunion—one of those encounters shaped as much by *what isn’t said* as by what is. To note: I don’t want to talk about the nature, context, and specifics of the reunion, because this is where I have found that some writers sometimes struggle (that is, to know exactly waht is going on in this experimental piece). Therefore, as part of our close read, I’d love for you to tell me what you infer from the piece, which we can then talk about.

Meanwhile, regardless of the specifics, this piece does revolve around separation and reunion, and flash offers a natural container for several reasons. First, the brevity mirrors the emotional reality of distance. The story is small, but the life around it is not. We feel the years that happened off-page.

As for restraint, one of the first things a reader notices is how little explanation the story offers (which is, of course, why sometimes readers miss the big shapes, but that’s a risk we take when we use restraint—and that would merit an entire essay in and of itself). In Daniel Island, there is no stage-setting monologue about the past, no tidy summary of what happened between these characters. Instead, tension appears through tiny fractures: a shift in tone, dialogue that feels misaligned, one person seeing the world with familiarity while the other sees it with estrangement. Flash excels at this because flash relies on *precision instead of accumulation*. Every sentence is a pressure point. Every small detail pulls more weight than in a longer form.

The setting—Daniel Island—functions as more than backdrop. It becomes the hinge between past and present. This is a key flash technique: *place as emotional proxy.* Rather than telling us how characters feel, Martinez lets the environment change—or fail to change—and the emotional meaning emerges through contrast. The place remembers. The people don’t quite.

There is a moment in all strong micro-flash where something shifts—not loudly, but indelibly. In this story, that turn is quiet, almost invisible, yet unmistakable. The reunion proves less about rekindling than about witnessing how time has unspooled them in separate directions. A flash piece doesn’t resolve this feeling. Instead, it leaves resonance—an open-ended echo that stretches beyond the closing line.

Flash is often misunderstood as a compressed short story, but stories like this reveal a different truth: flash is sometimes a *charged fragment*—a shard of something larger. Its power lies in the invitation it extends to the reader: **f***inish this story in your own body.* In the silences between words. In the ache of what was once known and is now irretrievable.

Martinez reminds us that in flash, the unsaid can speak louder than the spoken—and that sometimes the smallest stories hold the longest shadows.
