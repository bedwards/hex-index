So, over the weekend, my agent asked for a new author photo. The one we used in the proposal was really just a selfie, nothing I’d want to use in a more public facing situation, and, not high-res enough, anyway. Another one I have, from CAMP this summer—a rare candid that I love, and taken by the extremely talented , so, a professional shot, definitely high-res enough—is very casual and full torso, so, not quite right for the context. (No, its not a book jacket photo! Not quite yet.) The only other professional headshot I have that I like is also full torso, and quite beautifully edited. So beautifully edited that I look not quite like myself, so, also not quite right.

This is how a reasonable request, simple enough on the surface, immediately brought up a shadow of not enough. So I closed the email and sat still, feeling an old heat rise, and, at the same time, wondering, Why does this still matter? Why do I still care? And yet: I did.

Before school picture day, before yearbooks, before boys with passing comments that swung like blades—I already understood that a photograph was more than a picture—it was a freeze-frame of how others would see me.

I was a shy child with short, uneven, slightly boyish haircuts, perpetually growing out and never quite arriving. In photos from those years, I often look a little startled.

Then adolescence sharpened everything.

Farrah Fawcett winged hair ruled the hallways—soft, feathered, shining like something from another planet. I tried. I curled and brushed and sprayed. I coaxed and willed. But my very fine hair remained what we called, back then, “flat.”

That same era brought braces—not once, but twice. The orthodontist removed two teeth on the right side and none on the left, and my smile has lived slightly off-kilter ever since. Just uneven enough that I learned to edit my own expression—to smile with come combination of choreography and caution.

Around that time—when I was fourteen and hovering just above the cliff-edge where childhood collapses into something harder—a boy I knew, a boy who was a real friend, told me, almost gently: It’s good you have such a great laugh, since you’re not very pretty.

It wasn’t meant to be mocking or cruel. Simply observational, like noting the weather.

Another time, years later, when I was twenty-one and newly married, the husband of a friend told her he was shocked how beautiful I looked at my wedding—because in normal life, he said, I wasn’t very pretty at all.

These aren’t tragedies. They are just small stones. But enough stones make a wall. And walls stay with you.

Long after the people who stacked the stones are gone.

In middle school, the yearbooks became a marketplace of faces. Kids—laughing, bored, ruthless—spread their arms across cafeteria tables to rate each person’s photograph. Whispers, giggles, numbers: seven, four, eight, maybe a nine if she wore eyeliner.

I remember pretending disinterest, resting my chin in my hand like someone with bigger things to think about, while inside me something hovered and clawed: Please don’t say it. Please don’t confirm what I already fear.

That’s the thing about early judgments: they don’t stay in the past. They become architecture. Load-bearing.

But the request from my agent was simple and ordinary. Hmm. Nothing I had seemed to fit the bill. Where is the middle ground between exposure and erasure? Between truth and presentation? Between seen and safe?

Susan Sontag once wrote: “Photography is mainly a social rite, a defense against anxiety, and a tool of power.”1

A tool of power—that’s so interesting. Because yes, one body stands on one side of the lens. Another stands on the other. And not everyone wields the same kind of seeing.

Ansel Adams once said, “There are always two people in every picture: the photographer and the viewer.”

But I think there are three: The photographer, the viewer, and the self — the one inside the image—trying to reconcile them.

And then there is the image of the self, and how it accompanies us. John Berger, in his classic Ways of Seeing, wrote, “A woman must continually watch herself.”2

Even when she wishes not to. Even when it exhausts her.

In the end, I asked my son Max to take my picture with a real camera, since he has one, and since I generally love his photos of our family gatherings.

He is an architect, not a photographer—but maybe architecture and portraiture overlap more than we think. Both require attention to light, angle, space. Both attempt to hold a living thing still without killing it.

Max was unusually patient. We moved through rooms of the house, following light like migrating animals. If a spot wasn’t quite right, he’d adjust something— remove a knick-knack, climb on a stool, etc.

His direction was clear and helpful:

Slightly right. No—less. That’s it! Okay, now smile. A real smile, like, pretend something is actually funny. Good, now serious. You know, no-one-fucks-with-me face.

He took dozens in a row—fast, intuitive, unceremonious.

Then he sat with me for at least an hour, going over the haul until I could agree on a handful that were possibly usable, and watching as he edited for light and tone, and, in one, a very distracting crease from the way I was craning my neck.

Here are my favorites, in color and black and white. I feel like Max did a terrific job because to my eye, I look like myself, as seen through a loving lens. Like myself, as seen in good light. Like myself, the way I picture myself, which is not at all how every photograph turns out!

Ultimately, I prefer the serious one in black and white, but my agent likes smiling photos in general, and prefers the one in color—which I like enough to live with, too, so that’s the one we’ll use for this thing (which might or might not even happen; good thing I teach everyone to befriend uncertainty! ).

And here’s where I’ll admit here that sometimes—often—I feel foolish caring even about all this.

I mean, I have lived profound loss. I have lived life-saving art. I have lived heartbreak and astonishment, grief and resilience. And yet a single camera lens can still summon the eight-year-old me, the fourteen-year-old, the twenty-one-year-old, the versions still hoping to be seen kindly.

Maybe that isn’t vanity. Maybe it’s longing. A longing to be known without being measured. To be visible without being assessed.

Maybe to have a photograph taken is to ask: Can I exist as I am? And will you still choose me if I do?

Love,

Jeannine
