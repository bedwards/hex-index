### **Consumer Reports found lead in protein powders. How bad is it?**

Last week, Consumer Reports released an [analysis](https://www.consumerreports.org/lead/protein-powders-and-shakes-contain-high-levels-of-lead-a4206364640/) revealing elevated lead levels in several popular protein powders and shakes. Google searches for “lead in protein powder” spiked 300%, and influencers lit up social media. Depending on which news source you read, it was either a five-alarm fire or no big deal.

***So what’s actually going on?*** Lead is everywhere—soil, food, water, and air. Thankfully, overall exposure has [dropped dramatically](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30507765/) since the 1970s, and modern lab tests can now detect vanishingly small amounts (down to parts per billion). But detection does not necessarily equal danger.

***How bad is bad?*** That’s where things get tricky because not everyone agrees:

-   **California limit:** 0.5 mcg/day. This number comes from a very conservative calculation: regulators [took](https://oehha.ca.gov/proposition-65/chemicals/lead) the “no observable effect” level for reproductive harm for inhaled lead exposure in workplaces and divided it by 1,000. Many experts argue that this threshold is unrealistic. It’s also not linked to adverse health outcomes.
    
-   **FDA’s limit:** 2.2 mcg/day for kids, 8.8 mcg/day during pregnancy, and 12.5 mcg/day for other [adults](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0273230020300052) based on blood lead levels, [toxicology data](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0273230022000897?via%3Dihub), and a built-in ***10x safety factor***.
    
-   **[European Union limit:](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2023/915/oj/eng)** Allows up to 3 mg/kg (3 ppm) in food supplements—roughly *90 mcg* per 30-gram scoop of protein powder. In this case, the FDA is far more cautious than Europe (and that’s not usually how things go).
    

Back to the report: of the 23 protein supplements they tested, [two-thirds](https://article.images.consumerreports.org/image/upload/v1760108748/prod/content/dam/CRO-Images-2025/Special%20Projects/Consumer-Reports-Protein-Powders-and-Shakes-Contain-High-Levels-of-Lead-Methodology-Test-Results.pdf) exceeded “Level of Concern.” One brand (Naked Nutrition Vegan Mass Gainer) hit nearly 16 times the limit. But because Consumer Reports used California’s exceptionally strict benchmark, those numbers sound scarier than they really are.

The average American already gets 5.3 mcg of lead daily from food and the environment. That’s another reason California’s cutoff doesn’t make much sense. Still, some products identified in the report could push intake close to the pregnancy (8.8 mcg) or adult (12.5 mcg) daily thresholds.

***What this means for you:*** Don’t worry too much. While the FDA continues to reduce lead exposure through programs like [Closer to Zero](https://www.fda.gov/food/environmental-contaminants-food/closer-zero-reducing-childhood-exposure-contaminants-foods) and the [Total Diet Study](https://www.fda.gov/media/159745/download?attachment) (that is, if the funding continues), there are several things we can do in our own homes, especially for parents of kids and during pregnancy.

-   Check to see if your protein supplement (or any supplement, really) has third-party testing for heavy metals (like USP or Informed Sport).
    
-   Advocate for more pre-market regulatory oversight in the supplement industry (which has very little, if any at all) by writing to your local representative.
    
-   Eat a diet that includes a variety of nutrient-dense foods, which helps limit exposure to specific food sources and ensures we get an array of [protective nutrients](https://cris.msu.edu/news/protective-nutrition-news/protective-nutrition-heavy-metals/).
    
-   Prioritize getting your protein from whole food sources.

### **Fourteen states and Guam join forces to launch Governors’ Public Health Alliance**

Governors from 14 states and Guam announced the creation of the bipartisan Governors’ Public Health Alliance, which is a new effort to strengthen coordination and collaboration across state lines.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!fv4A!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F029f937e-1638-40dc-97d8-0b35d25f9bbb_1600x1310.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!fv4A!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F029f937e-1638-40dc-97d8-0b35d25f9bbb_1600x1310.png)

***Why do we need this?*** In the U.S., authority over health rests with the states, not the federal government. Health (encompassing both health care and public health) is not only the highest budget item for a state but also the primary reason for state bankruptcy. In other words, governors hold enormous power over your health.

Today, though, federal support is shifting fast, funding is drying up, and states are being forced to get creative. States must decide whether to maintain their public health departments (due to funding cuts), how to continue purchasing vaccines (if the federal government stops recommending them), whether to negotiate drug prices (like insulin), and more. We saw a similar challenge during the pandemic with bulk purchasing of PPE.

In general, the more coordinating, collaborating, and innovative thinking, the better.

However, I’m growing increasingly concerned about the partisan gaps in public health. Although some Republicans are on the advisory board and the initiative was framed as bipartisan, no Republican-led states have joined. This worries me for my friends in red states, like Texas, but it also has implications for everyone, as diseases don’t care about borders.

***What this means for you:*** If your state is included, you can rest assured your governor is talking to others, which is a helpful step toward innovative solutions. You could argue it was needed before this moment, too. Public health has been siloed for far too long.

### **Mammograms save lives. They’ve been wrongly targeted.**

Happy Breast Cancer Awareness month! Unfortunately, this month has driven some influencers to post false claims about the harms of mammograms. So let’s clear this up.

Breast cancer is the [second leading](https://www.cancer.org/cancer/types/breast-cancer/about/how-common-is-breast-cancer.html) cause of cancer death in women in the U.S., and accounts for 1 in 3 new cancers among women each year. (It affects men too, just at [lower](https://www.cancer.org/cancer/types/breast-cancer-in-men/about/key-statistics.html) rates.) There is strong scientific consensus in support of routine mammograms to prevent breast cancer and detect it early:

-   Randomized trials show that mammograms reduce the [risk of death from breast cancer by 12-20%](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2818283), depending on age. For every 1,000 mammograms, one death is prevented.
    
-   Regular mammograms starting at age 40 are recommended for everyone, but may have even greater benefit for Black women, who are 40% more likely to die from breast cancer than white women and more likely to have aggressive cancers, younger.
    

There’s some critical nuance here:

-   With each mammogram, breast tissue is exposed to a small amount of [ionizing radiation](https://www.cdc.gov/radiation-health/about/ionizing-radiation.html). But! ***We’re exposed to this type of radiation every day in our natural environment.*** At high doses, radiation exposure can change DNA and cause harm, but the amount of radiation exposure during a mammogram is [about the same](https://www.ewbc.com/posts/should-i-be-worried-about-radiation-and-mammography-2/) as flying from California to New York.
    
-   ***Organizations disagree on whether mammograms every year or every other year are optimal.*** Screening recommendations are based on evaluating science to maximize benefit (lives saved) while reducing patient worry, unnecessary costs, and diagnosing and treating cancers and pre-cancers that ultimately wouldn’t cause health problems—also called “overdiagnosis.” This is a balancing act.
    
-   ***Mammograms are just one tool for detecting cancer***, and women with dense breasts or high risk for cancer (e.g., family history, known genetic predispositions, or other [key risk factors](https://www.bcrf.org/about-breast-cancer/risk-factors-for-breast-cancer/)) may benefit from additional screening, such as through an MRI.
    

***What this means for you:*** The benefits of mammograms far outweigh the risks. The [U.S. Preventive Task Force](https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/breast-cancer-screening), the [American College of Radiology](https://www.jacr.org/article/S1546-1440\(23\)00334-4/fulltext?_gl=1*wbdh47*_ga*Mjk1NzY3ODUzLjE3MzYyMDg2NDg.*_ga_K9XZBF7MXP*MTczNjIwODY0Ny4xLjEuMTczNjIwODc4OC4wLjAuMA..), the [American Society of Breast Surgeons](https://www.breastsurgeons.org/docs/statements/asbrs-screening-mammography.pdf), and the [American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists](https://www.acog.org/news/news-releases/2024/10/acog-updates-recommendation-when-to-begin-breast-cancer-screening-mammography) recommend routine mammograms starting at age 40 for women at average risk of breast cancer. If you have [risk factors](https://www.bcrf.org/about-breast-cancer/risk-factors-for-breast-cancer/) for breast cancer, dense breasts, or you’re 75 or older, talk to your doctor about your screening goals and plan. Don’t know your risk? Here’s one [risk assessment tool](https://siteman.wustl.edu/prevention/ydr/).

For more, see YLE’s [deep dive](https://yourlocalepidemiologist.substack.com/p/new-breast-cancer-screening-guidelines?triedRedirect=true) on breast cancer screening recommendations.

### **Dog owners, check your pup’s food**

The FDA ​​recalled Raw Bistro frozen beef dog food for possible *Salmonella* contamination. The recalled [products](https://www.fda.gov/safety/recalls-market-withdrawals-safety-alerts/raw-bistro-pet-fare-voluntarily-recalls-frozen-beef-entree-because-possible-salmonella-health-risk) were sold directly to consumers and to select distributors between Sept. 1 and Sept. 17 in California, Colorado, Illinois, and Minnesota.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-Wf3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb9af7d41-ce52-41dc-821d-5a8608ff69de_1020x663.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-Wf3!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb9af7d41-ce52-41dc-821d-5a8608ff69de_1020x663.png)

*Salmonella* can make dogs sick, just like humans. Contaminated food can cause illness [days later](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/christie-horvath_pet-food-recallpdf-activity-7383849408766943233-LLiL?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAAutKMkB4POGANDqWFU5D9rKtVMx54bEllY) in dogs. And dog owners can get sick from handling contaminated food or dog bowls.

***What this means for you:*** Check the lot numbers on your dog’s food, and toss it if they are included in the FDA recall notice. Sanitize bowls if they held contaminated food, wash your hands, and watch for warning signs in your dog: lethargy, vomiting, diarrhea, and loss of appetite. If you notice these signs, take your pet to the vet.
