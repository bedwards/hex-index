**In isolation, OpenAI’s** two financial miscues over the past week weren’t exactly catastrophes. Sam Altman suggesting an investor [sell his shares](https://www.businessinsider.com/sam-altman-defends-openai-trillion-spending-2025-11) instead of questioning OpenAI’s spending might have been good-natured ribbing. And OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar asking for the federal government to guarantee its loans, before [walking it back](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/11/06/openai-cfo-sarah-friar-says-company-is-not-seeking-government-backstop.html), could’ve just been a conference slip-up.

The two flubs, whether you read them that way or not, generated a response that seemed out of proportion with the scale of the errors. Altman’s punchy response turned into a [multi-day, international news story](https://www.google.com/search?sca_esv=625717d0e0484ac3&sxsrf=AE3TifOd_QBS6AF9dhFfuBMwaduCdhf0Ug:1762530966759&q=sam+altman+sell+your+shares+brad&tbm=nws&source=lnms&fbs=AIIjpHxU7SXXniUZfeShr2fp4giZrjP_Cx0LI1Ytb_FGcOviEreERTNAkkP8Y6EXltYTGWs9RGaEMfZ2dZFFrbmM-rnqnRIo3vt2ixIz0IR9QbNR5IGohm7MEd4IHD5LU06zRu-YkF7_SXVLyS9Zp1k5NWg6Pt1ZHI6SHk5u8OsiJo7tceZBGBHlP6-yxJ4YFrqFqgKBMpLllCicA6OvDZLHekUvEyyjqw&sa=X&ved=2ahUKEwiFoLfBs-CQAxV6NPsDHRNYCjEQ0pQJegQIDhAB&biw=1453&bih=727&dpr=2). And Friar’s comments about a potential bailout evoked [a definitive “no”](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/11/06/trump-ai-sacks-federal-bailout-openai-friar.html) from the White House.

The intense scrutiny applied to every little financial statement may be new territory for OpenAI, but it’s going to be the status quo for the company moving forward. Through a flurry of deals and announcements in recent months — $100 billion from NVIDIA [in September](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/09/26/nvidias-investment-portfolio.html), a potential 10% stake in AMD [in October](https://www.reuters.com/business/nvidia-invest-100-billion-openai-2025-09-22/), for example — OpenAI has entwined itself with the public market and the broader economy. Its deal with Microsoft last week allowed it to [sell equity in its for-profit arm](https://spyglass.org/openai-microsoft-stake-pbc/) and put it firmly on track for an IPO. And it’s already been selling on the secondary market at a [$500 billion valuation](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/10/02/openai-share-sale-500-billion-valuation.html), which would place it among the 20 most valuable publicly traded companies in the world today.

But OpenAI is still a private company. And so without quarterly financial filings, its executives’ statements will serve as proxies for whether it’s acting with financial responsibility. Given how many companies are depending on its success today, that matters a lot.

Being private allows small companies to figure these things out on the way to an IPO. But OpenAI is now so large that it has effectively outrun the grace period.

The company’s ramp up has happened so fast this new environment may seem bewildering. OpenAI said ChatGPT had [200 million users](https://www.reuters.com/technology/artificial-intelligence/openai-says-chatgpts-weekly-users-have-grown-200-million-2024-08-29/) in August 2024, it [doubled](https://www.reuters.com/technology/artificial-intelligence/openais-weekly-active-users-surpass-400-million-2025-02-20/) that to 400 million users in February 2025, and it doubled it again to [800 million](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/06/sam-altman-says-chatgpt-has-hit-800m-weekly-active-users/) users last month. The company had [$5.5 billion](https://www.reuters.com/business/media-telecom/openais-annualized-revenue-hits-10-billion-up-55-billion-december-2024-2025-06-09/) in annualized revenue in 2024, it’s on track for [$20 billion annualized revenue](https://x.com/sama/status/1986514377470845007) by the end of this year. It is the fastest-growing startup of all time, and it’s not particularly close.

To his credit, Altman said he’d welcome public-market scrutiny, at least to some degree, after making his “If you want to sell your shares, I’ll find you a buyer,” [remark](https://www.businessinsider.com/sam-altman-defends-openai-trillion-spending-2025-11) to investor Brad Gerstner. Speaking of his skeptics, Altman said, “I would love to tell them they could just short the stock, and I would love to see them get burned on that.”

But until that happens, the public will look at whatever Altman and his lieutenants say to make their best guess as to whether this historic private company might tank the stock market or send it to new heights (and the economy, potentially, as well).

This will magnify every little utterance, and the OpenAI team will need to appreciate that to avoid further episodes like the one it’s just experienced. It may not be totally fair, but it is the new normal.
