In the latest episode of Lives Well Lived, the podcast I co-host with Kasia de Lazari-Radek, we speak with evolutionary anthropologist Anna Machin, who studies the science of love, attraction, and human relationships. In our conversation, she explains why the spark of attraction happens in the first seconds of meeting someone, how oxytocin and dopamine work together to quieten fear and motivate us to take the risk of love, and we talk about whether it is true that “love is blind.”

We discuss the difference between attraction, lust, and love, why romantic love is a Western cultural construct, and whether humans are truly monogamous. Anna also talks about jealousy as a signal of reproductive threat, whether animals experience love, and the unique role of fathers in human evolution.

Above all, Anna stresses that relationships sit at the centre of our existence. As she puts it, having a healthy social network is the number one factor in our health, longevity, and happiness.

Below are some highlights from our conversation, edited for clarity. You can listen to the full episode on Apple Podcasts, Spotify, or your preferred platform.

How love begins in the brain

Kasia de Lazari Radek: We walk into a pub, a conference hall, a classroom, or a gym. Across the room, someone catches our attention—interesting, attractive, engaging. Our eyes meet, a smile follows, and soon we are in conversation. The exchange flows effortlessly, laughter comes easily, and by the time we part ways, the whole evening feels transformed. We return home, close the door, and with a deep sigh, we say, “It must be love”. How is that possible? How can such a powerful feeling arise so suddenly, almost out of nowhere?

Anna Machin: How can it happen so quickly? First, I would dispute the word love at that point. We wouldn't describe that as love, but we would likely describe that as attraction and lust, which are the first steps towards romantic love.

And the reason why it can happen so suddenly is because it's a very ancient mechanism that we share with all the other mammals, which is a mechanism based on sensory input. You mentioned that your eyes meet across the crowded room, and when we first lock eyes with someone, we take in a lot of sensory information.

So, what do they look like? How do they move? What do they sound like? What's the pitch of their voice? If you're a woman, what do they smell like? We’re taking in all this sensory information, and we don't know we're doing it. And we put it into an algorithm, in our brain, which runs a little calculation of the biological market value of the person you’ve met. If they rate well in that respect, then ping, the three cherries come up on the slot machine, and your brain goes into overdrive.

Why evolution reduces our risk detection when we fall in love

Peter Singer: You said that oxytocin reduces the activity in the amygdala, which is the risk detection centre. That seems a strange thing for evolution to do to us. Because it means that we’re going to make riskier choices. And sometimes, of course, even though we feel love for someone, they’ll be bad choices.

Anna Machin: Absolutely. And it gets worse, later, when the conscious brain is engaged, an area of the brain related to mentalizing, which is the ability to guess someone's intention. It's mind-reading. And the mentalizing area of the brain deactivates in the first stages of a relationship.

So not only are we reducing your ability to detect risk, but we’re also shutting down the bit of your brain that detects someone who's going to do you wrong. And yeah, the question arises, why would you do that? Obviously, that opens you up to the risk of picking the wrong person. This is why people can be in obviously bad relationships in the early stages, but not see it. Everybody else can see that the person they are with is not a nice person, might be a liar, might be a cheat, whatever it might be. The person in the relationship can't see that, and that's related to the deactivation of the mentalizing area.

Romantic love as a Western construct

Peter Singer: So, I've noticed that your explanation so far has been really in evolutionary terms, so therefore universal to humans. And in fact, you even said “to mammals,” with individual variations of course, as you mentioned. But this is against the view that was popular some decades ago, that romantic love is a Western invention.

Anna Machin: We must define our terms. I tend to use “romantic love” because people understand what I mean. If I start talking about reproductive love, people get terribly upset. What I am talking about is reproductive love.

We use the term “romantic love.” You’re right, it’s a Western cultural construct. Therefore, other cultures don't discuss romantic love in the same way we do. They might call it passionate love. Romantic love means something very specific to those of us who study love. It's something cultural, Western, and rooted in the idea that there is one love - the most powerful love - and the love that overcomes all obstacles.

Are humans really monogamous?

Kasia de Lazari Radek: In Western culture, we do think that we want to find this one and only. But of course, with time, the love story that I began with may not end up in the right way. Sometimes we want someone else. Are we humans monogamous beings?

Anna Machin: This is a tricky one. And again, it comes back to the biocultural nature of our species. Most species are driven primarily by biology, but we are driven by both biology and culture. Now, if we look at biological definitions of monogamy, there are two forms of monogamy. There is sexual monogamy, and that means you are exclusive sexually to the person you're in a relationship with. There is also social monogamy. Now, social monogamy is you coexist with somebody to the exclusion of everybody else, but you're not necessarily sexually monogamous. So true, monogamy would be sexually and socially monogamous, as a species. We are not that; in fact, only 0.0015% of species are truly monogamous.

In most species, we have social monogamy, where we see pair bonding occurring in a species. But since the advent of DNA testing of offspring, it's very, very clear that quite often the offspring are not the product of both those people and what we call extra-pair populations have occurred outside the pair bond. So those are socially monogamous species, but not sexually monogamous. And that's what we tend to be in some cultures. Again, some cultures don't practice monogamy at all. But if we were talking about the West, we, in the West, exhibit social monogamy, but our infidelity rate is far too high for us to be defined as a truly monogamous species.

Why jealousy isn’t about love

Kasia de Lazari Radek: On the one hand, why put so much effort into building this culture, and talk about monogamy and adultery and so on? But on the other hand, we are jealous. And there is something in our psychology and in our nature that we want to be the one and only for our partner, unless we don't want that partner to be one and only for us.

Anna Machin: That's not really to do with love, though. That's to do with jealousy, which is related to a threat to your reproductive status, so it's not really to do with the emotional connection you have with that person. Like any emotion, jealousy draws our attention to a threat or a benefit in our environment. In this case, it's a threa. Our partner may be having a relationship with somebody else. That might be a threat to your reproductive status. For example, if you are a man and your female partner is having a relationship with someone else, the risk is that he will make her pregnant. And if he makes her pregnant, you can't make her pregnant for a good number of months, well over nine months. And that lowers your reproductive status.

The other way of looking at it is if you are a woman and your partner is having a relationship with someone else, obviously, he can go and make her pregnant and he can make you pregnant at the same time. But what you are risking is a split of his resources and a split of his investment. And that's what men bring to reproductive relationships: the resources and investment, and the help with raising a child. If you now have to share him 50/50, that's a risk to the survival of your offspring. Your offspring won't have such a beneficial environment. And that's why we get jealous.

Do animals love?

Peter Singer: It's interesting that in Why We Love you talk about many kinds of love. We have focused so far on romantic love, sexual love, and reproductive love, but you do write about love for friends and love for pets. And that's an interesting area. But in the context of that, you ask the question, can animals love? Do dogs really love us, for example?

Anna Machin: It's hard to say whether it's the same phenomenon. Because several things about human love make it special, one major part of it is that it obviously has a major cultural dimension, which animals don't have. So, we can never say that animals truly experience human love. But in a way, we're asking the wrong question. I followed the work of a wonderful, effective neuroscientist called Jaak Panksepp, who spoke a lot about whether animals experience love. And what he said is that love is present in a lot of animals. However, what humans have done is take love and overly embellish it. We've made it very complicated. He uses the analogy of having a cupcake, and love is the cupcake. And quite a lot of animals have that, certainly those that are up there with us in terms of cognition, so, elephants, cetaceans, or some of the apes. What we've done is we've taken the cupcake, which is love, and we've chucked loads of icing and sprinkles and chocolate on the top, and we've made it really, really complicated and put lots of other different bits. We have a form of love, but it's an overly romanticised form of love.

The unique role of fathers

Kasia de Lazari Radek: From an evolutionary perspective, are our roles as mothers and fathers very different?

Anna Machin: There are crossovers and then yes, there are distinct areas. And the reason for that is, we are, as a mammal, a rare species, because we have investing fathers. Only 5% of mammals have investing fathers. We are the only ape that has it.

Because parenting is such a unique role, evolution doesn't tend to promote redundancy—having two roles that do exactly the same thing. Evolution isn't very good with redundancy. So what it has done is make mothers' and fathers' roles complement rather than mirror each other. They fit together like a jigsaw puzzle, ensuring that developmental input is spread between the parents.

Certainly, there are things that both parents do. They're both very capable of nurturing, caretaking, teaching, and supporting emotional development. But when we look at how mothers and fathers interact with their children, they interact in distinctly different ways.

Mothers tend to interact from a place of nurture—that's where they start their relationship with their child. Fathers also have a nurturing aspect, absolutely, but we like to describe the father's relationship with his child as based on stimulation or challenge.

The ultimate secret to living well

Anna Machin: Having a healthy social network is the number one factor in your health—your mental and physical health, your longevity, and your happiness. It's more important than all the things governments tell you to do, which of course are important: maintaining a suitable weight, not smoking, getting exercise, eating lots of vegetables and fruit.

It's bigger than all of that because it's fundamental to your health. So yes, absolutely live well by doing all those things that public health campaigns recommend, but ultimately, nurture your relationships. Spend time in person with people, because that's when you get the wonderful hits of neurochemicals that underpin your mental health, your happiness, and your immune system. That's the important message, and that's why we call it social capital—because it's genuinely valuable.
