Ellen Langer is a pioneering figure in psychology, often described as the "mother of mindfulness." For decades, her research has challenged conventional thinking about health, ageing, and the power of the mind to influence the body. In our conversation, Ellen explains why she believes the mind and body are not separate entities, but a single, unified system and how this insight can dramatically reshape our understanding of illness, healing, and even longevity.

We discuss her landmark studies, including the “Counterclockwise” experiment, in which elderly men appeared to reverse the effects of ageing by mentally placing themselves in the past, and the hotel cleaner study, where simply *reframing* routine work as exercise produced measurable physical benefits. But psychology has been going through a “replication crisis” – in recent years, attempts to rerun some the most famous psychological studies have failed to find the same striking results. Isn’t that also true, we ask Ellen, of her hotel cleaner study? And what happened to a proposed re-creation of the Counterclockwise experiment?

Ellen responds to these challenges, and also shares her thoughts on the placebo effect, the dangers of medical fatalism, and why active noticing, not meditation, is the key to true mindfulness.

Below are some highlights from our conversation, edited for clarity. You can listen to the full episode on Apple Podcasts, Spotify, or your preferred platform.

**Mindfulness (No Meditation Required)**

**KASIA DE LAZARI RADEK:** Okay, Ellen. I’d love to get to the heart of it, what exactly do you mean by mindfulness, and how can we practise it?

**ELLEN LANGER:** Sure. When people hear the word “mindful,” they often mistakenly associate it with meditation, which is great, but it's very different from what I’m talking about. I actually did some of the early research on meditation. It’s a practice you engage in, ideally to reach a post-meditative state of mindfulness.

But the mindfulness I study is more immediate, and you can access it in one of two ways: top-down or bottom-up.

Top-down means recognising that everything is constantly changing. Everything looks different from different perspectives, which means, we never really *know*. Yet we’re taught in school to treat so many things as fixed facts.

Let me tell you how that changed for me. I was a straight-A student, one of those kids who memorised everything, even what was under the pictures in the textbook. One day, I was at a horse event. A man asked me to watch his horse while he went to get it a hot dog. I thought, “A hot dog? Horses don’t eat meat!” But I said okay. He came back and the horse ate the hot dog.

That moment changed everything. I realised that much of what I “knew” might be wrong. And instead of being upset, I was thrilled. It opened up a world of possibility. I started asking, “Why not? Who says so?

Let me give you another example I often use. Peter, how much is one plus one?

**PETER SINGER:** I think it’s two.

**ELLEN LANGER:** Right, that’s what we’ve all been taught. But even that depends on context. In base 10, sure, one plus one is two. In base 2, it's written as 10. But more importantly, in the real world? One pile of laundry plus one pile of laundry is still one pile. One wad of chewing gum plus another? One. Two clouds merging? Still one.

So when someone asks you, “How much is one plus one?” you might say two—but also one, or ten, depending on the context. That’s mindfulness. It gives you choices. Rote answers don’t.

We hold things still when they’re always changing. And once you see that, once you really internalise the uncertainty, it becomes liberating. You stop feeling bad about not knowing—because, really, nobody knows.

**PETER SINGER:** Nobody knows anything?

**ELLEN LANGER:** Nobody knows anything *for sure*. Because everything is in flux.

That’s the bottom-up approach: actively noticing. Go outside and try to notice three new things, even if you’ve lived there for 20 years. Do the same with your spouse, a colleague, anyone. You’ll start to realise how much you don’t really know. And soon, everything becomes new.

You don’t need to “practise” this in a formal way. Let’s say you both came to visit me here at my home in Dartmouth, Massachusetts, it’s beautiful, right on the river. You’d walk in and naturally notice everything. The books, the paintings, the view. It would all be new to you. But for me, it might seem old, unless I make a mindful effort to *see* it again.

We confuse the stability of our mindsets with the stability of the world around us. But when we start to see things anew, we re-engage with life. Unfortunately, most people live like robots and I have 50 years of data showing that we are mindless much of the time. When you’re mindless, you’re not aware of it, so you can’t snap out of it.

And can a robot be happy? No. Robots don’t have choices. Why live that way when we have the capacity to enjoy ourselves?

If you’re surfing, and every wave is exactly the same, like in a wave pool, it wouldn’t be fun anymore.

**PETER SINGER:** That’s why I don’t go to wave pools. They build machines to generate identical waves. But part of what I love about surfing is that every wave is different.

**ELLEN LANGER:** Exactly. It’s the difference that makes it exciting. Mindfulness is like that, it enlivens us, both literally and figuratively. It’s what you feel when you’re engaged and having fun.

So what do I recommend? Break the seal on the unlived lives that so many people are stuck in. At our age, Peter, people are obsessed with exercise and diet to extend their years. But I think we should focus on adding *life* to our years, not just years to our life. And when we do that, we find we’re not only happier but possibly living longer, too.

**KASIA DE LAZARI RADEK:** Okay, but how do we *do* that?

**ELLEN LANGER:** There are a few things. First, practice *active noticing*. Five, ten times a day, pay attention to something you’ve seen before and find something new in it. Once you truly grasp that you don’t know, the world becomes full of possibilities. Everyone is potentially interesting. Everything can be engaging.

Understand that uncertainty is the rule, not the exception, and it can be powerful.

We also need to stop thinking of outcomes as inherently good or bad. They’re just events. What causes stress is how we *interpret* those events. When you’re mindful, you see more possible interpretations and stress tends to dissolve.

This next part is more speculative, I don’t have data on it, but in my view, stress is the number one killer. And stress is psychological. It stems from two assumptions: something bad is going to happen, and it’s going to be awful. But prediction is an illusion.

So challenge the prediction. Come up with three, five reasons why it might *not* happen. Already, you’re less stressed.

Now do the harder thing: assume it *will* happen. Then ask yourself, how could that be a *good* thing?

**KASIA DE LAZARI RADEK:** But not under all circumstances. I’m thinking of something really hard, like losing a child.

**ELLEN LANGER:** Sure. No, you're right. And if I were a mindless academic, I might try to argue that with you. But let’s acknowledge: even someone who has lost a child has moments in the day when those thoughts aren’t front and centre.

The question is, how are they spending those moments?

If they’re spending them mindfully, then the neurons are firing. And that, in turn, helps them be healthier. And, possibly, happier.

**Mind–Body Unity: A Radical Reframe**

**PETER SINGER:** So what do you think is really the underlying mechanism that’s going on?

**ELLEN LANGER:** This is so funny, Peter. I’ve been answering a version of that question for 40 years. I was once the cover story of the magazine section of *The New York Times*, years and years ago. And the person writing the story, it took me forever to get them to understand, and then him forever to get the editorial board at *The New York Times* to understand.

So, when I say that mind and body are a single unit, one thing, you don’t have to ask how one affects the other, because it’s all the same thing. The main problem with mind-body dualism, which most people who haven’t read my work still believe in, is exactly that: people don’t realise they believe in it. But when they ask, “How is it happening?” that’s really what they’re assuming.

The question becomes: if you have a mind and a body, how do you get from this fuzzy thing called a thought to something material, like the body? And yet, everyone has experienced direct effects. You see someone vomiting and suddenly you feel nauseated. A leaf blows in your face and you’re startled, your blood pressure and pulse spike, until you realise, “Oh, it was just a leaf.”

So, I feel good that I’ve had some influence, but it’s taken so long, so many decades. People now talk about the mind-body *connection*, but even that still leads to your question: how are they connected?

What I want is for people to see it as a single unit. And if the unit is here, then every part of the unit, your toes, your mind, your bloodstream, your pancreas, is here too. It’s all part of the same system.

That doesn’t mean I’m saying nothing is happening “under the hood,” as my colleagues would put it. Of course, all sorts of changes are occurring. What I’m saying is that these changes happen more or less simultaneously. It’s not a matter of doing one thing, which then leads to another, which then leads to a third. Because the moment you think that way, you’re back to the old problem: how do you get from a thought to something physical?

It’s interesting that the medical world is finally starting to come around, which I’m thrilled about. But it’s taken them so long, despite the fact that I believe the strongest drug we have is the placebo. And the placebo, separate from our experiments, is the best evidence of what I’m talking about.

You take a sugar pill. You take nothing. But you believe it’s something, and then it does the work of something. Your beliefs are actually affecting the final outcome.

My whole journey started with my experience with my mother and grandmother. It’s clear that placebos are powerful medicine. And it’s not the placebo itself that’s making you better, by definition, it’s just a sugar pill. So we’ve created this drama: I go to a doctor, who prescribes a “nothing.” I take this nothing, thinking it’s something, and then I get better. I’m making myself better.

In *The Mindful Body*, toward the end of the book, I talk about a treatment for chronic illnesses that doesn’t even require engaging the medical world. And I have great respect for medical professionals, although sometimes it may not sound that way.

**KASIA DE LAZARI-RADEK:** Okay, so let’s talk about that a bit, because it seems to me there are illnesses where it wouldn’t work. I have a thyroid problem, for example. I need to take thyroid hormone. It wouldn’t help me if I took sugar.

**ELLEN LANGER:** I don’t agree with you. We don’t know. To assume that the mind isn’t crucially important in every disease, I think, limits us unnecessarily.

**KASIA DE LAZARI-RADEK:** Isn’t that a bit dangerous? Because on the one hand, you say you have respect for the medical field…

**ELLEN LANGER:** There’s nothing I recommend, either in these interactions or in any of my books, that should suggest you shouldn’t see a doctor. But, as everyone knows, doctors are very busy. There can be a long delay between getting a test and finding out the results.

And nothing I recommend can’t be done in coordination with your doctor. Let me give you an example, maybe this will even work for your thyroid issue. I don’t know that it would, but I also don’t want to discount the possibility prematurely.

When people are diagnosed with chronic illnesses, they tend to believe that means they have no control over it. But that’s not what “chronic” means. All a chronic illness is, is the medical world doesn’t yet have an answer. That doesn’t mean there *isn’t* an answer.

People with chronic illness often believe their symptoms will stay the same or get worse. But nothing moves in only one direction. Take the stock market. When it goes up, it doesn’t rise in a straight line. It goes up a little, down a little, up a little. The same thing happens with our symptoms. They’re always changing, but we hold them still in our minds.

Now, here’s what we did in our studies across very serious diseases: we’d call people periodically. “How is the symptom? Is it better or worse than the last time I called? And why?”

That simple question produces several effects. First, most people with a chronic illness feel helpless. Suddenly, you’re actively engaged in your own health care, and that feels good. Second, you start to notice: “Hey, now it’s a little better.” Or even if it’s worse, just recognising that *change* is interesting. Third, when you ask “Why now?” you engage in a mindful search for answers.

We have many studies showing that this kind of active noticing, this mindfulness, is good for your health. And finally, I think you’re more likely to find a solution if you’re actually looking.

We did this with people who have multiple sclerosis, Parkinson’s, stroke, arthritis, chronic pain, and more and in each case, we saw significant improvement in symptoms.

So how is this like the placebo? Well, the way I just described it, it’s not. But most people, in Poland, in Melbourne, where the two of you are now, in the United States, almost everyone has a smartphone. And if not, they should get one, or borrow one.

Set the phone to ring in an hour. When it rings, ask yourself: “How is this symptom? Better or worse than the last time?” Then set it to ring two hours and five minutes later. Keep varying it throughout the day and across the week. You can do it yourself. You don’t need an experimenter calling you.

**The Placebo as the Strongest Drug**

**PETER SINGER:** I just wanted to get back to the placebo effect. Placebos may have some effect, but they clearly don’t have the same effect as the drug, right? Otherwise, there’d be no point in doing placebo-controlled experiments.

**ELLEN LANGER:** Okay, Peter, this is a bigger conversation than we can cover here. But we don’t know how much of a drug’s effect is due to the drug itself.

Everyone needs to know: in order to bring a drug to market, it has to outperform the placebo. But many drugs have side effects, placebos don’t. So the presence of a side effect convinces the person they got the drug, and that belief helps recovery.

We want people to understand how powerful placebos are. Let’s say a drug helps 60% of people, and a placebo helps 50%. That’s still a lot of people helped with a sugar pill.

**The Power of Believing – and the Replication Crisis in Psychology**

**ELLEN LANGER:** We did a study where we taught hotel maids that their work is exercise. One group kept doing the same work, not thinking of it as exercise. The other group began to believe it was. They lost weight, their BMI changed, their blood pressure came down. The only change was in their minds.

Another famous study: we took elderly men to a retreat retrofitted to look like it did 20 years earlier. They lived there as if it were the past. As a result, their vision improved, their hearing, memory, strength. They looked noticeably younger. No medical intervention, just a shift in mindset.

**PETER SINGER:** There’s been an attempt to replicate that study, I’m not sure if you’ve seen it, by Dixie Denworth and others, using building service workers in a similar way. But they didn’t get the results you did...

**ELLEN LANGER:** I haven’t seen the paper, Peter, but honestly, some of that kind of research I find ridiculous, or even offensive. If I wanted to do a study and *not* get an effect, it’s easy enough to design it that way. The way the instructions are given matters, exactly what’s said and how it’s said. That’s crucial.

That original study has now been replicated and built upon in many papers we’ve published demonstrating mind-body unity. And my students, when they conduct these studies with me, are meticulous researchers. They know I’m not interested in doing work that won’t make a real difference. And if you’re aiming for something meaningful, there will always be people trying to tear it down.

One thing that distinguishes my research is that I’m testing new possibilities—not simply tweaking someone else’s study. But I’d be happy to revisit that paper and take a proper look. I’m sure it wouldn’t be difficult to address.

**PETER SINGER:** Do you think psychology, maybe in its earlier years, lacked the rigor it needed?

**ELLEN LANGER:** I’m not sure what we even mean by “rigor.” It depends on the study. Let’s say I do an experiment on an island with a group of monkeys, and I somehow manage to get one of them to talk. As I’m leaving on a boat, the monkey says, “Goodbye, Dr. Langer, it was wonderful.” Even if only one monkey spoke, it would be *phenomenal*.

Now, imagine I tried to get ten monkeys to talk and only one did—it would be hard to publish under today’s standards. In the past, that kind of result might have been more acceptable. But that doesn’t make it less remarkable.

**Judgment, Blame, and the Case Against Forgiveness**

**ELLEN LANGER:** If you ask ten people whether forgiveness is good, they’ll say yes. Ask whether blame is bad, they’ll say yes. But you can’t forgive without first blaming. So our forgivers are our blamers.

Better than forgiveness is understanding. You might hate me because I’m gullible. But from my perspective, I’m trusting. Kasia might be inconsistent, but from her perspective, she’s flexible.

No one wakes up and says, “Today I’ll be rude and obnoxious.” They’re trying to do something that makes sense to them. Behavior makes sense from the actor’s perspective, or else they wouldn’t do it.
