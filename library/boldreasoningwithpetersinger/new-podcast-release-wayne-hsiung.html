Wayne Hsiung is one of the most prominent voices in the animal rights movement today. In our conversation, Wayne explains why he is prepared to risk spending time in prison in order to rescue animals from factory farms and inform the public about the conditions in which those animals are living.

We talk about how his activism began, about how disrupting restaurants went viral – and whether it helped animals – and about the thinking behind Open Rescue. Wayne shares personal reflections on fear, courage, and the emotional toll of incarceration, as well as the power of storytelling in shifting public attitudes toward animal suffering.

We also explore his high-stakes legal battles—including a recent trial in rural Utah where, against the odds, and in one of the most conservative regions of the United States, he was acquitted by a jury after rescuing two piglets from a Smithfield farm. He also tells us why the prosecution argued against showing the jury video of the alleged crime.

Below are some highlights from our conversation, edited for clarity. You can listen to the full episode on Apple Podcasts, Spotify, or your preferred platform.

The Origin of 'In-Your-Face' Activism

PETER SINGER: Wayne, I first noticed your work as an animal activist when the organisation Direct Action Everywhere, which you started, began popping up at meetings and in restaurants, and I mean popping up quite literally.

Let me tell you about the first time I encountered it. I was a speaker on a panel in New York City at an event organised by some people who were planning a Museum of Food and Drink. They weren’t particularly animal activists, though they were interested in the issue. We were having a serious discussion of whether one should eat meat when suddenly a member of the audience stood up, unfolded a picture of a cow, gave the cow a name, and spoke out on her behalf, explaining what her life was like and how much suffering she had endured.

Then someone else stood up and did the same for a pig, and I think maybe someone did it for a chicken as well. The organisers were rather upset by this.

But to be honest, it did liven up what, until then, had been the kind of discussion I’d participated in many times before. I think it made the event more memorable. Pretty soon, activists with DXE, Direct Action Everywhere, became known for doing the same thing in restaurants where people were eating meat. It was a very in-your-face kind of activism. So my question is: do you think it worked?

WAYNE HSIUNG: It's a hard question. One of the most difficult things about social change work is that there are so many different theories. Depending on the theory you believe in, you're going to be looking at different metrics. The primary metric we were looking at was based on social mobilisation theory. Basically, get as many people as we can to speak out and create some sort of social contagion. And for that purpose, we needed to do things that would get lots of attention and that felt accessible.

Actually, that model of activism was kind of an accident. It wasn’t a planned strategy. One of my co-founders, Priya Sohani, who’s just this beautiful, elegant Indian woman, not someone you’d expect to do something disruptive, said, “What if I just went into the fanciest restaurant in the Bay Area and started yelling at people about eating animals?”

That’s what she did, and it went viral. It spread like wildfire. The most powerful thing I think it did was inspire a lot of people to speak out. I think that’s really important.

I know you previously had Cass Sunstein on your podcast, one of my former mentors. One of the biggest problems in social movements is just preference falsification. People are afraid to talk about things openly if they feel it goes against a preexisting social norm. Trying to overcome that is probably what we did most successfully and what I’m most proud of.

Open Rescue and the Philosophy Behind It

KASIA DE LAZARI RADEK: I think that was very powerful. Whether you can replicate that at scale is hard because it takes a lot of discipline, training, and maturity to be able to shout inside a restaurant in a way that makes people curious rather than angry. But I wanted to ask about another kind of action by Direct Action Everywhere, Open Rescue. Can you explain that to our listeners?

WAYNE HSIUNG: Open Rescue was actually the primary reason we started Direct Action Everywhere. The disruption stuff was almost accidental, one of my co-founders just ran into a restaurant and gave a speech.

Open Rescue is a tactic that originated in Australia in the 1980s and spread to Europe and the U.S. in the 1990s. It was a transformation of what the Animal Liberation Front had been doing. As Peter knows, whether you like or dislike them, the Animal Liberation Front was partly inspired by his book, perhaps even their name.

Their goal was to maximise economic damage to the industry, in part by rescuing animals, but also by releasing animals from fur farms and laboratories, often anonymously and covertly.

Open Rescue was different. It focused more on social, moral, and cultural disruption. Instead of sneaking around, activists went in openly, found suffering animals, gave them aid, and told their stories. And they stood behind their actions publicly, because when you're anonymous, people don’t find your message as credible.

I became aware of this tactic in the early 2000s, back when I was not someone who believed in disruptive protest. I’m Chinese. I’m a lawyer. My parents are socially conservative. The first protest I went to, I was literally shaking behind my sign, and it was just a peaceful protest organised by Gene Baur at Farm Sanctuary.

But then I read about the work of Compassion Over Killing. They went into facilities peacefully, with compassion, and brought out suffering animals. They exposed shocking conditions, filth, dead animals, pain, and yet they did it with kindness. They brought that kindness into dark places.

The Washington Post ran a story about them. I thought: how did they do this? I can’t even get my classmates or family to talk about farm animals. But they got one of the nation’s most important papers to run the story and it was positive.

That’s because it tapped into a basic moral intuition: when we see another being suffering, we want it to stop. Open Rescue tries to hack that intuition to create moral and cultural disruption. That’s what I’ve spent most of the last 15 years trying to scale.

Personal Vulnerability and Motivation

KASIA DE LAZARI RADEK: Since you've been repeatedly arrested, I wanted to ask, have you ever been scared?

WAYNE HSIUNG: I’m scared all the time.

KASIA DE LAZARI RADEK: You can't be! That kind of fear would probably cause trauma or stop you from acting. So really, what are the pros and cons of your actions, for your wellbeing?

WAYNE HSIUNG: One thing I learned from Peter, frankly, is that we should try to universalise our moral ethics. Don’t just think about yourself or your immediate circle, think about doing good in the world more broadly.

I've always tried to align my personal interests with that universal ethic. There’s also a long tradition in my family of Buddhist thinking, some of the wisest Buddhist scholars from centuries ago arrived at the same insights as modern utilitarian philosophy: moral universalism, the importance of avoiding suffering, the interchangeability of perspectives.

Courage, like a muscle, can be built through exertion over time. The first time I leafleted, I was shaking. I couldn’t even hand someone a leaflet, I was ashamed and embarrassed.

But over time, by taking on small challenges, I built that courage. It’s just like weight training: human beings can become incredibly strong, and that applies emotionally and morally too.

Surprisingly, the most difficult part of being jailed wasn’t the bad food or even the threat of violence, it was the social disconnection. In U.S. jails, you have no contact with the people you love. It’s incredibly isolating.

Still, I saw beauty in those moments. My cellmate, for example, was in for grand theft auto, largely due to addiction, but he cared deeply about animals. People who are most socially isolated often value animals the most, because sometimes only their dog loves them.

And yet, the greatest benefit of all of this, the legal precedents we’re trying to set aside, is the momentum and energy these campaigns generate. I consider myself an effective altruist. And while we’re often very reason-based, the truth is: emotion is the most powerful motivator in human history.

When I finally got The New York Times to publish an op-ed about animal rescue, it wasn’t because of an argument. It was because of the emotion. The story had power.

The Utah Trial Victory

PETER SINGER: You went on trial in rural Utah, a conservative part of the United States. That must have seemed like a tough place to make your case.

WAYNE HSIUNG: Absolutely. One in four people in that county worked for Smithfield, the giant meat producer. It’s by far the biggest employer. In both 2016 and 2020, 90% of the county voted for Donald Trump.

We thought we had maybe a 20% chance of success, and that was enough. I sold everything, gave friends access to my accounts, and prepared for a decade in prison.

Our legal team expected a hung jury at best, just enough of a deadlock to avoid conviction. What we got was a full acquittal.

We couldn’t even show the jury most of the evidence. The prosecution successfully argued it was too prejudicial to show videos of the animal cruelty. But one thing did get through: footage of a small piglet we rescued, hobbling on her injured leg.

After the verdict, one juror told us, ‘I just saw that little piglet, and I realised I liked her alive more than I liked her dead.’

That was enough.

Arrested in China at the Yulin Dog Meat Festival

PETER SINGER: One more episode I’d like to mention. You went to the Yulin Dog Meat Festival in China and rescued an animal there. Weren’t you afraid?

WAYNE HSIUNG: I was. The Yulin Dog Meat Festival was the largest dog slaughter event in the world. Over one weekend, 20,000 to 30,000 dogs were killed, many of them stolen companion animals.

I speak Mandarin and I’m Chinese, so I had some advantage in going undercover. I infiltrated a slaughterhouse and planted cameras. But I was caught and arrested, charged with espionage.

That’s a capital offence in China.

Luckily, we’d already uploaded the footage via Dropbox before the police raided us, deleted everything, and destroyed our cameras. And luckily, even in China, dog meat is controversial. The government didn’t want a diplomatic incident.

They released me after two days. I can’t go back, but we got the footage out.

And that was enough.
