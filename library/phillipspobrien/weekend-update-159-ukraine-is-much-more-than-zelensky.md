Hi All,

I am going to start this update with a short editorial. The last week has been a terrible one, rightly, for the vision of the Ukrainian government in Europe and North America. [The corruption scandal that broke out involving people very close to the Ukrainian president](https://kyivindependent.com/rage-panic-and-a-glimmer-of-hope-in-ukraine-as-corruption-scandal-unfolds/) cannot and should not be overlooked or shunted aside. It was and is terrible, points to the existence of real problems at the heart of the state, and rumors are circulating that it might even get worse. As I wrote two days, drastic action needs to be taken now to respond to this crisis and restore confidence in the Ukrainian government.

And while the Ukrainian government and President Zelensky are, rightly, being excoriated for this corruption crisis, it is important to remember that Ukraine’s struggle for freedom matters so much more than the reputation of any individual politician, [even one as widely known and hailed as Volodymyr Zelensky](https://open.substack.com/pub/phillipspobrien/p/zelensky-as-a-war-leader?r=1tgexa&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false). Ukrainians have been fighting and dying for their freedom, for their very lives, for 11 years now. They have suffered hundreds of thousands of dead and wounded, are now experiencing daily power cuts, cold days and nights, attacks on their homes and places of work—all the while being faced by an enemy that denies their very right to existence and regularly threatens them with extinction. This is an enemy that just two days ago, continued its campaign to destroy Ukraine’s ability to supply its population with life saving pharmaceuticals (more on that below).

Ukrainians are making this sacrifice every day to give Europe the vital time it still needs to prepare itself for a future, a future it was woefully unready to face. Arguably, they are fighting to see if the idea of Europe that has come into being after World War II has any chance of surviving. While much of the rest of the world retreats from democracy and flirts with, even embraces, the gooeyness of authoritarianism, the Ukrainians are doing the opposite. They are fighting for their lives, and fighting for democracy.

They would do this whether Zelensky was their president or otherwise. So, when you hear the voices start exclaiming, as they are now, that Ukraine should have no more help, that it needs to be cut off and left to its own devices, remember this. Ukraine is far more than Zelensky, far more than any one scandal. It is a people getting brutalized for wanting what any state should be entitled to, and at the same time making the ultimate sacrifice to gain the freedoms that too many other people take for granted or are too willing to sacrifice.

**Ukraine’s Ranged War: Thermal Plants and Ports**

Well, the Ukrainian ranged campaign this week showed how their targeting priorities are changing in ways that had been mostly hinted at before. As always, there were more attacks against Russian refineries. Last night, for instance, there were reports about Ukrainian attacks on a [Russian refinery located near the city of Samara](https://newsukraine.rbc.ua/news/unknown-drones-over-samara-explosions-reported-1763266414.html).

That being said, this week is was more than the refinery attacks that were worthy of notice. In particular two other targets, direct attacks on power plants and port facilities, reveal, possibly, how Ukrainian thinking is evolving as we head into Winter.

The Ukrainian attacks against port facilities involved the most important Russian oil exporting hub on the Black Sea, located at the port of Novorossiysk.

This port has become really the only place for the Russians to export out of the Black Sea (I have put a black box around it so you can see its location) and the attack on it seems to have caused some significant damage.

The [port has temporarily been closed, and that should affect about 2% of Russia’s oil exports](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2025/11/15/russia-temporarily-suspends-oil-exports-from-novorossiysk-following-ukrainian-attack).

That alone, will not make a massive difference, as the war has led to fewer and fewer Russian oil exports from Novorossiysk as it was. However, the fact that Ukraine is now attacking shipping facilities could make a major difference if it is indicative of Ukraine’s priorities. As Russia continues to lose refining capacity, its income will depend more and more on exports of unrefined (crude) oils. Stopping that export is difficult unless you can attack the ports from which that crude oil is shipped.

*Note—Russia is continuing to export diesel fuels, which opens up another question I hope to delve into this coming week.*

So, in trying to shut off crude exports, then Novorossiysk is probably important as an indicator. If Ukraine chooses to ramp up this campaign, and really hurt Russian exports, they will have to focus on the [shipping facilities near St Petersburg (Primorks and Ust-Luga)](https://www.bruegel.org/dataset/russian-crude-oil-tracker), which are handling far more Russian crude these days.

There are also facilities in the Far East, but those can only be damaged in something like a Spider Web attack. But, for now, pay attention to any port attacks from the facilities in the north.

The other thing worthy to note, which builds on attacks last week, is that [Ukraine this week directly attacked some Russian power generation plant](https://euromaidanpress.com/2025/11/15/frontline-report-2025-11-14/)s—not just transfer stations, etc. There are reports of direct attacks on thermal power plants near Voronezh and Orel. [These attacks led to immediate blackouts and heating cuts (of the type the Ukrainians are suffering almost daily)](https://abcnews.go.com/International/wireStory/ukrainian-strikes-disrupt-power-heating-2-major-cities-127349483).

Ukraine until recently has clearly not wanted to do this (for reasons discussed in past updates, primarily can these attacks be considered war crimes to attacking civilian targets or not). However they are making them more and more part of their plans—and one has to assume that it is directly related to what the Russians are doing to them.

They seem to have concluded that the only way to try and limit Russian attacks against their cities, is to make it clear that they are willing to do the same thing in return.

#### **Russia’s Ranged War: Darkness and Death**

Russia this week continued to attack Ukrainian power supply and heating supply—once more indicating (if any more evidence is needed) some of their highest priorities for the present campaign. There was a major attack, for instance, on the evening of November 14, t[hat was launched at targets in and around Kyiv](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/11/14/world/europe/ukraine-kyiv-russia-strike.html).

Of course, the Russian campaign has not moral debates about it. While it is trying to plunge Ukraine into darkness (and lack of heat) it also is directly aimed at killing Ukrainian civilians in numerous ways. The attack above, for instance, included numerous attacks on apartment buildings in Kyiv.

Though that was not all. The Russians also continued their campaign against Ukraine’s pharmaceutical industry and supply system. [They destroyed one of the remaining pharmaceutical supply warehouses in the Kyiv area for instance](https://united24media.com/latest-news/russian-drone-attack-wipes-out-another-ukrainian-medicine-warehouse-13463).

This attack is one of a series of attacks to try and deprive Ukraine of vital medicines this winter. There have been regular attacks on such targets this year, including a [large one in late October, that also damaged pharmaceutical production in Kyiv](https://www.pravda.com.ua/eng/news/2025/10/26/8004510/). They also in the Spring of 2025 d[estroyed a pharmaceutical operation owned by an Indian company which was located in Ukraine](https://www.ndtv.com/world-news/russian-missile-strikes-warehouse-of-indian-pharmaceutical-company-kusum-in-ukraine-kyiv-8149840) (picture below).

One great advantage Ukraine has, which Russia does not, is that it can be integrated into a European pharmaceuticals market as it has been into the European power grid.

This winter that advantage will save lives.

#### **Pokrovsk is all about the Relative Losses**

The fighting in and around Pokrovsk continues. The Russians made a few small gains this week, but overall the lines have not changed much. And that in and of itself is leading to debate. The big debate is about what Ukraine should do. [There is a loud group now saying that Ukraine should pull out and stop their losses in Pokrovsk at present.](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/11/13/world/europe/ukraine-pokrovsk-russia-battle.html) At the same time, the Ukrainian military, at least for now, does not seem willing to abandon the ruins of the town.

Should Ukraine withdraw? The only truth is that no one who does not know Ukrainian and Russian losses has no idea—and that means maybe no one knows. Pokrovsk is not strategically important, it makes no difference to the strategic calculus of the war, and therefore all that matters is the losses suffered by both sides in fighting over its ruins. The only other thing that could really matter is the length of time the campaign takes, as that reduces time for follow up operations afterwards.

Knowing when the Ukrainians should pull out of a town is a tricky question. Military decisions are not like war games (though analysts too often seem to think that they are). I do not have access to the data to understand exactly what Ukraine should do—so will not guess. We do know that Russia will continue to expend mass resources to take Pokrovsk, what the Ukrainians have to do, otoh, is not waste their own mass to keep them out. Ukrainian military lives are worth exponentially more than Russian military lives at present.
