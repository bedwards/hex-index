THE ONLY SPY AGENCY THAT EVER TRIED TO RECRUIT ME was South Korea’s National Intelligence Service, the NIS. It started sometime in 2009, when a young man from Seoul’s embassy in Washington asked to talk with me about articles and Op-eds I’d been writing in the New York Times, Washington Post and various journals advocating stronger responses by the U.S. and the United Nations to North Korea’s appalling human rights record.

“What motivates you to write these articles?” he asked. I explained my prior service as a human rights advocate at the State Department and private organizations and said that, although I was formally retired, I felt too little attention was being paid to the situation in North Korea.

That evidently interested those he reported back to at the embassy, because soon thereafter, a more senior officer, presumably from the NIS, became involved.

I began to receive invitations to lunch and on one occasion was asked to brief a group of visiting Korean diplomats and other officials on the perspectives of the U.S. and nongovernmental organizations on North Korea’s human rights situation.

We sat around a table in a dimly lit restaurant, where it was easy to identify which ones were intelligence officers, since they uttered not a word nor offered me their business cards, a standard practice with the Koreans. They were nonetheless paying close attention. A CIA friend told me later that they were probably sizing me up as a potential “agent of influence” for them in Washington on the subject of human rights in the north—an issue of extreme sensitivity to the high command in Pyongyang.

Some time later, the attitude of the senior NIS officer toward me began to change. He became more assertive, almost instructing me to write additional Op-eds and articles. Although our viewpoints were very similar, I told him firmly that I was “an independent scholar” and wrote only “on my own steam.”

He wasn’t deterred. He next showed up at a public meeting on North Korea at the Brookings Institution, from which I’d recently retired, and in front of a group I was talking with interrupted to ask whether or not I was going to write another article on North Korea. The damaging impression he left, intended or not, was that he would have some role in what I would be writing. Afterward, when he asked if we could meet alone, I told him to meet me at a nearby private club, Cosmos, where I was a member.

It was mid-afternoon, and we were the only two people in the bar. He handed me a manila envelope containing mainly news clippings, and again pressed me to write Op-eds, in particular one that would highlight U.N. action critical of the DPRK’s human rights record. This time, though, he added, “We’re prepared to pay you.”

“Look,” I said, “I’ve told you before I’m independent. I don’t write articles for governments.”

“Think about it,” he responded.

But there was a third person in the bar listening to us, I suddenly noticed, a man who had come in quietly and taken a seat very near us in the otherwise empty barroom. Was he an FBI agent, I wondered? Whatever, it was definitely time for me to exit.

But as I readied to leave, the Korean reached into his bag and brought forth a bottle of French wine. “At least you can accept this gift for the holidays,” he said. I didn’t see how I could turn that down without causing unnecessary offense, so I awkwardly took it, thanked him, and departed.

That night, my husband and I brought the bottle to a dinner party. Because we no longer drank or paid attention to vintages, we’d paid little attention to the label. But when our hostess saw the bottle, she gave out a small shriek: “Oh my goodness, you didn’t have to bring extravagant wine like that. We’re friends, after all.” Apparently it was worth well over a hundred dollars.

On the way home, my husband, a retired career diplomat, advised me to cut short my relationship with the South Korean. “The cost of that wine told me something that it should also tell you,” he said. “You must get out of this. You don’t know what you’re involved in and what it’s developing into. Today it’s Op-eds, tomorrow it will be something else.”

Indeed, it seemed proof of the time-worn adage: There are friendly countries, but there are no friendly intelligence services.

Not long thereafter, I asked a State Department official to contact the FBI about the incident, and the South Korean agent didn’t bother me again.

**Persistence**

Two years later, in 2011, another South Korean “diplomat” made contact. He came up to me at a meeting at which I was speaking and said, “I’ve come here today to meet you.” I was a little wary this time around, but when he invited me for lunch, I accepted. I’d become co-chair of a high-powered NGO dealing with human rights in North Korea and thought I could benefit from the latest information from, and cooperation with, officials from Seoul. Plus, South Korea was our ally, so I readily introduced him to people and organizations in Washington involved with human rights.

But once again I found myself becoming caught in a web of intrigue. Because I didn’t drive and taxi service was unreliable back then, he and his driver would sometimes offer me a lift to and from some gatherings. I thought they were just being friendly in return for the cooperation I gave them. But one morning when they came to pick me up, the “diplomat” told me, halfway through the ride, that he’d been disinvited from the meeting to which we were going. Some of the South Koreans speaking there, he said, didn’t want embassy officials present.

“Well,” I said, “you shouldn’t be driving me to a meeting you’re not going to.” No, he said, they were pleased to do so and insisted they would take me home as well.

Two hours later, they were waiting for me outside the meeting place. Finally, my alarm bell went off. They expected me to debrief them on what had transpired inside. This I could not do, so I answered with broad generalities and gave no specifics as to who said what, even as the officer kept trying to pry them out of me. I could feel his impatience, and see his expression of frustration at my responses.

Then this happened: When we arrived at my home, the now very obvious intelligence officer jumped out, stood in front of me, smiled and gave me a hug—which was quite out of line. And I wondered if the driver might have taken an incriminating photo. I’d gotten the impression from his involvement in the discussion that he was more than a driver.

In 2022, when another conservative South Korean government took office, Seoul again evinced a strong interest in human rights conditions in the north. This time, their intelligence agents in Washington seemed to understand not to make any untoward requests or place any pressure on me. That made it possible for me to readily accept speaking engagements or moderate panels at government-sponsored events, for which I was paid a modest honorarium like the other participants. It was a far healthier arrangement than before and more in line with how allied governments should be behaving toward one another’s citizens.

**Enter Pyongyang**

Above the Demilitarized Zone, the North Koreans were also reading my articles and statements. To them, though, I was part of the “human rights racket,” the epithet they used to describe experts and NGOs publicizing the regime’s repression of human rights. My own articles and statements focused on their famines, prison camps and the forced repatriation of those trying to escape, with policy recommendations for the U.S. and U.N. North Korea’s response was cyber warfare. When I opened my computer one morning in the early 2010s, I found the entire screen bright red with a white skull and crossbones in the center. Other times, the keys on my keyboard stopped responding when I was writing about North Korea. On another occasion, the words I typed came on the screen in Russian. On my landline phone I heard voices, whirring, and the constant echo of my own talking, as if it were being recorded. When Verizon came to investigate, they told me my line was connected to someone else’s, but would provide no details.

Over the next decade, according to [reports](https://www.dailynk.com/english/digital-warfare-north-korea-evolving-cyber-arsenal-global-threats/), the cyber warfare arm of North Korea’s premier intelligence agency, the Reconnaissance General Bureau, enhanced its skills. The RGB’s Bureau 121, launched in 1998, had become adept at gaining access to foreign computers, planting malware, redirecting email messages and creating havoc, such as when it hacked Sony Pictures in 2014, causing it to cancel the opening of *The Interview*, a comedy lampooning Kim Jong Un.

Pyongyang’s efforts against its critics continued to intensify, as when the U.N. Commission of Inquiry’s 400-page [report](https://www.ohchr.org/en/hr-bodies/hrc/co-idprk/reportofthe-commissionof-inquiry-dprk) on massive human rights violations in North Korea came out.

By 2020, with the aid of artificial intelligence technologies, North Korea’s psywar agents became more sophisticated. Sometimes they posed as a Western government official, sending me flattering invitations such as, “Your name is frequently mentioned by both government officials and experts in the Republic of Korea as a leading authority on North Korean issues, and we would greatly value your expertise.” One morning, I found a message on my phone from someone identifying himself as a contractor from a Defense Department “crime center.” He said he had “evidence” that I had been “in email communication with a hacker” who was a “sought after” North Korean “gekko” (hacker slang for a slick predatory operator), targeting me and trying to gain access to my email. He said he wanted to learn more about me and would tell me more about the hacker if I would provide him with my email address. I did not respond.

**Sustained Pressure**

I also began receiving an unusual number of “invitations.” One was in 2023, to an all-day conference on North Korea at a leading think tank in Washington. The supposed organizer attached the agenda and asked if I would like to speak on one of the panels. After I chose one, the person wrote back to say that the conference planners had decided to “exclude” me from the meeting. I immediately emailed a note of protest to a senior official I knew at the organization.

“We would never exclude you from a meeting, Roberta,” he said, “but there is no meeting.” It turned out I’d been corresponding with North Korean intelligence agents and was advised I had probably compromised my computer by clicking on the link to the “meeting agenda” in one of the emails.

Other bogus invitations arrived in 2024 and 2025. One was supposedly from a well-known professor at Princeton doing a project on food security in North Korea, for which he wanted to interview me. But again I would have to click on a link to give my approval for being cited—a clear setup to get into my computer. Another invite appeared to come from the South Korean embassy in Washington, asking me to brief a group of officials on food aid. False again. Still another came from “the Embassy of the Netherlands” in Seoul asking for my “insights” on “transformative shifts…within North Korea and internationally” that “might influence or reshape the way we address human rights in North Korea.” Phony to the core.

Sometimes North Korean agents have pretended to be from Reuters, the Voice of America*,* Arirang (South Korea’s broadcasting network) or other media. They have sought my opinions about such things as the raising of North Korean human rights issues at the U.N., whether support was growing for the lifting of sanctions against Pyongyang, or what President Trump’s new security measures would mean for Asia, and more. At times, I and others involved with North Korean issues unfortunately took the bait and replied. Other times, I found it useful to provide them with information on prison camps, hunger, or forced repatriations, on the off chance it might influence their thinking. (Escapees from North Korea often report that what made them defect was access to information and viewpoints contrary to the regime’s official line.)

For those involved with human rights in North Korea, learning to scrutinize the email addresses coming in has become a necessary routine. So, too, is taking steps to prevent one’s own name from being used to send false invitations and documents to others. Recently, a think tank colleague asked me if I had a Hotmail account, which I don’t. He had received a Hotmail message with my name on it, providing a link to a document, which if opened, could have disabled his computer.

Being prepared to purchase new computers and phones and engage IT and other preventative specialists has become yet another intrinsic part of advocating for human rights in North Korea. When I returned to Washington after addressing a large audience of Korean Americans in Chicago in 2024, I found my landline phone to be dead and no heat in the house. Perhaps I was being paranoid, but Henry Kissinger’s legendary quip that “even a paranoid can have enemies” suddenly made a great deal of sense to me.

“We would never exclude you from a meeting, Roberta,” he said, “but there is no meeting.”

What should human rights groups, democratic governments, the United Nations and, yes, U.S. and friendly intelligence agencies do? They must more strongly thwart Pyongyang’s cyber attacks but also penetrate North Korea with information about the hunger, poverty, corruption and repression that define their country. As I wrote to one of North Korea’s fake journalists: “Change will only come about in North Korea if enough people in their country begin to know about” how truly bad their conditions are and how they compare with those in South Korea and other parts of Asia. Then they will begin to “separate their thinking” from the propaganda imposed upon them by an unusually ruthless but threatened dictatorship.

*Ms. Cohen was a deputy assistant secretary of state in the Jimmy Carter administration.*
