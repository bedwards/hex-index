India is a linchpin in the strategic and technological competitions between the US-led coalition and Beijing, yet US – India relations are experiencing a [sudden slide](https://orfamerica.org/orf-america-comments/slide-us-india-relations). The US and India share enduring strategic and economic interests that will outlast the present moment.

Not only is India more compatible with US strategic necessities and technological ambitions, but it is also a more reliable economic partner than the PRC in many ways. While China’s market is larger, US exports to India are growing faster. Choosing China over India would yield few short-term economic benefits and likely permanently entrench the CCP as the world’s dominant technological power.

**The causes of the drift**

Dhruva Jaishankar, head of the Observer Research Foundation’s DC office and son to India’s Foreign Minister, [holds](https://orfamerica.org/orf-america-comments/slide-us-india-relations) that two factors are driving a wedge between DC and Delhi: Pakistan and trade.

The US-India-Pakistan triangle has shifted in recent months, due in large part to Pakistan’s [strikingly successful](https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/2025/08/20/pakistan-trump-india-munir-oil/) charm offensive in DC. In March, Pakistan [helped the US arrest](https://www.voanews.com/a/us-pakistan-operation-to-capture-top-terrorist-signals-deep-counterterrorism-cooperation-despite-cold-ties-experts-say-/7998510.html) an ISIS leader responsible for a 2021 attack that killed 13 Americans in Afghanistan. Meanwhile, after hostilities broke out between India and Pakistan in May, President Trump then [claimed credit](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c5y3599gx4qo) for brokering a ceasefire, triggering concerns in Delhi, which has a [deep-seated opposition](https://www.newindianexpress.com/explainers/2025/May/17/why-india-rejects-mediation-onkashmir) to third-party mediation. Islamabad also [nominated](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cwyx5yw8y28o) Trump for a Nobel Peace Prize while [signing a letter of intent](https://on.ft.com/3HsRwQX) with the founders of the Trump-backed crypto group World Liberty Financial. Pakistan’s careful courtship is paying off, for now: the U.S. currently applies lower tariffs to Pakistan ([19 percent](https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/2025/08/20/pakistan-trump-india-munir-oil/)) than it does to India ([50 percent](https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/2025/08/20/pakistan-trump-india-munir-oil/)).

Tariffs on India are highly damaging. India is perhaps America’s [most important](https://warontherocks.com/2025/08/sprinters-marathoners-skeptics-on-the-future-of-ai-power/) long-term technological partner, and tariffs will slow growth in both economies. The impacts will be felt acutely in India, where [over 400 million individuals](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099722104222534584/pdf/IDU-25f34333-d3a3-44ae-8268-86830e3bc5a5.pdf) subsist on less than $3.65 a day. Still, goods made up only two-thirds of Indian exports to the U.S. in 2024, and services trade [remains tariff-free](https://ustr.gov/issue-areas/services-investment/services). Accordingly, while goods tariffs will bite the Indian economy, overall impacts will be limited.

**US – India economic and tech cooperation is crucial to competing with China**

A US-India trade war will also damage US exporters in an important growth market. While annual goods and services exports to China are up only $10 billion since 2017, shipments to India have risen $35 billion in the same period.

But raw trade totals fail to demonstrate the integration of the US and Indian economies – especially in tech. The US will very likely lose the technological competition with China if it cannot draw on Indian talent.

All people are created equal: intrinsic talent is distributed roughly equally around the world. If the US cannot draw talent from other sources, it will almost certainly lose the technological and strategic competition with the PRC. It’s a matter of simple arithmetic: China has over 1.4 billion individuals; the US comprises only 350 million. Accordingly, US workers need to be roughly 4x as productive as their Chinese counterparts – an increasingly impossible gap given China’s education advances. Strengthening the U.S. domestic education system while bringing in talent from India, the world’s largest country by population, and other places is an obvious strategy. Indeed, that’s exactly what’s occurred: India is the largest or second-largest exporter of computer scientists to the U.S., as Stanford’s HAI data shows.

To outcompete China in the AI race, the US needs to draw talent from everywhere. India is the most logical partner: it is the world’s largest country by population, is largely English-speaking, and shares a compatible political system.

Given the fundamental convergence in interests, and contrary to [some arguments](https://carnegieendowment.org/emissary/2025/08/trump-tariffs-risk-us-india-relations?lang=en), a rupture in US-India relations appears unlikely. Still, the danger to US-India relations is real. Both DC and Delhi need to repair the relationship, and quickly. Otherwise, both countries should prepare for a world dominated by Chinese technology and overseen by the Chinese Communist Party.
