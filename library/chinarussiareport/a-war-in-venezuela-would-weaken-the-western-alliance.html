This edition, co-authored with Imran Bayoumi of the Atlantic Council’s GeoStrategy Initiative, examines how a large-scale US military intervention in Venezuela would weaken the Western alliance to the benefit of Beijing and Moscow. Thanks for reading.

DC’s military buildup in the Caribbean Sea off the coast of Venezuela poses underappreciated risks to Europe. Amid escalating tensions, the U.S. has moved the world’s largest aircraft carrier, the USS Gerald R. Ford, to the region, B-52H Stratofortress bombers continue to fly off the northern coast of South America, and Reuters reported on November 23 that the U.S. planned to launch a new phase of operations in Venezuela, likely including covert action against the regime of Venezuelan strongman Nicolas Maduro. While President Trump is reportedly seeking to negotiate directly with Maduro, his administration is also threatening airstrikes and appears intent on pressuring the regime through covert means and potentially small-scale military attacks. If these maneuvers fail to break the Maduro regime, however, DC may consider a large-scale military intervention.

The U.S. should pressure Maduro, who is one of the world’s worst dictators, to leave power. But a large-scale military intervention in Venezuela is deeply unwise and could significantly weaken the U.S. and its allies in the competition with Beijing and Moscow. Any large-scale intervention, such as the one seemingly under consideration, would hold enormous direct costs: the bill for an expanded Operation Southern Spear, Washington’s campaign against alleged Maduro-linked drug traffickers, could well run into the billions of dollars; escalation could also lead to significant casualties or crowd out other defense investments. Trade-offs in the great power competition are already consequential: over a dozen U.S. ships are deployed around Venezuela instead of higher-priority theaters, such as the Indo-Pacific. Finally, diesel market disruptions will tighten supplies, pushing global prices higher and harming the United States and especially Europe. Russia, the world’s second-largest diesel exporter, would benefit; Europe, historically the world’s largest diesel importer, would face weakened terms of trade. If DC conducts a large-scale military campaign in Venezuela, it will weaken the Western alliance and empower the PRC.

Great Power Implications

With the future of U.S. action in Venezuela, either covert or overt, now a serious risk, it’s worth tracking the effects on the three largest external actors and regions: European nations, China, and Russia.

European nations

Europe faces outsized and underappreciated risks from a large-scale intervention in Latin America. While the effects of the intervention are unlikely to produce anything approaching the 2022 crisis, Europe’s reliance on diesel imports leaves it disproportionately exposed to wide-scale outages in Venezuela and Colombia.

Both Venezuela and Colombia are exporters of heavy and medium sour crude oil that is highly suitable for diesel markets. Europe, meanwhile, historically is the world’s largest diesel importer and the world’s largest diesel consumer, by far, according to data from the Energy Institute Statistical Review.

Europe’s diesel dependency creates acute vulnerabilities that cannot be quickly mitigated. Europe’s diesel inventories are low, its refineries cannot rapidly shift to alternative crude sources, and global middle distillate markets – including for diesel – are already supply-constrained. Diesel price shocks would further pressure German manufacturing and European transportation networks. Accordingly, European leaders face a difficult dilemma: either watch energy price spikes harm their economies and strengthen Russia, or oppose U.S. intervention and risk damaging transatlantic ties amid Ukraine negotiations.

Indeed, in addition to the economic costs, DC’s military intervention in Venezuela would drive political wedges in the transatlantic relationship. For instance, even though the U.S. and the UK have the closest security relationship in history, the United Kingdom has ceased sharing intelligence with the U.S. about suspected drug trafficking vessels in the Caribbean over fears of complicity in potentially illegal U.S. military strikes. On the sidelines of the G7, the French Foreign Minister asserted that U.S. boat strikes violate international law. European allies will be reluctant to speak out forcefully against a large-scale U.S. military intervention in Venezuela, given their greater interests in Ukraine and their already-fraught relationship with DC. Still, an intervention in Venezuela may tempt some European leaders to further pursue strategic autonomy and triangulate between DC and Beijing, especially if the intervention sparks another bout of global energy and food inflation.

China

China faces moderate economic costs in a Venezuela escalation but calculates it could accrue significant geopolitical benefits. Accordingly, if DC escalates, Beijing will diplomatically and rhetorically back Maduro but offer very little substantive support to Caracas. Beijing is extremely unlikely to provide military aid or defense industrial base support to Caracas, owing to geography, the observability of such assistance, and, most fundamentally, Beijing’s limited interests in Venezuela. The Chinese economy would face some inflationary pressures if global diesel and food prices rise. Beijing would reap outsized geopolitical gains, however, if the U.S. commits to another expensive, prolonged, and divisive military campaign.

While a U.S. military intervention would grant Beijing significant political opportunities, it would bring non-trivial economic costs. About 75 to 90 percent of Venezuela’s oil exports flow to China either directly or indirectly via transshipment through Malaysia. Assuming a (very high) estimate of 900,000 barrels per day (bpd) in crude oil exports to China, Venezuelan shipments would total about 8 percent of China’s total refinery imports of ~11.1 million bpd. While Venezuelan crude is less important to China than Russian (~2.2 million bpd) or Saudi (~1.6 million bpd) shipments, disruptions would affect specific (“complex”) refineries specializing in heavy crude processing. Accordingly, Chinese and global diesel prices would be impacted, especially if the war spreads horizontally to Colombia. If the war impacts Trinidad and Tobago’s ammonia production, however, global food prices would rise modestly, impacting China’s food security. China’s exports to Venezuela are trivial from Beijing’s perspective but account for about 35 percent of Venezuela’s imports.

Politically, China regards Venezuela as a secondary priority, even within the region. For instance, Brazil under President Lula blocked the inclusion of Venezuela and Nicaragua into BRICS in 2024; Beijing’s acquiescence suggests that it prioritizes ties with Brazil over Venezuela. Still, Beijing has recently signaled some support for Caracas via Xi Jinping’s November 2025 birthday letter to Maduro detailing China’s continued backing for Venezuela’s sovereignty and security. In 2023, the two sides announced a political upgrade in ties to an “all-weather strategic partnership.”

China-Venezuela ties are far less developed than China-Brazil relations, suggesting that Beijing will offer some diplomatic support for Caracas, but will first look to Brasilia, its most important regional partner.

Russia

Russia, the world’s second-largest exporter of crude oil and second-largest exporter of diesel, will be a primary beneficiary of any U.S. military intervention in Venezuela. If a conflict expands horizontally, especially to oil-exporting Colombia or ammonia-exporting Trinidad and Tobago, supply outages will make Russia’s commodity and refined products exports – especially diesel – more valuable.

Russia often ranks as the world’s largest seaborne diesel exporter, although the full-scale invasion of Ukraine reshaped global diesel and crude markets. Instead of being processed at European or Russian refineries, Russian crude is now shipped to other markets – usually India, China, or Turkey – where it is then refined and re-exported, often to Europe. In the event of a large-scale U.S. military intervention, and outages across Venezuela and possibly Colombia, however, Russia’s diesel-suitable crude oil will become more valuable as feedstock or a product.

Let’s examine a highly conceivable scenario, where Venezuela faces supply outages of 500 kbpd for 6 months amid a large-scale U.S. military intervention. Assuming that crude and other non-diesel prices rise $5/barrel and diesel prices increase $10/barrel, all else being equal, then Russia will earn about $26 million per day from crude and other non-diesel exports ($5 lift with 5.2 million barrels exported per day) and another $8 million per day from diesel exports ($10 lift with 800,000 barrels exported per day). Assuming flat export volumes, we can estimate that Russia’s export earnings would rise about $34 million every day the conflict persists, or by about $6 billion for a 6-month conflict.

While the Kremlin has an interest in stimulating a conflict and weakening the U.S.-led alliance, any aid from Moscow to Caracas will likely take the form of rhetoric (and potentially intelligence support), not direct military or financial aid. Though Russia has had success in supporting its allies in the past, most notably Assad in Syria following the Arab Spring, the situation in Venezuela is different. Russia is much less capable of military power projection now than it was a decade ago, especially since its full-scale invasion of Ukraine has made it dependent on other countries, especially China, for defense industrial base needs. Finally, Russian security services will likely have little influence in a Venezuela contingency. Latin America is not traditionally a high priority for Russian security services, while their intelligence capabilities (especially SIGINT) have been severely degraded by the full-scale invasion.

Military escalation in Venezuela could severely weaken the transatlantic alliance

A large-scale, Washington-led military intervention in Venezuela poses major risks to the transatlantic alliance and global diesel prices. In addition to alienating key allies like the UK, France, Germany, and Colombia, gunboat diplomacy towards Venezuela runs the risk of emboldening and legitimizing China’s similar approach in the South China Sea, which Beijing uses to expand its territory and bully its neighbors. The U.S. has legitimate interests in expanding its presence in Latin America and the Caribbean and the actions of China and Russia in the region undermine the national security of the United States. Still, as Washington considers future actions towards Caracas, it should define a clear strategy that aligns means with ends and sets realistic objectives.

The situation in Venezuela is dire. We don’t presume to know how to fix it. Washington should back Venezuelans’ efforts to restore democracy and liberty, but not at the risk of sacrificing U.S. national security interests vis-à-vis Beijing and Moscow or undermining European allies and partners.
