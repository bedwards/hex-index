Gazprom CEO Alexey Miller claimed his company struck a [major natural gas deal](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-02/russia-china-sign-deal-for-power-of-siberia-2-gas-pipeline-ifx) between China and Russia over the Power of Siberia-2 (PoS-2) pipeline. I’m deeply skeptical the 50 billion cubic meter/year pipeline will move forward, however.

-   The pipeline will almost certainly be significantly more expensive than PoS-1, increasing already-challenging financing burdens;
    
-   contract terms (especially price) have not been announced;
    
-   China has energy alternatives (heat pumps, electric vehicles, etc) that it can produce domestically;
    
-   pipeline natural gas is less fungible and flexible than liquefied natural gas;
    
-   and Beijing understands that inking a megaproject with the Kremlin would be an immensely provocative act necessitating reciprocal measures by DC and Brussels.
    

Indeed, focusing on the megaproject may overlook the smaller but substantial deals that are \*far\* more likely to move forward. If you’re interested in learning more, please see this [analysis](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/why-china-and-russia-are-unlikely-to-move-the-power-of-siberia-2-pipeline-forward/) with my colleague Landon Derentz.

Significantly, Xi did not mention a Power of Siberia-2 pipeline deal in the public readout of an [address](http://politics.people.com.cn/n1/2025/0907/c1024-40558373.html) to the China-Russia friendship committee, suggesting negotiations are ongoing, at minimum – and possibly hinting that Miller spoke out of turn.

This edition is organized into two parts. The first section includes various analyses on the pipeline. If you wrote something and I missed it, please send it to me. The last section includes some short-form commentary on northern Chinese natural gas demand. Much more to come.

### **PoS-2 analyses**

[Power of Siberia 2: A litmus test of Sino-Russian relations - Henrik Wachtmeister for MERICS](https://merics.org/en/comment/power-siberia-2-litmus-test-sino-russian-relations)

If PoS2 proceeds, it will further cement the asymmetrical Sino-Russian relationship, with Russia becoming even more dependent on China for its energy exports. China, in turn, secures another cheap source of energy, insulated from western interference and Middle Eastern volatility, while simultaneously providing a channel for much-needed hard currency to its most important strategic partner in its quest to reshape the world order.

[How the Power of Siberia 2 Deal Could Reshape Global Energy – Jane Nakano and Leslie Palti-Guzman for CSIS](https://www.csis.org/analysis/how-power-siberia-2-deal-could-reshape-global-energy)

While Russia and China have not yet agreed on the crucial detail called “price,” the Power of Siberia 2 pipeline agreement could incrementally rewrite the geopolitical map, where Russia remains relevant to the global gas market while the U.S. role diminishes. All the while, China may continue its rise as a global leader with clout of its own.

[Power of Siberia 2: Russia’s Pivot, China’s Leverage, and Global Gas Implications – Anne-Sophie Corbeau, Erica Downs & Tatiana Mitrova for Center on Global Energy Policy at Columbia](https://www.energypolicy.columbia.edu/power-of-siberia-2-russias-pivot-chinas-leverage-and-global-gas-implications/)

But beyond the rhetoric and symbolism lie significant structural challenges. First, with the pipeline, Russia would be almost entirely reliant on a single buyer — China — giving the latter even greater leverage than it already has. According to the [Institute for Energy and Finance (FIEF)](https://newizv.ru/news/2025-05-14/ekspert-gromov-kak-bolshaya-politika-spasaet-gazprom-i-silu-sibiri-2-436869), a Moscow-based think tank closely connected to Russia’s corporate energy sector and policymaking circles, while the technical and feasibility groundwork for PoS2 is complete, “over the past two years, negotiations on the implementation of this project have been effectively frozen at the initiative of the Chinese side.” If and when the project is built, it will likely be on China’s terms.

….

For both Russia and China, the new memorandum is a symbolic gesture. The final pricing and contractual terms will ultimately reveal the balance of gains: the “take or pay” clauses will shape Russia’s returns and China’s flexibility, while the price will show whose negotiating hand is the strongest. More broadly, the memorandum indicates that the landscape of global energy relations is shifting, with both countries signaling to the US that its LNG may be less needed than anticipated.

[Why China and Russia are unlikely to move the Power of Siberia-2 pipeline forward – Joseph Webster and Landon Derentz for the Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/why-china-and-russia-are-unlikely-to-move-the-power-of-siberia-2-pipeline-forward/)

The pipeline is unlikely to advance — at least not with great urgency. Moscow’s enthusiasm for the project is not matched by Beijing.

Still, Gazprom’s announcement is significant. While media attention is understandably fixated on the potential 50 billion cubic meters per year (bcm/yr) from PoS-2, Miller also announced capacity expansions on the first Power of Siberia (PoS-1) pipeline and the Far Eastern Route totaling 8 bcm/yr. If true, these incremental volumes from capacity expansions would supplement other Russian natural gas exports to China already occurring directly via pipeline connections and Arctic LNG 2 and indirectly via Central Asia. Altogether, these smaller expansions approach the scale of another PoS-1 pipeline over time, even if not in a single megaproject.

Either strategically or inadvertently, Beijing and Moscow may be adopting a “Moneyball approach” to natural gas cooperation: while they may not be able to build a megaproject, they may be re-creating the volumes in the aggregate via several smaller projects.  

[Power of Siberia 2: Significant Shift in Global Gas Markets or Another Pipe(line) Dream? - Elizabeth Wishnick of CNA and Columbia for China’s Resource Risks](https://chinasresourcerisks.substack.com/p/power-of-siberia-2-significant-shift)

Russian President Vladimir Putin was enthusiastic about the pipeline in his comments to journalists, hailing the “[mutually beneficial agreements](http://www.en.kremlin.ru/events/president/transcripts/press_conferences/77914)” signed after protracted negotiations. “Everyone is satisfied, everyone is happy with this result, to be honest, and I am too,” Putin said.

Mongolian Prime Minister Gombojav Zandanshatar was equally effusive, calling Power of Siberia 2 an “[unprecedentedly large-scale project, not in a century but in a millennium](https://montsame.mn/en/read/377236).” Although the Prime Minister foresees great opportunities for Mongolia in the future with the construction of the pipeline, the Mongolian government is not counting on them any time soon, [omitting gas from this Russian pipeline](https://www.upstreamonline.com/production/mongolia-to-wrestle-with-energy-shortfall-without-cheap-russian-gas/2-1-1699922) from its energy supply projections to 2028.

It remains unclear what China plans to do or what Beijing is signaling. By agreeing to a provisional memorandum, President Xi Jinping shows support for Putin, but without committing China to any specific gas purchase or investment of funds.

### **Further thoughts on north Chinese natural gas** ***demand***

Heat pumps constrain heating demand, while vehicle electrification [pressures](https://ieefa.org/resources/surging-electric-truck-sales-stall-chinas-lng-trucking-boom-0) diesel-to-LNG switching in trucking. Furthermore, LNG markets are [seasonally transforming](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/global-lng-markets-are-transforming-its-an-opportunity-to-enhance-energy-security/). Rising winter temperatures are lowering winter heating demand in northern climates like north China, while building efficiency has been among the most important factors in reducing European natural gas demand. These trends are pressuring natural gas demand projections: in late 2023, CNPC lowered its [forecast](https://www.spglobal.com/commodityinsights/en/market-insights/latest-news/lng/120823-chinas-natural-gas-demand-to-peak-in-2040-at-6059-bcm-etri) of China’s peak natural gas demand by 50 Bcm to ~600 Bcm in 2040.  

Even that may be optimistic. EVs and battery tech (including solid-state) have since made huge advances; Chinese renewables deployment is rapid but disproportionately concentrated in north China; the PRC continues to expand its already-massive coal fleet; storage batteries continue to fall in price; and the Chinese long-distance transmission build-out is accelerating. Renewables largely don’t *directly* compete with natural gas in north China, as regional natural gas consumption is driven by industrial use and city gas (heating). Still, electricity-driven, renewables-powered heat pumps can directly displace natural gas. China dominates [heat pump supply chains](https://www.iea.org/commentaries/is-a-turnaround-in-sight-for-heat-pump-markets) and the technology continues to improve, suggesting China has alternatives to Russian gas.

With China leading supply chains for heat pumps, EVs, batteries, and their enablers, and with these technologies rapidly improving, Beijing has clear domestic alternatives. There are few apparent commercial or even energy security reasons for Beijing to strike a deal with Moscow over PoS-2, suggesting that any agreement would be motivated primarily by anti-Western animus.
