In the past two weeks, I’ve been thinking a lot about this newsletter and how to proceed with it. It’s Advent season, after all—a time that invites reflection, expectation, and the quiet anticipation of something new. The word “advent” itself carries this dual meaning: the coming of something — but also the end of something else. As the year winds down, we’re offered a new chance to redefine ourselves, to begin our lives again.

This idea of renewal has always been part of the season. Christmas, whether you celebrate it religiously or not, is about rebirth. Not only the rebirth of nature after the hiatus of winter, but also a spiritual rebirth that we experience every year. It is a moment to pause, to reflect, and to ask ourselves: who do I want to be in the year ahead? What do I want to change? What do I want to leave behind?

For Heidegger, being human means precisely this: to reinvent oneself. To be Dasein is not to be a fixed thing, a tool with a single purpose, waiting to be used. It is to be a being that defines itself through its choices, its projects, its openness to the future. But our societies do not want us to do this. The rules of capitalism, the structures we live in — they want us to be tools. They need us to be tools in order to function. The economic unit that is the human being must be, in Heidegger’s terms, zuhanden—ready-to-hand—for the machinery of society to keep turning.

A bus driver retreats into the background of our attention and becomes part of the bus. A cook or a waiter are perceived as nothing but available, ready-to-serve parts of the machinery of a restaurant. A teacher or a philosophy blogger are nothing but cogs in a system that provides knowledge for us to consume — so that we can eventually replace these cogs when they fail because of old age, reduced performance, or any other reason. This makes it necessary that we all train the next generation not only to be educated in particular ways, specific to the bus driver, the cook, or the teacher — but also to make them work in exactly the same way, so that they are ready-to-hand to replace us when we retire without upsetting the surrounding system that provides each service, without disrupting the supply that satisfies the society’s demand. The Chilean thinkers Maturana and Varela called this process autopoiesis — the self-creation and self-preservation of living systems. But it also applies to societies, schools, and transport companies. Every one of these systems depends on processes of self-creation to fabricate the units of which it is made of, so that it can go on existing independently of the failure and decay of particular units that comprise it.

A teacher or a philosophy blogger are nothing but cogs in a system that provides knowledge for us to consume.

But this is an inherently inhuman process. It is not what we are meant to be. When we allow ourselves to be reduced to mere tools, we lose something essential. As Kant would say, we become means rather than ends. And that is not only a moral failure—it is a failure of our humanity. It is our duty, and our only real chance at being truly human, to break out of this mold. To reclaim our agency. To become, again and again, the authors of our own lives, even if this means being less available to society, being less convenient, being less useful to it for its own ends.

I’ve written about this here on Daily Philosophy — how young people today often feel disconnected from work, how they can perceive their working lives as meaningless, just serving the ends of someone else, and often those of an anonymous corporation that does not care about them as persons. They are not wrong. This is the capitalist world what we created. But there is a way out of it, and this does not necessarily involve boycotting work, “lying low,” or “touching fish.”

It just means that we need to see ourselves again as individual human agents, as beings, like Aristotle said, whose virtues are moderated by rationality, and who strive to reach that very personal goal of a good life: the flourishing state of eudaimonia. This is not achieved by refusing to work, or dissociating oneself from one’s surroundings, but by virtuously engaging with the world in order to improve it and to claim one’s own, specific place in it. When we refuse to become identical cogs, autopoetic sub-units, and instead demand the space to develop into what makes us unique, we claim a place in the functional hierarchy of society that makes us indispensable. As in nature, where one lake can never replace another. One tree cannot replace another. One animal cannot replace another. So also in human society, we can create and claim our very own personal niche, the place that needs us very specifically, so that the whole can function in the best way, so that it can follow its path towards the flourishing of all.

A good tree, Aristotle would say, is a tree that is not only focused on its own well-functioning, but that contributes to the flourishing of all the creatures that depend on it: birds that nest in its branches, microbes and fungi populating its roots, travellers taking a break in its shade. In the same way, a good human being is not one who can indifferently be stuck in an arbitrary place as an exchangeable employee — but one who develops their very own talents and strengths, their own world-view and wisdom, so that they benefit the world in their very own, very personal way.

This brings me back to Daily Philosophy. Over the past few years, it has grown from a personal blog into something more like an online magazine. We’ve published the work of many brilliant scholars and writers, and I’m deeply grateful for every one of them. But in the process, I’ve found myself drifting away from what this project was originally meant to be.

I’ve become less of a writer and more of an editor. Less of a voice, and more of a platform. And while I’m proud of what we’ve all built together, I also feel that something has been lost. Daily Philosophy has become a collection of thematically diverse articles, but it no longer expresses a coherent worldview, a personal style, or a consistent way of interpreting what’s happening around us. And I miss that.

So I’ve been thinking: how can I reinvent DP? How can I make it more like what I envisioned in the beginning? Because even projects like this one can become part of the machinery that constrains us. They can become obligations, routines, molds that we pour ourselves into until we forget why we started in the first place.

That’s why I’ve been largely absent from this newsletter in recent weeks and months. I’ve been trying to rediscover my connection to it. To figure out what I want it to be—not just for you, the readers, but for me, the person who creates and manages it.

And I think that I have found a way forward.

Starting in the new year, I’ll be separating the “magazine” content from the newsletter content more clearly. Instead of including everything in the newsletter, I’ll publish the full contributed articles on the daily-philosophy.com website, and simply link to them here. That way, you won’t miss anything—you’ll always be one click away from the latest article. And our authors, along with outside readers who aren’t subscribed to the newsletter, will still have access to the guest articles on the main site.

But I will return with a more personal voice to this newsletter. I will inject more of my own philosophy, my own worldview, my own take on things into it. If you want to support the project, both the public-facing educational magazine, and the more personal blog, you’ll still be able to do so by subscribing to the newsletter. But if you’re only interested in the guest articles, you can read them on the main site without being bothered by my musings here.

This new structure will also allow for more flexibility. Until now, we’ve had a strict rhythm of one article per week. That meant I sometimes had to force myself to write something just to fill the space when no guest article was available. And at other times, when we had too many submissions, authors had to wait weeks to see their work published. Neither of these situations was ideal.

With this new arrangement, we can decouple the publishing cadence of the magazine from the rhythm of the newsletter. I’ll aim to write these personal reflections weekly, but I’ll also give myself the space to skip a week if I need to. And the magazine can publish articles as they come in, without being tied to the newsletter schedule.

Let’s try it and see how it works. And please—tell me what you think. I’d love to hear your thoughts in the comments.

And so now, with Advent upon us, let’s not just see it as the coming of the holiday, the coming of presents, or the coming of Christ—depending on your religious views. Let’s also see it as the coming of another opportunity. A chance to change ourselves. To define ourselves as what we want to be. To leave the Zuhandenheit behind and start thinking about how we can become ends in ourselves. How we can become Dasein. How we can bring our own vision of who we are to life.

Have a great Advent time!
