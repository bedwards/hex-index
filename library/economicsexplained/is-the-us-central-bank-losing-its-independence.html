Now, to understand what central banks like the Fed are supposed to do, one of the best places to start is with the historical problems that necessitated their formation. A very brief history of central banks is that they informally started with European banking houses set up by cities to manage their debts and issue coinage to facilitate easy trade.

The first formal central banks were the bank of England established in 1694 and before that the Sphargus Ricks Bank of Sweden which was established in 1688. This is the same institution that awards the Nobel Prize in economics. was a fun little side note. In these early days, they still hadn't quite grown into their role, but they were still operating as a bank for banks and for the government, as well as managing a universally recognized currency, which made commerce within these countries much smoother.

The US actually had a similar kind of early central bank starting all the way back in 1791. Alexander Hamilton awarded a charter to a bank that was founded partially by the government and a group of private investors to remove frictions in the financial system. It would also distribute and manage a universally recognized currency that people could trade freely with across all of the new United States, confident that private banks would accept these notes because the first Bank of America back in Washington guaranteed them. However, this first bank was abolished in 1811 because its charter was not renewed.

But then it was brought back again in 1816 before it was once again abolished in 1836 because the president at the time, Andrew Jackson, did not like how much power the institution had. something that would become a common theme amongst a lot of central banks and presidents into the future. Now, the thing with these banks is that they were just regular banks with a profit motive and special privileges given to them by the government. This made it hard for them to balance the priorities of returns to their investors with doing what was in the best interest of the new nation while also ensuring that they didn't anger lawmakers who could take away their charter of special privileges.

They were compromised at best. Unfortunately, after they were abolished, things didn't get much better as basically every regional bank started issuing their own depository notes, which made trade difficult because there was no guarantee that one bank would accept another bank's notes. And with so many different pieces of paper circulating around, forgeries were rampant. Now, at this time, it was important to remember that the US dollar technically already existed, but there was no paper notes, and the actual coinage was too limited to be used practically for day-to-day commerce.

So, private banks made up their own and people just accepted the numbers they wrote down on those pieces of paper. Realistically, it's not too dissimilar from today. If you really think about it, we don't carry around paper notes anymore. We have electronic bank accounts with numbers that a private banking institution writes down for us.

We assume that the numbers in our bank accounts will play nicely with the numbers in other banks. And instead of being secured by designs that are hard to forge, it's secured by electronic systems that are hard to hack. But hold up a bank account and a phone and old private paper bill and it would be very hard to explain the difference to an alien. Either way, these issues all came to a head in 1907 with a run on these independent, overleveraged and poorly regulated banks, which nearly crashed the entire financial system.

A group of financeers led by JP Morgan acted as a lender of last resort to give these failing institutions enough cash to continue their operations. But this showed the clear need for a more formal centralized system to stop the same thing from happening again. This led to the formation of the Federal Reserve banking system of the United States in 1913. 20 years later, gold convertability was suspended for citizens and the paper US dollar as we know it today was truly born.

At the time, this new institution only really had one responsibility, to avoid bank runs like what happened in 1907. It did this by being a place for private banks to deposit their cash and then lending money to any member bank if they saw a big spike in withdrawals that they didn't have enough cash on hand to fulfill. This would avoid a situation where a bank had to reject withdrawals which would cause a panic on the entire system. Now, this admittedly oversimplified history lesson is important because throughout their different incarnations, they were all trying to do one thing.

Create a reliable and flexible medium of exchange, which meant distributing, protecting, and managing a currency. Now, some versions of a central bank were more successful than others, but this was still the end goal. Over time, as more and more countries adopted their own central banks, it became clear that by being a bank for banks, they could achieve other goals in the economy as well. They could control inflation, which was just a function of how much of their currency was needed for a given amount of goods and services.

But they could also indirectly control employment, which by extension also meant economic growth. In the USA, these responsibilities were formalized in 1977 as the dual mandate to stabilize prices and maintain maximum employment. Most central banks around the world have similar responsibilities, usually listed on their website if you happen to be interested in looking up what your bank is formally working towards. So, how do they do this?

Central banks are very powerful institutions, but they actually don't have that many tools at their disposal to achieve these goals. Unlike, say, the federal government of the USA, which can just go out and employ people through big projects or hiring at government agencies, the Fed can't just hire a whole bunch of people to achieve their goal of maximum employment. Instead, they have two levers to pull, and that's pretty much it. Of course, the first is interest rates.

If inflation is too high, the Fed can raise its target rate, which means people can earn more by saving money and will pay more to borrow money. This means that people and businesses are more motivated to save and less encouraged to spend, which will lower aggregate demand in the economy. And if demand falls without a subsequent fall in supply, then prices will also naturally fall as well as businesses try to attract the few people still spending money through sales or generally more competitive prices. The opposite is also true.

If interest rates are lowered, then people will end up spending less on repaying their debts, which means they'll have more money to spend on everything else, increasing aggregate demand. And again, assuming that supply stays stable, prices will rise as more money is chasing fewer goods. Broadly, rising prices is just inflation. Now, here's the thing, though.

Regular people and businesses can't go and borrow money from the Fed or make deposits there. Only banks and governments can do that. So, the Fed can only change the rate it gives out to other banks that keep money with them. This could be thought of as the interest that banks receive for keeping their money in a bank account for banks.

Now, if, for example, the Fed is offering a 4% overnight cash rate, this is effectively the money that they can get risk-free. So, if they're going to make loans, they're going to want a higher interest rate. This is just like if an average person could get 4% in their highinterest federally insured savings account and they were presented with an even slightly risky investment opportunity that paid 3% interest. Well, of course, they wouldn't take it.

This basically sets the floor on borrowing costs for private banks, which gets passed along to every other participant in the economy. So, to influence prices, it's a bit of a Rube Goldberg machine. The Fed adjusts its overnight rate to influence banks, to influence borrowing and lending, to influence demand, to influence prices. Interest rates are kind of like the brake pedal on the economy.

If things are getting out of hand, they can be used to slow down inflation. However, in doing so, they will also slow down economic growth. Now, with that crude analogy, the accelerator would be open market operations. This is where the Fed will just directly intervene in the market, usually buying up government bonds, which to the average person is the lowest risk interestbearing asset they can own.

The most extreme version of open market operations is something called quantitative easing, where the Fed will create money and use it to pump liquidity into the market by buying government bonds, corporate bonds, mortgage back securities, and sometimes even shares and other equity. The goal is to get money out there in times of crisis and then hopefully sell back these assets over time to take the extra money back out of circulation. Economists will often refer to this as the Fed's balance sheet, which is honestly a little bit confusing because it's not really like a balance sheet in the traditional business because traditional businesses aren't allowed to bippity boop their own cash into existence to acquire these assets in the first place.
