Messianism is one of the most dangerous ideas in the history of mankind. Not only is the most famous example of messianic movement predicated on the messiah in question being an ironic inversion on the expected soteriological quality, but the helplessness of the idea-thinker in relation to the realisation of messianic promise makes for a strange jitteriness, an uncomfortable “otherness” to the object of desire—they are in constant threat of becoming the agent of Infinite Resignation[1](#footnote-1), handing off their responsibility for their salvation to some “Big Other”, the exercising of free will to surrender one’s capacity to live life.

In the moment of the messianic recallibration of reality, the slate is cleaned of its impurities and the totality of existence is reanalysed in the context of this new “breaking through” that takes whatever came before, reorganises without destroying it, and presents something new to the world. The constant danger sits on the periphery: the cry for a messiah imposes the craving for power of the individual onto the perfect image in order to justify any and all acts towards any and all ends.[2](#footnote-2)

*In part, a Christian critique begins first by examining the world and finding traces of itself woven into secular temptations.*

When this object becomes yoked to the individual as a *mere* aesthetic object, an “other” that pulls him from his position and tears up the foundations of his world, it becomes a desire that breaks through all walls and runs roughshod through all enemies that stand between the convert and his salvific object: the Maoist struggler, the liberal resister, the Islamist terrorist—all of them are captured by the basic logic of secularity. If not for the particular, world-inverting presence of a messiah, there would be little to stop the Christian call to opposition against the world from descending into an anthropocentric, domineering philosophy that sharpens ploughshares into swords and salivates at the thought of “an eye for an eye”. The very mode for us to recognise the good becomes inverted by human ingenuity—*Vordenken*[3](#footnote-3) allows for the very good to become a temptation[4](#footnote-4).

## Labadie against Marxist “Science”

Laurence Labadie had little time for Christianity in the same way that slugs have little time for salt or that Richard Dawkins has time for writing philosophy worth reading. As was appropriate for a thinker swept up in the “demand of the times” in a world different from ours, the threat of Messianism had become a reinvented problem that needed to be understood in a newer, deprecated form: the threat of state socialist Marxism, especially in the totalising realisation of a particular Marxist theory as the stateful imposition of a Soviet flavour, the new variation of a theme now stripped of its eschatological baggage and reduceable to a science.[5](#footnote-5) As part of this science, of course, there is the strange value-judgement of the impending doom of the adversary—an almost mystical collapse of the one who opposed my desire.

Labadie wrote:

> “Like Seventh-Day Adventists they have continually expected each business depression to be the messiah of the revolution which was, of course, always just around the corner like Hoover prosperity. They continually expected some vague monster called The Capitalist System to crumble to the ground. At this time the wise men should stop in with the blue prints of the cooperative commonwealth and show the people how to produce for use instead of profit. Pie was to be had, not in the sky, but here on earth, by and bye.”[6](#footnote-6)

Brought about through the “Adventist-like” gawping ambition of predicting the coming revolution, the state socialist finds themself in a never-ending cycle of doom-and-lust: each individual moment must be an inevitable step along the path to the inevitable *eschaton*, just as the ancient scriptures predicted[7](#footnote-7).

> “One thing may be well to keep in mind: *Every movement resorting to the State as a way out of man’s predicament is but accelerating a drift the resultant of which is what is currently termed Fascism*. And I believe the road is not hampered by misguided humanitarians who deem collectivism a solution and who expect to achieve their aims through a “revolution” in which they expect to “take over the means of production”. Without well-defined aims for the immediate future, yet tempered by expediency, the present turn of events becomes highly problematical. For while there is always a possibility for the better, history shows also a possibility for something decidedly worse.”[8](#footnote-8)

In a sense, Labadie offers a deconstruction in that tart Kierkegaardian-Derridean flavour: underneath the pomp and prigmarole of scientific façade, the sand foundation is exposed to sunlight as little more than an inversion of the liberal lust for power over the other, for violence against the identified enemy of the “in-group”, that first and foremost wishes for a messianic clearing-of-the-slate without the commitment to some grander thought beyond it. Or, to put it another way, their science floats at the mercy of some irrationalism hidden from the publicly-acceptable discourse.

But, we might ask, how can this happen? These are scientific socialists! How does their science collapse?

### The Salivating Science

When we engage in political thought—or, for the Kierkegaardian, similar fields that require the active engagement of the individual's life is an essential aspect of thinking itself, e.g., ethics, theology—the possibility of analysing “from nowhere” is an essentially obfuscating desire that covers over the cracks of the wayward messianist. In hiding from the reality of one’s desires when a proposal is offer, often hiding behind “straightforwardness” as a method of communication and “objectivity” as a method of thought, we find that the individual who would become “the One”[9](#footnote-9) that wields the crowd is often little more than a manipulator, a moulder and justified of the authority of “common sense”—a thing that was always altogether uncommon and altogether nonsensical prior to the point it took on its new guise. The liberal agent, exposing a desire for power in the hungry maw hanging agape at the thought of dictating *ought*s and *ought not*s to the scattered populace, uses the malleability of language to destabilise all categories and assert a selection from amongst them as the *objectively* best—but this desire on which all is built, as is all desires, is still merely a subjective desire.

In this sense, we find the idealism at the heart of both the liberal project which Marx had criticised and also the Marxist project[10](#footnote-10) which Labadie had criticised: there is a form of society, an image of “what may be”, which must come about in order for freedom to be realised. Hidden inside the language of the professor, of the dialectician, these sworn enemies fight over the right to seize the object of the same theme. Sitting on the horizon, “the Revolution”, this mythical event of messianic justice, attempts to seize the Christian crown and obfuscate the source of its reality: the rupture will serve *our purposes*, says the idealist, who has elected that their idea of reality is, indeed, actually reality itself.

### Bring Labadie Back to Ground

Labadie’s position, overlooking “all the kingdoms of the world in a moment of time” and threatening to “throw himself down”, is a tempting one. We find, firstly, an analysis that brings us to the point of doubt about what kind of socio-political claims we can feel confident about—and what others we complement as being outstanding examples of speculative philosophy, beautiful and defined by their fiction.

His adversary tempts him to view this fumble, this final misstep of the obviously muddleheaded bourgeois, as the very opportunity which allows Marx's thought (or, more appropriately, the flag-waving, utopian inversion of Marx's thought) to finally emerge from being mere philosophy and blossom in the full-blooded, teeth-gnashing reality of opportunism. Labadie refuses: the blueprint of a perfect society, the very central notion of utopianism that Marx himself saw as the creeping heart of humanity's desire to dominate the other, is a complete fabrication that hides a more fundamental desire to capture society and subject it to one's stipulations that, all the while, happily parades itself as science to the scientifically-minded eyes of the gawping crowd.

Much like Vigilius Haufniensis, S. K.’s pseudynmous atheist assessor of the notion of sin *qua* object of psychological inquiry[11](#footnote-11), Labadie's temptor does not fool him by way of programmatic and systematic language—no, the very language which can be used to transform the One's desire to control into the very entertainment that the controlled adore does not hide that basic desire from the sceptical eye. As Barrett notes, while “Vigilius often does sound clinical and disengaged, his voyeuristic fascination with sin is palpable.”[12](#footnote-12) Such is the way of humanity’s ever-present slyness[13](#footnote-13)—there’s always a way to misinterpret and misunderstand the viewpoint that demands our detachment when the object of our desire draws out the subjective desire for power and for the power to turn the neighbour underfoot.

The thing that Labadie failed to recognise is that the papers he poured over, the ink he spilt, the endless rejections from editors and those even less worthy to judge the artistic and radical merit of a piece of writings—they are little more than that. While a particular book might uncover that embarrassing earnestness in us that turns out the “locked jam” of our inwardness to the world or a particular article might expose some radical nerve, now twinged, in our backs that drives us to action—they are dead and inert and little more than that. A book is merely a book, a selection of papers in a binding; without a human touch, without the human decision to lend it authority, it’s merely papers in a binding. Ink is merely ink, black and blotted

> “The Christian language uses the same words we men use, and in that respect desires no change. But its use of them is qualitatively different from our use of them; it uses the words inversely for Christianity makes manifest one sphere more or a higher sphere than the one in which we men naturally live, and in this sphere ordinary human language is reflected inversely.
> 
> …
> 
> And so we let Christianity talk away—and afterwards preach it in our own language and call it Christianity. As in music we speak of transposing a part to a key different from that in which it was originally written, so the Christian language is wholly and entirely qualitatively different from our language at every point, even though we use the same words. Christendom’s great feat was to transpose Christian language back into the old wretched gabble—and in this way we all have become Christians.”[14](#footnote-14)

For Labadie, there is no particular object that draws us out into the wide-eyed gawp that legitimates our unsettledness with life. For S. K., there is One Who “draws unto Him” (***John 12:32***)[15](#footnote-15). The distinction in these opposites comes from the understanding that this thing, the *me* that I have in some way, is not mine to do with as I might; it floats in the groundlessness of reality, neither bringing itself into this state of existence nor deciding to be such-and-such a thing that achieves such-and-such a goal. Only by recognising that this groundlessness is the opportunity for existence and that existence must constantly travel *forward*, must be *pulled* from the passive intellectualism of political philosophy—that is when we overcome Labadie’s nihilism, through sojourning in a way which surrenders our right to make *our* ideas the source of our plan for society.
