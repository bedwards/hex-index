Breakthroughs are rare by their very nature. If we see the intellectual capacities and norms of particular human groups throughout history, from prehistory until this very time that we live within, they are typified by the discordant collection of ideas that combine and produce a “discourse” which dominates the age. As the produced and reproduced mode of life that proceeds from this amalgamated discourse starts to tighten and constrict under the weight of its contradictions, the struggling presence of “common sense” or “received knowledge” starts to take on the characteristics of a demiurge that hangs over creation and applies pressure to those who would resist against it, if only they could find a way to manoeuvre around it. Face to face with “the limit” of the knowable1, the agent finds themselves contending with the episteme of the age and attempting to unravel the problem of knowledge itself, where only knowledge can suffice as a tool—it is, by its nature, a restriction imposed upon us.2

Of course, not all breakthroughs are always so grand. Indeed, prior to the point where the discursive reality around us crumbles to nothing (a veritable fantasy of the intellectualists, inverting the radical political manipulator’s desire for “The Revolution”3 into increasingly grating efforts to establish new intellectual foundations, the new territory on which Babel shall be erected4—regardless of whether such a thing is actually worth such high praise), there is a question of the minimal condition on which this breakthrough could emerge: in the mind and life of that single individual who turns around to see the world in a way as to yet unrealised.

For this author, my reader, I found Karl Barth’s unveiling of Christianity as not a civilising mission to the world5 utterly liberating: it is not the task of God’s elect to conform the world to its will, so much as merely to be and become the church in the world’s presence.6 And, unlike the accelerationist tendencies of the radical extremes of the world, I’m afraid that the aesthetic desire for revolution—for the violent upturning of academy or society—misdiagnoses the path forward and, as such, can only ever continue the logic of some preexisting state.

To expose this misdiagnosis, however, like exposing all misdiagnoses, is often a difficult task. As such, I start via negativa: how ought the church be, if not a civilising mission?

Harvey Cox and Bourgeois Authority

In a sense, this distinctive severing of state and church—or, more properly, world and church—delivers us into a position within the broadest possible understanding of the liberal theological tradition. However, as all good theologically homeless meanderers, this position is not an affirmation of the liberal position and, in fact, should be considered the critique from within it in order to break out of its archic claim to authority over the world. And, to do this, we must consult one of the great liberal theologians and radical political theologians of the 20th century: Harvey Cox.

For those who might be unaware of Cox’s work, he is a prolific and voracious interpreter of the Western theological tradition by way of wielding liberation theology back against the stifling presence of conservative thought. This radical attack on theological conservatism was, first and foremost, aimed to uproot the fundamentalist tendencies which would want to turn “Christian faith” into the excuse for an irresponsible reactionary turn against modernity and towards some imagined genealogical myth of “the good old days” where Christianity reigned supreme from on high. As the radical “avant-garde of God’s kingdom” that comes “from below and the edge”, Cox’s reorientation for theology was by identifying the broken and the oppressed as the ones who bear the face of Christ7:

The church is the avant-garde of the new regime, but because the new regime breaks in at different points and in different ways, it is not possible to forecast in advance just what appearance the church will have. It is not even possible to delineate the mission of the church “in the city.” Cities differ, and the visage of the church in any given urban environment will differ. There are, however, certain basic facts about urban secular life that will need to be taken into consideration by any church. Let us take the three elements of the church’s task as avant-garde—kerygma, diakonia, and koinonia—and see how they work out in a typical urban setting8.

At this point, we should be clear that Cox views the church as the avant-garde to the secular city—it is the kerygmatic function that “broadcasts the seizure of power”9 to the world, signalling a way for change that is not merely a Christian excuse for reactionary power-politics.

The Coxan position, granular and delicate in the way that all good theologies are, places the reality of Christian life outside of the received structures of Christendom and post-Christendom, i.e., outside of official state churches, bureaucratic church organisation, and also the church drawn into complicity with the state despite legislative separation. In the context of the apparent realisation of God’s liberatory mission to save humanity qua the secular city, it is the church that operates as a kind of “holy invisible dictator” (per Bakunin)10. From below and the edge, the church emerges in an environment of practice—of praxis—where the faith of the individual within a community and the community around the individual is the real expression of faith. Against the might of the church, the ekklesia appears.

We have seen this position taken over and over again in the history of theology, which might bring us to say that Cox is on the right track. Indeed, as is so often the case for the theological liberal, there is actually a great deal of value in these ideas: the recognition of faith, not the declaration; the importance of the individual believer, not the abstracted number of congregants; the position of a self-sacrificial faith as below the powerful, not the sword to wield against the world. In many senses, you, my reader, might even assume that I am an avowed fan of Mr. Cox and his liberationist theology.

Identifying the liminality of faith

However, before we get carried away, let us stop to consider what it means to take on this Coxan character qua “the avant-garde”:

“...in order even to be avant-garde, attention must be paid to which way the world is going, so that one can stay in front. The avant-garde continually must look back to see whether the world is following.”11

Much in the fashion of the beret-wearing, cigarette-smoking diletente, Cox poses the Christian avant-garde as the trend-setter of a world which is continuously marching in some direction—into the apparent unknown possibility of the future, where our present ways-of-life come under the strain of being abolished and realised through change and repetition, the Christian “vanguard” of human reality beats the drum and sets the pace. However, quite like everyone who has led a march before, there is an element of chaos that proceeds from the procession behind the leader; it is a process that draws the leader, this trend-setter, into conversation with the led and, by way of sheer force, the leader can become inverted into a mere figurehead at the mercy of the marchers.

In the same sense that the liberal “marketplace of ideas” is an open attempt at manipulating those drawn into it by way of promoting the authority and celebration of “chatter”, of life qua consumable content, is an assertion of bourgeois authority, Cox proposes that the church enter into debate with the world: it is the avant-garde, it is the oh so sophisticated bleeding edge of human knowledge—all it needs to do is to present itself as itself and the world will surely follow!

From the subversive liminal to the unknowingly subservient

But where precisely do we draw this image of the body of Christians—or, really, even Christ—as the teacher to the world that is constantly looking over its shoulder to ensure the world follows it? If there is such a claim, possibly in scripture or possibly in the developing body of Christian thought within the faith-community that gathers and has gathered together since that blessed point in history where the Christianity of the New Testament became possible, we would presumably find clues in the guiding hand of the spirit—or, in the example of Christ. Yet, Christ’s life is possibly better understood as an absolute indifference to the world in favour of living out what it means for the Son of Man to become the figure that best reflects the holy, holy, holy nature of God that shines into humanity from the point of contradiction and allows us to participate in the light of His freedom.

For freedom Christ has set us free; stand fast therefore, and do not submit again to a yoke of slavery (Gal. 5:1)12

“...Matthew 6:3 should never be taken as a guarantee that if you seek first the kingdom, God will give you any specified item out of the “all the rest”.”13

“Yes, social justice, equality, and help for the poor are involved in the simple life; yet keep them where they belong, not as its motive and rationale, but as a “plus,” a gift, a freedom, a grace out of the “all the rest” that will come to you as well.”14

It appears, as far as Eller was concerned throughout his illustrious yet quiet career, that the Christian teaching is freedom from the world in toto—not one that starts from the presumed authority to abstractly teach the world how it ought to act, but rather one that exists in opposition to the world through its own positive statement of existential goodness. There is a danger when we take such a high-minded and congratulatory view of the world that we lose the freedom of Christ: in complete obedience to God’s law, the individual becomes “free to achieve the simplicity and singleness of his own authentic existence.”15

Cox’s view of Christianity begins to sound a little less Christian when we identify an air of sophistication and perfection of the emergent elite that sits in relation to the world: it is, very much in both theoretical and real senses, Christendom trading its claim to authority from the hands of vox civitatis and handing it over to a more universal vox populi—yet, still, it receives its marching orders, submitting God to an arky amongst arkies.

“...the avant-garde is very much of the world in the sense that its goals and values are determined totally by its relationship to the world—by trying to place itself where the world will want to be. There should be little question as to which of the roles complies with the call of Jesus and the model of the New Testament church.”16

Captured by the wandering, invisible hand of world history (which, of course, I don't suppose is a random, unknowable path, but rather not the object of a science in the sense that a Marxist or Cox might accidentally imply), the church is then bound to the world and caught up in the logic of its opposite. In an absolute relation to the state, the church has given itself over to the world by way of allowing the world to dictate what constitutes faith—for the Protestant, this may even rise to the absolute crisis of standing against the papal magisterium by way of preferring submission to the bourgeois elected official of vox populi17. The church, captured by the world, lacks the freedom that the apostles promised was really there.

Against progress, against reaction

At this point, I should be clear: a reactionary turn against the present state of things by way of a reunification (if there was ever a real severing) of the church and state is no answer here—it would be a rather “slack” dialectic that would only allow for church-domination and state-domination, especially when neither notion is granted more than holy indifference in the gospel message. For both the state-churchman and the Coxan avant-gardist, the relation between the world and the church is one in which they are absolutely tied to one another—one holds the other as its primary value, from which all other values proceed. In the medieval church, this realised itself as the church's domination of society via the state; in modernity, it may be a fun little exercise to consider how the church has become the victim of the state's domination today. But, of course, this is a part of how the church is manipulated today: in an effort to keep up with “progress”, the church accepts that there is a progress that is appropriate for Christianity, which we find outside of the bounds of what constitutes Christianity.

When the church and state exist in a way which presupposes that one decides the values that the other holds and the other merely marches to the beat of that drum, it doesn't matter if this absolute relation is one where the church dominates the world or the world dominates the church—it is a fundamental error to take up this relational connection because such a connection denies that the ontological fact of the church's existence within and through the world could ever transcend above the mere material which constitutes the fact itself. There is only causation, there is no possibility for the freedom promised in scripture. The avant-garde, sophisticated to a tee, is bound by the thing it opposes by electing itself as the leader of the world, as the arky towards which the world should navigate.

This is, in effect, the disappointing position to which Cox leads us: having understood that we are not contemporaries of Christ's earthly revelation in ancient Palestine, he illustrates that the God Who would make the world good and good for His creations would not abandon them to the forces of world-history. This, of course, seems like good theology—and it is, if we remove it from Cox’s grander claim regarding the divinity of the secular city. This notion that the church, having its role as the discoverer and avant-garde edge of God's liberatory “clearing away” of immediacy and the false arkies of human idealism, is degredated by then blurring the lines between God's exodic revelation in time and the mere events of history—or, to be clearer, the progressive church self-elects itself to this position by way of dismissing the one-two punch of divine negation and liberatory revelation and appropriating the world's conception of goodness.

Against the tit-for-tat bludgeoning of “chatter”

I have little interest in outlining the particular failings of the progressive church or the ironic mirror-image of conservative failings opposite it. However, I do applaud Eller for long ago recognising this to-ing and fro-ing across the matter of worldliness by conservatives and progressives meant that worldliness was always accepted as the axis along which the theological world spins:

“Conservative churches tend to be very sensitive to the worldlinesses of the liberals, liberals tend to be very sensitive to the worldlinesses of the conservatives, and each tends to be blind to its own worldliness. But as with commissary/ caravan, we contend that the avant-garde/expediti distinction will not begin to correspond to the traditional theological categories.”18

Until we can navigate to a position where the world does not dictate to us the allowed categories of analysis—or, even worse, where we fall into the arms of the world in order to accept the “myth of progress” as not only true but also Christian!—we cannot begin to explore what genuine Christian progress might be. It may, of course, possibly mean reversing out of the dead-end we find ourselves in19.

A path forward

Of course, in Eller's mind, there was more to the church than merely becoming “the avant-garde”. Indeed, the church is not the effete body that stands before the society and receives its marching orders on how it ought to travel onwards—the notion of leadership to the world should be dismissed at this point, if you have not already had the good sense to do away with the notion yourself, my reader—but rather something altogether rather different. It is an organ of freedom, a freedom that comes about through the responsibility of indifference: learning to take what is important as actually important and to dismiss what isn’t as actually unimportant20.

Instead of ideological capture, my reader, I see that the unusual theological minds of Kierkegaard, Ellul, and Eller saw a path through the false dialectic of world-dominating and world-dominated. There is a thread that we can pull on, a thread which leads to the delicate balance of freedom in the body of Christ within the context of the world—neither as educator to those without the eyes to see or the ears to hear nor as a quaint artefact of history that allows for silly trivialities such as “community” or “increased birth rates” (utterly aesthetic values when they come from the mouths of faithlessness). No, there is a way to march which does not propose that we demand the world follow us; there is a way to establish the church, which is already established and to which we can offer not a single iota, that is free from the technique-efficiency21 of community-building and the bourgeois demand for the numerical22.

And to understand that difference, we must explore the march of the expedite.

This is part of a short excursion through Eller’s The Outward Bound, particularly the second chapter on his view of the church qua existential body of Christian faith. It might be as short as three pieces, it might as long as six—such is the joy of Eller’s wandering mind, both imprecise in its theatricality and laser-guided in its dialectical insights.
