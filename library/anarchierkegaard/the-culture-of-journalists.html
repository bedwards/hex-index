“A thinker on the balcony is the patron of truth, a thinker on the road becomes truth’s servant. And what truth needs is not patrons but servants, servants in whose life, thought and action, theory and practice are one, thinkers who think not concepts but things... The man who makes truths for himself on the balcony is a pure impressionist in his thinking and a pure opportunist in his living. The man who has been made by a truth which has met him on the road will be ready to die for the truth that found him.”1

Another week has passed away, another murder has occurred. From the balcony, I see my fellow balconeers—they cannot help noticing, they cannot help chattering. Like all mortal beings, caught in the necessity of their finitude, the event that captures their interest, sets them to work, gives them something to chatter about. Something to muse on, something to reflect on, something to issue oughts and ought nots over.

The road sits underneath them, opposite them; at very least, they are protected from the road by the comfort of the balcony.

All good and perfect things, delivered into the mouth of the journalist, are those things which immediately set the culture of chatter to action at the hands of something beyond their control. “I am not a man! I am dynamite!”, says the radical—and, like dynamite, he longs for his fuse to be lit, something to set him into action; something beyond himself to capture him, twist him, turn him into a chatterer, give him purpose in the form of eye-catching headlines and that terrible gawping desire for bloodshed and broken bones, twist him into a journalist, turn him into the chatterer who produces and reproduces the contagious condition of the culture of chattering by way of a clever turn-of-phrase, an insightful reflection to the applause of those sitting besides him. Nothing is offensive, everything is entertainment; nothing is forbidden, everything is permitted. The journalist is like the jellyfish, given life by that which lies beyond him and forced to feed on the corpse of whatever befalls him by way of the uncaring, capricious turning of the tides around him; some foodstuff or other presents itself and the jellyfish can do no other but feast; some tragedy befalls the neighbour and the individual can do no other put stare in horror; some tragedy appears before the journalist and he can do not other but turn it into shit for the crowd to gleefully feast upon. All things are consumable, all things must be consumed; the production and reproduction of everyday life is little more than providing the crowd the means by which it will eat itself to death, raised on a diet of death and wonder.

The balconeer, loafing in his lackadaisical reflections, looks one way and then the other. He recollects the moment of entertainment, the aesthetic beauty of the life of the animal—now exsanguinated to the point where it can be prepared for the feast—spilling out onto the floor in front of us in an act of power, and is again ready to survey the balcony as it extends outwards the left and right (although, it occurs to him, that this distinction is rather arbitrary and often descends into little more than which way we can signal to the audience a collective code than any real division) and he begins again—he now knows the position he has chosen for himself, in the same sense that the alcoholic leisurely browses the selection of gourmet beer available to him in his shakes and his sweats. He notices to the right that the formerly brash and boisterous have taken up the façade of the preening moralism of the elderly woman in the front pew—“how dare you”s and “how could you”s ring forth. He notices to the left that the formerly preening and moralising have taken up the façade of the brash, boisterous agent of chaos, satisfied by the spilling of blood for his purposes. On a different day, the loafer would no doubt find himself noting the once again switched positions or even the back-and-forth volley which would leave the less well-practiced neck-craner with a nasty case of whiplash. It appears, then, that these united opposites—preening and moralising contra brutishness and boisterousness—are enjoyed in the free play of desire, in the random chain of events that produce events, in the capricious turning of the tide. The problem isn’t one which can be given a simple solution: indeed, the problem is that the balcony is overwhelming, totalising, and appropriating of all it can touch. It is, fundamentally, a culture of journalists, a culture of chatter that produces and reproduces those who can’t help but share their thoughts with the crowd—creating and recreating it towards whatever end is vogue—and produces and reproduces those who can’t help but read, watch, oggle with the ravenous hunger of the cannibal. When self-abstraction is the first mode of action—indeed, the only mode of action in that it allows us to retreat into the ideal, the glassy-eyed, the cowardly fawning or critical, the overly familiar or distant, the merely imaginary—then no faith or morality is possible; only humans, only that single individual, can be faithful or moral, but no such person can be found in the crowd of gawpers, reflectors, balconeers, academics, and cannibals.

Maybe it is wrong that I have chosen today to pray for grace to extend to someone that strove for discord and thrived on controversy. But a father died in view of his children and all that I can do is exploit the event for my own ends. I can pretend that I am providing a service, that I am offering something useful for those stuck on the road or those who idle on their balconies, but I know that it isn’t the case; I am a cannibal that feeds on the flesh of his fallen neighbour, that turns his life into a source of death and his death into a source of life. I do not sit outside the culture of journalists, judging it and correcting it towards my nobler, greater ends—I am one of them, I am one of the sick in need of a doctor. The ancient pagan cowered before the gods, offering blood sacrifice in order to protect his life; the writer today—no, me, me today—bristling in the arrogance that he is—I am—oh so ever sophisticated in comparison with his—no, my—temporally-separated counterpart, fails to see he is—me, damn it, I am—little more than the faithlessly fearing who still requires blood sacrifice so that life comes alive in him—in me. From the balcony, I struggle to find a way down onto the road, some way to leave this realm of ideas and again rejoin the realm of life. For that, I am a sinner who cannot avert his gaze from sin, requiring the death of my neighbour to operate at all—so help me God.
