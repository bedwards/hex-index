Rooted in the hidden core of Malatesta’s secular anarchism, an attempt to free the abstract common man from church and state, was the necessary binding to violence which typifies worldliness. While those plucky Romantic-idealists genuinely believed that they could storm the walls of those humble Italian towns, seize the crown of power, and then burn the authority that they carried in an act of iconoclastic rage, the truth was that these ideas were only held aloft by their particular moral orientation; their will to power, the unmediated desire to bring the world under one's power as the arky that determines the ontological truth of reality. Or, to indulge in the sin of the “Old Adam”.

When this will to power became frayed from the delicate moral life that held it in control, that steered the horses of power under the control of the delicate but determined reins in the hands of carriage driver, the secular anarchist movement descended into the aesthetic demand for power—and the complete collapse of the ability to hold oneself back. Eager to liberate by way of violence, violence plays its dreadful trick and moralising reaction of Ravanchol and Henry, it appears, became captured and enslaved to that very committment to and worship of violence that had driven them to that form-of-life: having turned anarchy from a vision of the sublime creeping into politics, like the beauty of the very divine unfurled in the dissolving flesh of the salted slug, to a call for total against all, their terrorism for terrorism’s sake was their own self-damnation. Analogous to the anxious militarism of the fascist and the insecurity of the Stirnerite, the anarchist became little more than an agent of power that sought to attain power of what he perceived to be his property—*all of it*. Against the ideal of the unity of means and ends, the only end justifiable was the aesthetic and the means could be any flavour that would satisfy the desire for desire once again. All flavours, of course, but salt.

## The possession of property

For the Christian anarchist, as a corrective to these secular wayfarers standing in the shadows of the quiet men of faith in times gone past, the unity of ends and means remained as an important corrective. First, to the possible risk of temptation and its poisonous effect on the desire to become the kind of person that could love God and the neighbour without the threat of a theological structure hanging over them; secondly, towards the end of building a world which would allow this flower of “holding one's self back” to bloom. As a part of the unity of means and ends, of course, there are also visible signs of success: the increase of faithfulness, the emergence from the crucible of despair and temptation as faithful agents that both assert that the church *is* and identify where the world *begins*, and so on. The *always already* possible ethical need, against the consequentialists who push their goals out of the realms of possibility in order to absolve themselves from responsibility, is to establish a church which produces Christians—the individual who, in faith, can “stand by oneself—through another’s help!”[1](#footnote-1) The will to power, we might suggest, exists in tension with the will to control one's power and direct it towards actions that produce the kind of life that one wants to lead and that one sees oneself, as one actually is, reflected in.

Contrary to this, of course, is the temptation of property. In the spirit of—but not wholly accepting the tradition of—ascetism, S. K. offers us an insight into the notion of possession, in the broad sense:

> "The world can be possessed only by its possessing me, and this in turn is the way it possesses the person who has won the world, since one who possesses the world in any other way possesses it as the accidental, as something that can be diminished, increased, lost, won, without his possession being essentially changed.”[2](#footnote-2)

Here, the world must be understood in its biblical sense, as in the contradictory element that stands against the divine in immediacy. S. K. was aware that not only did possession constantly bring about the risk of creating “wayward desires” in our fundamental views of the world, but also that no particular objective organisation of those possessions would liberate us from the cold claim that the world places on hearts of deadness and minds of the machine: all forms-of-life[3](#footnote-3), whilst granting the possibility of passionate, directed living, also carry the danger of being reduced to the mechanical, nihilist passivity of the person who *is* but fails to *exist*[4](#footnote-4).

Existence in the world, where things must happen and possibilities must be realised whether we like it or not, we are at constant risk of being exposed in weakness to “the catastrophe of dispossession”[5](#footnote-5), i.e., the possibility for us to cede our “right” to material possessions in order to “let go” of our claim on the reality that we choose for ourselves and sets to enforcing reality to fall in line with our idealism. But what undermines this possibility? How do we identify the potential weakness that allows for the retreat into the freedom that is granted by God’s omnipotence?[6](#footnote-6)

### Identifying the object of desire

When the object of our desire, the thing itself that either “draws” us or tempts us, becomes understood as a merely rational, a merely intellectual objective that sits in relation to some consequentialist goal or as the product of “common sense” that proceeds from whichever position the “common sense” thinker emerges from, here the danger of the will to possess and subjugate comes to the fore. When those Italian anarchists stormed into Letino and set to turning the world to their goal, their overarching aims to bring about an anarchist society through their objectivising, political mechanications were little more than an idealism given flesh—they had taken actions to allow for a “liberated” relation between subject and object, but they had not ascended beyond the need for a mere possession-relation with their objective reality.

Here lies the fundamentally “political” character of secular anarchism, where it fails to move beyond the recognition of a [sceptical position on the usefulness of political ideas](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/why-anarchism) by way of continuing to absent-mindedly make political actions that assume the politician’s objective relation to the unwashed masses: regardless of what the people want or do, they are now in the “correct” objective relation to their surroundings and, as such, are liberated! All life is a great puzzle which merely needs the correct solution, so why not start by playing the little game of anarchist solutions?

But think back, my reader, to S. K.’s warning about possession: not only can we not possess the world in a sense that implies some essential change in our very being, but that, in our accidental possession of the world, we are at constant risk of becoming possessed by the world itself. The thought goes as such:

1.  When we are in relation to external objects, these relations are always in relation to some relative factor. As objects appear within particular relations to particular agents, they can only ever be relative. As such, the most that an external relation can ever be is [the absolute relation to the relative](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/absolutely-relating-to-the-relative).
    
2.  When my relation to the external is objective, i.e., it is not preceded or in line with a similar movement of the internal, then I am in an objective relation with some relative factor of reality and this relation itself becomes the grounds for action—for as long as the relation is maintained, I find that I can become motivated to pursue some related goal as if caused by the ghostly causative qualities of externality.
    
3.  When my relation to the external, objective in character, is severed, i.e., I fail to achieve some particular relation of possession with the object, my absolute desire for an absolute relation to the object brings about changes in my relation to the object. Failure to possess the external, so to speak, in the same way that I can possess my internal processes leads to an intensification of my desire for the object and a new consequentialist aim.
    
4.  When this relation to the external, objective in character and severed from my possession, collapses into “the catastrophe of dispossession” against my will, my desire to find the security of an absolute relation is revealed to be merely related to the relative and the absolute relation itself, now undermined, is exposed as [a relative relation](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/absolutely-relating-to-the-relative).
    

In this way, the secular anarchist is not merely immoral for their actions (and addressing the problem at this stage no doubt leads to the detached and shrill moralism of particularly bulldogish thinkers) but, rather, they are engaging with the [necessarily insecure as if it were secure](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/absolutely-relating-to-the-absolute)—and the fallout from this epistemological collapse in the unveiling of the world's “clay legs”, doomed to crumble into the Heraclitan confusion of halfway rigorous thinker in the crashing waves of liberal society, is that there is no relation to the ideal at all and, indeed, all things are permissible. Whereas Christian terms relate to something lofty and “other” to our understanding through the very words of our understanding, their aestheticism leads it back to its worldly function.[7](#footnote-7)
