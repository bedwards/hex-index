The following isn’t intended to be revolutionary or majorly corrective for the vast majority of philosophically trained minds in our current postmodernity. It is merely a note on the problem of “arguments for the existence of God” that leads me to say that they are, especially in their classical forms, useless at best and misleadingly comparative at worst. It is an honest attempt to frame an overly philosophical approach to proofs (both apologetically and polemically) as lacking the real “bite” with which we can apply them to anything outside of the proofs themselves—or, in short, they seem to neither give us the objective proof sufficient to believe in a God (but, even then, this phrase could only ever mean “mere acknowledgement”) nor clear the slate in demonstrating the non-existence of this divine object. Even if we could demonstrate such a thing, the proof would merely leave us at the first step of the journey with no obvious movement from mere disbelief.

With that in mind, we draw from the writings of Johannes Climacus in order to dismiss the problem of “objective” and “serious” philosophical theology.

In their most basic form, an argument for the existence of God is intended to, by way of propositions, logical movement, and a little bit of philosophical sleight of hand, show that the statement “God exists” is both a) meaningful, in the sense that it communicates a statement about the world which can be either true or false and b) true. Various kinds of arguments have been offered since antiquity towards various ends, e.g., the very first formulations were wielded in defence of pagan thought, and there are also “ecumenical” grounds for inter-faith dialogue today found in our faithful commonality, especially amongst Christians, Jews, and Muslims. You, my reader, are no doubt at least aware of some of these approaches, such as cosmological and teleological arguments.

As with all good teaching, let us first erect a strawman to quickly dispose of. Here is a standard formulation of a teleological argument that a young student might be expected to interact with:

  1. Some things in nature (or nature itself, the cosmos) are design-like (exhibit a cognition-resonating, intention-shaped character R)

  2. Design-like properties (R) are not producible by (unguided) natural means—i.e., any phenomenon exhibiting such Rs must be a product of intentional design.

  3. Therefore, some things in nature (or nature itself, the cosmos) are products of intentional design, which in turn requires agency of some type.1

As such, we have a basic formulation for the design argument. Now for some boring commentary, followed by some more rigorous commentary.

The boring commentary

The most basic problem for these kinds of arguments is that they are, after having dug under the skin of the proof, simply question-begging.

They have presented a case that says that, because of the evidence of apparent design within reality, there must be a designer who did in fact design this such-and-such a thing that is within this reality. It looks like it was designed; designers design designed things; therefore, it was designed (to varying degrees of complexity and scientific specification, much of which is far above both my own pay grade and also of those most willing to use these kinds of arguments, I’m sure). QED, there is a God. Hallelujah, we have toppled the heathen scourge.

Sadly not, my reader. As with all things philosophical, these things are altogether far more complicated than one would first assume. We will stage our boring commentary on two fronts.

Presumptive design

Firstly, although it may seem obvious or even necessary for the theist to suggest that there is evidence of design within creation, the sceptic is less content to accept this notion. In fact, the very idea seems to question-beg in a serious, serious way: to suggest there is design within nature, regardless of how nuanced and technical our constructed case for the apparent presence of design appears to be, seems to already presume that there is a designer. However, we are attempting to make an existential statement about the reality of said designer! Therefore, we can’t make this movement without including the conclusion of our argument within the argument as a premise—obviously, a hidden one that we might not even diagnose as being present within our thought processes.

In some sense, the preexisting faith of the believer is ready to take up the idea of design because the thought-object of God is present to them: “Of course there is design! There is a designer, and, therefore, there is design—and I know this because the thought-object, God, refers to some particular thing as opposed to some abstraction.” For the interlocutor who doesn’t have these “filled” thought-objects, the a priori reflection on what it means for there to be a designer is merely an empty concept, a simulacrum of a thought. Without this preexisting faith, we set ourselves up to fail by attempting to demonstrate the existence of the thing we are interrogating—to demonstrate that something exists means that we must be thinking of it qua existing prior to the demonstration and, therefore, we end up in a jumbled position. The idea that we could demonstrate the non-existent is to say that either a) I am thinking of a thing and that thing has no qualities that I can think of, in a proper sense, due to its nonexistence, or b) this proof is false. From this muddle-head point, it presumably would even justify us in establishing a kind of Kantian rationalism or, Lord forbid, consequentialism.

Starting from different positions, it appears that the thought-object is not transmitted through this argument.

Empty concepts? Filled concepts?

Secondly, and more seriously, there is a problem of how one knows that their thought-objects actually correspond to some particular object within reality.

It is all well and good for us to assert that the things we are thinking of are the things as they are within reality, but, again, dealing with the sceptic means that we would be expected to show that this is the case if we believe that a) we can demonstrate these things philosophically or b) that philosophy has the tools to demonstrate these things. Therefore, any basic arguments of these forms already presume their truth by way of attempting to make the thought-object both a premise in the argument and also the conclusion of the argument and also that the thought-object relates to reality in a way which is meaningful. In short, we have presumed everything that the argument is meant to demonstrate instead of demonstrating that the argument is true.

Something of note here, however, is that we’ve not established that the thought-object presumed by the believer is false—indeed, we’ve only shown that this argument doesn’t demonstrate it. In part, this is due to a basic failure from antiquity onwards to establish what is meant by an existential statement, or, in short, to diagnose the strangeness of “X exists” statements. Dalferth notes:

“Existential statements differ from both definitions and factual statements. Definitions define the meaning of a term, while factual statements assert facts that can be true in a possible world, even if they are wrong in our actual world (“x is F”). Existential statements state facts that are, or are not, the case in the world in which we live (“There is at least one x that has the property F”). In an important sense, existential statements are always also about us because they cannot be true unless they are true in the world in which we exist (“There is at least one x that has the property F” can only be true if it is true in the world in which we exist).”2

Of course, these arguments have presumed the object of their investigation! As we are making existential statements, these statements could only ever presume their object as existential statements should always be understood to mean that there is at least one object of this kind that I am identifying and it has the following properties. This is a play on Kant, in a way, in asserting that “X exists” is an empty concept that is really a (possibly unknowingly) concealed statement by the speaker whereby the interesting content of the statement, i.e., “…that has the property F”, is elided. In that sense, this form of question is something which the philosopher cannot touch in a way that pleases him and, as such, we must pass over it in silence.

The theologically important commentary

However, this isn’t the entirety of the problem. In fact, the agnostic stance adopted by the thinker searching for the “god of the philosophers” is a detriment to ever discovering whether any particular deity might actually exist. Indeed, we struggle to make interesting statements, i.e., “…that has the property F” statements, when we strip so liberally from the concept of what a god-figure might be. As opposed to the problem above, where there is a concern about the emptiness of or the presumed fullness of concepts that are mishandled in our theological play.

The main problem that proceeds from teleological arguments—by which I mean both classical forms and also contemporary “fine-tuning” revisions—that start from the agnostic position is that the position they are attempting to present as evidence, i.e., this is a world which shows evidence of a designer, is also an empty concept in the sense that it matters. I appreciate that not every working philosopher of religion is an active theist of a rather conventional kind, so I will start with something from rather close to the beginning of scripture that throws the spanner in the works for our philosopher:

Then God saw everything that He had made, and indeed it was very good. (Genesis 1:31)

It is not enough to assert that there is design to any degree of technical complexity, but rather there must be clear evidence that this design is also very good in the sense that a divine being would assent to its existence as being as such. Or, to be a little clearer, teleological arguments presuppose that the problem of evil is solved—not only is the world designed and fine-tuned, but also not evil and tov!

For those in the dark about this problem:

  1. There are events in our world — such as an animal’s dying an agonizing death in a forest fire, and a child’s undergo lingering suffering and eventual death due to cancer — such that the actions of allowing those events, when one could prevent them, both (a) have very serious, known wrongmaking characteristics, and (b) have no rightmaking characteristics of which we are aware that are sufficient to balance out the known wrongmaking properties.

  2. Therefore, it is likely that: or any such action, the totality of the wrongmaking properties, both known and unknown, outweighs the totality of the rightmaking properties, both known and unknown, and

  3. Any action whose wrongmaking properties outweigh its rightmaking properties is morally wrong.

  4. Such actions are morally wrong (from 2 and 3).

  5. For any action whatever, an omnipotent and omniscient being is capable of not performing that action (from 2, 3, and 4).

  6. If there is an omnipotent and omniscient being, then that being performs some morally wrong actions (from 4 and 5).

  7. A being that performs morally wrong actions is not morally perfect (from 4, 5, and 6).

  8. If there is an omnipotent and omniscient being, that being is not morally perfect (from 5 and 6).

  9. God is by definition an omnipotent, omniscient, and morally perfect person.

  10. God does not exist (from 8 and 9).3

Needless to say, this is a problem that has roots in antiquity and remains as a difficult problem that the theist-philosopher must contend with. There are multiple attempted solutions to it, of course, which the theist might be content to present as a counter-argument: sceptical theism, for example, would assert that God’s justice in its completeness is unknowable to us and, as such, our failure to climb this ladder of syllogisms to heaven is merely another example of Babelic failure; from another position, we might dismiss the problem outright as overcome in the sacrifice of Christ and the overthrow of the adversary means that we now all live in a state of posse non peccare4; from one further position, we might even turn our guns towards the entire endeavour of systematic theology as an intellectual pursuit, suggesting that such a thing is best understood as an exercise in uncovering paradox as opposed to creating rational arguments with which to resurrect the Pelagian case.5

It is entirely possible that our theological philosopher will not be happy with these solutions, though, but it is important that we remain grounded in times of intellectual struggle—otherwise, we wouldn't be able to do the important work. The bigger problem that I would like to address is that, from our position contra the “philosopher of the god of philosophers”, it seems that we must delve into particular traditions in order to navigate a path out. Without it, it appears that we are stuck in a deadlock—indeed, without some knowledge of God qua God-as-He-is as opposed to mere propositions, we are caught in the wilderness.

Regardless of whether we see the problem of evil as successfully disproving the existence of God6, successfully dismantling the possibility of systematic theology, or a trivial intellectual exercise, it is clear that the work of the stance-agnostic intellectual, like all intellectualisms, has questionable relation to reality as it actually is, i.e., proceeding from the point and towards the τέλος of a fuller theological claim, one which is useful in a sense that matters. Without that, a thinker who focuses on teleological or fine-tuning arguments without a robust response to the problem of evil is getting on the bus at the wrong stop—they lack, it seems, the proper footing to really engage in discussing whether some thing or other exists or if we have some piece of evidence or other that makes it worth engaging with. For that, we join the philosophers in scoffing.

In a sense, my reader, the person who would be convinced with these kinds of arguments (after a little interrogation, of course) is one who already has some particular content qua thought-object when they reflect on the idea of God or Creator or Designer. There is a thing that they are thinking of that, obviously, it seems to me, others aren’t. In this sense, the “thrust” of an ontological argument—and not necessarily an Anselmian or a Plantigaean argument—is seemingly prior to all arguments for the existence of God: we must address the possibility and reality of this thought-object that fills the basic assumptions of these theistic arguments and what it means for faith as a real thing that people have, not merely the object of reflection that philosophers have decided to select. It seems, for the believer who is interested in engaging with all this kind of thing, tackling said “thrust” is more important than merely presuming that causation implies a causer or that motion implies a mover.
