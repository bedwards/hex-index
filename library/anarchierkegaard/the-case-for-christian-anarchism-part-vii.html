1. When Christianity is held as a theory, faith then becomes exposed as nihilism.

Jesus said to him, “Again it is written, ‘You shall not put the Lord your God to the test.’” (Matthew 4:7)

I. What is the basis of philosophy? Philosophers, on the whole, present propositions together in order to reach some particular conclusion that follows on from said propositions and the logical form applied to them. When presenting ideas in the propositional form, the philosopher assumes the worthwhileness of “approximation-knowledge”1 and is open to challenge and counter-challenge.

II. What is the basis of faith? Proceeding from the Absolute, the Lord God, faith is the fullness of time2, the ultimate content-giving content of all contents3, the wrestle with “the absurd”4—the “salt” to Christian character5.

III. What does the propositional form lend to the discussion of faith? Nothing at all whatsoever.

IV. What does the propositional form lend to the discussion of life? Little at all, but plenty in the eyes of the aesthete—exhausted by the categories of his exhausting imagination.

Philosophers are, as a breed, an immodest bunch. While the scientist accepts that, in their production of scientific approximations, there is a certain “limit” to their methods and what they could show, the philosopher might be quite so ready to think that there can be some undemonstrable notion that sits outside of their expertise. The problem, it seems, arises from the type of content that the expert deals with: the biologist must deal with living things, the architect must deal with housing things, but the philosopher is doomed to wrestle not only with ideas but also directly through the mode of his wrestle at once—that is to say, he must think of ideas and the idea of ideas without falling into conflation and philosophical incoherence.

As a part of this, the propositional form, i.e., the ability to express certain facts (or would-be facts) about reality as statements which can be assessed as either true or false, is often assumed as being the point of contact between the philosopher’s grasp and their target area of inquiry. When we engage in reflection on the world, we take the content of that reflection from direct experience or some other piece of knowledge otherwise gathered from the inquiries of others. For many philosophers throughout the history of the bloodsport, this form has been either sufficient for discussing whatever matter is at hand or superior to other forms of knowing, e.g., mere belief, faith, revelation, “hunches”, tradition, etc.—if they are seen as containing any knowledge whatsoever. Of course, this mode has emerged in a variety of ways through the likes of Descartes, Kant, Hegel, and Heidegger, but the central point is the same: philosophy deals in arranging knowledge in such a way that we can arrange the content of “the sciences” into a systematic and possibly even complete totality of knowledge. Here, we move from the mere abstractions of historical reflection to a figure in particular: Hegel.

Before we begin to bring the dance to an end, my reader, a note on framing: this piece, as all good collections require, is a corrective for that which came before. It may sit unkindly or even obscurely with the other pieces in this series; however, it is here for a purpose and that purpose is, in part, to encourage you not to—ironically—hold up this compilation as an objective truth that must be lashed onto any particular life in order to bring about the results. We leave those kinds of half-baked conclusions to our consequentialist-minded friends. No, this is a call to subjectivity that recognises and is recognised as it is: as the believer within the caravan, sojourning within the Body of Christ towards eschaton.

Hegel and the abstraction of faith

Here, my reader, we have elected our sacrificial figure, our scapegoat that we will sacrifice to the gods in order to absolve us of our sin and force the divine to make itself good with us—Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel, the systematic thinker par excellence.

“The term system is often misunderstood. It does not denote a philosophy, the principle of which is narrow and to be distinguished from others. On the contrary, a genuine philosophy makes it a principle to include every particular principle.”6

Hegel set out to systematise all existing knowledge into a form that would elucidate the interconnectedness of everything and illustrate the very joints of nature; something that would appear to be a kind of 19th-century “compendium of everything”, but also using philosophy to provide the appropriate links and turns—providing our ideas a way to become qualified notions and providing those notions a place with which to understand them properly. To some extent, this image of philosophy isn’t quite dismissed to the dusty annals of historical investigation.

Muddled conceptualisation

However, there is a clear problem here7: Hegel appears to be building his house upon the sand, rendering it—should S. K. be correct in this assessment—no better than a thought experiment, but a thrillingly interesting one8. The very notions on which the system is erected: “the universal”9, already a “notion” far too impersonal to ever really broach the claims of Christianity10, is presented as if we have some mere idea of God Himself that philosophy helps us to tease out and refine. From this perspective, we might say that philosophy is the thing which gives Christianity its form and shape, allowing a better type of Christianity to emerge.

This perspective, for S. K., is deeply worrying on two fronts:

i) In order for philosophy to provide Christianity with a “better version” of itself, it would need to explain Christian ethics—however we would like to frame that—in a way that is superior to Christ’s explanation. But, before we can even make some kind of comparative study of these things, it appears that Hegel has no real system of ethics to appeal to whatsoever: indeed, the analogical study of ethics is nothing more than a relativist study of “whatever happens within the social order is just” and this Sittlichkeit is then fumbled in place of an ethics in any real sense of the word11.

ii) In order for philosophy to provide Christianity with a system on which to rest, it would need to be able to explain God, Christ, and everything in between those two points—however, this would imply that God’s absolute “otherness” and Christ’s existence as that holy contradiction of the Man-God must then be compatible with a immanent metaphysics. To suggest that such a thing is possible, S. K. notes, is to draw God into merely the immanent and, much like Spinoza and Kant before, Hegel collapses any possible idea of God into the limits of his thought. Completely idealised, Christianity can no longer accept something which is “Wholly Other” and, instead, is reduced to the gentlemanly play of approximation-facts and propositional wrestling. Like the great Greek heroes of antiquity, Hegel ascends Olympus to fell the gods—this time, by a ladder of syllogisms instead of by the sword12.

“A thinker erects a huge building, a system, one that encompasses the whole of life and world-history, etc.—and if one then turns attention to his personal life one discovers to one’s astonishment the appalling and ludicrous fact that he himself does not live in this huge, high-vaulted palace, but in a store-house next door, or a kennel, or at most in the janitor’s quarters. If one took it upon oneself to draw attention with but a single word to this contradiction, he would be insulted. For so long as he can complete the system—with the help of his error—being in error is not what he is afraid of.”13

Putting the muddle-headed “back on his feet”

The danger proposed in our opening discussion, my reader, becomes apparent with Hegel: having pulled the ideas of Being and Nothing from, amusingly, nothing, it appears that they lack an object in reality—or, more importantly, they might lack an object in reality and Hegel would have no mode by which to identify this problem. We could be, as such, tricking ourselves into thinking that we are “developing concepts” or similar when, really, we are doing little more than playing with “floating signifiers”. Lacking the “criterion of verifiability”14, the pursuit becomes impossible. When we take to this kind of high-concept metaphysics, the content of our reflections might be little more than linguistic conventions and historical (and potentially rootless) debates, not the very thing that we are attempting to investigate. Indeed, we lack the proper equipment to really begin with philosophy, let alone to reduce “living life” to a string of propositions that beats the interlocutor into submission.

It is worth bearing in mind that this is not solely a Hegelian problem, though, my reader (and some would venture that this “Hegelian problem” wasn’t actually a problem for Hegel due to S. K.’s uncharitable and sarcastic misinterpretation15). In truth, my reader, the particular interpretation of Hegel’s work isn’t of central importance: the larger point is that Hegel, like those before him and those after him, made the task of faith a matter of relating to God and of understanding Christianity a matter of objectively relating to some relative collection of propositions amongst propositions, a matter of debate, a matter of intellectualist musing.

“Philosophy and Christianity can never be united, for if I’m to hold fast to what is one of the most essential features of Christianity, redemption, then of course for it really to amount to anything it must extend to the whole man. Or am I supposed to consider his moral powers defective but his cognition unimpaired? Certainly I can conceive of such a philosophy after Christianity, or after a person has become a Christian, but then it would be a Christian philosophy. The relation would not be one of philosophy to Christianity but of Christianity to Christian knowledge, or, if you absolutely must, to Christian philosophy—unless one is willing to have it that philosophy has to conclude beforehand, or prior to Christianity, that the riddle of life cannot be solved.”16

Subjectivity—the resistance against abstraction

The idea that this riddle of life—and, by extension, the riddle of the life of the church—cannot be given objective meaning is truly one of those great Messianic proclamations come manifest: blessed is he who is not offended because of Me (Matthew 11:6). In a moment where the conventional, almost absent-mindedly demand for objective proof comes to the fore, as all being at the fore and under the spotlight of the age must come if it wishes to be taken seriously, where the lazy half-thinker who has taken to the task of understanding the matter of his very life and the relations of his very life as an object worthy of objective propositional jousting, Christ stands against the “demand of the times” and says: no! First seek the kingdom of God! (Matthew 6:33)—something that only a subject can do and that only a subject can do subjectively by way of finding the God-relationship17.

“Far from espousing Cartesian or even Kantian dualism, Kierkegaard seeks to find a place for subjectivity within the seamless spatio-temporal chain by exploiting (in a fashion exceeding that philosopher’s own account of ‘the soul’) Aristotle’s problematic admission of ‘possibility’ and ‘becoming’ (kinesis) within the embrace of ‘Being’, which would seem hospitable only to ‘the actual’. Thus he contends that the real exhibits infinitely many transitions from rest to motion, and from possibility to act, and that these transitions, despite the regional operation of habitual causal patterns, have the character of positive ‘leaps’ which display no logic outside that of their own occurrence.

This kind of transition, which, not being a ‘state’, is an invisible vanishing point for thought (doomed to the effort of representation) he names ‘the Moment’, and claims that the Moment is the site of specifically human, spiritual existence—‘spirit’ being that which binds soul (thinking possibility) and body (living actuality) together.”18

First, brought out of the malaise of common existence, the individual finds themselves confronting the utterly beyond, “the Wholly Other” reaching out towards them qua universality breaking through and identifying particularity—the existence-communication19; secondly, the confused image of self-assurance in secularity is broken up in this moment and is replaced by the sublime, the transcendent tearing up old soil in the apocalyptic realisation of possibility—the thoughts that wound from behind20; and thirdly, when the desire for the safety and approximation of propositional thought is surrendered and dismissed, the fullness of time rips through and allows for the complementary nature of particularity and universality—not the Platonic fallacy of their mutual opposition!—reconciles in the life of the believer who, in particularity, discover the universal through the particularity of a particular faith in the universal love of the universal God that is—reliance not on good, but sola Gratia21. These three things, the process by which the individual makes an “about turn”22 in the broken neck change of qualitative change, should not be considered sequential or normative—only indicative inasmuch as they become blurred in their subjectivity and subjectivity in their blurring.

In this spiritual moment of existence, where “living actuality” is found in the meeting of material bruteness and spiritual “pulling”, where the idealist position of Descartes or Kant is inverted into a profoundly anti-humanist surrender of the self to the “Wholly Other”, to the One Who simply affirms that He is, to the One Who weeps with His creation, the individual rises up against the fallen self and finds that the Knight of Faith, so firey in his faith in S. K.’s Fear and Trembling and twice so in Christian Discourses, understands that to receive everything that one must first surrender all—self and other—to the Lord God23. The plea for rest can only be found in the retreat to God, but the retreat to God is always companion with the yes! of faithfulness: the earnest belief of the divine that humanity is worth loving and, as part of that, you, my reader, the fractured you across time, is worth loving. Fractured and invigorated, caught between actuality and possibility, we find the Christian acting as the Christian sojourner:

“It is by no means the case that the fiction is a lens we must use to enhance our sight, although it also distorts it. On the contrary, the manifestatory expression-through-possibility belongs wholly within ontology, and does not merely mediate between Being and Knowledge. No abyss sunders us, the knowing subjects, from reality, but rather reality is itself incessantly fractured between the actual and the possible, and within this rift ‘subjectivity’ comes to be/becomes possible.”24

In this fracture, the caesura of the self and the caesurae of the church, the possible is given life as that essential “salt”—from mere ingredients to the finest bread to share in eschaton25; from mere individuals, plural and distinct qua subjects subjectively relating to one another, to the particular servants of God arranged against and found within the absolute relation of His wonderous universality. In the “negative space” that the world cannot bear to broach, where subjectivity becomes subjective against the pressure to fit into the objectivising and arbitrary lens that “the One” attempts to impose upon creation, that, my reader, my friend—that is where the Christian emerges.

Only with the brutalist realisation of the “Wholly Other”, the terrifying Transcendent, the beautiful sublimity are we given the shape of the individual and of collective of individuals to be what they might be and only with this brutality, this terror, this beauty, in this risk of living life26 do we find that ideas—no! spirit! truly and completely—can move the dead material of a fallen creation when it is exposed to becoming “born again”, a “new creature” who is capable of becoming—no! commanded to become perfect! (Matthew 5:48) Together, never one or the other, material and ideal are united qua Spirit—first, capable of becoming more than merely the aesthete or merely the ethical man; secondly, in realising that one is both more than that and also, against the universal, at once, finite and infinite qua the human self that walks amongst human selves:

“To believe the forgiveness of one's sins is the decisive crisis whereby a human being becomes spirit; one who does not believe this is not spirit. Maturity of the spirit means that immediacy is completely lost, that a person is not only capable of nothing by himself but is capable only of injury to himself. But how many in truth come in a wholly personal way to understand of themselves that one is brought to this extremity. (Here lies the absurd, offense, the paradox, forgiveness of sins.)”27

In this space, where the involved participation of first the individual and then, together, the collective is required to both recognise and then act within the caesura, then we can start to act with the faith that fills the totality of reality with the particularity of subjectivity—then, we see creation as it might once have been, as it “as it actually is”, and how it will be once again in eschaton28.
