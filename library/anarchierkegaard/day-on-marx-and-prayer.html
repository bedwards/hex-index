Religious suffering is, at one and the same time, the expression of real suffering and a protest against real suffering. Religion is the sigh of the oppressed creature, the heart of a heartless world, and the soul of soulless conditions. It is the opium of the people.1

“Where, then, is the hope? Is the hope in hardship’s rushing gales? Ah, no, no more than God’s voice was in the rushing gales but was in the gentle breeze... But what, then, does hardship want? It wants to have this whisper brought forth in the innermost being. But then does not hardship work against itself, must not its storm simply drown out this voice? No, hardship can drown out every earthly voice; it is supposed to do just that, but it cannot drown out this voice of eternity deep within. Or the reverse.”2

In 1927, a young woman embarked on her journey to take up the Catholic faith. In some ways, this is rather unremarkable: people join the church—Catholic or otherwise—every day for a variety of reasons with a variety of expectations and a variety of goals (or, even, a total lack thereof). However, alongside the remarkable miracle of an individual turned towards God, there was another, lesser remarkable event: a sinner, in her sin, had recognised her need for God in a turn brought about by the responsibility of a child, a life that reaches out in the need for a companion and a sojourner. This life was marked by sin—most notably, her profession as a journalist—and by saintliness, by love and by struggle; by very much only those things which are possible for a self. And, of course, it is only a self which can doubt without merely becoming a caricature of a given actuality.

Of course, Dorothy Day herself was aware of the struggle that emerges for an intellectual and passionate individual emerging from the chaos in the shattered carcass of Christendom. In her readings of Marx, where she had broached the infamous passage regarding religion’s place as the “opiate of the people”, she made a brave movement that the faithful rarely do—she agreed with him. In the dark night of the prole, it is absolutely true that those who live and have lived and will continue to live underfoot do indeed grasp for religious mysticism in an effort to escape their condition, to escape the cruel reality of being less than they might be and more than they would prefer they weren’t. Understanding all too well that faith is something that a person has and not something that the crowd can have, she agreed that religion is the opiate of the masses and was concerned, even if only momentarily against the backwash of eternity, that her prayers merely made her feel better.3

Now, here in this tantalising realisation, we are placed onto the dangerous knife-edge, onto the dangerous tightrope that faith demands by virtue of offering us no objective grounding on which to right ourselves—or, at the very least, to pretend we have righted ourselves upon. How can I tell if my prayers and the reflections of my prayers are genuine in that they move me towards God and turn my hands from mere flesh and bone into instruments that may do His wonders? How can I discern the working of the Spirit from my mere ideal musings on some fleeting desire or other? But, as Wittgenstein once noted, faith is indeed that holy tightrope which seems impossible to walk upon—and, yet, so many are held aloft.

As always, it appears that Day’s worry, that holy angst that acts as a blaring siren to the world that searches for a sign that saints do indeed walk amongst them, had played a part in the ideal musings of a loafer in Northern Europe 100 years prior. In the quiet concern for the fealty of one’s praying, there must be a position that we can occupy between scrupulosity and a conscious and upbuilding sense of self-judgement—otherwise, as Schleiermacher would accidentally have it, prayer is mere fiction in a determinist universe4. Scandalously in an age of consumption, our melancholic loafer furiously notes in his prophetic vision: it is possible that life itself may need a *brake*—a gadfly, a presence that turns the horse from its feed bag in that refusal to be swatted.

Prayer changes the prayer

S. K., in his youth and under the tutelage of his would-be rival Martensen, came across Schleiermacher’s infamous notion of “total dependence”—the sensation of relying upon God for all things in order was an aspect of faith that burned in the individual. However, this posed somewhat of a problem for the young Dane. Firstly, so to speak, as this meant that prayer seemed to be utterly pointless:

“...whereas Schleiermacher sees this as disallowing any element of activity on the part of the dependent self (which is why it is absolute dependence), Kierkegaard suggests that such a state would not negate activity but should involve both activity and passivity. This relates to the possibility of prayer since if there is no activity on the part of the self in relation to God, ‘Wouldn’t prayer have to be regarded as a fiction?’”5

In this obscure note on the notion of prayer, we find S. K.’s most stunning insight: that the particular and the universal are not divided, but are rather dialectical partners6. Consider the church that both is posed against the world and signifies to the world that it is as such, whilst at the same time existing within the world as a product of it. The individual is not in a total dependence upon God and subject to the capriciousness of His will, but rather in a position of total dependence on God’s power and gifted a modicum of it to do His wonders. In the apparent passivity of our condition, activity emerges as the difference-maker for the individual on the backdrop of his universal reality—when I do, I do become a self in relation to the One Who gives every good and perfect gift7 and the other who is the object of my attention.

For S. K., the evidence of Christianity's reality is that, when engaged in the imitatio Christi and giving the interiority of faith an externality in active practice directed at the other, one finds the unexplained appearance of “the wounded consciousness”8—the rupture of the self over the stumbling block of “the offense” (Matthew 11:6), the seeping-in of externality where the internal is first moulded.

“You Changeless One, whom nothing changes! You who are changeless in love, who just for our own good do not let yourself change-would that we also might will our own well-being, let ourselves be brought up, in unconditional obedience, by your changelessness to find rest and to rest in your changelessness! You are not like a human being. If he is to maintain a mere measure of changelessness, he must not have too much that can move him and must not let himself be moved too much. But everything moves you, and in infinite love. Even what we human beings call a trifle and unmoved pass by, the sparrow’s need, that moves you; what we so often scarcely pay attention to, a human sigh, that moves you, Infinite Love. But nothing changes you, you Changeless One! O you who in infinite love let yourself be moved, may this our prayer also move you to bless it so that the prayer may change the one who is praying into conformity with your changeless will, you Changeless One!”9

At the break of Day

Day’s concern, of course, is not an easy one to dismiss: how can I discern my own private self-torturings from the intervention of the spirit’s pruning—if such a thing is there at all?

In part, this was why S. K. asserted so consistently that there couldn't be a given Christian politics or, really, any objective and imposed Christian system of thought which rose above some humanly grounded piece of Vordenken10. When religious faith is merely the creation and recreation of religious ideas—a feckless engagement with fleeting opinions as if they were revelatory—then the Marxian is correct, but only partially; these people have cried from the suffering and retreated into the imagination which subverts their perception of the Lord, an idealist par excellence who thinks he can think himself (or, even worse, his neighbour) into freedom, whilst also basically unaware of the despair that tears up the soil in which their fruit might have thrived11.

Becoming a Christian, becoming a self, requires first to accept that the self that I am is not merely an idea that I have for my limited time on this planet, picked as an idea amongst ideas and an accident of history—power is found not in creating plans that grate against creation, but rather requires that I accept the life that I find myself in and the movements which ripple through me as me in the moment of participation in the universal12. “I am dynamite!” cries the aesthete, discontented with the realisation that contours of his life are indeed contours—and attempts to resist in an explosion of expression against finitude muster little more than the final destruction of self outright. “No!”, I respond, it is the quiet acceptance that becoming a power that breaches the imposition of the limit first comes in accepting that dynamite may be the most powerful when the fuse is wet:

“For Kierkegaard, the “night” of religious experience appears only “as the purely negative horror of darkness or the dazzling nature of the ‘sunlight of eternity’ ‘in the hour of death.’” However, for genuine “Catholic night-mysticism,” the way of the “night” issues in an earthly blessedness. In short, Kierkegaard preserves “a final residual effect of the Lutheran denial of gratia inhabitans, [a denial] of the inner possession—and not just the touching—of redemption.””13

No, dear Karl and dear Friedrich, it is not the earnestness of a young mother, aware of the sin transgressed in a life from which she emerges and now striding forward in the freedom of faith: that “wounded consciousness”14 is the evidence that we can indeed be broken over the working of the spirit, moulded again into something new, into what we always already are and what we always already ought to be. The realist, fellow traveller to neither the idealist who only deals in self-assured fantasy and the authority of the mind's baronian castle nor the vulgar materialists who deals only in the measurable, the quantifiable, the limiting of the possible to dirt in front of him. In recognising the movements that we are moved to play a part in, always already had in the opportunity for faith, always already situated for the broken neck of faith’s merciful grace—that is the sign which breaks open reality and hands the keys to the kingdom into the meek, the poor, the weary, and the hungry.

Day, dear faithful sojourner, fear not a second longer! Against Marx, we are moved to confess: I am moved to faith by that which is both beyond me and totally within my grasp. It is no opium which runs in my veins—it is the reality, the undoubtable realness of my very blood and bones, my very subjectivity itself against the object of my struggle, which grants me my faith and assures me against the scoffing of the Flammenzeichen who retreat into the petty intellectualism of vulgar materialism when that individual, that single individual, rises out from amongst them and, in the blink of the eye, disappears once again. In the quiet assurance that the aesthetic demand for a display of power, a show amongst shows for viewing pleasure of a society of spectators, is undermined by the repose of the simple prayer that moves more than the scholar's pen or the policeman's baton ever could. There is something caused in me when I pray in earnest that saves me from the panic, the dizziness of the fad and the insecurity and yokes me to a path altogether different from merely what I would desire—let this revelation be all that is needed to know that Marx stood on one side of the boundary and I the other.
