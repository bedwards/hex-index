6.  In running up against the non-Christian as a Christian, the Christian acts kerygmatically.
    
    \---
    

I. What is the *kerygma*? It is the proclamation, not a preaching; the Word sounding, not spoken.

II. What is it to act kerygmatically? It is not to proclaim, because the proclamation has already sounded. It is to hear, to act, and to obey in a way which makes the proclamation resonate through reality—when the “infinitely qualitatively different” is turned from a *mere* ideal into a fact of reality, where the Word once again finds itself incarnated and opposite the incarnated.

III. What does it mean for the world to “run up” against the *kerygma*? The world doesn’t recognise the “reason for” and the “in order to”—to meet the *kerygma* as the *kerygma* is to meet Christ as He is, contemporaneously and simultaneously. Where once a German theologian declared that Christ is dead, the proclamation is His resurgence felt again in time; the eternal, once more, enters into reality to both refract through and lift up the believer in “the moment” of faith.

## Against the Christian Individual

There is always a certain temptation, when one decides to write down their ideas and decide that they’re clever enough and correct enough to share with the world, to believe that we are somehow praiseworthy merely for collecting information, filtering it through a life, and then presenting it in a way which—at the very least—isn’t distasteful to the reader. In presenting ourselves, through the written word, to the other, we enter into the delicate and often caustic process of being afforded authority, i.e., someone looks at you as a teacher. We can be quick to lose ourselves in the adulation of praise and the torment of critique, leading us into similar situations, despite the aesthetic difference between these two opposite approaches, that force us into a situation that lies at the very heart of faith: will we allow ourselves to do as any particular desire forces us to or will we be able to hold ourselves back \[*at holde igjen paa sig selv*\][1](#footnote-1)? The teacher—or, rather, the one who allows himself to be mistaken for a teacher—is a particular individual with a particular responsibility now; will they be able to say merely what needs to be said, regardless of who will hear it *or* will they find that they dance in the temptation too long and allow themselves to become the object of praise, the doxological point of focus for his age?

In part, this rather unimportant reflection on temptation, our example barely scratching the surface of what it means to be *tempted* towards something, provides a simple example of an altogether more dangerous deception that manifests itself in two ways: the dialectical desire of both being oneself and not being oneself and also the temptation to view oneself as an individual as separated from the other. Between these two dialectics, there is a possibility to discover the very meaning of *agape* that S. K. illustrate in his *Works of Love*:

> “To stand by oneself—through another's help!”[2](#footnote-2)

\---

Christian commentary can often be deeply self-indulgent, overly reflective, and ready to dismiss the very principles that it takes to be admirable: a preference for a love of the abstracted distancing of secularity than for God and the neighbour, for the self-affirming love of “the God of Provenance”[3](#footnote-3) that is altogether more of a figure of flattery than a challenge of “offense”, for an intellectual figure to be understood objectively as opposed to subjectively interacted with. These issues, always a little too clear, always a little too elegant in the way they wrestle with the object of their desire, have made themselves a kind of “faux-Christianity” that sits in front of the faith and is suitably interesting for the kind of people that we ought to avoid taking too much advice from, i.e., YouTubers, Redditors, and other simpler forms of life. They chop up the meat of what is, essentially, “living life”, a topic altogether too grand for the philosopher to even begin to contemplate—and yet, they attempt it. And in attempting this, much to the applause of the audience, they seem to have convinced us that life is reducible to little more than a mathematical proof, a sufficiently complex logic, a treatise of suitably clear propositions that illustrate the difference between good thinking and bad thinking. Reaching a culmination in the sociology of *abstracta*, i.e., capitalist liberalism, we find that these rather misguided attempts to reduce “living life” to some particularly pithy piece of writing are actually applauded.

In no short part, of course, this critique is an expansion on the (in)famous *“Attack” Upon Christendom* from the pen of S. K. himself—having first turned to the library or the pulpit or the university or so on and so on *instead of* seeking first the Kingdom of God, the verb-phrase “living life” is reduced to merely a verb-phrase amongst verb-phrases, a coy notion amongst coy notions[4](#footnote-4). Christianity takes comfort in becoming a kind of entertainment—one that is often not very entertaining to its audience, especially in the modern world—or possibly into an intellectual pursuit—one that is often not very intellectually stimulating to its audience, especially in the modern world—that admits that we’re not really discussing anything of importance. Hence, first and foremost, Redditors find it desperately interesting. This ought to be our clearest warning sign.

In some sense, though, this assertion about the threat that the clergymen and theologians of the world have had on the prospect of the faith, as if a handful of educated men and women could untie the very connective tissue between the immanent and the transcendent within the life of the believer, is a little too easy today—a little too much of an excuse, some problem that is conveniently found objectively “to” the believer. Where once S. K. stood at the door of the Danish Lutheran church to tear down the sign that had hidden the falseness behind the front of piety, now tearing at the sign of the church would only reveal a falseness that exposes our failure to “live life”. Such a position, lazily adopted, would absolve the believer of their duty to God on the grounds that some spectacled theologian on the other side of the world had done too much in separating the life of thought and the life of prayer[5](#footnote-5)—if there is to be another “attack”, it would seem that the church that the Anabaptists dreamed[6](#footnote-6) of must be turned back out against “the world” in identifying it for what it is.

No, my reader, this seems to lead us into a blind end: by making our condition possibly irredeemable by way of a socially enforced and intellectually protected condition of sin, we ironically end up in a kind of twist on Pelagianism—not only have humanity, without God’s help, made salvation impossible by way of dithering abstractions, but we have also figured out the mode by which we shall asserted the totality of this great commission that we seemingly intentionally fail! Indeed, however radical S. K.’s words might have rung out in the mid-1800s, they were intended as a corrective which has seemed to have failed to correct[7](#footnote-7)—the danger of the wayward theologian, now, is only one often adopted in an attempt to hide from the truth, a simulcra of a protest of the distant past that now hangs over us as a tradition that has idealistically lifted off from over the purchase of the material. Those most ready to accuse others of, e.g., docetism, seem to find no irony in their own self-spiritualisation. Those most ready to accuse others of, e.g., failures in faith, seem to find no irony in their own self-historicization.

As with Paul’s radical statement of our fundamental egality and S. K.’s radical statement of distrust in *bourgeois* preacher-entertainer aestheticism, we find here that the *kerygma*’s importance by way of breaking up the false objectivity that the ideologue would desire: in a world which has entirely become “fabulously textual”[8](#footnote-8), where everything is funneled through the gluttonous maw of consumerism, it is now a time for the reinvention of kerygmatic theology that doesn’t abandon the body. Or, in short, the Christian is found only inasmuch as it provokes a witness to witness.

## Breaking up the individual

Kerygmatic schools of theology go in and out of fashion at the drop of a hat. Long gone are the days of Rudolf Bultmann, an incisive and powerful thinker who stood as one of the last great liberal theologians in his attempt to defend the Christian message against the swelling tide of secularity. While this overly Romantic turn of phrase is intended as complimentary to Bultmann’s efforts, it’s worth remembering that idea of “defending” the all-powerful Lord of Creation is a very funny one. Taken more literally than poetically, this would be a completely pagan notion that only those living in antiquity could say with enduring sincerity from behind the veil of ignorance. The idea that the omniscient and omnipotent Lord Who brings reality into existence merely through speech, the omniscient and omnipotent Godhead Who sits beyond the intellectual capacities of His lowly creation and yet apparently still fears the sword or a pithily worded and philosophically suspect aphorisms from the generational minds of “professional quote makers”—all this should be rejected out of hand as a basic misunderstanding. However, we should at least stop to appreciate the political insecurity with which Bultmann jousted, not only living within Nazi Germany, but also wielding the now highly-influential conceptual analysis of a Nazi academic back against “the world”[9](#footnote-9).

His attempt to reinvigorate the message passed down from antiquity was not a childish retreat into the carefully arranged “allowed categories” of secularity setting the “allowed tone” for mousey, intellectually-insecure theologians, but rather an attempt to shake the Christians into action against the world by way of presenting the case of proclamation to the world come of age in a way that it could understand. Having seen the various issues that his particular contemporaneity had thrown upon him (and we would be wise to look at his sociological assessment with a sceptical comb to strip away the chaff of his self-assuredness), Bultmann was ready to strip back scripture until he found the point which could deliver us the same message, the same important central “knot”, that would deliver Christ to the world once again. The proclamation of Christ, in all its kerygmatic beauty, would be a new challenge for the modern man that arose from that old theme—where the oh so confident preachers of secular apocalypse had once assumed that they could overlook the pitiful *pianissimo* of Pauline religion, there was instead a *crescendo* rising up on the horizon[10](#footnote-10).

> “Since human life is lived out in time and space, man's encounter with God can only be a specific event here and now. This event, our being addressed by God here and now, our being questioned, judged, and blessed by him, is what we mean when we speak of an act of God.”[11](#footnote-11)

Bultmann’s errors, of course, could be laid out in many great tomes that would only just fall short of a brief Kierkegaardian reflection. And, of course again, they have been—repeatedly and brutally. It is now, once again, unfashionable to see value in Bultmann, instead rejecting his work with a kind of patronising academic chuckle that he was “historically significant” or “influential, if ultimately misguided”. Again, of course and of course again, these things are true and we should understand that they are true. But the reason for their truth is not because of their intellectual shortcomings or their potential to be revitalised with a newer, presumably postmodernist undergirding[12](#footnote-12). No, there is something that we must add to Bultmann’s work to save him from the collapse of liberal theology—but, in doing so, we shall abandon *sola scriptura*:

> “A transcendent God would have to first reveal himself in order for humanity to be able to speak about him... Whereas Bultmann “finally returns to the Socratic,” Barth “resembles the alternative proposed by Climacus.””[13](#footnote-13)

*As Marx had done with Hegel, Kierkegaard must be wielded against Bultmann to bring him back to reality and out of the ideal*

The problem of modernity, then, cannot be *merely* tackled as an exercise of intellectual struggle—especially in Christian cases of “minimal programmes” that allow for a “hook” with which the gospel can snag and grasp at these newly liberated freethinkers. Indeed, it might be the false impression that intellectual struggle is *the* issue of importance in a world that loves “chatter” that sets up the basic error of modern evangelism. We can take various modes of thought, obfuscating them into serving towards whichever purpose and whatever end we desire by way of a clever flourish or reimagined lexicon. There is nothing new under the sun in the mind of the radically dissident writer, who takes great pleasure in the revision and inversion of Marx, Spengler, Carlyle, Kropotkin, inevitably Hegel—and so on and so on, towards some pompous chest-bearing rhetoric that amounts to little more than the self-indulgence of a journalist, swaddled in the safety of some misdirecting identity.

But this makes a serious, basic philosophical error that these people don’t seem to be aware of—firstly, they attempt to speak about a human concern from the position of a journalist, but only a person who basically misunderstands the terms could ever mistake the contents of journalism as having any similarity with human concerns; but secondly, and more importantly, there is an unidentified smudging of the unknowability of the human agent, of the individual within a community, of *that individual* within “the Crowd” *a priori* that means we could never provide a *philosophical argument* for how we ought to act—instead, we can only ever yoke ourselves to the actual, to be human in the mode of “the Human One”.[14](#footnote-14) The witticisms and intellectual subversions of well-known works in the history of philosophy and theology become a kind of ash in the mouth for the person crying out for guidance on *what to do*. They need ash on the *forehead*—a sign of having done something, a sign that life has been lived!—not more empty inversions on empty inversions!

*A seemingly innocuous symbol is the seemingly innocuous beginnings for thought—in real life, not scholarly abstraction.*

The idea that “living life” could be reduced to a number of propositions is, again, I remind you, my reader, laughable and something that no reasonable person would ever assent to—unless they’re backed into a corner by philosophical methods and attempt to give those very methods a bit more life than they actually possess. There is something lost in ethics when we put our lives into words and those words onto paper, as if “living life” is something that can be abstracted, chopped up into little pieces, compared with various other amusingly reflection-worthy little pieces, and then organised into what is essentially an arbitrary podium of best “living life”s to worst “living life”s. This kind of approach, one can only assume, is what makes theories like consequentialism or, more particularly, utilitarianism attractive to otherwise seemingly thoughtful and reflective people—they pretend to themselves that the thought-object of their reflection actually is a kind of quantifiable “living life” that they can prod like a dead frog in a tray. The notion of “the good life”, if at all the idea is broached, is placed outside of the bounds of human possibility[15](#footnote-15), either by its consumerist deification of maximisation at all costs or by way of our self-idealisation, where the first step to finding what we ought to do is stripping away our material lives.

In that sense, as has been noted by both the *Flammenzeichen* that stood at the foot of modernity as enemies united against the void[16](#footnote-16) and the panicked, contemporary sojourners of the church militant that attempt to clear away the last vestiges of the false impression of Christendom’s church triumphant[17](#footnote-17), our task remains in the struggle against nihilism—a nihilism that has cleverly disguised itself in the garb of the age, as “the demand of the times”[18](#footnote-18), as an intellectual exercise amongst intellectual exercises[19](#footnote-19). In a world where the mass object of society doesn’t have an iota of philosophical training, they take to philosophy unguided—and don’t seem to notice the bounds where philosophy runs out of ground, where these very methods themselves lack purchase and begin to float away from reality. The philosophical novice, led by seemingly plausible philosophical experts, is guided onto the path of ideology and takes themselves to be sufficient modes for determining the value of their life and the way they interact with the other. Here, [the nihilism](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/christian-anarchism-and-the-problem) we identify is the rhetoric of the paper tigers, the intellectualisation of real life and real struggle into the abstraction of modernity and the isolation of the individual—to *become a self* in modernity is a rare thing, but to *become a self* is prior to all other things.

> “Apart from right relation to God and to oneself, a person cannot relate rightly to oneself or to others. This is why mere cognitive knowledge, even of necessary orthodox religious truth (i.e. atonement, Christology, sin), while necessary for religious transformation, is not sufficient to become a self, in the sense of Kierkegaard’s ideal.”[20](#footnote-20)

Knowing is not radical. Knowing is not even praiseworthy, taken from a certain angle—anyone can “know” something inasmuch as it suitable to then demand praise for what one “knows”, but it is a pretense, an emptiness, a vacuous self-presentation of the selfless individual before the approving audience to imply that knowing something which is publically accessible and the object of an applause could ever be something that could lead to something *humanly radical*. But the *humanly radical* must first reject that they could ever stand alone with a variety of suitably interesting facts to act as the raft beneath their knees that holds them up and over the deep that looms without end.

> “The majority of men in every generation, even those who, as it is described, devote themselves to thinking, (dons and the like), live and die under the impression that life is simply a matter of understanding more and more, and that if it were granted to them to live longer, that life would continue to be one long continuous growth in understanding. How many of them ever experience the maturity of discovering that there comes a critical moment when everything is reversed, after which the point becomes to understand more and more that there is something which cannot be understood. That is Socratic ignorance, and that is what the philosophy of our times requires as a corrective.”[21](#footnote-21)

Only then, my reader, do we find the mode where Christianity can emerge: in the life of the believer who does not *merely* exist as intellectualising “chatter” amongst intellectualising “chatter”.

### The Kerygma of the Caravan

Emerging as a Christian, then, is not a matter of intellectual jousting with the adversary. The devil himself would not be put down by way of a suitably tricky set of propositions or Bultmann’s intellectualist efforts to counter-manoeuver the world around him—no, instead, we must find a way to make Christianity the fruitful produce of Christian *life*. Here is where we find the Barthian-Kierkegaardian corrective to deliver Rudolf Bultmann: instead of taking the *kerygma* to be something that *leaps* out from scripture when we have made the embarrassed and nervous admission that we do or don’t believe this or that particular notion within the Christian positions (or the fundamentalist inversion to the strong assertion that we believe any all particular notions within the Christian tradition, as long as they are in the seemingly arbitrary bounds of whatever the particular preacher defines—if they define it at all), the proclamation becomes the strong link between the realisation of [ontological](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/the-case-for-christian-anarchism) *[change](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/the-case-for-christian-anarchism)* [in the believer](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/the-case-for-christian-anarchism) and their unfolding possibility in the flux of life. They are, in the very Kierkegaardian sense, the human realisation of [deliberation](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/search-for-a-method-deliberation).

“But this is no argument!”, says our unimpressed atheist interlocutor, having failed to understand a single word said so far. The entire point, my reader, is that Christianity *is not an argument*! Christ’s life *was not an argument*! The continuing power of the *kerygma*, the working of the Spirit, the emergence of “new creatures” in the life of the world—these *are not arguments*! Turning to a short story from Malcolm’s account of Wittgenstein’s reflections on what faith *actually* means as opposed to what the philosophy would desire for it to be:

> I believe that Wittgenstein was prepared by his own character and experience to comprehend the idea of a judging and redeeming God. But any cosmological conception of a Deity, derived from the notions of cause or infinity, would be repugnant to him. He was impatient with ‘proofs’ of the existence of God, and with attempts to give religion a *rational* foundation. When I once quoted to him a remark of Kierkegaard’s to this effect: ‘How can it be that Christ does not exist, since I know that He has saved me?’, Wittgenstein exclaimed: ‘You see! It isn’t a question of *proving* anything!’[22](#footnote-22)

This isn’t a radical proclamation of fideism, but rather that the proclamation of Christ, in the creative power of the Spirit to smooth away the caustic characteristics which lead us into temptation is prior to all philosophically “basic” propositions—the life of the believer emerges from the working of God in the history of the world, first choosing and turning those individuals towards Him and turning their lives from the pitiful determinism of *non posse non peccare* towards the freedom of *posse non peccare*[23](#footnote-23). It becomes *possible* *not to sin*, therefore it becomes possible to freely endeavour towards “living life” as it ought to be.

> “They are born from the Gospel's profound spirit of individualism, which refuses to let one's own actions and conduct depend in any way on somebody else's acts. The Christian refuses to let his acts be mere *reactions*—such conduct would lower him to the level of his enemy.”[24](#footnote-24)

Against the temptation to make the Lord into an intellectual exercise amongst exercises, we find that the life of the Christian is one which begins with the notion of *existence*, with standing out from merely *being*:

> “Existence, as its root meaning (*existere*) suggests, includes an *ex*, a standing-out, a coming out of being into definite manifestations. Existence requires a “standing” (*sistere*) “out from” (*ex*) being. The English language has to stretch in order to say that being in this sense may be thought of as the ground of existing, but the Greek and Latin terms that were familiar to the early church teachers made this point more transparent.”[25](#footnote-25)

Against the Catholic vision of *analogia entis*, we find that something was prior to all this that collapses our best attempts to put God in a box and to make the congregation into a wonderful concept amongst concepts—the *analogia fidei*, where God’s faithfulness in humanity, where God’s faithfulness *in you* and *for you*, my reader, is prior to all other Christian actions[26](#footnote-26). Here is some knowledge, the “little knowledge of revelation”[27](#footnote-27) which alters all other knowledge in relation to itself, that makes the reality of God clear to the believer prior to all other thought—we do not find God floating freely at the end of an argument or in the pages of scripture, but rather through His intervention in reality which we are called to respond to. By the way of the Lord, you and I can find that it is now possible to “stand out” from mere *being*, to “stand out” from the givenness of our actuality, and become something that we choose.

The invisible hand of the Spirit that descends onto the believer and the believer amongst believers is prior to all other content, all other intellectualist desires for a proof that saves their blushes from “the world”—the root of Christianity is not *merely* rational, but rather an absolute paradox brought into reality by the striving life of the believer:

> “That being said, calling the absolute paradox something that is not-rational, ir-rational, or supra-rational is not quite correct given that these negations of the term rational continue to show the priority of rationality. A more correct explanation of the absurd might be to call it otherwise than rational because this designation appreciates ‘the radical difference of order’ involved with the absolute paradox as the absurd.”[28](#footnote-28)

The notion of the “new creature”, who is awake in the possibility *not to sin*, to *hold himself back*[29](#footnote-29), to *[not choose whatsoever](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/on-the-art-of-not-choosing)*, is something of a “radical difference of order” that escapes our usual categories of rationality and irrationality. Having reached into creation, the very God Who sits beyond the limits of our horizon sets us apart as showing us what is possible to do *right here, right now*. And this possibility, should we choose to take it upon ourselves in the choice to choose ourselves and this life that is possible for us, is one which can only come to fruition through the *kerygma*—now that there is something different within *you*, my reader, there is a chance for you to endeavour forwards in the knowing unknowingness that sets apart the message of Christ from all other messages.

The [deliberation](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/search-for-a-method-deliberation) of human subject and the divine, then, is found replicated in the *imitatio Christi* as this change in the individual, whereby all categories are reevaluated, whereby all values are transvaluated, as a part of the caravan, the collective that endeavours towards *eschaton* together in the journey which gives them, those individuals in the collective of individual believers, the realisation of *being*, *becoming*, and the *kerygma*. We don’t so much hold Bultmann’s distinction between *kerygma* and myth as tantalisingly bring both together, assert that myth is necessary to understand *kerygma*, and then say that the individual *qua* faithful reflection of God’s will and love in creation *participates* in that myth as part of the ongoing narrative of the Christian story[30](#footnote-30).

> “Disciples gather and greet; are reconciled with God and one another; hear and share their common story; offer their needs and resources; remember Jesus and invoke his Spirit; and then share communion, before being sent out. Through worship–preparation, performance, repetition–God gives his people the resources they need to live in his presence.”[31](#footnote-31)

Free from the temptation of merely reacting, free from *non posse non peccare*, the *kerygma* is now capable of being seen by the eyes of secularity in its givenness: in the life of the believers that believe in community, sanctifying in their practice and thought in the journey towards *eschaton* through the *repetition* of the good—there, witness occurs. There, secularity is thrown back upon itself by the unveiling of the very goodness that was previously thought to be *impossible*.

But, like most mistaken commentators on the Christian message *qua* intellectualist pursuit that would make for wonderful pamphleteering, the mistaken Christian commentator on “deconstruction” fails to identify its deeply kerygmatic roots: deconstruction, in the proper Derridean sense, seems to be the same as “the collision”, “the double movement”, and “transcendence in repetition”[32](#footnote-32), exposing the love of apocalypse that opens up the the two-fold path of God’s intercession: firstly, in the acceptance of all change, even the change that no reasonable person could accept, because creation is, was, and always shall be *good* (***Genesis 1***); secondly, the life of the believer, in that realisation of their participation in the fundamental goodness of creation, knows that there is always more goodness available to them in the destruction of reality and the recreation in the Spirt of the Lord. When the congregation begins to imitate Christ, to imitate those humble early Christians in their emphatic sojourning from the state to state, from goodness to goodness, *that*, my reader, is where Christianity emerges—by the extension of myth by way of the *kerygma*, by way of progression towards the past, by way of seeing Christ’s face in the face of the other and doing His will in forgiveness, mercy, and the honest love for the neighbour which tears us all categories and makes them anew.

\---

My reader, if you pay attention to nothing else, then pay attention to this: this entire exercise in political theology is bankrupt, sophistry, nothingness if *you* and *I* do *nothing* after having read it. It is an intellectual struggle amongst intellectual struggles in a world which doesn’t need *more* intellectual struggles, but an earnest struggle of concreteness that comes down from the comfort of the balcony[33](#footnote-33) and plunges itself into and amongst “the Crowd”—as salt is plunged into and amongst the other ingredients in a stew (***Matthew 5:13-16***). Contrary to the modern leading theorists of an analytical persuasion, it seems plainly apparent that reason—even *good* reason—does not have any causative influence whatsoever and is as easily dismissed as it is received. Good reason, whatever that might refer to today and however that may change in the time to come, is not sufficient for anything aside from presenting good reason—but good works, my reader, *these* are the things of change, of reality, of concrete goodness. When we are capable of standing alone with the help of the other, we discover that Christianity as the expression of Christian character is not concerned with votes (irrelevance!) or particular propositions (obfuscation!) or even holding to an interpretation of some commandments over others against our better judgement (paganism!)—it is, first and foremost, to understand that to will is to do and to do is to *proclaim*.

In that sense, “politics”, i.e., [the objectifying dicta of political action as given by “the One”](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/contra-the-crowd) *[qua](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/contra-the-crowd)* [political agent over “the Crowd”](https://anarchierkegaard.substack.com/p/contra-the-crowd), is incompatible with Christianity. There is no Christian politics in the sense that I could give you that is more than a handful of lazy propositions which seem like perfectly socially acceptable sentimentalisms that lack salt—charitable, for example, is in constant danger of degrading the importance of mercy in the life of the giver into a wealth-management function where only the rich are permitted to be faithful. No, my reader, like salt, the Christian must accept that their addition is a necessary *je ne sais quoi* that the world will attempt to do without by way of force and power.

If there is a possibility of a Christian politics, i.e., a particular collection of practices available to the Christian in faith that occupies but does not conform to the practices of the world that we refer to as “politics”, then it is one which is a fundamental reanalysis of all things in the light of the gift of faith, the fire of the Spirit, and the new creature that walks amongst the world and refuses to *merely* react.
