# **Introduction**

Welcome back Vitalians!

Last month we announced the launch of our new Venture Studio - VitaLabs - now we would like to give you more information on how you can get involved!!

In order to attract top talent into VitaLabs, we are running a Fellowship program looking for creative and independent thinkers passionate about longevity to collaborate, ideate, and develop groundbreaking ideas.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!FddM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0e3966f3-e564-413b-a7dc-54bec7bb9176_1600x618.png)

We're looking for ideas that could result in longevity therapeutics or diagnostics, but are perhaps too early, risky or “out-of-the-box” for traditional funding. The bolder and more disruptive the better!

We are currently in the **Ideation Stage** of our roadmap!

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0h-E!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F461d36ac-ef4b-4277-9f16-46993b3d7b40_1600x704.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0h-E!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F461d36ac-ef4b-4277-9f16-46993b3d7b40_1600x704.png)

Prospective Fellows are required to ideate with the community and submit a project proposal by 30th December 2024, with up to 4 projects receiving a split of $200k from VitaDAO.

VitaLabs will then commission CROs to conduct experiments, with promising projects then going on to be minted as an IP-NFT and tokenised (IPT) to raise further funding, and potentially spun out into new start-ups. 

Project leads (2 per project max) of funded projects will become VitaLabs Fellows, receiving: mentorship and support, **a minimum of 10% of IPTs, a $2k/month stipend for 4 months and a performance bonus in VITA tokens**.

However, you don't need to be a project lead to get involved and get rewards - we will also be offering IPTs for community members who make creative/intellectual contributions to funded projects, no matter how small!

**What do I need to know?** 

Whether or not you have an idea, please **[apply to the VitaLabs Fellowship Program](https://airtable.com/app2qj6LmnX3PAPkk/pagJMduJMFRRmWs7Y/form)**. Then come and join the **[VitaLabs channel](https://discord.com/channels/810893413880561704/1298692730448515132)** in VitaDAO’s Discord and introduce yourself!

If you have an idea, great, come and discuss your idea with the VitaDAO community on the channel. We’ll all help you to strengthen your idea and formulate a plan to put a solid funding proposal together.

If you don't have an idea, don’t worry, come and join the discussions for any projects that interest you and be **rewarded with a share of the IPT** for any contribution, no matter how small!

Full information about the timeline and roadmap of the program can be found [here](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1zdzqoybZgxLYkiU2RTFuiMqKcHw7AoPP/view). We look forward to welcoming you into the **VitaLabs community**!

**Published Research Papers**

**[Implausibility of radical life extension in humans in the twenty-first century  
](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43587-024-00702-3)**The paper examines trends in life expectancy from 1990 to 2019 in the world's longest-lived populations, revealing a deceleration in mortality improvements and suggesting that radical life extension is unlikely without significant breakthroughs in slowing biological aging.  
*But don’t take our word for it - check out our full interview with Jay Olshansky himself down below!*

**[Metformin decelerates aging clock in male monkeys](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0092867424009140)**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!E-Gx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F173a0619-e4a4-4ccf-a234-df3efc80fc58_1024x1024.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!E-Gx!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F173a0619-e4a4-4ccf-a234-df3efc80fc58_1024x1024.png)

A 40-month study on male cynomolgus monkeys revealed that metformin significantly delays aging, including a remarkable reversal of brain aging by about six years, while preserving brain structure and cognitive function. Using advanced biological analyses, the study demonstrated metformin's potential to reduce overall biological age in primates, highlighting its promise for developing anti-aging therapies in humans.

**[Longevity biotechnology: bridging AI, biomarkers, geroscience and clinical applications for healthy longevity](https://www.aging-us.com/article/206135/text)**

At the 2023 Aging Research and Drug Discovery Meeting, experts highlighted the role of AI, biomarkers, geroscience, and biotechnology in combating age-related diseases and extending healthspan through innovative interventions and technologies. Huge collaborative effort!

**[Identifying specific functional roles for senescence across cell types](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674\(24\)01069-9)**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BWeH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6ff7a6b3-8d42-4a44-a0ad-78042645956a_1024x1024.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BWeH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6ff7a6b3-8d42-4a44-a0ad-78042645956a_1024x1024.png)

This study developed a genetic toolkit to trace, ablate, and manipulate p16Ink4a+ senescent cells, uncovering their distinct roles in liver fibrosis and repair. It revealed that clearing p16Ink4a+ macrophages reduces liver damage, while removing p16Ink4a+ endothelial cells worsens injury, highlighting the need for cell-type-specific senolytic therapies to address aging and disease.

**[Dietary restriction impacts health and lifespan of genetically diverse mice](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-08026-3)**

This study evaluated the effects of caloric restriction and intermittent fasting on the health and lifespan of genetically diverse female mice, finding that both approaches extended lifespan in proportion to the level of restriction. However, health effects varied, with 40% caloric restriction showing the strongest lifespan benefits but causing lean mass loss and immune changes, while intermittent fasting had limited effects in certain mice, underscoring the complexity of aging interventions and the need to define appropriate health and lifespan metrics.

**[A hematology-based clock derived from the Study of Longitudinal Aging in Mice to estimate biological age](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43587-024-00728-7)**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bSk_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6245b121-08f4-40bd-95ef-e091ffdc3544_1024x1024.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bSk_!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6245b121-08f4-40bd-95ef-e091ffdc3544_1024x1024.png)

This study developed a blood-based aging clock using routinely collected hematological markers and a deep neural network to predict biological age and assess the "aging gap" between biological and chronological age in mice. The aging gap was linked to frailty and mortality risk, with platelets emerging as the strongest age predictor, highlighting the potential of this approach as a practical tool for studying aging variability.

**[Is castration leading to biological aging in dogs? Assessment of lipid peroxidation, inflammation, telomere length, mitochondrial DNA copy number, and expression of telomerase and age-related genes](https://bmcvetres.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12917-024-04337-9)**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!FElO!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb1fe2326-6653-434d-805d-b056c1916517_1024x1024.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!FElO!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb1fe2326-6653-434d-805d-b056c1916517_1024x1024.png)

This study investigated the effects of castration on biological aging in male dogs, finding that castrated dogs exhibited reduced lipid peroxidation and inflammation, along with preserved telomere length and mitochondrial DNA copy number. Gene expression analysis also indicated delayed age-related changes, suggesting that castration may have a protective effect against biological aging in dogs.

**[Human iPSCs from Aged Donors Retain Their Mitochondrial Aging Signature](https://www.mdpi.com/1422-0067/25/20/11199)**

Aging is a major risk factor for neurodegenerative diseases, partly due to mitochondrial dysfunction, a key hallmark of aging. This study reveals that induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) from aged donors show impaired mitochondrial function, offering a potential model to screen drugs that target mitochondrial aging and promote healthy aging.

**Published Literature Reviews, Hypothesis, Perspectives and more**

**[Selective autophagy: a therapeutic target for healthy aging?](https://journals.lww.com/aa/fulltext/2024/01010/selective_autophagy__a_therapeutic_target_for.2.aspx)**

Aging at the molecular level involves the accumulation of cellular damage to DNA, proteins, and organelles, which autophagy—a cellular recycling process—helps mitigate by degrading and recycling damaged components. This review explores the roles of macroautophagy, microautophagy, and chaperone-mediated autophagy in maintaining cellular health, their contributions to aging, and how enhancing these pathways could offer therapeutic strategies for healthy aging.

**[The brain–body energy conservation model of aging](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43587-024-00716-x)**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XfdK!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb2c9f3f2-9a78-4011-9445-c6ad38ee502c_1024x1024.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XfdK!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb2c9f3f2-9a78-4011-9445-c6ad38ee502c_1024x1024.png)

Aging involves increased cellular energy expenditure due to damage-induced stress responses, while overall body energy expenditure remains stable or decreases, a paradox explained by the brain’s role in energy conservation. The brain–body energy conservation (BEC) model suggests the brain responds to energy demands from damaged cells by suppressing low-priority functions, driving aging symptoms like fatigue and immune changes, and shaping how aging trajectories can be modified by interventions.

**[The 3 I’s of immunity and aging: immunosenescence, inflammaging, and immune resilience](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/aging/articles/10.3389/fragi.2024.1490302/full?utm_source=twitter&utm_medium=social&utm_content&utm_campaign=imp_impartaut-_05-24_fragi_en_n--ww)**

Immunosenescence refers to the decline in immune system function with age, affecting both innate and adaptive immunity, leading to increased infection risk, reduced vaccine efficacy, chronic inflammation, and age-related diseases. This review explores the factors influencing immune aging, including sex, age, social determinants, and gut microbiota, while highlighting strategies to enhance immune resilience through lifestyle changes, stress management, and therapeutic interventions to support robust immunity in older adults.

**[Immunosenescence: A new direction in anti-aging research](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1567576924014218)**

Immune aging, the gradual decline in immune system function with age, leads to dysregulated immune cell responses, such as impaired neutrophil chemotaxis and reduced macrophage phagocytosis, resulting in poor vaccination responses, increased inflammation, and autoimmune disorders. This review highlights the cumulative effects of immune aging, including thymic atrophy, reduced hematopoiesis, and chronic inflammation, which elevate the risk of age-related diseases like Alzheimer’s and osteoporosis, suggesting that targeting immune system changes could be a promising anti-aging strategy.

**[Contextualizing aging clocks and properly describing biological age](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/acel.14377)**

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!H0pD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd305a565-6caa-4727-ae26-1166cbd327d4_1024x1024.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!H0pD!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd305a565-6caa-4727-ae26-1166cbd327d4_1024x1024.png)

The term "biological age" is often used interchangeably with the outputs of aging clocks, but it is a broader concept reflecting an individual's internal, external, and functional aging. Since aging is tissue-specific and highly complex, the authors recommend labeling aging clock outputs based on their input data (e.g., epigenetic age or transcriptomic age) or the clock's name to avoid conflating specific biomarkers with the multifaceted concept of whole-body biological age.

**[The role of the dynamic epigenetic landscape in senescence: orchestrating SASP expression](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41514-024-00172-2)**

Senescence and epigenetic alterations stand out as two well-characterized hallmarks of aging. When cells become senescent, they cease proliferation and release inflammatory molecules collectively termed the Senescence-Associated Secretory Phenotype (SASP). Senescence and SASP are implicated in numerous age-related diseases. Senescent cell nuclei undergo epigenetic reprogramming, which intricately regulates SASP expression. This review outlines the current understanding of how senescent cells undergo epigenetic changes and how these alterations govern SASP expression.

# **Interview with S. Jay Olshansky, PhD**

S. Jay Olshansky received his Ph.D. in Sociology at the University of Chicago in 1984. He is currently a Professor in the School of Public Health at the University of Illinois at Chicago, Research Associate at the Center on Aging at the University of Chicago and at the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine, and Chief Scientist at Lapetus Solutions, Inc. The focus of his research to date has been on estimates of the upper limits to human longevity, exploring the health and public policy implications associated with individual and population aging, forecasts of the size, survival, and age structure of the population, pursuit of the scientific means to slow aging in people (The Longevity Dividend), and global implications of the re-emergence of infectious and parasitic diseases.

**What inspired you to enter longevity research?  
**I was in graduate school at the University of Chicago in the late 1970s with an interest in adolescent fertility, but my advisor suggested I take a course in human development from Bernice Neugarten. During that class, Bernice was discussing her now famous phrase distinguishing between health and age – “young-old and old-old” – and I commented that I thought all older people were the same. Bernice of course knew otherwise as I was a young inexperienced graduate student, but instead of correcting me, she gave me an assignment to write a paper on what the demographic consequences would be if science were to successfully slow aging in people. This was the topic of a recent book she published on the topic, which she handed to me at the time. I entered the Regenstein library that semester, and it felt like I didn’t leave for two years – I was enthralled with the question about how long humans can live. Bernice encouraged me to turn the resulting paper into my master’s thesis, which I did, and she later became part of my dissertation committee on a related topic about human longevity.

**Which of the current theories of ageing do you think are the most convincing?  
**The answer depends on which theories you’re referring to – the “why” question or the “how” question. My first instinct is to think of evolutionary theories of senescence because evolution is always at the top of my thinking about ageing and longevity. If that’s what you mean, I favor all three major theories developed by Sir Peter Medawar, George Williams, and Tom Kirkwood; indeed, all three may be correct. If you mean the “how” question, I hold no favorite.

**How has the field changed since you started?  
**When I first started working in the field of ageing in the late 1970s, the field of gerontology itself was still nascent. The founding director of the National Institute on Ageing – Dr. Robert Butler (Uncle Bob as my wife and I used to refer to him) brought me under his wing. Bob spent an enormous amount of time educating the public on the importance and value of gerontology, and he appreciated my angle on the world of ageing because it combined the demographic and biological sciences into the new field of Biodemography that my colleagues and I helped to launch in the 1990s. Bob invited me to speak at most if not all of the Age Boom Academy meetings he held in New York through the International Longevity Center he created. I still recall a scientific meeting that Bob convened in Arizona decades ago where we (scientists from various disciplines) spent time trying to think of an alternative phrase to ‘anti-ageing medicine’ to describe the valuable work that was going on in the various fields of ageing (none of us were against growing older, it’s the consequences of ageing we were after). I believe we settled at the time on “Ageing Science”, but this eventually morphed into “The Longevity Dividend”, and then “Geroscience”. 

There are several important developments that have happened in the last four decades. The theoretical underpinnings of Geroscience have come together to answer the “why” question about ageing. Gerontology itself has come of age as the world has rapidly grown older; the baby boom generation is driving much of the demand for advancements in the science of ageing since this generation has reached the age where senescence has become noticeable; advances along multiple fronts in efforts to understand and modulate ageing across multiple species have been dramatic; funding for Geroscience has accelerated dramatically; the FDA now fully understands the value of having a single intervention influence multiple disease endpoints; entire new departments at Universities across the globe now support Geroscience; Biodemography and The Longevity Dividend/Geroscience are now the subject of both conferences and labels for entire University departments; funding for Geroscience has skyrocketed for a multitude of reasons; and large companies and billionaires have jumped in for profit or fame or both. The National Geographic special entitled The Age of Aging that came out in 2016 proved to be an important catalyst for the field. I could go on because the list is long but suffice it to say that the field has changed dramatically in the last few decades – mostly for the good in recent years.

**What mistakes do you think the longevity field has made?  
**The longevity field dates back thousands of years, and therein lie the earliest mistakes; many of which are still being made today – exaggeration, embellishment, dangerous experiments, emergence of the wild west of aging interventions, and the 10-year curse. I addressed these issues years ago in an essay entitled “Don’t Fall for the Cult of Immortality” and a book chapter entitled “Why Do Anti-Aging Doctors Die?”, so I won’t repeat those arguments here. For now, I’ll focus on the present.

**Here are some of the mistakes in a nutshell.  
**Embellishment/exaggeration: There is a tendency among some in the field to claim that one intervention or another will allow humans to live to a specified age; 100, 150, 200, 500, 1000, immortality – I’ve seen all of these numbers used. They’re all made up out of thin air – there is no scientific basis for choosing any of them, especially not by extrapolating from other species to humans where life is lived on different time scales, and there can be no empirical evidence that whatever is being sold can make humans live to any of these ages. Since claims of radical life extension are untestable scientific hypotheses because of the time it takes to verify the claim, there is no point in making them up. I’m a bit surprised that investors are buying into these claims since they should already know that those making them cannot demonstrate success.

Dangerous experiments and the wild west of ageing interventions: As I read contemporary claims of radical life extension without evidence to back it up, I’m reminded of the excitement about similar claims being made for using growth hormone as an elixir of youth in the 1980s – until it was later discovered to be linked to cancer. This ruse even went so far as to have physicians prescribing GH under off-label use as an anti-ageing therapeutic, which was later shown to be illegal – not to mention dangerous. I attended a conference in early 2024 in Roatan where an effort is underway to create an environment where experiments in ageing science can be accelerated outside of the purview of the FDA or its equivalent safeguards – by allowing people to come there and experiment on their own bodies with the latest ageing therapies. At one level the folks creating this accelerated “research” environment are taking matters into their own hands because they recognize the importance of getting a gerotherapeutic into the hands of a rapidly ageing world – including especially those funding these experiments. If their experiments actually work, they will have contributed to one of the most important public health advances in medical history and would indeed be held up as heroes in the medical and ageing worlds. I have great admiration for this goal as I hold the same ideals, but not at the expense of potentially risky experiments and naïve billionaires or millionaires willing to experiment on themselves or others. If anyone dies during these experiments in Roatan or elsewhere, even if unrelated to the ongoing interventions, it will likely bring a lot of good work in the field to a grinding halt.  

The 10-year curse: Go back in time to the Prolongevists of the past 2,000 years, and you’ll find a common theme – all described in the first chapter of my book The Quest for Immortality. When asked when their potion or elixir will generate everlasting life, or at the least radical life extension, the answer given is almost always 10 years. Even the alchemists from centuries ago fell prey to the 10-year curse as they needed to justify to their investors that the effort to transmute lead into gold would occur in enough time for the investor to benefit from the advance. I vividly recall a well-known researcher in the field of ageing sitting across from me during an interview with the BBC in the year 2000. When asked when the “breakthrough” would happen, this researcher naturally fell prey to the curse. I smiled when I heard this answer, only to later give the answer to this question that I think is most appropriate – which is that I hope a gerotherapeutic comes online and is shown to be safe and efficacious in enough time to benefit most people alive today.

**Other than your own, what do you think have been the biggest/important discoveries in the field?  
**My own research does not qualify as one of the most important discoveries in the field. My first instinct is to once again pivot to the field of evolution biology because it was the answer to the “why” question that drives all of our understanding of the timing of death and the expected duration of life of species. In my view (influenced by Theodosius Dobzhansky’s famous quote), “nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution” – so I would count the evolutionary theories of senescence as foundational. Then I move to the central question I’ve been addressing, which is how long humans can live at the individual and population level. Here the answer is easy – Benjamin Gompertz and his discovery of the “law of mortality” in his 1825 article. Many researchers in the field may be unaware of Gompertz, but our current understanding of why humans and other species live as long as we do, and how much longer we might live, has its intellectual and mathematical origins with Gompertz. When Gompertz’s “law of mortality” was explained by the evolutionary theories of senescence, it became immediately obvious how long humans can live and why. All of the advances in public health and medicine and aging science in the last two hundred years, are fundamentally influenced by these discoveries. Public health had the greatest impact on allowing ageing to become commonplace as it allowed most people to experience this phenomenon – ageing is rarely seen in most animals in the wild. Later in the 20th century advances in medicine led to the manufacture of survival time beyond what I refer to as the natural limit to human longevity – which has been estimated to be in the 30-60-year range – the first “soft” limit. Since most people alive today past age 60 are living on ‘manufactured time’ that was granted to us by medicine, long-lived populations are essentially patched together quite effectively by technological advances that did not exist for most of humanity’s existence. Humanity broke through the first ‘soft limit’ to longevity in the early 20th century when life expectancy at birth exceeded 60 years – I believe we’re now approaching the second soft limit as life expectancy approaches 85-87 years. The life expectancy limitations I speak of in the latest paper represent the diminishing longevity returns that follow naturally from the competing risk environment the world has entered.

I realize you might have been expecting an answer that veered into aging biology, but it’s too early to tell which of the many developments in the field will become the most important discoveries. However, if pressed to give an answer, it would be the discovery that ageing itself is inherently modifiable – it is a legitimate target that is ripe for intervention. If the avoidance of ill health is the metric of success representing the value of a discovery, there is no doubt that public health’s success in reducing early age mortality enabled most people to live long enough to experience ageing. These developments added a massive number of person-years of life more than a century ago, but this advance influenced only a relatively small segment of the population. By contrast, a successful gerotherapeutic will prove to be far more important (and valuable) as a medical breakthrough because of its ability to manufacture healthy life for a much larger segment of the population (nearly everyone in developed nations) relative to gains produced by public health.  

**What advice would you give to people currently working in longevity research?  
**Longevity research, or ageing science as I prefer, has emerged as potentially one of the most important new developments in public health to come along since the introduction of vaccines and antibiotics. Scientists and public health advocates and funders for this groundbreaking work, are now in the process of funding, developing, testing for safety and efficacy, and eventually manufacturing what I consider to be the most precious commodity on earth – healthy life. To the investors, your contribution to the advancement of science and medicine is foundational and at least on par with the great public health innovators of the 19th and 20th centuries. To the scientists, you’ve embarked on a public health journey of great importance, but this journey comes with a heavy responsibility to ensure safety first, then efficacy. When the science is done right and these pillars of public health are upheld, a viable gerotherapeutic will carry the designation of one of the most important public interventions in the modern era. The value of such an intervention to the individuals whose lives will be changed cannot easily be calculated in monetary terms since it’s difficult to put a price on a healthy year of life, but for national economies the value of a gerotherapeutic has been estimated to be $38T for just one year of healthy life. There is reason to believe the value to national economies of a successful gerotherapeutic could be much greater. In short, for those investing in and working on ageing science, we are on the cusp of a breakthrough that will change the face of human ageing for most current and all future generations.

**In light of your recent paper “Implausibility of radical life extension in humans in the twenty-first century” how do you see current public perceptions around "radical life extension" diverging from the scientific reality?  
**As I was doing interviews with the media following the publication of our Nature article, one common theme that emerged was that the reporters almost universally believed the narrative that all of humanity has been promised a lifespan of 100 years. At least that’s what they came to believe from some scientists in the past three decades, so they were more than a bit surprised at our conclusions. Once I explained that life expectancy at birth must decelerate as long as the biological process of ageing is immutable – and now we have three papers published during the last 34 years documenting the fact that the deceleration has already occurred – they understood the message. The reporters came into the interview expecting a dour assessment of the human condition, but our argument is that the slowdown in the rise in life expectancy is a product of success – not failure. Ours is a positive message – not a negative one. When enough people in a given cohort become exposed to the currently immutable force of the ageing of their bodies, the patchwork of manufactured time that modern medicine has given humanity must yield diminishing longevity benefits. The evidence for this phenomenon is now definitive. My message to the reporters was to find a way to communicate this reality to the outside world – not as a message that humanity is at the end of our longevity rope, but rather, as a celebration of the success of medicine in manufacturing our longer lives and as a clarion call to change our goal from life extension to health extension. We too would like to see humanity live longer; just not at the expense of extending healthy life. Since claims of radical life extension remain untestable scientific hypotheses, in Geroscience we don’t need embellishment and exaggeration to justify the new science of ageing that has the potential to transform what it means to grow older. 

Almost without exception, I’ve seen a gratifying turnaround in public perceptions about ageing and longevity just since the beginning of October. On the one hand I’ve seen realism set in that a life expectancy and lifespan of 100 is not in the cards for most people and countries – contrary to claims made by some mathematical demographers – but this has been accompanied by the excitement attached to contemporary efforts to extend healthspan through anticipated advances in Geroscience. The positive but realistic message we have been conveying in various ways since our original article in Science was published in 1990, has become reality – at least from what we’ve seen just within the past six weeks.

**You introduce the concepts of 'second soft limit' to life expectancy and "manufactured time". Can you elaborate on what these entails and how they might influence future longevity research priorities?  
**Let’s begin with the “first soft limit”, which is defined as how long humans would live in the absence of all medical interventions (no surgeries, dentistry, vaccines, pharmaceuticals, etc. etc.). Under these conditions I believe the life expectancy at birth for humans would be around 60 years – perhaps lower – which would be consistent with the historical record. Humanity broke through that record in the early 20th century by successfully manufacturing more than two decades of life with a life expectancy of about 80 years. It is important to recognize that the natural limit to human longevity is behind us, not ahead of us, as this provides perspective on the concept of “manufactured time”. Most people now alive past age 60 are living on survival time that was created for us by modern medicine. The result has been a rise in the prevalence of ageing related conditions, which realistically should be thought of as a product of success. To be clear, the rise of cancer, cardiovascular diseases, other ageing related causes of death, and a suite of non-fatal disabling conditions, are byproduct of success, not failure. We experience these conditions because we now routinely survive into age windows where these diseases have an opportunity to express themselves. We surmised in our Science article in 1990 that when about 80 percent of a birth cohort survives beyond age 65, the rise in life expectancy at birth should slow to trickle – and perhaps even stop rising altogether once it reaches 85 (82 for men and 88 for women). In our latest paper we provided evidence to suggest these limits could be slightly higher than previously thought (87 for national populations, 84 for men and 90 for women). This should not be thought of as a brick wall for longevity, but rather, a second “soft limit” that emerges as the currently immutable force of biological ageing becomes the dominant risk factor in a population. Humanity is nearing this second soft limit now.

**What role do you believe geroscience and the development of gerotherapeutics will play in overcoming these longevity limitations? What do you think are the most promising areas of this research?  
**This question implies that Geroscience is being developed exclusively to overcome longevity limitations. I see this as the wrong way to look at Geroscience. This question addresses, in part, the earlier question about what mistakes are being made in the field. While I have no problem recognizing that further life extension will result from Geroscience, this should be a secondary goal – the primary goal should be health extension. My view here harkens back to a foundational view that the natural limit to human longevity is actually far behind us – by decades – and that most people alive today at later ages are living on survival time that has already been successfully manufactured by modern medicine. By contrast, protagonists for limitless longevity hold that the life expectancy limit for our species remains ahead of us on the invisible horizon. It is critically important to understand the distinction between moving away from a longevity limit vs moving toward one. The former is like stretching a longevity rubber band, so the further from the natural limit the more difficult it becomes to patch us together to live longer (this is what our research during the last three decades has demonstrated); the latter incorrectly assumes that a natural longevity limit is beyond the horizon and humanity just needs to avoid the harmful behavioral risk factors that are holding us back. Indeed, many of today’s medical and pharmaceutical interventions have successfully overcome the harmful behavioral risk factors that have accumulated in longer-lived populations today. Stents and statins and pharmaceuticals and surgery – among many other medical interventions – successfully combat the obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, cancer, and many other ailments linked to the ageing of our bodies – at least for those able to avail themselves of these developments.

As for promising areas of research in Geroscience, I’ll leave that to the growing number of scientists in the field that are conducting groundbreaking work on efforts to demonstrate that modulating ageing in humans is possible. My preference is that we pursue all of the options that offer promise – not knowing in advance which one will work. It seems likely that there will be multiple pathways to an ageing intervention – it’s a race to healthspan extension that I’m glad is ongoing.

**You famously bet with Steven Austad that no one born before 2000 would live to be 150 years old. Do you still feel confident in your position, or have any recent scientific advances made you reconsider / what would data could make you reconsider?  
**I’m more confident now than when we made the bet. Since then, there has been absolutely no movement in the tail of the human survival distribution – no one has even come close to the world record 122-year longevity experienced by Jeanne Calment. In fact, there has been imperceptible movement in the number of supercentenarians. Even if a discovery was made today that was documented to be a gerotherapeutic with a systemic influence on multiple disease endpoints, the youngest person to use it would be 25 years old – with a quarter century of accumulated damage to their body. I’m hopeful that a gerotherapeutic will become available before the birth cohort of 2000 reaches 35 years of age, but with each passing day the probability that Steve will win the bet grows dimmer because the available population from which at least one lucky person must emerge and benefit from a theoretical breakthrough in ageing science and live decades beyond the current world longevity record, becomes smaller across time. The only data that would make me reconsider my highly favorable position on this bet is if a gerotherapeutic comes online and those taking it experience either zero mortality for an extended period at older ages, or we see a significant change in the slope of the intrinsic mortality curve past age 65. As such, I’d like to thank Steve in advance for contributing to the future success of my genetic lineage.
