After dinner one evening, my teenage daughter exclaimed, “Put me on aux!”

We’ve developed a family [ritual](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jiIAWY2MTQY&t=237s) of listening to music while my wife and I make dinner. When the kids were little, they would crawl around the kitchen floor or sit in their highchair as we played Gen X and millenial hits, dancing and cooking. The adults may have been the DJ, but our song choices were intentional. My wife would usually play something from [Backstreet Boys](https://backstreetboys.com/), and I would counter with *[I Want You Back](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y2bVIBwpCTA)* by the Jackson 5, my *original* boy band. We would go back and forth layering with our favorite songs in a sort of *Songversation*.[1](#footnote-1) We wanted to share the soundtracks of our lives with our children. We would play songs we grew up listening to and, inevitably, the teacher (and music aficionado) in me would want to trace it back to the original influences. As our children grew, they requested songs for us to play. The only rule was *no judgement until the end of the song*. You didn’t have to like a song, but you had to listen to it fully before making a pronouncement. Love it or hate it, you had to give it a chance.

At the end of the evening, after we put the kiddos to bed, we would pick an album, pour a bourbon, sit and listen to it, start to finish. Certain albums demand to be listened to in their entirety: The Beatles’ *[Sgt. Pepper’s Lonely Hearts Club Band](https://www.thebeatles.com/sgt-peppers-lonely-hearts-club-band-0)*, *[Blue](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MvR7Dkg4NQU)* by Joni Mitchell, Nirvana’s *[Nevermind](https://www.getalternative.com/nirvanas-nevermind-track-track-25-years-later/)*, *[Songs in the Key of Life](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-features/inside-stevie-wonders-epic-magnum-opus-songs-in-the-key-of-life-124478/)* by Stevie Wonder, *[The Miseducation of Lauryn Hill](https://pitchfork.com/reviews/albums/22035-the-miseducation-of-lauryn-hill/)* by Lauryn Hill, *[Tapestry](https://www.theguardian.com/music/2021/feb/12/50-years-of-carole-king-tapestry)* by Carole King. These albums can’t be played in the background; they require active listening to fully appreciate.

So when my now teenage daughter demanded to be *[put on aux](https://www.urbandictionary.com/define.php?term=I%27m%20on%20aux)*, I knew that I was in for a treat. She wanted to share *her* favorite music. After a few songs, my teenage son joined in offering some of *his* favorites. [Listening to my teenage children’s taste in music evolve as they get older is a privilege](https://bsky.app/profile/mrneibauer.bsky.social/post/3m5fmbg4ckk26). As I sat listening to [Ava Max](https://www.avamax.com/), [Daft Punk](https://daftpunk.com/), [Yungblud](https://www.yungbludofficial.com/), [Katy Perry](https://www.katyperry.com/), [Eminem](https://www.eminem.com/), and [Rihanna](https://www.rihannanow.com/), I could follow the musical thread back to [Aerosmith](https://www.aerosmith.com/), [Tupac](https://www.2pac.com/), [Run-D.M.C](https://rockhall.com/inductees/run-dmc/)., [The Rolling Stones](https://rollingstones.com/), [The Velvet Underground](https://www.velvetundergroundmusic.com/), [The Cure](https://www.thecure.com/), [Kraftwerk](https://kraftwerk.com/), [Mariah Carey](https://mariahcarey.com/), [Beyoncé](https://beyonce.com/), [Madonna](https://www.madonna.com/?srsltid=AfmBOorEajZfCET3beAPy5lmlk8K6rL6st99MzSz4alzm93lKsRUJSXl), [Britney Spears](https://britneyspears.com/), and [Aaliyah](https://www.biography.com/musicians/aaliyah).

# Now Spinning Classic Albums

Professor Michael Spitzer, musicologist and author of *[The Musical Human: A History of Life on Earth](https://www.bloomsbury.com/us/musical-human-9781526602749/)*, says about the relationship between music and the human species,

> *Music is participatory in a duel and interlinked sense. We fill our lives with music as a soundtrack. We sink ourselves into the music in an act of focused listening.*

Throughout my career as an educator, I’ve watched my students interact with pop culture. First it was the Internet, then video games, and now social media and music streaming. I’ve observed how my students don’t really *listen* to music with intention. [It plays in the background of their favorite TikTok or video game](https://open.substack.com/pub/adrianneibauer/p/know-thy-music?r=gtvg8&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false). *How often do ten-year-olds actually sit down and listen to an entire album?* From my observations, it is rare.

This is why, during the summer of 2023, I designed a listening learning experience focused on the concept of what it means for something to be called a “Classic Album.” I called it *Now Spinning* and my [plan was to pick my favorite albums from the 1920s through the 2020s](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1xKb4Y5eUu-tJSSA7y9KGSQ8PJrrGK7yeRCffnXIznxU/edit?usp=sharing) and listen to them throughout the school year. I modeled it after [Questlove’s NYU Classic Albums course](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/questlove-heads-to-the-classroom-for-classic-albums-course-at-nyu-101427/). Starting on the first day of school, each morning I featured each day with an artist, album and song from a particular decade. A journal prompt and class discussion helped students take a close look at the lyrics and the political, economic, socio-cultural factors that influenced each song/artist. I gave students context as to why these songs and artists have lasted throughout history. During the 2023-2024 school year, I reserved the beginning of the day for students to listen to music as a purposeful activity, not just as background objects.

## Attentive Listening

I started playing music when students walked into my classroom. On the board, I usually wrote something I wanted them to listen for (e.g.: *What instruments do you hear).* Once the bell rang, I picked a song and gave them a bit of background information before dropping the needle. As the music played, I struggled to keep students quiet. I would often pause the song and encourage them to focus on a particular melody or lyric. What I observed was that most students only half-listened and would miss the point I was trying to make. I did some research and found music analysis guides with guiding reflection questions and frameworks such as [SQUILT](https://www.squiltmusic.com/) (Super Quiet Uninterrupted Listening Time). However, listening frameworks are only helpful if students are actively engaged in the music. Before I could get to music history, I learned quickly that I needed to teach my student how to actively listen to music. Teaching my student to *really* listen to a particular song is much harder than it sounds.

### Types of Listening

Instead of repeatedly telling my students to listen, *really listen!* I taught them about the listening tiers: *[affective listening, structural listening, and dialogic listening](https://campuspress.yale.edu/yctl/active-listening/)*.

**Affective listening** is the most basic. What instruments do you hear? What genre of music is this? What emotions does this evoke? How fast or slow is it?

**Structural listening** is more focused on a particular element of a song. How does the artist or composer move from one idea to another? Why? What underlying questions does the piece pose and how does it answer these questions, if at all? How does the lyrics relate to the sounds?

**Dialogic listening** is the most complex and time-consuming. Here, students are analyzing what they are hearing with other primary and secondary sources. For example, I might ask students to compare one song to different song by the same artist. I could have students listen to a song and then read a newspaper article or review from the same era.

Once students learned the language neuroscientists use to describe listening in the brain, I gave them more specific directions. As a class, we practiced affective listening before we “leveled up” to structural listening. It took most of the school year, but by April, students were able to sit quietly during a song and actively listen and analyze it.

After a year with *Now Spinning Classic Albums*, [I realized that there is just too much good music and not enough time in the day](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1xKb4Y5eUu-tJSSA7y9KGSQ8PJrrGK7yeRCffnXIznxU/edit?usp=sharing). The main constraint I had was squeezing this listening learning experience into the first ten minutes of the day. I would love to teach music and history all day long, but I still have the other content areas to teach. I decided that if I want students to study political, economic, or socio-cultural factors that influence songs and artists, I need to give them more than a ten-minute factoid.

# The *Now Spinning* Club

*Now Spinning* needed to move to an after school club. I would keep playing music in my classroom, but in order to go into more depth with students, I needed more time, and to find students who were more interested to listening to and discussing music.

I created a *Now Spinning Journal* where students recorded their listening observations, reflections, and analyses. I read *[This Is What It Sounds Like: What the Music You Love Says About You by Dr. Susan Rogers and Ogi Ogas](https://www.thisiswhatitsoundslike.com/)* and learned why we like certain music, and how we each possess a unique “listener profile” based on our brain’s reaction to seven key dimensions of any record: authenticity, realism, novelty, melody, lyrics, rhythm, and timbre. And finally, I gathered resources on ***ethnomusicology***, the study of music in its social and cultural contexts. Ethnomusicologists examine [“music as a social process in order to understand what music is and what it means to its practitioners and audiences.”](https://www.ethnomusicology.org/page/AboutEthnomusicol)[2](#footnote-2) The main points I wanted to teach students would be:

1.  Employing a global perspective on music (encompassing all geographic areas and types of music).
    
2.  Understanding music as social practice (viewing music as a human activity that is interrelated with its social and cultural contexts).

My first after-school *Now Spinning* club included 15 students. We met every Tuesday in my classroom and spent time getting to know each other musically. I taught them about ethnomusicology, how our brains listen to new and familiar music, and how we each have a unique listener profile. After a few meetings, some students decided to bring their parents’ vinyl records to play on my turntable, and we would all chat about a particular song, or I would share some cool facts about the entire album. It was a fantastic after-school experience! I helped that group of students broaden their musical tastes and, in the process, they learned more about themselves musically. We even attempted to create a [student music magazine resembling](https://www.canva.com/design/DAFK7oEVX_s/NDlPwFyOIcbZkuwDcUCnUQ/edit?utm_content=DAFK7oEVX_s&utm_campaign=designshare&utm_medium=link2&utm_source=sharebutton) *[Rolling Stone Magazine](https://www.canva.com/design/DAFK7oEVX_s/NDlPwFyOIcbZkuwDcUCnUQ/edit?utm_content=DAFK7oEVX_s&utm_campaign=designshare&utm_medium=link2&utm_source=sharebutton)*.

I was able to run *Now Spinning* for a couple of years before funding for clubs disappeared and my time after school was filled with standardization meetings. Reflecting on my time with *Now Spinning*, I learned that we can’t blame students for not actively listening to music. Students want more for their education than basal readers and standardized written responses. When I provided opportunities for students to listen to music and talk about music and connect with others through music, they flocked to *Now Spinning*. Students don’t talk about music because we don’t give them a chance to listen to real music. If students don’t show an interest in the creative arts, it’s because we limit their exposure. We allow them to plug into TikTok and Xboxs for hours at a time, and then complain that all they want to do is play video games. If we want students to deepen their musical interests, we have to give them interesting music to listen to and then teach them how to actively listen to that music.

The only reason my daughter was able to take control of the music is because we have created a culture around listening to and discussing music. That night, I learned a lot about my children. They shared music that resonates with them and turned me onto some new artists. Music is a human activity and listening to music is a social practice. It’s a shame that these types of experiences are being cut from public education in the name of standardization, efficiency, and higher test scores. If we want our students to lead rich lives, we have to give them richer experiences than prescribed templates. Otherwise, they will go through life, as if on a guided tour, unable to truly understand themselves or take control of their lives. They will consume substandard content instead of [rich artistic media](https://www.contemporaryartissue.com/what-are-the-7-forms-of-art-a-complete-overview/), never creating for themselves, or feeling creative.

Playing music for my students may never fit in any sort of traditional public education classroom. *Now Spinning* may have been a fleeting after school club. However, these experiences taught me how important it is to stay connected to my humanness and unapologetically bring that to my classroom every day. As says in *[Music: A Subversive History](https://bookshop.org/a/117824/9781541644366),*

> *Music is a change agent in human life, a force of transformation and enchantment.*

Hopefully, one day I will have an opportunity to resurrect this listening learning experience for students. I can’t wait for them to want to be on aux.
