#### *I fell in love the way you fall asleep: slowly, and then all at once.*

John Green, *[The Fault in Our Stars](https://bookshop.org/a/117824/9780142424179)*

I first flirted with the idea of being a teacher in high school. My mom ran a daycare center out of our suburban home. We had cribs, playpens, plastic toys, and Fisher-Price furniture spread throughout the house. Weekdays, working parents would drop their babies off early before rush hour traffic. If I wasn’t awake, dressed, and out of my bedroom by 6:00 AM, my mother was kicking me out to put a baby down for their morning nap. Once my door closed, my bedroom was no longer my own; off limits.

I learned to change diapers before I learned to drive. When I got home from school, it was my job to gather the sleepy-eyed napping babies, change their diapers, and play until pickup. For years, I was eager to come home from school and be joyfully ambushed by kids. Hide-and-seek. Horsey. Coloring books. Snack time. Simon Says. Duck, duck, goose. My afternoons were filled with laughter before I retreated to my bedroom to do homework. There is nothing like the dopamine rush I would get when making those little ones laugh. I watched babies grow from drooling and babbling to toddling and talking. When some toddlers aged into preschool, more babies were introduced to our little daycare family. At 16, however, I wanted a *paying* job, so I naturally when looking for something where I could continue working with children.

My first serious job was working summers at [Camp Greenwood](https://clubgreenwood.com/programs/camp-greenwood/), a daycare and sports camp attached to an athletic center. I worked my way up from games and crafts to coaching, lifeguarding, and eventually, planning field trips as the head counselor. The closer I got to graduating high school, though, the greater the pressure was to attend college, decide on a major, and start preparing for my future career. I knew that I loved making kids laugh, but I couldn’t be a professional camp counselor or run a daycare. Teachers worked with children, but I wasn’t a very good student. I didn’t like school. Learning was hard and my classes were boring. You have to be smart to be a teacher. I loved playing with kids, and making them laugh, but that didn’t fit with my school experiences. In my teenage brain, and I had only one option: join the circus!

# Clown College

Located in Sarasota, Florida, [Ringling Brothers and Barnum & Bailey Clown College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ringling_Bros._and_Barnum_%26_Bailey_Clown_College) was founded in 1968. This tuition-free training program was rigorous. Running for 10.5 weeks, a typical day consisted of classes in acrobatics, juggling, stilt walking, makeup, and costuming. Here, I would learn the history of clowning, the different types of clowns, and develop my own character and routine. My teachers would be legends such as [Dick Monday](https://www.youtube.com/live/BAT9D7TiAfI?si=QPwkHOsjD82sw3tO) and [Tim Holst](https://www.circusringoffame.org/inductees/tim-holst/). Upon graduating, I would become a member of [Clown Alley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clown_alley), move into their 52-car train, and travel around the world spending 11 months a year (2–3 days each week) covering 13,000 miles and visiting as many as 45 cities every year. Applications were by invitation only after an audition.

I was so nervous printing out my MapQuest directions and driving my brown and tan 1986 Ford Bronco to the auditions. I told no one, especially not my parents. When I arrived, I parked in an empty parking lot. It was hot that day and the shimmer floated just above the empty concrete spaces. I didn’t see any other cars in the arena parking lot normally reserved for professional basketball games. I made my way inside to center stage, and found chairs circling a huge box of of props. I was surrounded by hundreds of empty arena seats; the spotlights were aimed directly at this circle of folding chairs. I took my seat. A man with a clipboard gave us directions. One by one, we were asked to approach the chair, use any props we wanted, and be funny. There were no other directions; no guidance on *how* to be funny or *what* they wanted us to do. We each had ten minutes and a box of props. One lady grabbed a dusty red bowler hat and pretended that she couldn’t get it off her head. She tried and tried, but the hat didn’t budge. Another guy tried stuffing all of the clothing items (shirts, pants, belts, shoes) into his own pants. It was hilarious because it looked like he packing for a trip.

Lots of people showed off their balancing and acrobatic and tumbling skills. People balanced broom handles, huge PVC pipes, even the folding chair in the center ring. A few girls did cartwheels and handstands. Each time someone took their turn, my stomach churned. I felt like I was going to be sick when they announced, *Who’s next?* Without thinking, I stood up and took my place next to the props. I chose a short wicker broom and pretended to tidy up from everyone’s audition. Suddenly, I began studying the broom, and discovered it was actually a baseball bat, and I was a famous baseball player. I walked up to the imaginary plate, used the broom to knock the dirt from my cleats, and began taking a few warm up swings. I eyed the facilitator and gave him my best evil-eye glare. I waited for the right pitch and swung. *Strike One!* I was so nervous that I never spoke. In fact, it wasn’t until *Strike Two!* that I noticed how quiet it was. I was the only one who didn’t speak during the audition. I finally connected with a base hit past second base. Scrambling, I made it to third base and slid into clothes I had swept into a pile. In hindsight, I didn’t have a lot of skills. I couldn’t juggle. I had terrible balance. I wasn’t very graceful or athletic. I just played.

Once we were seated, the facilitator said a few words about how clowns are not actors who portray roles. Professional clowns let their inner child come out to play for everyone to see. Great clowns invite the audience to play along. The facilitator passed out course catalogs to those they wanted to apply. My shaky hands accepted a catalog.

I did not become a professional clown. [Ringling Bros. closed its college in 1997 due to declining attendance, high operating costs, and controversies over animal handling](https://youtu.be/KUYCe9e7_dQ).

\---

My path to teaching has since led me through college, student teaching, teaching abroad, being a substitute teacher, and finally, to my own classroom. The waymarks that have kept me steadfast are connection, joy, laughter, and humanity. I had no idea I was going to be a teacher, but what kept me racing home each day after school was the joyful bonding I found with the toddlers in my mother’s daycare. We played. We laughed. And though I didn’t know it at the time, we learned. I learned how to make learning fun. I learned that kids are more likely to do hard things when they feel you are there next them. Whether aged 3 or 13, we all learn best when we feel connected, and one of the [quickest ways to connect is through joy and laughter](https://open.substack.com/pub/adrianneibauer/p/levity?r=gtvg8&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false).

I love teaching. It was love at first sight. I was never drawn to the intellectuality of teaching and learning. I never felt a pursuit in the noble and selfless act of teaching, making the world a better place. I didn’t become a teacher because I felt that I had something of great value to offer students. I had no notion of passing along wisdom. I fell in love with the inherent joy of connecting with others while teaching them something. I became a teacher because I wanted meaningful connections with my students. When I was a student, I didn’t feel connected with anyone or anything in school. I can count on one hand the number of teachers who made a lasting impact on me, and most of them entered my life in college as I was studying to become a teacher.

As with most nascent relationships, I didn’t realize how hard teaching would be. I only expected fun and laughter because that is what I first fell in love with. I am a loyal partner, however, and so I have stuck with teaching these 24 years, and in that time have deepened my affection. Through hardships and happiness, I have learned to appreciate teaching more than ever. I have learned how to be a good teacher, and as with any relationship, I have learned how to give and take, even when I give more than I should. I give because I keep chasing what I first fell in love with: laughter. Making others laugh brings joy, and this joy creates human connection. When [teaching feels heavy](https://open.substack.com/pub/thebrokencopier/p/the-heaviness-of-teaching?r=gtvg8&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false), and I’m feeling more overwhelmed than ever, these are the times I go looking for that original spark I first fell in love with. I chase the dopamine rush.

# Glimmers

[Deb Dana](https://www.rhythmofregulation.com/about), LCSW, is a clinician, consultant, and speaker specializing in [complex trauma](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/24881-cptsd-complex-ptsd). She coined the term *glimmers* in her book *[Polyvagal Theory in Therapy](https://www.pesi.com/item/the-polyvagal-theory-therapy-42303?utm_term=&utm_campaign=US+%7C+BH+%7C+NB+%7C+N/A+%7C+Dynamic+%7C+Performance+Max+%7C+US+%7C+RET&utm_source=google&utm_medium=cpc&hsa_acc=7268932594&hsa_cam=20947151930&hsa_grp=&hsa_ad=&hsa_src=x&hsa_tgt=&hsa_kw=&hsa_mt=&hsa_net=adwords&hsa_ver=3&gclsrc=aw.ds&gad_source=1&gad_campaignid=20940663585&gbraid=0AAAAABu1_nxjDL2d2AhgOvmiBxmATbE5F&gclid=CjwKCAjwjffHBhBuEiwAKMb8pJWj0O8GsQFu32xH83DcjdWDgTDsAimqkifCjw-sdsjxZ3LE5t8KrRoCSQcQAvD_BwE)*. She was looking for a word to use with clients who were complex trauma survivors, struggling to find moments that did not trigger a bodily danger response. Glimmers are micro-moments in the day that spark a sense of joy, and help you feel grounded and connected, even during stressful times. From cuddling with your pet to making your toddler laugh, glimmers tell your nervous system that you are safe. Your body shifts your system’s response from defense to calm. Dana explains that when we notice and name glimmers, we train our nervous system to be open to more of these moments and expand our sense of well-being. We must make time to notice glimmers in our daily lives or busyness and stress will lead to fatigue, weariness, and burnout.

No one can learn under chronic stress. Although eustress (short-term positive and manageable stress) can increase one’s motivation, focus, and performance, distress leads to anxiety, reduced ability to concentrate, and ultimately, underachievement. Dana taught her clients how to find ways to ameliorate chronic stress with glimmers. She explains that glimmers are as unique as people. What bring us joy may not be the same for the other person, but certain micro-moments of goodness help our brain and body to restore to our thriving state of being.

> *You feel something happen inside, there’s an energy that happens around a glimmer, and your brain then marks it as well.*

By understanding what glimmers spark our joy (and the joy of others) we can work to promote a greater sense of connection with those we engage with.

Last week, on our daily [Pack Walk](https://adrianneibauer.substack.com/i/154093919/a-daily-pack-walk), I blasted music from my fanny pack speakers and danced and embarrassed my students. In that micro-moment, I was “so cringe”, but every student had a huge smile on their face. My cringe was their joy, and I played that glimmer moment at full volume for the entire class to experience together.

During my lunch break, I walked through the main office to make copies. I noticed a third-grader, barely tall enough to reach the office window, asking for help to find his coat. I walked him to his classroom, and although he stammered severely, that didn’t stop him from talking the entire time. As we retraced his steps, he told me all about being in third grade and how cold it was outside and how much he needed to find his new coat or his mom would be angry. When he finally located it, the smile on his face beamed and warmed my heart. This glimmer stayed with me the rest of the day.

At my tea station, students have taken to placing instant ramen bowls and Cup Noodles[1](#footnote-1) for me fill with hot water before lunch. On Thursday, I counted 14 Cup Noodles, every one with a student’s name written in Sharpie. As I resigned myself to boiling water and filling this assembly line of ramen, I noticed that one cup had a name I didn’t recognize. When I asked who this student was, my students told me that a student in another class snuck in his ramen bowl for me to fill up, too. I had to laugh!

\---

Looking for glimmers reminds me of why I fell in love with teaching 25 years ago. The stressors are real and ever-increasing. When I talk with teachers, everyone is feeling more stressed. There are more unreasonable expectations and tighter administrative control. Teachers feel like they have less autonomy than ever before. Between the endless meetings and myriad frustrations, teachers are overwhelmed and fatigued. The glimmers are there, though, if you look for them. I can’t control how much pressure I feel, but I can steal a moment to make my students laugh. My relationship with teaching has been a wild ride, and I don’t expect the heaviness of this school year to abate any time soon. [This, too, shall pass](https://open.substack.com/pub/learned/p/this-too-shall-pass?r=gtvg8&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false). I’ve decided to return to the wayfinding glimmers that have helped lead me to today: connection, joy, laughter, and humanity.

This is why I became a teacher, and how I will remain a teacher for many years yet.

Have a great week!

— Adrian
