I first learned that I was dyslexic when my middle son was diagnosed in the second grade. My wife, and her entire family, are excellent readers, so given the hereditary nature of the learning disability, and my lack of childhood reading experiences, it made sense that my son’s dyslexia came from me. When we discovered that our youngest daughter was also dyslexic, it confirmed that my school struggles and lack of positive reading memories came because of my dyslexic brain. Now, as a veteran teacher, in hindsight, I’m amazed that I was never labeled with a learning disability. I never had an IEP or spent any time in a SPED classroom. Somehow, despite my dyslexia, I learned how to read; I progressed through each grade level without note.

I read my first novel in the fifth grade. Little House in the Big Woods by Laura Ingalls Wilder introduced me to a world that was larger and more significant than my suburban world. Laura’s life, while an idealised depiction of living in the woods of Wisconsin in the 1870s, felt more real to me than my privileged one. Pa smoked venison inside a hollow log, hunted alone all day in the bitter cold of the Big Woods, and played the fiddle in the evenings. The work he did was vital to his family’s survival and wellbeing. My dad, and the other dads in the neighborhood, wore suits and “went to work” doing something in offices. We never really knew what our fathers did every day. But, I remember sitting in my classroom, reading Winter DAYS and Winter Nights, and thinking that a meaningful life comes from hard work, family, and making a tangible difference in the lives of those you love. Reading about the Ingalls gave me a perspective of what family life could look like. Their lives were immeasurably more difficult than my own, but they were happy. I didn’t realize a family could be that close, and that happiness can come from hard work, togetherness, and loving care.

I don’t remember reading Little House anywhere, but at my desk. I don’t remember what my other classmates were reading. I don’t remember talking about the little log cabin with my teacher. I definitely didn’t talk about what I was reading at home. All I remember is losing myself in the Big Woods of Wisconsin. It is a fleeting and solitary memory. After that, my next vivid reading memory comes much later, my sophomore english teacher, Mr. D, reading Don Quixote aloud every day in class. I grew up believing that reading was something one did alone and at school, so this read aloud felt subversive. Occasionally, a teacher might ask their students to discuss a particular chapter or symbol, but mostly, reading was a non-event. It was something that existed outside of me, and I had no interest in making this banality a larger part of my life.

Balanced literacy was an emerging approach to reading instruction as a way of bridging the growing divide between phonics and whole language practices. Even though, in 1967, Harvard researcher Jeanne Chall published Learning to Read: The Great Debate, concluding that phonics instruction was far superior to using a look-say method, especially for those marginalized students from underprivileged families, Balanced Literacy pedagogy was gaining traction in U.S. classrooms. A three-cueing system encouraged students to use context clues, sentence structure, and pictures to identify unfamiliar words. As a result, more teachers employed independent reading (Drop Everything And Read or DEAR) time, where students selected books they were interested in. The rationale was that if interest and motivation were high enough, proficient students could improve their reading levels and fluency independently. This would give teachers time to teach lessons in phonics and decoding to their most struggling readers, while maintaining student choice and independent reading.

My memory of reading Little House in the Big Woods probably took place during DEAR time. Since I don’t remember any lessons in phonics and decoding, I can only assume that either my dyslexic brain doesn’t recall, or my teacher favored more independent reading time for her fifth-graders. Now that I’m a fifth-grade teacher, the later makes sense because by the time students reach me, it’s a safe assumption that they know how to read, so my job then is to give them practice reading while teaching them academic content, like science and social studies, through reading and writing.

In 1990, when I was in the fifth grade, classrooms were teacher-centered. Pedagogy was traditional, emphasizing rote learning and memorization. There was minimal differentiated instruction, so I probably did not receive any individual attention. I have no memories of a boisterous classroom and only remember one special project.

When my son was a fifth-grader in 2019, education journalist Emily Hanford, now famous for her Sold a Story podcast, reported on why, despite overwhelming research on how children learn to read, reading scores on state reading tests were dropping. In her report, Hard Words: Why American Kids Aren’t Being Taught to Read, she detailed how whole language and balanced literacy were ineffective. Hanford explains,

The students who suffer most when schools don’t give their students insight into the [reading] code are kids with dyslexia. They have an especially hard time understanding the relationship between sounds and letters. If you’re a child with dyslexia from an upper-income family, someone is probably going to notice that you’re struggling and pay for you to get the help you need. But kids from poor families often get left behind.

My son’s second-grade teacher caught his dyslexia early; he started receiving some reading intervention within the year because we paid for outside testing and pushed for an IEP. The cost of tutoring was unsustainable, so we relied on him getting SPED small-group phonics instruction for the remainder of his elementary years. This however, was not what he received. While his general education classroom teachers used whole language practices (i.e., cueing), his SPED teachers modified his classwork and taught him strategies for coping with his dyslexia. Not phonics. He received accommodations for working around dyslexia. Unfortunately, by the time we realized this lacking, he was in high school and very accustomed to circumventing reading.

We noticed our daughter’s dyslexia even earlier because we were primed to notice dyslexic thinking. We knew SPED services wouldn’t be adequate. Fortunately, we found a teacher (herself dyslexic and working to become a certified dyslexia specialist) willing to tutor our daughter in phonics a couple of days a week for free. When our daughter reached the fifth-grade in 2021, she had the phonics foundation that my son never received. She also viewed reading as more than just decoding words.

While both my son and daughter had vastly different schooling experiences, what they had in common was a culture of literacy at home. At school, reading may have been seen as a series of discrete tasks that one does to decode a text in order to answer test questions, but at home, however, reading was a shared experience. We read as a family. We talked about what we were reading at the dinner table. Since they were babies, I had a weekly ritual of taking them to the public library for storytime and books. Even when they outgrew storytime, we continued stocking up on books every Sunday to read together. As calls it, we formed our own Book Party.

Books possess great power. Learning to read should be viewed as taking the initial steps toward a magical rite of passage into literacy; membership into a hallowed club. Young children are inherently playful and curious. As they near school age, playing with letter sounds, getting swept up into magical picture-book stories, and exploring language should be daily, familial experiences. Once students begin school, they are now entering a new world; learning those basic letter sounds unlocks the mysterious code that is reading. Kindergarten through the second grade are years for exploring the magic of reading while preparing children to unlock future wonder. Grades 3-4 are liminal spaces in school. Students are still learning to read, but are beginning to use reading as a tool for learning everything. Children are sponges, soaking up any new piece of knowledge, edging closer to their own personal enlightenment. When students enter the upper grades (Grades 5-6), literacy involves literature and students read to expand their minds. At this age (10-12), students are beginning to think for themselves. They are still decoding, especially as they encounter increasingly complex text, but hopefully, at this point, they see reading as a fully-human experience. They have cracked the code and with every novel they finish or article they analyze, students now understand that books provide connection, salvation, and wisdom.

I do not remember that first moment of knowing I could read, but some of my memories - of a tiny, two-room school with eight grades and two teachers - evokes many pieces of what the language expect Anthony Bashir calls the ‘natural history’ of the reading life. The natural history of reading begins with simple exercises, practices, and accuracy, and ends, if one is lucky, with the tools and the capacity to ‘leap into transcendence.’

Maryanne Wolf, Proust and the Squid

As children enter their tween and early teen years (Grades 7-8), their reading comprehension and analytical skills deepen. They can analyze texts, understand nuanced themes, and critically engage with others. Becoming ever more fluent, students start to understand that our world is complex and filled with varying, often conflicting, viewpoints. The more one reads, and discuss it with others, the more exposure one has to multiple perspectives, diverse lived experiences, and differing ideas. Reading, especially reading great literature, is a never-ending quest for meaning. Teenagers grow into young adults and continue this search. They read to enhance what they already know developing new ideas for concepts they haven’t yet explored. As says, “reading literature helps us find meaning and understand the world.” Reading may not come easily for everyone, but given the proper teaching, support, and framing as an important initiation into the sanctity of literature (and the literate), everyone must be given the chance to become a reader.

As a fifth-grade teacher, I do not teach students how to read. I rely on their primary teachers, specializing in phonics and phonemic awareness, to teach students how to decode text using letter-sound relationships and break words into individual sound units (phonemes), and then blend them all back together to form words. When students enter my classroom, my assumption is that they can read at (or close to) grade level. That way, I can teach students how to learn from reading, instead of how to read. However, I can no longer assume that my students read at proficiently, or even read independently at all. In fact, many of my students never received direct phonics instruction; they are products of a decades-long balanced literacy approach, using pictures to guess what a text says. It’s hard to see reading as an magical experience if for six years, you have been taxing your brain to guess what a book is about and no one has helped you see the incredible power in deciphering words to unlock their magic. The older students get, the less likely they are to sound out words, especially if they have little phonemic awareness. As Maryanne Wolf states in Proust and the Squid,

“As every teacher knows, emotional engagement is the tipping point between leaping into the reading life ... An enormously important influence on the development of comprehension in childhood is what happens after we remember, predict, and infer: we feel, we identify, and in the the process we understand more fully and can’t wait to turn the page. The child ... often needs heartfelt encouragement from teachers, tutors and parents to make a stab at more difficult reading material.

This is the greatest tragedy of our current literacy crisis. Generations of students haven’t been given the chance for a reading life, and as such, are unable to connect with books. Since 2002, public education’s overemphasis on standardized testing, basal readers, and reading curricula have had deleterious effects on students. Many do not see themselves as readers. Instead of viewing reading as an lifelong experience, it is seen as a chore, especially for students impacted with learning disabilities.

Technology has only exasperated the problem. Today, students grow up with pervasive technology at their fingertips. Many parents hand their toddler a screen instead of opening a book together. Family literacy practices, reading for pleasure, and what Maryanne Wolf defines as “deep reading” in her book, The Reading Brain in a Digital World, have all dramatically decreased with the advent of smartphones. This is a systemic, societal problem. In the US, about a third of 12th graders do not have basic reading skills. In the UK, 40% of adults have not read a book in the past year. Those of us who do read, have to fight the urge to bounce between headlines and hyperlinks. Classroom teachers constantly vie for our students’ attention. It’s an impossible battle.

Instead of novel studies, read alouds, and book clubs, teachers hand students a laptop and reading textbook on the first day of school. As a result, students read less and have few experiences reading physical books. Over one-third of fourth graders read below grade level, and more than half of high school graduates are not proficient readers. Professors complain that incoming freshman are not able to read effectively.

“When I look back, I am so impressed again with the life-giving power of literature. If I were a young person today, trying to gain a sense of myself in the world, I would do that again by reading, just as I did when I was young.”

Maya Angelou

Books, once standard fixtures in American elementary classrooms, are now either gone, replaced with shelves of basal textbooks and laptop charging stations, or markedly shorter and less complex. Booksellers are opting for shorter books to accommodate for shorter attention spans. The current state for teachers and students has every minute optimized to standardize teaching and learning. School districts, in a panic response to being blamed for creating a post-literate society, are desperately trying to improve standardized reading scores. Teachers in the primary grades (Kindergarten through Grade 2) are returning to phonics instruction. Third through fifth-grade teachers are drilling in specific reading standards, while trying to make up for years of lost phonics instruction. In between summarizing textbook passages, students who were never properly taught how to read, are getting crammed with short phonics lessons in small groups or one-on-one with their teacher. Teachers are plugging holes in the leaky dike, but we don’t have enough fingers. We are all are trying to convince students reading has value, and all the while, students have missed what Karen Swallow Prior, author of On Reading Well: Finding the Good Life through Great Books calls the “intellectual, aesthetic, or spiritual rewards” of deep reading.

Reading as Communion

The most literary man I’ve ever known was dyslexic. When I first joined his family, I heard stories of Mike, my father-in-law, in graduate school walking home with stacks of books to read, spending 12-16 hours a day reading in his basement. Any time I saw Mike, he wanted to talk about what I was reading, tell me about what he was reading, and share reading recommendations. Sometimes he would give me a tattered copy of the book he wanted to talk about with me, scribbled with his marginalia. Other times, I’d drive straight from his house to the library. Many times, I struggled to read the books he suggested. Mike was an academic and published poet who taught at a prestigious University for years before becoming a high-school creative writing teacher. Looking back, it never really mattered whether I fully understood the themes or allusions; all that he cared about was our shared reading experience. I have, since then, spent most of my adult life trying to catch up on reading all of the books I missed out on from my childhood, while desperately trying to be a teacher that helps my students be lifelong readers of literature. I want to give them a human connection through literature, just as my father-in-law did for me. Unfortunately, I can’t ask him how he became a reader despite his learning disability. I do know that he didn’t have screens competing for his attention. Despite his struggles, he was able to immerse himself in novels primarily, I believe, because he read widely and deeply, read what he liked, had a family reading community, and believed in the sanctity of the humanities.

Literacy as a sacred skill has origins as far back as antiquity. In the ancient world, general literacy rates were extremely low, often below 10%. Reading was viewed as such a specialized skill that only the elite, like scribes, priests, and the wealthy could read. In fact, the word hieroglyphics originates from the ancient Greek term hieroglyphikos meaning “sacred carving.” Most texts in antiquity were read aloud during communal worship in order to transmit religious knowledge to the masses. In early Christianity, knowledge of the scriptures was seen as a way of keeping both religious and earthly rulers accountable to God’s holy instructions.

In the 16th-century, the Protestant Reformation was a pivotal moment in the history of literacy as a holy act. Salvation depended on access to the scriptures so reading was seen as a spiritual practice. With the invention of the printing press by Johannes Gutenberg in the 1450s books, especially Bibles, became much more common and less expensive. Translating the Bible into vernacular languages, such as Martin Luther’s German Bible and William Tyndale’s English Bible, was a revolutionary act that gave more people access to scripture. Literacy became more than just a skill; it was something “almost sacred, woven into one’s entire worldview and approach to living.”

Sitting across from Mike, as an 18-year-old burgeoning reader, we discussed Shakespeare and T.S. Eliot (Imagine Adrian: when I studied Eliot, he was “modern.” Ha!) or W.B. Yeats (He experienced poetry as the essence of his whole life and that’s from his youth. He understood what writing is about!) or Berryman, always Mr. B. (You should enjoy and learn from Mr. B. He particularly revered Will Shakespeare and Yeats.). I was experiencing a literary communion. Not only was I communing with writers long dead, Mike was giving me a crash course on the writers that made him feel most human, sharing literature that connected us, across generations, as we exchanged ideas. Initially, our conversations were pretty one-sided: me auditing free literary lectures; but over time, I had ideas of my own. I didn’t know where they came from, but they spewed out of me as soon as I walked in the door and sat down. Once I officially became a part of the family, our reading fellowship deepened. At Christmas, he would buy me a single book, often spending (according to my new mother-in-law) hours in the bookstore picking out something special he wanted to discuss with me face to face. Each novel brought me closer to him, developing an intimacy I lacked in my own non-reading family. I was now a part of his family’s culture of reading and readers. It was special.

In the ensuing years, I cultivated a love of literature. My literacy became something I wanted to strengthen. Once I became a classroom teacher, I vowed to help my students cultivate a love of reading the way Mike did for me. No matter their struggles or previous experiences, I promised to earnestly protect the sanctity of literature. With Mike, I encountered reading as an experience, not merely decoding words and completing book reports. He helped me develop a reading practice and awakened in me a fierce devotion to reading as an intimacy with writers, both living and long dead.

Rectifying the Reading Crisis

Human beings did not evolve to read. Our brains are not hardwired for reading. Reading must be explicitly taught, through phonics and decoding, but not so that students can eventually pass standardized tests. We teach students how to connect sounds with letters so that they can begin their rite of passage into literacy and great literature. Reading must be viewed as more than a science; reading is a culture that strengthens our humanity and which desperately needs to be reintegrated back into public education and society. If we are to avoid living in a post-literate world and ameliorate the reading crisis, it is going to take much more than a Science of Reading grassroots movement. Our American literacy crisis stems from the slow, standardized dehumanization of learning. Learning is a human experience that requires humans teaching humans. Reading may be a solitary act, but it is also a shared magical experience that everyone can, and should participate in. We have generations of students who see reading as banal activities that must be suffered through for the required thirteen years of schooling; not as a portals into other worlds, but as peremptory orders, demanding standardized compliance. And many of them, my son included, are rightfully cynical and angry. We didn’t show them how reading is a boon that helps make sense of ourselves and the world. We didn’t promote having a shared intellectual love of literature and literacy. Instead, we optimized pedagogy, expecting students to figure reading out on their own. We inundated them with standardized tests and reduced them to data points. Reading became a means to a standardized (and fundable) end, not the magical mana that makes us more fully human.

The exponential growth of technology, leading to the rise of constant connectivity, both in the classroom and at home, is more of a symptom to the literacy crisis than its sole cause. Screens make an easy scapegoat. But as a fifth-grader, sitting at my desk reading Little House in the Big Woods, I didn’t have a screen distracting me. I became a non-reader, not because I was addicted to my Nintendo or The Oregon Trail. I grew into an adult who didn’t read because reading was not a part of my culture; not at school nor at home. I sat at my desk and read during DEAR time; it was a small part of my school day. I have no memories of any shared reading experiences in my classroom. I didn’t get to experience the sanctity of literacy and literature until I met my wife and his dyslexic dad. Growing up, reading was a non-event. It was something that existed, but never protected and cultivated. Reading was a thing I did in school to appease my teachers, not a key to being a better human and understanding the world.

I’m not a reading expert, nor a literary scholar. I’m a dyslexic teacher who’s felt something inside of me open up from having literary communion with my father-in-law and building a literature-positive culture in my family. Reading literature revived something in me I didn’t know existed, and sharing these experiences has made me a better version of myself. I still read slowly and am easily distracted, but I persevere. This is what I hope to give my students and what I fight for every single day. Reading gives us innumerable chances to be better, to better understand ourselves, and to make the world better. As a teacher, I take serious the responsibility to protect the humanities, so that my students can cultivate a literacy practice of their own. Classrooms must be sanctuaries for literacy and havens for great literature; students need opportunities to engage with literature for humanity’s sake. I can’t guarantee everyone will fall in love with reading, or become a literary scholar, but every single student must be given a chance to join the great fellowship of reading and readers.

Have a great week!

— Adrian
