We all feel it. Some days are heavier than others. A human life contains myriad highs and lows; joys and sorrows; relaxations and stresses. Sometimes glimmers are temporary: we have a good day at school or work, a kind smile from a stranger warms us against the November wind. Sometimes stress and anxiety seem to drag on forever, lasting weeks, months, or years. Teaching during this school year feels heavy. Inside the school building, exhaustion and stress are palpable. Teachers are fatigued and weary. Outside, our polarized world is becoming increasingly volatile and unstable.

I don’t usually feel this way until December, but this school year is different. The whirlwind of school is now a hurricane of meetings, pacing guides, and testing. Everything feels like it’s moving at breakneck speed, and yet it is only November.

“Life meanders like a path through the woods. We have seasons when we flourish and seasons when the leaves fall from us, revealing our bare bones. Given time, they grow again.”

Katherine May, Wintering: The Power of Rest and Retreat in Difficult Times

Winter is coming and I don’t want to enter this “dark half of the year” holding onto cynical and nihilistic thoughts. If this is, indeed, a crucible to endure, I plan to do so, focusing on the simple pleasures I can control, instead of the madness I cannot. Here are five things1 I enjoy and make time for as I slow down, rest, and prepare for Spring.

1. Loose-Leaf Green Tea

The first thing I do each morning upon waking is boil water for my first cup of tea. As I quietly wait and listen for the water to warm, I spoon a heaping teaspoon of loose-leaf green tea into a steeping basket, and stare out into the darkened neighborhood from my kitchen window. Once ready, I pour the 190°F water onto perfumy hand-rolled tea leaves, letting a Jasmine fragrance waft into my face. This little ritual is how I start my day, and it is a simple pleasure that I look forward to each morning. Smelling my first cup of tea in my darkened kitchen feels mysteriously sacramental.

2. Listening to an Entire Album, Start to Finish

Being a Gen Xer, I remember sitting in my bedroom listening to an entire album on cassette, and later on CD. I cashed my first paycheck, drove to Blockbuster Music , and purchased my first CD, Gordon by Barenaked Ladies. Even though I could easily skip tracks on my Discman, I sat in my Ford Bronco and listened to that entire album, start to finish. Sitting and listening to an album is a pleasure that has been lost with the advent of digital streaming services like Spotify, Apple iTunes, and Amazon Music.

Listening to an album is so important, I started class each day playing music, and encouraging my students to actively listen. This led to my afterschool Now Spinning club where students would listen to and discuss albums together in community.

I still, whenever I purchase a new album, take the time to listen to it in full without interruption. A couple of weeks ago, I got the MTV Unplugged: 10,000 Maniacs album and Nirvana’s first live album, MTV Unplugged in New York.2 These live performances are excellent, small pleasures and demand to be listened to from start to finish.

3. Whisky

Bourbon tastes of fall. The tongue-prick from the first sip. The oak, vanilla, and leather flavors and aromas all make me feel wrapped up in the fall season. I am no whiskey sommelier, but I do love the history and stories embedded in distilleries.

I’m also a sucker for a good celebrity endorsement. When Matthew McConaughey partnered with Master Distiller Eddie Russell to create Wild Turkey Longbranch3, I ran to the nearest liquor store. It has since been a mainstay in my liquor cabinet.

With whisky (sans e), I have less experience. The flavor of scotch never appealed to me, especially those peaty whiskies. However, when Harrison Ford and director, Joel Edgerton, created a hilarious ad campaign for Glenmorangie, I had to try it. I watched the mini-movie, Once Upon a Time in Scotland and the subsequent twelve episodes, before buying a bottle of Glenmorangie and sipping a dram. I know that my brain and taste buds are susceptible to moviestar persuasion, but after a challenging day, sitting in front of a fire and sipping Scotch, is a simple pleasure that I enjoy. Nice!

4. A Great Audiobook

My earliest memory of being read aloud to was in my sophomore English class. Mr. D was a large man. Our classroom was located in the basement of the English Department, a corner room with low ceilings and hopper windows. I would sit in class and watch dozens of pairs of shoes crisscross, moving from one end to the other. My sophomore year was Mr. D’s last year before retiring. He had taught for over 30 years and for this, his final year, he decided that the entire class would be a read aloud. Each day, we would enter the classroom take our seats, and prepare for that day’s reading. We all had the responsibility of keeping a 3-Ring binder in the back of the classroom up to date with a synopsis from each day’s reading; taking turns to write about that day’s reading. For that entire school year, Mr. D read to us The Ingenious Gentleman Don Quixote of La Mancha by Miguel de Cervantes4. I don’t remember much of the plot, or whether we read the entire novel. I remember Mr. D’s hearty laugh at periodic spots in the story, and asking peers to tell me what had happened because I zoned out.

Today, I always have at least two books going at any given time, one physical book on my nightstand and one audiobook in my car. Audiobooks have made the act of reading attainable for me. Being dyslexic, I never had a reading habit. Reading was a school chore. Now, I love reading, especially listening to a great narrator while I follow along in the text. My absolute favorite audiobook narrator is George Guidall. He has a fantastic voice and has narrated hundreds of incredible books. To me, Guidall is my Quixote, just like to my kids, Jim Dale is their Harry Potter, Ron Weasley, Hermione Granger, Hagrid, Dumbledore, and the rest of the characters of the Potterverse. Listening to a well-narrated audiobook is one of my most cherished simple pleasures.

5. Predawn Mornings

In Wintering, The Power of Rest and Retreat in Difficult Times5, describes how, before the Industrial Revolution, our circadian rhythms more closely followed the rising and setting of the sun. People would go to bed at sundown (in the fall, around 4:00 PM), and wake up before sunrise (around 4:00 AM). In these predawn hours, everything was quiet and dark. Without electric light, you couldn’t work. Many used this time for meditation, prayer, or quiet conversations with family members. When scientists recreate these conditions without electric light, very quickly, people fall back into pre-Industrial Revolution sleep and wake cycles. Even our blood is different at dawn, having a unique hormonal profile akin to that of breastfeeding babies. Dawn is a time when prolactin levels in our blood naturally peak, giving us a dreamy, contented headspace before sunrise. May encourages us to embrace “this secret room we have in our lives.” Without lightbulbs and screens, predawn mornings are beautiful moments where we can reconnect with ourselves, feel more grounded, and slowly prepare for the day. Quiet rituals in the morning remind of D.H. Lawrence.

We must get back into relation: vivid and nourishing relation to the cosmos and the universe . . . We must once more practice the ritual of dawn and noon and sunset, the ritual of kindling fire and pouring water, the ritual of the first breath and the last.

My predawn ritual involves quietly unloading the dishwasher, listening to the sound of my electric kettle heating water for my first cup of tea, letting it steep for three minutes, and reading a novel. I wish I could extend this time, but even just this hour of darkness helps me mentally prepare for the day. It is a simple pleasure I hold onto.

What are simple pleasures you take for yourself, especially when life feels heavy?

Have a great week!

— Adrian
