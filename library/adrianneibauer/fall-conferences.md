Fall parent conferences are, in many ways, like a third date. By November, I have already met most of my students’ parents. At the beginning of the school year, we host Parent Connection Meetings in lieu of a Back to School Night; the idea being that one-on-one, ten-minute conversations are more personal than presenting to a room full of anxious and eager parents. After the first trimester, once report cards are sent home, I schedule conferences for parents to meet with me to discuss their child’s progress thus far. At their scheduled time, we meet in my classroom, sometimes with their child present, and chat about the first third of the school year. First and second dates are for pleasantries and making good impressions, a screener to see if two people are compatible. A third date is for conversations of consequence, trying to build caring connections. Even in disagreement you are building a relationship.

I used to be scared of parents. As a 23-year-old, novice teacher, I worried that I needed to prove myself as a capable educator. At fall conferences, I stressed about providing enough evidence to justify the grades I marked on students’ report cards. No matter how large a body of evidence I collected, I anxiously braced myself for pushback.

As I got older, had children of my own, and attended my children’s conferences, I got to sit on the other side of the table, anxiously awaiting the teacher’s pronouncement about how my child was doing. I nervously sat hoping for good news, bracing for bad.

As both teacher and parent, I have delivered and received praise and feedback. On the teacher side, I want my students’ parents to have an honest, accurate, and a caring academic appraisal of their child. On the parent side, I want my children’s teachers to care about my child, and assure me that they are a competent student. On the teacher side, I want my students’ parents to trust my earnest assessment of their child, and see me as a competent and caring teacher. The anticipation of judgement on both sides always leads to anxiety and unfounded feelings of insecurity and self-doubt.

When I meet with parents in November, we’ve already exchanged pleasantries and had conversations. I’ve called home at least a few times, telling them about something incredible their child did or said, or just that I appreciate their student’s hard work. For some, I’ve messaged parents about concerns, from [disruptive recalcitrance](https://adrianneibauer.substack.com/p/recalcitrance-in-my-classroom) to learning struggles. So, by the time we meet in person, we know each other a bit, and are working to form a genuine partnership in service to their child’s success.

I never start conferences with the report card. The first questions I always ask are, “*How is your child enjoying 5th Grade? Is your child happy to come to school? Do they come home from school happy?*” My students’ humanity is my primary concern. Yes, we will address grades and behavior, but the first thing I want to know is how my student is feeling about being in my classroom. I want to know if they come home stressed or if there are conflicts happening with other students. I want to know how they feel about themselves as learners. We may take our students’ happiness for granted, but unhappy students (or unhealthy, stressed, overwhelmed, or those who have experienced trauma) struggle to learn; not because they can’t learn, but because they are struggling in ways that make accessing learning challenging. My primary job is to ensure that my students feel safe and welcome in my classroom. If not, I have very little chance of helping them persevere through challenging learning experiences. Kids before grades.

I spend these first five minutes ensuring my students feel comfortable with me as their teacher and that I am not, inadvertently, preventing them from learning. Only then, do we begin to have a serious conversation about how they are progressing. As I’ve aged, I find that many of my recommendations blend with parenting advice. *How much time is R spending on his phone? What time is M going to bed? What is J eating for breakfast? Is A reading at home?* This can be a tricky conversation to navigate, but I find that by first establishing my care for students, parents are more receptive to forming a strong relationship of care. This is not an interrogation. I am not examining my parents looking for faults. [No judgement](https://mindfulambition.net/non-judgment/) in my questions. Just care and concern.

[Dr. Brené Brown discusses a workshop she once held with Episcopalian deacons](https://youtu.be/w5TkA7d7eTw?si=6y9JXaq0_ZZku2Nb). She asks the room a difficult question.

> ### *Do you believe that people are doing the best they can?*

Over 50% of the deacons answered, *No, people are not doing the best they can.* Brown then poses this thought experiment.

> *Picture someone in your life who you believe is not doing the best they can; who really irritate you. What if God[1](#footnote-1) came down to you and said this person that you wrote down, he or she is doing the very best they can right now? How would you feel?*

She then tells a story about two deacons, both married, who had a strong reaction to this question. They both wrote down the same person, someone who they have been trying to help, but who continues to struggle. This person has had their children removed several times for abuse or neglect. Any money they receive, they gamble instead of using it for diapers and formula. The deacons, feeling frustrated and angry, both visualized this person during the thought experiment, and had an epiphany. They could either continue to help with a loving, non-judgemental heart, or stop helping through hatred because that goes against their integrity and faith.

*All I know is that my life is better when I assume that people are doing their best. It keeps me out of judgment and lets me focus on what is, and not what should or could be.*

Brené Brown, [Daring Greatly](https://brenebrown.com/book/daring-greatly/)

As a young teacher, it was easy for me to judge my parents’ decisions. *If they would only just \[FILL IN THE BLANK\].* As I’ve gotten older, though, and made many, many mistakes, I better understand that we are all doing our best given our current resources. This does not mean that I ignore opportunities to offer caring advice for my students’ wellbeing. It means that I try to help without judgement and resentment. I assure parents that, even though things are tough, their child will be okay as long as we all continue to love and support them, and help them develop healthy habits. My job, as a teacher, is to lovingly push my students to grow in all academic areas. But I also take seriously my responsibility to help students grow to be happy and healthy human beings. I offer my students compassion and encouragement. I’ve learned that motivation through encouragement is stronger than through fear of disappointment. Sometimes, my encouragement can feel like a push. I’m a [warm demander](https://www.edutopia.org/blog/warm-demander-equity-approach-matt-alexander); I expect a great deal of my students and convince them daily that they are brilliant.

Teaching is hard. Being a parent is hard. Being a good human can be hard some days. There are so many aspects to our lives that make it easy to numb and remain stagnant. We have ubiquitous, addictive devices that continuously suck our attention by offering immediate and unlimited gratification. Most adults struggle to control that vice. Imagine being ten-years-old and having to choose between playing *Fortnight* or practicing your times tables. It isn’t surprising that the majority of my students, given the option, choose to stay up all night on their tablets instead of reading a novel with their parents and going to bed early. Family literacy practices, [reading for pleasure](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2025-08-20/reading-for-pleasure-on-steep-decline-study-finds), and what [Maryanne Wolf](https://www.maryannewolf.com/) defines as “deep reading” in her book, *[The Reading Brain in a Digital World](https://www.maryannewolf.com/reader-come-home-1)*, have all dramatically decreased since the advent of smartphones. This isn’t a problem with parents, but a systemic, societal problem. In the US, about [a third of 12th graders do not have basic reading skills](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/09/09/us/12th-grade-reading-skills-low-naep.html). In the UK, [40% of adults have not read a book in the past year](https://www.theguardian.com/books/2025/mar/06/new-poll-finds-40-of-britons-have-not-read-a-book-in-the-past-year). Those of us who do read, have to fight the urge to bounce between headlines and hyperlinks. Skimming is now the new normal.[2](#footnote-2) The solution isn’t to shame parents and children, but to motivate and encourage healthier habits.

I sometimes forget that, being born in 1980, had a technology-free childhood. Sure, there was that time in high school when I spent an entire summer watching MTV. But, television remained my only real distraction to reading until I was in my twenties. Even once I could “connect” with others via social media and buy things online, I still had a 20-year head start in resisting such pervasive dopamine triggers. I didn’t own any gaming systems or a personal computer. I didn’t start checking email until I was living abroad after college. My childhood *was* unplugged entertainment.

As an elder millennial teacher, I can offer my professional opinions from a place of humility. I was not a strong reader in school and today, have to work at ignoring distractions in order to read literature deeply. Technology has helped me and my teenage children learn, but we still battle over technology time versus outdoor time. Fall conferences are a chance for me to remind students and parents we are all on the same team with the same goal, sharing the same struggles. Learning is a team sport, and we still have two trimesters to develop healthy habits, both in the classroom and at home. Instead of me judging parents and using grades to motivate behavior changes, or parents defensively justifying their decisions, we must work together in a loving, non-judgemental way to help each student reach their fullest potential. To paraphrase , we have to make some difficult choices in order to avoid having a post-literate society. Teachers and parents can share this burden. I may disagree with how my students are spending time at home, or what parents allow their children to do, but fall conferences are for building worthwhile partnerships in service to students’ wellbeing. Judgment serves no one here and we have much work to do.

For me success is not about the wins and losses; it’s about helping these young fellows be the best versions of themselves on and off the field.

Ted Lasso, S01E03
