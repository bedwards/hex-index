I was able to buy us an extra week. Knowing that our grade-level team was not fully prepared to implement our first 15 Day Challenge (of many), I successfully urged everyone to delay for a week. This gave me five free days to try something new with my students before we were all subjected to three weeks of identical instruction of a single standard, known colloquially in our school district as Same Way, Same Day.

For the last six weeks, I have been teaching my students how to closely read, analyze, and discuss various short stories and poems. Using Marissa Thompson’s TQE Method as a foundational structure, students have been learning how ’s high school students Read Like an Octopus in their AP Literature course. By this point in the year, students are familiar with the process. FIRST READ: Students write down the title and author of the story, and a one-sentence summary. SECOND READ: Students highlight key lines from the text and annotate in the margins using Depth and Complexity Icons (What are your thoughts and questions?). They then compare their annotations with other students. THIRD READ: Students review their Dialectic Notebooks and highlighted text looking for epiphanies or big ideas/themes. What is the significance of this story/poem? What problem is the author addressing?

Each week, students have been reading, annotating, and discussing longer, more complex stories and poems.1 We recently read Rod Sterling’s teleplay, The Monsters are Due on Maple Street, one of my all-time favorite Twilight Zone episodes. The students loved reading, annotating, and then watching the Twilight Zone episode. It led to such a great conversation about prejudice, scapegoating, and mob mentality.

Now that I had this week of freedom, how would I use it? Would we read another short story and continue practicing annotating the text? I have been teaching my students how to have academic classroom discussions using Matthew Kay’s discussion guidelines from his books Not Light, but Fire and We’re Gonna Keep On Talking. Should we read another poem and discuss it as a class, or should I try something different?

The Socratic Method

Many are familiar with the origins of the Socratic Seminar. Developed by the Greek philosopher, Socrates (470-399 BCE), the Socratic Method is a dialogue between teacher and student. This method of questioning, with a teacher’s continual probing questions (elenchus), Socrates believed would challenge students’ assumptions, exposes contradictions and misconceptions, and ultimately, shape their views and opinions. Today, the Socratic Method has involved to a shared dialogue between a teacher and a classroom of students. The teacher poses thought-provoking questions using a central text, and students actively engage by asking questions of their own. To make sure everyone participates, and to accommodate ever-increasing class sizes, many teachers often opt for a fishbowl discussion. A group of students sit in an inner circle (“fishbowl”) of two concentric circles, actively discussing a topic. Students sitting in the outer circle listen carefully to the conversation, often noting who is participating and how often. Midway through the discussion, students switch roles.

I’ve been a huge fan of the Socratic Seminar for many years, and yet, I’ve been fearful to fully implement it in my classroom. While I have no problem with adapting classroom practices that are commonly reserved for middle and high school classrooms, every time I consider doing a Socratic Seminar in my classroom, I feel overwhelmed. My own teenage children have shared stories of the weeks of preparation (and hours of homework) necessary for participating in Socratic Seminars. They show me pages of notes and worksheets with the mandatory participation markings required of the teacher to justify a passing grade in the seminar. These experiences seem more about a strict adherence a specific format and holding students accountable for participating, rather than free-flowing academic discourse. None of that interests me so, instead, I tend to opt for less formal class discussions.

However, with just five weekdays in front of me, I reconsidered whether I could design a modified Socratic Seminar for my fifth-graders. The time constraint meant I would be forced to eliminate some of the pre-seminar preparation and most of the accountability measures. Perhaps we could read a single, great short story, annotate it with our thoughts, questions, and epiphanies, and discuss it in an open forum.

I just had to find the perfect story to use.

The Lottery by Shirley Jackson

I started looking for a good story that would elicit discussion from my students. It had to be relatively short because we would only have five days to read and prepare for the seminar. Over the weekend, I consumed stories such as The Monkey’s Paw by W.W Jacobs, Eleven by Sandra Cisneros, and The Veldt by Ray Bradbury. All of these have excellent prose, and would make for fantastic discussions, but none of them seemed to fit with the tone and tenor of our previous reads, Main Street by Jacqueline Woodson and Rod Sterling’s The Monsters are Due on Maple Street. There was one story, however, that kept showing up on every list I found of compiled short stories: The Lottery.

Somehow, I graduated high school without reading The Lottery by Shirley Jackson. It’s reputation precedes it and many believe it to be the quintessential short story in American literature. I had heard of the controversy surrounding its original publication in The New Yorker in 1948; how readers cancelled their subscriptions en masse outraged that such a disgusting story would be publically printed. In fact, I had even heard a rumor that some people wrote letters to the editors at The New Yorker asking where the town was located so that they could witness the next June lottery.

I’m never one to shy away from controversy or doing challenging things in my classroom, but since Jackson’s story is synonymous with the high school English classroom, I hesitated. Would the story be too difficult for my fifth-grade students? Is it appropriate to discuss its themes and symbolism in my elementary classroom? In reading The Lottery, I decided that my students would love Jackson’s shocking twist at the end. A mob mentality within a quiet community fit perfectly with our previous discussions of prejudice and herd behavior in The Monsters are Due on Maple Street. Immediately, I started designing a week-long Socratic Seminar experience around the dangers of blindly following tradition, conformity, and collective cruelty and violence.

I planned to introduce the Socratic Seminar, lay out the rules for engagement, and discuss Depth of Knowledge questions on Monday, read and analyze The Lottery on Tuesday and Wednesday, and prepare and practice Thursday for our Friday seminar. It was an aggressive schedule, but doable. I was excited to try a new and rigorous learning experience with my students and nervous this seminar would fail miserably.

Our Socratic Seminar

I learned much about my students (and myself) throughout week. Overall, the seminar was a success. The majority of my class participated. Their thoughts and questions were insightful, and a few students posed some pretty incredible epiphanies. I had anticipated that six weeks into the school year would be too early for such a sophisticated discussion. I was absolutely wrong! Pushing my students to find quotes from the text to answer Level 2 and 3 questions proved to be challenging. However, I discovered that when I let go of the quantity of evidence I asked them to locate in the text to support their questions, it freed students up to ask deeper questions that went beyond the text. For example, many students asked questions such as What would happen if Tessie Hutchinson never made it to the lottery? and How did the lottery start? and What would you if your name was chosen? These questions led to the most lively moments in the seminar. My favorite sound during the seminar was the rustling shuffle of papers as students located textual evidence to support their ideas. When there wasn’t pressure to find a quote that absolutely answered a student’s question (like you can with Level 1 questions), students got creative, flipping through the story to share excerpts they felt related to the discussion. When one student asked, How long ago did this lottery happen?, another student surprised me by remembering that Mr. Summers was wearing “his clean white shirt and blue jeans” making the setting more modern. Another student shared, Old Man Warner wants to keep the lottery going because he is older and has never been chosen. The younger villagers think the lottery is unfair because they have more life to live. My fifth-graders never cease to amaze me! I was astonished that this level of thinking could emerge after such a short amount of time reading a short story. If I had made this student find a quote to support her thinking (while still an important skill and something we have been practicing these six weeks), she probably wouldn’t have shared it during the seminar, thinking it was “not correct.”

I had a few ephanies, too. Despite this being my first Socratic Seminar, I felt like I spoke too much. At one point in the seminar, I heard myself interjecting more than listening and facilitating, a good reminder that I need to police my own voice. I don’t want my students to ever feel like I must validate or qualify their thinking in order to be shared to the larger group. Sometimes, the teacher in me feels compelled to respond to every comment, and I learned during this experience that amplifying my students’ voices means stepping back and listening more than speaking.

Was this a perfect Socratic Seminar? No. As expected, some students dominated the discussion, sharing more than others, and while I tried to encourage more voices, some students just didn’t participate. However, everyone did have their annotated copy of The Lottery and prepared Socratic Seminar questions. I have these to either grade or use as evidence of my students’ thinking.

Lessons Learned

Overall, this was a great learning experiment. I learned that students can handle rigor when it is properly scaffolded. Students are capable of learning about and earnestly discussing complex issues, such as prejudice, racism, murder, and scapegoating. At no point during the week, did I feel like I had to water down the content or difficulty of the story. In fact, when I second-guessed myself, thinking that students would benefit from watching a video of the story, I found a horrible AI-generated video that only distracted students from the text. The simpler I kept our Socratic Seminar, the better.

What have been your experiences with Socratic Seminars? Do you prefer a Fishbowl discussion instead? I’d love to hear about how you have used seminars with students.

Have a great week!

— Adrian
