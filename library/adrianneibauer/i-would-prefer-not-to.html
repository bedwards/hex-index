Time is a funny thing. We all get 24 hours in a day, and we choose to (or are obliged to) spend those hours as we (or others) see fit. As a teacher, I spend seven hours, five days a week, teaching my students. For 2,100 hours each week, my job is to instruct, motivate, inspire, correct, coach, and assess my students. Every day, I oscillate between teacher, counselor, disciplinarian, and, much to my chagrin, sometimes an entertainer. Some days, when we lose ourselves in collective flow states, those seven hours fly by. Lately, though, the hours are monotonous; every day seems the same.

It is a very dull, wearisome, and lethargic affair. I can readily imagine that to some sanguine temperaments it would be altogether intolerable.

Herman Melville, Bartleby the Scrivener

An engaging learning experience demands that you are fully present with your students. Teaching in a classroom is a dynamic process with interpersonal give and take. It can almost feel like breathing. There is so much variability, even from minute to minute, that it is impossible to be on autopilot. Masterful teachers make learning feel alive because they realize that no two students, classrooms, or school years are ever the same. What worked last period will most certainly need to be tweaked for the next. A lesson that landed beautifully last year, might completely bomb this year. The nature of learning is what makes teaching so exhausting and ultimately, rewarding.

This year, teaching feels more machine-like: administering worksheets and managing compliance instead of engaging and inspiring students to learn and grow. After two back-to-back 15-Day Challenges, and two more upcoming, I’m beginning to feel like Bartleby the Scrivener. Work is dull and wearisome. The expectations are rigid and stressful. I feel it. Other teachers in the building feel it. Students feel it. We are all feeling restricted and confined. Student misbehavior is increasing while teacher morale is plummeting. Everyone is trying their best to keep it together, but stress cracks are starting to show. Even our most sanguine staff are becoming cynical.

Passive Resistance

Herman Melville’s 12-page short story, Bartleby, The Scrivener: A Story Of Wall-street, was first published in Putnam’s Magazine in December, 1853. Bartleby is a legal scrivener who endures the mind-numbingly boring, soul-crushingly banal task of repetitively copying legal documents by rote within a sterile Wall Street law office. Normally a very diligent worker, Bartleby one day, politely refuses to proofread a document with the quiet phrase, I would prefer not to.

“I would prefer not to.”
“You will not?”
“I prefer not.”

Bartleby’s passive resistance is powerful, disarming both the reader and the narrator. Jane Desmarais, Professor of English and Director of the Decadence Research Centre, explains the power quietly hiding in the word prefer.

The semblance of choice in his response, “prefer” is both illusive and allusive. Unspecific in what it refers to, the word alludes to a choice which it denies. The implicit suggestion that there might be something Bartleby would prefer to do is an illusion. The use of the word, “prefer”, then, appears contradictory and strikes an ambiguous note in the story.1

The narrator eventually admits, “Nothing so aggravates an earnest person as a passive resistance.” Bartleby is an unpretentious character, and by simply refusing to complete tasks which he sees as meaningless and banal, he effectively protests productivity, capitalism, and his dehumanizing working conditions. It’s brilliant!

This past week was a “very dull, wearisome, and lethargic affair.” Every day was filled with obligatory busywork. Instead of excitedly discussing literature, wrestling with a mathematical puzzle, or exploring a scientific phenomena, I managed standardized academic tasks, forcing my students to practice the same isolated skill ad nauseum. We had no peak academic moments. Just determining the main idea of a text, explaining how it is supported by key details and summarizing the text over and over and over again.

I wish my experiences were isolated and temporary. , co-founder of The Human Restoration Project, recently attended his child’s parent conference, and astutely observed, “it’s clear the purpose isn’t to teach kids, it’s to “get through” the curriculum. There’s never a charge to maintain fidelity to children, there is only fidelity to the curriculum.” Teachers across the country are feeling increasing pressure to prepare students for standardized testing. Unfortunately, not only does forcing teachers to follow standardized curricula limit their responsiveness to their students learning needs, repetitively adhering to shallow standards removes all creativity from teaching. My students have been finding key details and summarizing texts since October and will continue doing so until January because this skill is deemed necessary to answer nearly all literacy standardized testing questions. These 15-Day Challenges are just standardized test prep disguised as a “student-learning cycles.” The day-to-day classroom experience is now a very structured and boring routine.

Powerful Moments

Chip and Dan Heath understand the importance of “rising above the routine.” In their book, The Power Of Moments: Why Certain Experiences Have Extraordinary Impact, they discuss how we all have an innate desire to improve the experiences of those we care about. This includes the students we teach and the children we raise. As parents and educators we want to create memories that matter. Chip and Dan Heath use real-world examples to explain that humans rate experiences based on two moments: (1) the best or worst moment, known as the “peak”; and (2) the ending. When people do a quick assessment at the end of an experience, psychologists call this the peak-end rule.

We don’t average our minute-by-minute sensations. Rather, we tend to remember flagship moments: the peaks, the pits, and the transitions.2

Chip and Dan Heath explain that anyone can create a meaningful and memorable moment using four elements: ELEVATION, PRIDE, INSIGHT, and CONNECTION (The EPIC Framework). Moments of elevation are experiences that make us feel engaged, joyful, surprised, motivated. To create them, we can either boost the sensory appeal, raise the stakes; and/or break the script.3 Moments of pride commemorate people’s achievements.4 These include recognizing others (e.g., birthdays) and celebrating milestones (e.g., retirement). Moments of insight deliver abrupt realizations and transformations. Having a moment of insight, whether through reading and discussing an impactful book or a conversation that challenges one’s thinking, is a memorable experience. Self-insights are rare occurrences in complacent classrooms. Moments of connection bond people together. We see groups unite when they struggle together toward a meaningful goal; they often begin their work with what Chip and Dan Heath call a “synchronized moment” (e.g., Black Lives Matter).

I always stack the first six weeks of the school year with peak moments. During the first week, I build community with my students using all four elements of the EPIC Framework. I want them to remember, especially when learning and friendships become challenging, that our classroom is a safe haven. I shock my new students with a classroom that is both human-centered and rigorous. We read and annotate poems and plays and discuss literary themes. I introduce my students to engaging work and pedagogy that is usually reserved for advanced middle school and high school classes. This year, in anticipation of the upcoming lethargic curricula and monotonous sameness, I designed a week-long Socratic Seminar using Shirley Jackson’s The Lottery as our central text. While not a perfect learning experience, I could feel moments of pride, insight, and connection crystalize as students ventured outside of their comfort zones, and were rewarded with recognition and support from their peers.

I would prefer not to continue repeating 15-Day Challenges for the rest of the school year. And so, I find ways to passively resist and create memorable moments. This week, despite the monotony, I was able to create (and receive) a few moments of learning and joy, while resisting efforts to dehumanize my pedagogy and classroom.

Monday Mingle

I started having a soft start to our day when I returned to the classroom to teach during the pandemic. It was a scary time; nothing felt normal. Everything was in flux and it didn’t seem appropriate to start teaching as soon as the bell rang. Since then, I’ve noticed that my students appreciate a soft start to their school day, especially on Mondays. So, every Monday morning, I post a puzzle or guessing game for students to work on while they are eating their breakfast. I got the idea of using the Blocks Puzzle from Marc Hodgkinson, a grade 7 teacher in London, Ontario. It may not seem like much, but reclaiming the first 10-15 minutes of our day for community building, socializing, and having fun is a rare, yet memorable moment.5

Playing with Students

There are a ton of different online review games available for teachers to use with students. They all have their own strengths and weaknesses. Kahoot! is considered the original review game for teachers and students. Their live, interactive quizzes let teachers create or find a quiz and launch it as a presentation for students join in using their own devices. Blooket is another platform designed to gamify quizzes. My students love the variety of video game modes. Gimkit uses game-based learning and a virtual currency for correct answers, which students can then spend on power-ups to enhance their gameplay. The best part of Gimkit is that I can play along with students, both in answering questions and playing the video game. I decided one afternoon to scrap my original plan and instead, play Gimkit with my students. We started with a fun quiz before moving onto review from our solar system unit. I have to admit, playing a video game alongside students was more fun than I expected. I had forgotten how competitive I used to be when playing Super Mario Bros. I’m not sure if students improved their recall of solar system facts, but I do know that any time you play a game with your students, it is a great way to strengthen community.

Staying Human

There will always be stressful times in the classroom. Hopefully, when teachers and students are feeling engaged and creative, these moments are infrequent. However, I’ve noticed that the more stressed I feel, the more stress I unintentionally put onto my students, and lately, this has been happening more often. Whenever I feel especially frazzled, my students are the ones who remind me of our shared humanity. This week, I lost my temper as I desperately tried to force students to complete a worksheet in a short amount of time. As kids do, they resisted and the assignment took longer than I had budgeted, which made for less time for the next task. Afterwards, I addressed students and apologized for raising my voice, sharing that I was feeling overstressed. Without prompting, a quiet student from the back of the classroom stood up, walked over to me, and gave me a hug. In front of his classmates, he told me that we all get stressed, and it was going to be okay. One hug led to five hugs, which led to a group hug with 20 students. In that moment, I stopped caring if we were on pace with the curriculum guide. I stopped feeling frustrated with my students and, instead, felt human in a room with loving, caring, human students.

Unplugging from Technology

So much of what teachers are expected to do with students involves using technology. Everything from our Learning Management System (LMS) to the assessments students take, the practice review games, the Google Docs and Slides students use to complete assignments, and the videos and slides I use to teach, it’s easy for students to spend an entire day watching a screen. The more students are plugged into their computers, the more I feel like a more like monitor, keeping students on task, than a teacher.

This week, instead of introducing students to our life cycles unit using the prescribed online activity, I decided to use a food chain card game I found on Mystery Science.6

Students make food chains answering the questions, What does [BLANK] eat? and What is eaten by [BLANK]? I assumed that students would comply for a few minutes, making a few food chains, before I would facilitate a discussion about the movement of matter between living things. 35 minutes later, students were heatedly playing, stealing cards from each other (as per the rules) and creating longer and longer food chains. It was so much fun to see everyone engaged, loudly debating whether a frog could eat a salamander or a pigeon could eat a snail. While there are plenty of great online food chain games and simulators, having students use physical cards was a welcome reprieve from the silent blue glow of their computer screens.

My passive resistance to the current push for dehumanizing, standardized pedagogy and curricula will likely look different day to day, week to week. Whereas in the past, I could quietly, subversively teach my students in ways that met their needs as learners and human beings, now, I’m feeling more constricted than ever. I may not be able to simply refuse, as Bartleby does, but I will continue to create peak moments for my students. I want them to remember their fifth-grade year as one of connection, fun, and learning, not endless summaries. I would prefer not to kill their joy of learning.

Have a great week!

— Adrian
