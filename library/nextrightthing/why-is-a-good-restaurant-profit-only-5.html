Last week I bemoaned a $44 chicken entree at a restaurant I love and today I’m going to share some of the hidden costs to operating restaurants that may help explain how that chicken dish becomes $44. I remain firm on my position that there should have been more ounces of chicken in that dish but all it took was a look around the restaurant to figure out why it cost that much. I am not gaslighting you into thinking that going out to eat has not gotten expensive. It has. But I am here to tell you that in most cases it is not due to owner greed. The costs to operate a restaurant in 2025 are exorbitant.

Sometimes when I want to get a migraine I scroll through reddit threads about tipping culture and the cost of going out to eat these days “when I can make the same thing at home!” The summary is that people are really mad at how frequently they are presented with a tip screen and that many people feel like there is no value in going out to eat. Through sharing some of these costs I’m also hoping to shed some light as to why so many restaurants in NY and LA are closing, some of which are run by experienced operators.

As an average diner you go out to eat and you know you are paying for the food itself, you’re contributing towards the labor cost of the staff that is going to prepare it for you, serve it to you and clean up after you. I think most people’s understanding of operating costs ends somewhere around here. They may loosely recognize that the price of rent, which in major cities in “cool” neighborhoods can get up to $40k per month, is factored into their dishes. The amount of times we’ve been presented with an amazing marquee opportunity in Williamsburg to discover the rent is $36k… how can I opt out of being sent these “incredible” opportunities?

For every 50 aggrieved redditors there seems to be a least one person who has operated a small business and mentions the costs of operating like insurance (GL, disability, workers comp, health), permits & licenses, unavoidable citations, building maintenance, trash, pest control, equipment rentals, bookkeeping, CPAs, utilities, security, fire suppression, payroll taxes and fees, real estate tax (on a building you don’t own!), unsecured property tax (every year in Los Angeles at each location we have to pay several hundreds of dollars in tax on furniture and equipment that we bought and already paid sales tax on years ago) to name a few.

How about the design of the space, the art on the walls, the chairs you’re sitting on, the plates you’re eating off of, the glasses that break and need to be replaced? The milk containers that people steal because they’re so cute and the less cute ones that staff sometimes accidentally throw out into the trash and it’s too dirty to fish it out. The takeout containers, the leftover boxes. The takeout utensils and bags. There’s a fly problem, there’s a bee problem, there’s a problem with the iPad and the speakers. All of those things are just getting replaced and purchased constantly.

When we opened the newest location in Prospect Heights I overheard several guests saying “ok so it’s LaLou…” in regards to how little we did to the space. In addition to a deep clean and a hefty amount of maintenance on unsexy but expensive and essential things like HVAC, we painted the walls, the facade and the garden, changed the light fixtures, got rid of a ton of equipment we didn’t need, got equipment we did need and thusly had to repipe the fire suppression system and pay that company to come out and do the “pre-test” before the actual test that happens with the FDNY. We engaged an attorney to “transfer” the liquor license to us which all in all was a $15k line item and is still pending a year later! I’m getting fingerprinted tomorrow. We actually did do a lot but to the average diner the space looks the same. Which at first really bugged me because I had put my blood, sweat and tears into reopening this place but we started to break even in month 3 and now most weeks are profitable. By the end of the year we will be even on our investment. Industry average is three years ROI. If we’d spent tons of money on a new build out so my ego could feel better it could be years before breaking even let alone turning a profit. The fact that we liked the existing design and the layout worked for our concept was another reason we bought the restaurant from the prior operators. Also, lucky for us, because of Chris’ relentless pursuit of perfection and consistency nobody is coming to us for the design of our restaurants. They are coming for the food.

Nowadays a good restaurant profit is considered ~5%. For the sake of simple math let’s say if a “successful” restaurant brings in $100, the owner may see $5. It used to be 15-20% which is still a relatively low profit margin in comparison to other industries. Industry gold standard was to keep controllable expenses to 30% food cost, 30% labor cost, 10% occupancy cost and hope to squeeze out a 15% profit. From taking a look around the room at the restaurant with the $44 chicken dinner I could tell they were getting crushed on labor which is a tough pill to swallow especially when the service is nothing notable. I’m going to do a post soon on why counter service is the future of restaurants but what I’ll share today is that we crush these numbers with our operational prowess. We make strategic real estate decisions, we have engineered our menu to be super lean and tight, and there’s several reasons we don’t offer table service. The difference is stark between our restaurants that operate on the counter service model and the single unit that still does table service.

Even outside of the restaurant industry people are talking about the rising price of utilities and insurance. This is felt by us too. At one location alone the water bill for the year is north of $25k. So if your bills are going up that means the restaurant’s bills are going up and therefore you’re going to feel it when you get your check. Many insurers declined coverage at one of our locations because the area is not considered safe and so we had no choice but to get a bare bones policy with a monthly premium of $800. What happened when there was a break in last year and we needed to use the insurance to replace a glass door and lost inventory? Months of back and forth, resulting in no insurance payout and the carrier declined to renew the policy. But it’s mandatory to have this insurance in place. Operators must respond to the changing environment but we also need the support of city hall.

If cities want to remain relevant places that people live in for culture they can’t get elsewhere then city council should make efforts to create more business friendly environments. Our Montana Ave location used to have outdoor seating, just a few tables out front on the sidewalk, there was plenty of space for people to still use the sidewalk. The city of Santa Monica wanted $60k per year for us to keep this seating and additional fees and a lengthy process including architectural plans for permits. We said no and got rid of the seating. Guests were upset the outdoor seating was gone. But what’s a business owner to do? In no way does spending $60k per year to have eight extra seats make sense for the health of our business. When you live in a big city with lots going on you expect to pay more for everything from toilet paper to meals but when the people in charge make it so damn expensive to operate, the guest feels it and adjusts their behavior to stop going out and then we have no more restaurants, not to mention jobs disappear! The city should take a less punishing view of small business (looking at you West Hollywood!) and recognize it as a valuable contributor to the local economy. Make it easier please. I’m sick of navigating endless bureaucracy and multiple agencies to do something simple like place a high end attractive trash receptacle on the sidewalk that is not in anyone’s way.
