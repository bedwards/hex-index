This past weekend I was covering at our Carroll Gardens location for someone who had to attend a funeral. I saw a lot of regulars which I always love. A young couple that’s been coming for a few years had their first baby. There’s something deliciously sweet about witnessing people’s lives develop and change as time goes on. Another couple that we have befriended came in and they noticed the butters on top of the cakes were square instead of round– hi Sara! Without getting into everything I’m about to share below in regards to operations, I said I preferred it this way and she said something along the lines of I’m sure the change makes sense but you guys are so consistent that any little change is noticeable. Consistency is a hallmark of a great restaurant so I took this as a huge compliment and it got me thinking about how we achieve it.

SYSTEMS

One of the main things that distinguishes a good chef from a very good home cook or a recipe developer is that they can distill their creation into a formula that other people can understand, execute correctly and replicate at scale and with speed. More steps + more ingredients = more room for error. A classic case of too many cooks in the kitchen. A large part of the reason our restaurants are consistent from coast to coast is because we have created fool proof systems that are simple and easy to follow. This takes time, lots of trial and error and honestly it’s something that we will always continue to work on. When you’re so used to doing something a certain way sometimes it’s hard to even recognize that another way is possible! It’s happened on multiple occasions where one of us makes a micro change that seems so f*cking obvious after the fact. We usually both start cracking up and wonder why we ever did it the old way, sometimes even did it that way for years.

If you want to have a successful restaurant or a successful anything really you must have systems in place. Your team should be there to execute, not to create new things on the fly. It’s beautiful when someone contributes a meaningful creation but the day to day role of your staff for the health of the business should be successfully executing the systems you’ve put in place. Systems create clarity, efficiency and ease. As Queen says, who doesn’t love that?

BUTTER

We are a pancake restaurant that doesn’t serve our signature oatmeal griddle cakes with syrup. *The shock! The horror!* It is funny because if I say pancake you (probably) say syrup. The family lore is that Mama Salty (Chris’ mom) didn’t want to clean up syrup when she made the oatmeal griddle cakes for her two young boys and so she served it with butter and homemade cinnamon sugar instead. Chris adapted the family recipe and created a cultured cinnamon molasses butter to replace the syrup. Because the batter contains molasses and oats, it makes for a wetter and sweeter blank pancake. Syrup is not needed. It would be too wet, too sweet… but I have seen people BYOS. One time a guy shamelessly brought in a Costco size vat of syrup to the West Village.

“I’m sure it tastes good with syrup, but I’ve never felt the need to try.” - Mama Salty

Butter is one of our highest cogs so we’ve obsessed over it and dialed it in until it makes sense. We improve upon an already perfect product by adding cinnamon and sea salt, then whipping it up and culturing it a little bit. It used to be side work for FOH to hand scoop the whipped butter with a melon baller into 4 quart containers. As we got busier this proved to be tedious and inefficient. So we switched to silicone molds where the butter could be taken straight from the mixer and spatula-ed into the mold, refrigerated and then popped out into containers for service. It’s a satisfying process to see the butter harden and neatly pop out of the silicone. The first molds we tried were round and a little bit smaller than the melon baller scoop. Guests kept asking for extra butter so we tried a different size and shape– this time square. We love the way the square pats look on the round cakes and it’s the perfect size– not too much, not too little. I love how uniform the crisp edges are. When the servers used to do it the size and shape totally depended on who was working. FOH still helps the kitchen and transfers it from the silicone into the 6 pans needed for service but the uniformity and consistency of the new process is beautiful.

It sounds ridiculous that this minor operational upgrade could be a point of contention but some guests were actually upset about the change in butter shape, which is another thing to consider. How will customers react to a change? We have successfully implemented the butter mold roll out in all of our New York stores over the course of the past year. Because there are only two of us and we can’t be everywhere, we didn’t force the change in LA but our kitchen manager at WeHo saw the square butters on Instagram and asked how to implement it in his kitchen. Success! A simple process that can be easily repeated. All of New York and now WeHo are on the square silicone mold butters and Montana should follow soon– it might be a challenge of fridge space for them. Every time we introduce a change, we have to think about how to communicate it with the team — whose responsibility is it? — and consider the storage realities, equipment needs, and quirks of each space. Butter shapes aside, consistency is one of the most difficult things to get right in this industry and once you’re tuned in to it, you can’t not notice it when someone drops the ball.

A BALL IS DROPPED

High on consistency and efficiency we went to dinner at a local place we like. I wouldn’t call the restaurant in question fine dining, but it is very different than what we offer so this isn’t a direct comparison to us. I’m writing from my POV as a diner who frequently goes out to eat and has eaten at this place between 5 and 10 times over the past year. We ordered the chicken which we’ve gotten pretty much every time and this time it seemed even smaller than usual. My expectation at this place is small portions at high prices but what was on my plate last night gave me a jump scare. For $44 we got less than 2 oz of chicken— it was like half of a breast.

Chris calculated a roughly 8% food cost on it— he’s really good at math. The average food cost that is considered “good” for a restaurant is 25-30% though fine dining can edge closer to 40%. Breakfast famously has a lower food cost (closer to 20%) because of the nature of the ingredients. It’s interesting to see famous chefs like Nancy Silverton and Phil Rosenthal enter the breakfast space. At Breakfast by Salt’s Cure, we sell 4oz of chicken that we get whole, grind in house, then cure it in ginger and sage and we sell two patties for $6 at a 23.3% food cost. It’s that way at each location and it has been that way for many years. Getting back to the infamous chicken dish… we’ve had it about six times and it just keeps shrinking while getting more expensive. What was once a reason to return for a standout chicken dish is no longer. The taste was great but neither of us can comprehend the portion size for the price.

Consistency is love and in restaurants it is also respect for the guest, for the cook, for the business. It’s a beautiful thing to return to a place knowing you’ll get exactly what you loved last time.
