We laid out in the warm September sun, clear blue skies overhead, soaking up every moment of our annual “big” vacation. In this context “big” means more than a few days and a 6+ hour flight. I asked Chris if he wanted to swim in Lake Garda once more before we headed back to the room to process payroll. We jumped in, letting the surge of cold water refresh us before we paddled back and forth along the coastline a couple of times. I joked “*this is why we work so hard, so we get to do this three days out of the year*”. The night before we had met friends for dinner nearby at [Villa Feltrinelli](https://www.villafeltrinelli.com/en/the-villa) where Mussolini spent his final years under house arrest, allegedly sneaking out to see his mistress on another part of the lake. We were luxuriating in juicy Italian bliss.

We had left our phones in the room, presuming that no emergency could be so immediate that we needed to have them right next to us. Being that we were six and nine hours ahead respectively, business related messages tended to creep in around lunchtime. When we got back to the room Chris opened a video of a toilet overflowing with shit water. I texted our drain guy (love you, Joe) and told him about the emergency. I opened a message from a different location that the internet was totally out and Spectrum would not make a service call because we were using a third party router and the signal was fine on their end. Easy— I DoorDashed a new router to Brooklyn. The FDNY had popped by another location which is not really cause for concern but it could result in a surprise violation and accompanying fees because of something like paperwork not being mounted to the wall– you never really know!

Then came the worst news of the hour, a thread we were both on that we had received certified mail containing a lawsuit and eviction papers from a landlord. The suit has since been dropped. It was a scare tactic used to intimidate us because they could likely rent the space for a higher amount and we’ve got years left on the lease. We always pay our bills on time and we have awesome in house counsel so even though we knew everything would turn out fine it still ruffled our feathers. When you go from lazy lounging in the sun to being confronted by a major issue at each location it does harsh the mellow a bit.

We showered and Chris went downstairs to retrieve negroni sbaliatos (the official drink of the trip) to sip on our patio while I processed five payrolls in my bathrobe overlooking the periwinkle lake. It could be worse.

Still, that perfect afternoon interrupted by headaches 4,000 miles away is sort of a taste of what it’s like to run these things. Even when you have an amazing team in place, your operations are streamlined, you have dedicated regulars, you think all of the maintenance has been taken care of, all the bills have been paid… there’s always something. And lately it feels like there are more “somethings” than ever.

It’s no secret that operating restaurants is a challenging endeavor and it’s even more difficult to do it successfully and squeeze out a little profit. You don’t get into this business to get rich. You do it because you love it and there’s nothing else you could possibly do. The industry is at an inflection point, at least in New York and Los Angeles, where I have experience.

Restaurateurs from coast to coast are sounding the alarm that things may not be as glossy as they seem on Instagram. Bonafide establishments and playful experiments alike are closing left and right. It is estimated that over [100 restaurants closed](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/09/11/dining/los-angeles-restaurants-closings.html) in Los Angeles in 2024. 2025 is on track to be similar. NYC is doing better than LA but still it isn’t all smooth sailing. Most operators are struggling to make the numbers work.

Jeremy Solomon, the owner of Agi’s Counter which just three years ago was on the [list](https://www.bonappetit.com/story/50-best-new-restaurants-america-2022?srsltid=AfmBOoq5PjvVqIOyjO9ewgoaz9Ss_TvoHtcbWrwZCK_EbPaV0JIZzNxe) of *Bon Appetit’s* 50 Best New Restaurants in the Country, [took to social media](https://www.instagram.com/p/DPzulZ3Cceh/?hl=en) to courageously share his struggles and fears as an owner/operator. Solomon also runs Pitt’s in Red Hook, which I consider to be a well respected, successful operation. Across the country in West Hollywood, Cobi Levy of [Prince Street Hospitality](https://www.princestreethg.com/overview/) (Cucina Alba, Lucali) [wrote a powerful letter](https://wehoonline.com/business-owner-city-council-weho-dying/) entitled WEHO IS DYING to city council outlining the many difficulties West Hollywood operators face alongside what I consider to be well thought out proposed solutions. If these best in class operators are struggling to make it work, then who can?

While it’s nice to think that supporting your local spots in a time of need is an answer, it’s really just a bandaid to cover the realities that operators face: astronomical rents, the rising cost of goods across the board, insurance premiums that just continue to go up (with benefits that seem to vanish when you actually need to use them!), increasing payroll in an uncertain economy, the triple net lease which renders the lessee responsible for any and all maintenance in a building they do not own as well as paying the property taxes on the building…

Many in the industry are wondering how we will continue on if things don’t change. Bureaucratic red tape makes simple compliance tasks cumbersome and endless. It’s almost impossible to understand the language of a summons when you receive one and it certainly doesn’t need to be this way. Why is it that when you email or call the given contacts on a notice, no one gets back to you and it becomes a wild goose chase to find out how to fix the problem? Like, I want to fix it! I want to comply! Please don’t make it so difficult.

What I’m hoping to do here is to invite you in to my world as an owner/operator. There’s a lot of romance that goes along with being a restaurateur but also a lot of challenges and things I would hardly consider glamorous. It doesn’t seem like anybody is really talking about what happens in between the splashy press article announcing a new opening (we don’t have a PR team! and I will talk about this later) and the sad day of a final service. Some posts will be more industry focused while others will be more personal blog style sharing my love for the cities I exist in.

Thanks for reading! I’m so happy to be back.
