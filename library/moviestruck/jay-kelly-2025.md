***Jay Kelly*** **(2025)**

*In theaters and streaming on Netflix*

It can sound pretentious to mention “art” in the same breath as “movie star”. But this movie, however tentatively, suggests such a connection – only to let it drop just when we’re most intently wondering if it could be true.

Jay Kelly (George Clooney) is an A-List movie actor turning 60, and he’s proud/nervous about reaching that milestone.

His agent-manager and closest friend Ron (Adam Sandler) reminds him, at a moment when Jay’s feeling down, that he’s not just a bankable leading man but also an “artist”.

We only hear the word “artist” applied to Jay that one time. It might have helped if we’d gotten to hear it – and watch him absorb it – more often.

Jay is at the peak of his career when we meet him, but it feels as though he’s on a cliff edge, maybe about to tip over into decline. Self-doubt is making him desperate.

Gazing anxiously in a mirror, his voice quivering, he chants to himself, in a self-healing mantra, *Gary Cooper*, *Cary Grant*, *Clark Gable*, *Robert De Niro* – and adds, tremblingly, *Jay Kelly*.

He needs to feel he falls securely within that leading man pantheon. Does he belong there or not? The movie struggles to make up its mind.

But aging, an occupational hazard he surely must have seen coming, isn’t all that’s freaking him out. The screenplay by the director, Noah Baumbach, and Emily Mortimer, shows Jay suddenly – too suddenly, it seems to me – catching hell from all sides.

With a couple of free weeks between film projects, he all but begs his younger daughter Daisy (Grace Edwards) to spend some quality time with him.

No thanks, she declares jauntily. Before starting college in the fall, she’s off to France and Italy with friends.

Jay asks if he could join them. Come on, Dad, she counters. She and her friends will be living cheaply and traveling on trains, which he well knows mega movie stars can’t do. (We can’t miss that, in this scene, father and daughter never touch.)

Sad but also piqued, Jay refuses to accept rejection. He makes a counter move. Even faithful Ron is surprised at his mood swing.

The actor has ordered Ron to turn down a “tribute” in Italy. To Jay, such celebrations feel like a performer’s past-his-prime kiss of death.

However, now that he knows about Daisy’s travel plans – which will culminate in (*ring-a-ding-ding*) Italy – he instructs Ron to wire the Italians with his warm acceptance of their award.

Also, his longtime publicist Liz (Laura Dern) plus his regular entourage will need to travel with him. Movie superstars don’t do “alone”.

Liz is fond of Jay and Ron, having worked with them for more than 20 years, but she’s suspicious about their traveling by train, when they could have flown.

Sure enough, it turns out that a member of Jay’s team has hacked a friend of Daisy’s cellphone. Tracking the friend’s credit card expenditures, Jay knows precisely which train Daisy and her four friends will be on.

Book seats for us on it, Jay insists, and once they’re on board, just as Liz suspected, Jay “happens” to run into Daisy and friends. Realizing Jay has stalked her, Daisy storms off, and flatly declines to attend the tribute.

But Jay has discovered “regular people” on board the train and found that they’re “really nice.” Indeed, they all relax and happily bask in the movie star’s presence, and with selfies safely secured, they don’t oppress him. He can breathe among “regular people”.

When a woman’s handbag is stolen and the thief leaps from the train, Jay – ever the action star – surprises even himself by lighting out after the purse-snatcher and tackling him, to the passengers’ delighted applause.

This “warm” humanizing of a star-hero is meant to bring us closer to Jay, but it isn’t repairing his estrangement from those around him. On the topsy-turvy train ride, an exasperated Liz, to Ron’s astonishment, decides she’s had enough of Jay’s self-centeredness.

“We’ll never be to him what he is to us,” she ruefully tells Ron. They’ll always be adjuncts, deeply attached to the icon Jay but never essential to who he is or may decide he needs to be, moment by moment. “Save yourself,” she fervently advises Ron as she departs Italy, leaving Jay’s life for good.

This severance in the middle of the journey struck me as too contrived. Nothing we’ve seen Jay do has threatened Liz’s job or personal life; after more than 20 years, I think she’d wait until they returned home to explain to her employer and friend, whom she says she “loves”, why she wants to move on.

But Jay keeps being cut off for reasons, or on impulses, that the script didn’t make clear to me. The jettisoning isn’t over. Earlier we’ve seen Jay unable to win over his elder daughter Jessica (Riley Keough) because he “didn’t spend time with her.”

From Italy he telephones her in San Diego and says he’ll happily fly her to Italy if she’ll come to the tribute. He wants family there. Their exchange is intriguingly shot in a foggy, shadowy forest with Jessica suddenly appearing and walking beside him.

(I felt an echo of the opening lines of Dante’s ***Inferno*** where the poet tells us, *Midway upon the journey of our life/I found myself within a dark forest/for the straight way had been lost.* Nice Italian literature callout, Baumbach!)

What made the scene powerful for me – contrary to the rest of the script – were these words from Jay in his own defense: *I was young and I wanted something very badly. And I was afraid that if I took my eye off of it, I couldn’t have it. And I was right. There’s no other way to do it.*

At last, I thought. Here was Jay standing up for himself. And Jessica’s wailing, “The way I knew you didn’t spend time with me was because you didn’t spend time with me,” rang hollow to me.

She castigates Jay without offering any accounting of what it takes to sustain a career while being a parent, a delicate balancing act for any professional.

That his daughters fail so self-righteously to grasp their father’s dilemma, or cite instances of cruelty or neglect, seems to me to throw the screenplay out of whack.

The screenwriters show Jay in pain and with an enormous burden of guilt, but there’s hardly any concession for the artist – okay, rich movie actor – he was trying to be.

But this is no ***Mommie Dearest*** (1981), where Joan Crawford was shown to be – as her daughter Christina’s book had detailed – a pathologically disturbed parent. Heck, Crawford was a film artist, too, though it doesn’t absolve her of the charge of truly harmful cruelty.

**Clooney carries the uncertain, frightened look of a man, not just an actor, who senses his future isn’t guaranteed. Whatever it holds, Jay isn’t sure he’s ready for it.**

**He’s just at the point where he could explode into a terrifying but clarifying rage. Not against those around him so much as at fate itself, what lies in store for him, what feels like the beginning of the dying of the light.**

But Jay? Why can’t this script give him a break? Or at least more pushback, more fight. Ron and Liz stuck with him for decades. Wasn’t his talent the overriding reason?

There’s a flashback scene where we see the young Jay (Charlie Rowe) tear off the corner of a magazine page bearing a photo of Brando and swallow it before heading into an audition.

Did Jay reach Brando’s heights? Has any actor since Brando? That’s still a living question, and maybe Jay deserved to have it asked about him here, even if we’re sure the answer is no. But his actual achievement, however short he fell of his ideals, never gets a hearing.

I see no evidence in the script that Jay is an egomaniac who overestimates his impact. Sure, by the end we see that he’d like a chance to do some things over. As with a scene on a movie set, maybe that extra take would be better than those that preceded it, or maybe not.

But is Jay a guy who gave it his best or a slacker who was never all he could have been, onscreen or off? I can’t tell which case this screenplay wants to make.

That’s regrettable because the production is outstanding in many ways. It’s impeccably shot. Baumbach is fluid but never a showoff. France and Italy are gorgeous, but the movie never devolves into a travelogue. Lives are playing out, and we want to see which way they’ll go.

It’s remarkable, especially in the train sequences, how Baumbach can pack the screen with extras but keep the frame from becoming overly busy. We don’t lose track of the central characters and what they’re up to.

Adam Sandler may be the movie’s biggest surprise. This wasn’t the arm-waving Sandler performance one might have expected. He maintains a seasoned affection, patience and ruefulness throughout. He’s thoroughly believable as the devoted companion and loyal agent to an erratic superstar.

That his work burns with a kind of fierce allegiance is surprising because the performance is so quiet. He rarely raises his voice. Sandler steadying his liquid brown eyes for tenderness instead of rolling them for laughs is one sign of a mature actor. Growth and sobriety suffuse his approach.

The major miscalculation here is with Clooney, though of course the role must have seemed made for him. Surely it was written for him and in many ways, it fits him – our image of him – snugly.

But here over and above the script he has a magnetism that’s lit from deeper down than I’ve found in his work before. I admired his performance in ***Michael Clayton*** (2007) precisely because his looks and supercharged onscreen presence didn’t get in the way of a compromised character looking for a way out of a moral quagmire.

Acting with a completely different tone, he was fine as Edward R. Murrow in the video capture of his Broadway performance this year in ***Good Night, and Good Luck***.

From his breakout TV role as Dr. Doug Ross on the NBC series ***ER*** (1994-1999), I was impressed by Clooney’s striking – and not easily classified – good looks.

From the slight crinkle in his eyes, to the way he could hold his head sideways as he sized up a woman or a situation, I thought Clooney’s handsomeness had a tragic dimension way down deep under the impish flirtatiousness.

Over the years I’ve waited for him to mature into the potential tragic bearing, the doomed countenance, of the prideful man undone.

With this performance, it seems to me he’s clearly there. I’ve never seen Clooney this earnestly bewildered. His Jay Kelly is a man lost, not just to his colleagues and his children but to himself.

He carries the uncertain, frightened look of a man, not just an actor, who senses his future isn’t guaranteed. Whatever it holds, Jay isn’t sure he’s ready for it.

He’s just at the point where he could explode into a terrifying but clarifying rage. Not against those around him so much as at fate itself, what lies in store for him, what feels like the beginning of the dying of the light.

I doubt Baumbach ever came up with any such towering framework for the character. I think the filmmaker’s “sympathy” for Jay blindly softens the impact of a performer who’s at a deep personal turning point.

So, we get here a gentle, fond romantic portrait of a “beloved” man in distress. I don’t think we’re ever meant to imagine a future for Jay. Just to give him a hug.

I can’t say what could have been, because I don’t think Baumbach, or Hollywood, or, very likely, Clooney himself picture the acting transformation I see him ready to make.

I won’t go as far as proposing Shakespeare for him, though if Charlton Heston, Mel Gibson, Denzel Washington and, yes, Brando took on those roles, I see no reason Clooney shouldn’t.

For some time, I’ve hoped he’d age into the major role of James Tyrone, Sr. in O’Neill’s great ***Long Day’s Journey into Night***. Alas, a new movie version with Ed Harris in the role, opposite Jessica Lange, is due to be released shortly, so that possibility seems foreclosed.

But I’m hopeful that Clooney, having put this expanded, undeniably sorrowful, range before us, will keep digging deeper. No one can now doubt that the man’s got the goods.
