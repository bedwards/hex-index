#57: Worldbuilding Efficiently with Neologisms and “Box-Words”

This fall, I’m once again teaching my “Worldbuilding for Science Fiction and Fantasy” undergrad course at Arizona State University, which I’ve taught once a year for the past half-decade or so. The reading list has frequently changed, but one book I always include is China Miéville’s 2011 novel Embassytown, one of the best science fiction novels of 21st century and one of my absolute favorites to teach.

If you aren’t familiar with Embassytown, it’s set in the titular human colony on the alien planet Arieka, where the native Ariekei—most often referred to as the Hosts—speak capital-L Language, which they sound using two mouths that humans call the Cut and the Turn. Ordinary human beings can’t be understood by the Ariekei—in fact, they’re not even recognized by the Hosts as sentient beings—so the humans have created cloned twins bred and augmented to speak simultaneously. These clones—called Ambassadors—each mimic one half of Ariekei speech, allowing interspecies communication and trade.

The other wrinkle to communication here is that Language doesn’t allow for lying or imagining, speculation, or even figurative language. As Avice Benner Cho, the novel’s protagonist explains, “For Hosts, speech was thought. It was as nonsensical to them that a speaker could say, could claim, something it knew to be untrue as, to me, that I could believe something I knew to be untrue. Without language for things that didn’t exist, they could hardly think them; they were vaguer by far than dreams.”

After first contact, the Hosts begin to slowly expand the bounds of Language by hosting Festivals of Lies, where they titter at the taboo of partial untruths, and by making “true” similes, allowing them to use of figurative languages without uttering obvious errors. (As a child Avice was made into one of their similes by being put through an experience that made her into “the girl who was hurt in the dark and ate what was given to her,” a nonsensical-to-humans simile that is nonetheless popular among the Hosts.) Almost the entirety of the plot of Embassytown revolves around the changes to Language wrought by continued contact with humans, some benign, some accidentally dangerous, and some purposely disastrous.

So Embassytown is a book about language, but it’s also a book powered by all the tools human language, right down to the level of Miéville’s own dense, often baroque prose. Among other things, the novel is dense with neologisms—invented words and phrases—some of whose meanings are easily guessable, some of which rely on extended context or even leaps of imagination. To give a taste of the novel’s invented or repurposed language, here’s a list of new terminology that appears in just the first 25 pages of the novel or so:

immer, immersers, the manchmal, floaking, the out, kilohours, voidcraft, terretech, automs, biorigging, aeoli, Ariekei, hosts, exotorres or exots, shiftparents, southgoer, indigenes, ambassadors, enginarii, dopples, links, Language, The Wreck, miabs, centaurs, corvids…

It’s a lot to take in, a lot to process—and the source of much of the joy of reading the novel. I’m not alone in thinking so. Ursula K. Le Guin wrote a rave review of Embassytown in The Guardian, in which she talks about the pleasure of its language, while also coining a new term of her own (all emphasis mine):

When everything in a story is imaginary and much is unfamiliar, there’s far too much to explain and describe, so one of the virtuosities of SF is the invention of box-words that the reader must open to discover a trove of meaning and implication. The imaginative leaps involved in decoding such inventions and appreciating their wit can give a reader much pleasure. Miéville sets the bar rather high – I still haven’t figured out what a miab is – but most of his neologisms come clear with a nice shock of revelation. My favourite is the immer, which is to our space-time reality as the sea is to our lands: therefore, to travel through space is to immerse. Other elegant images follow, for this is a book by a writer who loves language…

I love Le Guin’s term box-word (which was first used in this review, as far as I can tell), as well as its implications: the neologisms and other kinds of invented or repurposed language so common to science fiction and fantasy (but increasingly of use in other modes of fiction too) are a kind of gift to the reader, a box that must be “opened” by the reader’s intellect and imagination in order to be fully understood. These invented terms are of one the best tools of imaginative writing, and come with almost unlimited potential for conjuring fantastical imagery, settings, and activities.

So how do we invent our own evocative box-words? Most often, they’ll be fashioned similarly to the neologisms that appear in our real-life languages, in that the most successful ones will likely be derived from existing words. To begin, we might:

  • Form compound words, like spaceship, made from from space and ship

  • Turn abbreviations into common words, like scuba diving as opposed to SCUBA

  • Clip or shorten existing words, as in phone instead of telephone, as in cell instead of cellphone

  • Onomatopoeias, where a word imitates a sound, as in zap—a word that didn’t take off until the 20th century, presumably because it took enough people having electricity around for us to hear it

  • Loan words from other languages, like emoji enter English from Japanese (where it’s a compound word, as I understand it: e ‘picture’ + moji ‘letter, character’)

  • Portmanteaus, blended or combined words, like chortle mashing up chuckle and snort

  • Names that become words, usually adjectives like Kafkaesque or Orwellian

  • “Verbifying” or “enverbing” or otherwise pressing one part of speech into service as another, as when Google the proper noun becomes google the verb

All of these tactics can be found in the above list from Embassytown, including miab, the one Le Guin said she couldn’t figure out, which is an abbreviation turned word: miab is short for message in a bottle, transformed over an in-book period of time long enough for spacefaring humans to forget where Earth is located. But even if its origin isn’t clear, its in-world meaning is provided by context clues (and I’m sure Le Guin wasn’t confused about that). As an example, here’s the first appearance of miab:

Irregularly throughout my youth, scheduled between incoming ships, miabs would arrive. Uncrewed little boxes full of oddments, steered by ’ware. Plenty were lost en route: became hazards forever, I later learned, in variously strange corroded forms, in the immer they’d been built to travel…

Miabs are autonomous, uncrewed vehicles sent through space (called immer here), which may or may not reach their destination—just like the seafaring messages in bottles that gave them their name. Very little exposition is required to explain them sufficiently, and it’s possible that even less would suffice: we’re so tempted to explain our linguistic inventions to the reader, but often the reader simply needs to see the words put into practice.

As Samuel Delany said, “Everything in a science-fiction novel should be mentioned at least twice (in at least two different contexts).” Readers are savvy and good at making connections, if given the chance to do so—and anything the reader figures out for themselves matters more to them than anything the author explains. Simply showing your neologisms in different contexts allows the reader to suss out what the word means, and how it’s used in your world. As they read on, they’ll continue to further open the box-words you’ve provided, participating in the game of constructing the collaborative world that exists between the writer’s words and their own own imagination.

To bring this around to the writer’s process, I also think that discovering the right neologisms or box-words or other kinds of invented language often clarifies a world for me, as I’m creating it. Just as having the right jargon or term can make something more easily understood in our everyday lives, having the correct terminology enter into a story I’m writing not only brings the scene I’m writing into focus, but also tends to reveal something else about the way people live in the world I’m inventing. A really great SF box-word—gas giant, say, or robot, or cyberspace, all of which appeared in literature before they described anything in the real world—might make new ideas and inventions possible, in your book and perhaps even in this world we share.
