I’m not burying my head in the sand when it comes to not paying a lot of attention to current events, especially when I’m on the road for long stretches. I didn’t know about the bombing of Iran3. I didn’t hear about the floods in Texas. There’s probably a longer list of things I’m not even aware I missed given how fast the news cycle moves. I’m noticing other things though. I’m noticing just how much roadkill there is strewn all over our highways, left roadside in a display of disrespect for those we share our world with that leaves me unsurprised that we are also so often awful to each other as humans. I’m noticing how low the rivers already are, to the extent that restrictions around Montana are happening far earlier than I can seem to recall from the past. I’m noticing other things too, and catching up on stuff I missed now that I’ve settled in at home for a couple weeks.

When I’m on the road, staying abreast of everything else is too much to carry along with the simple grind of facing audiences and not knowing what I’ll have to deal with. The overwhelming majority of it all has been absolutely positive. But even in the midst of an otherwise wonderful evening, there is still the Old White Guy who tries to get me to admit that, because of all the “progress”, Natives have actually really benefitted from settler colonialism. Never mind that in about 500 years this progress has pushed us to the brink of global catastrophe. “Would you rather you were still riding around on horseback?” the guy finally asked in exasperation, and I said, “Yes!” In particular, in a theme I intend to expand on in a future newsletter, after reading, Cobalt Red: How the Blood of the Congo Powers Our Lives by Siddharth Kara, I’m convinced that all of our technological progress has simply stirred up the basest kind of evil in all of us. The kind of evil that manifests as not really caring much about how our lifestyles effect others. Myself included and I’ve really been in a kind of despair I don’t like to admit to ever since that I’m trying to work through.4

And then there was the drunk (I think) Old White Lady who said I don’t even look like an Indian. That I should be more tan. And then my sister, also in the room, stood up for me, as she has countless times from way back when we were both much, much younger. That was a highlight among many in this wondrous effort of supporting the book.

There is also some levity in the ignorance I’ve encountered. Last week an Old White Lady in Shelby rushed to my signing table after the bloviating part of the event not because she was so eager to throw down some hard-earned cash for a book or three, but because she needed me to know she is “Icelandic! 100%!” and also that the Icelandic people were in North America before even the Native Americans were. I said, “No they weren’t.” Her eyes bulged. “Yes they were!” This essential “Uh huh! Huh uh!” exchange went back and forth a bit. She claimed this “discovery” happened sometime in the 1300s5. Whatever. The Anzick Boy was already in his Turtle Island grave6 about twelve centuries by the 1300s but don’t let me be the one to suggest a good story should be derailed by truth. In the end, she brayed, “I know my history!” and I said, “I’m sure you do. It’s just wrong.” She stormed out, her husband7 sheepish in her wake while casting an uncomfortable, embarrassed smile my way.

I was very fortunate to get to do two readings last weekend at the Montana Folk Festival in Butte with the other two Indigenous poets laureate from Montana, Mandy Smoker and Henry Real Bird. On the second day, Mandy talked about being fortunate, finally, to feel like Native people are allowed to tell our own stories, even if it’s sometimes exhausting. In the past, as exemplified by the image of George Turner’s Book of American Indians above, it wasn’t us doing the telling. We spoke to full crowds both days and I felt very grateful to be in the company of these two mighty representatives of their people and communities.

I have mixed feelings about the festival though and doubt I’d return unless there was a check with a couple more zeroes on it than the one I got. I felt we – Mandy, Henry, and I – were made largely invisible and overlooked by the failings of the organizers. It’s a longer story in how it happened, but while our event appears on the program schedule, if you look on the website, or in the big newspaper insert publication, not one of us is mentioned. No photographs, no bios, nothing. The purpose of the event – titled “Stories from Montana’s Native Poet Laureates [sic]” in one slot and “Montana’s Indigenous Poet Laureates [sic]” featuring Chris La Tray, M.L. Smoker, and Henry Real Bird” in the other – is not described. There is no way to know what it even means. To say I was pissed is kind of an understatement and I said so on the mic. I think I do a pretty good job representing my people. Mandy and Henry certainly do, certainly as well as any person at that festival represents whatever folk tradition they do, regardless of their degree of fame and fortune. I felt tremendously disrespected as a Native person by the oversight. It’s a clueless erasure and I felt we were tokenized just so someone could say, “Hey, we got some Indians!” and called it good. It’s unconscionable.

If it isn’t already obvious I’ve been feeling particularly irritable for a while though I’ve tried to keep it squished down. I’ve been in the midst of a bit of spiritual crisis for some months, something I’ve been meaning to take out of the tumult of personal reflection and talk about here but just haven’t found the time or energy. It really ramped up when I read Leah Sottile’s excellent, Blazing Eye Sees All: Love Has Won, False Prophets, and the Fever Dream of the American New Age. It’s about all kinds of new agey, white lightey stuff, and cults, and just how much bullshit is out there.8 Whether it is the appropriation of Eastern traditions as hijacked by the Wellness Industrial Complex or weird pseudo-Indian/Christian garbage disguised as Indigenous spirituality … there’s just a lot. And it’s led me to question my own practices and the legitimacy of them. Is it all performative? Did my ancestors really offer tobacco and sweetgrass and all the ways I perform my Anishinaabe culture – at least as I understand it essentially without any true experience of it outside of my solitary efforts – as we say they did, or is all that so much bullshit too? Do we even know for sure? Never mind that I was eleven years old when it stopped being illegal for Native people to practice our spiritual traditions at all. Doing what we can whenever and wherever is necessary in the wake of that, regardless of my interior questions and struggles about it all.

I realize the generation ahead of me, my parents’ generation, is the oldest generation of elders. It won’t be long and my generation will be the older end of elderhood and I don’t feel like we know shit. So much has been lost and time is slipping away to preserve it. Meanwhile the gatekeeping and lateral violence flourishes.

And yet….

Before each of our folk festival events last weekend, before us poets took to the stage, it was occupied by the Little Mountain Cree drum group from Rocky Boy9. They were magnificent. Particularly, on Saturday afternoon when they began to play they drew people to pack our little pavilion as if by magic. Which it is; or medicine, rather. It was certainly medicine for me. The tears opened up immediately. I’m becoming verklempt just recollecting it. The drum – Dewe’igan in Anishinaabemowin, or “the instrument that makes the sound of the heart” – is not an inanimate, it is a relative. And they spoke to me in a voice I needed to hear, all the way down into my heart and through that pounding doorway into something far deeper and more timeless. It spoke to my sorrow and loneliness and despair and lifted me free of it. At risk of sounding melodramatic it revived my spirit in a way that reminds me of the necessity of going out and being a warrior for my people10. Not with violence, but with words, and actions. I needed something and dewe’igan answered. I am grateful.

I’ve watched this short film at least half-a-dozen times in the last twenty-four hours. I love it even as it breaks my heart.

At the very end, Willie Dunn, whose song the film is focused on (covered in the film by the magnificent Leanne Betasamosake Simpson), says the following:

"The only thing stronger than actual film as far as getting things across is actual direct action, putting your body in a position where people have to react against it. Film is a strong thing. I don't think it's the end all, I think it's a beginning."

All of this mayhem in the world – genocide of people, older-than-human11 and human alike, and despots and destruction – and it’s accompanying sadness and despair, isn’t an end of anything. It’s a beginning, and it’s up to us to begin a response of something new in opposition to it.

It’s good to be reminded, and what better than artistic expression to perform that reminding?
