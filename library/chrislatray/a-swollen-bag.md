*When I talk about “the superfund site” near my home, this is the remains of it, photographed in 2017, though most of what is shown here has been torn down and hauled off. It is the paper mill where my dad worked for 40+ years. That’s my bulbous shadow in the foreground taking the photograph.*

I was digging in the bottom drawer of my dresser the other day trying to sort what needs to stay and what needs to go, mostly to make room for the changeover from summer to winter. I hauled everything out and was momentarily startled by the discovery of a pistol tucked way down in the bottom under a blanket I’d stashed there some months ago. It is a particularly notorious weapon, as it features in the essay “My Own Private Arsenal” from my first book, ***[One-Sentence Journal](https://factandfictionbooks.com/book/9781732496804)***. Here is an excerpt where it appears:

> **9mm Pistol**
> 
> In his final years, his health deteriorating, my dad liked to tell me that if he got too bad, he’d just “take care of himself.” He even had a gun and a plan for it, he claimed. Those were frightening conversations, even though they felt more like bluster. That said, I took this pistol off his desk a few days after he died in his sleep the morning of October 30th, 2014. Is this a gun he might have hefted in his hand and contemplated the idea of using to end his life? Thinking about that, holding it in my own hand, evokes a creepy, macabre sensation. It’s in my office closet too. I see it every time I turn the light on in there and look for anything on the shelf. I don’t have any ammo for the thing, nor do I even have a clip. It is, for all intents and purposes, inert and harmless, lacking any possibility of harm to anyone unless an effort was made to make it so. Of all the guns I own, this is the only one I’ve ever discussed selling. It’s the one that freaks me out a little bit.

The close reader will take note that this is the 11th anniversary of the awful morning referenced in the excerpt, at least it is as I am writing late in the evening, waiting for tomorrow’s travel laundry to at least hit the dryer before I call it a night. I’ve been thinking all day about the weight of this date and trying, and mostly failing, to not be overly melodramatic about it. I had lunch with my mom earlier and we didn’t talk about the significance of the day. It was on my mind but I didn’t bring it up. Being in her company for a spell was something I didn’t realize I needed until it was happening. Most of the rest of the day I spent in the solitude of being surrounded by strangers, which was likely for the best.

One of the most difficult aspects of how I make my living anymore – honestly, I don’t feel like a writer, just someone who once wrote and now spends a lot of time discussing that writing with not much opportunity to write again – is reliving that day over and over again in front of different groups of people. Wondering about the relationship my dad had with his ancestors, if any, and, in many cases, standing up for him to non-Native strangers who can’t seem to understand why so many Indian people don’t want to claim their Indianness. It is a kind of *[Groundhog Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groundhog_Day_\(film\))* experience, tearing the bandages from the wounds and reliving it over and over and over again, going into events wondering to what degree it will surface. This isn’t complaining, this is just how it is. I’m not the only person who faces such challenges.

My friends, I am not a violent man. I’ve never struck anyone in my entire life. I know it lives in me as an inheritance from my father though, who lived a life much different from mine. I’m fortunate to be able to channel my aggression elsewhere. Lifting weights and hitting the heavy bag at the gym. The full body experience that band practice and playing shows provides. Difficult hikes. These things are getting more challenging though because all the road time makes it difficult to maintain steady efforts, and my failings in other forms of self care add to the burden. I’m still mostly holding my own.

I’m a little frightened when this darker inheritance really manifests. It did just a couple weeks ago when I was presenting in Spokane. I don’t need to go into the details, but a guy in the audience essentially said, among other things, that the reason my father and grandfather didn’t claim their Native heritage is because they weren’t Native, that only “full-bloods” are Native, and so on and so forth. I ultimately told him to leave. He baited me the entire way, filming me with his phone as he left. I’m told he posted the video to Facebook later and supposedly referred to me as a “white man” in his descriptions.

This is why I don’t have Facebook.

I was mad enough in the moment, but it was later when I was reflecting on what had happened and how his words had been hurtful to other people in the room when I became dangerously angry. There was a point where all I wanted to do was turn Spokane upside down looking for him, but I didn’t. Early the next morning I left that city for Minneapolis and had a brief emotional respite but I have to admit I’ve been carrying that with me ever since and I’m not over it.

This is part of the wondering about my dad. What was his inner world like? What did he carry? Grudges, for one thing, I know, and I have mostly managed to overcome doing so myself. Usually. Relatedly, I’ve also inherited from him an almost pathological aversion to conflict of any kind, something I’ve only come to realize in the last few years because it’s gotten worse. Is it because the relationship he had with his abusive father when he was younger made any conflict at all potentially violent? Could I have inherited this fear of escalating conflict from him even though he never struck me? Of course I could have. Today, and tonight, I am aching with compassion for him for what I can only guess his younger life must have been like. There were many ways he was able to break the cycle of abuse in how he lived his life but I think his emotional baggage was probably a constant weight dragging at his spirit. I’m helping break some of those, I think, but not all of them. I’ve got a few years left to try.

This afternoon I was sitting in my car in the parking lot at the gym after I’d completed my workout, chewing on what to do next. It was gloriously sunny and cool, maybe 45°or so. My windows were down to catch the breeze as my body was still overheated. The longer I sat I felt myself sliding into a depression I didn’t want. With two hours of free time before my next obligation – band practice – I decided I wanted to be with my dad. First I thought I’d sit down at a bar somewhere, order a beer for him and for me, and that would suffice. I could only think of two times we ever had a beer together in a bar, though. First was at the VFW in Plains the day we buried his father and we stopped for a quick one on the way to the cemetery, and the other was in the **[50,000 Silver Dollar Bar](https://www.50000silverdollar.com/)** in Haugan on our way to visit the Cabela’s store in Post Falls, Idaho, just for the heck of it. Both places were too far away to consider. I never had a beer with him around Missoula except for on his porch, and that porch is no longer accessible to me. None of the places we lived together are.

Next I considered where I might go to have a meal somewhere we visited when I was a kid. Tower Pizza and Village Inn Pizza are long gone. Joker’s Wild, a diner/family restaurant where we used to meet for breakfast, is gone. The Nine Mile House west of Missoula? Burned down a few years ago. Brownie’s Drive-In, or Big Sky Drive-In, both on West Broadway headed out of town? Gone and gone. Live long enough in a place and it seems like all you are left with eventually are ghosts of not just people, but places.

I ended up at **[Double Front Chicken](http://doublefrontchicken.com/)**. I don’t know if we ever dined there when I was a kid but we certainly brought it home frequently. I can remember many times being asked to pick it up on my way home and driving the thirty-plus minutes with that overwhelming smell wafting from the paper bag riding shotgun. More importantly I remember Double Front was a place I went to with him near the end of his life. I joined him and a couple of his friends at a table that was available when I entered the restaurant. I sat in the same spot I did when we met there; to my left was the empty chair where he had sat. I ordered a regular dinner minus french fries. I ate most of it. I pushed the plate back and paid the server and reflected a few minutes more. Then I went to band practice.

Dad died peacefully in his sleep eleven years ago. He was ready to go, I think, but not to the point– nor do I think he ever would have seriously considered it – of facilitating that end himself. I still miss his presence desperately and I’m grateful that I do. He is still here, providing for me. What would I be doing without the story he left behind, untold? It is a gift he wrapped up and left with me, along with enough clues to show me where to start. I like to think he had some encouragement from the spirits of his, of our, ancestors, to do so.

My friends, this post, despite its opening whiff of suicidal ideation and hints of potential violence, is still a celebration. For all the ways I am twisted up still over the passing of my father I nonetheless remain a swollen bag of tremendous inheritances. Particularly, the capacity to love deeply and widely is a trait that I’ve gained from the combination of my father and my mother and I wouldn’t trade that for the world. I love the deep connection to this land that is my inheritance as well. I love the won’t-back-downedness nature of the long line of people I come from, and how that has facilitated our survival. My dad had that in abundance, and so does my mom. And now so do I.

What a fortunate person I am.
