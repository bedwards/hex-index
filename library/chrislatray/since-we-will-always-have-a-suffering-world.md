*Boozhoo, indinawemaaganidog! Aaniin! That is to say hello, all of my relatives! Welcome to another edition of* An Irritable Métis*. Today finds us on the opposite side of the seasonal turning of the year from Ziigwan, or Spring, where day and night are of equal length.[1](#footnote-1) Which means it is the Autumn Equinox, the beginning of Dagwaagin, or Fall. I couldn’t be happier because this is by far my favorite season; the elk bugling in the hinterlands*[2](#footnote-2)*, the geese gathering in huge vees across the sky, the mist rising every morning from off the river … my entire being sighs with pleasure just typing those words. It has been beautiful here in Western Montana these last few days too, and today promises more of the same*[3](#footnote-3)*. This is when I feel most connected to my poet self and I hope you are having a similar experience wherever you are.*

*This edition of the newsletter is largely a catch-up of everything I’ve been up to, and what is looming on the immediate horizon. Your continued and generous and essential support of this newsletter via paid subscriptions remains the bedrock supporting pretty much all of my efforts. As I scratch out notes and reminders that will emerge as a couple new books, I’m reminded that sooner than later I might need to take my foot off the gas and make time to actually write them. If you like what you read here, and if you’ve got a spare $50 to throw this way for the next year, or can even scrape off $5/month, it is eminently helpful and appreciated.*

*Inaugural IPFEST’s “The Perils and Pitfalls of Cultural Ambassadorship” panel, featuring Aspen Decker (t̓at̓ʔayáqn, Ql̓ispè, & Ksanka), James Vukelich Kaagegaabaw (Ojibwe), Shane Doyle (Apsáalooke), and Jesse DesRosier (Amskapi Pikuni), moderated by yours truly, 2 August, 2025*

As **[we](https://static.wixstatic.com/media/a35cab_8adbcc37819e441fa300a2002868d9cc~mv2.png/v1/fill/w_1564,h_1042,al_c,q_90,usm_0.66_1.00_0.01,enc_avif,quality_auto/2025_IPFEST_Crew_WhitneySnow-Photographe.png)** hurtled through July with IPFEST right in front of us, there were still so many details to address that, at least from my perspective, I lost track in some ways of what was actually happening. Once August landed and folks started arriving, there were moments where the glorious reality of the unfolding festival burned through the fog in my mind brain but for the most part my consciousness remained a weird mix of disassociated blur and extremely in-the-moment focus. There are a myriad of highlights that sparked in my memory once it ended and I took a little time for reflection, but I got back to the churn so soon in the aftermath that many things didn’t sink in. I really didn’t know what it was like to be at the festival as a guest because I wasn’t one and, aside from trips to the airport after it was over, I didn’t have much opportunity to discuss the event with the folks we’d invited.

Until this past week, when I had an opportunity to, perhaps, experience something akin to what our invited participants may have. At the Mississippi Book Festival I was on a panel with three other magnificent people, all of who were Native: [Rebecca Nagle](https://bookshop.org/p/books/by-the-fire-we-carry-the-generations-long-fight-for-justice-on-native-land-rebecca-nagle/288762f2ed30e05a?ean=9780063112056&next=t), [Jay Wesley](https://www.mississippifreepress.org/sinners-puts-truth-on-screen-for-the-mississippi-band-of-choctaw-indians/), and [Tammy Greer](https://mississippifolklife.org/articles/making-a-space-for-the-four-directions-growing-the-southern-miss-medicine-wheel-garden), who moderated the discussion. The entire festival was held at the Mississippi State Capitol in Jackson; our panel was held in the “old Supreme Court” chamber. Which made it a little weird because we were seated where the judges would be, way up high and imperiously speaking down to everyone in the room. There was a moment when Jay was talking when I literally got a chill. It was a powerful, powerful moment to look down the line of my co-panelists and see only Native people sharing Native perspectives to a well-attended audience. Jay was talking about his Choctaw language and culture, what they mean to his people. He could have been talking about my people too, word for word. I felt the experiences that bind all of us on Turtle Island grow a little tighter. It was thrilling and, even if the four of us were the only Indians in the area[5](#footnote-5), it felt mighty.

Still buzzing from that experience I drove the next morning from Jackson to Memphis to catch my flight home. While at the airport I fired up my laptop and participated in another panel, this one as part of Virtual Day at the Brooklyn Book Festival. My co-panelists were [Joseph Lee](https://bookshop.org/p/books/nothing-more-of-this-land-community-power-and-the-search-for-indigenous-identity-joseph-lee/25e989856c375355?ean=9781668087251&next=t) and [Leanne Betasamosake Simpson](https://bookshop.org/p/books/theory-of-water-nishnaabe-maps-to-the-times-ahead-leanne-betasamosake-simpson/1bb1e893e2fe4066?ean=9798888903681&next=t), moderated by [Lily Philpott](https://www.lilyphilpott.com/). I loved the panel and the discussion[6](#footnote-6) and I was so uplifted by that way-too-short hour or so that I was practically giddy when it was over.

This wasn’t the first time I’ve felt this way. I’m reminded last year of the panel Sasha LaPointe and I did together at the Portland Book Festival but these moments are a rarity. I am almost never joined by other Native people in the bulk of what I do. It’s getting better at festivals and things; for example, the first time I was invited to Mountain Words in Crested Butte in 2021, I’m pretty sure I was the only Native person there. Fast forward to this past spring at the same festival and there were several of us on multiple panels. That’s a big change and I’m grateful for it. Still – and I imagine it’s the same for most people from marginalized communities, particularly women – it gets lonely being the only person representing a community and sometimes feels downright spooky. It would be great if this evolution in representation would speed up but it’s nice to see it happening, even if more quickly in some places than others.

I could expand this entire thought and perhaps will at some point but for now I just want to share something with you that made – makes – me happy. This newsletter is already too long and I’m getting tired. I just hope all this makes sense.

Mostly I hope the folks we brought to Missoula for IPFEST felt something similar to what I did when I left these panels. I suspect they did and that makes me happy too.
