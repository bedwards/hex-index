Why?

Last weekend I attended the American Statistical Association’s Virtual Sports Analytics Conference. The conference’s first speaker was Michael Lopez, the head of data at the NFL. Dr. Lopez mentioned that the NFL is averaging 10 fewer plays per game than it did a decade ago. Fewer plays complicates data analyses slightly by reducing sample size. But the motivation of this write-up is to dig into this statement by Lopez, try to find out why, and to spark some conversations about pace of play in the NFL.

Hopefully, this write-up resonates with you as a conversation starter. I’m not trying to propose that I’ve solved this problem or that I’ve solved quantifying pace of play. I just have a lot of questions and write a lot of SQL. But pace of play dominates almost every other sport. It’s the reason why the MLB implemented the pitch clock. It’s why Ryan Hammer includes tempo in his college basketball trapezoid of excellence. It’s why your favorite NBA players keep rupturing their achilles tendons. It’s why you hate the group in front of you on the golf course with four carts.

What’s a play in this context? A play is an offensive snap without a penalty. I’m filtering out punts, kickoffs, field goals, and extra point attempts. I’ve kept in spikes and QB kneels. The key distinction here between an offensive play and an offensive snap is the penalty portion. Snaps count the penalty nullified events, while the plays count I’m calculating here doesn’t.

Metric 2: Seconds Per Play (SPP)

Seconds per play is more aimed at quantifying offensive tempo. I’m using nflfastR’s game_seconds_remaining field here in a SQL LAG query, which is a fancy word for putting the current row and the previous row in the same row so that we can compare. Taking the game seconds remaining before the play and after the play can give us an idea of how long each NFL team takes before and during plays. For these results, I filtered out 1999 because there were huge gaps in this column being filled on a play by play basis. Here are those results:

While this y-axis is silly again, Putting spikes, kneels, and touchbacks back into this would just mess with it even further as those plays take weird amount of seconds off the clock. If a QB kneels with 38 seconds left, that play would technically take 38 seconds off the clock. And spikes ideally take 1 second or less. I don’t including these outliers would help inform us.

On this chart, a higher dot on our line means a slower pace for league offenses. Teams were much faster in the early 2010s. Over 60 plays, the 2025 seconds per play pace can eat up 2 more precious game clock minutes than the 2013 pace would.

Metric 3: Estimated Time Remaining on the Play Clock Using Eligible Plays

As you can tell the creative name juices are flowing. The play clock is usually 40 seconds long. After stoppages in play, it’s 25 seconds long. There is a play_clock field in nflfastR but all its values are zeroes. The issue here is probably the same one that I encountered. There are a few seconds unaccounted for in between the stoppage in play and the referee blowing their whistle to resume play.

So as my contribution to the pace of play conversation, I filtered to a clean dataset where I know for certain the play clock was 40 seconds long. This is a subset per team of in-bounds rushing plays and completed passes with no penalties, timeouts, or turnovers, as well as excluding kneeldowns. Using these filters, here’s what a line chart of league-wide averages of estimated seconds remaining on the play clock looks like from 2000-2025:

My Guesses

If someone asked me why there are fewer plays in the NFL now than there were 10 years ago, my answer would include:

  • There were roughly 9 more passing attempts per game back then than there are now. Maybe we experienced a passing renaissance with the peaks of Peyton Manning, Phillip Rivers, Aaron Rodgers, Tom Brady, and Drew Brees at the same time, and those are actually outlier years. More pass attempts = more incomplete passes = more clock stoppages = more plays.

  • More pre-snap motion. Motion rates are up year over year since 2019, as far as Next Gen Stats team offensive splits go back. I don’t see a source for pre-2019 motion data, but my hunch tells me the modern game has more pre-snap motion than we had 10 years ago. On a team by team level, more motion doesn’t relate to more play clock used though.

  • There are fewer turnovers now than there were back then. In 2015, a fumble occurred every 123 plays in the NFL at a .81% rate. Now it’s a fumble every 150 plays (.67%). Same with interceptions. In 2015 2.15% of all pass attempts were intercepted, now it’s 1.92%. Fewer turnovers means fewer drives, which means fewer plays.

  • The game itself is getting more advanced, which leads to small increases in calling individual plays. It’s taking longer for a play caller to decide which play to call, and also taking longer for quarterbacks to read the defense. Over time, these extra seconds spent in the huddle compound into more clock chewing. Downstream copycats of Mike MacDonald’s Seahawks defense will only magnify this.

  • It’s death by a thousand cuts. There’s no specific thing we can point to (at least with nflfastR data) that is the one reason why we have fewer plays than we did ten years ago in NFL games. The dynamic kickoff was also researched, as well as different touchback field position yardlines and those results were inconclusive too. It’s a mix of more runs, fewer turnovers, more motion, and tiny increases in the amount of play clock seconds used by offenses.
