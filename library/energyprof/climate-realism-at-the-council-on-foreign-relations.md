We centrists often feel lonely these days. But the good folks at the Council on Foreign Relations (CFR) prove that the center is not yet completely dead.

One of the CFR’s new initiatives is called Climate Realism. It is an attempt to redirect American energy and climate policy in a new geopolitical environment, where multilateral cooperation toward a net zero economy seems increasingly implausible.

The CFR’s initiative has four pillars. First, climate change is a major threat to the United States. Second, our efforts to address climate change under the Paris Agreement have mostly failed. Third, we need to focus on clean technologies where we have a competitive advantage. And finally, we need to consider emergency measures such as geoengineering.

Overall, I find Climate Realism a welcome addition to the climate discourse. However, I doubt it will have much impact given the extreme degree of political polarization in the United States.

1\. Climate change is a major threat but our efforts have failed

I am going to tackle the first two points together.

The problem with most people who claim to be “realistic” about climate change is that they are only realistic about one side of the problem.

Some people, mostly on the political right, say realism means accepting that fossil fuels will be here for a long time and the energy transition is very hard.

Others, mostly on the political left and in the climate science community, say realism means accepting climate change as an existential threat.

The CFR initiative is clear that both things are true.

We are not on track with limiting global warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius or even well below 2.0 degrees Celsius. The energy transition is a slow process. Global energy demand is still growing, and we are not yet even meeting that demand growth entirely with clean energy. The energy transition is a massive and expensive undertaking.

But climate change is also an existential threat. The fact that moving away from fossil fuels is hard has no impact on the reality that we are on a dangerous trajectory. Human societies are not prepared for the kind of accelerated climate change that we are seeing today.

This part of climate realism is spot on.

2\. What should we do with clean technology?

The CFR Climate Realists argue that the United States must acknowledge China’s lead and focus our efforts on technologies where we could have an advantage. They specifically mention next-generation geothermal, solid-state batteries, and advanced nuclear.

In principle, I agree with this stance. Many American policymakers have not yet grasped the enormous advantage that China has in mass manufacturing solar panels, wind turbines, lithium batteries, and so on.

But investing in an advantage in a technology that may or may not work is a gamble. This is where the CFR’s Climate Realism is a little less realistic than ideal.

Next-generation geothermal is [an unproven technology](https://www.woodmac.com/blogs/the-edge/the-coming-geothermal-age/). While many companies, governments, and researchers find it promising, the learning curve that would make geothermal competitive without massive subsidies is a steep one.

Even if the United States has an advantage in it, the technology may never reach a point where it can compete with renewables and battery storage. The scale may be so limited that adds very little value.

The same is true of solid-state batteries and advanced nuclear. Both have compelling technological foundations. Lithium batteries have many problems and [other battery technologies](https://builtin.com/hardware/new-battery-technologies) may play a critical role in different parts of the energy system. Advanced nuclear could generate reliable power at low prices if the technology reaches commercial pricing.

But again, we don’t know if these technologies will work. Today, they are far from commercial application. Nobody has a crystal ball that proves for sure that they will be competitive in the next decade or two.

I have no problem with strategic industrial investments in new technologies and I completely agree that competing with China on mass manufacturing is foolish. But I am not sure the United States will succeed in “picking winners” in clean technology.

An alternative scenario is that the United States becomes Fortress America behind President Trump’s tariffs. We continue to use fossil fuels because our protectionism keeps superior clean technologies out of the market.

This part of climate realism is a little too realistic for my taste. It amounts to the United States mostly giving up on decarbonization, except in a few early-stage technologies.

3\. It is time for emergency measures

The CFR Climate Realists say that the United States must be prepared for extreme measures, such as geoengineering to deflect sunlight away from the planet.

I agree with this sentiment completely. If climate change is a major security threat and the energy transition is hard, we must prepare for all the following:

· Major losses to our economy, security, and quality of life

· Substantial investments in adaptive measures

· Emergency measures to avoid catastrophic, runaway global warming

Here the CFR Climate Realists have it correct. The combination of a major threat and the difficulty of dealing with it means that all options must be on the table. We cannot delude ourselves by expecting the Paris Agreement to suddenly turn the tide. We must continue mitigation efforts while investing in adaptation and preparing for a doomsday scenario.

4\. A welcome initiative with limitations

CFR’s brand of Climate Realism brings a lot of useful insights into the climate discourse. Most importantly, it is realistic both about the threat and the difficulty of dealing with the threat.

However, Climate Realism also has its limits. First, it does not provide that much guidance into how to deal with the problem. It is somewhat frustrating to see an initiative that basically says the United States will not be an important part of the solution. The CFR’s defeatism may be warranted but it isn’t exactly fun. I for one am not yet ready to surrender to climate disruption.

Second, the specific guidance on clean technology is highly uncertain. I would not be surprised if the United States simply will not become a major player in the competitive field of clean technology.

Finally, and most importantly, the politics of our nation are not favorable. Being a centrist is great in principle, but today’s extreme levels of political polarization are a powerful centrifugal force. Many American people responding to Climate Realism will either believe climate change is going to kill all of us next week or that there is no need to stop using fossil fuels.

The reality is that the United States will not lead the world to decarbonization. But I hope we can at least follow and not become an isolated safe haven for otherwise irrelevant fossil fuels.
