One approach to mitigating climate change is a sustainable lifestyle. This could include public transit, biking, avoiding flights, a smaller apartment, and a plant-based diet.

How important are such actions? Here in the United States, our individual carbon footprints are large. If we focus on consumption emissions, a metric that considers imported goods for our use, the average American [produced](https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/consumption-co2-per-capita?mapSelect=~USA) 16.5 tons of carbon dioxide in 2022.

Using results from a recent study and a plausible carbon price, I am going to calculate how much climate damage the average American would avoid with a mostly plant-based diet.

The answer turns out to be $324 a year.

1\. What’s the matter with meat?

Humans eat a lot of meat when they can afford it. Global meat consumption grows every year, as economic growth enables millions to add something new on their plate. Besides being tasty, meat packs a lot of punch when it comes to protein. The figure below shows the relentless rise in global meat production, with China leading the way.

One problem with increased meat consumption is the environmental impact. Besides greenhouse gas emissions, notably methane from livestock, meat production requires a lot of land. Meat production also consumes a lot of water and produces water pollution and waste.

At the global level, meat consumption is a significant driver of climate change. According to the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization, greenhouse gas emissions from meat production are equal to [12%](https://foodandagricultureorganization.shinyapps.io/GLEAMV3_Public/) of the global total. That is a large number.

But how important is meat consumption at the individual level? How much damage is caused here?

2\. The study

[A new study](https://www.dtu.dk/english/newsarchive/2025/04/a-sustainable-diet-leaves-room-for-two-chicken-breasts-a-week) from the Technical University of Denmark suggests that a sustainable diet should contain at most 255 grams of pork or poultry (no beef, sorry). This is quite a decrease from the two kilograms of meat per week that the average American ate [in 2022](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/charts-of-note/chart-detail?chartId=103767).

The study evaluated 100,000 different diet variations to identify those that met basic nutritional needs while limiting environmental impact. As for greenhouse gas emissions, a sustainable diet would generate about five kilograms of carbon dioxide per week.

These kinds of studies have their limitations. The debate on optimal nutrition is far from settled. The planet responds to total greenhouse gas emissions, not food-specific emissions. Meat can be produced in different ways, so the confidence intervals around greenhouse gas emissions per gram of meat are wide.

But having some sense of what a sustainable diet might look like is still useful. So let’s work with what our Danish friends gave us.

3\. Monetizing damage from meat consumption

Based on the study, I am going to compare the climate impact of the typical American diet to the sustainable diet. The carbon dioxide effect of the American diet is 35 kilograms per week. As noted above, the carbon dioxide effect of the sustainable diet is 5 kilograms per week.

Switching from the American to the sustainable diet avoids 30 kilograms per week. There are 52 weeks in a year. That means the diet switch avoids 1.62 tons of carbon dioxide per year.

Next, we need an estimate of the damage caused by carbon dioxide. The idea here is simple: how much should we pay to avoid one ton of carbon dioxide?

Unfortunately, no easy answer exists to this question. Estimating climate damages is an uncertain enterprise that depends on how sensitive global temperatures are to emissions, how rising temperatures change our living conditions, and – this is the hardest part – how well we adapt.

Nobody really knows how much damage a ton of carbon dioxide causes. If someone claims to know the exact number, they are in the business of selling something, whether degrowth ideology or a direct air capture technology.

But we have a sense of what that damage might be. It is not going to be a dollar per ton. It is not going to be a thousand dollars per ton.

For our purposes, I am going to set the cost of a ton of carbon dioxide at $200. The Environmental Protection Agency of the United States raised their “social cost of carbon” to $190 per ton in [December 2023](https://www.resources.org/in-focus/in-focus-the-us-environmental-protection-agencys-new-social-cost-of-carbon/).

I find this number a good starting point. The world generated about 54 billion tons of carbon dioxide equivalent – basically converting all greenhouse gases into carbon for purposes of comparison – in 2023. If we multiply 54 billion by $200, we get $10.8 trillion.

This is about 10% of the global GDP. While the number might seem too high initially, remember that carbon dioxide stays in the atmosphere for centuries. Unless we heavily discount the future, we must consider how each ton of carbon dioxide continues to make the planet a worse place to live for generations to come.

A simple calculation, 1.62 tons multiplied by $200, then yields an annual avoided damage of $324.

That’s it. No rocket science here.

4\. So what?

If the annual avoided damage from a sustainable diet is $324, at the population level the total savings are $107 billion. That is 0.3 % of our GDP in 2024.

The avoided damages from a sustainable diet are substantial, but they are not decisive. The right way to deal with this problem is to place a tax on the greenhouse gas emissions.

If our government approached climate change rationally and taxed greenhouse gas emissions, many Americans would pay their meat tax of $324 and keep going. A heart surgeon with an annual income of $500,000 would continue eating meat, perhaps paying attention to the health issues.

But others, especially in the lower income strata, might adjust their behavior. A single parent working at minimum wage might shift their family’s diet toward plant-based proteins, such as lentils or beans, to save that $324.

If everyone in the world adopted the American diet, with meat produced in the same conditions, that would be a very different story. The annual cost would be over two trillion dollars. That would be a major problem at almost 2% of global GDP.

To summarize, switching to a sustainable diet brings modest climate benefits at the individual level. It could also bring health benefits if the meat eaten is processed and/or high in saturated fat.

But the real problem here is that we do not tax greenhouse gas emissions. With a rational approach to climate change, people would internalize the damage from their lifestyle choices, food and beyond.
