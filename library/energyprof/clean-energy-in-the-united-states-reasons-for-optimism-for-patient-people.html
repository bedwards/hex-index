Those of us in the clean energy-industrial complex had a great run during the past four years. China doubled down on renewables, electric vehicles, and other clean technologies. In the United States, the Biden administration’s Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) gave domestic clean energy a big push.

In contrast, 2025 has been a tough year. While China continues to push forward with authoritarian decisiveness, federal policy puts clean energy subsidies at risk. The House of Representatives put forward a One Big Beautiful Bill Act that would remove almost all of the clean energy support. The Senate version phases out clean energy tax credits slightly more gradually but still amounts to a clear change of direction.

President Trump is also aggressively dismantling environmental regulations that would have forced older, inefficient coal-fired power plant to retire promptly. At this time, the Trump administration is attempting to repeal the Environmental Protection Agency’s limits on pollution from power plants.

Despite these setbacks, I believe that the outlook for clean energy is still very good in the United States. In what follows, I outline reasons for optimism for those with patience.

1. Electricity demand is growing fast

At the most fundamental level, American electricity demand is again growing fast. Between artificial intelligence, other data centers, electric vehicles, heat pumps, and other electric technologies, the need for more power generation is greater than in decades.

According to a December 2024 report by Grid Strategies, peak load-growth forecasts over five years have increased fivefold between 2022-2024, from 23 gigawatts to 127 gigawatts. This is critical because clean energy is far more competitive against new fossil fuel capacity than in replacing existing fossil fuel capacity. Recent estimates of clean energy costs show that even without federal subsidies, a combination of clean energy and battery storage is close to competitive with new natural gas generation (though these measures do not consider the full system cost of adding variable renewables).

In this situation, opportunities to deploy clean energy abound. Because we need more power generation than ever before, clean energy will prove competitive in many an instance.

Across the country, there will be many opportunities to deploy solar or wind power with battery storage. Consider Texas. By the end of 2024, Texas had added almost 10 gigawatts of battery storage capacity, and the growth is accelerating. Other states are bound to follow suit.

2. Clean energy is improving every year

A major difference between renewable power generation and fossil fuels is that the former is a new technology. Every year, technological innovations reduced the cost of renewable power generation and battery storage. New technologies become available for electric heating and industrial applications.

While these improvements are not fast enough to avoid some pain in the short run, they accumulate. Over the coming decade, all kinds of clean energy will become less expensive and more useful.

The past decade shows the power of this accumulation. According to the International Renewable Energy Agency, solar photovoltaic costs decreased 90% between 2010-2023 and battery storage costs by 89%. While the cost reductions in the future will be more gradual, they will continue.

Every year, the set of potential energy needs that are best met with clean technology expands. Even if federal subsidies are phased out, new opportunities continue to emerge in clean energy. This process will not end any time soon, given that our capitalist system rewards innovation with profit.

3. Creative destruction

One of the main reasons why capitalism is such a productivity miracle is creative destruction. When competition is free and new players can enter the market, aggressive pursuit of profit results in the destruction of inefficient producers. While this process is ugly and painful, it is necessary for enhanced productivity.

If generous federal subsidies are phased out, clean energy producers must face the music. Those companies that have excellent business processes, robust engineering, and a good understanding of the market will grow. Those that were coasting on taxpayer largesse will fade into the night.

This process will strengthen the clean energy industry because it weeds out the weak and allows the strong to grow.

4. The future of clean energy in the United States

The United States will never beat China in clean technology. We are decades behind. The toxic combination of fossil fuel lobbying and a divided public make concentrated long-term investments impossible.

But as clean energy continues to improve, we will deploy more solar, wind, geothermal, nuclear, battery storage, and electric end-use technologies. As time goes by, fossil fuels remain stuck in place.

This process will last longer than it should. The United States will likely not play a leadership role in global decarbonization. China will continue to dominate the global clean technology industry, exporting vast quantities to emerging economies.

But in the end, the United States will follow the rest of the world to the future.
