La Guajira is a windy and sunny peninsula in northern Colombia. With an exceptional renewable resource, the area is key to Colombia’s energy transition aspirations.

But so far La Guajira has failed to deliver. Even though the first wind farm was opened in La Guajira in 2004, development has since then stalled. The region has not added any meaningful capacity.

The main obstacle to developing wind capacity in La Guajira is the difficulty of community engagement. La Guajira is populated by the Wayúu people, an indigenous community that has not reached an agreement with wind project developers.

In what follows, I analyze La Guajira’s wind energy difficulties based on a series of interviews and conversions in the region in August 2025.

1.  La Guajira’s energy resource
    

La Guajira offers some of the best renewable energy conditions in Latin America. Average wind speeds reach [9 meters per second along the coast](https://theenergyyear.com/articles/la-guajira-colombias-prospective-renewables-powerhouse/)—nearly double Colombia’s national average—and estimates suggest the region could generate be 18 GW of electricity if projects were fully realized. These conditions rival leading wind corridors worldwide, positioning the peninsula as a cornerstone of Colombia’s decarbonization strategy.

Solar potential is equally striking. With [irradiance levels](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2213138819300992) above 6 kWh/m²/day and more than 2,500 hours of sunshine annually, La Guajira could support hybrid projects that combine wind and solar to stabilize generation. The complementarity of resources offers natural risk mitigation: wind production peaks at night and in certain seasons, while solar provides predictable daytime output.

Despite these resources, La Guajira has only a few active wind farms. What gives?

2.  Possible explanations for La Guajira’s wind failure
    

There are a few possible explanations for La Guajira’s wind failure.

The first is infrastructure. La Guajira does not have a well-developed electricity infrastructure. But given the quality of the wind resource, this problem could be overcome. It is a technical and financial challenge that has not stopped renewable energy development elsewhere.

The second is permitting and regulations. These issues have caused delays and increased costs. But wind projects in La Guajira have not failed just because permits, licenses, and regulations are cumbersome.

The third possibility is difficulties with community engagement and public support. As we shall see below, efforts to engage with the Wayúu community have proven difficult.

3.  The challenge of community engagement
    

While La Guajira is sparsely populated outside of Wayúu settlements, it is far from empty. Much of the land holds cultural, spiritual, and economic significance for indigenous families who have relied on herding, artisanal fishing, and seasonal agriculture [for generations](https://revistas.icanh.gov.co/index.php/rca/article/view/2798).

The Wayúu are the largest indigenous group in La Guajira. They are semi-nomadic, with many living in rancherías - small family settlements made up of multiple huts - that depend on goats, cattle, fishing, and occasional small-scale agriculture when rainfall allows. Their population is over 400,000 people, making them not only the largest indigenous group in the region but a significant portion of the overall population.

Why has engagement with the Wayúu been so difficult? With billions of dollars at stake, it is hard to explain these problems by simply referring to cultural differences or past mistakes. Wind development in La Guajira could be profitable both for the developers and for the local communities, as well as the country as a whole.

The problems with community engagement can be classified under three categories.

First, early mistakes have contributed to a vicious cycle. When wind energy development started over two decades ago, developers went into the process with little experience in engaging with the Wayúu. This resulted in missteps, such as limiting community engagement to the legally required prior consultation stage, instead of continuous and proactive discussions.

Second, the Wayúu community itself is fragmented and without a clear leadership structure. The Wayúu do not have an institutional leadership that could negotiate with the developers in a coherent and consistent manner. Instead, wind energy developers must deal with a wave of demands from different stakeholders, such as clan leaders and property owners. Some of these stakeholders are opportunistic, trying to gain concessions from developers with little regard to the broader indigenous community.

Finally, the Wayúu see wind energy projects as a legitimate threat to their culture and livelihoods. The Wayúu people that I interviewed said that they face constant demands for surrendering their rights to land and livelihoods, whether it is for wind, natural gas, or hydrogen projects.

All this results in a difficult combination of collective action and bargaining failure. The Wayúu are not effectively organized to promote their collective interest, resulting in internal competition that complicates reaching a beneficial agreement with the developers.

Additionally, the Wayúu fear that the continuation of energy development in the region – not just wind, but also solar and natural gas – will result in further encroachment on their territory, eventually destroying the community.

This level of complexity means that wind energy developers face a long road to profitable operation. It is hard to reach an agreement with a fragmented and suspicious counterparty.

4.  The way forward
    

A few solutions are worth considering.

First, wind energy developers and the Colombian government need a consistent approach to engaging with Wayúu communities. Instead of focusing on particular projects, the Colombian government should convene wind energy developers and the Wayúu for a continuing dialogue.

Here the Colombian government must play a more active role. Individual wind energy developers are primarily interested in their own projects, while the Wayúu lack the structures needed to strike a grand bargain. Whoever wins the Colombian presidential election in 2026 has an opportunity to prioritize accelerated but socially beneficial renewable energy development in La Guajira.

In both regards, Ecopetrol, Colombia’s state-owned energy giant, is probably in the best position to develop wind energy in La Guajira. Indeed, Ecopetrol has acquired the rights to a number of failed wind farms in the region. For example, in July 2025 Ecopetrol acquired the rights to a [205-megawatt wind farm](https://www.ecopetrol.com.co/wps/portal/Home/es/noticias/detalle/ecopetrol-sa-adquiere-la-compania-wind-autogeneracion-sas-propietaria-del-proyecto-eolico-windpeshi-en-la-guajira) in La Guajira from Enel, with the goal of starting operations by 2028.

As a domestic, state-owned company, Ecopetrol may benefit from a stronger government backing than previous developers. Much depends on how Colombia’s next government approaches La Guajira’s wind energy opportunity.
