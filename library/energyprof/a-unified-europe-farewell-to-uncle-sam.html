Since the end of the Second World War, transatlantic cooperation has been a constant in the turbulent world of international relations. Or at least it was until January 20, 2025.

When President Trump started his second term, the transatlantic alliance died. Trade tariffs. Threats to occupy Greenland and Canada. Reduced support to Ukraine.

This hostile behavior came as a great shock to Canadians and Europeans, who had seen the United States as a somewhat arrogant and temperamental but ultimately reliable leader of the free world.

In this situation, the European Union is faced with a massive challenge: how to survive and thrive in a cold and cruel world without Uncle Sam in your corner.

In what follows, I discuss Europe’s dilemma. The analysis is partly academic and based on geopolitics.

But it is also personal. I was born in Finland and still have my European passport. I am also a naturalized American citizen who has spent his entire adult life in the United States.

1. European and American dreams and nightmares

Europeans and Americans have always had different ideas of what a good life looks like.

The American dream is based on the idea that anyone can succeed in a capitalist society. This success then leads to home ownership, marriage, and family.

In Europe, the idea that anyone can succeed in capitalism is less popular. Europeans have always had far more support for equalizing policies such as free healthcare, free college, and generous social security.

In practice, neither the American nor the European dream is a reality for most people. In the United States, the wealthiest 10% is now responsible for 50% of consumer spending. European welfare states are struggling because of low productivity and aging.

Marriage and birth rates are declining on both sides of the Atlantic. The one institution that was common to almost everyone, family, is no longer the foundation of society.

The crises that engulf American and European societies are different facets of the same underlying problem. Industrialized countries built their social and economic systems on the assumptions of continued population and economic growth in a stable cultural environment built around the family unit.

Now the population is not growing. The economy sometimes still grows, but almost all the gains go to a elite. The cultural environment is completely chaotic.

If Europeans want to build a nicer society, they need more independence of the United States.

2. Europe’s dependence on Uncle Sam

Since 1945, the European Union has depended on the United States for defense. After seeing Germany start two World Wards, American policymakers created a transatlantic alliance based on free trade and a peaceful, democratic Germany.

This dependence has for decades provoked complains by American policymakers. During the Cold War, the complaints did not gain much traction because the primary focus was on countering the Soviet threat. After the Cold and especially after the 9/11 terrorist attacks, the United States started demanding European defense contributions more vocally.

But President Trump’s second presidency has turned this simmering resentment into a crisis. He has called for NATO members to spend 5% of their GDP on defense while casting doubt on U.S. commitment to collective defense.

At the same time, President Trump has also made it clear that he has no interest in respecting international trade law. The Trump administration has hit the European Union with various tariffs, including a 10% baseline tariff.

These changes have come as a great shock to Europeans. Public opinion toward the United States in Europe has shifted rapidly over the past six months. In a recent poll, the majority of British, German, Swedish, and German citizens said they now held an unfavorable opinion toward the U.S.

3. What the European Union needs to do

The European Union does not need to beat the United States in economic growth, population growth, or military capability. Global dominance is not necessary for a reinvigorated Europe.

What Europe needs to do is to achieve genuine sufficiency in economic, demographic, and defense sectors. This triad would allow Europe to pursue its own goals without obsessively worrying about American policies.

First, the European Union needs to generate enough economic growth to pay for its welfare state. To enhance economic growth, European countries need to invest in technology and infrastructure while removing red tape. Europe does not need to grow faster than the United States. It just needs to grow fast enough to pay for whatever public services Europeans want to provide for themselves.

This economic dynamism is hard to achieve without demographic change. Without a significant increase in worker immigration and/or the average European birth rate, EU members will continue to age, setting the stage for negative or low economic growth.

In the short run, Europe probably cannot increase birth rates fast enough to avoid major hardship. But in theory, European countries could encourage immigration for work and develop institutions to welcome productive immigrants.

This is a bitter pill for the increasingly popular alt-right parties in Europe. These parties incorrectly believe – or at least claim to believe – that pronatalist policies can quickly increase family sizes.

This is pure fantasy. While pro-family policies can increase birth rates, this is a slow social process. There are no examples of instant wins in pro-natalist policymaking.

Finally, Europe needs to build a collective defense capability. Today, European countries are not able to defend themselves without U.S. support. It will take years and large investments to achieve this goal. But it is necessary, considering that the United States is no longer a reliable ally and Russian aggression cannot be ruled out.

To strengthen European defenses, reduced dependence on fossil fuels and especially Russian natural gas is necessary. The Europeans are correct to push for electrification of their economy, but they must do so using efficient policy instruments. Net zero is a good goal, but how Europe gets there is equally important.

The European Union must also find a way to deal with subversive members – yes, Hungary, I am looking at you. One of Europe’s great weaknesses is that it is not a nation. European countries must come together and take advantage of their vast population and economy.

If Europe can achieve all three goals, it can craft its own future without worrying too much about external threats. A common European defense will allow Europe to stand up for its rights against China or the United States. Russia is far too weak to challenge a Europe with its own defense. A combination of economic immigration and stabilized birth rates will contribute to Europe’s economic success.

With these fundamentals in place, Europeans can be in charge of their own future, instead of begging Uncle Sam for mercy or surrendering to Russian aggression.

4. Is it possible?

Will the European Union succeed? To be honest, I am not sure. If the past decade has taught us anything, it is that many of our assumptions about “advanced” liberal democracies were wrong.

The United States is dealing with a massive constitutional crisis. European social democracies are voting fascist parties into government.

These things were not supposed to happen. The transatlantic alliance was supposed to have engineered robust political institutions that stroke a beautiful balance between government effectiveness and constitutional safeguards.

But crises tend to bring people together. This is the first time since 1945 that European policymakers finally recognize the ugly truth. The United States as a country is not committed to the transatlantic alliance.

If that is not a powerful call to action, I don’t know what is.
