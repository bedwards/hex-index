The energy transition is complex business. On the one hand, someone must lead. The world will be stuck with polluting fossil fuels for a long time unless major countries put in place effective policies to encourage clean technology adoption.

On the other hand, governments must also adapt to the transition. Moving too fast can be expensive and risky, as clean technologies are expensive in the early years of their development. Moving too slow can leave a country in deep trouble if others are taking advantage of clean technology.

Colombia’s recent promises to stop fossil fuel extraction highlight the difficulties. The Colombian government has promised to stop extracting and producing fossil fuels without a realistic plan to replace the lost export income.

**Colombia’s Leftist President, Gustavo Petro.**

1\. The promises

Colombia’s leftist government, led by President Gustavo Petro, quickly established itself as the darling of the international energy transition community.

It began as soon as Petro was inaugurated in August 2022. Colombia [declared](https://www.argusmedia.com/es/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2629633-cop-colombia-s-climate-plan-to-address-fossil-fuels?utm_source=chatgpt.com) that Colombia would cease signing new oil and gas exploration contracts. This was a bold move for a country that gained over 50% of its export revenue from fossil fuels, notably oil and coal.

Then, in December 2023, Colombia became the first major fossil-producing nation to [join](https://fossilfueltreaty.org/colombia-press-release) the Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty Initiative. Except for Timor-Leste, the other members of the Initiative are small island nations.

It is not unusual for countries that do not produce fossil fuels to commit to lofty goals. After all, such cheap talk does not cost them anything.

But Colombia is a very different story.

Heavily dependent on oil and coal exports, Colombia also faces the challenge of limited oil reserves. In 2022, Colombia only had [reserves](https://www.reuters.com/markets/commodities/colombias-proven-oil-reserves-tick-up-slightly-75-years-worth-2023-05-25/) equivalent to 7.5 years of production. While this is not necessarily a problem, Petro’s plans to cease new oil exploration would rapidly bring oil production in Colombia to an end.

In what follows, I argue that Colombia’s grandiose promises are helping neither the country nor the world with the energy transition. The energy transition is an important reality for which Colombia needs to prepare, but promises to abruptly stop oil production are not helpful.

2\. The problem with the promises

Bold announcements to stop fossil fuel exploration are an important form of leadership by example – but only if they are backed by concrete action.

In Colombia’s case, ceasing fossil fuel exploration would result in an economic disaster. Colombia cannot realistically replace the over $20B a year with other natural resource or industrial exports. Service industries or remittances are also not growing fast enough.

In the long run, Colombia has different opportunities to diversify its economy. From ecotourism to agrobusiness and mining minerals for the clean technology industry, Colombia can over time build a more diversified and productive economy.

But such overhaul takes decades. Historical experiences of successful industrial transformation, such as South Korea, Vietnam, or Mexico, take massive investments over long periods of time.

3\. Future scenarios

Predicting the future is a dangerous business, but sometimes it is necessary.

One possible outcome is that the left loses the 2026 Colombian elections. In this case, the next government will probably abandon Petro’s lofty promises. Petro’s predecessor, Ivan Duque (Democratic Center), supports the energy transition but [maintains](https://resourcegovernance.org/es/publications/mas-diversificacion-que-transicion-el-balance-del-gobierno-duque-en-materia-de) that Colombia must be careful not to abandon fossil fuels until the country is ready.

Similarly, Duque’s Minister for Mines and Energy between 2020-2022, Diego Mesa (Colombian Conservative Party), has emphasized the [importance](https://energytransition.org/2022/10/colombian-conundrum-global-demand-for-its-fossil-fuels-face-pleas-for-reform/) of coal mining as a source of employment in Colombia.

If the left manages to win the election, the next president will find themselves in a pickle. While Petro himself cannot run again because of Colombia’s one-term limit for presidency, a left-wing candidate from Petro’s coalition will find themselves between Petro’s promises and the impossibility of replacing fossil fuel exports with other goods in the short run.

Either way, Colombia finds itself in a difficult situation. It does not have a realistic roadmap to economic diversification in the short run, yet its promises to stop fossil fuel exploration have drawn global attention. On the other hand, Colombia now also has a cloud of uncertainty surrounding its fossil fuel industry because of potential policy volatility.

If the Democrats had won the U.S. presidential elections, Colombia’s situation might be even more precarious. The Trump administration’s rejection of the energy transition gives Colombia a possible exit ramp, as global pressure to stop fossil fuel exploration has eased.

4\. A better way forward

Petro’s announcements achieved their goal of receiving a lot of global attention. But they did not prepare Colombia for the energy transition.

A better way forward would be to prepare a country-level road map toward a more diversified economy in the context of the global energy transition. Such a road map would identify important national and global milestones for reducing dependence on fossil fuels.

First, the road map would identify important milestones that the country must achieve before reducing the production and exports of fossil fuels. When Colombia hits its economic diversification goals, it can safely accelerate the move away from fossil fuels.

Equally important, the road map would identify milestones for the global energy transition. Fossil fuel production and exports would depend on future demand projections based on factors such as electric vehicle sales, petrochemical demand, economic growth, and so on.

Based on these milestones, Colombia has a better chance of adapting to the energy transition but without creating unnecessary economic hardship in the country.
