Landlocked and with a population of only 7.6 million, Laos is one of the less visible economies in Southeast Asia. Yet this tiny country could play a critical role in the region’s energy transition because of its abundant hydro resources.

To put it simply, Laos has bet the farm on hydroelectric exports. Financed with Chinese money, Laos is expanding its hydroelectric production at a breakneck pace. But the financial, ecological, and social sustainability of this strategy is unclear.

1. Southeast Asia’s energy transition

Southeast Asia is a dynamic region, with a real GDP growth rate of 4.6% in 2024, well above the global growth of 3.2%. According to the International Energy Agency, Southeast Asia could account for 25% of global energy demand growth till 2035. The region also produces and uses a lot of coal. In 2023, half of the region’s electricity was generated with coal.

As Southeast Asia adapts to the energy transition, meeting the growth in energy demand without fossil fuels becomes critical. The economic success of the region’s large economies – Indonesia, Malaysia, The Philippines, Thailand, and Vietnam – creates robust demand for alternatives.

Given that the region consists of multiple major economies with different energy and economic profiles, electricity trading can play a role. Since 1997, the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) has pursued a regional strategy for a regional power grid. One study estimates that decarbonization in the ASEAN could cost US $800 billion less with regional electricity interconnection and coordinated energy storage investments.

This situation creates an opportunity for the small, landlocked country of Laos. Laos is one of the most hydro-rich countries in the world, with a vast river network, namely the Mekong River and its major tributaries. This river network provides the country with approximately 23,000 MW of untapped hydropower potential. Currently, Laos’ hydropower capacity stands at 9.6 GW and accounts for more than 80% of the country’s electricity generation.

In the past decade, Laos government has taken decisive action to realize this opportunity. Largely supported by foreign investments, the government has rapidly undertaken initiatives to expand its hydropower capacity, with over 80 operational hydropower plants supplying both domestic needs and electricity exports. Moreover, Laos has actively pursued power purchase agreements (PPAs) with Thailand Vietname Cambodia, and China, much in part with the ASEAN Power Grid’s goals of interconnected electricity in Southeast Asia.

A major milestone was the launch of the Lao PDR-Thailand-Malaysia-Singapore Power Integration Project (LTMS-PIP) in 2022, which marked the first multilateral power trade under the ASEAN Power Grid. This project allows the country to export nearly 100 MW of hydro generated electricity to Singapore, setting a strong precedent for future regional electricity interconnection.

2. A Laotian dilemma

But hydroelectric power presents at least three major challenges. The first is financing. At the end of 2023, Laos carried a balance of $13.8 billion in public and publicly guaranteed debt, or over 100% of the country’s GDP. Of this total, over five billion was owed to China alone.

This debt burden is a problem because Laos has yet to see significant economic returns from the hydroelectric investments. While the country has dramatically expanded its hydropower capacity, revenues remain low, partly due to export agreements that do not favor Laos and challenges in transmission infrastructure. Additionally, the country has struggled with high construction costs and financing terms that have increased its fiscal risks.

Ironically, one reason behind Laos’s poor hydroelectric performance could be climate change. Some of the dams built recently have not generated much electrical energy because of persistent droughts.

The contractual arrangements between Laos and international offtakers, notably the Electricity Generating Authority of Thailand (EGAT), create further complications. While Électricité du Laos, the local state-run monopoly, sold electricity to Thailand at $0.05 / kilowatt-hour in 2020, it bought back electricity from Thailand’s massive power generation surplus for $0.11 / kilowatt-hour, resulting in losses.

Another key issue is ecology. The construction of large-scale dams in Laos, particularly within the Mekong River and its tributaries, has significantly disrupted the natural ecosystem. The blocking of fish migration routes has already threatened the livelihood of its wildlife, which are crucial for regional and local food security, as well as retaining its biodiversity. Moreover, sediment flow disruption from hydropower projects is causing land erosion and declining soil fertility in the Mekong Delta, affecting agriculture all the way through Vietnam and Cambodia.

Finally, there is the question of social sustainability, both locally and nationally. At the local level, compensation provided for the villagers who lost their homes and livelihoods may not cover their losses. Villagers have complained that the monies received are far below the market value of their assets before dam construction.

3. Energy transition lessons from Laos

Laos’s problems with hydroelectric power generation suggest a few lessons for countries trying to capitalize on the energy transition. These include treading with caution, avoiding dependence on one lender, and navigating regional politics.

First, moving too fast without a proof of concept is a real danger in the energy transition. While hydroelectric power will be in high demand in the long run, developing a business model that makes financial and social sense is not easy. In Laos, the combination of expensive debt, droughts reducing output, and problems with selling electricity resulted in years of lossmaking. A more cautious approach would have avoided this situation.

Second, depending on one lender – China – is risky. While Laos has good political relations with China, the power imbalance between the two countries leaves Laos completely at Beijing’s mercy. Without China’s continued support, the Lao economy would collapse under the debt burden.

Finally, regional politics create a dilemma for smaller, less influential countries. ASEAN countries have made impressive progress on economic integration, but electricity trade remains a politically sensitive topic. The power purchase arrangements that Laos made with Chinese companies and EGAT in Thailand have left the country vulnerable to droughts and other problems.
