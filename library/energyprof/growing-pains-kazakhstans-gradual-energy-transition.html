With a population of 21 million people and a land area of 2.7 million square kilometers, Kazakhstan has plenty of space for modern renewables. Abundant land, high insolation, and robust wind speeds create excellent conditions for using wind and solar power to meet growing power demand.

But Kazakhstan has been slow to embrace the energy transition. In 2023, less than 5% of the country’s electricity was generated with wind and solar power. Coal remains the largest source of power generation, responsible for 58% of all electrical energy.

Why has Kazakhstan struggled with wind and solar power despite excellent conditions? The answer turns out to be a combination of the resource curse, inflexible coal-fired power generation, and policy uncertainty.

  1. Kazakhstan’s power sector

Kazakhstan’s total final energy consumption in 2023 was about 1.8 million terajoules. Of this, 16% was electrical energy. Kazakhstan is a major producer of both coal and natural gas. Together, these fossil fuels account for 87% Kazakhstan’s electricity. The remainder is mostly from hydroelectric dams (8%), with modern renewables and a tiny amount of petroleum responsible for the rest.

Power sector infrastructure in Kazakhstan is outdated, resulting in frequent outages. The January 2022 blackout made this clear. A sudden imbalance shut down the North-South power line, cutting power not only in Kazakhstan but also in Uzbekistan and Kyrgyzstan. The deeper issue is aging infrastructure. Old transmission lines make it hard to integrate renewables and scare off investors. Officials admit upgrades are needed, but progress is slow. High costs, limited private funding, and budget pressures keep projects on hold.

Electricity demand is growing in Kazakhstan. GlobalData forecasts show consumption rising from 90.9 TWh in 2024 to 111 TWh by 2035, driven by industrial growth and the digital economy. Energy-intensive sectors such as mining and oil extraction will continue to dominate demand, while household consumption is also rising as living standards improve. Together, these trends are putting more strain on the country’s aging grid and raising concerns about long-term energy security.

  1. Energy transition in Kazakhstan: promises and goals

Kazakhstan’s government has set various goals for the energy transition over the years. In the 2013 “Green Economy Concept” and later plans, it pledged to raise renewables to 3% of electricity by 2020, 10% by 2030, and 50% by 2050. The National Green Growth Plan also set a 2030 energy mix of 49% coal, 21% gas, 10% hydropower, and 8% nuclear, with the rest from renewables. By 2050, at least half of Kazakhstan’s power should come from non-fossil sources. Achieving this will require new nuclear plants and significant growth in renewables beyond hydropower.

To meet these goals, the government has rolled out renewable energy auctions and long-term power purchase agreements. It also set up the Financial Settlement Center (FSC) as the sole buyer of renewable power, creating a clear and centralized payment system.

Progress on the ground is visible. The Badamsha wind farm and several solar plants in the south show visible progress. In December 2024, the government approved a National Project to modernize energy and utilities. In 2025 alone, roughly US$278 million has been allocated to repair 8,500 km of networks and replace more than 42,000 units of equipment.

These steps aim to align with climate commitments and attract foreign investment. But progress has been slower than expected. By 2020, non-hydro renewables still made up only 3% of electricity, highlighting the gap between goals and reality.

  1. Why all the growing pains?

Kazakhstan’s energy transition faces three primary challenges: a resource curse caused by fossil fuel legacies, an inflexible coal-fired power generation fleet, and policy uncertainty.

First, Kazakhstan’s status as a fossil fuel superpower slows down the energy transition. The country is one of the world’s top ten coal producers and has big oil and gas reserves. Fossil fuels dominate both domestic use and export earnings. Coal alone generates almost 70% of the country’s electricity. This deep reliance makes change hard, as both the economic and political interests benefit from the current system. On top of that, subsidies, especially for coal, keep fossil fuels cheap and make renewable energy less competitive.

In this situation, shifting away from coal and natural gas to renewables is a tall order. Powerful economic interests stand to benefit from expanding fossil fuel generation. Technical experts are familiar with fossil fuel infrastructure. The entire power system was built to accommodate large fossil fuel plants for dispatchable, baseload generation.

Second, Kazakhstan’s coal-fired plants tend to be outdated and inflexible. With most electricity coming from coal-fired power plants built in the Soviet era, accommodating more than marginal amounts of renewables would require flexibility, which the existing coal-fired power plants cannot provide. As a result, battery storage would be necessary to avoid curtailment, resulting in higher costs. Furthermore, the concentration of coal-fired power plants in the northern regions, combined with the uneven distribution of renewable resources across the country, creates significant logistical and infrastructural challenges.

Phasing out coal in Kazakhstan is further complicated by combined heat and power generation. With cold winters across the country, coal provides 80% of residential heating energy, mostly through combined heat and power from the coal-fired fleet. Replacing these coal-fired power plants with nuclear power or modern renewables would leave a need for alternative heat sources.

Finally, Kazakhstan’s government has not fully committed to the energy transition. Years of slow progress have shown that policy implementation does not always match stated ambition. Government targets do not come with technical programs or road maps that would result in implementation. Regulatory frameworks lack consistency, and enforcement mechanisms are weak. Moreover, the policy environment has generated uncertainty for investors, with recurring delays in power purchase agreements. Taken together, these factors demonstrate why Kazakhstan’s renewable energy deployment has fallen significantly short of the government’s stated ambitions.

  1. Kazakhstan’s renewable energy future

Kazakhstan has a significant opportunity in modern renewables, but the country’s government needs to take action to accelerate growth.

The first important goal should be to modernize the coal-fired power plants and transmission infrastructure to enable more variable renewables. Kazakhstan will be dependent on coal-fired power plants for years to come, but focusing on flexibility instead of rigid baseload production would open the door to clean energy growth.

The good news for Kazakhstan is that this modernization is necessary in any case. The country’s outdated coal-fired power plants will have to be decommissioned or modernized regardless of renewables. This moment leaves an opportunity to accommodate growing shares of renewable energy as Kazakhstan’s population and economy grow.

Same goes for the electric grid. Kazakhstan’s electric grid averaged losses of 17% in 2024, well above the 8% global average as of 2022. A grid upgrade is necessary regardless of which sources fuel Kazakhstan’s power sector in the future.

Another important priority would be to develop institutions, policies, regulations, and technical knowledge for modern renewables. Kazakhstan’s renewable energy potential is large enough to attract international investors and sustain a capable domestic workforce, but reaching these goals requires consistent policy implementation.

Recent developments suggests that Kazakhstan’s energy transition is accelerating. In 2025, the government signed a US$1.4 billion deal with the United Arab Emirates to build a 1 GW wind farm in the Zhambyl region, plus a 300 MW storage system. It is one of the country’s biggest renewable projects and shows a stronger commitment to clean energy. Other wind projects are also moving forward. Under the CPIH Kazakhstan Wind Portfolio, five plants with a total capacity of 220 MW are now in development.

Furthermore, Kazakhstan is investing in nuclear energy. As the world’s largest uranium producer in 2024, the country has both the resources and expertise to support this move. Its vast uranium reserves strengthen not only domestic projects but also its role as a global fuel supplier. Recently, the government has revived long-debated plans and is now pursuing two nuclear projects. One will be led by Russia’s Rosatom through an international consortium. At the same time, Astana is working with China National Nuclear Corporation (CNNC) on a second plant in the Almaty region. Some observers believe Kazakhstan’s long-term strategy could include building two or even three nuclear power plants.

Kazakhstan’s interest in nuclear power is partly geopolitical. Nuclear power cannot meet Kazakhstan’s immediate power needs because the construction takes a decade. But it can contribute to a cleaner energy future while balancing Kazakhstan’s position between the historically dominant Russia and the much larger China.

Overall, Kazakhstan is late to the energy transition party. As a major fossil fuel producer in Russia’s shadow, Kazakhstan has faced several headwinds in developing wind and solar power. But with a growing risk of a power sector crisis and excellent conditions for both wind and solar power, the country is now moving toward a renewable energy future. Meanwhile, the country is also expanding its nuclear capacity, though it will be a decade before these plants come online.
