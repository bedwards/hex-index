**This is a guest post by Natalie Thomas, a graduate of the Master of Arts in Sustainable Energy ([MASE](https://energy.sais.jhu.edu/)) program at Johns Hopkins University.**

Jordan was once celebrated as a success story for renewable energy adoption in the Middle East and Northern Africa (MENA) region. Jordan scaled up its domestic renewable energy capacity rapidly, investing in solar and wind to improve energy security by reducing the reliance on imported fossil fuels. In 2014, the percentage of electricity produced from renewables in Jordan was less than half a percent. By 2021, the percentage had rocketed up to [23%](https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/share-electricity-renewables?tab=line&country=~JOR), one of the highest shares in the region. Today, however, renewable energy development in Jordan has effectively come to a halt.

Rapid growth of renewable energy capacity alone does not guarantee a smooth energy transition. I had the privilege of speaking with Mary Warzola and Brian Wood, energy sector leads at Deloitte and USAID in Jordan, to understand the factors behind this gridlock and to draw lessons for countries under pressure to scale up renewables.

**1\. The Constant Shift in Jordan’s Energy Sources**

As a country poor in domestic fossil-fuel resources, Jordan has relied heavily on imports of oil and gas from neighboring countries. Which particular neighbor supplies the energy, however, has changed repeatedly due to regional conflicts and resource discoveries. In the 1980s Saudi Arabia was the largest [supplier](https://www.merip.org/2025/10/energy-water-and-the-cost-of-jordans-dependence-on-israel/) of oil, which was the major fuel import at the time, but shifted to Iraq following the first Gulf War in 1990. In the late 1990s Egypt made major offshore gas discoveries, developing the Arab Gas Pipeline which officially opened to Jordan in [2003](https://www.memr.gov.jo/EN/ListDetails/Projects/72/31). With the 2003 US-led invasion [of Iraq](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/371831345_The_geopolitical_economy_of_an_undermined_energy_transition_The_case_of_Jordan), and a pipeline of cheap gas from Egypt, gas supplanted oil as the major fuel import and Egypt became Jordan’s main supplier. This shift greatly reduced the cost of power generation for Jordan. By 2011, when an explosion destroyed parts of the pipeline during the Arab Spring conflicts, Egypt had been supplying gas that fueled over [80%](https://www.tni.org/en/article/the-energy-sector-in-jordan) of Jordan’s power generation.

While the pipeline was soon fixed, Egypt was running out of surplus gas to export, and from 2011 to 2015 the National Electric Power Company (NEPCO) had to import oil at high cost to displace the loss of gas supply, incurring massive debt. This prompted the build of a liquid natural gas (LNG) receiving terminal to diversify gas supply becoming operational in 2015. A gas deal with Israel in 2016 supplied significant cost savings, but was highly controversial with Jordanian citizens who have solidarity with the Palestinian cause. Multiple gas cutoffs from Israel occurred in 2023 and 2025 due to regional conflict at high costs to Jordan.

Geopolitical exposure has left Jordan vulnerable to energy price shocks and contributed to the massive deficit of the National Electric Power Company (NEPCO), which as of 2024 [exceeds $8.7 billion](https://www.nepco.com.jo/store/DOCS/web/2024_AR_NEW.pdf). This financial vulnerability has been the most significant driver pushing Jordan toward renewable energy generation, but not the only one. First, the country’s dry and sunny climate provides excellent solar and wind resources. At the same time, the cost of renewable technologies is falling rapidly, making them increasingly competitive with imported fossil fuels. Building domestic energy capacity also promises much-needed socio-economic benefits through local job creation, a priority as Jordan’s unemployment rate has climbed to 18% in the past year. And finally (though likely a secondary motive), the environmental benefits of reduced emissions, along with Jordan’s international commitments under the Paris Agreement, continue to shape its long-term energy planning.

**2\. Renewable Energy Policy and its Challenges**

Jordan enjoys up to [330 sunny days per year](https://jordantimes.com/news/local/jordan%E2%80%99s-energy-forecast-sunny-thanks-renewables%E2%80%99), with high solar irradiation. The diverse topography of the country, from 600 m below sea level to over 1,700 m above, presents [windy areas across the country](https://www.windfarmbop.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/Wind-energy-in-Jordan-use-and-perspectives.pdf). IRENA also identifies potential for bioenergy, geothermal, and hydropower, though these resources have not yet been explored at any significant scale.

The cornerstone of Jordan’s renewable energy push is the Renewable Energy & Energy Efficiency Law (Law No. 13, 2010). The policy introduced a competitive bidding process, favorable power purchase agreements (PPAs), and guaranteed grid access. Updated in 2012, the law includes guidelines for distributed renewables and net-metering.

Warzola and Wood emphasized that the financial incentives and private sector engagement under this law were critical to early growth. PPAs guarantee that NEPCO pays for all renewable power entering the grid at a fixed rate, doubly backed by the government in case of non-payment. These 20-year PPA contracts created a low-risk environment that attracted private investment.

Renewable capacity growth was rapid until 2021, when it suddenly slowed. Three distinct reasons explain this behavior:

· **Grid infrastructure lag**: Following a major power outage in [2021](https://jordantimes.com/news/local/jordan-restores-power-after-major-outage), NEPCO halted new renewable connections to the grid to avoid instability. Accepting intermittent renewables requires upgrades such as advanced transmission lines, smart grid technologies, or load balancing measures. Upgrades, which have not progressed at the rate of capacity growth. Existing tensions between NEPCO and the government over debt, expensive PPAs, and the gap between electricity generation costs and sale prices, have worsened the problem.

· **Lack of energy storage**: While Jordan plans a pumped-storage hydropower project (delayed waiting for financing approval), it has missed opportunities to deploy incremental battery storage alongside renewable capacity, at increasingly affordable prices. NEPCO insists they need a large-scale energy storage project to minimize storage costs and maximize controllability, but as a large project it has been much more challenging to implement. Energy storage would smooth out the intermittency of solar and wind and relieve grid strain.

· **Focus on mega-projects**: Jordan focused on large-scale renewable generation projects, reflecting the appeal of visible, high-impact investments. Furthermore, it’s easy to see how the enthusiasm for renewable capacity growth was not matched for the large-scale transmission or grid modernization projects, which are less politically and publicly appealing. This imbalance in attention and funding contributed to Jordan’s underdeveloped transmission and distribution grid infrastructure, slowing small-scale projects such as rooftop solar.

**3\. Toward Affordable Energy Security**

Regional grid interconnection would allow Jordan to continue its large-scale renewables development and export clean energy. This, however, depends on regional stability long enough to reassure investors.

More concrete, is the upcoming Water Desalination and Conveyance Project, a large-scale desalination plant and water pipeline initiative, providing opportunity for significant progress. [Funded in part by the EU](https://www.eib.org/en/projects/all/20190712), the project will supply 300 million cubic meters of water to the Amman region every year and a key condition is that most of its energy supply comes from renewables. This project could drive industrial-scale renewable demand.

At the same time, residential energy expansion will continue to face grid limitations and NEPCO-government tensions. In the interim, Jordan could invest more in energy efficiency, reducing overall demand and alleviating pressure on the grid while renewable capacity ramps up.

**4\. Conclusion**

Jordan’s renewable energy journey shows both the potential and limits of rapid growth. Geopolitical risks, financial pressures, and infrastructure constraints have slowed progress, even with strong policy and abundant renewable resources. For other countries pursuing clean energy under similar pressures, Jordan’s experience highlights the importance of balancing policy, investment, and grid readiness to achieve a sustainable energy transition.
