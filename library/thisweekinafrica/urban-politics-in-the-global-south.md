It’s workshop month! Today I am at the Bloomberg Center for Cities at Harvard University for their [Urban Research Conference](https://cities.harvard.edu/news/bloomberg-center-for-cities-at-harvard-university-launches-international-urban-research-conference/). It’s an honor to share my research with dozens of scholars from across the world on the politics of urban infrastructure in Africa. I look forward to learning from all of them. In this extra post, I review last week’s online workshop “Urban Politics in the Global South.” I plan to do the same for the workshops I participate in over the course of the next month. Let’s get to it!

~~

Comparative urban politics – the political processes, institutions, and power relations across different urban contexts – is one of the most exciting subfields of political science today. Much of the [scholarly innovation](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-polisci-042716-102405) is happening in Global South cities.

I have played a small part of [developing this growing subfield](https://www.dropbox.com/scl/fi/z2f2nfbdwupgsno6pjwn8/Short-course-reflection.pdf?rlkey=8eop1ds5n9zwuxk5z4egi5mpk&st=mfnzuaop&dl=0) through organizing workshops before the American Political Science Annual Meetings and working to incorporate the study of African cities and their neighborhoods into the larger study of urban politics.

Last week, we featured some of this exciting new research in our annual “[Urban Politics in the Global South Virtual Conference Series](https://www.globalsouthurbanpolitics.com/).” One of the goals of this workshop is to break out of our silos and engage with policymakers, politicians, and of course, scholars from other disciplines. To extend this endeavor, here are a few insights from the conference:

**1\. For development innovations, look to “slums” and informal settlements.**

Creative experimentation happens in the world’s poorest neighborhoods. The [African Cities Research Consortium](https://www.african-cities.org/) is working on these exciting interventions. This group of scholars, including [Smith Ouma](https://research.manchester.ac.uk/en/persons/smith.ouma), have demonstrated how the shifting political landscape across the continent impacts housing and land use policy, especially with respect to slum-upgrading. The fast and changing pace of politics and policy requires innovative partnerships, which is why [Diana Mitlin](https://research.manchester.ac.uk/en/persons/diana.mitlin) and [Ezana Weldeghebrael](https://www.birmingham.ac.uk/staff/profiles/gov/weldeghebrael-ezana) propose [urban reform coalitions](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/09562478251327007) that bring together scholars, policymakers, and activists to discuss, co-create, and implement urban policy.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gHGj!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc2195ce9-3488-4b0d-a824-66f734c760f2_2414x1216.png)



](https://www.african-cities.org/)

Informal settlements take [many different shapes and sizes](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/0956247818798744?journalCode=eaua) and are not only the dense containers of poverty and violence in the middle of cities that are portrayed in the popular public. For example, [Fizzah Sajjad](https://www.lse.ac.uk/people/fizzah-sajjad) and [Umair Javed](https://lums.edu.pk/lums_employee/5846) demonstrate the political process of land acquisition in Lahore, Pakistan, which involves tapping into family networks to incorporate villages into the city. In peri-urban Bangalore, [Raksha Balakrishna](https://rakshabalakrishna.com/) shows how residents solve collective action dilemmas to access shared groundwater. And [Yunhan Wen](https://www.yunhanwen.com/) shows how local political party dynamics in Shenzhen, China, helps shape not only urban development, but builds the Chinese state. The implications are clear: understanding Chinese state-building is impossible without understanding the political dynamics of its urban villages.

Party dynamics at the periphery are equally important in Mexico City and São Paulo, especially with respect to land formalization or regularization. [Marcela Alonso Ferreira](https://sites.google.com/view/marcelaalonsoferreira) finds that municipal bureaucrats with close links to social movements were nominated to key government roles, giving them the skills to navigate the fragmented governance arrangements. In Mexico City, on the other hand, policymakers confronted numerous veto points in the struggle to regularize the land of informal settlements. This argument resembles those made in [Benjamin Bradlow](https://bradlow.princeton.edu/)’s excellent book *[Urban Power](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691237121/urban-power?srsltid=AfmBOorxxm_3ciwqiuZgLOSbVhedNjt41QdGUj2puDXxNT4HDC9Na7EI)*, which also finds that social movement activists embedded in local governments were able to place the interests of informal settlements on the periphery into mainstream policy, in effect building state power that also developed cohesion across multiple scales of government.

[

](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691237121/urban-power?srsltid=AfmBOorxxm_3ciwqiuZgLOSbVhedNjt41QdGUj2puDXxNT4HDC9Na7EI)

Social movements need political power to turn their ideas into policy. [Sofia Vidotto](https://www.sofiavidotto.com/) finds that in Argentina, the appointment of bureaucrats who were tied to pro-poor movements helped expand access to drinking water and sewage. This is because they are dually embedded: they have the power to influence implementation, but also have first-hand knowledge of community needs. In six highly polluted cities, [Ella Henninger](https://ib.ethz.ch/people/ella-henninger.html) finds that politicians are responsive to local advocates and economic elites when it comes to climate mitigation efforts, suggesting that social movements and civil society can place pressure on the state to improve everyday lives.

**2\. The importance of neighborhood associations.**

Neighborhood associations have emerged as a crucial intermediary in the relationship between state bureaucrats and local residents. And they are more than just party brokers, as [María José Alvarez Rivadulla](https://cienciassociales.uniandes.edu.co/profesores/maria-jose-alvarez/) shows with evidence from [Montevideo](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1548-2456.2012.00142.x). They are [opinion leaders and political representatives](http://cambridge.org/core/books/democracy-in-ghana/20E72D3CCE54A8636AC900430C9F2C47) in their own right.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!hNHE!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Faf181b2a-e867-4677-a44f-f3292843712f_3733x5111.webp)



](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-319-54534-9)

[Shagun Gupta](https://shagungupta.org/) explains how neighborhood associations in Delhi affect trust in government, either by amplifying positive performance or signaling negative experiences with the state. Similarly, [Vidhu Mukundan](https://www.bu.edu/polisci/profile/vidhu-mukundan/) suggests that women’s help groups in urban India open up new opportunities to demand services like water and sanitation from local governments.

These findings build on [Adam Auerbach](https://sites.google.com/site/adammichaelauerbach/)’s [research](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0305750X15311797) that shows how India’s Slum Development Committees give poor neighborhoods a degree of organizational formality that can help residents improve sanitation and livelihoods. My colleagues and I have [similar findings](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0305750X25000956) in Lagos and Accra: poor neighborhoods turn to concerned youth organizations to exercise their voice. Even more crucially, neighborhood organizations can help organize residents into viable voting blocs, serving as a powerful mechanism to hold politicians accountable for delivering public services, as [Alicia Cooperman](https://www.aliciacooperman.com/) has found in [Brazil](https://www-cambridge-org.ezproxy.its.uu.se/core/journals/american-political-science-review/article/bloc-voting-for-electoral-accountability/F38BFCA72DBA1C035668576393BCFF89).

But neighborhood associations are not just representatives of the poor. They are a powerful mechanism through which wealthy residents build community in the [peripheries of the city](https://www.ucpress.edu/books/the-suburban-frontier/paper), but also exclude others who have different visions of the future. For example, neighborhood associations have long played an important role in NIMBY movements that have [restricted development of affordable housing](https://www.amazon.com/Housing-Crisis-Development-Democracy-Atlantic/dp/1638931968).

Yet neighborhood associations and their leaders can serve as important representatives of residents’ interests, and even open [pathways to the state](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10767-023-09449-y). For example, [Care Blocks in Bogota](https://www-tandfonline-com.ezproxy.its.uu.se/doi/epdf/10.1080/13604813.2024.2412469?needAccess=true) and [Women’s Health Desks](https://www-cambridge-org.ezproxy.its.uu.se/core/journals/american-political-science-review/article/institutional-recognition-activating-representation-to-build-police-responsiveness-to-women/2A72A2FB2D565C51DD03BB6017A20C68#article) in India are innovative associations that mediate citizen-state relations. Interestingly, Nicole Wilson’s [research](https://www.nicoleewilson.com/research) uncovers the impact of property managers in Lagos gated estates, and finds that they help elicit tax compliance.

Neighborhood associations and other civil society representatives can serve the interests of their constituents, but they play different roles across contexts. They [compete with other authorities](https://www.cambridge.org/core/elements/everyday-choices/41C482AE689FE13A4A4A4EFA480032D3) at the local level, including politicians and bureaucrats. It’s time to take these [neighborhood associations seriously as political actors](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4496935) in their own right, especially with respect to how they hold the state accountable – or become victims of interest group capture.

**3\. The building blocks of urban life.**

Authority and power lie at the heart of these studies. Why do some citizens have the power to influence public policy, while others do not? We know how important [resources, education, and identity](https://opportunityinsights.org/) are to empowering individuals and groups. Tanu Kumar’s exciting new book *[Building Social Mobility](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/building-social-mobility/354E0FC60AACAC92F6D43AD9C538D8CF)* demonstrates how housing spurs political participation, dignity, and social mobility in India.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Bqog!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff62513c6-a919-4743-9f2b-95a3a0ff4a08_180x273.jpeg)



](http://cambridge.org/core/books/building-social-mobility/354E0FC60AACAC92F6D43AD9C538D8CF)

A less obvious but perhaps just as important answer lies in infrastructure and the built environment. While social scientists typically treat infrastructure as an outcome to be distributed, several of the scholars in the workshop emphasized the potential of the built environment to shape political participation and claim-making.

For example, [Lois Matthew](https://government.cornell.edu/lois-matthew) finds that digital connectivity in African democracies is the key factor that turns youth grievances into collective protest. When citizens don’t trust the police, [Isabella Montini](https://imontini.github.io/) and [Jessie Trudeau](https://jessietrudeau.com/) [show](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1Kmpfx5ifEZhhorkT0rjv5XD5kG20vPTB/view) how citizens use anonymous hotlines to report gender-based violence in Rio de Janeiro. Digital infrastructure is emerging as an important force in politics today.

Yet it is traditional forms of physical infrastructure – what used to be called public works – that continue to structure political life. In Damascus, [Motasem Abuzaid](https://www.politics.ox.ac.uk/read-motasem-abuzaids-story) shows how the interaction between bridges and social networks shaped the revolutionary networks that emerged during the Syrian uprising. [Ghada Rafai](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=5DCmY6AAAAAJ&hl=en) explains how the physical rebuilding of the city after war has significant implications for shaping identity and power, having the potential to re-traumatize populations who are left out of the rebuilding process.

As [Noah Nathan](https://nlnathan.mit.edu/) emphasizes in [his work](https://muse.jhu.edu/article/971543), the built form of cities is not just a backdrop on which political struggles play out, but a formative political actor in that struggle. For example, his [paper](https://bpb-us-e1.wpmucdn.com/sites.mit.edu/dist/9/706/files/2025/10/BollenNathan_compounds_20251017.pdf) with Paige Bollen shows how vernacular architecture in Ghana – the compound house – shapes access to political information and the potential for collective action.

The practice of politics is embedded in, and cannot be separated from, the material elements and physical infrastructure of the city. As Brenda Chalfin shows in *[Waste Works: Vital Politics in Urban Ghana](https://www.dukeupress.edu/waste-works)*, contestation over waste infrastructure grounds claims to rights and belonging in the city. The built environment opens up claims to space, but also restricts urban citizenship. Chalfin encourages us to pay attention to daily infrastructure – like public toilets and plastic water tanks – to better understand the politics of a city.

It is in this realm where Lisa Björkman introduces the democratic stakes of infrastructure politics. As all of these studies show, the built environment structures the public arena of politics. These public spheres become stages in the “[drama of democracy](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517918941/drama-of-democracy/),” “producing and inhabiting a lively terrain of relationality, contestation, and public debate that is proper to democracy” (2025: 21). These “stages of urban life” require deeper scholarly investigation.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AhO_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa170d21a-52ec-439e-8fd2-2036a78a5be5_596x921.avif)



](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517918941/drama-of-democracy/)

Informal settlements, neighborhood associations, and the built environment are emerging as the real stars in the political drama that is urban life. A [framework of city-making](https://doi.org/10.1017/asr.2025.22) that can bring these disparate actors together is a possible way forward.
