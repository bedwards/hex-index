Here are our top stories of the year 2025:

1. Sudan’s civil war goes on

Two years on, Sudan’s civil war has created the largest displacement crisis in the world. The country faces erasure, again. A millennia of history has vanished in the war. The war fuels a new regional conflict economy, and the conflict has become a world war. Mai Hassan and Ahmed Kodouda discuss Sudan’s protracted war on this podcast. Khalid Mustafa Medani provides this helpful primer on the war. Eiad Husham outlines the current political actors and institutions worth paying attention to. Eisa Dafallah argues that foreign interests now drive local dynamics, while Rahiem Shadad outlines the issues of identity that are at stake in the war.

The Gulf States’ proxy war continues to destabilize the country. This report details the RSF’s business networks in the UAE. This is why Middle Eastern monarchies involve themselves in the conflict. Outside forces contribute to the unraveling of Sudan. The Crisis Group offers its reflections on how to end Sudan’s atrocious war.

Pro-democracy groups have been instrumental in providing aid during the war in Sudan, as this community kitchen demonstrates. Lovise Aalen and Mai Azzam discuss the role of civil society organizations who are helping save lives. Michelle D’Arcy argues that there is a united civilian front that is fighting to end the war in Sudan, represented by coalitions like Somoud. Mark Leon Goldberg argues that Sudan’s Emergency Response Rooms should win the Nobel Peace Prize. Somoud and other pro-democracy forces should be at the negotiating table during peace negotiations.

2. The siege of El Fasher and mass atrocities in Darfur

Sudan’s Rapid Support Forces now control the city of el-Fasher in Darfur. The seizure of the city divides the country. The World Health Organization accused the group of killing 460 people at el-Fasher hospital in a particularly brazen attack. Satellite imagery shows mass burials in the city. This report documents killings, kidnappings, and detentions following the RSF takeover of El Fasher. The world has failed. Mo Ibrahim says the world ignores the conflict because of the skin colour of its people.

The siege has redrawn Sudan’s map. This is how it turned into a genocidal slaughter, 20 years after the first Darfur Genocide. Watch this video report for more. The Sudan War Monitor, Centre for Information Resilience, and the Yale Humanitarian Research Lab investigate. This is a thorough multimedia investigation into the massacre. The fall of the city marked a bloody new chapter in the war.

3. Trump 2.0

President Trump tries to reshape Africa. He has slashed foreign aid, promoted trade, implemented tariffs, and chased minerals deals. His approach is very transactional, a “carrot and stick” approach to the continent. Experts outline the challenges and opportunities for the Trump administration in Africa. Zainab Usman outlines six of Trump’s executive orders that African countries should pay attention to. While the continent is “relatively” immune to the tariffs, they signal an end to trade deal that meant to boost development. It has been quite the year for Africa and Trump.

Unserious men for unserious times.

4. A different future for foreign aid

The US Government effectively shut down USAID. Anne Pitcher explains what happened. The cost of the executive order measured in lives. Aid cuts are taking their toll on the continent and imperils the lives of millions across the globe. The decision created a man-made hunger crisis. The UN sketches a global map of mayhem from US aid freeze. Chris Ogunmodede provides these 16 thoughts on what the Trump aid cuts mean for Africa. What is the role of donors in a post-aid world? What does a world without foreign aid look like? Listen to This American Life episode for more.

The shutdown is not just a humanitarian issue, but also threatens American interests. It creates an opening for China. But it also presents an opportunity to African countries. CGD outlines these four ways to improve aid. Gregory Rockson explains how to make foreign aid work for Africa. Ken Opalo argues that reflexive catastrophizing holds back policy imagination in African states. His posts on the aid cuts, a new aid paradigm, and ending aid dependency are worth reading. Laura Robson discusses the end of aid. Read these alongside W. Guyde Moore’s post on the need for a united Africa and a world without the US at the center, and Grieve Chelwa’s post on the importance of taxes. Duncan Green discusses what the future of aid might look like. Foreign Policy’s Fall Issue assesses international development after foreign aid. Adam Tooze’s lead essay situates the issue within a context of “the end of development.” Clearly, Africa seeks a post-West plan.

5. Authoritarianism and resistance in Tanzania

Samia Suluhu Hassan and the CCM deepen their control of Tanzania, but opposition is growing. There were “elections” this year, but opposition figures were barred from running. The ruling party has dismantled the opposition. The public is getting fed up with the government’s increasing opposition crackdowns and forced disappearances. Hassan is engaged in “reform-washing” while repressing political opponents.

In resistance to the sham elections, youth took to the streets in protest. Security forces fired tear gas at protesters. Post-election violence has threatened Tanzania’s reputation as a peaceful country. The protests illustrated years of bottled-up anger finally being let out. Hidden graves in Dar es Salaam provide evidence for mass killings after elections.

Michaela Collord explains what happens now after Hassan’s coronation. Journalist Erick Kabendera’s memoir tells the story of Tanzania’s fall from Nyerere’s idealism to Magufili’s tyranny. Dan Paget argues that Hassan’s grip in power has been shaken and wonders if the country has reached its breaking point and if it will never be the same. Read this report on protest and crackdown in Tanzania. Nick Westcott asks if the CCM losing Tanzania. Yonatan Morse argues that the CCM looks more vulnerable than ever.

6. Kenyans exploited in Saudi Arabia

Abdi Latif Dahir, Vivian Nereim, and Justin Scheck spent months documenting the struggles of Kenyan mothers and children who are trapped in Saudi Arabia. This story of a mother who is fighting to free her daughter from the country is especially harrowing. Read this excellent article where mothers tell their stories. Kenya is betting on its women abroad, while politicians and businesses exploit the practice.

7. Gold mining threatens environment but provides opportunities

This is an important article on the economic scale and environmental impact of artisanal mining across Africa. The practice, called galamsey in Ghana, is wreaking environmental devastation. This is how Ghana’s gold rush threatens to fuel illicit trade. As gold prices soar, Uganda’s artisanal miners get edged out. This article provides perspectives on artisanal mining in Liberia. Global chaos is a boon for African gold miners with shovels. Could high gold prices be the boon Ghana’s economy needs?

8. Burkina Faso’s leader gains popularity

Burkina Faso’s coup leader Ibrahim Traoré has captured hearts and minds across the world. He is celebrated for his youth and attempts to securitize and development spaces beyond the capital. This feature explains how he became a pan-African hero. Will he save Burkina Faso?

9. Challenges of democratization

Africans are growing disillusioned with democracy. Its failure to deliver jobs, economic development, and security threatens its endurance. Joseph Asunka argues that delivery jobs and justice are the real tests of Ghana’s storied democracy. Abubakar Sidiq Jaafar outlines what the African continent teaches us about democracy. Frances Z. Brown outlines the four big questions shaping democracy in Africa. Danielle Resnick and Landry Signé published “Prospects for democratic resilience in Africa during uncertain times.” This is part of a larger project on the state of democracy in Africa. Make sure to read the new OSF Report “Analysis of the Nexus between Democratic Governance and Economic Justice in Africa.” Here are some insights into reimagining a global democratic future.

10. Protests in Togo, Kenya, Madagascar, Morocco and beyond

Protesters in Togo took to the streets in Lomé to demonstrate against president Faure Gnassingbé’s grip on power. He dissolved term limits on his rule. The Gnassingbé family has ruled the country for 58 years.

Kenyans protested the government after teacher and blogger Albert Ojwang died in police custody. Police clashed with protesters. Rose Njeri Tunguru tells the story of being arrested for helping Kenyans email Parliament. Security forces killed dozens of people in protests against President Ruto’s government. Judy Mbugua explains why Kenya’s Gen Z are protesting.

Gen Z’ers in Madagascar protested for electricity, water, and dignity for all. Now they have a military regime. Carolyn Holmes explains the rise of the protests, and why they might not lead to much substantive change. Read this article to learn more about the small but powerful elite.

Youth participated in anti-government protests in Morocco. The protests are part of a wave of demonstrations across African countries, as well as Gen Z protests across the world. African youth are no not content sitting on the sidelines and are taking to the streets in protest. D.S. Battistoli argues that there is a generational rebellion.

11. Africa’s place in a new world order

The changing world order offers new opportunities for African nations. Tinashe Sithole outlines an Africa without borders. Zachariah Mampilly argues that the “Global South” is really a modern gloss for old divisions. Amy Niang analyzes Western centrism and the world order. Ken Opalo calls for a new model of Pan-Africanism. Africa is no longer looking from the sidelines as part of BRICS. “Middle powers” are reshaping Africa. African students are pivoting to China to pursue higher education. Charles Kenny examines the changing scope of Chinese investment in Africa.

Chris Ogunmodede argues that African countries have not given up on multilateralism. Carlos Lopes suggests that African countries have an opportunity to reshape global power relations. The late Ngũgĩ wa Thiong’o addresses the importance of decolonizing knowledge production and the language of conquest. This is what African actors make of the new multipolarity.

This is cool: Non-Western visions of the international order. And the new politics of territorial expansion.

12. Political uncertainty in South Sudan and Ethiopia

South Sudan was (is?) on the brink of a full-blown civil war. The peace agreement between the two opposing sides collapsed. Clémence Pinaud explains why South Sudan could be returning to war.

Meanwhile, there is fear that a new war between Eritrea and Ethiopia will occur. A split in TPLF leadership threatens stability in the region. Mebratu Kelecha argues that Ethiopia-Eritrea relations are becoming more strained. The risk of a new Eritrean-Ethiopian war is growing. This post outlines the shifting contours of conflict in the Horn of Africa. This is a good account of everyday life in Mekelle. There are several overlapping conflicts in the Horn worth paying attention to. Yohannes Woldemariam outlines the ideological basis behind Ethiopia’s imperial ambitions.

13. Rebels control Goma and Bukavu, but there is also a deal

M23, a rebel movement backed by Rwanda, took control of Goma. They later took Bukavu. Jason Stearns and Kristof Titeca are quite concerned about a broader war. The Continent goes inside Goma, while Prosper Heri Ngorora explains Bukavu’s century-long resilience. This brief explains the M23 and three decades of prolonged conflict. The M23 uses wealth and warfare to expand their power in the country. UN experts claim that Rwanda exercises command and control over M23 rebels.

Here is a brief summary of the conflict. Ian Wafula explains the role of foreign mercenaries. Jason Stearns argues that the West could easily stop the conflict by holding Rwanda to account. The crisis extends beyond “conflict minerals.” Filip Reyntjens suggests that the fall of Goma marks a low point in the long conflict. Kristof Titeca provides this helpful analysis of what the war is all about. This briefing outlines three decades of war in the DR Congo. Check out this photo essay “The DRC’s Cobalt.” Katongo Seyuba’s report examines climate, peace, and security in eastern DR Congo. Nina Wilén provides this helpful analysis of the situation.

Rwanda and DR Congo signed a peace agreement at the White House. Even though the two countries signed the deal, it ignores the root causes of the conflict. Norman Ishimwe Sinamenya argues that the Congo-Rwanda peace deal will only hold if Rwanda is not rewarded for aggression. Paul Kagame remains the cynical master of power politics. This is why the DR Congo-Rwanda peace deal is so fragile.

14. Zohran Mamdani is the new mayor of NYC

Zohran Kwame Mamdani is the new mayor of New York City. He was born in Uganda and raised in Cape Town and New York City. His mother is filmmaker Mira Nair (Mississippi Masala, Queen of Katwe) and his father is professor Mahmood Mamdani. This is how his dad’s ideas could influence his leadership. Howard French helps us understand Mamdani by analyzing his father Mahmood Mamdani’s memoir.

15. Migration is changing the world

Lydia Polgreen examines the era of mass migration. She “went to Dubai and caught a glimpse of the future.” She went to Sweden and explains the experience of African migrants. Africa’s crisis is Europe’s too. Yotam Gidron and Kristof Titeca examine the changing refugee and immigration landscape in Uganda. The country has already announced that it will stop granting refugee status to new arrivals from Ethiopia, Somalia, and Eritrea.

In an ideological shift, the US granted 54 Afrikaners refugee status. The Trump administration claims they have been victims of racial persecution in South Africa. This is how the narrative made its way to the White House. Michael Albertus sets the record straight on Trump’s fantasy about Afrikaner land. Meanwhile, Trump continues to ban immigrants from many African countries from traveling to the US.

16. Critical minerals

The race for critical minerals is recreating colonial models. Analysts fear that the US’s push into the Lobito Corridor is a new way to exploit the continent’s minerals. Gulf states are now targeting African mines. Even Bill Gates and Jeff Bezos get in on the critical minerals action. This is a helpful report on critical minerals and Great Power Competition. The power play for Congo’s cobalt is on. Watch this explainer on Africa’s critical minerals dilemma, and this symposium on the geopolitics of critical minerals. Bartering critical minerals for security is a bad idea.

This case study of a Ghana Lithium project provides insights into how critical minerals can help people on the ground. Jabulani Shaba explores how benefits from Zimbabwe’s lithium can flow to local communities. This is how African states are adapting to newly assertive African states.
