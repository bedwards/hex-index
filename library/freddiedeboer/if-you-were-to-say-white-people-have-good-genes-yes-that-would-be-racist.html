Longtime readers are aware that I have had to wrestle with the distinction between genetic influence on individuals and genetic influence on groups, such as racial groups or sexes, ever since publication of my 2020 book The Cult of Smart. The book argues that our educational discourse fails in part because we refuse to accept that there are genetic influences on intellectual ability and thus on academic outcomes. As I said at the time, I was moved to write it in part because I was frustrated by how discussion of the racial achievement gap dominated education research, policy, and rhetoric; an early working title, in fact, was After the Achievement Gap. It seemed and seems to me that this fixation could eventually lead us to a future where we achieve racial proportionality in educational outcomes and yet still have vast numbers of failing students, just without obvious racial gaps. For those failing students, the elimination of said gaps would not be of much comfort. And yet my experience doing education research and coursework in grad school showed that the whole apparatus funneled attention back to race again and again.

My book was therefore an argument for focusing on individual educational differences, not genetic. I don’t, as it happens, think that the racial achievement gap is genetic in origin, and the book said so. I’m of course not suggesting that no group differences have genetic influence; skin color is a paradigmatic case of there being obvious differences in environmental pressures (UV index) leading to different adaptations (differing average levels of melanin) that are passed down through biological parentage. I don’t think it’s true, though, that our perceived educational gaps are the result of genetic factors, but rather of a vast number of environmental, economic, and social influences that are each of very small effect. (Which is why individual variables and interventions so rarely seem to matter, in the research record, and also why the problem is so difficult to solve.) I’m not here to debate that topic today - I don’t have the rest of my life - but to point out the senselessness of mistaking the individual claim for the group.

Alas….Unsurprisingly, the usual suspects declared that the book was making the exact argument it was not making and had the exact focus it did not have. Mostly this was just dishonesty and petty personal politics regarding a particularly unpopular figure. (That is, me.) But some of it really did seem to stem from a failure to understand the difference between saying that our individual genotypes influence our individual selves and saying that any given group differences are genetic in origin. Being erroneously accused of arguing that Black people are genetically unintelligent was frustrating but expected; being forced back into a conversation about group genetics when I was ardently trying to have a conversation about individual genetics was irritating but inevitable; realizing how many educated people sincerely did not know the difference was… disturbing! So let’s clear the basic distinction up.

When we say that someone has “good genes,” the phrase is a shorthand acknowledgment that an individual’s genetic inheritance - say, they way their genotype has provided them with pleasing facial symmetry, clear skin, or (just throwing out examples here) big bazoongas - has given them some advantage. The concept is imprecise and the claim is always disputable; obviously, clearer skin can be achieved with creams and serums, facial symmetry can be approximated with makeup and lighting, and big bazoongas can be purchased at any number of trendy clinics. But still, the idea of “good genes” corresponds roughly to something real: each person’s unique genetic makeup affects how their bodies grow, look, and function. There is nothing inherently sinister in recognizing that genetic variation influences human traits. I don’t know how a honest person could fail to acknowledge that the individual genetic makeups of Lebron James, Natalie Portman, or Aretha Franklin all had significant impact on their level of professional and personal success.

Of course, when that same language is expanded from the individual to the group - if someone were to declare that “white people have good genes” - the meaning and implications change entirely. What was once a comment about personal inheritance becomes an assertion of collective biological hierarchy. Such beliefs are the basis of racism. What seems nutty to me is how many people profess not to understand (or, even worse, seem to sincerely not understand) the difference between saying “this person’s good genes made them beautiful/a great running back/a great opera singer” and “this person’s genes were called good, that must be a statement of their racial superiority.” Those aren’t even close to the same thing!

The difference between those two types of claims, individual versus group, is not fundamentally one of ideology or values. Equating the two reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of how genes, variation, and populations actually work. When we talk about an individual’s genes, we’re referring to the particular configuration of alleles they have inherited from their parents. Each human genome contains about three billion base pairs of DNA, and the small variations that exist among us (differences in a single nucleotide here, a small deletion or duplication there) are what create the vast diversity of human phenotypes. Two siblings can differ dramatically in height, intelligence, disease risk, or temperament even though they share most of their genetic material, because what matters are specific combinations, interactions, and expressions of genes.

That is what people are gesturing toward when they say “she’s got good genes.” (Who might “she” be? Who’s to say! This is all a hypothetical, unconnected to any long-boiling public debate you’re already sick of.) “Good genes” means that, among the thousands of possible configurations of genetic traits, hers happened to yield something advantageous - perhaps an efficient metabolism, perhaps a resilient immune system, or perhaps big bazoongas. The claim isn’t even necessarily comparative, by the way; it does not necessarily imply that others have worse genes, only that this individual was genetically fortunate in some way. Of course, sometimes specific traits really are worse than others if we set a benchmark or task; my genes for lateral quickness, I can assure you, really are worse than those of your average NBA point guard. With physical attractiveness, though, the reality is naturally more debatable and subjective.

By contrast, saying that a race or an ethnic group has good or bad genes is bad for all the reasons we’ve spent several centuries discussing. “White people have good genes” or “Black people have good genes” are, obviously controversial claims that justifiably inspire great rancor. But it’s just completely senseless to hear the first kind of claim and immediately insist that it’s the second kind of claim.

Genes are transmitted through individuals, recombined in offspring, and shuffled continuously across generations. Yes, there are genetic similarities that are passed down in lines of familial lineage, and we have grafted the concept of races onto certain consistent features shared between people of closer genetic relationship; epicanthic folds are found consistently in people of recent East Asian ancestry and not in people of recent African ancestry, and the way that feature is passed down is through our genomes. There are patterns of allele frequency that correlate with geography and ancestry, patterns which reflect long histories of migration, isolation, and interbreeding. Population genetics recognizes, well, populations that may, over time, diverge in certain alleles due to selection or drift. Even then, these differences are probabilistic and usually modest. But more to the point, though, no honest person would claim that the statement “She has good genes” is a reference to someone’s racial ancestry in this sense and not to her individual genotype.

The moral danger of group-level genetic claims is not only that they are inaccurate but that they are inevitably hierarchical. To say that one group has “good genes” is to imply that others do not. It invites a ranking of human value according to biology, which is the essence of racist ideology. Even when phrased positively - “this group is naturally talented” rather than “that group is inferior” - the logic is the same. The supposed compliment carries the same essentialism: people are what their genes dictate, and their potential is limited or enhanced by racial ancestry. By contrast, recognizing that individuals differ genetically need not carry any such baggage. It is a basic observation of biology that no two people are identical. Acknowledging individual genetic variation also allows for an essential type of compassion - the understanding, for example, that talents and aptitudes emerge from a mix of nature and nurture, including the kinds of talents and aptitudes that can secure financial stability or make it inaccessible. Indeed, this was the moral impetus for writing The Cult of Smart, my experience in the education system demonstrating to me how individuals suffered from a lack of intrinsic academic talent that was not acknowledged by the system, leaving them to be categorized as losers for conditions out of their control.

To say that an individual has “good genes” is to comment, however loosely, on a personal quirk of inheritance; to say that a race has “good genes” is to collapse that individuality into stereotype, to erase personhood in favor of typology. One is an observation about biology, the other an assertion of ideology. Confusing the two does more than spread pseudoscience; it props up the oldest and most destructive myth in modern history, the notion that humanity can be divided into higher and lower breeds. To my dismay, both the worst people on the right and the most self-righteous people on the left routinely muddle the line between individual and group genetic differences, when that line is in fact conceptually and scientifically simple. If I’m being repetitive here it’s only because I have had to repetitively explain and defend this distinction for the past half-decade, in defense of a set of arguments that are ultimately profoundly progressive.

Understanding the science of genes should make us humble, not haughty. It reveals how random and contingent our own traits are, how much of what we call “talent” comes down to luck of birth and circumstance. You can only understand that latter part, though, if you’re willing to acknowledge that we are not all blessed with the same inherent traits, abilities, and advantages. This is why I say that a refusal to grapple with the influence of individual genetic endowments leads to political conservatism - if we can’t speak plainly about how some people are more genetically lucky than others, we contribute to the false notion that individuals have total control over their outcomes, to bootstraps mentality. We don’t choose our genomes and thus can’t choose all of our fate. The genes we have are simply the ones that survived, were shuffled through countless ancestors, shaped by time, environment, and chance. They do not confer moral worth or cosmic hierarchy. They make us human, all in the same way… but not all to the same effect. Genes do not and cannot change our legal rights, our intrinsic human value, or our entitlement to society’s help when society’s help is needed. But genes certainly do influence individual success, like who makes millions in the NFL, who becomes a research physicist, who plays violin for the New York Philharmonic - and, as it happens, who has big bazoongas.
