Matt Yglesias had [a good post recently](https://www.slowboring.com/p/more-streaming-video-is-bad) stating what should be the obvious: the seemingly endless expansion of short-form video into more and more of the waking hours of ordinary people has obvious destructive consequences.

> *most* of what people consume on TikTok or YouTube or any of Meta’s properties is not social at all. It’s just media. The companies agglomerate huge amounts of video, and then feed you videos based on what they think will keep you clicking and scrolling. These companies **invest a lot of effort and expertise in fine-tuning this process so that users find it enjoyable on a moment-to-moment basis and spend a lot of time watching these videos**. \[emphasis added\]

If anything, the bolded portion undersells things. This year’s book *[Careless Peopl](https://read.macmillan.com/fib/careless-people/)e* by former Meta employee Sarah Wynn-Williams, which Meta tried very hard to keep from public view, provides direct insider knowledge that the company intentionally used its platform and algorithms to manipulate user behavior, emotions, and perception of reality in pursuit of profit. I mean, this has been obvious for a long time, right, but this kind of actual from-the-horse’s mouth corroboration is really important. What distinguishes many of the concerns of the current moment from technological fears of the past is that **these systems are individualized and reactive, enabling them to become far more addictive than older technologies like television.** (I would also argue that the television panic was justified! A lot of people really did waste tons of their lives addicted to old-fashion linear TV, in a deeply sad way.)

You may contrast Yglesias’s piece with [this one from my friend David Sessions](https://www.hdavidsessions.com/p/why-tech-moral-panic-matters), who has been chewing at this question from the opposite direction lately. The piece, titled “Why Tech Moral Panic Matters,” argues that the current widespread anxieties about technology (especially concerns over loneliness, smartphones, and social media, which he terms “neo-atomization discourses”) have coalesced into a moral panic that is being used to promote social conservatism. He argues that, even among liberals, the language of “epidemics” and “addiction” about tech-driven alienation has replaced explicit moralizing, creating an uncritical consensus that technology is an overwhelming force we must be saved from. Sessions contends that this monocausal focus on technology as the source of social breakdown serves as a Trojan horse for right-wing tropes. He argues that this tech panic is not new, tracing similar “alienation panic” tropes in the 1950s that shaped liberal discourse. In particular, Sessions challenges the factual basis of the panic, claiming that scientific research on the “loneliness epidemic” and the link between phones/social media and poor mental health is highly contested, and that “porn addiction” is a non-medical rebrand of religious moralism.

There are much worse versions of this, but still - I object! I in fact have two objections, one to a very specific tactic here and another far broader, to the larger world of “stop worrying about technology/modernity” essays. The first is pretty simple, and was best voiced by [LM Sacasas](https://theconvivialsociety.substack.com/), who has diligently and intelligently worked this beat for decades. Sessions does the “we’ve had moral panics before” routine in his piece. Sacasas [succinctly rejected](https://thefrailestthing.com/2014/03/30/10-points-of-unsolicited-advice-for-technology-writers/) that thinking back in 2014:

> Do not cite apparent historical parallels to contemporary concerns about technology as if they invalidated those concerns. That people before us experienced similar problems does not mean that they magically cease being problems today.

Indeed: *the fact that people have had fears in the past, whether they turned out to be founded or not, does not automatically undermine the fears we experience today.* The fact that we have had technological problems in the past does not make those problems not problems or our own problems not problems. And the fact that skeptics so often reach for this particular type of argumentative kabuki makes me deeply suspicious of the ultimate source of their skepticism.

And then there’s the broader milieu here. I am increasingly frustrated by what I would call the *digital immune system*. It’s this tendency where worries over the deleterious effects of technology are met with kneejerk defense, a kind of [isolated demand for rigor](https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/08/14/beware-isolated-demands-for-rigor/) that holds those fears to higher evidentiary standards than most sociocultural claims are held to and reflexively questions the motives of those who are worried. It’s the impulse to dismiss any critique of the new, any hesitation toward the latest technology, any skepticism toward the modern turn, any wistfulness for the past, as the cranky mutterings of the old. If something new develops in culture or technology or the economy, and some concerted effort is made to question the impact of that development, you can bet that there’s a condescending “Well, actually!” coming that insists that everything new is good and every skeptic is just an old fogey. This is most prominent and obvious when it comes to technology, but note that it can happen for all kinds of new developments. Making this all the more annoying, this is often inflected with a particularly clumsy form of faux-political argument that assumes that the progressive or left stance is always in pursuit of the new and the conservative or right stance always in defense of the past, which is wrong and which I will discuss later on in this piece.

At its worst - and I don’t think David is anything like the worst, for the record - this all represents one of the most tedious and self-satisfied reflexes of our era. It’s also remarkably promiscuous, voiced by people from all manner of different ideological bents and coming from all manner of directions, joined together only by a strange and reflexive desire to defend the latest development from various forms of cultural criticism. In columns at the most hallowed newspapers as well as in the most niche Substacks, in the discourse around social media and around the Fed, in technology writing as well as religious commentary, in cultural analysis and sports analytics, you’ll find it. And frankly,it’s very annoying!

The assumption is that to resist the latest development in any form - to look at an emergent technology, a prominent fad, a much-ballyhooed “new paradigm,” the latest sad practices of the kids, the most recent ugly turn in this recent ugly time for our society, and say, “wait, maybe this isn’t good” - is to be a Luddite, a reactionary, a grumpy old fogey incapable of adjusting to progress. Such attitudes are reflexive and ubiquitous, come from all manner of ideological and philosophical directions, and tend to be express in the most meme-and-one-liner-heavy way imaginable.

To show my cards here, I suspect that this tendency isn’t even actually about defending progress, but rather about defending *the* *self*. Specifically, it’s about the terror that educated modern people feel at the prospect of appearing old, old and therefore irrelevant. A studied, ideological commitment to newness in and of itself is stupid, but at least principle-bearing, idea-loaded. No, the kind of kneejerk defense of the now that I’m talking about isn’t even that substantive. It’s not the newness of things that excites these defenders of the latest thing so much as the youthfulness that (they hope) newness represents. They mock the “old man yelling at cloud” because they can’t bear to think that one day they might be that man themselves, because they already feel the stiff joints of irrelevance creeping in. And the best defense is a good offense.

If you spend any time reading modern cultural criticism and journalism (and I’ve spent too much) you’ll notice how anxious the writers who craft them are to present themselves as with it, current, digitally native, part of the zeitgeist. A type of people who once built their careers by reading books now ostentatiously declare that no one reads anymore and that this is fine. (A classic bit of “everything new is good,” that, the dismissal of concerns about [the decline in reading](https://jmarriott.substack.com/p/the-dawn-of-the-post-literate-society-aa1).) The same people who once dismissed any complaints about reality TV now cheerfully call TikTok “a new kind of storytelling.” They’ll tell you that video essays and AI-generated images are the future of intellectual culture, not because they really believe this, but because to dissent would mark them as fossils. They rush to accept each new turn in cultural or technological evolution as inevitable, and therefore good, because they’re frightened of being left behind; that doing so enables them to theatrically look down on others in the competitive world of elite social jockeying is a nice bonus.

And that’s what this all comes down to: social competition and fear. The competition to appear to be the coolest and most forward-thinking person and the fear, the fear of irrelevance. Both become more acute in a culture that increasingly sees aging as a fate worse than death. (But there I go again!) Fear of becoming passé and the desire to be able to accuse your rivals of being out of date. Fear of waking up one day and realizing the culture no longer speaks your language, and the pleasure of condemning others for being out of fashion. We’re ruled by intellectuals who are terrified of being seen as the old guy who doesn’t get it, and in modern intellectual culture, every personal anxiety becomes a weapon to use to bash the competition. Too often our thought leaders don’t defend the new because they love what the new has brought but because it flatters them to think they’re still part of the future.

This is the hell of cultural commentary in the 21st century: every anxiety that a given commentator fears might be true about themselves gets turned into a weapon to wield against everyone else, projection as the intellectual’s obsession.

Here’s the thing, gang: sometimes, older conditions really *were* better. There are scenarios where this is effectively indisputable; nobody prefers today’s college tuition rates to those of the American mid-century, even though the causes of that increase and what to do about it are hotly debated. Few people prefer the atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration of 2025 to that of 1800, as controversial as climate politics are in general. But it goes deeper than the lowest-hanging fruit. Sometimes older technologies, older social arrangements, older expectations produced richer, healthier, more human conditions than what replaced them. And, yes, while there’s a lot of useless nostalgia out there, there’s also a broad, ubiquitous, and childish refusal among many modern essayists to accept that some change is regression. We know this to be true in many other areas of life, and yet somehow when it comes to culture or technology, we’re supposed to believe that every “innovation” is progress.

Yes, there is a lot of toxic nostalgia out there too. But that is a known, named problem that people complain about constantly. The obverse, I claim, is just as common and more pernicious but has nothing receives nothing like as much attention or criticism.

As I said above, a lot of this is bound up in a dopey misapprehension about what defines the political poles, what we mean by “left” and “right.” The notion that the political left is the natural defender of everything new while the right is the reflexive guardian of the old is one of the most tedious and intellectually bankrupt clichés in our discourse. It reduces all political and cultural disagreement to a meaningless aesthetic binary: change versus tradition, youth versus age, novelty versus nostalgia. In reality, some of the most profound and enduring left-wing movements in history (labor organizing, agrarian socialism, anti-consumerist environmentalism, the communalism of early Marxists and anarchists) were rooted in the recovery of older ways of being, older forms of solidarity, and older moral economies destroyed by so-called capitalist progress. Whether those things are *good* or not is not the point; the point is that they are indisputably *left.* The arch-capitalist preferences of conservatism, meanwhile, frequently point in the direction of “creative destruction” and madcap change. The idea that skepticism toward the latest app, social fad, or techno-solutionism marks one as politically conservative is not analysis but bullying, a way to shame people out of raising legitimate concerns about what “progress” has cost us. It’s a cudgel masquerading as sophistication, and it allows shallow thinkers to mistake their fear of seeming old for political conviction.

The problem is not change itself, which is inevitable. The problem is the unthinking worship of change, the automatic belief that newer equals better, that to resist is to sin against the god of progress. History gives us no reason to accept that dogma. The twentieth century alone offers countless examples of advances that turned out to be nightmares: the misapplication of genetic research that led to forced sterilizations of the unwanted, the industrial practices that poisoned the air and water, the car culture that disfigured our cities, the mass media that hollowed out civic life and left us with two warring cultural camps that are incapable of governing. The notion that the mere fact of something being new gives it moral authority is one of the dumbest ideas human beings have ever come up with, and yet it remains an unspoken but prevalent assumption.

The idea that all resistance to modernity is just nostalgia is itself a modern invention, a rhetorical bludgeon invented to protect the ideology of progress from scrutiny. It’s a way of pathologizing dissent: if you don’t like where we’re going, it’s not because you’ve seen the destination and found it wanting, it’s because you’re irrationally attached to the past. You can’t possibly have reasons; you only have feelings. But that’s a lie. (And yes, tu quoque, I’m guilty too, I get it.) There are plenty of sound reasons to reject elements of our present condition: the digitization of everything, the destruction of attention spans, the commercialization of private life, the constant ambient surveillance, the relentless degradation of the environment, the death of human connection. To call such objections nostalgic is to dodge the question entirely.

I’ve written, in the past, that I think the 1990s [were an objectively better time to be alive](https://freddiedeboer.substack.com/p/its-so-sad-when-old-people-romanticize) than now. There are of course a thousand caveats and provisos and exceptions and downsides. Certainly there are a number of diseases and disorders that are a lot more survivable than they once were. But it’s also the case that the vast majority of the work of reducing the death rate from infectious diseases was achieved long before the 1990s, and given that racial progress has stalled out in recent decades, the hoary old “Prefer the past? You just love Jim Crow, huh?” stuff gets less and less salient over time. If nothing else, I’d like to fight for the idea that we’re free to perform such an analysis, that the very concept of a pro-and-con list approach to past and present is legitimate, without being told that the very attempt is inherently pathological, conservative, or irrational. Maybe my analysis is wrong! Maybe the things I value are dumb. Maybe I’m not applying the correct weighting to my pluses and minuses. Maybe. But I can actually do that math myself, guys. It is not in fact the case that simply by trying I am guaranteed to collapse into false memory and toxic romanticism.

What’s more, this cult of newness depends on an idea that’s both false and pernicious: that the future is inevitable, yhat there’s no alternative to the march of progress, and therefore the only intelligent thing to do is to keep up. You see this logic everywhere; “AI is coming whether you like it or not,” “The Internet is the future of culture, deal with it.” But as [the great bard of technological-cultural change told us](https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/232117-there-is-absolutely-no-inevitability-as-long-as-there-is), nothing humans do is inevitable. Everything we have ever built could have been built differently, or not at all. The choices that created our current reality were human choices, and they can be replaced with other human choices. If we’ve built a world of dehumanizing technology and atomized loneliness, we can build a different one… or rediscover an older one that worked better. But that would require admitting that the past contains lessons we might have forgotten, and for a generation of intellectuals desperate to seem modern, that’s intolerable. That admission would entail acknowledging that there are things worth conserving, that progress is not a one-way street, that sometimes the best way forward is to turn around. It would mean admitting that being older - having seen things before, remembering how they used to be - can actually be a source of wisdom rather than embarrassment.

Instead, they shout “OK boomer,” not because they’re confident in their modernity but because they fear they’re halfway to being boomers themselves.

What would it look like to reject this reflex? To simply evaluate things on their merits, old or new, without the moral panic of aging hanging over us? It would look like honesty. It would look like the courage to say, “This isn’t progress. This is just change, and change isn’t always good.” It would mean acknowledging that in some ways, life was better when people read more, when they spent less time online, when social life wasn’t mediated through screens. That isn’t reactionary. It’s human. And sensible, frankly, in a very direct and simple sense; the biggest advantage of the present, after all, is that in the present we have access to all the wisdom and genius of the past, should we choose to access it. \[Insert ironic contemplation on the digital availability of past wisdom here.\]

In the end, the rush to embrace the new is often less about hope than despair: despair that the past can’t be recovered, despair that one’s best years are already gone, despair that the only way to escape one’s unhappiness is to keep running toward the next upgrade. The people who laugh loudest at the nostalgists are the ones most desperate to believe that the future will save them. But it won’t. The future doesn’t care about you! It’s just the next version of the same software, the next round of loneliness. And sometimes lonely people unthinkingly embrace the future because they’ve been so unhappy in the past. I have great sympathy for them. But I’m no longer willing to pretend that living that way is any more rational or constructive than spending your whole life in an imaginary past.
