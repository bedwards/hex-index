As a man with a seven month old baby, I spend a lot of time typing with one hand, or no hands, while holding a bottle clutched between my neck cheek and my shoulder in just exactly the way we used to do with old corded telephones. Babies strain a lot of things - and, yes, he is still severely straining our capacity to operate without sleep - and one thing my Junho has definitely strained is my ability to do various basic life tasks with my hands full.

Which means I rely heavily on voice-to-text, or speaking into my phone and having it turn my speech into writing - usually for text messages, occasionally for emails, and too often for argumentative Substack Notes. Now voice-to-text is not a technology that’s treated as some sort of vanguard tech, early adopter tech; in fact it’s the kind of affordance that most of us just casually assume is mature and reliable. And yet! I have tested this proposition across multiple empires of consumer electronics - a Samsung Galaxy, a Google Pixel, and my wife’s iPhone, all of them expensive flagship phones - and I can confidently report that in all cases the voice-to-text tech is janky, inconsistent, and a constant source of absurd errors. My friends and family are used to receiving texts like “buy more brain jelly baby later?” or “goat not sleep now why” - both of those are real examples, for the record - and they just nod along, like, “Poor thing, just gets no sleep with his baby, he’s losing his mind.” And, you know, it’s fine. They are patient with me and I am apologetic to them. That’s kind of the societal deal we’ve made with so many technologies: we’ve all quietly agreed to pretend this stuff works. But it doesn’t work. And if we can’t get a phone to correctly hear “bring wipes” instead of “brain wives,” then maybe, just maybe, we’re not ready for all sorts of other incredible technological feats that are perpetually just around the corner.

Take recent assurances that we have reached the magical age of real-time machine translation - you talk, “AI” (algorithms) translate what you say for the other person, they speak, they do the same for you. This concept has floated around for many years as a kind of holy grail of next-level smartphone functionality, with companies like Microsoft and Google prominently involved in such efforts. (People have used Google Translate in an effort to navigate their spring break in Ibiza for years, though the quality of this translation has always been… rough.) But recently many people have declared live translation solved. Why? Because Apple has made a big deal of incorporating such functionality into their latest AirPods and iPhones, and the tech press goes along with whatever Apple says - if they act like it’s something an iPhone can do, the gadget media will simply assume that it’s something an iPhone can do. But this is pretty bizarre if we locate machine translation in the broad world of “artificial intelligence” - Apple has notoriously shit the bed when it comes to fulfilling their AI promises. But Apple can always rely on the tech press to act as an unpaid marketing arm for their products, and people fell in line.

Unfortunately, it appears that the tech is janky, inconsistent, and not nearly as functional as brief pre-tailored industry showcases would suggest. And here we get back to voice to text: your machine translation can’t work if it first fails to translate your own speech into text it can translate! Thus we have the recent proliferation of shop owners and taxi drivers staring incomprehensibly at iPhones as they garble out speech that sounds like the lingua franca of the given country and yet has no earthly rhyme or reason to it. And there’s a growing body of YouTube and TikTok videos that demonstrate this failure. The question for me is, like… why? Why do we put up with this? What is happening that compels us to keep pretending that broken technology works? Here I come back to my same old song: we’re living in a half-century-plus long period of technological stagnation, but our art and culture industry has insisted to us that exponential scientific and technological growth are our birthright, so to confront the cognitive dissonance, we’ve simply decided to believe that tech works better than it does.

There’s a long, proud tradition in the tech press of telling you that something works when it very obviously does not work. This tradition goes back decades, and like all noble traditions, it persists no matter how many times it humiliates those who practice it. A company releases a product that’s supposed to “revolutionize” something, a bunch of people who spend their lives live-blogging product launches dutifully line up to say it has revolutionized something, and then the rest of us, the saps, discover that the thing doesn’t even do what it says on the box. It’s a story as old as Wired magazine.

Tech journalism has this weird condition where “coming soon” and “already here” are synonyms. You’ll read an article about some augmented reality glasses that can “seamlessly overlay digital information onto your world” and think, “Wow, that’s neat,” until you realize that “seamlessly overlay” means “flicker weirdly while the battery lasts for six minutes.” There’s this permanent slippage between what’s real and what’s promised. That slippage is not a bug but a feature. It’s not that the tech press doesn’t know better. They do. Every person writing about some new wearable that can “track your emotional well-being” knows that, in reality, it’s going to misinterpret your brisk walk as an anxiety attack. But there’s no structural incentive to slow down and wait until something works before you declare that it does, not in these sad last days of clickbait media where actual humans tried to scrounge for whatever pennies they can find in the trash heap before the internet is so awash in slop there’s no professional media left anymore. The incentives all run the other way: toward hype, toward optimism, toward the belief that the future is here, if only you’d preorder it.

After all, what’s the business model of tech journalism? Report on new tech, attract clicks from people who want to believe that the new tech is magic, sell ads… to the companies that make the tech you’re hyping. That’s the circle of life. The clicks only come if your article is the text version of YouTube thumbnail face, and the cozy relationship with tech firms who might advertise their wares on your site are obviously helped if you give those wares positive reviews. Professional YouTubers are operating under brutally competitive conditions on a platform that’s increasingly intent on stealing back every dime they make, and I say that as someone who is not exactly inclined to sympathize towards people in that profession. There’s no room in that reality for, “Actually, this doesn’t really work yet.” Being the voice of reason doesn’t pay as well as being the voice of anticipation. The boring truth about most new gadgets - that they’re half-broken, wildly overpromised, and ultimately exist to raise stock prices rather than rake in revenue - is not what gets clicks.

So the coverage bends toward fantasy. You get headlines like “Apple’s Vision Pro Will Replace Your Laptop” from someone who would never dream of getting rid of their MacBook or “This Robot Dog Can Feel Love” from someone who doesn’t actually believe that or “Soon You’ll Never Have to Type Again” from someone whose next video will be a roundup of gaming keyboards. And people believe all of it, for a while, until reality comes knocking. Then the next thing comes along and the same people who told you the last one would change the world tell you this one will. It’s the Groundhog Day of hype.

Nowhere is this clearer than with AI. If there were an Olympic event for declaring victory too early, the AI industry would sweep the podium. Large language models have indeed made incredible progress in a very limited and contextual set of texts. I’m not denying that. But the sheer volume of breathless, barely-literate enthusiasm about AI would make you think we’re six months away from replacing human cognition entirely. Every week some piece appears saying, “Actually, ChatGPT is about to cure cancer and make art obsolete and also babysit your kids.” Every week the same thing happens: someone asks the AI to count to three, it gets it wrong, but the bubble of techno-rapture never pops. Why? Because we need AI to work. We’ve pinned too much of our collective fantasy of progress on it. The economy doesn’t have any other narrative engines left. We’re in a sluggish, financialized world where most “innovation” is some new app for food delivery, a slightly-thinner and significantly-worse laptop, or an orange iPhone. And so, when AI came along and actually looked like a paradigm shift, we decided (consciously or not) that it simply had to be real. Hey, someone has to be consuming all this slop!

We’ve built an entire cultural and financial edifice on that decision, on the choice believe rather than being forced to accept the sadder, slower, staler reality. Stock valuations depend on it. Institutional investors depend on it. Tech workers and founders depend on it. The whole fucking economy depends on it. It’s not that people looked at the evidence and concluded AI will save us; it’s that people looked at the stagnation everywhere else and decided it must save us. This is what I call the tail wagging the dog: when the need for technology to advance becomes itself a kind of evidence of technology’s advance, in our media and our collective headspace. We are communally manifesting belief in AI the way a cult manifests belief in the apocalypse: not because the signs are convincing, but because the alternative is too depressing to contemplate.

This is why every glitch is treated as charming rather than catastrophic, why the absolutely mountainous evidence of relentless AI hallucinations routinely gets dismissed as irrelevant. (Hallucinations, by the way, which even OpenAI admits are mathematically inevitable.) When a chatbot hallucinates fake court cases or an image generator gives you a child with seven fingers, our social media hivemind doesn’t respond with caution and concern but with delight. “Look at how creative it is!” No one in the hype ecosystem can afford to say, “This is evidence that we’re still nowhere near the technology we’re pretending to have.” Again, the tail is wagging the dog: the need for tech to work outpacing the reality of what tech can do. Which is particularly scary in reference to technology that, we’re told, will soon fly our airplanes, replace our doctors, and conduct our wars.

And it’s not just the tech press. The broader media, the finance world, academia, even government policy… everyone’s implicated in the same mass suspension of disbelief. You can’t sell “steady, incremental progress” to an investor. You can’t put “mildly useful, sometimes unreliable” in a headline. Nobody gets tenure because they identify a technology as promising but several decades away. And you can’t rally the public around a vision of the future that involves a lot of debugging. The future has to shimmer. It has to sparkle. And so we pretend it does, even when the shimmer is just marketing dust in the air.

None of this means AI is fake or useless. Some of it really is remarkable, though in just about all cases it has limitations, contexts, and qualifications. But if you want to understand why people keep insisting that broken things already work, you have to understand that belief itself is the product now. Belief is what sells. Belief is what keeps the machine running. And when the economic and psychological incentives all depend on the next miracle, there’s no room for saying, “Maybe this one needs another few years in the lab.” It’s an old story, told again with better graphics and ever-more sweat desperation. In the 1990s, it was the Internet. In the 2000s, it was mobile. Now it’s AI. Each time, the same cycle: breathless hype, inflated expectations, disillusionment, and then the slow settling into reality. The only difference now is that we’ve bet the entire market - and maybe our sanity - on skipping the “disillusionment” phase.

But reality always shows up eventually. And when it does, the same people who told you your toaster would soon be sentient will write long essays about what went wrong that conspicuously avoid actually admitting that they were wrong, which continue to kick the can down the road as a means of avoiding confronting failure, and which insist that this time, their timelines and schedules and projections are real, this time Ray Kurtzweil really will become immortal, this time we will reach AI nirvana. And then, a few months later, they’ll be back with headlines about quantum computing or brain implants or some new “AI-powered toothbrush” that’s definitely, totally, this time for real, the future, the beautiful future, our beautiful future, our escape from the ordinary.
