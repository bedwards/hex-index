Full circle story:

It was a full-circle moment for one soon-to-be medical school graduate.

Shay Taylor-Allen learned last Friday that she had matched with her first-choice residency pick, Yale School of Medicine, a hospital that has played an outsized role in her life.

Taylor-Allen wasn’t just born there, she also worked there as a janitor when she was 18, she told ABC News.

The 32-year-old shared a video clip of her joyous reaction to being matched on Instagram, where it has quickly gone viral with more than 3.7 million views and counting.

“I was jumping up and down to the point I think the concrete was going to break,” Taylor-Allen told ABC News of her excitement.

Taylor-Allen, who attends Howard University College of Medicine, said it feels “surreal” to be returning to her hometown of New Haven, Connecticut, for her upcoming residency.

“I am still just feeling like I’m in a dream, because I could have never imagined that I’ll be going back to the same hospital I was not only born at, but a janitor at, to be a doctor for my community,” she said.

Taylor-Allen said she didn’t always know she wanted to go to medical school and was motivated to become a doctor after her experience helping her mom when she was sick.

Switching:

Are you the same religion right now as the one you were raised in? I bet for readers of my newsletter, “switching” is probably a whole lot more common than among the public at large. Why do I make that assumption? Well, all you wonderful folks who manage to find your way here are not just a random sample of all Americans. Religion has to matter to you at some deep level. Which means you are, statistically speaking, outliers.

The more I look at data, the more I’m convinced that this is not the median American. In fact, when it comes to something so basic as whether an individual is retained in the religion of their youth, what I’ve come to conclude is that inertia may be one of the most powerful forces in the social world. It’s just easier to stay the same than it is to change. …

I’ve categorized retention here in a broad way. If someone moves from one type of Protestant to another type of Protestant, that’s not a switch. Here, I’m interested in movement between major religious families, not denominational reshuffling. But if someone becomes Catholic, non-religious, or anything else besides Protestant, that counts as a change in religion.

Using this criteria, the retention rate of the entire sample in 1973 was a whopping 89%. Yup—just about one in ten Americans had changed religions in the early 1970s. Today’s retention number is 74%, which is probably surprisingly high for folks who read this newsletter. If you grabbed four Americans right now and asked if they were still affiliated with the same religion today as the one from their youth, just one would report a change. Inertia is strong! …

Evangelical retention has been remarkably stable. It was 77% in the early 1970s and has fallen less than two points over the last 45 years, sitting at 75.6% today. In contrast, mainline Protestant retention has taken a nosedive—from 78% in 1973 down to 57% today. That helps explain why the mainline is shrinking so rapidly.

For the Black Church, the story is also sobering. Retention was very high in 1973 at 84%, but today it’s about 65%. That puts it slightly above the mainline, but statistically similar to American Catholics. …

Retention has dropped sharply for Catholics and Protestants—down 21 points and 17 points, respectively—while non-religious retention has more than doubled. That’s a tectonic shift in American religion.

There’s a lot to ponder here. A few things stand out:

  1. Inertia still rules American religion. Despite decades of change, most Americans remain in the same broad religious group they were raised in. Switching gets attention, but stability is still the norm.

  2. Decline is about retention, not just recruitment. Catholics and mainline Protestants are shrinking largely because they lose people raised in those traditions. Evangelicals stand out mainly because they’ve been much better at holding onto their own.

  3. The non-religious have become just as “sticky” as Protestants. In the past, most nones came from religious homes; today, most were raised without religion. Non-religion is no longer just an endpoint—it’s increasingly a starting point.

One of my favorite Substacks is by John Hawthorne, a sociologist (no surprise once you read the following clip):

Still, I was surprised in reading the paper on Saturday to see an op-ed titled “America is sociologically ignorant, and it shows.” It was written by San Jose State sociologist Megan Theile Strong and originally printed in the Chicago Tribune. I don’t normally expect to see pieces about sociology in my local paper (or any paper, for that matter). She writes:

For those of us who make it to college, only 7% to 10% of students take an introduction to sociology course. Women make up about 75% of majors. Other social sciences, such as economics (Trump) and political science (Vice President JD Vance) dominate the elite circles of policymaking. Compared with economists, sociologists are considerably underused in federal and state governments.

We are sociologically ignorant, and it shows.

The impacts of this exclusivity of higher education and deprioritization of sociology are damning. White supremacy, anti-LGBTQ+ bigotry and misogyny are making comebacks while the wealth gap is wider than ever. People are banning books while conspiracy theories thrive. Nearly one in five Americans believed in QAnon four years ago, an alt-right extremist fantasy that was as popular as some major religions. Could it be that so many people track down false roots to problems they see in society because they lack access to critical education and sociological insight? That certain sectors of our public are easy to rally against higher education because they have been widely excluded from it?

If we invest more in our schooling systems and sociology, we could be as inspired by truth, justice and equity as we are by alternative facts.

Our collective conversations could be more informed, expansive and democratic. We could vote differently.

To be sure, educational systems need to be reformed, not romanticized. For too long, they have hoarded opportunities for the already advantaged. But dismantling them in the name of ideological purity is not reform. It’s sabotage. The answers to the problems that plague our society need better-funded, more accessible and more sociological education.

Professor Strong takes a pretty standard sociological approach here. Acknowledging inequality as a central feature of late capitalist society, she addresses the role of race, heterosexism, economics, and coercive control. If only, she argues, people had better sociological education, they wouldn’t be so reactionary. She rightly observes that other disciplines like economics and political science don’t offer the same kinds of tools desperately needed today.

Bobby Gilles:

I recently wondered aloud to a woman ministry colleague why some women in complementarian spaces stay silent, even if they don’t personally believe that God commands women never to teach the Bible to men. She said:

Just remember, many women have been trained not to rock the boat. The worst thing you could be is a loud, nagging, and divisive woman. (Well, the worst thing next to being promiscuous). Parents probably reinforced this for many. Churches, college ministries, mentors, etc., even more so. Over and over for years and years. It is deeply ingrained, and even more so in some women than others.

Now, I do know something about how this feels. Long-time readers know I grew up in a racist, misogynistic doomsday cult. For three decades, the “end times apostle” taught me to obey his message without question. He said that before the “rapture” happened, we would “enter the days of Ananias and Sapphira,” the couple from early Acts whom God struck dead for lying. He said that one day, someone in our church would oppose his message, and the Spirit would strike. I used to wonder, “Is it me?”

How could I speak up? How dare I risk defying the Lord’s anointed? Yet I did, twenty-three years ago, and I don’t regret it.

Still, I don’t know what that’s like as a woman. I had privileges throughout my life as a male. We were the leaders. We could become preachers. We could be bold warriors for Christ. The church officers were all male, just like us. This is a self-esteem boost that girls don’t get.

One unfortunate side effect for men is that our comfort (and the desire for unity with our message) becomes paramount. The plight of women who are restricted from doing things they know God has gifted them to do? Not so much. …

I will say, as a man, that it is time for us men to listen to the women who do speak up — the ones who say “the quiet things out loud.” It’s time for us to join them. Patriarchy imprisons us, too, with rigid expectations, broken promises, a false sense of security and superiority, “role” descriptions that many men aren’t suited for, and relationships that are far less than they could be.

What if it’s time for husbands, brothers, and friends to cowboy up and stop letting women take the attacks alone? What if, to riff on the conclusion that Beth Allison Barr writes in The Making of Biblical Womanhood, it is time for all of us to be free?

In our classrooms, for sure:

We have always known that trauma exists. What we may not have realized is how prevalent it is.

Research prior to the COVID-19 pandemic found that 61.8% of adolescents reported experiencing at least one potentially traumatic event in their lifetime. Since then, many students have faced increased grief, family instability, exposure to violence, natural disasters, and social disruption. The number of trauma-impacted learners has likely risen significantly.

Trauma is not defined simply by what happens to a child. It is defined by how the child’s nervous system responds to overwhelming stress. Events may be natural (wildfires, floods), human-made (abuse, neglect, war), or historical and systemic (racism, genocide, homophobia). What makes them traumatic is the body’s inability to cope.

When stress becomes chronic and unbuffered, it can become toxic, altering brain development, affecting learning, and shaping behaviour.

And so, whether we know it or not, trauma is sitting in our classrooms.

Pauline Naftel, a school principal at Ottawa Christian School, once shared with me the story of losing her brother in a car accident when she was in third grade. Though surrounded by a loving Christian family, she wondered whether it was even acceptable to cry. “God gives and takes away,” she had been told. The loss was rarely discussed. At school, teachers and classmates did not know what to say.

So she carried on.

Only later did she recognize her anxiety, bargaining prayers, and “good kid” behaviour as trauma responses.

Years later, as a principal, she walked her own school community through the death of a beloved staff member, and the mother of three students. There was no formal protocol. But there was presence.

Leaders visited the family. They listened and prayed. They asked how the school could follow the family’s lead. When the children returned, their classmates were gently prepared. Space was made for tears, silence, laughter, questions. Meals were delivered. Letters were sent to families with guidance on how to speak to grieving children.

The school became a community that bore witness to sorrow together.

This is what it means for a school to become a place of healing.
