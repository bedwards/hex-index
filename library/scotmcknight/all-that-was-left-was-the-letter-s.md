By Jen Ellis

*With 25 years in global missions leadership and first hand experience of moral injury, Jen Ellis works as a speaker and trainer, equipping healthcare and other frontline professionals to build trustworthy teams, navigate brokenness wisely, and lead with lasting influence.*

Hindsight continues to be one of the most effective teachers in my life. Particularly as I grow older and have more of it available to me. Hindsight shows not only the outcomes of a singular decision, but it reveals patterns when viewing multiple past processes.

This dynamic has given me much to ponder in relation to the role and value of women in society, but particularly in the church. One of these hindsight moment started over a cup of coffee but ended two years later.

Having spent most of my adult life overseas with a missions organization, catching up with friends was a highlight of holidays in the USA. During one such time, my friend and I were joined by her husband for a conversation composed of the familiar and the funny.

At one point, her husband brought up the subject of their daughter and a workplace scenario. One of her co-workers, an older man, often made belittling comments about her capabilities because she was female. She found them frustrating and painful, and her father was particularly angry over this treatment of his daughter.

Conversation then turned to women in the church, specifically related to authority, leadership and public roles. The husband’s view was that women did not have the capacity to handle the financial, spiritual, and organizational decisions that a male elders’ group often consider. I wondered at the time whether he recalled his earlier comments about his daughter in her workplace…

Although I had an egalitarian mission career, I grew up in a complementarian church. I didn’t understand this biblical restriction on women in ministry and had questions about what I saw even as a child. I was twelve when I tried to find out why women were not allowed to collect the offering at a service, but they were allowed to do the work of accounting and bookkeeping for that same money. The leader I asked about the collection protocol mumbled for a while before answering “it just doesn’t look right.” Even then, I knew that *looking right* wasn’t a suitable reason for biblical imperatives.

The mission organization with which I worked welcomed any gender or ethnicity to serve at every level of leadership. I have helped to train and disciple hundreds of people, led and pioneered ministries and training programs. My daily context was comprised of individuals from 25+ nations, and I have worked in a similar number of countries. Because of the welcome and freedom given to all individuals related to any leadership role, I do not carry any ongoing personal pain or difficulty related to this issue.

But this coffee time with friends prompted me to start writing what I thought would be a short article addressing the inconsistencies and the questions I have encountered over decades related to the roles of women in Christian contexts. But as I considered one question, it seemed that two more would raise their hands and beg to be heard. In short, what was to be an article has turned into a bit of an essay.

In the first part of this essay, I will first list the questions or situations I have experienced over the years as it pertains to women in church roles. These questions are not theoretical, but actual “boots on the ground” questions I have experienced, worked within, or have had to answer. After my years of working in a mainly Christian context, I feel that I can answer each of them easily and consistently with my view that women can carry authority and exercise influence in leadership roles. But if that is not your view on women in the church, then you need to find those real-life answers for these questions, for they spring from the lives of real people. I cannot stress enough that these questions are not rhetorical.

In the second part of this essay, I will lay out the framework I use for helping to ascertain whether any idea or decision lines up with the character and nature of God, and therefore, the ways in which I think he would make decisions and give instructions for the governance of our lives. This would include the dynamic of women in leadership roles within the church.

## Part One - Questions to be answered

1\. First and foremost, what is *the reason* for this limitation on women as far as God is concerned? It certainly is not clear in scripture, and I can see no rational nor implicational reason for this command to ban women from leadership within the church.

2\. At what age does a male child become a man and therefore no longer young enough for a woman to teach them? What about differences in an individual male’s capacity for spiritual matters as all individuals mature at different rates?

3\. Why do denominations with this policy in their home churches seem content to have female missionaries teaching and preaching in other cultural contexts? What has changed except the ethnicity or culture of the audience? Doesn’t this raise a further question?

4\. What should be done in a church planting situation when there are many women in the new church, and the few men that attend do not have the character, calling or giftings to carry out a leadership role? Do you promote a man who struggles with alcoholism and domestic violence to this role ahead of a woman who is called, gifted, and qualified?

5\. Why does it seem that in relation to women in ministry, the key issue is the same as it is in real estate: location, location, location?

> \- If a woman may speak with a man on the street about the Trinity, the gospel, or other biblical doctrines, what do I do (as a woman) if his male friend joins us? If more people gather, how many men are too many men before I must stop?
> 
> \- If we allow street preaching as acceptable, what should one do if the crowd of numerous men and women must move into a church when it starts to rain?
> 
> \- Why does the location change the validity or value of truth when taught? What changes when a woman speaks about biblical principles related to finance in a boardroom vs. a sanctuary?
> 
> \- Do these examples not indicate a sacred and secular divide?

6\. If women are more prone to error or weaker in understanding of biblical concepts, then why is the aspect of accountability disproportional to the maturity of the audience? It would seem to me that a woman speaking for 20 minutes to a public congregation with mature believers within it would be a safer place for any feedback needed than when a woman teaches a two-hour seminary lecture alone with students or preaches in a missional context (as exampled above) to unbelievers.

Or to put it another way, why do male pastors and teachers generally seem to teach those who have the most knowledge of biblical texts? Why are they not teaching those most vulnerable to error and most in need of accurate teaching?

7\. It is often stated that women are not to teach biblical doctrine to men. If doctrine is the generally accepted beliefs of the church, shouldn’t *every* believer be able to teach and pass that onto others as they grow in maturity?

8\. If we profess that women cannot teach doctrine to men, then what truths are ‘doctrine,’ and which are not? If women are to abstain from teaching doctrine to men, there must be a list of doctrinal vs. non-doctrinal topics, including how to navigate sharing the doctrine of salvation in evangelism.

9\. We must consider the impact of absence as well. If women had held leadership roles at every level for centuries, would sexual misconduct including child sexual abuse, and domestic violence have flourished as they have? Would we have created and sustained the unequal standards of sexual purity for men and women?

10\. What was the message given to the wife who was abused during the week by her husband, an elder in the church of my youth? Why did the other elders and men in the church slap him on the back and tell him what a great guy he was whilst knowing about his abuse? What was the message given to every other wife in the church? What was the message received by this couple’s daughters? By their sons?

11\. Why is the church surprised to encounter a movement of women asking society for help and recognition of their rights? They are often carrying such pain from the control, hypocrisy, injustice, and violence done and excused by Christians as “biblical submission.” How does this “biblical” treatment reflect the nature and character of God?

12\. Why do I see gifts of leadership, prophecy, teaching and training in women that show very clearly their capacity to lead hundreds or thousands? Why would God gift women in such a way if their ability to use these gifts is extremely limited?

13\. What do women do if their gifts and desires for involvement in Christian circles *are not* caring for children, baking and cooking, cleaning, and music? What if their gifts are communication, teaching, and leadership? Did God get confused when distributing gifts at their conception?

14\. Why do women carry much of the church’s work—outreach, youth, and hospitality—yet hold so little authority to make decisions about that work? How can those farthest from the day-to-day realities make the wisest decisions for those most affected by them?

15\. Is it not infantilizing to give little authority to those with much responsibility? Don’t we, as parents, work to release authority and freedom in decision making to our kids as they take on more responsibility for their lives? Are women therefore, like perpetual children within the church?

16\. Why can a woman run a business that sees over a million dollars a year pass through her hands, but upon crossing the threshold of the church, she becomes unable to bear the responsibility of deciding about a $10,000 carpeting issue? Or for that matter, a $500 printer?

17\. If women must be married to have spiritual covering, to minister but only with their husband present, and are considered to have added value as wives and mothers, then what occurs in the hearts of single women related to their worth and value? What is the view of the church of such individuals?

18\. Many of us pray every week that “God’s will be done on earth as in heaven.” If the goal of the church on earth is to recreate Eden and to mirror the New Jerusalem, how then should we define the goals of such prayers when we consider women in Christian ministry? For in Eden and heaven, Adam, Eve and all humanity are equal in every way.

19\. If women are considered inferior teachers, or more prone to deception, then what is the message we give to women when we say that a female teacher or speaker is acceptable for their gender, but not men?

20\. And if women are less in value, in capacity, weaker in all ways, and meant to serve only, then what does that say about a God who sovereignly chose their gender? Did He willingly and purposely create a gender to be the biblically mandated servant of others simply based on a DNA characteristic?

21\. Why do the exclusions, limitations and many consequences for women in the church mirror the symptoms of slavery and spiritual abuse that I have experienced, heard or read about?

22\. Why are we selective in our values over those ‘results of the fall’ issues in Genesis 3? We claim it is Eve who first sinned, and this marks all women as under the penalty of that sin at the fall and as such, they must remain in subjection to men (v. 16b.) Yet we as humans seem to actively work against the other penalties of that fall, such as safer and less painful childbirth (v. 16a,) progressive economic/farming practices to make our work more productive (vs. 17-19.)

23\. Why do I observe the same effects on women in rural Minnesota—where many churches restrict women from public or leadership roles—as I did on the mission field, counseling women from male-dominated societies who struggle with shame, identity, and a sense of value?

24\. At an AGM in a local church, an individual asked a question as to whether women were allowed to be elders. An uncomfortable silence fell over the large room, after which the convener simply went on with business. No one in the room that day received an answer about women in ministry if they were unsure. But all in that room knew that there were consequences for asking that question. Why was that question not answered or ‘allowed’ to be asked if this is a clear biblical instruction?

25\. And this issue of transparency leads to another question: If women are not biblically allowed to teach or lead men, why are the boundaries and expectations that church leadership groups have for this issue almost always unclear?

These are some of the types of questions and inconsistencies that I have experienced through almost 30 years of Christian missions and church involvement, as well as working with those who have never heard of Jesus. And as I wrote the initial questions out, I realized a need to write that framework I (most times unconsciously) use for understanding and applying the truth of what I understand God’s character to be, and how he might make those same decisions.

## Part Two – Consistency with the Character, Nature, and Ways of God

I have never been able to reconcile New Testament passages presented as limiting women in ministry with the broader witness of God’s character, nature, and consistent ways of acting. God’s actions flow from who He is, just as ours do. Therefore, I have approached these texts with the conviction that any scriptural command must be interpreted in a manner consistent with God’s revealed character and patterns of action.

As such, this essay related to women in leadership is purposely not focused on those few scriptures in the New Testament that speak about women in leadership or public roles. It is not that I dismiss them as such, but with much Christian teaching abounding both for and against women in leadership based on these scriptures alone, I felt I had to consider a bigger overview of God’s story with humanity which we have in the Bible.

It also pays to remember that we belong to a global and centuries old church. The Church’s fullest expression is not complete nor found in the local 300 member American evangelical congregation in the 21st century that you or I might attend. Most of what I read on this subject (both for and against) is focused on and the result of the experiences within this very limited and carefully controlled framework of the modern western church.

As I mentioned previously, in this second part of this essay are some concepts that I feel are true about God and his ways. I see them expressed in scripture, and have experienced them in real life, as have generations before me. I am considering the dynamics below as I express the concepts that follow:

> o logical and implicational thinking about the consequences of decisions
> 
> o how a ‘truth’ agrees or conflicts with other truths we believe about God
> 
> o how they line up with the nature, character and ways of God which have not and will never change
> 
> o a view that any of God’s instructions to us will be consistent with the whole of the wisdom we are given in scripture

## Concept 1: God should have an underlying reason for excluding women from ministry roles.

What are the underlying reasons for our benefit and good for which God ordained the exclusion of women in ministry? Or equally, what are the negative outcomes that we avoid by excluding women from leadership?

God is not in the habit of making random requests or commands. I have not established random boundaries or instructions for my children that have no purpose. For example, I do not ask them to jump on one foot five times before every meal. There would be no purpose behind such a command.

However, I have told them that they cannot step on the sidewalk outside our front yard without an adult, as it is next to a busy road, and I want them to be safe. They may not understand the underlying reasons due to child development dynamics (dying or “becoming a vegetable” are not concepts they can grasp,) but there are reasons and purposes behind my instruction.

Let me use the commandment against lying as an example. When I lie to others, it breeds mistrust. I cannot foster intimacy in relationships, my friendships will fail or be non-existent, and I will fail to thrive in my work environment.

Lying will eventually lead to societal breakdown when practiced routinely and frequently throughout that people group. The law enforcement and judicial systems will be a farce, and business dealings will be chaotic. There are *reasons for our benefit* that God has established truth-telling as a command and principle.

So how does sole male leadership benefit the church? Perhaps a more honest question is whether there are or would be negative consequences with women serving in leadership that have not also appeared under exclusively male leadership. Deception, disunity, heresy, independence, rebellion, disloyalty to God, and various forms of abuse have all occurred within church contexts led solely by men.

If women were to lead, then my experience has been and my expectation is that there would also be these failures in female leaders as there are with male leaders. Conversely, I think there would be growth and expansion in the church with women in leadership roles as there has been at times with sole male leadership.

## Concept 2: God gave men and women authority and responsibility to steward the earth and care for one another.

The second overarching issue has to do with God’s desires for the world in which we live. At the beginning of it all, we were placed in Eden. God gave both sexes the authority to carry out the responsibilities he gave at that time. Adam and Eve were equal in their roles in that garden and were to work together at that stewardship. In heaven, the New Jerusalem, we see there is no male or female, with an equality that exists regardless of ethnicity or sex. In the beginning, God gave authority and responsibility equally to both genders, and I can see no evidence that suggests he has taken that authority away from women.

We often speak of bringing the Kingdom of God to earth, to see more of God’s desires for humanity fleshed out before us on this earth. If we as Christians are to live as “little Christs,” to ensure we act and live in a way to see the answers to our Lord’s prayer that his “will be done, on earth as in heaven…,” what does that mean for this issue?

Experience has shown me that both men and women can give wisdom in a situation, be used of God to miraculously heal people, to influence and lead both genders, and show the full range of all the spiritual gifts we see in scripture. Both within and outside of the church.

If we do hold that God has taken authority to lead and teach men away from women, then it must be a global exclusion as there were no boundaries given at that moment in Eden. Therefore, there should be no women leading any men in any business, government office, or entity that is not solely female in nature. It cannot be applied to just the church.

(Anecdotally, I have seen that in Christian groups or communities where there are extreme severe needs of any kind, more freedom is given for women to lead and contribute in significant ways. These needs could be the need of the gospel, basic life necessities, extreme poverty, extreme injustice and trauma of any kind. Those involved in such a Christian context seem to give more weight and focus to the needs of those around them than the gender of the primary leader.)

## Concept 3: If I cannot disciple people in a behavior without creating many “rules,” then I should not be presenting that behavior as essential to following Christ.

For 30 years, one of my main roles has been helping people live out the theological truths that they have been taught. Bringing the theory down to a daily life level may be another way of speaking about the process of discipleship.

We should be able to follow Christ as disciples and teach others to do the same as simply as Jesus taught his own disciples. We should not need a separate rule book over any issue related to our following of Christ, such as the one the pharisees made for the Sabbath.

In the typical western church, I find we are good at teaching, but not so great at discipling people. I deeply value both, but here let me clarify the difference I see between teaching and discipleship.

Teaching is information based and is often driven by the teacher. A teacher has very little ongoing relational accountability with those taught and everyone gets the same information. It looks like sermons, bible class, bible studies and recorded teaching sessions. Teaching is most often designed to reach and change the mind.

Discipleship involves a much more relational context and for discipleship to “work”, it must be driven by the disciple’s own desire. It centers around our relationships with God and people. It involves personal questions and felt needs, and seeking understanding on how to apply that truth. It looks more like a training for a sport, teary phone calls at 10 pm, long conversations and mentoring. Discipleship is designed to change our hearts and souls.

I presuppose that what Jesus Christ required from his disciples is what he would require of any disciple, male or female, in any era of history, in any part of the world. I think those central truths related to living as a follower of Christ have not changed since he discipled that mixed bag of individuals in Israel so long ago.

With this in mind, the Christian beliefs we profess and teach to be necessary must be robust enough to apply to any and every people group on the earth, in any age past, present, or future. Otherwise, we promote a relative truth that is dependent upon personal or societal context.

Having worked within so many different churches, cultures and ethnic situations, I find that the theological view of limiting women in ministry is unworkable and unproductive. And try as I might, I find that I need a written rule book to cover each context in which this situation might occur.

I firmly believe that if a certain belief or conclusion related to biblical truth is from God, it should be simple to understand even if we struggle with the practice. A good example might be loving one’s enemies. Easy to understand, but hard to do at times!

Let us again take lying as a case study. Lying was wrong for Abraham when asked about his sister/wife 4,000 years ago in the Middle East; for a maid when asked about a theft in 16th century France; and it will be wrong for someone writing Artificial Intelligence programming 50 years from now, if robots have not taken over the world by then. All three of these people may easily discern whether they are deliberately presenting or misrepresenting the truth.

Yet when we put my above questions about women in ministry through this framework, we will need to create a rule for each context. But let us use one from the paragraph above - should females 50 years from now be able to write Artificial Intelligence programming for doctrinal subjects that will be used to teach men? If the answer is no, women should not write about doctrinal issues, what boundaries should be created and how will those boundaries be implemented?

I will need to know these answers to any and every context where women may lead men and be able to apply these answers if I am to be obedient to Gods instructions not to preach to or lead men. I have found the practical application of this ‘truth’ becomes extremely difficult once you go outside the confines and control of the church walls.

## Concept 4: God’s expectations and commands are clear and transparent.

This concept follows on from number three. But it is one of the most overlooked and unaddressed issues for this topic. Unspoken boundaries or rules breed confusion, create insecurity as expectations remain unclear, and often signal underlying leadership dysfunction in any group, whether within or outside of the church.

Any instruction or teaching that is true and from God should be transparent and without any hiddenness. All that can be known about God, we can seek to know. Yet churches with this principle restricting women in leadership do not teach on this in specifics but often have many unspoken rules as to how the “women should not lead, carry authority over or teach men.”

An example of an unspoken rule may be something like this: a woman can *sing* the Lord’s Prayer from the front of the church and invite the congregation to join her for the second time; but she could not *speak* Lord’s prayer and then lead the congregation in prayer.

Perhaps one of the reasons boundaries or rules related to restriction on women in the church may remain unspoken is simply because when they are expressed, they often sound ridiculous.

I also include here special in-house terminology that is used to communicate a rule without having to speak about it. For example, in one Christian group, I am asked to “share” a message at their public event, with the expectation that I will preach or teach. In another Christian group, when asked to “share,” I am only supposed to talk about factual things I have done. No scripture, no teaching or preaching. Unless I am told the special meaning of these words for that particular group, there is no way I can differentiate the meaning and purposes behind them and indeed, I have not been able to do so when it occurred.

## And so…

At the end of this rather long article that started with a coffee time with friends, I bring forward one last (sobering) observation related to women in church and societies. Over the years, the numerous individuals of different ethnicities and cultures that I have had the privilege to know show forth patterns of beliefs and values that stem from their worldview on this issue.

Amongst those girls and women that come from cultures and sub-cultures where females have limiting cultural expectations and roles, whether these are considered “biblical,” from another religious belief system, or cultural, I find certain outcomes are consistent, such as the following:

· women lack self-value and self-worth (In fact, in looking at an American website that listed women speakers for events, I was shocked by the sheer number whose topic was helping women recognize their value and worth.)

· women are more hesitant to pursue their God given gifts and desires for training or work

· women struggle with their identity and how God has uniquely designed them

· women have difficulty distinguishing their personal domain as an individual and establishing boundaries related to their needs, desires, and safety

· women have younger average ages related to marriage

· men treat women with less respect, and the cultures exhibit greater levels of permissiveness for the physical, emotional, mental and psychological abuse of women

We end this essay by going back to its beginning and that coffee time with my friends, where there was great disconnect between the father’s value and view of his daughter at work compared to his value and view of her or any woman once they stepped into a church.

Two years later I saw the printed program for this daughter’s wedding. One thing that captured my attention was the titles and names of the two getting married. This daughter went into the service “Miss Elizabeth Montgomery,” and came out “Mrs. Samuel Jones.” One might be forgiven for thinking that she had disappeared altogether by the end of that service, for all that was left of her was the small letter ‘s’ in the word Mrs..
