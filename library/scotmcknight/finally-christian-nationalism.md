The book has been gearing for its last chapter as one would find in a good novel, when at the end the whole story makes better sense. The case *for* the First Amendment and thus the case against the establishment of religion in the USA are the heart of Randall Balmer’s ***[America’s Best Idea: The Separation of Church and State](https://amzn.to/4mASrxx)***. His last chapter leads from yesterday’s Substack (about the nativist international policies of the current administration) directly into Christian nationalism (CN).

“When the Bible comes bundled with the Constitution, the Declaration of Independence, and the Pledge of Allegiance, as was the case with the *God Bless the USA Bible* that Donald Trump promoted for $59.99 while a presidential candidate in 2024, you can bet something more is at stake.” My money is on Balmer’s “more.” Balmer punches a nose with “Despite–or perhaps because of–the messenger, the message of Christian nationalism has been amplified in recent years, although this is nothing new in American history.” As Balmer’s tract for our times has already shown.

Confederates were upset by the absence of God and Christianity in the US Constitution enough that they drew up their own “Christian enterprise.” Lincoln wisely contended that we ought not to be revising the Constitution “hastily.”

Instead of CN proposing a new Constitution, though I’m not persuaded the current administration might not have that in mind, CN folks have two agendas. First, “To subvert the First Amendment, both by legislation and by judicial rulings” and in some states taxpayer funds fund Christian schools (cf. *Espinoza v. Montana* and *Carson v. Makin*). Second, “Simply declare that, all evidence to the contrary notwithstanding, the United States is and always has been a Christian nation.” The influence of David Barton is noticeable in the second strategy. By the way, Warren Throckmorton and Michael Coulter have taken to pieces the shabby, ideological scholarship of David Barton. Their book is called *Getting Jefferson Right: Fact-Checking Claims about Thomas Jefferson*.

The fact is that the founders intentionally and in writing made clear that “The new government should have no entanglement with religion.” They knew what they meant in the First Amendment: “Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof.” Balmer observes that CN advocates love to say that *separation of church and state* are not words found in the Constitution, and they are right about that. It comes from a letter of Jefferson. But the substance of that wall is found undeniably in the First Amendment, which is why CNers want to diminish the First Amendment. That the USA is a “Christian nation,” Balmer states (not for the first time), therefore, “falters on legal grounds.”

“Barton and his right-wing compatriots also like to assert that the founders themselves were evangelical Christians. This is so ludicrous that I’m tempted not to dignify it with a response. Many of the founders were deists, who understood God as a remote and disinterested entity.” John Adams denied the deity of Christ; Jefferson denied the supernatural and miraculous and cut those lines out of his version of the New Testament. Let’s be Scrooge-ish and say Bah, Humbug! to the claim that founders founded a Christian nation. It was, of course, Christian-ish because there were so many Christian-ish people in the colonies and country. Balmer renders this judgment: “Indeed, no founder, with the possible exceptions of John Witherspoon, Presbyterian minister and president of the College of New Jersey, and Benjamin Rush, a physician, would qualify for membership in any of the churches now advocating for Christian nationalism.”

Balmer’s thesis, which has been a solid conclusion of his for decades, is that America’s best idea is the separation of church and state, or the First Amendment, for because of them *the Christian faith has flourished in the USA*. “The disestablishment and free exercise clauses set up a free marketplace for religion in the United States, where no one faith enjoys the sanction of the state.” Evangelicalism has flourished because of these American ideas. “No group has benefited more from the First Amendment than evangelicals, the very cohort that is now trying to breach the wall of separation between church and state in the name of Christian nationalism.”

The attraction of CN to many evangelicals is their sense of victimization by the progressive, liberal media and universities and elites. “There’s a smidgeon of truth to that, I suppose … but the rhetoric of victimization, especially when combined with nostalgia for a supposed halcyon past, is catnip to white evangelicals, and one of the attractions of Donald Trump is that he speaks the language of victimization better than anyone I’ve seen. It’s always about him, of course; he’s the victim. But evangelicals recognize that vocabulary because the genius of the religious right is that its leaders have always portrayed themselves–and by extension, all evangelicals–as marginal and as victims.”

The blame has turned toward immigrants and minorities, the very opposite move found in both the Bible and America’s founding documents. We are not a majoritarian society; we are society based on laws that protect what Walter Isaacson calls the world’s greatest sentence, the heart of which is that we are all created equal.

An impulse has to be checked. We have to learn how to disagree, even with prophetic jeremiads, without turning to hatred for fellow Americans. In her book, *Prophecy Without Contempt,* about a decade ago, Cathleen Kaveny made a beautiful distinction between two words: *condemn* and *contemn*. To “condemn” means “To pronounce an adverse judgment on; to express strong disapproval of, censure, blame.” To “contemn” means “The action of contemning or despising; the holding or treating as of little account, or as vile and worthless; the mental attitude in which a thing is so considered.” We can condemn but we are not to contemn. (It doesn’t work orally.)

Let us disagree with passion and even at times prophetic jeremiads but do so with the humility of Abraham Lincoln. But let us not fall into hatred of those made in God’s image or despising someone created as our equal. Forming relationships and even conversing with one another may not change minds, but they do make for a civil America. Easy to say; difficult to do.
