**[Gotta give thanks for our teachers](https://www.npr.org/sections/goats-and-soda/2025/11/26/g-s1-98523/kindness-thankful-sympathy-goodness?utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=npr&utm_term=nprnews&utm_source=bsky.app)**:

> On Nov. 13, we marked the 27th year of “World Kindness Day,” a chance to pause, reflect and ask: How does kindness really change lives? We then invited you, our readers, to share acts of kindness that left a lasting impression, perhaps even reshaping the way you see the world.
> 
> What you sent us was compelling, heartwarming and refreshingly unexpected, reminding us that goodness is alive and well in a world often overshadowed by displays of cruelty and grim headlines.
> 
> Do small acts of kindness really matter? We’ll let your stories speak for themselves. (But the answer is yes. Always yes.)
> 
> Here are some of the highlights.
> 
> **Teacher Steps Up**
> 
> When I was in second grade, my only pair of shoes was worn out, with holes in the soles. At that time, my parents couldn’t afford to buy me new ones, so I wasn’t able to go to school. My teacher, Mrs. Wortz, called my mom to find out why I wasn’t in school. My mom told her the truth, even though it was probably embarrassing. Mrs. Wortz replied, “Get her ready for school in the morning and I will come pick her up.”
> 
> The next morning, my teacher drove me straight to a Buster Brown shoe store and bought me a new pair of shoes.
> 
> After Mrs. Wortz retired and moved to a smaller house, she called and gave me her library of books. I was an avid reader and loved books. Still do. Her kindness made a huge difference in my life. I will never forget it.
> 
> Teresa Staats  
> Ohio

**[Giving thanks today for this group of folks. May we never forget.](https://mississippitoday.org/2025/11/24/barn-emmett-till-sacred-site/)**

> The long-hidden monument to bigotry and brutality — the Mississippi Delta barn where Emmett Till was tortured and killed in 1955 — will soon become a “sacred site” for all to see.
> 
> On Monday, the Emmett Till Interpretive Center [announced](http://emmett-till.org/barn) that it had purchased the barn, thanks to a $1.5 million gift from TV producer Shonda Rhimes, who was moved to donate after reading about the barn, [saying](https://abcnews.go.com/GMA/Culture/shonda-rhimes-honor-emmett-tills-story-new-passion/story?id=105699839), “My hope is that this story never gets lost.” …
> 
> For Keith Beauchamp, producer of the “Till” film and director-producer of “The Untold Story of Emmett Louis Till,” the barn’s preservation brings mixed emotions.
> 
> “On one hand, it’s significant that a physical site connected to Emmett Till’s story will be preserved for future generations,” he said. “On the other hand, it’s also a place that represents deep pain and injustice. Regardless, it is a part of American history that must be acknowledged rather than forgotten, because remembering helps us understand and avoid repeating past mistakes.”
> 
> Beauchamp thanked Rhimes for her “generous gift to help preserve this history, especially during a time of debate over how our past should be remembered.”
> 
> He praised Jeff Andrews, who bought the property that included the barn in 1994. After learning about the barn, Andrews began to let Till’s family and other visitors spend time at the historic site.
> 
> Beauchamp praised his care, “maintaining the barn and welcoming the public, kept the site meaningful long before any official preservation began.”

**[Thankful today for Bobby Gilles](https://bobbygilles.substack.com/p/evangelical-women-preaching-is-not)**:

> You’re talking with your cousin, TheoBro Ted, and he shakes his head when you walk him through Bible passages that show women *did* preach and exercise authority in scripture, and that there is no prohibition on them doing so now. Ted shakes his head and says, “Look, I just believe what everyone believed until five minutes ago. This whole ‘women preacher’ thing is just a new feminist idea. Women didn’t get the idea until after the 1960s, when they were all burning their bras and hating on men.”
> 
> Indeed, this is what more learned complementarians express, albeit more eloquently:
> 
> > > The role of women in church ministry was simply not a burning question until it asserted itself in recent decades in conjunction with the modern women’s movement.[1](https://bobbygilles.substack.com/p/evangelical-women-preaching-is-not#footnote-1-174656616)
> 
> Is that true? And what of the related charge that only “liberal” people think women can preach (defined, by TheoBro Ted, as people who don’t believe the Bible, who don’t take it seriously, and who feel free to ignore passages they don’t like)?
> 
> What do historical trends in American evangelicalism, fundamentalism, and other conservative movements reveal about the reasons behind patterns of acceptance or refusal of a woman’s call to preach? When women are speaking, is the Holy Spirit sanctifying and sending God’s people?
> 
> Let’s dive in. We will specifically look at the preaching record of women within conservative evangelical and fundamentalist movements, to avoid a counter charge of, “Okay, maybe women preached before the late 1960s, but it was only in ‘liberal’ spaces.” *I will show that women preached in the 18th through the early 20th century, even in church movements that prized everything contemporary fundamentalists and conservative evangelicals prize about the scriptures (whether called the inerrancy, authority, or literal truthfulness of scripture).* Then we will examine one reason the tide turned after World War II.
